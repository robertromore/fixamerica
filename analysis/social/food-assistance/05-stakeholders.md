# Food Assistance: Stakeholders

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size |
|-------|--------|------|
| SNAP recipients | Direct beneficiaries of nutrition benefits | 42 million |
| Children in food-insecure households | Developmental, educational, health impacts | 13 million |
| WIC participants | Mothers, infants, young children receiving nutrition support | 6 million |
| School meal recipients | Children receiving free/reduced-price meals | 30 million |
| Seniors facing food insecurity | Elderly with limited income and mobility | 5 million |
| Food bank clients | Those relying on emergency food assistance | 53 million annually |
| Working poor | Employed but food insecure | 15+ million |
| Undocumented immigrants | Excluded from federal programs, high food insecurity | 11 million |

### Secondary Stakeholders

**Families and Communities**:

- Parents struggling to feed children
- Multi-generational households pooling resources
- Communities with high food insecurity rates
- Schools serving hungry students
- Healthcare systems treating diet-related disease

**Food System Workers**:

- Grocery store employees accepting SNAP/EBT
- Food bank volunteers and staff
- School nutrition workers
- WIC clinic staff
- SNAP caseworkers and administrators

**Healthcare Providers**:

- Physicians treating malnutrition and diet-related disease
- Pediatricians monitoring child development
- Emergency rooms treating food insecurity complications
- Community health centers integrating food and health

## Stakeholder Interests

### Beneficiary Populations

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Secondary Interest |
|-------------|------------------|-------------------|
| SNAP recipients | Adequate benefits, easy access | Dignity, choice, flexibility |
| Working poor | Benefits that don't create cliff effects | Work supports, childcare |
| Children | Adequate nutrition for development | School meal quality |
| Seniors | Home-delivered meals, easy enrollment | Social connection, health |
| Immigrants | Access regardless of status | Protection from enforcement |

### Institutional Stakeholders

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Position on Expansion |
|-------------|------------------|----------------------|
| USDA | Effective program administration | Generally supportive within budget |
| State agencies | Administrative simplicity, federal funding | Varies by state politics |
| Food retailers | SNAP sales ($60+ billion annually) | Strongly supportive |
| Food manufacturers | Market for products | Supportive of spending, oppose restrictions |
| Food banks | Adequate resources, complementary role | Supportive of public programs |
| Healthcare systems | Reduce diet-related disease burden | Increasingly supportive |
| Schools | Nutrition program funding, administrative ease | Support universal meals |

### Political Stakeholders

| Stakeholder | Interest | Current Position |
|-------------|----------|------------------|
| Farm state legislators | Maintain farm-nutrition coalition | Generally supportive |
| Urban legislators | Constituent access to benefits | Strongly supportive |
| Fiscal conservatives | Budget restraint | Favor restrictions, work requirements |
| Anti-poverty advocates | Maximum benefit and access | Favor expansion |
| Public health advocates | Nutrition quality, health outcomes | Favor expansion with nutrition standards |

## Power Dynamics

### The Farm Bill Coalition

**How It Works**:

- SNAP is 80% of farm bill spending
- Urban Democrats support nutrition programs
- Rural Republicans support agricultural subsidies
- Coalition provides mutual support
- Breaking coalition risks both sets of programs

**Tensions**:

- House Freedom Caucus challenges coalition
- Some urban progressives question farm subsidies
- Coalition has weakened in recent farm bills
- Work requirement demands threaten partnership

### Industry Influence

**Food Retail Industry**:

- $60+ billion in annual SNAP sales
- Opposes restrictions on SNAP purchases
- Supports SNAP funding
- Lobbies against online purchasing restrictions
- Resists healthy food requirements

**Food Manufacturing Industry**:

- Major beneficiary of SNAP purchasing
- Opposes nutrition standards
- Lobbies against soda/junk food restrictions
- Supports program funding
- "Food freedom" messaging

### State Administrator Discretion

**Variation Creates Inequality**:

| State Choice | Range of Options |
|--------------|------------------|
| ABAWD waivers | Request or don't request |
| Asset limits | $0 to $2,750+ |
| Recertification | 6 months to 2 years |
| Online applications | Full function to minimal |
| Call center capacity | Minutes to hours wait |
| Error tolerance | Strict to flexible |

### Anti-Hunger Advocacy

**Key Organizations**:

- **Food Research and Action Center (FRAC)**: Federal policy, research
- **Feeding America**: Food bank network, advocacy
- **Center on Budget and Policy Priorities**: Policy analysis
- **No Kid Hungry/Share Our Strength**: Child hunger focus
- **Bread for the World**: Faith-based advocacy
- **National WIC Association**: WIC program advocacy

**Strengths**: Expertise, moral authority, grassroots networks

**Limitations**: Underfunded compared to industry, sometimes fragmented

## Coalitions and Conflicts

### Natural Alliances

| Coalition | Members | Shared Interest |
|-----------|---------|-----------------|
| Anti-hunger coalition | Advocates, food banks, faith groups | Program expansion |
| Farm-nutrition coalition | Farm groups, anti-hunger advocates | Farm bill passage |
| Child nutrition coalition | Schools, pediatricians, advocates | School meal expansion |
| Public health coalition | Healthcare systems, advocates, researchers | Nutrition quality |
| Food industry coalition | Retailers, manufacturers, SNAP supporters | Program funding |

### Key Conflicts

| Issue | Pro | Anti |
|-------|-----|------|
| Work requirements | Conservatives, some moderates | Anti-hunger advocates |
| SNAP restrictions (soda, junk food) | Public health advocates | Food industry, some advocates |
| Universal school meals | Anti-hunger advocates, schools | Fiscal conservatives |
| Immigrant eligibility | Immigrant advocates, anti-hunger groups | Restrictionists |
| Benefit increases | Anti-hunger advocates | Fiscal conservatives |
