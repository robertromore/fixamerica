# Food Assistance: Implementation Roadmap

## Strategic Approach

Food assistance reform requires navigating the farm bill cycle, state implementation variation, and persistent ideological opposition. The roadmap sequences achievable wins to build momentum while pursuing structural changes that address root causes of food insecurity.

## Phase 1: Foundation (Years 1-2)

### Administrative Actions (No Legislation Required)

**Thrifty Food Plan Modernization**:

- USDA authority to update plan methodology
- Base on realistic meal preparation time (30 min vs. 2+ hours)
- Account for actual food costs and waste rates
- Projected impact: 15-20% benefit increase

**Milestones**:

- Revised methodology published within 6 months
- New benefit levels effective October Year 1
- Monitoring system for adequacy

**Expand SNAP Online Purchasing**:

- Require all states to offer online purchasing
- Expand retailer participation
- Include hot foods and prepared meals where state law permits

**Milestones**:

- Universal online SNAP availability by Year 2
- 50% increase in online-accepting retailers

**Streamline Applications**:

- Model state best practices guidance
- Fund state IT modernization
- Promote online and mobile applications

**Milestones**:

- 80% of applications submitted online by Year 2
- Reduce average processing time to 7 days

### Congressional Actions (Year 1)

**Permanent Summer EBT**:

- Build on pandemic Summer EBT success
- Automatic enrollment for school meal participants
- $40/child/month during summer months

**Milestones**:

- Legislation enacted by summer Year 1
- 30 million children receiving Summer EBT by Year 2

**ABAWD Time Limit Suspension**:

- Eliminate 3-month limit for able-bodied adults without dependents
- Remove state waiver complexity
- Redirect enforcement resources to voluntary E&T

**Milestones**:

- Legislation or administrative action Year 1
- 750,000+ people retain benefits annually

### State-Level Opportunities

**Encourage Best Practices**:

- Broad-based categorical eligibility adoption (11 states not using)
- Maximum certification periods (24 months)
- Elimination of state asset tests
- Online application improvements

**Milestones**:

- 5 additional states adopt BBCE by Year 2
- All states offer functional online applications
- Average certification period increased

## Phase 2: Building Momentum (Years 3-5)

### Farm Bill Reauthorization

**SNAP Provisions**:

- Codify Thrifty Food Plan methodology improvements
- Expand gross income limit to 185% FPL
- Eliminate asset tests federally
- Simplify recertification requirements
- Increase minimum benefit to $50/month

**Child Nutrition Provisions**:

- Lower Community Eligibility threshold to 25%
- Increase per-meal reimbursement by 50%
- Allow state-wide CEP elections
- Strengthen nutrition standards

**Senior Nutrition Provisions**:

- Increase Older Americans Act nutrition funding 50%
- Eliminate Meals on Wheels waitlists
- Support innovative delivery models

**Milestones**:

- Farm bill enacted by Year 3
- 5 million additional people reached
- SNAP participation rate exceeds 90%

### Universal School Meals Progress

**Federal Pilot Program**:

- Fund 10-state universal meals pilot
- Evaluate participation, stigma, academic outcomes
- Develop implementation best practices

**State Adoption**:

- Support state universal meal legislation
- Technical assistance for implementation
- Track outcomes across states

**Milestones**:

- 20 states offer universal school meals by Year 5
- Federal pilot demonstrates effectiveness
- National legislation momentum builds

### Integrated Application Systems

**Federal Coordination**:

- Require data sharing across SNAP, WIC, Medicaid, TANF
- Fund integrated eligibility systems
- Develop national application platform

**State Implementation**:

- Express lane eligibility in all states
- Single application access
- Automatic cross-enrollment

**Milestones**:

- 30 states with integrated systems by Year 5
- 50% reduction in administrative burden
- 10% increase in cross-program enrollment

## Phase 3: Structural Reform (Years 5-10)

### Universal School Meals

**National Program**:

- Free breakfast and lunch for all K-12 students
- Eliminate income verification
- Enhanced per-meal reimbursement
- Local food procurement support

**Estimated Cost**: $10-15 billion annually

**Milestones**:

- Legislation enacted by Year 6
- 100% school participation by Year 8
- 30 million+ children receiving universal meals

### Comprehensive SNAP Reform

**Eligibility Expansion**:

- 200% FPL gross income limit
- Remove categorical restrictions
- Eliminate work requirements entirely
- Full immigrant eligibility restoration

**Benefit Adequacy**:

- Benefits sufficient for 30 days of food
- Regional cost adjustment
- $100 minimum monthly benefit

**Administration**:

- Automatic enrollment via tax system
- 36-month certification periods
- Federal administration option for states

**Milestones**:

- SNAP serves 50+ million during normal times
- Food insecurity rate below 5%
- Administrative costs reduced 25%

### Health-Nutrition Integration

**Medicaid Integration**:

- Nutrition screening in Medicaid
- Produce prescription programs
- Medically tailored meals coverage

**Medicare Integration**:

- SNAP enrollment assistance for Medicare beneficiaries
- Nutrition services in Medicare Advantage
- Dual eligible nutrition coordination

**Healthcare System Partnerships**:

- Hospital-based food insecurity screening
- Referral systems to nutrition programs
- Food as medicine research

**Milestones**:

- 50% of Medicaid programs include nutrition services
- 10 million seniors receiving integrated services
- Measurable reduction in diet-related disease

## Risk Mitigation

### Political Resistance

**Risks**:

- Conservative opposition to expansion
- Budget constraints
- Work requirement demands
- Farm bill coalition breakdown

**Mitigation**:

- Build bipartisan rural-urban coalition
- Emphasize cost-effectiveness and economic stimulus
- Demonstrate work requirement failures
- Maintain agricultural program support

### Implementation Challenges

**Risks**:

- State capacity constraints
- IT system limitations
- Retailer participation
- Program integrity concerns

**Mitigation**:

- Federal technical assistance
- IT modernization funding
- Retailer incentives
- Enhanced monitoring systems

### Unintended Consequences

**Risks**:

- Benefit cliffs from expansion
- Food industry capture of policy
- Charitable sector displacement
- Quality concerns with universal programs

**Mitigation**:

- Gradual phase-outs rather than cliffs
- Nutrition standards maintenance
- Clear roles for food banks
- Quality monitoring and improvement

## Coalition Building

### Essential Allies

| Actor | Interest | Approach |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Anti-hunger advocates | Program expansion | Lead coalition, policy development |
| Agricultural interests | Farm bill passage, SNAP market | Maintain coalition, emphasize rural access |
| Healthcare systems | Reduce diet-related disease | Emphasize cost savings, prevention |
| Schools | Nutrition programs, simplified admin | Support universal meals |
| Food retailers | SNAP sales | Engage as allies, maintain standards |
| Faith communities | Moral imperative | Grassroots mobilization |

### Persuadable Opponents

| Actor | Concern | Accommodation |
|-------|---------|---------------|
| Fiscal moderates | Cost | Emphasize economic returns, efficiency |
| States' rights advocates | Federal control | Maintain state flexibility within standards |
| Work requirement supporters | Dependency | Voluntary E&T investment |
| Nutrition quality advocates | SNAP purchases | Education and incentives over restrictions |
