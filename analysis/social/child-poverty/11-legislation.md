# Child Poverty: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Reducing child poverty requires comprehensive legislative action at federal and state levels. The 2021 Child Tax Credit expansion proved that bold policy can cut child poverty nearly in half. This document provides model legislation for permanently expanding the CTC, creating a universal child allowance, and implementing comprehensive child poverty reduction strategies. The legislation draws on evidence from the 2021 expansion and international best practices.

## Constitutional Considerations

### Child Welfare and Federal Power

The federal government has clear constitutional authority to address child poverty through:

| Power | Application |
|-------|-------------|
| Taxing and Spending | CTC, EITC, appropriations for programs |
| Commerce Clause | Regulation affecting child welfare |
| Fourteenth Amendment | Equal protection concerns |

### Constitutional Amendment: Children's Rights

**Purpose**: Establish constitutional protection for children's basic needs.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. The right of all children within the United States to
freedom from poverty and deprivation is recognized as fundamental.
No child shall be deprived of adequate nutrition, housing,
healthcare, or education.

SECTION 2. Congress and the States shall have the power to enact
legislation to ensure that:
(a) all children have access to adequate nutrition;
(b) all children have access to stable housing;
(c) all children have access to healthcare;
(d) all children have access to quality education;
(e) families have adequate income to provide for children's
    basic needs.

SECTION 3. In all actions concerning children, the best interests
of the child shall be a primary consideration.

SECTION 4. Congress shall have the power to enforce this article
by appropriate legislation.

SECTION 5. This article shall take effect three years after the
date of ratification.

```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 1**: Establishes positive rights for children
- **Section 2**: Empowers government action; lists basic needs
- **Section 3**: Incorporates international standard (UN Convention)
- **Section 4-5**: Enforcement and implementation

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Justiciability | Courts can develop standards over time |
| Cost | Flexibility in implementation methods |
| Federalism | Both Congress and states empowered |
| Political feasibility | Build public support; generational effort |

## Federal Legislation

### American Family Act (Permanent Expanded CTC)

**Purpose**: Make the 2021 Child Tax Credit expansion permanent, providing monthly payments to all families with children.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "American Family Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) The 2021 Child Tax Credit expansion reduced child poverty
    by 46 percent, lifting 3.7 million children out of poverty.
(2) Child poverty in America affects over 11 million children,
    with rates 2-3 times higher than peer nations.
(3) The expanded CTC showed no reduction in parental employment,
    contradicting predictions of work disincentive.
(4) Monthly payments provided income stability for families,
    reducing hardship between paychecks.
(5) Expiration of the expansion caused child poverty to more
    than double, demonstrating its direct impact.
(6) Permanent expansion would provide a stable foundation for
    families raising children.

SEC. 3. PERMANENT CHILD TAX CREDIT EXPANSION.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 24 of the Internal Revenue Code of 1986
    is amended to read as follows:

"SEC. 24. CHILD TAX CREDIT.

"(a) CREDIT AMOUNT.—

    "(1) IN GENERAL.—For purposes of this subtitle, the child
        tax credit shall be an amount equal to the sum of—
        "(A) $3,600 for each qualifying child who has not
            attained 6 years of age before the close of the
            taxable year; and
        "(B) $3,000 for each qualifying child who has attained
            6 years of age before the close of the taxable year
            but has not attained age 18.

    "(2) ADJUSTMENT FOR INFLATION.—The dollar amounts in
        paragraph (1) shall be adjusted annually for inflation.

"(b) REFUNDABILITY.—

    "(1) IN GENERAL.—The credit determined under this section
        shall be allowed as a refundable credit. If such credit
        exceeds the taxpayer's liability for tax, such excess
        shall be paid to the taxpayer as a refund.

    "(2) NO PHASE-IN.—The full credit amount shall be available
        without regard to earned income.

"(c) QUALIFYING CHILD.—For purposes of this section, the term
    'qualifying child' means a child who—
    "(1) has not attained age 18 by the close of the taxable year;
    "(2) is the taxpayer's son, daughter, stepchild, foster child,
        brother, sister, stepbrother, stepsister, or a descendant
        of any such individual;
    "(3) has the same principal place of abode as the taxpayer
        for more than one-half of the taxable year;
    "(4) has not provided more than one-half of such child's own
        support for the taxable year; and
    "(5) is a citizen, national, or resident of the United States.

"(d) PHASE-OUT.—

    "(1) IN GENERAL.—The amount of the credit under this section
        shall be reduced (but not below zero) by $50 for each
        $1,000 (or fraction thereof) by which the taxpayer's
        modified adjusted gross income exceeds the threshold
        amount.

    "(2) THRESHOLD AMOUNT.—For purposes of paragraph (1), the
        threshold amount shall be—
        "(A) $150,000 in the case of a joint return or surviving
            spouse;
        "(B) $112,500 in the case of a head of household; and
        "(C) $75,000 in any other case.

"(e) ADVANCE PAYMENTS.—

    "(1) IN GENERAL.—The Secretary shall establish a program
        under which taxpayers may elect to receive the credit
        under this section through monthly advance payments.

    "(2) AMOUNT.—Each monthly payment shall be equal to the
        estimated annual credit divided by 12.

    "(3) RECONCILIATION.—Any difference between advance payments
        received and the final credit determined on the tax
        return shall be reconciled, with overpayments subject
        to a safe harbor to prevent hardship.

    "(4) SAFE HARBOR.—No repayment shall be required for
        overpayments up to $2,000 per return for taxpayers
        with income below $40,000 ($80,000 for joint filers).

"(f) IDENTIFICATION REQUIREMENT.—

    "(1) IN GENERAL.—No credit shall be allowed under this
        section with respect to any qualifying child unless
        the taxpayer includes the Social Security number of
        such child on the return.

    "(2) INDIVIDUAL TAXPAYER IDENTIFICATION NUMBER.—For
        taxpayers who are not eligible for a Social Security
        number, an Individual Taxpayer Identification Number
        shall satisfy this requirement.

"(g) SPECIAL RULES FOR 17-YEAR-OLDS.—Children who are age 17
    shall be treated as qualifying children for purposes of
    this section."

SEC. 4. OUTREACH AND ENROLLMENT.

(a) IRS OUTREACH.—The Secretary of the Treasury shall conduct
    aggressive outreach to ensure that all eligible families
    are aware of and can access the credit, including—
    (1) multilingual materials and assistance;
    (2) partnerships with community organizations;
    (3) simplified filing options for non-filers;
    (4) direct notification to likely eligible families.

(b) NON-FILER PORTAL.—The IRS shall maintain a simplified online
    portal for non-filers to claim the credit.

(c) NAVIGATOR PROGRAM.—There is established a grant program to
    fund community-based organizations to assist families in
    claiming the credit.

SEC. 5. REPORTING.

(a) ANNUAL REPORT.—The Secretary shall report annually to Congress
    on—
    (1) the number of children for whom the credit was claimed;
    (2) the total amount of credits paid;
    (3) estimated impact on child poverty;
    (4) program costs and administration.

(b) CHILD POVERTY TRACKING.—The Census Bureau shall publish
    quarterly estimates of child poverty rates.

SEC. 6. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated such sums as may be
necessary to carry out this Act.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall apply to taxable years beginning after
December 31 following enactment.

```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3(a)**: Restores 2021 amounts with inflation indexing
- **Section 3(b)**: Full refundability reaches poorest children
- **Section 3(d)**: Phase-out at moderate incomes preserves targeting
- **Section 3(e)**: Monthly payments provide stability
- **Section 3(f)**: Allows ITINs for immigrant families
- **Section 4**: Ensures eligible families can access credit

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Cost (~$100B/year) | ROI of 7:1; offsets elsewhere |
| Work incentive concerns | 2021 data shows no work reduction |
| Fraud concerns | Existing IRS enforcement; no 2021 fraud issues |
| Non-filer access | Portal and navigator programs address |

### Child Allowance Act

**Purpose**: Create a universal monthly child allowance modeled on successful international programs.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Child Allowance Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) Every developed peer nation provides a universal child
    benefit to families, acknowledging the cost of raising
    children and investing in the next generation.
(2) Canada's Child Benefit reduced child poverty by one-third.
(3) Universal benefits have higher participation rates, reduce
    stigma, and build broader political support.
(4) A monthly allowance provides more stability than annual
    tax credits for families managing irregular incomes.

SEC. 3. CHILD ALLOWANCE.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established a Child Allowance
    program administered by the Social Security Administration.

(b) ELIGIBILITY.—A child shall be eligible for the allowance if—
    (1) the child is under 18 years of age;
    (2) the child is a citizen or lawful permanent resident of
        the United States, or has resided in the United States
        for at least 6 months;
    (3) the child resides with a parent, guardian, or other
        qualifying caretaker.

(c) AMOUNT.—

    (1) IN GENERAL.—The monthly allowance shall be—
        (A) $500 per month for each child under age 6;
        (B) $400 per month for each child age 6 through 17.

    (2) INFLATION ADJUSTMENT.—The amounts in paragraph (1) shall
        be adjusted annually for inflation.

(d) PHASE-OUT.—

    (1) IN GENERAL.—The allowance shall be reduced by 5 percent
        of household income exceeding—
        (A) $200,000 for married couples filing jointly;
        (B) $150,000 for heads of household;
        (C) $100,000 for other filers.

    (2) MINIMUM.—No family shall receive less than $50 per child
        per month regardless of income.

(e) PAYMENT.—

    (1) MONTHLY PAYMENTS.—The allowance shall be paid monthly,
        on the 15th of each month.

    (2) RECIPIENT.—Payment shall be made to the parent, guardian,
        or caretaker with whom the child resides.

    (3) DIRECT DEPOSIT.—The Administration shall encourage direct
        deposit but provide alternative payment methods for those
        without bank accounts.

(f) AUTOMATIC ENROLLMENT.—

    (1) AT BIRTH.—The Administration shall establish a system for
        automatic enrollment of children at birth based on birth
        registration.

    (2) EXISTING CHILDREN.—Parents of existing children may enroll
        through the Administration or through the tax filing
        process.

SEC. 4. RELATIONSHIP TO CHILD TAX CREDIT.

(a) TRANSITION.—Upon full implementation of the Child Allowance,
    the Child Tax Credit under section 24 of the Internal Revenue
    Code shall be repealed.

(b) TRANSITION PERIOD.—During the transition period, families
    shall receive the greater of the Child Allowance or the
    Child Tax Credit.

SEC. 5. ADMINISTRATION.

(a) SOCIAL SECURITY ADMINISTRATION.—The Social Security
    Administration shall administer the Child Allowance program.

(b) DATA SHARING.—Federal agencies shall share relevant data to
    facilitate enrollment and prevent improper payments.

(c) APPEALS.—The Administration shall establish a process for
    appealing eligibility determinations.

SEC. 6. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated such sums as may be
necessary to carry out this Act.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

(a) IN GENERAL.—This Act shall take effect 18 months after
    enactment.

(b) PHASE-IN.—The Commissioner of Social Security may phase in
    implementation beginning with children under 6.

```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3(c)**: Higher amount for young children (highest costs)
- **Section 3(d)**: Gradual phase-out maintains universality
- **Section 3(f)**: Automatic enrollment maximizes participation
- **Section 4**: Transition from CTC to allowance
- **Section 5**: SSA administration (existing infrastructure)

### Child Poverty Reduction Act

**Purpose**: Establish national child poverty reduction targets with accountability mechanisms.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Child Poverty Reduction Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) More than 11 million American children live in poverty,
    harming their development, education, and health.
(2) Child poverty costs America an estimated $1 trillion
    annually in lost productivity and increased social costs.
(3) The National Academies of Sciences identified evidence-based
    policies capable of reducing child poverty by 50 percent.
(4) Setting measurable goals and accountability mechanisms
    is essential to sustained progress.

SEC. 3. NATIONAL CHILD POVERTY REDUCTION GOALS.

(a) TEN-YEAR GOALS.—It is the goal of the United States to achieve,
    within 10 years of enactment of this Act:
    (1) Reduction of the child poverty rate to below 5 percent;
    (2) Reduction of the deep child poverty rate to below 1 percent;
    (3) Elimination of racial disparities in child poverty rates;
    (4) Reduction of child food insecurity to below 5 percent;
    (5) Reduction of child homelessness by 90 percent.

(b) INTERIM GOALS.—

    (1) FIVE-YEAR.—Within 5 years, the child poverty rate shall
        be reduced to below 8 percent.

    (2) ANNUAL.—The President shall establish annual interim
        goals to achieve the objectives in subsection (a).

SEC. 4. INTERAGENCY COUNCIL ON CHILD POVERTY.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established within the Executive Office
    of the President an Interagency Council on Child Poverty.

(b) MEMBERSHIP.—The Council shall be chaired by a director appointed
    by the President, confirmed by the Senate, and shall include:
    (1) Secretary of Health and Human Services;
    (2) Secretary of the Treasury;
    (3) Secretary of Education;
    (4) Secretary of Housing and Urban Development;
    (5) Secretary of Agriculture;
    (6) Commissioner of Social Security;
    (7) Such other officials as the President designates.

(c) DUTIES.—The Council shall:
    (1) Develop the National Child Poverty Reduction Strategy;
    (2) Coordinate federal child poverty reduction efforts;
    (3) Monitor progress toward goals;
    (4) Report annually to Congress.

SEC. 5. NATIONAL CHILD POVERTY REDUCTION STRATEGY.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Within one year of enactment, and every four years
    thereafter, the Council shall publish a National Child Poverty
    Reduction Strategy.

(b) CONTENTS.—The Strategy shall include:
    (1) Assessment of current child poverty levels and causes;
    (2) Evaluation of program effectiveness;
    (3) Specific policy recommendations;
    (4) Resource requirements and proposed funding;
    (5) Performance metrics and milestones.

(c) COMMUNITY INPUT.—The Strategy shall incorporate input from:
    (1) State and local governments;
    (2) Child welfare organizations;
    (3) Parents and youth with lived experience;
    (4) Academic researchers.

SEC. 6. CONGRESSIONAL ACCOUNTABILITY.

(a) REPORT.—If the annual child poverty rate exceeds the interim
    goal by more than 1 percentage point, the Council shall
    submit to Congress within 90 days:
    (1) An explanation of why the goal was not met;
    (2) Recommended legislative actions;
    (3) Proposed budget amendments.

(b) CONGRESSIONAL CONSIDERATION.—Legislation proposed under
    subsection (a) shall receive expedited consideration.

SEC. 7. DATA AND MEASUREMENT.

(a) QUARTERLY ESTIMATES.—The Census Bureau shall publish quarterly
    estimates of child poverty using the Supplemental Poverty
    Measure.

(b) CHILD WELL-BEING INDEX.—The Census Bureau shall develop and
    publish an annual Child Well-Being Index incorporating:
    (1) Poverty rates;
    (2) Food insecurity;
    (3) Housing instability;
    (4) Health outcomes;
    (5) Educational achievement.

SEC. 8. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated $10,000,000 annually for
the Council and related activities.

SEC. 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect upon enactment.

```

### Children's Basic Income Act

**Purpose**: Establish a pilot program for unconditional basic income for children in deep poverty.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Children's Basic Income Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) Deep child poverty—living below 50 percent of the poverty
    line—affects 3.6 million American children.
(2) Children in deep poverty face the most severe deprivation
    and the greatest barriers to healthy development.
(3) Existing programs often fail to reach the most marginalized
    families due to administrative complexity, stigma, and
    work requirements.
(4) Guaranteed basic income pilots have shown positive effects
    on family stability, child well-being, and economic security.
(5) Unconditional cash transfers empower families to meet their
    most pressing needs.

SEC. 3. CHILDREN'S BASIC INCOME DEMONSTRATION.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary of Health and Human Services shall
    establish a Children's Basic Income Demonstration in at least
    5 communities representing diverse geographic and demographic
    characteristics.

(b) ELIGIBILITY.—Participating families shall:
    (1) Have at least one child under age 18;
    (2) Have household income below 50 percent of the poverty line;
    (3) Reside in a participating community.

(c) BENEFIT.—

    (1) AMOUNT.—Participating families shall receive $1,000 per
        month per child, with no conditions on use.

    (2) DURATION.—Benefits shall continue for at least 3 years or
        until the child reaches age 18.

    (3) NON-COUNTABLE.—Benefits received under this section shall
        not be counted as income for purposes of any other federal
        means-tested program.

(d) COMMUNITIES.—The Secretary shall select communities including:
    (1) At least one urban area with high child poverty;
    (2) At least one rural area with persistent poverty;
    (3) At least one Native American reservation;
    (4) At least one area with high immigrant population;
    (5) At least one area in the Deep South.

SEC. 4. EVALUATION.

(a) RIGOROUS EVALUATION.—The Secretary shall contract with
    independent researchers to conduct a rigorous evaluation.

(b) OUTCOMES.—The evaluation shall measure:
    (1) Child poverty and deep poverty rates;
    (2) Material hardship (food, housing, utilities);
    (3) Parental employment;
    (4) Child health and development outcomes;
    (5) Educational outcomes;
    (6) Family stability;
    (7) Parental stress and mental health.

(c) COMPARISON.—The evaluation shall compare outcomes with
    similar families not receiving the benefit.

(d) REPORTS.—The Secretary shall report to Congress annually
    on demonstration progress and a final evaluation report
    within one year of conclusion.

SEC. 5. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated $500,000,000 for each
of fiscal years 2026 through 2030 to carry out this Act.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect upon enactment, with first payments
beginning within 18 months.

```

## State Model Legislation

### State Child Tax Credit Act

**Purpose**: Create a refundable state-level child tax credit.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Child Tax Credit Act".

SECTION 2. LEGISLATIVE FINDINGS.

The Legislature finds that:
(a) Child poverty in [State] affects [X] percent of children,
    approximately [Y] children;
(b) A state child tax credit supplements federal efforts and
    provides additional support to families;
(c) [Number] states have established state child tax credits;
(d) Refundable credits reach the families most in need.

SECTION 3. STATE CHILD TAX CREDIT.

(a) CREDIT ALLOWED.—A resident taxpayer shall be allowed a credit
    against the tax imposed by [state income tax code] equal to
    $1,000 per qualifying child.

(b) REFUNDABILITY.—The credit shall be fully refundable. If the
    credit exceeds the taxpayer's tax liability, the excess shall
    be refunded to the taxpayer.

(c) QUALIFYING CHILD.—A qualifying child means a child who:
    (1) Is under age 18 at the close of the taxable year;
    (2) Is claimed as a dependent by the taxpayer on the
        taxpayer's federal return;
    (3) Resides with the taxpayer for more than half the year.

(d) INCOME LIMIT.—The credit shall be reduced by 5 percent of
    adjusted gross income exceeding:
    (1) $100,000 for married couples filing jointly;
    (2) $75,000 for heads of household;
    (3) $50,000 for other filers.

(e) INFLATION ADJUSTMENT.—The credit amount and income thresholds
    shall be adjusted annually for inflation.

SECTION 4. ADMINISTRATION.

(a) The Department of Revenue shall administer this credit.

(b) The Department shall develop an outreach plan to ensure
    eligible families are aware of the credit.

(c) The credit shall be claimed on the annual state income tax
    return or through a simplified process for non-filers.

SECTION 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect for taxable years beginning after
December 31, [year].

```

## Regulatory Framework

### IRS Child Tax Credit Regulations

**Existing Authority**: Internal Revenue Code Section 24

**Draft Regulation**:

```text
PART 1—INCOME TAXES

Sec. 1.24-1 Child Tax Credit.

(a) General rule. A taxpayer may claim a credit for each qualifying
    child as provided in section 24 of the Internal Revenue Code.

(b) Advance payment program.

    (1) Election. A taxpayer may elect to receive the credit through
        monthly advance payments by registering with the IRS through
        the online portal or by indicating the election on the
        prior year's tax return.

    (2) Payment schedule. Advance payments shall be made on the
        15th of each month. If the 15th falls on a weekend or
        holiday, payment shall be made on the preceding business day.

    (3) Amount. Monthly payments shall be calculated based on the
        most recent tax return information, adjusted for known
        changes in qualifying children.

    (4) Reconciliation. Any difference between advance payments
        received and the final credit calculated on the annual
        return shall be reconciled, subject to safe harbor
        provisions.

(c) Safe harbor for overpayments.

    (1) General rule. No repayment shall be required for over-
        payments up to the safe harbor amount.

    (2) Safe harbor amounts.
        (i) For taxpayers with AGI below $40,000 ($80,000 for
            joint filers): $2,000 per return.
        (ii) For taxpayers with AGI between $40,000-$80,000
            ($80,000-$150,000 for joint): $1,000 per return.

(d) Non-filer access.

    (1) Portal. The IRS shall maintain a simplified online portal
        allowing non-filers to claim the credit and elect advance
        payments.

    (2) Simplified form. A simplified paper form shall be available
        for those without internet access.

(e) Documentation. Taxpayers shall maintain documentation of
    qualifying child status but shall not be required to submit
    documentation with the return absent specific request.

```

### HHS Child Poverty Coordination Regulations

**Draft Regulation**:

```text
PART 1000—CHILD POVERTY REDUCTION

Sec. 1000.1 Purpose.

This part establishes requirements for coordination of federal
programs affecting child poverty.

Sec. 1000.10 Integrated Application for Families.

(a) States receiving TANF, CCDF, or Medicaid funds shall develop
    an integrated application process for families with children.

(b) The integrated application shall allow families to apply
    simultaneously for:
    (1) TANF;
    (2) SNAP;
    (3) Medicaid and CHIP;
    (4) Childcare assistance;
    (5) LIHEAP;
    (6) Housing assistance (where applicable).

(c) Timeline. States shall implement integrated applications
    within 3 years of this regulation's effective date.

Sec. 1000.20 Child Poverty Impact Assessment.

(a) Before implementing any policy change that may affect child
    poverty, states shall assess the impact on:
    (1) The number of children in poverty;
    (2) Material hardship among children;
    (3) Racial disparities.

(b) Assessments shall be submitted to the Secretary and made public.

Sec. 1000.30 Continuous Eligibility for Children.

(a) States shall provide 12-month continuous eligibility for all
    programs serving children.

(b) Recertification shall not be required more frequently than
    annually for children.

Sec. 1000.40 Reporting.

States shall report annually on:
(a) Child poverty rates using SPM;
(b) Program participation among eligible children;
(c) Administrative barriers identified and addressed.

```

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

**Spending Power**

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| CTC expansion | Clearly constitutional under taxing power |
| Child allowance | Constitutional as spending for general welfare |
| Conditions on states | Subject to coercion limits |
| Non-citizen eligibility | Constitutional but politically contested |

**Equal Protection**

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| Age-based distinctions | Rational basis; easily satisfied |
| Income-based phase-outs | Constitutional; rational basis |
| Citizenship requirements | Generally constitutional; policy choice |
| Racial impact | Disparate impact insufficient; no intent shown |

### Preemption Questions

| Area | Federal vs. State |
|------|-------------------|
| Tax credits | States can supplement federal |
| Benefit programs | Federal sets floor; states can exceed |
| Work requirements | Federal limits on state restrictions |
| Data sharing | CMPPA, Privacy Act considerations |

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Application |
|-----------|-------------|
| Individual rights | Tax credits enforceable through tax system |
| State compliance | Funding conditions; administrative review |
| Private litigation | Limited private right of action |
| Administrative enforcement | IRS, SSA enforcement |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### American Family Act (Permanent Expanded CTC)

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Non-filer exclusion | Poorest families may not file taxes | High |
| ITIN restrictions | Some immigrant families excluded | Medium |
| Custody disputes | Credit may go to wrong parent | Medium |
| Income timing | AGI fluctuations affect eligibility | Low |
| Advance payment errors | Over/underpayment issues | Medium |
| Identity theft | Fraudulent claims using children's SSN | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Delivered through tax system | Excludes those disconnected from IRS | Tax-based delivery |
| Annual reconciliation | Can create tax debt for poor families | IRS process |
| Not indexed to local costs | Same amount in high/low cost areas | Design choice |
| Phase-out cliff effects | Some families face high marginal rates | Design choice |
| Political vulnerability | Next Congress could reverse | Legislative process |
| Citizenship requirement | Undocumented children excluded | Political compromise |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Non-filer access**: Mandatory outreach; simplified portal; navigator program; SSA alternative delivery
2. **ITIN expansion**: Allow ITINs for all children; streamline ITIN process
3. **Custody rules**: Clear rules; shared credit option; mediation resources
4. **Income volatility**: Prior-year safe harbor; multi-year averaging option
5. **Advance payment errors**: Generous safe harbor; no recapture below poverty; phase-in reconciliation
6. **Identity protection**: IP PIN for all children; data matching with birth records
7. **Local cost adjustment**: Phase in geographic adjustment; cost-of-living supplement
8. **Political durability**: Build broad coalition; entrenchment through universality
9. **Undocumented children**: Future legislative fix; state supplements
