# Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC): Stakeholders

## Who Benefits from the EITC

### Current EITC Recipients

**The 31 Million Claimants**

| Group | Number | Average Credit | Key Characteristics |
|-------|--------|----------------|---------------------|
| Families with children | 22 million | $3,500 | Core beneficiaries |
| Childless workers | 7 million | $290 | Minimal support |
| Working poor | 20+ million | Varies | Primary target |
| Near-poor workers | 10 million | Lower end | Phase-out region |

**What They Need**

- Adequate income to meet basic needs
- Work support that doesn't create cliffs
- Predictable, reliable payments
- Simple claiming process
- Larger credit for childless workers

**Level of Political Power**: **Low-Medium**

- Vote but not organized around EITC
- Rarely identified as "EITC recipient" identity
- Often don't know they receive credit
- Seasonal engagement (tax season)
- No dedicated EITC constituency organization

### Working Families with Children

**22 Million Families**

| Category | Number | Average Credit |
|----------|--------|----------------|
| 1 child | 10.5 million | $2,400 |
| 2 children | 8.5 million | $4,100 |
| 3+ children | 5 million | $5,800 |

**Key Needs**

- Supplement low wages
- Childcare support
- Healthcare access
- Housing affordability
- Pathway out of poverty

**Level of Political Power**: **Medium**

- "Working families" resonates politically
- Child-focused framing has appeal
- Represented by advocacy organizations
- But not organized as EITC constituency

### Childless Workers

**7 Million Current Claimants, 25+ Million Potentially Eligible**

| Status | Number | Situation |
|--------|--------|-----------|
| Current claimants (25-64) | 7 million | Token $290 average |
| Excluded by age (under 25) | 6 million | Low-wage, no credit |
| Excluded by age (over 64) | 3 million | Working seniors |
| Would benefit from expansion | 17 million | Current claimants need more |

**What They Need**

- Credit comparable to workers with children
- Removal of age restrictions
- Recognition as deserving workers
- Same work incentive support

**Level of Political Power**: **Low**

- Not organized as constituency
- Less sympathetic framing than families
- Politically divided demographic
- Overlooked in policy debates

### Children in EITC Households

**Approximately 30 Million Children**

- Indirect beneficiaries
- Credit supports household income
- Research shows developmental benefits
- Better nutrition, health, education outcomes

**Level of Political Power**: **None**

- Cannot vote
- Depend on adult advocates
- Benefit from family-focused framing

## Who Provides or Administers the EITC

### Internal Revenue Service (IRS)

**Role and Responsibilities**

| Function | Activity |
|----------|----------|
| Administer credit | Process claims, issue refunds |
| Enforce compliance | Audits, verification |
| Outreach | Awareness campaigns |
| Data collection | Track claims, demographics |

**IRS Interests**

- Reduce improper payments
- Simplify administration
- Adequate resources for enforcement
- Balance access with accuracy

**Challenges Faced**

| Challenge | Impact |
|-----------|--------|
| Underfunding | Limited capacity |
| Complex rules | Difficult to enforce |
| Improper payment pressure | Congressional scrutiny |
| Customer service | Hard to help claimants |

**Level of Power**: **High** (within administrative bounds)

### Tax Preparers

**Paid Preparers**

| Type | Share of EITC Returns | Characteristics |
|------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Tax preparation chains | 35% | H&R Block, Jackson Hewitt |
| Independent preparers | 25% | Small offices |
| CPAs/professionals | 5% | Higher-income clients |
| Self-prepared | 35% | TurboTax, Free File |

**Interests**

- Fee income from preparation
- Return customers
- Accuracy (liability concerns)
- Refund anticipation loans (declining)

**Problems**

| Issue | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| High fees | $200-300 for EITC return |
| Incentive to maximize | More credit = more fees |
| Due diligence requirements | Must verify eligibility |
| Quality variation | Some preparers poor quality |

### VITA Programs

**Volunteer Income Tax Assistance**

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Sites nationwide | 11,000+ |
| Returns prepared | 3.5+ million |
| Volunteers | 90,000+ |
| EITC claims | 40%+ of returns |

**Key Organizations**

- IRS VITA program
- AARP Tax-Aide
- United Way partnerships
- Community organizations
- Libraries and schools

**Interests**

- Help low-income families claim credits
- Reduce paid preparer costs
- Improve accuracy
- Community benefit

**Level of Power**: **Medium** (advocacy, service delivery)

## Who Benefits from Status Quo

### Low-Wage Employers

**Indirect Beneficiaries**

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Wage subsidy | EITC allows lower wages |
| Labor supply | More workers available |
| Reduced pressure | Less minimum wage demand |
| Taxpayer funding | Workers subsidized by government |

**Industries Most Affected**

| Industry | Low-Wage Workers | EITC Benefit |
|----------|------------------|--------------|
| Retail | 4.6 million | Substantial |
| Food service | 3.7 million | Substantial |
| Agriculture | 2.6 million | Moderate |
| Home health | 2.4 million | Moderate |
| Warehousing | 1.6 million | Moderate |

**Level of Political Power**: **High**

- Organized through trade associations
- Campaign contributions
- Lobbying presence
- Prefer EITC to minimum wage

### Business Groups

**Chamber of Commerce, Business Roundtable, etc.**

| Interest | Position |
|----------|----------|
| Keep wages low | Support EITC over minimum wage |
| Avoid mandates | EITC is tax-based, not employer mandate |
| Maintain flexibility | No new employer requirements |
| Public subsidy | Taxpayers fund wage supplement |

**Typical Positions**

- Support EITC in concept
- May support modest expansion
- Oppose dramatic expansion (cost/taxes)
- Prefer EITC to minimum wage increase

### Fiscal Conservatives

**Some Conservative Economists and Think Tanks**

| Interest | Rationale |
|----------|-----------|
| Work-based support | Not "welfare" |
| Market-based | Wages set by market |
| Cost containment | Limit expansion |
| Efficiency | EITC more efficient than minimum wage |

**Typical Positions**

- Support existing EITC
- Cautious about expansion
- Oppose unconditional transfers
- May support replacing other programs with EITC

## Who Advocates for Change

### Anti-Poverty Organizations

**Major Advocates**

| Organization | Focus | Position |
|--------------|-------|----------|
| Center on Budget and Policy Priorities | Federal policy | Expand EITC, especially childless |
| Children's Defense Fund | Child poverty | EITC + CTC expansion |
| CLASP | Work-family policy | EITC expansion, advance payment |
| Economic Policy Institute | Labor policy | EITC as complement to minimum wage |
| Prosperity Now | Asset building | EITC savings programs |

**Key Positions**

- Expand childless worker EITC
- Remove age restrictions
- Improve participation
- Consider advance payment
- Simplify claiming

**Level of Power**: **Medium**

- Research shapes debate
- Limited campaign finance influence
- Coalition with labor, faith groups
- Expertise valued in policy process

### Tax Justice Organizations

**Groups Focused on Tax Policy**

| Organization | Focus |
|--------------|-------|
| Tax Policy Center | Research, analysis |
| Institute on Taxation and Economic Policy | State/local taxes |
| National Taxpayers Union (some support) | Efficient tax policy |

**Positions**

- EITC as efficient transfer
- Simplification important
- State EITC expansion
- Consider integration with other credits

### Labor Unions

**AFL-CIO and Member Unions**

| Interest | Position |
|----------|----------|
| Higher wages for members | EITC supplements |
| Organize low-wage workers | EITC supports members |
| Minimum wage primary | EITC is complement |
| Political power | Support expansion |

**Key Positions**

- EITC expansion alongside minimum wage increase
- Not substitute for employer responsibility
- Support for childless worker expansion
- Oppose erosion of minimum wage advocacy

**Level of Power**: **Medium** (declining but still significant)

### State-Level Advocates

**Organizations Working on State EITCs**

| Focus | Activity |
|-------|----------|
| Create new state EITCs | 19 states without |
| Expand existing credits | Increase percentage |
| Make refundable | Convert non-refundable |
| Increase outreach | Participation rates |

## Government Stakeholders

### Congress

**Relevant Committees**

| Committee | Role |
|-----------|------|
| House Ways and Means | Tax policy, EITC legislation |
| Senate Finance | Tax policy, EITC legislation |
| House Appropriations | IRS funding |
| Senate Appropriations | IRS funding |

**Key Dynamics**

- Bipartisan support exists (in principle)
- Cost concerns limit expansion
- Improper payments create tension
- Budget reconciliation pathway

**Positions Vary**

| Position | Typical Supporters |
|----------|-------------------|
| Maintain status quo | Fiscal conservatives |
| Modest expansion | Moderate bipartisan |
| Major expansion | Progressive Democrats |
| Replace other programs | Some libertarians |

### State Governments

**State Interests**

| Interest | Action |
|----------|--------|
| Supplement federal | Create state EITC |
| Administer programs | Coordinate with federal |
| Revenue constraints | Limit state credit size |
| Political priorities | Vary by state |

**31 States + DC with EITCs**

- Policy leaders (CA, NY, DC)
- Recent adopters (CO, VA)
- Non-refundable only (OH, VA, SC)
- No state EITC (TX, FL, GA, etc.)

### Treasury Department and IRS

**Executive Branch Interests**

| Interest | Action |
|----------|--------|
| Program effectiveness | Reduce improper payments |
| Participation | Increase take-up |
| Administration | Simplify where possible |
| Resources | Seek adequate funding |

## Strategic Implications

### Allies for Reform

**Natural Allies**

| Group | Reason | Strength |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Anti-poverty organizations | Mission-aligned | Medium |
| Child advocacy groups | Family benefits | Medium |
| Labor unions | Worker support | Medium |
| Faith communities | Moral case | Medium |
| Some economists | Efficiency argument | Low-Medium |

**Potential Allies**

| Group | Pitch | Barrier |
|-------|-------|---------|
| Some business | Alternative to minimum wage | Cost/taxes |
| Some conservatives | Work-based | Deficit concerns |
| Tax reformers | Simplification | Complexity |
| State governments | Federal dollars | Politics |

### Building Power

**Short-Term Strategies**

- Highlight 2021 expansion success
- Focus on childless worker gap
- Build bipartisan support where possible
- State-level expansion as proof points

**Long-Term Strategies**

- Integrate EITC into broader anti-poverty agenda
- Connect to minimum wage (not substitute)
- Build claimant awareness and engagement
- Expand state-level coalition
