# Poverty: Stakeholders

## Who Is Affected by Poverty

### People Living in Poverty

**The 37.9 Million**

| Group | Number | Key Characteristics |
|-------|--------|---------------------|
| Children | 11.6 million | Most vulnerable; long-term effects |
| Working-age adults | 20.8 million | Many employed but poor |
| Elderly | 5.5 million | Fixed incomes; medical costs |
| Deep poverty | 17.8 million | Extreme deprivation |

**What They Need**

- Adequate income
- Affordable housing
- Healthcare access
- Childcare
- Transportation
- Education and training
- Voice in policy

**Level of Political Power**: **Very Low**

- Less likely to vote
- Under-organized
- Underrepresented in advocacy
- Stigma silences
- Survival consumes time/energy

### The Near-Poor

**The 84 Million at 100-200% of Poverty**

- Face similar hardships as poor
- One crisis away from poverty
- Often ineligible for programs
- Working but struggling
- "ALICE" households

**What They Need**

- Higher wages
- Affordable healthcare
- Housing assistance
- Retirement security
- Emergency savings

**Level of Political Power**: **Low-Medium**

- More likely to vote than poor
- But still under-organized
- Often don't see selves as "poor"
- May oppose programs they could benefit from

### Working Families

**Those Balancing Work and Poverty**

- 6.3 million workers in poverty
- 6.9 million families with workers in poverty
- Need work to be a path out of poverty
- Face childcare, scheduling, benefit cliff barriers

**What They Need**

- Higher minimum wage
- Benefits (healthcare, paid leave)
- Affordable childcare
- Predictable schedules
- Pathway to advancement

### Children

**The Most Vulnerable**

- 16.3% poverty rate
- 11.6 million affected
- Brain development impacted
- Educational achievement reduced
- Health effects lasting
- Not responsible for circumstances

**What They Need**

- Basic security (food, housing, stability)
- Quality early childhood programs
- Good schools
- Healthcare
- Safe environments

**Level of Political Power**: **None**

- Cannot vote
- Dependent on adults to advocate
- Future consequences invisible today

### The Elderly Poor

**5.5 Million in Poverty**

- Fixed incomes (Social Security)
- Rising medical costs
- Housing cost burdens
- Social isolation
- Disproportionately women, minorities

**What They Need**

- Higher Social Security benefits
- Medicare improvements
- Housing assistance
- Nutrition programs
- Prescription drug help

**Level of Political Power**: **Medium**

- Higher voting rates
- AARP advocacy
- But poor elderly less organized
- Ageism compounds

## Who Benefits from Status Quo

### Low-Wage Employers

**Industries That Rely on Cheap Labor**

| Industry | Low-Wage Workers |
|----------|------------------|
| Fast food | 3.3 million |
| Retail | 4.6 million |
| Agriculture | 2.5 million |
| Hospitality | 6.5 million |
| Home health | 2.4 million |

**What They Gain**

- Lower labor costs
- Higher profits
- Taxpayer subsidy (workers on public benefits)
- Weak bargaining power of workers
- Flexible scheduling

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **High**

- Organized through industry associations
- Major campaign contributors
- Lobby against minimum wage, benefits mandates
- Frame as "job killing"

### The Wealthy and Corporations

**Those Who Benefit from Inequality**

- Pay lower taxes when safety net is weak
- Benefit from low-wage labor
- Political power enhanced by poverty of others
- Investments in low-wage sectors profitable

**What They Gain**

- Lower taxes (less redistribution)
- Lower labor costs
- Weaker unions
- Policy influence
- Reduced competition for housing, services

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **High**

- Fund opposition campaigns
- Support think tanks opposing programs
- Lobby against tax increases
- Promote "dependency" narrative

### Politicians Who Exploit Poverty Narratives

**Electoral Benefits of Anti-Poor Politics**

- "Welfare" as wedge issue
- Racial resentment mobilized
- Tax opposition tied to "giving money away"
- "Dependency" narrative popular with some voters

**What They Gain**

- Electoral advantage (certain constituencies)
- Donor support (from wealthy, corporations)
- Ideological consistency
- Media attention

## Who Has Power to Change Things

### Federal Government

**Congress**

| Power | Scope |
|-------|-------|
| Appropriations | Fund or defund programs |
| Legislation | Create new programs, reform existing |
| Tax policy | EITC, CTC, rates |
| Oversight | Program administration |

**Level of Power**: **Very High**

**Current Obstacles**:
- Partisan gridlock
- Filibuster blocks action
- Influence of wealthy donors
- Geographic overrepresentation of rural areas

**Executive Branch**

| Power | Scope |
|-------|-------|
| Regulations | Implement programs |
| Enforcement | Agency priorities |
| Waivers | State flexibility |
| Executive orders | Limited but real |

**Level of Power**: **High** (within limits)

**Current Obstacles**:
- Subject to legislative constraints
- Court challenges
- Reversible by next administration

### State Governments

**State Policy Levers**

| Area | State Power |
|------|-------------|
| Minimum wage | Can exceed federal |
| TANF | Administer with flexibility |
| Medicaid | Expansion decision |
| SNAP | Some policies set state |
| Childcare | State supplement |
| Taxes | State EITC, other credits |

**Level of Power**: **High** (within state)

**Variation**:
- Some states progressive
- Some states restrictive
- Creates geographic inequality

### Local Governments

**Local Policy Levers**

| Area | Local Power |
|------|-------------|
| Minimum wage | Can exceed state (in some states) |
| Housing | Zoning, affordability requirements |
| Schools | Funding, programs |
| Services | Local safety net programs |
| Economic development | Job creation, subsidies |

**Level of Power**: **Medium** (limited by preemption)

### Advocacy Organizations

**Major Anti-Poverty Advocates**

| Organization | Focus |
|--------------|-------|
| Center on Budget and Policy Priorities | Research, advocacy |
| Children's Defense Fund | Child poverty |
| National Low Income Housing Coalition | Housing |
| Food Research and Action Center | Food assistance |
| CLASP | Work-family policy |
| Economic Policy Institute | Labor, wages |
| Urban Institute | Research |
| Brookings Institution | Research, policy |

**Level of Power**: **Medium**

- Research shapes debate
- Limited political influence
- Under-resourced vs. opposition
- Coalition building essential

### Labor Unions

**Role in Anti-Poverty**

- Raise wages for members
- Set standards for non-members
- Political advocacy
- Coalition partners

**Level of Power**: **Medium** (declining)

- 10% union membership
- Political influence still significant
- Fight for $15 success
- But organizing barriers remain

### Faith Communities

**Role in Poverty Response**

| Role | Activity |
|------|----------|
| Direct service | Food pantries, shelters, aid |
| Advocacy | Policy positions, lobbying |
| Community organizing | Base building |
| Moral voice | Prophetic tradition |

**Level of Power**: **Medium**

- Broad reach
- Moral authority
- But divided by denomination
- Often focus on charity over policy

### Foundations and Philanthropy

**Role in Poverty Work**

| Activity | Impact |
|----------|--------|
| Research funding | Knowledge production |
| Advocacy support | Organizational capacity |
| Direct programs | Service provision |
| Policy innovation | Testing new approaches |

**Major Funders**

- Ford Foundation
- Annie E. Casey Foundation
- Robert Wood Johnson Foundation
- Kellogg Foundation
- Open Society Foundations

**Level of Power**: **Medium**

- Resources significant
- Shape nonprofit sector
- But cannot replace government
- Own biases shape field

## Potential Allies

### Cross-Class Coalitions

**Middle-Class Interests**

- Retirement security (Social Security)
- Healthcare affordability
- Education access
- Childcare (universal benefits)
- Climate adaptation

**Business Interests (Some)**

- Consumer demand requires income
- Skilled workforce requires investment
- Infrastructure benefits all
- Social stability good for business

### Generational Alliance

**Young People**

- Face declining mobility
- Student debt burden
- Housing unaffordability
- Climate stakes
- More favorable to redistribution

### Geographic Coalition

**Rural and Urban Alliance**

- Both face poverty concentration
- Both underinvested
- Different manifestations, shared causes
- Infrastructure needs overlap

## Strategic Implications

### Advantages for Reform

1. **Numbers**: Vast majority would benefit from reform
2. **Moral case**: Poverty amid wealth is unjust
3. **Evidence**: We know what works
4. **Recent proof**: 2021 CTC expansion succeeded
5. **Public support**: Polling favors action

### Challenges for Reform

1. **Power imbalance**: Opponents well-resourced
2. **Political structure**: Filibuster, gerrymandering
3. **Narrative dominance**: "Dependency" frame
4. **Fragmentation**: Poor not organized as bloc
5. **Stigma**: Recipients silenced

### Building Power

**Short-Term**

- Support specific campaigns (minimum wage, CTC)
- Elect reform candidates
- Build local coalitions
- Document and communicate impact

**Long-Term**

- Organize poor and working people
- Build narrative power
- Campaign finance reform
- Cross-racial solidarity
- Universal programs for broader coalition
