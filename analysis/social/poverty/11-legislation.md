# Poverty: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Reducing poverty requires comprehensive legislative action addressing income support, labor markets, and supportive services. This document provides model legislation for federal and state levels, including constitutional considerations and regulatory frameworks. The legislation draws on evidence from the 2021 Child Tax Credit expansion and international best practices.

## Constitutional Amendments

### Economic Security Amendment

**Purpose**: Establish a constitutional commitment to freedom from poverty, providing a foundation for poverty reduction legislation.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. The right of all persons within the United States to
freedom from poverty and economic deprivation is recognized as a
fundamental aspect of human dignity.

SECTION 2. Congress and the States shall have the power to enact
legislation ensuring that all persons have access to:
(a) adequate income to meet basic needs;
(b) employment at living wages;
(c) healthcare;
(d) housing;
(e) nutrition; and
(f) education.

SECTION 3. No law shall operate to deprive any person of assistance
necessary to meet basic needs based solely on:
(a) race, national origin, or immigration status;
(b) family structure or marital status;
(c) disability or health status; or
(d) prior criminal conviction.

SECTION 4. The rights recognized in this article shall not be
construed to diminish other rights retained by the people.

SECTION 5. Congress shall have the power to enforce this article
by appropriate legislation.

SECTION 6. This article shall take effect three years after the
date of ratification.

```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 1**: Establishes positive economic rights as constitutional principle
- **Section 2**: Enumerates specific protections; empowers Congress and states
- **Section 3**: Prohibits categorical exclusions; addresses historical discrimination
- **Section 4**: Non-diminishment clause
- **Section 5**: Enforcement power
- **Section 6**: Implementation period for legislative development

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Justiciability | Courts can develop standards; self-restraint appropriate |
| Cost | Rights don't mandate specific programs; flexibility |
| Federalism | Both Congress and states empowered |
| Political feasibility | Would require mass movement |

## Federal Legislation

### Poverty Reduction Act

**Purpose**: Establish national poverty reduction goals, accountability mechanisms, and coordination requirements across federal programs.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Poverty Reduction Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) The United States has the highest poverty rate among
    developed nations, with over 37 million people in poverty.
(2) Child poverty in America exceeds 16 percent, harming
    children's development and perpetuating disadvantage.
(3) The 2021 Child Tax Credit expansion demonstrated that
    poverty can be dramatically reduced through policy,
    cutting child poverty nearly in half.
(4) Other developed nations achieve far lower poverty rates
    through comprehensive income support and services.
(5) Poverty imposes significant costs on society through
    reduced productivity, increased healthcare costs, and
    diminished human potential.
(6) A coordinated national effort is required to achieve
    meaningful poverty reduction.

SEC. 3. NATIONAL POVERTY REDUCTION GOALS.

(a) TEN-YEAR GOALS.—It is the goal of the United States to achieve,
    within 10 years of enactment of this Act:
    (1) Reduction of the overall poverty rate to below 6 percent;
    (2) Reduction of the child poverty rate to below 4 percent;
    (3) Reduction of the deep poverty rate to below 2 percent;
    (4) Elimination of racial disparities in poverty rates;
    (5) Reduction of material hardship rates by 50 percent.

(b) INTERIM GOALS.—The President shall establish interim annual
    goals to achieve the objectives in subsection (a).

SEC. 4. INTERAGENCY COUNCIL ON POVERTY REDUCTION.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established within the Executive Office
    of the President an Interagency Council on Poverty Reduction.

(b) MEMBERSHIP.—The Council shall be chaired by the Director of the
    Domestic Policy Council and include the heads of:
    (1) Department of Health and Human Services;
    (2) Department of Labor;
    (3) Department of the Treasury;
    (4) Department of Housing and Urban Development;
    (5) Department of Agriculture;
    (6) Department of Education;
    (7) Social Security Administration;
    (8) Such other agencies as the President may designate.

(c) DUTIES.—The Council shall:
    (1) Coordinate federal poverty reduction efforts;
    (2) Develop and update the National Poverty Reduction Strategy;
    (3) Identify barriers to program access and propose solutions;
    (4) Monitor progress toward goals;
    (5) Submit annual reports to Congress.

SEC. 5. NATIONAL POVERTY REDUCTION STRATEGY.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Within one year of enactment, and every four years
    thereafter, the Council shall develop and publish a National
    Poverty Reduction Strategy.

(b) CONTENTS.—The Strategy shall include:
    (1) Assessment of current poverty levels and trends;
    (2) Analysis of program effectiveness;
    (3) Specific policy recommendations;
    (4) Resource requirements;
    (5) Implementation timeline;
    (6) Performance metrics.

(c) PUBLIC INPUT.—In developing the Strategy, the Council shall
    solicit input from:
    (1) State and local governments;
    (2) Advocacy organizations;
    (3) Academic researchers;
    (4) Persons with lived experience of poverty.

SEC. 6. POVERTY IMPACT ASSESSMENT.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Before any major legislation is considered by
    Congress, the Congressional Budget Office shall prepare a
    Poverty Impact Assessment.

(b) CONTENTS.—The Assessment shall estimate:
    (1) The number of persons moved into or out of poverty;
    (2) The change in the child poverty rate;
    (3) The change in the deep poverty rate;
    (4) Racial and ethnic distributional effects;
    (5) Geographic distributional effects.

(c) THRESHOLD.—An Assessment shall be required for any legislation
    with an estimated budgetary effect exceeding $1,000,000,000
    annually or that amends any major means-tested program.

SEC. 7. DATA AND MEASUREMENT.

(a) MODERNIZED POVERTY MEASURE.—The Census Bureau shall:
    (1) Publish the Supplemental Poverty Measure as the primary
        federal poverty measure;
    (2) Develop and publish annual geographic adjustments at the
        county level;
    (3) Produce quarterly poverty estimates;
    (4) Publish program-by-program poverty reduction impacts.

(b) MATERIAL HARDSHIP.—The Census Bureau shall publish annual
    material hardship measures including:
    (1) Food insecurity;
    (2) Housing instability;
    (3) Healthcare access;
    (4) Utility disconnection;
    (5) Inability to meet essential expenses.

SEC. 8. ADMINISTRATIVE SIMPLIFICATION.

(a) INTEGRATED APPLICATION.—Within three years of enactment,
    federal agencies shall develop a unified application for:
    (1) SNAP;
    (2) Medicaid and CHIP;
    (3) Housing assistance;
    (4) LIHEAP;
    (5) School meals;
    (6) Such other programs as the Council designates.

(b) DATA SHARING.—Agencies shall share data to:
    (1) Reduce duplicative verification;
    (2) Identify eligible non-participants;
    (3) Prevent administrative churn.

(c) PRESUMPTIVE ELIGIBILITY.—Eligibility for one means-tested
    program shall create presumptive eligibility for other
    programs with similar thresholds.

SEC. 9. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated such sums as may be
necessary to carry out this Act.

SEC. 10. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect upon enactment.

```

**Explanation**:

- **Sections 2-3**: Establish findings and specific, measurable goals
- **Section 4**: Creates coordination mechanism across agencies
- **Section 5**: Requires comprehensive strategy with accountability
- **Section 6**: Integrates poverty analysis into legislative process
- **Section 7**: Modernizes measurement and data
- **Section 8**: Reduces administrative barriers

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Goal enforcement | Goals are aspirational but create accountability |
| Agency turf | Council provides coordination mechanism |
| CBO capacity | Phase in assessment requirement |
| Data privacy | Balance with verification simplification |

### Poverty Impact Assessment Act

**Purpose**: Require systematic analysis of legislation's effects on poverty before enactment.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Poverty Impact Assessment Act".

SEC. 2. POVERTY IMPACT ASSESSMENTS REQUIRED.

(a) IN GENERAL.—The Congressional Budget Office shall prepare a
    Poverty Impact Assessment for any legislation that:
    (1) Has a projected annual budgetary impact exceeding
        $500,000,000;
    (2) Amends any means-tested program;
    (3) Modifies tax provisions affecting low-income families;
    (4) Establishes or modifies minimum wage or labor standards;
    (5) Is requested by the Chair or Ranking Member of the
        Committee of jurisdiction.

(b) CONTENTS.—Each Assessment shall include:
    (1) Estimated change in the number of persons in poverty;
    (2) Estimated change in the number of persons in deep poverty;
    (3) Estimated change in the child poverty rate;
    (4) Distributional analysis by:
        (A) Race and ethnicity;
        (B) Age;
        (C) Family structure;
        (D) Geographic region;
        (E) Income quintile;
    (5) Estimated impact on material hardship measures;
    (6) Comparison with alternative policy options.

(c) METHODOLOGY.—The Director of the Congressional Budget Office
    shall develop and publish the methodology for Assessments,
    incorporating input from the Census Bureau, academic experts,
    and the public.

(d) TIMING.—An Assessment shall be completed and publicly available
    at least 72 hours before any floor vote on the legislation.

SEC. 3. ADMINISTRATIVE ACTIONS.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—The Office of Management and Budget shall require
    a Poverty Impact Assessment for any proposed regulation with
    an estimated annual effect exceeding $100,000,000.

(b) CONTENTS.—Regulatory Assessments shall include the elements
    specified in Section 2(b) as applicable.

SEC. 4. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated to the Congressional
Budget Office such sums as necessary to carry out this Act,
including for additional staff and modeling capacity.

SEC. 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect one year after enactment.

```

### Poverty Measurement Modernization Act

**Purpose**: Update the federal poverty measure to reflect modern economic realities.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Poverty Measurement Modernization Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) The Official Poverty Measure, developed in the 1960s, is
    based on outdated assumptions about family spending patterns.
(2) The Official Poverty Measure does not account for geographic
    cost differences, non-cash benefits, work expenses, or
    medical costs.
(3) The Supplemental Poverty Measure, developed in 2011, provides
    a more accurate picture of economic hardship.
(4) Accurate poverty measurement is essential for effective
    policy design and evaluation.

SEC. 3. PRIMARY POVERTY MEASURE.

(a) DESIGNATION.—The Supplemental Poverty Measure shall be the
    primary federal poverty measure for purposes of:
    (1) Statistical reporting;
    (2) Policy analysis;
    (3) Program evaluation.

(b) PROGRAM ELIGIBILITY.—Unless otherwise specified by law,
    program eligibility based on poverty status shall be
    determined using the Supplemental Poverty Measure methodology.

(c) TRANSITION.—Agencies shall have two years from enactment to
    transition eligibility determinations to the new measure,
    provided that no individual shall lose eligibility solely
    due to the change in measurement.

SEC. 4. GEOGRAPHIC ADJUSTMENT.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—The Census Bureau shall develop and publish
    poverty thresholds adjusted for geographic cost of living
    at the county level or smaller geographic units.

(b) METROPOLITAN AREAS.—For metropolitan statistical areas,
    adjustments shall be published at the MSA level.

(c) ANNUAL UPDATE.—Geographic adjustments shall be updated
    annually based on cost of living indices.

SEC. 5. ENHANCED DATA COLLECTION.

(a) QUARTERLY ESTIMATES.—The Census Bureau shall publish
    quarterly poverty rate estimates within 45 days of
    each quarter's end.

(b) MONTHLY ADMINISTRATIVE DATA.—Federal agencies administering
    means-tested programs shall provide monthly participation
    data to the Census Bureau.

(c) MATERIAL HARDSHIP.—The Census Bureau shall conduct annual
    surveys measuring:
    (1) Food insecurity at multiple levels of severity;
    (2) Housing instability and cost burden;
    (3) Healthcare access and unmet needs;
    (4) Utility service disruption;
    (5) Inability to meet essential expenses.

SEC. 6. POVERTY REDUCTION ANALYSIS.

(a) PROGRAM IMPACT.—The Census Bureau shall publish annual
    estimates of the number of persons kept out of poverty by
    each major federal program.

(b) POLICY SIMULATIONS.—The Census Bureau shall maintain a
    microsimulation model to estimate the poverty impact of
    proposed policy changes.

SEC. 7. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated to the Census Bureau
$50,000,000 annually to carry out this Act.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect upon enactment, with full implementation
within two years.

```

### Asset-Building Tax Credit Act

**Purpose**: Create tax incentives for low-income wealth building to address asset poverty.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Asset-Building Tax Credit Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) Wealth inequality in America has reached extreme levels,
    with the bottom 50 percent of households holding less than
    3 percent of total wealth.
(2) Asset poverty—having insufficient savings to survive three
    months at the poverty level—affects over 25 percent of
    households.
(3) Without assets, families cannot weather emergencies, invest
    in education, or build toward homeownership and retirement.
(4) Tax policy currently subsidizes wealth building primarily
    for high-income households through mortgage interest
    deductions and retirement account preferences.
(5) A refundable tax credit for asset building can help low-
    income families begin accumulating wealth.

SEC. 3. ASSET-BUILDING TAX CREDIT.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 25B of the Internal Revenue Code of 1986
    is amended to read as follows:

"SEC. 25B. ASSET-BUILDING CREDIT.

"(a) ALLOWANCE OF CREDIT.—In the case of an eligible individual,
    there shall be allowed as a credit against the tax imposed
    by this subtitle for the taxable year an amount equal to
    the applicable percentage of so much of the qualified
    savings of such individual for such taxable year as does
    not exceed $2,000.

"(b) APPLICABLE PERCENTAGE.—

    "(1) IN GENERAL.—The applicable percentage shall be:
        "(A) 80 percent for taxpayers with AGI below 100 percent
            of the federal poverty level;
        "(B) 60 percent for taxpayers with AGI at 100-150 percent
            of the federal poverty level;
        "(C) 40 percent for taxpayers with AGI at 150-200 percent
            of the federal poverty level;
        "(D) 20 percent for taxpayers with AGI at 200-300 percent
            of the federal poverty level.

"(c) QUALIFIED SAVINGS.—For purposes of this section, qualified
    savings means contributions to:
    "(1) An individual retirement account;
    "(2) An employer-sponsored retirement plan;
    "(3) An education savings account under section 530;
    "(4) A first-time homebuyer savings account established
        under section 530A;
    "(5) An emergency savings account.

"(d) REFUNDABILITY.—The credit allowed under this section shall
    be fully refundable.

"(e) ELIGIBLE INDIVIDUAL.—For purposes of this section, the term
    'eligible individual' means an individual—
    "(1) who has attained the age of 18 before the close of
        the taxable year; and
    "(2) whose adjusted gross income does not exceed 300
        percent of the federal poverty level.

"(f) FIRST-TIME HOMEBUYER SAVINGS ACCOUNT.—

    "(1) IN GENERAL.—A first-time homebuyer savings account is
        an account established for the exclusive purpose of
        paying qualified acquisition costs for a principal
        residence for the account holder who is a first-time
        homebuyer.

    "(2) TAX TREATMENT.—Contributions to and earnings on such
        accounts shall be treated in the same manner as
        contributions to and earnings on Roth IRAs.

    "(3) QUALIFIED DISTRIBUTIONS.—Distributions for qualified
        acquisition costs shall not be included in gross income.

"(g) EMERGENCY SAVINGS ACCOUNT.—

    "(1) IN GENERAL.—An emergency savings account is an account
        established for the purpose of emergency expenses.

    "(2) CONTRIBUTION LIMIT.—Total contributions to such account
        shall not exceed $10,000.

    "(3) TAX TREATMENT.—Contributions shall be made from
        after-tax income; earnings shall not be taxed."

(b) EFFECTIVE DATE.—This section shall apply to taxable years
    beginning after December 31 following enactment.

SEC. 4. BABY BONDS.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary of the Treasury shall establish
    an American Opportunity Account for each child born in the
    United States or who becomes a citizen.

(b) INITIAL DEPOSIT.—Upon establishment, the Secretary shall
    deposit $1,000 in each Account.

(c) ANNUAL DEPOSITS.—Based on household income relative to the
    federal poverty level, the Secretary shall make annual
    deposits as follows:
    (1) Households below 100% FPL: $2,000 annually;
    (2) Households at 100-200% FPL: $1,500 annually;
    (3) Households at 200-300% FPL: $1,000 annually;
    (4) Households at 300-400% FPL: $500 annually;
    (5) Households above 400% FPL: $0 annually.

(d) INVESTMENT.—Funds shall be invested in a diversified
    portfolio managed by the Treasury.

(e) ACCESS.—Account holders may access funds upon reaching age 18
    for:
    (1) Post-secondary education;
    (2) First-time home purchase;
    (3) Starting a business;
    (4) Retirement savings contribution.

SEC. 5. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated such sums as may be
necessary to carry out this Act.

```

## State Model Legislation

### State Poverty Reduction Goals Act

**Purpose**: Establish state-level poverty reduction goals and accountability.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Poverty Reduction Goals Act".

SECTION 2. LEGISLATIVE FINDINGS.

The Legislature finds that:
(a) Approximately [X] percent of [State] residents live in
    poverty, including [Y] percent of children;
(b) Poverty imposes significant costs on the state through
    reduced economic productivity, increased healthcare costs,
    and diminished educational outcomes;
(c) Evidence-based policies can effectively reduce poverty;
(d) A coordinated state effort is necessary to achieve
    meaningful poverty reduction.

SECTION 3. POVERTY REDUCTION GOALS.

(a) The goal of the State is to reduce the poverty rate to:
    (1) Below [X-2] percent within five years;
    (2) Below [X-4] percent within ten years.

(b) The goal for child poverty is to reduce the rate to:
    (1) Below [Y-3] percent within five years;
    (2) Below [Y-6] percent within ten years.

SECTION 4. POVERTY REDUCTION TASK FORCE.

(a) There is established a Poverty Reduction Task Force.

(b) The Task Force shall consist of:
    (1) The Secretary of [Human Services];
    (2) The Secretary of [Labor];
    (3) The Secretary of [Education];
    (4) The Secretary of [Housing];
    (5) The Tax Commissioner;
    (6) Four members appointed by the Governor, including at
        least two persons with lived experience of poverty;
    (7) Two members appointed by the legislative leadership.

(c) The Task Force shall:
    (1) Develop a State Poverty Reduction Strategy;
    (2) Monitor progress toward goals;
    (3) Report annually to the Legislature.

SECTION 5. STATE POVERTY REDUCTION STRATEGY.

(a) Within one year of enactment, the Task Force shall develop
    and publish a State Poverty Reduction Strategy.

(b) The Strategy shall include:
    (1) Assessment of current poverty in the state;
    (2) Identification of populations at highest risk;
    (3) Specific policy recommendations;
    (4) Resource requirements;
    (5) Performance metrics.

SECTION 6. ANNUAL REPORT.

The Task Force shall submit an annual report to the Legislature
including:
(a) Current poverty rates and trends;
(b) Progress toward goals;
(c) Policy recommendations;
(d) Budget requests.

SECTION 7. POVERTY IMPACT STATEMENTS.

(a) Before any legislation with a significant fiscal impact is
    considered, the Legislative Fiscal Office shall prepare a
    Poverty Impact Statement.

(b) The Statement shall estimate the legislation's effect on
    poverty rates and the number of persons in poverty.

SECTION 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect upon enactment.

```

## Regulatory Framework

### HHS Poverty Program Coordination Regulations

**Existing Authority**: Social Security Act

**Draft Regulation**:

```text
PART 260—INTERPROGRAM COORDINATION

Sec. 260.1 Purpose.

This part establishes requirements for coordination among federal
programs serving low-income individuals to reduce administrative
burden and improve access.

Sec. 260.10 Integrated Application.

(a) States shall develop and implement an integrated application
    for TANF, SNAP, Medicaid, CHIP, and LIHEAP within three years.

(b) The integrated application shall:
    (1) Collect information once for all programs;
    (2) Allow applicants to indicate programs of interest;
    (3) Provide for electronic submission;
    (4) Be available in multiple languages.

Sec. 260.20 Express Lane Eligibility.

(a) Eligibility for one means-tested program shall create
    presumptive eligibility for other programs with similar
    income thresholds.

(b) States shall implement automatic cross-enrollment where
    permitted by law.

Sec. 260.30 Verification Simplification.

(a) States shall accept self-attestation for income verification
    for initial eligibility, subject to post-enrollment review.

(b) States shall share verification documents across programs.

(c) States shall not require in-person interviews when electronic
    or telephone alternatives are available.

Sec. 260.40 Continuous Eligibility.

(a) States shall provide 12-month continuous eligibility for
    programs serving children.

(b) States shall minimize mid-year recertification requirements.

Sec. 260.50 Performance Metrics.

(a) States shall report annually on:
    (1) Application processing times;
    (2) Denial and churning rates;
    (3) Participation rates among eligible populations;
    (4) Client satisfaction measures.

(b) The Secretary shall publish state performance comparisons.

```

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

**Spending Power**

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| CTC expansion | Clearly constitutional under taxing/spending power |
| Block grants with conditions | Constitutional but subject to coercion limits |
| Individual mandates | Subject to NFIB limits |
| Program requirements | Generally constitutional |

**Equal Protection**

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| Means-testing | Rational basis review; constitutional |
| Categorical exclusions | May face heightened scrutiny |
| Racial disparate impact | Generally insufficient to invalidate |
| Intentional discrimination | Unconstitutional |

**Takings and Due Process**

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| Benefit reduction | Due process requires notice and hearing |
| Retroactive changes | Problematic if substantial |
| Property interests | Benefits can create protected interests |

### Preemption Questions

| Area | Federal vs. State |
|------|-------------------|
| Minimum wage | States can exceed federal |
| TANF | Federal sets minimums; state flexibility |
| SNAP | Federal program; limited state flexibility |
| Tax credits | States can supplement federal |
| Labor standards | Some ERISA preemption issues |

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Application |
|-----------|-------------|
| Individual rights | Some programs create enforceable rights |
| Administrative enforcement | Agency compliance reviews |
| Funding conditions | Withholding for non-compliance |
| Private litigation | Where authorized by statute |

### Sunset and Review Provisions

**Recommended**:

- 10-year reauthorization for major programs
- 5-year GAO evaluation requirement

- Built-in triggers for adjustments

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Poverty Reduction Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Goal non-enforcement | Goals are aspirational; no penalty for missing | Medium |
| Council effectiveness | Interagency coordination historically weak | Medium |
| Data manipulation | Measure changes could affect reported poverty | Medium |
| Waiver abuse | Agencies may grant waivers undermining coordination | Low |
| Funding dependence | Requires appropriations; may be underfunded | High |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No automatic programs | Requires annual appropriations | Budget rules |
| Goals not binding | Congress not required to act | Separation of powers |
| Measurement lag | Poverty data delayed by 6-18 months | Survey methodology |
| State variation | States can undermine coordination | Federalism |
| Political vulnerability | Next administration could abandon | Executive authority limits |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Goal enforcement**: Tie goals to automatic stabilizers; require congressional action if goals missed
2. **Council effectiveness**: Require OMB budget integration; publish agency grades
3. **Data integrity**: Independent statistical agency review; protect measurement from political interference
4. **Waiver limits**: Require secretarial approval; publish all waivers; time limits
5. **Funding protection**: Mandatory spending where possible; multi-year authorizations
6. **Political durability**: Build constituency through universal elements; bipartisan governance structure

### Poverty Impact Assessment Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Threshold gaming | Legislation designed to fall below $500M | Medium |
| Timing manipulation | Assessments delayed until after votes | High |
| Methodology disputes | CBO methodology challenged | Medium |
| Scope limitations | Only covers poverty, not material hardship | Low |
| Evasion through regulations | Shift policy to regulations to avoid | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| CBO capacity | May not have resources for timely analysis | Staffing |
| Modeling limitations | Poverty impacts hard to model precisely | Technical |
| No enforcement | Assessment required but not outcome | Design |
| Dynamic effects | Long-term impacts hard to estimate | Methodology |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Threshold evasion**: Lower threshold; cover packages not just individual bills
2. **Timing manipulation**: Automatic delay of vote if assessment not complete; publish draft assessments
3. **Methodology**: External review panel; transparency in assumptions; sensitivity analysis
4. **Scope expansion**: Include material hardship, wealth effects, geographic distribution
5. **Regulatory coverage**: Apply to significant regulations; coordinate with OMB OIRA
6. **CBO resources**: Dedicated funding; hiring authority; outside contractor use

### Poverty Measurement Modernization Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Implementation delay | Census lacks resources for full implementation | Medium |
| Geographic gaming | Jurisdictions may manipulate cost data | Low |
| Program eligibility confusion | Transition could cause loss of benefits | High |
| Political interference | Administrations may pressure measurement | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| SPM complexity | Harder to communicate than OPM | Communication |
| Program interaction | Some programs reference OPM in statute | Legal inertia |
| Historical comparability | Breaks time series | Methodology change |
| Geographic data quality | County-level cost data imprecise | Data limitations |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Implementation resources**: Dedicated multi-year appropriation; hiring authority
2. **Geographic data**: Use multiple sources; transparent methodology; appeal process
3. **Eligibility protection**: Hold-harmless provision during transition; parallel systems
4. **Political independence**: Statistical agency independence protections; professional standards
5. **Communication**: Develop accessible explanations; maintain unofficial OPM for comparison
6. **Program updates**: Systematic review of statutory references; model legislation for states

### Asset-Building Tax Credit Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Churning | Deposit and withdraw to claim credit repeatedly | High |
| Income timing | Manipulate AGI to maximize credit | Medium |
| Account misuse | Use funds for non-qualified purposes | Medium |
| Identity fraud | False claims using others' information | Medium |
| Financial industry gaming | High-fee products targeting credit | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Non-filer exclusion | Poorest may not file taxes | Tax system design |
| Savings capacity | Poor may not have funds to save | Poverty itself |
| Financial access | Unbanked cannot participate easily | Banking deserts |
| Benefit timing | Annual credit less helpful than monthly | Tax system design |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Churning prevention**: Holding period requirement; limit claims per account; audit patterns
2. **Income timing**: Prior-year AGI averaging; documentation requirements
3. **Account misuse**: Clawback provisions; verification of qualified use; penalty for misuse
4. **Identity fraud**: IRS identity protection; cross-reference with other data
5. **Financial industry**: Require low-fee account options; fiduciary standard; disclosure
6. **Non-filer inclusion**: Auto-enrollment via SSA; connect to other benefits; simplified filing
7. **Banking access**: Require account access; postal banking option; mobile banking support
