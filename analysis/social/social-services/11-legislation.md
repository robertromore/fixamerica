# Social Services: Legislation

## Overview

Social services legislation operates through multiple federal programs with extensive state discretion. Reform requires amendments to the Social Security Act (TANF, child welfare), the Community Services Block Grant Act, and the Head Start Act, among others. This document presents model federal legislation to strengthen social services, scale evidence-based programs, and address systemic gaps, along with state-level reforms and regulatory approaches.

## Federal Legislation

### Social Services Block Grant Reform Act

**Purpose**: Reform the Social Services Block Grant (SSBG) to increase funding, improve accountability, and prioritize evidence-based services while maintaining state flexibility.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Social Services Block Grant
Reform Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that:
(1) The Social Services Block Grant provides flexible
funding for social services but has been significantly
reduced over time.
(2) Current SSBG funding of approximately $1.7 billion
serves an estimated 23 million recipients annually.
(3) States use SSBG for child care, child welfare,
home-based services, employment services, and other
critical social services.
(4) Increased investment in prevention services reduces
long-term costs.
(5) Evidence-based programs demonstrate significant
returns on investment.

SEC. 3. AUTHORIZATION INCREASE.

Section 2003(c) of the Social Security Act (42 U.S.C.
1397b(c)) is amended to read as follows:

"(c) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—
"(1) IN GENERAL.—There are authorized to be appropriated
to carry out the purposes of this title:
"(A) $3,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2026;
"(B) $3,500,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
"(C) $4,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2028; and
"(D) such sums as may be necessary for each fiscal
year thereafter, adjusted for inflation.

"(2) INFLATION ADJUSTMENT.—For fiscal year 2029 and
each subsequent fiscal year, the amount authorized shall
be the amount for the previous fiscal year increased by
the percentage change in the Consumer Price Index."

SEC. 4. EVIDENCE-BASED SERVICES PRIORITY.

Section 2002 of the Social Security Act is amended by
adding:

"(d) EVIDENCE-BASED SERVICES.—
"(1) PRIORITY.—States shall prioritize funding for
services that meet evidence standards established by
the Secretary.

"(2) MINIMUM ALLOCATION.—Not less than 30 percent of
amounts received shall be used for services that meet
the 'promising,' 'supported,' or 'well-supported'
evidence standards.

"(3) TRANSITION.—States shall have 3 years from the
date of enactment to meet the requirements of this
subsection.

"(4) EVIDENCE STANDARDS.—The Secretary shall establish
evidence standards consistent with those used for
prevention services under section 471(e)(4)."

SEC. 5. PREVENTION SERVICES.

Section 2002 of the Social Security Act is amended by
adding:

"(e) PREVENTION SERVICES.—
"(1) ALLOWABLE USES.—Funds may be used for prevention
services including:
"(A) Home visiting programs that meet evidence standards;
"(B) Parent education and support programs;
"(C) Family preservation services;
"(D) Early intervention services;
"(E) Services to prevent child maltreatment; and
"(F) Two-generation programs.

"(2) DEFINITION.—For purposes of this section, 'prevention
services' means services provided to families to prevent
or address problems before they require more intensive
intervention."

SEC. 6. REPORTING AND ACCOUNTABILITY.

Section 2006 of the Social Security Act is amended to
require States to report annually to the Secretary:

(1) Number of individuals served, disaggregated by
service type, age, race, and ethnicity;
(2) Expenditures by service category;
(3) Proportion of funds used for evidence-based services;
(4) Outcomes achieved, using measures established by
the Secretary; and
(5) Administrative costs.

SEC. 7. TWO-GENERATION SERVICES.

Section 2002 of the Social Security Act is amended to add:

"(f) TWO-GENERATION SERVICES.—
"(1) AUTHORIZATION.—States may use funds for two-generation
programs that provide coordinated services to parents
and children together.

"(2) DEFINITION.—Two-generation programs are those that
intentionally and simultaneously serve parents and
children, addressing economic security, education and
skills development, and social capital for the whole
family.

"(3) BONUS.—States that demonstrate expansion of
two-generation services shall be eligible for additional
funding equal to 5 percent of their base allocation."

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on October 1 of the fiscal
year following enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 3 more than doubles SSBG authorization with inflation indexing
- Section 4 requires evidence-based services priority
- Section 5 explicitly authorizes prevention services
- Section 6 strengthens accountability and data collection
- Section 7 promotes two-generation approaches

**Potential Challenges**:

- Cost of authorization increase
- State resistance to evidence requirements
- Defining and monitoring "evidence-based"
- Political opposition to spending increase

**Refinements**:

- Phase-in evidence requirements over 5 years
- Technical assistance for states
- Flexibility within evidence framework
- State matching incentives

### Evidence-Based Program Expansion Act

**Purpose**: Dramatically expand evidence-based social services programs, particularly home visiting, to reach all eligible families.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Evidence-Based Program
Expansion Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that:
(1) Evidence-based home visiting programs such as
Nurse-Family Partnership demonstrate returns of $5.70
per $1 invested.
(2) Currently, home visiting programs reach only 3 percent
of eligible families.
(3) Head Start serves only 25 percent of eligible children.
(4) Scaling evidence-based programs would significantly
reduce child poverty and improve outcomes.
(5) Prevention is more cost-effective than crisis response.

SEC. 3. HOME VISITING EXPANSION.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—Section 511(j) of the Social Security
Act (42 U.S.C. 711(j)) is amended to read:

"(j) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—
"(1) IN GENERAL.—There are authorized to be appropriated
to carry out this section:
"(A) $1,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2026;
"(B) $2,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
"(C) $3,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2028;
"(D) $4,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2029; and
"(E) $5,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2030 and each
fiscal year thereafter, adjusted for inflation.

"(2) INFLATION ADJUSTMENT.—For fiscal year 2031 and
each subsequent fiscal year, the amount authorized shall
be adjusted for inflation using the Consumer Price Index."

(b) UNIVERSAL ACCESS GOAL.—It is the sense of Congress
that home visiting programs should be available to all
high-risk families by 2035.

SEC. 4. HEAD START EXPANSION.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—Section 639 of the Head Start Act
(42 U.S.C. 9834) is amended to read:

"SEC. 639. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

"(a) IN GENERAL.—There are authorized to be appropriated
to carry out this subchapter:
"(1) $15,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2026;
"(2) $18,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
"(3) $21,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2028;
"(4) $25,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2029; and
"(5) $30,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2030 and each
fiscal year thereafter, adjusted for inflation.

"(b) QUALITY IMPROVEMENTS.—Of amounts appropriated,
not less than 10 percent shall be used for quality
improvements, including workforce compensation."

(b) COMPENSATION.—Section 644 of the Head Start Act is
amended to add:

"(e) COMPENSATION REQUIREMENTS.—
"(1) SALARY STANDARD.—By fiscal year 2030, all Head
Start teachers shall be compensated at rates comparable
to elementary school teachers in the same geographic area.

"(2) PHASE-IN.—Programs shall increase compensation by
at least 10 percent annually until the standard is met.

"(3) FEDERAL SUPPORT.—The Secretary shall provide
additional funds to support compensation increases."

SEC. 5. EVIDENCE STANDARDS.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary shall establish a
unified evidence standard for social services programs
that includes:

(1) Well-supported programs with multiple randomized
controlled trials;
(2) Supported programs with at least one randomized
controlled trial;
(3) Promising programs with at least one quasi-experimental
study; and
(4) Theory-based programs with a clear theory of change
and initial evidence.

(b) CLEARINGHOUSE.—The Secretary shall establish or
designate a clearinghouse for evidence on social services
programs.

(c) TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE.—The Secretary shall provide
technical assistance to States and programs seeking to
implement evidence-based practices.

SEC. 6. SCALING GRANTS.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
$500,000,000 annually for grants to scale evidence-based
social services programs.

(b) ELIGIBLE RECIPIENTS.—States, localities, tribes, and
nonprofit organizations are eligible for grants.

(c) USES.—Grants may be used for:
(1) Expanding proven programs to new populations;
(2) Building organizational capacity;
(3) Workforce development;
(4) Evaluation; and
(5) Technical assistance.

(d) MATCHING.—Grant recipients shall provide a 25 percent
match, which may be in-kind.

SEC. 7. WORKFORCE DEVELOPMENT.

(a) LOAN FORGIVENESS.—Section 428K of the Higher
Education Act is amended to include social services
workers in evidence-based programs as eligible for
loan forgiveness under the same terms as teachers.

(b) SCHOLARSHIP PROGRAM.—The Secretary shall establish
a scholarship program for individuals pursuing degrees
in social work, child development, or related fields who
commit to 5 years of service in evidence-based programs.

(c) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized $100,000,000
annually for workforce development.

SEC. 8. EVALUATION.

(a) REQUIRED EVALUATION.—Programs receiving funding
under this Act shall participate in evaluation activities.

(b) FEDERAL EVALUATION.—The Secretary shall conduct
ongoing evaluation of the expansion of evidence-based
programs under this Act.

(c) REPORT.—The Secretary shall report to Congress
annually on the expansion of evidence-based programs.

SEC. 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect upon enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 3 expands home visiting 10-fold over current levels
- Section 4 nearly triples Head Start funding with quality requirements
- Section 5 establishes unified evidence standards
- Section 6 creates scaling grants for proven programs
- Section 7 addresses workforce pipeline
- Section 8 ensures ongoing evaluation

**Potential Challenges**:

- Significant budget increase
- Workforce capacity to scale
- Maintaining quality at scale
- Implementation timeline

**Refinements**:

- Longer phase-in period
- Capacity building investment
- Quality monitoring requirements
- Regional variation in implementation

### Community Services Investment Act

**Purpose**: Strengthen and modernize Community Action Agencies and the Community Services Block Grant to enhance anti-poverty services.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Community Services
Investment Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that:
(1) Community Action Agencies serve 16 million Americans
annually through 1,000+ local agencies.
(2) The Community Services Block Grant has lost over
40 percent of its value to inflation since the 1980s.
(3) Community Action Agencies provide comprehensive
anti-poverty services including employment, housing,
education, and emergency assistance.
(4) Community-based service delivery is effective at
reaching isolated populations.
(5) Investment in community action strengthens local
capacity to address poverty.

SEC. 3. AUTHORIZATION INCREASE.

Section 674(a) of the Community Services Block Grant
Act (42 U.S.C. 9903(a)) is amended to read:

"(a) AUTHORIZATION.—
"(1) AMOUNTS.—There are authorized to be appropriated
to carry out this subtitle:
"(A) $1,500,000,000 for fiscal year 2026;
"(B) $1,750,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
"(C) $2,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2028; and
"(D) $2,500,000,000 for fiscal year 2029 and each
fiscal year thereafter.

"(2) INFLATION ADJUSTMENT.—Beginning in fiscal year
2030, the amount authorized shall be adjusted annually
for inflation."

SEC. 4. TWO-GENERATION PROGRAMS.

Section 675 of the Community Services Block Grant Act
is amended by adding:

"(g) TWO-GENERATION PROGRAMS.—
"(1) SET-ASIDE.—Not less than 10 percent of amounts
received by States shall be used for two-generation
programs.

"(2) DEFINITION.—Two-generation programs are those
that simultaneously serve parents and children, addressing
economic security, education, and social capital.

"(3) EXAMPLES.—Two-generation programs include:
"(A) Coordinated early childhood and parent employment
services;
"(B) Family literacy programs;
"(C) Parent education combined with child development; and
"(D) Whole family case management."

SEC. 5. EVIDENCE-BASED PRACTICES.

Section 678B of the Community Services Block Grant Act
is amended by adding:

"(e) EVIDENCE-BASED PRACTICES.—
"(1) PRIORITY.—Eligible entities shall prioritize
services that meet evidence standards established by
the Secretary.

"(2) CONTINUOUS IMPROVEMENT.—The Secretary shall
support continuous quality improvement among eligible
entities.

"(3) TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE.—The Secretary shall provide
technical assistance to eligible entities implementing
evidence-based practices."

SEC. 6. DATA AND ACCOUNTABILITY.

Section 678E of the Community Services Block Grant Act
is amended to require:

(1) Standardized outcome measures across all eligible
entities;
(2) Disaggregated data collection by race, ethnicity,
age, and geography;
(3) Longitudinal tracking of family outcomes;
(4) Annual public reporting; and
(5) Continuous improvement based on data.

SEC. 7. COMMUNITY ACTION MODERNIZATION.

(a) TECHNOLOGY GRANTS.—The Secretary shall award grants
to eligible entities for technology improvements including:
(1) Client data systems;
(2) Benefits screening tools;
(3) Referral networks; and
(4) Service coordination platforms.

(b) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized $100,000,000
annually for technology grants.

SEC. 8. INNOVATION FUND.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established a Community
Action Innovation Fund.

(b) PURPOSE.—The Fund shall support innovative approaches
to poverty reduction by eligible entities.

(c) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized $200,000,000
annually for the Fund.

(d) ELIGIBLE USES.—
(1) Pilot programs;
(2) Scaling promising practices;
(3) Cross-sector partnerships;
(4) Research and evaluation; and
(5) Capacity building.

SEC. 9. TRIPARTITE BOARD STRENGTHENING.

Section 676B is amended to add requirements for:

(1) Training for board members;
(2) Meaningful participation by low-income community
members;
(3) Strategic planning with community input; and
(4) Accountability to community served.

SEC. 10. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect upon enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 3 more than triples CSBG funding
- Section 4 mandates two-generation programs
- Section 5 promotes evidence-based practices
- Section 6 strengthens data and accountability
- Section 7 supports technology modernization
- Section 8 creates innovation fund
- Section 9 strengthens community governance

**Potential Challenges**:

- Budget increase magnitude
- Capacity to expand effectively
- Balancing flexibility and accountability
- Evidence requirements for flexible funding

**Refinements**:

- Phase-in funding increase
- Technical assistance for implementation
- Flexibility in evidence requirements
- Innovation authority for new approaches

### Two-Generation Opportunity Act

**Purpose**: Create a comprehensive federal framework for two-generation (whole family) approaches to poverty reduction.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Two-Generation Opportunity
Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that:
(1) Traditional anti-poverty programs address either
parents or children separately, missing opportunities
to address family needs holistically.
(2) Two-generation approaches that serve parents and
children together show improved outcomes for both.
(3) Parent economic success and child development are
interconnected.
(4) Current program silos prevent effective whole-family
service delivery.
(5) Federal policy should support innovation in
two-generation approaches.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

For purposes of this Act:

(1) TWO-GENERATION APPROACH.—The term "two-generation
approach" means an approach that:
(A) Intentionally and simultaneously serves children
and the adults in their lives together;
(B) Addresses child development, parent education and
economic advancement, and family social capital; and
(C) Measures success by outcomes for the whole family.

(2) ELIGIBLE ENTITY.—The term "eligible entity" means:
(A) A State;
(B) A local government;
(C) An Indian tribe or tribal organization;
(D) A community-based organization; or
(E) A Head Start or Early Head Start program.

SEC. 4. TWO-GENERATION DEMONSTRATION GRANTS.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—The Secretary is authorized to award
grants to eligible entities to develop, expand, and
evaluate two-generation programs.

(b) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There is authorized
to be appropriated $300,000,000 annually for grants
under this section.

(c) USE OF FUNDS.—Grants may be used for:
(1) Early childhood education combined with parent
career pathways;
(2) Family literacy and parent education;
(3) Whole-family case management;
(4) Coordinated health and social services;
(5) Housing combined with family support services;
(6) Economic coaching and asset building; and
(7) Evaluation and continuous improvement.

(d) PRIORITY.—The Secretary shall give priority to
applications that:
(1) Demonstrate coordination across multiple service
systems;
(2) Include parent voice in program design;
(3) Build on existing programs;
(4) Commit to rigorous evaluation; and
(5) Serve populations with greatest need.

SEC. 5. TANF TWO-GENERATION OPTION.

(a) TANF AMENDMENT.—Section 407 of the Social Security
Act is amended by adding:

"(j) TWO-GENERATION PARTICIPATION.—
"(1) COUNTABLE ACTIVITY.—Participation in an approved
two-generation program shall count as a work activity
under this section.

"(2) HOURS.—Participation in a two-generation program
shall count for up to 30 hours per week toward work
participation requirements.

"(3) APPROVAL.—The Secretary shall approve two-generation
programs that meet criteria established by regulation."

(b) INCENTIVE.—States operating approved two-generation
programs shall receive an enhanced federal match rate
of 90 percent for families enrolled in such programs.

SEC. 6. INTEGRATED SERVICE DELIVERY.

(a) WAIVERS.—The Secretary of Health and Human Services,
in coordination with the Secretaries of Education, Labor,
and Housing and Urban Development, shall establish a
process for eligible entities to obtain waivers of
program requirements that impede two-generation service
delivery.

(b) DATA SHARING.—Federal agencies shall facilitate data
sharing across programs to support two-generation approaches,
consistent with privacy protections.

(c) COORDINATED FUNDING.—The Secretary shall issue guidance
on braiding and blending federal funding streams to
support two-generation programs.

SEC. 7. RESEARCH AND EVALUATION.

(a) EVALUATION REQUIREMENT.—Programs receiving funding
under this Act shall participate in evaluation activities.

(b) FEDERAL RESEARCH.—The Secretary shall conduct
research on effective two-generation approaches, including:
(1) Randomized controlled trials where feasible;
(2) Implementation research;
(3) Cost-effectiveness analysis; and
(4) Long-term outcome tracking.

(c) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized $50,000,000
annually for research and evaluation.

SEC. 8. TWO-GENERATION RESOURCE CENTER.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary shall establish a
national Two-Generation Resource Center to:
(1) Provide technical assistance to programs;
(2) Develop and disseminate best practices;
(3) Support peer learning networks;
(4) Maintain a clearinghouse of evidence; and
(5) Convene stakeholders.

(b) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized $20,000,000
annually for the Resource Center.

SEC. 9. STATE PLANNING GRANTS.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—The Secretary shall award planning
grants to States to develop comprehensive two-generation
strategies.

(b) USES.—Grants may be used for:
(1) Needs assessment;
(2) Stakeholder engagement;
(3) Policy analysis;
(4) Implementation planning; and
(5) Pilot design.

(c) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized $50,000,000
annually for State planning grants.

SEC. 10. REPORTS.

(a) ANNUAL REPORT.—The Secretary shall report annually
to Congress on the implementation of two-generation
programs.

(b) CONTENTS.—Reports shall include:
(1) Programs funded;
(2) Populations served;
(3) Outcomes achieved;
(4) Lessons learned; and
(5) Recommendations.

SEC. 11. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect upon enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 3 defines two-generation approach
- Section 4 creates demonstration grant program
- Section 5 allows 2Gen to count toward TANF work requirements
- Section 6 addresses program integration barriers
- Section 7 establishes research agenda
- Section 8 creates national resource center
- Section 9 supports state planning

**Potential Challenges**:

- Complexity of cross-program coordination
- Evaluation of complex interventions
- State capacity
- Defining and monitoring fidelity

**Refinements**:

- Technical assistance investment
- Phased implementation
- Flexibility in approach
- Multiple evidence standards

## Regulatory Framework

### HHS Guidance on Two-Generation TANF

**Authority**: 42 U.S.C. 604 (authority to issue regulations)

**Draft Guidance**:

```text
DEPARTMENT OF HEALTH AND HUMAN SERVICES
Administration for Children and Families

TANF PROGRAM INSTRUCTION

TO: State TANF Directors
FROM: Assistant Secretary for Children and Families
RE: Two-Generation Approaches in TANF

PURPOSE: This guidance supports State implementation
of two-generation approaches within TANF programs.

1. COUNTABLE ACTIVITIES

Two-generation program participation may count toward
work participation requirements when programs:

(a) Address parent economic advancement and child
development together;
(b) Meet evidence standards or have evaluation plan;
(c) Include case management; and
(d) Track outcomes for parents and children.

2. ELIGIBLE ACTIVITIES

Two-generation activities include:
(a) Head Start or Early Head Start combined with
parent career services;
(b) Family literacy programs;
(c) Parent education with workforce development;
(d) Community college combined with child care; and
(e) Comprehensive family services.

3. DOCUMENTATION

States shall document:
(a) Program design and services;
(b) Evidence base or evaluation plan;
(c) Enrollment and participation; and
(d) Family outcomes.

4. TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE

The Office of Family Assistance will provide technical
assistance to States implementing two-generation
approaches.

EFFECTIVE DATE: This guidance is effective immediately.
```

### HHS Evidence Standards for Social Services

**Draft Regulatory Framework**:

```text
DEPARTMENT OF HEALTH AND HUMAN SERVICES

EVIDENCE STANDARDS FOR SOCIAL SERVICES PROGRAMS

1. PURPOSE

These standards establish a unified evidence framework
for social services programs receiving federal funding.

2. EVIDENCE TIERS

(a) WELL-SUPPORTED.—Programs with:
(1) Multiple randomized controlled trials; and
(2) Demonstrated favorable outcomes.

(b) SUPPORTED.—Programs with:
(1) At least one randomized controlled trial with
favorable outcomes; and
(2) Implementation in multiple sites.

(c) PROMISING.—Programs with:
(1) At least one well-designed quasi-experimental study
with favorable outcomes; or
(2) Multiple correlational studies with strong
methodology.

(d) THEORY-BASED.—Programs with:
(1) Clear theory of change;
(2) Initial evidence of implementation; and
(3) Commitment to rigorous evaluation.

3. APPLICATION

(a) Priority funding shall be given to well-supported
and supported programs.
(b) Promising programs may receive funding with
evaluation requirements.
(c) Theory-based programs may receive pilot funding
with rigorous evaluation.

4. CLEARINGHOUSE

The Department shall maintain a clearinghouse of
programs meeting each evidence tier.
```

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Authority

**Spending Power**:

- Social services programs operate under Congress's spending power (Art. I, Sec. 8)
- Conditions on federal funds must be related to federal interest
- Clear statement required for conditions
- *South Dakota v. Dole* framework applies

**Federal-State Structure**:

- Block grants preserve state flexibility
- Federal can set conditions on funding
- States retain substantial discretion
- Evidence requirements are permissible conditions

### Federalism Questions

**State Flexibility**:

- Current programs provide significant state discretion
- Federal standards must balance flexibility and accountability
- Enhanced match rates can incentivize without mandating
- Technical assistance supports implementation

**Preemption**:

- Federal minimum standards do not preempt more generous state policies
- States may exceed federal requirements
- State innovations may inform federal policy

### Enforcement Mechanisms

**Federal**:

- Withholding of federal funds for non-compliance
- Technical assistance and corrective action plans
- Data collection and public reporting
- Office of Inspector General audits

**State**:

- State administrative procedures
- State legislative oversight
- State auditor review
- Public accountability

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Block grant diversion | States redirect funds from core services | Critical |
| Evidence standard gaming | Programs claim evidence without rigor | High |
| Two-generation dilution | Programs claim 2Gen without integration | High |
| Workforce shortcuts | Training reduced to meet scale demands | Moderate |
| Data manipulation | Outcomes reported selectively | Moderate |
| Supplantation | Federal funds replace rather than supplement state | High |
| Administrative overhead | Excessive costs reduce service delivery | Moderate |
| Contractor capture | Same organizations continue despite poor performance | Moderate |
| Cherry-picking | Programs serve easiest-to-serve populations | High |
| Fidelity drift | Programs diverge from evidence-based models | High |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Block grant erosion | Funding declines without indexing | No automatic adjustment |
| State variation | Unequal services across states | No federal minimum standards |
| Scale vs. quality trade-off | Quality suffers as programs expand | Insufficient workforce investment |
| Integration barriers | Siloed programs despite coordination goals | Different federal agencies, funding streams |
| Short-term funding | Programs cannot plan or invest | Annual appropriations |
| Evidence definition disputes | What counts as "evidence-based" contested | No unified standard |
| Workforce shortage | Cannot implement quality programs | Low pay, burnout, training gaps |
| Prevention undervaluation | Crisis response prioritized | Short-term political incentives |
| Coordination authority | No one responsible for family outcomes | Siloed program accountability |
| Political vulnerability | Programs cut during budget constraints | Weak political constituency |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Block Grant Protection**: Require inflation indexing in all block grants. Establish spending floors for core services (50%+ for direct services). Annual public reporting on spending by category. Penalties for supplantation.

2. **Evidence Standards**: Establish unified federal evidence clearinghouse. Require independent verification of evidence claims. Phase-in requirements with technical assistance. Allow multiple pathways to evidence tier.

3. **Two-Generation Fidelity**: Define essential elements of two-generation approach. Require measurement of both parent and child outcomes. Certification process for programs claiming 2Gen status. Site visits and quality monitoring.

4. **Workforce Capacity**: Fund workforce pipeline before service expansion. Require competitive compensation as condition of funding. Establish caseload standards with monitoring. Investment in training infrastructure.

5. **Data Integrity**: Standardized outcome measures across programs. Independent data collection/verification. Public dashboards with program-level data. Penalties for data manipulation.

6. **Integration Authority**: Designate coordinating entity with authority. Waiver process for cross-program coordination. Shared outcome accountability. Braided funding guidance and support.

7. **Long-Term Sustainability**: Multi-year appropriations where possible. Counter-cyclical funding triggers. Endowment or trust fund approaches. Building broader political coalition.

8. **Quality at Scale**: Phase expansion to match capacity. Quality standards as condition of expansion. Continuous quality improvement requirements. Independent monitoring.

9. **Cherry-Picking Prevention**: Require service to full eligible population. Risk-adjustment in outcome measurement. Penalties for inappropriate exclusion. Universal access goals.

10. **Model Fidelity**: Clear model specifications for evidence-based programs. Training and certification requirements. Monitoring and technical assistance. Adaptation guidelines within fidelity.

### General Implementation Concerns

**Transition Challenges**:

- Existing programs may resist change
- Workforce retraining needed
- IT systems require updates
- Political resistance from current beneficiaries

**Funding Sustainability**:

- Appropriations process creates uncertainty
- Evidence-based programs more expensive
- State fiscal capacity varies
- Economic downturns threaten funding

**Political Durability**:

- Administration changes can reverse progress
- State implementation varies
- Program cuts during fiscal stress
- Need for sustained coalition

**Unintended Consequences**:

- Evidence requirements may exclude innovative programs
- Scale demands may reduce quality
- Two-generation requirements may burden programs
- Reporting requirements may divert resources
