# Social Services: Stakeholders

## Who Is Affected by Social Services

### Families Receiving Services

**Primary Recipients**

| Population | Number | Key Needs |
|------------|--------|-----------|
| Families in poverty | 11 million | Comprehensive support |
| Children in poverty | 11.6 million | Development, stability |
| TANF recipients | 2 million | Employment, support |
| Head Start children | 833,000 | Early education |
| Foster care children | 391,000 | Safety, permanency |
| Home visiting families | 150,000 | Parent support |
| Community Action clients | 16 million | Various services |

**What Recipients Need**

| Need | Service |
|------|---------|
| Income stability | Employment services, cash assistance |
| Child care | Head Start, child care subsidies |
| Housing | Assistance, stability |
| Healthcare | Medicaid, FQHCs |
| Mental health | Treatment, support |
| Navigation | Case management |
| Crisis response | Emergency services |

**Political Power of Recipients**: **Very Low**

- Time consumed by survival
- Stigma silences advocacy
- Less likely to vote
- No organized political voice
- Dependent on others to advocate

### Children

**The Most Affected, Least Powerful**

| Group | Number |
|-------|--------|
| Children in poverty | 11.6 million |
| In foster care | 391,000 |
| In Head Start | 833,000 |
| Receiving child welfare services | 3+ million investigated |
| In home visiting | ~100,000 |

**Children Cannot**:
- Vote
- Organize
- Lobby
- Testify effectively
- Hold officials accountable

**Political Power**: **None** (depend entirely on adult advocates)

### Social Service Workers

**The Workforce**

| Category | Number | Key Issues |
|----------|--------|------------|
| Social workers (all) | 708,000 | Pay, caseloads, burnout |
| Child welfare workers | 50,000+ | High turnover, trauma |
| Head Start staff | 256,000 | Low wages |
| Community Action staff | 100,000+ | Variable |
| Home visitors | 10,000+ | Training, support |
| Case managers | 100,000+ | High caseloads |

**What Workers Need**

| Need | Current Reality |
|------|-----------------|
| Competitive pay | 20-30% below comparable |
| Reasonable caseloads | Often 2-3x recommended |
| Professional support | Inadequate supervision |
| Career advancement | Limited pathways |
| Safety | Sometimes threatened |
| Recognition | Undervalued profession |

**Political Power**: **Moderate**

- Professional associations (NASW)
- Some union representation
- Can organize
- But scattered across many employers

## Who Has Power Over Social Services

### Federal Government

**Executive Branch**

| Agency | Authority |
|--------|-----------|
| HHS (ACF) | TANF, Head Start, child welfare, home visiting |
| HHS (HRSA) | FQHCs, health workforce |
| USDA | SNAP employment services |
| DOL | Workforce programs |
| HUD | Housing services |
| DOE | Education services |

**Key Federal Powers**

| Power | Impact |
|-------|--------|
| Funding levels | Appropriations determine scale |
| Regulations | Implementation rules |
| Waivers | Allow state experimentation |
| Enforcement | Hold states accountable |
| Research | Evidence development |

**Political Power**: **Very High**

**Congress**

| Committee | Jurisdiction |
|-----------|--------------|
| House Ways and Means | TANF, child welfare |
| Senate Finance | TANF, child welfare |
| House Education and Labor | Head Start, workforce |
| Senate HELP | Head Start, workforce |
| Appropriations | All funding |

**Key Congressional Powers**

| Power | Importance |
|-------|------------|
| Appropriations | Sets funding levels |
| Authorization | Program design |
| Oversight | Holds agencies accountable |
| Reauthorization | Major reform opportunities |

**Political Power**: **Very High**

### State Governments

**State Agencies**

| Agency Type | Responsibility |
|-------------|---------------|
| Human Services/Social Services | TANF, welfare |
| Children and Families | Child welfare |
| Health | Medicaid, public health |
| Education | Pre-K, school services |
| Workforce | Employment programs |

**State Powers**

| Power | Scope |
|-------|-------|
| TANF spending | Enormous discretion |
| Child welfare policy | Investigation, placement |
| Medicaid administration | Coverage, services |
| Regulation | Provider standards |
| State funding | Supplements federal |

**Political Power**: **Very High** within state

**State Variation Examples**

| Policy | Progressive State | Restrictive State |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|
| TANF cash assistance share | 50%+ | 5% |
| Child welfare caseloads | 12:1 | 30:1 |
| State EITC | Generous | None |
| Medicaid expansion | Yes | No |

### Local Government

**Local Authority**

| Area | Local Role |
|------|------------|
| County welfare | Direct administration (many states) |
| Child protective services | Investigation, case management |
| Schools | Service coordination |
| Community development | Local programs |
| Emergency services | Crisis response |

**Political Power**: **Moderate** (constrained by state/federal)

## Service Delivery Organizations

### Nonprofit Organizations

**The Delivery System**

| Type | Number | Role |
|------|--------|------|
| Community Action Agencies | 1,000+ | Comprehensive anti-poverty |
| United Way agencies | 1,800+ | Funding, coordination |
| Family service agencies | Thousands | Case management, counseling |
| Child welfare agencies | Many | Foster care, adoption |
| Faith-based organizations | Thousands | Various services |

**What Nonprofits Need**

| Need | Current Reality |
|------|-----------------|
| Adequate contracts | Below-cost reimbursement |
| Multi-year funding | Short contracts |
| Reasonable overhead | Indirect rates capped |
| Timely payment | Delays common |
| Flexibility | Rigid requirements |

**Political Power**: **Moderate**

- Can advocate (with limits)
- Employ many people
- Community presence
- But depend on government funding

## Power Analysis

### Who Has Most Power Over Social Services

| Actor | Power Level | Key Leverage |
|-------|-------------|--------------|
| Congress | Very high | Appropriations, authorization |
| State legislatures | Very high | State policy, funding |
| Governors | High | Administration, budget |
| Federal agencies | High | Regulations, waivers |
| Advocacy coalitions | Moderate | Expertise, mobilization |
| Foundations | Moderate | Funding, research |
| Service providers | Moderate | Implementation, information |
| Recipients | Very low | Moral authority only |
| Children | None | Completely dependent on others |

### Power Imbalance

The fundamental problem: those most affected have least power.

| Group | Stake | Power |
|-------|-------|-------|
| Children | Highest | None |
| Low-income families | Very high | Very low |
| Social workers | High | Moderate |
| Nonprofits | High | Moderate |
| Taxpayers | Moderate | High (through votes) |
| Elected officials | Lower direct stake | Highest power |

## Strategic Implications

### Building Power for Social Services

**Short-Term**

| Strategy | Actors |
|----------|--------|
| Coalition building | Unite fragmented advocates |
| State campaigns | Win where possible |
| Business allies | Frame as workforce issue |
| Bipartisan evidence | Emphasize ROI |

**Long-Term**

| Strategy | Actors |
|----------|--------|
| Organize recipients | Build political voice |
| Narrative change | Challenge stigma |
| Universal programs | Broaden constituency |
| Workforce advocacy | Professional organizing |

### Potential Bipartisan Issues

| Issue | Republican Interest | Democratic Interest |
|-------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Evidence-based programs | Accountability | Funding what works |
| Child welfare reform | Family preservation | Prevention |
| Workforce development | Employment focus | Quality jobs |
| Two-generation | Work + family | Whole family |
