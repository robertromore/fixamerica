# Safety Net Design: Stakeholders

## Primary Stakeholders

### Low-Income Families

**Who They Are**:
- 37+ million people in poverty
- 11+ million children
- Working poor earning below living wage
- Elderly on fixed incomes
- People with disabilities
- Single parents

**Stakes in Design**:
- Administrative burden determines access
- Benefit cliffs affect work decisions
- Program coordination affects total support
- Dignity in system interaction
- Stability for family planning

**Current Experience**:
- Navigate 3-5 programs typically
- 20-40 hours to apply/maintain benefits
- Monthly income volatility
- Fear of cliff effects
- Documentation burdens

**Desired Outcomes**:
- Simplified access
- Seamless transitions
- Adequate support
- Work always rewarded
- Respectful treatment

**Power/Influence**: Very low
- Diffuse, unorganized
- Low voter participation
- Stigma limits advocacy
- Not campaign donors
- Limited media access

### Children in Low-Income Families

**Who They Are**:
- 11+ million in poverty
- 5+ million in deep poverty
- 21 million in near-poverty (100-200% FPL)

**Stakes in Design**:
- Program complexity reduces family resources
- Churn disrupts healthcare, nutrition
- Parental stress from navigation
- Benefit cliffs affect family decisions
- Long-term development affected

**Current Experience**:
- Coverage gaps from administrative churn
- Food insecurity from SNAP gaps
- Parental attention diverted to bureaucracy
- Family stress from system navigation

**Desired Outcomes**:
- Continuous coverage
- Stable nutrition
- Family stability
- Parental time preserved

**Power/Influence**: None directly
- Cannot advocate for selves
- Dependent on adult advocates
- Powerful rhetorically
- Bipartisan concern (in theory)

### Workers in the Benefit Cliff Zone

**Who They Are**:
- Earnings $20,000-$50,000
- Often single parents
- Multiple part-time jobs common
- Service sector predominant

**Stakes in Design**:
- Benefit cliffs affect promotion decisions
- EMTRs can exceed 100%
- Hours worked carefully managed
- Career advancement penalized

**Current Experience**:
- Calculate cliff effects on every raise
- May refuse overtime or promotions
- Track multiple benefit thresholds
- Plan income around cliffs

**Desired Outcomes**:
- Work always rewarded
- Gradual benefit phase-outs
- Career advancement possible
- Predictable transitions

**Power/Influence**: Low
- Working, limited time for advocacy
- Some union representation
- Not typically organized on this issue

## Government Stakeholders

### Federal Agencies

**Department of Health and Human Services**:
- Administers TANF, Medicaid, CHIP, LIHEAP, childcare
- Largest safety net role
- Multiple internal divisions
- State oversight responsibility

*Interests*:
- Program integrity
- Meeting statutory requirements
- Budget management
- Political direction

*Position on Integration*:
- Supportive in principle
- Protective of authority
- Resource constraints
- Technical challenges cited

**Department of Agriculture (USDA)**:
- Administers SNAP, WIC, school meals
- Food focus
- Farm bill politics

*Interests*:
- Agricultural constituencies
- Nutrition mission
- Program integrity
- Farm bill reauthorization

*Position on Integration*:
- Defends SNAP in USDA
- Resistant to HHS merger
- Open to coordination
- Protective of food focus

**Department of Housing and Urban Development**:
- Section 8, public housing
- Limited reach (5M of 17M+ eligible)
- Waitlist management

*Interests*:
- Housing industry relationships
- Limited budget management
- Program integrity
- Local housing authority oversight

*Position on Integration*:
- Generally open to coordination
- Technical barriers cited
- Limited capacity for change

**Social Security Administration**:
- SSI, SSDI
- Individual determination focus
- Highly automated (for its programs)

*Interests*:
- Accurate eligibility determination
- Disability focus
- Individual assessment culture
- Automation investment

*Position on Integration*:
- Resistant to expanded role
- Technical excellence cited
- Capacity concerns
- Would resist non-disability programs

**Department of Treasury/IRS**:
- EITC, CTC
- Tax-based delivery
- Annual cycle

*Interests*:
- Tax administration efficiency
- Fraud prevention
- Simplicity
- Annual filing cycle

*Position on Integration*:
- Delivery capacity demonstrated
- Monthly payment possible (2021 CTC)
- Resistant to welfare integration
- Identity verification concerns

### State Agencies

**State Human Services Departments**:
- Administer TANF, often SNAP, Medicaid
- Major employers
- State budget significant

*Interests*:
- Federal funding maximization
- Administrative efficiency
- Error rate minimization
- Staff management
- Political direction

*Position on Integration*:
- Varies by state dramatically
- Some leading (Colorado, Rhode Island)
- Others resistant
- Capacity constraints real

**State Workforce Agencies**:
- Unemployment insurance
- WIOA programs
- Work requirement coordination

*Interests*:
- Job placement metrics
- Federal performance requirements
- Employer relationships
- Training programs

*Position on Integration*:
- Work-focused
- Support coordination with welfare
- Technical barriers

### Local Government

**Counties and Cities**:
- Often administer state programs
- General Assistance (some)
- Local funding contributions

*Interests*:
- Cost management
- Service delivery
- Local political environment
- Staff capacity

*Position on Integration*:
- Highly variable
- Some innovating
- Others constrained
- Funding concerns dominant

## Power Analysis

### High Power, High Interest

- Federal agencies (within jurisdiction)
- State governors/legislatures
- Congressional committees
- OMB/White House

### High Power, Lower Interest

- General public
- Most employers
- Most of Congress

### Lower Power, High Interest

- Low-income families
- Advocacy organizations
- Researchers
- Recipient organizations

### Strategic Implications

**Key Leverage Points**:
1. States as innovators (Colorado, Rhode Island)
2. Federal agency cooperation (HHS-USDA)
3. Bipartisan cliff elimination support
4. Child poverty as unifying concern
5. Administrative cost savings argument

**Blocking Coalitions**:
1. Program-specific interests
2. Agency turf defenders
3. Work requirement proponents (for some reforms)
4. Anti-spending conservatives
