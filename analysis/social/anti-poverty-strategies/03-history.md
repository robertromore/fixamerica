# Anti-Poverty Strategies: History

## Overview

Anti-poverty policy in America has cycled through periods of ambition and retrenchment, reflecting broader political and economic shifts. From the Progressive Era's settlement houses through the War on Poverty to welfare reform and beyond, the nation has experimented with a wide range of approaches--work-based incentives, direct cash assistance, in-kind programs, asset-building, and comprehensive strategies. Understanding this history is essential to grasping current debates and charting effective paths forward.

## Timeline of Major Developments

## Early Anti-Poverty Approaches (1900-1960)

### Progressive Era (1900-1920)

**Settlement House Movement**

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Leaders | Jane Addams, Lillian Wald |
| Approach | Community-based services, education |
| Hull House | Chicago; model for movement |
| Philosophy | Environment shapes outcomes |
| Limitation | Local, voluntary, limited reach |

**Mothers' Pensions (1910s-1930s)**

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Purpose | Support for widowed mothers with children |
| States | 40 states by 1920 |
| Benefit | Cash assistance to stay home |
| Coverage | Limited; mostly white widows |
| Significance | First government cash assistance |

**Key Characteristics**

- State-level, not federal
- Categorical (only "deserving" recipients)
- Moralistic administration
- Race and gender discrimination
- Precursor to ADC/TANF

### New Deal Era (1930s)

**Social Security Act of 1935**

| Program | Purpose |
|---------|---------|
| Old Age Insurance | Retirement income |
| Unemployment Insurance | Job loss protection |
| Aid to Dependent Children (ADC) | Support for single mothers |

**ADC (Precursor to AFDC/TANF)**

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Purpose | Cash for children in single-parent homes |
| Administration | Federal-state partnership |
| Coverage | Initially limited; excluded many |
| Benefit | Modest; state variation |
| Evolution | Became AFDC, then TANF |

**Work Relief**

| Program | Scale |
|---------|-------|
| WPA | 8+ million jobs |
| CCC | 3+ million young men |
| PWA | Major infrastructure |
| Approach | Government as employer of last resort |

**Significance**: New Deal established federal role in anti-poverty but excluded many (agricultural, domestic workers) and maintained categorical approach.

## The Negative Income Tax Experiments (1960s-1970s)

### The NIT Concept

**Milton Friedman's Proposal (1962)**

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Guaranteed minimum | Floor income for all |
| Phase-out rate | Benefits reduce as income rises |
| Replace welfare | Unified, simple system |
| Work incentive | Always better to work |
| Bipartisan appeal | Market-friendly poverty solution |

**Nixon's Family Assistance Plan (1969)**

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Proposal | Guaranteed income for families |
| Amount | $1,600/year for family of 4 |
| Significance | Conservative president proposes NIT |
| Fate | Passed House; died in Senate |
| Opposition | Liberals (too low), conservatives (welfare) |

### The Experiments

**Four Major Experiments**

| Experiment | Years | Location |
|------------|-------|----------|
| New Jersey | 1968-1972 | Urban, New Jersey |
| Rural | 1970-1972 | Iowa, North Carolina |
| Gary | 1971-1974 | Gary, Indiana |
| Seattle-Denver (SIME/DIME) | 1970-1980 | Seattle, Denver |

**Key Findings: Work Effects**

| Experiment | Primary Earner Hours | Secondary Earner Hours |
|------------|---------------------|------------------------|
| New Jersey | -1 to -2% | -5 to -6% |
| Rural | -1% | -2% |
| Gary | -1 to -2% | -3 to -4% |
| Seattle-Denver | -1 to -5% | -5 to -9% |

**Interpretation**

| Finding | Implication |
|---------|-------------|
| Minor work reduction | "Fears overblown" |
| Mainly secondary earners | Mothers with children |
| Used for productive activities | Education, caregiving |
| Poverty reduction | Substantial |

**Why Experiments Didn't Lead to NIT**

| Factor | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Marriage effects | Seattle-Denver showed divorce increase (later disputed) |
| Political backlash | Reagan era shifted focus |
| Welfare reform direction | Toward work requirements |
| Timing | Economic turmoil, political shifts |

## The EITC Era (1975-Present)

### Origins of EITC

**1975 Creation**

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Purpose | Offset payroll taxes for low-income workers |
| Initial size | Small (10% of earnings up to $4,000) |
| Key feature | Work-conditional; refundable |
| Political framing | Reward work, not dependency |

### Major Expansions

| Year | Change | President |
|------|--------|-----------|
| 1986 | Indexed to inflation | Reagan |
| 1990 | Increased rates | Bush 41 |
| 1993 | Major expansion | Clinton |
| 2001 | Marriage penalty reduction | Bush 43 |
| 2009 | Larger credit, more children | Obama |
| 2021 | Childless expansion (temporary) | Biden |

**Growth in EITC**

| Year | Maximum Credit (3+ children) | Claims (millions) |
|------|------------------------------|-------------------|
| 1975 | $400 | 6.2 |
| 1990 | $953 | 12.5 |
| 2000 | $3,888 | 19.3 |
| 2010 | $5,666 | 27.4 |
| 2020 | $6,660 | 25.2 |
| 2024 | $7,830 | ~31 |

**Why EITC Succeeded Politically**

| Factor | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| Work-conditioned | "Reward work, not welfare" |
| Bipartisan | Expanded by both parties |
| Invisible | Through tax system |
| Effective | Lifts millions from poverty |
| Research support | Strong evidence base |

## Welfare Reform Era (1990s)

### The Road to 1996

**AFDC Critics**

| Critique | From |
|----------|------|
| "Dependency" | Conservatives |
| Work disincentives | Economists |
| Bureaucracy | Both sides |
| Racial coding | Political strategists |

**State Waivers (1992-1996)**

| State | Experiment |
|-------|------------|
| Wisconsin | W-2 program (work-focused) |
| Michigan | Work requirements |
| New Jersey | Family cap |
| Multiple | Time limits |

### Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act (1996)

**Key Changes**

| Before (AFDC) | After (TANF) |
|---------------|--------------|
| Entitlement | Block grant |
| Federal standards | State flexibility |
| No time limit | 60-month lifetime limit |
| Limited work requirements | Mandatory work requirements |
| Growing caseloads | Caseload targets |

**Immediate Effects**

| Metric | Change |
|--------|--------|
| Caseloads | Dropped 60%+ |
| Poverty rate | Declined (strong economy + EITC) |
| Deep poverty | Increased |
| Extreme poverty ($2/day) | Increased |

**Long-Term Assessment**

| Finding | Evidence |
|---------|----------|
| Caseload reduction | Dramatic and sustained |
| Poverty reduction | Minimal; other factors (economy, EITC) |
| Deep poverty | Increased |
| Work increase | Initial; faded |
| Material hardship | Mixed; some worse |

## Child Tax Credit Evolution (1997-Present)

### Creation and Expansion

| Year | CTC Amount | Refundability |
|------|------------|---------------|
| 1997 | $500 | None |
| 2001 | $1,000 | Partial (ACTC) |
| 2017 | $2,000 | Partial ($1,400) |
| 2021 | $3,600/$3,000 | Full |
| 2022 | $2,000 | Partial ($1,700) |

### The 2021 Transformation

**American Rescue Plan CTC**

| Element | Change |
|---------|--------|
| Amount | Increased to $3,600 (under 6), $3,000 (6-17) |
| Refundability | Made fully refundable |
| Earnings requirement | Removed |
| Delivery | Monthly advance payments |

**Results**

| Outcome | Value |
|---------|-------|
| Child poverty reduction | 46% |
| Children lifted out | 3.7 million |
| Employment effect | None/positive |
| Food hardship | Reduced |
| Material hardship | Reduced |

**Expiration and Reversal**

| Metric | 2021 | 2022 |
|--------|------|------|
| SPM child poverty | 5.2% | 12.4% |
| Children in poverty | 3.8 million | 9.0 million |

## Guaranteed Income History

**Thomas Paine (1797)**

- *Agrarian Justice*
- Proposed lump sum at age 21
- Funded by ground rent

**Henry George (1879)**

- Single tax on land
- Citizens' dividend from land rent

## Lessons from History

### What Has Worked

| Strategy | Evidence |
|----------|----------|
| EITC expansion | Bipartisan, effective, durable |
| CTC expansion | 2021 showed dramatic impact |
| Child allowances | International success |
| Goal-setting | UK child poverty decline |
| Cash transfers | NIT experiments, modern pilots |

### What Has Failed

| Strategy | Outcome |
|----------|---------|
| Work requirements | Do not increase work; cause churn |
| Time limits | Push to deep poverty |
| Block grants | Inadequate, declining value |
| Categorization | Leave many out |

### Recurring Patterns

| Pattern | Manifestation |
|---------|---------------|
| Work ethic concerns | Every era; often override evidence |
| Racial politics | Shape program design, perceptions |
| Federal-state tension | Block grants vs. national standards |
| Universal vs. targeted | Ongoing debate |
| Ambitious to retrenchment | Cyclical |
