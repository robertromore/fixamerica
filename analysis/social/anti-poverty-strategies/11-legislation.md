# Anti-Poverty Strategies: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Implementing comprehensive anti-poverty strategies requires legislative action at federal and state levels. This document provides model legislation for key anti-poverty approaches: a national anti-poverty strategy with goal-setting, Universal Basic Income pilot programs, baby bonds (children's savings accounts), and comprehensive poverty elimination. Each proposal includes draft text, explanations, constitutional considerations, and analysis of potential loopholes and shortcomings.

## Constitutional Considerations

### Federal Authority for Anti-Poverty Legislation

The federal government has broad constitutional authority to enact anti-poverty legislation through:

| Power | Application |
|-------|-------------|
| Taxing and Spending | Tax credits (EITC, CTC), appropriations |
| Commerce Clause | Regulation affecting employment, wages |
| Fourteenth Amendment | Equal protection, fundamental rights |
| General Welfare | Broad spending authority |

### Constitutional Amendment: Right to Economic Security

**Purpose**: Establish a constitutional foundation for comprehensive anti-poverty policy.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. The right of all persons within the United States to
economic security and freedom from poverty is recognized as
fundamental to human dignity and democratic participation.

SECTION 2. Congress and the States shall have the power to enact
legislation ensuring that:
(a) all persons have access to adequate income to meet basic needs;
(b) all persons have access to employment opportunities at
    living wages;
(c) all children have access to conditions supporting healthy
    development;
(d) all persons have access to healthcare, housing, and nutrition;
(e) all persons have the opportunity to build assets and wealth.

SECTION 3. The Congress shall establish and regularly update
national poverty reduction goals, and shall report annually on
progress toward such goals.

SECTION 4. No law shall operate to deprive any person of assistance
necessary to meet basic needs based solely on:
(a) race, national origin, or immigration status;
(b) family structure or marital status;
(c) disability or health status;
(d) prior criminal conviction; or
(e) failure to meet work requirements when work is unavailable
    or the person is unable to work.

SECTION 5. The rights recognized in this article shall not be
construed to diminish other rights retained by the people.

SECTION 6. Congress shall have the power to enforce this article
by appropriate legislation.

SECTION 7. This article shall take effect three years after the
date of ratification.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 1**: Establishes positive economic rights as constitutional principle
- **Section 2**: Enumerates specific areas of protection
- **Section 3**: Requires accountability through goal-setting
- **Section 4**: Prohibits categorical exclusions that have historically discriminated
- **Section 5-7**: Standard enforcement and implementation provisions

## Federal Legislation

### National Anti-Poverty Strategy Act

**Purpose**: Establish a coordinated national strategy with explicit poverty reduction goals and accountability mechanisms.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "National Anti-Poverty Strategy Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) The United States has the highest poverty rate among
    developed nations, with over 37 million people in poverty.
(2) Child poverty in America exceeds 16 percent, harming
    children's development and perpetuating disadvantage.
(3) The 2021 Child Tax Credit expansion demonstrated that
    poverty can be dramatically reduced through policy,
    cutting child poverty nearly in half.
(4) Other developed nations achieve far lower poverty rates
    through explicit poverty reduction strategies and goals.
(5) The United Kingdom reduced child poverty from 26 percent
    to 17 percent between 1999-2010 through an explicit
    commitment to poverty reduction targets.
(6) Poverty imposes significant costs on society, estimated
    at over $1 trillion annually through reduced productivity,
    increased healthcare costs, and other impacts.
(7) A coordinated national strategy with explicit goals is
    necessary to achieve meaningful poverty reduction.

SEC. 3. NATIONAL POVERTY REDUCTION GOALS.

(a) TEN-YEAR GOALS.—It is the goal of the United States to achieve,
    within 10 years of enactment of this Act:
    (1) Reduction of the overall poverty rate to below 6 percent
        as measured by the Supplemental Poverty Measure;
    (2) Reduction of the child poverty rate to below 4 percent;
    (3) Reduction of the deep poverty rate (below 50 percent of
        the poverty threshold) to below 2 percent;
    (4) Elimination of racial disparities in poverty rates;
    (5) Reduction of material hardship rates by 50 percent.

(b) INTERIM GOALS.—
    (1) FIVE-YEAR.—Within 5 years, the poverty rate shall be
        reduced to below 8 percent.
    (2) ANNUAL.—The President shall establish interim annual
        goals to achieve the objectives in subsection (a).

(c) BINDING COMMITMENT.—The goals established in this section
    shall guide federal policy-making, budgeting, and program
    administration.

SEC. 4. INTERAGENCY COUNCIL ON POVERTY REDUCTION.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established within the Executive Office
    of the President an Interagency Council on Poverty Reduction
    (hereinafter "the Council").

(b) DIRECTOR.—The Council shall be headed by a Director, appointed
    by the President with the advice and consent of the Senate,
    who shall have the rank of a Cabinet member.

(c) MEMBERSHIP.—The Council shall include the heads of:
    (1) Department of Health and Human Services;
    (2) Department of the Treasury;
    (3) Department of Labor;
    (4) Department of Housing and Urban Development;
    (5) Department of Agriculture;
    (6) Department of Education;
    (7) Social Security Administration;
    (8) Office of Management and Budget;
    (9) Council of Economic Advisers;
    (10) Such other agencies as the President may designate.

(d) DUTIES.—The Council shall:
    (1) Develop and update the National Poverty Reduction Strategy;
    (2) Coordinate federal poverty reduction efforts across agencies;
    (3) Identify barriers to program access and propose solutions;
    (4) Monitor progress toward goals;
    (5) Submit annual reports to Congress;
    (6) Propose legislative and budgetary changes as needed.

(e) AUTHORITY.—The Director shall have authority to:
    (1) Convene agencies for coordination;
    (2) Review agency budgets for poverty reduction alignment;
    (3) Request information from agencies;
    (4) Recommend personnel and budget allocations.

SEC. 5. NATIONAL POVERTY REDUCTION STRATEGY.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Within one year of enactment, and every four years
    thereafter, the Council shall develop and publish a National
    Poverty Reduction Strategy.

(b) CONTENTS.—The Strategy shall include:
    (1) Assessment of current poverty levels and trends;
    (2) Analysis of program effectiveness;
    (3) Specific policy recommendations;
    (4) Resource requirements and proposed funding sources;
    (5) Implementation timeline and milestones;
    (6) Performance metrics and accountability measures;
    (7) Strategies for addressing racial and geographic disparities.

(c) PUBLIC INPUT.—In developing the Strategy, the Council shall
    solicit input from:
    (1) State and local governments;
    (2) Advocacy organizations;
    (3) Academic researchers;
    (4) Persons with lived experience of poverty;
    (5) The general public through notice and comment.

(d) CONGRESSIONAL REVIEW.—The Strategy shall be transmitted to
    Congress for review. Committees of jurisdiction shall hold
    hearings on the Strategy within 90 days.

SEC. 6. POVERTY IMPACT ASSESSMENT.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Before any major legislation is considered by
    Congress, the Congressional Budget Office shall prepare a
    Poverty Impact Assessment.

(b) CONTENTS.—The Assessment shall estimate:
    (1) The number of persons moved into or out of poverty;
    (2) The change in the child poverty rate;
    (3) The change in the deep poverty rate;
    (4) Racial and ethnic distributional effects;
    (5) Geographic distributional effects;
    (6) Effects on material hardship measures.

(c) THRESHOLD.—An Assessment shall be required for any legislation
    with an estimated budgetary effect exceeding $1,000,000,000
    annually or that amends any major means-tested program.

(d) TIMING.—An Assessment shall be completed and publicly available
    at least 72 hours before any floor vote on the legislation.

SEC. 7. ANNUAL REPORTING.

(a) ANNUAL REPORT.—The Council shall submit an annual report to
    Congress including:
    (1) Current poverty rates by the Official and Supplemental
        Poverty Measures;
    (2) Child poverty and deep poverty rates;
    (3) Progress toward 10-year goals;
    (4) Program participation and effectiveness data;
    (5) Assessment of policies enacted or needed;
    (6) Proposed legislation and budget recommendations.

(b) PUBLIC DASHBOARD.—The Council shall maintain a public dashboard
    with real-time poverty indicators updated quarterly.

(c) CONGRESSIONAL HEARING.—Committees of jurisdiction shall hold
    an annual hearing on the report within 60 days of submission.

SEC. 8. ACCOUNTABILITY MECHANISMS.

(a) REMEDIAL ACTION.—If the annual poverty rate exceeds the interim
    goal by more than 1 percentage point, the Council shall submit
    to Congress within 90 days:
    (1) An explanation of why the goal was not met;
    (2) Recommended legislative actions;
    (3) Proposed budget amendments.

(b) EXPEDITED CONSIDERATION.—Legislation proposed under subsection
    (a) shall be eligible for expedited consideration under
    procedures established by each House.

(c) AGENCY ACCOUNTABILITY.—Each agency represented on the Council
    shall include poverty reduction as a strategic priority and
    report on progress in annual performance plans.

SEC. 9. DATA AND MEASUREMENT.

(a) PRIMARY MEASURE.—The Supplemental Poverty Measure shall be the
    primary federal poverty measure for purposes of this Act.

(b) QUARTERLY ESTIMATES.—The Census Bureau shall publish quarterly
    poverty estimates within 45 days of each quarter's end.

(c) PROGRAM IMPACT.—The Census Bureau shall publish annual estimates
    of the number of persons kept out of poverty by each major
    federal program.

(d) MATERIAL HARDSHIP.—The Census Bureau shall publish annual
    material hardship measures including food insecurity, housing
    instability, healthcare access, and utility disconnection.

SEC. 10. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

(a) COUNCIL.—There are authorized to be appropriated $25,000,000
    annually for the operations of the Council.

(b) DATA.—There are authorized to be appropriated $50,000,000
    annually to the Census Bureau for enhanced poverty measurement.

SEC. 11. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect upon enactment.
```

### Universal Basic Income Pilot Program Act

**Purpose**: Establish a rigorous federal pilot program to test unconditional cash transfers.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Universal Basic Income Pilot Program Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) Guaranteed income pilots in Stockton, California, and other
    cities have demonstrated positive outcomes including
    increased employment, improved well-being, and reduced
    material hardship.
(2) The Stockton SEED pilot showed that recipients receiving
    $500 per month had higher rates of full-time employment
    than the control group.
(3) Unconditional cash transfers respect the dignity and
    autonomy of recipients, allowing them to address their
    most pressing needs.
(4) Automation and changing labor markets may require new
    approaches to income security.
(5) A rigorous federal pilot is needed to generate evidence
    for national policy decisions.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
(1) BASIC INCOME.—The term "basic income" means a regular,
    unconditional cash payment to individuals.
(2) PILOT SITE.—The term "pilot site" means a geographic area
    selected for participation in the pilot program.
(3) SECRETARY.—The term "Secretary" means the Secretary of
    Health and Human Services.

SEC. 4. PILOT PROGRAM ESTABLISHMENT.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary shall establish a Universal Basic
    Income Pilot Program in at least 10 communities representing
    diverse geographic, demographic, and economic characteristics.

(b) DURATION.—The pilot shall operate for at least 5 years to
    enable assessment of long-term effects.

(c) SELECTION CRITERIA.—Pilot sites shall include:
    (1) At least 2 urban areas with high poverty rates;
    (2) At least 2 rural areas with persistent poverty;
    (3) At least 1 area with a majority-minority population;
    (4) At least 1 Native American reservation (with tribal
        consent);
    (5) At least 2 areas with significant immigrant populations;
    (6) Geographic distribution across regions.

SEC. 5. PILOT DESIGN.

(a) PARTICIPANTS.—

    (1) ELIGIBILITY.—Adults 18 and older residing in pilot sites
        shall be eligible to participate.

    (2) SAMPLE SIZE.—Each pilot site shall include at least 1,000
        participants receiving the basic income and an equal-sized
        control group.

    (3) RANDOM ASSIGNMENT.—Participants shall be randomly assigned
        to treatment and control groups.

(b) BENEFIT AMOUNT.—

    (1) IN GENERAL.—Treatment group participants shall receive
        $1,000 per month, adjusted annually for inflation.

    (2) VARIATION.—The Secretary may test variations in benefit
        amount across sites to assess dose-response effects.

(c) UNCONDITIONAL.—

    (1) NO WORK REQUIREMENT.—Receipt of the basic income shall not
        be conditioned on employment or work activity.

    (2) NO USE RESTRICTIONS.—Participants may use funds for any
        legal purpose.

(d) PAYMENT.—

    (1) MONTHLY.—Payments shall be made monthly, on a consistent
        date.

    (2) DIRECT DEPOSIT.—The Secretary shall encourage direct deposit
        but provide alternative payment methods for those without
        bank accounts.

(e) INTERACTION WITH OTHER BENEFITS.—

    (1) NON-COUNTABLE.—Basic income payments shall not be counted
        as income for purposes of determining eligibility or
        benefit amounts for any federal means-tested program
        during the pilot period.

    (2) STATE COORDINATION.—The Secretary shall coordinate with
        states to seek similar treatment for state programs.

SEC. 6. EVALUATION.

(a) INDEPENDENT EVALUATION.—The Secretary shall contract with one
    or more independent research organizations to conduct a
    rigorous evaluation of the pilot.

(b) METHODOLOGY.—The evaluation shall use randomized controlled
    trial methodology with pre-registered analysis plans.

(c) OUTCOMES.—The evaluation shall measure:
    (1) Employment and earnings;
    (2) Poverty and income;
    (3) Material hardship (food, housing, healthcare);
    (4) Physical and mental health;
    (5) Child outcomes (development, education);
    (6) Financial stability and debt;
    (7) Social and community engagement;
    (8) Crime and safety;
    (9) Time use and caregiving;
    (10) Entrepreneurship and education;
    (11) Well-being and life satisfaction.

(d) SUBGROUP ANALYSIS.—The evaluation shall examine effects by:
    (1) Race and ethnicity;
    (2) Age;
    (3) Family structure;
    (4) Prior employment status;
    (5) Geographic area type.

(e) COST ANALYSIS.—The evaluation shall assess:
    (1) Program costs;
    (2) Administrative costs;
    (3) Impacts on other program costs;
    (4) Fiscal effects (tax revenue, reduced benefits);
    (5) Long-term cost-benefit analysis.

(f) REPORTS.—
    (1) ANNUAL.—The Secretary shall report annually to Congress
        on pilot implementation and preliminary findings.
    (2) FINAL.—A comprehensive final evaluation shall be submitted
        within 2 years of pilot conclusion.

SEC. 7. ADVISORY COMMITTEE.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary shall establish an Advisory
    Committee on Basic Income.

(b) MEMBERSHIP.—The Committee shall include:
    (1) Researchers with expertise in poverty, labor, and policy;
    (2) Representatives of pilot communities;
    (3) Persons with lived experience of poverty;
    (4) Representatives of relevant federal agencies;
    (5) State and local government representatives.

(c) DUTIES.—The Committee shall advise on pilot design,
    implementation, and evaluation.

SEC. 8. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

(a) PILOT OPERATIONS.—There are authorized to be appropriated
    $500,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2026 through 2032 for
    pilot operations and payments.

(b) EVALUATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated
    $50,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2026 through 2034 for
    independent evaluation.

SEC. 9. RECOMMENDATIONS TO CONGRESS.

Within 2 years of the completion of the evaluation, the Secretary
shall submit to Congress recommendations regarding:
(1) Whether to expand the basic income program nationally;
(2) Recommended program design based on evidence;
(3) Cost estimates and funding options;
(4) Legislative changes needed.

SEC. 10. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect upon enactment, with first payments
beginning within 18 months.
```

### Baby Bonds Act

**Purpose**: Establish federally-funded savings accounts for children to address the wealth gap.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Baby Bonds Act" or the "American
Opportunity Accounts Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) Wealth inequality in America has reached extreme levels,
    with the bottom 50 percent of households holding less than
    3 percent of total wealth.
(2) The racial wealth gap is profound: the median Black family
    has approximately one-eighth the wealth of the median white
    family.
(3) Children born into families without wealth face compounding
    disadvantages throughout life, including reduced access to
    education, homeownership, and entrepreneurship.
(4) Asset ownership at young adulthood significantly improves
    life outcomes, educational attainment, and economic mobility.
(5) The United Kingdom's Child Trust Fund demonstrated the
    feasibility of universal children's savings accounts.
(6) Connecticut has enacted the first state baby bonds program.
(7) Providing capital at the start of adulthood can help break
    the cycle of intergenerational poverty.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
(1) ACCOUNT.—The term "American Opportunity Account" means an
    account established under this Act.
(2) BENEFICIARY.—The term "beneficiary" means the child for
    whom an Account is established.
(3) SECRETARY.—The term "Secretary" means the Secretary of
    the Treasury.

SEC. 4. ESTABLISHMENT OF AMERICAN OPPORTUNITY ACCOUNTS.

(a) IN GENERAL.—The Secretary shall establish an American
    Opportunity Account for each child who:
    (1) Is born in the United States or becomes a citizen or
        lawful permanent resident before age 18; and
    (2) Has a valid Social Security number or Individual
        Taxpayer Identification Number.

(b) AUTOMATIC ENROLLMENT.—
    (1) AT BIRTH.—For children born in the United States, the
        Secretary shall establish an Account within 90 days of
        birth based on birth registration data.
    (2) NEW RESIDENTS.—For children who become citizens or
        lawful permanent residents, the Secretary shall establish
        an Account within 90 days of such status.

(c) INITIAL DEPOSIT.—Upon establishment of each Account, the
    Secretary shall deposit $1,000.

SEC. 5. ANNUAL DEPOSITS.

(a) AMOUNT.—Based on household income relative to the federal
    poverty level for the preceding year, the Secretary shall
    make annual deposits as follows:

    (1) Households below 100% of FPL: $2,000;
    (2) Households at 100-200% of FPL: $1,500;
    (3) Households at 200-300% of FPL: $1,000;
    (4) Households at 300-400% of FPL: $500;
    (5) Households above 400% of FPL: $0.

(b) MAXIMUM TOTAL.—Total federal deposits to any Account shall
    not exceed $50,000.

(c) DETERMINATION.—
    (1) DATA SOURCES.—The Secretary shall determine household
        income using IRS data, or other sources for non-filers.
    (2) SIMPLIFIED.—The determination process shall be simplified
        to minimize administrative burden on families.

(d) INFLATION ADJUSTMENT.—The deposit amounts shall be adjusted
    annually for inflation.

SEC. 6. ACCOUNT MANAGEMENT.

(a) INVESTMENT.—
    (1) DEFAULT FUND.—Accounts shall be invested in a default
        fund managed by the Treasury offering a diversified
        portfolio appropriate for long-term growth.
    (2) ALTERNATIVE OPTIONS.—At age 16, beneficiaries may choose
        among alternative investment options approved by the
        Secretary.
    (3) FEES.—Investment fees shall not exceed 0.25 percent annually.

(b) TAX TREATMENT.—
    (1) CONTRIBUTIONS.—Federal deposits shall not be included in
        the gross income of the beneficiary or parents.
    (2) EARNINGS.—Account earnings shall not be subject to
        federal income tax.
    (3) DISTRIBUTIONS.—Qualified distributions shall not be
        subject to federal income tax.

(c) ACCOUNT STATEMENTS.—The Secretary shall provide annual
    statements to beneficiaries and parents, including projected
    value at age 18.

SEC. 7. DISTRIBUTIONS.

(a) ELIGIBILITY.—A beneficiary may access Account funds upon
    reaching age 18.

(b) QUALIFIED USES.—Distributions shall be made for:
    (1) Post-secondary education expenses;
    (2) First-time homeownership (down payment, closing costs);
    (3) Starting or expanding a business;
    (4) Retirement savings contributions.

(c) DOCUMENTATION.—The Secretary shall establish reasonable
    documentation requirements to verify qualified use.

(d) PARTIAL DISTRIBUTIONS.—Beneficiaries may take partial
    distributions for different qualified purposes.

(e) ROLLOVER.—Unused funds may remain invested and be used for
    qualified purposes until age 30.

(f) NON-QUALIFIED DISTRIBUTIONS.—
    (1) PENALTY.—Distributions not for qualified purposes shall
        be subject to a 10 percent penalty plus income tax on
        earnings.
    (2) EXCEPTION.—Hardship exceptions may be granted for medical
        emergencies or disability.

SEC. 8. FINANCIAL EDUCATION.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—The Secretary, in coordination with the
    Department of Education, shall develop financial education
    curriculum to accompany the program.

(b) DELIVERY.—Financial education shall be provided through:
    (1) Schools (integrated into existing curriculum);
    (2) Online resources;
    (3) Community-based organizations;
    (4) Account statements and communications.

(c) TOPICS.—Financial education shall cover:
    (1) Saving and investing;
    (2) Budgeting;
    (3) Credit and debt;
    (4) Education and career planning;
    (5) Homeownership and entrepreneurship.

SEC. 9. INTERACTION WITH OTHER PROGRAMS.

(a) ASSET EXCLUSION.—Account balances shall not be counted as
    assets for purposes of determining eligibility for:
    (1) TANF;
    (2) SNAP;
    (3) SSI;
    (4) Medicaid;
    (5) Housing assistance;
    (6) Federal student aid;
    (7) Other means-tested programs.

(b) INCOME EXCLUSION.—Distributions from Accounts shall not be
    counted as income for means-tested program purposes.

SEC. 10. ADMINISTRATION.

(a) TREASURY.—The Secretary shall administer the program, with
    authority to contract with private entities for account
    management and investment.

(b) DATA SHARING.—Federal agencies shall share data as necessary
    to facilitate enrollment and income determination.

(c) OUTREACH.—The Secretary shall conduct outreach to ensure
    families are aware of the program, particularly in
    underserved communities.

(d) REGULATIONS.—The Secretary shall issue regulations within
    18 months of enactment.

SEC. 11. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated such sums as may be
necessary to carry out this Act.

SEC. 12. EFFECTIVE DATE.

(a) IN GENERAL.—This Act shall take effect upon enactment.

(b) FIRST ACCOUNTS.—The first Accounts shall be established
    within 24 months of enactment.

(c) RETROACTIVE ENROLLMENT.—Children born within 1 year prior
    to the first account establishment date shall be eligible
    for Accounts with prorated initial deposits.
```

### Poverty Elimination Act

**Purpose**: Provide a comprehensive framework for eliminating poverty through multiple coordinated strategies.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Poverty Elimination Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds the following:
    (1) The United States has the capacity to eliminate poverty.
    (2) The annual cost of poverty exceeds $1 trillion; the cost
        to eliminate poverty is approximately $175 billion.
    (3) Evidence-based policies can dramatically reduce poverty.
    (4) A comprehensive, coordinated approach is required.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to provide a framework
    for eliminating poverty in the United States within 20 years.

SEC. 3. POVERTY ELIMINATION GOAL.

It is the policy of the United States to eliminate poverty,
defined as reducing the poverty rate to below 2 percent as
measured by the Supplemental Poverty Measure, within 20 years
of enactment.

SEC. 4. UNIVERSAL CHILD ALLOWANCE.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established a Universal Child Allowance
    administered by the Social Security Administration.

(b) AMOUNT.—Each child under age 18 shall receive:
    (1) $500 per month for children under age 6;
    (2) $400 per month for children age 6 through 17.

(c) PHASE-OUT.—Benefits shall be reduced by 5 percent of household
    income exceeding $150,000 (single) or $200,000 (married).

(d) PAYMENT.—Payments shall be made monthly, automatically enrolled
    at birth.

SEC. 5. ENHANCED EARNED INCOME TAX CREDIT.

(a) CHILDLESS WORKERS.—The EITC for workers without qualifying
    children is increased to a maximum of $3,000.

(b) AGE EXPANSION.—The age range is expanded to 19-67.

(c) ADVANCE PAYMENT.—Workers may elect monthly advance payment.

SEC. 6. FEDERAL JOBS GUARANTEE.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Department of Labor shall establish a
    program guaranteeing employment at $15 per hour plus benefits
    to any adult who seeks work.

(b) JOBS.—Jobs shall focus on public service including
    infrastructure, care, environment, and community needs.

(c) IMPLEMENTATION.—The program shall be phased in over 5 years
    beginning with highest-poverty areas.

SEC. 7. ASSET LIMITS REFORM.

(a) SSI.—The asset limit for SSI is increased to $25,000
    (individual) and $50,000 (couple), indexed to inflation.

(b) TANF.—States receiving TANF funds shall not impose asset
    limits below $25,000.

(c) VEHICLE EXEMPTION.—One vehicle per adult shall be fully
    exempt from asset tests.

(d) RETIREMENT EXEMPTION.—Retirement accounts shall be fully
    exempt from asset tests.

SEC. 8. TANF REFORM.

(a) MINIMUM BENEFIT.—States shall provide cash benefits of at
    least 50 percent of the poverty line.

(b) INFLATION INDEXING.—The TANF block grant shall be indexed
    to inflation and poverty levels.

(c) PARTICIPATION METRICS.—States shall be evaluated on poverty
    reduction, not caseload reduction.

(d) TIME LIMIT FLEXIBILITY.—The 60-month lifetime limit is
    eliminated; states may establish reasonable limits.

SEC. 9. ADMINISTRATIVE SIMPLIFICATION.

(a) INTEGRATED APPLICATION.—A unified application shall be
    developed for SNAP, Medicaid, TANF, LIHEAP, and housing.

(b) CONTINUOUS ELIGIBILITY.—Programs serving children shall
    provide 12-month continuous eligibility.

(c) DATA SHARING.—Agencies shall share data to reduce
    verification burden.

SEC. 10. HOUSING ASSISTANCE EXPANSION.

(a) ENTITLEMENT.—Housing assistance (vouchers or equivalent)
    shall be available to all households with children below
    50 percent of area median income.

(b) PHASE-IN.—The entitlement shall be phased in over 10 years.

SEC. 11. FUNDING.

(a) PROGRESSIVE REVENUE.—The Secretary of the Treasury shall
    propose revenue measures including:
    (1) Increased rates on income above $1,000,000;
    (2) Wealth tax on net worth above $50,000,000;
    (3) Financial transaction tax;
    (4) Carbon tax with per-capita rebate;
    (5) Corporate minimum tax.

(b) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated such
    sums as may be necessary.

SEC. 12. OVERSIGHT AND ACCOUNTABILITY.

(a) The Interagency Council on Poverty Reduction (established
    under the National Anti-Poverty Strategy Act) shall oversee
    implementation.

(b) Annual reports shall track progress toward elimination goal.

(c) If poverty rate exceeds interim targets, expedited legislative
    process shall apply to corrective measures.

SEC. 13. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect upon enactment, with programs phased
in as specified.
```

## State Model Legislation

### State Anti-Poverty Strategy Act

**Purpose**: Establish state-level poverty reduction goals and coordination.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Anti-Poverty Strategy Act".

SECTION 2. LEGISLATIVE FINDINGS.

The Legislature finds that:
(a) Approximately [X] percent of [State] residents live in
    poverty, including [Y] percent of children;
(b) Poverty costs [State] an estimated $[Z] billion annually;
(c) Evidence-based policies can effectively reduce poverty;
(d) A coordinated state strategy with explicit goals is needed.

SECTION 3. POVERTY REDUCTION GOALS.

(a) The goal of the State is to:
    (1) Reduce the poverty rate to below [X-4] percent within
        5 years;
    (2) Reduce the poverty rate to below [X-7] percent within
        10 years;
    (3) Reduce the child poverty rate to below [Y-5] percent
        within 5 years;
    (4) Eliminate racial disparities in poverty within 15 years.

SECTION 4. POVERTY REDUCTION TASK FORCE.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established a Poverty Reduction
    Task Force.

(b) MEMBERSHIP.—The Task Force shall include:
    (1) Secretaries of relevant agencies;
    (2) Legislative representatives;
    (3) At least 4 persons with lived experience of poverty;
    (4) Academic experts;
    (5) Community representatives.

(c) DUTIES.—The Task Force shall:
    (1) Develop State Poverty Reduction Strategy;
    (2) Monitor progress;
    (3) Report annually to Legislature.

SECTION 5. POVERTY IMPACT STATEMENTS.

Before major legislation is considered, the Legislative Fiscal
Office shall prepare a Poverty Impact Statement estimating
effects on poverty.

SECTION 6. ANNUAL REPORTING.

The Task Force shall report annually on:
(a) Current poverty rates;
(b) Progress toward goals;
(c) Policy recommendations;
(d) Budget proposals.

SECTION 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect upon enactment.
```

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### National Anti-Poverty Strategy Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Goal non-enforcement | Goals aspirational; no penalty | High |
| Council effectiveness | Interagency coordination historically weak | Medium |
| Measurement manipulation | Administrations may change measures | Medium |
| Reporting delays | Data lag allows evasion | Low |
| Expedited process circumvented | Parliamentary maneuvers | Medium |
| Director vacancy | Position left unfilled | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No automatic programs | Goals don't fund programs | Separation of powers |
| Political vulnerability | Can be repealed | Democratic process |
| Data lag | Poverty data delayed 6-18 months | Survey methodology |
| State non-participation | Federal strategy; states implement | Federalism |
| Measurement disagreement | OPM vs. SPM debates | Political |
| Council authority limited | Advisory, not operational | Design choice |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Goal enforcement**: Tie goals to automatic stabilizer triggers; require mandatory spending if goals missed by specified margin
2. **Council effectiveness**: Give Director budget review authority; require agency compliance plans; publish performance scorecards
3. **Measurement integrity**: Protect Census independence; codify SPM as primary measure; establish independent statistical review
4. **Reporting timeliness**: Invest in real-time data; quarterly estimates; administrative data integration
5. **Expedited process**: Codify in House and Senate rules; point of order against circumvention
6. **Director position**: Require acting within 90 days; mandatory confirmation hearings; holdover provisions
7. **State alignment**: Federal incentives for state strategies; matching funds; minimum standards

### Universal Basic Income Pilot Program Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Site selection bias | Choose favorable sites | Medium |
| Attrition | Participants leave, biasing results | Medium |
| Control contamination | Control group affected | Medium |
| Short duration | 5 years may miss long-term effects | Medium |
| Non-scalable design | Pilot conditions differ from national | Medium |
| Cherry-picking findings | Selective reporting | Low |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Pilot only | Not national implementation | Political caution |
| Limited generalizability | Context matters | Research design |
| Hawthorne effects | Awareness changes behavior | Experimental method |
| Funding uncertainty | May be cut mid-pilot | Appropriations process |
| Benefit interactions | States may not cooperate | Federalism |
| Timeline to policy | Slow path to implementation | Evidence-based process |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Site selection**: Independent selection panel; pre-specified criteria; transparency; diverse sites required
2. **Attrition**: Intent-to-treat analysis; incentives for continued participation; tracking of dropouts
3. **Control contamination**: Buffer zones; select comparison sites; account for contamination in analysis
4. **Duration**: Authorize 7-year extension option; longitudinal follow-up studies; early indicators
5. **Generalizability**: Include varied contexts; simulation modeling for national scale; sensitivity analysis
6. **Reporting**: Pre-registered analysis plan; independent evaluation; public data release; multiple evaluators
7. **Funding**: Multi-year appropriation; mandatory spending portion; foundation supplement
8. **State coordination**: Federal-state agreements; explicit non-countability provisions; incentives for cooperation
9. **Scale pathway**: Automatic expansion triggers if success criteria met; expedited consideration of national program

### Baby Bonds Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Income manipulation | Families adjust income to maximize deposits | Low |
| Delayed enrollment | Children miss early years | Medium |
| Fraud (false claims) | Fraudulent account establishment | Low |
| Improper distributions | Non-qualified use | Medium |
| Investment losses | Poor fund performance | Medium |
| Financial industry gaming | High-fee products | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| 18-year wait | Benefits not immediate | Design for wealth-building |
| Complexity | Multiple income tiers | Progressivity goal |
| Undocumented exclusion | Some children ineligible | Political compromise |
| Restricted uses | May not meet all needs | Paternalism concern |
| Implementation scale | 4 million births/year | Administrative |
| Long-term funding | 18+ years of commitment | Budget process |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Income verification**: Use prior-year data; average multiple years; audit high-deposit accounts
2. **Enrollment**: Automatic at birth via SSA/vital records; retroactive catch-up option; outreach for missed children
3. **Fraud prevention**: Cross-reference birth records; identity verification; audit; penalties
4. **Distribution verification**: Documentation requirements; third-party verification for large amounts; clawback for misuse
5. **Investment protection**: Diversified default fund; fiduciary standard; fee caps; conservative option available
6. **Financial industry**: Regulate account providers; disclosure requirements; fee limits; no marketing to minors
7. **Undocumented children**: Future legislative fix; state supplements; ITIN option
8. **Use flexibility**: Expand qualified purposes over time; hardship exceptions; rollover to retirement
9. **Long-term funding**: Dedicated revenue source; sovereign wealth fund model; mandatory spending

### Poverty Elimination Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| 20-year timeline | Easy to delay indefinitely | High |
| Funding not guaranteed | Requires appropriations | High |
| Multiple components | Failure of one undermines all | Medium |
| Work requirements added | States undermine cash programs | Medium |
| Measurement changes | Redefine poverty to claim success | Medium |
| Political reversibility | Comprehensive packages vulnerable | High |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Comprehensive = complex | Many moving parts | Scope of problem |
| Revenue not specified | Funding uncertain | Political feasibility |
| Implementation capacity | Government not equipped | Administrative state |
| State variation | Uneven implementation | Federalism |
| Economic conditions | Recession could derail | External factors |
| Political sustainability | 20-year commitment | Electoral cycle |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Timeline enforcement**: Intermediate milestones with consequences; automatic triggers; sunset review
2. **Funding lock-in**: Mandatory spending designations; dedicated revenue streams; trust fund model
3. **Component integration**: Sequenced implementation; redundancy; each component can succeed independently
4. **State compliance**: Strong federal standards; withholding for non-compliance; federal fallback
5. **Measurement integrity**: Codify SPM; independent measurement; multiple measures; no political manipulation
6. **Political durability**: Build constituency through universality; entrenchment through automatic mechanisms; bipartisan governance
7. **Capacity building**: Multi-year phase-in; technical assistance; technology investment; hiring authority
8. **Economic resilience**: Automatic stabilizers; countercyclical design; reserve funds
9. **Administration continuity**: Independent commission; staggered terms; professional staff
