# Native American Voting Rights: Solutions

## Overview

Addressing the persistent barriers to Native American voting requires interventions at every level of government and across multiple dimensions: geographic access, identification and addressing, language access, redistricting, legal enforcement, and civic engagement. The solutions presented here range from administrative fixes that could be implemented immediately to structural reforms requiring federal legislation. Because the barriers are interconnected -- geographic isolation compounds addressing problems, which compound ID requirements, which compound registration obstacles -- effective reform must address multiple barriers simultaneously.

## Solution Categories

### 1. Physical Access to Voting

#### 1A. Mandatory Satellite Voting Locations on Reservations

**Problem**: Many reservation voters must travel 50-150+ miles round trip to reach polling places, registration offices, or ballot drop-off locations.

**Proposal**: Require states to establish satellite early voting locations and Election Day polling places on every reservation with a voting-age population above a threshold (e.g., 500 persons), funded by federal grants.

| Investment | Cost | Impact |
|-----------|------|--------|
| Satellite polling place grants (facilities, equipment) | $30-50 million nationally | Reduce average distance to polls on reservations to under 25 miles |
| Poll worker recruitment and training on reservations | $10-15 million/year | Ensure culturally competent, local poll workers |
| Mobile voting units for small/dispersed communities | $15-20 million initial | Reach communities too small for permanent sites |
| Road maintenance grants for election-season access | $5-10 million/year | Ensure passable roads to polling places during elections |

**Evidence of effectiveness**: The Navajo Nation's satellite voting sites in Arizona, established through tribal-state agreements, increased turnout by an estimated 10-15% in precincts served (Four Directions, 2020).

#### 1B. Ballot Collection Legalization

**Problem**: *Brnovich v. DNC* (2021) upheld Arizona's ballot collection ban, and similar bans in other states eliminate one of the few methods reservation communities had developed to overcome distance barriers.

**Proposal**: Federal legislation preempting state ballot collection bans for reservations and requiring states to permit designated tribal ballot collectors.

- Tribal governments may designate official ballot collectors authorized to transport sealed ballots
- Chain-of-custody requirements including signed receipts and tamper-evident seals
- Collectors must deliver ballots to election officials within 48 hours of collection
- Criminal penalties for tampering with collected ballots

**Precedent**: Alaska permits ballot delivery by personal representative; several states allow ballot collection by family members or caregivers without distance limitations.

#### 1C. Vote-by-Mail Accommodations

**Problem**: Mail-in voting is impractical on many reservations due to unreliable postal service, P.O. box dependence, and remote delivery routes.

**Proposal**: Create reservation-specific vote-by-mail accommodations:

- Establish tribal government offices, community centers, and health clinics as official ballot distribution and return sites
- Provide prepaid ballot return envelopes that work with both USPS and tribal mail systems
- Extend ballot return deadlines for reservation residents to account for mail delays
- Permit electronic ballot request and tracking for reservation residents

### 2. Addressing and Identification Reforms

#### 2A. Non-Traditional Address Acceptance

**Problem**: State voter registration systems require standard residential street addresses that do not exist for many reservation homes.

**Proposal**: Require states to accept alternative address formats for voter registration:

- GPS coordinates, map descriptions, or narrative directions
- Tribal enrollment office address as a residential proxy
- BIA or tribal housing authority address designations
- "Indian Country address" category in voter registration databases with fields accommodating non-standard formats

**Implementation**: The Election Assistance Commission would develop model data standards; states would be required to update registration systems within 2 years.

#### 2B. Tribal ID Acceptance

**Problem**: Voter ID laws in multiple states do not accept tribal IDs or impose requirements (residential addresses, expiration dates) that many tribal IDs do not include.

**Proposal**: Federal legislation requiring all states to accept tribal government-issued identification for voting purposes:

- Any ID issued by a federally recognized tribal government shall be accepted
- States may not impose residential address requirements on tribal IDs beyond what the tribal government provides
- States may not impose expiration date requirements on tribal IDs if the tribal government does not include them
- The Election Assistance Commission shall work with tribal governments to develop model tribal voter ID standards

**Evidence of effectiveness**: States that accept tribal IDs (Montana, Minnesota) have higher Native voter registration rates than comparable states with restrictive ID requirements (McCool, Olson, and Robinson, 2007).

#### 2C. Reservation Addressing Projects

**Problem**: The fundamental addressing gap on reservations persists because no government entity has systematically assigned addresses to reservation homes.

**Proposal**: Fund a national reservation addressing initiative:

- $50-100 million federal grant program for tribal addressing projects
- Tribal governments lead addressing with federal technical assistance
- Addresses integrated into USPS, 911, and voter registration databases
- Completion target: all reservation homes with standard addresses within 5 years

**Precedent**: The Bureau of Indian Affairs and tribal governments have completed addressing projects on some reservations (e.g., Navajo Nation's Rural Addressing Program); federal funding would accelerate nationwide completion.

### 3. Language Access Improvements

#### 3A. Expanded Section 203 Coverage

**Problem**: Current Section 203 coverage thresholds leave many Native language communities uncovered, and covered jurisdictions often struggle to comply.

**Proposal**: Lower the Section 203 coverage threshold for AI/AN languages and provide federal compliance assistance:

- Reduce the triggering threshold from 5% of voting-age citizens or 10,000 persons to 2% or 5,000 persons
- Federal grants for translation and interpreter services in covered jurisdictions
- Training programs for bilingual poll workers in Native languages
- Culturally appropriate voter education materials in Native languages

#### 3B. Oral Language Assistance Requirements

**Problem**: Many Native languages are primarily oral, making ballot translation impractical; in-person oral assistance is the only viable accommodation.

**Proposal**: Require in-person oral language assistance at all polling places serving Section 203-covered AI/AN language populations:

- At least one fluent Native language speaker at each covered polling place
- Telephone interpretation available as backup
- Audio ballot guides in Native languages
- Voter education campaigns conducted in Native languages through tribal media

| Investment | Cost | Impact |
|-----------|------|--------|
| Bilingual poll worker recruitment and pay | $5-8 million/year | Ensure language access at every covered polling place |
| Audio ballot and voter guide production | $2-3 million/cycle | Provide accessible election materials for oral language speakers |
| Telephone interpretation services | $1-2 million/year | Backup for in-person assistance gaps |
| Tribal media voter education campaigns | $3-5 million/cycle | Reach Native language speakers through trusted channels |

### 4. Registration Reforms

#### 4A. Same-Day and Automatic Voter Registration

**Problem**: Registration deadlines, limited registration sites, and paperwork requirements disproportionately burden reservation residents who face distance and infrastructure barriers.

**Proposal**: Federal legislation requiring same-day voter registration and automatic registration for all eligible citizens, with specific provisions for Indian Country:

- Same-day registration at all polling places, including satellite reservation locations
- Automatic registration through IHS, BIA, and tribal government contacts
- Tribal enrollment offices designated as official voter registration sites
- Online registration available without driver's license number (using tribal enrollment or social security number)

**Evidence of effectiveness**: Montana's same-day registration is disproportionately used by Native voters; eliminating it would reduce reservation turnout by an estimated 5-10% (Montana Native Vote, 2020).

#### 4B. Felon Re-Enfranchisement

**Problem**: AI/AN incarceration rates are 4-5 times the national average; felon disenfranchisement laws affect a disproportionate share of the Native voting-age population.

**Proposal**: Federal legislation restoring voting rights for all citizens upon completion of incarceration for federal offenses, and incentivizing states to adopt similar provisions:

- Automatic restoration of federal voting rights upon release from incarceration
- Federal grants to states that adopt automatic re-enfranchisement
- Notification requirements: correctional facilities must inform individuals of voting rights restoration
- Registration assistance at release and through tribal reentry programs

### 5. Redistricting Reforms

#### 5A. Tribal Communities as Communities of Interest

**Problem**: Redistricting commissions and legislatures routinely split reservations and tribal communities across districts, diluting Native voting strength.

**Proposal**: Federal legislation requiring redistricting processes to treat reservations and tribal communities as communities of interest that should be kept whole:

- Reservations shall not be divided across congressional or state legislative districts absent compelling justification
- Adjacent tribal communities with shared interests shall be grouped together where feasible
- Tribal governments shall have standing to challenge redistricting plans that divide or dilute their communities
- Section 2 VRA protections shall be explicitly applied to redistricting affecting tribal communities

#### 5B. Independent Redistricting Commissions

**Problem**: Partisan gerrymandering by state legislatures is one of the primary mechanisms for diluting Native voting strength.

**Proposal**: Federal requirement for independent redistricting commissions with tribal representation:

- Commissions must include at least one member nominated by tribal governments in states with reservations
- Commissions must consult with tribal governments during the redistricting process
- Tribal population data must be analyzed for Gingles preconditions before any plan is adopted
- Plans that reduce the number of majority-minority or influence districts must be justified

### 6. Legal Enforcement Reforms

#### 6A. Restore Preclearance for Native Voting Areas

**Problem**: *Shelby County v. Holder* (2013) eliminated Section 5 preclearance, the most effective preventive tool for protecting Native voting rights.

**Proposal**: Enact the John R. Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act with enhanced Native-specific provisions:

- New preclearance formula covering jurisdictions with recent voting discrimination (within 25 years)
- Specific trigger for jurisdictions with significant AI/AN populations and a history of barriers
- Preclearance required for polling place closures, voter ID changes, and redistricting in covered jurisdictions
- Native-specific practices (addressing requirements, tribal ID acceptance, ballot collection rules) included in preclearance review

#### 6B. Strengthen Section 2 Enforcement

**Problem**: *Brnovich v. DNC* (2021) made it significantly harder to challenge facially neutral laws that disproportionately burden Native voters.

**Proposal**: Legislatively override *Brnovich* by restoring the pre-*Brnovich* Section 2 standard:

- Eliminate the "total opportunities" framework that allows courts to discount barriers if any alternative voting mechanism exists
- Establish that a significant disparate impact on Native voters is sufficient for a Section 2 violation
- Provide for attorneys' fees for successful Section 2 plaintiffs to reduce the cost of enforcement
- Create a dedicated Native voting rights enforcement unit within the DOJ Voting Section

#### 6C. Native American Voting Rights Act (NAVRA)

**Problem**: Current federal voting rights law does not specifically address the unique barriers Native voters face.

**Proposal**: Enact NAVRA with the following provisions:

- Require satellite voting locations on reservations
- Mandate acceptance of tribal IDs and non-traditional addresses
- Require language assistance for all Section 203-covered AI/AN communities
- Permit tribal ballot collection
- Establish federal grants for tribal voter registration and education
- Create tribal election observer program
- Mandate tribal consultation before any election change affecting Indian Country

### 7. Civic Engagement and Capacity Building

#### 7A. Tribal Voter Education Programs

**Problem**: Historical exclusion has created generational disengagement from the electoral process.

**Proposal**: Federal funding for tribal-led civic engagement programs:

- $20 million annual appropriation for tribal voter education and registration
- Programs administered by tribal governments and Native-led organizations
- Emphasis on youth engagement, first-time voter outreach, and elder access
- Culturally appropriate materials connecting voting to tribal sovereignty and community well-being

#### 7B. Native Candidate Pipeline

**Problem**: Native Americans are dramatically underrepresented in elected office, partly because the barriers to voting are compounded by barriers to candidacy.

**Proposal**: Support programs that develop Native political leadership:

- Tribal college civic engagement curricula
- Campaign training programs for Native candidates
- Networks connecting Native elected officials for mentorship
- Public financing options accessible to reservation-based candidates

## Priority Recommendations

1. **Immediate**: Enact NAVRA and require tribal ID acceptance, non-traditional address acceptance, and satellite voting locations on reservations
2. **Short-term**: Pass the John R. Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act with enhanced Native provisions and legislatively override *Brnovich*
3. **Medium-term**: Fund reservation addressing projects and expand Section 203 language coverage
4. **Long-term**: Require independent redistricting commissions with tribal representation; build sustained civic engagement infrastructure in Indian Country
