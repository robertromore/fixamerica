# Native American Voting Rights: Current State

## Barrier Analysis

### Distance and Geographic Isolation

Native voters on reservations face geographic barriers unlike those encountered by any other voting population in the United States:

- **Vast distances**: Some reservation voters must travel over 150 miles round trip to reach the nearest polling place or voter registration office (Native American Voting Rights Coalition, 2018)
- **Poor road infrastructure**: Many reservation roads are unpaved, seasonal, or impassable in bad weather; the Bureau of Indian Affairs road system includes over 29,000 miles of roads, of which approximately 75% are unimproved (BIA, 2023)
- **Limited transportation**: Public transportation is essentially nonexistent on most reservations; many households lack reliable personal vehicles
- **Mail service limitations**: Many reservation residents do not receive mail at their homes; they rely on P.O. boxes or general delivery at distant post offices, making mail-in voting impractical without specific accommodations

**Impact**: Distance alone reduces voter participation. Research by Herron and Smith (2016) found that each additional mile to a polling place reduces Native voter turnout by approximately 2 percentage points on some reservations.

### Residential Address Requirements

Most state voter registration systems require a standard residential street address. This creates a fundamental barrier on many reservations:

- **No standard addresses**: Many reservation homes lack standard street addresses; residents use P.O. boxes, rural routes, or descriptive directions
- **BIA housing**: Many reservation residents live in BIA or tribally assigned housing that may not have formal addressing
- **Homeless and transient populations**: High rates of homelessness and doubling-up create address instability
- **Address-based voter rolls**: State systems that tie registration to a specific street address can reject applications from reservation residents

**Impact**: The Native American Voting Rights Coalition's 2018 survey found that 18% of Native respondents on reservations reported difficulties with voter registration related to addressing.

### Voter Identification Requirements

Voter ID laws have proliferated since 2010 and disproportionately affect Native voters:

**State-by-State Status (Selected)**

| State | ID Requirement | Tribal ID Accepted? | Key Issue |
|-------|---------------|--------------------|-----------|
| North Dakota | Strict photo ID with residential address | Partially; tribal ID must include residential address | Many tribal IDs show P.O. box, not residential address |
| Arizona | Photo ID or two forms of non-photo ID | Yes, if government-issued | Expiration date requirements may affect older tribal IDs |
| Montana | Photo ID or voter registration confirmation | Yes | Acceptable, but outreach is limited |
| South Dakota | Photo ID with name, photo, and expiration date | Yes, if meets requirements | Some tribal IDs do not include expiration dates |
| Wisconsin | Strict photo ID | Limited acceptance | Most tribal IDs do not meet stringent requirements |
| Alaska | Photo ID or voter registration receipt | Yes | Geographic barriers compound ID requirements |

**North Dakota Spotlight**: North Dakota's voter ID law, upheld by the Eighth Circuit in *Spirit Lake Tribe v. Benson County* and challenged in *Brakebill v. Jaeger*, requires a residential address on the ID. Many reservation residents' IDs show only P.O. boxes because they have no assigned street address. The state offered a "set-aside ballot" process for those without qualifying ID, but this created an additional barrier with a separate validation process.

### Language Access

Section 203 of the Voting Rights Act requires bilingual election materials and assistance in jurisdictions where a language minority population meets certain thresholds:

| Oklahoma | 1 county | Cherokee |

**Challenges with language compliance**:

- Many Native languages have no standardized written form, making ballot translation complex
- Oral languages require in-person interpreters at polls, which is expensive and logistically difficult
- Election officials in covered jurisdictions often lack the linguistic capacity for compliance
- The Department of Justice has reduced enforcement resources since *Shelby County*

### Registration Barriers

Beyond addressing and ID issues, Native voters face additional registration obstacles:

- **Registration deadlines**: Many states require registration 15-30 days before Election Day; voters who become motivated close to elections are unable to register
- **Limited registration sites**: County clerk offices may be located far from reservations; mobile registration events are infrequent
- **Online registration limitations**: Requires a driver's license or state ID number, which many reservation residents lack
- **Felon disenfranchisement**: AI/AN incarceration rates are 4-5 times the national average; disenfranchisement laws affect a disproportionate share of the Native population (Bureau of Justice Statistics, 2023)

## Registration and Turnout Data

### Registration Rates

Precise Native voter registration data is difficult to obtain because most states do not track registration by race. Available estimates suggest:

- National AI/AN voter registration: approximately 66% of eligible population, compared to 72% nationally (NCAI, 2020)
- Registration rates vary dramatically by state and proximity to reservations
- Urban Native registration rates are closer to the general population average; reservation registration rates are significantly lower

### Impact of Native Vote

Despite lower turnout, the Native vote has been decisive in close elections:

- **2020 Arizona**: Biden won by 10,457 votes; estimated Native voter margin exceeded 60,000 votes
- **2020 Wisconsin**: Biden won by 20,682 votes; Native vote contributed a significant margin
- **2018 Montana**: Senator Tester won by approximately 18,000 votes; Native voter turnout was critical
- **2012 North Dakota**: Senator Heitkamp won by approximately 3,000 votes; reservation turnout was decisive

## Legal Landscape

### Supreme Court Decisions

**Shelby County v. Holder, 570 U.S. 529 (2013)**

The Court struck down Section 4(b) of the VRA, the coverage formula determining which jurisdictions required Section 5 preclearance before changing election procedures. This effectively eliminated preclearance, the most powerful tool for preventing discriminatory election changes before they took effect.

*Impact on Native voting*: Several states with significant Native populations (Arizona, Alaska, South Dakota) had been wholly or partially covered by preclearance. After *Shelby County*, these states could and did enact voting changes without federal pre-approval, including:

- Closure or relocation of polling places on or near reservations
- Implementation of voter ID requirements
- Changes to early voting and absentee ballot rules
- Redistricting changes that diluted Native voting strength

**Brnovich v. DNC, 594 U.S. 647 (2021)**

The Court upheld two Arizona voting restrictions (prohibition on out-of-precinct voting and ballot harvesting ban) and established new, restrictive guidelines for Section 2 "results" claims challenging voting practices. The Court articulated several factors courts should consider, including the availability of alternative voting mechanisms and the size of the disparate impact.

*Impact on Native voting*: The ban on ballot collection ("harvesting") particularly affected reservation communities where organized ballot collection was one of the few ways to overcome distance barriers. The new Section 2 framework makes it harder to challenge facially neutral laws that disproportionately burden Native voters.

### Ongoing Litigation

- **Navajo Nation v. San Juan County, Utah**: Successful Section 2 challenge to at-large county commission elections that diluted Navajo voting strength; resulted in district-based elections
- **Brakebill v. Jaeger (North Dakota)**: Ongoing challenge to North Dakota's voter ID law as applied to reservation residents
- **Alaska redistricting challenges**: Multiple challenges to state redistricting plans as diluting Alaska Native voting strength
- **South Dakota redistricting**: Challenge to legislative districts that pack or crack Native communities
- **Swinomish Indian Tribal Community v. Skagit County**: Section 2 challenge to county commissioner districts in Washington state

### Federal Legislation

- **John R. Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act**: Would restore preclearance for jurisdictions with recent voting discrimination; has passed the House but not the Senate; includes specific provisions for areas with significant Native populations
- **Native American Voting Rights Act (NAVRA)**: Would address Native-specific voting barriers including addressing, polling place access, language assistance, and voter registration; introduced in multiple Congresses but not enacted
- **Freedom to Vote Act**: Comprehensive voting reform that would address some Native voting barriers through automatic registration, same-day registration, and vote-by-mail provisions

## Redistricting

### Current Districting Landscape

Redistricting after the 2020 Census has produced mixed results for Native representation:

- **Arizona**: Redistricting preserved one majority-Native congressional district and created effective influence districts in the state legislature
- **Montana**: New second congressional district created without majority-Native population; Native influence depends on coalition with other voters
- **New Mexico**: Redistricting preserved Native influence in northern and western districts
- **South Dakota**: Challenges to state legislative districts that split Pine Ridge Reservation were filed under Section 2
- **Alaska**: Redistricting board's plan challenged for splitting Alaska Native communities in ways that dilute voting strength
- **Oklahoma**: Creek Nation and other tribal territories not used as communities of interest in state redistricting despite McGirt implications

### Vote Dilution Patterns

Common redistricting practices that dilute Native voting strength include:

- **Packing**: Concentrating Native voters in one district to limit their influence to a single seat
- **Cracking**: Splitting reservation or Native population centers across multiple districts to prevent a majority in any district
- **At-large elections**: County and local elections using at-large rather than district systems, preventing Native communities from electing candidates of choice
- **Ignoring communities of interest**: Failing to treat reservations and tribal communities as communities of interest in redistricting

## Election Administration on Reservations

### Polling Place Access

- Many counties with reservation land have reduced the number of polling places on reservations in recent years
- Polling place hours may be shorter on reservations than in county population centers
- Facilities available for polling on reservations are often inadequate (no accessibility compliance, no heat/cooling, limited parking)
- Poll worker recruitment on reservations is challenging due to low pay and distance

### Early Voting and Absentee Access

- States with early voting may not establish satellite early voting locations on reservations
- Mail-in voting is impractical where mail service is unreliable or residents lack standard addresses
- Ballot drop-off locations are often located far from reservation communities
- Some states have restricted third-party ballot collection, which reservation communities relied on to overcome distance barriers

### Tribal-State Coordination

- Coordination between tribal governments and state/county election officials varies widely
- Some tribes have developed cooperative agreements for voter registration, polling place establishment, and election day operations
- In other jurisdictions, tribal and county government have adversarial relationships that impede voting access
- Tribal facilities can serve as polling places, registration sites, and early voting locations if tribal-state agreements are in place
