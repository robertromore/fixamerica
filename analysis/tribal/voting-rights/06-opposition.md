# Native American Voting Rights: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to Native voting rights takes both overt and covert forms. Unlike many policy areas where opponents acknowledge the issue but disagree on solutions, voting rights opposition often involves denying that barriers exist or arguing that the barriers are justified by other policy goals (election security, administrative efficiency, state sovereignty). The most effective opposition operates through facially neutral laws and administrative decisions that disproportionately burden Native voters while providing plausible deniability of discriminatory intent.

## Categories of Opposition

### State Legislatures Pursuing Restrictive Election Laws

**Who**: State legislators, primarily Republican, in states with significant Native populations (Arizona, North Dakota, South Dakota, Montana, Wisconsin, Alaska, Oklahoma).

**Arguments**:

- Voter ID laws protect election integrity and prevent fraud
- Uniform election rules should apply to all voters regardless of residence
- Ballot collection ("harvesting") creates opportunities for fraud and coercion
- Registration deadlines are necessary for orderly election administration
- States have sovereign authority over election administration under the Constitution

**Actual Motivations**:

- Native voters disproportionately support Democratic candidates; reducing their turnout benefits Republican electoral prospects
- *Shelby County* removed the preclearance check that previously deterred discriminatory changes
- Political advantage is the primary driver, with election integrity serving as a justifying narrative
- Some legislators represent counties where white majorities have historically dominated politics and view Native political empowerment as threatening

**Assessment**: This is the most impactful form of opposition because state legislatures have direct authority over election rules. The combination of political motivation and plausible deniability (framing restrictions as "election security") makes this opposition difficult to counter without strong federal enforcement mechanisms.

**Evidence**:

- North Dakota's voter ID law was enacted shortly after the 2012 election in which the Native vote was decisive in the Senate race
- Arizona's ballot collection ban was enacted after *Shelby County* removed preclearance requirements
- Post-*Shelby County* polling place closures and changes in covered states disproportionately affected Native communities

### County Election Officials

**Who**: County clerks, election boards, and county commissions in jurisdictions with significant reservation populations.

**Arguments**:

- Establishing and staffing polling places on remote reservations is expensive
- Low turnout on reservations does not justify the cost of maintaining full polling services
- Reservation residents can use absentee ballots or travel to county polling places
- County budgets do not allow for expanded services

**Actual Motivations**:

- Some officials genuinely face resource constraints, particularly in large, sparsely populated western counties
- Others use cost arguments to justify decisions that align with their political interests
- County officials in jurisdictions with histories of racial polarization may have political incentives to limit reservation voting access
- Reducing reservation turnout helps maintain existing power structures

**Assessment**: County-level opposition is particularly insidious because it operates through administrative decisions (polling place closures, reduced hours, understaffing) that are difficult to challenge legally and receive little media attention. The distinction between genuine resource constraints and deliberate suppression is often unclear.

**Evidence**:

- NARF documented polling place closures and hour reductions on reservations in multiple states following *Shelby County*
- Section 2 litigation has revealed county officials making polling place decisions with awareness of racial impact (e.g., *Navajo Nation v. San Juan County*)
- Turnout data showing lower participation in counties that have reduced reservation polling access

### Election Security Advocacy Organizations

**Who**: Heritage Foundation, Honest Elections Project, Public Interest Legal Foundation, and similar organizations focused on election integrity.

**Arguments**:

- Voter ID requirements prevent impersonation fraud
- Voter roll accuracy requires standard addressing
- Ballot collection creates chain-of-custody risks
- Federal voting mandates infringe on state sovereignty
- The Voting Rights Act provisions requiring accommodations for specific groups are race-based preferences

**Actual Motivations**:

- Some organizations genuinely believe in election security goals
- Others use election security as a vehicle for policies that reduce minority voter participation
- The overlap between election security advocacy and Republican political strategy is well-documented
- The organizations rarely acknowledge the disparate impact of their proposals on Native voters

**Assessment**: These organizations provide intellectual and legal support for state-level restrictions. Their expertise in litigation and legislative drafting amplifies the impact of state-level opposition. Their refusal to acknowledge or address the disparate impact on Native voters undermines their credibility as good-faith election security advocates.

**Evidence**:

- Heritage Foundation's lobbying for voter ID laws in North Dakota coincided with restrictive ID requirements that excluded tribal IDs
- Public Interest Legal Foundation challenges to voter rolls have disproportionately affected minority voters
- These organizations filed amicus briefs supporting the state in *Brnovich v. DNC*

### Anti-VRA Legal Movement

**Who**: Conservative legal scholars, organizations, and Supreme Court justices who view the Voting Rights Act as constitutionally problematic.

**Arguments**:

- The VRA's coverage formula was based on outdated conditions (*Shelby County* majority)
- Section 2 has been expanded beyond its original intent through judicial interpretation
- Race-conscious redistricting is constitutionally suspect under the Equal Protection Clause
- Federal oversight of state elections exceeds congressional authority
- The VRA creates a race-based "entitlement" to proportional representation

**Actual Motivations**:

- Genuine constitutional concerns about federal-state power allocation
- Concerns about race-conscious government action in a post-civil-rights framework
- Political alignment with state-level interests in reduced federal oversight
- Belief that the VRA's mission has been accomplished and continued enforcement is unnecessary

**Assessment**: This movement has been extraordinarily successful in the Supreme Court, producing *Shelby County* and *Brnovich*. Its legal arguments have effectively shifted the burden of proof from jurisdictions seeking to change election rules to minority voters challenging discriminatory practices.

### Assimilationist Opposition

**Who**: Individuals and organizations that view Native-specific voting accommodations as race-based preferences rather than responses to unique structural barriers.

**Arguments**:

- All citizens should be treated equally; Native-specific provisions create preferential treatment
- Tribal IDs should not receive special acceptance beyond other forms of identification
- If Native voters cannot meet standard requirements (addresses, IDs), they should obtain them rather than receive accommodations
- The political classification of Indian status is really a racial classification and should be subject to strict scrutiny

**Assessment**: This perspective fails to account for the unique circumstances created by federal Indian policy -- the reservation system, the trust land status, the addressing gaps -- that make standard election requirements functionally discriminatory when applied to Indian Country. Accommodations for Native voters are responses to unique structural barriers, not racial preferences.

## Opposition Coordination

Unlike some policy areas where opposition is diffuse, voting rights opposition has a coordinated infrastructure:

1. **Legislative development**: Heritage Foundation and similar organizations develop model voter ID and election security legislation
2. **State adoption**: State legislators introduce and pass bills based on model legislation
3. **Legal defense**: When challenged, organizations like the Honest Elections Project provide legal resources and amicus support
4. **Judicial confirmation**: The appointment of judges sympathetic to state sovereignty and skeptical of VRA enforcement affects case outcomes
5. **Narrative framing**: "Election integrity" framing gives cover for discriminatory effects

## Key Observation

The most significant opposition to Native voting rights operates through the legal system rather than the political process. The Supreme Court decisions in *Shelby County* and *Brnovich* have done more to undermine Native voting access than any state legislature, because they removed the preventive enforcement mechanisms that protected Native voters for nearly five decades. Rebuilding those protections requires either new legislation (the John R. Lewis Act, NAVRA) or a change in judicial philosophy -- both of which face significant opposition.
