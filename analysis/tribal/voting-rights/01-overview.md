# Native American Voting Rights: Overview

## Executive Summary

Native Americans are the last racial group in the United States to have been granted full voting rights, and barriers to the exercise of those rights persist to a degree unmatched by any other population. Despite the Indian Citizenship Act of 1924, which conferred citizenship on all Native Americans born in the United States, many states continued to bar Native voting through literacy tests, residency requirements, and guardianship provisions well into the 1960s. Even after the Voting Rights Act of 1965 began dismantling these barriers, Native voters continue to face a distinctive constellation of obstacles: vast distances to polling places, lack of residential addresses in a system that requires them, language barriers for speakers of Native languages, voter ID laws that disproportionately exclude reservation residents, and discriminatory redistricting that dilutes Native voting strength.

These barriers are not accidental. They reflect the intersection of two systems: a federal Indian policy that isolated Native communities on remote reservations far from civic infrastructure, and a state-controlled election system designed without regard for the unique circumstances of Indian Country. The result is that Native Americans register and vote at significantly lower rates than the general population, are underrepresented in elected office at every level, and lack the political power needed to address the myriad challenges facing their communities.

The right to vote is the foundation of all other rights in a democratic system. Until Native Americans can exercise that right as effectively as other citizens, the disparities documented throughout this project -- in health, education, housing, economic opportunity, and justice -- will persist, because the political system will not be accountable to Native communities.

## Scope

### What This Subtopic Covers

- **Legal history**: Indian Citizenship Act, state exclusion laws, Voting Rights Act protections
- **Contemporary barriers**: Distance, addresses, ID requirements, language access, registration obstacles
- **Redistricting and dilution**: Gerrymandering, at-large elections, and Section 2 vote dilution claims
- **Voter suppression**: Targeted efforts to reduce Native voter participation
- **Political representation**: Native elected officials at federal, state, and local levels
- **Federal protections**: Voting Rights Act enforcement, Section 203 language provisions, Department of Justice oversight
- **Reservation-specific challenges**: Election administration in Indian Country, coordination with tribal governments
- **Recent legal developments**: *Brnovich v. DNC* (2021), *Shelby County v. Holder* (2013), and their impact on Native voting

### What This Subtopic Does Not Cover

- **Tribal elections**: Internal governance processes of tribal nations (covered in [Tribal Sovereignty](../tribal-sovereignty/01-overview.md))
- **General electoral reform**: Broader voting rights issues affecting all Americans (covered in [Political: Voting Rights](../../political/voting-rights/01-overview.md))
- **Tribal sovereignty**: The broader legal framework of tribal self-governance (covered in [Tribal Sovereignty](../tribal-sovereignty/01-overview.md))

## Core Tensions

- **Citizenship vs. sovereignty**: Native Americans are simultaneously citizens of the United States, citizens of their tribal nations, and residents of states -- creating complex questions about which elections matter most and how participation in one system affects another
- **State control vs. federal protection**: Elections are administered by states, but federal protections are needed to prevent states from disenfranchising Native voters
- **Individual rights vs. structural barriers**: The right to vote is formally guaranteed, but structural barriers (distance, addresses, IDs) make exercise of that right functionally unequal
- **Representation vs. dilution**: Majority-minority districts can concentrate Native voting power but may also isolate it; at-large systems can dilute it entirely
- **Engagement vs. alienation**: Historical exclusion and ongoing barriers have created distrust of the electoral system among many Native voters, further reducing participation
- **Federal oversight vs. state sovereignty**: *Shelby County v. Holder* (2013) gutted Section 5 preclearance, the most effective tool for preventing discriminatory election changes in areas with significant Native populations

## Key Questions

1. **What barriers prevent Native Americans from voting?** How do distance, addresses, ID requirements, and language access create functionally unequal voting rights?

2. **How has redistricting diluted Native voting power?** What role do gerrymandering, at-large elections, and county boundary manipulation play?

3. **What is the impact of *Shelby County* and *Brnovich*?** How have these Supreme Court decisions affected Native voting rights enforcement?

4. **What role does the Voting Rights Act play?** How effective are Section 2 and Section 203 protections, and what are their limitations?

5. **How can voter registration and turnout be increased?** What strategies effectively reach Native voters on reservations and in urban areas?

6. **What would equal voting access look like?** What policy and administrative changes would give Native voters the same practical ability to vote as other citizens?

## Vision of Success

A system with equal Native voting access would feature:

- Polling places and voter registration sites accessible within reasonable distance on every reservation
- Acceptance of tribal IDs and non-traditional addresses for voter registration and identification
- Ballots, voter information, and election materials available in all Section 203-covered Native languages
- Redistricting that preserves Native voting strength and avoids dilution
- Voter registration rates and turnout comparable to the general population
- Native representation in elected office roughly proportional to population at every level
- Effective federal enforcement mechanisms to prevent discriminatory election practices
- Election administration that accommodates reservation geography and infrastructure
