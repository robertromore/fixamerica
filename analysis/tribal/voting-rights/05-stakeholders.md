# Native American Voting Rights: Stakeholders

## Overview

Native voting rights involves an unusual set of stakeholders because it sits at the intersection of tribal sovereignty, federal Indian policy, state election administration, and civil rights law. The stakeholder landscape is shaped by the fact that states administer elections, the federal government enforces voting rights, and tribal nations have their own governance structures alongside the U.S. political system.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Native American Voters

**Interest**: Equal access to the ballot, effective representation, and a political system responsive to their concerns.

**Power Level**: Low individually; potentially significant collectively in states where AI/AN populations are large enough to be electorally decisive (Arizona, Alaska, Montana, New Mexico, South Dakota, North Dakota, Wisconsin, Oklahoma).

**Key Concerns**:

- Physical access to polling places and registration sites
- Voter ID laws that exclude reservation residents
- Language access for Native language speakers
- Redistricting that preserves rather than dilutes voting strength
- Candidates and officials who are responsive to Native issues
- Trust that votes will be counted and the system is fair

**Internal Diversity**: Native voters span the political spectrum. They are not a monolithic voting bloc, though they tend to vote Democratic at higher rates than the general population. Urban Native voters face different barriers than reservation voters. Elders and youth have different engagement patterns.

### Tribal Governments

**Interest**: Political influence to protect sovereignty, secure funding, and advance tribal interests in the state and federal systems; facilitating member participation in U.S. elections while maintaining their own governance structures.

**Power Level**: Moderate to high, depending on the state, through government-to-government relationships, lobbying, litigation, and voter mobilization.

**Key Concerns**:

- Tribal ID acceptance as valid voter identification
- Polling place and registration access on tribal lands
- Redistricting that preserves tribal political influence
- State laws that affect tribal interests (gaming, taxation, jurisdiction)
- Coordination with county election officials
- Protecting tribal sovereignty in the electoral process

### Native Voting Rights Organizations

**Interest**: Expanding and protecting Native voting access through litigation, advocacy, and voter mobilization.

**Power Level**: Moderate through legal victories, public advocacy, and grassroots organizing.

**Key Organizations**:

- **Native American Rights Fund (NARF)**: Primary litigation organization for Native voting rights; maintains the Native American Voting Rights database
- **Four Directions**: National voter registration and turnout organization; worked on *Brakebill v. Jaeger* and other cases
- **National Congress of American Indians (NCAI)**: Largest national AI/AN advocacy organization; coordinates national voting rights advocacy
- **Native Vote**: Nonpartisan voter engagement initiative
- **First Nations Development Institute**: Supports civic engagement capacity in tribal communities
- **Illuminatives**: Native-led narrative change organization that addresses Native voter invisibility

## Federal Stakeholders

### Department of Justice, Voting Section

**Interest**: Enforcing the Voting Rights Act, including Section 2 (vote dilution and discrimination) and Section 203 (language access).

**Power Level**: High through litigation authority, but capacity has been reduced.

**Key Concerns**:

- Post-*Shelby County* enforcement is reactive rather than preventive
- Post-*Brnovich* Section 2 standard makes cases harder to win
- Limited staffing for the number of jurisdictions requiring monitoring
- Need for data on Native voting patterns and barriers
- Coordination with tribal and state authorities

### Election Assistance Commission (EAC)

**Interest**: Supporting state and local election administration, including accessibility improvements.

**Power Level**: Low to moderate; provides guidance and funding but cannot mandate state compliance.

**Key Concerns**:

- Help America Vote Act (HAVA) funding for polling place improvements
- Developing best practices for election administration in Indian Country
- Voter registration system standards that accommodate non-traditional addresses

### U.S. Commission on Civil Rights

**Interest**: Investigating and reporting on civil rights issues, including Native voting rights.

**Power Level**: Low (advisory); reports carry moral and informational weight.

**Key Contributions**: Published *An Assessment of Minority Voting Rights Access in the United States* (2018) and *Broken Promises* (2018), both documenting Native voting barriers.

### Congress

**Interest**: Enacting and reauthorizing voting rights protections.

**Power Level**: High through legislative authority.

**Key Actors**:

- **Senate Committee on Indian Affairs**: Jurisdiction over Native-specific voting legislation (NAVRA)
- **Senate and House Judiciary Committees**: Jurisdiction over the VRA and general voting rights legislation
- **Congressional Native American Caucus**: Champions Native voting issues
- **Individual sponsors**: Representatives and Senators from states with significant Native populations

## State and Local Stakeholders

### State Legislatures

**Interest**: Setting election rules within their states; interests vary from expanding access to restricting participation depending on political orientation.

**Power Level**: High over election administration within their states.

**Key Concerns**:

- Voter ID requirements and accepted forms of identification
- Voter registration procedures and deadlines
- Polling place establishment and resource allocation
- Mail-in and absentee voting rules
- Ballot collection (harvesting) regulations
- Redistricting (in states with legislative redistricting)

### Secretaries of State / Chief Election Officers

**Interest**: Administering fair and efficient elections.

**Power Level**: High in setting administrative procedures and guidance for county officials.

**Key Concerns**:

- Compliance with federal voting rights requirements
- Uniformity of election administration across counties
- Voter registration system management
- Addressing complaints about access barriers

### County Election Officials

**Interest**: Administering elections within their counties, including any reservation areas within county boundaries.

**Power Level**: High at the operational level -- they decide where polling places go, how many poll workers are hired, and how resources are allocated.

**Key Concerns**:

- Cost of establishing and staffing polling places on remote reservations
- Recruiting poll workers on reservations
- Managing voter registration with non-standard addresses
- Complying with Section 203 language requirements
- Coordination with tribal governments

**Tension**: County officials may face genuine resource constraints in serving remote reservation areas, but they may also lack motivation to prioritize Native voter access when Native voters do not support their preferred candidates.

### State Courts

**Interest**: Adjudicating election law disputes within state constitutional frameworks.

**Power Level**: High in state redistricting, voter ID, and election administration disputes.

## Advocacy and Research Stakeholders

### Civil Rights Organizations

- **NAACP Legal Defense Fund**: Voting rights litigation that sometimes involves Native claims
- **ACLU**: State-level voting rights litigation including Native-specific cases
- **Brennan Center for Justice**: Research and advocacy on voting rights, including Native access
- **Campaign Legal Center**: Redistricting and election law litigation

### Academic Institutions

- **University of New Mexico**: Significant research program on Native voting
- **University of Colorado**: Indian law clinic involved in voting rights
- **UCLA Voting Rights Project**: Research on racial minority voting access
- **Tribal college and university programs**: Civic engagement research and programming
