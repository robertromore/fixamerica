# Native American Voting Rights: History

## Pre-Citizenship Era (Before 1924)

### Constitutional Framework

The original Constitution did not address Native American voting directly. The relevant provisions were exclusionary by design:

- **Article I, Section 2**: Apportionment excluded "Indians not taxed" from population counts, reflecting their status as members of separate nations rather than citizens of the United States
- **Fourteenth Amendment (1868)**: Granted citizenship to "all persons born or naturalized in the United States, and subject to the jurisdiction thereof" -- but was interpreted to exclude Native Americans because they were considered under tribal rather than U.S. jurisdiction
- **Fifteenth Amendment (1870)**: Prohibited denying the vote based on "race, color, or previous condition of servitude" -- but applied only to citizens, which most Native Americans were not

### *Elk v. Wilkins* (1884)

The Supreme Court's decision in *Elk v. Wilkins* explicitly held that the Fourteenth Amendment did not confer citizenship on Native Americans born in the United States. John Elk, who had voluntarily left his tribe and lived in Omaha, Nebraska, was denied the right to register to vote. The Court ruled that:

- Birth within the territorial limits of the United States was insufficient for citizenship if the person was born into a tribal nation
- Voluntary separation from a tribe did not automatically confer citizenship
- Only Congress or a treaty could grant citizenship to Native Americans

This decision meant that most Native Americans had no path to citizenship or voting rights absent specific congressional action.

### Piecemeal Citizenship Grants

Before universal citizenship, Congress granted citizenship to Native Americans through various mechanisms:

- **Treaty provisions**: Some treaties included citizenship for tribal members who accepted individual allotments
- **Dawes Act (1887)**: Granted citizenship to Native Americans who received allotments and "adopted the habits of civilized life" -- but citizenship came with the loss of communal land
- **Military service**: The Act of November 6, 1919, granted citizenship to Native veterans of World War I who applied
- **Specific tribal acts**: Congress granted citizenship to members of specific tribes through individual legislation
- **State-by-state**: Some states allowed Native voting before federal citizenship, while others explicitly prohibited it

By 1924, approximately two-thirds of Native Americans had obtained citizenship through one of these mechanisms, but the process was fragmented, paternalistic, and often tied to assimilation requirements.

## Indian Citizenship Act of 1924

### Enactment

On June 2, 1924, President Calvin Coolidge signed the Indian Citizenship Act, which declared: "all non-citizen Indians born within the territorial limits of the United States be, and they are hereby, declared to be citizens of the United States."

### Motivations

The Act was motivated by multiple factors:

- Recognition of Native military service in World War I (approximately 12,000 served, many without citizenship)
- Continuation of assimilation policy -- citizenship was seen as a tool for dissolving tribal identity
- Pressure from reformers who viewed the exclusion of Native Americans from citizenship as unjust
- Political calculation that newly enfranchised Native voters would support incumbent administrations

### Limitations

The Citizenship Act did not guarantee the right to vote. The Constitution gives states the authority to set voter qualifications (subject to federal constitutional constraints), and many states used this authority to continue excluding Native Americans:

- The Act stated that citizenship should not "impair or otherwise affect the right of any Indian to tribal or other property" -- but did not explicitly address voting
- States were not required to change their election laws to accommodate new Native citizens
- The Fifteenth Amendment prohibited racial discrimination in voting but enforcement mechanisms were weak

## State-Level Exclusion (1924-1965)

### Methods of Exclusion

After the Citizenship Act, states employed various tactics to prevent Native Americans from voting:

**Literacy Tests**

- Arizona, New Mexico, and other states required literacy tests that disproportionately excluded Native voters
- Tests were administered subjectively, with registrars using stricter standards for Native applicants
- Native language literacy was not accepted; only English literacy counted

**Residency Requirements**

- Several states argued that reservation residents were not "residents" of the state because reservations were federal land
- Arizona used this argument until *Harrison v. Laveen* (1948), in which the Arizona Supreme Court ruled that reservation residents were state residents

**Guardianship Provisions**

- Utah classified Native Americans as persons "under guardianship" (referencing the federal trust relationship) and therefore ineligible to vote
- This exclusion was not repealed until 1957, making Utah the last state to formally remove Native voting barriers

**Property and Tax Requirements**

- Some states required property ownership or tax payment as voter qualifications
- Because trust land cannot be taxed and is not individually owned in the conventional sense, these requirements excluded many reservation residents

**Poll Taxes**

- Where poll taxes existed, they disproportionately affected impoverished reservation populations
- Poll taxes were not eliminated nationally until the Twenty-Fourth Amendment (1964) and *Harper v. Virginia* (1966)

## Voting Rights Act Era (1965-2013)

### Voting Rights Act of 1965

The VRA was the most significant federal legislation for Native voting rights. Key provisions:

**Section 2**: Prohibited voting practices that resulted in discrimination against racial minorities, including Native Americans. Section 2 cases became the primary tool for challenging vote dilution through at-large elections, redistricting, and other structural barriers.

**Section 5 (Preclearance)**: Required covered jurisdictions to obtain federal approval before changing election procedures. Coverage was determined by Section 4(b)'s formula based on historical use of tests or devices and low voter registration/turnout. Covered jurisdictions with significant Native populations included:

- Arizona (entire state)
- Alaska (entire state)
- Selected counties in South Dakota, North Carolina, and other states

**Section 203 (Language Minority Provisions)**: Added in 1975, required bilingual election materials and assistance in jurisdictions where a language minority exceeded 5% of voting-age citizens or 10,000 persons.

### Impact on Native Voting

The VRA had transformative effects:

- **Preclearance blocked discriminatory changes**: Arizona and Alaska could not implement election changes without federal review, preventing many discriminatory proposals
- **Section 2 litigation**: Native plaintiffs successfully challenged at-large election systems in counties, school boards, and other local governments across the West and Midwest
- **Language access**: Section 203 required election materials in Navajo, Lakota, Cherokee, Yup'ik, and other languages for the first time
- **Federal observers**: The Department of Justice sent federal observers to polling places in areas with histories of discrimination against Native voters

### Key VRA Cases Involving Native Voters

| Case | Year | Issue | Outcome |
|------|------|-------|---------|
| *Windy Boy v. Big Horn County* | 1986 | At-large county commission elections diluting Crow and Northern Cheyenne votes | Plaintiffs prevailed; district elections ordered |
| *Buckanaga v. Sisseton School District* | 1986 | At-large school board elections diluting Native vote | Plaintiffs prevailed |
| *United States v. Blaine County* | 1999 | At-large elections for county commission | Plaintiffs prevailed; district elections ordered |
| *Bone Shirt v. Hazeltine* | 2004 | South Dakota legislative redistricting diluting Native vote | Plaintiffs prevailed; new districts drawn |
| *Navajo Nation v. San Juan County* | 2016/2018 | At-large county commission and school board elections | Plaintiffs prevailed; district elections ordered |

### 1982 VRA Amendments

The 1982 amendments strengthened Section 2 by establishing a "results" test: plaintiffs no longer had to prove discriminatory intent, only that a practice "results in" a denial or abridgment of the right to vote on account of race. The Senate Report accompanying the amendments articulated factors (the "Senate factors") that courts should consider, including the history of discrimination, racially polarized voting, and the extent to which members of the minority group have been elected to office.

## Post-*Shelby County* Era (2013-Present)

### *Shelby County v. Holder* (2013)

The Supreme Court struck down Section 4(b), the coverage formula for preclearance, holding that it was based on decades-old data and conditions that no longer justified treating some jurisdictions differently from others. Chief Justice Roberts wrote that "things have changed dramatically" since the VRA was enacted.

**Consequences for Native voting**:

- Arizona and Alaska were immediately freed from preclearance requirements
- South Dakota counties with preclearance obligations were also freed
- States could now implement election changes without federal review
- The Department of Justice lost its most effective preventive enforcement tool

### Post-*Shelby County* Developments

Within months and years of the decision:

- **Arizona**: Enacted restrictions on ballot collection that particularly affected reservation communities; restricted out-of-precinct voting
- **North Dakota**: Implemented strict voter ID law requiring residential addresses, effectively excluding many reservation residents
- **Alaska**: Reduced early voting sites and changed election procedures
- **South Dakota**: Made changes to polling place locations and voter registration procedures
- **Montana**: Attempted to eliminate same-day voter registration, which Native voters disproportionately use (blocked by state courts)

### *Brnovich v. DNC* (2021)

The Court further weakened Section 2 enforcement by establishing new requirements for challenging voting practices. Justice Alito's opinion articulated five "guideposts" that made it significantly harder to prove Section 2 violations, including requiring consideration of the "total opportunities" available to voters and the disparate impact's magnitude.

**Impact on Native voting**:

- The ballot collection ban upheld in *Brnovich* directly undermined one of the few mechanisms reservation communities had developed to overcome distance barriers
- The "total opportunities" framework allows courts to discount Native voting barriers if any alternative voting mechanisms exist, even if they are impractical for reservation residents
- Section 2 litigation costs and complexity increased, reducing the ability of Native plaintiffs to challenge discriminatory practices

## Recent Positive Developments

Despite judicial setbacks, some progress has occurred:

- **Tribal polling places**: Some states have established satellite polling locations and early voting sites on reservations through tribal-state agreements
- **Voter registration drives**: Organizations like Four Directions, Native Vote, and Rock the Vote Native have increased registration
- **State reforms**: Some states (e.g., Montana with same-day registration, Colorado with all-mail voting) have adopted systems that reduce barriers for all voters including Native Americans
- **Native candidates**: Record numbers of Native Americans have run for and won elected office at all levels
- **Congressional representation**: The 119th Congress includes six AI/AN members, the most in history
