# Native American Voting Rights: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative landscape for Native American voting rights encompasses four main areas: (1) comprehensive Native-specific voting access legislation (NAVRA), (2) restoration of the Voting Rights Act enforcement mechanisms weakened by *Shelby County* and *Brnovich*, (3) federal standards for tribal ID acceptance and non-traditional addressing, and (4) redistricting protections for tribal communities. This document presents draft legislation for each area along with analysis of potential challenges and refinements.

## Constitutional Amendments

No constitutional amendment is strictly required to address Native voting barriers. Congress possesses ample authority under the Fourteenth and Fifteenth Amendments' enforcement clauses (Sections 5 of each), the Indian Commerce Clause (Article I, Section 8, Clause 3), and the Elections Clause (Article I, Section 4) to enact comprehensive voting rights protections for Native Americans. However, a constitutional amendment could provide more durable protection against judicial erosion of statutory voting rights.

### Voting Rights Enforcement Amendment (Optional)

**Purpose**: Restore and strengthen congressional authority to enforce voting rights, addressing the limitations imposed by *Shelby County* and *Brnovich*.

**Draft Text**:

```text
Section 1. The right of citizens of the United States to vote
shall not be denied or abridged by any State on account of race,
color, ethnicity, or membership in a Native nation, and Congress
shall have the power to enact prophylactic legislation to prevent
such denial or abridgment, including preclearance requirements
for jurisdictions with a history of voting discrimination.

Section 2. No State shall impose voter identification
requirements, registration procedures, or election administration
practices that result in a disproportionate burden on the voting
rights of any racial, ethnic, or language minority group.

Section 3. Congress shall have the power to enforce this article
by appropriate legislation.

Section 4. This article shall take effect one year after the
date of ratification.
```

**Explanation**: Section 1 explicitly authorizes prophylactic measures including preclearance, overriding the *Shelby County* limitation on congressional enforcement power. Section 2 constitutionalizes the "results" test, preventing courts from requiring proof of discriminatory intent. The inclusion of "membership in a Native nation" clarifies that the political classification of tribal membership is protected against discrimination in the voting context.

**Potential Challenges**: Ratification requires three-fourths of state legislatures, many of which oppose expanded federal voting rights authority. The "disproportionate burden" standard in Section 2 could face opposition as overly broad.

**Refinements**: Narrow Section 2 to require "significant" disproportionate burden; include a sunset provision requiring reauthorization; define "history of voting discrimination" by reference to specific statutory criteria.

## Federal Legislation

### Bill 1: Native American Voting Rights Act (NAVRA)

**Purpose**: Address the unique barriers to voting access faced by Native Americans on reservations and in communities served by tribal governments, through federal requirements for polling place access, identification, addressing, language assistance, and ballot collection.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Native American Voting Rights Act
of 2026".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) Native Americans were the last racial group in the United
States to receive the right to vote and continue to face barriers
to the exercise of that right unmatched by any other population;
(b) these barriers result from the intersection of federal Indian
policy, which isolated Native communities on remote reservations,
and state election systems designed without regard for the unique
circumstances of Indian Country;
(c) voter registration and turnout among Native Americans
significantly lag behind the general population, with gaps of
15-21 percentage points in some states;
(d) the Supreme Court's decisions in Shelby County v. Holder,
570 U.S. 529 (2013), and Brnovich v. DNC, 594 U.S. 647 (2021),
have weakened the enforcement mechanisms that previously protected
Native voting rights;
(e) existing federal law does not adequately address the specific
barriers of distance, addressing, identification, and language
access that Native voters face; and
(f) Congress has authority to enact this legislation under the
Fourteenth and Fifteenth Amendments' enforcement clauses and the
Indian Commerce Clause.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
(a) INDIAN COUNTRY.--The term "Indian Country" has the meaning
given in section 1151 of title 18, United States Code.
(b) INDIAN TRIBE.--The term "Indian tribe" means any Indian tribe,
band, nation, or other organized group or community that is
recognized as eligible for the special programs and services
provided by the United States to Indians because of their status
as Indians.
(c) TRIBAL GOVERNMENT.--The term "tribal government" means the
recognized governing body of any Indian tribe.
(d) COVERED STATE.--The term "covered State" means any State
containing Indian Country.
(e) RESERVATION.--The term "reservation" includes any Indian
reservation, pueblo, rancheria, colony, or other land held in
trust by the United States for an Indian tribe or individual Indian.
(f) TRIBAL IDENTIFICATION.--The term "tribal identification" means
any document issued by a tribal government that identifies the
bearer as a member or citizen of the issuing tribe.

SEC. 4. POLLING PLACE ACCESS.

(a) REQUIREMENT.--Each covered State shall, in each Federal
election, provide at least one polling place or satellite voting
location on each reservation with a voting-age population of 500
or more, as determined by the most recent decennial census or
American Community Survey estimate.
(b) EARLY VOTING.--In any State that provides early voting, at
least one early voting location shall be established on each
reservation described in subsection (a) for a period of not less
than 10 days before election day.
(c) HOURS.--Polling places and early voting locations established
under this section shall maintain hours of operation no less than
those maintained at polling places in the same county outside
Indian Country.
(d) MOBILE VOTING UNITS.--For reservations with a voting-age
population of fewer than 500, the State shall provide either a
fixed polling place or a mobile voting unit that visits the
reservation at least twice during the early voting period and
on election day.
(e) CONSULTATION.--Before establishing polling places or voting
locations under this section, the State shall consult with the
affected tribal government regarding the location, hours, and
staffing of such facilities.
(f) FEDERAL FUNDING.--There is authorized to be appropriated
$50,000,000 for each fiscal year for grants to States to
implement this section.

SEC. 5. TRIBAL IDENTIFICATION ACCEPTANCE.

(a) REQUIREMENT.--Any tribal identification shall be accepted as
a valid form of identification for purposes of voter registration,
voting, and any State identification requirement.
(b) ADDRESS REQUIREMENTS.--No State shall require that a tribal
identification contain a residential street address as a condition
of acceptance under this section.
(c) EXPIRATION REQUIREMENTS.--No State shall require that a tribal
identification contain an expiration date as a condition of
acceptance under this section.
(d) SUPPLEMENTAL IDENTIFICATION.--A State may request, but not
require, supplemental information to accompany a tribal
identification, provided that failure to provide such information
does not prevent the bearer from casting a regular ballot.

SEC. 6. NON-TRADITIONAL ADDRESS ACCOMMODATION.

(a) VOTER REGISTRATION.--No State shall reject a voter
registration application from a resident of Indian Country solely
because the applicant does not have a traditional residential
street address, provided the applicant provides:
  (1) a description of the physical location of the applicant's
  residence, which may include a narrative description, GPS
  coordinates, map reference, or any other designation sufficient
  to identify the location;
  (2) a mailing address, which may be a post office box, tribal
  government office address, or other address where mail may be
  received; and
  (3) the name of the Indian tribe with which the applicant is
  affiliated, if applicable.
(b) VOTER ROLLS.--Each covered State shall modify its voter
registration database to accommodate non-traditional address
formats from Indian Country within 2 years of the date of
enactment.
(c) TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE.--The Election Assistance Commission
shall develop and publish model data standards for non-traditional
address accommodation within 1 year of the date of enactment.
(d) FEDERAL FUNDING.--There is authorized to be appropriated
$25,000,000 for grants to States for voter registration system
modifications under this section.

SEC. 7. BALLOT COLLECTION.

(a) DESIGNATION.--A tribal government may designate official ballot
collectors to collect, transport, and deliver completed mail-in or
absentee ballots from voters within Indian Country.
(b) REQUIREMENTS.--Each designated ballot collector shall:
  (1) be designated in writing by the tribal government;
  (2) provide a signed receipt to each voter whose ballot is
  collected;
  (3) use tamper-evident containers or bags for transport;
  (4) deliver all collected ballots to an election official or
  designated ballot drop-off location within 48 hours of
  collection; and
  (5) maintain a log of all ballots collected including the date,
  voter name, and ballot number.
(c) PREEMPTION.--This section preempts any State law that
prohibits, restricts, or imposes criminal penalties on the
collection, transport, or delivery of ballots by designated
tribal ballot collectors acting in compliance with this section.
(d) PENALTIES.--Any person who tampers with, destroys, or
deliberately fails to deliver a ballot collected under this
section shall be subject to a fine of not more than $100,000
and imprisonment for not more than 5 years.

SEC. 8. LANGUAGE ACCESS.

(a) EXPANDED COVERAGE.--Section 203 of the Voting Rights Act
(52 U.S.C. 10503) is amended by adding the following:
  "(c) INDIAN COUNTRY COVERAGE.--Notwithstanding the population
  thresholds in subsection (b), any jurisdiction containing Indian
  Country in which the number of American Indian or Alaska Native
  citizens of voting age who are members of a single language
  minority and have limited English proficiency exceeds 2 percent
  of the voting-age citizens or 2,500 persons, whichever is less,
  shall be subject to the requirements of this section with respect
  to that language."
(b) ORAL LANGUAGE ASSISTANCE.--In any jurisdiction covered under
this section or section 203 of the Voting Rights Act with respect
to an AI/AN language, at least one fluent speaker of the covered
language shall be present at each covered polling place or available
by telephone.
(c) AUDIO BALLOT GUIDES.--Each covered jurisdiction shall produce
audio ballot guides in covered AI/AN languages for distribution
at polling places and through tribal media.
(d) FEDERAL FUNDING.--There is authorized to be appropriated
$15,000,000 for each fiscal year for grants to jurisdictions to
implement language assistance under this section.

SEC. 9. VOTER REGISTRATION.

(a) TRIBAL REGISTRATION SITES.--Each covered State shall designate
tribal government offices, IHS facilities, and tribal colleges
as official voter registration sites.
(b) SAME-DAY REGISTRATION.--Each covered State shall permit voter
registration at any polling place or voting location established
under section 4 on any day that voting is permitted, including
election day.
(c) AUTOMATIC REGISTRATION.--Federal agencies providing services
in Indian Country, including the Indian Health Service and Bureau
of Indian Affairs, shall offer voter registration to all eligible
individuals at the time of service delivery.

SEC. 10. TRIBAL CONSULTATION.

(a) REQUIREMENT.--Before implementing any change to election
administration practices, including polling place location or
closure, voter registration procedures, voter identification
requirements, or ballot handling procedures, that would affect
voters in Indian Country, a State or political subdivision shall
consult with affected tribal governments.
(b) NOTICE.--Consultation under this section shall include at
least 90 days' written notice to affected tribal governments
and a meaningful opportunity for input.
(c) DOCUMENTATION.--The State or political subdivision shall
document the consultation process and make such documentation
publicly available.

SEC. 11. FEDERAL ELECTION OBSERVER PROGRAM.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.--The Attorney General is authorized to deploy
federal election observers to any polling place in or near Indian
Country upon request by a tribal government or upon a determination
that conditions warrant observation.
(b) SCOPE.--Observers may monitor voter check-in procedures,
identification requirements, language access, ballot handling,
and any other election administration practice.
(c) REPORTS.--The Attorney General shall publish reports on
observer findings within 90 days of each federal election.

SEC. 12. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.--Any individual or tribal government
aggrieved by a violation of this Act may bring a civil action
in Federal district court.
(b) ATTORNEYS' FEES.--The court shall award reasonable attorneys'
fees to the prevailing plaintiff in any action under this section.
(c) DOJ ENFORCEMENT.--The Attorney General may bring a civil
action to enforce any provision of this Act.
(d) PRELIMINARY RELIEF.--In any action under this section, the
court may grant preliminary injunctive relief upon a showing that
a violation is likely and that voters will be irreparably harmed
absent relief.

SEC. 13. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

In addition to amounts authorized in specific sections, there is
authorized to be appropriated $20,000,000 for each fiscal year
for general implementation of this Act, including tribal voter
education programs, poll worker recruitment and training, and
data collection on Native voter participation.

SEC. 14. EFFECTIVE DATE.

(a) GENERAL.--This Act shall take effect 180 days after the date
of enactment, except as otherwise provided.
(b) DATABASE MODIFICATIONS.--Section 6(b) shall take effect 2
years after the date of enactment.
(c) SAME-DAY REGISTRATION.--Section 9(b) shall take effect for
the first federal election occurring at least 1 year after the
date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: NAVRA is the most comprehensive Native-specific voting rights legislation proposed. It addresses each of the major barrier categories identified in the analysis: distance (Section 4), identification (Section 5), addressing (Section 6), ballot access (Section 7), language (Section 8), registration (Section 9), and accountability (Sections 10-12). The bill creates enforceable federal standards backed by funding, a private right of action, and DOJ enforcement authority.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Senate filibuster makes passage difficult without 60 votes or filibuster reform
- Appropriations may not match authorization levels
- State sovereignty arguments against federal mandates on election administration
- *Brnovich*-style judicial challenges to the ballot collection preemption
- Implementation costs and capacity in rural western counties

**Refinements**:

- Include mandatory appropriations to prevent underfunding
- Add a state opt-in provision with enhanced federal funding for voluntary compliance
- Strengthen the ballot collection chain-of-custody provisions to withstand judicial scrutiny
- Include sunset and reauthorization provisions at 10 years

### Bill 2: Voting Rights Act Restoration -- Native Provisions

**Purpose**: Restore the Voting Rights Act enforcement mechanisms weakened by *Shelby County* and *Brnovich* with specific provisions addressing Native voting discrimination.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Native Voting Rights Restoration
Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. PRECLEARANCE RESTORATION.

(a) COVERAGE FORMULA.--Section 4(b) of the Voting Rights Act
(52 U.S.C. 10303(b)) is amended to provide that a State or
political subdivision shall be subject to preclearance under
section 5 if, during the preceding 25 calendar years--
  (1) 3 or more voting rights violations have been committed by
  the State or any political subdivision within the State;
  (2) 1 or more voting rights violations have been committed by
  the State itself or a political subdivision with a population
  of 10,000 or more; or
  (3) the Attorney General has issued 3 or more objections to
  voting changes in the State.
(b) NATIVE-SPECIFIC TRIGGER.--In addition to the criteria in
subsection (a), a State or political subdivision shall be subject
to preclearance if, during the preceding 25 years--
  (1) a federal court has found that the State or subdivision
  violated section 2 of the Voting Rights Act with respect to
  Native American voters; or
  (2) the State or subdivision has reduced the number of polling
  places in or near Indian Country by more than 25 percent
  without prior consultation with affected tribal governments.

SEC. 3. SECTION 2 RESTORATION.

(a) STANDARD.--Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act (52 U.S.C.
10301) is amended by adding:
"(c) In determining whether a violation of subsection (a) has
occurred, a court shall not--
  (1) require that the challenged practice impose a total
  deprivation of the right to vote;
  (2) consider the availability of alternative voting mechanisms
  when assessing the burden imposed by the challenged practice;
  (3) require that the disparity in burden be of any particular
  magnitude, so long as it is more than de minimis;
  (4) consider the race-neutral justifications for a practice as
  a defense where the practice results in a disparate burden on
  minority voters; or
  (5) weigh the strength of the state's interest in the challenged
  practice against the burden imposed on minority voters."
(b) NATIVE-SPECIFIC FACTORS.--In evaluating claims under section 2
involving Native American voters, courts shall consider:
  (1) the geographic isolation of the Native community;
  (2) the availability of standard residential addresses;
  (3) the reliability of postal service;
  (4) the distance to polling places and election offices;
  (5) the acceptance of tribal identification; and
  (6) the history of federal Indian policy as it affects the
  community's access to the electoral process.

SEC. 4. ATTORNEYS' FEES.

Section 14(e) of the Voting Rights Act (52 U.S.C. 10310(e)) is
amended to provide that a court shall award reasonable attorneys'
fees, including expert witness fees, to the prevailing plaintiff
in any action under sections 2, 4, 5, or 203.

SEC. 5. NATIVE VOTING RIGHTS ENFORCEMENT UNIT.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--There is established within the Voting Section
of the Civil Rights Division of the Department of Justice a Native
Voting Rights Enforcement Unit.
(b) DUTIES.--The Unit shall:
  (1) monitor election administration in jurisdictions containing
  Indian Country;
  (2) investigate complaints of voting discrimination against
  Native Americans;
  (3) bring enforcement actions under sections 2 and 203 of the
  Voting Rights Act on behalf of Native American voters;
  (4) coordinate with tribal governments on voting access issues;
  (5) publish biennial reports on the state of Native voting rights.
(c) AUTHORIZATION.--There is authorized to be appropriated
$10,000,000 for each fiscal year for the operation of the Unit.
```

**Explanation**: This bill directly addresses the two most damaging Supreme Court decisions for Native voting rights. Section 2 restores preclearance with both a general formula (based on the John R. Lewis Act model) and a Native-specific trigger. Section 3 legislatively overrides the *Brnovich* guideposts that made Section 2 claims harder to prove. Section 5 creates a dedicated enforcement unit to address the DOJ's historically inadequate attention to Native voting cases.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Senate filibuster
- Constitutional challenges arguing that Congress cannot override Supreme Court interpretations of the VRA
- Arguments that the Section 3 override effectively amends the Constitution by statute
- Appropriations for the enforcement unit

**Refinements**:

- Strengthen the constitutional basis by framing the Section 2 amendments as clarifications of legislative intent rather than overrides of judicial interpretation
- Include a bail-in/bail-out mechanism for preclearance so jurisdictions can exit coverage by demonstrating compliance
- Add a preclearance exemption for de minimis election changes

## State Model Legislation

### Model Tribal Voter Identification Acceptance Act

**Purpose**: Require states to accept tribal government-issued identification for all voting purposes.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Tribal Voter Identification
Acceptance Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Tribal identification" means any document issued by a
federally recognized Indian tribe that identifies the bearer as a
member or citizen of the issuing tribe and contains a photograph
and the bearer's name.
(b) "Voting purposes" includes voter registration, presenting
identification at a polling place, and any other purpose for which
voter identification is required under [State] law.

SECTION 3. ACCEPTANCE REQUIREMENT.

(a) Any tribal identification shall be accepted as a valid form
of identification for all voting purposes under [State] law.
(b) No additional requirements shall be imposed on tribal
identification beyond those set forth in section 2(a), including
but not limited to requirements for:
  (1) a residential street address;
  (2) an expiration date;
  (3) a barcode, magnetic strip, or machine-readable feature; or
  (4) issuance by a specific level of tribal government.

SECTION 4. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect immediately upon enactment.
```

### Model Non-Traditional Address Accommodation Act

**Purpose**: Require state voter registration systems to accept non-traditional address formats from reservation residents.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Indian Country Voter
Address Accommodation Act".

SECTION 2. NON-TRADITIONAL ADDRESS ACCEPTANCE.

(a) The [State election authority] shall accept voter registration
applications from residents of Indian Country who do not have a
traditional residential street address, provided the applicant
provides:
  (1) a description of the physical location of the residence,
  which may include a narrative description, GPS coordinates, map
  reference, or any other designation sufficient to identify the
  location; and
  (2) a mailing address where the applicant may receive
  correspondence, which may be a post office box, general delivery
  address, tribal government office, or other mailing address.
(b) The [State election authority] shall modify the State voter
registration database to accommodate non-traditional address
formats within 1 year of the effective date of this Act.

SECTION 3. ASSIGNMENT TO VOTING DISTRICTS.

The [State election authority] shall work with tribal governments
to assign voters registered under this section to the appropriate
voting district based on the physical location of their residence.

SECTION 4. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on [date].
```

## Regulatory Framework

### Executive Order on Tribal Voter Access

**Existing Authority**: The President may issue executive orders directing federal agencies to take specific actions within their existing authority.

**Draft Provisions**:

- Direct the Election Assistance Commission to develop model non-traditional address standards within 180 days
- Direct the Department of Justice to issue guidance on tribal ID acceptance under HAVA within 90 days
- Direct the Indian Health Service and Bureau of Indian Affairs to offer voter registration at all service points
- Direct the Interagency Council on Homelessness to include reservation addressing in its work plan
- Establish an interagency tribal voter access task force co-chaired by the Domestic Policy Council and the Department of the Interior

### DOJ Guidance on Section 203 Compliance

**Existing Authority**: The Attorney General has authority to issue guidance on VRA compliance.

**Draft Guidance Topics**:

- Minimum standards for oral language assistance at covered polling places
- Acceptable methods for ballot and voter guide translation in primarily oral languages
- Training requirements for bilingual poll workers
- Monitoring and enforcement procedures for Section 203 compliance in AI/AN jurisdictions

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Congressional authority**: Congress has clear authority under the Fourteenth and Fifteenth Amendment enforcement clauses, the Indian Commerce Clause, and the Elections Clause to enact NAVRA and VRA restoration legislation
- **Ballot collection preemption**: Congress may preempt state ballot collection bans under the Elections Clause (Article I, Section 4); the *Brnovich* decision did not hold that ballot collection bans are constitutionally required
- **Tribal ID acceptance mandate**: Federal preemption of state voter ID requirements is supported by the Elections Clause; *Crawford v. Marion County* (2008) did not address tribal ID specifically and left room for as-applied challenges
- **Non-traditional addressing**: The National Voter Registration Act already imposes federal standards on state voter registration; NAVRA extends this authority to addressing formats
- **Section 2 amendment**: Congress has broad authority to define the meaning of statutes it enacts; the *Brnovich* guideposts interpreted congressional intent, which Congress may correct through clarifying amendments

### Preemption Questions

- NAVRA's ballot collection provisions would preempt state bans as applied to designated tribal collectors operating in Indian Country
- Tribal ID acceptance provisions would preempt more restrictive state ID requirements for tribal identification
- Non-traditional addressing provisions would preempt state registration systems that reject non-standard address formats
- States would retain authority over election administration not specifically addressed by NAVRA

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- Private right of action with attorneys' fees for NAVRA violations
- DOJ enforcement authority through the Native Voting Rights Enforcement Unit
- Federal election observer program for monitoring compliance
- Preclearance for covered jurisdictions under restored Section 5
- Judicial review of any election change affecting Indian Country

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- NAVRA authorization expires after 10 years unless reauthorized
- Preclearance coverage formula reviewed every 10 years using updated data
- GAO review of NAVRA implementation at years 3, 5, and 8
- Biennial DOJ reports on Native voting rights enforcement
- Section 203 coverage determinations updated every 5 years

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Native American Voting Rights Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Population threshold avoidance | States could manipulate census data or reservation boundary definitions to keep populations below the 500-person threshold for mandatory polling places | Medium |
| Consultation without compliance | The tribal consultation requirement (Section 10) does not require states to adopt tribal recommendations, only to consult | High |
| Funding dependence | Authorized appropriations may not be appropriated; states may use lack of funding as justification for non-compliance | Critical |
| Tribal ID variability | States could challenge specific tribal IDs as insufficient based on tribal government capacity or document quality | Medium |
| Ballot collector abuse | Designated collectors could be pressured to selectively collect ballots or could fail to deliver ballots from voters supporting disfavored candidates | Medium |
| Same-day registration circumvention | States could impose onerous provisional ballot requirements that reduce the effectiveness of same-day registration | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No mandatory appropriations | All NAVRA provisions depend on annual appropriations; chronic underfunding could render the Act ineffective | Political resistance to mandatory spending |
| Consultation without teeth | Tribal governments have input but not veto power over election changes affecting their communities | Balancing tribal sovereignty against state election authority |
| Urban Native exclusion | NAVRA focuses primarily on reservation-based barriers; urban Native voters face different challenges (awareness, cultural isolation) that are less directly addressed | Legislative scope limitations |
| No redistricting provisions | NAVRA does not directly address redistricting, leaving vote dilution to Section 2 litigation | Political difficulty of federal redistricting mandates |
| Judicial interpretation risk | Courts applying *Brnovich* reasoning could narrow NAVRA provisions or the Section 2 restoration | Independent judiciary |
| Limited coverage of state elections | NAVRA's strongest provisions apply to federal elections; state and local elections governed by state law may not be fully covered | Elections Clause scope |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Mandatory appropriations**: Amend NAVRA to include mandatory rather than discretionary appropriations, or create a dedicated tribal election access trust fund
2. **Consultation strengthening**: Add a provision requiring states to explain in writing any decision to reject tribal government recommendations and provide judicial review of such decisions
3. **Urban Native provisions**: Add a section requiring states to provide voter education and registration assistance at urban Indian health centers, friendship houses, and tribal organization offices
4. **Redistricting cross-reference**: Add a findings section stating that reservations and tribal communities shall be considered communities of interest under Section 2 for redistricting purposes
5. **Anti-circumvention clause**: Add a provision that any practice adopted by a State for the purpose or with the effect of circumventing NAVRA protections shall be considered a violation of the Act
6. **State election coverage**: Frame applicable provisions under both the Elections Clause (federal elections) and the Fifteenth Amendment enforcement clause (all elections) to maximize coverage
7. **Ballot collector safeguards**: Require random audits of ballot collector logs; impose penalties for selective collection; permit voters to request that a specific collector not handle their ballot

### Voting Rights Act Restoration -- Native Provisions

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Coverage formula manipulation | Jurisdictions could avoid triggering the preclearance formula by settling Section 2 cases without an admission of violation | High |
| De minimis exploitation | The "more than de minimis" standard in the Section 2 restoration could be interpreted narrowly by unsympathetic courts | Medium |
| Enforcement unit understaffing | Even with authorization, the Native Voting Rights Enforcement Unit could be understaffed through budget decisions | Medium |
| Bail-in avoidance | Jurisdictions could make minimal changes to avoid triggering the Native-specific preclearance trigger while still suppressing Native votes through other means | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No guaranteed preclearance for all states with reservations | Only states meeting the coverage formula or Native-specific trigger are covered, potentially leaving some jurisdictions unmonitored | Political compromise to pass legislation |
| Judicial appointment dependence | Effectiveness depends on sympathetic judicial interpretation, particularly of the Section 2 restoration provisions | Independent judiciary |
| Retroactivity unclear | Whether the Section 2 restoration applies to currently pending cases is ambiguous | Drafting limitation |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Settlement counting**: Amend to count consent decrees and settlements involving voting practices as violations for coverage formula purposes
2. **Judicial review standard**: Include legislative history explicitly instructing courts to interpret "more than de minimis" broadly and consistent with the pre-*Brnovich* case law
3. **Enforcement unit staffing floor**: Establish a minimum staffing level for the Native Voting Rights Enforcement Unit (e.g., not fewer than 10 attorneys)
4. **Retroactivity clause**: Include explicit provision that the Section 2 restoration applies to all pending cases as well as future litigation
5. **Universal preclearance option**: Include an alternative provision for optional nationwide preclearance if the targeted formula is struck down

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| State resistance to federal mandates | Conditional funding (compliance required for election administration grants); DOJ enforcement authority |
| County capacity limitations | Federal technical assistance; regional election support centers; shared resource agreements between counties and tribal governments |
| Data gaps on Native voting | Mandatory collection of race/ethnicity data in voter registration and turnout; Census Bureau partnership with NCAI |
| Tribal government capacity | Federal grants for tribal election administration; training programs for tribal voter access coordinators |
| Judicial hostility to VRA expansion | Build a strong legislative record; frame amendments as clarifications of congressional intent; include severability provisions |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- NAVRA authorization expires after 10 years unless reauthorized
- Section 2 restoration effective upon enactment with no sunset
- Preclearance coverage formula reviewed every 10 years using updated data
- GAO comprehensive review of Native voting rights at years 5 and 10
- Biennial DOJ reports on NAVRA implementation and enforcement
- Inspector General audit of federal grant expenditures under NAVRA
