# Native American Voting Rights: Root Causes

## Overview

The persistent gap between Native Americans' formal right to vote and their practical ability to exercise that right is not the result of a single cause but of multiple interlocking systems. These root causes operate at different levels -- historical, structural, institutional, and geographic -- and reinforce each other in ways that make any single intervention insufficient.

## Root Cause 1: Reservation Geography Created by Federal Policy

### Description

The federal government deliberately placed Native communities on geographically isolated, resource-poor lands far from population centers and civic infrastructure. This was not accidental -- it was the explicit purpose of the reservation system to remove Native people from lands desired by settlers and to confine them in areas where they would be invisible and politically irrelevant.

### Mechanism

- Reservations were established in remote areas with poor infrastructure, far from county seats and civic institutions
- Election administration is organized at the county level, with polling places, registration offices, and election officials located in county population centers, often 50-100+ miles from reservation communities
- State and county governments have no obligation to establish election infrastructure on reservations proportional to the population
- Road infrastructure on reservations is poor: 75% of BIA roads are unimproved, and many are impassable in winter or during heavy rain
- Postal service is unreliable on many reservations, making mail-in voting impractical

### Consequences

- Native voters face the longest average distance to polling places of any racial group in the United States
- Each additional mile to a polling place reduces reservation turnout by approximately 2 percentage points (Herron and Smith, 2016)
- Weather-related road closures on election day can effectively disenfranchise entire communities
- The cost of voting -- in time, transportation, and lost wages -- is dramatically higher for reservation residents

### Evidence

- Native American Voting Rights Coalition survey (2018): 18% of Native respondents cited distance as a significant barrier to voting
- BIA road infrastructure data documenting the poor condition of reservation transportation networks
- Turnout data showing persistent gaps between reservation and non-reservation areas within the same counties and states

## Root Cause 2: State-Controlled Election Administration Without Federal Accountability

### Description

The Constitution grants states primary authority over election administration. This creates a structural problem for Native voting rights because the same state governments that have historically sought to suppress Native political participation are responsible for administering elections.

### Mechanism

- States set voter registration procedures, ID requirements, polling place locations, early voting rules, and ballot handling processes
- County election officials have broad discretion in implementing state law, including decisions about where to place polling places and how to allocate resources
- Federal oversight mechanisms have been weakened: Section 5 preclearance was struck down in *Shelby County* (2013); Section 2 enforcement was constrained by *Brnovich* (2021)
- The Department of Justice's Voting Section has limited capacity to monitor all jurisdictions with Native voting issues
- State legislatures may enact facially neutral laws (voter ID, ballot collection bans) that have disproportionate impact on Native voters without triggering federal intervention

### Consequences

- States with histories of Native voting suppression can now implement discriminatory changes without prior federal approval
- County officials can close, relocate, or under-resource polling places on reservations without accountability
- Voter ID laws, registration procedures, and ballot handling rules are adopted without meaningful assessment of their impact on reservation communities
- Native voters must challenge each discriminatory practice individually through expensive and time-consuming Section 2 litigation

### Evidence

- Multiple states (Arizona, North Dakota, South Dakota) implemented restrictive voting changes within months of *Shelby County*
- The Native American Rights Fund has documented over 100 Section 2 cases involving Native plaintiffs since 1982, indicating persistent state-level discrimination
- Post-*Shelby County* polling place closures on reservations in Arizona, South Dakota, and Alaska

## Root Cause 3: The Address System Incompatibility

### Description

American election administration is built on a residential address system that does not function on many reservations. This is a design failure, not a Native failure -- the system was designed for a land ownership and postal delivery model that was never implemented in Indian Country.

### Mechanism

- The General Land Office survey system divided the country into numbered sections, townships, and ranges -- but excluded reservations from this system
- Standard addressing (street name, house number) depends on this survey system; reservations were not surveyed because the land was not being sold
- The postal system delivers mail based on addresses; reservation residents without addresses receive mail at P.O. boxes or general delivery
- State voter registration systems require a residential street address for registration and often for voter ID purposes
- When states implement strict address requirements (as North Dakota did), reservation residents who use P.O. boxes are effectively excluded

### Consequences

- Voter registration applications from reservation residents are rejected or flagged as incomplete
- Voter ID that shows a P.O. box rather than a residential address does not meet some state requirements
- Voter rolls that depend on residential addresses cannot accurately track reservation voters
- Address-based "voter caging" (challenging registrations based on undeliverable mail) disproportionately affects reservation residents

### Evidence

- North Dakota's voter ID law litigation (*Brakebill v. Jaeger*) centered on the residential address requirement
- Native American Voting Rights Coalition (2018): 18% of reservation respondents reported address-related registration difficulties
- Rural and tribal addressing projects remain incomplete in many reservation areas despite federal grants

## Root Cause 4: Historical Exclusion Creating Cultural Alienation

### Description

The 150-year history of formal and informal exclusion of Native Americans from the political process has created deep alienation from the electoral system. This is not apathy -- it is a rational response to a system that excluded, ignored, and suppressed Native political participation for generations.

### Mechanism

- From 1789 to 1924, most Native Americans were formally excluded from citizenship and voting
- From 1924 to the 1960s, state-level barriers continued exclusion despite formal citizenship
- Federal policy -- allotment, termination, relocation -- repeatedly demonstrated that the political system did not serve Native interests
- Historical trauma from broken treaties and federal betrayals creates distrust of government institutions including elections
- Low rates of Native candidate success reinforce the perception that the system is unresponsive
- Media coverage of elections rarely addresses Native issues, reinforcing invisibility

### Consequences

- Lower rates of voter registration and turnout, even after controlling for structural barriers
- Lower rates of political engagement (contacting elected officials, attending political events, following campaigns)
- Difficulty recruiting Native candidates for elected office
- Generational transmission of political disengagement

### Evidence

- Survey data showing that Native non-voters cite "the system doesn't work for us" and "my vote doesn't matter" at higher rates than other racial groups (NCAI, 2020)
- Historical exclusion documented in McCool, Olson, and Robinson, *Native Vote: American Indians, the Voting Rights Act, and the Right to Vote* (2007)
- Studies showing that historical exclusion reduces political participation even after formal barriers are removed (Nunn, 2014, on the long-term effects of historical trauma)

## Root Cause 5: Racially Polarized Voting and Structural Dilution

### Description

In jurisdictions with significant Native populations, voting is frequently racially polarized -- white voters and Native voters prefer different candidates. This creates a structural incentive for the white majority to adopt election systems and district maps that dilute Native voting strength.

### Mechanism

- At-large election systems allow the white majority to outvote concentrated Native communities, preventing Native candidates from winning office
- Redistricting can "crack" reservation populations across multiple districts (diluting them in each) or "pack" them into a single district (limiting their influence to one seat)
- County and municipal boundaries can be drawn to exclude reservation areas from political units where their votes would matter
- Election methods (plurality voting, runoff requirements) can disadvantage minority candidates even in majority-minority districts
- Racially polarized voting means that Native-preferred candidates cannot win without a Native majority or near-majority in the electorate

### Consequences

- Native communities have less political representation than their population would warrant
- Policy decisions affecting Native communities are made by officials who are not accountable to them
- Public resources (roads, services, polling places) are allocated away from Native areas because elected officials do not depend on Native votes
- The cycle reinforces itself: underrepresentation leads to unresponsive government, which deepens alienation, which reduces participation

### Evidence

- Over 100 Section 2 VRA cases documenting vote dilution affecting Native voters (NARF database)
- Expert testimony in *Windy Boy v. Big Horn County*, *Bone Shirt v. Hazeltine*, and *Navajo Nation v. San Juan County* documenting racially polarized voting
- Thornburg v. Gingles (1986) established the framework for proving vote dilution, with Native cases consistently meeting the three Gingles preconditions in jurisdictions with significant Native populations

## Interaction of Root Causes

```
Geographic Isolation (RC1) ──> Distance to polls, mail barriers
         │
         └──> State Control Without Accountability (RC2)
                    │
                    ├──> Address System Incompatibility (RC3)
                    │         │
                    │         └──> Registration and ID barriers
                    │
                    ├──> Historical Exclusion (RC4)
                    │         │
                    │         └──> Low participation ──> Reduced political power
                    │
                    └──> Racially Polarized Voting (RC5)
                              │
                              └──> Structural dilution ──> Underrepresentation
                                        │
                                        └──> Officials not accountable to Native voters
                                                  │
                                                  └──> Reinforces RC1 (no infrastructure investment)
                                                       and RC2 (no reform incentive)
```

The root causes form a self-reinforcing cycle. Geographic isolation makes voting harder. State control without accountability allows barriers to persist. The address system excludes reservation residents. Historical exclusion reduces engagement. Racially polarized voting enables structural dilution. And underrepresentation means the officials in power have no incentive to break the cycle.
