# Federal Recognition: Solutions

## Overview

Reforming the federal recognition process requires addressing multiple overlapping failures: the evidentiary standards that penalize colonial destruction of records, the chronic underfunding that creates decades-long delays, the political interference that undermines administrative integrity, and the legal contradictions that create two tiers of recognized tribes. The solutions presented here range from incremental administrative fixes to fundamental structural reforms.

## Solution Categories

### 1. Part 83 Process Reforms

#### 1A. Adequate Funding for OFA

**Problem**: The Office of Federal Acknowledgment has 6-8 staff reviewing ~14 active petitions, leading to 15-30+ year processing times.

**Proposal**: Increase OFA funding from ~$1.5 million to $10-15 million annually.

| Investment | Cost | Impact |
|-----------|------|--------|
| Additional research staff (15-20 FTE) | $3-5 million/year | Reduce processing time to 3-5 years |
| Technology and database systems | $1-2 million initial | Standardize evidence review |
| Expert consultants | $2-3 million/year | Anthropological and genealogical expertise |
| Administrative support | $1-2 million/year | Case management and petitioner communication |

**Evidence of effectiveness**: The 2015 reforms showed that procedural improvements alone cannot overcome staffing shortages. Every GAO report since 2001 has recommended increased funding.

#### 1B. Petitioner Funding Program

**Problem**: Compiling a Part 83 petition costs $500,000-$2,000,000+, which unrecognized tribes cannot afford.

**Proposal**: Establish a federal grant program for petitioning tribes:

- $5-10 million annual appropriation for petitioner assistance grants
- Grants cover genealogical, anthropological, and historical research
- Legal assistance through expanded NARF or similar programs
- Technical assistance office within OFA to guide petitioners

**Precedent**: The federal government provides legal aid in immigration, public defense, and other contexts where individuals face the government in proceedings affecting their fundamental rights.

#### 1C. Revised Evidentiary Standards

**Problem**: Current standards demand Western documentary evidence that colonialism destroyed.

**Proposals**:

- **Oral history recognition**: Accept oral traditions, elder testimony, and community narratives as evidence alongside documentary sources
- **Adverse inference doctrine**: Where evidence shows that records were destroyed by governmental action (e.g., Virginia's Racial Integrity Act), apply a presumption in favor of the petitioner
- **Community-based evidence**: Accept evidence of cultural continuity, language retention, ceremonial practice, and community cohesion as indicators of political authority and community existence
- **Relaxed gap analysis**: Gaps in documentation should be evaluated in light of the historical circumstances that created them, not treated as evidence of non-existence

#### 1D. Binding Timelines

**Problem**: Despite the 2015 reforms' attempt to set timelines, OFA regularly exceeds them without consequence.

**Proposal**: Establish statutory (not merely regulatory) deadlines:

- Letter of intent to completeness review: 2 years
- Completeness review to proposed finding: 3 years
- Proposed finding to final determination: 2 years
- Maximum total processing time: 7 years
- Consequence for exceeding deadlines: Automatic referral to independent commission for expedited review

### 2. Structural Reforms

#### 2A. Independent Recognition Commission

**Problem**: The recognition process is housed within the Interior Department, making it susceptible to political interference.

**Proposal**: Create an independent Federal Tribal Recognition Commission:

**Structure**:

- 7-member commission appointed by the President with Senate confirmation
- Members serve staggered 7-year terms for independence
- At least 3 members must be enrolled members of federally recognized tribes
- At least 1 member must be a scholar of federal Indian law or policy
- Bipartisan composition requirement

**Authority**:

- Sole authority to evaluate and decide Part 83 petitions
- Authority to issue subpoenas and compel production of evidence
- Authority to conduct hearings and take testimony
- Decisions appealable only to the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals

**Advantages**: Insulation from political pressure, dedicated expertise, consistent decision-making, enhanced legitimacy.

**Challenges**: Requires congressional legislation; staffing and funding; potential for appointment battles.

#### 2B. Congressional Standards Framework

**Problem**: Congressional recognition is inconsistent, applying no standardized criteria.

**Proposal**: Enact a Congressional Recognition Standards Act:

- Establish minimum evidentiary standards for congressional recognition
- Require a finding by the House Natural Resources Committee and Senate Indian Affairs Committee that criteria are met
- Prohibit recognition legislation from being attached as riders to unrelated bills
- Require GAO analysis of fiscal impact before floor votes
- Create a fast-track process for tribes with previous federal relationships (treaties, inclusion on historical rolls)

### 3. Alternative Pathways

#### 3A. Prior Federal Relationship Fast Track

**Problem**: Some unrecognized tribes had clear historical relationships with the federal government -- treaties, inclusion on BIA rolls, receipt of federal services -- that were unilaterally terminated or administratively abandoned.

**Proposal**: Create an expedited recognition pathway for tribes that can demonstrate any prior federal relationship:

- Treaty signing (1778-1871)
- Inclusion on historical BIA rolls or census schedules
- Receipt of annuities, services, or allotments
- Executive order establishment of reservations
- Congressional mention in legislation
- Department of War or Interior correspondence

**Process**: A tribe demonstrating any prior federal relationship would be presumptively eligible for recognition, with the burden shifting to the government to show why the relationship was properly terminated.

#### 3B. State Recognition Pathway

**Problem**: Over 60 state-recognized tribes have no path to federal recognition that accounts for their established status.

**Proposal**: Create a pathway from state to federal recognition:

- State recognition for 25+ years creates a rebuttable presumption of tribal existence
- Petitioners must still demonstrate descent, unique membership, and governing documents
- Community and political authority evidence is supplemented by state recognition history
- Expedited 3-year review process

#### 3C. Graduated Recognition

**Problem**: The current binary (recognized/not recognized) creates an all-or-nothing dynamic.

**Proposal**: Create intermediate recognition categories:

| Category | Rights | Pathway to Full Recognition |
|----------|--------|-----------------------------|
| Preliminary acknowledgment | Cultural protections, NAGPRA coverage, limited funding | Continue petition with reduced burden |
| Provisional recognition | IHS access, education programs, cultural funding | 5-year probationary period with full review |
| Full recognition | Complete sovereignty, gaming eligibility, land-into-trust | Final determination or successful provisional period |

### 4. Legal Reforms

#### 4A. Carcieri Legislative Fix

**Problem**: *Carcieri v. Salazar* (2009) prevents tribes recognized after 1934 from taking land into trust under the IRA.

**Proposal**: Amend 25 U.S.C. Section 5129 (formerly Section 19 of the IRA) to define "Indian" as any member of a federally recognized tribe, regardless of when recognition was obtained.

**Status**: The Carcieri fix has had bipartisan support and has been included in multiple bills but has never passed as standalone legislation due to political dynamics.

#### 4B. Judicial Review Reform

**Problem**: Current judicial review of Part 83 decisions is limited and deferential.

**Proposal**: Provide for meaningful judicial review:

- De novo review by Article III courts for petitioners denied recognition
- Standing for petitioning groups in federal court
- Availability of injunctive relief against OFA for processing delays
- Attorney fees for successful petitioners

### 5. Fraud Prevention

#### 5A. Enhanced Verification

**Problem**: Fraudulent claims undermine the process for legitimate petitioners.

**Proposal**: Strengthen anti-fraud measures without increasing burdens on legitimate tribes:

- DNA testing available (but not required) to confirm descent claims
- Cross-referencing with existing tribal enrollment databases
- Penalties for knowingly submitting fraudulent petitions
- Background checks on non-Indian parties involved in petitions (gaming developers, investors)
- Clear separation between gaming applications and recognition petitions

## Priority Recommendations

1. **Immediate**: Increase OFA funding and establish petitioner assistance grants
2. **Short-term**: Revise evidentiary standards and enact binding timelines
3. **Medium-term**: Pass Carcieri fix and establish prior federal relationship fast track
4. **Long-term**: Create independent recognition commission with comprehensive reform legislation
