# Federal Recognition: Stakeholders

## Overview

Federal recognition involves a broad range of stakeholders with varying interests, resources, and levels of influence. The stakes are high: recognition determines access to sovereignty, healthcare, education, housing, and economic development resources. Understanding who participates in and is affected by the recognition process is essential to evaluating reform proposals.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Petitioning Tribal Communities

**Interest**: Obtaining federal recognition to exercise sovereignty, access federal programs, and protect cultural heritage.

**Profile**:

- Approximately 200-400 tribal communities currently lack federal recognition
- ~14 groups have active petitions; ~50+ have filed letters of intent
- Concentrated in the Eastern United States, where colonization's documentary destruction was most severe
- Generally economically disadvantaged, with poverty rates exceeding national averages
- Many have maintained cultural identity, community cohesion, and internal governance for centuries despite lack of federal acknowledgment

**Key concerns**:

- Prohibitive cost of the petition process ($500,000-$2,000,000+)
- Processing times measured in decades
- Evidentiary standards that penalize colonial destruction of records
- Lack of technical assistance and funding
- Political interference in administrative decisions

**Influence level**: Low. Petitioners lack political power, federal access, and financial resources.

### Federally Recognized Tribes

**Interest**: Protecting the integrity of the recognition process and ensuring adequate resources for existing recognized tribes.

**Profile**:

- 574 federally recognized tribes with government-to-government relationships
- Represented by the National Congress of American Indians (NCAI) and other inter-tribal organizations
- Some tribes with gaming revenue have significant political influence
- Internally divided on recognition reform

**Key concerns**:

- Dilution of limited federal resources by expanding the number of recognized tribes
- Fraudulent claims undermining the legitimacy of tribal sovereignty
- Territorial disputes with petitioning groups claiming overlapping ancestry or territory
- Maintaining the rigor of the recognition process

**Internal divisions**:

- **Gaming tribes**: May oppose recognition of groups near their markets or support expansion of Indian Country
- **Non-gaming tribes**: Often more supportive of expanding recognition, as they share the poverty and marginalization of unrecognized communities
- **Tribes with related petitioners**: Some recognized tribes sponsor or support petitions by related groups; others actively oppose them

**Influence level**: Moderate to high. NCAI and individual tribes engage in lobbying, litigation, and political advocacy.

### Bureau of Indian Affairs / Office of Federal Acknowledgment

**Interest**: Administering the Part 83 process according to regulations and fulfilling the federal trust responsibility.

**Profile**:

- OFA is a small office within the BIA with 6-8 staff members
- Career staff conduct detailed anthropological, genealogical, and historical research
- Political leadership at the Assistant Secretary level makes final decisions

**Key concerns**:

- Chronic understaffing and underfunding
- Political pressure from Congress, the White House, and interest groups
- Maintaining credibility and consistency in decisions
- Balancing thoroughness with timeliness

**Influence level**: High in technical determinations; limited in resource allocation and political dynamics.

## Secondary Stakeholders

### State Governments

**Interest**: Regulatory authority, taxation, gaming control, and land use within their borders.

**Key concerns**:

- Loss of jurisdiction over newly recognized tribal lands
- Revenue impact of tribal gaming (which is exempt from state taxation)
- Land-into-trust removing property from state and local tax rolls
- Environmental and land use regulation authority
- Law enforcement jurisdiction changes

**Notable state actors**:

- **Connecticut**: Strongly opposed Part 83 reform due to gaming concerns
- **Virginia**: Supported congressional recognition of six tribes in 2018
- **California**: Addressed recognition issues related to numerous small tribes
- **New York**: Complex relationships with both recognized and unrecognized groups

**Influence level**: High. State governments have significant congressional influence and legal standing to challenge decisions.

### Local Governments and Communities

**Interest**: Property taxes, land use control, environmental regulation, and economic development in areas adjacent to tribal lands.

**Key concerns**:

- Land taken into trust reduces local property tax base
- Casino development impacts (traffic, infrastructure, competition with local businesses)
- Changes in law enforcement jurisdiction
- Environmental regulation gaps
- Community character and planning control

**Influence level**: Moderate. Local governments participate in the Part 83 process as third parties and lobby congressional delegations.

### Gaming Industry

**Interest**: Economic opportunities related to tribal gaming or protection of existing gaming operations.

**Key players**:

- **Commercial casino operators**: May oppose tribal gaming as unfair competition
- **Tribal gaming enterprises**: Existing tribal casinos may oppose new tribal gaming in their markets
- **Gaming developers**: Some non-Indian developers have historically supported tribal recognition petitions to partner in casino development
- **State gaming commissions**: Concerned about regulatory authority

**Influence level**: High. The gaming industry is among the most well-funded lobbying interests in Indian affairs.

### Congressional Delegations

**Interest**: Constituent concerns, political positioning, and exercise of plenary power.

**Key players**:

- **Indian Affairs Committee members**: Direct oversight of recognition policy
- **State delegations**: Particularly from states with active petitions
- **Congressional leadership**: Controls floor scheduling for recognition legislation

**Notable actions**:

- Congress has directly recognized over 40 tribes through legislation
- Members have pressured BIA to reverse or reconsider decisions
- Congressional oversight hearings have highlighted process failures
- Carcieri fix legislation has been introduced but not passed in multiple sessions

**Influence level**: Very high. Congress has plenary power and can override any administrative decision.

### Academic Researchers and Experts

**Interest**: Accurate historical and anthropological assessment of tribal claims.

**Key players**:

- Anthropologists and ethnohistorians who serve as expert witnesses
- Genealogists who compile descent documentation
- Historians specializing in federal Indian policy
- Law professors and legal scholars

**Key concerns**:

- Academic standards vs. practical justice
- The ethics of applying Western documentary standards to Indigenous communities
- Potential for hired experts to produce biased work for paying clients (petitioners or opponents)

**Influence level**: Moderate. Expert testimony is crucial in Part 83 proceedings.

### Legal Advocacy Organizations

**Interest**: Tribal rights, civil rights, and reform of the recognition process.

**Key organizations**:

| Organization | Role |
|-------------|------|
| Native American Rights Fund (NARF) | Provides legal representation to petitioning tribes |
| National Congress of American Indians (NCAI) | Inter-tribal advocacy; policy positions on recognition |
| Association on American Indian Affairs (AAIA) | Advocacy for unrecognized tribes |
| Indian Law Resource Center | International and domestic indigenous rights law |
| State-specific tribal organizations | Regional advocacy and coordination |

**Influence level**: Moderate. These organizations provide legal resources, policy analysis, and advocacy but face resource limitations.

## Affected Populations

### Members of Unrecognized Tribes

The most directly affected stakeholders are the estimated tens of thousands of members of unrecognized tribal communities:

| Impact Area | Consequence of Non-Recognition |
|-------------|-------------------------------|
| Healthcare | No access to Indian Health Service; higher rates of chronic disease |
| Education | No access to BIA education programs or tribally controlled schools |
| Housing | No access to HUD's Indian housing programs |
| Land | No trust land; no protection for sacred sites |
| Governance | No jurisdictional authority; no government-to-government relationship |
| Cultural preservation | No access to NAGPRA protections, language preservation funding |
| Economic development | No gaming authority; no tribal enterprise framework |
| Legal protection | No tribal court system; no sovereign immunity |
| Identity | Official non-existence despite centuries of cultural continuity |

### Adjacent Non-Indian Communities

Communities near tribal lands or potential tribal lands are affected by:

- Changes in property tax base if land is taken into trust
- Economic impacts (positive and negative) of tribal enterprises
- Law enforcement and jurisdictional changes
- Environmental and land use regulation changes

| Stakeholder | Interest Level | Political Power | Resources | Position on Reform |
|-------------|---------------|----------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Petitioning tribes | Very High | Low | Low | Strongly favor |

| Non-gaming recognized tribes | Medium | Medium | Low | Generally favor |
| State governments | High | High | High | Generally oppose |
| Local governments | High | Medium | Medium | Generally oppose |
