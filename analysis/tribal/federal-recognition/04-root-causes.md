# Federal Recognition: Root Causes

## Overview

The federal recognition system's failures stem from a confluence of structural, historical, legal, and political factors. Understanding why the system fails requires examining both the inherent contradictions in the concept of governmental recognition of inherent sovereignty and the specific design flaws of the Part 83 process. The root causes fall into six major categories: colonial destruction of evidence, structural design flaws, resource deprivation, political interference, legal contradictions, and gaming-driven distortions.

## Root Cause Analysis

### 1. Colonial Destruction of Documentary Evidence

**The fundamental problem**: The Part 83 process demands documentary evidence of continuous community and political authority -- the very evidence that colonialism systematically destroyed.

#### Mechanisms of Destruction

| Mechanism | Impact on Recognition | Affected Regions |
|-----------|----------------------|------------------|
| Racial reclassification (e.g., Virginia's Racial Integrity Act) | Destroyed official records of Indian identity | Virginia, Southeast |
| Forced removal and relocation | Scattered communities and disrupted governance | Eastern tribes, Oklahoma |
| Allotment (Dawes Act) | Broke up land base that evidenced community | Nationwide |
| Boarding schools | Suppressed language, culture, and governance | Nationwide |
| Census misclassification | Enumerated Indians as "colored," "mulatto," or "white" | Eastern states |
| War and disease | Reduced populations below visibility thresholds | Northeast, Southeast |
| Missionization | Replaced traditional governance with church structures | California, Southwest, Northwest |

#### The Documentation Paradox

The tribes most harmed by colonialism are least able to produce the documentation required for recognition:

- Tribes removed from ancestral lands lost local records
- Tribes subjected to racial reclassification lost vital records
- Tribes whose leaders were killed or imprisoned lost governance continuity
- Tribes scattered by disease and warfare lost community cohesion documentation
- The BIA itself failed to maintain records for many Eastern tribes it previously dealt with

### 2. Structural Design Flaws in Part 83

#### Bureaucratic Process for Sovereignty Claims

The Part 83 process locates the determination of inherent sovereignty within a regulatory framework administered by the same bureau historically responsible for termination, allotment, and forced assimilation. This creates fundamental problems:

- **Conflict of interest**: The BIA both evaluates claims and administers programs for recognized tribes that may oppose recognition
- **Adversarial design**: The process functions more like litigation than a good-faith inquiry
- **One-size-fits-all criteria**: The same criteria apply to a small New England tribe and a large Oklahoma group, despite vastly different historical circumstances
- **Binary outcome**: Recognition is all-or-nothing, with no graduated categories or alternative relationships

#### Evidentiary Standards

| Issue | Description | Consequence |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| Western academic bias | Evidence must conform to Western documentary standards | Oral histories, cultural practices, and Indigenous knowledge systems are devalued |
| Continuous demonstration | Gaps in documentation are treated as gaps in existence | Communities that survived colonialism "underground" cannot prove continuity |
| Third-party burden | Any interested party can challenge petitions with evidence | Well-funded opponents can overwhelm under-resourced petitioners |
| Expert requirements | Anthropological, genealogical, and historical expert testimony required | Cost barrier of $500,000-$2,000,000+ |

### 3. Chronic Resource Deprivation

#### Underfunded Review Process

The Office of Federal Acknowledgment operates with minimal resources:

| Resource Gap | Detail | Impact |
|-------------|--------|--------|
| OFA staff | 6-8 FTE for all petitions | 15-30+ year processing times |
| OFA budget | ~$1.5 million/year | Cannot hire adequate researchers or historians |
| Petitioner funding | No federal assistance for petitioners | Only wealthy or externally funded groups can compile adequate petitions |
| Technical assistance | Minimal guidance available | Petitioners make costly procedural errors |

#### Asymmetric Resources

The recognition process creates a resource asymmetry:

- **Petitioning tribes**: Often among the poorest communities in the country, lacking funds for genealogists, anthropologists, historians, and lawyers
- **Opponents**: May include state governments, local municipalities, existing gaming tribes, and private interests with significant legal and lobbying resources
- **OFA**: Understaffed and underfunded, leading to delays that disproportionately harm petitioners

### 4. Political Interference

#### Congressional Inconsistency

Congress has used its plenary power over Indian affairs in contradictory ways:

- Recognized tribes through legislation without applying Part 83 criteria, creating perceptions of political favoritism
- Pressured the BIA to reverse positive preliminary findings (as in the Eastern Pequot and Schaghticoke cases during the Bush administration)
- Failed to pass Carcieri fix legislation, creating two tiers of recognized tribes
- Used recognition as a political bargaining chip, tying tribal status to gaming concessions or campaign contributions

#### Executive Branch Influence

The recognition process is housed within the Department of the Interior, making it susceptible to:

- Political appointee pressure on career staff
- Shifting administration priorities on Indian policy
- Interior Department conflicts between tribal trust responsibility and other missions (resource extraction, land management)
- Lobbying by interested parties with access to political leadership

### 5. Legal Contradictions

#### Inherent Sovereignty vs. Federal Permission

The most fundamental contradiction in federal recognition law is conceptual: if tribal sovereignty is inherent -- predating the Constitution and existing independently of federal acknowledgment -- then a federal process that "grants" recognition is logically incoherent. Yet without federal recognition, tribes cannot exercise sovereignty in practice.

This contradiction manifests in several ways:

- **Recognition as creation vs. acknowledgment**: The Part 83 process frames recognition as a factual determination (does this group qualify?), but the practical effect is to create legal capacity where none existed before
- **Plenary power vs. self-determination**: Congress claims unlimited authority over Indian affairs while simultaneously promoting tribal self-determination
- **Trust responsibility vs. bureaucratic barriers**: The federal government has a trust responsibility to Indian peoples, but the recognition process prevents many Indian peoples from accessing that trust

#### Carcieri's Two-Tier System

*Carcieri v. Salazar* (2009) created a practical legal contradiction: tribes recognized after 1934 may not be able to take land into trust under the IRA, even though they are otherwise "recognized" with all other attributes of sovereignty. This means:

- A tribe can be recognized but unable to establish a reservation
- Land-into-trust, a core mechanism for tribal economic development, is unavailable to some recognized tribes
- The date of recognition becomes more important than the historical legitimacy of the claim

### 6. Gaming-Driven Distortions

#### Economic Stakes Transform the Process

The Indian Gaming Regulatory Act (1988) fundamentally changed the recognition landscape:

- **Financial incentive**: Gaming revenue potential makes recognition enormously valuable economically
- **Opponent mobilization**: States, municipalities, and competing gaming interests now vigorously oppose petitions
- **Public perception**: Media coverage frames recognition primarily as a gaming issue, obscuring sovereignty and justice concerns
- **Developer involvement**: Non-Indian gaming developers have sometimes supported or even initiated tribal petitions, raising legitimacy concerns
- **Political pressure**: State and local officials lobby Congress and the Interior Department to oppose recognition based on gaming fears

#### The Fraud Problem

Gaming economics have attracted fraudulent claims:

- Self-created "tribes" with fabricated histories have filed petitions
- "Reservation shopping" -- where gaming developers seek to pair a tribal recognition claim with a specific gaming location -- has occurred
- These fraudulent claims taint the process for legitimate petitioners
- The response to fraud has been to raise evidentiary barriers, which disproportionately harms legitimate tribes with destroyed records

## Causal Chain

```text
Colonial violence destroys records
        ↓
Tribes lack documentary evidence
        ↓
Part 83 demands documentary evidence → Legitimate tribes cannot meet criteria
        ↓
Process takes decades, costs millions → Only well-funded groups can participate
        ↓
Gaming creates financial stakes → Opposition intensifies, fraud emerges
        ↓
Political interference distorts outcomes → Trust in process erodes
        ↓
Unrecognized tribes remain in legal limbo → Trust responsibility unfulfilled
```

## Systemic Reinforcement

These root causes are not independent but mutually reinforcing:

- Colonial destruction of records makes the process longer and more expensive
- The longer and more expensive the process, the more political it becomes
- The more political it becomes, the more susceptible it is to gaming-driven distortions
- Gaming distortions increase pressure for rigorous standards
- Rigorous standards further disadvantage tribes with destroyed records
- The cycle perpetuates itself
