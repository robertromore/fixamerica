# Federal Recognition: Current State

## The Part 83 Administrative Process

### How It Works Today

The Bureau of Indian Affairs' Office of Federal Acknowledgment (OFA) administers the federal recognition process under 25 C.F.R. Part 83, as revised in 2015. The process requires petitioning groups to demonstrate that they meet seven mandatory criteria:

| Criterion | Requirement | Evidence Expected |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| (a) Identification | Identified as an American Indian entity since 1900 | Newspaper articles, government records, scholarly references |
| (b) Community | Distinct community from historical times to present | Residence patterns, social interaction, shared activities |
| (c) Political authority | Political influence or authority over members from historical times to present | Governance records, leadership documentation, meeting minutes |
| (d) Governing document | Governing document or statement of criteria for membership | Constitution, bylaws, enrollment ordinance |
| (e) Descent | Members descend from a historical Indian tribe | Genealogical records, census data, allotment rolls |
| (f) Unique membership | Membership composed principally of persons not enrolled in other recognized tribes | Membership rolls cross-checked against other tribes |
| (g) Not terminated | Not subject of congressional legislation terminating the relationship | Legislative history |

### 2015 Reforms

The Obama administration's 2015 Part 83 revision made several significant changes:

- **Reduced the starting date** for demonstrating community and political authority from "historical times" to 1900, reducing the evidentiary burden
- **Allowed previous federal acknowledgment** (such as treaty relationships or inclusion on federal rolls) to satisfy criteria (a) through (c) and (e)
- **Created phased review** with an initial review period before proceeding to a proposed finding
- **Set time limits** for each phase of review (though these are frequently exceeded)
- **Allowed third-party comments** but limited their scope to evidence-based objections

## Congressional Recognition

### Current Landscape

Congress retains plenary power to recognize tribes directly through legislation, bypassing the Part 83 process entirely. This pathway has been used over 40 times, most recently for Virginia tribes in 2018.

| Legislation | Tribes | Year |
|-------------|--------|------|
| Thomasina E. Jordan Indian Tribes of Virginia Federal Recognition Act | 6 Virginia tribes | 2018 |
| Little Shell Tribe of Chippewa Indians Restoration Act | Little Shell Tribe (Montana) | 2019 |

Congressional recognition is controversial because it lacks standardized criteria, is subject to political dynamics including lobbying by gaming interests, and may be influenced by factors unrelated to the merits of a tribe's claim. However, it can resolve cases where the Part 83 process has failed legitimate tribes -- as with the Virginia tribes that had clear historical continuity but whose records were destroyed by Walter Plecker's Racial Integrity Act of 1924.

### Pending Legislation (119th Congress)

Several recognition-related bills have been introduced or reintroduced:

- Bills to recognize specific tribes that have had difficulty navigating Part 83
- Proposals to reform the Part 83 process legislatively
- Measures addressing the status of state-recognized tribes

## State Recognition

### Current Status

Over 60 tribes hold state recognition without federal recognition, concentrated in states along the Eastern Seaboard where colonization's impact on tribal records has been most severe.

### Limitations of State Recognition

State recognition provides:

- Some state-level protections and symbolic acknowledgment
- Limited state funding in some cases
- No access to IHS, BIA programs, or other federal tribal services
- No trust land or sovereignty protections
- No government-to-government relationship with the federal government
- No ability to conduct gaming under IGRA

## Key Challenges in the Current System

### Evidentiary Burden

The Part 83 process requires extensive documentation spanning decades or centuries. For many Eastern tribes, this evidence was systematically destroyed:

- **Virginia's Racial Integrity Act (1924)**: Walter Plecker, the state registrar of vital statistics, reclassified all non-white Virginians as "colored," destroying the documentary evidence of Indian identity
- **Termination Era records destruction**: Some tribes lost records during the federal termination period
- **Colonial-era disruptions**: Forced relocations, missionization, and displacement scattered communities and destroyed governance records
- **Deliberate erasure**: Census enumerators frequently classified Indians as "mulatto," "colored," or "free persons of color," erasing tribal identity from official records

### Cost and Capacity

The financial burden of compiling a Part 83 petition is staggering:

| Cost Category | Estimated Range |
|---------------|----------------|
| Genealogical research | $100,000-$500,000 |
| Anthropological documentation | $100,000-$300,000 |
| Historical research | $100,000-$300,000 |
| Legal representation | $200,000-$1,000,000+ |
| Total petition cost | $500,000-$2,000,000+ |

Most unrecognized tribes are economically disadvantaged communities that cannot afford these costs without external assistance.

### Processing Delays

Despite the 2015 reforms' attempt to impose timelines, the OFA remains significantly backlogged:

- Staff levels have rarely exceeded 5-8 full-time employees
- Each petition requires thousands of hours of review
- Third-party challenges can restart review timelines
- Political interference has reversed tentative decisions (as with the Eastern Pequot and Schaghticoke cases during the Bush administration)

### Gaming-Related Complications

The Indian Gaming Regulatory Act of 1988 (IGRA) transformed federal recognition from a primarily cultural and legal matter into a multi-billion-dollar economic question:

- Opponents of recognition frequently cite gaming concerns as grounds for objection
- The "Carcieri fix" debate (addressing land-into-trust authority for tribes recognized after 1934) intersects with gaming
- Some petitions face opposition from existing gaming tribes concerned about competition
- Allegations of "reservation shopping" by gaming developers complicate legitimate claims

The OFA's chronic underfunding is itself a significant barrier to recognition. The Government Accountability Office has repeatedly recommended increased staffing and funding (GAO-2012-348; GAO-2001-550).

## Recent Developments (2024-2025)

- The Supreme Court's continued engagement with tribal jurisdiction issues in cases such as *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023) has reinforced the political classification of Indian status, with implications for recognition law
- The Biden administration emphasized tribal consultation and self-determination but did not pursue fundamental Part 83 reform
- Several state-recognized tribes continue exploring both administrative and legislative recognition pathways
- Academic and advocacy organizations have increasingly called for recognition process reform

## Comparative Context

Other nations handle indigenous recognition differently:

| Country | Approach | Notes |
|---------|----------|-------|
| Canada | Section 35 of the Constitution Act, 1982 recognizes "Indian, Inuit and Metis peoples" | Less formal recognition process; treaty negotiation ongoing |
| Australia | No formal recognition process; Native Title Act 1993 addresses land rights | Constitutional recognition debate ongoing |
| New Zealand | Treaty of Waitangi framework | Tribunal process for claims; no recognition question |
| Norway | Sami Parliament established 1989 | Self-identification for Sami electoral roll |
