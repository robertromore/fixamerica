# Federal Recognition: History

## Pre-Constitutional Era (Pre-1789)

### Colonial Treaties and Relationships

Before the founding of the United States, European colonial powers and subsequently the colonies themselves conducted diplomacy with Indian tribes as sovereign nations. This established the precedent of government-to-government relationships:

- **British Crown treaties**: Extensive treaty-making with tribes as independent powers, particularly through the Royal Proclamation of 1763
- **Colonial compacts**: Individual colonies negotiated agreements with local tribes for land, trade, and military alliance
- **Continental Congress**: Adopted the British treaty model; the first Indian treaty under the new government was with the Delaware Nation in 1778

The concept of "recognition" was not yet formalized -- tribes were simply dealt with as they existed, as self-evident political entities.

## Early Republic Period (1789-1871)

### Constitutional Framework

The Constitution established three references to Indian tribes:

- **Article I, Section 8, Clause 3** (Commerce Clause): Congress may regulate commerce "with the Indian Tribes"
- **Article I, Section 2** and **Fourteenth Amendment, Section 2**: Indians "not taxed" excluded from apportionment
- **Treaty Clause**: Treaties with tribes (like treaties with foreign nations) are "the supreme law of the land"

### Marshall Trilogy (1823-1832)

Chief Justice John Marshall's three landmark decisions established the legal framework still governing tribal recognition:

| Case | Year | Holding |
|------|------|---------|
| *Johnson v. M'Intosh* | 1823 | Discovery doctrine; tribes retained occupancy rights but not full title |
| *Cherokee Nation v. Georgia* | 1831 | Tribes are "domestic dependent nations," not foreign states |
| *Worcester v. Georgia* | 1832 | State laws have no force within tribal territory; federal-tribal relationship is exclusive |

### Treaty Era (1778-1871)

Between 1778 and 1871, the United States ratified 374 treaties with Indian tribes. Treaty-making itself served as de facto recognition -- signing a treaty acknowledged a tribe's sovereign status.

Key aspects:

- Treaties typically involved land cessions in exchange for reserved lands, annuities, and services
- The treaty relationship established the federal trust responsibility
- Congress ended treaty-making with the Indian Appropriations Act of 1871, though existing treaties remained in force
- Some tribes that signed treaties later lost federal recognition despite these binding agreements

## Allotment and Assimilation Era (1871-1934)

### The Dawes Act (1887)

The General Allotment Act fundamentally altered the recognition landscape:

- Broke up communal tribal lands into individual allotments
- "Surplus" lands were opened to non-Indian settlement
- Tribal land base shrank from 138 million acres to 48 million acres
- Individual allottees were made U.S. citizens, weakening tribal political structures
- Many tribes lost the land base and community cohesion that would later be required for recognition

### Administrative Dissolution

During this period, the federal government unilaterally ceased dealing with numerous tribes:

- Small tribes in the East were considered "extinct" or "absorbed"
- Federal agents stopped maintaining rolls for many communities
- Tribes that had been removed from ancestral lands lost contact with federal agencies
- No formal process existed to determine tribal status

### Curtis Act (1898)

The Curtis Act dissolved tribal governments in Indian Territory (Oklahoma), further eroding the political structures that would later be demanded as evidence of continuous tribal existence.

## Indian Reorganization Era (1934-1953)

### Indian Reorganization Act (1934)

The IRA, also known as the Wheeler-Howard Act, reversed allotment policy and encouraged tribal self-governance:

- Ended allotment of tribal lands
- Provided for tribal constitutions and corporate charters
- Enabled land purchases to restore tribal land bases
- Defined "Indian" for purposes of the Act, creating the first statutory framework for tribal identity

The IRA created a fundamental distinction: tribes that reorganized under the IRA gained a clear federal relationship, while others were left in ambiguous status.

### Tribes Left Out

Not all tribes benefited from the IRA:

- Some tribes voted against reorganization
- Many Eastern tribes were not contacted or included
- The BIA's limited resources meant outreach was uneven
- Some tribes had already been administratively abandoned

## Termination Era (1953-1968)

### Termination Policy

House Concurrent Resolution 108 (1953) declared the federal goal of ending the special relationship with tribes "as rapidly as possible." Over 100 tribes were terminated:

| Category | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| Tribes formally terminated | 109 tribes and bands |
| Individuals affected | ~13,000 tribal members |
| Land removed from trust | ~2.5 million acres |
| States affected | 12 states |
| Major terminations | Menominee (Wisconsin), Klamath (Oregon), 41 Western Oregon tribes |

### Public Law 280 (1953)

Concurrent with termination, P.L. 280 transferred criminal and some civil jurisdiction over Indian Country to six states, further eroding the federal-tribal relationship:

- Mandatory states: California, Minnesota, Nebraska, Oregon, Wisconsin, Alaska
- Some tribes in these states lost functional federal recognition despite not being formally terminated

### Impact on Future Recognition

The Termination Era created distinct categories of tribes:

- **Terminated tribes**: Formally lost federal recognition through specific legislation
- **Administratively abandoned tribes**: Never formally terminated but ceased to have active federal relationships
- **State-recognized tribes**: Maintained state relationships but lacked federal recognition
- **Unrecognized communities**: Continued to exist as cohesive communities without any governmental recognition

## Self-Determination Era (1968-Present)

### Policy Reversal

President Nixon's 1970 Special Message to Congress on Indian Affairs explicitly repudiated termination:

> "The time has come to break decisively with the past and to create the conditions for a new era in which the Indian future is determined by Indian acts and Indian decisions."

### Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act (1975)

P.L. 93-638 established the framework for tribes to contract with the federal government to run their own programs, but only federally recognized tribes could participate.

### Restoration of Terminated Tribes

Beginning in the 1970s, Congress restored several terminated tribes:

| Tribe | State | Terminated | Restored |
|-------|-------|-----------|----------|
| Menominee | Wisconsin | 1961 | 1973 |
| Siletz | Oregon | 1956 | 1977 |
| Coquille | Oregon | 1956 | 1989 |
| Grand Ronde | Oregon | 1956 | 1983 |
| Klamath | Oregon | 1961 | 1986 |
| Wyandotte | Oklahoma | 1956 | 1978 |

Approximately 35 terminated tribes have been restored through congressional action.

## Creation of the Part 83 Process (1978)

### Why It Was Created

Before 1978, there was no formal administrative process for tribes to seek federal recognition. Groups either had recognition (based on historical treaty relationships, IRA organization, or executive orders) or they did not. The Department of the Interior created the Part 83 process through regulation to address the growing number of petitions from groups claiming tribal status.

### Original 25 C.F.R. Part 83 (1978)

The original regulations established seven criteria similar to the current framework but with important differences:

- Required demonstration of community and political authority from "historical times" (pre-contact or first sustained European contact) to the present
- No time limits on review
- Limited procedural protections for petitioners
- No funding assistance for petitioners

### Early Controversies

The process immediately faced criticism:

- **Mashpee Wampanoag**: Filed in 1990, received final positive determination in 2007 -- a 17-year process
- **Eastern tribes**: Groups with centuries of documented existence were told they lacked sufficient evidence of "continuous" community
- **Connecticut gaming disputes**: The Mashantucket Pequot recognition (congressional, 1983) and subsequent gaming success intensified scrutiny of all New England petitions

## Part 83 Reforms

### 2015 Revision

The Obama administration conducted the first major revision of Part 83 since 1978:

- **Changed the starting date** for demonstrating community and political authority from "historical times" to 1900
- **Previous federal acknowledgment**: Tribes that could show a prior federal relationship (treaties, inclusion on rolls, etc.) could satisfy certain criteria automatically
- **Time limits**: Established review phases with target timelines
- **Third-party comments**: Maintained public comment but limited scope
- **Guidance documents**: Published enhanced guidance on evidence types

### Criticism of Reforms

- **Tribes opposed to change**: Some recognized tribes, particularly in Connecticut, argued the revisions weakened standards and would lead to fraudulent recognitions
- **Insufficient reform**: Petitioning tribes argued the changes did not go far enough, particularly regarding funding and evidentiary burdens
- **Gaming concerns**: State and local governments worried about the land-into-trust and gaming implications of expanded recognition

## Key Legal Developments

### Landmark Cases

| Case | Year | Significance |
|------|------|-------------|
| *Montoya v. United States* | 1901 | Defined "tribe" as "a body of Indians of the same or a similar race, united in a community under one leadership or government" |
| *Carcieri v. Salazar* | 2009 | Limited land-into-trust authority to tribes "under federal jurisdiction" in 1934, complicating recognition for subsequently recognized tribes |
| *Haaland v. Brackeen* | 2023 | Upheld ICWA; affirmed that Indian classification is political, not racial, reinforcing the legal basis for recognition |
| *McGirt v. Oklahoma* | 2020 | Affirmed that tribal reservations established by treaty are not disestablished absent clear congressional intent |

### Carcieri Decision Impact

*Carcieri v. Salazar*, 555 U.S. 379 (2009), created particular problems for federal recognition:

- The Supreme Court held that the IRA's land-into-trust authority applied only to tribes "under federal jurisdiction" as of June 18, 1934
- Tribes recognized after 1934 face challenges taking land into trust -- a core benefit of recognition
- The "Carcieri fix" has been proposed repeatedly in Congress but has not passed
- This decision effectively created two classes of recognized tribes: those with clear pre-1934 federal relationships and those without

| Period | Era | Key Development |
|--------|-----|-----------------|
| Pre-1789 | Colonial | Treaties as de facto recognition |
| 1789-1871 | Treaty Era | 374 treaties ratified; Marshall Trilogy |
