# Federal Recognition: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to federal recognition reform -- and to individual tribal petitions -- comes from a diverse coalition of interests that often have conflicting motivations. Understanding these opposition dynamics is essential because they have shaped the recognition process itself, transforming it from a fact-finding exercise into a quasi-adversarial proceeding. The opposition landscape includes state and local governments, commercial and tribal gaming interests, some already-recognized tribes, taxpayer groups, and ideological opponents of expanded tribal sovereignty.

## Major Sources of Opposition

### State and Local Governments

**Core argument**: Federal recognition removes land from state jurisdiction, reduces local tax revenue, and creates regulatory gaps.

**Specific objections**:

- **Tax base erosion**: When land is taken into trust, it is removed from state and local property tax rolls. Even small parcels can represent significant revenue losses in rural communities
- **Jurisdictional complexity**: Recognition creates "checkerboard" jurisdictions where tribal, state, and federal authority overlap unpredictably
- **Environmental regulation**: Tribes may not be subject to state environmental standards, creating potential regulatory gaps
- **Law enforcement**: Criminal jurisdiction questions, particularly involving non-Indians on tribal land, create public safety concerns
- **Land use and zoning**: Trust land is exempt from local zoning and land use regulations

**Key actors**:

- Connecticut's congressional delegation historically led opposition to Part 83 reform, driven by gaming concerns
- State attorneys general in states with active petitions
- National Governors Association has weighed in on gaming and land-into-trust issues
- Municipal governments near proposed tribal lands

**Counterargument**: Federal recognition fulfills treaty obligations and trust responsibilities that predate state constitutions. Tribes also provide services and economic activity that benefit surrounding communities.

### Commercial Gaming Industry

**Core argument**: Tribal gaming operates with an unfair competitive advantage due to tax exemptions and reduced regulation.

**Specific objections**:

- Tribal casinos are not subject to state gaming taxes
- Tribal-state compacts may allow games or conditions not available to commercial operators
- New tribal recognition could authorize casinos in previously non-gaming markets
- "Reservation shopping" allegations, where gaming developers seek recognition for tribal groups near lucrative markets

**Key actors**:

- Major commercial casino operators and their trade associations
- State gaming commissions
- Horse racing and pari-mutuel industries
- Local businesses that view casinos as competition

**Financial influence**: The commercial gaming industry spent over $100 million in federal lobbying between 2000 and 2020, a significant portion directed at Indian gaming issues (Center for Responsive Politics).

**Counterargument**: Tribal gaming is an exercise of sovereignty, not a special privilege. Gaming revenue funds tribal government services that the federal government has chronically underfunded. The vast majority of tribal recognition petitions have nothing to do with gaming.

### Existing Recognized Tribes

**Core argument**: Expanding recognition dilutes limited federal resources and risks fraudulent claims.

**Specific objections**:

- **Resource competition**: Federal funding for Indian programs is already inadequate; adding recognized tribes spreads resources thinner
- **Territorial disputes**: Some petitioning groups claim ancestry or territory overlapping with existing tribes
- **Gaming competition**: Recognized gaming tribes may oppose new recognition that could create competing casinos
- **Integrity concerns**: Legitimate tribes worry that fraudulent recognitions undermine the credibility of tribal sovereignty
- **Enrollment disputes**: Questions about whether petitioning group members should properly be enrolled in existing tribes

**Notable examples**:

- The Mashantucket Pequot and Mohegan tribes in Connecticut have opposed recognition of additional Connecticut groups
- Some Oklahoma tribes have opposed recognition of splinter groups claiming shared ancestry
- Inter-tribal boundary and enrollment disputes complicate specific petitions

**Counterargument**: The fear of resource dilution should be addressed through increased federal funding, not by denying recognition to legitimate tribes. Territorial and enrollment disputes can be resolved through negotiation and established legal mechanisms.

### Ideological Opposition

#### Fiscal Conservatives

**Argument**: Recognition creates new federal spending obligations through BIA, IHS, HUD, and other programs. Expanding the number of recognized tribes increases the federal government's financial liabilities.

**Counterargument**: The trust responsibility exists regardless of recognition status. Denying recognition does not eliminate the obligation; it simply shifts costs to emergency rooms, state welfare programs, and law enforcement.

#### Anti-Sovereignty Movement

**Argument**: A small but vocal movement opposes the concept of tribal sovereignty itself, arguing that separate tribal governments within the United States are anachronistic, racially divisive, or contrary to equal citizenship.

**Key actors**:

- Citizens Equal Rights Alliance (CERA)
- Some academic critics of the "sovereignty paradigm"
- Property rights organizations concerned about land-into-trust

**Counterargument**: Tribal sovereignty is constitutionally recognized, treaty-based, and affirmed by centuries of law. The Supreme Court has consistently held that Indian classifications are political, not racial (*Morton v. Mancari*, 1974; *Haaland v. Brackeen*, 2023).

#### States' Rights Advocates

**Argument**: Federal recognition preempts state authority and creates federal enclaves within state borders without state consent.

**Counterargument**: The Commerce Clause and centuries of federal Indian law establish that the federal-tribal relationship is exclusive, predating state constitutions.

### Neighboring Property Owners

**Core argument**: Casino development, land-into-trust, and jurisdictional changes harm property values and community character.

**Specific objections**:

- Traffic, noise, and environmental impacts from casino development
- Reduced property values near casino sites
- Loss of community control over land use
- Fear of increased crime or social problems

**Counterargument**: Economic studies show mixed impacts; many tribal enterprises increase property values and economic activity. Tribes are subject to federal environmental review and often negotiate impact agreements with local communities.

## Opposition Strategies

### Legal Challenges

| Strategy | Description | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| Third-party comments | File voluminous evidence challenging petitions during public comment periods | Connecticut towns challenging Schaghticoke petition |
| Litigation | Sue to overturn positive recognition decisions | States challenging land-into-trust decisions |
| Carcieri challenges | Argue that newly recognized tribes cannot take land into trust | Multiple post-2009 challenges |
| Standing challenges | Argue that petitioning groups lack standing as tribes | Various Part 83 proceedings |

### Political Strategies

| Strategy | Description | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| Congressional pressure | Lobby Congress to intervene in administrative decisions | Bush-era intervention in Connecticut cases |
| Appropriations riders | Attach provisions blocking specific recognitions to spending bills | Attempted riders regarding gaming and recognition |
| Lobbying | Direct lobbying of Interior Department and White House | Gaming industry lobbying against Part 83 reform |
| State legislation | Pass state laws opposing or complicating tribal recognition | Various state ballot measures targeting tribal rights |

### Public Relations Strategies

| Strategy | Description | Effect |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| "Casino tribe" framing | Characterize all recognition petitions as gaming schemes | Delegitimizes sovereignty and justice claims |
| Fraud allegations | Highlight fraudulent claims to discredit all petitioners | Raises evidentiary standards for legitimate tribes |
| Tax impact messaging | Emphasize lost property tax revenue | Mobilizes local opposition |
| Equal rights framing | Argue tribal sovereignty creates racial inequality | Reframes sovereignty as discrimination |

## Effectiveness of Opposition

Opposition to recognition reform has been highly effective:

- The Part 83 process has been modified to increase, rather than decrease, scrutiny over time
- The 2015 reforms were modest compared to what advocates sought, in part due to opposition pressure
- Congressional recognition remains ad hoc and politically dependent
- The Carcieri fix has failed in multiple sessions despite bipartisan support
- Gaming-related opposition has blocked or delayed numerous petitions
- Political interference reversed positive preliminary findings in at least two cases

## Legitimate Concerns vs. Bad-Faith Opposition

It is important to distinguish between legitimate concerns that should be addressed in reform and opposition that is fundamentally self-interested:

### Legitimate Concerns

- Preventing fraudulent claims that undermine tribal sovereignty
- Ensuring adequate funding for expanded obligations
- Addressing genuine jurisdictional and public safety issues
- Maintaining the integrity of the recognition process

### Self-Interested Opposition

- Blocking recognition solely to prevent gaming competition
- Using fraud allegations to delegitimize all petitioners
- Opposing recognition to maintain state revenue at the expense of federal obligations
- Leveraging political power to override factual determinations
