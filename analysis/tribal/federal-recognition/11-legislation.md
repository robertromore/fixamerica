# Federal Recognition: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative landscape for federal recognition reform encompasses three main areas: (1) reforming the administrative Part 83 process through statutory requirements, (2) fixing the Carcieri land-into-trust problem, and (3) creating alternative recognition pathways. This document presents draft legislation for each area along with analysis of potential challenges and refinements.

## Constitutional Amendments

No constitutional amendment is required for federal recognition reform. Congress already possesses plenary power over Indian affairs under the Commerce Clause (Article I, Section 8, Clause 3) and has ample authority to reform the recognition process through ordinary legislation. However, a constitutional amendment could provide stronger protections for tribal sovereignty against future congressional erosion.

### Tribal Sovereignty Protection Amendment (Optional)

**Purpose**: Constitutionalize the federal trust responsibility and limit Congress's ability to unilaterally terminate tribal recognition.

**Draft Text**:

```text
Section 1. The inherent sovereignty of Indian tribes recognized by
the United States shall not be diminished or terminated except by
mutual consent of the affected tribe, expressed through a
referendum of its adult members.

Section 2. The United States shall honor all treaty obligations
to Indian tribes and shall provide adequate funding for the
fulfillment of the federal trust responsibility.

Section 3. Congress shall have the power to enforce this article
by appropriate legislation.

Section 4. This article shall take effect two years after the
date of ratification.
```

**Explanation**: Section 1 prevents unilateral termination, which historically devastated over 100 tribes. Section 2 elevates the trust responsibility from a judicially recognized doctrine to a constitutional command.

**Potential Challenges**: Ratification requires supermajority support; states may resist limitations on state jurisdiction; defining "adequate funding" is subjective.

**Refinements**: Include a sunset clause requiring congressional reauthorization; define "adequate funding" by reference to demonstrated need assessments.

## Federal Legislation

### Bill 1: Federal Tribal Recognition Reform Act

**Purpose**: Overhaul the Part 83 process with binding timelines, revised evidentiary standards, petitioner funding, and enhanced anti-fraud provisions.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Federal Tribal Recognition Reform
Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) the federal recognition process under part 83 of title 25,
Code of Federal Regulations, has failed to provide timely and just
determinations for petitioning tribal communities;
(b) processing times averaging 15 to 30 years deny petitioning
communities access to healthcare, education, and other federal
services;
(c) evidentiary standards that demand documentation destroyed by
colonialism systematically disadvantage legitimate tribal
communities;
(d) the Office of Federal Acknowledgment lacks adequate staffing
and funding to fulfill its responsibilities; and
(e) reform is necessary to fulfill the federal trust responsibility.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
(a) COMMISSION.--The term "Commission" means the Federal Tribal
Recognition Commission established under section 5.
(b) PETITIONER.--The term "petitioner" means any group that has
submitted or intends to submit a petition for federal acknowledgment.
(c) PRIOR FEDERAL RELATIONSHIP.--The term "prior federal
relationship" means any documented interaction between a petitioning
group and the federal government evidencing recognition of the group
as an Indian tribe, including treaty signing, inclusion on federal
rolls, receipt of annuities or services, or correspondence from
federal officials acknowledging tribal status.

SEC. 4. REVISED EVIDENTIARY STANDARDS.

(a) ORAL HISTORY.--Oral histories, elder testimony, and
community narratives shall be accepted as evidence alongside
documentary sources in all proceedings under this Act.
(b) ADVERSE INFERENCE.--Where evidence demonstrates that
governmental action destroyed or suppressed records relevant to a
petition, the Commission shall apply a presumption favorable to
the petitioner regarding the facts those records would have
established.
(c) COMMUNITY-BASED EVIDENCE.--Evidence of cultural continuity,
language retention, ceremonial practice, and community cohesion
shall be accepted as indicators of political authority and
community existence.
(d) GAP ANALYSIS.--Gaps in documentation shall be evaluated in
light of the historical circumstances that created them and shall
not be treated as evidence of non-existence.

SEC. 5. FEDERAL TRIBAL RECOGNITION COMMISSION.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--There is established an independent commission
to be known as the Federal Tribal Recognition Commission.
(b) MEMBERSHIP.--
  (1) The Commission shall consist of 7 members appointed by the
  President, with the advice and consent of the Senate.
  (2) At least 3 members shall be enrolled members of federally
  recognized Indian tribes.
  (3) At least 1 member shall be a scholar of federal Indian law.
  (4) No more than 4 members shall belong to the same political
  party.
(c) TERMS.--Members shall serve staggered terms of 7 years.
(d) AUTHORITY.--The Commission shall have sole authority to
evaluate and decide petitions for federal acknowledgment, including
the power to issue subpoenas and compel production of evidence.
(e) DECISIONS.--Decisions of the Commission shall be final agency
action, subject to judicial review in the United States Court of
Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit.

SEC. 6. BINDING TIMELINES.

(a) COMPLETENESS REVIEW.--The Commission shall complete a
determination of petition completeness within 2 years of receipt.
(b) PROPOSED FINDING.--The Commission shall issue a proposed
finding within 3 years of the completeness determination.
(c) FINAL DETERMINATION.--The Commission shall issue a final
determination within 2 years of the proposed finding.
(d) MAXIMUM PROCESSING TIME.--No petition shall remain pending for
more than 7 years from the date of the completeness determination.
(e) CONSEQUENCE.--If the Commission fails to meet any deadline
under this section, the petition shall be referred to an expedited
review panel of 3 commissioners for decision within 180 days.

SEC. 7. PETITIONER ASSISTANCE.

(a) GRANT PROGRAM.--The Commission shall establish a grant program
to assist petitioning groups with the costs of compiling evidence
for petitions.
(b) AUTHORIZATION.--There is authorized to be appropriated
$10,000,000 for each fiscal year for grants under this section.
(c) TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE.--The Commission shall maintain a
technical assistance office to provide guidance to petitioners on
evidentiary requirements and petition preparation.

SEC. 8. PRIOR FEDERAL RELATIONSHIP FAST TRACK.

(a) EXPEDITED PROCESS.--A petitioner demonstrating a prior federal
relationship as defined in section 3(c) shall be eligible for an
expedited review process.
(b) PRESUMPTION.--A prior federal relationship shall create a
rebuttable presumption that the petitioner satisfies criteria
relating to identification, community, and political authority.
(c) TIMELINE.--Expedited petitions shall be resolved within 3 years.

SEC. 9. ANTI-FRAUD PROVISIONS.

(a) PENALTIES.--Any person who knowingly submits fraudulent evidence
in connection with a petition shall be subject to a civil penalty
of not more than $250,000 and criminal penalties under 18 U.S.C.
section 1001.
(b) BACKGROUND CHECKS.--The Commission shall conduct background
checks on any non-Indian parties providing financial support to
petitioners.
(c) GAMING SEPARATION.--No petition shall be considered in
conjunction with any gaming application or proposal.

SEC. 10. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There is authorized to be appropriated $25,000,000 for each fiscal
year for the operations of the Commission, petitioner assistance,
and implementation of this Act.

SEC. 11. TRANSITION.

(a) PENDING PETITIONS.--All petitions pending before the Office of
Federal Acknowledgment on the date of enactment shall be transferred
to the Commission with all evidence preserved.
(b) NO LOSS OF PROGRESS.--No petitioner shall be required to
re-submit evidence previously submitted to the Office of Federal
Acknowledgment.
(c) TIMELINE.--The transition shall be completed within 1 year
of the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This comprehensive bill addresses the core failures of the current system: understaffing (Section 5), evidentiary bias (Section 4), processing delays (Section 6), petitioner costs (Section 7), and political interference (Section 5's independence provisions). The prior federal relationship fast track (Section 8) addresses the most clear-cut cases first.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Appropriations may not match authorization levels
- Senate confirmation of commissioners could become politicized
- Gaming industry lobbying against the bill
- Some recognized tribes may oppose expanded recognition

**Refinements**:

- Add a mandatory appropriations provision to prevent underfunding
- Include a gaming impact study requirement to address opposition concerns
- Establish an advisory committee of recognized tribal leaders

### Bill 2: Carcieri Fix Act

**Purpose**: Restore the Secretary of the Interior's authority to take land into trust for all federally recognized tribes, regardless of when recognition was obtained.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Land-into-Trust Parity Act".

SEC. 2. AMENDMENT TO THE INDIAN REORGANIZATION ACT.

Section 19 of the Act of June 18, 1934 (25 U.S.C. 5129), is
amended by striking "The term 'Indian' as used in this Act shall
include all persons of Indian descent who are members of any
recognized Indian tribe now under Federal jurisdiction" and
inserting "The term 'Indian' as used in this Act shall include all
persons of Indian descent who are members of any federally
recognized Indian tribe".

SEC. 3. RATIFICATION.

Any land taken into trust by the Secretary of the Interior pursuant
to the Act of June 18, 1934, before the date of enactment of this
Act for the benefit of an Indian tribe that was federally recognized
on the date such land was taken into trust is hereby ratified and
confirmed.

SEC. 4. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This two-page bill resolves the Carcieri problem by simply removing the temporal limitation in the IRA's definition of "Indian." Section 3 ratifies past land-into-trust actions that may have been legally questionable under Carcieri.

**Potential Challenges**: State and local opposition to expanded land-into-trust; gaming-related concerns; potential for land-into-trust abuse.

**Refinements**: Add consultation requirements for affected state and local governments; include environmental review requirements.

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Tribal Recognition Act

**Purpose**: Standardize state recognition processes and create a meaningful relationship between state and federal recognition.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Indian Tribe Recognition Act".

SECTION 2. RECOGNITION CRITERIA.

A group seeking state recognition as an Indian tribe shall
demonstrate:
(a) Continuous existence as a distinct community within [State]
since 1900;
(b) Descent from a historically documented Indian tribe;
(c) Maintenance of political influence or authority over its
members;
(d) A governing document describing membership criteria and
governance structure; and
(e) Members who are not primarily enrolled in a federally
recognized tribe.

SECTION 3. STATE TRIBAL AFFAIRS COMMISSION.

(a) There is established a [State] Commission on Indian Affairs
to evaluate petitions under this Act.
(b) The Commission shall include at least 3 members who are
enrolled in state- or federally recognized tribes within [State].

SECTION 4. BENEFITS OF STATE RECOGNITION.

State-recognized tribes shall be eligible for:
(a) State cultural preservation grants;
(b) State education programs for Native students;
(c) Consultation rights on state actions affecting tribal interests;
(d) Technical assistance for pursuing federal recognition.

SECTION 5. RELATIONSHIP TO FEDERAL RECOGNITION.

State recognition under this Act shall not confer any federal
benefits, gaming rights, or sovereignty status available only to
federally recognized tribes.
```

**Explanation**: This model legislation creates consistent state recognition standards that can support federal recognition efforts while clearly distinguishing state from federal recognition.

**Adaptations**: States should adjust criteria based on their specific history, existing tribal communities, and constitutional frameworks.

## Regulatory Framework

### Revised Part 83 Regulations (Interim)

**Existing Authority**: The Secretary of the Interior has authority to revise 25 C.F.R. Part 83 through notice-and-comment rulemaking under the Administrative Procedure Act.

**Draft Regulatory Revisions**:

- Incorporate oral history guidance into evidentiary standards
- Establish adverse inference procedures for destroyed records
- Publish transparent timelines for each pending petition
- Create a technical assistance program within existing BIA authority
- Mandate annual reporting on petition processing statistics

**Explanation**: These regulatory changes can be implemented before legislative reform, providing immediate improvements.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Commerce Clause authority**: Congress has clear authority under the Indian Commerce Clause to reform the recognition process
- **Due Process**: Petitioners may have procedural due process claims when the government fails to process petitions in a timely manner
- **Equal Protection**: The distinction between tribes recognized before and after 1934 (Carcieri) may raise equal protection concerns, though Indian classifications are political, not racial
- **Appointments Clause**: An independent commission must be structured to comply with the Appointments Clause

### Preemption Questions

- Federal recognition reform would preempt conflicting state laws regarding tribal status
- State recognition would remain a separate category unless Congress creates a federal-state pathway
- Land-into-trust decisions preempt local zoning under existing law

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- Independent commission decisions enforceable through federal courts
- Anti-fraud provisions enforced through civil penalties and criminal prosecution
- Timeline enforcement through automatic referral to expedited review

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- 10-year reauthorization requirement for the commission
- Biennial GAO review of processing times and outcomes
- Annual report to Congress on petition status and resource needs

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Federal Tribal Recognition Reform Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Appropriations shortfall | Authorization does not guarantee funding; Congress may underfund the commission | Critical |
| Commissioner vacancies | Senate confirmation battles could leave the commission understaffed | High |
| Gaming separation gaming | The gaming separation provision could be circumvented through intermediaries or delayed applications | Medium |
| Adverse inference abuse | Groups without genuine tribal history could exploit the adverse inference provision | Medium |
| Expedited track manipulation | Groups could fabricate or exaggerate prior federal relationships to access the fast track | Medium |
| Third-party obstruction | Well-funded opponents could overwhelm the commission with frivolous evidence during comment periods | High |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No mandatory appropriations | Commission effectiveness depends on annual appropriations | Political resistance to mandatory spending |
| Limited judicial review scope | D.C. Circuit review may be too narrow to correct factual errors | Deference to agency factfinding |
| No graduated recognition | Bill maintains binary recognized/not-recognized framework | Complexity of designing intermediate status |
| State recognition not integrated | State-recognized tribes do not receive an expedited pathway | Political difficulty of standardizing state-federal relationships |
| Retrospective application unclear | How revised standards apply to previously denied petitions is ambiguous | Complexity of reopening closed cases |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Mandatory appropriations**: Amend to include mandatory appropriations tied to a formula based on active petitions, or create a dedicated trust fund
2. **Recess appointments**: Include provision for interim commissioners if Senate fails to act within 120 days
3. **Enhanced anti-intermediary provisions**: Require disclosure of all financial relationships between petitioners and non-Indian parties for 10 years following recognition
4. **Adverse inference guardrails**: Require corroborating evidence from at least two independent sources before adverse inference applies
5. **Fast track verification**: Require independent historical verification of prior federal relationship claims
6. **Comment period limits**: Cap third-party submissions at reasonable page limits and impose relevance standards
7. **Previously denied petitions**: Include explicit provision allowing previously denied petitioners to reapply under revised standards without prejudice

### Carcieri Fix Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Ratification scope | Section 3 ratification may not cover all past land-into-trust actions due to ambiguous timing | Medium |
| Definition breadth | "Federally recognized" may be challenged as overly broad if it includes groups recognized through non-traditional processes | Low |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No consultation requirement | Does not require state/local consultation before land-into-trust | Political trade-off for simplicity |
| No environmental review enhancement | Does not address environmental concerns about trust land conversion | Separate regulatory framework applies |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Clarify ratification**: Amend Section 3 to specify that ratification covers all land-into-trust actions taken for the benefit of any tribe that was federally recognized at the time the action was taken
2. **Add consultation**: Include a 90-day consultation period with state and local governments before land-into-trust decisions, without granting veto power

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Transition disruption | Detailed transition plan with dedicated transition team and timeline |
| Staff retention | Competitive pay and benefits for commission staff; retention bonuses for transferring OFA employees |
| Political appointee capture | Strong independence provisions; removal only for cause; staggered terms |
| Interagency coordination | Memoranda of understanding with BIA, IHS, DOJ, and other affected agencies |
| Public confidence | Transparent processes; annual reports; independent audits |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Commission authorization expires after 10 years unless reauthorized
- GAO audit of commission operations required at years 3, 5, and 8
- Annual report to Congress including statistics on petitions received, processing times, outcomes, and costs
- Inspector General oversight of anti-fraud provisions
- Public database of all pending and decided petitions with full documentation
