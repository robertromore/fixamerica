# Federal Recognition: Implementation Roadmap

## Strategic Framework

Reforming the federal recognition process requires a sequenced strategy that builds political support incrementally, starting with consensus measures and building toward structural reform. The roadmap accounts for political realities: gaming-related opposition, congressional dynamics, and the need to maintain existing recognized tribes' support.

## Phase 1: Administrative Quick Wins (Years 1-2)

### Objective

Implement reforms that do not require legislation, demonstrating commitment and building momentum.

### Actions

| Action | Mechanism | Lead Actor | Timeline |
|--------|-----------|------------|----------|
| Increase OFA staffing through budget request | Executive budget process | Interior Department | Year 1 |
| Issue revised guidance on oral history evidence | Secretarial order | Secretary of Interior | Year 1 |
| Establish petitioner technical assistance program | Existing BIA authority | BIA | Year 1 |
| Publish transparent processing timeline for each petition | Administrative policy | OFA | Year 1 |
| Conduct tribal consultation on recognition reform | Executive Order 13175 | White House | Year 1 |
| Update Part 83 regulatory guidance on adverse inference | Regulatory guidance | BIA/OFA | Year 2 |
| Create online petition tracking system | IT procurement | OFA | Year 2 |

### Success Metrics

- OFA staff doubled from 6-8 to 12-16 FTE
- Average petition processing time reduction of 25%
- Technical assistance provided to all active petitioners
- Public dashboard tracking all pending petitions

### Political Strategy

- Frame as efficiency and good governance reform, not expansion of recognition
- Engage recognized tribes through NCAI consultation
- Highlight cost savings from reduced litigation

## Phase 2: Legislative Foundation (Years 2-4)

### Objective

Pass targeted legislation addressing the most acute problems: the Carcieri fix, OFA funding, and petitioner assistance.

### Priority Legislation

#### Carcieri Fix

- **Vehicle**: Standalone legislation or inclusion in a broader Indian affairs package
- **Strategy**: Bipartisan framing -- the Carcieri decision was 6-3 and has created practical problems recognized by both parties
- **Coalition**: NCAI, NARF, tribal gaming associations, Interior Department
- **Opposition management**: Address state concerns through gaming impact studies and consultation requirements

#### Federal Recognition Process Reform Act

- **Key provisions**:
    - Statutory funding authorization for OFA ($10-15 million/year)
    - Petitioner assistance grant program ($5-10 million/year)
    - Binding processing timelines with enforcement mechanisms
    - Revised evidentiary standards incorporating oral history and adverse inference
    - Anti-fraud provisions
- **Strategy**: Introduce in Senate Indian Affairs Committee; build House Natural Resources Committee support
- **Coalition building**: Engage both tribal organizations and good-government groups

### Stakeholder Engagement

| Stakeholder | Engagement Strategy | Concessions |
|-------------|--------------------|----|
| Recognized gaming tribes | Assure that gaming rights are not affected by process reform | Anti-fraud provisions; gaming impact separation |
| State governments | Consultation requirements for land-into-trust | State comment periods; impact studies |
| Local governments | Impact mitigation funds from gaming revenue | Community benefit agreements |
| Conservative members | Fiscal responsibility framing; fraud prevention | Cost-neutral funding mechanisms |
| Progressive members | Justice and civil rights framing | Expanded evidentiary standards |

### Success Metrics

- Carcieri fix enacted
- OFA funding increased to authorized levels
- Petitioner assistance program operational
- First petitions completed under revised timelines

## Phase 3: Structural Reform (Years 4-7)

### Objective

Establish an independent recognition commission and create alternative pathways.

### Independent Tribal Recognition Commission

**Implementation sequence**:

1. **Year 4**: Introduce legislation; begin hearings
2. **Year 5**: Pass legislation; begin commission establishment
3. **Year 6**: Appoint commissioners; transfer pending petitions
4. **Year 7**: Commission fully operational

**Design considerations**:

- Transition plan for pending petitions (no group should be sent back to square one)
- Staffing plan drawing on OFA expertise
- Funding mechanism (authorized appropriations with backstop)
- Relationship to Interior Department (independent but coordinating)

### Alternative Pathways

| Pathway | Year | Prerequisites |
|---------|------|---------------|
| Prior federal relationship fast track | Year 4 | Carcieri fix enacted; historical records compiled |
| State recognition pathway | Year 5 | Commission operational; state consultation complete |
| Graduated recognition | Year 6 | Full commission operational; pilot program results |

### Success Metrics

- Commission established with bipartisan appointments
- All pending petitions transferred with preserved evidence
- At least one alternative pathway operational
- Processing times under 5 years

## Phase 4: Systemic Integration (Years 7-10)

### Objective

Ensure the reformed system is sustainable, adequately funded, and producing just outcomes.

### Actions

| Action | Timeline | Mechanism |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| Comprehensive review of recognition outcomes | Year 8 | GAO study |
| Evaluate alternative pathways effectiveness | Year 8 | Commission report |
| Assess funding adequacy | Year 9 | Congressional hearing |
| Consider constitutional protections | Year 10 | Congressional study |

### Long-Term Goals

- All legitimate petitions resolved within 5 years of filing
- Adequate funding permanently authorized
- Fraud rate near zero
- Broad tribal support for the process
- Public confidence in recognition decisions
- Recognized tribes receiving adequate resources without dilution concerns

## Risk Assessment

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|--------|------------|
| Gaming industry opposition blocks legislation | High | High | Separate gaming issues from recognition reform |
| Recognized tribes oppose expansion | Medium | High | Address resource concerns through increased funding |
| Political interference in commission | Medium | Medium | Strong independence provisions; staggered terms |
| Fraudulent claims test the system | Low | High | Enhanced verification; anti-fraud provisions |
| Federal budget constraints limit funding | High | Medium | Cost-neutral mechanisms; mandatory appropriations |
| Carcieri fix attached to controversial legislation | Medium | Medium | Pursue standalone or must-pass vehicle |

## State-Level Action Map

While federal recognition is inherently a federal process, state actions can support reform:

| State Action | Status | Impact |
|-------------|--------|--------|
| Strengthen state recognition standards | Variable | Builds case for state-to-federal pathway |
| State funding for tribal services | Some states | Fills gaps while federal process continues |
| State consultation policies | Growing | Demonstrates government-to-government capacity |
| State lobbying for federal reform | Variable | Adds political pressure for congressional action |

## Coalition Map

### Core Coalition

- National Congress of American Indians
- Native American Rights Fund
- Association on American Indian Affairs
- State-recognized tribal organizations
- Academic experts in federal Indian law

### Potential Allies

- Civil rights organizations (ACLU, NAACP -- given racial reclassification history)
- Good-government organizations
- Religious organizations with historical ties to tribes
- Environmental organizations (tribal stewardship)

### Persuadable Opposition

- Non-gaming recognized tribes (address resource concerns)
- Moderate state officials (impact mitigation)
- Fiscal conservatives (efficiency and cost-reduction framing)

### Likely Persistent Opposition

- Commercial gaming industry
- Some gaming tribes in competitive markets
- Anti-sovereignty organizations
- States with strong anti-recognition positions (historically Connecticut)
