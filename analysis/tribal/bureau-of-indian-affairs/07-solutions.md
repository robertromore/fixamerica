# Bureau of Indian Affairs: Solutions

## Overview

Reforming the Bureau of Indian Affairs requires action on multiple fronts: resolving the bureau's inherent mission conflicts, expanding tribal self-governance to replace BIA direct services, modernizing trust land administration, adequately funding trust obligations, fixing the federal recognition process, and establishing accountability mechanisms. These solutions draw on evidence from self-governance compacts, the HEARTH Act, international models, and the recommendations of tribal leaders, GAO, and the Secretarial Commission on Indian Trust Administration and Reform (2013).

## Solution 1: Universal Self-Governance Compacting

### Description

Extend self-governance compacting to all 574 federally recognized tribes and all federal agencies that serve tribal communities, making BIA direct service delivery the exception rather than the norm.

### Evidence Base

- 284 tribes already participate in self-governance compacts, operating approximately 80% of BIA program funding (BIA OSG, 2023)
- Self-governance tribes report 15-20% greater program efficiency compared to BIA-administered programs (Self-Governance Communication and Education Tribal Consortium, 2022)
- Harvard Project on American Indian Economic Development research consistently links self-determination to improved socioeconomic outcomes
- IHS Title V self-governance compacts demonstrate the model works across agencies

### Implementation

1. **Mandatory BIA compacting offer**: BIA must offer compacting to every federally recognized tribe within 3 years
2. **Government-wide authority**: Extend compacting authority to DOT, EPA, HUD, Education, Agriculture, Commerce, and other agencies
3. **Intertribal compacting**: Authorize and fund intertribal compacts that allow small tribes to share administrative resources
4. **Technical assistance**: $200 million over 5 years for intertribal organizations to provide capacity-building support
5. **Flexible funding**: Allow tribes to reallocate compact funds among programs based on community priorities
6. **Reduced reporting**: Streamline accountability requirements to outcome-based metrics rather than process-based compliance

### Cost Estimate

- Technical assistance and capacity building: $200 million over 5 years
- Administrative transition: $150 million over 5 years
- Net savings: Self-governance compacts reduce administrative overhead by 10-15%, producing estimated annual savings of $200-300 million once fully implemented

### Timeline

- Legislation: 1-2 years
- Universal BIA compacting offers: 3 years
- Government-wide compacting implementation: 3-5 years
- Full tribal participation: 5-10 years (phased based on tribal readiness)

### Challenges

- Not all tribes have immediate administrative capacity for compacting
- Some tribes prefer direct BIA service delivery and resist change
- Federal agencies beyond BIA resist ceding program authority
- Ensuring adequate contract support cost funding (historically underfunded before *Salazar v. Ramah Navajo Chapter*, 2012)

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline | Target (5-year) |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| Tribes in self-governance compacts | 284 | 450+ |
| Federal agencies offering compacting | 2 (BIA, IHS) | 8+ |
| BIA program funding through compacts | ~80% | 95%+ |
| Administrative overhead | Baseline | 15% reduction |

## Solution 2: Trust Land Administration Modernization

### Description

Overhaul BIA trust land administration through technology modernization, processing timeline mandates, expansion of the HEARTH Act model, and dedicated resources for the fractionation crisis.

### Evidence Base

- Land-into-trust processing averages 3-5 years; comparable federal land transactions take months (BIA Realty, 2023)
- HEARTH Act (2012) allowed tribes to approve their own leases, reducing processing time from months to days; participating tribes report 80% faster lease approvals (BIA, 2023)
- TAAMS (Trust Asset and Accounting Management System) remains incomplete after two decades of implementation
- GAO-18-164 (2018) documented BIA trust administration deficiencies in detail
- Cobell settlement funded $1.9 billion in land buy-back but fractionation continues to grow

### Implementation

1. **Processing timeline mandates**: 180 days for uncontested land-into-trust; 365 days for contested applications
2. **HEARTH Act expansion**: Extend tribal self-approval authority to all trust land transactions (leases, rights-of-way, business site leases, residential leases)
3. **Technology modernization**: $500 million investment over 5 years to complete TAAMS deployment, digitize remaining paper records, and implement modern GIS and land title systems
4. **Fractionation resolution**: Extend and expand the Land Buy-Back program with an additional $1 billion; reform Indian probate law to reduce future fractionation
5. **Staffing**: Hire 200 additional realty specialists and 100 additional title examiners over 3 years
6. **Completeness standards**: Publish clear standards for what constitutes a "complete" application, preventing BIA from using technical deficiencies to reset processing clocks

### Cost Estimate

- Technology modernization: $500 million over 5 years
- Additional staffing: $75 million annually
- Extended Land Buy-Back program: $1 billion over 10 years
- Total: approximately $1.75 billion over 10 years

### Timeline

- Processing mandates: Effective 1 year after enactment
- HEARTH Act expansion: 1-2 years
- Technology modernization: 3-5 years
- Fractionation program: Ongoing (10-20 year horizon)

### Challenges

- BIA IT infrastructure is severely outdated; modernization requires sustained investment
- Paper records in poor condition may be difficult to digitize accurately
- Fractionation grows faster than consolidation efforts can resolve it
- HEARTH Act expansion requires tribes to develop regulatory capacity for self-approval

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline | Target (5-year) |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| Average land-into-trust processing time | 3-5 years | 6 months |
| Pending land-into-trust applications | 300+ | Under 100 |
| Lease approval time (HEARTH tribes) | Days | Maintained |
| Lease approval time (non-HEARTH tribes) | 6-24 months | 30 days |
| Fractionated ownership interests | 5+ million | 3.5 million |
| Trust records digitized | ~70% | 98%+ |

## Solution 3: Adequate and Stable Funding

### Description

Convert BIA funding from discretionary to mandatory appropriations, or at minimum extend advance appropriations to all BIA programs, ensuring adequate and predictable funding for trust obligations.

### Evidence Base

- U.S. Commission on Civil Rights: federal government underfunds tribal programs by approximately 60% (*Broken Promises*, 2018)
- IHS advance appropriations (achieved FY 2024) demonstrated that stable funding improves service delivery and planning
- Government shutdowns have repeatedly disrupted BIA services, including law enforcement, social services, and trust administration
- BIA partial advance appropriations authority was granted by Congress, establishing precedent

### Implementation

1. **Mandatory funding**: Reclassify core BIA programs (law enforcement, trust administration, social services, self-governance funding) as mandatory spending
2. **Formula-based allocation**: Transparent, needs-based formula considering tribal population, land area, service need, and treaty provisions
3. **Inflation adjustment**: Automatic annual adjustment indexed to actual cost increases in tribal service areas
4. **Advance appropriations fallback**: For programs not reclassified, extend advance appropriations to all BIA programs
5. **Shutdown protection**: Exempt all BIA programs from government shutdown effects

### Cost Estimate

- Additional annual cost to bring BIA funding to adequate levels: $3-5 billion (based on USCCR estimates adjusted for BIA-specific programs)
- This represents fulfillment of existing obligations, not new spending

### Timeline

- Advance appropriations expansion: 1-2 years
- Mandatory reclassification: 2-4 years (requires overcoming appropriations committee resistance)
- Formula development: 1-2 years with tribal consultation
- Full funding adequacy: 5 years (phased increase)

### Challenges

- Appropriations committees resist losing control over spending
- CBO scoring will show significant mandatory spending increase
- May require budget reconciliation to overcome Senate filibuster
- Formula design complex given tribal diversity

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline | Target (5-year) |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| BIA law enforcement per-capita spending | $38/person | $100+/person |
| Trust administration funding vs. need | 41% | 80%+ |
| Government shutdown impact | Full service disruption | Zero disruption |
| Social services funding vs. need | 38% | 75%+ |

## Solution 4: Law Enforcement and Public Safety Reform

### Description

Increase BIA Office of Justice Services staffing to adequate levels, modernize equipment and facilities, and improve coordination with tribal, state, and federal law enforcement.

### Evidence Base

- BIA has approximately 350 law enforcement officers for 56 million acres across 200+ reservations; DOJ estimates 3,000 officers are needed (DOJ, 2022)
- Average response time on remote reservations: 30-90 minutes vs. 15-20 minutes for comparable rural areas (DOJ, 2022)
- Federal declination rate for Indian Country cases: 52% (DOJ, 2022)
- Violent crime rate in Indian Country: 2.5x national average (DOJ, 2022)
- Tribal Law and Order Act (2010) authorized improvements but funding has not followed

### Implementation

1. **Staffing surge**: Hire 1,500 additional BIA law enforcement officers over 5 years (reaching approximately 1,850 total)
2. **Tribal police funding**: Double funding for tribally operated police departments under 638 contracts
3. **Detention facilities**: Modernize or replace 24 BIA detention facilities; close substandard facilities
4. **Cross-deputization**: Facilitate federal-tribal-state cross-deputization agreements to close jurisdiction gaps
5. **MMIWP**: Fully fund the Missing and Murdered Indigenous Persons Unit with dedicated investigators
6. **Victim services**: Expand victim advocacy and services in Indian Country

### Cost Estimate

- Additional law enforcement staffing: $400 million annually when fully staffed
- Detention facility construction/modernization: $500 million over 5 years
- Tribal police funding increase: $300 million annually
- MMIWP and victim services: $100 million annually
- Total: approximately $1.2 billion annually at full implementation

### Timeline

- Hiring surge begins: Year 1
- Half of additional officers hired: Year 3
- Full staffing: Year 5
- Facility modernization: 3-7 years

### Challenges

- Recruiting law enforcement officers for remote locations is historically difficult
- BIA law enforcement academy capacity must expand
- Jurisdictional complexity (federal, tribal, state) requires coordination protocols
- Detention alternatives may be preferable in some tribal communities

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline | Target (5-year) |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| BIA law enforcement officers | ~350 | 1,850 |
| Average response time (remote reservations) | 30-90 minutes | 15-25 minutes |
| Officer-to-acreage ratio | 1:160,000 | 1:30,000 |
| Violent crime rate (Indian Country vs. national) | 2.5x | 1.5x |

## Solution 5: Federal Recognition Process Reform

### Description

Reform the Part 83 federal recognition process to be faster, fairer, more transparent, and adequately resourced, while maintaining rigorous standards for genuine Indigenous community identity.

### Evidence Base

- Average processing time: 15-25 years from petition to decision (OFA, 2023)
- Cost to petitioning groups: $500,000-$5 million in historical research and legal fees
- Office of Federal Acknowledgment staff: approximately 12 (OFA, 2023)
- Revised regulations in 2015 improved some aspects but did not address staffing or timeline fundamentals
- Congressional recognitions (7 since 2000) bypass the administrative process, creating inequity

### Implementation

1. **Staffing**: Triple OFA staff from 12 to 36, including historians, anthropologists, and genealogists
2. **Timeline mandate**: Preliminary findings within 3 years of receipt of documented petition; final determination within 5 years
3. **Technical assistance**: Provide federal assistance to petitioning groups in compiling documentation, reducing the financial barrier
4. **Transparent criteria**: Publish detailed guidance on evidentiary standards with case examples
5. **Independent review**: Establish an independent review panel for denied petitions, replacing the current IBIA (Interior Board of Indian Appeals) process
6. **Congressional recognition standards**: Develop standards for congressional recognition to ensure consistency

### Cost Estimate

- OFA staffing expansion: $10 million annually
- Technical assistance for petitioning groups: $5 million annually
- Independent review panel: $3 million annually
- Total: approximately $18 million annually

### Timeline

- Staffing expansion: 1-2 years
- Timeline mandate effective: 2 years after enactment
- Active petitions resolved under new timelines: 3-5 years
- Backlog cleared: 5-7 years

### Challenges

- Some existing tribes oppose faster recognition (resource competition, gaming concerns)
- Maintaining rigor while increasing speed requires skilled staff
- Political interference in recognition decisions is a persistent risk
- Congressional recognition pathway may resist standardization

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline | Target (5-year) |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| Average petition-to-decision time | 15-25 years | 5 years |
| OFA staff | ~12 | 36 |
| Cost to petitioning groups | $500K-$5M | $100K-$500K |
| Active pending petitions | 8 | 0 (current backlog resolved) |

## Solution 6: BIA Organizational Restructuring

### Description

Restructure the BIA to separate its conflicting functions -- trust administration, self-governance facilitation, and direct service delivery -- into distinct organizational units with clear missions and accountability.

### Evidence Base

- Secretarial Commission on Indian Trust Administration and Reform (2013) recommended separating trust functions from program administration
- OST (Office of the Special Trustee) was created specifically because trust fund management conflicted with BIA's administrative role
- Self-governance compacting has demonstrated that tribal management outperforms BIA management in most program areas
- Other trust beneficiary systems (e.g., state trust funds, private fiduciary management) separate trustee and service provider functions

### Implementation

1. **Trust Administration Office**: Create a standalone Office of Trust Administration (separate from BIA) within the Department of the Interior, responsible solely for trust land, trust funds, and fiduciary duties
2. **Self-Governance Facilitation Office**: Designate BIA's primary remaining mission as facilitating self-governance compacting, providing technical assistance, and ensuring adequate compact funding
3. **Residual Service Delivery**: For tribes that have not compacted, maintain BIA service delivery capacity but with clear sunset provisions and transition support
4. **Accountability framework**: Establish measurable performance standards, tribal satisfaction surveys, and independent oversight for each unit
5. **AS-IA reform**: Elevate the Assistant Secretary for Indian Affairs position to Under Secretary, increasing tribal policy visibility within Interior

### Cost Estimate

- Reorganization transition costs: $150 million over 3 years
- IT system separation: $200 million over 5 years
- New oversight mechanisms: $25 million annually
- Net long-term savings from reduced duplication and improved efficiency

### Timeline

- Legislative authority: 1-2 years
- Organizational design (with tribal consultation): 1-2 years
- Implementation: 3-5 years
- Full transition: 5-7 years

### Challenges

- Reorganizations disrupt services during transition
- Federal employee unions resist restructuring
- Tribal consultation on design will be complex given diverse tribal interests
- Congressional committee jurisdiction changes may be needed

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline | Target (5-year) |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| Trust administration processing times | 3-5 years (land-into-trust) | 6 months |
| Tribal satisfaction with BIA services | Low (surveys) | Moderate-to-high |
| Self-governance compact processing time | 1-2 years | 6 months |
| Trust fund accounting accuracy | Improved post-Cobell but ongoing | 99.9%+ |

## Solution 7: Bureau of Indian Education Reform

### Description

Fundamentally reform the Bureau of Indian Education to improve student outcomes, modernize facilities, and increase tribal control over education.

### Evidence Base

- BIE reading proficiency: 19% (vs. 33% national average); math proficiency: 14% (vs. 36% national) (BIE/NAEP, 2023)
- 63% of BIE schools in poor or critical condition (GAO, 2023)
- $1.3 billion deferred maintenance backlog (BIE Construction, 2023)
- GAO-17-421 documented BIA education management failures; GAO-23-106280 documented dangerous school conditions
- Tribally controlled schools under P.L. 93-638 and the Tribally Controlled Schools Act generally outperform BIA-operated schools

### Implementation

1. **Transfer BIA-operated schools to tribal control**: Over 5 years, transfer all 55 BIA-operated schools to tribal operation with full funding
2. **Facilities emergency**: $2 billion emergency construction and renovation program over 5 years
3. **Teacher recruitment and retention**: Competitive salary supplements, housing assistance, and student loan forgiveness for BIE teachers
4. **Culturally responsive curriculum**: Fund tribal language and culture programs integrated into academic instruction
5. **Technology**: Ensure broadband and modern technology in all BIE schools
6. **Accountability**: Implement outcome-based accountability using both academic standards and tribal-defined indicators (language proficiency, cultural knowledge, community engagement)

### Cost Estimate

- Facilities construction/renovation: $2 billion over 5 years
- Teacher recruitment/retention: $150 million annually
- Technology and curriculum: $100 million over 5 years
- Transition support: $75 million over 5 years
- Total: approximately $3.1 billion over 5 years

### Timeline

- Emergency facilities funding: Year 1
- Teacher recruitment programs: Year 1-2
- School transfers to tribal control: Years 1-5
- Full implementation: 5-7 years

### Challenges

- Remote locations make teacher recruitment difficult
- Construction in remote areas is expensive and slow
- Some tribes may not be ready to operate schools immediately
- Balancing academic standards with cultural education priorities

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline | Target (5-year) |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| Reading proficiency | 19% | 28%+ |
| Math proficiency | 14% | 25%+ |
| Schools in poor/critical condition | 63% | 20% |
| Tribally controlled schools (% of total) | 70% (128 of 183) | 95%+ |
| Teacher vacancy rate | High | Below 10% |

## International Models

### Canada: Indigenous Services Canada

- In 2017, Canada split the former Department of Indigenous and Northern Affairs into two departments: Indigenous Services Canada (service delivery) and Crown-Indigenous Relations and Northern Affairs (relationship management)
- The split addressed the same trustee-vs-service-provider conflict that plagues the BIA
- **Applicability**: Organizational separation model directly relevant to BIA restructuring

### New Zealand: Te Puni Kokiri (Ministry of Maori Development)

- Operates as a policy advisory ministry rather than a direct service provider
- Advocates for Maori interests across all government agencies
- Direct services delivered by mainstream agencies with Maori community input
- **Applicability**: Model for BIA transition from service provider to advocacy/facilitation role

### Australia: National Indigenous Australians Agency

- Created in 2019 as a standalone agency reporting directly to the Prime Minister
- Elevated political visibility of Indigenous affairs above departmental level
- "Closing the Gap" framework sets measurable targets for reducing disparities
- **Applicability**: Elevated organizational status and target-setting framework relevant

## Sequencing Strategy

### Phase 1: Immediate (Years 1-2)

- Expand self-governance compacting offers to all tribes (Solution 1)
- Mandate trust land processing timelines (Solution 2)
- Secure advance appropriations for all BIA programs (Solution 3)
- Begin law enforcement hiring surge (Solution 4)
- Triple OFA staffing (Solution 5)

### Phase 2: Near-Term (Years 2-5)

- Implement government-wide compacting (Solution 1)
- Complete HEARTH Act expansion (Solution 2)
- Achieve mandatory funding reclassification (Solution 3)
- Complete law enforcement staffing increase (Solution 4)
- Begin BIA organizational restructuring (Solution 6)
- Launch BIE facilities emergency program (Solution 7)

### Phase 3: Long-Term (Years 5-10)

- Full implementation of restructured BIA (Solution 6)
- Complete BIE school transfers to tribal control (Solution 7)
- Resolve federal recognition backlog (Solution 5)
- Evaluate and adjust all reforms based on outcomes data
