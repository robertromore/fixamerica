# Bureau of Indian Affairs: History

## Origins and Early Development (1824-1887)

### Establishment

- **1824**: Secretary of War John C. Calhoun created the Office of Indian Affairs within the War Department, without congressional authorization, to manage trade, treaty negotiations, and Indian removal
- **1832**: Congress formally authorized the position of Commissioner of Indian Affairs
- **1849**: The Office of Indian Affairs was transferred from the War Department to the newly created Department of the Interior, reflecting a shift from military to civilian administration (though the military continued to play a major role in Indian affairs for decades)

### Removal Era (1830s-1850s)

The BIA's earliest major function was administering the forced removal of eastern tribes to Indian Territory (present-day Oklahoma):

- **1830**: Indian Removal Act signed by President Andrew Jackson, authorizing negotiation of removal treaties
- **1831-1838**: Cherokee, Choctaw, Chickasaw, Creek, and Seminole nations forcibly relocated on the Trail of Tears; approximately 15,000 deaths
- The BIA managed removal logistics, treaty negotiations, and distribution of annuity payments promised in exchange for land cessions
- Corruption was rampant; agents embezzled funds intended for tribal annuities and supplies

### Reservation System (1850s-1880s)

- The BIA established and administered reservations, confining tribes to designated areas far smaller than their traditional territories
- Indian agents wielded enormous power over reservation life, controlling food distribution, movement, education, and governance
- Corruption among Indian agents was so pervasive that President Ulysses Grant's "Peace Policy" (1869) assigned agency positions to religious organizations

### Key Legislation

| Year | Law | Impact |
|------|-----|--------|
| 1830 | Indian Removal Act | Authorized forced removal of eastern tribes |
| 1834 | Trade and Intercourse Act | Defined Indian Country; regulated commerce |
| 1849 | Interior Department Act | Transferred Indian Affairs to DOI |
| 1871 | End of Treaty-Making | Congress ended treaty-making with tribes; shifted to statutes and executive orders |
| 1885 | Major Crimes Act | Federal jurisdiction over serious crimes in Indian Country |

## Allotment and Assimilation Era (1887-1934)

### The Dawes Act (1887)

The General Allotment Act represented the BIA's most destructive policy, breaking up communal tribal lands into individual allotments:

| Provision | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Allotment size | 160 acres to household heads; 80 acres to single adults; 40 acres to children |
| Trust period | 25 years (land held in trust; no sale or taxation) |
| "Surplus" lands | Remaining reservation land opened to non-Indian settlement |
| Citizenship | Allottees granted citizenship upon receiving allotments |

### Impact of Allotment

| Metric | Before Allotment (1887) | After Allotment (1934) | Change |
|--------|------------------------|----------------------|--------|
| Total Indian land | 138 million acres | 48 million acres | -65% |
| "Surplus" land sold | -- | 60 million acres | Opened to settlement |
| Allotted land lost | -- | 27 million acres | Sold, foreclosed, or swindled |
| Quality of remaining land | Diverse, including productive agricultural land | Disproportionately arid, marginal land | Best land taken |

### Boarding Schools

The BIA operated one of the most extensive forced assimilation programs in history:

- **1879**: Carlisle Indian Industrial School established by Richard Henry Pratt under the motto "Kill the Indian, save the man"
- **Peak operation**: Over 350 government-funded boarding schools operating by 1900
- **Children affected**: Estimated 100,000+ Native American children removed from families
- **Practices**: English-only education, suppression of Native languages and cultural practices, military discipline, forced labor, physical punishment
- **Abuse**: Federal Indian Boarding School Initiative (2022-2024) documented widespread physical, sexual, and emotional abuse; at least 87 burial sites identified at former school locations
- **Lasting impact**: Disrupted intergenerational cultural transmission, family bonding, and parenting practices; contributed to intergenerational trauma

### Meriam Report (1928)

The Institute for Government Research (Brookings Institution) published *The Problem of Indian Administration*, documenting:

- Poverty, disease, and malnutrition on reservations
- Failed allotment policy
- Inadequate education and health services
- Bureau incompetence and corruption
- Recommended shifting from assimilation to supporting tribal self-governance

## Indian Reorganization Era (1934-1953)

### Indian Reorganization Act (1934)

Commissioner John Collier championed the IRA (Wheeler-Howard Act), which reversed allotment policy:

| Provision | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| End of allotment | Prohibited further allotment of tribal lands |
| Land restoration | Authorized acquisition of land for tribes |
| Tribal organization | Allowed tribes to adopt constitutions and form governments |
| Revolving credit fund | Created a loan fund for tribal economic development |
| Indian preference | Established preference for hiring Indians in the Indian Service |
| Opt-out | Tribes could vote to reject the IRA (77 tribes did) |

### IRA Criticisms

- Imposed Western-style constitutional governance on tribes with different traditional structures
- BIA retained approval authority over tribal constitutions, bylaws, and major decisions
- The "boilerplate" constitutions drafted by BIA attorneys often did not reflect tribal values or governance traditions
- Tribal governments created under the IRA were dependent on BIA for funding and approval

## Termination Era (1953-1968)

### Termination Policy

Congress adopted a policy of ending the federal-tribal relationship for certain tribes deemed "ready" for assimilation:

- **1953**: House Concurrent Resolution 108 declared Congress's intent to terminate federal supervision of tribes "at the earliest possible time"
- **1953**: Public Law 280 transferred criminal and some civil jurisdiction from federal to state governments in six states (later expanded)
- **1954-1966**: 109 tribes, bands, and communities terminated; approximately 13,000 individuals lost tribal status
- **BIA role**: The BIA administered the termination process, including distributing tribal assets and ending federal services

### Terminated Tribes: Impact

| Metric | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| Tribes terminated | 109 |
| Land lost | 1.3 million+ acres |
| Individuals affected | ~13,000 |
| Services lost | Health, education, housing, social services |
| Economic impact | Poverty increased dramatically in terminated communities |
| Restoration | Most terminated tribes have been restored through federal legislation (1970s-2000s) |

### Notable Terminations

| Tribe | Year Terminated | Year Restored | Key Impact |
|-------|----------------|---------------|-----------|
| Menominee (WI) | 1961 | 1973 | Lost 235,000-acre reservation; became poorest county in Wisconsin |
| Klamath (OR) | 1954 | 1986 | Lost 860,000-acre reservation; timber liquidated |
| Western Oregon Tribes | 1954-1956 | 1977-1989 | 62 bands terminated; decades to restore |
| Alabama-Coushatta (TX) | 1954 | 1987 | Services eliminated; land base preserved |

## Self-Determination Era (1968-Present)

### Policy Reversal

- **1968**: President Johnson's "Special Message on Indian Affairs" called for an end to termination
- **1970**: President Nixon's "Special Message on Indian Affairs" formally repudiated termination and endorsed self-determination "without termination"
- **1975**: Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act (P.L. 93-638) allowed tribes to contract to run BIA programs
- **1994**: Self-Governance Act made permanent the tribal self-governance demonstration project, allowing tribes to compact for block grants of BIA funding

### Self-Determination Milestones

| Year | Development | Significance |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| 1975 | P.L. 93-638 enacted | Tribes can contract to run individual BIA programs |
| 1988 | Self-Governance Demonstration Project | Pilot for tribal block grants |
| 1994 | Self-Governance Act | Made self-governance compacts permanent |
| 2000 | Tribal Self-Governance amendments | Streamlined compacting process |
| 2020 | 284 tribes in self-governance compacts | Majority of tribal program funding flows through compacts |

### Trust Fund Scandal and Cobell Litigation

The BIA's mismanagement of Individual Indian Money (IIM) accounts became one of the largest financial scandals in federal history:

- **1887-1990s**: BIA failed to properly account for billions of dollars in trust fund revenues (oil, gas, timber, grazing leases) owed to individual Indian allottees
- **1996**: Elouise Cobell (Blackfeet) and four other named plaintiffs filed *Cobell v. Babbitt*, a class action on behalf of approximately 500,000 IIM account holders
- **1999-2008**: Court found BIA trust administration "unreformed and unreformable" -- Judge Royce Lamberth held Interior Secretary in contempt of court twice
- **2009**: $3.4 billion settlement approved
    - $1.5 billion in direct payments to approximately 500,000 account holders
    - $1.9 billion for Trust Land Consolidation Fund (land buy-back)
    - $60 million for scholarship fund
- **Aftermath**: Led to creation of Office of the Special Trustee, IT modernization, and ongoing reform efforts

### BIA Reorganization Attempts

| Year | Proposal | Outcome |
|------|----------|---------|
| 1977 | American Indian Policy Review Commission | Recommended major reform; partially implemented |
| 1999 | Reorganization Act | Assistant Secretary position established; structural changes limited |
| 2013 | Secretarial Commission on Indian Trust Administration and Reform | Recommended OST consolidation, IT modernization; partially implemented |
| 2017 | Interior reorganization proposal | Proposed regional restructuring of all Interior bureaus; did not proceed |
| 2021 | Biden administration initiatives | OST reorganization into BIA; boarding school investigation; consultation reform |

### Federal Indian Boarding School Initiative (2021-Present)

- **2021**: Secretary Deb Haaland (Laguna Pueblo), first Native American cabinet secretary, launched investigation
- **2022**: Volume 1 identified 408 schools across 37 states
- **2024**: Volume 2 identified 523 schools, 87 associated burial sites, and documented systemic abuse
- **Oral history project**: DOI conducted nationwide listening sessions with boarding school survivors
- **Significance**: First comprehensive federal accounting of the boarding school system since its operation
