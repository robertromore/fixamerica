# Bureau of Indian Affairs: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Reforming the Bureau of Indian Affairs requires legislation addressing its structural mission conflicts, trust land administration backlogs, chronic underfunding, law enforcement gaps, the federal recognition process, and the Bureau of Indian Education's failures. This document presents draft federal legislation for each of these areas, along with regulatory framework proposals, legal considerations, loopholes analysis, and implementation concerns.

The federal trust responsibility -- arising from treaties, statutes, executive orders, and the historic exchange of vast land cessions for promises of protection and services -- provides the legal foundation for all proposed legislation. These proposals aim to align BIA operations with existing obligations and the self-determination policy that has been bipartisan federal policy since 1975.

## Constitutional Amendments

BIA-specific constitutional amendments are not proposed in this document. The structural constitutional protections for tribal sovereignty (including limitations on plenary power and mandatory funding of treaty obligations) are addressed in [Tribal Sovereignty: Legislation](../tribal-sovereignty/11-legislation.md). The legislation proposed here operates within existing constitutional authority, primarily the Indian Commerce Clause (Art. I, Sec. 8, Cl. 3), as affirmed in *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023).

## Federal Legislation

### Bill 1: Universal Tribal Self-Governance Act

**Purpose**: Extend self-governance compacting authority to all federal agencies serving tribal communities, make compacting the default service delivery model, and provide resources for tribes transitioning from BIA direct services.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Universal Tribal Self-Governance
Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) the Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act
    (P.L. 93-638), enacted in 1975, established the policy of
    tribal self-determination as the foundation of the federal-
    tribal relationship;
(b) self-governance compacting, authorized on a permanent basis
    by the Tribal Self-Governance Act of 1994, has demonstrated
    superior outcomes compared to federal direct service delivery,
    with participating tribes reporting 15-20 percent greater
    program efficiency;
(c) 284 tribes currently participate in self-governance compacts
    with the Bureau of Indian Affairs or the Indian Health Service,
    operating approximately 80 percent of BIA program funding;
(d) extending self-governance compacting to all federal agencies
    and all willing tribes will further the policy of self-
    determination, reduce federal administrative overhead, and
    improve outcomes for tribal communities; and
(e) some tribes, particularly smaller tribes, require technical
    assistance and transitional support to develop the
    administrative capacity for compacting.

SEC. 3. GOVERNMENT-WIDE SELF-GOVERNANCE.

(a) IN GENERAL.--Each Federal agency that administers programs,
    services, functions, or activities for the benefit of Indian
    tribes shall, upon request of an eligible tribe, negotiate and
    enter into a self-governance compact and funding agreement with
    such tribe.

(b) ELIGIBLE AGENCIES.--The following agencies shall participate:
    (1) Department of the Interior (all bureaus and offices);
    (2) Department of Health and Human Services;
    (3) Department of Transportation;
    (4) Environmental Protection Agency;
    (5) Department of Housing and Urban Development;
    (6) Department of Education;
    (7) Department of Agriculture;
    (8) Department of Commerce;
    (9) Department of Energy;
    (10) Department of Labor; and
    (11) such other agencies as the President may designate.

(c) ELIGIBLE TRIBES.--Any federally recognized Indian tribe
    may request a self-governance compact with any eligible
    agency.

(d) MANDATORY OFFER.--Within 3 years of the date of enactment,
    the Bureau of Indian Affairs shall offer a self-governance
    compact to every federally recognized tribe that does not
    currently participate in a compact.

SEC. 4. COMPACT PROVISIONS.

(a) FLEXIBLE FUNDING.--Tribes may reallocate compact funds
    among programs based on community priorities, provided
    that all applicable program requirements are met.

(b) REDUCED REPORTING.--Compact accountability requirements
    shall be based on outcome measures rather than process
    compliance, to the maximum extent permitted by law.

(c) CONTRACT SUPPORT COSTS.--Full contract support costs
    shall be provided for all compacts, as required by
    Salazar v. Ramah Navajo Chapter (2012).

(d) NO FUNDING REDUCTION.--No tribe shall receive less
    funding through a compact than the tribe would have
    received through direct federal administration of the
    same programs.

SEC. 5. INTERTRIBAL COMPACTING.

(a) Two or more tribes may enter into an intertribal compact
    to jointly administer programs under this Act.

(b) Intertribal compacts shall be eligible for supplemental
    administrative funding to cover coordination costs.

(c) An intertribal compact organization may serve as the
    fiscal agent and administrative entity for participating
    tribes.

SEC. 6. TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE.

(a) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.--There is authorized to
    be appropriated $40,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027
    through 2031 for technical assistance to tribes developing
    self-governance capacity.

(b) ELIGIBLE PROVIDERS.--Technical assistance shall be
    provided by intertribal organizations with demonstrated
    expertise in self-governance compacting.

SEC. 7. REPORTING.

Not later than 2 years after the date of enactment and
biennially thereafter, the Director of the Office of
Management and Budget, in consultation with tribal nations,
shall submit to Congress a report on the implementation of
this Act across all participating agencies.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 180 days after the date of
enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill extends the self-governance model that has been the most successful BIA reform to date across the entire federal government. It builds on 50 years of experience under P.L. 93-638 and the Self-Governance Act of 1994. The intertribal compacting provision addresses the concern that small tribes lack administrative capacity by allowing shared arrangements.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Federal agencies beyond BIA and IHS will resist ceding program control
- Not all programs are suitable for compacting (some require uniform national standards)
- OMB and agency budget offices may resist the administrative costs of negotiating compacts with 574 tribes
- Some tribes prefer direct federal services and may resist the "default compacting" approach

**Refinements**:

- Include a list of programs explicitly excluded from compacting (national defense, intelligence, etc.)
- Phase agency participation over 5 years, beginning with agencies that already serve tribal communities
- Establish a dispute resolution mechanism for compact negotiations
- Include workforce transition provisions for federal employees whose positions are transferred

### Bill 2: Trust Land Administration Reform Act

**Purpose**: Modernize BIA trust land administration through processing timeline mandates, HEARTH Act expansion, technology investment, and fractionation resolution.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Trust Land Administration
Reform Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) the Bureau of Indian Affairs administers 56 million acres
    of tribal trust land and 10 million acres of individual
    trust land, requiring approval of leases, rights-of-way,
    mortgages, and other transactions;
(b) average processing time for land-into-trust applications
    is 3-5 years, imposing substantial costs on tribal
    economic development;
(c) the HEARTH Act of 2012 demonstrated that tribal self-
    approval of leases reduces processing time from months
    to days without adverse consequences;
(d) the allotment era created over 5 million fractionated
    ownership interests, costing the BIA over $100 million
    annually to administer; and
(e) the Trust Asset and Accounting Management System (TAAMS)
    remains incomplete after more than two decades of
    development.

SEC. 3. PROCESSING TIMELINE MANDATES.

(a) LAND-INTO-TRUST.--
    (1) UNCONTESTED APPLICATIONS.--The Secretary shall process
        all uncontested land-into-trust applications within
        180 days of receipt of a complete application.
    (2) CONTESTED APPLICATIONS.--For applications receiving
        objections, the Secretary shall complete processing
        within 365 days of receipt.
    (3) ENFORCEMENT.--If the Secretary fails to meet the
        timelines in this subsection, the applicant tribe may
        seek a writ of mandamus in the appropriate United
        States District Court.

(b) LEASE AND RIGHT-OF-WAY APPROVALS.--
    (1) The Secretary shall approve or deny lease and right-
        of-way applications within 60 days of receipt.
    (2) Applications not acted upon within 60 days shall be
        deemed approved, provided all regulatory requirements
        have been met.

(c) COMPLETENESS STANDARDS.--
    (1) The Secretary shall publish, within 180 days of
        enactment, detailed standards for what constitutes
        a "complete" application for each transaction type.
    (2) The Secretary may not reset processing timelines by
        finding applications incomplete on technical grounds
        after initial acceptance.

SEC. 4. HEARTH ACT EXPANSION.

(a) The authority of Indian tribes to approve leases of trust
    land without Secretarial approval, as established by the
    HEARTH Act (25 U.S.C. 415(h)), is hereby extended to:
    (1) rights-of-way;
    (2) business site leases;
    (3) residential leases;
    (4) agricultural leases;
    (5) wind and solar energy leases; and
    (6) all other transactions currently requiring
        Secretarial approval on trust land.

(b) Any tribe with an approved HEARTH Act leasing regulation
    may exercise expanded authority under this section upon
    notification to the Secretary.

(c) Tribes without existing HEARTH Act authority may develop
    and submit regulations for Secretarial approval under
    the existing HEARTH Act framework.

SEC. 5. TECHNOLOGY MODERNIZATION.

(a) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.--There is authorized to
    be appropriated $100,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027
    through 2031 for trust land technology modernization.

(b) REQUIREMENTS.--Funds appropriated under this section shall
    be used for--
    (1) completion of TAAMS deployment and data migration;
    (2) digitization of all remaining paper trust records;
    (3) deployment of modern Geographic Information System
        (GIS) technology for trust land management;
    (4) creation of a unified online portal for trust land
        transactions accessible to tribal governments; and
    (5) cybersecurity improvements for trust data systems.

(c) TRIBAL ACCESS.--Tribal governments shall have full online
    access to trust records for land within their jurisdiction.

SEC. 6. FRACTIONATION RESOLUTION.

(a) LAND BUY-BACK EXTENSION.--The Indian Land Consolidation
    Program established under the Cobell settlement is
    hereby extended and reauthorized with an additional
    appropriation of $1,000,000,000 to remain available
    until expended.

(b) PROBATE REFORM.--The American Indian Probate Reform Act
    (25 U.S.C. 2201 et seq.) is amended to--
    (1) establish presumptive tribal acquisition of fractional
        interests below 5 percent upon the death of the owner,
        with fair market compensation to heirs;
    (2) simplify probate proceedings for estates consisting
        solely of fractional trust interests; and
    (3) authorize tribes to establish their own probate codes
        for trust land within their jurisdiction, subject to
        Secretarial approval.

SEC. 7. CARCIERI FIX.

(a) Section 19 of the Indian Reorganization Act (25 U.S.C.
    5129) is amended by striking "now under Federal
    jurisdiction" and inserting "now or hereafter under
    Federal jurisdiction, including any Indian tribe that
    has been federally recognized at any time".

(b) Any land-into-trust application denied or delayed solely
    on the basis of the decision in Carcieri v. Salazar
    (2009) shall be reconsidered under the standard
    established by subsection (a).

SEC. 8. STAFFING.

The Secretary shall hire not fewer than 200 additional realty
specialists and 100 additional title examiners within 3 years
of the date of enactment.

SEC. 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

(a) Sections 3, 4, and 7 shall take effect 1 year after the
    date of enactment.
(b) All other sections shall take effect on the date of
    enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the most persistent operational failure of the BIA -- trust land administration. The processing timeline mandates with mandamus enforcement give tribes a legal remedy when BIA fails to act. The HEARTH Act expansion builds on proven success. The Carcieri fix resolves an inequity that has persisted since 2009.

**Potential Challenges**:

- BIA may argue it cannot meet 180-day timelines without additional resources (addressed by staffing provision and technology investment)
- State and local governments will oppose expedited land-into-trust processing
- Deemed-approved provisions for leases may face legal challenges
- Fractionation probate reforms may face due process challenges from heirs

**Refinements**:

- Include payments-in-lieu-of-taxes provisions for local governments affected by trust acquisitions
- Phase deemed-approval provision with a 2-year implementation delay to allow BIA staffing
- Include environmental review streamlining that maintains NEPA compliance while reducing redundant reviews
- Provide tribal probate code technical assistance

### Bill 3: BIA Funding Stability Act

**Purpose**: Convert core BIA programs to mandatory appropriations and provide advance appropriations for remaining programs, ensuring adequate and predictable funding.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "BIA Funding Stability Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) the United States Commission on Civil Rights found in 2018
    that the federal government underfunds tribal programs by
    approximately 60 percent relative to need;
(b) the BIA's annual budget of approximately $4.1 billion is
    subject to government shutdowns, sequestration, and
    continuing resolutions that disrupt service delivery;
(c) BIA law enforcement is funded at $38 per capita compared
    to $130 per capita for comparable rural jurisdictions;
(d) advance appropriations for the Indian Health Service
    demonstrated that funding stability improves service
    delivery; and
(e) treaty obligations are binding legal commitments that
    should not depend on annual discretionary appropriations.

SEC. 3. MANDATORY APPROPRIATIONS.

(a) IN GENERAL.--The following BIA programs are hereby
    reclassified as mandatory spending:
    (1) Public Safety and Justice (BIA Office of Justice
        Services, tribal courts, detention facilities);
    (2) Trust Land Administration (realty services, title
        plant, appraisals, resource management);
    (3) Self-Governance Compact Funding (compact base
        funding and contract support costs); and
    (4) Social Services (welfare assistance, Indian Child
        Welfare Act programs, housing improvement).

(b) FUNDING LEVELS.--
    (1) INITIAL LEVEL.--For the first fiscal year beginning
        after the date of enactment, the mandatory funding
        level for each program shall be not less than the
        greater of--
        (A) the fiscal year 2024 enacted level plus 25
            percent; or
        (B) the amount determined by the funding formula
            established under subsection (c).
    (2) ANNUAL ADJUSTMENT.--Funding levels shall be adjusted
        annually based on the Consumer Price Index, changes
        in eligible population, and documented cost changes.

(c) NEEDS-BASED FORMULA.--The Secretary, in consultation with
    tribal nations, shall develop a transparent formula
    considering tribal population, geographic service area,
    demonstrated need, and treaty provisions. The formula
    shall be reviewed every 5 years.

SEC. 4. ADVANCE APPROPRIATIONS.

For all BIA programs not reclassified under Section 3, advance
appropriations shall be provided for the fiscal year following
the fiscal year for which the regular appropriation is made.

SEC. 5. SHUTDOWN PROTECTION.

All BIA programs, whether funded through mandatory or
discretionary appropriations, shall be exempt from the effects
of any lapse in appropriations that results in a government
shutdown.

SEC. 6. SEQUESTRATION EXEMPTION.

Programs funded under this Act shall be exempt from
sequestration under the Balanced Budget and Emergency Deficit
Control Act of 1985 (2 U.S.C. 900 et seq.).

SEC. 7. NO-REDUCTION FLOOR.

Funding for any program under this Act shall not fall below
the highest level previously enacted for that program,
adjusted for inflation.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect beginning with the first fiscal
year that begins after the date that is 1 year after the
date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill treats core BIA trust obligations as what they are -- mandatory, not discretionary. The 25% initial increase begins to close the 60% funding gap documented by the U.S. Commission on Civil Rights. The needs-based formula prevents arbitrary allocation. The no-reduction floor prevents future backsliding.

**Potential Challenges**:

- CBO scoring will show significant mandatory spending increases ($2-4 billion annually)
- Appropriations committees will resist losing discretionary control
- May require budget reconciliation to overcome Senate filibuster
- Formula design will be contentious given tribal diversity

**Refinements**:

- Phase mandatory conversion over 3 years, starting with law enforcement and trust administration
- Include a budget offset package to address fiscal concerns
- Start with advance appropriations for all programs, then pursue mandatory conversion

### Bill 4: Indian Country Law Enforcement Enhancement Act

**Purpose**: Increase BIA law enforcement staffing, modernize equipment and facilities, and improve coordination with tribal and federal law enforcement.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Indian Country Law Enforcement
Enhancement Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) the BIA Office of Justice Services employs approximately
    350 law enforcement officers to serve 56 million acres
    of trust land across more than 200 reservations;
(b) the Department of Justice has estimated that approximately
    3,000 officers are needed for adequate coverage;
(c) average response times on remote reservations range from
    30 to 90 minutes, compared to 15-20 minutes for
    comparable rural areas;
(d) the violent crime rate in Indian Country is 2.5 times the
    national average; and
(e) the federal declination rate for Indian Country criminal
    cases is 52 percent.

SEC. 3. STAFFING.

(a) The Secretary shall increase BIA Office of Justice Services
    law enforcement staffing to not fewer than 2,000 officers
    within 5 years of the date of enactment.

(b) The Secretary shall provide funding for tribal law
    enforcement programs under P.L. 93-638 at levels
    sufficient to achieve an overall staffing ratio of not
    more than 1 officer per 30,000 acres of trust land.

SEC. 4. EQUIPMENT AND FACILITIES.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.--There is authorized to be appropriated
    $100,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2031
    for law enforcement equipment modernization.

(b) DETENTION FACILITIES.--There is authorized to be
    appropriated $500,000,000 for fiscal years 2027 through
    2033 for detention facility construction, modernization,
    or replacement.

SEC. 5. CROSS-DEPUTIZATION.

(a) The Attorney General, in coordination with the Secretary,
    shall facilitate cross-deputization agreements among
    federal, tribal, and state law enforcement agencies in
    Indian Country.

(b) Cross-deputization agreements shall be standardized to the
    extent practicable and shall include provisions for
    liability, training, and jurisdiction.

SEC. 6. MISSING AND MURDERED INDIGENOUS PERSONS.

(a) The BIA Missing and Murdered Indigenous Persons Unit shall
    be staffed with not fewer than 50 dedicated investigators.

(b) There is authorized to be appropriated $25,000,000 annually
    for the MMIP Unit.

SEC. 7. RECRUITMENT AND RETENTION.

(a) BONUSES.--BIA law enforcement officers serving in remote
    duty stations shall receive recruitment bonuses of up to
    $30,000 and retention bonuses of up to $20,000 annually.

(b) HOUSING.--The Secretary shall provide or subsidize housing
    for BIA law enforcement officers in communities where
    adequate housing is unavailable.

(c) ACADEMY EXPANSION.--The Indian Police Academy at Artesia,
    New Mexico, shall expand capacity to train not fewer than
    500 officers annually.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the most acute public safety crisis in BIA administration. The staffing mandate is based on DOJ's own estimate of need. The recruitment and retention provisions address the practical reality that remote duty stations are difficult to fill.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Recruiting 1,650 additional officers for remote locations will be difficult even with bonuses
- Detention facility construction in remote areas is expensive and slow
- Jurisdictional complexity means more officers alone do not solve the prosecution gap
- State and local law enforcement may resist cross-deputization

**Refinements**:

- Pair with tribal criminal jurisdiction restoration (see [Tribal Sovereignty: Legislation](../tribal-sovereignty/11-legislation.md)) to address prosecution gaps
- Include alternatives to incarceration (restorative justice, treatment programs) for tribal communities that prefer them
- Phase staffing increases to align with academy capacity expansion
- Include tribal police officer training at the Indian Police Academy

### Bill 5: Federal Recognition Reform Act

**Purpose**: Reform the Part 83 federal recognition process to be faster, fairer, and adequately resourced.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Federal Recognition Reform Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) the Office of Federal Acknowledgment employs approximately
    12 staff to process federal recognition petitions;
(b) average processing time from petition to decision is 15-25
    years;
(c) the cost to petitioning groups ranges from $500,000 to
    $5,000,000 in historical research and legal fees;
(d) these delays and costs effectively deny access to
    recognition for legitimate Indigenous communities with
    limited resources; and
(e) seven tribes have been recognized by Congress since 2000,
    bypassing the administrative process and creating
    inconsistency.

SEC. 3. OFA STAFFING.

(a) The Secretary shall increase OFA staffing to not fewer
    than 36 full-time employees within 2 years of the date
    of enactment, including historians, anthropologists,
    genealogists, and administrative support.

(b) AUTHORIZATION.--There is authorized to be appropriated
    $10,000,000 annually for OFA operations.

SEC. 4. PROCESSING TIMELINES.

(a) PRELIMINARY FINDING.--The OFA shall issue a preliminary
    finding on each complete petition within 3 years of
    receipt.

(b) FINAL DETERMINATION.--The OFA shall issue a final
    determination within 5 years of receipt.

(c) ACTIVE PETITIONS.--Petitions pending on the date of
    enactment shall receive preliminary findings within 2
    years.

SEC. 5. TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE.

(a) The Secretary shall provide technical assistance to
    petitioning groups in compiling documentation required
    under 25 C.F.R. Part 83.

(b) AUTHORIZATION.--There is authorized to be appropriated
    $5,000,000 annually for petitioner technical assistance.

(c) Technical assistance shall include access to federal
    records relevant to the petition.

SEC. 6. INDEPENDENT REVIEW.

(a) REVIEW PANEL.--There is established an independent Federal
    Recognition Review Panel consisting of 5 members appointed
    by the Secretary, including at least 2 persons with
    expertise in anthropology or ethnohistory and at least 1
    member who is an enrolled member of a federally recognized
    tribe.

(b) AUTHORITY.--Any petitioning group denied recognition may
    appeal to the Review Panel within 180 days of the final
    determination.

(c) STANDARD OF REVIEW.--The Review Panel shall conduct de
    novo review of the factual record and may affirm, reverse,
    or remand the determination.

SEC. 7. TRANSPARENCY.

(a) The OFA shall publish detailed guidance on evidentiary
    standards for each recognition criterion, including case
    examples from resolved petitions.

(b) All petition materials, OFA analyses, and determinations
    shall be publicly available, subject to privacy and
    culturally sensitive information protections.

SEC. 8. CONGRESSIONAL RECOGNITION STANDARDS.

(a) Congress should apply the same evidentiary criteria used
    in the Part 83 process when considering recognition by
    legislation.

(b) Congressional committees considering recognition
    legislation shall request and consider an OFA report on
    whether the petitioning group meets Part 83 criteria.

SEC. 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill maintains the substantive rigor of the Part 83 process while making it accessible and timely. Tripling OFA staff and providing technical assistance to petitioners addresses the two main barriers: institutional capacity and petitioner cost.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Some existing tribes may oppose faster recognition (resource competition)
- Independent review panel may be viewed as duplicating IBIA functions
- Congressional recognition standards are advisory only (Congress cannot bind itself)
- 5-year timeline may still be insufficient for complex petitions

**Refinements**:

- Include a provision that new recognitions do not reduce funding to existing tribes
- Clarify relationship between Review Panel and IBIA
- Allow timeline extensions for petitions requiring additional research with petitioner consent
- Include provisions for state-recognized tribes to use state records in federal petitions

## State Model Legislation

### State-Tribal Cooperative Agreement Act

**Purpose**: Provide a framework for state and tribal government cooperation on services, jurisdiction, and revenue sharing.

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Tribal Cooperative
Agreement Act".

SECTION 2. AUTHORIZATION.

The Governor is authorized to enter into cooperative
agreements with federally recognized Indian tribes within
or adjacent to [State] for the purposes of:
(a) sharing law enforcement, emergency, and public safety
    services;
(b) coordinating environmental protection and natural
    resource management;
(c) providing joint infrastructure services (roads,
    utilities, broadband);
(d) establishing revenue sharing for economic activities
    generating cross-jurisdictional benefits; and
(e) resolving jurisdictional questions through mutual
    agreement rather than litigation.

SECTION 3. STATE TRIBAL LIAISON OFFICE.

There is established within the Office of the Governor a
State Tribal Liaison Office, responsible for coordinating
state-tribal relations and facilitating cooperative
agreements under this Act.

SECTION 4. PAYMENTS IN LIEU OF TAXES.

(a) The State shall negotiate PILT agreements with tribes
    acquiring trust land within [State] to offset local
    government revenue impacts.
(b) PILT agreements shall be based on documented revenue
    impact and shall not exceed the property tax that would
    otherwise be assessed.
```

## Regulatory Framework

### BIA Trust Land Processing Regulations

**Existing Authority**: 25 C.F.R. Parts 150-162 (trust land transactions); 25 C.F.R. Part 151 (land-into-trust).

**Draft Regulation**:

The Bureau of Indian Affairs shall promulgate updated regulations:

1. **Application completeness**: Publish detailed checklist standards for each transaction type; applications meeting the checklist are deemed complete upon submission; BIA may not request additional information after 30 days of receipt
2. **Processing tracking**: Implement an online tracking system allowing tribal applicants to monitor application status in real time
3. **Expedited processing**: Establish a "fast track" for applications involving housing construction, infrastructure development, or emergency needs
4. **Environmental review streamlining**: Adopt categorical exclusions for trust land transactions on previously developed land or land adjacent to existing trust land
5. **Tribal notification**: BIA must notify tribal applicants within 14 days of any deficiency and provide specific guidance on resolution

### BIA Performance Accountability Regulations

**Existing Authority**: Interior Department organizational authority; Government Performance and Results Act (GPRA).

**Draft Regulation**:

1. **Performance metrics**: BIA shall establish and publish measurable performance standards for all major functions (processing times, service delivery quality, staffing levels, trust fund accuracy)
2. **Tribal satisfaction surveys**: BIA shall conduct annual tribal satisfaction surveys and publish results; survey design shall be developed in consultation with NCAI
3. **Regional office accountability**: Regional Directors shall be evaluated against performance metrics; persistent underperformance shall trigger corrective action plans
4. **Independent oversight**: An independent Office of Trust Accountability, staffed by personnel outside the BIA chain of command, shall audit trust administration quarterly and report to the Secretary and Congress

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Authority

- **Commerce Clause**: Congress has clear authority under the Indian Commerce Clause (Art. I, Sec. 8, Cl. 3) to restructure BIA and regulate Indian affairs, as affirmed in *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023)
- **Spending Clause**: Mandatory funding for treaty obligations is within congressional spending power; treaty obligations provide stronger legal foundation than typical spending legislation
- **Property Clause**: Congressional authority over trust land administration is well established
- **Necessary and Proper Clause**: Supports legislation implementing treaty obligations and trust responsibility

### Administrative Law Implications

- Processing timeline mandates are enforceable through mandamus actions under 28 U.S.C. 1361
- Deemed-approval provisions for leases are consistent with other federal administrative frameworks
- Performance accountability regulations are within the Secretary's organizational authority
- Independent review for federal recognition creates a quasi-judicial process subject to APA review

### Federal Workforce Considerations

- Self-governance expansion will reduce the need for some BIA positions; legislation should include workforce transition provisions (retraining, reassignment, early retirement)
- Indian Preference (25 U.S.C. 5116) applies to BIA hiring and must be maintained in any reorganization
- Federal employee unions (AFGE) must be consulted on workforce impacts under existing labor agreements

### Trust Responsibility Implications

- Mandatory funding strengthens trust responsibility by ensuring obligations are met
- Self-governance compacting transfers management but not the underlying trust obligation
- Trust administration separation (creating a standalone office) must ensure fiduciary standards are maintained or enhanced
- HEARTH Act expansion transfers approval authority but tribes assume fiduciary responsibility for transactions they approve

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Universal Tribal Self-Governance Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Agency delay | Federal agencies could slow-walk compact negotiations indefinitely | High |
| Inadequate contract support costs | Historical pattern of underfunding contract support costs could continue despite bill language | High |
| Program exclusion | Agencies could exclude major programs from compacting eligibility on thin justifications | Medium |
| Reporting burden creep | Despite "reduced reporting" language, agencies could reimpose burdensome requirements through regulation | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Capacity gap for small tribes | Smallest tribes may not benefit from compacting even with technical assistance | Inherent limitation of institutional reform for communities with fewer than 100 members |
| No enforcement mechanism for mandatory offers | Bill requires BIA to offer compacts but has no penalty for failure | Oversight gap |
| Does not restructure BIA itself | Bill expands compacting but does not address BIA's internal mission conflicts | Focused scope |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Include specific timelines for compact negotiation (120 days) with escalation to the Secretary for delays
2. Establish an automatic contract support cost calculation that agencies cannot reduce without tribal consent
3. Require agencies to publish lists of eligible programs with justification for any exclusions; tribes may challenge exclusions through administrative appeal
4. Cap reporting requirements at outcome-based metrics; prohibit agencies from adding process requirements without OMB approval

### Trust Land Administration Reform Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Completeness gamesmanship | BIA could define "complete" narrowly to reject applications and restart the clock | High |
| Environmental review bottleneck | NEPA review could become the new bottleneck if BIA shifts delay to environmental compliance | High |
| Deemed-approval gaming | BIA could issue last-minute denials to avoid deemed approval | Medium |
| Carcieri workaround | Future administrations could interpret "under Federal jurisdiction" narrowly despite the fix | Low |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address Alaska Native land issues | ANCSA land regime is separate from trust land system | Requires Alaska-specific legislation |
| Technology modernization may take longer than authorized | IT projects in government routinely exceed timelines | Government IT procurement challenges |
| Fractionation continues to grow despite buy-back | Buy-back addresses existing fractionation but new fractionation occurs through inheritance | Requires fundamental probate reform |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Require BIA to accept applications that meet published checklist standards; completeness determinations must be made within 14 days
2. Establish categorical NEPA exclusions for trust acquisitions on previously developed land
3. Require 30-day advance notice before any denial, with opportunity for applicant to cure deficiencies
4. Include a severability clause ensuring the Carcieri fix survives if other provisions are challenged

### BIA Funding Stability Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Formula manipulation | Future administrations could adjust formula inputs to reduce funding | High |
| CPI inadequacy | National CPI may not reflect costs in remote tribal communities | Medium |
| Program reclassification | Future Congresses could reclassify programs back to discretionary | High |
| Administrative absorption | Federal agencies could absorb disproportionate shares for overhead | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Initial 25% increase may still be inadequate | 60% underfunding requires more than 25% increase to close | CBO scoring constraints and political feasibility |
| Does not cover all tribal programs | Only core BIA programs reclassified; other agencies remain discretionary | Scope limitation |
| Formula cannot capture all tribal diversity | 574 tribes have vastly different circumstances | Inherent limitation of formula-based allocation |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Require tribal advisory commission participation in all formula revisions; no formula changes without tribal consultation
2. Develop tribal-specific cost indices reflecting actual costs in reservation communities
3. Include a "ratchet" provision (Section 7) preventing funding reductions below highest enacted level
4. Require that at least 85% of appropriated funds reach tribal programs directly; cap federal administrative absorption at 15%

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Federal agency resistance to compacting | Mandatory negotiation timelines with escalation; OMB oversight |
| BIA institutional inertia on trust reform | Processing mandates with mandamus enforcement; independent accountability office |
| Data gaps on BIA performance | Require standardized data collection and public reporting |
| Workforce transition challenges | Retraining, reassignment, and early retirement provisions; tribal hiring preference for transferred positions |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Funding formula reviewed every 5 years with tribal consultation (Sec. 3(c) of Funding Act)
- Self-governance implementation reported biennially to Congress (Sec. 7 of Self-Governance Act)
- Trust land processing compliance audited annually by Interior IG
- Federal recognition timelines reviewed after resolution of current backlog
