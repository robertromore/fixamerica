# Bureau of Indian Affairs: Actions

## Overview

Reforming the Bureau of Indian Affairs requires action at every level -- from individual education about the federal trust relationship to organized political campaigns for institutional change. This document provides specific, actionable steps for citizens, communities, and organizations to support BIA reform and tribal self-governance.

## Individual Actions

### Educate Yourself

- **Understand the trust relationship**: Learn what the federal trust responsibility is, how it differs from a welfare program, and why it exists (treaties exchanging land for perpetual obligations)
- **Read foundational works**: *The Great Father* by Francis Paul Prucha (institutional history), *Blood Struggle* by Charles Wilkinson (self-determination era), and *The Heartbeat of Wounded Knee* by David Treuer (contemporary context)
- **Follow the numbers**: The U.S. Commission on Civil Rights' *Broken Promises* report (2018) documents the gap between trust obligations and actual funding
- **Understand self-governance**: Learn how 638 contracts and self-governance compacts work -- they are the most successful reform in BIA history
- **Follow Indigenous media**: Indian Country Today, Native News Online, Indianz.com, and High Country News (Indigenous Affairs) cover BIA issues

### Direct Support

- **Support tribal governance capacity**: Organizations like the Self-Governance Communication and Education Tribal Consortium and First Nations Development Institute provide technical assistance to tribal governments
- **Donate to legal advocacy**: NARF (Native American Rights Fund) litigates cases that hold the BIA accountable for trust administration failures
- **Support BIE students**: Organizations working on tribal education include the National Indian Education Association and specific tribal education departments
- **Attend public comment periods**: When BIA proposes rule changes, land-into-trust decisions, or policy modifications, public comment periods are open to all citizens

### Professional Actions

- **Legal professionals**: Provide pro bono support for federal recognition petitions (which cost petitioning groups $500K-$5M in legal and research fees)
- **IT professionals**: BIA technology modernization needs are enormous; participate in federal contracting opportunities or volunteer with tribal organizations
- **Educators**: Incorporate accurate federal Indian policy history into curricula, including the boarding school era and self-determination movement
- **Government employees**: If you work for a federal agency, support tribal consultation requirements and self-governance compacting expansion
- **Journalists**: Cover BIA reform issues; the bureau receives minimal national media attention despite managing $4.1 billion and serving 9.7 million people

## Community Actions

### Local Government

- **Support land-into-trust applications**: When tribes in your area apply to take land into trust, provide constructive input rather than reflexive opposition; understand that trust land supports tribal economic development that benefits the surrounding community
- **Develop cooperative agreements**: Encourage municipal and county governments to develop cooperative service agreements with nearby tribal nations (emergency services, road maintenance, environmental protection)
- **Advocate for payments-in-lieu-of-taxes**: Support PILT provisions that offset local revenue impacts from trust land acquisitions
- **Engage in tribal-local coordination**: Attend intergovernmental meetings and build relationships between local and tribal officials

### Educational Institutions

- **Curriculum on federal Indian policy**: Support inclusion of the BIA's history -- removal, allotment, boarding schools, termination, self-determination -- in U.S. history courses
- **Tribal partnerships**: Encourage universities to partner with tribal colleges and universities on research relevant to BIA reform
- **Research ethics**: Support tribal data sovereignty protocols in academic research involving BIA data or tribal communities
- **Support boarding school accountability**: University archives may contain records related to the Federal Indian Boarding School Initiative; facilitate access for tribal researchers

### Faith Communities

- **Boarding school reconciliation**: Many religious denominations operated BIA-funded boarding schools; support institutional accountability, apology, and restitution
- **Sacred sites protection**: Advocate for BIA trust land management that protects Indigenous sacred sites
- **Social service partnerships**: Support faith-based organizations working alongside (not replacing) tribal social service programs

## Political Actions

### Federal Level

- **Contact your members of Congress**: Call or write to support:
    - Self-governance compacting expansion to all federal agencies
    - Advance appropriations or mandatory funding for all BIA programs
    - Land-into-trust processing reform (180-day timeline mandate)
    - Federal recognition reform (staffing and timeline)
    - BIE facilities emergency funding
    - BIA law enforcement staffing increase
    - Carcieri fix legislation

- **Engage the Senate Committee on Indian Affairs**: Monitor committee hearings on BIA oversight and submit public comments
- **Support Interior Department accountability**: Advocate for strong, qualified nominees for Assistant Secretary for Indian Affairs
- **Engage GAO and IG processes**: When GAO or the Interior Inspector General issue reports on BIA deficiencies, cite them in communications with your representatives

### State Level

- **Support state-tribal cooperative agreements**: Advocate for state-level coordination with BIA and tribal governments on law enforcement, social services, and natural resources
- **Oppose state interference with trust land**: Monitor state legislative proposals that seek to restrict land-into-trust or expand state jurisdiction over trust land
- **Support retrocession in PL 280 states**: In states where PL 280 transferred jurisdiction without tribal consent, advocate for tribal-initiated retrocession to BIA/federal jurisdiction
- **Advocate for state tribal liaison offices**: Support creation of state-level offices that coordinate with BIA and tribal governments

## Template: Email to Member of Congress

> Subject: Support Bureau of Indian Affairs Reform
>
> Dear [Senator/Representative] [Last Name],
>
> As your constituent in [City, State], I am writing to urge your support for reforming the Bureau of Indian Affairs. The BIA manages a $4.1 billion budget, administers 66 million acres of trust land, and serves 574 tribal nations -- yet it is chronically underfunded, understaffed, and burdened by processing backlogs that delay tribal economic development by years.
>
> I urge you to support:
>
> 1. **Self-governance compacting expansion**: Allow all tribes to manage their own programs across all federal agencies, building on 50 years of proven success under P.L. 93-638
>
> 2. **Advance appropriations for all BIA programs**: Protect tribal services from government shutdowns and budget uncertainty, following the IHS precedent
>
> 3. **Trust land processing reform**: Mandate 180-day processing for land-into-trust applications, which currently take 3-5 years
>
> 4. **Law enforcement funding**: BIA has 350 officers for 56 million acres; the Department of Justice estimates 3,000 are needed
>
> 5. **BIE school facilities**: 63% of Bureau of Indian Education schools are in poor or critical condition, with a $1.3 billion deferred maintenance backlog
>
> These are trust obligations -- promises the federal government made to tribal nations in exchange for the land our country occupies. Honoring them is a matter of national integrity.
>
> Thank you for your attention to this important matter.
>
> Sincerely,
> [Your Name]
> [Your Address]

## Talking Points

### For Conservative Audiences

- Self-governance compacting reduces federal bureaucracy: tribes running their own programs are 15-20% more efficient than BIA administration
- The BIA is the poster child for bloated government: 5,000 federal employees, $4.1 billion budget, and some of the worst service outcomes in the federal government
- Self-reliance, not dependency: the self-determination model empowers tribal communities to solve their own problems
- Contract compliance: treaties are contracts; the federal government should honor its contracts

### For Liberal Audiences

- The BIA's history is one of America's greatest civil rights failures: removal, allotment, boarding schools, termination
- Trust obligations are social justice obligations: the federal government owes tribal nations services in exchange for hundreds of millions of acres of land
- The BIE achievement gap is an education equity crisis: 19% reading proficiency vs. 33% nationally
- Self-determination is empowerment: tribal management produces better outcomes than federal administration

### For General Audiences

- The BIA has 350 law enforcement officers for an area the size of the state of Nevada -- that is one officer per 160,000 acres
- The federal government admits it underfunds tribal programs by approximately 60% (U.S. Commission on Civil Rights, 2018)
- When tribes manage their own programs, they do it better and cheaper than the federal government
- BIA schools have a $1.3 billion maintenance backlog; 63% of school buildings are in poor or critical condition

## Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Reality |
|--------------|---------|
| "The BIA takes care of Native Americans" | The BIA chronically underfunds its trust obligations; it spends $38/person on law enforcement compared to $130/person for comparable rural areas |
| "Self-governance means tribes don't need federal money" | Self-governance compacts transfer management, not the obligation to fund; tribes still depend on federal appropriations to fulfill treaty promises |
| "The BIA is a welfare agency" | The BIA administers trust obligations arising from treaties -- legally binding agreements in which tribes exchanged land for federal services |
| "BIA reform is an Indian issue" | The BIA manages resources worth billions and serves millions of people; its dysfunction costs all taxpayers through inefficiency, litigation, and unrealized economic potential |
| "Tribes already get enough money" | The U.S. Commission on Civil Rights found a 60% funding shortfall; IHS spends one-third per capita compared to Medicare; BIE schools are literally falling apart |
