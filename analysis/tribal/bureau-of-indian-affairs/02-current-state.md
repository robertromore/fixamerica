### Fractionation Crisis

The allotment era (1887-1934) divided communally held tribal land into individual allotments. As allottees died without wills, ownership interests fractionated across generations:

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Fractionated land interests | 5+ million | ILCA Program, 2023 |
| Most fractionated tract | Over 10,000 co-owners | BIA records |
| Administrative cost per transaction | Up to $50,000 for probate of a single allotment | GAO, 2018 |
| Annual cost of fractionation administration | $100+ million | BIA Trust Services |
| Cobell settlement -- land buy-back | $1.9 billion authorized; $2.1 billion spent as of 2023 | DOI, 2023 |
| Acres consolidated through buy-back | 3.1 million acres of fractional interests | DOI, 2023 |

### Trust Performance Issues

| Issue | Description | Status |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| Cobell litigation legacy | Class action over trust fund mismanagement; $3.4 billion settlement (2009) | Settlement implemented; systemic reforms ongoing |
| Appraisal backlogs | Land appraisals for leasing/sales delayed 6-18 months | Persistent |
| Leasing delays | Approval of agricultural, business, and residential leases takes 6-24 months | Improving under HEARTH Act (tribes can approve own leases) |
| Probate backlogs | Estate proceedings for deceased allottees backlogged by thousands of cases | ~7,000 pending cases |
| Title records | Land title records often incomplete, inaccurate, or in paper-only format | TAAMS system partially modernized |
| Oil and gas accounting | Revenue collection and distribution from energy production on trust land | Improved post-Cobell but ongoing concerns |

### Criticism of the Process

- Process is prohibitively expensive for petitioning groups ($500,000 to $5 million in historical research and legal fees)
- Criteria have been applied inconsistently across petitions
- Political interference has been alleged in several decisions
- The process requires continuous community and political authority since "historical times," a standard some scholars consider ahistorical
- Groups denied recognition have limited appeal options

## Recent Developments (2024-2025)

### Administrative Actions

- **Federal Indian Boarding School Initiative**: DOI released Volume 2 of the investigative report (2024), identifying 523 boarding school sites, 87 burial sites, and documenting widespread abuse; oral history collection continues
- **Office of the Special Trustee reorganization**: Ongoing consolidation of trust functions into BIA; OST transitioning to Office of Trust Administration within AS-IA
- **HEARTH Act expansion**: Additional tribes approved to exercise leasing authority without BIA approval
- **MMIWP Unit**: Not Invisible Act Commission issued recommendations; DOI created dedicated Missing and Murdered Unit

### Congressional Action

- **Advance Appropriations**: BIA received partial advance appropriations authority, providing some funding stability
- **Contract Support Costs**: Full funding maintained following *Salazar v. Ramah Navajo Chapter* (2012)
- **Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act**: $13 billion for tribal infrastructure, with BIA receiving approximately $3 billion for roads, bridges, and facilities

### GAO/IG Reports

- GAO-23-105442 (2023): Criticized BIA and DOJ data collection on Indian Country criminal justice
- Interior IG (2023): Identified significant IT security vulnerabilities in BIA trust systems
- GAO-23-106280 (2023): Found BIE school facilities in dangerous condition; recommended accelerated construction timeline
