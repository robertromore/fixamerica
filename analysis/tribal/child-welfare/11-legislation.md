# Tribal Child Welfare: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative framework for tribal child welfare centers on the Indian Child Welfare Act of 1978 (ICWA), which establishes minimum federal standards for the removal and placement of Indian children. While ICWA's constitutionality was upheld in *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023), significant enforcement gaps remain. The legislation proposed here addresses those gaps through federal enforcement mechanisms, expanded tribal capacity, workforce development, and kinship care reform. These proposals complement ICWA rather than replacing it, building on four decades of implementation experience to close the loopholes that have allowed continued disproportionate harm to Native children and families.

## Federal Legislation

### ICWA Enforcement Act

**Purpose**: Establish mandatory federal compliance monitoring and enforcement mechanisms for ICWA, with financial consequences for state non-compliance.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Indian Child Welfare Act
Enforcement Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(1) the Indian Child Welfare Act of 1978 (25 U.S.C. 1901 et seq.)
    has been in effect for over 47 years;
(2) despite ICWA's requirements, state compliance remains
    inconsistent, with estimated national compliance rates between
    40 and 60 percent;
(3) American Indian and Alaska Native children remain approximately
    2.7 times more likely to be in foster care than their share of
    the child population;
(4) the Supreme Court upheld ICWA's constitutionality in Haaland
    v. Brackeen, 599 U.S. 255 (2023); and
(5) effective enforcement mechanisms are necessary to ensure ICWA's
    protections are realized for all Indian children.

SEC. 3. MANDATORY COMPLIANCE REPORTING.

(a) IN GENERAL.--Each State receiving funds under part B or part E
of title IV of the Social Security Act (42 U.S.C. 620 et seq.,
670 et seq.) shall, as a condition of such funding, submit to the
Secretary of Health and Human Services an annual report on the
State's compliance with the Indian Child Welfare Act, including--
    (1) the number of child welfare proceedings involving children
        identified as Indian children;
    (2) the rate of tribal notification within required timelines;
    (3) the rate of active efforts documentation;
    (4) the rate of compliance with placement preferences;
    (5) the number and disposition of transfer petitions;
    (6) the number and basis of good cause findings to deviate
        from placement preferences; and
    (7) outcomes data for Indian children in the State's care.

(b) TRIBAL VERIFICATION.--Each report under subsection (a) shall
include a certification from each federally recognized Indian tribe
with members in the State regarding the accuracy of the State's
compliance data, or a statement from the State describing its
efforts to obtain such certification.

SEC. 4. FEDERAL COMPLIANCE AUDITS.

(a) AUDIT AUTHORITY.--The Secretary of Health and Human Services
shall conduct compliance audits of not fewer than 10 States per
year to verify the accuracy of reports submitted under section 3.

(b) TRIBAL PARTICIPATION.--Each audit shall include meaningful
participation by representatives of Indian tribes whose children
are served by the State.

(c) PUBLIC REPORTING.--Audit results shall be published annually
and made available to Indian tribes and the public.

SEC. 5. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) CORRECTIVE ACTION.--A State found to be in substantial
non-compliance with ICWA shall submit a corrective action plan
within 90 days of notification.

(b) FINANCIAL PENALTIES.--If a State fails to submit or implement
a corrective action plan--
    (1) the Secretary may withhold up to 5 percent of the State's
        Title IV-E funding; and
    (2) the Secretary shall redirect withheld funds to Indian
        tribes within the State for child welfare services.

(c) TRIBAL ENFORCEMENT.--An Indian tribe may bring a civil action
in Federal district court against a State agency for a pattern or
practice of ICWA non-compliance, seeking injunctive relief and
reasonable attorneys' fees.

SEC. 6. ICWA OMBUDSMAN.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--There is established within the Department of
Health and Human Services the position of ICWA Ombudsman.

(b) DUTIES.--The ICWA Ombudsman shall--
    (1) receive and investigate complaints of ICWA non-compliance;
    (2) provide technical assistance to States and tribes;
    (3) publish annual reports on ICWA compliance nationwide; and
    (4) make recommendations to Congress for legislative action.

SEC. 7. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated $15,000,000 for each of
fiscal years 2027 through 2032 to carry out this Act.
```

**Explanation**: This bill creates the enforcement infrastructure that ICWA has always lacked. The original Act established substantive requirements but provided no mechanism for monitoring or penalizing non-compliance. By conditioning Title IV-E funding on compliance reporting and authorizing tribal enforcement actions, this bill creates real accountability.

**Potential Challenges**: States may argue that funding penalties are coercive. The Spending Clause analysis under *NFIB v. Sebelius* (2012) could be raised if penalties are deemed disproportionate. The 5% threshold is designed to fall within permissible bounds.

**Refinements**: Consider graduated penalty schedules based on severity and duration of non-compliance. Include safe harbor provisions for states demonstrating good faith compliance efforts.

### Tribal Title IV-E Equity Act

**Purpose**: Streamline tribal access to Title IV-E foster care and prevention funding, eliminating barriers that have limited tribal participation to 12 agreements in 16 years.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Tribal Title IV-E Equity Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. STREAMLINED TRIBAL PLAN APPROVAL.

(a) SIMPLIFIED PLANS.--The Secretary of Health and Human Services
shall establish a simplified plan template for Indian tribes
seeking to operate a Title IV-E program, reducing administrative
requirements while maintaining child safety standards.

(b) TIMELINE.--The Secretary shall approve or provide specific
written objections to a tribal Title IV-E plan within 180 days
of submission.

(c) CONSORTIUM AUTHORITY.--Two or more Indian tribes may submit a
joint Title IV-E plan, sharing administrative infrastructure.

SEC. 3. TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE.

(a) DEDICATED TEAMS.--The Secretary shall establish not fewer than
5 regional technical assistance teams dedicated exclusively to
assisting Indian tribes in developing and implementing Title IV-E
plans.

(b) STARTUP GRANTS.--The Secretary shall award startup grants of
up to $500,000 per tribe to support the development of Title IV-E
plans and administrative infrastructure.

SEC. 4. PREVENTION SERVICES ACCESS.

(a) FAMILY FIRST ACT.--Indian tribes with Title IV-E agreements
shall have full access to prevention services funding under the
Family First Prevention Services Act (Public Law 115-123).

(b) CULTURALLY ADAPTED SERVICES.--The Secretary shall ensure that
the Title IV-E Prevention Services Clearinghouse includes culturally
adapted programs developed for and with Indian communities.

SEC. 5. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated $50,000,000 for each of
fiscal years 2027 through 2032 to carry out this Act.
```

**Explanation**: Since 2008, only 12 tribal IV-E agreements have been approved. The application process is complex and resource-intensive, effectively excluding most tribes. This bill reduces barriers while maintaining accountability.

**Potential Challenges**: HHS capacity to process additional applications and provide technical assistance. Concern that simplified plans could compromise child safety standards.

**Refinements**: Include phased implementation allowing tribes to assume responsibilities incrementally. Require HHS to report annually on application processing times and outcomes.

### Indian Country Child Welfare Workforce Act

**Purpose**: Address the severe shortage of child welfare professionals serving Indian Country through training programs, loan forgiveness, and competitive compensation.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Indian Country Child Welfare
Workforce Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. TRIBAL CHILD WELFARE TRAINING GRANTS.

(a) GRANTS.--The Secretary of Health and Human Services shall
award grants to institutions of higher education, in partnership
with Indian tribes and tribal colleges, to establish or expand
programs training students for careers in tribal child welfare.

(b) PRIORITY.--Priority shall be given to programs that--
    (1) enroll American Indian and Alaska Native students;
    (2) include practicum placements in tribal child welfare
        settings; and
    (3) incorporate culturally grounded practice models.

(c) SERVICE OBLIGATION.--Grant recipients shall commit to a minimum
of 3 years of service in tribal child welfare upon program
completion.

SEC. 3. LOAN FORGIVENESS.

(a) PROGRAM.--The Secretary of Education, in consultation with
the Secretary of Health and Human Services, shall establish a
loan forgiveness program for individuals employed in tribal child
welfare positions.

(b) AMOUNT.--Eligible individuals shall receive forgiveness of
up to $50,000 in student loans after 5 years of full-time
employment in tribal child welfare.

SEC. 4. COMPETITIVE COMPENSATION.

(a) BIA SALARY PARITY.--The Secretary of the Interior shall adjust
compensation for Bureau of Indian Affairs social service positions
to be competitive with comparable state positions in the same
geographic area.

(b) TRIBAL SALARY SUPPORT.--The Secretary of Health and Human
Services shall award grants to Indian tribes to supplement
compensation for tribal child welfare workers to achieve parity
with state counterparts.

SEC. 5. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated $30,000,000 for each of
fiscal years 2027 through 2032 to carry out this Act.
```

**Explanation**: BIA funds only 310 social workers for 574 tribes. Average caseloads are 2-3 times recommended levels. Workers are paid less than state counterparts. This bill addresses recruitment, retention, and capacity simultaneously.

**Potential Challenges**: Competition with other health professions loan forgiveness programs for funding. Ensuring service obligation is enforceable.

**Refinements**: Include continuing education requirements. Develop career ladder opportunities for tribal child welfare workers.

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Indian Child Welfare Act

**Purpose**: Provide a template for state legislation that meets or exceeds federal ICWA requirements.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Indian Child Welfare Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Indian child" has the meaning given in section 4 of the
Indian Child Welfare Act (25 U.S.C. 1903).

(b) "Active efforts" means affirmative, active, thorough, and
timely efforts intended primarily to maintain or reunite an
Indian child with his or her family, as further defined in
25 C.F.R. 23.2.

SECTION 3. APPLICABILITY.

(a) This Act applies to all child custody proceedings, as
defined in 25 U.S.C. 1903(1), involving an Indian child in
this State.

(b) The existing Indian family doctrine is not recognized in
this State.

SECTION 4. TRIBAL NOTIFICATION.

(a) Within 5 days of initiating any child custody proceeding
involving a child who is or may be an Indian child, the agency
or petitioner shall provide written notice to the child's tribe
and the BIA, as required by 25 U.S.C. 1912(a).

(b) The court shall not proceed until proper notice has been
given and either 30 days have elapsed or the tribe has responded.

SECTION 5. ACTIVE EFFORTS.

(a) Before any Indian child is removed from the custody of a
parent or Indian custodian, the agency shall demonstrate to the
court that active efforts have been made to provide remedial
services and rehabilitative programs designed to prevent the
breakup of the Indian family, and that these efforts have proved
unsuccessful.

(b) Active efforts shall include, at minimum, the services
specified in 25 C.F.R. 23.2.

SECTION 6. PLACEMENT PREFERENCES.

(a) In any foster care, pre-adoptive, or adoptive placement of
an Indian child, preference shall be given, in the absence of
good cause to the contrary, to placement with--
    (1) a member of the child's extended family;
    (2) a foster home licensed, approved, or specified by the
        child's tribe;
    (3) an Indian foster home licensed or approved by an
        authorized non-Indian licensing authority; or
    (4) an institution for children approved by an Indian tribe
        or operated by an Indian organization.

(b) Good cause to deviate from placement preferences must be
documented in writing with specific findings on the record.

SECTION 7. MANDATORY TRAINING.

All child welfare workers, judges, attorneys, guardians ad litem,
and court-appointed special advocates involved in child custody
proceedings in this State shall complete at least 8 hours of
initial training and 4 hours of annual continuing education on
ICWA requirements and culturally appropriate practice with
Indian families.

SECTION 8. COMPLIANCE MONITORING.

The [State Agency] shall report annually to the legislature on
ICWA compliance, including the data elements specified in this Act.
```

**Explanation**: This model legislation addresses the most common state-level compliance failures by codifying federal requirements, rejecting the existing Indian family doctrine, and mandating training and reporting.

**Adaptations**: States may need to adjust timelines, training requirements, and reporting structures based on existing infrastructure and tribal population.

## Regulatory Framework

### Enhanced AFCARS Reporting

**Existing Authority**: 42 U.S.C. 679 (AFCARS data collection authority)

**Proposed Regulation**: Amend AFCARS reporting requirements to include mandatory tracking of ICWA-specific data elements: tribal notification dates and responses, active efforts documentation, placement preference compliance, QEW testimony, and transfer petition outcomes.

**Explanation**: Current AFCARS data does not systematically track ICWA compliance indicators. Without this data, national compliance cannot be meaningfully assessed or improved.

### Kinship Care Licensing Flexibility

**Existing Authority**: 42 U.S.C. 671(a)(10) (foster family home standards)

**Proposed Regulation**: Issue guidance clarifying that states and tribes may adopt flexible licensing standards for kinship foster homes that prioritize child safety without imposing requirements unrelated to safety (e.g., bedroom-per-child mandates, minimum square footage unrelated to overcrowding).

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Post-*Brackeen* landscape**: ICWA's constitutionality has been upheld. Future challenges will likely focus on as-applied equal protection claims.
- **Spending Clause**: Federal funding conditions must meet the standards of *South Dakota v. Dole* and *NFIB v. Sebelius*. Penalties must be proportionate and related to the program.
- **Preemption**: ICWA preempts conflicting state law. State ICWA laws that exceed federal requirements are permissible.

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- Tribal enforcement through federal court civil actions
- Federal compliance audits with financial consequences
- State-level reporting requirements
- Ombudsman investigation authority

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- ICWA Enforcement Act authorized for 6 years with mandatory congressional review
- Annual reporting requirements enable ongoing assessment
- GAO review required at 3-year mark

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### ICWA Enforcement Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Self-reporting bias | States may underreport non-compliance in mandatory reports | High |
| Audit sampling | Only 10 states audited per year; non-audited states may escape scrutiny | Medium |
| Good cause ambiguity | "Good cause" exceptions to placement preferences remain subjectively defined | High |
| Tribal verification gap | Small tribes may lack capacity to verify state compliance data | Medium |
| Penalty avoidance | 5% Title IV-E penalty may be insufficient to motivate compliance in large states | Medium |
| Non-Indian child misidentification | Failure to identify Indian children at intake prevents ICWA from applying | Critical |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address private adoptions | ICWA compliance in private agency adoptions harder to monitor | Private agencies less subject to federal oversight |
| Does not reform mandated reporting | Disproportionate reporting continues to funnel Native families into system | Separate from ICWA enforcement per se |
| Limited tribal court resources | Transfer to tribal court meaningless if tribal courts are overwhelmed | Enforcement bill does not address tribal capacity |
| Historical records | Does not address recovery of records from pre-ICWA adoptions and boarding schools | Separate historical justice issue |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Counter self-reporting bias**: Require tribal verification of state reports; fund tribal capacity for independent compliance monitoring
2. **Expand audit scope**: Increase audited states to 15-20 per year; prioritize states with high Native child welfare populations
3. **Define good cause**: Issue binding regulations specifying the only permissible grounds for good cause deviation from placement preferences
4. **Support small tribes**: Provide technical assistance and shared-service models for tribal compliance verification
5. **Graduated penalties**: Implement escalating penalties for repeated non-compliance, up to 10% of Title IV-E funding
6. **Identification protocols**: Require states to implement systematic Indian child identification at initial contact, not just at adjudication

### Tribal Title IV-E Equity Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Simplified plans too simplified | Reduced requirements could compromise child safety standards | Medium |
| HHS bottleneck | Even with 180-day timeline, HHS may lack capacity to process applications | Medium |
| Consortium governance | Multi-tribal consortia may face governance disputes | Low |
| Prevention clearinghouse bias | Clearinghouse may exclude tribally developed programs that lack RCT evidence | High |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address states' IV-E funding loss | States may resist tribal IV-E as reduction in their allocation | Zero-sum funding structure |
| Infrastructure gap | Tribes need more than plans -- they need courts, data systems, and staff | Capacity takes years to build |
| Limited to 574 federally recognized tribes | State-recognized tribes excluded | Federal recognition politics |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Safety floor**: Maintain non-negotiable child safety standards in simplified plans while reducing administrative burden
2. **HHS capacity**: Appropriate dedicated staff for tribal IV-E plan review; prohibit diverting staff to other functions
3. **Consortium model legislation**: Develop model inter-tribal agreements addressing governance, cost-sharing, and dispute resolution
4. **Clearinghouse reform**: Create a dedicated tribal evidence track that accepts practice-based evidence and culturally validated outcomes alongside RCT evidence

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Coordination failure between BIA, HHS, and DOJ | Establish permanent interagency tribal child welfare working group |
| Political vulnerability to administration changes | Codify reforms in statute rather than executive orders; include anti-rollback provisions |
| Funding fragmentation across agencies | Develop consolidated tribal child welfare block grant option |
| Data system incompatibility | Fund interoperable data systems linking tribal, state, and federal child welfare databases |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- All legislation authorized for 6-year terms with mandatory reauthorization
- GAO review at 3-year mark for each bill
- Annual compliance reports to Congress
- Tribal consultation required before any regulatory modifications
- Automatic inflation adjustments for authorized appropriation levels
