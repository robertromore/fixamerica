# Tribal Child Welfare: Implementation Roadmap

## Overview

This roadmap outlines a phased approach to strengthening tribal child welfare over a 10-year horizon. The strategy prioritizes immediate enforcement improvements that can be achieved through executive action and regulatory reform, followed by legislative changes that require congressional action, and concludes with structural transformations that require sustained investment and capacity building. Each phase builds on the previous one, creating cumulative momentum toward the vision of a child welfare system that respects tribal sovereignty and protects Native children.

## Phase 1: Immediate Actions (Years 1-2)

**Theme**: Strengthen enforcement within existing legal framework.

### Administrative Actions (No Legislation Required)

| Action | Responsible Entity | Timeline | Status |
|--------|-------------------|----------|--------|
| Issue updated ICWA compliance guidance post-*Brackeen* | BIA | Months 1-6 | In progress |
| Incorporate ICWA compliance indicators into CFSR process | HHS/Children's Bureau | Months 3-12 | Planned |
| Establish ICWA ombudsman within HHS | HHS Secretary | Months 6-12 | Proposed |
| Require ICWA compliance certification as Title IV-E condition | HHS/Children's Bureau | Months 6-18 | Proposed |
| Update AFCARS data collection to track ICWA-specific indicators | HHS/ACF | Months 12-24 | Proposed |
| Expand technical assistance for tribal IV-E plan development | Children's Bureau | Months 1-12 | In progress |

### Executive Order Priorities

1. **Tribal child welfare executive order**: Direct all federal agencies to prioritize tribal child welfare in funding, policy, and technical assistance
2. **Interagency coordination**: Establish interagency working group (BIA, HHS, DOJ, HUD) on Native child welfare
3. **Consultation requirement**: Mandate meaningful tribal consultation before any policy change affecting Native children

### Quick Wins

- Distribute ICWA judicial benchcards to all state court systems nationally
- Fund NICWA to expand ICWA compliance training for state court judges
- Publish model state ICWA legislation for states without existing laws
- Increase BIA ICWA grant funding through existing appropriations flexibility

## Phase 2: Legislative Foundation (Years 2-4)

**Theme**: Enact federal legislation to close enforcement gaps and increase funding.

### Priority Federal Legislation

| Bill | Purpose | Coalition | Difficulty |
|------|---------|-----------|------------|
| ICWA Enforcement Act | Mandatory compliance reporting, federal enforcement authority, penalties | Bipartisan (post-*Brackeen*) | Moderate |
| Tribal Title IV-E Equity Act | Streamline tribal IV-E applications, increase technical assistance | Moderate bipartisan support | Moderate |
| Indian Country Child Welfare Workforce Act | Loan forgiveness, training programs, competitive compensation | Broad support | Low |
| Kinship Care Parity Act | Equal financial support for kinship and non-kinship foster care | Bipartisan | Moderate |
| Family Preservation in Indian Country Act | Dedicated prevention funding, culturally adapted services | Moderate support | Moderate |

### Funding Targets

| Program | Current | Year 2 Target | Year 4 Target |
|---------|---------|---------------|---------------|
| BIA child welfare | $72 million | $100 million | $150 million |
| ICWA grants | $22.7 million | $40 million | $60 million |
| Tribal IV-E technical assistance | $3 million | $10 million | $15 million |
| Workforce development | $5 million | $15 million | $25 million |
| Prevention services | Minimal tribal-specific | $25 million | $50 million |

### State-Level Campaign

- Target 10 additional states for state ICWA legislation by Year 4
- Develop model legislation adaptable to state contexts
- Build state-level coalitions of child welfare professionals, tribes, and legal advocates
- Provide technical assistance to tribal-state compact development

## Phase 3: Capacity Building (Years 4-7)

**Theme**: Build tribal infrastructure for comprehensive child welfare systems.

### Tribal Program Development

| Initiative | Scope | Investment | Expected Outcome |
|-----------|-------|------------|-----------------|
| Title IV-E expansion | 30 additional tribal IV-E agreements | $50 million over 3 years | Tribal control over 50%+ of Native child welfare cases |
| Tribal foster home recruitment | National campaign targeting tribal communities | $25 million over 3 years | 2,000 additional licensed Native foster homes |
| Kinship navigator programs | 100 tribal communities | $30 million over 3 years | Increased kinship placements, reduced out-of-culture placements |
| Data system development | Tribal child welfare data infrastructure | $20 million over 3 years | Real-time outcomes tracking with data sovereignty |
| Treatment capacity | 20 new residential treatment facilities in Indian Country | $100 million over 3 years | Reduced parental substance abuse as a removal driver |

### Workforce Milestones

| Milestone | Target | Timeline |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| New tribal child welfare workers trained | 500 | Years 4-7 |
| Average caseload reduction | From 30-45 to 15-20 | By Year 7 |
| Cultural competency certification program | National standard established | Year 5 |
| University-tribal training partnerships | 15 new programs | Years 4-7 |
| Social worker retention rate improvement | From ~60% to ~80% | By Year 7 |

### Research and Evaluation

- Fund longitudinal studies of outcomes for Native children placed according to ICWA preferences vs. outside preferences
- Develop tribally validated risk assessment instruments
- Evaluate culturally adapted prevention programs for inclusion in Title IV-E prevention clearinghouse
- Support tribal participatory research on child welfare outcomes

## Phase 4: Structural Transformation (Years 7-10)

**Theme**: Achieve systemic change in how Native children are served.

### Long-Term Goals

| Goal | Metric | Target |
|------|--------|--------|
| Eliminate disproportionality | AI/AN foster care rate vs. population share | 1.0x (parity) by Year 10 |
| Full ICWA compliance | National compliance rate | 95%+ by Year 10 |
| Tribal program coverage | Tribes with comprehensive child welfare programs | 200+ tribes by Year 10 |
| Family preservation | Families served through prevention vs. removal | 3:1 ratio by Year 10 |
| Cultural connection | Children in ICWA-compliant placements | 90%+ by Year 10 |

### Structural Changes

- Transition from state-administered to tribally administered child welfare for reservation-based families
- Establish tribal child welfare court partnerships as the norm, not the exception
- Integrate child welfare with broader tribal family support systems (housing, health, education, economic development)
- Develop inter-tribal networks for foster care, adoption, and service delivery

### Sustainability

- Shift from discretionary to mandatory appropriations for tribal child welfare
- Build endowments and self-sustaining funding mechanisms
- Develop tribal child welfare leadership pipeline
- Institutionalize research-practice partnerships

## Risk Assessment

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|------------|--------|------------|
| Congressional inaction | Moderate | High | Maximize administrative actions; build bipartisan support |
| State resistance to compliance | Moderate | Moderate | Use funding leverage; support state ICWA advocacy |
| Insufficient appropriations | High | High | Build broad coalition; demonstrate cost-effectiveness |
| Tribal capacity constraints | Moderate | Moderate | Phase approach; provide sustained technical assistance |
| New legal challenges | Low | Moderate | *Brackeen* forecloses major challenges; prepare for as-applied cases |
| Administration change | Moderate | Moderate | Codify reforms in legislation; build institutional capacity |
