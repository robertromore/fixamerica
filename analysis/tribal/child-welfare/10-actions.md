# Tribal Child Welfare: Actions

## Overview

This document outlines actionable steps for individuals, communities, organizations, and policymakers who want to improve outcomes for Native children and families. Actions range from immediate personal steps to sustained advocacy campaigns, organized by the level of commitment and resources required.

## Individual Actions

### Learn and Educate

1. **Study ICWA**: Read the Indian Child Welfare Act (25 U.S.C. 1901-1963) and the BIA 2016 regulations (25 C.F.R. Part 23). Understanding the law is the foundation for effective advocacy.

2. **Understand the history**: Read about the boarding school era and the Indian Adoption Project. Resources include the DOI Federal Indian Boarding School Initiative reports (2022, 2024) and Margaret Jacobs's *A Generation Removed*.

3. **Read the *Brackeen* decision**: The Supreme Court's 2023 ruling is the most important recent development in tribal child welfare law.

4. **Follow tribal media**: Subscribe to Indian Country Today, Tribal Business News, and local tribal media for ongoing coverage of child welfare issues.

5. **Share accurate information**: Counter misinformation about ICWA by sharing evidence-based resources from NICWA, NARF, and other reputable organizations.

### Take Direct Action

1. **Become a foster or adoptive parent**: If you are a tribal member, consider becoming a licensed foster parent. Contact your tribe's social services department or NICWA for information.

2. **Support kinship caregivers**: If relatives in your community are caring for children, offer practical support -- meals, transportation, childcare respite, and help navigating systems.

3. **Serve as a qualified expert witness**: If you have expertise in tribal culture and child-rearing, contact NICWA about QEW training and service.

4. **Volunteer as a CASA/guardian ad litem**: Court Appointed Special Advocates serve as voices for children in court proceedings. Ensure you receive ICWA-specific training.

5. **Mentor families at risk**: Participate in or establish peer mentoring programs that pair experienced parents with families navigating challenges.

### Advocate Politically

1. **Contact your state legislators**: Urge them to enact or strengthen state ICWA laws. NICWA provides model legislation and advocacy toolkits.

2. **Contact your congressional delegation**: Support increased appropriations for BIA child welfare, ICWA grants, and tribal Title IV-E technical assistance.

3. **Attend public hearings**: Testify at state legislative hearings on child welfare policy, bringing data and personal experience.

4. **Vote**: Support candidates who prioritize tribal sovereignty and Native child welfare.

5. **Engage local school boards**: Advocate for culturally accurate education about Native history, including boarding school history and ICWA.

## Community Actions

### Tribal Communities

1. **Strengthen kinship networks**: Formalize kinship care systems within the community; establish kinship navigator programs.

2. **Develop prevention programs**: Create community-based family support services that integrate traditional practices -- talking circles, elder mentoring, ceremonial participation.

3. **Recruit foster families**: Launch tribal-led foster home recruitment campaigns targeting community members who can provide culturally grounded care.

4. **Build healing programs**: Establish intergenerational healing initiatives addressing boarding school trauma and its ongoing effects on parenting and family stability.

5. **Advocate for tribal child welfare programs**: Support your tribal government in developing or expanding child welfare services, including pursuing Title IV-E agreements.

6. **Protect children through culture**: Integrate language revitalization, traditional arts, and ceremonial participation into family support programs.

### Non-Native Communities

1. **Learn about ICWA**: Community organizations, churches, and civic groups should educate their members about why ICWA exists and what it requires.

2. **Support culturally appropriate foster care**: Non-Native foster parents caring for Native children should actively facilitate cultural connections -- tribal enrollment, language classes, ceremonial participation.

3. **Advocate for compliance**: If your community includes child welfare professionals, advocate for robust ICWA training and compliance monitoring.

4. **Avoid "savior" narratives**: Recognize that Native children's cultural connection is essential to their well-being, not an obstacle to their welfare.

## Professional Actions

### Child Welfare Professionals

1. **Complete ICWA training**: Every child welfare worker, supervisor, and administrator should complete comprehensive ICWA training. NICWA and Casey Family Programs offer curricula.

2. **Implement active efforts**: Understand the difference between ICWA's "active efforts" and the lower "reasonable efforts" standard. Document active efforts thoroughly.

3. **Identify Indian children early**: Implement systematic procedures for determining whether a child may be an Indian child at the earliest possible point in proceedings.

4. **Notify tribes promptly**: Ensure tribal notification occurs within required timelines and is sent to the correct tribal ICWA designated agent.

5. **Search for kinship placements**: Conduct diligent searches for extended family and other ICWA-preferred placements before considering non-Indian homes.

6. **Retain qualified expert witnesses**: Develop relationships with qualified expert witnesses who can testify about tribal culture and child-rearing practices.

7. **Challenge cultural bias**: Examine your own assumptions about Native families and child-rearing. Seek cultural consultation when working with Native families.

### Legal Professionals

1. **Attorneys**: Learn ICWA requirements and raise ICWA compliance issues in every applicable proceeding. File motions for transfer to tribal court when appropriate.

2. **Judges**: Attend ICWA judicial training (NCJFCJ Benchbook). Use ICWA compliance checklists in every case involving a child who may be Indian.

3. **Guardians ad litem**: Ensure recommendations account for the child's tribal connections and cultural needs, not just immediate placement preferences.

4. **Law professors**: Include ICWA and federal Indian law in family law and child welfare curricula.

### Medical and Mental Health Professionals

1. **Understand historical trauma**: Incorporate trauma-informed, culturally responsive approaches when working with Native families.

2. **Mandated reporting**: Apply mandated reporting obligations with awareness of cultural context. Do not equate poverty with neglect.

3. **Culturally appropriate treatment**: Seek training in culturally adapted therapeutic approaches for Native clients.

4. **Support traditional healing**: Respect and, where possible, integrate traditional healing practices into treatment plans.

## Organizational Actions

### Nonprofits and Foundations

1. **Fund tribal child welfare capacity**: Direct funding to tribal child welfare programs, ICWA compliance monitoring, and workforce development.

2. **Support NICWA**: The National Indian Child Welfare Association is the leading organization working on these issues and needs sustained support.

3. **Fund research**: Support tribally led research on child welfare outcomes, culturally adapted interventions, and ICWA compliance.

4. **Build partnerships**: Collaborate with tribes and tribal organizations rather than designing programs for them.

### Academic Institutions

1. **Train Native social workers**: Develop partnerships with tribal colleges and communities to train Native child welfare professionals.

2. **Research with tribes**: Conduct community-based participatory research that respects tribal data sovereignty and benefits tribal communities.

3. **Include ICWA in curricula**: Social work, law, and public policy programs should include substantive coverage of ICWA and federal Indian law.

4. **Evaluate programs**: Partner with tribes to evaluate prevention and family preservation programs for inclusion in evidence-based clearinghouses.

## Advocacy Campaign Framework

### Short-Term Campaign (1-2 Years)

**Goal**: Increase ICWA compliance in your state.

| Step | Action | Timeline |
|------|--------|----------|
| 1 | Assess current state ICWA compliance (NICWA resources) | Month 1-3 |
| 2 | Build coalition of tribal leaders, child welfare professionals, and legal advocates | Month 3-6 |
| 3 | Draft state ICWA legislation or compliance requirements | Month 6-9 |
| 4 | Lobby state legislature and governor | Month 9-18 |
| 5 | Monitor implementation and compliance | Ongoing |

### Medium-Term Campaign (2-5 Years)

**Goal**: Increase federal funding for tribal child welfare.

| Step | Action | Timeline |
|------|--------|----------|
| 1 | Document funding gaps with data and testimony | Year 1 |
| 2 | Build congressional support through tribal leaders, NICWA, and NCAI | Year 1-2 |
| 3 | Advocate for increased appropriations in Interior and HHS budgets | Year 2-3 |
| 4 | Support introduction of tribal child welfare workforce legislation | Year 3-4 |
| 5 | Monitor implementation and demonstrate impact | Year 4-5 |
