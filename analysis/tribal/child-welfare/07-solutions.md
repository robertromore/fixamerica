# Tribal Child Welfare: Solutions

## Overview

Solutions for tribal child welfare must address multiple dimensions simultaneously: strengthening ICWA enforcement, building tribal program capacity, expanding the foster and kinship care infrastructure, tackling the root causes of family instability, and reforming the systems that produce disproportionate Native child removals. The most effective approaches are those driven by tribal communities themselves, supported by adequate federal funding, and embedded in culturally grounded frameworks.

## Solution Categories

### 1. Strengthen ICWA Enforcement

#### Federal Enforcement Mechanisms

**Problem Addressed**: Inconsistent state compliance with ICWA requirements.

**Proposed Solutions**:

- **Mandatory ICWA compliance reporting**: Require states to report ICWA compliance indicators as a condition of Title IV-E funding
- **Federal compliance audits**: Empower HHS to conduct targeted audits of state ICWA compliance, with financial penalties for non-compliance
- **ICWA ombudsman**: Establish a federal ombudsman position within HHS to receive and investigate ICWA compliance complaints
- **Tribal standing to enforce**: Clarify tribal standing to bring enforcement actions in federal court for ICWA violations

**Evidence of Effectiveness**: States with dedicated ICWA compliance mechanisms (Minnesota, Washington) demonstrate significantly higher compliance rates (NICWA, 2023).

#### State-Level Enforcement

**Problem Addressed**: Lack of state-level accountability for ICWA compliance.

**Proposed Solutions**:

- **State ICWA laws**: Encourage all states to adopt state ICWA statutes that meet or exceed federal requirements
- **Mandatory ICWA training**: Require all child welfare workers, judges, attorneys, and CASAs to complete ICWA training before handling cases involving Indian children
- **Dedicated ICWA units**: Fund specialized ICWA compliance units within state child welfare agencies
- **Judicial benchcards**: Develop and distribute ICWA compliance checklists for state court judges
- **ICWA coordinators**: Require each state agency to designate an ICWA coordinator responsible for compliance

**Evidence of Effectiveness**: Minnesota's state ICWA law and dedicated compliance infrastructure correlate with higher placement preference adherence and lower disproportionality (NICWA state assessments).

### 2. Expand Tribal Child Welfare Capacity

#### Title IV-E Direct Tribal Access

**Problem Addressed**: Only 12 tribes/consortia have Title IV-E agreements; most tribal families depend on state systems.

**Proposed Solutions**:

- **Streamline tribal IV-E application process**: Reduce administrative burden of applying for tribal Title IV-E agreements
- **Federal technical assistance**: Provide dedicated TA teams to assist tribes in developing IV-E plans
- **Consortium models**: Support inter-tribal consortia to share administrative costs
- **Transitional funding**: Provide bridge funding while tribes develop IV-E capacity
- **Family First Act tribal access**: Ensure tribes can fully access prevention funding under the Family First Prevention Services Act

**Evidence of Effectiveness**: Tribes with IV-E agreements (e.g., Port Gamble S'Klallam, Navajo Nation) report greater program control and improved outcomes (Children's Bureau, 2024).

#### Workforce Development

**Problem Addressed**: Chronic shortage of tribal child welfare workers; caseloads 2-3x recommended levels.

**Proposed Solutions**:

- **Tribal child welfare training programs**: Fund university-tribal partnerships to train Native social workers
- **Student loan forgiveness**: Include tribal child welfare workers in public service loan forgiveness programs
- **Competitive compensation**: Increase BIA and tribal social worker salaries to be competitive with state positions
- **Cultural competency certification**: Develop national standards for cultural competency in Indian child welfare
- **Supervisor and leadership development**: Invest in management training for tribal child welfare administrators
- **Retain experienced workers**: Address burnout through manageable caseloads, adequate support, and professional development

**Evidence of Effectiveness**: IHS health professions scholarship programs have successfully recruited Native professionals; similar models can work for child welfare (IHS, 2023).

### 3. Strengthen Family Preservation and Prevention

#### Prevention Services

**Problem Addressed**: Insufficient investment in preventing family disruption; most funding flows to out-of-home care.

**Proposed Solutions**:

- **Home visiting programs**: Expand culturally adapted home visiting programs (e.g., Family Spirit, Nurse-Family Partnership adapted for tribal communities)
- **Substance abuse treatment**: Increase residential and outpatient treatment capacity in Indian Country, including culturally integrated approaches
- **Parent mentoring**: Develop peer mentoring programs pairing at-risk parents with experienced community members
- **Economic support**: Direct financial assistance, employment services, and housing support for families at risk of child welfare involvement
- **Early childhood services**: Expand Head Start, Early Head Start, and tribal childcare programs
- **Domestic violence services**: Increase shelter capacity and advocacy services in Indian Country

**Evidence of Effectiveness**: Family Spirit home visiting program demonstrated 11% reduction in externalizing behaviors and improved parenting knowledge in randomized trial (Barlow et al., *JAMA Pediatrics*, 2015).

#### Culturally Grounded Interventions

**Problem Addressed**: Most evidence-based interventions were developed for non-Native populations and may not be culturally appropriate.

**Proposed Solutions**:

- **Traditional healing integration**: Incorporate traditional ceremonies, sweat lodges, talking circles, and elder mentoring into family support programs
- **Language revitalization as prevention**: Connect language programs with family strengthening to build cultural identity
- **Community-based approaches**: Develop wraparound services rooted in tribal community norms
- **Intergenerational healing programs**: Address boarding school trauma through community healing initiatives
- **Tribally developed curricula**: Support tribes in creating their own evidence-based parenting programs

**Evidence of Effectiveness**: Culturally adapted interventions show higher engagement and retention rates among Native populations (Gone, Joseph P. "Redressing First Nations Historical Trauma." *Transcultural Psychiatry* 50, no. 5 (2013): 683-706).

### 4. Reform Foster and Kinship Care

#### Kinship Care Enhancement

**Problem Addressed**: Kinship caregivers provide the majority of ICWA-compliant placements but receive inadequate support.

**Proposed Solutions**:

- **Kinship care parity**: Require states to provide kinship caregivers the same financial support as licensed foster parents
- **Flexible licensing**: Adapt foster home licensing standards to account for reservation housing conditions without compromising safety
- **Kinship navigator programs**: Fund dedicated kinship navigators to help relatives access services, benefits, and legal support
- **Background check reform**: Allow individualized assessments for kinship caregivers with minor or historical convictions
- **Legal guardianship support**: Streamline guardianship processes and provide ongoing subsidies for kinship guardianship

**Evidence of Effectiveness**: States with kinship care parity laws show increased kinship placements and improved placement stability (Annie E. Casey Foundation, 2023).

#### Foster Home Recruitment

**Problem Addressed**: Insufficient Native foster homes to meet ICWA placement preference requirements.

**Proposed Solutions**:

- **Tribal foster home recruitment**: Fund tribal-led foster home recruitment campaigns targeting tribal communities
- **Community-based foster care**: Develop models where foster families are embedded in tribal communities
- **Respite care networks**: Create respite care systems to support foster families and prevent burnout
- **Foster parent training**: Develop culturally appropriate training curricula for foster parents serving Native children
- **Cross-tribal placements**: Facilitate placements with willing families from other tribes when same-tribe placements are unavailable

### 5. Improve Data and Accountability

#### Data Systems

**Problem Addressed**: Inadequate data collection prevents monitoring outcomes and identifying compliance gaps.

**Proposed Solutions**:

- **AFCARS improvements**: Enhance federal foster care data to track ICWA-specific indicators (notification, active efforts, placement preference compliance)
- **Tribal data sovereignty**: Support tribes in developing their own child welfare data systems while sharing aggregate data for federal monitoring
- **Real-time compliance tracking**: Develop systems for tracking ICWA compliance at the case level in state courts
- **Outcome measurement**: Track long-term outcomes (educational achievement, behavioral health, cultural connection) for Native children in care
- **Interagency data sharing**: Improve information sharing between state agencies, tribal programs, and federal oversight bodies

### 6. Address Systemic Bias

#### Mandated Reporting Reform

**Problem Addressed**: Implicit bias in mandated reporting drives disproportionate investigations of Native families.

**Proposed Solutions**:

- **Differential response**: Implement alternative response systems that address poverty-related concerns without formal investigations
- **Cultural competency requirements**: Require mandated reporters to complete cultural competency training specific to Native families
- **Community-based screening**: Allow tribal representatives to participate in screening decisions for reports involving Native children
- **Poverty exclusion**: Codify that poverty alone is not grounds for a neglect finding or child removal

#### Assessment Reform

**Problem Addressed**: Risk assessment tools calibrated on non-Native populations produce biased results for Native families.

**Proposed Solutions**:

- **Tribally validated tools**: Develop and validate risk and safety assessment instruments for use with Native families
- **Structured decision-making**: Implement structured decision-making protocols that account for cultural context
- **QEW standards**: Establish clear national standards for qualified expert witnesses in ICWA proceedings
- **Cultural consultants**: Integrate cultural consultants into state child welfare investigation processes for cases involving Native children

## Models Worth Studying

| Model | Location | Key Features | Outcomes |
|-------|----------|-------------|----------|
| Port Gamble S'Klallam Title IV-E | Washington | First tribe with direct Title IV-E agreement | Increased tribal control, improved placement stability |
| Minnesota ICWA framework | Minnesota | State ICWA law + dedicated ICWA unit + mandatory training | Higher compliance, lower disproportionality |
| Family Spirit | Multiple tribes | Culturally adapted home visiting | Improved parenting, reduced risk factors |
| Navajo Nation ICWA program | Navajo Nation | Large-scale tribal child welfare operation | Demonstrates tribal capacity for comprehensive services |
| Wabanaki Truth and Reconciliation | Maine | Tribal-state truth and reconciliation process for child welfare | Improved state-tribal relations, policy changes |
