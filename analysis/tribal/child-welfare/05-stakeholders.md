# Tribal Child Welfare: Stakeholders

## Overview

Tribal child welfare involves a complex web of stakeholders spanning tribal governments, federal and state agencies, legal advocates, children and families, and private organizations. The interests of these stakeholders sometimes align -- particularly around child safety -- but frequently diverge on questions of jurisdiction, cultural standards, and the balance between family preservation and child protection. Understanding these stakeholder dynamics is essential for crafting effective policy and building sustainable coalitions for reform.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Native Children

**Interest**: Safety, stability, cultural connection, and belonging.

| Concern | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Physical safety | Protection from abuse and neglect in any setting |
| Cultural identity | Connection to tribe, language, ceremony, and extended family |
| Placement stability | Minimizing disruptions; permanent placement in timely fashion |
| Voice in proceedings | Age-appropriate participation in decisions about their futures |
| Access to services | Therapeutic, educational, and developmental support |

**Power Level**: Low -- children are subjects of proceedings, not parties with independent legal power in most jurisdictions.

**Key Dynamic**: Children's interests are mediated through adults (parents, caseworkers, judges, guardians ad litem) who may have conflicting interpretations of "best interest."

### Native Parents and Families

**Interest**: Family preservation, adequate support services, fair treatment, and access to culturally appropriate resources.

| Concern | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Family unity | Keeping children at home with adequate support |
| Due process | Fair proceedings free from cultural bias |
| Access to treatment | Substance abuse, mental health, and parenting support |
| Economic support | Housing, employment, and basic needs |
| Cultural parenting | Freedom to raise children according to tribal traditions |

**Power Level**: Low to moderate -- parents have legal rights but face systemic disadvantages in navigating child welfare systems.

### Extended Family and Kinship Caregivers

**Interest**: Supporting relatives' children, maintaining family connections, receiving adequate support.

| Concern | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Placement priority | ICWA placement preferences honored |
| Financial support | Comparable assistance to licensed foster parents |
| Licensing accessibility | Flexible standards that account for reservation conditions |
| Legal recognition | Guardianship and custody rights formalized |
| Support services | Respite care, training, and access to child's services |

**Power Level**: Moderate -- kinship caregivers are increasingly recognized as essential but often lack formal standing.

### Tribal Governments

**Interest**: Exercising sovereign authority over their children and families, building tribal child welfare capacity, and preserving cultural continuity.

| Concern | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Sovereignty | Control over child welfare decisions for tribal citizens |
| Notification | Timely and accurate notice of proceedings involving tribal children |
| Transfer | Ability to transfer cases to tribal court |
| Capacity | Resources to operate comprehensive child welfare programs |
| Cultural standards | Tribal definitions of family, care, and child-rearing respected |
| Data sovereignty | Control over data about tribal children and families |

**Power Level**: Moderate to high -- tribes have legal standing, government-to-government relationship, and ICWA protections, but limited by funding constraints and state resistance.

**Key Organizations**:

- National Congress of American Indians (NCAI)
- National Indian Child Welfare Association (NICWA)
- Individual tribal social services departments
- Tribal courts

## Federal Government Stakeholders

### Bureau of Indian Affairs (BIA)

**Role**: Administers ICWA grants, issues guidelines and regulations, funds tribal social services.

| Function | Status |
|----------|--------|
| ICWA regulation (25 C.F.R. Part 23) | Binding since 2016; upheld in *Brackeen* |
| Social services funding | $72 million annually; covers ~30% of need |
| Technical assistance | Limited capacity for monitoring and support |
| Guidelines | Updated in 2015 and 2016 |

**Power Level**: High -- regulatory and funding authority.

### Children's Bureau (HHS/ACF)

**Role**: Administers Title IV-E and IV-B funding, oversees Child and Family Services Reviews (CFSRs), approves tribal Title IV-E plans.

| Function | Status |
|----------|--------|
| Title IV-E tribal agreements | 12 tribes/consortia approved |
| Title IV-B tribal set-aside | 3% ($19.9 million) |
| CFSR monitoring | Assesses state compliance including ICWA indicators |
| Technical assistance | Limited tribal-specific resources |

**Power Level**: High -- controls major funding streams.

### Department of Justice

**Role**: Prosecutes crimes in Indian Country, enforces civil rights, provides tribal justice assistance.

| Function | Status |
|----------|--------|
| Criminal prosecution of child abuse | High declination rates (52% overall) |
| Office of Tribal Justice | Coordination and policy development |
| OVW grants | Violence prevention funding with child welfare components |

### Congressional Committees

**Role**: Authorizing legislation, appropriations, oversight hearings.

- Senate Committee on Indian Affairs
- House Natural Resources Subcommittee on Indian, Insular and Alaska Native Affairs
- Appropriations subcommittees (Interior and HHS)

## State Government Stakeholders

### State Child Welfare Agencies

**Interest**: Compliance with federal law (ICWA, ASFA, Title IV-E), child safety, managing caseloads within budget constraints.

| Concern | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| ICWA compliance | Varying commitment; some states have dedicated ICWA units |
| Caseload management | ICWA requirements add procedural steps to already heavy caseloads |
| Training | Workers need ICWA-specific training; turnover undermines investment |
| Foster home resources | Insufficient Native foster homes in most states |
| Liability | Concern about liability for non-compliance in either direction |

**Power Level**: High -- state agencies make day-to-day removal and placement decisions.

### State Courts

**Interest**: Adjudicating cases fairly, following applicable law, managing dockets.

| Concern | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| ICWA application | Judges must determine Indian child status, apply ICWA procedures |
| Transfer requests | Must evaluate tribal court transfer petitions |
| Good cause findings | Must document reasons for deviating from placement preferences |
| QEW requirements | Must ensure qualified expert witnesses are available |
| Training | Many state judges receive minimal ICWA training |

**Power Level**: High -- judges make binding custody and placement decisions.

### State Legislators

**Interest**: Varies by state; some have enacted state ICWA laws, others have resisted.

- States with state ICWA laws: Minnesota, Wisconsin, Washington, Oklahoma, Oregon, Iowa, Michigan, Nebraska, others
- Opposition to ICWA in some state legislatures linked to adoption industry interests and anti-tribal sovereignty sentiment

## Advocacy and Legal Stakeholders

### Pro-ICWA Advocacy Organizations

| Organization | Role |
|-------------|------|
| National Indian Child Welfare Association (NICWA) | Leading national ICWA advocacy, training, and research |
| Native American Rights Fund (NARF) | Legal defense and amicus participation |
| Association on American Indian Affairs (AAIA) | Historical research and advocacy; original ICWA research |
| Tribal Law and Policy Institute | Training and technical assistance |
| National Congress of American Indians (NCAI) | Policy coordination and government relations |
| Casey Family Programs | Research, funding, and technical assistance |

### Anti-ICWA or ICWA-Critical Organizations

| Organization | Position |
|-------------|----------|
| Goldwater Institute | Argues ICWA constitutes racial discrimination; filed amicus in *Brackeen* |
| Texas Attorney General's Office | Led state challenge to ICWA in *Brackeen* |
| Some adoption attorneys and agencies | Argue ICWA delays adoptions and prioritizes tribal interests over children |
| Individual Tribal Members Against ICWA (ad hoc) | Small number of Native individuals who have publicly opposed ICWA based on personal experience |

### Legal Aid and Representation

| Entity | Role |
|--------|------|
| Tribal court advocates | Represent families in tribal court proceedings |
| Legal aid organizations | Provide representation to indigent Native parents in state proceedings |
| Court Appointed Special Advocates (CASA) | Volunteers serving as guardians ad litem; ICWA training varies |
| Public defenders | Often represent parents; ICWA knowledge inconsistent |

## Private Sector Stakeholders

### Adoption Agencies

**Interest**: Facilitating adoptions; some view ICWA as an obstacle to placement of available children.

**Power Level**: Moderate -- agencies influence placement decisions and were plaintiffs in *Brackeen*.

### Foster Care Providers

**Interest**: Caring for children placed with them; maintaining licensure; financial support.

**Power Level**: Low to moderate -- individual providers have limited systemic influence.

### Child Welfare Workforce (Social Workers, Therapists)

**Interest**: Adequate caseloads, training, compensation, and culturally appropriate tools.

| Concern | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Caseload size | 30-45 families vs. recommended 12-15 |
| Training | Insufficient ICWA and cultural competency training |
| Compensation | Tribal and BIA social workers paid less than state counterparts |
| Burnout and turnover | High turnover rates undermine relationship-based practice |
| Cultural competency | Need for Native practitioners and culturally grounded approaches |

| Low Power, Low Interest | General public, most adoption agencies |

## Key Dynamics

1. **Sovereignty tension**: Tribal governments assert sovereign authority while states resist ceding jurisdiction
2. **Funding leverage**: Federal funding conditions (Title IV-E, IV-B) create compliance incentives but enforcement is weak
3. **Narrative competition**: ICWA framed as "protecting culture" by supporters vs. "race-based discrimination" by opponents
4. **Post-*Brackeen* realignment**: Supreme Court decision resolved constitutional questions, shifting debate to implementation
5. **Workforce crisis**: All stakeholders affected by inadequate staffing, but tribes bear the greatest burden
