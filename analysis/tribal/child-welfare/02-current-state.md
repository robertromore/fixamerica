# Tribal Child Welfare: Current State

## ICWA Legal Framework Today

### Statutory Structure

The Indian Child Welfare Act (25 U.S.C. 1901-1963) establishes the following framework:

| Provision | Requirement | Implementation Status |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------|
| Tribal jurisdiction (1911) | Exclusive jurisdiction over children domiciled on reservation; right to transfer off-reservation cases | Widely recognized but transfer requests sometimes denied |
| Notice requirements (1912) | Tribes must be notified of involuntary proceedings involving Indian children | Compliance inconsistent; many states lack verification protocols |
| Active efforts (1912) | States must make "active efforts" to prevent breakup of Indian family before removal | Standard varies by state; BIA 2016 guidelines provide definition |
| Qualified expert witness (1912) | Testimony from QEW required before foster care or termination | Definition and qualification standards vary |
| Placement preferences (1915) | Extended family, tribal members, other Indian families, in order of priority | Override rates vary; "good cause" exceptions applied inconsistently |
| Record-keeping (1917) | State courts must maintain records and provide access to adult adoptees | Compliance uneven; historical records often incomplete |
| Full faith and credit (1911) | State and federal courts must give tribal court orders full faith and credit | Generally respected but enforcement gaps exist |

### 2016 BIA Final Rule

The Bureau of Indian Affairs issued binding regulations (25 C.F.R. Part 23) in 2016 that clarified ICWA requirements:

- Defined "active efforts" as distinct from the lower "reasonable efforts" standard
- Specified that ICWA applies regardless of whether the child is in the physical custody of an Indian parent at the time of proceedings
- Established that "existing Indian family" exceptions are invalid
- Required documentation of compliance at each stage of proceedings
- Mandated that placement preferences apply to all placements, not just final adoptive placement

### Post-*Brackeen* Legal Landscape

The Supreme Court's decision in *Haaland v. Brackeen*, 599 U.S. 255 (2023), resolved several major constitutional challenges:

| Issue | Holding | Practical Effect |
|-------|---------|-----------------|
| Equal protection | ICWA's classifications are political, not racial | Eliminates primary constitutional attack on ICWA |
| Tenth Amendment | Congress may require state courts to apply ICWA as valid preemption | States cannot refuse to apply ICWA on anti-commandeering grounds |
| Non-delegation | Congress permissibly delegated authority to BIA for placement standards | 2016 regulations remain valid |
| Standing | Individual plaintiffs lacked standing for some claims; Texas lacked parens patriae standing | Narrows future challenges |
| Unresolved | Individual as-applied challenges remain possible | Future case-by-case litigation likely |

## Recent Developments (2024-2025)

### Federal Actions

- **Haaland v. Brackeen implementation**: Following the 2023 Supreme Court ruling, BIA issued guidance reinforcing ICWA's applicability nationwide and the binding nature of 2016 regulations
- **Federal Indian Boarding School Initiative Volume 2**: DOI's 2024 report documented additional school sites and child deaths, increasing momentum for reparative policies
- **Family First Prevention Services Act**: Additional tribes pursuing Title IV-E agreements to access prevention funding
- **ICWA Compliance Monitoring**: HHS announced enhanced review protocols for state compliance as part of Child and Family Services Reviews (CFSRs)

### State Actions

- **Oklahoma**: 2023 amendments to state ICWA law strengthened notification requirements and qualified expert witness standards
- **California**: Expanded tribal-state foster care agreements and increased ICWA training requirements for judicial officers
- **South Dakota**: Ongoing advocacy for improved compliance following documented failures in Pennington County
- **Minnesota**: Continued implementation of strengthened state ICWA provisions with increased tribal liaison funding

### Tribal Initiatives

- **National Indian Child Welfare Association**: Expanded technical assistance programs for ICWA compliance monitoring
- **Title IV-E expansion**: Port Gamble S'Klallam Tribe and Confederated Salish and Kootenai Tribes among recent additions to tribal IV-E programs
- **Kinship care networks**: Multiple tribes developing formalized kinship navigator programs
- **Data sovereignty initiatives**: Several tribes implementing their own child welfare data systems

## The Gap Between Law and Practice

| ICWA Requirement | Legal Standard | Reality on the Ground |
|------------------|---------------|----------------------|
| Tribal notification | Notice to tribe within 10 days of proceeding | Many states lack tracking; notifications sent late or to wrong tribe |
| Active efforts | Affirmative, active, thorough, and timely efforts | Many states apply "reasonable efforts" standard instead |
| Placement preferences | Extended family, tribal members, other Indian families | "Good cause" exceptions applied broadly; insufficient Native foster homes |
| Qualified expert witness | Testimony on cultural standards before placement/TPR | QEWs hard to find; some courts accept unqualified testimony |
| Transfer to tribal court | Presumptive right absent good cause | Some state courts routinely deny transfers; backlogs in tribal courts |
| Record-keeping | Court records maintained and accessible | Historical records lost; ongoing record-keeping inconsistent |
