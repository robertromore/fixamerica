# Tribal Child Welfare: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to the Indian Child Welfare Act and tribal child welfare sovereignty comes from several directions: libertarian and conservative legal organizations that view ICWA as racial classification, adoption industry actors who see it as an obstacle to placement, some state governments that resist federal preemption of family law, and a small number of individuals with personal grievances against ICWA's application. The Supreme Court's decision in *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023) resolved the primary constitutional challenges, but opposition continues through implementation resistance, political advocacy, and potential as-applied challenges.

## Opposition Categories

### 1. Constitutional/Legal Opposition

**Primary Actors**: Goldwater Institute, Texas Attorney General, some constitutional law scholars

**Core Arguments**:

| Argument | Claim | Counter |
|----------|-------|---------|
| Equal protection | ICWA classifies children by race, violating the Fourteenth Amendment | Supreme Court found tribal membership is a political classification, not racial (*Morton v. Mancari*, 1974; *Brackeen*, 2023) |
| Anti-commandeering | ICWA unconstitutionally compels state courts to apply federal placement preferences | Court held ICWA is valid preemption, not commandeering (*Brackeen*, 2023, 7-2) |
| Non-delegation | Congress impermissibly delegated authority to BIA for placement standards | Court upheld BIA's regulatory authority (*Brackeen*, 2023) |
| Best interest of the child | ICWA places tribal interests above individual children's welfare | ICWA's legislative history and tribal advocates argue cultural connection *is* in the child's best interest |
| Individual rights vs. group rights | Children should not be assigned to a political group without consent | Tribal membership, like citizenship, confers rights and obligations at birth |

**Current Status**: Constitutional arguments largely foreclosed by *Brackeen*; opposition has shifted to as-applied challenges and implementation resistance.

**Funding and Support**:

- Goldwater Institute: Libertarian legal organization funded by conservative donors; has litigated and filed amicus briefs in multiple ICWA cases
- Texas Public Policy Foundation: Supported Texas's challenge
- Cato Institute: Filed amicus briefs questioning ICWA's constitutionality

### 2. Adoption Industry Opposition

**Primary Actors**: Some private adoption agencies, adoption attorneys, prospective adoptive parents

**Core Arguments**:

| Argument | Claim | Counter |
|----------|-------|---------|
| Delays adoption | ICWA notification and transfer requirements slow permanent placement | Delays result from non-compliance, not ICWA itself; early tribal engagement reduces delays |
| Limits placement options | Placement preferences restrict the pool of potential families | Preferences are hierarchical, not absolute; "good cause" exceptions exist |
| Separates bonded children | Transfer to tribal jurisdiction may remove children from established homes | Early ICWA compliance prevents late-stage disruptions |
| Bureaucratic burden | ICWA adds procedural steps to already complex proceedings | Procedures protect constitutional rights of tribes and families |

**Current Status**: Active; some agencies continue to resist ICWA compliance; *Baby Girl* (2013) provided some favorable precedent for non-custodial parent cases.

**Notable Cases**:

- *Adoptive Couple v. Baby Girl* (2013): Adoption industry groups filed multiple amicus briefs supporting adoption over ICWA preferences
- *Brackeen v. Haaland*: Adoption agencies were original plaintiffs

### 3. State Government Opposition

**Primary Actors**: Some state attorneys general, state legislators, state child welfare administrators

**Core Arguments**:

| Argument | Claim | Counter |
|----------|-------|---------|
| Federal intrusion | Family law is traditionally a state function; ICWA preempts state authority | Indian affairs are expressly federal under the Commerce Clause; *Brackeen* affirmed this |
| Unfunded mandate | ICWA compliance requires resources states do not receive | Title IV-E and IV-B provide federal funding; states also benefit from tribal family preservation reducing caseloads |
| Inconsistent standards | ICWA creates different procedures for Indian vs. non-Indian children | Different legal framework reflects the unique government-to-government relationship |
| Implementation burden | Identifying Indian children, locating tribes, and complying with preferences is administratively difficult | Difficulty is often a function of inadequate systems rather than inherent impossibility |

**State-Level Opposition Patterns**:

- **Active resistance**: Texas (led *Brackeen* challenge), some Southern and Western states
- **Passive non-compliance**: States with weak ICWA training, no dedicated ICWA units, no state ICWA law
- **Selective compliance**: States that comply in some counties but not others

### 4. Individual/Personal Opposition

**Primary Actors**: Some adoptive parents, some Native adoptees, occasional tribal members

**Core Arguments**:

| Argument | Claim | Counter |
|----------|-------|---------|
| Personal harm | Specific families harmed by ICWA's application in their case | Individual hardship cases do not negate the law's broader necessity |
| Adoptee perspective | Some Native adoptees raised in non-Native homes oppose ICWA retroactively | Other adoptees testify that cultural disconnection caused lasting harm |
| Tribal politics | Tribal enrollment decisions sometimes exclude children who should be included | Tribal membership criteria are an exercise of tribal sovereignty |

**Note**: Individual opposition is sometimes amplified by well-funded legal organizations seeking test cases for as-applied challenges.

## Opposition Strategies

### Legal Strategies

1. **As-applied challenges**: Since *Brackeen* left open the possibility of individual equal protection claims, opponents may pursue case-specific challenges
2. **State court litigation**: Challenging ICWA application in state courts where judges may be sympathetic
3. **Existing Indian family arguments**: Despite BIA rule rejecting this doctrine, some state courts may still apply it
4. **"Good cause" expansion**: Advocating for broad interpretation of exceptions to placement preferences

### Political Strategies

1. **Federal legislation**: Attempts to amend or weaken ICWA (historically unsuccessful but periodically introduced)
2. **State legislation**: Opposing state ICWA laws or weakening existing ones
3. **Regulatory challenges**: Seeking to modify or repeal the 2016 BIA rule
4. **Media campaigns**: Highlighting individual cases where ICWA produced unfavorable outcomes for children

### Cultural Strategies

1. **"Color-blind" framing**: Arguing that treating children differently based on tribal membership is inherently discriminatory
2. **Individual rights narrative**: Framing opposition as protecting individual children rather than opposing tribal sovereignty
3. **Adoption promotion**: Emphasizing adoption as a positive outcome over family preservation in tribal communities

## Addressing Opposition

### Factual Responses

| Opposition Claim | Evidence-Based Response |
|------------------|----------------------|
| ICWA is race-based | Tribal membership is a political classification affirmed by Supreme Court in *Morton v. Mancari* (1974) and *Brackeen* (2023) |
| ICWA harms children | Studies show Native children placed according to ICWA preferences have better outcomes (NICWA, 2023) |
| ICWA delays permanency | Early tribal engagement and compliance with ICWA reduces delays; non-compliance causes disruptions |
| Tribes cannot protect children | Many tribes operate excellent child welfare programs; capacity limitations reflect underfunding, not inability |
| ICWA is outdated | ICWA addresses ongoing problems; Native children remain 2.7x overrepresented in foster care |

### Strategic Responses

1. **Strengthen implementation**: Better compliance reduces the cases that generate opposition
2. **Build tribal capacity**: Demonstrating effective tribal child welfare programs undermines "incapacity" arguments
3. **Engage adoptive families**: Recruitment of Native and culturally competent foster/adoptive families reduces tension
4. **Data transparency**: Publishing outcome data showing ICWA's benefits
5. **Training investment**: Equipping state workers and judges to apply ICWA correctly reduces hostile or negligent application
6. **Coalition building**: Engaging child welfare professionals, pediatricians, and legal scholars as ICWA supporters
