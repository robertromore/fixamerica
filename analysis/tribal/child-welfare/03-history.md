# Tribal Child Welfare: History

## Overview

The history of Native child welfare in the United States is inseparable from the broader history of federal Indian policy. For over a century, the systematic removal of Native children from their families and communities served as a deliberate tool of cultural destruction -- first through the boarding school system, then through adoption and foster care practices that separated children from their tribes. Understanding this history is essential to comprehending why the Indian Child Welfare Act exists, why its implementation remains contested, and why the effects of past policies continue to shape outcomes for Native families today.

## Timeline

### Pre-Contact to Early Republic (Before 1800)

- Indigenous nations maintained sophisticated child-rearing practices grounded in extended kinship networks
- Children raised communally with multiple caregivers, elders, and clan members
- Adoption and informal care arrangements common and culturally integrated
- Child welfare was a community responsibility, not an individualized nuclear family function
- Governance of children's welfare was an inherent sovereign function of tribal nations

### The Civilization Program (1800-1879)

- **1819**: Civilization Fund Act authorized federal spending on "civilizing" Native people, including education of children
- **1824**: Bureau of Indian Affairs established within War Department
- **1849**: BIA transferred to Department of the Interior
- **1860s-1870s**: Reservation system consolidated Native populations; government agents exercised increasing control over family life
- **1869**: Board of Indian Commissioners created; recommended "Christian education" for Native children
- Treaty-making era (ended 1871) included provisions for "education" that often meant removing children

### The Boarding School Era (1879-1969)

- **1879**: Captain Richard Henry Pratt established Carlisle Indian Industrial School in Pennsylvania with the explicit philosophy "Kill the Indian, save the man"
- **1880s-1900s**: Over 350 boarding schools established across the country
- Children forcibly removed from families, sometimes at gunpoint
- Punished for speaking Native languages, practicing ceremonies, or wearing traditional clothing
- Rampant physical and sexual abuse documented at numerous institutions
- Disease and malnutrition led to significant child mortality -- DOI has documented over 900 deaths
- **1891**: Congress authorized the Commissioner of Indian Affairs to enforce compulsory school attendance
- **1893**: Appropriations Act authorized withholding rations from families who refused to send children to school
- **1900**: Approximately 21,568 Native children in boarding schools (Census, 1900)
- **1926**: Nearly 83% of school-age Native children in boarding schools (Meriam Report data)
- **1928**: Meriam Report (*The Problem of Indian Administration*) documented deplorable conditions -- malnutrition, overcrowding, child labor, inadequate medical care
- **1934**: Indian Reorganization Act marked shift in federal policy, but boarding schools continued operating
- **1969**: Kennedy Report (*Indian Education: A National Tragedy -- A National Challenge*) again documented failures
- Last federal boarding schools closed in the 1960s-1970s, though some continued into the 1990s

### The Indian Adoption Era (1940s-1978)

- **1940s-1950s**: As boarding schools began closing, child removal continued through foster care and adoption
- **1958**: Indian Adoption Project launched by Bureau of Indian Affairs and Child Welfare League of America
    - Explicitly designed to place Native children with non-Indian families
    - Operated through 1967; placed 395 children through the formal program
    - Inspired hundreds of additional placements through state agencies
- **1960s-1970s**: State social workers removed Native children at staggering rates
    - AAIA surveys (1969-1974): 25-35% of all Native children separated from families
    - 85% placed in non-Indian homes
    - Minnesota: Native children placed in foster care at 5x the rate of non-Native children
    - South Dakota: 40% of adoptions were of Native children, though they comprised 7% of the child population
    - Washington state: Native children in foster care at 19x the rate of non-Native children
- Removal often based on culturally biased assessments
    - Extended family arrangements labeled "neglect"
    - Poverty equated with parental unfitness
    - Traditional practices deemed inappropriate
    - Non-nuclear family structures viewed as deficient
- **1968**: Devil's Lake Sioux Tribe (Spirit Lake Nation) filed one of the first formal complaints about child removals
- **1974**: First congressional hearings on Indian child welfare (Senate Subcommittee on Indian Affairs)

### Enactment of ICWA (1978)

- **1974-1977**: Extensive congressional hearings documented the crisis
    - Testimony from tribal leaders, social workers, adoptees, and birth parents
    - Evidence of systemic cultural bias in state child welfare systems
    - Documentation of psychological harm to removed children
    - Recognition that child removal threatened tribal existence
- **November 8, 1978**: President Carter signed the Indian Child Welfare Act (P.L. 95-608)
    - Congressional findings: "an alarmingly high percentage of Indian families are broken up by the removal, often unwarranted, of their children" (25 U.S.C. 1901(4))
    - Established tribal jurisdiction over reservation-domiciled children
    - Created placement preference hierarchy
    - Required "active efforts" before removal
    - Mandated tribal notification in state proceedings
    - Authorized BIA grants for tribal family service programs

### Early Implementation (1979-1995)

- **1979**: BIA issued initial ICWA guidelines (non-binding)
- **1979-1980s**: States began implementing ICWA with varying degrees of compliance
- **1982**: *Mississippi Band of Choctaw Indians v. Holyfield*, 490 U.S. 30 (1989) -- Supreme Court broadly interpreted ICWA's jurisdictional provisions, holding that domicile of illegitimate children follows the mother's domicile
- **1980s**: "Existing Indian family" doctrine emerged in state courts
    - Some states held ICWA inapplicable if the child or parent had no significant ties to a tribe
    - Directly contradicted ICWA's text and purpose
    - Adopted in approximately 12 states at its peak
- **1987**: BIA issued revised guidelines (still non-binding)
- **1990s**: Growing recognition of ICWA's importance alongside persistent implementation problems
- Title IV-E of the Social Security Act amended to reference ICWA compliance requirements

### Modern Challenges (1996-2016)

- **1996**: Adoption and Safe Families Act (ASFA) created tensions with ICWA
    - ASFA's timeline for termination of parental rights (15 of 22 months) conflicted with ICWA's family preservation emphasis
    - Debate over whether ASFA or ICWA takes precedence
- **2000s**: Continued state-level resistance to ICWA
    - *Existing Indian family* doctrine applied in multiple states despite opposition from tribes and the federal government
    - Foster care disproportionality persisted
- **2007**: National Indian Child Welfare Association published comprehensive compliance study documenting widespread failures
- **2013**: *Adoptive Couple v. Baby Girl*, 570 U.S. 637 -- Supreme Court narrowly interpreted ICWA provisions regarding a non-custodial father
    - Held that "active efforts" and placement preferences did not apply when Indian parent never had custody
    - Created confusion about ICWA's scope
    - Tribal advocates concerned about weakening ICWA's protections
- **2015**: BIA updated ICWA guidelines (non-binding)

### The 2016 Rule and Constitutional Challenge (2016-2023)

- **June 2016**: BIA issued binding regulations (25 C.F.R. Part 23)
    - First binding federal rule implementing ICWA in 38 years
    - Rejected "existing Indian family" doctrine
    - Defined "active efforts"
    - Strengthened placement preference requirements
    - Clarified applicability standards
- **2017**: State of Texas, individual plaintiffs, and adoption agencies challenged ICWA's constitutionality in *Brackeen v. Bernhardt*
    - Argued ICWA violated equal protection (racial classification)
    - Argued ICWA violated Tenth Amendment (anti-commandeering)
    - Argued ICWA violated non-delegation doctrine
- **2018**: Federal district court struck down ICWA as unconstitutional
- **2019**: Fifth Circuit panel reversed; en banc rehearing followed
- **2021**: Fifth Circuit en banc issued fractured decision with various provisions upheld and struck down
- **June 15, 2023**: Supreme Court decided *Haaland v. Brackeen*, 599 U.S. 255
    - 7-2: ICWA does not violate the Tenth Amendment's anti-commandeering doctrine
    - Unanimously: Individual plaintiffs and Texas lacked standing on equal protection claims
    - Left open the possibility of future as-applied equal protection challenges
    - Major victory for tribal sovereignty and ICWA's continued viability

### Federal Indian Boarding School Initiative (2021-Present)

- **June 2021**: Secretary of the Interior Deb Haaland (Laguna Pueblo) launched the Federal Indian Boarding School Initiative
- **May 2022**: Volume 1 report identified over 400 federal boarding schools across 37 states and territories
    - Documented over 500 child deaths (later revised upward)
    - Found 53 marked and unmarked burial sites
- **July 2024**: Volume 2 report expanded findings
    - Identified additional school sites and deaths
    - Total documented deaths exceeded 900
    - Recommended healing programs and further investigation
- **2023-2024**: Road to Healing tour brought Department of the Interior officials to tribal communities to hear survivors' testimony

## Key Legislation Timeline

| Year | Legislation | Impact on Native Child Welfare |
|------|------------|-------------------------------|
| 1819 | Civilization Fund Act | Authorized federal funding for "civilizing" Native children |
| 1891 | Compulsory Attendance Act | Authorized forcing children into boarding schools |
| 1934 | Indian Reorganization Act | Began shift from assimilation; did not end boarding schools |
| 1975 | Indian Self-Determination Act | Enabled tribes to operate federal programs including social services |
| 1978 | Indian Child Welfare Act | Established protections for Native children and families |
| 1980 | Adoption Assistance and Child Welfare Act | Federal foster care framework; created Title IV-E |
| 1994 | Multiethnic Placement Act | Initially exempted ICWA; created ongoing tension |
| 1996 | Adoption and Safe Families Act | Timeline conflicts with ICWA family preservation goals |
| 2008 | Fostering Connections Act | Allowed tribal Title IV-E agreements |
| 2013 | VAWA Reauthorization | Expanded tribal jurisdiction; tangential child welfare impacts |
| 2016 | BIA Final Rule (25 C.F.R. 23) | First binding ICWA regulations |
| 2018 | Family First Prevention Services Act | Expanded prevention funding; tribal access limited |

## Lasting Impact

The cumulative effect of boarding schools, the Indian Adoption Project, and pre-ICWA child welfare practices includes:

- **Intergenerational trauma**: Survivors of boarding schools and forced adoption passed trauma to subsequent generations through disrupted parenting patterns, unresolved grief, and substance abuse
- **Language loss**: Children separated from communities lost fluency; many languages now critically endangered
- **Cultural disruption**: Ceremonial knowledge, traditional practices, and clan systems weakened by decades of family separation
- **Identity displacement**: Adoptees raised outside their culture often experienced identity crises, belonging to neither their birth culture nor their adoptive one
- **Ongoing distrust**: Native communities harbor deep and justified distrust of state child welfare systems
- **Structural disadvantage**: Generations of disrupted families contribute to current poverty, housing instability, and health disparities that drive continued disproportionate child welfare involvement
