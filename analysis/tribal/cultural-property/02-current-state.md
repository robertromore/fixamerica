# Tribal Cultural Property: Current State

## Legal Framework Today

### NAGPRA (25 U.S.C. 3001-3013)

The Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act applies to all federal agencies and institutions that receive federal funding. Key provisions:

| Provision | Requirement | Implementation Status |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------|
| Inventories (Section 5) | Institutions must inventory human remains and associated funerary objects and identify cultural affiliation | Largely completed but accuracy disputed; many "culturally unidentifiable" classifications |
| Summaries (Section 6) | Institutions must provide summaries of unassociated funerary objects, sacred objects, and cultural patrimony | Completed for most institutions; quality varies |
| Repatriation (Section 7) | Institutions must repatriate items upon request from lineal descendants or affiliated tribes | Over 67,000 individuals repatriated; 108,000+ remaining |
| Intentional excavation (Section 3) | Excavation on federal/tribal land requires tribal consultation and consent | Generally followed on tribal land; compliance on federal land inconsistent |
| Inadvertent discovery (Section 3) | Discovery of remains requires cessation of activity and tribal notification | Compliance variable; construction projects create pressure to minimize disruption |
| Trafficking penalties (Section 4) | Criminal penalties for illegal trafficking in human remains and cultural items | Rarely prosecuted; penalties often insufficient |
| Grants (Section 10) | Grants for tribes and museums for repatriation-related activities | $2.7 million annually; dramatically underfunded |

### 2024 Updated NAGPRA Regulations (43 C.F.R. Part 10)

The Department of the Interior published a final rule effective January 12, 2024, significantly strengthening NAGPRA implementation:

| Change | Previous Rule | 2024 Rule |
|--------|--------------|-----------|
| Right of possession | Institutions could claim "right of possession" to resist repatriation | Eliminated; museums cannot assert right of possession as defense |
| Culturally unidentifiable remains | Institutions could hold remains indefinitely if they deemed them "culturally unidentifiable" | Must consult with geographically and culturally relevant tribes; mandatory disposition |
| Consent for research | Institutions could study remains without tribal consent | Free, prior, and informed consent required for any new research or exhibition |
| Consultation requirements | Limited consultation standards | Enhanced duty to consult in good faith; tribal traditional knowledge must be given equal weight to scientific evidence |
| Timeline | No enforceable deadlines | 5-year deadline for completing inventories and initiating consultation |
| Civil penalties | Maximum $14,527 per violation per day | Enhanced penalty authority; referral for federal enforcement |

### Archaeological Resources Protection Act (16 U.S.C. 470aa-470mm)

| Provision | Requirement | Status |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| Excavation permits | Permits required on federal/tribal land; tribal consent on tribal land | Generally enforced; permit violations common |
| Trafficking | Prohibits sale, purchase, or transport of illegally obtained archaeological resources | Enforcement limited; penalties often insufficient deterrent |
| Penalties | Up to $100,000 fine and 1 year imprisonment (first offense); $250,000 and 5 years (subsequent) | Actual penalties far below maximums |
| Rewards | Rewards for information leading to conviction | Program exists but rarely publicized |
| Confidentiality | Location of sites can be kept confidential | Important protection but limits public oversight |

### National Historic Preservation Act (54 U.S.C. 300101 et seq.)

| Provision | Requirement | Status |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| Section 106 | Federal agencies must consider effects on historic properties, including TCPs | Consultation required but outcomes not binding |
| Tribal Historic Preservation Officers | Tribes may assume SHPO functions on tribal land | Over 200 THPOs established |
| Traditional Cultural Properties | Cultural landscapes and places may be listed on National Register | Recognition growing but protections limited |
| Section 110 | Federal agencies must identify and protect historic properties on their land | Compliance varies by agency |

## Recent Developments (2024-2025)

### Regulatory Changes

- **January 2024**: Updated NAGPRA regulations took effect, representing the most significant strengthening of NAGPRA since its enactment
- **Implementation guidance**: National NAGPRA program issued implementation guidance for the 2024 rule, addressing common questions from institutions and tribes
- **5-year deadline**: Institutions now have until January 2029 to complete updated inventories and initiate consultation under the new standards

### Institutional Responses

- **Museum closures and pauses**: Several major museums temporarily closed Native American exhibition halls to reassess holdings and compliance (AMNH, Harvard Peabody, others)
- **University of California**: Faced state legislative hearings and media scrutiny over slow repatriation; accelerated under SB 1007 and new university policies
- **Smithsonian Institution**: National Museum of the American Indian accelerated repatriation, returning thousands of items under the NMAI Act's separate framework

### Enforcement Actions

- **Online sales crackdowns**: Federal investigations into online trafficking of Native cultural items through eBay and auction platforms
- **International auctions**: Continued sales of Native American sacred items at European auction houses, particularly in Paris, despite diplomatic protests
- **Operation Cerberus Action** and similar operations: Multi-agency law enforcement operations targeting cultural property trafficking networks

### Tribal Initiatives

- **Tribal cultural heritage codes**: Increasing number of tribes enacting comprehensive cultural heritage protection ordinances
- **Digital repatriation**: Tribes developing digital archives of repatriated items and 3D scanning protocols that protect cultural sensitivities
- **International advocacy**: Tribal leaders testifying before UN forums and engaging with UNDRIP implementation mechanisms

## The Gap Between Law and Practice

| NAGPRA Requirement | Legal Standard | Reality on the Ground |
|-------------------|---------------|----------------------|
| Inventory completion | Must identify all holdings and cultural affiliation | Many institutions filed incomplete or inaccurate inventories; "culturally unidentifiable" used to avoid repatriation |
| Tribal consultation | Good faith consultation with affiliated tribes | Some institutions treated consultation as a box-checking exercise |
| Repatriation upon request | Must repatriate within 90 days of valid request | Delays of years or decades common; bureaucratic obstacles imposed |
| Research consent | Free, prior, and informed consent (2024 rule) | Previously conducted research without consent; ongoing resistance from some researchers |
| Civil penalties | Up to $14,527 per violation per day | Only 7 penalty cases in 34 years; near-zero enforcement |
| Criminal trafficking | Up to $100,000 fine and imprisonment | Rarely prosecuted; penalties do not deter organized trafficking |
