# Tribal Cultural Property: Overview

## Executive Summary

The protection and repatriation of Native American cultural property encompasses one of the most significant intersections of federal law, tribal sovereignty, human rights, and museum practice in the United States. For over a century, the remains of Native ancestors and their sacred and cultural objects were systematically collected, excavated, traded, and displayed by museums, universities, federal agencies, and private collectors -- often without the knowledge or consent of descendant communities. The Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act of 1990 (NAGPRA, 25 U.S.C. 3001-3013) established the legal framework for returning these items to lineal descendants and culturally affiliated tribes, but more than three decades after its enactment, implementation remains painfully incomplete.

As of 2024, an estimated 108,000 Native American ancestors remain in the possession of institutions subject to NAGPRA, and hundreds of thousands of associated funerary objects, sacred objects, and objects of cultural patrimony have yet to be repatriated. The Department of the Interior's 2024 updated regulations strengthened NAGPRA's requirements -- eliminating the "right of possession" defense, requiring disposition of culturally unidentifiable remains, and mandating free and informed consent for research -- but resistance from some institutions and the sheer scale of unrepatriated holdings ensure that the repatriation process will continue for decades.

Beyond NAGPRA, tribal cultural property protection involves combating the ongoing looting and trafficking of archaeological resources, addressing the international art market's role in trafficking stolen cultural items, protecting cultural landscapes and traditional cultural properties from destruction, and asserting tribal authority over Indigenous cultural heritage. These issues implicate the Archaeological Resources Protection Act (ARPA), the National Historic Preservation Act (NHPA), international agreements including the UNESCO Convention, and emerging tribal cultural heritage codes.

## Scope

### What This Subtopic Covers

- **NAGPRA implementation**: Repatriation of human remains, funerary objects, sacred objects, and objects of cultural patrimony
- **Archaeological resource protection**: ARPA enforcement, looting prevention, site security
- **Cultural heritage trafficking**: Domestic and international trafficking of Native cultural items
- **Tribal cultural heritage authority**: Tribal cultural heritage codes, permits, and intellectual property
- **Historic preservation**: NHPA Section 106 consultation, Traditional Cultural Properties, National Register nominations
- **Museum practices**: Ethical standards, decolonization, collaborative curation
- **International frameworks**: UNESCO Convention, UNDRIP, bilateral agreements
- **Digital cultural property**: Online sales, 3D scanning, digital repatriation

## Core Tensions

- **Scientific study vs. repatriation**: Some researchers argue that study of human remains and objects advances knowledge; tribes assert that ancestors deserve respectful return regardless of scientific interest
- **Property rights vs. cultural rights**: Museums and collectors claim legal ownership; tribes assert inalienable cultural patrimony rights
- **Institutional inertia vs. legal obligation**: Many institutions have delayed NAGPRA compliance for decades, citing cost, staffing, or complexity
- **Documentation vs. oral tradition**: NAGPRA requires evidence of "cultural affiliation," which some institutions interpret to require Western documentation standards rather than accepting tribal oral history and traditional knowledge
- **National heritage vs. tribal sovereignty**: Tension between preserving objects as "national heritage" and respecting tribal authority over their own cultural patrimony
- **Enforcement vs. resources**: NAGPRA compliance monitoring is severely underfunded, allowing non-compliance to persist

## Key Questions

1. **Why has NAGPRA compliance been so slow?** What institutional, legal, and financial barriers have allowed over 108,000 ancestors to remain unrepatriated after 34 years?

2. **How effective are the 2024 updated regulations?** Will the elimination of the "right of possession" defense and strengthened consent requirements accelerate repatriation?

3. **How can looting and trafficking be more effectively addressed?** Why are ARPA penalties and enforcement insufficient to deter ongoing site destruction?

4. **What role should tribes play in determining the disposition of their cultural heritage?** How can tribal cultural authority be strengthened?

5. **How should "cultural affiliation" be determined?** What standards of evidence are appropriate, and who should decide?

6. **What is the international dimension?** How can the U.S. address trafficking of Native cultural items through international art markets?

7. **What does decolonization of museum practice look like?** How can institutions move beyond compliance to genuine partnership with tribes?

## Vision of Success

A system that fully protects tribal cultural property would feature:

- All ancestors currently held by institutions repatriated to descendant communities
- Sacred objects and objects of cultural patrimony returned to tribes with full provenance documentation
- Effective enforcement against looting and trafficking with penalties that deter offenders
- Tribal cultural heritage codes recognized and respected by federal and state authorities
- Collaborative museum practices built on partnership, consent, and shared stewardship
- International cooperation to prevent trafficking of Native cultural items
- Adequate federal funding for NAGPRA compliance, tribal cultural preservation, and enforcement
- Digital cultural property protections that prevent unauthorized reproduction and commercialization
