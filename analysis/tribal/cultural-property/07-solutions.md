# Tribal Cultural Property: Solutions

## Overview

Effective cultural property protection and repatriation requires action across multiple domains: legislative reform to strengthen NAGPRA and close enforcement gaps, administrative improvements to accelerate repatriation and combat trafficking, international cooperation to address the global art market, and community-based approaches to build tribal capacity. No single reform is sufficient; a comprehensive strategy must address the interconnected root causes identified in the analysis.

## Legislative Solutions

### 1. NAGPRA Enforcement and Funding Act

**Problem addressed**: NAGPRA enforcement has been virtually non-existent (7 civil penalty cases in 34 years), and funding ($2.7 million annually) is dramatically insufficient.

**Proposal**: Enact comprehensive legislation that:

- Increases NAGPRA grant funding from $2.7 million to $50 million annually
- Creates a dedicated NAGPRA enforcement division within the Department of the Interior with at least 50 compliance officers
- Establishes mandatory compliance audits of all institutions with NAGPRA obligations on a 5-year cycle
- Increases civil penalties to $50,000 per violation per day (from $14,527)
- Creates criminal penalties for willful non-compliance (up to $250,000 fine and 2 years imprisonment for institutional officials who knowingly obstruct repatriation)
- Establishes a private right of action for tribes to enforce NAGPRA in federal court
- Authorizes attorney fees for prevailing tribal plaintiffs
- Creates a NAGPRA ombudsman to receive and investigate complaints from tribes

**Evidence of effectiveness**: CalNAGPRA (California's SB 1007) demonstrates that state-level enforcement with teeth accelerates repatriation. The University of California system accelerated repatriation significantly after state legislative pressure.

### 2. Cultural Property Trafficking Prevention Act

**Problem addressed**: Trafficking of Native American cultural items through domestic and international markets continues with inadequate deterrence.

**Proposal**: Enact legislation that:

- Extends NAGPRA's trafficking prohibition to all sales (not just federally funded institutions)
- Creates a federal offense for the sale, purchase, or transport of stolen tribal cultural patrimony regardless of the seller's institutional status
- Establishes mandatory provenance disclosure requirements for sales of Native American cultural items above $1,000
- Authorizes federal seizure and forfeiture of illegally trafficked cultural items
- Creates a cultural property task force within the FBI with dedicated agents and prosecutors
- Mandates that online platforms implement policies to prevent sale of protected cultural items
- Authorizes the State Department to negotiate bilateral cultural property agreements specifically addressing Native American items
- Increases ARPA penalties and establishes mandatory minimum sentences for organized looting operations

**Evidence of effectiveness**: Italy's cultural property enforcement model, including the Carabinieri cultural heritage unit, demonstrates that dedicated law enforcement produces significant recoveries and deterrence.

### 3. Expanding NAGPRA's Scope

**Problem addressed**: NAGPRA applies only to federal agencies and institutions receiving federal funding. Private collectors, non-federally-funded institutions, and international holders are not covered.

**Proposal**:

- Extend NAGPRA's repatriation requirements to all institutions holding Native American human remains and cultural items, regardless of federal funding status
- Create a voluntary repatriation program for private collectors with tax incentives for returning items to tribes
- Establish a cultural property amnesty program allowing holders to return items without legal consequences for prior possession
- Require state-licensed dealers to report sales of Native American cultural items to a federal registry

**Rationale**: The current scope limitation allows institutions to avoid NAGPRA by declining federal funding. Expanding scope ensures comprehensive coverage.

### 4. International Cultural Property Recovery Act

**Problem addressed**: Native American sacred objects and cultural items are sold at international auctions (particularly in Paris) with no legal mechanism for recovery.

**Proposal**:

- Authorize the State Department to designate categories of Native American cultural items subject to import/export restrictions under the UNESCO Convention
- Establish bilateral cultural property agreements with major art market countries (France, United Kingdom, Germany, Belgium, Switzerland)
- Create a federal repatriation fund ($10 million annually) for purchasing items at international auction when diplomatic recovery fails
- Authorize the Attorney General to bring civil forfeiture actions against trafficked cultural items entering the United States
- Support tribal delegations to international forums and auction protests

## Administrative Solutions

### 5. Strengthened NAGPRA Implementation

**Problem addressed**: The 2024 updated NAGPRA regulations strengthened requirements but implementation depends on adequate federal resources and institutional compliance.

**Proposal**:

- Fund the National NAGPRA program at $15 million annually (from current ~$5 million operational budget)
- Staff the program with at least 30 additional compliance specialists
- Establish regional NAGPRA coordinators to provide direct technical assistance to institutions and tribes
- Create a public compliance dashboard tracking institutional progress toward the 2029 deadline
- Develop standardized training for institutional NAGPRA officers
- Establish inter-agency coordination with the Smithsonian's NMAI Act repatriation program

### 6. Tribal Cultural Heritage Authority Recognition

**Problem addressed**: Tribal cultural heritage codes and determinations are not consistently recognized by federal and state authorities.

**Proposal**:

- Issue executive order directing all federal agencies to recognize tribal cultural heritage codes and determinations
- Establish formal protocols for agency recognition of tribal cultural authority over objects and sites
- Support tribes in developing and codifying cultural heritage protection ordinances
- Create a tribal cultural heritage certification program funded by BIA
- Develop model tribal cultural heritage code templates

### 7. Digital Cultural Property Protections

**Problem addressed**: Online sales platforms, 3D scanning, and digital reproduction create new threats to cultural property protection.

**Proposal**:

- Require online platforms to implement cultural property compliance programs, including automated screening for listed items
- Establish federal standards for 3D scanning of tribal cultural items, requiring tribal consent before scanning
- Create a tribal digital cultural property registry that platforms must check before listing Native American items
- Develop data sovereignty protocols ensuring tribal control over digital representations of their cultural heritage
- Fund tribal digital preservation capacity ($5 million annually)

### 8. Looting Prevention and Site Security

**Problem addressed**: Archaeological site looting destroys irreplaceable cultural resources and feeds the trafficking market.

**Proposal**:

- Increase BLM cultural resource law enforcement from 39 positions to 200 positions
- Establish a cultural resource site monitoring program using remote sensing and satellite technology
- Fund tribal site steward programs ($10 million annually) modeled on successful state programs
- Create a confidential looting tip line with cash rewards (up to $25,000)
- Develop inter-agency intelligence sharing for cultural property crime
- Install monitoring technology at high-priority archaeological sites on federal and tribal land

## Legal and Judicial Solutions

### 9. Strategic Litigation

**Problem addressed**: Court interpretations have limited NAGPRA's effectiveness, and enforcement has been inadequate.

**Proposal**:

- Develop a coordinated litigation strategy targeting the most egregious cases of non-compliance
- Bring test cases challenging institutions that have delayed repatriation for decades
- Pursue criminal trafficking cases against organized looting networks
- File international complaints through UNESCO and other forums
- Build factual records demonstrating the spiritual, cultural, and community harm caused by retention of ancestors

### 10. Tribal Court Jurisdiction

**Problem addressed**: Tribal courts lack recognized jurisdiction over cultural property claims against non-tribal actors.

**Proposal**:

- Legislatively recognize tribal court jurisdiction over cultural property disputes arising on tribal land
- Establish full faith and credit for tribal court cultural property judgments
- Support tribal court capacity for cultural property adjudication
- Create inter-tribal cultural property dispute resolution mechanisms

## Community-Based Solutions

### 11. Tribal Repatriation Capacity Building

**Problem addressed**: Many tribes lack the staff, expertise, and funding to navigate NAGPRA's complex processes.

**Proposal**:

- Fund at least one full-time NAGPRA coordinator for every federally recognized tribe ($30 million annually)
- Establish a national tribal NAGPRA training program
- Create a NAGPRA legal assistance fund for tribes ($5 million annually)
- Support inter-tribal repatriation consortia for small tribes that cannot sustain individual programs
- Fund repatriation-related travel, ceremony, and reburial costs ($10 million annually)

### 12. Collaborative Museum Practices

**Problem addressed**: Museum practices regarding Native collections often exclude tribal perspectives and perpetuate colonial frameworks.

**Proposal**:

- Develop national museum standards for ethical Native American collections management, guided by tribal input
- Establish collaborative curation programs pairing museums with tribal cultural experts
- Fund tribal curatorial internships and museum studies scholarships ($5 million annually)
- Create model loan and exhibition agreements that give tribes veto authority over display of sacred and sensitive items
- Support museum decolonization initiatives that center Indigenous voices and perspectives

### 13. Public Education and Awareness

**Problem addressed**: Public understanding of cultural property issues remains limited, reducing political support for reform.

**Proposal**:

- Develop K-12 curriculum materials on NAGPRA, repatriation, and cultural property rights
- Support Indigenous-led media projects documenting repatriation stories
- Fund public awareness campaigns about the harm of purchasing stolen cultural items
- Engage the art world in ethical standards development for Native American cultural property
- Create traveling exhibitions about the repatriation movement

### 14. International Cooperation

**Problem addressed**: The global art market enables trafficking of Native American cultural items beyond the reach of U.S. law.

**Proposal**:

- Establish an interagency cultural property working group (State, Justice, Interior, Homeland Security)
- Develop diplomatic strategies for each major art market country
- Support INTERPOL's cultural property database with Native American item listings
- Engage with UNDRIP implementation mechanisms to strengthen international norms
- Build coalitions with Indigenous peoples globally to advance cultural property rights at the UN
