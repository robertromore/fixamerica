# Tribal Cultural Property: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to stronger cultural property protections and accelerated repatriation comes from a range of institutional, commercial, and ideological actors. Some opponents resist specific reforms while supporting others; some resist the fundamental premise that tribes have inalienable rights to their cultural heritage. Understanding the arguments, motivations, and strategies of opponents is essential for crafting reforms that can navigate political resistance while achieving meaningful protection and repatriation.

## Major Opposition Groups

### 1. Museums and Universities Resisting Repatriation

#### Institutional Resistors

**Key actors**: Select university anthropology and archaeology departments, museum boards and administrators, institutional legal counsel, and collection managers at institutions with large NAGPRA holdings

**Arguments**:

- Collections have scientific and educational value that would be lost through repatriation
- Institutions are better equipped to preserve and conserve fragile objects than tribal communities
- Cultural affiliation determinations require rigorous scientific evidence, not merely oral tradition
- Repatriation costs strain institutional budgets and divert resources from core missions
- Some items were lawfully acquired and institutions have a legal right of possession

**Underlying motivations**:

- Collections are assets that drive research grants, faculty recruitment, museum attendance, and institutional prestige
- Staff positions and career trajectories depend on collection-based research
- Insurance valuations and endowment structures are tied to collection size
- "Universal museum" ideology holds that cultural objects belong to all humanity
- Institutional legal counsel often default to protecting institutional interests rather than facilitating compliance
- Decades of professional training taught that human remains and cultural objects are scientific resources

**Strategies**:

- Classifying remains as "culturally unidentifiable" to avoid repatriation
- Demanding documentary evidence of cultural affiliation that is impossible to produce for ancient remains
- Hiring experts to challenge tribal affiliation claims
- Interpreting NAGPRA requirements narrowly to minimize obligations
- Citing cost and staffing limitations to justify delays
- Lobbying quietly against strengthened NAGPRA regulations

**Scale of resistance**: While the overall trend in museum practice is moving toward repatriation, significant pockets of resistance remain. ProPublica's 2023 investigation documented that many institutions had done little to repatriate remains decades after filing initial NAGPRA inventories. Over 600 institutions still hold outstanding NAGPRA obligations as of 2024.

### 2. Scientific and Academic Opposition

#### Researchers and Professional Organizations

**Key actors**: Physical anthropologists and bioarchaeologists who study human remains, some archaeological researchers, individual faculty members whose careers depend on collection access

**Arguments**:

- Study of human remains advances knowledge about human migration, disease, diet, and adaptation
- Ancient DNA analysis can contribute to tribal knowledge about their own history
- Repatriation of ancient remains (pre-contact) eliminates irreplaceable scientific data
- The 2024 NAGPRA rule's consent requirement for research creates insurmountable barriers to study
- Scientific evidence should be the primary basis for cultural affiliation, not oral tradition
- Future analytical techniques may reveal information that current methods cannot

**Underlying motivations**:

- Career incentives: publications, tenure, and grants depend on access to collections
- Professional identity invested in the study of human remains
- Genuine belief that scientific knowledge production is a public good
- Resistance to ceding authority over evidence standards to non-scientists
- Economic interests of university departments and research programs

**Strategies**:

- Publishing academic articles arguing that repatriation destroys scientific knowledge
- Advocating for study periods before repatriation
- Challenging cultural affiliation findings through scientific publications
- Seeking legal standing to intervene in repatriation decisions (as in the Kennewick Man case)
- Lobbying professional organizations to resist consent requirements

**Evolution**: The scientific community has evolved significantly. The Society for American Archaeology now supports NAGPRA and ethical consultation. However, pockets of resistance persist, particularly among older researchers and in subfields directly dependent on access to human remains.

### 3. Art Market Participants

#### Auction Houses and Dealers

**Key actors**: European auction houses (Drouot, Millon, EVE), domestic dealers, online platforms (eBay, Etsy, specialized auction sites), Antique Tribal Art Dealers Association (ATADA)

**Arguments**:

- Items in the commercial market were lawfully acquired and can be lawfully sold
- Restricting the art market harms legitimate collectors and the broader cultural economy
- NAGPRA applies only to federally funded institutions, not private transactions
- Distinguishing between lawfully acquired items and illegally obtained items is impractical
- Restricting trade drives the market underground, making items harder to track
- European auction houses are not subject to U.S. law

**Underlying motivations**:

- Revenue: Native American items generate significant sales commissions
- Inventory value: dealers and collectors hold substantial inventory at risk from expanded restrictions
- Market access: maintaining open markets for trading Indigenous cultural items
- Legal liability avoidance: resist provenance requirements that could reveal problematic acquisition histories

**Strategies**:

- Opposing legislation that would expand NAGPRA's scope to private sales
- Resisting provenance disclosure requirements
- Moving sales to jurisdictions beyond U.S. legal reach (particularly Paris)
- Lobbying against international cultural property agreements
- Arguing that restricting trade harms tribal artists who sell their own work

### 4. Private Collectors

**Key actors**: Individual collectors of Native American art and artifacts, collector associations, estate holders

**Arguments**:

- Private property rights protect lawfully purchased items
- Collectors are stewards who preserve cultural heritage
- Many items were obtained through legitimate purchase or gift
- Retroactive application of new laws to existing private collections would be unconstitutional
- Collectors contribute to cultural knowledge through documentation and exhibition

**Underlying motivations**:

- Financial investment in collections worth thousands to millions of dollars
- Personal attachment to collected items
- Social prestige associated with collection ownership
- Estate value and inheritance planning
- Fear of government seizure of private property

### 5. Property Rights and Anti-Regulation Advocates

**Key actors**: Pacific Legal Foundation, property rights organizations, anti-regulation think tanks

**Arguments**:

- Expanding NAGPRA to private sales would constitute government overreach
- Restricting the sale of lawfully obtained property violates the Fifth Amendment
- Provenance requirements impose unreasonable burdens on property owners
- Federal cultural property regulation should be narrowly limited to federal land and federally funded institutions

**Underlying motivations**:

- Philosophical commitment to property rights and limited government
- Concern about precedent-setting expansion of federal regulatory authority
- Strategic interest in limiting the regulatory state
- Some alignment with collector and dealer financial interests

### 6. International Actors Resisting Repatriation

**Key actors**: European auction houses (particularly French), foreign museums holding Native American collections, foreign governments resisting cultural property return

**Arguments**:

- U.S. domestic law has no extraterritorial application
- Items acquired before NAGPRA were lawfully obtained under the law of the time
- Cultural objects in international collections serve universal education and appreciation
- Bilateral cultural property agreements are the appropriate mechanism, not unilateral demands
- French law protects the right to auction any legally held item

**Underlying motivations**:

- Revenue from Native American item sales
- National prestige of museum collections
- Sovereignty concerns about applying foreign law domestically
- Market freedom ideology in the art world

## Common Arguments Against Stronger Protections

### The "Scientific Knowledge" Argument

**Claim**: Repatriation of human remains and cultural objects destroys irreplaceable scientific data essential for understanding human history.

**Analysis**: This argument privileges Western scientific knowledge over Indigenous cultural values and sovereignty. It assumes that institutional study is the only valid way to generate knowledge about the past and that institutional custody is the only way to preserve that knowledge. Tribes have the right to determine the disposition of their ancestors regardless of scientific interest. Many tribes also welcome collaborative research conducted with consent -- the issue is consent, not opposition to knowledge.

### The "Lawful Acquisition" Argument

**Claim**: Institutions and collectors who obtained items through legal purchase have a right to keep them.

**Analysis**: "Lawful acquisition" is a contested concept in this context. Many items were obtained through grave robbing, battlefield scavenging, coerced sales, or purchases from intermediaries who acquired items through illegal means. Even where acquisition was technically legal, it often occurred under conditions of extreme power asymmetry. The 2024 NAGPRA regulations eliminated the "right of possession" defense, recognizing that legal acquisition does not override cultural patrimony rights. Objects of cultural patrimony, by definition, cannot be alienated by an individual because they belong to the community.

### The "Universal Heritage" Argument

**Claim**: Cultural objects in museum collections serve all humanity and should remain accessible in major institutions.

**Analysis**: This argument assumes that institutional display is the highest use of cultural objects and ignores their ceremonial, spiritual, and community functions. It also assumes that "universal heritage" is best served by Western institutional control. The argument has been deployed historically to justify colonial-era collection practices and continues to serve institutional interests in retaining holdings.

### The "Preservation Capacity" Argument

**Claim**: Institutions have superior preservation facilities and expertise compared to tribal communities, and repatriation risks the deterioration or loss of items.

**Analysis**: This argument is paternalistic and factually contested. Many tribes have built or are building cultural centers with modern preservation facilities. More fundamentally, tribes define "preservation" differently -- some items are meant to be used ceremonially, not stored indefinitely in climate-controlled rooms. The argument also ignores that many institutions have poorly maintained Native collections for decades.

### The "Burden of Proof" Argument

**Claim**: Tribes should bear the burden of proving cultural affiliation before repatriation occurs.

**Analysis**: Placing the burden on tribes reverses the moral equation. Institutions possess items that were taken from tribal communities, often without consent. The 2024 NAGPRA regulations addressed this by shifting the burden: institutions now must initiate consultation and cannot rely on "culturally unidentifiable" classifications to retain items indefinitely.

### The "Slippery Slope" Argument

**Claim**: Expanding repatriation requirements will lead to the emptying of museum collections and the end of anthropological research.

**Analysis**: This argument is not supported by evidence. NAGPRA applies specifically to human remains, funerary objects, sacred objects, and objects of cultural patrimony -- not to all Native American items in museum collections. Decades of NAGPRA compliance have demonstrated that repatriation and museum education can coexist. Many museums have developed collaborative exhibition practices that are stronger and more accurate than pre-NAGPRA displays.

## Counter-Strategies for Reform Advocates

### Addressing Institutional Resistance

- Document non-compliance publicly to create reputational consequences (as ProPublica's investigation demonstrated)
- Build coalitions within museums among progressive curators, educators, and administrators
- Support state-level NAGPRA equivalents (like California's CalNAGPRA) that create additional compliance pressure
- Advocate for increased enforcement of existing NAGPRA penalties
- Frame repatriation as opportunity for collaborative curation and improved museum practice

### Addressing Scientific Opposition

- Promote collaborative research models where tribes and scientists work together with tribal consent
- Highlight successful examples of tribal-scientist partnerships
- Support tribal capacity for archaeological and cultural research
- Emphasize that consent requirements do not end research -- they ensure it is ethical
- Engage sympathetic professional organizations (SAA, AAA) to strengthen ethical standards

### Addressing Market Opposition

- Support legislation extending cultural property trafficking protections to private sales
- Advocate for international cultural property agreements specifically addressing Native American items
- Push online platforms to enforce policies against selling protected cultural items
- Support customs enforcement at borders to intercept trafficked items
- Develop provenance databases to identify items with problematic acquisition histories

### Addressing Property Rights Arguments

- Distinguish between general property rights and cultural patrimony (which cannot be alienated)
- Emphasize that NAGPRA does not apply to all Native American items, only specific categories
- Argue that strengthening cultural property protections reinforces property rights by recognizing tribal ownership
- Frame the issue as correcting theft rather than restricting property rights

### Building Unusual Alliances

- Religious communities share the principle that ancestral remains deserve respectful treatment
- Environmental organizations support cultural landscape protection
- Historic preservation advocates share the framework of protecting irreplaceable cultural resources
- Human rights organizations connect repatriation to international Indigenous rights standards
- Museums with strong repatriation records can serve as models and advocates for lagging institutions
