# Tribal Cultural Property: Root Causes

## Overview

The ongoing failure to fully protect and repatriate tribal cultural property results from a convergence of institutional inertia, economic incentives, legal limitations, and deep-seated assumptions about who "owns" the past. These root causes are interconnected: colonial-era attitudes about Native culture as "primitive" or "vanishing" laid the groundwork for systematic collection, which created institutional interests in retaining collections, which in turn generated legal frameworks that privileged institutional possession over Indigenous cultural rights. Addressing the problem requires understanding and dismantling each layer.

## Root Cause Analysis

### 1. Colonial Ideology and the "Vanishing Indian" Narrative

**Description**: The foundational assumption that Native cultures were inferior and destined to disappear justified the systematic collection, study, and display of Native remains and cultural objects as acts of "preservation" rather than dispossession.

**How It Operates**:

- "Salvage ethnography" and "salvage archaeology" treated collecting as urgent before cultures "vanished"
- Native cultural items reclassified from living heritage to scientific specimens or art objects
- Western knowledge systems privileged over Indigenous knowledge claims
- Assumption that institutions could better preserve cultural heritage than the communities that created it
- Dehumanization of Native people made grave robbing and display of remains acceptable

**Evidence**:

- Surgeon General's 1862 order explicitly sought crania for "racial science"
- Museum collecting expeditions framed as "salvage" operations
- Thomas, David Hurst. *Skull Wars.* 2000.

### 2. Institutional Economic and Prestige Interests

**Description**: Museums, universities, and federal agencies have significant financial and reputational investments in their Native American collections, creating institutional resistance to repatriation.

**How It Operates**:

- Collections drive museum attendance, research grants, and institutional prestige
- Deaccessioning perceived as weakening the institution's scholarly and educational mission
- Staff positions tied to collection management; repatriation eliminates the need for some positions
- Insurance valuations and endowment structures tied to collection size
- "Universal museum" ideology argues that cultural objects belong to "all humanity" and should remain accessible in major institutions
- Some institutions treat NAGPRA compliance as a cost center rather than a legal and moral obligation

**Evidence**:

- ProPublica's 2023 investigation found institutions holding remains for decades despite clear affiliation
- University of California system held ~12,000 individuals despite state and federal law
- Several institutions cited cost as a reason for delayed compliance

### 3. Inadequate Enforcement

**Description**: NAGPRA enforcement has been virtually non-existent for most of the law's history. Only 7 civil penalty cases have been brought in 34 years, and criminal prosecutions for trafficking are rare.

**How It Operates**:

- National NAGPRA program has minimal staff for oversight of 1,100+ institutions
- Civil penalty process is cumbersome and rarely used
- No systematic compliance monitoring or auditing
- ARPA enforcement relies on a handful of federal law enforcement officers covering hundreds of millions of acres
- Criminal penalties for trafficking are inadequate to deter organized looting operations
- No private right of action for tribes to enforce NAGPRA directly in federal court

**Evidence**:

- Only 7 NAGPRA civil penalty cases in 34 years (National NAGPRA program, 2024)
- BLM has 39 law enforcement positions for cultural resources across 245 million acres
- GAO reports have repeatedly documented inadequate enforcement

### 4. "Cultural Affiliation" as Bureaucratic Obstacle

**Description**: NAGPRA's requirement that tribes demonstrate "cultural affiliation" with human remains and objects has been weaponized by some institutions to delay or deny repatriation by demanding levels of proof that are impossible to meet for ancient remains.

**How It Operates**:

- Institutions define "cultural affiliation" narrowly, requiring Western scientific evidence
- Oral traditions and traditional knowledge downweighted compared to archaeological data
- "Culturally unidentifiable" classification used for remains that institutions cannot or will not affiliate
- Ancient remains (pre-contact) particularly difficult to affiliate using institutional criteria
- The burden of proof falls on tribes rather than institutions
- Some institutions hire their own experts to challenge tribal affiliation claims

**Evidence**:

- Kennewick Man/The Ancient One: 9,000-year-old remains held for 20 years despite tribal claims
- 108,000 individuals remain unrepatriated; many classified as "culturally unidentifiable"
- 2024 regulations addressed this by eliminating "culturally unidentifiable" as a permanent category

### 5. International Art Market and Trafficking Networks

**Description**: A lucrative global market in Native American cultural items creates economic incentives for looting, trafficking, and resale, while international law provides limited mechanisms for recovery.

**How It Operates**:

- Native American items sold at European auction houses (particularly in Paris) where NAGPRA does not apply
- Online platforms (eBay, Etsy, specialized auction sites) facilitate anonymous sales
- Art dealers purchase items of uncertain provenance with minimal due diligence
- No bilateral cultural property agreements specifically addressing Native American items
- Demand from private collectors drives prices that incentivize looting
- Stolen sacred objects laundered through multiple transactions to obscure origin

**Evidence**:

- Hopi and other tribal delegations have traveled to Paris to protest auction sales of sacred objects
- Estimated 200-500+ lots of Native American items sold annually at major European auction houses
- UNESCO estimates global illicit antiquities market at $3-6 billion annually

### 6. Inadequate Funding

**Description**: Federal funding for NAGPRA implementation, tribal repatriation activities, ARPA enforcement, and cultural preservation is dramatically insufficient relative to the scope of need.

**How It Operates**:

- NAGPRA grants total $2.7 million annually for all tribes and institutions nationwide
- Average grant of ~$80,000 insufficient for complex repatriation projects
- Tribal NAGPRA offices understaffed; many tribes have no dedicated NAGPRA staff
- Federal law enforcement for cultural resources chronically underfunded
- No dedicated funding for international recovery of trafficked items
- Cost of repatriation (travel, ceremonial protocols, reburial) borne primarily by tribes

**Evidence**:

- NAGPRA grant program has not been substantially increased since the 1990s
- BIA has no dedicated cultural property budget line
- Tribes report spending scarce general funds on repatriation activities

### 7. Fragmented Legal Authority

**Description**: Responsibility for cultural property protection is split among multiple federal agencies, state governments, tribal governments, and international bodies, creating gaps and inconsistencies.

**How It Operates**:

- NAGPRA administered by National Park Service (DOI)
- ARPA enforced by BLM, NPS, USFS, and tribal police depending on land jurisdiction
- NHPA Section 106 administered by Advisory Council on Historic Preservation
- Trafficking enforcement divided among FBI, BLM, NPS, and tribal law enforcement
- International trafficking addressed by State Department, Customs and Border Protection, and DOJ
- State laws vary dramatically; most states have no NAGPRA equivalent
- Tribal cultural heritage codes not recognized by all jurisdictions

**Evidence**:

- GAO. "Native American Cultural Property: Additional Agency Actions Would Enhance Implementation." GAO-23-105111, 2023.
- Multiple agencies with overlapping but incomplete jurisdiction

### 8. Digital Era Challenges

**Description**: The internet and digital technologies have created new threats to cultural property protection while also offering new tools for preservation.

**How It Operates**:

- Online marketplaces enable anonymous sale of cultural items with minimal provenance verification
- 3D scanning and digital reproduction raise questions about digital cultural property rights
- Social media posts revealing site locations enable looting
- Digital databases of remains and objects may violate cultural protocols about who can view sacred items
- Tribes lack consistent authority to control digital representations of their cultural heritage

**Evidence**:

- ATADA monitoring documents thousands of questionable online listings annually
- Multiple looting incidents traced to publicly shared GPS coordinates of archaeological sites
- Tribal objections to 3D scanning of repatriated or sacred objects

## Root Cause Interaction Map

| Primary Cause | Reinforced By | Creates |
|---------------|---------------|---------|
| Colonial ideology | Institutional interests, Western scientific hegemony | Continued framing of remains as "specimens" rather than ancestors |
| Institutional interests | Inadequate enforcement, legal framework | Resistance to repatriation; slow compliance |
| Inadequate enforcement | Underfunding, fragmented authority | Consequence-free non-compliance |
| Cultural affiliation barriers | Western evidence standards, institutional resistance | Perpetual holding of "unidentifiable" remains |
| Art market trafficking | International legal gaps, digital platforms | Continued looting and sale of sacred items |
| Underfunding | Political will, competing priorities | Insufficient capacity for enforcement and repatriation |
| Fragmented authority | Multiple statutes and agencies | Gaps in coverage, inconsistent enforcement |
| Digital challenges | Technology outpacing law, global reach | New avenues for trafficking and exploitation |

## Systemic vs. Individual Factors

| Factor Type | Examples | Policy Implication |
|-------------|----------|-------------------|
| Systemic/Structural | Colonial ideology, institutional interests, funding structure | Requires legal and institutional reform |
| Economic | Art market incentives, institutional collection valuations | Requires market regulation and enforcement |
| Legal/Regulatory | Enforcement gaps, fragmented authority, international law | Requires legislative strengthening and coordination |
| Cultural | Western scientific privilege, dehumanization | Requires education, ethics reform, and cultural shift |
| Technological | Digital platforms, 3D scanning, social media | Requires updated legal frameworks and digital protections |
