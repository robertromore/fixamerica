# Tribal Cultural Property: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative landscape for tribal cultural property protection centers on NAGPRA (25 U.S.C. 3001-3013), which established the federal framework for repatriation in 1990, and ARPA (16 U.S.C. 470aa-470mm), which addresses archaeological resource protection. While NAGPRA represented a landmark achievement, more than three decades of inadequate enforcement, insufficient funding, and limited scope have left the framework unable to fully accomplish its goals. The 2024 updated regulations (43 C.F.R. Part 10) significantly strengthened implementation requirements, but legislative action is needed to address enforcement gaps, expand scope, combat trafficking, and build tribal capacity. The proposals below address these needs through multiple legislative strategies.

## Constitutional Amendments

No constitutional amendment is proposed for cultural property protection specifically. Congress has clear authority under the Indian Commerce Clause (Art. I, Sec. 8, Cl. 3) to legislate regarding tribal cultural property, as affirmed in *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023). The Commerce Clause and Necessary and Proper Clause provide additional authority for trafficking and market regulation. Legislative solutions are more feasible than constitutional amendment and can be crafted within existing constitutional authority.

## Federal Legislation

### NAGPRA Enforcement and Funding Act

**Purpose**: Strengthen enforcement of the Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act, increase funding for repatriation, establish a private right of action for tribes, and create a dedicated enforcement division within the Department of the Interior.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "NAGPRA Enforcement and Funding Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSES.

(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that—
    (1) the Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act
    (25 U.S.C. 3001-3013) was enacted in 1990 to address the
    systematic dispossession of Native American human remains,
    funerary objects, sacred objects, and objects of cultural
    patrimony;
    (2) more than 35 years after enactment, approximately 108,000
    Native American individuals remain in the custody of
    institutions subject to the Act;
    (3) only 7 civil penalty cases have been brought under the
    Act in its entire history, representing near-total absence
    of enforcement;
    (4) annual federal funding for NAGPRA grants ($2.7 million)
    is dramatically insufficient relative to the scope of
    repatriation need;
    (5) the Department of the Interior issued updated NAGPRA
    regulations effective January 2024, establishing a 5-year
    compliance deadline; and
    (6) legislative action is necessary to ensure that the
    updated regulations are effectively enforced and that
    repatriation is completed.

(b) PURPOSES.—The purposes of this Act are to—
    (1) provide adequate funding for NAGPRA implementation and
    repatriation activities;
    (2) establish a dedicated enforcement division to monitor
    institutional compliance;
    (3) create a private right of action for tribes to enforce
    NAGPRA in federal court;
    (4) increase civil and criminal penalties for non-compliance;
    and
    (5) ensure the complete repatriation of all Native American
    human remains and cultural items held by covered institutions.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act, terms have the meanings given in section 2 of the
Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act (25 U.S.C.
3001).

SEC. 4. NAGPRA ENFORCEMENT DIVISION.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established within the Department of
the Interior a NAGPRA Enforcement Division (hereinafter "Division").

(b) STAFFING.—The Division shall employ not fewer than 50
compliance officers and 10 attorneys dedicated to NAGPRA
enforcement.

(c) FUNCTIONS.—The Division shall—
    (1) conduct mandatory compliance audits of all institutions
    with NAGPRA obligations on a 5-year rotating cycle;
    (2) investigate complaints from Indian tribes, lineal
    descendants, and Native Hawaiian organizations regarding
    institutional non-compliance;
    (3) initiate civil penalty proceedings against non-compliant
    institutions;
    (4) refer willful violations to the Department of Justice
    for criminal prosecution;
    (5) maintain a public compliance dashboard reporting
    institutional progress toward repatriation obligations;
    (6) provide technical assistance to institutions and tribes
    on NAGPRA compliance; and
    (7) submit an annual report to Congress on enforcement
    activities and repatriation progress.

(d) NAGPRA OMBUDSMAN.—The Division shall include a NAGPRA
Ombudsman appointed by the Secretary to receive and investigate
complaints from Indian tribes and lineal descendants regarding
institutional non-compliance.

SEC. 5. INCREASED PENALTIES.

(a) CIVIL PENALTIES.—Section 9 of the Native American Graves
Protection and Repatriation Act (25 U.S.C. 3007) is amended—
    (1) by striking "$14,527" and inserting "$50,000"; and
    (2) by adding at the end the following:
    "(c) WILLFUL NON-COMPLIANCE.—Any museum that willfully fails
    to comply with this Act after receiving a written notice of
    non-compliance from the Secretary shall be subject to a civil
    penalty of not less than $10,000 and not more than $100,000
    per violation per day of continued non-compliance."

(b) CRIMINAL PENALTIES.—Section 4 of the Native American Graves
Protection and Repatriation Act (25 U.S.C. 3002) is amended by
adding the following:
    "(e) INSTITUTIONAL OFFICER LIABILITY.—Any officer, director,
    curator, or employee of a museum or Federal agency who
    knowingly obstructs, delays, or prevents the repatriation of
    human remains, funerary objects, sacred objects, or objects of
    cultural patrimony in violation of this Act shall be fined not
    more than $250,000 or imprisoned not more than 2 years, or
    both."

SEC. 6. PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.

The Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act is
amended by adding at the end the following new section:

"SEC. 14. PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Any Indian tribe, lineal descendant, or Native
Hawaiian organization aggrieved by a violation of this Act may
bring a civil action in United States district court.

(b) REMEDIES.—The court may—
    (1) issue temporary restraining orders, preliminary
    injunctions, and permanent injunctions to enforce
    compliance with this Act;
    (2) order the immediate repatriation of human remains,
    funerary objects, sacred objects, or objects of cultural
    patrimony found to be held in violation of this Act;
    (3) award compensatory damages for harm caused by
    non-compliance; and
    (4) award declaratory relief.

(c) ATTORNEY FEES.—The court shall award reasonable attorney
fees and costs to a prevailing plaintiff under this section.

(d) STATUTE OF LIMITATIONS.—An action under this section may
be brought within 6 years of the date the plaintiff knew or
should have known of the violation."

SEC. 7. EXPANDED SCOPE.

(a) The definition of "museum" in section 2 of the Native
American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act (25 U.S.C. 3001)
is amended to read as follows:
    "(8) 'museum' means any institution or State or local
    government agency (including any institution of higher
    education) that has possession of, or control over, Native
    American human remains, funerary objects, sacred objects,
    or objects of cultural patrimony."

(b) TRANSITION.—Institutions not previously subject to this Act
shall have 3 years from the date of enactment to complete
inventories and initiate consultation.

SEC. 8. VOLUNTARY PRIVATE REPATRIATION PROGRAM.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary shall establish a Voluntary
Private Repatriation Program to facilitate the return of Native
American cultural items held by private individuals and
non-covered entities.

(b) TAX INCENTIVE.—Individuals who donate Native American human
remains, funerary objects, sacred objects, or objects of cultural
patrimony to the appropriate Indian tribe through the Program
shall be entitled to a charitable deduction equal to the fair
market value of the donated item, as determined by a qualified
appraiser.

(c) AMNESTY.—No criminal or civil penalties shall be imposed
on individuals who voluntarily return items through the Program,
regardless of how such items were originally acquired.

SEC. 9. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated—
(a) $50,000,000 annually for NAGPRA repatriation grants to
Indian tribes and museums;
(b) $15,000,000 annually for the NAGPRA Enforcement Division;
(c) $10,000,000 annually for tribal NAGPRA coordinator
positions;
(d) $10,000,000 annually for repatriation-related travel,
ceremony, and reburial costs; and
(e) $5,000,000 annually for tribal NAGPRA legal assistance.
```

**Explanation**: This comprehensive legislation addresses the core enforcement gap that has allowed NAGPRA non-compliance to persist for over three decades. The enforcement division creates dedicated institutional capacity for monitoring and enforcement. The private right of action empowers tribes to enforce their own rights without depending on federal agency initiative. The expanded scope closes the loophole that allows non-federally-funded institutions to hold remains without NAGPRA obligations. The voluntary repatriation program with tax incentives creates a pathway for private collectors to return items without legal risk.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Institutional lobbying, particularly from universities and museums, will resist expanded scope and increased penalties
- Budget hawks will object to the $90 million annual authorization
- Private property advocates will resist the expanded definition of "museum"
- Some institutions may argue that the expanded scope raises constitutional questions
- The voluntary repatriation program's tax incentive may face scrutiny as a subsidy

**Refinements**:

- Consider a phased penalty escalation to give institutions an initial compliance window
- Establish a NAGPRA compliance assistance program alongside enforcement
- Address the Smithsonian's separate NMAI Act framework to ensure coordination
- Include provisions for institutions facing genuine financial hardship

### Cultural Property Trafficking Prevention Act

**Purpose**: Strengthen federal prohibitions against trafficking in Native American cultural property, establish provenance disclosure requirements, and create dedicated law enforcement capacity.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Cultural Property Trafficking
Prevention Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
(1) the illegal trafficking of Native American cultural property
is a significant and growing problem, driven by a lucrative
domestic and international market;
(2) existing penalties under NAGPRA and ARPA are insufficient
to deter organized trafficking networks;
(3) online platforms have become major venues for the sale of
illegally obtained cultural items;
(4) the sale of Native American sacred objects and cultural
patrimony at international auction houses, particularly in Europe,
continues despite diplomatic protests; and
(5) dedicated law enforcement resources are necessary to combat
cultural property trafficking effectively.

SEC. 3. ENHANCED TRAFFICKING PROHIBITION.

(a) IN GENERAL.—It shall be unlawful for any person to sell,
purchase, transport, or offer to sell, purchase, or transport
any Native American human remains, funerary objects, sacred
objects, or objects of cultural patrimony—
    (1) obtained in violation of the Native American Graves
    Protection and Repatriation Act (25 U.S.C. 3001 et seq.),
    the Archaeological Resources Protection Act of 1979
    (16 U.S.C. 470aa et seq.), or any applicable State or
    tribal law; or
    (2) that the person knows or has reason to know were obtained
    through theft, unauthorized excavation, or violation of any
    Federal, State, or tribal law.

(b) PENALTIES.—
    (1) FIRST OFFENSE.—A person who violates subsection (a)
    shall be fined not more than $250,000 or imprisoned not
    more than 5 years, or both.
    (2) SUBSEQUENT OFFENSE.—A person who violates subsection (a)
    after a prior conviction shall be fined not more than
    $500,000 or imprisoned not more than 10 years, or both.
    (3) ORGANIZED TRAFFICKING.—A person who violates subsection
    (a) as part of an organized trafficking operation involving
    three or more persons shall be fined not more than
    $1,000,000 or imprisoned not more than 15 years, or both.

SEC. 4. PROVENANCE DISCLOSURE.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Any person who sells or offers to sell a Native
American cultural item with a value of $1,000 or more shall
disclose to the buyer, in writing—
    (1) the item's known provenance, including the identity of
    all prior owners known to the seller;
    (2) any export or import documentation associated with the
    item;
    (3) any cultural affiliation determination or NAGPRA notice
    associated with the item; and
    (4) a statement of the seller's basis for believing the sale
    is lawful.

(b) RECORD KEEPING.—Sellers subject to subsection (a) shall
maintain provenance records for not less than 10 years.

(c) PENALTY.—Failure to comply with this section shall be
punishable by a fine of not more than $50,000 for each violation.

SEC. 5. ONLINE PLATFORM RESPONSIBILITIES.

(a) POLICY REQUIREMENT.—Online marketplace platforms with annual
gross revenues exceeding $100,000,000 shall—
    (1) implement a cultural property compliance program
    designed to prevent the sale of illegally obtained Native
    American cultural items;
    (2) provide a mechanism for tribes and the public to report
    suspected illegal listings;
    (3) respond to tribal reports within 48 hours; and
    (4) cooperate with law enforcement investigations.

(b) SAFE HARBOR.—Platforms that comply with subsection (a) in
good faith shall not be liable for individual listings that
evade compliance measures.

(c) PENALTY.—Platforms that fail to implement the required
compliance program shall be subject to a fine of not more than
$500,000 per month of non-compliance.

SEC. 6. FBI CULTURAL PROPERTY TASK FORCE.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established within the Federal
Bureau of Investigation a Cultural Property Task Force.

(b) STAFFING.—The Task Force shall employ not fewer than—
    (1) 25 special agents dedicated to cultural property
    investigations;
    (2) 10 intelligence analysts; and
    (3) 5 prosecutors detailed from the Department of Justice.

(c) FUNCTIONS.—The Task Force shall—
    (1) investigate trafficking in Native American and other
    cultural property;
    (2) coordinate with tribal, State, and local law
    enforcement;
    (3) collaborate with international law enforcement agencies,
    including INTERPOL;
    (4) maintain an updated database of stolen and trafficked
    cultural items; and
    (5) provide training to tribal and Federal law enforcement
    on cultural property crimes.

SEC. 7. INTERNATIONAL CULTURAL PROPERTY RECOVERY.

(a) The Secretary of State is authorized to negotiate bilateral
cultural property agreements with foreign nations for the
protection and return of Native American cultural items.

(b) REPATRIATION FUND.—There is established a Cultural Property
Repatriation Fund of $10,000,000 annually to facilitate the
recovery of Native American cultural items from international
markets, including purchase at auction when diplomatic recovery
is not feasible.

(c) TRIBAL DELEGATION SUPPORT.—The Secretary of State shall
provide logistical and financial support for tribal delegations
engaged in international cultural property recovery.

SEC. 8. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated—
(a) $15,000,000 annually for the FBI Cultural Property Task Force;
(b) $10,000,000 annually for the Cultural Property Repatriation
Fund;
(c) $5,000,000 annually for ARPA enforcement enhancement; and
(d) $5,000,000 annually for tribal delegation and international
advocacy support.
```

**Explanation**: This legislation addresses the trafficking dimension of cultural property protection. The enhanced criminal penalties provide meaningful deterrence for organized trafficking. The provenance disclosure requirement brings transparency to the market. The online platform responsibilities address the growing role of digital commerce in trafficking. The FBI Cultural Property Task Force creates dedicated law enforcement capacity. The international provisions address the extraterritorial dimension of the problem.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Art market industry will lobby intensely against provenance disclosure requirements
- Online platform companies will resist compliance mandates and associated costs
- International provisions face sovereignty concerns from foreign governments
- Budget requirements are significant ($35 million annually)
- Defining "Native American cultural item" for the provenance disclosure requirement may be contentious

**Refinements**:

- Phase in provenance disclosure requirements with an initial education period
- Develop safe harbor provisions for good-faith sellers
- Coordinate with existing art market regulatory frameworks
- Address the intersection with the Indian Arts and Crafts Act to avoid conflicting requirements
- Consider a graduated threshold for provenance disclosure based on item category

## State Model Legislation

### State Cultural Property Repatriation Act

**Purpose**: Extend repatriation requirements to all institutions within the state regardless of federal funding status, following California's CalNAGPRA model.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "[State] Cultural Property
Repatriation Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.
(a) "Cultural item" means human remains, funerary objects, sacred
objects, or objects of cultural patrimony, as those terms are
defined in the Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation
Act (25 U.S.C. 3001).
(b) "Agency or institution" means any state agency, state-funded
institution, museum, university, or other entity within the state
that has possession of or control over cultural items.
(c) "Indian tribe" means any Indian tribe, band, nation, or
organized group or community recognized by the state or federal
government.

SECTION 3. INVENTORY AND CONSULTATION.
(a) Every agency or institution within the state that has
possession of or control over cultural items shall, within 2
years of the effective date of this Act:
    (1) complete a comprehensive inventory of all cultural items
    in its possession or control;
    (2) initiate consultation with potentially affiliated Indian
    tribes regarding the cultural affiliation, repatriation, or
    other disposition of such items; and
    (3) submit an inventory report to the [state agency
    administering the program].
(b) Consultation shall give equal weight to tribal oral
tradition, traditional knowledge, and scientific evidence in
determining cultural affiliation.

SECTION 4. REPATRIATION.
Upon a request from a lineal descendant or culturally affiliated
Indian tribe, an agency or institution shall repatriate cultural
items within 180 days unless a genuine dispute over cultural
affiliation exists.

SECTION 5. PENALTIES.
(a) An agency or institution that fails to comply with this Act
shall be subject to a civil penalty of not more than $25,000 per
violation.
(b) State funding may be conditioned on compliance with this Act.

SECTION 6. ENFORCEMENT.
An aggrieved Indian tribe or lineal descendant may bring a civil
action in state court to enforce this Act. The court may award
attorney fees to a prevailing plaintiff.
```

**Explanation**: This model legislation extends repatriation requirements to all institutions within the state, closing the gap left by NAGPRA's limitation to federally funded institutions. California's CalNAGPRA (SB 1007) provides a working model.

**Adaptations**: States with large Indigenous populations and significant institutional holdings (Arizona, New Mexico, Oklahoma, Ohio, Tennessee, New York) should prioritize enactment. Penalty levels should be adjusted to state context.

## Regulatory Framework

### Strengthened NAGPRA Regulations

**Existing Authority**: NAGPRA (25 U.S.C. 3001-3013); 2024 updated regulations (43 C.F.R. Part 10)

**Proposed Regulatory Actions**:

- Issue supplemental guidance interpreting the 2024 regulations, addressing common institutional questions and delay tactics
- Establish compliance audit procedures and protocols
- Develop standardized cultural affiliation determination process that gives equal weight to oral tradition
- Create mandatory reporting requirements for institutions discovering previously uninventoried Native American items
- Establish inter-agency coordination protocols between the National NAGPRA program and the Smithsonian's NMAI Act program

### Digital Cultural Property Regulations

**Existing Authority**: NAGPRA trafficking provisions; ARPA; Indian Arts and Crafts Act

**Proposed Regulatory Actions**:

- Establish federal standards for 3D scanning of Native American cultural items, requiring tribal consent
- Create a digital cultural property registry that platforms must check before listing items
- Develop data sovereignty protocols for tribal cultural heritage documentation
- Require that digital reproductions of repatriated items be controlled by the repatriating tribe

### ARPA Enforcement Enhancement

**Existing Authority**: ARPA (16 U.S.C. 470aa-470mm)

**Proposed Regulatory Actions**:

- Increase BLM cultural resource law enforcement positions from 39 to 200
- Establish a confidential archaeological site monitoring program using remote sensing technology
- Develop inter-agency intelligence sharing protocols for cultural property crime
- Create a cash reward program for information leading to ARPA convictions

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Indian Commerce Clause**: Congress has broad authority under Art. I, Sec. 8, Cl. 3 to legislate regarding tribal cultural property. *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023) affirmed this authority comprehensively.
- **Commerce Clause**: The trafficking and provenance disclosure provisions are supportable under the Commerce Clause, as the cultural property market is interstate and international in character.
- **Political Classification**: Tribal-specific protections are based on the political, not racial, classification of tribal membership. *Morton v. Mancari*, 417 U.S. 535 (1974).
- **Takings Clause**: Requiring repatriation from non-federally-funded institutions may raise takings concerns. However, items of cultural patrimony are inalienable under NAGPRA's framework -- they were never lawfully the property of the holder. The voluntary repatriation program with tax incentives mitigates takings risk for private holders.
- **First Amendment**: No First Amendment concerns arise from repatriation requirements. Museums do not have a First Amendment right to possess and display stolen cultural items.

### Preemption Questions

- Federal NAGPRA legislation preempts conflicting state law but does not prevent states from enacting stronger protections
- State model legislation provides supplementary protections beyond the federal floor
- Tribal law governs cultural property on tribal land; federal legislation applies to federal land and federally covered institutions
- Online platform provisions would preempt inconsistent state regulations to create uniform national standards

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- NAGPRA Enforcement Division with compliance audit authority
- Private right of action for tribes in federal court
- Criminal penalties for trafficking and willful non-compliance
- Civil penalties for institutional non-compliance
- FBI Cultural Property Task Force for criminal investigation
- Online platform compliance requirements
- International enforcement through bilateral agreements

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- NAGPRA Enforcement Division reauthorization every 10 years
- Annual reporting requirement to Congress on repatriation progress and enforcement
- GAO review of implementation effectiveness every 5 years
- FBI Cultural Property Task Force annual performance report

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### NAGPRA Enforcement and Funding Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Institutional dissolution | Institutions could dissolve or reorganize to avoid expanded NAGPRA obligations | Medium |
| Shell entity transfers | Institutions could transfer collections to non-covered entities before the expanded scope takes effect | High |
| Financial hardship claims | Institutions could claim inability to pay penalties, rendering enforcement toothless | Medium |
| Compliance delay tactics | Institutions could engage in prolonged "consultation" without good faith intent to repatriate | High |
| Ombudsman limitations | Ombudsman has investigative but not enforcement authority; findings could be ignored | Medium |
| Voluntary program gaming | Private collectors could claim tax deductions for items of minimal value while retaining valuable items | Low |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Retroactivity concerns | Expanded scope may face legal challenges from institutions not previously covered | Constitutional due process considerations |
| Appropriations dependence | $90 million annual authorization subject to annual appropriations process | Congressional budget politics |
| Tribal capacity gaps | Not all tribes have the capacity to engage in NAGPRA processes even with increased funding | Historical underfunding; workforce limitations |
| Smithsonian coordination | Smithsonian operates under the NMAI Act, not NAGPRA; coordination gaps may persist | Separate statutory frameworks |
| International holdings | Act has no extraterritorial reach; international collections remain outside scope | Sovereignty limitations |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Include anti-evasion provisions prohibiting transfers intended to avoid NAGPRA obligations, with clawback authority
2. Establish mandatory minimum penalties that cannot be waived for financial hardship without documented inability to pay
3. Define "good faith consultation" with specific procedural requirements and timelines; authorize the Enforcement Division to determine when consultation has been unreasonably delayed
4. Grant the Ombudsman authority to issue binding compliance orders subject to judicial review
5. Require coordination MOU between the National NAGPRA program and the Smithsonian's repatriation office
6. Authorize advance appropriations for the Enforcement Division (modeled on IHS advance appropriations authority)

### Cultural Property Trafficking Prevention Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Provenance fabrication | Sellers could fabricate or falsify provenance documentation | High |
| Jurisdictional arbitrage | Trafficking could shift to jurisdictions with weaker enforcement | High |
| Platform evasion | Sales could move to smaller platforms below the $100 million revenue threshold | Medium |
| Definition ambiguity | "Native American cultural item" may be difficult to define precisely for market regulation | Medium |
| International sales | Foreign auction houses remain outside U.S. jurisdiction | High |
| Private sales exemption | Direct private-to-private sales may be difficult to regulate and enforce | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Extraterritorial limitations | U.S. law cannot reach sales in European auction houses | Sovereign limitations of national law |
| Enforcement resources | FBI task force is small relative to the scale of the market | Budget constraints |
| Burden on legitimate trade | Provenance requirements may burden legitimate sales of non-restricted Native American art | Difficulty distinguishing protected from non-protected items |
| Proof of knowledge | "Knows or has reason to know" standard may be difficult to prove in practice | Evidentiary challenges |
| Cultural item identification | Law enforcement may lack expertise to identify protected items | Training and capacity limitations |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Establish a federal provenance verification database that sellers can use to authenticate documentation; make fabrication a separate criminal offense
2. Invest in international law enforcement cooperation through INTERPOL and bilateral agreements; make jurisdictional arbitrage a sentencing enhancement
3. Lower the platform threshold or create a tiered compliance system for smaller platforms
4. Develop a detailed regulatory definition of "Native American cultural item" with tribal input, including illustrative examples and exclusions for contemporary art
5. Create training programs and reference materials for law enforcement and customs officials
6. Establish a strict liability standard for dealers (as opposed to casual sellers) to reduce the knowledge-proof burden

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Institutional resistance to new requirements | Mandatory compliance; significant penalties; public accountability |
| Underfunded tribal NAGPRA offices | Dedicated funding for tribal NAGPRA coordinators ($10 million annually) |
| Inter-agency coordination failures | Mandatory coordination MOU; unified database; annual joint reporting |
| Political vulnerability of administrative protections | Legislative codification of key protections |
| Art market opacity | Provenance disclosure requirements; platform compliance; database development |
| International enforcement gap | Bilateral agreements; international cooperation; diplomatic engagement |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Annual Enforcement Division report to Congress on compliance and repatriation progress
- GAO review every 5 years on implementation effectiveness
- Reauthorization of Enforcement Division and FBI Cultural Property Task Force every 10 years
- Mandatory congressional hearing when repatriation of all institutionally held remains is achieved
