# Tribal Cultural Property: History

## Overview

The history of Native American cultural property in the United States is a story of systematic dispossession, collection, and commodification of Indigenous heritage -- followed by a long, still-incomplete struggle for repatriation and cultural authority. From the earliest colonial encounters through the era of institutional archaeology and into the modern period, Native remains and cultural objects were treated as scientific specimens, curiosities, and commodities rather than as the ancestral heritage of living peoples. Understanding this history is essential to comprehending why current law exists, why its implementation remains contested, and why many tribal communities view repatriation as a matter of fundamental justice rather than museum policy.

## Timeline

### Colonial Era and Early Republic (1500s-1850s)

- European colonizers collected Native cultural items as curiosities and trophies from earliest contact
- Grave robbing of Native burials for skulls and skeletons began in the 1700s and accelerated in the 1800s
- **1787**: Thomas Jefferson excavated a Native American burial mound in Virginia, documenting his methods -- one of the first recorded archaeological excavations in North America
- **1830s**: Indian Removal Act and subsequent forced relocations separated communities from ancestral burial sites and cultural landscapes
- **1846**: Smithsonian Institution founded; became major repository for Native remains and objects
- Cabinet of curiosities and early museums displayed Native human remains alongside natural history specimens

### The Era of Scientific Collecting (1850s-1906)

- **1862**: Surgeon General's Order directed Army field surgeons to collect Native American crania for the Army Medical Museum
    - Over 4,000 skulls collected from battlefields, burial grounds, and hospital morgues
    - Used for "racial science" studies purporting to demonstrate Native inferiority
- **1867-1868**: Battle of Sand Creek and other military conflicts generated "specimen" collections from battlefields
- **1879**: Bureau of American Ethnology established within the Smithsonian; massive collecting expeditions
- **1880s-1900s**: Museum competition drove aggressive collecting
    - Franz Boas and others conducted extensive Northwest Coast collecting for natural history museums
    - "Salvage ethnography" rationalized removing cultural items before cultures "vanished"
    - Collectors purchased, traded, and sometimes stole sacred objects from tribal communities
- **1890**: Wounded Knee Massacre; remains and possessions of victims collected as "artifacts"
- **1891**: World's Columbian Exposition planning drove large-scale acquisition of Native cultural items
- Major museums built their collections: Smithsonian, American Museum of Natural History, Field Museum, Peabody Museum (Harvard), University of Pennsylvania Museum

### The Antiquities Act and Early Preservation (1906-1935)

- **1906**: Antiquities Act established federal authority over archaeological resources on federal land
    - Authorized excavation permits on federal land
    - Created concept of archaeological resources as government property
    - Did not recognize tribal interests or require consultation
    - Primarily aimed at preventing commercial looting of Southwestern sites
- **1906-1930s**: Expansion of archaeological excavation under permit system
    - Large-scale excavations at Mesa Verde, Chaco Canyon, Pueblo Bonito
    - Massive collections generated and distributed to museums nationwide
    - Native communities not consulted about excavation of their ancestral sites
- **1935**: Historic Sites Act established National Historic Landmarks program

### New Deal and Postwar Archaeology (1935-1966)

- **1935-1942**: Works Progress Administration (WPA) funded massive archaeological excavations
    - Tennessee Valley Authority dam projects excavated thousands of burial sites before flooding
    - Southeastern states: tens of thousands of individuals excavated and sent to universities
    - CCC and WPA workers excavated sites with minimal training or cultural sensitivity
- **1946**: River Basin Surveys program continued dam-related excavation
- **1950s-1960s**: Highway construction destroyed countless archaeological sites
    - Interstate Highway System construction often bypassed environmental review
    - Sites on tribal lands affected by federal infrastructure projects
- Professional archaeology grew as an academic discipline; Native remains treated as research material

### The Rise of Repatriation Advocacy (1966-1990)

- **1966**: National Historic Preservation Act established Section 106 review process
    - Required federal agencies to consider effects on historic properties
    - Did not initially address tribal interests specifically
- **1971**: Maria Pearson (Running Moccasins), a member of the Yankton Sioux Tribe, began advocacy in Iowa after discovering that non-Native remains were reburied while Native remains were sent to a university
    - Her advocacy led to Iowa's Burial Act (1976), the first state repatriation law
- **1979**: Archaeological Resources Protection Act (ARPA) replaced the Antiquities Act for archaeological resources
    - Strengthened criminal penalties for looting
    - Required tribal consent for excavation on tribal land
    - Improved but still did not mandate repatriation
- **1980s**: Repatriation movement gained national momentum
    - American Indian Movement (AIM) and other organizations protested museum displays of remains
    - Tribal leaders testified before Congress about the trauma of ancestor desecration
    - Multiple tribes demanded return of remains and sacred objects
- **1989**: National Museum of the American Indian Act (NMAI Act)
    - Created the National Museum of the American Indian within the Smithsonian
    - Required the Smithsonian to inventory and repatriate Native remains and objects
    - Separate from NAGPRA (Smithsonian is not subject to NAGPRA)
- **1989**: Nebraska enacted the Unmarked Human Burial Sites and Skeletal Remains Protection Act
    - First comprehensive state repatriation law
    - Required repatriation of remains held by state institutions

### NAGPRA Enactment and Early Implementation (1990-2010)

- **November 16, 1990**: President George H.W. Bush signed the Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act (P.L. 101-601)
    - Bipartisan support; passed with broad margins
    - Required inventories within 5 years (by 1995)
    - Required summaries within 3 years (by 1993)
    - Established repatriation process based on cultural affiliation
    - Created NAGPRA Review Committee
    - Authorized grants for repatriation activities
- **1993**: Summary deadline passed; most institutions filed summaries (quality varied)
- **1995**: Inventory deadline passed; many institutions filed incomplete inventories
    - Large number of remains classified as "culturally unidentifiable" to avoid repatriation
    - Compliance monitoring minimal
- **1996**: *Bonnichsen v. United States* (Kennewick Man case) filed
    - 9,000-year-old remains found in Washington state
    - Scientists sued to prevent repatriation to Confederated Tribes of the Colville Reservation and others
    - Litigation lasted nearly two decades
- **2000**: Implementing regulations (43 C.F.R. Part 10) finalized
- **2000s**: Repatriation progressed slowly
    - Many institutions delayed consultation and compliance
    - "Culturally unidentifiable" classification used to hold remains indefinitely
    - Tribal capacity for repatriation limited by funding constraints

### The Kennewick Man Resolution and Modern Era (2010-Present)

- **2010**: DOI issued rule on disposition of culturally unidentifiable remains
    - Allowed transfer to tribes from the geographic area
    - Major step forward but implementation slow
- **2015**: Kennewick Man/The Ancient One identified as Native American through DNA analysis
- **2016**: Congress passed legislation transferring Kennewick Man remains to tribes for reburial
    - Symbolic victory; demonstrated power of DNA analysis and legislative action
- **2016**: DOI updated NAGPRA regulations to address disposition of culturally unidentifiable remains
- **2020-2022**: ProPublica investigation "The Repatriation Project" documented widespread non-compliance
    - Found that many institutions had done little to repatriate after filing initial inventories
    - Universities held tens of thousands of ancestors decades after NAGPRA's enactment
    - Galvanized public attention and congressional interest
- **2022**: DOI proposed updated NAGPRA regulations
- **2023**: Major museums began reassessing practices
    - American Museum of Natural History closed Native American halls
    - Harvard Peabody Museum restricted access to Native collections
    - Multiple universities accelerated repatriation programs
- **January 2024**: Updated NAGPRA regulations took effect
    - Eliminated "right of possession" defense
    - Required disposition of all "culturally unidentifiable" remains
    - Mandated free, prior, and informed consent for research
    - Set 5-year compliance deadline
- **2024**: DOI Federal Indian Boarding School Initiative reports documented connections between boarding schools and cultural property removal

## Lasting Impact

The history of cultural property dispossession continues to affect tribal communities:

- **Spiritual harm**: Tribes believe that ancestors' spirits cannot rest while their remains are held in museum drawers; ongoing spiritual injury to communities
- **Knowledge loss**: Sacred objects held in museums cannot perform their ceremonial functions, disrupting cultural practice
- **Trust deficit**: Decades of exploitation by researchers and institutions have created deep mistrust of museums, universities, and federal agencies
- **Ongoing commodification**: Cultural items continue to appear in commercial markets, perpetuating the treatment of heritage as commodity
- **Burden on tribes**: Repatriation places the burden on tribes to prove affiliation, locate ancestors, fund retrieval, and conduct reburial -- essentially requiring victims to prove their claim
- **Intergenerational trauma**: Knowledge that ancestors were dug up, studied, and stored in boxes contributes to collective grief and historical trauma
