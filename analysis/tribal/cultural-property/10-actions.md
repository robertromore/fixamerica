# Tribal Cultural Property: Actions

## Overview

This document outlines concrete actions that individuals, organizations, and communities can take to support cultural property protection and repatriation. Actions are organized by effort level and stakeholder type.

## Individual Actions

### Immediate (Low Effort)

1. **Learn about NAGPRA and repatriation**: Understand what NAGPRA requires and why repatriation matters. ProPublica's "The Repatriation Project" is an accessible starting point for understanding the scope of the problem.

2. **Contact your elected representatives**: Call or write your U.S. Senators and Representative to express support for cultural property protections. Key asks:
    - Support the NAGPRA Enforcement and Funding Act
    - Increase NAGPRA grant funding to $50 million annually
    - Support the Cultural Property Trafficking Prevention Act
    - Fund the National NAGPRA program adequately for compliance monitoring

3. **Do not purchase Native American cultural items of uncertain provenance**: Refuse to buy items that may have been looted or illegally obtained. If you see suspicious items for sale online, report them to the FBI Art Crime Team (<https://tips.fbi.gov>) or the platform's trust and safety team.

4. **Follow and amplify Indigenous voices**: Follow tribal nations, Indigenous journalists, and cultural property advocacy organizations on social media. Amplify their messages about repatriation and cultural heritage protection rather than speaking on their behalf.

5. **Donate to advocacy organizations**: Financial support for organizations working on cultural property protection:
    - Association on American Indian Affairs (AAIA)
    - Native American Rights Fund (NARF)
    - National Congress of American Indians (NCAI)
    - Cultural Survival
    - First Nations Development Institute

### Medium Effort

6. **Submit public comments**: When federal agencies open comment periods on NAGPRA regulations, cultural property rules, or land management plans affecting archaeological sites, submit comments supporting tribal positions. Monitor regulations.gov and the Federal Register for relevant comment periods.

7. **Educate your community**: Share information about repatriation and cultural property issues with your civic organization, faith community, or professional association. Many people are unaware that over 108,000 Native American ancestors remain in institutional custody.

8. **Visit and support tribal cultural centers**: When available, visit tribally operated museums and cultural centers that tell the story of cultural property and repatriation from Indigenous perspectives. Support these institutions financially.

9. **Practice responsible behavior at archaeological sites**: When visiting public lands:
    - Never collect artifacts, pottery sherds, or other cultural items -- it is illegal under ARPA on federal and tribal land
    - Do not disturb archaeological sites, structures, or features
    - Report evidence of looting or vandalism to the appropriate land management agency
    - Stay on designated trails near sensitive archaeological areas
    - Do not share GPS coordinates of archaeological sites publicly

10. **Write letters to the editor and op-eds**: Respond to news coverage of NAGPRA compliance, repatriation, and cultural property issues. Connect cultural property protection to broadly shared values like respecting the dead, honoring commitments, and doing the right thing.

### Sustained Engagement

11. **Join or support a repatriation advocacy campaign**: Volunteer with organizations working on specific repatriation campaigns or cultural property protection efforts.

12. **Engage your professional expertise**: Lawyers, museum professionals, archaeologists, journalists, educators, and technology professionals can contribute specialized skills:
    - Lawyers: pro bono NAGPRA compliance assistance for tribes
    - Museum professionals: advocate for accelerated repatriation within your institution
    - Archaeologists: adopt ethical practices that prioritize consultation and consent
    - Journalists: investigate institutional non-compliance
    - Educators: include NAGPRA and repatriation in curricula
    - Technology professionals: help tribes develop digital cultural heritage tools

13. **Monitor online sales platforms**: Volunteer with organizations that monitor eBay, Etsy, and auction sites for potentially illegal sales of Native American cultural items. Report suspicious listings.

## Organizational Actions

### Museums and Universities

- **Complete NAGPRA compliance immediately**: Do not wait for the 2029 deadline. Initiate consultation with all potentially affiliated tribes now.
- **Adopt transparent repatriation practices**: Publish a public accounting of Native American holdings, affiliation status, and repatriation timeline.
- **Hire tribal liaisons**: Employ staff from tribal communities to guide repatriation and collection management.
- **Fund repatriation**: Commit institutional resources to cover the full costs of repatriation, including tribal travel, ceremony, and reburial.
- **Develop collaborative exhibition practices**: Work with tribes as equal partners in any exhibition involving Native American cultural items.
- **Establish ethical acquisition policies**: Refuse to acquire any Native American items without clear, documented provenance and tribal consent.
- **Restrict access to sensitive materials**: Implement protocols ensuring that sacred and culturally restricted materials are not accessed, studied, or displayed without tribal consent.

### Faith Communities

- Adopt resolutions supporting NAGPRA compliance and repatriation as a matter of respect for the dead
- Educate congregations about the shared moral principle that ancestors deserve respectful burial
- Partner with local tribal nations on cultural property advocacy
- Connect the repatriation movement to broader interfaith commitments to justice and healing

### Environmental Organizations

- Integrate archaeological site protection into land conservation campaigns
- Support tribal efforts to prevent looting on federal and tribal land
- Advocate for cultural resource protections in environmental legislation
- Include tribal cultural property in environmental impact assessments

### Legal Organizations

- Provide pro bono legal support for tribal NAGPRA claims
- File amicus briefs in cultural property litigation
- Train tribal attorneys in NAGPRA law and enforcement
- Develop legal scholarship on cultural property rights
- Challenge institutional non-compliance through legal action

### Technology Companies

- Implement robust cultural property compliance policies on marketplace platforms
- Develop automated screening tools to detect and flag potentially illegal cultural item listings
- Work with tribal nations to develop platform-specific reporting mechanisms
- Refuse to host sales of items flagged by tribal cultural property registries

### Media Organizations

- Investigate institutional NAGPRA non-compliance (following ProPublica's model)
- Center Indigenous voices and perspectives in reporting on cultural property issues
- Provide sustained coverage, not only crisis-driven reporting
- Document the cultural, spiritual, and community impact of repatriation on tribal communities

## State and Local Government Actions

### State Legislatures

- Enact state NAGPRA equivalents covering state-funded institutions (following California's CalNAGPRA model)
- Create state cultural property registries
- Require state agencies to consult with tribes before actions affecting archaeological sites
- Appropriate state funds for tribal repatriation and cultural preservation programs
- Strengthen state penalties for looting and vandalism of archaeological sites

### Local Governments

- Include tribal cultural resource considerations in local land use planning
- Establish local preservation ordinances that protect archaeological sites
- Partner with tribal nations on cultural tourism development
- Train local law enforcement to recognize and report cultural property crimes

## Federal Employee Actions

### Interior Department and NPS Staff

- Prioritize NAGPRA compliance within your institution or program
- Ensure adequate staff time and resources are dedicated to consultation
- Process NAGPRA notices and determinations promptly
- Engage THPOs and tribal NAGPRA officers as partners, not obstacles
- Report institutional non-compliance through internal channels

### Law Enforcement

- Prioritize cultural property crimes in enforcement activities
- Develop expertise in identifying looted and trafficked cultural items
- Coordinate with tribal law enforcement on cultural property investigations
- Maintain confidentiality about archaeological site locations

### Congressional Staff

- Brief your Member on NAGPRA compliance issues and pending legislation
- Arrange meetings between tribal leaders and your Member
- Monitor committee activity on cultural property bills
- Ensure tribal testimony is included in relevant hearings

## Tribal Nation Actions

### Government Actions

- Designate NAGPRA coordinators and fund their work
- Develop comprehensive tribal cultural heritage codes
- Pursue repatriation claims actively with all holding institutions
- Establish Tribal Historic Preservation Officers where not yet in place
- Build inter-tribal coalitions for shared repatriation efforts
- Engage in international advocacy for cultural property recovery
- Develop digital cultural heritage protocols

### Community Actions

- Support intergenerational knowledge transfer about cultural objects and their significance
- Maintain ceremonial practices associated with repatriated objects
- Document oral histories related to cultural property (with appropriate cultural protocols)
- Monitor known archaeological sites for looting
- Engage tribal youth in cultural heritage stewardship
- Support tribal museums and cultural centers
