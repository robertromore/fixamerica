# Tribal Housing: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to tribal housing reform comes from several directions: fiscal conservatives concerned about increased federal spending, state and local governments wary of expanded trust land and reduced tax base, free-market advocates who question government housing programs, and bureaucratic inertia within federal agencies reluctant to change long-established processes. Understanding these opposition arguments -- and their strengths and weaknesses -- is essential for designing reforms that can survive political and legal challenge.

## Opposition Landscape

| Opposition Group | Primary Concern | Intensity | Political Influence |
|-----------------|----------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Fiscal conservatives in Congress | Federal spending; mandatory appropriations | High | Very High (appropriations control) |
| State and local governments | Tax base; jurisdiction over trust land | Medium | Medium (litigation, lobbying) |
| BIA institutional interests | Maintaining administrative role and authority | Medium | Medium (bureaucratic resistance) |

## Key Opposition Arguments

### Argument 1: NAHASDA Funding Increases Are Fiscally Irresponsible

**Claim**: Tripling NAHASDA funding from $772 million to the estimated $2.9 billion annual need is fiscally irresponsible given federal budget constraints and competing priorities.

**Evidence Cited**: The federal deficit exceeds $1 trillion annually. Discretionary spending is capped under budget agreements. NAHASDA has received bipartisan support at current levels, suggesting that the political system has found an equilibrium. Proponents of increased funding point to estimated "need" figures that lack independent verification.

**Reality**: The "fiscal responsibility" argument treats current funding levels as a baseline rather than as the product of decades of chronic underfunding. The $2.9 billion estimated annual need is based on documented housing shortages, overcrowding rates, and infrastructure deficits corroborated by multiple federal agencies (HUD, Census Bureau, IHS). The U.S. Commission on Civil Rights found in 2018 that federal funding for Native Americans "has not been sufficient to meet the needs of the communities it was intended to serve." The annual funding gap of $2.1 billion represents approximately 0.03% of the federal budget.

**Rebuttal**: The federal government has a trust responsibility to provide housing support as part of treaty obligations. The cost of inaction -- in health expenditures, emergency services, and lost economic productivity -- likely exceeds the cost of adequate funding. One-time investments through CARES Act ($1.1 billion) and American Rescue Plan ($750 million) demonstrated that higher funding levels produce measurable results, with tribes reporting accelerated construction and rehabilitation.

### Argument 2: Trust Land Barriers Should Be Resolved by Eliminating Trust Status

**Claim**: The simplest way to resolve trust land barriers to housing finance is to convert trust land to fee simple ownership, allowing conventional mortgages and market transactions.

**Evidence Cited**: Anderson and Parker (2008) found that fee simple land on reservations generates more economic activity per acre than trust land. Some tribal members have expressed frustration with trust restrictions that prevent them from using their land freely. Fee simple conversion would allow standard mortgage processes without BIA involvement.

**Reality**: This argument replicates the logic of the Dawes Act, which resulted in the loss of approximately 90 million acres of tribal land. Every historical period in which trust protections were weakened has resulted in massive land alienation. Fee simple conversion exposes tribal land to state taxation, creditor seizure, and sale to non-Indians. The Menominee Termination experience (1954-1973), during which the tribe lost significant land and housing deteriorated catastrophically, demonstrates the consequences of removing trust protections.

**Rebuttal**: The HEARTH Act demonstrates that trust protections and economic development are compatible. Reforms can streamline trust land processes -- such as tribal leasing authority, expedited BIA approvals, and enhanced Section 184 processing -- without removing the trust protections essential to tribal land preservation. The solution is to fix the trust system, not to eliminate it.

---

### Argument 3: Tribal Housing Authorities Lack Capacity to Manage Increased Funding

**Claim**: Many TDHEs lack the administrative capacity to effectively manage significantly increased funding. Rapid funding increases would lead to waste, mismanagement, and poor-quality construction.

**Evidence Cited**: HUD Inspector General reports have documented compliance issues at some TDHEs. Sixty-two percent of TDHEs report difficulty hiring qualified staff. Some small TDHEs have struggled with audit requirements and reporting. Historical examples of poor-quality construction in tribal housing (particularly pre-NAHASDA) raise quality concerns.

**Reality**: Capacity concerns are legitimate for some TDHEs but should not be used to justify continued underfunding for all tribes. Large TDHEs like the Navajo Housing Authority, Cherokee Nation, and Choctaw Nation have demonstrated the ability to manage substantial programs effectively. The American Rescue Plan's $750 million supplemental IHBG funding was deployed successfully, with tribes reporting accelerated construction without the widespread mismanagement predicted by critics. Moreover, capacity constraints are themselves a product of underfunding -- TDHEs cannot hire qualified staff when grants are too small to pay competitive salaries.

**Rebuttal**: The solution to capacity gaps is capacity building, not funding denial. Phased funding increases paired with technical assistance, training programs, and regional cooperation agreements can build TDHE capacity alongside increased resources. NAIHC already provides training and technical assistance; expanded resources would allow this to scale. Large TDHEs could serve as mentors and partners for smaller entities.

### Argument 4: The Private Market Should Provide Housing, Not the Federal Government

**Claim**: Federal housing programs create dependency and crowd out private housing development. Market-based approaches -- removing regulatory barriers, incentivizing private construction, and building credit access -- would be more effective and sustainable.

**Evidence Cited**: Private housing markets serve most Americans without government construction programs. The LIHTC program has successfully leveraged private investment for affordable housing in non-tribal areas. Some tribal members have successfully obtained Section 184 mortgages and built homes without direct government construction assistance.

**Reality**: The private market has comprehensively failed in Indian Country because the market conditions that support private housing -- clear title, functioning credit markets, infrastructure, population density, and comparable sales for appraisals -- do not exist on most reservations. This is not a temporary market failure but a structural condition created by the trust land system, geographic remoteness, poverty, and the absence of financial infrastructure. The Section 184 program has been successful, but the vast majority of its 47,000 loans are on fee land, not trust land. LIHTC has been underutilized in Indian Country because tax credit investors are unfamiliar with tribal housing and deterred by trust land complexity.

**Rebuttal**: Market-based approaches should complement, not replace, direct federal investment. Reforms to expand Section 184 on trust land, simplify LIHTC use in Indian Country, capitalize Native CDFIs, and build credit access are all valuable. But they cannot substitute for direct NAHASDA funding to build housing in areas where no private market exists or is likely to exist. The private market cannot deliver housing to communities with 25% poverty rates, no bank branches, no comparable sales data, and construction costs double the national average.

---

### Argument 5: BIA Processes Exist to Protect Tribal Interests

**Claim**: BIA lease approvals, Title Status Reports, and other trust land processes, while slow, exist to protect tribal and individual Indian interests. Streamlining these processes risks exposing trust land to exploitation.

**Evidence Cited**: The BIA's trust responsibility includes protecting trust land from improvident transactions. Historical instances of exploitation -- including predatory leasing and mineral rights theft -- justify careful federal review. The Cobell litigation documented systematic trust mismanagement, suggesting that less oversight is risky.

**Reality**: While the protective purpose of BIA processes is legitimate, the current system has calcified into a bureaucratic obstacle that prevents housing development without providing meaningful protection. A Title Status Report that takes 6-12 months does not protect anyone -- it merely delays housing construction while families remain in overcrowded conditions. The Cobell litigation documented failures of federal management, not failures of tribal management. The HEARTH Act, which allows tribes to approve their own leases, has produced faster processing without any documented cases of exploitation.

**Rebuttal**: Trust protection can be maintained while dramatically streamlining processes. Tribal authority over housing-related approvals -- modeled on the HEARTH Act -- would reduce processing times from years to weeks while keeping land in trust. The BIA's protective function can shift from pre-approval gatekeeping to post-action oversight and technical assistance.

---

### Argument 6: Infrastructure Should Be Addressed Separately from Housing

**Claim**: Housing and infrastructure are separate policy domains that should be funded and managed independently. Combining them in a single housing bill would create an unwieldy program and blur accountability.

**Evidence Cited**: Federal programs are organized by function (housing at HUD, water at IHS/EPA, roads at BIA/DOT, broadband at FCC/NTIA). Each has its own authorizing legislation, appropriations subcommittee, and regulatory framework. The Bipartisan Infrastructure Law already addresses tribal infrastructure needs.

**Reality**: On reservations, housing and infrastructure are inseparable. A house without water, sewer, or electricity is not habitable. Building housing without infrastructure wastes construction investment. The current fragmented system requires small TDHEs to navigate multiple federal agencies with different application processes, reporting requirements, and timelines -- an impossible burden for organizations with three or four staff members. The Bipartisan Infrastructure Law is a welcome investment but was a one-time authorization that does not address the ongoing annual infrastructure funding gap.

**Rebuttal**: While full program consolidation may not be politically feasible, reforms should include coordination requirements, joint planning provisions, and "one-stop" application processes for tribal housing projects that require multiple funding streams. At minimum, NAHASDA reauthorization should expand eligible activities to include infrastructure directly supporting new housing construction.

## Counter-Strategies

### Addressing Fiscal Concerns

- Frame tribal housing investment as fulfillment of legal trust obligations, not discretionary spending
- Document cost savings from reduced emergency health care, disease treatment, and social services
- Propose phased funding increases with performance benchmarks
- Highlight the one-time CARES Act and American Rescue Plan results as evidence of effective fund deployment

### Addressing Capacity Concerns

- Pair funding increases with dedicated capacity-building programs
- Support regional TDHE cooperatives for shared administrative functions
- Expand NAIHC training and technical assistance programs
- Implement graduated funding increases rather than immediate tripling

### Building Private Sector Engagement

- Reform Section 184 to streamline trust land mortgage processing
- Create tax incentives specifically designed for tribal housing investment
- Capitalize Native CDFIs at levels sufficient to serve as primary reservation lenders
- Develop standardized trust land mortgage products that secondary market entities can purchase

### Overcoming Bureaucratic Inertia

- Enact statutory processing timelines for BIA trust land approvals
- Expand HEARTH Act authority to include residential site leasing
- Fund BIA realty specialist hiring to address staffing shortfalls
- Support tribal assumption of housing-related trust functions
