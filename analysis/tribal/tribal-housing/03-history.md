# Tribal Housing: History

## Pre-Colonial Housing

### Indigenous Housing Traditions

Before European contact, Indigenous peoples across North America developed diverse housing forms precisely adapted to their environments, climates, cultures, and social structures:

- **Southwest pueblos**: Multi-story adobe and stone structures housing entire communities, among the oldest continuously inhabited settlements in North America (Acoma Pueblo dates to approximately 1100 CE)
- **Plains tipis**: Portable, efficient shelters designed for nomadic life, using buffalo hides over pole frames
- **Pacific Northwest longhouses**: Large cedar-plank structures housing extended families, reflecting abundant timber resources
- **Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) longhouses**: Communal structures up to 200 feet long housing clan families under matrilineal social organization
- **Earth lodges**: Semi-subterranean structures used by Mandan, Hidatsa, and other Missouri River nations, providing excellent insulation
- **Chickees**: Open-sided, elevated platforms with thatched roofs used by the Seminole in Florida's subtropical climate
- **Wattle-and-daub structures**: Used throughout the Southeast by Cherokee, Creek, and other nations

### Key Characteristics

- Housing was collectively designed and built by community members
- Construction reflected deep knowledge of local materials, climate, and ecology
- Housing forms embodied cultural values: communal living, clan organization, seasonal movement
- Land tenure was communal; housing was built on collectively held land
- Communities controlled their own housing decisions without external interference

## Colonial and Early American Period (1492-1871)

### Displacement and Destruction

European colonization systematically destroyed Indigenous housing and communities:

- Settlement displaced tribes from homelands where their housing traditions had developed over centuries
- Military campaigns specifically targeted housing: burning villages was a standard tactic from the Pequot War (1637) through the Indian Wars of the nineteenth century
- The Navajo Long Walk (1864) included the destruction of homes, orchards, and livestock at Canyon de Chelly
- General Sherman's 1868-69 winter campaign destroyed Southern Cheyenne and Arapaho villages

### Forced Removal (1830-1850s)

The Indian Removal Act and subsequent relocations severed tribes from their housing traditions:

- Removed tribes were forced to rebuild in unfamiliar environments
- The Cherokee, Creek, and other southeastern nations lost permanently built homes, farms, and infrastructure
- Replacement housing in Indian Territory was minimal; many removed people lived in temporary shelters for years
- The transition from permanent southeastern architecture to Plains-style housing represented a devastating loss of material culture

### Reservation Confinement

As tribes were confined to reservations, housing became a tool of federal control:

- The Bureau of Indian Affairs provided minimal housing, often inappropriate for local climates
- Government-issued housing replaced culturally adapted designs with standardized structures
- Housing allocation became a mechanism of social control on reservations
- Overcrowding on diminished land bases began the pattern that persists today

## Allotment and Assimilation Era (1887-1934)

### The Dawes Act and Housing

The General Allotment Act (1887) fundamentally restructured the relationship between tribal people and housing:

- Communal land was divided into individual allotments of 80-160 acres
- The allotment system assumed individual homesteading as the housing model
- "Surplus" lands were opened to non-Indian settlement, shrinking the land available for tribal housing
- Tribes lost approximately 90 million acres between 1887 and 1934
- The fractionated ownership created by allotment inheritance remains a major barrier to housing development today

### Government Housing Programs

- The BIA constructed rudimentary housing on some reservations, typically small frame houses of poor quality
- Construction reflected assimilation goals: Western-style houses replacing traditional housing forms
- Housing was often culturally inappropriate, poorly built, and inadequate for climate conditions
- No tribal input into design or construction was sought

### Boarding Schools and Housing Disruption

The boarding school system (1879-1960s) disrupted housing knowledge transmission:

- Children were removed from homes where traditional construction knowledge was transmitted
- Students were trained in Western construction methods as part of assimilation curriculum
- The disruption of family and community life undermined communal housing traditions
- Graduates returning to reservations faced housing shortages with diminished traditional skills

## Indian Reorganization and Post-War Period (1934-1968)

### Indian Reorganization Act (1934)

The IRA ended allotment and began to restore some tribal land:

- Land loss was halted, but the existing land base remained far smaller than treaty-recognized territories
- Some land was returned to tribal control, but housing needs continued to grow
- Tribal governments reorganized under IRA constitutions had limited housing authority and virtually no housing resources

### Post-War Federal Housing

Following World War II, the federal government began providing more systematic housing assistance to tribes:

- The Indian Health Service began sanitation facility construction in the 1950s
- Public housing programs extended to Indian Country in the late 1950s and 1960s
- The Housing Act of 1961 created public housing specifically for Indian reservations
- The Mutual Help Homeownership Program (1963) allowed tribal members to work toward homeownership through "sweat equity"

### Termination Era Housing Impacts

The termination policy (1953-1968) devastated housing for affected tribes:

- Terminated tribes lost access to federal housing programs
- Reservation lands were often sold, displacing residents
- The Menominee Termination Act (1954) resulted in severe housing deterioration as federal maintenance ceased
- Relocation programs (1952-1972) moved thousands of Native families to cities with minimal housing support, contributing to urban Native homelessness

### Dam Construction and Displacement

Federal dam projects destroyed some of the best tribal housing:

- Pick-Sloan Missouri Basin dams flooded the most fertile and well-housed areas of multiple reservations
- The Three Affiliated Tribes (Fort Berthold) lost 155,000 acres including their best agricultural land and primary communities
- Standing Rock, Cheyenne River, and other Sioux reservations lost riverside communities with the most established housing
- Replacement housing was often substandard and located in less desirable areas

## Federal Housing Programs Era (1968-1996)

### HUD Indian Housing Programs

The establishment of the Department of Housing and Urban Development (1965) and subsequent legislation created dedicated Indian housing programs:

| Program | Year | Purpose |
|---------|------|---------|
| Mutual Help Homeownership | 1963 | Sweat equity homeownership; tribal member contributes labor |
| Turnkey III | 1968 | Rental-to-own housing development |
| Public housing (Section 6) | 1970s | Conventional rental public housing on reservations |
| Section 8 (Indian set-aside) | 1974 | Rental assistance vouchers for Native families |
| Indian CDBG | 1977 | Community development block grants for tribes |

### Problems with the Pre-NAHASDA System

The pre-NAHASDA system suffered from multiple deficiencies:

- **One-size-fits-all**: HUD applied urban public housing rules to tribal communities, resulting in culturally inappropriate housing
- **Design failures**: Standard designs were not adapted to reservation climates (identical houses built in Arizona and South Dakota)
- **Quality problems**: Rapid construction with minimal oversight produced housing that deteriorated quickly
- **Maintenance neglect**: Neither HUD nor many tribes had adequate maintenance programs
- **Administrative burden**: Multiple separate programs with different rules created a compliance nightmare for tribal housing authorities
- **Paternalism**: HUD made most housing decisions without meaningful tribal input
- **Underfunding**: Even at their peak, federal programs never approached the scale of tribal housing need

### The Mutual Help Program

The Mutual Help Homeownership Opportunity Program was the signature pre-NAHASDA housing program:

- Tribal members contributed "sweat equity" (labor) toward homeownership
- After 25 years of contributions, the home could be conveyed to the occupant
- The program built approximately 40,000 homes across Indian Country
- However, many Mutual Help homes were poorly constructed, and the 25-year timeframe proved unrealistic
- By the 1990s, the program was widely seen as a failure: homes were deteriorating, occupants were not achieving homeownership, and maintenance was deferred

## NAHASDA Era (1996-Present)

### The NAHASDA Reform

The Native American Housing Assistance and Self-Determination Act of 1996 represented a fundamental shift:

- **Consolidation**: Merged multiple HUD programs into a single Indian Housing Block Grant
- **Self-determination**: Gave tribes flexibility to design housing programs meeting local needs
- **Formula funding**: Replaced competitive grants with formula-based allocation
- **Tribal planning**: Required Indian Housing Plans developed by tribes, not HUD
- **Expanded activities**: Allowed tribes to use funds for a broader range of housing-related activities

### NAHASDA Achievements

| Achievement | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| Self-determination | Tribes design their own housing programs |
| Flexibility | Funds can be used for construction, rehabilitation, rental assistance, crime prevention |
| Reduced federal paternalism | HUD role shifted from directing to monitoring |
| Section 184 Loan Guarantee | Over 47,000 loans guaranteed for Native homeownership |
| Cultural appropriateness | Tribes can design homes reflecting cultural values |
| Some construction success | Thousands of new units built annually by better-resourced TDHEs |

### NAHASDA Shortcomings

| Shortcoming | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| Chronic underfunding | $772 million vs. $2.9 billion estimated need |
| Flat funding | Real (inflation-adjusted) funding has declined |
| Capacity gaps | Small TDHEs lack staff and expertise |
| Administrative burden | Reporting requirements consume limited resources |
| Trust land barriers | NAHASDA cannot solve trust land financing problems |
| Infrastructure dependency | Housing cannot be built without water, sewer, and roads |
| Data gaps | HUD's housing needs data is incomplete and outdated |
| Reauthorization failure | Not comprehensively reauthorized since FY 2007 |

### COVID-19 Impact

The pandemic exposed and exacerbated tribal housing conditions:

- Overcrowded housing accelerated COVID-19 transmission (Navajo Nation had among the highest per-capita infection rates in the country)
- The Navajo Nation's 30% lack of running water made handwashing and sanitation nearly impossible
- CARES Act provided $1.1 billion in emergency tribal housing and infrastructure funding, demonstrating what higher funding levels could achieve
- American Rescue Plan provided additional $750 million for tribal housing
- Post-pandemic, these one-time funds are largely expended, and tribes have returned to baseline funding levels

## Patterns and Lessons

### Recurring Themes

1. **Chronic underfunding**: Every era has featured federal housing programs that fell far short of demonstrated need
2. **Paternalism followed by neglect**: Federal control over housing design and management, followed by withdrawal of support
3. **Cultural imposition**: Western housing models imposed without tribal input, producing culturally inappropriate and climate-inappropriate structures
4. **Land base erosion**: The progressive loss of tribal land directly created the housing shortage by eliminating the land on which homes could be built
5. **Infrastructure interdependence**: Housing cannot exist without water, sewer, electricity, and roads, but infrastructure is funded separately and also chronically underfunded
6. **Temporary fixes**: One-time emergency funding (CARES Act, ARP) demonstrates what higher funding could achieve but does not address the structural deficit
