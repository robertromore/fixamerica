# Tribal Housing: Actions

## Overview

Citizens can play a meaningful role in advancing tribal housing reform through civic engagement, advocacy, education, and direct support for tribal organizations. This document provides specific, actionable steps for individuals and communities to support housing reform at the federal, state, and local levels.

## Individual Actions

### Educate Yourself

1. **Understand the housing crisis**: Read the NAIHC report "Too Far, Too Expensive, Too Slow" (2023) for an accessible overview of tribal housing conditions
2. **Learn about your region**: Identify which tribal nations are in your area and research their specific housing challenges through tribal government websites
3. **Follow tribal media**: Read Indian Country Today (indiancountrytoday.com), High Country News Indigenous Affairs coverage, and NAIHC updates (naihc.net)
4. **Understand NAHASDA**: The Congressional Research Service report R44194 provides a comprehensive overview of how federal tribal housing programs work
5. **Learn about trust land**: The Indian Land Tenure Foundation (iltf.org) offers resources explaining how trust land status affects housing development

### Contact Elected Officials

Federal legislation is essential for tribal housing reform. Contact your U.S. senators and representative about:

- **NAHASDA reauthorization**: Urge support for reauthorization at significantly increased funding levels (current: $772 million; need: $2.9 billion)
- **Trust land mortgage reform**: Support legislation streamlining BIA processing for residential leases and Title Status Reports
- **Tribal housing infrastructure**: Advocate for dedicated tribal housing infrastructure funding
- **Section 184 expansion**: Support expansion of the HUD Section 184 loan guarantee program for trust land lending
- **Urban Native housing**: Advocate for dedicated urban Native housing funding within NAHASDA

### Template Email for Federal Legislators

```text
Subject: Support Tribal Housing Reform and NAHASDA Reauthorization

Dear [Senator/Representative] [Last Name],

I am writing to urge your support for tribal housing reform,
specifically:

1. NAHASDA Reauthorization: The Native American Housing Assistance
and Self-Determination Act has not been comprehensively
reauthorized since its authorization expired in FY 2007. Current
funding of $772 million meets only 27% of the estimated $2.9
billion annual need. Please support reauthorization at
significantly increased funding levels.

2. Trust Land Mortgage Reform: BIA processing delays of 6-12
months for residential leases and Title Status Reports prevent
families from building or purchasing homes on trust land. Please
support legislation establishing 90-day processing timelines.

3. Tribal Housing Infrastructure: Approximately 189,000 tribal
homes lack safe water access, and 14,000 lack electricity.
Housing cannot exist without infrastructure. Please support
dedicated tribal housing infrastructure funding.

The tribal housing crisis -- with overcrowding rates eight times
the national average and tens of thousands of homes lacking basic
plumbing -- is a direct consequence of federal policy and a matter
of federal trust responsibility. These reforms have bipartisan
support and would benefit the 9.7 million Americans who identify
as American Indian or Alaska Native.

Thank you for your consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your name]
[Your address]
```

## Community Actions

### At the Local Level

- **Support tribal-community partnerships**: Advocate for cooperation between local governments and tribal housing authorities on infrastructure, building codes, and service delivery
- **Volunteer with tribal housing organizations**: Organizations like Habitat for Humanity operate tribal housing programs in many states
- **Support local businesses working with tribes**: Encourage contractors, suppliers, and service providers to engage with tribal housing projects
- **Attend public hearings**: When tribal housing projects or trust land applications are discussed, attend and provide supportive public comment
- **Donate to tribal housing funds**: Many tribal housing authorities and Native CDFIs accept donations for housing construction and rehabilitation

### At the State Level

- Contact state legislators about state-tribal housing cooperation
- Advocate for state housing finance agency engagement with tribal housing needs
- Support state LIHTC allocation policies that prioritize tribal housing projects
- Oppose state legislation that would restrict tribal housing development
- Advocate for state revenue-sharing agreements that support tribal infrastructure

## Talking Points

### For Conversations with Neighbors and Community Members

1. **The housing crisis is severe and documented**: Overcrowding on reservations is more than eight times the national average. Tens of thousands of homes lack running water, electricity, or indoor plumbing. This is not a developing-world problem in someone else's country -- it is here in the United States.

2. **The federal government created this crisis**: Forced removal destroyed established communities. Allotment eliminated the land base. Termination withdrew housing support. Trust land restrictions block conventional mortgages. And NAHASDA has been chronically underfunded since its enactment in 1996.

3. **Funding meets only 27% of the need**: The $772 million annual NAHASDA appropriation covers barely one-quarter of the estimated $2.9 billion annual need. In inflation-adjusted terms, funding has declined 32% since 1998.

4. **One-time investments proved that more funding works**: The CARES Act and American Rescue Plan together provided nearly $2 billion in one-time tribal housing funds. Tribes deployed these funds effectively, accelerating construction and rehabilitation. Then baseline funding returned to inadequate levels.

5. **Housing affects health**: Overcrowded housing accelerated COVID-19 transmission on the Navajo Nation, which had among the highest per-capita infection rates in the country. Lack of running water made basic handwashing impossible. Poor housing is a public health emergency.

6. **This is a trust responsibility**: The federal government has a legal trust responsibility to tribal nations stemming from treaties in which tribes ceded billions of acres of land in exchange for services including housing support.

### For Conversations with Skeptics

- **"Why should taxpayers fund tribal housing?"** The federal government has a legal trust responsibility based on treaties in which tribes ceded nearly all of their land. Housing support is part of the services promised in exchange. This is not charity -- it is a legal obligation comparable to contract enforcement.

- **"Can't tribes use casino revenue?"** Only about 20% of tribes operate profitable gaming enterprises. Most reservation communities have no gaming revenue. Even gaming tribes cannot address decades of federal underfunding from gaming revenue alone. The housing crisis is nationwide across Indian Country, not limited to non-gaming tribes.

- **"Won't more federal money just be wasted?"** The American Rescue Plan's $750 million supplemental tribal housing funds were deployed effectively, with tribes reporting accelerated construction and no widespread mismanagement. Larger TDHEs have demonstrated the ability to manage substantial programs. Where capacity gaps exist, the solution is capacity building, not funding denial.

- **"Why can't they just move to where there's housing?"** Many tribal citizens live on reservations because that is their homeland -- where their families, cultures, ceremonies, and communities are rooted. Telling people to abandon their homes rather than fixing the crisis those homes face ignores the reality of place-based communities and repeats the logic of forced relocation.

- **"The private market should handle housing."** The private market has comprehensively failed in Indian Country because the conditions for private housing markets -- clear title, credit access, infrastructure, population density -- do not exist on most reservations. This is a structural market failure created by federal policy, not a temporary gap.

## Professional Actions

### For Architects and Planners

- Offer pro bono design services for tribal housing authorities
- Develop expertise in climate-resilient and culturally appropriate design for tribal communities
- Participate in tribal housing design competitions and research
- Advocate within professional associations for tribal housing design standards

### For Bankers and Financial Professionals

- Learn about the Section 184 loan guarantee program and become a participating lender
- Support Native CDFI capacity through investment and technical assistance
- Develop trust land mortgage products and expertise
- Advocate within industry for reduced barriers to tribal lending

### For Construction Professionals

- Offer expertise to tribal housing authorities for construction management
- Support tribal construction apprenticeship programs
- Develop experience with modular and prefabricated housing for remote delivery
- Advocate for building code adaptations appropriate for reservation conditions

### For Lawyers

- Volunteer with Native American Rights Fund or tribal legal aid programs
- Provide pro bono assistance on trust land leasing and mortgage issues
- File amicus briefs supporting tribal housing programs in federal litigation
- Advocate within bar associations for federal Indian housing law education

### For Healthcare Professionals

- Document and publicize the health impacts of substandard tribal housing
- Support research on housing-health connections in Indian Country
- Advocate for housing as a social determinant of health in tribal communities
- Partner with tribal housing authorities on healthy homes initiatives

### For Educators and Researchers

- Incorporate tribal housing issues into curricula on housing policy, public health, and American Indian studies
- Conduct and publish research on tribal housing conditions and solutions
- Partner with tribal communities on participatory research projects
- Support tribal college programs in housing, construction, and planning
