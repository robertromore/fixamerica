# Tribal Housing: Current State

## Housing Conditions Today

### The Scale of the Crisis

The tribal housing shortage is among the most severe in the developed world. While precise numbers are difficult to establish due to data gaps, multiple sources converge on a picture of extreme need:

| Metric | Value | National Comparison | Source |
|--------|-------|---------------------|--------|
| Housing unit shortage | 68,000-200,000 units | -- | NAIHC, 2023; HUD Housing Needs Study, 2017 |
| Overcrowded households | 15.9% | 2.0% | Census ACS, 2022 |
| Severely overcrowded (>1.5 persons/room) | 5.1% | 0.5% | Census ACS, 2022 |
| Lacking complete plumbing | 5.8% | 0.3% | Census ACS, 2022 |
| Lacking complete kitchen | 4.7% | 0.8% | Census ACS, 2022 |
| Using wood/coal as primary heating | 12.3% | 0.4% | Census ACS, 2022 |
| Homeownership rate | 51.5% | 65.4% | Census ACS, 2022 |
| Median home value (reservation) | $112,000 | $281,900 | Census ACS, 2022 |

### Regional Variation

Housing conditions vary significantly across Indian Country:

| Region | Key Housing Challenges | Notable Conditions |
|--------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Navajo Nation | 30,000-40,000 unit shortage; 30% lack running water; 10% lack electricity | Largest reservation; extreme remoteness; hauled water common |
| Northern Plains (Pine Ridge, Rosebud, Standing Rock) | Severe overcrowding (15-20 people per home common); extreme cold | Some of the most severe housing conditions in the U.S.; -40F winters |
| Alaska Native villages | Aging housing stock; permafrost thaw damaging foundations; fuel costs | Climate change actively destroying infrastructure |
| Oklahoma tribal areas | Urban/suburban mix; trust land fractionation; tornado exposure | Less severe overcrowding but significant substandard housing |
| Southwest pueblos | Aging adobe structures; water scarcity; limited infrastructure | Traditional architecture in need of maintenance and improvement |
| Pacific Northwest | Mold and moisture issues; limited land base | Smaller reservations with high construction costs |

### Health Impacts of Housing Conditions

Poor housing conditions directly contribute to health disparities in Indian Country:

| Housing Condition | Health Impact | Evidence |
|-------------------|--------------|----------|
| Overcrowding | Respiratory infections, TB transmission, COVID-19 spread | CDC, 2020; IHS, 2022 |
| Lack of plumbing | Gastrointestinal illness, skin infections, hepatitis A | NIHB, 2021 |
| Mold and moisture | Asthma, allergies, respiratory illness | IHS Environmental Health, 2022 |
| Inadequate heating | Hypothermia, fire risk (space heaters), carbon monoxide | NAIHC, 2023 |
| Structural deficiencies | Injury risk, pest infestation, lead exposure | HUD, 2017 |
| Psychological stress | Depression, anxiety, family conflict, substance use | SAMHSA, 2021 |

### Homelessness

Native American homelessness is underounted and underserved:

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Native Americans as share of homeless population | 5% (vs. 2.9% of general population) | HUD AHAR, 2023 |
| Native Americans experiencing homelessness | Approximately 32,000 on a given night | HUD Point-in-Time Count, 2023 |
| On-reservation homelessness | Largely uncounted; "hidden homelessness" (doubling up) | NAIHC, 2023 |
| Unsheltered rate | Higher than any other racial group | HUD AHAR, 2023 |
| Youth homelessness | 11% of Native youth experience homelessness | Voices of Youth Count, 2017 |

"Hidden homelessness" -- individuals and families doubling up with relatives due to the housing shortage -- is pervasive on reservations but not captured by HUD's Point-in-Time Count methodology, which focuses on sheltered and unsheltered populations.

## Federal Housing Programs

### NAHASDA and the Indian Housing Block Grant

The Native American Housing Assistance and Self-Determination Act (NAHASDA) of 1996 consolidated multiple HUD programs into a single Indian Housing Block Grant (IHBG):

| Program Element | Description | Status |
|----------------|-------------|--------|
| Indian Housing Block Grant | Formula grant to tribes and TDHEs for housing activities | $772 million (FY 2024) |
| Formula factors | Population, poverty, housing cost, substandard units | Based on Census ACS data; updated periodically |
| Eligible activities | New construction, rehabilitation, rental assistance, homeownership, crime prevention, model activities | Broad tribal discretion |
| Tribally Designated Housing Entities | 580+ TDHEs administer IHBG funds | Capacity varies enormously |
| Indian Housing Plan | Required annual plan submitted to HUD | Administrative burden noted by small tribes |
| NAHASDA reauthorization | Originally authorized through FY 2007; extended through appropriations | Long-overdue comprehensive reauthorization |

### NAHASDA Funding History

| Fiscal Year | IHBG Appropriation | Inflation-Adjusted (2024$) |
|------------|--------------------|-----------------------------|
| FY 1998 | $600 million | $1.14 billion |
| FY 2005 | $622 million | $990 million |
| FY 2010 | $700 million | $1.00 billion |
| FY 2015 | $650 million | $859 million |
| FY 2020 | $655 million | $810 million |
| FY 2024 | $772 million | $772 million |

NAHASDA funding in real terms has declined significantly since enactment. The $772 million FY 2024 appropriation represents approximately 27% of the estimated $2.9 billion annual need identified by the NAIHC.

### Section 184 Loan Guarantee Program

The HUD Section 184 program is the primary federal tool for supporting Native homeownership:

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Loans guaranteed (cumulative since 1994) | Over 47,000 | HUD ONAP, 2024 |
| Total loan volume (cumulative) | Over $9 billion | HUD ONAP, 2024 |
| Average loan size | Approximately $190,000 | HUD ONAP, 2024 |
| Default rate | Under 2% (extremely low) | HUD ONAP, 2024 |
| Geographic distribution | 50 states; heaviest use in Oklahoma, Arizona, New Mexico | HUD ONAP, 2024 |
| Trust land loans | Minority of total loans; most are on fee land | HUD ONAP, 2024 |

While Section 184 has been successful for Native borrowers on fee land, its utilization on trust land remains limited due to the complexity of trust land mortgage processes.

## Financing Barriers

### Trust Land and Mortgage Access

The trust land system creates fundamental barriers to housing finance:

| Barrier | Description | Impact |
|---------|-------------|--------|
| No individual title | Trust land is held by the federal government for the benefit of tribes or individuals | Cannot be used as collateral for conventional mortgage |
| BIA lease approval | Residential leases on trust land require BIA approval; process can take months to years | Delays and uncertainty deter lenders |
| Foreclosure complexity | Foreclosing on trust land is extremely difficult; lenders cannot easily recover assets | Lenders avoid trust land lending |
| Appraisal challenges | Limited comparable sales on reservations make appraisal difficult | Homes often appraised below construction cost |
| Title Status Report | Required from BIA for trust land transactions; can take 6-12 months | Major bottleneck in the process |
| Fractionated ownership | Allotted trust land may have hundreds of ownership interests | Consolidating interests for a homesite is extremely difficult |

### Construction Cost Premium

Building on reservations costs significantly more than comparable construction elsewhere:

| Cost Factor | Premium over Urban Average | Reason |
|-------------|---------------------------|--------|
| Materials delivery | 30-50% | Remote locations; limited road access |
| Labor | 20-40% | Small local workforce; travel costs for imported labor |
| Infrastructure (water/sewer) | 100-300% | Often requires new well, septic, or utility extension |
| Land preparation | Variable | Challenging terrain; environmental clearances |
| Climate adaptation | 10-30% | Extreme temperatures, permafrost, high winds |
| Regulatory compliance | 10-20% | Environmental review, BIA approvals, Davis-Bacon wages |
| **Total estimated premium** | **40-100%** | **Per-unit cost: $250,000-$500,000+ vs. $150,000-$250,000** |

## Tribal Housing Authorities

### Structure and Capacity

Tribally Designated Housing Entities (TDHEs) are the primary administrators of NAHASDA funds:

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Number of TDHEs | 580+ | HUD ONAP, 2024 |
| Largest TDHEs | Navajo Housing Authority (14,000+ units), Cherokee Nation, Choctaw Nation | HUD, 2024 |
| Smallest TDHEs | Manage fewer than 50 units | HUD, 2024 |
| Average IHBG grant (small tribes) | Under $200,000 | HUD, 2024 |
| Staff capacity challenges | 62% of TDHEs report difficulty hiring qualified staff | NAIHC Survey, 2023 |

### TDHE Challenges

| Challenge | Description | Impact |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| Capacity constraints | Small TDHEs lack staff for planning, grant management, and construction oversight | Delayed projects; compliance issues |
| Administrative burden | Indian Housing Plan and Annual Performance Report requirements consume limited staff time | Resources diverted from housing production |
| Construction management | Many TDHEs lack construction management expertise | Cost overruns; quality issues |
| Maintenance funding | Aging housing stock requires growing maintenance expenditure | Less funding available for new construction |
| Waitlists | Most TDHEs maintain long waitlists for housing | Average wait: 3-5 years; some wait 10+ years |

## Recent Developments (2024-2025)

### Bipartisan Infrastructure Law Implementation

The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (2021) included significant tribal allocations:

- $3.5 billion for Indian Health Service water and sanitation projects
- $3 billion for tribal broadband expansion
- $216 million for BIA road maintenance
- $150 million for tribal dam safety
- Implementation has been slower than anticipated; many projects in planning phases

### American Rescue Plan Housing Funds

The American Rescue Plan Act (2021) provided $750 million in supplemental IHBG funding. This one-time infusion was the largest single-year tribal housing appropriation in history and demonstrated what higher funding levels could achieve:

- Tribes reported accelerated construction and rehabilitation
- Some tribes were able to address years of deferred maintenance
- The temporary nature highlighted the inadequacy of baseline funding

### NAHASDA Reauthorization Efforts

NAHASDA has not been comprehensively reauthorized since its original authorization expired in FY 2007. Multiple reauthorization bills have been introduced:

- Most recent: NAHASDA Reauthorization Act (introduced in 118th and 119th Congresses)
- Key provisions under discussion: increased authorization levels, expanded eligible activities, streamlined reporting, improved data collection
- Status: Committee consideration; no floor action as of early 2026

### HUD Regulatory Actions

- Updated Section 184 loan guarantee program rules to streamline processing
- Revised IHBG formula negotiation rulemaking (ongoing)
- Tribal consultation on NAHASDA regulatory improvements

## The Funding Gap

| Category | Annual Need | Current Funding | Gap |
|----------|------------|-----------------|-----|
| NAHASDA/IHBG | $2.9 billion | $772 million | $2.1 billion (73% underfunded) |
| IHS Sanitation Facilities | $500 million | $196 million | $304 million (61% underfunded) |
| BIA Roads | $800 million | $310 million | $490 million (61% underfunded) |
| Total estimated annual gap | $4.2 billion | $1.3 billion | $2.9 billion |

The cumulative effect of decades of underfunding has created a housing and infrastructure deficit that would require sustained, multi-year investment far beyond current levels to address.
