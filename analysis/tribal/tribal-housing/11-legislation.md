# Tribal Housing: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative approach to tribal housing reform requires action in five areas: (1) reauthorizing and significantly expanding NAHASDA funding, (2) removing trust land barriers to housing construction and mortgage financing, (3) establishing dedicated tribal housing infrastructure funding, (4) building TDHE capacity and expanding homeownership opportunities, and (5) addressing Native American homelessness. This document provides draft legislation, regulatory frameworks, and legal analysis for each area.

## Federal Legislation

### The NAHASDA Reauthorization and Housing Investment Act

**Purpose**: Reauthorize the Native American Housing Assistance and Self-Determination Act with significantly increased funding, expanded eligible activities, streamlined reporting for small TDHEs, and a dedicated urban Native housing set-aside.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "NAHASDA Reauthorization and
Tribal Housing Investment Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
(a) the Native American Housing Assistance and Self-Determination
Act of 1996 (25 U.S.C. 4101 et seq.) has not been
comprehensively reauthorized since its original authorization
expired in fiscal year 2007;
(b) the Indian Housing Block Grant, funded at $772,000,000 in
fiscal year 2024, meets only 27 percent of the estimated
$2,900,000,000 annual need identified by the National American
Indian Housing Council;
(c) in inflation-adjusted terms, IHBG funding has declined
approximately 32 percent since fiscal year 1998;
(d) the tribal housing shortage of 68,000 to 200,000 units,
overcrowding rates more than eight times the national average,
and tens of thousands of homes lacking basic plumbing or
electricity constitute a housing crisis of national significance;
(e) the federal government has a trust responsibility to tribal
nations that includes adequate housing support;
(f) one-time investments through the CARES Act and American
Rescue Plan demonstrated that tribes can effectively deploy
significantly higher funding levels.

SEC. 3. REAUTHORIZATION OF NAHASDA.

(a) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—Section 108 of the
Native American Housing Assistance and Self-Determination Act
of 1996 (25 U.S.C. 4117) is amended to read as follows:

"SEC. 108. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

"There are authorized to be appropriated for grants under
this title—
"(1) $1,500,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through
2029;
"(2) $2,000,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2030 through
2033;
"(3) $2,500,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2034 through
2036;
"(4) $2,900,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2037 through
2042.".

SEC. 4. EXPANDED ELIGIBLE ACTIVITIES.

Section 202 of the Native American Housing Assistance and
Self-Determination Act of 1996 (25 U.S.C. 4132) is amended—
(a) by adding at the end the following:
    "(8) INFRASTRUCTURE.—The construction, improvement, or
    maintenance of water, sewer, electrical, and road
    infrastructure directly necessary for the construction or
    habitability of housing units funded under this title.
    "(9) CLIMATE RESILIENCE.—The design, construction, or
    retrofitting of housing to address climate-related threats
    including extreme temperatures, flooding, permafrost thaw,
    and wildfire.
    "(10) HOMELESSNESS PREVENTION.—Programs to prevent and
    address homelessness, including emergency shelter, rapid
    rehousing, and permanent supportive housing for Native
    Americans.".

SEC. 5. URBAN NATIVE HOUSING SET-ASIDE.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 302 of the Native American Housing
Assistance and Self-Determination Act of 1996 (25 U.S.C. 4152)
is amended by adding at the end the following:

"(d) URBAN NATIVE HOUSING SET-ASIDE.—
    "(1) The Secretary shall set aside not less than 10 percent
    of the total amount appropriated under section 108 for each
    fiscal year for housing activities benefiting Native Americans
    residing in urban areas.
    "(2) Amounts set aside under paragraph (1) shall be
    distributed through a competitive grant program to tribally
    designated housing entities, tribal organizations, and urban
    Indian organizations serving Native Americans in urban areas.".

SEC. 6. STREAMLINED REPORTING FOR SMALL TDHES.

(a) Section 404 of the Native American Housing Assistance and
Self-Determination Act of 1996 (25 U.S.C. 4164) is amended by
adding at the end the following:

"(d) SIMPLIFIED REPORTING.—
    "(1) Any tribally designated housing entity that receives
    less than $500,000 in IHBG funds in a fiscal year may submit
    a simplified annual performance report, in a form prescribed
    by the Secretary, in lieu of the report required under
    subsection (a).
    "(2) The simplified report shall include basic financial
    information and a narrative description of housing activities
    undertaken during the fiscal year.".

SEC. 7. COMPREHENSIVE HOUSING NEEDS ASSESSMENT.

(a) The Secretary shall conduct a comprehensive assessment of
the housing needs of American Indians and Alaska Natives in
tribal areas not later than 2 years after the date of enactment
of this Act, and every 5 years thereafter.

(b) The assessment shall include—
    (1) an estimate of the total housing unit shortage;
    (2) documentation of overcrowding, substandard conditions,
    and lack of utilities;
    (3) assessment of infrastructure needs;
    (4) analysis of homelessness, including hidden homelessness;
    (5) regional breakdowns by BIA region;
    (6) cost estimates for addressing identified needs.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on October 1 of the first fiscal
year beginning after the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill reauthorizes NAHASDA with a phased funding increase from $1.5 billion to $2.9 billion over 15 years, expanding eligible activities to include infrastructure, climate resilience, and homelessness prevention. The urban Native set-aside addresses the housing needs of the growing off-reservation population. Streamlined reporting reduces administrative burden for small TDHEs that currently spend disproportionate resources on compliance.

**Potential Challenges**: The phased funding increase will face budget process opposition, particularly in later years. The urban set-aside may face opposition from reservation-based tribes concerned about diversion of funds. Expanding eligible activities could dilute housing-specific focus.

**Refinements**: If the full authorization level is politically infeasible, a lower initial target with a mandatory escalation clause tied to appropriations growth could be substituted. The urban set-aside percentage could be negotiated downward (5-7%) as a compromise.

### The Trust Land Housing Finance Reform Act

**Purpose**: Remove the most significant barriers to housing construction and mortgage financing on trust land by establishing mandatory processing timelines, expanding tribal leasing authority, and creating standardized trust land mortgage products.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Trust Land Housing Finance
Reform Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that—
    (1) the trust land system creates fundamental barriers to
    housing finance, as trust land cannot be used as collateral
    for conventional mortgages;
    (2) Bureau of Indian Affairs processing of residential
    leases and Title Status Reports routinely takes 6 to 12
    months or more, delaying housing construction and
    discouraging lender participation;
    (3) the HUD Section 184 Indian Home Loan Guarantee Program
    has a default rate under 2 percent, demonstrating that
    Native borrowers are excellent credit risks;
    (4) the HEARTH Act of 2012 demonstrated that tribal leasing
    authority produces faster outcomes without risk to trust
    land protections.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to make homeownership
on trust land as accessible as homeownership on fee land while
maintaining trust land protections essential to tribal sovereignty.

SEC. 3. MANDATORY PROCESSING TIMELINES.

(a) RESIDENTIAL LEASES.—The Secretary of the Interior shall
approve or deny any application for a residential lease on
trust land within 90 days of receipt of a complete application.

(b) TITLE STATUS REPORTS.—The Secretary shall issue a Title
Status Report for any residential trust land transaction within
90 days of request.

(c) DEEMED APPROVAL.—If the Secretary fails to act within the
timeframes established in subsections (a) and (b), the
application shall be deemed approved, provided it meets
standard requirements established by regulation.

(d) QUARTERLY REPORTING.—The Secretary shall publish quarterly
reports on processing times for residential leases and Title
Status Reports, disaggregated by BIA regional office.

SEC. 4. EXPANSION OF HEARTH ACT AUTHORITY.

(a) Section 2 of the HEARTH Act of 2012 (25 U.S.C. 415(h))
is amended—
    (1) by striking "surface leases" and inserting "surface
    leases, residential site leases, and homesite assignments";
    (2) by adding at the end: "A tribe exercising authority under
    this subsection may approve residential leases for periods
    of up to 50 years, including renewals, without further
    approval of the Secretary.".

SEC. 5. STANDARDIZED TRUST LAND MORTGAGE PRODUCT.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary of Housing and Urban
Development, in consultation with the Secretary of the Interior,
the Federal Housing Finance Agency, and tribal organizations,
shall develop a standardized trust land mortgage product within
18 months of enactment of this Act.

(b) FEATURES.—The standardized product shall include—
    (1) a uniform residential lease form approved for use on
    all trust land;
    (2) standardized appraisal guidelines for trust land
    residential properties;
    (3) a leasehold mortgage instrument eligible for purchase
    by Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, and Ginnie Mae;
    (4) streamlined foreclosure procedures consistent with
    trust land protections.

(c) SECONDARY MARKET.—The Federal Housing Finance Agency shall
direct Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac to develop programs to
purchase trust land leasehold mortgages that conform to the
standardized product.

SEC. 6. SECTION 184 PROGRAM EXPANSION.

(a) TRUST LAND PRIORITY.—The Secretary of Housing and Urban
Development shall prioritize expansion of the Section 184
Indian Home Loan Guarantee Program for loans on trust land,
including—
    (1) dedicated outreach to lenders for trust land
    origination;
    (2) reduced guarantee fees for trust land loans;
    (3) training programs for lenders on trust land mortgage
    processing.

(b) GUARANTEE AUTHORITY.—The guarantee authority under section
184 of the Housing and Community Development Act of 1992 (12
U.S.C. 1715z-13a) is increased to $5,000,000,000.

SEC. 7. BIA STAFFING.

There are authorized to be appropriated $30,000,000 for each
of fiscal years 2027 through 2032 for the hiring of additional
Bureau of Indian Affairs realty specialists to meet the
processing timelines established in section 3.

SEC. 8. TRUST LAND RECORDS MODERNIZATION.

(a) The Secretary of the Interior shall modernize the trust
land title and records system to enable electronic processing
of residential lease applications and Title Status Reports.

(b) There are authorized to be appropriated $20,000,000 for
the modernization described in subsection (a).
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the most critical trust land barriers by establishing mandatory 90-day processing timelines with a deemed-approval provision, expanding HEARTH Act authority for residential leasing, creating a standardized trust land mortgage product eligible for secondary market purchase, and expanding the Section 184 program. The deemed-approval provision provides enforcement teeth that mere timeline mandates lack.

**Potential Challenges**: BIA will resist the deemed-approval provision. The standardized mortgage product requires interagency cooperation and GSE willingness. Lender recruitment for trust land will be challenging even with reforms. The 90-day timeline may be unrealistic for complex title situations.

**Refinements**: The deemed-approval provision could include a 30-day extension for complex cases with documented justification. The standardized mortgage product could be piloted with a small number of tribes before national rollout. A trust land appraisal training program could address the shortage of qualified appraisers.

### The Tribal Housing Infrastructure Coordination Act

**Purpose**: Establish a dedicated Tribal Housing Infrastructure Fund and create a single-application process for infrastructure needed to support housing construction on tribal land.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Tribal Housing Infrastructure
Coordination Act".

SEC. 2. TRIBAL HOUSING INFRASTRUCTURE FUND.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established in the Treasury of the
United States a fund to be known as the "Tribal Housing
Infrastructure Fund".

(b) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There are authorized to
be appropriated to the Fund $750,000,000 for each of fiscal
years 2027 through 2042.

(c) USE OF FUNDS.—Amounts in the Fund shall be available for—
    (1) construction, improvement, and maintenance of water
    supply systems serving tribal housing;
    (2) construction, improvement, and maintenance of
    wastewater and sanitation systems serving tribal housing;
    (3) extension of electrical service to tribal housing;
    (4) construction and improvement of access roads serving
    tribal housing developments;
    (5) tribal infrastructure planning and engineering;
    (6) operations and maintenance of infrastructure systems
    serving tribal housing.

SEC. 3. SINGLE APPLICATION PROCESS.

(a) IN GENERAL.—The Secretary of Housing and Urban Development,
in coordination with the Director of the Indian Health Service,
the Secretary of the Interior, the Administrator of the
Environmental Protection Agency, and the Secretary of Energy,
shall establish a single application process for tribal housing
infrastructure funding.

(b) LEAD AGENCY.—The Department of Housing and Urban Development
shall serve as the lead agency for processing applications
under this section.

(c) INTERAGENCY AGREEMENTS.—The agencies listed in subsection
(a) shall enter into memoranda of understanding establishing—
    (1) a unified application form;
    (2) coordinated environmental review;
    (3) consolidated reporting requirements;
    (4) a single point of contact for applicant tribes.

(d) PROCESSING TIMELINE.—Applications shall be processed
within 120 days of receipt of a complete application.

SEC. 4. INTEGRATION WITH HOUSING PLANS.

(a) Each tribally designated housing entity receiving funds
under the Native American Housing Assistance and
Self-Determination Act of 1996 may include an infrastructure
component in its Indian Housing Plan.

(b) The infrastructure component shall identify infrastructure
needs for planned housing construction and may be submitted as
the basis for an application under section 3.

SEC. 5. TRIBAL INFRASTRUCTURE PRIORITY SETTING.

(a) Each tribe receiving funds under this Act shall develop a
tribal housing infrastructure plan identifying—
    (1) current infrastructure deficiencies;
    (2) infrastructure needed for planned housing construction;
    (3) priority projects ranked by need and cost-effectiveness;
    (4) estimated costs and timelines.

(b) Tribal infrastructure plans shall be developed through
tribal consultation and community engagement processes.

SEC. 6. OPERATIONS AND MAINTENANCE.

(a) Not less than 15 percent of amounts appropriated under
section 2 may be used for operations and maintenance of
existing infrastructure serving tribal housing.

(b) This provision recognizes that infrastructure investment
without ongoing maintenance funding produces deteriorating
assets that fail to support housing over time.
```

**Explanation**: This bill creates a dedicated infrastructure fund specifically for housing-supporting infrastructure and addresses the fragmentation problem by establishing a single-application process across HUD, IHS, BIA, EPA, and DOE. The operations and maintenance set-aside addresses the historical pattern of building infrastructure without funding its upkeep. Integration with Indian Housing Plans ensures that infrastructure investment is coordinated with housing construction.

**Potential Challenges**: Interagency coordination is difficult; agencies may resist ceding authority to HUD as lead. The $750 million annual authorization will face budget opposition. Environmental review consolidation may face legal challenges. Operations and maintenance funding may be seen as diverting resources from new construction.

**Refinements**: If full fund establishment is not feasible, a coordination mandate with set-aside provisions within existing programs could achieve some of the same goals. The operations and maintenance percentage could be adjusted based on infrastructure age and condition assessments.

### The Native American Homeownership and Credit Access Act

**Purpose**: Expand homeownership opportunities for Native Americans through Native CDFI capitalization, credit-building programs, down payment assistance, and financial literacy education.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Native American Homeownership
and Credit Access Act".

SEC. 2. NATIVE CDFI CAPITALIZATION.

(a) IN GENERAL.—The Secretary of the Treasury shall provide
$200,000,000 in capital grants to certified Native community
development financial institutions for the purpose of expanding
mortgage lending capacity in Indian Country.

(b) ELIGIBLE USES.—Capital grants under this section may be
used for—
    (1) mortgage loan origination on trust and fee land;
    (2) construction lending for housing on tribal land;
    (3) loan loss reserves supporting trust land lending;
    (4) credit-building products for tribal members.

(c) ANNUAL OPERATING SUPPORT.—There are authorized to be
appropriated $50,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through
2037 for operating support grants to Native CDFIs engaged in
housing finance.

SEC. 3. NATIVE AMERICAN DOWN PAYMENT ASSISTANCE PROGRAM.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary of Housing and Urban
Development shall establish a Native American Down Payment
Assistance Program.

(b) ELIGIBILITY.—An individual shall be eligible for
assistance under this section if such individual—
    (1) is a member of a federally recognized Indian tribe or
    is an Alaska Native;
    (2) is a first-time homebuyer;
    (3) has completed a HUD-approved homebuyer education course;
    (4) has a household income below 120 percent of the area
    median income (or the national median income, whichever is
    greater).

(c) ASSISTANCE AMOUNT.—Assistance under this section shall not
exceed $25,000 per household.

(d) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There are authorized to
be appropriated $75,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027
through 2037 for the program established under this section.

SEC. 4. CREDIT-BUILDING INITIATIVE.

(a) The Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, in
consultation with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau and
tribal organizations, shall establish a credit-building
initiative for Native Americans.

(b) The initiative shall include—
    (1) programs using alternative credit data, including rent
    and utility payment history, to establish credit scores;
    (2) financial literacy education developed in consultation
    with tribal communities;
    (3) Individual Development Account programs for
    homeownership savings;
    (4) partnerships with Native CDFIs for credit-building
    products.

(c) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There are authorized to
be appropriated $20,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027
through 2037.

SEC. 5. TRIBAL HOUSING COUNSELING.

(a) The Secretary of Housing and Urban Development shall
establish a Tribal Housing Counseling Program to provide
pre-purchase, post-purchase, and foreclosure prevention
counseling services in Indian Country.

(b) Counseling services may be provided by tribally designated
housing entities, Native CDFIs, or HUD-approved housing
counseling agencies with tribal expertise.

(c) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There are authorized to
be appropriated $10,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027
through 2037.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the credit and financial access barriers that prevent homeownership even where housing is available. The Native CDFI capitalization addresses the fundamental absence of lending institutions on reservations. Down payment assistance and credit-building programs address the income and credit gaps that prevent mortgage qualification. The income threshold of 120% of area median income (or national median, whichever is greater) accounts for the extremely low area median incomes on many reservations.

**Potential Challenges**: The $200 million CDFI capitalization is a significant upfront cost. Alternative credit data programs require cooperation from credit bureaus. Some tribes may view federal homeownership programs as inconsistent with communal land tenure values.

**Refinements**: The CDFI capitalization could be phased over 3 years. The down payment assistance amount could be adjusted by region to reflect cost differences. The program could be designed to support both homeownership and lease-to-own models consistent with tribal land tenure.

## State Model Legislation

### Model State-Tribal Housing Cooperation Act

**Purpose**: Establish a framework for state-tribal cooperation on housing and infrastructure, including coordination of state housing finance resources with tribal housing programs.

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Tribal Housing
Cooperation Act".

SECTION 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.—The Legislature finds that—
    (1) tribal communities within this state face severe
    housing challenges including overcrowding, substandard
    conditions, and limited access to housing finance;
    (2) state and tribal housing programs can achieve better
    outcomes through coordination and cooperation;
    (3) investment in tribal housing generates economic
    benefits for surrounding communities through employment,
    purchasing, and service demand.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to establish
mechanisms for state-tribal cooperation on housing and
related infrastructure.

SECTION 3. STATE HOUSING FINANCE AGENCY TRIBAL LIAISON.

(a) The [State Housing Finance Agency] shall designate a
Tribal Housing Liaison responsible for—
    (1) coordinating with tribally designated housing entities
    on the use of state housing finance resources;
    (2) facilitating tribal access to Low-Income Housing Tax
    Credits and other state housing finance programs;
    (3) providing technical assistance to tribes on state
    housing programs.

SECTION 4. LIHTC TRIBAL SET-ASIDE.

(a) The [State Housing Finance Agency] shall establish a
set-aside of not less than 5 percent of the state's annual
Low-Income Housing Tax Credit allocation for housing projects
on tribal land or primarily serving tribal members.

SECTION 5. INFRASTRUCTURE COORDINATION.

(a) The [State Department of Transportation/Infrastructure]
shall coordinate with tribal governments on infrastructure
projects that support tribal housing, including—
    (1) road construction and maintenance;
    (2) water and sewer extensions;
    (3) broadband deployment.

SECTION 6. ANNUAL REPORTING.

The Tribal Housing Liaison shall submit an annual report to the
Legislature on the status of state-tribal housing cooperation,
including the number of LIHTC units allocated on tribal land,
infrastructure coordination activities, and identified barriers
to cooperation.
```

## Regulatory Framework

### NAHASDA Regulatory Modernization

**Existing Authority**: 24 C.F.R. Part 1000

**Proposed Regulatory Changes**:

- Establish simplified reporting format for TDHEs receiving under $500,000
- Update IHBG formula to reflect current Census data and housing cost indices
- Expand eligible activities consistent with legislative amendments
- Reduce Indian Housing Plan complexity while maintaining accountability
- Establish tribal housing data collection standards

### BIA Trust Land Processing Regulations

**Existing Authority**: 25 C.F.R. Part 162 (leasing); 25 C.F.R. Part 150 (title)

**Proposed Regulatory Changes**:

- Implement 90-day processing timelines for residential leases
- Establish deemed-approval procedures consistent with statutory mandate
- Create standardized residential lease forms acceptable to secondary market lenders
- Expand HEARTH Act tribal leasing authority to residential site leases
- Develop electronic processing systems for trust land title work

### Section 184 Program Regulations

**Existing Authority**: 24 C.F.R. Part 1005

**Proposed Regulatory Changes**:

- Reduce guarantee fees for trust land loans
- Streamline lender participation requirements for trust land lending
- Establish standardized appraisal guidelines for trust land residential properties
- Create trust land foreclosure procedures that protect both lender and trust interests
- Develop secondary market eligibility criteria for trust land leasehold mortgages

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Plenary power**: Congress has clear authority under the Indian Commerce Clause to enact all proposed legislation
- **Trust responsibility**: Federal trust responsibility provides additional constitutional and moral basis for housing investment
- **Equal protection**: All proposed programs are grounded in the political classification of tribal sovereignty, not racial classification (*Morton v. Mancari*, 1974; *Brackeen v. Haaland*, 2023)
- **Spending Clause**: Mandatory appropriations for tribal housing are within Congress's spending authority; phased increases reduce fiscal objections
- **Property rights**: Deemed-approval provisions for trust land processing must be designed to protect individual Indian allottee interests

### Preemption Questions

- NAHASDA preempts conflicting state housing regulations on trust land
- Trust land processing timelines preempt BIA internal processing discretion
- State model legislation operates within state authority and does not raise preemption issues
- Infrastructure coordination provisions do not alter existing federal-state-tribal jurisdictional frameworks

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- Deemed-approval provision in Trust Land Housing Finance Reform Act provides automatic enforcement of processing timelines
- Quarterly reporting on BIA processing times creates public accountability
- GAO audit authority provides independent performance evaluation
- NAHASDA annual performance reporting and HUD review maintain program accountability
- Mandamus actions available if BIA fails to meet statutory processing timelines

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- NAHASDA authorization periods of 3-6 years require periodic reauthorization
- Tribal Housing Infrastructure Fund reviewed at Year 10 for continuation
- Native CDFI capitalization is a one-time investment with ongoing operating support
- Comprehensive housing needs assessment every 5 years provides data for legislative adjustment

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### NAHASDA Reauthorization and Housing Investment Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Authorization vs. appropriation gap | Authorization of $2.9 billion does not guarantee appropriation at that level | High |
| Urban set-aside competition with reservation needs | 10% urban set-aside reduces already insufficient reservation funding | Medium |
| Expanded eligible activities dilution | Tribes may use housing funds for infrastructure or homelessness at the expense of new construction | Medium |
| Simplified reporting exploitation | Reduced reporting could enable mismanagement at small TDHEs | Low |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No mandatory appropriations | Funding remains subject to annual appropriations process | Budget rules and political dynamics |
| Formula allocation may not reflect updated need | IHBG formula uses Census data that undercounts tribal populations | Census methodology limitations |
| No enforcement mechanism for phased funding increases | Later-year authorization levels are aspirational | Congressional appropriations discretion |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Pursue mandatory appropriations through budget reconciliation if discretionary path fails
2. Require HUD to update formula factors within 2 years using comprehensive needs assessment data
3. Establish a congressional reporting requirement if appropriations fall below 75% of authorized levels
4. Include maintenance of effort provision preventing reduction below current funding levels

### Trust Land Housing Finance Reform Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Deemed-approval bypass | BIA could declare applications "incomplete" to avoid 90-day timeline | High |
| Standardized mortgage product delayed | Interagency product development could take years without enforcement | Medium |
| HEARTH expansion limited by tribal adoption | Tribes must voluntarily adopt HEARTH authority; many small tribes lack capacity | Medium |
| Lender participation voluntary | Cannot compel lenders to originate trust land mortgages | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address fractionated land | Housing on fractionated allotments still requires consolidating ownership interests | Separate legislation needed (Land Rights) |
| Appraisal challenges unresolved | Limited comparable sales data on reservations continues to undervalue trust land homes | Market structure on reservations |
| 90-day timeline may be insufficient for complex title situations | Some title issues genuinely require more than 90 days | Title complexity variation |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Define "complete application" by regulation with a maximum 30-day completeness review period
2. Establish interagency task force with statutory deadline for standardized product development
3. Provide technical assistance grants for tribal HEARTH adoption
4. Create lender incentives (CRA credit, reduced fees) for trust land origination
5. Allow 30-day extensions for documented complex title situations with tribal consent

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| BIA staffing insufficient for reform implementation | Mandatory appropriation for 200 additional realty specialists |
| Interagency coordination historically weak | Statutory lead agency designation (HUD) with binding MOUs |
| TDHE capacity varies enormously | Phased implementation with capacity building preceding or accompanying funding increases |
| Political vulnerability to administration changes | Codify reforms in statute; seek mandatory appropriations; build bipartisan support |
| Data gaps undermine evidence-based policy | Mandatory comprehensive needs assessment every 5 years |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- All programs subject to GAO performance review every 3 years
- Comprehensive needs assessment every 5 years provides basis for legislative adjustment
- NAHASDA authorization periods require periodic reauthorization
- Infrastructure fund reviewed at Year 10 for continuation or modification
- CDFI operating support reviewed at Year 10
