# Tribal Housing: Stakeholders

## Overview

Tribal housing involves a complex network of stakeholders with diverse -- and often conflicting -- interests. Tribal nations and their citizens are the primary stakeholders, but federal agencies, state and local governments, lending institutions, construction industries, advocacy organizations, and housing policy experts all play significant roles in shaping housing outcomes in Indian Country. The stakeholder landscape is further complicated by the unique legal status of trust land and the fragmented federal bureaucracy responsible for tribal housing programs.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Tribal Nations and Citizens

| Stakeholder | Interest | Power Level | Position |
|-------------|----------|-------------|----------|
| 574 federally recognized tribes | Adequate housing for citizens; sovereignty over housing decisions | Medium (sovereign but dependent on federal funding) | Strongly favor increased NAHASDA funding and reduced federal barriers |
| Tribal citizens on reservations | Safe, affordable housing; homeownership opportunity | Low (dependent on TDHE waitlists and federal programs) | Desperate for housing; average waitlist of 3-5 years |
| Urban Native Americans | Housing access and affordability in cities | Very Low (often excluded from tribal housing programs) | Seek inclusion in NAHASDA-funded programs and urban housing support |
| Tribal elders | Accessible, climate-appropriate housing; aging-in-place | Low | Disproportionately affected by substandard conditions |
| Native youth and families | Adequate space; safe conditions for children | Low | Most affected by overcrowding and hidden homelessness |

### Tribally Designated Housing Entities (TDHEs)

| Stakeholder | Interest | Power Level | Position |
|-------------|----------|-------------|----------|
| Large TDHEs (Navajo, Cherokee, Choctaw) | Increased funding; reduced administrative burden; construction capacity | Medium-High | Advocate for NAHASDA reauthorization at higher funding levels |
| Small TDHEs (fewer than 50 units) | Adequate base funding; technical assistance; capacity building | Low | Struggle to administer programs with grants under $200,000 |
| National American Indian Housing Council (NAIHC) | National advocacy for tribal housing funding and policy | Medium | Leading voice for NAHASDA reauthorization and increased appropriations |
| TDHE staff and administrators | Professional development; competitive compensation; manageable workload | Low | 62% report difficulty hiring qualified staff |

### Federal Government

| Stakeholder | Interest | Power Level | Position |
|-------------|----------|-------------|----------|
| HUD Office of Native American Programs (ONAP) | NAHASDA administration; Section 184 program management | High | Supportive of tribal housing programs; constrained by appropriations |
| Bureau of Indian Affairs | Trust land management; lease approvals; Title Status Reports | High | Institutional processes create bottlenecks for housing development |
| Indian Health Service | Sanitation facilities construction (water/sewer) | Medium | Critical infrastructure role; severely underfunded ($196M vs. $500M need) |
| Department of the Interior | Trust responsibility oversight; land-into-trust decisions | High | Generally supportive; varies by administration |
| USDA Rural Housing Service | Rural housing programs with tribal set-asides | Medium | Limited engagement with tribal-specific needs |
| Department of Energy | Weatherization; tribal energy programs | Low-Medium | Growing role in energy efficiency and electrification |
| Congress | Appropriations; NAHASDA reauthorization; authorizing legislation | Very High | NAHASDA reauthorization overdue since FY 2007; bipartisan support but no action |

### State and Local Governments

| Stakeholder | Interest | Power Level | Position |
|-------------|----------|-------------|----------|
| State housing finance agencies | Housing policy coordination; LIHTC allocation | Medium | Limited engagement with tribal housing; LIHTC underutilized in Indian Country |
| County governments near reservations | Infrastructure coordination; service delivery | Low-Medium | Mixed; some cooperate on infrastructure, others resist tribal development |
| State governors | Federal-state-tribal coordination | Medium | Varied by state; some actively support tribal housing, others are indifferent |

## Financial Stakeholders

### Lenders and Financial Institutions

| Stakeholder | Interest | Power Level | Position |
|-------------|----------|-------------|----------|
| HUD Section 184 participating lenders | Loan origination with federal guarantee | Medium | Willing to lend with guarantee; reluctant on trust land |
| Native CDFIs (69 institutions) | Community lending; credit building; homeownership support | Low-Medium | Critical but undercapitalized; serve as primary financial access point |
| Commercial banks | Mortgage lending; construction financing | High (through absence) | Largely absent from reservation lending due to trust land barriers |
| Federal Home Loan Banks | Community investment; affordable housing programs | Medium | Increasing engagement through tribal set-asides |
| Fannie Mae / Freddie Mac | Secondary mortgage market | High (through absence) | Limited involvement in trust land mortgages; some pilot programs |

### Construction and Development

| Stakeholder | Interest | Power Level | Position |
|-------------|----------|-------------|----------|
| Tribal construction companies | Employment; economic development; cultural design | Low-Medium | Limited capacity; need training and bonding support |
| National and regional builders | Construction contracts on reservations | Low (limited interest) | Deterred by remoteness, trust land complexity, and Davis-Bacon requirements |

## Coalition Dynamics

### Pro-Funding Coalition

| Coalition Member | Common Interest | Potential Friction |
|-----------------|----------------|-------------------|
| TDHEs and NAIHC | Increased NAHASDA funding | May disagree on formula allocation priorities |
| Tribal governments | Housing for citizens | May prioritize other needs over housing |
| Liberal and progressive legislators | Social justice; treaty obligations | Tribal housing competes with other housing priorities |
| Affordable housing advocacy groups | Increased housing spending nationally | Tribal housing is a small share of national advocacy |
| Native CDFIs | Homeownership and credit access | May prefer direct lending support over block grants |

### Trust Land Reform Coalition

| Coalition Member | Common Interest | Internal Tensions |
|-----------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Tribal governments | Reduced barriers to building on trust land | May disagree on approaches to trust reform |
| HUD/ONAP | Streamlined Section 184 processing | Institutional inertia in BIA processes |
| Lenders | Clearer legal framework for trust land mortgages | Want maximum security; may push for reforms that weaken trust protections |
| Libertarian and conservative allies | Reduced federal bureaucracy | May prefer fee simple conversion over trust reform |
| Self-governance advocates | Tribal authority over housing approvals | May resist any continued federal role |

## Power Analysis

### Who Has the Most Power

1. **Congress**: Controls NAHASDA appropriations and reauthorization; plenary power over Indian affairs
2. **HUD Secretary**: Administers NAHASDA; sets regulatory policy for tribal housing programs
3. **BIA**: Controls trust land approval processes that determine where housing can be built
4. **OMB**: Budget recommendations shape the funding landscape for all tribal programs

### Who Has the Least Power

1. **Tribal citizens on waitlists**: No individual voice in funding decisions; average 3-5 year wait
2. **Urban Native Americans**: Largely excluded from reservation-based housing programs
3. **Small TDHEs**: Insufficient funding to build even one house per year; limited advocacy capacity
4. **Tribal construction workers**: Small workforce with limited training infrastructure

### Power Imbalances

The fundamental power imbalance in tribal housing is that the federal government -- which created the housing crisis through centuries of land dispossession, forced relocation, inappropriate housing programs, and chronic underfunding -- also controls the resources needed to resolve it. Tribes depend on the same Congress that has underfunded NAHASDA since its enactment to appropriate the funds needed to close the housing gap. Meanwhile, the trust land system that protects tribal sovereignty simultaneously creates barriers to housing finance that only federal action can resolve. This structural dependency means that tribal housing outcomes are determined primarily by federal political will rather than by tribal decision-making.
