# Tribal Housing: Solutions

## Overview

Addressing the tribal housing crisis requires a comprehensive approach that dramatically increases federal funding, removes trust land barriers to housing finance, invests in supporting infrastructure, builds tribal housing authority capacity, and supports innovative construction and design approaches. These solutions draw on successful tribal programs, international models, and the demonstrated results of recent one-time federal investments.

## Solution 1: NAHASDA Reauthorization and Funding Increase

### Description

Reauthorize and significantly expand the Native American Housing Assistance and Self-Determination Act, increasing the Indian Housing Block Grant from $772 million to $2 billion annually, with a 10-year escalation path to $2.9 billion. Include expanded eligible activities, streamlined reporting, improved data collection, and a dedicated set-aside for urban Native housing.

### Evidence Base

- NAIHC estimates an annual need of $2.9 billion based on housing shortages, overcrowding, and substandard conditions (NAIHC Congressional Testimony, 2023)
- One-time CARES Act ($1.1 billion) and American Rescue Plan ($750 million) investments demonstrated that tribes can effectively deploy significantly higher funding levels
- In inflation-adjusted terms, NAHASDA funding has declined approximately 32% since 1998
- The U.S. Commission on Civil Rights found in 2018 that federal funding for Native Americans is "grossly inadequate" (*Broken Promises*, 2018)

### Cost Estimate

- **Year 1-3**: $1.5 billion/year (phased increase from $772 million)
- **Year 4-7**: $2.0 billion/year
- **Year 8-10**: $2.5 billion/year
- **Year 10+**: $2.9 billion/year (full estimated need)
- **10-year total**: Approximately $20 billion (vs. ~$7.7 billion at current levels)

### Implementation Timeline

- **Year 1**: Enact NAHASDA reauthorization with increased authorization levels
- **Years 1-3**: Phase in increased appropriations; expand eligible activities
- **Years 4-7**: Reach $2 billion annual funding; assess impact on housing shortage
- **Years 8-10**: Adjust funding based on demonstrated progress and updated needs assessment

### Key Features

- Phased funding increases to allow TDHE capacity to grow alongside resources
- Urban Native housing set-aside (10% of total) addressing off-reservation needs
- Expanded eligible activities including infrastructure directly supporting housing
- Streamlined Indian Housing Plan and Annual Performance Report requirements for small TDHEs
- Comprehensive tribal housing needs assessment every 5 years (replacing outdated 2017 study)

## Solution 2: Trust Land Mortgage Reform

### Description

Reform the trust land mortgage process to make homeownership on trust land as accessible as homeownership on fee land, through streamlined BIA approvals, expanded Section 184 processing, standardized trust land mortgage products, and tribal mortgage processing authority.

### Evidence Base

- The Section 184 program has a default rate under 2%, demonstrating that Native borrowers are excellent credit risks (HUD ONAP, 2024)
- Despite this, the majority of Section 184 loans are on fee land because trust land processing remains prohibitively complex
- BIA Title Status Reports take 6-12 months; residential lease approvals can take years
- The HEARTH Act model demonstrates that tribal processing authority dramatically reduces approval times

### Cost Estimate

- **BIA realty specialist hiring**: $30 million/year for 200 additional staff focused on housing
- **Section 184 program expansion**: $10 million/year for outreach, lender recruitment, and processing
- **Tribal mortgage processing grants**: $25 million/year for tribes to assume mortgage-related approval functions
- **IT modernization for trust land records**: $20 million (one-time)
- **Total annual cost**: $65 million; one-time cost: $20 million

### Implementation Timeline

- **Year 1**: Hire BIA realty specialists; begin IT modernization
- **Year 2**: Implement 90-day processing target for residential leases and Title Status Reports
- **Years 2-3**: Pilot tribal mortgage processing authority at 10-15 tribes
- **Years 3-5**: Expand tribal processing authority; achieve 90-day standard

### Key Features

- Statutory 90-day processing timeline for residential lease approvals and Title Status Reports
- Tribal HEARTH-style authority for residential leasing without BIA approval
- Standardized trust land mortgage product eligible for secondary market purchase
- Native CDFI capitalization for trust land mortgage origination
- Lender education program to increase Section 184 participation

## Solution 3: Tribal Housing Infrastructure Fund

### Description

Establish a dedicated Tribal Housing Infrastructure Fund to finance water, sewer, electricity, and road infrastructure needed to support new housing construction, consolidating fragmented infrastructure funding into a single tribal-directed program.

### Evidence Base

- Approximately 189,000 tribal homes lack safe water access; 135,000 lack adequate sanitation (IHS SDS, 2023)
- IHS Sanitation Facilities Construction backlog exceeds $3.7 billion
- Infrastructure costs add 100-300% to per-unit housing construction costs on reservations
- The Bipartisan Infrastructure Law provided significant but one-time tribal infrastructure funding that does not address ongoing annual needs

### Cost Estimate

- **Annual appropriation**: $750 million (covering water, sewer, electricity, and roads for housing)
- **10-year total**: $7.5 billion
- **Cost offset**: Consolidating existing fragmented programs would reduce administrative overhead by an estimated $50 million annually

### Implementation Timeline

- **Years 1-2**: Establish fund; develop tribal infrastructure plans coordinated with housing plans
- **Years 3-5**: Intensive infrastructure construction in highest-need areas
- **Years 5-10**: Continued buildout; transition to maintenance and replacement funding
- **Ongoing**: Annual appropriation adjusted based on needs assessment

### Key Features

- Single application process replacing separate IHS, BIA, EPA, and DOE applications
- Tribal infrastructure planning integrated with Indian Housing Plans
- Priority for infrastructure that supports new housing construction
- Tribal authority to determine infrastructure investment priorities
- Maintenance and operations funding included (not just construction)

## Solution 4: Tribal Housing Authority Capacity Building Program

### Description

Establish a comprehensive capacity-building program for TDHEs, including training, technical assistance, regional cooperation, competitive compensation support, and shared services for small TDHEs.

### Evidence Base

- 62% of TDHEs report difficulty hiring qualified staff (NAIHC Survey, 2023)
- Average IHBG grant for small tribes is under $200,000, insufficient for meaningful housing production
- Successful TDHEs (Navajo Housing Authority, Cherokee Nation) demonstrate that capacity investment produces results
- Regional cooperation models (tribal housing cooperatives) have shown promise in reducing administrative costs

### Cost Estimate

- **Training and technical assistance**: $30 million/year through NAIHC and partner organizations
- **Competitive compensation grants**: $40 million/year to help TDHEs offer market-rate salaries
- **Shared services program**: $20 million/year for regional TDHE cooperatives
- **Construction management training**: $15 million/year for tribal workforce development
- **Total annual cost**: $105 million

### Implementation Timeline

- **Year 1**: Expand NAIHC training programs; launch competitive compensation grants
- **Years 2-3**: Establish regional TDHE cooperatives in 6 regions
- **Years 3-5**: Develop tribal construction management workforce pipeline
- **Ongoing**: Continuous training, technical assistance, and compensation support

### Key Features

- National tribal housing training center or partnership with existing institutions
- Regional TDHE cooperatives sharing construction management, accounting, and planning functions
- Competitive compensation grants allowing TDHEs to offer salaries comparable to state housing agencies
- Mentorship program pairing large, successful TDHEs with smaller entities
- Tribal construction apprenticeship programs building local workforce capacity

## Solution 5: Tribal Homeownership and Credit Access Initiative

### Description

Expand homeownership opportunities for Native Americans through Native CDFI capitalization, credit-building programs, down payment assistance, financial literacy education, and reforms to make conventional and FHA lending accessible in Indian Country.

### Evidence Base

- Native American homeownership rate is 51.5% (vs. 65.4% nationally) (Census ACS, 2022)
- An estimated 16.3% of reservation households are unbanked; 20-30% lack credit scores (FDIC, 2021; CFPB, 2022)
- 69 Native CDFIs serve all of Indian Country; most are undercapitalized
- Section 184 loans have an extremely low default rate, demonstrating creditworthiness

### Cost Estimate

- **Native CDFI capitalization**: $200 million (one-time capital infusion)
- **Annual CDFI operating support**: $50 million/year
- **Down payment assistance program**: $75 million/year
- **Credit-building and financial literacy**: $20 million/year
- **Total annual cost**: $145 million; one-time cost: $200 million

### Implementation Timeline

- **Year 1**: Capitalize Native CDFIs; launch credit-building programs
- **Years 2-3**: Implement down payment assistance; expand financial literacy
- **Years 3-5**: Establish Native CDFIs as primary reservation lending institutions
- **Year 5+**: Achieve measurable increase in Native homeownership rate

### Key Features

- $200 million capital infusion to Native CDFIs for mortgage lending capacity
- Individual Development Account (IDA) program for Native homeownership savings
- Down payment assistance of up to $25,000 for first-time Native homebuyers on trust or fee land
- Credit-building programs using alternative credit data (rent, utility payments)
- Financial literacy curriculum developed with tribal cultural context

## Solution 6: Climate-Resilient and Culturally Appropriate Housing Design Initiative

### Description

Fund research, design, and construction of housing specifically designed for the diverse climates, cultures, and conditions of Indian Country, replacing the one-size-fits-all approach that has produced poor housing outcomes for decades.

### Evidence Base

- Historical federal housing programs imposed identical designs across vastly different climates and cultures
- Alaska Native villages face unique challenges from permafrost thaw, extreme cold, and remote access
- Navajo Nation housing must address extreme heat, cold, and water scarcity
- Northern Plains reservations require housing for -40F winters
- Tribal communities have expressed strong interest in housing that reflects cultural values and traditional knowledge

### Cost Estimate

- **Research and design program**: $25 million/year
- **Climate-resilient construction pilots**: $50 million/year (first 5 years)
- **Tribal design authority support**: $10 million/year
- **Total annual cost**: $85 million (declining after Year 5 as designs are standardized)

### Implementation Timeline

- **Years 1-2**: Establish tribal housing design partnerships; begin climate research
- **Years 3-5**: Construct pilot homes in 20+ tribal communities across climate zones
- **Years 5-7**: Refine designs based on performance data; develop model plans
- **Ongoing**: Integrate climate-resilient designs into standard TDHE construction

### Key Features

- Tribal-led design process incorporating cultural values and traditional construction knowledge
- Climate-specific designs for Arctic, subarctic, desert, plains, and coastal environments
- Energy-efficient and renewable-energy-integrated designs reducing long-term utility costs
- Modular and prefabricated construction methods addressing remote delivery challenges
- Building performance monitoring to evaluate and improve designs over time

## Solution 7: Native American Homelessness Prevention and Response

### Description

Establish dedicated programs to prevent and address Native American homelessness, both on reservations ("hidden homelessness" through doubling up) and in urban areas where Native Americans are disproportionately represented among homeless populations.

### Evidence Base

- Native Americans are 5% of the homeless population but 2.9% of the general population (HUD AHAR, 2023)
- Approximately 32,000 Native Americans are homeless on any given night (HUD PIT Count, 2023)
- "Hidden homelessness" on reservations -- families doubling up due to housing shortages -- is pervasive but uncounted
- 11% of Native youth experience homelessness (Voices of Youth Count, 2017)
- Native Americans have the highest unsheltered rate of any racial group

### Cost Estimate

- **On-reservation homelessness prevention**: $50 million/year
- **Urban Native homelessness services**: $75 million/year
- **Data collection and needs assessment**: $10 million/year
- **Youth homelessness programs**: $30 million/year
- **Total annual cost**: $165 million

### Implementation Timeline

- **Year 1**: Establish baseline data on Native homelessness (reservation and urban)
- **Years 1-3**: Fund tribal and urban Indian organization homelessness programs
- **Years 3-5**: Scale effective programs; integrate with mainstream homelessness systems
- **Ongoing**: Annual data collection and program evaluation

### Key Features

- Reservation-specific homelessness definition that includes doubling up and hidden homelessness
- Dedicated HUD funding stream for tribal and urban Indian homelessness programs
- Culturally appropriate services addressing the unique factors in Native homelessness
- Coordination between tribal housing programs and urban homeless services
- Youth-focused prevention programs addressing the 11% Native youth homelessness rate

## International Models

### Canada: First Nations Housing Programs

- **Mechanism**: First Nations Market Housing Fund provides loan guarantees for on-reserve mortgages
- **Scale**: CAD $300 million fund; enabled home construction and renovation on reserves
- **Lessons**: Demonstrates that trust-like land tenure barriers can be overcome through guarantees and tribal authority; capacity building is essential

### New Zealand: Maori Housing

- **Mechanism**: Te Puni Kokiri (Ministry of Maori Development) funds housing programs on Maori communal land
- **Scale**: NZ$400 million Maori housing investment over 5 years
- **Lessons**: Direct government investment in Indigenous housing on communal land is feasible; community-led design produces better outcomes

### Australia: Aboriginal Housing Programs

- **Mechanism**: National Partnership Agreement on Remote Indigenous Housing (NPARIH)
- **Scale**: AUD $5.5 billion over 10 years for remote Indigenous housing
- **Lessons**: Large-scale investment can reduce overcrowding (Australia achieved a 20% reduction in severe overcrowding in targeted communities); sustained funding is critical; community engagement in design improves outcomes

## Solution Sequencing

### Priority Order

1. **NAHASDA reauthorization and funding increase** (immediate -- establishes the funding foundation for all other reforms)
2. **Trust land mortgage reform** (immediate -- removes barriers blocking housing construction)
3. **Tribal housing infrastructure fund** (concurrent -- infrastructure is prerequisite for housing)
4. **TDHE capacity building** (concurrent -- enables effective use of increased funding)
5. **Homeownership and credit access** (Years 2-3 -- builds on trust land and CDFI reforms)
6. **Climate-resilient design initiative** (Years 2-5 -- improves quality of new construction)
7. **Homelessness prevention and response** (immediate for data collection; Years 2-5 for full programs)

### Dependencies

- Funding increase (Solution 1) is prerequisite for all other solutions
- Infrastructure investment (Solution 3) is prerequisite for new housing construction
- Trust land reform (Solution 2) is prerequisite for expanded homeownership (Solution 5)
- Capacity building (Solution 4) is necessary for effective use of increased funding
- Climate-resilient design (Solution 6) improves the quality of housing built under all other solutions
- Homelessness response (Solution 7) can proceed independently but benefits from all other solutions
