# Tribal Housing: Overview

## Executive Summary

Housing in Indian Country is in a state of severe crisis. The National American Indian Housing Council (NAIHC) estimates a shortage of at least 68,000 housing units across tribal communities, with some estimates placing the need as high as 200,000 units when substandard conditions are fully accounted for. Overcrowding rates on reservations are more than eight times the national average. Tens of thousands of homes lack adequate plumbing, electricity, or heating. Homelessness among Native Americans is disproportionately high and largely invisible in federal data systems.

The roots of this crisis lie in centuries of federal policy -- from forced removal and allotment that destroyed tribal land bases, to termination that eliminated housing support, to the current system of chronic underfunding and regulatory barriers that prevent tribes from building the homes their citizens need. The Native American Housing Assistance and Self-Determination Act (NAHASDA) of 1996 consolidated federal housing programs for tribes into a single block grant administered by the Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD), giving tribes greater flexibility. However, NAHASDA funding has remained essentially flat since its enactment, failing to keep pace with inflation, population growth, or the scale of need.

The tribal housing crisis is compounded by unique obstacles: trust land restrictions that make mortgage financing nearly impossible, infrastructure deficits that make construction prohibitively expensive, geographic remoteness that increases material and labor costs, and jurisdictional complexity that deters private investment. These barriers interact with broader socioeconomic challenges -- poverty rates nearly triple the national average, unemployment well above national rates, and limited access to banking and financial services -- to create a housing environment fundamentally different from any other in the United States.

Addressing tribal housing requires a comprehensive strategy: dramatically increased NAHASDA funding, reform of trust land barriers to mortgage financing, massive infrastructure investment, support for tribal housing authority capacity, and innovative approaches to construction in remote and extreme-climate environments.

## Scope

### What This Subtopic Covers

- **Federal housing programs**: NAHASDA, Indian Housing Block Grant (IHBG), HUD programs
- **Housing conditions**: Overcrowding, substandard housing, lack of utilities, health impacts

## Core Tensions

- **Trust land vs. financing**: Trust land status prevents conventional mortgages, but trust status is essential for tribal sovereignty and land protection
- **Self-determination vs. federal dependency**: Tribes want housing self-determination but remain dependent on chronically underfunded federal programs
- **Remote locations vs. construction costs**: Preserving communities on traditional lands means building in areas where construction is extraordinarily expensive
- **Individual homeownership vs. communal land**: Western homeownership models conflict with tribal communal land tenure systems
- **Urban vs. reservation needs**: Growing urban Native populations need housing support, but NAHASDA primarily serves reservation communities
- **Infrastructure vs. housing**: Housing cannot be built without water, sewer, and electricity, but infrastructure funding is a separate (also underfunded) stream

## Vision of Success

A system that adequately addresses tribal housing would feature:

- Sufficient housing stock to eliminate overcrowding and the waitlist backlog
- All tribal homes meeting modern building codes with complete plumbing, electricity, heating, and kitchen facilities
- Accessible mortgage financing on trust land through reformed programs and tribal lending institutions
- Adequate water, sewer, electricity, and road infrastructure supporting all housing areas
- Well-funded, capacity-rich tribal housing authorities capable of planning, building, and maintaining housing
- Homeownership rates approaching the national average where desired by tribal citizens
- Tribal-designed homes that reflect cultural values and climate-appropriate construction
- Effective programs addressing Native American homelessness both on and off reservations
- Housing stock that supports healthy living and reduces the disease burden attributable to housing conditions
