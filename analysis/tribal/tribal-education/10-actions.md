# Tribal Education: Actions

## Overview

Citizens can play a meaningful role in advancing tribal education reform through civic engagement, advocacy, direct support for educational organizations, and personal education about Indigenous history and contemporary issues. This document provides specific, actionable steps for individuals and communities.

## Individual Actions

### Educate Yourself

1. **Learn about the boarding school era**: Read the DOI Federal Indian Boarding School Initiative Report (doi.gov/boarding-school-initiative) to understand the scope and impact of federal boarding school policies
2. **Understand treaty education obligations**: Many treaties include specific education commitments that remain legally binding; learn which treaties apply to the tribal nations in your area
3. **Explore tribal college resources**: Visit aihec.org to learn about the 37 tribal colleges and their programs
4. **Follow Indigenous education media**: Read Indian Country Today (indiancountrytoday.com), NIEA publications, and tribal education department reports

### Contact Elected Officials

Federal legislation is essential for tribal education reform. Contact your U.S. senators and representative about:

- **BIE mandatory funding**: Urge transition from discretionary to mandatory appropriations for BIE schools
- **Tribal college parity**: Support full authorized funding for TCUs under the Tribally Controlled Colleges Act
- **Language revitalization**: Advocate for increased funding for the Esther Martinez program and new language immersion initiatives
- **Boarding school reconciliation**: Support completion of the DOI investigation and establishment of a permanent reconciliation commission
- **BIE facility construction**: Urge dedicated funding to eliminate the $3.4 billion deferred maintenance backlog

### Template Email for Federal Legislators

```text
Subject: Support Tribal Education Reform

Dear [Senator/Representative] [Last Name],

I am writing to urge your support for tribal education reform,
specifically:

1. Mandatory Funding for BIE Schools: The Bureau of Indian
Education serves 46,000 students at 183 schools, yet 43% of
those schools are rated in "poor" condition with a $3.4 billion
deferred maintenance backlog. Transitioning BIE funding from
discretionary to mandatory appropriations would fulfill treaty-
based education obligations and provide the stability these
students deserve.

2. Tribal College Parity: The 37 tribal colleges and universities
receive only 60% of their authorized federal funding. Full funding
at parity with comparable institutions would expand higher
education access for approximately 30,000 students and strengthen
workforce development in tribal communities.

3. Language Revitalization: 71% of the 245 Indigenous languages
spoken in the United States are endangered. Increased funding for
immersion schools and language programs is urgent because elder
speakers are aging and language loss is irreversible.

4. Boarding School Reconciliation: The federal government operated
over 400 Indian boarding schools. Completing the DOI investigation
and establishing a reconciliation commission is essential for
addressing intergenerational trauma that directly affects current
educational outcomes.

These reforms honor treaty obligations, support tribal self-
determination, and would improve outcomes for the 644,000 AI/AN
students in American schools.

Thank you for your consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your name]
[Your address]
```

## Community Actions

### At the Local Level

- **Advocate for accurate Indigenous history in local school curricula**: Contact your school board and ask about Indigenous content in history, social studies, and language arts
- **Support Land Acknowledgments at schools**: Encourage your school district to adopt Land Acknowledgment statements that recognize Indigenous peoples
- **Invite tribal speakers to schools**: Partner with local tribal education departments to bring Indigenous speakers, knowledge keepers, and artists into classrooms
- **Support tribal charter schools and immersion programs**: Advocate for state policies that allow tribal charter schools and language immersion programs

### At the State Level

- **Advocate for Indian Education for All**: Montana's 1999 law requiring Indigenous content across all subjects and grade levels is a model that other states should adopt
- **Support state funding for AI/AN student programs**: Urge state legislators to supplement federal funding with state resources for AI/AN students in public schools
- **Support tribal college partnerships**: Advocate for state university partnerships with TCUs for program articulation, credit transfer, and resource sharing
- **Monitor Johnson-O'Malley fund use**: Ensure that JOM funds intended for AI/AN students are actually reaching those students

## Talking Points

### For Conversations with Neighbors and Community Members

1. **Education is a treaty obligation**: The federal government promised to provide education to tribal nations in exchange for land cessions totaling over 1.5 billion acres. These are legal debts, not optional spending.

2. **BIE schools are crumbling**: 43% of Bureau of Indian Education schools are rated in "poor" condition, with a $3.4 billion deferred maintenance backlog. No student should attend school in a building with structural deficiencies, inadequate heating, or lead contamination.

3. **The achievement gap is a policy choice**: AI/AN students graduate at the lowest rate of any demographic group (74% vs. 87% national average). This gap reflects chronic underfunding and institutional neglect, not student or community capability.

4. **Languages are dying**: 71% of the 245 Indigenous languages spoken in the U.S. are endangered. When a language dies, an irreplaceable knowledge system -- including ecological knowledge, governance traditions, and cultural wisdom accumulated over thousands of years -- dies with it.

5. **Tribal colleges work**: TCUs provide culturally relevant higher education that produces graduates with employment rates and incomes significantly above community averages. They do this with 60% of their authorized funding.

6. **The boarding school era is not ancient history**: The federal government operated over 400 boarding schools that forcibly removed children from their families, punished them for speaking their languages, and subjected many to abuse. The last federally operated boarding school closed in the 1960s. Survivors are alive today, and the trauma persists across generations.

### For Conversations with Skeptics

- **"Spending is already higher than public schools"**: BIE per-pupil figures include transportation, boarding, and facility costs that public schools fund separately. True instructional spending is below the national average, and the $3.4 billion facility backlog speaks for itself.

- **"Why not just send them to public schools?"**: 93% of AI/AN students already attend public schools, where they also face achievement gaps. Many BIE school communities have no nearby public school. And public schools have their own history of failing AI/AN students through cultural erasure and discrimination.

- **"Language immersion hurts academic performance"**: Research consistently shows that immersion students perform at or above grade level in English by middle school. The Hawaiian language immersion program has produced students who score above state averages.

- **"The boarding school era was a long time ago"**: The last federally operated boarding school closed in the 1960s. Documented intergenerational trauma from boarding schools directly affects current students' educational outcomes. You cannot fix current education without addressing this history.

## Professional Actions

### For Educators

- Complete cultural competency training for working with AI/AN students
- Incorporate accurate Indigenous history and contemporary issues into curricula
- Partner with tribal education departments for curriculum resources
- Consider teaching in Indian Country (contact Teach For America or BIE recruitment)
- Use resources from the National Museum of the American Indian and Smithsonian

### For Attorneys

- Volunteer with NARF or tribal legal departments on education rights cases
- Provide pro bono representation in education funding disputes
- Research treaty education obligations in your jurisdiction
- File amicus briefs supporting tribal education rights

### For Healthcare Professionals

- Support school-based mental health services in BIE schools
- Learn about trauma-informed approaches for AI/AN youth
- Partner with tribal health departments on youth wellness programs
- Advocate for integration of traditional healing practices in school counseling

### For Business Leaders

- Support tribal college workforce development programs
- Create internship and employment pipelines from TCUs
- Donate technology and resources to BIE schools
- Sponsor scholarships through the American Indian College Fund
