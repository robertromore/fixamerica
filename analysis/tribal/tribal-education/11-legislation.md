# Tribal Education: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative approach to tribal education reform addresses five critical areas: (1) mandatory funding to fulfill the federal education trust obligation, (2) tribal education sovereignty to empower tribal control over schools, (3) Indigenous language revitalization to prevent irreversible language loss, (4) tribal college parity to close the funding gap, and (5) boarding school reconciliation to address historical trauma. This document provides draft legislation, regulatory frameworks, and legal analysis for each area.

## Federal Legislation

### The Tribal Education Full Funding Act

**Purpose**: Transition BIE education funding from discretionary to mandatory appropriations at levels that fully meet treaty obligations and demonstrated need, with dedicated capital funding for facility construction and renovation.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Tribal Education Full Funding Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
(a) the United States has a trust responsibility and treaty-
based obligation to provide education to American Indian and
Alaska Native students;
(b) the Bureau of Indian Education serves approximately 46,000
students at 183 schools on 64 reservations;
(c) 43 percent of BIE school facilities are rated in "poor"
condition, with a deferred maintenance backlog of $3.4 billion;
(d) BIE graduation rates of 53-58 percent are the lowest of any
school system in the United States;
(e) discretionary appropriations have failed to meet the
federal education trust obligation for over 150 years;
(f) mandatory appropriations are necessary to provide the
funding stability and adequacy that treaty obligations require.

SEC. 3. MANDATORY APPROPRIATIONS FOR BIE OPERATIONS.

(a) IN GENERAL.—There are appropriated, out of any money in the
Treasury not otherwise appropriated, for the Bureau of Indian
Education—
    (1) for the Indian School Equalization Program, $800,000,000
    for fiscal year 2027, adjusted annually by the Consumer
    Price Index;
    (2) for student transportation, $100,000,000 for fiscal year
    2027, adjusted annually;
    (3) for facilities operations and maintenance, $200,000,000
    for fiscal year 2027, adjusted annually;
    (4) for administrative cost grants, $100,000,000 for fiscal
    year 2027, adjusted annually;
    (5) for tribal grant support costs, $120,000,000 for fiscal
    year 2027, adjusted annually.

(b) FORMULA.—The Secretary of the Interior shall distribute
funds under paragraph (a)(1) using a weighted student formula
that accounts for—
    (1) student enrollment;
    (2) geographic isolation and transportation costs;
    (3) special education needs;
    (4) English language learner status;
    (5) poverty concentration.

SEC. 4. BIE SCHOOL CONSTRUCTION AND RENOVATION FUND.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established in the Treasury a fund
to be known as the "BIE School Construction and Renovation Fund".

(b) APPROPRIATION.—There is appropriated to the Fund
$500,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2037.

(c) USE OF FUNDS.—Amounts in the Fund shall be available for—
    (1) replacement of schools rated in "poor" condition;
    (2) major renovation of existing facilities;
    (3) broadband and technology infrastructure;
    (4) teacher housing construction;
    (5) energy efficiency improvements.

(d) PRIORITIZATION.—The Secretary shall prioritize projects
based on facility condition assessments, with the worst-
condition schools receiving first priority.

SEC. 5. REPORTING.

The Secretary shall submit annual reports to Congress on
expenditures, facility conditions, student outcomes, and teacher
workforce metrics.

SEC. 6. SUNSET AND REVIEW.

The construction fund under Section 4 shall be reviewed by
Congress in fiscal year 2035. If the Secretary certifies that
the deferred maintenance backlog has been eliminated, the
appropriation may be reduced to $200,000,000 annually for
ongoing maintenance.
```

**Explanation**: This bill transitions BIE education funding from discretionary to mandatory appropriations, ensuring stable, adequate funding regardless of annual budget politics. The weighted student formula distributes operational funds based on need. The dedicated construction fund addresses the $3.4 billion deferred maintenance backlog with $500 million annually for 10 years, plus $200 million annually thereafter for ongoing maintenance.

**Potential Challenges**: Mandatory appropriations face strong opposition from budget hawks and the appropriations committees, which lose control over spending. The $1.5+ billion total annual cost will face deficit concerns.

**Refinements**: If mandatory funding is politically infeasible, advance appropriations (as achieved for IHS in 2024) would provide a partial solution. A two-year pilot at reduced levels could demonstrate effectiveness before full mandatory implementation.

### The Tribal Education Sovereignty Act

**Purpose**: Empower tribal nations to exercise comprehensive authority over educational programs, curricula, staffing, and standards through education sovereignty compacts.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Tribal Education Sovereignty Act".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

As used in this Act—
(a) "Education sovereignty compact" means an agreement between
the Secretary of the Interior and a federally recognized Indian
tribe under which the tribe assumes authority over educational
programs within its jurisdiction.
(b) "Tribal Education Agency" means an agency established by a
tribe to manage educational programs under an education
sovereignty compact.

SEC. 3. EDUCATION SOVEREIGNTY COMPACTS.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—Any federally recognized Indian tribe may
enter into an education sovereignty compact with the Secretary
to assume full authority over educational programs for students
within its jurisdiction.

(b) SCOPE.—An education sovereignty compact may include
authority to—
    (1) establish and operate schools;
    (2) set academic standards, curricula, and graduation
    requirements;
    (3) hire, evaluate, and compensate teachers and
    administrators;
    (4) develop culturally responsive assessment systems;
    (5) manage school facilities;
    (6) administer federal education funds;
    (7) establish language immersion programs;
    (8) develop and implement culturally based curricula.

(c) FUNDING.—A tribe operating under an education sovereignty
compact shall receive no less than the amount it would have
received under BIE formula funding, plus a tribal sovereignty
supplement of 10 percent to support tribal education
administration.

(d) ACCREDITATION.—Schools operated under education sovereignty
compacts shall be eligible for accreditation by regional
accrediting bodies or by a tribal accreditation system
approved by the Secretary.

SEC. 4. TRIBAL EDUCATION AGENCY CERTIFICATION.

(a) A tribe may establish a Tribal Education Agency (TEA) to
serve the functions of a state education agency for schools
within its jurisdiction.

(b) A certified TEA shall have authority to—
    (1) set educational standards;
    (2) certify teachers;
    (3) approve curricula;
    (4) collect and report educational data;
    (5) receive and distribute federal education funds.

SEC. 5. PUBLIC SCHOOL TRIBAL CONSULTATION.

(a) Any public school district in which AI/AN students
constitute 10 percent or more of enrollment shall establish
a formal consultation agreement with the relevant tribal
education department.

(b) Such consultation shall address—
    (1) culturally responsive curriculum content;
    (2) AI/AN student support services;
    (3) Johnson-O'Malley fund distribution;
    (4) hiring of AI/AN staff and teachers;
    (5) language and cultural programming.

SEC. 6. JOHNSON-O'MALLEY REFORM.

(a) Funds appropriated under the Johnson-O'Malley Act (25
U.S.C. 5342-5348) shall be distributed directly to tribes or
tribal education departments, not to state education agencies.

(b) Tribes shall report annually on the use of JOM funds and
the outcomes achieved for AI/AN students.

SEC. 7. TRANSITION FUNDING.

There is authorized to be appropriated $75,000,000 for each of
fiscal years 2027 through 2032 for tribal capacity building
under this Act.
```

**Explanation**: This bill creates a comprehensive framework for tribal educational sovereignty, modeled on the self-governance compact approach. Tribes can assume all BIE functions, establish their own education agencies, set their own standards and curricula, and certify their own teachers. The 10% supplement acknowledges the additional administrative costs of tribal management. The public school consultation provision addresses the 93% of AI/AN students who attend state public schools.

**Potential Challenges**: State education agencies may object to tribal education agencies setting different standards. Accreditation bodies may resist tribal accreditation systems. The 10% sovereignty supplement will face budget scrutiny.

**Refinements**: The public school consultation threshold could be adjusted (higher or lower than 10%). Tribal accreditation could be structured as a cooperative arrangement with existing regional accreditors rather than a separate system.

### The Indigenous Language Revitalization Act

**Purpose**: Establish a comprehensive federal initiative to prevent the extinction of endangered Indigenous languages through immersion schools, language nest programs, teacher training, and technology.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Indigenous Language Revitalization
Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
(a) approximately 245 Indigenous languages are spoken in the
United States, of which 71 percent are endangered;
(b) Indigenous language loss is directly attributable to federal
policies, including the boarding school system that punished
children for speaking their languages;
(c) language revitalization is a matter of cultural survival
for Indigenous nations;
(d) research demonstrates that language immersion programs
produce students who perform at or above grade level in English
while achieving heritage language fluency;
(e) once an Indigenous language becomes extinct, it cannot be
recovered.

SEC. 3. OFFICE OF INDIGENOUS LANGUAGE REVITALIZATION.

(a) There is established within the Bureau of Indian Education
an Office of Indigenous Language Revitalization.

(b) The Office shall—
    (1) administer language revitalization grants;
    (2) provide technical assistance to tribal language
    programs;
    (3) support language documentation and archiving;
    (4) develop language education standards in consultation
    with tribes;
    (5) coordinate with TCU language programs.

SEC. 4. LANGUAGE REVITALIZATION GRANTS.

(a) IMMERSION SCHOOL GRANTS.—The Office shall award grants to
tribes for the establishment and operation of Indigenous
language immersion schools, at $1,000,000-$3,000,000 per school
per year.

(b) LANGUAGE NEST GRANTS.—The Office shall award grants for
early childhood language immersion programs (language nests),
at $250,000-$500,000 per program per year.

(c) MASTER-APPRENTICE GRANTS.—The Office shall fund master-
apprentice programs pairing fluent elder speakers with
apprentice teachers, at $50,000 per pair per year.

(d) LANGUAGE DOCUMENTATION GRANTS.—The Office shall fund
comprehensive language documentation projects for critically
endangered languages, at $200,000-$500,000 per language.

(e) TECHNOLOGY GRANTS.—The Office shall fund development of
language learning applications, online resources, and digital
archives, at $100,000-$300,000 per project.

SEC. 5. ASSESSMENT EXEMPTION.

(a) Students enrolled in Indigenous language immersion programs
shall be exempt from standardized English-language assessments
for the first 3 years of immersion.

(b) After the exemption period, students shall be assessed
using culturally and linguistically appropriate instruments
developed in consultation with the relevant tribe.

SEC. 6. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated to carry out this Act
$200,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2042.
```

**Explanation**: This bill creates a comprehensive language revitalization infrastructure, with diverse program models (immersion schools, language nests, master-apprentice, documentation, technology) to address the range of language situations from critically endangered to vulnerable. The assessment exemption removes the most significant policy barrier to immersion education. The 15-year authorization reflects the long-term nature of language revitalization.

**Potential Challenges**: The assessment exemption will face opposition from standardized testing advocates. $200 million annually is significant but may be insufficient for the scale of the crisis. State education agencies may resist accommodating immersion programs.

**Refinements**: The assessment exemption could be modified to require bilingual assessment (in both the immersion language and English) rather than a full exemption. Funding could be phased, starting at $100 million and scaling up as program capacity develops.

### The Tribal College Parity Act

**Purpose**: Fully fund tribal colleges and universities at authorized levels and invest in facility construction, program expansion, and research capacity.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Tribal College Parity Act".

SEC. 2. FULL FUNDING.

(a) There are appropriated for each fiscal year beginning with
fiscal year 2027 such sums as are necessary to fully fund the
Tribally Controlled Colleges and Universities Assistance Act
(25 U.S.C. 1801 et seq.) at the authorized level per Indian
student full-time equivalent.

(b) The authorized level per Indian student full-time equivalent
shall be adjusted annually by the Higher Education Price Index.

SEC. 3. TCU FACILITY FUND.

(a) There is authorized to be appropriated $40,000,000 for each
of fiscal years 2027 through 2037 for TCU facility construction
and renovation.

(b) Priority shall be given to projects that—
    (1) replace facilities in poor condition;
    (2) expand instructional capacity;
    (3) improve broadband and technology infrastructure;
    (4) support four-year degree program expansion.

SEC. 4. TCU PROGRAM EXPANSION GRANTS.

(a) There is authorized to be appropriated $40,000,000 for each
of fiscal years 2027 through 2037 for grants to TCUs to develop
bachelor's degree programs, professional programs, and research
capacity.

(b) Priority shall be given to programs in—
    (1) education and teacher preparation;
    (2) health sciences;
    (3) Indigenous language and cultural studies;
    (4) natural resource management;
    (5) business and economic development.

SEC. 5. TCU RESEARCH CAPACITY.

(a) There is authorized to be appropriated $30,000,000 annually
for the establishment of TCU Centers of Research Excellence in
areas of tribal priority.

(b) Such centers shall be eligible for all federal research
funding programs on the same basis as other institutions of
higher education.

SEC. 6. FACULTY RECRUITMENT.

There is authorized to be appropriated $25,000,000 annually for
competitive faculty compensation supplements at TCUs.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the TCU funding disparity by mandating full authorized funding, establishing a facility fund, supporting program expansion into bachelor's degrees, and building research capacity. Faculty recruitment supplements address the compensation gap that makes it difficult for TCUs to attract and retain qualified faculty.

**Potential Challenges**: Full mandatory funding will face budget process opposition. The Higher Education Price Index adjustment may be contested as too generous. Some will argue that TCU functions should be absorbed by mainstream community colleges.

**Refinements**: If mandatory funding is infeasible, a 5-year ramp-up to full authorized levels would be more politically achievable. Research capacity grants could be competed to ensure quality.

### The Boarding School Accountability and Healing Act

**Purpose**: Complete the federal boarding school investigation, establish a permanent reconciliation commission, and fund comprehensive healing programs for survivors, descendants, and affected communities.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Boarding School Accountability and
Healing Act".

SEC. 2. COMPLETION OF INVESTIGATION.

(a) The Secretary of the Interior shall complete the Federal
Indian Boarding School Initiative investigation, including—
    (1) identification of all federal Indian boarding schools
    and their locations;
    (2) identification and memorialization of all student burial
    sites;
    (3) documentation of abuses, deaths, and family separations;
    (4) identification of the religious organizations involved;
    (5) assessment of ongoing impacts on affected communities.

(b) The investigation shall be completed not later than 3 years
after the date of enactment of this Act.

(c) There is authorized to be appropriated $50,000,000 to
complete the investigation.

SEC. 3. NATIONAL BOARDING SCHOOL RECONCILIATION COMMISSION.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established the National Indian
Boarding School Reconciliation Commission.

(b) MEMBERSHIP.—The Commission shall consist of—
    (1) 5 members appointed by tribal governments through NCAI;
    (2) 2 members appointed by the Secretary of the Interior;
    (3) 2 members appointed by the President;
    (4) at least 3 members shall be boarding school survivors
    or direct descendants.

(c) DUTIES.—The Commission shall—
    (1) receive testimony from boarding school survivors and
    their descendants;
    (2) document the experiences of boarding school students;
    (3) assess the ongoing impact of the boarding school
    system on Indigenous communities;
    (4) develop recommendations for healing and reconciliation;
    (5) recommend appropriate memorialization of boarding school
    sites and student burial locations.

(d) REPORT.—The Commission shall submit a final report to
Congress and the President within 5 years of its establishment.

SEC. 4. COMMUNITY HEALING PROGRAMS.

(a) There is authorized to be appropriated $75,000,000 annually
for community healing programs in communities affected by the
boarding school system.

(b) Such programs shall include—
    (1) culturally appropriate mental health and counseling
    services;
    (2) intergenerational healing programs;
    (3) family reunification and history documentation;
    (4) traditional healing practices and ceremonies;
    (5) educational programming about the boarding school era.

(c) Programs shall be designed and administered by tribal
communities.

SEC. 5. REPATRIATION AND MEMORIALIZATION.

(a) The Secretary shall facilitate the repatriation of remains
of boarding school students to their tribal communities upon
request.

(b) There is authorized to be appropriated $30,000,000 for
memorialization of boarding school sites, including markers at
burial sites, educational exhibits, and digital archives.
```

**Explanation**: This bill provides the legislative framework for completing the boarding school investigation, establishing a formal reconciliation commission modeled on Canada's Truth and Reconciliation Commission, and funding community-directed healing programs. The tribal-majority composition of the commission ensures that the process is controlled by affected communities.

**Potential Challenges**: The investigation's scope is extensive and may require more than 3 years. The $75 million annual healing program budget may face opposition. Religious organizations implicated in boarding school operations may resist documentation.

**Refinements**: A phased approach to healing programs could start with the most severely affected communities. The investigation timeline could be extended to 5 years with interim reports. Religious organization engagement could be structured as voluntary cooperation with legal discovery as a backstop.

## Regulatory Framework

### BIE Education Standards Reform

**Existing Authority**: 25 C.F.R. Part 36 (BIE school standards)

**Proposed Regulatory Changes**:

- Allow tribally developed academic standards as alternatives to state standards for tribally controlled schools
- Establish culturally responsive education requirements for all BIE schools
- Create Indigenous language immersion program standards
- Modify teacher certification to recognize tribal certification and cultural competency

### Johnson-O'Malley Fund Distribution Reform

**Existing Authority**: 25 U.S.C. 5342-5348; 25 C.F.R. Part 273

**Proposed Regulatory Changes**:

- Redirect JOM funds from state education agencies to tribal education departments
- Establish accountability requirements ensuring funds benefit AI/AN students
- Require annual reporting on JOM fund use and student outcomes
- Increase JOM student count accuracy through tribal-verified enrollment data

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Spending Clause**: Congress has clear authority to fund tribal education under spending power and Indian Commerce Clause
- **Political classification**: Tribal education programs are based on the political relationship with tribal nations, not race (*Morton v. Mancari*, 1974; *Brackeen v. Haaland*, 2023)
- **Free Exercise**: Language revitalization and cultural education programs align with First Amendment protections for Indigenous religious and cultural practices
- **Tenth Amendment**: Federal education programs for tribal schools operate within federal trust responsibility, not state education authority

### Preemption Questions

- Tribal Education Sovereignty Act creates a parallel education authority that preempts state standards within tribal jurisdiction
- Assessment exemptions for immersion programs preempt ESSA testing requirements within their scope
- JOM fund reform preempts state distribution of federal funds for AI/AN students

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- Mandatory appropriations enforceable through budget process
- Tribal education sovereignty compacts monitored through annual reporting
- Language revitalization grants subject to performance metrics
- TCU funding administered through existing AIHEC/BIE mechanisms

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Construction fund reviewed at Year 10 with certification requirement for backlog elimination
- Language revitalization funding authorized for 15 years with review at Year 10
- Reconciliation commission has a 5-year mandate with option for extension
- All programs subject to GAO audit every 3 years

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Tribal Education Full Funding Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Formula manipulation | Weighted formula may be gamed through enrollment classification changes | Medium |
| Construction prioritization bypass | Political pressure may override condition-based prioritization | Medium |
| Inflation adjustment lag | CPI adjustment may not keep pace with actual education cost increases | Low |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address 93% of AI/AN students in public schools | Majority of AI/AN students see no direct benefit | Federal-state education divide |
| BIE structural issues not addressed | Funding without governance reform may produce limited improvement | Institutional resistance |
| No teacher compensation mandate | Schools may use funds for other purposes rather than salary parity | Local discretion |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Add minimum teacher salary requirement (parity with surrounding district average)
2. Include public school supplement for districts with 10%+ AI/AN enrollment
3. Pair funding with BIE governance reform requirements
4. Use Higher Education Price Index rather than CPI for inflation adjustment

### Indigenous Language Revitalization Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Assessment exemption abuse | Schools may use exemption to avoid accountability entirely | Medium |
| Language definition disputes | Disputes over language vs. dialect classification could create eligibility issues | Low |
| Program quality variation | Grant program may fund ineffective approaches | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Elder speaker attrition | Elder speakers are dying faster than programs can train replacements | Demographic reality |
| Insufficient scale | $200M/year spread across 245 languages is ~$800K per language on average | Scope of crisis |
| Limited teacher pipeline | Not enough certified language teachers to staff programs | Historical language suppression |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Require assessment in immersion language alongside English by Year 4 of enrollment
2. Establish emergency documentation funding for languages with fewer than 10 speakers
3. Double funding for master-apprentice programs to accelerate teacher pipeline
4. Create language revitalization performance metrics with tribal consultation

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| BIE within Interior rather than Education | Establish BIE as an independent agency or transfer to DOEd with tribal governance |
| Multiple federal funding streams | Create unified tribal education funding portal |
| State resistance to tribal education authority | Phase implementation; build from successful precedents |
| Data gaps on AI/AN student outcomes | Invest in tribal education data systems with tribal data sovereignty |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- All programs subject to GAO audit every 3 years
- Construction fund reviewed at Year 10 for continuation
- Language revitalization reviewed at Year 10 for funding adequacy
- Reconciliation commission issues final report at Year 5
- Mandatory funding subject to 10-year reauthorization
