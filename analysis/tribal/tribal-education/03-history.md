# Tribal Education: History

## Overview

The history of education for American Indian and Alaska Native peoples is one of the most painful chapters in American history. From pre-contact Indigenous education systems rooted in cultural transmission and community responsibility, through the deliberate destruction of those systems via federal boarding schools, to the modern era of self-determination and cultural revitalization, educational policy has been a primary instrument of both oppression and, ultimately, resistance and resilience.

---

## Era 1: Indigenous Education Systems (Pre-Contact)

### Traditional Education

Prior to European contact, Indigenous nations maintained sophisticated educational systems tailored to their cultures, environments, and governance structures. These systems included:

- **Oral tradition**: Knowledge transmitted through stories, songs, ceremonies, and direct instruction
- **Apprenticeship**: Practical skills taught through mentorship and hands-on learning
- **Ceremonial education**: Spiritual and cultural knowledge transmitted through age-graded ceremonies
- **Community responsibility**: Education was a communal function, with elders, parents, and community members all serving as teachers
- **Environmental knowledge**: Detailed understanding of ecology, agriculture, medicine, and resource management

These systems produced individuals who were competent in their cultural context and capable of sustaining complex societies for thousands of years.

---

## Era 2: Colonial and Early Federal Period (1600s-1870s)

### Missionary Schools

European colonizers established mission schools beginning in the early 1600s, primarily aimed at converting Indigenous peoples to Christianity and European cultural practices:

- **1617**: First documented English mission school for Indigenous children (Virginia)
- **1636**: Harvard College charter included education of "English and Indian Youth"
- **1754**: Moor's Indian Charity School (later Dartmouth College) established
- **1819**: Civilization Fund Act -- first federal appropriation for Indian education ($10,000 annually to subsidize missionary schools)

### Treaty-Based Education Obligations

Beginning in the late 18th century, education provisions were included in treaties:

- **1794**: Treaty of Canandaigua (Six Nations) included education provisions
- **1830s-1860s**: Over 100 treaties included specific education commitments
- **1868**: Treaty of Fort Laramie required the United States to provide schools and teachers for the Sioux

These treaty provisions created legally binding federal obligations that remain in force today.

## Era 3: Boarding School Era (1879-1960s)

### Origins

The federal boarding school system was established explicitly to destroy Indigenous cultures:

- **1879**: Captain Richard Henry Pratt founded the Carlisle Indian Industrial School in Pennsylvania, articulating the infamous motto: "Kill the Indian in him, and save the man"
- **1882**: Congress appropriated funds for additional boarding schools
- **1893**: Congress authorized the BIA to compel school attendance

### Scale and Methods

| Period | Schools Operating | Estimated Enrollment | Key Policy |
|--------|-------------------|---------------------|------------|
| 1879-1900 | 25 off-reservation, 150+ reservation schools | 20,000+ | Rapid expansion; compulsory attendance |
| 1900-1928 | 400+ total (boarding and day) | 60,000+ | Peak enrollment; "outing" system |
| 1928-1945 | 350+ (some closures) | 40,000+ | Meriam Report criticism; some reforms |
| 1945-1969 | 200+ (declining) | 30,000+ | Transition to public schools |

*Source: DOI Federal Indian Boarding School Initiative Report, Volume 1, 2022*

### Boarding School Practices

- **Language suppression**: Students punished for speaking Indigenous languages; some had mouths washed with soap
- **Cultural destruction**: Hair forcibly cut, traditional clothing confiscated, spiritual practices forbidden
- **Forced labor**: Students performed manual labor (agriculture, laundry, maintenance) that subsidized school operations
- **Physical and sexual abuse**: Documented abuse was widespread and systematic
- **Child deaths**: DOI has identified 973+ student deaths at boarding schools, with investigations ongoing; actual numbers are believed to be far higher
- **Family separation**: Children as young as 4-5 were taken from families, sometimes by force

### The Meriam Report (1928)

The *Problem of Indian Administration* (Meriam Report), commissioned by the Interior Department, documented the catastrophic conditions in boarding schools:

- Malnutrition, disease, overcrowding, and high death rates
- Inadequate funding and staffing
- Failure to prepare students for either traditional or mainstream life
- Recommended reform toward day schools and community-based education

## Era 4: Reform and Transition (1930s-1960s)

### Indian New Deal

Under Commissioner of Indian Affairs John Collier (1933-1945), the Johnson-O'Malley Act (1934) authorized federal contracts with states to educate Indian students in public schools, beginning the transition away from the boarding school model.

### Termination Era Impact on Education

During the termination era (1953-1968), education policy mirrored broader assimilation goals:

- Tribal schools were closed as tribes were terminated
- Indian students were pushed into public schools without adequate support
- The Indian Education Act (precursor) was not yet enacted

### Kennedy Report (1969)

The *Indian Education: A National Tragedy -- A National Challenge* (Kennedy Report) documented:

- Dropout rates of 50-60% for Indian students in public schools
- Curricula that denigrated or ignored Indigenous cultures
- Teachers with no training in Indian education or cultural competency
- Recommendations for tribal control and cultural responsiveness

## Era 5: Self-Determination and Tribal Control (1970s-Present)

### Key Legislation

| Year | Legislation | Significance |
|------|------------|-------------|
| 1972 | Indian Education Act | First federal Indian education program administered by the Department of Education |
| 1975 | Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act | Allowed tribes to contract for educational services |
| 1978 | Tribally Controlled Community College Assistance Act | Provided federal funding for tribal colleges |
| 1988 | Tribally Controlled Schools Act | Allowed tribes to operate BIA schools through grants |
| 1990 | Native American Languages Act | Declared federal policy to preserve Indigenous languages |
| 2006 | Esther Martinez Native American Languages Preservation Act | Funded language immersion programs |
| 2015 | Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA) | Included provisions for tribal consultation on AI/AN student services |
| 2019 | Esther Martinez Reauthorization | Expanded language immersion and revitalization programs |

### Tribal College Movement

- **1968**: Navajo Community College (now Dine College) founded -- first tribally controlled college
- **1972**: American Indian Higher Education Consortium (AIHEC) established
- **1978**: Tribally Controlled Community College Assistance Act provided federal funding
- **1994**: TCUs granted land-grant status under the Equity in Educational Land-Grant Status Act
- **Present**: 37 accredited TCUs across 16 states serve approximately 30,000 students

### Bureau of Indian Education Reform Efforts

- **2014**: BIE reorganization moved from line-authority management to school support model
- **2017**: Secretarial Order creating BIE as a distinct bureau within the Department of the Interior
- **2022**: Federal Indian Boarding School Initiative launched, investigating historical boarding school operations

## Lessons from History

1. **Education as a weapon of colonialism**: The boarding school era demonstrates that education can be intentionally used to destroy cultures. This history must inform all current education policy to ensure it does not reproduce assimilationist dynamics.

2. **Tribal control produces better outcomes**: Since the self-determination era, tribally controlled schools and colleges have consistently produced more culturally relevant and effective education than federally managed institutions.

3. **Language is the foundation of cultural survival**: The boarding school era's systematic suppression of Indigenous languages was its most devastating and lasting impact. Language revitalization is inseparable from educational reform.

4. **Federal promises require funding**: Treaty-based education obligations have never been fully funded. Without adequate, mandatory funding, legal commitments remain empty promises.

5. **Intergenerational trauma affects educational outcomes**: The boarding school legacy continues to affect AI/AN student performance through intergenerational trauma, family disruption, and community distrust of educational institutions.

6. **Community-based solutions work**: The tribal college movement demonstrates that institutions created by and for Indigenous communities produce superior outcomes for their students.
