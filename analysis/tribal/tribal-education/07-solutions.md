# Tribal Education: Solutions

## Overview

Addressing the systemic challenges in tribal education requires a comprehensive approach that fulfills the federal funding obligation, empowers tribal control, invests in culturally responsive curricula and language revitalization, rebuilds school infrastructure, expands tribal college capacity, and addresses the boarding school legacy. These solutions draw on successful domestic models, international Indigenous education practices, and the expertise of tribal communities themselves.

## Solution 1: Full Funding of Federal Education Trust Obligation

### Description

Transition BIE education funding from discretionary to mandatory appropriations at levels that fully meet treaty obligations and demonstrated need, with annual inflation adjustments.

### Evidence Base

- U.S. Commission on Civil Rights (2018) documented chronic underfunding of tribal education relative to need
- BIE deferred maintenance backlog of $3.4 billion demonstrates inadequate capital investment (GAO-24-105, 2024)
- The Indian Health Service precedent: the 2024 advance appropriations for IHS demonstrates that mandatory/advance funding for trust programs is politically achievable
- Research shows that increased per-pupil spending in high-poverty schools produces measurable achievement gains (Jackson et al., 2016, *Quarterly Journal of Economics*)

### Cost Estimate

- **BIE operations increase**: $400-500 million annually above current levels
- **Facility construction and renovation**: $500 million annually for 10 years to clear backlog
- **TCU full funding**: $150 million annually (to reach authorized levels)
- **Total annual increase**: $1.05-1.15 billion
- **Funding mechanism**: Mandatory appropriations with inflation adjustment

### Implementation Timeline

- **Year 1**: Enact mandatory funding legislation; begin facility assessments
- **Years 2-5**: Implement increased operational funding; begin priority construction projects
- **Years 5-10**: Complete facility renovation backlog; achieve full operational funding

### Key Features

- Mandatory appropriations eliminating annual budget uncertainty
- Formula-based distribution ensuring equitable allocation across BIE schools
- Separate capital budget for facility construction and renovation
- Annual cost-of-living adjustment linked to Consumer Price Index
- Accountability measures tracking expenditures to student outcomes

## Solution 2: Tribal Education Sovereignty Act

### Description

Strengthen tribal control over education by expanding tribally controlled school authority, creating education sovereignty compacts, and giving tribes meaningful voice in public school education of AI/AN students.

### Evidence Base

- Tribally controlled grant schools (128 of 183 BIE schools) have demonstrated tribal management capacity
- The Rough Rock Demonstration School (Navajo Nation) pioneered community-controlled education in 1966 and demonstrated that tribal control improves outcomes and cultural relevance
- Self-governance compact model has been successful in other areas of federal-tribal relations (BIA, IHS)
- Research shows that culturally responsive, community-controlled education improves AI/AN student outcomes (Demmert and Towner, 2003)

### Cost Estimate

- **Tribal capacity building grants**: $75 million annually
- **Education compact transition funding**: $50 million over 5 years
- **Tribal education department support**: $30 million annually
- **Total annual cost**: $105 million (initial), declining to $75 million

### Implementation Timeline

- **Year 1**: Enact legislation; begin tribal consultation on compact design
- **Years 2-3**: Pilot education sovereignty compacts with 15-20 tribes
- **Years 3-5**: Expand to all interested tribes; complete transition of BIE-operated schools to tribal control

### Key Features

- Education sovereignty compacts allowing tribes to assume all BIE functions
- Tribal authority over curriculum, staffing, standards, and assessment
- Tribal Education Agency certification for tribes choosing to establish independent education agencies
- Tribal consultation mandate for public school districts serving AI/AN students
- Johnson-O'Malley Act reform ensuring funds reach AI/AN students

## Solution 3: Indigenous Language Revitalization Initiative

### Description

Establish a comprehensive federal initiative to reverse Indigenous language loss through immersion schools, language nest programs, teacher training, and technology development, funded at $200 million annually.

### Evidence Base

- 71% of 245 Indigenous languages spoken in the U.S. are endangered (UNESCO, 2023)
- Hawaiian language revitalization, from fewer than 50 child speakers in the 1980s to thousands today, demonstrates that language death can be reversed with sustained investment (Wilson and Kamana, 2009)
- Immersion students perform at or above grade level in English by middle school while achieving heritage language fluency (Genesee, 2004; Ka Papahana Kaiapuni data)
- New Zealand's Maori language revitalization provides a successful international model with government investment of NZ$300+ million annually

### Cost Estimate

- **Immersion school establishment grants**: $50 million annually (target: 100+ new programs)
- **Language nest (early childhood) programs**: $30 million annually
- **Master-apprentice and language documentation**: $25 million annually
- **Teacher training in Indigenous languages**: $40 million annually
- **Technology and materials development**: $25 million annually
- **Language revitalization research**: $15 million annually
- **Program administration**: $15 million annually
- **Total annual cost**: $200 million

### Implementation Timeline

- **Year 1**: Enact legislation; establish language revitalization office within BIE
- **Years 1-3**: Fund 50 new immersion programs; train 500 language teachers
- **Years 3-5**: Scale to 100+ programs; develop comprehensive materials for 50 languages
- **Years 5-10**: Achieve measurable increase in child speakers for 100+ languages

### Key Features

- Tribally directed: tribes identify priority languages and program designs
- Master-apprentice programs pairing fluent elders with apprentice teachers
- Technology platform for language documentation, instruction, and community use
- Exemption from standardized testing requirements for immersion students in early grades
- Integration with TCU language programs and teacher preparation

## Solution 4: BIE School Infrastructure Modernization

### Description

Eliminate the $3.4 billion deferred maintenance backlog at BIE schools within 10 years and establish ongoing capital investment levels sufficient to maintain facilities in good condition.

### Evidence Base

- GAO-24-105 (2024) documented 78 schools (43%) in "poor" condition
- Research shows that school facility quality directly affects student achievement, attendance, and teacher retention (Earthman, 2002)
- The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (2021) provided initial broadband funding for BIE schools, demonstrating federal investment capacity
- DOD school construction provides a model for systematic facility modernization

### Cost Estimate

- **Initial backlog clearance**: $500 million annually for 7 years ($3.5 billion total)
- **Ongoing maintenance**: $200 million annually
- **Broadband and technology**: $100 million over 5 years
- **Teacher housing**: $150 million over 5 years
- **Total 10-year investment**: $5.5 billion

### Implementation Timeline

- **Years 1-2**: Complete facility assessments; prioritize worst-condition schools
- **Years 2-7**: Systematic renovation and new construction
- **Years 8-10**: Complete backlog clearance; transition to maintenance mode

## Solution 5: Tribal College Parity Act

### Description

Fully fund tribal colleges at parity with comparable mainstream institutions, expand program offerings, and invest in TCU facilities and research capacity.

### Evidence Base

- TCUs receive 60% of authorized federal funding (AIHEC, 2024)
- TCU graduates have employment rates and median incomes significantly above community averages (American Indian College Fund, 2019)
- TCUs serve critical community development functions beyond academics
- Land-grant institution funding models provide a precedent for equitable federal investment

### Cost Estimate

- **Operational funding increase**: $150 million annually (to reach authorized level)
- **Facility construction and renovation**: $200 million over 5 years
- **Research capacity development**: $30 million annually
- **Faculty recruitment and retention**: $25 million annually
- **Program expansion grants**: $40 million annually
- **Total annual cost**: $245 million (initial), declining to $195 million

### Implementation Timeline

- **Year 1**: Enact full funding legislation; begin facility assessments
- **Years 2-3**: Implement funding increase; begin construction projects
- **Years 3-5**: Expand bachelor's degree programs at 10+ TCUs
- **Years 5-10**: Full parity achieved; research capacity established

## Solution 6: Teacher Recruitment and Retention Initiative

### Description

Address the teacher workforce crisis at BIE schools and in AI/AN-serving public schools through competitive compensation, loan forgiveness, housing, cultural competency training, and a grow-your-own pipeline.

### Evidence Base

- BIE teacher vacancy rate of 15-20% compared to 6% nationally (BIE/GAO, 2023)
- Annual turnover of 25-30% at BIE schools prevents continuity
- Teacher housing shortages are a primary barrier to recruitment
- Grow-your-own programs (training community members as teachers) produce teachers who stay longer and are more effective in Indigenous communities (Ingersoll, 2001)

### Cost Estimate

- **Salary supplements**: $100 million annually (to achieve parity with surrounding districts)
- **Loan forgiveness program**: $50 million annually
- **Teacher housing construction**: $200 million over 5 years
- **Grow-your-own pipeline programs**: $30 million annually
- **Cultural competency training**: $15 million annually
- **Total annual cost**: $195 million (initial), declining to $155 million

### Implementation Timeline

- **Year 1**: Implement salary supplements; begin loan forgiveness program
- **Years 2-3**: Begin teacher housing construction; launch grow-your-own programs at TCUs
- **Years 3-5**: Reduce vacancy rate to under 10%; reduce turnover to under 20%
- **Years 5-10**: Achieve vacancy and turnover parity with national averages

## Solution 7: Boarding School Reconciliation and Healing

### Description

Complete the federal boarding school investigation, establish a permanent reconciliation commission, and fund comprehensive healing programs for survivors, descendants, and affected communities.

### Evidence Base

- DOI Federal Indian Boarding School Initiative has identified 408+ schools and 973+ documented deaths (2022)
- Canada's Truth and Reconciliation Commission (2015) provides a comprehensive model, with 94 Calls to Action and measurable impacts on educational policy
- Research documents intergenerational trauma from boarding schools affecting current educational outcomes (Evans-Campbell, 2008; Brave Heart, 2003)
- Community healing programs in Indigenous communities have demonstrated effectiveness in improving educational engagement (Chandler and Lalonde, 2008)

### Cost Estimate

- **Complete boarding school investigation**: $50 million over 3 years
- **Permanent reconciliation commission**: $20 million annually for 10 years
- **Community healing programs**: $75 million annually
- **Memorialization and repatriation**: $30 million over 5 years
- **Total annual cost**: $95 million (declining after investigation completes)

### Implementation Timeline

- **Years 1-3**: Complete DOI investigation (Volumes 2 and 3)
- **Year 2**: Establish permanent reconciliation commission with tribal representation
- **Years 2-5**: Launch community healing programs in affected communities
- **Years 5-10**: Complete memorialization; integrate reconciliation findings into educational curricula

## Solution 8: Culturally Responsive Education Standards

### Description

Develop and implement culturally responsive education standards for all schools serving AI/AN students, with tribally developed content, culturally appropriate assessment, and teacher training requirements.

### Evidence Base

- Demmert and Towner (2003) meta-analysis found that culturally responsive education significantly improved AI/AN student achievement
- Montana's Indian Education for All Act (1999) provides a successful state-level model for integrating Indigenous content across all subjects
- Alaska's Alaska Standards for Culturally Responsive Schools (1998) demonstrates standards development in partnership with Indigenous communities
- Research shows that culturally relevant curricula improve engagement, attendance, and achievement for AI/AN students (Castagno and Brayboy, 2008)

### Cost Estimate

- **Standards development (tribal-led)**: $15 million over 3 years
- **Curriculum materials development**: $25 million over 5 years
- **Teacher training and professional development**: $30 million annually
- **Assessment tool development**: $10 million over 3 years
- **Total annual cost**: $30 million (ongoing after initial development)

### Implementation Timeline

- **Years 1-2**: Convene tribal-led standards development process
- **Years 2-3**: Develop curriculum materials and assessment tools
- **Years 3-5**: Implement in BIE schools and pilot in public schools
- **Years 5-10**: Expand to all public schools serving significant AI/AN populations

## International Models

### New Zealand: Maori Education

- **Kura Kaupapa Maori**: Maori-medium schools operating within the state system since 1985; 73 schools serving ~6,000 students
- **Wananga**: Maori universities providing culturally grounded higher education
- **Te reo Maori revitalization**: Government investment of NZ$300+ million annually in language revitalization through education
- **Lessons**: Sustained government investment and community control produce measurable language and educational outcomes

### Canada: First Nations Education

- **First Nations Schools**: 518 federally funded schools on reserves serving ~72,000 students
- **Jordan's Principle**: Federal policy ensuring First Nations children receive services without jurisdictional disputes
- **Truth and Reconciliation Commission**: 94 Calls to Action (2015) included specific education reforms, many implemented
- **Lessons**: Reconciliation process has catalyzed policy reform; Jordan's Principle model could apply to education service gaps

### Australia: Indigenous Education

- **Closing the Gap**: National framework with specific targets for Indigenous educational outcomes
- **Aboriginal Community Controlled Organizations**: Community-run educational services
- **Both-Ways education**: Integrating Indigenous and Western knowledge systems in curriculum
- **Lessons**: National targets with accountability framework; community control produces better engagement

## Solution Sequencing

### Priority Order

1. **Full funding of trust obligation** (most impactful; prerequisite for all other reforms)
2. **BIE infrastructure modernization** (urgent safety and quality issue)
3. **Teacher recruitment and retention** (workforce enables all other improvements)
4. **Tribal education sovereignty** (structural reform enabling long-term improvement)
5. **Language revitalization** (time-sensitive; elder speakers aging)
6. **TCU parity** (builds long-term community capacity)
7. **Culturally responsive standards** (improves outcomes for 93% of AI/AN students in public schools)
8. **Boarding school reconciliation** (essential for healing; complements all other reforms)

### Dependencies

- Infrastructure modernization depends on funding
- Teacher recruitment depends on compensation parity and housing
- Tribal education sovereignty depends on tribal capacity building
- Language revitalization depends on teacher training and immersion program funding
- Culturally responsive standards depend on tribal-led development process
