# Tribal Education: Implementation Roadmap

## Overview

This roadmap outlines a phased approach to reforming tribal education over 15 years. The strategy prioritizes funding and infrastructure first (because nothing else works without them), then builds toward tribal sovereignty over education, language revitalization, and cultural responsiveness. Each phase builds on the preceding one.

---

## Phase 1: Foundation -- Funding, Infrastructure, and Workforce (Years 1-3)

### Objectives

- Secure mandatory funding for BIE operations and construction
- Begin clearing the facility deferred maintenance backlog
- Address the teacher workforce crisis
- Complete the boarding school investigation
- Launch language revitalization at scale

| Begin BIE facility assessments and priority construction | Administrative | BIA/BIE | Years 1-2 | $500M/year | Critical |
| Implement BIE teacher salary supplements to parity | Administrative | BIE | Year 1 | $100M/year | Critical |
| Launch teacher loan forgiveness program | Administrative | DOEd | Year 1 | $50M/year | High |
| Begin teacher housing construction on 20 reservations | Administrative | BIA/BIE | Years 1-3 | $200M total | High |

## Risk Mitigation

### Political Risks

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation Strategy |
|------|-----------|--------|---------------------|
| Congressional resistance to mandatory funding | High | Very High | Build bipartisan coalition; frame as treaty obligation; cite IHS advance appropriation precedent |
| Administration change reversing executive actions | Medium | Medium | Codify reforms in statute; build institutional support within BIE and tribal systems |
| Reduced appropriations in subsequent Congresses | Medium | High | Mandatory appropriations structure; build constituency support |
| State opposition to culturally responsive standards | Medium | Medium | Frame as locally adapted; use Montana Indian Education for All as precedent |

### Implementation Risks

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation Strategy |
|------|-----------|--------|---------------------|
| Tribal capacity gaps for education sovereignty | Medium | Medium | Multi-year transition funding; technical assistance; peer learning networks |
| Construction delays for facility modernization | High | Medium | Prioritize worst-condition schools; use modular construction for speed |
| Teacher pipeline insufficient for demand | Medium | High | Start grow-your-own programs early; expand recruitment incentives |
| Language revitalization programs lack qualified instructors | High | High | Master-apprentice programs; elder speaker documentation; TCU language training |

### Program Risks

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation Strategy |
|------|-----------|--------|---------------------|
| Immersion programs show initial test score dip | High | Low | Educate stakeholders about documented temporary nature; use culturally appropriate assessment |
| TCU expansion outpaces quality | Low | Medium | Maintain accreditation standards; phased expansion |
| Reconciliation process is retraumatizing | Medium | Medium | Trauma-informed approach; culturally appropriate healing services alongside investigation |
| Data gaps prevent accurate outcome measurement | Medium | Medium | Invest in AI/AN education data systems with tribal data sovereignty |

## Funding Strategy

### Total Estimated Costs by Phase

| Phase | Total Cost | Annual Average | Primary Funding Source |
|-------|-----------|----------------|----------------------|
| Phase 1 (Years 1-3) | $5.7 billion | $1.9 billion | Mandatory appropriations + construction |
| Phase 2 (Years 4-8) | $8.5 billion | $1.7 billion | Mandatory appropriations |
| Phase 3 (Years 9-15) | $8.4 billion | $1.2 billion | Mandatory appropriations (declining) |
| **Total** | **$22.6 billion** | **$1.5 billion** | **Mandatory appropriations** |

### Funding Justification

- Total 15-year cost of $22.6 billion is approximately $1,500 per AI/AN person per year -- a fraction of the cost of the educational, economic, and social consequences of continued underinvestment
- Annual average of $1.5 billion represents 6% of total federal spending on tribal programs ($24.7 billion, FY 2024)
- Investment produces measurable returns in workforce development, economic self-sufficiency, and reduced social services costs
- Fulfills legally binding treaty obligations that have been systematically underfunded for over 150 years

## Accountability Framework

### Reporting Requirements

- Annual progress report from BIE to Congress with outcome metrics
- Tribal education sovereignty compact performance reports
- GAO audit of program effectiveness every 3 years
- Tribal Education Advisory Committee with authority to recommend modifications
- Tribal data sovereignty provisions ensuring tribes control their educational data

### Course Correction Triggers

- If graduation rates do not improve by Year 3, conduct independent review and reallocate resources
- If teacher vacancy rates remain above 12% after Year 3, increase compensation supplements by 25%
- If language programs fail to produce measurable child speaker increases by Year 5, expand master-apprentice programs
- If facility backlog is not declining at projected rate, authorize emergency construction funding
