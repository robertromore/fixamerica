# Tribal Education: Stakeholders

## Overview

Tribal education involves a wide range of stakeholders, from tribal nations and families to federal agencies, state education departments, teachers' unions, and advocacy organizations. The complex governance structure -- spanning BIE schools, tribally controlled schools, public schools, and tribal colleges -- means that stakeholders operate across multiple systems with different authorities, funding streams, and accountability frameworks.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Tribal Nations and Communities

| Stakeholder | Interest | Power Level | Position |
|-------------|----------|-------------|----------|
| 574 federally recognized tribes | Educational sovereignty, cultural preservation, student outcomes | Medium (sovereign but funding-dependent) | Strongly support tribal control and increased funding |
| Tribal education departments | Curriculum development, school management, cultural programs | Medium | Advocate for tribal authority over education |
| AI/AN students (~644,000) | Quality education, cultural relevance, equitable outcomes | Low (beneficiaries with limited voice) | Need culturally responsive, well-funded schools |
| AI/AN families and parents | Student success, cultural transmission, community engagement | Low-Medium | Want improved schools with cultural relevance |
| Tribal elders and knowledge keepers | Cultural and linguistic preservation | Medium (cultural authority) | Support language immersion and cultural curricula |

### Tribal Colleges and Universities

| Stakeholder | Interest | Power Level | Position |
|-------------|----------|-------------|----------|
| 37 accredited TCUs | Funding parity, program expansion, facility improvement | Medium | Advocate for full authorized funding |
| American Indian Higher Education Consortium (AIHEC) | National TCU advocacy | Medium | Lobbies for TCU funding and policy support |
| TCU faculty and staff | Compensation, facilities, professional development | Low-Medium | Seek competitive salaries and resources |
| TCU students (~30,000) | Affordable, culturally relevant higher education | Low | Need expanded programs and support services |

### Federal Government

| Stakeholder | Interest | Power Level | Position |
|-------------|----------|-------------|----------|
| Bureau of Indian Education | School management, funding distribution | High | Institutional interest in maintaining role |
| Department of the Interior | Trust responsibility oversight | High | Supports tribal education; constrained by budget |
| Department of Education | Federal education programs for AI/AN students | Medium-High | Manages Title VI Indian Education and ESSA tribal provisions |
| Congress | Appropriations, legislation, oversight | Very High | Varied; generally underfunds tribal education |
| White House Domestic Policy Council | Policy direction and coordination | High | Administration-dependent |

### State and Local Education Agencies

| Stakeholder | Interest | Power Level | Position |
|-------------|----------|-------------|----------|
| State education departments | Standards compliance, student outcomes | Medium-High | Varied; some supportive, others disengaged |
| Local school districts serving AI/AN students | Funding, compliance, student services | Medium | Often lack capacity for culturally responsive education |
| State legislatures | Education funding formulas, policy | Medium-High | Some states have AI/AN-specific programs |

## Key Decision Makers

### Federal Decision Makers

| Position | Authority | Current Stance |
|----------|-----------|---------------|
| Secretary of the Interior | Oversight of BIE; budget recommendations | Supportive of tribal education reform (as of 2025) |
| BIE Director | Day-to-day management of BIE schools | Focused on reorganization and improvement |
| Secretary of Education | Title VI programs; ESSA implementation | Generally supportive; limited tribal-specific authority |
| Senate Committee on Indian Affairs | Authorizing legislation | Bipartisan support for most education reforms |
| House Education and Workforce Committee | Education legislation | Mixed; education funding is partisan |
| Senate/House Appropriations Subcommittees | BIE and DOEd funding levels | Constrained by budget caps and competing priorities |

## Coalition Dynamics

### Pro-Tribal Education Reform Coalition

| Coalition Member | Common Interest | Potential Friction |
|-----------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Tribal governments | Educational sovereignty and funding | May disagree on BIE reform approach (fix vs. bypass) |
| NIEA/AIHEC | Student outcomes and institutional capacity | May prioritize different funding targets |
| Education equity organizations | Closing achievement gaps | May not understand unique tribal sovereignty context |
| Language revitalization advocates | Cultural preservation through education | May conflict with standardized testing requirements |
| Boarding school survivors and descendants | Reconciliation and healing | May prioritize acknowledgment over systemic reform |

### Competing Priorities

| Stakeholder Group | Priority Conflict | Impact |
|------------------|-------------------|--------|
| Tribal governments | Education competes with health, infrastructure, housing | Limited advocacy bandwidth |
| Federal budget process | BIE funding competes with other Interior programs | Education loses to land and resource management |
| State education agencies | AI/AN students a small percentage of enrollment | Limited attention and resources |
| Teacher unions | Tribal school governance may not include union representation | Potential opposition to tribal control |

## Power Analysis

### Who Has the Most Power

1. **Congress**: Controls appropriations for BIE, DOEd Indian education programs, and TCU funding
2. **Secretary of the Interior**: Direct authority over BIE structure, budget, and policy
3. **State education agencies**: Set standards and manage the public schools where 93% of AI/AN students are enrolled
4. **Tribal councils**: Govern tribally controlled schools and set community education priorities

### Who Has the Least Power

1. **AI/AN students**: Primary beneficiaries with essentially no policy voice
2. **Tribal elders and language speakers**: Hold irreplaceable cultural knowledge but limited institutional power
3. **BIE teachers**: Low compensation, high turnover, limited collective bargaining
4. **TCU faculty**: Underpaid and under-resourced relative to mainstream counterparts

### Power Imbalances

The fundamental power imbalance is that the federal government, which created the current educational crisis through boarding school policies and systematic underfunding, controls the resources necessary to fix it. Tribal nations have the sovereignty and knowledge to design effective educational systems but lack the funding to implement them. AI/AN students, who bear the consequences of this imbalance, have the least say in its resolution. Additionally, 93% of AI/AN students attend state public schools where tribal nations have minimal authority over curriculum, standards, or resource allocation.
