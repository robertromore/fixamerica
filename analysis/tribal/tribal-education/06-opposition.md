# Tribal Education: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to tribal education reform comes from several directions: fiscal conservatives concerned about increased spending, state education agencies resistant to tribal influence, standardized testing advocates, those who oppose the concept of separate tribal educational systems, and stakeholders comfortable with the current bureaucratic structure. Understanding these arguments is essential for designing reforms that can withstand political challenge.

## Opposition Landscape

| Opposition Group | Primary Concern | Intensity | Political Influence |
|-----------------|----------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Fiscal conservatives | Federal spending levels | Medium-High | High (budget process) |
| State education agencies | Jurisdiction and standards | Medium | Medium (regulatory authority) |
| Standardized testing advocates | Accountability and metrics | Medium | Medium (policy influence) |
| BIE institutional interests | Bureaucratic self-preservation | Medium | Medium (internal resistance) |
| Anti-tribal sovereignty groups | Separate educational systems | Medium | Low-Medium |
| Some state legislators | Cost and jurisdictional concerns | Low-Medium | Medium (state-level) |

## Key Opposition Arguments

### Argument 1: Federal Spending on Tribal Education Is Already Sufficient

**Claim**: BIE per-pupil expenditures already exceed the national average for public schools, so the problem is not funding but management and accountability.

**Evidence Cited**: BIE per-pupil expenditure is approximately $16,000, compared to the national public school average of approximately $14,000 (NCES, 2023). Critics argue this demonstrates adequate funding and point to management issues as the primary problem.

**Reality**: The comparison is misleading. BIE per-pupil figures include costs that public schools fund through separate mechanisms: student transportation over vast distances (some routes exceed 100 miles), boarding programs, facility maintenance on remote reservations, and administrative costs of the trust relationship. When these are accounted for, BIE per-pupil spending on instruction and student services is significantly below the national average. The U.S. Commission on Civil Rights (2018) concluded that BIE schools are "chronically underfunded relative to the unique challenges they face." The $3.4 billion deferred maintenance backlog alone demonstrates that current funding is inadequate for facilities.

**Rebuttal**: True per-pupil instructional spending at BIE schools is below the national average when accounting for the unique costs of serving remote reservation communities. The comparison to public schools is inappropriate because BIE schools serve as the entire educational infrastructure -- including transportation, housing, and facilities -- in communities with no alternative. The proper comparison is to the actual cost of providing equivalent educational services in comparable remote, high-poverty settings.

### Argument 2: Separate Tribal Education Systems Create Inequity

**Claim**: Operating separate BIE and tribally controlled school systems rather than integrating AI/AN students into state public schools creates a separate-but-unequal system that harms students.

**Evidence Cited**: BIE graduation rates (53-58%) are significantly lower than AI/AN graduation rates at public schools (74%), which are themselves below the national average. Critics argue that tribal schools perpetuate poor outcomes by operating outside the state accountability framework.

**Reality**: The comparison is misleading because BIE schools disproportionately serve the most isolated, highest-poverty communities where public school alternatives are unavailable. Students who attend BIE schools often do so because there is no nearby public school, not because they chose a lower-performing option. Moreover, the history of AI/AN students in public schools -- where they were subjected to cultural erasure, discriminatory treatment, and persistent achievement gaps -- demonstrates that public school integration alone does not produce equitable outcomes. Tribally controlled schools, when adequately funded, have demonstrated the ability to achieve strong academic results while maintaining cultural relevance.

**Rebuttal**: The solution is not to eliminate tribal schools but to adequately fund them and empower tribal control. Schools like the Rough Rock Demonstration School (Navajo Nation) and Waadookodaading Ojibwe Language Immersion Charter School have shown that tribally controlled schools can produce outcomes at or above state averages when given resources and autonomy. The "integration" argument ignores both the historical trauma of forced integration and the reality that many tribal communities lack nearby public school options.

### Argument 3: Language Immersion Programs Harm Academic Achievement

**Claim**: Teaching students in Indigenous languages rather than English puts them at an academic disadvantage, reducing their ability to succeed in English-dominant assessments and workplaces.

**Evidence Cited**: Some critics point to lower standardized test scores in the early grades of language immersion programs, arguing that time spent on Indigenous language instruction comes at the expense of English proficiency and core academic skills.

**Reality**: Research consistently demonstrates that well-designed immersion programs produce students who perform at or above grade level in English by the middle grades, while also achieving fluency in their heritage language. The Hawaii immersion program (Ka Papahana Kaiapuni) has produced students who score above state averages on standardized assessments by 6th grade (Wilson and Kamana, 2009). Canadian research on French immersion provides extensive evidence that immersion students eventually match or exceed their peers in the dominant language while gaining bilingual proficiency (Genesee, 2004). Language immersion also produces documented benefits for cognitive development, cultural identity, and mental health.

**Rebuttal**: The temporary dip in English-language standardized test scores during the early years of immersion is well-documented in bilingual education research and resolves by middle school. The long-term benefits -- bilingualism, cultural identity, cognitive development, and community connection -- far outweigh this temporary difference. Moreover, Indigenous language loss is irreversible: when a language dies, an entire knowledge system dies with it. The cost of not investing in language immersion is the permanent loss of cultural heritage.

### Argument 4: BIE Should Be Abolished and Students Moved to Public Schools

**Claim**: The BIE is a failed institution that should be abolished, with its students transferred to state public school systems that have better outcomes and accountability.

**Evidence Cited**: GAO reports documenting BIE management failures, facility deterioration, and low academic outcomes are cited as evidence that the institution is beyond reform. The argument is that students would be better served by existing public school systems.

**Reality**: Abolishing the BIE without creating an adequate alternative would replicate the termination era's educational consequences. Many BIE schools serve communities where no public school exists within a reasonable distance. The federal trust obligation to provide education cannot be fulfilled simply by directing students to state systems that have historically failed AI/AN students. Public schools serving AI/AN students also have significant achievement gaps; the problem is not unique to BIE. The appropriate reform is to strengthen BIE, increase tribal control, and invest in the infrastructure and staffing that BIE schools need.

**Rebuttal**: The BIE's failures are the product of chronic underfunding and bureaucratic dysfunction, not an inherent inability to serve AI/AN students. Tribally controlled grant schools within the BIE system have demonstrated that tribal management can produce strong outcomes. The solution is to complete the transition to tribal control, adequately fund school operations and facilities, and reform the BIE's administrative role from management to support and technical assistance.

### Argument 5: Boarding School Reconciliation Is Too Costly and Backward-Looking

**Claim**: The federal boarding school investigation and any resulting reconciliation programs are expensive, backward-looking, and will not improve current educational outcomes.

**Evidence Cited**: The DOI Boarding School Initiative costs millions to operate, and critics argue that the resources would be better spent on current students. They question whether identifying burial sites and documenting historical abuses will produce tangible improvements in educational outcomes.

**Reality**: Reconciliation is not merely backward-looking; it is a prerequisite for healing the intergenerational trauma that directly affects current educational outcomes. Research documents that boarding school attendance by parents and grandparents is a significant predictor of poor educational outcomes for current students (Evans-Campbell, 2008). Without addressing the trauma that causes community distrust of educational institutions, family disruption, and cultural disconnection, current reform efforts will be undermined. Canada's Truth and Reconciliation Commission (2015) demonstrated that public acknowledgment and documentation are essential first steps in healing that produces measurable improvements in community engagement with education.

**Rebuttal**: The boarding school investigation costs a tiny fraction of the BIE's total budget and is essential for both historical accountability and current healing. Intergenerational trauma is a documented factor in educational outcomes, and addressing it is not a luxury but a necessary component of any effective education reform strategy. The Canadian experience shows that reconciliation can catalyze broader institutional reforms when accompanied by policy changes and funding commitments.

---

### Argument 6: Tribal Colleges Are Not Academically Rigorous

**Claim**: Tribal colleges are substandard institutions that provide lower-quality education than mainstream community colleges and universities.

**Evidence Cited**: Critics point to limited program offerings (most TCUs offer only associate degrees), lower average spending on instruction, and less-developed research infrastructure compared to mainstream institutions.

**Reality**: TCUs are accredited by the same regional accrediting bodies as mainstream institutions and must meet the same academic standards. Their limited program offerings and research capacity are the direct result of receiving 60% of their authorized federal funding -- not a reflection of institutional quality. TCU students transfer to four-year institutions at rates comparable to community college students generally. A study by the American Indian College Fund (2019) found that TCU graduates had employment rates and median incomes significantly above community averages. TCUs also serve critical functions beyond academics: community development, language preservation, cultural programming, and workforce training that mainstream institutions do not provide.

**Rebuttal**: The solution to TCU limitations is not to redirect students to mainstream institutions but to fully fund TCUs so they can expand program offerings, hire competitive faculty, and develop research capacity. TCUs' cultural relevance, community connections, and understanding of AI/AN student needs give them irreplaceable advantages that mainstream institutions cannot replicate.

## Counter-Strategies

### Addressing Funding Opposition

- Document the economic return on investment from tribal education spending (workforce development, reduced social services costs)
- Frame education funding as fulfilling legally binding treaty obligations, not discretionary spending
- Highlight the $3.4 billion deferred maintenance backlog as a safety issue, not just an education issue
- Build bipartisan support by emphasizing educational achievement and workforce readiness

### Countering Integration Arguments

- Present evidence from successful tribally controlled schools
- Document public school failures for AI/AN students (achievement gaps persist in public schools)
- Emphasize that tribal educational sovereignty is a component of broader tribal sovereignty
- Provide data on cultural relevance and student engagement in tribal schools

### Defending Language Programs

- Present immersion program outcome data showing long-term academic benefits
- Frame language revitalization as irreversible: once a language dies, it cannot be recovered
- Connect language preservation to cognitive benefits, cultural identity, and mental health outcomes
- Highlight successful programs (Hawaiian immersion, Mohawk immersion) as proof of concept

### Building Support for Reconciliation

- Connect intergenerational trauma to current educational outcomes with evidence
- Use Canada's Truth and Reconciliation Commission as a precedent
- Frame reconciliation as a component of current reform, not a separate initiative
- Engage boarding school survivors and their descendants as powerful advocates
