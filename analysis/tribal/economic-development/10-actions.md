# Tribal Economic Development: Actions

## Overview

This document outlines concrete actions for individuals, organizations, and communities to support tribal economic development. Actions range from personal consumer choices to systemic advocacy.

## Individual Actions

### Learn

- **Understand the structural barriers**: Read about the trust land system, capital access desert, and infrastructure deficits that constrain reservation economies. The Harvard Project on American Indian Economic Development (hpaied.org) provides accessible summaries
- **Know whose land you are on**: Use native-land.ca to identify Indigenous territories in your area and learn about local tribal economic initiatives
- **Follow tribal economic news**: Tribal Business News (tribalbusinessnews.com) and Indian Country Today (indiancountrytoday.com) cover tribal economic development
- **Learn about CDFIs**: Community Development Financial Institutions serving Indian Country provide the backbone of reservation lending. Visit oweesta.org to learn about Native CDFIs

### Support Tribal Economies

- **Buy from Native-owned businesses**: Seek out and purchase from Native-owned businesses. The National Center for American Indian Enterprise Development (ncaied.org) maintains a business directory
- **Support tribal tourism**: Visit tribal cultural sites, museums, and destinations. Spend tourism dollars in tribal communities. The American Indian Alaska Native Tourism Association (aianta.org) provides destination guides
- **Purchase authentic Indigenous art and crafts**: Look for the "Indian Arts and Crafts Act" verification. Purchase directly from Indigenous artists and tribally owned stores when possible
- **Bank with Native CDFIs**: If you are near a Native CDFI, consider opening an account or making a deposit. These institutions recirculate capital in underserved communities
- **Invest in Native-focused funds**: Impact investment funds targeting Indian Country (e.g., through Calvert Impact Capital, Native-focused investment vehicles) direct private capital to tribal development

### Advocate

- **Contact your representatives**: Express support for:
    - Increased Native CDFI Fund appropriations
    - Trust land reform legislation
    - Tribal infrastructure investment
    - Expansion of Section 184 Home Loan Guarantee Program
- **Support bipartisan tribal economic legislation**: Treaty funding and infrastructure investment have bipartisan potential -- tell your representatives you support both
- **Vote**: Support candidates who endorse tribal economic development and trust land reform
- **Write and speak publicly**: When tribal economic issues appear in the news, share informed perspectives that counter the "special privileges" narrative with facts about structural barriers

## Organizational Actions

### For Businesses

- **Partner with tribal enterprises**: Explore supply chain, joint venture, and procurement partnerships with tribally owned businesses
- **Hire from tribal communities**: Recruit from tribal colleges and universities, which graduate students with relevant skills and deep community connections
- **Invest in reservation infrastructure**: Businesses operating near tribal lands can contribute to infrastructure that benefits both tribal and non-tribal communities
- **Support Native procurement set-asides**: Advocate within your industry for procurement policies that include Native-owned businesses
- **Engage in tribal consultation**: When business activities may affect tribal communities, consult early and genuinely

### For Financial Institutions

- **Expand lending in Indian Country**: CRA obligations can be met through tribal lending; the Section 184 guarantee program reduces risk
- **Partner with Native CDFIs**: Provide correspondent banking, loan participation, and technical assistance to CDFIs serving tribal communities
- **Develop reservation-appropriate products**: Home loans, small business lending, and consumer financial products adapted for trust land and reservation conditions
- **Invest in CDFI bonds and notes**: Native CDFIs issue investment-grade notes that provide market-rate returns while supporting community development
- **Train staff on Indian Country lending**: Federal Indian law and trust land transactions require specialized knowledge; invest in staff education

### For Educational Institutions

- **Partner with tribal colleges**: Develop articulation agreements, faculty exchanges, and joint programs
- **Provide scholarships for Native students**: Support students pursuing business, engineering, law, and other fields critical to tribal economic development
- **Conduct research**: Academic research on tribal economic conditions, policy effectiveness, and development strategies provides evidence base for reform
- **Offer executive education**: Programs like Harvard's Tribal Leaders Executive Education provide critical capacity building

### For Philanthropy and Foundations

- **Invest in Native CDFIs**: Grant and program-related investment in CDFIs multiplies the impact through leveraged lending
- **Fund tribal economic planning**: Support strategic planning, feasibility studies, and market analysis for tribal enterprises
- **Support policy advocacy**: Fund organizations that advocate for trust land reform, capital access, and infrastructure investment
- **Build capacity**: Fund organizational development, training, and technical assistance for tribal economic development institutions
- **Practice trust-based philanthropy**: Provide flexible, multi-year funding that respects tribal self-determination in setting economic priorities

### For State and Local Governments

- **Negotiate tax coordination agreements**: Replace adversarial tax disputes with cooperative agreements that benefit both jurisdictions
- **Include tribes in regional economic planning**: Tribal economies are integral to regional economies; include tribal governments in economic development planning
- **Share infrastructure**: Joint infrastructure projects (roads, broadband, water) benefit both tribal and non-tribal communities and reduce per-unit costs
- **Support tribal Opportunity Zones**: Work with tribal governments to maximize Opportunity Zone investment on tribal lands
- **Recognize tribal economic contributions**: Tribal enterprises employ both Indian and non-Indian workers and generate regional economic activity; acknowledge this in public communications

## Community Actions

### Build Economic Relationships

- **Attend tribal economic events**: The Reservation Economic Summit (RES), regional tribal economic conferences, and local tribal events provide networking and learning opportunities
- **Form community-tribal economic partnerships**: Joint economic development committees, shared marketing initiatives, and collaborative workforce programs
- **Support cross-cultural business relationships**: Encourage business connections between tribal and non-tribal enterprises in your community

### Engage in Civic Processes

- **Monitor federal funding programs**: Track IIJA, IRA, and other federal funding flows to tribal communities; ensure programs are fully funded and effectively implemented
- **Submit public comments**: Participate in federal rulemaking processes related to trust land, environmental review, and economic development regulations
- **Attend congressional hearings**: Senate Indian Affairs Committee and House Natural Resources Subcommittee hearings on economic development are critical opportunities for public engagement

## Measuring Your Impact

| Action | Indicator of Success |
|--------|---------------------|
| Buying from Native businesses | Increased revenue for Indigenous enterprises |
| Contacting representatives | Legislative co-sponsorship of tribal economic bills |
| Investing in CDFIs | Expanded lending capacity in Indian Country |
| Business partnerships | New tribal-non-tribal economic relationships |
| Educational partnerships | Increased Native student enrollment and graduation |
