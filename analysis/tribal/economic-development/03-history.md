# Tribal Economic Development: History

## Overview

The economic history of tribal nations in the United States is a history of systematic dispossession followed by imposed dependency, punctuated by periods of resistance and recovery. Every era of federal Indian policy has had profound economic consequences, and the present-day economic conditions on reservations are the direct result of deliberate policy choices -- not inevitable outcomes of geography, culture, or chance.

## Timeline of Major Eras

### Pre-Contact Economies (Pre-1492)

Indigenous peoples maintained diverse, sophisticated economies adapted to their environments:

- **Pacific Northwest**: Salmon-based economies with extensive trade networks spanning thousands of miles; potlatch systems for wealth distribution
- **Great Plains**: Buffalo-based economies supporting complex societies; seasonal migration patterns; inter-tribal trade networks
- **Southwest**: Agricultural economies (corn, beans, squash) with irrigation systems; extensive trade reaching into Mesoamerica
- **Eastern Woodlands**: Mixed agriculture, hunting, and fishing; wampum as medium of exchange; Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) Confederacy managed complex political economy
- **Great Basin and Plateau**: Diverse economies based on seasonal resource use; extensive trading networks

**Key Point**: Pre-contact Indigenous economies were not "primitive" or subsistence-only. Archaeological and historical evidence documents complex trade networks, resource management systems, and wealth accumulation practices (Mann, *1491: New Revelations of the Americas Before Columbus*, 2005).

### Colonial Period and Early Republic (1492-1830)

**Economic Impact of Contact**:

- Disease epidemics reduced Indigenous populations by an estimated 90% in many regions, devastating labor forces and trade networks (Thornton, *American Indian Holocaust and Survival*, 1987)
- Fur trade integration created new economic dependencies and altered traditional resource management
- Treaty land cessions progressively reduced the Indigenous land base and resource access
- Trade and Intercourse Acts (1790 onwards) regulated economic interactions between settlers and tribes

**Key Development**: The trade relationship between European powers and Indigenous nations was initially mutually beneficial, but power asymmetries grew as settler populations expanded and Indigenous populations declined from disease.

### Removal Era (1830-1860)

**Economic Devastation**:

- Forced removal of southeastern tribes to Indian Territory (present-day Oklahoma) destroyed established agricultural economies
- Cherokee, Creek, Chickasaw, Choctaw, and Seminole nations had developed plantation agriculture, written constitutions, and market-integrated economies before removal
- The Cherokee Nation operated a national newspaper, schools, and farms before being forcibly dispossessed
- Estimated economic losses from removal: billions in modern value (land, improvements, livestock, personal property)
- Removal shattered intergenerational wealth accumulation and economic institutions

### Reservation Era (1860-1887)

**Imposed Economic Confinement**:

- Tribes confined to reservations -- often on marginal land selected specifically for its poor agricultural value
- Traditional economies (hunting, fishing, gathering) disrupted by territorial confinement and resource depletion
- Federal ration system created dependency by design: rations were used as a tool of social control
- Annuity payments promised in treaties were frequently delayed, embezzled by federal agents, or distributed as inferior goods
- Buffalo herds deliberately destroyed to force Plains tribes into economic submission (Isenberg, *The Destruction of the Bison*, 2000)

### Allotment Era (1887-1934)

**Systematic Land Theft**:

- **Dawes Act (1887)**: Broke up communal tribal lands into individual 160-acre allotments; "surplus" lands opened to white settlement
- **Land loss**: Tribal land base shrank from approximately 138 million acres in 1887 to 48 million acres by 1934 -- a loss of 90 million acres (Cohen, *Handbook of Federal Indian Law*, 2012)
- **Burke Act (1906)**: Accelerated trust-to-fee conversion, enabling land sales by individual allottees (often under pressure or fraud)
- **Oklahoma land fraud**: Systematic fraud, guardianship abuse, and murder of Osage and other Oklahoma allottees for their oil-rich land and headrights (Grann, *Killers of the Flower Moon*, 2017)
- **Economic disruption**: Allotment destroyed communal resource management systems without providing adequate individual economic support
- **Fractionation**: Inheritance of allotments without subdivision created parcels with dozens to hundreds of co-owners, making economic use virtually impossible

**Legacy**: The allotment era created the "checkerboard" pattern of land ownership (trust, fee, state, federal) that remains the single greatest structural barrier to reservation economic development.

### Indian Reorganization and Early Self-Governance (1934-1953)

**Partial Recovery**:

- **Indian Reorganization Act (1934)**: Ended allotment, restored some lands, and encouraged tribal constitutions and corporate charters
- **Indian Arts and Crafts Board (1935)**: Created federal support for Indigenous artisans and markets
- **Tribal corporate charters**: IRA authorized tribes to form business entities, though within a restrictive federal framework
- **Credit programs**: IRA established a revolving loan fund for tribal economic development (initially $10 million)
- **Limited recovery**: Land restoration was modest; most allotted land was not returned

### Termination Era (1953-1968)

**Renewed Economic Devastation**:

- **House Concurrent Resolution 108 (1953)**: Declared policy of terminating federal-tribal relationships "at the earliest possible time"
- **109 tribes terminated**: Federal services ended, lands sold or taxed, trust relationships dissolved
- **Menominee Nation**: Termination forced sale of tribal forest lands; county created from reservation could not fund basic services; poverty skyrocketed from manageable to catastrophic (Peroff, *Menominee Drums*, 1982)
- **Klamath Tribes**: Termination resulted in sale of 862,662 acres of tribal forestland; per capita payments quickly dissipated; poverty followed
- **Urban relocation program**: Federal program relocated approximately 100,000 Native Americans to cities, often to poverty conditions, as part of termination-era assimilation policy

### Self-Determination Era (1968-Present)

**Economic Rebuilding**:

- **1968**: Indian Civil Rights Act
- **1975**: Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act (P.L. 93-638) -- tribes contract to run federal programs, gaining management experience and operational control
- **1982**: Indian Mineral Development Act -- tribes negotiate mineral agreements directly rather than through BIA
- **1988**: Indian Gaming Regulatory Act (IGRA) -- establishes framework for tribal gaming, triggering the most significant economic transformation in modern tribal history
- **1994**: Native American Housing Assistance and Self-Determination Act (NAHASDA) -- tribal control of housing programs
- **2005**: Indian Tribal Energy Development and Self-Determination Act -- streamlines tribal energy development
- **2012**: HEARTH Act -- allows tribes to approve certain leases without BIA approval
- **2021-2022**: Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act ($13 billion for tribal infrastructure) and Inflation Reduction Act ($720 million for tribal clean energy)

## Key Turning Points

### The Gaming Revolution (1988-Present)

The Indian Gaming Regulatory Act of 1988 created the legal framework for tribal gaming, which became the most significant engine of tribal economic development:

| Period | Development | Impact |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| 1979-1987 | *Cabazon Band v. California* era; tribes assert gaming rights | Legal foundation established |
| 1988 | IGRA enacted | Regulatory framework created |
| 1990s | Rapid casino expansion | Revenue growth from near-zero to billions |
| 2000s | Maturation; non-gaming diversification begins | $25+ billion in annual revenue |
| 2010s | Continued growth; online gaming emerges | $33+ billion by 2019 |
| 2020 | COVID-19 closures | Revenue drop; rapid recovery |
| 2023 | Record revenue | $41.4 billion (NIGC, 2024) |

**Transformative Impact**: Gaming revenue has funded tribal government services, infrastructure, education, health care, and per capita payments. The Mashantucket Pequot Tribal Nation's Foxwoods Resort Casino, for example, generated over $900 million in peak annual revenue and funded comprehensive tribal services.

**Limitations**: Gaming benefits are concentrated among tribes near population centers. The majority of tribes (57%) have no gaming operations, and many small operations generate minimal revenue.

### The Harvard Project Findings (1987-Present)

The Harvard Project on American Indian Economic Development, established in 1987, has produced the most rigorous research on tribal economic development. Key findings:

- **Self-governance matters more than resources**: Tribes with strong self-governance institutions outperform resource-rich tribes with weak governance
- **Cultural match**: Economic institutions must align with tribal cultural values to be sustainable
- **Separation of powers**: Tribes with independent dispute resolution mechanisms attract more investment
- **Strategic orientation**: Long-term planning outperforms short-term political spending
- **Federal policy is the variable**: When federal policy supports self-determination, tribal economies improve; when it imposes paternalism, they decline

## Patterns and Lessons

### Recurring Pattern: Federal Policy as the Key Variable

| Era | Federal Policy | Economic Outcome |
|-----|---------------|-----------------|
| Treaty era (1778-1871) | Bilateral negotiation | Tribes retain economic resources through treaties |
| Allotment (1887-1934) | Imposed individualization | 90 million acres lost; communal economies destroyed |
| IRA (1934-1953) | Partial self-governance | Modest recovery; institutional development begins |
| Termination (1953-1968) | Imposed assimilation | Devastating poverty; land and service loss |
| Self-determination (1975-present) | Tribal control | Significant economic gains where implemented |

**The lesson is consistent**: When tribes control their own economic strategies, outcomes improve. When the federal government imposes economic structures, outcomes deteriorate.
