# Tribal Economic Development: Stakeholders

## Overview

Tribal economic development involves diverse stakeholders with varying interests, from tribal governments seeking self-sufficiency to state governments concerned about competitive dynamics. This analysis identifies key groups, their positions, and their capacity to affect reform.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Tribal Governments (574 Federally Recognized)

**Interest**: Economic self-determination, revenue generation, employment for citizens, diversification

**Position**: Universally support removing barriers to economic development while maintaining sovereignty protections

**Key Organizations**:

| Organization | Focus |
|--------------|-------|
| National Congress of American Indians (NCAI) | National advocacy for economic policy reform |
| National Center for American Indian Enterprise Development (NCAIED) | Business development, Reservation Economic Summit (RES) |
| National Indian Gaming Association (NIGA) | Gaming industry advocacy |
| Council of Large Land Based Tribes | Land and resource policy for large reservations |
| Intertribal Agriculture Council | Agricultural and natural resource development |
| Native CDFI Network | Community development financial institutions |

**Internal Diversity**: Tribal economic priorities vary significantly:

- **Gaming tribes**: Protect gaming sovereignty; diversify revenue; resist state taxation
- **Resource-rich tribes**: Develop energy and mineral resources; balance extraction with environmental values
- **Small/remote tribes**: Access basic infrastructure and services; develop any viable economic base
- **Urban-adjacent tribes**: Real estate development, retail, tourism
- **Alaska Native corporations**: Federal contracting, resource extraction, shareholder dividends

### Tribal Citizens

**Interest**: Employment, income, housing, services, cultural preservation

**Population**: 9.7 million American Indians and Alaska Natives (U.S. Census Bureau, 2020)

**Economic Profile**:

| Segment | Situation | Priority |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| On-reservation (30%) | Higher poverty, limited employment | Job creation, services, infrastructure |
| Urban (70%) | Better employment but disconnected from tribal community | Cultural connection, tribal enterprise employment |
| Youth (43% under 25 on reservations) | High unemployment, limited opportunity | Education, training, entrepreneurship |
| Elders | Dependent on tribal and federal services | Service stability, cultural programs |
| Entrepreneurs | Face capital access and regulatory barriers | Lending, technical assistance, market access |

### Tribal Enterprises and Businesses

**Interest**: Reduced regulatory burden, capital access, market access, workforce availability

**Landscape**:

| Enterprise Type | Number | Key Issues |
|-----------------|--------|------------|
| Tribal government enterprises | Hundreds | Trust land restrictions, regulatory complexity |
| Tribal casinos | 524 operations | IGRA framework, state compacts, market competition |
| Native-owned small businesses | Estimated 300,000+ | Capital access, SBA lending, procurement set-asides |
| Alaska Native corporations | 200+ village + 13 regional | 8(a) contracting, shareholder governance |
| Tribal agricultural operations | Thousands | Land fractionation, water rights, market access |

## Secondary Stakeholders

### Federal Government

| Entity | Role | Position |
|--------|------|----------|
| Bureau of Indian Affairs | Trust land management, economic development programs | Supports reform; constrained by bureaucratic inertia |
| Small Business Administration | 8(a) program, Native lending | Supportive of expanded lending; limited tribal capacity |
| Department of Commerce (EDA) | Economic development grants | Provides grants; tribal allocation is small |
| Department of Energy | Tribal energy development | Supportive; TERA program expands tribal authority |
| USDA Rural Development | Rural infrastructure and business loans | Serves some tribal areas; coordination gaps |
| CDFI Fund (Treasury) | Native CDFI support | Key funder; demand exceeds capacity |
| Federal Reserve (Minneapolis) | Research, financial inclusion | Center for Indian Country Development provides critical data |

### State Governments

**Interest**: Tax revenue, regulatory authority, competitive dynamics with tribal enterprises

**Position**: Mixed; varies by state and issue

| State Approach | Examples | Issues |
|----------------|----------|--------|
| Cooperative | Minnesota, Montana, Wisconsin | Gaming compacts, tax agreements, joint economic planning |
| Adversarial | Oklahoma, South Dakota | Jurisdiction disputes, gaming opposition, taxation conflicts |
| Mixed | Washington, Arizona, New Mexico | Cooperation on some issues, conflict on others |

**Key Concerns**:

- Tribal tax-exempt status creates competitive advantage for tribal businesses
- State regulatory authority does not extend to trust land
- Gaming compacts are the primary mechanism for state-tribal economic negotiation
- Resource development jurisdictional conflicts (water, energy, minerals)

### Financial Institutions

**Interest**: Lending opportunities, regulatory compliance, community development obligations

| Institution Type | Role | Engagement Level |
|------------------|------|-----------------|
| Native CDFIs (70) | Primary lenders in Indian Country | High; mission-driven |
| Mainstream banks | Limited presence; CRA obligations | Low; market perceived as high-risk |
| SBA lenders | 8(a) and other programs | Moderate; program participation |
| Federal Home Loan Banks | Housing finance, community grants | Moderate; tribal member programs |
| Impact investors | Social enterprise and community investment | Growing; ESG interest |

### Private Sector

**Interest**: Market access, resource development partnerships, supplier diversity

**Key Sectors**:

- **Energy companies**: Partner with tribes on oil, gas, renewable energy development
- **Technology firms**: Broadband deployment, digital services
- **Construction and engineering**: Infrastructure projects
- **Tourism and hospitality**: Resort and destination development partnerships
- **Agriculture and food**: Supply chain partnerships, food processing

## Key Dynamics

### Coalition Opportunities

- **Trust land reform**: Tribes, federal reformers, financial institutions, and private sector all benefit from streamlined transactions
- **Infrastructure investment**: Near-universal support; recent federal investments create momentum
- **CDFI expansion**: Bipartisan support for community development lending
- **Energy development**: Tribal renewable energy aligns with both tribal sovereignty and clean energy policy

### Coalition Barriers

- **Gaming expansion**: State governments and commercial gaming industry resist; generates political opposition
- **Tax jurisdiction**: States oppose any reduction in their tax base
- **Regulatory streamlining**: Environmental groups may oppose reduced environmental review for tribal projects
- **Federal bureaucratic inertia**: BIA organizational culture resistant to reducing its own role
