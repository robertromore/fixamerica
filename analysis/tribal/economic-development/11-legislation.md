# Tribal Economic Development: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Tribal economic development requires legislative action across multiple fronts: reforming the trust land system to enable efficient economic transactions, expanding access to capital, investing in reservation infrastructure, streamlining regulatory processes, strengthening tribal taxation authority, and supporting economic diversification. This document presents draft federal legislation addressing each of these needs, along with analysis of legal considerations, potential loopholes, and implementation challenges.

The federal trust responsibility -- arising from treaties in which tribal nations ceded hundreds of millions of acres in exchange for binding promises of protection and support -- provides the legal foundation for all proposed legislation. The Indian Commerce Clause (Art. I, Sec. 8, Cl. 3) provides Congress clear constitutional authority to legislate in this area, as affirmed in *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023). These proposals aim to remove structural barriers to tribal economic self-determination rather than create new forms of dependency.

## Constitutional Amendments

### Tribal Economic Sovereignty Amendment

**Purpose**: Provide explicit constitutional protection for tribal economic self-governance, including taxation authority, land management, and regulatory autonomy, preventing unilateral diminishment by Congress or the states.

**Draft Text**:

```text
Section 1. Indian tribal nations shall possess the inherent
authority to regulate economic activity, levy taxes, manage
lands and natural resources, and conduct commerce within their
territorial boundaries, subject only to such limitations as are
agreed upon by the affected tribal nation and the United States.

Section 2. No State shall impose taxes, fees, or regulatory
requirements on economic activity within Indian Country without
the consent of the affected tribal nation, except as provided
by agreement between the tribal nation and the State.

Section 3. The trust responsibility of the United States to
Indian tribal nations includes the obligation to invest in the
infrastructure, capital access, and institutional capacity
necessary for tribal economic self-sufficiency. Congress shall
appropriate funds sufficient to meet this obligation.

Section 4. Congress shall have the power to enforce this article
by appropriate legislation, provided that such legislation shall
not diminish the economic sovereignty recognized herein without
the consent of the affected tribal nation or nations.

Section 5. This article shall take effect two years after the
date of ratification.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 1 affirms tribal authority over economic matters within their territory, addressing the root cause of federal paternalism in the trust land system
- Section 2 resolves the persistent tribal-state tax jurisdiction conflicts that deter investment and create uncertainty
- Section 3 elevates the economic development dimension of the trust responsibility to constitutional obligation, addressing chronic underfunding
- Section 4 preserves congressional authority but requires tribal consent for diminishment
- Section 5 provides a transition period for implementation

**Potential Challenges**:

- Constitutional amendments require two-thirds vote of both chambers and ratification by 38 states
- States will strongly resist Section 2's limitation on state tax authority in Indian Country
- "Sufficient funds" language in Section 3 will generate litigation over adequacy
- Defining "territorial boundaries" may reopen reservation boundary disputes

**Refinements**:

- Consider a narrower amendment focused solely on taxation and regulatory authority if the full version is politically infeasible
- Develop model state ratification resolutions emphasizing economic benefits of reduced jurisdictional conflict
- Include a transitional framework for existing tribal-state agreements

## Federal Legislation

### Bill 1: Tribal Trust Land Modernization Act

**Purpose**: Comprehensively reform the trust land system to enable efficient economic transactions while preserving protection from predatory land loss, addressing the single most significant structural barrier to tribal economic development.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Tribal Trust Land Modernization
Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) the trust land system, while designed to protect tribal
    land from alienation, imposes severe economic costs on
    tribal nations by requiring federal approval for routine
    land transactions;
(b) the Bureau of Indian Affairs requires an average of 49
    days to process routine lease approvals, and many
    transactions take months or years;
(c) the trust land system reduces the economic value of
    reservation land by an estimated 30 to 50 percent
    compared to otherwise identical fee-simple land;
(d) fractionation of allotted lands has created over 10.8
    million fractional interests among 245,000 owners,
    rendering millions of acres economically inactive;
(e) the HEARTH Act of 2012 has demonstrated that tribes can
    responsibly manage their own leasing decisions, with
    over 80 tribes successfully participating; and
(f) reform of the trust land system is essential to tribal
    economic self-determination and is consistent with the
    federal trust responsibility.

SEC. 3. UNIVERSAL TRIBAL LEASING AUTHORITY.

(a) IN GENERAL.--Section 415 of title 25, United States
    Code, is amended to provide that any federally
    recognized Indian tribe may, without the approval of
    the Secretary of the Interior, approve any lease of
    trust or restricted land for--
    (1) residential purposes;
    (2) commercial purposes;
    (3) agricultural purposes;
    (4) renewable energy development;
    (5) business or industrial purposes; and
    (6) public, educational, or charitable purposes.

(b) TRIBAL REGULATIONS.--A tribe exercising authority under
    this section shall adopt tribal leasing regulations
    that--
    (1) provide for environmental review consistent with
        tribal environmental protection standards;
    (2) establish procedures for public notice and
        opportunity to comment;
    (3) include provisions for lease enforcement and
        dispute resolution; and
    (4) are submitted to the Secretary for review within
        180 days of adoption.

(c) SECRETARIAL REVIEW.--The Secretary shall review tribal
    regulations submitted under subsection (b) and shall
    approve or provide specific objections within 120 days.
    If the Secretary fails to act within 120 days, the
    regulations shall be deemed approved.

SEC. 4. BIA TRANSACTION PROCESSING DEADLINES.

(a) DEADLINES.--For any trust land transaction for which
    BIA approval remains required under this Act or any
    other provision of law, the Secretary shall--
    (1) complete processing of routine transactions
        within 30 days of receipt of a complete
        application;
    (2) complete processing of complex transactions
        (including rights-of-way and multi-party
        agreements) within 90 days of receipt;
    (3) provide written notice of incompleteness within
        15 days of receipt if an application is not
        complete, specifying all deficiencies.

(b) AUTOMATIC APPROVAL.--If the Secretary fails to act
    within the deadlines established in subsection (a),
    the transaction shall be deemed approved, provided
    that the applicant has submitted all required
    documentation.

(c) COMPLETENESS STANDARDS.--The Secretary shall publish
    standardized completeness checklists for each
    transaction type within 180 days of enactment. No
    application shall be found incomplete for failure to
    provide documentation not specified on the applicable
    checklist.

SEC. 5. FRACTIONATION RESOLUTION.

(a) LAND BUY-BACK REAUTHORIZATION.--The Cobell Land
    Buy-Back Program for Tribal Nations is hereby
    reauthorized and appropriated an additional
    $5,000,000,000, to remain available until expended.

(b) TRIBAL PREFERENCE RIGHTS.--Indian tribal nations
    shall have a right of first refusal to purchase
    fractional interests within their reservations at
    fair market value.

(c) CONSOLIDATED MANAGEMENT.--Where the tribe or a
    single owner holds a majority interest in a
    fractionated parcel, that party may lease, manage,
    or develop the parcel without the consent of
    minority interest holders, provided that minority
    interest holders receive their proportional share of
    any revenue generated.

(d) PROBATE REFORM.--The Secretary shall, in consultation
    with tribal nations, promulgate regulations to
    reduce further fractionation through inheritance,
    including options for tribal land consolidation upon
    the death of an allottee.

SEC. 6. TITLE MODERNIZATION.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.--There is authorized to be appropriated
    $200,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through
    2031 to develop modern electronic title recording and
    search systems for trust land.

(b) REQUIREMENTS.--Systems developed under this section
    shall--
    (1) be interoperable with state and county recording
        systems;
    (2) provide real-time title search capability;
    (3) reduce title search costs to levels comparable
        to fee-simple land; and
    (4) be developed in consultation with and accessible
        to tribal governments.

SEC. 7. TRIBAL LAND USE PLANNING.

(a) AUTHORITY.--A tribe that adopts a comprehensive land
    use plan, reviewed and approved by the Secretary,
    may self-certify compliance with the National
    Environmental Policy Act for projects consistent
    with that plan.

(b) PLAN REQUIREMENTS.--A tribal land use plan under
    this section shall--
    (1) include environmental impact assessment for
        planned development categories;
    (2) provide for protection of cultural and
        archaeological resources;
    (3) include provisions for public participation;
    (4) be reviewed and updated at least every 10 years.

(c) CATEGORICAL EXCLUSIONS.--Routine development projects
    on tribal trust land (including residential
    construction, small commercial buildings, and minor
    infrastructure improvements) shall be categorically
    excluded from individual NEPA review if consistent
    with an approved tribal land use plan.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 180 days after the date of
enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the trust land system comprehensively: expanding tribal leasing authority (building on the successful HEARTH Act model), imposing enforceable processing deadlines on BIA, resolving fractionation, modernizing title records, and enabling tribal land use planning to reduce NEPA delays. The automatic approval provision for missed deadlines creates real accountability.

**Potential Challenges**:

- BIA institutional resistance to automatic approval and processing deadlines
- Environmental groups may oppose NEPA self-certification and categorical exclusions
- Fractionation reform involves complex property rights affecting individual allottees
- $5 billion Land Buy-Back reauthorization faces appropriations resistance

**Refinements**:

- Phase NEPA self-certification through a pilot program with 20 tribes before national expansion
- Include Inspector General oversight for BIA processing compliance
- Pair with environmental capacity building for tribes assuming NEPA authority
- Include appeals process for minority interest holders in fractionation consolidation

### Bill 2: Indian Country Capital Access Act

**Purpose**: Dramatically expand financial infrastructure serving Indian Country, addressing the capital desert that costs an estimated $44 billion annually in foregone economic activity.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Indian Country Capital Access
Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) 86 percent of Native communities lack a bank branch
    within their boundaries;
(b) Native-owned businesses receive approximately 1
    percent of SBA loans while comprising 2.9 percent of
    the United States population;
(c) trust land cannot be used as collateral for
    conventional loans, excluding most reservation
    businesses from standard lending;
(d) only 70 Native Community Development Financial
    Institutions serve the 574 federally recognized
    tribal nations;
(e) the Federal Reserve Bank of Minneapolis estimates
    that the capital access gap costs Indian Country
    approximately $44,000,000,000 annually in foregone
    economic activity; and
(f) expanding financial infrastructure in Indian Country
    is essential to tribal economic self-determination.

SEC. 3. NATIVE CDFI FUND EXPANSION.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.--There is authorized to be appropriated
    to the Native American CDFI Assistance Program
    $200,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through
    2036.

(b) CAPACITY BUILDING.--Not less than 25 percent of
    funds under this section shall be used for technical
    assistance and capacity building to establish new
    Native CDFIs, with a goal of increasing the number
    of Native CDFIs from 70 to 200 within 10 years.

(c) OPERATING SUPPORT.--Funds may be used for multi-year
    operating grants to enable Native CDFIs to achieve
    financial sustainability.

SEC. 4. SECTION 184 LOAN GUARANTEE EXPANSION.

(a) GUARANTEE AUTHORITY.--The Section 184 Indian Home
    Loan Guarantee Program (12 U.S.C. 1715z-13a) is
    amended to increase guarantee authority from
    $800,000,000 to $3,000,000,000.

(b) COMMERCIAL EXTENSION.--The guarantee authority
    under this program is extended to include commercial
    and small business loans on trust land, in addition
    to home loans.

(c) STREAMLINED PROCESSING.--HUD shall process Section
    184 guarantee applications within 30 days of receipt
    of a complete application.

SEC. 5. TRIBAL ENTERPRISE LOAN GUARANTEE FUND.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--There is established in the Treasury
    of the United States a fund to be known as the
    "Tribal Enterprise Loan Guarantee Fund".

(b) CAPITALIZATION.--There is authorized to be
    appropriated $5,000,000,000 to capitalize the Fund.

(c) GUARANTEE AUTHORITY.--The Secretary of the Treasury,
    acting through the CDFI Fund, may use the Fund to
    guarantee up to 80 percent of the principal of loans
    made to--
    (1) tribally owned enterprises;
    (2) Native-owned small businesses operating on
        tribal land; and
    (3) tribal infrastructure projects.

(d) MAXIMUM GUARANTEE.--The maximum guarantee for any
    single borrower shall not exceed $20,000,000.

(e) FEES.--The Secretary shall establish a fee schedule
    sufficient to ensure the long-term solvency of the
    Fund.

SEC. 6. SBA NATIVE LENDING IMPROVEMENT.

(a) SET-ASIDE.--The Administrator of the Small Business
    Administration shall ensure that not less than 3
    percent of all SBA 7(a) and 504 loan dollars are
    made to Native-owned businesses.

(b) NATIVE BUSINESS CENTERS.--There is authorized to be
    appropriated $50,000,000 for each of fiscal years
    2027 through 2031 to establish 50 new Native
    Business Development Centers providing technical
    assistance, business planning, and loan packaging
    services.

(c) 8(a) PROGRAM PROTECTION.--The 8(a) Business
    Development Program shall continue to be available
    to Native-owned businesses and Alaska Native
    Corporation subsidiaries, and the Administrator
    shall not impose additional eligibility requirements
    beyond those in effect on the date of enactment.

SEC. 7. TRIBAL FINANCIAL INSTITUTIONS.

(a) TRIBAL BANK CHARTERS.--The Comptroller of the
    Currency shall establish a simplified chartering
    process for tribally owned banks, including reduced
    minimum capital requirements appropriate for
    reservation communities.

(b) TRIBAL CREDIT UNIONS.--The National Credit Union
    Administration shall establish procedures for
    chartering tribally owned credit unions with
    simplified regulatory requirements appropriate for
    low-income and underserved communities.

(c) FEDERAL RESERVE ACCESS.--Tribally chartered banks
    and credit unions shall have access to Federal
    Reserve services on the same terms as other
    federally regulated financial institutions.

SEC. 8. CREDIT BUILDING.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.--There is authorized to be
    appropriated $25,000,000 for each of fiscal years
    2027 through 2031 for financial literacy and
    credit-building programs administered through
    tribal colleges and Native CDFIs.

(b) ALTERNATIVE CREDIT.--Federal lending programs
    serving Indian Country shall accept alternative
    credit histories (including rent payments, utility
    payments, and tribal program participation) in
    evaluating creditworthiness.

SEC. 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill creates a comprehensive financial infrastructure for Indian Country. The Native CDFI expansion addresses the institutional gap; Section 184 expansion and the Tribal Enterprise Loan Guarantee Fund address the collateral problem; SBA reforms address the documented lending disparity; and tribal financial institution provisions build permanent capacity. The 3% SBA set-aside reflects the documented gap between Native population share (2.9%) and current lending share (1%).

**Potential Challenges**:

- $5 billion loan guarantee fund faces appropriations resistance
- SBA set-aside may face legal challenges as a race-based preference (addressed by *Morton v. Mancari* political classification doctrine)
- Simplified bank chartering may raise safety and soundness concerns from bank regulators
- Commercial extension of Section 184 is a significant expansion of the program's scope

**Refinements**:

- Phase loan guarantee fund capitalization over 3-5 years
- Frame SBA set-aside as addressing documented disparity rather than population-based allocation
- Include FDIC/NCUA technical assistance for tribally chartered institutions
- Pilot commercial Section 184 with 50 tribes before national expansion

### Bill 3: Tribal Infrastructure Acceleration Act

**Purpose**: Eliminate critical infrastructure deficits on tribal lands that make economic development physically impossible, including broadband, roads, water, energy, and housing.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Tribal Infrastructure
Acceleration Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) 65 percent of tribal lands lack access to broadband
    service at 25/3 Mbps;
(b) 25 percent of Bureau of Indian Affairs roads are in
    poor or very poor condition, with a deferred
    maintenance backlog of $3,200,000,000;
(c) 46 percent of tribal water systems are below EPA
    standards, with a sanitation backlog of
    $3,800,000,000;
(d) 14 percent of homes on the Navajo Nation lack
    electricity;
(e) Indian Country faces an estimated housing shortage
    of 68,000 units;
(f) the total tribal infrastructure backlog is estimated
    at $40,000,000,000 to $60,000,000,000; and
(g) these deficits are the direct result of chronic
    federal underfunding of treaty-obligated services
    and infrastructure.

SEC. 3. TRIBAL INFRASTRUCTURE TRUST FUND.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--There is established in the Treasury
    of the United States a trust fund to be known as
    the "Tribal Infrastructure Trust Fund".

(b) AUTHORIZATION.--There is authorized to be
    appropriated to the Fund $4,000,000,000 for each of
    fiscal years 2027 through 2036.

(c) ALLOCATION.--Funds shall be allocated as follows:
    (1) Broadband and telecommunications: 20 percent;
    (2) Roads and bridges: 20 percent;
    (3) Water and sanitation: 20 percent;
    (4) Housing: 25 percent;
    (5) Energy infrastructure: 10 percent; and
    (6) Tribal discretionary: 5 percent.

(d) FORMULA DISTRIBUTION.--Not less than 60 percent of
    funds in each category shall be distributed by
    formula based on tribal population, infrastructure
    deficit, and geographic factors. The remainder shall
    be available through competitive grants.

SEC. 4. TRIBAL BROADBAND COMPLETION.

(a) MANDATE.--All tribal lands shall have access to
    broadband service of not less than 100/20 Mbps by
    December 31, 2030.

(b) TRIBAL ISPs.--Tribal nations establishing broadband
    internet service providers shall receive priority
    in funding allocations and technical assistance.

(c) COORDINATION.--The NTIA shall coordinate tribal
    broadband funding under this Act with existing
    programs under the Infrastructure Investment and
    Jobs Act to prevent duplication and maximize
    coverage.

SEC. 5. ROAD AND BRIDGE MODERNIZATION.

(a) BIA ROAD DEFERRED MAINTENANCE.--The Bureau of
    Indian Affairs shall eliminate the road deferred
    maintenance backlog within 10 years of enactment.

(b) BRIDGE REPLACEMENT.--All structurally deficient
    bridges on tribal lands shall be repaired or
    replaced within 7 years of enactment.

(c) TRIBAL TRANSPORTATION PROGRAMS.--Tribes may assume
    direct management of BIA roads within their
    jurisdiction through self-governance compacting,
    with full transfer of associated funding.

SEC. 6. WATER AND SANITATION.

(a) BACKLOG ELIMINATION.--The Indian Health Service
    Sanitation Facilities Construction Program shall
    eliminate the sanitation backlog within 10 years
    of enactment.

(b) EPA COMPLIANCE.--All tribal water systems shall be
    brought into compliance with EPA Safe Drinking Water
    Act standards within 7 years of enactment.

(c) TRIBAL WATER SYSTEMS.--Tribes shall have the option
    to assume direct operation and maintenance of water
    and sanitation systems through self-governance
    compacting.

SEC. 7. TRIBAL HOUSING ACCELERATION.

(a) NAHASDA FUNDING.--The Native American Housing
    Assistance and Self-Determination Act of 1996 (25
    U.S.C. 4101 et seq.) is amended to authorize
    appropriations of $2,000,000,000 for each fiscal
    year.

(b) HOUSING SHORTAGE ELIMINATION.--The Secretary of
    Housing and Urban Development shall work with tribal
    housing authorities to eliminate the 68,000-unit
    housing shortage within 12 years of enactment.

(c) WORKFORCE HOUSING.--Not less than 20 percent of
    housing funds shall be allocated to workforce
    housing development to support economic development.

SEC. 8. TRIBAL ENERGY INFRASTRUCTURE.

(a) RENEWABLE ENERGY.--There is authorized to be
    appropriated $500,000,000 for grants and loans to
    tribal renewable energy projects, including solar,
    wind, geothermal, and hydroelectric development.

(b) GRID CONNECTIONS.--The Department of Energy shall
    prioritize connecting tribal renewable energy
    projects to the electrical grid.

(c) ELECTRIFICATION.--All homes on tribal lands shall
    have access to electricity within 5 years of
    enactment.

SEC. 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill creates a dedicated, sustained tribal infrastructure fund with clear timelines for eliminating specific deficits. The 10-year, $40 billion commitment matches the scale of the documented backlog. Formula-based distribution ensures equitable allocation while competitive grants reward innovation. Self-governance compacting provisions allow tribes to manage their own infrastructure programs.

**Potential Challenges**:

- $4 billion annual appropriation over 10 years is a significant commitment during fiscal austerity
- Construction workforce availability in remote areas may constrain implementation timelines
- Broadband completion by 2030 may be technically infeasible in the most remote areas
- Coordination with existing IIJA and IRA programs requires careful implementation

**Refinements**:

- Phase appropriations with trigger provisions tied to demonstrated absorption capacity
- Include tribal workforce training provisions to build local construction capacity
- Extend broadband deadline to 2032 for communities with exceptional geographic challenges
- Establish interagency coordination council to prevent duplication with IIJA/IRA

### Bill 4: Tribal Revenue Sovereignty Act

**Purpose**: Clarify and strengthen tribal taxation authority, resolve tribal-state tax jurisdiction conflicts, and expand tax incentives for economic development in Indian Country.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Tribal Revenue Sovereignty Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) tribal nations possess inherent sovereign authority
    to levy taxes on economic activity within their
    jurisdiction;
(b) conflicting state and tribal tax claims create
    uncertainty that deters investment in Indian Country;
(c) dual taxation of businesses operating on tribal
    lands increases costs by 20 to 40 percent;
(d) tribal taxation revenue is essential to fund tribal
    government services; and
(e) clarification of tribal tax authority will promote
    economic development and reduce litigation.

SEC. 3. CODIFICATION OF TRIBAL TAX AUTHORITY.

(a) IN GENERAL.--Indian tribal nations shall have the
    authority to levy and collect taxes on all economic
    activity occurring within their territorial
    jurisdiction, including--
    (1) sales and excise taxes;
    (2) business income taxes;
    (3) property taxes on trust and fee land within the
        reservation;
    (4) employment taxes;
    (5) severance taxes on natural resource extraction;
        and
    (6) hospitality and tourism taxes.

(b) PREEMPTION.--Where a tribal nation imposes a tax on
    economic activity within Indian Country, no State or
    political subdivision thereof shall impose a
    duplicative tax on the same activity, unless
    authorized by a tribal-state tax coordination
    agreement under Section 4.

(c) NON-INDIAN TRANSACTIONS.--For transactions between
    non-Indians occurring on trust land, the tribal tax
    shall take precedence over state tax, subject to
    coordination agreements.

SEC. 4. TRIBAL-STATE TAX COORDINATION.

(a) MODEL FRAMEWORK.--The Secretary of the Treasury, in
    consultation with tribal nations and state
    governments, shall develop and publish a model
    tribal-state tax coordination framework within 1
    year of enactment.

(b) ELEMENTS.--The model framework shall include--
    (1) provisions for revenue sharing between tribal
        and state governments;
    (2) procedures for resolving tax jurisdiction
        disputes;
    (3) mechanisms for preventing double taxation;
    (4) data sharing agreements for tax administration;
        and
    (5) provisions for goods and services that cross
        reservation boundaries.

(c) INCENTIVES.--States that enter into tax coordination
    agreements with tribal nations shall receive a 10
    percent increase in their annual allocation of State
    Small Business Credit Initiative funds.

(d) GOOD FAITH REQUIREMENT.--States shall negotiate tax
    coordination agreements in good faith. If a State
    refuses to negotiate or fails to negotiate in good
    faith within 2 years of a tribal request, the
    tribal tax shall be the exclusive tax on economic
    activity within Indian Country in that State.

SEC. 5. FEDERAL TAX INCENTIVES.

(a) INDIAN EMPLOYMENT CREDIT.--Section 45A of the
    Internal Revenue Code of 1986 is amended to--
    (1) make the Indian Employment Credit permanent;
    (2) increase the credit from $20,000 to $40,000
        per eligible employee; and
    (3) expand eligibility to all businesses employing
        tribal members, regardless of location.

(b) OPPORTUNITY ZONES.--All tribal trust lands shall be
    designated as Qualified Opportunity Zones under
    Section 1400Z of the Internal Revenue Code.

(c) NEW MARKETS TAX CREDIT.--Not less than 10 percent
    of New Markets Tax Credit allocations shall be
    reserved for investments in Indian Country.

(d) TRIBAL BOND AUTHORITY.--Tribal governments shall
    have authority to issue tax-exempt bonds for any
    governmental purpose, including bonds for tribally
    owned enterprises that serve governmental functions,
    on the same terms as state and local governments.

(e) ACCELERATED DEPRECIATION.--Businesses locating on
    tribal lands shall be eligible for accelerated
    depreciation of capital assets, with a 3-year
    recovery period for qualified tribal enterprise
    property.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment,
except that Section 3(b) shall take effect 2 years after
enactment to allow for negotiation of tax coordination
agreements.
```

**Explanation**: This bill resolves the persistent tribal-state tax jurisdiction conflicts that create uncertainty and deter investment. Codifying tribal tax authority addresses the root cause; the tax coordination framework provides a cooperative mechanism; federal preemption of state taxation in the absence of good-faith negotiation creates incentive for states to participate. Federal tax incentives (permanent Indian Employment Credit, Opportunity Zones, New Markets Tax Credit) provide additional tools for attracting investment.

**Potential Challenges**:

- State opposition to Section 3(b) preemption will be intense
- Commerce Clause challenges to state tax preemption (though Congress has clear authority under Indian Commerce Clause)
- Revenue impact of expanded tax incentives on federal receipts
- Definition of "Indian Country" boundaries may generate litigation

**Refinements**:

- Phase preemption provisions over 3 years with negotiation windows
- Include payments-in-lieu-of-taxes for affected local governments during transition
- Cap federal tax incentive costs with sunset and renewal provisions
- Clarify that existing tribal-state compacts remain in effect unless amended by mutual agreement

### Bill 5: Tribal Regulatory Streamlining and Energy Sovereignty Act

**Purpose**: Reduce the regulatory burden on tribal economic activity, expand tribal energy development authority, and establish tribal-federal coordination mechanisms.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Tribal Regulatory Streamlining
and Energy Sovereignty Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) regulatory compliance costs for businesses on tribal
    lands are estimated to be 30 to 40 percent higher
    than comparable off-reservation businesses;
(b) the Bureau of Indian Affairs historically required 49
    steps to approve a tribal energy lease compared to 4
    steps for comparable private land;
(c) overlapping federal, tribal, and state regulatory
    requirements create confusion and delay;
(d) tribal lands contain an estimated 5.4 percent of
    total United States renewable energy potential, less
    than 1 percent of which has been developed; and
(e) streamlining regulatory processes while maintaining
    environmental and safety protections is essential to
    tribal economic development.

SEC. 3. TRIBAL ENERGY RESOURCE AGREEMENTS.

(a) STREAMLINED ELIGIBILITY.--The Indian Tribal Energy
    Development and Self-Determination Act (25 U.S.C.
    3501 et seq.) is amended to--
    (1) reduce TERA application requirements to a
        demonstration of tribal capacity to manage
        energy development, environmental review, and
        public participation;
    (2) eliminate the requirement for a finding of
        capacity by the Secretary, replacing it with
        tribal self-certification subject to Department
        of Energy technical review;
    (3) require the Secretary to approve or deny TERA
        applications within 270 days of receipt.

(b) DEFAULT APPROVAL.--If the Secretary fails to act
    within 270 days, the TERA shall be deemed approved.

(c) CAPACITY BUILDING.--There is authorized to be
    appropriated $100,000,000 for each of fiscal years
    2027 through 2031 for tribal energy department
    capacity building, including--
    (1) environmental regulatory staff;
    (2) geological and engineering expertise;
    (3) legal and negotiation capacity; and
    (4) energy resource assessment.

SEC. 4. TRIBAL ENVIRONMENTAL REGULATORY AUTHORITY.

(a) TREATMENT AS STATE.--All federally recognized tribes
    shall be eligible for Treatment as State status under
    the Clean Water Act, Clean Air Act, and Safe Drinking
    Water Act. The EPA shall process Treatment as State
    applications within 1 year.

(b) TRIBAL ENVIRONMENTAL PROGRAMS.--There is authorized
    to be appropriated $150,000,000 for each of fiscal
    years 2027 through 2031 for tribal environmental
    regulatory capacity building.

(c) TRIBAL ENVIRONMENTAL STANDARDS.--Tribes assuming
    environmental regulatory authority may adopt
    standards that are at least as stringent as federal
    standards.

SEC. 5. ONE-STOP PERMITTING.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--The Secretary of the Interior shall
    establish tribal-federal one-stop permitting
    centers for reservation development projects.

(b) PARTICIPATING AGENCIES.--The Bureau of Indian
    Affairs, Environmental Protection Agency,
    Department of Energy, Army Corps of Engineers, and
    other relevant federal agencies shall co-locate
    staff and coordinate review processes through
    one-stop centers.

(c) TIMELINE.--All permit applications submitted through
    a one-stop center shall receive a final decision
    within 180 days.

(d) COORDINATION.--One-stop centers shall develop
    coordinated review processes that eliminate
    duplicative requirements across agencies.

SEC. 6. NEPA STREAMLINING FOR TRIBAL PROJECTS.

(a) CATEGORICAL EXCLUSIONS.--The following categories of
    projects on tribal trust land shall be categorically
    excluded from individual NEPA review:
    (1) residential construction of 50 units or fewer;
    (2) commercial buildings of 25,000 square feet or
        less;
    (3) renewable energy installations of 10 megawatts
        or less;
    (4) road repair and maintenance;
    (5) broadband and telecommunications infrastructure;
        and
    (6) water and sanitation system improvements.

(b) TRIBAL ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW.--Nothing in this
    section precludes a tribe from conducting its own
    environmental review of projects categorically
    excluded from NEPA.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 180 days after the date of
enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the regulatory complexity that adds 30-40% to development costs. TERA reform makes tribal energy sovereignty practical (only 2 tribes have completed TERAs under current onerous requirements). Treatment as State provisions shift environmental regulatory authority to tribes rather than eliminating environmental review. One-stop permitting reduces coordination costs. NEPA categorical exclusions for routine projects match exclusions already available for federal agency actions.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Environmental organizations may oppose NEPA categorical exclusions
- EPA may resist accelerated Treatment as State processing
- One-stop permitting requires significant interagency coordination
- State environmental agencies may object to tribal regulatory assumption

**Refinements**:

- Frame as shifting environmental authority to tribal governments, not eliminating protections
- Include environmental review training and certification for tribal staff
- Pilot one-stop centers at 10 locations before national expansion
- Include dispute resolution mechanisms for state-tribal environmental regulatory conflicts

## State Model Legislation

### Model Tribal-State Economic Partnership Act

**Purpose**: Provide a template for state-tribal economic cooperation agreements that reduce jurisdictional conflict and promote shared prosperity.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Tribal-State Economic
Partnership Act".

SECTION 2. FINDINGS.

The Legislature finds that--
(a) tribal nations within this State are significant
    economic contributors, employing both tribal and
    non-tribal citizens;
(b) jurisdictional conflicts between State and tribal
    taxation and regulation deter investment and increase
    costs for businesses and consumers;
(c) cooperative agreements between the State and tribal
    nations promote economic development that benefits
    all communities.

SECTION 3. TAX COORDINATION.

(a) The Governor is authorized to negotiate tax
    coordination agreements with any federally
    recognized tribal nation within the State.

(b) Tax coordination agreements shall address--
    (1) allocation of sales and excise tax revenue for
        transactions involving tribal and non-tribal
        parties;
    (2) mechanisms for preventing double taxation;
    (3) data sharing for tax administration; and
    (4) dispute resolution procedures.

(c) Tax coordination agreements shall be submitted to
    the Legislature for ratification.

SECTION 4. JOINT ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENT ZONES.

(a) The State and tribal nations may designate Joint
    Economic Development Zones in areas adjacent to or
    overlapping reservation boundaries.

(b) Businesses operating within Joint Economic
    Development Zones shall receive--
    (1) streamlined permitting through a joint
        state-tribal review process;
    (2) tax incentives agreed upon by both the State
        and the tribal nation; and
    (3) access to state and tribal workforce
        development programs.

SECTION 5. INFRASTRUCTURE COOPERATION.

(a) The State shall include tribal infrastructure
    needs in state infrastructure planning processes.

(b) The State may enter into cooperative agreements
    with tribal nations for joint infrastructure
    projects, including roads, broadband, water, and
    energy systems that cross or adjoin reservation
    boundaries.

SECTION 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on [date].
```

**Explanation**: This model legislation provides a cooperative framework that states can adopt to replace adversarial jurisdictional disputes with productive economic partnerships. Joint Economic Development Zones are modeled on enterprise zone concepts already familiar to state legislatures.

**Adaptations**: States with multiple tribal nations (Oklahoma, Arizona, New Mexico) may need provisions for multi-tribal negotiations. States with existing compact frameworks (gaming states) may integrate economic development provisions into existing agreements.

## Regulatory Framework

### Bureau of Indian Affairs: Trust Land Transaction Processing Standards

**Existing Authority**: 25 U.S.C. 2201 et seq.; 25 C.F.R. Part 162.

**Draft Regulation**:

The Bureau of Indian Affairs shall promulgate regulations requiring:

1. **Processing standards**: All routine trust land transactions (leases, rights-of-way, encumbrances) shall be processed within 30 days of receipt of a complete application. Complex transactions shall be processed within 90 days.

2. **Completeness determination**: BIA regional offices shall issue completeness determinations within 15 business days of application receipt, specifying all deficiencies in a single notice.

3. **Performance metrics**: BIA shall publish quarterly reports on transaction processing times by regional office, including average processing time, backlog volume, and automatic approval rate.

4. **Electronic filing**: BIA shall accept and process all trust land transaction applications electronically within 1 year of regulation issuance.

5. **Staff allocation**: BIA regional directors shall allocate sufficient staff to meet processing standards, with authority to request temporary duty assignments from other offices.

### Department of the Treasury: Native CDFI Standards

**Existing Authority**: 12 U.S.C. 4701 et seq.

**Draft Regulation**:

The CDFI Fund shall establish:

1. **Streamlined certification**: A simplified CDFI certification process for tribally chartered lending institutions, recognizing tribal regulatory oversight as an alternative to state regulation.

2. **Multi-year awards**: Native CDFI Fund awards shall be available on a 3-year cycle (rather than annual) to reduce administrative burden and provide stability for long-term lending.

3. **Performance metrics**: Native CDFIs shall report on lending volume, job creation, business formation, and housing development, with benchmarks appropriate to reservation conditions.

4. **Technical assistance**: The CDFI Fund shall provide dedicated technical assistance for tribes seeking to establish new CDFIs.

### Department of Energy: Tribal Renewable Energy Development

**Existing Authority**: 25 U.S.C. 3501 et seq.; Energy Policy Act of 2005.

**Draft Regulation**:

The Department of Energy shall:

1. **Resource assessment**: Provide free renewable energy resource assessments for all requesting tribes within 1 year.

2. **Grid interconnection**: Prioritize interconnection requests for tribal renewable energy projects, with expedited processing within 180 days.

3. **Technical assistance**: Maintain a Tribal Energy Development Office with dedicated staff to provide technical assistance for renewable energy projects.

4. **Power purchase agreements**: Establish a program to facilitate long-term power purchase agreements between tribal renewable energy producers and federal facilities.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Authority

- **Indian Commerce Clause**: Congress has clear and broad authority under the Indian Commerce Clause (Art. I, Sec. 8, Cl. 3) to regulate commerce with Indian tribes, as affirmed in *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023)
- **Treaty power**: Treaties with tribal nations create binding obligations that support infrastructure and economic development legislation
- **Trust responsibility**: The judicially recognized trust responsibility provides additional authority for protective legislation
- **Spending Clause**: Congressional spending authority supports appropriations for tribal programs
- **Political classification**: *Morton v. Mancari* (1974) established that Indian classifications are political, not racial, and subject to rational basis review

### Preemption and State Authority

- Federal preemption of state taxation in Indian Country is constitutionally sound under the Indian Commerce Clause and the Supremacy Clause
- The Tribal Revenue Sovereignty Act's preemption provisions are consistent with existing federal preemption in gaming (IGRA) and environmental regulation (Clean Water Act Treatment as State)
- States retain concurrent jurisdiction where authorized by federal law or tribal-state agreement
- Tax coordination agreements provide a cooperative mechanism that preserves state participation

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- BIA processing deadlines enforced through automatic approval provisions and congressional oversight
- Tax preemption enforceable through tribal court actions and federal court injunctions
- Loan guarantee program obligations enforced through standard contract law and Treasury oversight
- Infrastructure mandates enforced through annual reporting requirements and appropriations oversight
- TERA and environmental authority subject to DOE and EPA oversight with tribal appeal rights

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Tax incentives (Indian Employment Credit, Opportunity Zones, New Markets Tax Credit set-aside) shall be reviewed every 5 years for effectiveness and reauthorized
- Loan guarantee fund adequacy reviewed every 3 years with authority for additional capitalization
- BIA processing standards reviewed annually through Inspector General audit
- Infrastructure fund allocation formula reviewed every 5 years through tribal consultation
- NEPA categorical exclusions reviewed every 10 years for environmental impact assessment

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Tribal Trust Land Modernization Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Completeness gaming | BIA could find applications "incomplete" on technical grounds to circumvent deadlines, repeatedly returning applications with new deficiency notices | High |
| Environmental self-certification abuse | Tribes under economic pressure might approve projects inconsistent with their own land use plans, undermining environmental protections | Medium |
| Fractionation buy-out pricing | Fair market value for fractional interests may be inflated or depressed depending on assessment methodology, creating unfair outcomes | Medium |
| Automatic approval gaming | Applicants could submit incomplete applications knowing BIA will miss the deadline, obtaining automatic approval of inadequate applications | Medium |
| Secretarial regulation review delay | Secretary could delay review of tribal leasing regulations beyond 120 days through procedural maneuvers | Low |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address fee-to-trust process | Tribes acquiring new land still face years-long land-into-trust processing | Scope limited to existing trust land management |
| Applies only to surface leases | Subsurface mineral rights require separate authorization under different statutes | Multiple statutory frameworks govern different land uses |
| Does not resolve checkerboard jurisdiction | Trust, fee, state, and federal land patterns within reservations remain complex | Jurisdictional complexity requires broader sovereignty legislation |
| BIA staffing constraints | Deadlines are meaningless if BIA is not staffed to meet them | Requires sustained appropriations for BIA personnel |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Publish standardized completeness checklists that limit BIA's discretion to reject applications on technical grounds; prohibit serial deficiency notices
2. Require annual environmental compliance audits of tribal self-certification programs by independent reviewers
3. Establish a standing appraisal methodology for fractional interests, developed through tribal consultation, to ensure consistent and fair valuation
4. Require complete applications to include a certification of completeness by a qualified professional, reducing automatic approval of inadequate submissions
5. Include a companion provision for expedited fee-to-trust processing (see Tribal Sovereignty legislation)

### Indian Country Capital Access Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| CDFI capture | New CDFIs established to meet the 200-institution target may lack genuine community connection, serving as pass-throughs for external capital | Medium |
| Guarantee fund moral hazard | 80% federal guarantee may encourage reckless lending by reducing lender exposure | High |
| SBA set-aside circumvention | SBA lenders could nominally meet the 3% target through loans to well-established Native businesses near urban areas, bypassing reservation-based businesses | Medium |
| Tribal bank undercapitalization | Simplified chartering with reduced capital requirements could create unstable institutions vulnerable to failure | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address fundamental collateral problem | Trust land still cannot serve as collateral; programs mitigate but do not solve this | Trust land system is addressed in separate legislation |
| Native CDFI capacity constraints | Scaling from 70 to 200 CDFIs requires qualified staff that may not be available | Limited pool of community development finance professionals in Indian Country |
| Commercial Section 184 is untested | Extending home loan guarantees to commercial loans involves different risk profiles | Program expansion into unfamiliar territory |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Require new CDFIs to demonstrate community governance (tribal member board majority) and a minimum of 50% lending within their target tribal community
2. Reduce guarantee percentage to 60% for loans above $5 million to limit moral hazard while maintaining access for smaller borrowers
3. Require SBA to track and report set-aside lending by borrower location (on-reservation vs. off-reservation) and business size, with penalties for failure to serve underserved communities
4. Establish graduated capital requirements for tribal banks that increase over a 5-year development period

### Tribal Infrastructure Acceleration Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Formula manipulation | Future administrations could manipulate formula inputs (population data, deficit measurements) to reduce allocations to specific tribes | High |
| Construction cost overruns | Remote location construction costs are difficult to estimate; projects may consume more than allocated funding | Medium |
| Broadband definition gaming | Service providers could meet 100/20 Mbps technical requirements while providing inadequate actual service through oversubscription | Medium |
| Competitive grant capture | Larger, better-resourced tribes may dominate competitive grant allocations, leaving smaller tribes underserved | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Workforce availability | Remote reservations may lack construction workforce to execute projects within timelines | Geographic isolation and small labor pools |
| Maintenance funding gap | Building infrastructure without funding ongoing maintenance creates future liability | Bill focuses on capital construction, not operations |
| Climate resilience | Infrastructure built to current standards may be inadequate given climate change projections | Bill does not require climate-adaptive design standards |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Establish an independent tribal data commission to develop and maintain formula inputs, insulated from political manipulation
2. Include a 15% contingency in all project budgets for remote location cost overruns
3. Require broadband providers to meet performance standards through actual speed testing, not just capacity specifications
4. Set aside 30% of competitive grant funds for tribes with fewer than 5,000 members
5. Include a companion operations and maintenance funding provision at 5% of capital investment annually

### Tribal Revenue Sovereignty Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Good-faith negotiation standard | States could engage in prolonged bad-faith negotiations that technically meet the "negotiate" requirement while never reaching agreement | High |
| Boundary disputes | Preemption depends on defining "Indian Country" boundaries, which are contested in several states | High |
| Non-Indian transaction carve-outs | Non-Indian businesses could structure transactions to occur nominally off-reservation to avoid tribal taxation | Medium |
| Tax haven perception | Broad tribal tax authority without coordination could lead to "race to the bottom" dynamics | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| State political opposition | Strong state opposition may prevent enactment or lead to litigation delays | States view tax authority as core sovereign prerogative |
| Tribal tax administration capacity | Many tribes lack the administrative infrastructure to collect and enforce taxes | Revenue authority requires institutional capacity |
| Local government revenue impact | Local governments near reservations may lose revenue from preempted state taxes | Structural feature of exclusive tribal taxation |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Define "good faith" negotiation with specific benchmarks (designated negotiator, timeline for offers, mediation requirement) and authorize an independent mediator if benchmarks are not met
2. Codify the *McGirt* standard for reservation boundaries and fund a boundary clarification process administered jointly by BIA and affected tribes
3. Include anti-avoidance provisions that treat transactions with a substantial nexus to Indian Country as subject to tribal taxation regardless of nominal situs
4. Fund tribal tax administration capacity building at $50 million annually for 5 years

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| BIA institutional resistance to deadline enforcement | Mandatory Inspector General audits; congressional reporting requirements; performance-based evaluations for BIA managers |
| Interagency coordination failures | White House Council on Tribal Economic Development with authority to resolve interagency disputes |
| Tribal capacity variation across 574 tribes | Tiered implementation allowing tribes to assume authority at their own pace; technical assistance for capacity building |
| Data gaps on tribal economic conditions | Fund tribal economic data collection through the Center for Indian Country Development; require federal agencies to disaggregate tribal data |
| State litigation to delay implementation | Expedited judicial review provisions; federal defense of tribal authority by DOJ |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- All tax incentives shall be reviewed every 5 years through a joint tribal-federal assessment of economic impact
- The loan guarantee fund shall be reviewed every 3 years for solvency and adequacy, with authority for additional capitalization
- BIA processing standards shall be audited annually by the Inspector General, with results reported to Congress
- Infrastructure fund allocation formulas shall be updated every 5 years through tribal consultation
- NEPA categorical exclusions shall be reviewed every 10 years for environmental impact
- The Tribal-State tax coordination framework shall be reviewed every 5 years
- All implementing regulations shall be reviewed every 3 years through tribal consultation
