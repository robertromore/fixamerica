# Tribal Economic Development: Solutions

## Overview

Solutions to tribal economic underdevelopment must address the structural root causes identified in the analysis: trust land restrictions, capital access gaps, infrastructure deficits, and regulatory complexity. The Harvard Project on American Indian Economic Development has demonstrated that the most important factor in tribal economic success is self-governance -- when tribes control their own economic strategies, outcomes improve.

## Solution Categories

### Solution 1: Trust Land Reform

**Problem Addressed**: Trust land restrictions add cost, delay, and uncertainty to every economic transaction on reservations

**Proposal**: Comprehensive reform of the trust land system to enable economic activity while preserving protection from predatory land loss

**Key Components**:

- **Expand HEARTH Act**: Extend leasing authority to all federally recognized tribes (currently opt-in; over 80 tribes participating as of 2024). Allow tribes to approve all surface leases without BIA involvement
- **Streamline BIA approval**: Set 30-day deadlines for BIA approval of trust land transactions, with automatic approval if the deadline is not met. Reduce energy lease approval from the current 49 steps to no more than 10
- **Fractionation resolution**: Expand the Land Buy-Back Program beyond its current $3.4 billion authorization. Allow tribes to purchase fractional interests through a standing tribal preference right
- **Tribal land use planning**: Allow tribes with approved land use plans to self-certify NEPA compliance for projects consistent with those plans
- **Title standardization**: Fund development of modern title recording systems for trust land, reducing title search costs from $5,000-$15,000 to comparable off-reservation levels

**Estimated Cost**: $500 million annually for BIA process reform, land consolidation, and title modernization

**Feasibility**: High. HEARTH Act and Land Buy-Back demonstrate that incremental reform is politically achievable. Industry and financial sector support for streamlining is strong.

### Solution 2: Capital Access Expansion

**Problem Addressed**: Reservation communities are capital deserts; conventional lending is virtually impossible

**Proposal**: Dramatically expand financial infrastructure serving Indian Country

**Key Components**:

- **Native CDFI Fund**: Increase Native CDFI Fund allocation from the current $26 million to $200 million annually, supporting expansion from 70 to 200+ Native CDFIs
- **Section 184 expansion**: Increase funding for the HUD Section 184 Indian Home Loan Guarantee Program from $800 million to $2 billion in guarantee authority
- **Tribal lending institutions**: Provide federal charters for tribally owned banks and credit unions with simplified regulatory requirements
- **SBA Native set-aside**: Establish a dedicated 8% Native set-aside within SBA lending programs, reflecting the scale of the current gap
- **Federal guarantee fund**: Create a $5 billion federal loan guarantee fund specifically for tribal enterprise development, reducing lender risk for reservation-based businesses
- **Credit building**: Fund financial literacy and credit-building programs through tribal colleges and CDFIs

**Estimated Cost**: $500 million annually in direct funding; $5 billion in one-time guarantee fund capitalization

**Feasibility**: Moderate-High. CDFI expansion has bipartisan support. Loan guarantee programs are well-understood mechanisms. The SBA set-aside may face political challenge but is justified by documented disparities.

### Solution 3: Infrastructure Investment

**Problem Addressed**: Reservation infrastructure deficits make economic development physically impossible in many communities

**Proposal**: Dedicated tribal infrastructure fund targeting the most critical deficits

**Key Components**:

- **Tribal Broadband**: Complete deployment of broadband (100/20 Mbps) to all tribal lands by 2030, building on IIJA allocations. Estimated additional need: $3 billion beyond current IIJA allocation
- **Road and bridge modernization**: Eliminate the $3.2 billion BIA road deferred maintenance backlog over 10 years ($320 million annually)
- **Water and sanitation**: Eliminate the $3.8 billion IHS sanitation backlog over 10 years ($380 million annually)
- **Energy infrastructure**: Fund tribal energy development, including renewable energy projects and grid connections, at $1 billion over 10 years
- **Housing**: Increase NAHASDA funding from $700 million to $2 billion annually to address the 68,000-unit housing shortage

**Estimated Cost**: $4 billion annually for 10 years (approximately $40 billion total)

**Feasibility**: Moderate. IIJA and IRA demonstrate political will for infrastructure investment. Tribal infrastructure is broadly popular. The challenge is sustained funding over a decade.

### Solution 4: Regulatory Streamlining

**Problem Addressed**: Overlapping federal, tribal, and state regulatory requirements add 30-40% to development costs

**Proposal**: Reduce regulatory burden on tribal economic activity without compromising environmental or safety protections

**Key Components**:

- **Tribal Energy Resource Agreements (TERAs)**: Expand the TERA program (Indian Tribal Energy Development and Self-Determination Act) to allow tribes to approve energy development without federal review. Only 2 tribes have completed TERAs due to onerous requirements; streamline eligibility and process
- **Tribal environmental programs**: Authorize tribes to assume EPA-delegated environmental regulatory authority under the Clean Water Act and Clean Air Act (Treatment as State programs), with federal funding for tribal environmental departments
- **One-stop permitting**: Establish tribal-federal one-stop permitting centers for reservation development projects, combining BIA, EPA, and other agency reviews into a single coordinated process
- **Categorical exclusions**: Expand NEPA categorical exclusions for routine development on tribal lands (housing, commercial buildings, small infrastructure) comparable to those available for federal agency actions

**Estimated Cost**: $200 million annually for tribal regulatory capacity building and one-stop permitting centers

**Feasibility**: Moderate. Regulatory streamlining has bipartisan appeal. Environmental groups may resist reduced review; framing as shifting review authority to tribal environmental programs (not eliminating review) is key.

### Solution 5: Workforce Development

**Problem Addressed**: Reservation unemployment rates of 23% overall (50%+ on some reservations) reflect workforce development gaps

**Proposal**: Comprehensive workforce development system for Indian Country

**Key Components**:

- **Tribal college and university funding**: Increase federal funding for 37 tribal colleges from $108 million to $300 million annually, with emphasis on career and technical education programs aligned with regional employment opportunities
- **Apprenticeship programs**: Fund tribal-employer apprenticeship partnerships in construction, health care, energy, information technology, and agriculture
- **Career pathways**: Develop career pathway programs connecting K-12 education through tribal colleges to employment in tribal enterprises
- **Remote work infrastructure**: Pair broadband deployment with digital skills training to enable participation in the remote work economy
- **Tribal employment preference**: Strengthen tribal employment rights ordinances (TEPOs) and fund enforcement capacity

**Estimated Cost**: $500 million annually

**Feasibility**: High. Workforce development has strong bipartisan support. Tribal colleges have demonstrated track record of effectiveness. Pairs naturally with infrastructure investment (broadband enables digital workforce).

---

### Solution 6: Tax and Revenue Authority

**Problem Addressed**: Uncertain tribal taxation authority and state tax jurisdiction conflicts create economic uncertainty

**Proposal**: Clarify and strengthen tribal taxation and revenue authority

**Key Components**:

- **Codify tribal tax authority**: Federal legislation expressly confirming tribal authority to levy taxes on all economic activity within their jurisdiction, preempting conflicting state claims
- **Tax coordination agreements**: Model federal framework for tribal-state tax coordination, preventing double taxation while preserving tribal revenue authority
- **Federal tax incentives**: Expand and make permanent the Indian Employment Credit (26 U.S.C. Sec. 45A), New Markets Tax Credit tribal allocation, and Opportunity Zone designations for all tribal lands
- **Tribal bond authority**: Expand tribal access to tax-exempt bond financing, including essential governmental function bonds for tribal enterprises that serve governmental purposes

**Estimated Cost**: $300 million annually in foregone federal tax revenue; no direct spending

**Feasibility**: Moderate. Tax incentive expansion has bipartisan history (Indian Employment Credit). State opposition to codified tribal tax authority will be strong. Tax coordination agreements offer a compromise path.

---

### Solution 7: Diversification Support

**Problem Addressed**: Many tribal economies depend on one or two industries (often gaming or federal government employment), creating vulnerability

**Proposal**: Federal support for tribal economic diversification

**Key Components**:

- **Tribal Innovation Fund**: $500 million competitive grant program for tribal economic diversification projects (technology, manufacturing, value-added agriculture, renewable energy)
- **Tribal trade missions**: SBA-funded international trade missions for tribal enterprises, connecting tribal products and services with global markets
- **E-commerce platforms**: Fund development of tribal e-commerce infrastructure, enabling reservation-based businesses to reach national and global markets
- **Cultural economy support**: Expand the Indian Arts and Crafts Board; fund cultural tourism development; protect tribal intellectual property
- **Agriculture and food sovereignty**: Support tribal food production, processing, and distribution systems, building on traditional food practices

**Estimated Cost**: $750 million annually

**Feasibility**: Moderate-High. Diversification has broad appeal across political perspectives. Innovation funds have bipartisan precedent (EDA, USDA, DOE competitive programs).

## Implementation Synergies

- **Trust land reform + capital access**: Streamlined trust transactions make lending viable; capital access enables development on reformed land
- **Infrastructure + workforce development**: Broadband enables digital workforce; improved roads enable commuting; housing enables worker recruitment
- **Regulatory streamlining + energy development**: Simplified energy leasing combined with renewable energy incentives accelerates tribal energy sovereignty
- **Tax authority + diversification**: Stable tax frameworks attract investment; diversified economies build tax bases
