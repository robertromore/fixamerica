# Tribal Land Rights: Current State

## Overview

The current state of tribal land rights reflects the cumulative impact of nearly two centuries of federal land policies -- from treaty-making and forced removal to allotment, termination, and self-determination. Today, approximately 56.2 million acres are held in trust for 574 federally recognized tribes and individual Indian landowners (BIA, 2023). This represents a land base that, while growing slowly through the land-into-trust process, remains a fraction of treaty-guaranteed territories and faces persistent challenges from fractionation, bureaucratic delays, and legal uncertainty.

## Fractionation Crisis

### Scale of the Problem

The allotment era (1887-1934) divided communal tribal lands into individual parcels, which have since been inherited by successive generations without consolidation. The result is extreme ownership fragmentation.

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Total fractionated tracts | 245,000 | BIA Land Buy-Back Program, 2023 |
| Total fractional interests | Over 4 million | BIA, 2023 |
| Most fractionated tract | Approximately 10,000 ownership interests | DOI Inspector General, 2019 |
| Average number of owners per allotment | 16+ owners | Indian Land Tenure Foundation, 2024 |
| Annual administrative cost of fractionation | $50-60 million | BIA TAAMS data, 2023 |
| Percentage of trust land affected | Approximately 40% | GAO-15-234, 2015 |

### Land Buy-Back Program Results

The Cobell Settlement's Land Buy-Back Program (2013-2023) authorized $1.9 billion to purchase fractionated interests at fair market value from willing sellers.

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Total interests purchased | 1.2 million+ |
| Total acres consolidated | 3.1 million equivalent acres |
| Total amount paid to sellers | $2.1 billion |
| Number of tribes participated | 53 |
| Average price per interest | Approximately $1,750 |
| Program expiration | November 2023 (extended by legislation) |

*Source: DOI Land Buy-Back Program Final Report, 2024*

### Ongoing Fractionation

Despite the Buy-Back Program, fractionation continues through inheritance. Each generation further subdivides existing interests, meaning the problem grows faster than consolidation efforts can address it.

### Legal Challenges

The land-into-trust process has faced significant legal challenges:

| Case | Year | Issue | Outcome |
|------|------|-------|---------|
| *Carcieri v. Salazar* | 2009 | Whether tribes not "under federal jurisdiction" in 1934 can acquire trust land | Limited trust acquisition for some tribes |
| *Nebraska v. Parker* | 2016 | Whether reservation boundaries were diminished | Upheld reservation boundaries |
| *McGirt v. Oklahoma* | 2020 | Whether Muscogee reservation was disestablished | Affirmed reservation status; major implications for land jurisdiction |
| *Oklahoma v. Castro-Huerta* | 2022 | State criminal jurisdiction on tribal land | Allowed concurrent state jurisdiction over non-Indians |
| *Brackeen v. Haaland* | 2023 | Constitutionality of Indian Child Welfare Act (affecting land/family sovereignty) | Upheld ICWA and federal Indian law framework |

*Source: Supreme Court opinions; legal databases*

### Management Deficiencies

Despite reforms following the Cobell litigation, trust management continues to face criticism:

- **Appraisal backlogs**: 3,200+ pending appraisal requests as of FY 2023 (BIA data)
- **Lease processing delays**: Average 6-18 months to approve surface leases (GAO-23-105, 2023)
- **Revenue collection shortfalls**: Estimated $200 million annually in uncollected mineral royalties (DOI Inspector General, 2021)
- **Information technology gaps**: Trust asset management systems remain outdated and fragmented (GAO-22-103, 2022)

## Sacred Sites and Cultural Lands

### Current Protections

| Protection Mechanism | Authority | Scope |
|---------------------|-----------|-------|
| National Historic Preservation Act | 54 U.S.C. 306108 | Consultation for federal undertakings |
| American Indian Religious Freedom Act | 42 U.S.C. 1996 | Policy statement; limited enforcement |
| Executive Order 13007 (1996) | Presidential directive | Federal agency accommodation of sacred sites |
| Native American Graves Protection Act | 25 U.S.C. 3001 et seq. | Repatriation and burial site protection |
| Antiquities Act | 54 U.S.C. 320301 | Presidential monument designation |

### Ongoing Threats

- **Bears Ears National Monument**: Boundaries reduced in 2017, restored in 2021; ongoing management disputes (DOI, 2022)
- **Oak Flat (Chi'chil Bildagoteel)**: Apache sacred site threatened by copper mining under 2014 land exchange legislation (Apache Stronghold v. United States, 2023)
- **Mauna Kea**: Ongoing disputes over telescope construction on Native Hawaiian sacred site (Hawaii Supreme Court, 2023)
- **Dakota Access Pipeline**: Constructed near Standing Rock Sioux water sources and sacred sites despite tribal objections (Army Corps of Engineers EIS, 2022)

### Development Barriers

- **BIA approval requirements**: All leases, rights-of-way, and surface use agreements require BIA approval
- **Environmental review delays**: NEPA reviews add 1-3 years to project timelines
- **Infrastructure deficits**: Many tribal lands lack roads, transmission lines, and pipeline access
- **Capital access limitations**: Trust land cannot be mortgaged, limiting financing options

## Trends

### Positive Developments

- **Land-into-trust acquisitions increasing**: FY 2023 saw the highest acreage acquired into trust in a decade (BIA, 2023)
- **Post-McGirt jurisdictional recognition**: The McGirt decision reaffirmed reservation boundaries and tribal jurisdiction in eastern Oklahoma
- **HEARTH Act implementation**: 70+ tribes now have authority to approve surface leases without BIA involvement (BIA, 2024)
- **Tribal renewable energy development**: Growing investment in wind and solar projects on tribal lands (DOE, 2024)

### Concerning Trends

- **Carcieri uncertainty**: The 2009 decision continues to create legal uncertainty for tribes seeking trust acquisitions
- **State opposition to land-into-trust**: Increasing legal challenges from state and local governments
- **Continued fractionation**: The Buy-Back Program's expiration leaves no comprehensive solution
- **Infrastructure pressure**: Energy corridors, pipelines, and highway projects continue to affect tribal lands
- **Climate change impacts**: Tribal lands disproportionately affected by drought, wildfire, and flooding (EPA, 2023)
