# Tribal Land Rights: Solutions

## Overview

Addressing the systemic challenges in tribal land rights requires a comprehensive approach that resolves the allotment-era legacy of fractionation, streamlines the land-into-trust process, reforms trust management, protects sacred sites, and empowers tribal nations to manage their own lands and resources. These solutions draw on successful domestic reforms, international models, and tribal best practices.

## Solution 1: Comprehensive Fractionation Resolution

### Description

Establish a permanent, fully funded land consolidation program to resolve fractionated ownership on Indian trust land, replacing the expired Cobell Land Buy-Back Program with a more comprehensive, tribally directed approach.

### Evidence Base

- The Land Buy-Back Program consolidated 3.1 million equivalent acres and purchased 1.2 million interests between 2013-2023 (DOI, 2024)
- Despite this success, an estimated 3+ million fractionated interests remain (BIA TAAMS data, 2024)
- The Indian Land Tenure Foundation estimates that without intervention, fractionation will double within 30 years
- The Indian Land Consolidation Act (2000 amendments) provides authority for tribal land consolidation plans

### Cost Estimate

- **Startup**: $500 million (FY 2027) for program establishment and initial purchases
- **Annual operating budget**: $300-400 million per year for 15 years
- **Total estimated cost**: $5-6 billion over 15 years
- **Funding source**: Mandatory appropriations from trust settlement funds and general revenue

### Implementation Timeline

- **Years 1-2**: Program design with tribal consultation; establish tribal acquisition priorities
- **Years 3-7**: Intensive purchasing phase targeting most fractionated tracts
- **Years 8-15**: Continued consolidation with declining annual costs as problem diminishes

### Key Features

- Tribal nations set consolidation priorities for their reservations
- Fair market value compensation for willing sellers
- Streamlined probate to prevent new fractionation
- Indian Land Consolidation Act reform to allow tribal acquisition of interests at probate
- Mandatory inheritance reform requiring consolidation or sale of interests below a minimum threshold

## Solution 2: Streamlined Land-into-Trust Process

### Description

Reform the land-into-trust process to reduce processing times from years to months, resolve the Carcieri uncertainty through legislation, and establish clear criteria and timelines for BIA action.

### Evidence Base

- Current average processing time of 2-5 years for uncontested applications is unacceptable (GAO-15-234, 2015)
- The HEARTH Act model demonstrates that tribal authority can dramatically reduce processing times for leasing
- Canada's Additions to Reserve process, while imperfect, provides useful procedural models
- Over 2,300 applications were pending as of FY 2023 (BIA, 2023)

### Cost Estimate

- **BIA staffing increase**: $25-35 million annually for additional realty specialists
- **IT modernization**: $15-20 million for upgraded processing systems
- **Legal support**: $10 million annually for DOJ defense of trust acquisitions
- **Total annual cost**: $50-65 million

### Implementation Timeline

- **Year 1**: Enact Carcieri fix legislation and processing reform statute
- **Year 2**: Implement new processing regulations with 180-day target for uncontested applications
- **Years 3-5**: Clear existing backlog; achieve target processing times

### Key Features

- Carcieri legislative fix confirming trust authority for all federally recognized tribes
- Mandatory 180-day processing timeline for uncontested applications
- Expedited process for on-reservation and contiguous acquisitions
- Categorical exclusions for small acquisitions (under 100 acres) within existing reservation boundaries
- Mandatory revenue-sharing negotiations for acquisitions above 500 acres

## Solution 3: Trust Management Reform

### Description

Reform the trust management system to give tribes direct control over their trust assets while maintaining protections against alienation, modeled on the self-governance compact approach and the HEARTH Act.

### Evidence Base

- The HEARTH Act (2012) demonstrated that tribal authority over leasing decisions produces faster, better outcomes without risking land loss; over 70 tribes have adopted HEARTH authority (BIA, 2024)
- Self-governance compacts under the Indian Self-Determination Act have shown tribes manage programs more effectively than BIA in most cases (SGCA, 2023)
- The Cobell litigation documented systematic failures in BIA trust management spanning more than a century
- Anderson and Parker (2008) found that tribes with greater administrative control over their lands achieved better economic outcomes

### Cost Estimate

- **Transition funding**: $100-150 million over 5 years for tribal capacity building
- **Ongoing tribal grants**: $50-75 million annually for trust management functions transferred to tribes
- **Net savings**: BIA administrative savings of $50-100 million annually as functions transfer

### Implementation Timeline

- **Years 1-3**: Develop tribal trust management compacts with interested tribes
- **Years 3-5**: Pilot tribal trust management authority at 20-30 tribes
- **Years 5-10**: Expand to all interested tribes; transition BIA role to oversight and technical assistance

### Key Features

- Tribal Trust Management Authority Act allowing tribes to compact for trust management functions
- Tribal appraisal authority for land transactions on their reservations
- Tribal probate processing with federal backstop
- Expanded HEARTH Act authority to include subsurface leasing and rights-of-way
- Tribal bonding authority for capital improvements on trust land

## Solution 4: Sacred Sites Protection Act

### Description

Enact comprehensive federal legislation protecting tribally identified sacred sites on federal and private land, with enforcement mechanisms and tribal co-management authority.

### Evidence Base

- Current protections under AIRFA (1978) and NHPA are procedural only, with no substantive requirements
- *Lyng v. Northwest Indian Cemetery Protective Association* (1988) eliminated First Amendment protection for sacred sites
- Bears Ears National Monument (2016, restored 2021) demonstrates that co-management can work
- New Zealand's Te Urewera Act (2014) provides an international model for recognizing Indigenous land as a legal entity

### Cost Estimate

- **Federal co-management programs**: $30-50 million annually
- **Sacred sites inventory and documentation**: $20 million (one-time)
- **Enforcement and monitoring**: $15-20 million annually
- **Total annual cost**: $45-70 million

### Implementation Timeline

- **Year 1**: Enact Sacred Sites Protection Act with tribal consultation requirements
- **Years 1-3**: Conduct comprehensive sacred sites inventory with tribal guidance
- **Years 3-5**: Establish co-management agreements for priority sites
- **Ongoing**: Continuous monitoring and enforcement

### Key Features

- Federal agency obligation to protect identified sacred sites, not merely consult
- Tribal standing to challenge sacred site threats in federal court
- Co-management authority for sacred sites on federal land
- Conservation easement and purchase programs for sacred sites on private land
- Criminal penalties for intentional desecration of sacred sites

## Solution 5: Tribal Resource Sovereignty Act

### Description

Reform federal resource management on tribal lands to give tribes primary authority over mineral, timber, and renewable energy development, with streamlined approval processes and fair revenue sharing.

### Evidence Base

- BIA lease approval delays cost tribes an estimated $200+ million annually in lost revenue (DOI IG, 2021)
- Tribes receive less favorable royalty rates than private landowners for comparable resources (GAO-20-375, 2020)
- The Indian Tribal Energy Development and Self-Determination Act (2005, amended 2017) established Tribal Energy Resource Agreements (TERAs) but only 2 tribes have completed the process due to regulatory complexity
- The Southern Ute Indian Tribe's successful energy development demonstrates tribal management capacity (Southern Ute Growth Fund, 2023)

### Cost Estimate

- **TERA simplification and support**: $15 million annually
- **Tribal energy development grants**: $50 million annually
- **Technical assistance programs**: $20 million annually
- **Total annual cost**: $85 million

### Implementation Timeline

- **Year 1**: Simplify TERA approval process; remove barriers to tribal energy self-determination
- **Years 2-3**: Support 20+ tribes in developing energy management capacity
- **Years 3-5**: Full tribal authority over resource development on trust land

## Solution 6: Reservation Boundary Clarity Act

### Description

Resolve long-standing disputes over reservation boundaries through a systematic federal process, codifying boundaries and providing legal certainty for tribes, states, and private parties.

### Evidence Base

- The *McGirt v. Oklahoma* decision (2020) demonstrated that many reservation boundaries were never properly disestablished despite decades of contrary assumption
- Post-McGirt litigation has consumed enormous federal, state, and tribal resources (Oklahoma AG reports, 2021-2024)
- GAO has documented at least 50 reservations with unresolved boundary disputes (GAO-18-112, 2018)

### Cost Estimate

- **Federal survey and boundary determination**: $50 million over 5 years
- **Legal resolution support**: $30 million over 5 years
- **Total**: $80 million over 5 years

### Implementation Timeline

- **Years 1-2**: Establish federal boundary commission; begin surveying disputed boundaries
- **Years 3-5**: Issue binding boundary determinations; implement transitional agreements

---

## Solution 7: Anti-Fractionation Probate Reform

### Description

Reform the Indian probate system to prevent further fractionation through mandatory consolidation mechanisms, simplified inheritance rules, and tribal buyback authority at probate.

### Evidence Base

- The American Indian Probate Reform Act (AIPRA, 2004) attempted to address fractionation through probate but has been only partially effective
- Average BIA probate processing time remains 2-4 years (BIA, 2023)
- Without probate reform, fractionation will continue to worsen regardless of other interventions

### Cost Estimate

- **BIA probate staffing increase**: $20 million annually
- **Tribal probate program grants**: $15 million annually
- **Total annual cost**: $35 million

### Implementation Timeline

- **Year 1**: Enact AIPRA amendments strengthening consolidation at probate
- **Years 2-3**: Implement tribal probate authority programs
- **Years 3-5**: Achieve 12-month average probate processing time

## International Models

### Canada: Additions to Reserve (ATR) Policy

- **Mechanism**: Federal policy allowing First Nations to add land to their reserves
- **Timeline**: Revised policy (2016) targets 2-year processing for most applications
- **Lessons**: Demonstrates that faster processing is achievable; Canadian ATR still has significant delays but is improving

### New Zealand: Treaty of Waitangi Settlements

- **Mechanism**: Comprehensive treaty settlement process returning land and compensation to Maori iwi
- **Scale**: Over NZ$2.4 billion in settlements since 1992; significant land returned
- **Lessons**: Shows that a systematic, negotiated approach can address historical land claims at scale; process is slow but produces durable agreements

### Australia: Native Title Act (1993)

- **Mechanism**: Legal framework recognizing Indigenous land rights based on traditional connection to land
- **Scale**: Over 40% of Australian land area now subject to native title determinations
- **Lessons**: Demonstrates the feasibility of recognizing Indigenous land rights over large areas; process is litigation-heavy and could be improved through negotiation-first approaches

## Solution Sequencing

### Priority Order

1. **Carcieri fix and trust process reform** (immediate legislative priority -- removes barriers for all subsequent actions)
2. **Anti-fractionation probate reform** (stops the problem from growing)
3. **Comprehensive fractionation resolution** (addresses existing problem)
4. **Trust management reform** (builds on established self-governance framework)
5. **Sacred sites protection** (requires sustained advocacy and coalition-building)
6. **Reservation boundary clarity** (resolves jurisdictional uncertainty)
7. **Tribal resource sovereignty** (builds on trust management reform)

### Dependencies

- Fractionation resolution depends on probate reform to prevent recurrence
- Trust management reform builds on HEARTH Act and self-governance compact experience
- Resource sovereignty requires trust management reform as a prerequisite
- Sacred site protection can proceed independently but benefits from broader land rights momentum
