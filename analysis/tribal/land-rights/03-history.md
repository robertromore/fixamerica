# Tribal Land Rights: History

## Overview

The history of tribal land rights in the United States is a story of dispossession on a continental scale -- the systematic transfer of approximately 1.5 billion acres from Indigenous nations to the federal government, states, and private interests through treaties, forced removal, legislative schemes, and outright seizure. Understanding this history is essential because virtually every contemporary challenge in tribal land policy -- fractionation, trust management failures, jurisdictional confusion, and sacred site threats -- traces directly to specific historical policies and their intended consequences.

---

## Era 1: Pre-Contact and Early Colonial Period (Pre-1776)

### Indigenous Land Stewardship

Prior to European contact, Indigenous nations exercised sovereignty over the entirety of what is now the continental United States, Alaska, and Hawaii. Land was managed through diverse systems ranging from individual family plots to communal tribal territories, with sophisticated governance structures determining land use, resource allocation, and territorial boundaries.

### Colonial Land Policies

European colonial powers employed several strategies to acquire Indigenous lands:

- **Doctrine of Discovery (1493)**: Papal bulls and subsequent European legal theory claimed that "discovery" gave European nations sovereignty over Indigenous lands
- **Treaty-making**: Colonial governments negotiated land cessions, often under duress
- **Purchase and trade**: Some lands were acquired through transactions, though the concept of absolute land alienation was often foreign to Indigenous parties
- **Military conquest**: Colonial expansion frequently involved armed conflict and forced displacement

### Key Developments

- **1608**: Powhatan Confederacy grants limited land use rights to Jamestown colonists
- **1763**: Royal Proclamation establishes boundary line restricting colonial settlement west of the Appalachians
- **1768**: Treaty of Fort Stanwix cedes large portions of Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) territory

## Era 2: Treaty Era (1776-1871)

### Constitutional Framework

The U.S. Constitution established the federal government's authority over Indian affairs through the Indian Commerce Clause (Art. I, Sec. 8, Cl. 3) and the Treaty Clause (Art. II, Sec. 2, Cl. 2). Treaties with tribal nations were ratified by the Senate and given the same legal standing as treaties with foreign nations.

### Major Land Policies

- **Northwest Ordinance (1787)**: Stated that Indigenous lands "shall never be taken from them without their consent" -- a promise promptly and repeatedly broken
- **Trade and Intercourse Acts (1790-1834)**: Established that land transfers from tribes could occur only through federal treaties, not state or private transactions
- **Indian Removal Act (1830)**: Authorized forced relocation of southeastern tribes to Indian Territory (present-day Oklahoma), resulting in the Trail of Tears and similar forced marches

### Scale of Treaty Land Cessions

| Period | Approximate Acreage Ceded | Major Treaties |
|--------|--------------------------|----------------|
| 1778-1800 | 100+ million acres | Treaty of Fort Stanwix (1784), Treaty of Greenville (1795) |
| 1800-1830 | 200+ million acres | Treaty of Fort Wayne (1809), Treaty of Indian Springs (1825) |
| 1830-1850 | 300+ million acres | Treaty of Dancing Rabbit Creek (1830), Treaty of New Echota (1835) |
| 1850-1871 | 400+ million acres | Treaty of Fort Laramie (1851, 1868), Treaty of Medicine Lodge (1867) |

*Source: National Archives Treaty Records; Francis Paul Prucha, "American Indian Treaties" (1994)*

### End of Treaty-Making

In 1871, Congress enacted the Indian Appropriations Act rider ending treaty-making with tribes, though existing treaties remained in force. This shifted federal Indian policy from negotiated agreements to unilateral congressional legislation.

## Era 3: Allotment and Assimilation (1887-1934)

### The Dawes Act (1887)

The General Allotment Act (Dawes Act) was the most devastating single piece of legislation for tribal land holdings. It authorized the division of communal reservation lands into individual allotments:

- **Individual allotments**: 160 acres to heads of household, 80 acres to others
- **"Surplus" lands**: Remaining reservation lands declared "surplus" and opened to non-Indian settlement
- **Trust period**: 25-year trust period on allotments, after which land was converted to fee simple and became taxable
- **Citizenship tie**: U.S. citizenship offered to allottees who "adopted the habits of civilized life"

### Land Loss Under Allotment

| Mechanism | Acreage Lost | Percentage of Pre-Allotment Land |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------------------|
| "Surplus" land sales | 60 million acres | 43% |
| Fee patenting and tax forfeiture | 27 million acres | 20% |
| Fraud and coercion | 3+ million acres | 2% |
| **Total loss (1887-1934)** | **~90 million acres** | **~65%** |

*Source: National Congress of American Indians, 2023; Delos Sacket Otis, "The Dawes Act and the Allotment of Indian Lands" (1934)*

### The Burke Act (1906)

Amended the Dawes Act to allow the Secretary of the Interior to issue fee patents to "competent" Indians before the 25-year trust period expired. Under Commissioner of Indian Affairs Cato Sells' "Declaration of Policy" (1917), thousands of allottees were issued fee patents without request or consent, resulting in rapid land loss through taxation and sale.

### Curtis Act (1898)

Extended allotment to the Five Civilized Tribes in Indian Territory, which had been exempt from the Dawes Act, dismantling their governmental structures and communal land systems.

## Era 4: Indian Reorganization and Early Trust Reform (1934-1953)

### Indian Reorganization Act (1934)

The IRA (Wheeler-Howard Act) reversed allotment policy:

- Ended further allotment of tribal lands
- Extended existing trust periods indefinitely
- Authorized the Secretary of the Interior to acquire land in trust for tribes
- Provided for tribal reorganization and self-governance
- Established a revolving credit fund for tribal development

### Land Restoration Under IRA

Between 1934 and 1953, approximately 2 million acres were restored to tribal ownership through trust acquisitions. However, this represented only a small fraction of allotment-era losses.

---

## Era 5: Termination Era (1953-1968)

### House Concurrent Resolution 108 (1953)

Congress declared its intent to terminate the federal-tribal relationship for certain tribes, making them subject to state law and ending trust protections. Over 100 tribes were terminated.

### Land Consequences of Termination

| Tribe | Year Terminated | Land Lost | Outcome |
|-------|----------------|-----------|---------|
| Menominee (WI) | 1961 | 234,000 acres (converted to county) | Restored 1973 |
| Klamath (OR) | 1961 | 862,000 acres (sold) | Partially restored 1986 |
| Mixed-Blood Utes (UT) | 1961 | Share of reservation assets distributed | Ongoing claims |
| 44 California rancherias | 1958-1970 | 4,600+ acres | Many restored 1983+ |

*Source: Charles Wilkinson, "Blood Struggle" (2005)*

## Era 6: Self-Determination and Land Restoration (1968-Present)

### Key Legislation

- **Indian Civil Rights Act (1968)**: Applied modified Bill of Rights protections in tribal courts
- **Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act (1975)**: Allowed tribes to contract for federal services
- **American Indian Religious Freedom Act (1978)**: Policy protecting access to sacred sites
- **Indian Land Consolidation Act (1983)**: Attempted to address fractionation (partially struck down by Supreme Court)
- **HEARTH Act (2012)**: Authorized tribes to approve surface leases without BIA approval
- **Helping Expedite and Advance Responsible Tribal Homeownership (HEARTH) Act (2012)**: Streamlined tribal land leasing

### Cobell Litigation (1996-2009)

The landmark class action *Cobell v. Salazar* exposed massive federal mismanagement of Individual Indian Money (IIM) trust accounts:

- Filed 1996 by Elouise Cobell (Blackfeet Nation)
- Documented $137+ billion in unaccounted trust revenue over 100+ years
- Settled in 2009 for $3.4 billion, including $1.9 billion for the Land Buy-Back Program
- Led to creation of the Office of the Special Trustee for American Indians

### McGirt and Its Aftermath (2020-Present)

*McGirt v. Oklahoma* (2020) affirmed that the Muscogee (Creek) reservation was never disestablished by Congress, returning significant portions of eastern Oklahoma to recognized reservation status. Subsequent cases extended similar recognition to the Cherokee, Chickasaw, Choctaw, and Seminole nations.

## Turning Points

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1787 | Northwest Ordinance | Established (unmet) principle of Indigenous land consent |
| 1830 | Indian Removal Act | Authorized forced relocation from ancestral lands |
| 1871 | End of treaty-making | Shifted land policy from negotiation to legislation |
| 1887 | Dawes Act | Allotment caused loss of 90 million acres |
| 1934 | Indian Reorganization Act | Ended allotment; began trust land restoration |
| 1953 | HCR 108 (Termination) | Dissolved tribal land bases and trust protections |
| 1975 | Self-Determination Act | Empowered tribal control over programs and resources |
| 2009 | Cobell Settlement | Addressed trust mismanagement; funded land consolidation |
| 2020 | McGirt v. Oklahoma | Reaffirmed reservation boundaries and tribal jurisdiction |

## Lessons from History

1. **Federal promises require enforcement mechanisms**: From the Northwest Ordinance to modern treaties, land protection promises without enforcement have been consistently broken.

2. **Allotment was intentionally destructive**: The Dawes Act was explicitly designed to break up communal land bases and force assimilation, and its architects openly stated this purpose.

3. **Land loss and political power loss are inseparable**: Every era of land dispossession corresponded to diminished tribal political influence and self-governance capacity.

4. **Restoration is possible but slow**: From the IRA through the Buy-Back Program, land restoration has occurred but at a pace vastly slower than the original dispossession.

5. **Litigation can drive reform**: The Cobell case and McGirt decision demonstrate that legal action can produce systemic change, but only after decades of effort.

6. **Termination's legacy persists**: Tribes terminated in the 1950s-1960s are still working to restore their land bases and governmental authority.
