# Tribal Land Rights: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative approach to tribal land rights reform requires action in five areas: (1) resolving the Carcieri uncertainty that limits trust acquisitions for some tribes, (2) streamlining the land-into-trust process, (3) establishing a permanent fractionation resolution program, (4) enacting enforceable sacred sites protections, and (5) reforming trust management to give tribes greater control over their lands and resources. This document provides draft legislation, regulatory frameworks, and legal analysis for each area.

## Federal Legislation

### The Carcieri Fix and Trust Land Restoration Act

**Purpose**: Confirm the Secretary of the Interior's authority to take land into trust for all federally recognized tribes, regardless of when they received federal recognition, resolving the uncertainty created by *Carcieri v. Salazar* (2009).

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Carcieri Fix and Trust Land
Restoration Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
(a) the Indian Reorganization Act of 1934 (25 U.S.C. 5101
et seq.) was intended to apply to all federally recognized Indian
tribes;
(b) the Supreme Court's decision in Carcieri v. Salazar, 555
U.S. 379 (2009), created an unjust two-tier system based on the
historical accident of a tribe's status in 1934;
(c) the Carcieri decision disproportionately affects tribes in
the eastern United States that were historically recognized but
may lack clear documentation of their 1934 status;
(d) all federally recognized tribes have an inherent right to
a homeland and the ability to restore their land base.

SEC. 3. AMENDMENT TO THE INDIAN REORGANIZATION ACT.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 19 of the Act of June 18, 1934 (25
U.S.C. 5129), is amended by striking "any recognized Indian
tribe now under Federal jurisdiction" and inserting "any federally
recognized Indian tribe".

(b) EFFECTIVE DATE.—The amendment made by subsection (a)
shall be effective as if included in the Act of June 18, 1934 (25
U.S.C. 5101 et seq.), on the date of its enactment.

(c) RATIFICATION.—Any land taken into trust by the Secretary
of the Interior pursuant to the Act of June 18, 1934, before the
date of enactment of this Act is hereby ratified and confirmed.

SEC. 4. TRUST LAND ACQUISITION PROCESS REFORM.

(a) PROCESSING TIMELINES.—The Secretary of the Interior shall—
    (1) process uncontested applications for land to be taken
    into trust within 180 days of receipt of a complete
    application;
    (2) process contested applications within 365 days of
    receipt of a complete application;
    (3) publish quarterly reports on application processing
    times and backlogs.

(b) CATEGORICAL EXCLUSIONS.—The Secretary shall establish
categorical exclusions from the full trust acquisition process
for—
    (1) acquisitions of parcels of 100 acres or less within
    existing reservation boundaries;
    (2) acquisitions of land contiguous to existing trust land;
    (3) acquisitions of land previously held in trust that was
    removed from trust through the allotment or termination
    process.

(c) REVENUE-SHARING NEGOTIATIONS.—
    (1) For acquisitions of more than 500 acres outside
    existing reservation boundaries, the Secretary shall
    require that the applicant tribe negotiate in good faith
    with affected state and local governments regarding
    revenue-sharing or service delivery agreements.
    (2) Such negotiations shall not delay the trust acquisition
    process beyond the timelines established in subsection (a).

SEC. 5. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated to the Secretary of the
Interior $50,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2032
to carry out this Act.
```

**Explanation**: This bill resolves the Carcieri problem by amending the IRA's definition of "Indian" to encompass all federally recognized tribes, with retroactive effect. It also reforms the trust acquisition process by establishing mandatory processing timelines, creating categorical exclusions for routine acquisitions, and requiring revenue-sharing negotiations for large off-reservation acquisitions to address legitimate state and local concerns.

**Potential Challenges**: State governments and gaming competitors will oppose the Carcieri fix. Constitutional challenges based on the retroactivity provision are possible. The revenue-sharing requirement may face tribal opposition as a concession to state interests.

**Refinements**: If retroactivity is struck down, the prospective application alone would still resolve the issue for future acquisitions. The revenue-sharing provision could be made voluntary rather than mandatory if tribal opposition is strong.

### The Comprehensive Fractionation Resolution Act

**Purpose**: Establish a permanent, tribally directed program to consolidate fractionated ownership interests on Indian trust land, replacing the expired Cobell Land Buy-Back Program.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Comprehensive Fractionation
Resolution Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that—
    (1) the allotment policy initiated by the General Allotment
    Act of 1887 created a fractionation crisis affecting over
    4 million ownership interests on 245,000 tracts of Indian
    trust land;
    (2) the Land Buy-Back Program established under the Cobell
    Settlement consolidated 3.1 million equivalent acres but
    left millions of fractionated interests unresolved;
    (3) fractionation costs the Bureau of Indian Affairs an
    estimated $50-60 million annually in administrative costs
    and prevents productive use of affected trust land;
    (4) without comprehensive intervention, fractionation will
    continue to worsen through inheritance.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to establish a
permanent, tribally directed land consolidation program to
resolve fractionation and prevent its recurrence.

SEC. 3. PERMANENT LAND CONSOLIDATION FUND.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established in the Treasury of the
United States a fund to be known as the "Indian Land
Consolidation Fund".

(b) MANDATORY APPROPRIATION.—There is appropriated to the Fund
$400,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2042.

(c) USE OF FUNDS.—Amounts in the Fund shall be available to—
    (1) purchase fractionated ownership interests from willing
    sellers at fair market value;
    (2) provide grants to tribal governments for land
    consolidation programs;
    (3) fund tribal land planning and management capacity;
    (4) support probate reform implementation.

SEC. 4. TRIBAL CONSOLIDATION AUTHORITY.

(a) TRIBAL PRIORITY SETTING.—Each tribe with fractionated
interests within its reservation shall develop a consolidation
plan identifying priority tracts and acquisition strategies.

(b) TRIBAL ACQUISITION AUTHORITY.—Tribes may acquire
fractionated interests directly, without BIA intermediation,
using Fund grants under standards established by the Secretary.

(c) TRIBAL BUYBACK AT PROBATE.—At the probate of any individual
Indian trust allotment, the tribe in whose reservation the
allotment is located shall have the right of first refusal to
acquire any fractionated interest at fair market value.

SEC. 5. PROBATE REFORM AMENDMENTS.

(a) MINIMUM INTEREST THRESHOLD.—Any undivided interest in
trust land representing less than 2 percent of the total
ownership shall, at probate, be offered to the tribe for
purchase at fair market value. If the tribe declines to purchase,
the interest shall escheat to the tribe.

(b) SIMPLIFIED INHERITANCE.—The Secretary shall promulgate
regulations establishing simplified inheritance rules for
fractionated interests, including the designation of a single
heir for interests below the minimum threshold.

SEC. 6. REPORTING.

The Secretary shall submit annual reports to Congress on Fund
expenditures, interests consolidated, and the rate of new
fractionation.

SEC. 7. SUNSET AND REVIEW.

This Act shall be reviewed by Congress no later than fiscal
year 2037. If the Secretary certifies that fractionation has
been reduced to fewer than 500,000 interests, mandatory
appropriations may be reduced proportionally.
```

**Explanation**: This bill establishes a permanent, mandatory-funded program to resolve fractionation, with tribal control over prioritization and acquisition. The probate reform provisions prevent new fractionation by establishing minimum interest thresholds below which interests escheat to tribes. The 15-year funding horizon reflects the scale of the problem.

**Potential Challenges**: The mandatory appropriation of $400 million annually will face budget process opposition. The escheat provision may face Fifth Amendment takings challenges, though the Supreme Court in *Babbitt v. Youpee* (1997) suggested that reasonable escheat provisions could survive constitutional scrutiny if they provide compensation.

**Refinements**: If mandatory appropriations are politically infeasible, a tiered approach could authorize discretionary appropriations for the first 5 years with mandatory funding triggered by demonstrated program effectiveness. The escheat threshold could be raised from 2% to 5% to address takings concerns.

### The Sacred Sites Protection Act

**Purpose**: Establish enforceable federal protections for tribally identified sacred sites on federal land, with co-management authority and acquisition programs for sacred sites on private land.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Sacred Sites Protection Act".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

As used in this Act—
(a) "Sacred site" means a specific, discrete, narrowly
delineated location on federal land that is identified by an
Indian tribe or Native Hawaiian organization as sacred by virtue
of its established religious significance to, or ceremonial use
by, an Indian religion.
(b) "Federal land managing agency" means any federal agency
with administrative jurisdiction over federal land.
(c) "Tribal co-management" means a cooperative agreement
between a federal agency and one or more tribes for the joint
management of federal land.

SEC. 3. FEDERAL AGENCY OBLIGATIONS.

(a) PROTECTION REQUIREMENT.—Each federal land managing agency
shall, to the maximum extent practicable and not clearly
inconsistent with essential agency functions—
    (1) avoid adversely affecting the physical integrity of
    identified sacred sites;
    (2) protect the confidentiality of sacred site locations
    where requested by the identifying tribe;
    (3) provide reasonable access to sacred sites for
    traditional cultural and religious purposes.

(b) CONSULTATION.—Before taking any action that may affect an
identified sacred site, a federal agency shall engage in
meaningful, government-to-government consultation with affected
tribes, with a minimum 90-day consultation period.

(c) CO-MANAGEMENT AUTHORITY.—Federal land managing agencies
are authorized to enter into co-management agreements with
tribes for the management of federal land containing sacred
sites.

SEC. 4. TRIBAL STANDING.

(a) Any Indian tribe or Native Hawaiian organization may bring
a civil action in United States district court to enforce the
provisions of this Act.

(b) In any action brought under this section, the court may
grant temporary restraining orders, preliminary injunctions,
or permanent injunctions to prevent harm to sacred sites.

SEC. 5. SACRED SITES INVENTORY.

(a) The Secretary of the Interior, in consultation with
Indian tribes, shall establish and maintain a confidential
inventory of sacred sites on federal land.

(b) Identification of a sacred site for inclusion in the
inventory shall be at the sole discretion of the relevant tribe.

SEC. 6. PRIVATE LAND SACRED SITES CONSERVATION.

(a) The Secretary of the Interior is authorized to acquire,
by purchase from willing sellers or by donation, conservation
easements or fee interests in private land containing sacred
sites.

(b) There is authorized to be appropriated $25,000,000 for
each of fiscal years 2027 through 2037 for acquisitions under
this section.

SEC. 7. CRIMINAL PENALTIES.

Any person who knowingly desecrates, damages, or destroys an
identified sacred site on federal land shall be subject to a
fine of not more than $250,000, imprisonment for not more than
2 years, or both.

SEC. 8. SEVERABILITY.

If any provision of this Act is held invalid, the remainder
shall not be affected.
```

**Explanation**: This bill strengthens sacred site protections by requiring federal agencies to affirmatively protect sacred sites (not merely consult), granting tribal standing to enforce protections in court, authorizing co-management agreements, and creating a conservation program for sacred sites on private land. The criminal penalty provision deters intentional desecration.

**Potential Challenges**: The Establishment Clause could be raised against protections that appear to favor a particular religion. The "maximum extent practicable" standard is flexible enough to survive scrutiny but may be criticized as too weak by tribal advocates.

**Refinements**: The bill could be strengthened by establishing an independent Sacred Sites Commission with authority to make binding protection determinations, or by creating a categorical prohibition on federal actions that would irreversibly damage sacred sites.

### The Tribal Trust Management Reform Act

**Purpose**: Reform the trust management system to give tribes direct control over trust asset management while maintaining protections against alienation.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Tribal Trust Management Reform Act".

SEC. 2. TRIBAL TRUST MANAGEMENT COMPACTS.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—Any federally recognized Indian tribe may
enter into a compact with the Secretary of the Interior to
assume trust management functions for trust land within its
reservation or jurisdiction.

(b) SCOPE.—A trust management compact may include authority
to—
    (1) approve surface and subsurface leases without BIA
    approval;
    (2) approve rights-of-way and easements;
    (3) conduct appraisals of trust property;
    (4) manage trust funds and accounts;
    (5) process probate of trust allotments;
    (6) grant and manage permits for land use.

(c) TRUST STATUS PRESERVATION.—Land managed under a trust
management compact shall retain its trust status and shall not
be subject to alienation, state taxation, or state jurisdiction
except as otherwise provided by law.

SEC. 3. TRIBAL BONDING AUTHORITY.

(a) A tribe that has entered into a trust management compact
may issue revenue bonds secured by the revenue stream from
trust land assets managed under the compact.

(b) Such bonds shall not create a lien on the underlying
trust land itself.

SEC. 4. TRANSITION FUNDING.

There is authorized to be appropriated $30,000,000 for each
of fiscal years 2027 through 2032 to support tribal capacity
building for trust management compact implementation.

SEC. 5. OVERSIGHT AND ACCOUNTABILITY.

(a) Tribes operating under trust management compacts shall
submit annual reports to the Secretary on trust management
activities.

(b) The Secretary retains authority to reassume trust
management functions if a tribe fails to meet compact
requirements, after providing notice and an opportunity for
corrective action.
```

**Explanation**: This bill extends the self-governance compact model to trust land management, allowing tribes to assume BIA functions like lease approval, appraisal, and probate processing. The bonding authority addresses the economic development barrier of trust land not being usable as collateral, while the trust status preservation provision ensures that tribal management authority does not risk land alienation.

**Potential Challenges**: BIA resistance to transferring functions is likely. Concerns about tribal capacity and accountability will be raised. The bonding authority may need careful structuring to avoid creating pathways to land alienation.

**Refinements**: A pilot program with 10-20 tribes could demonstrate effectiveness before broader implementation. The bonding authority could be limited to specific revenue streams (e.g., mineral royalties, lease income) to reduce risk.

## Regulatory Framework

### BIA Trust Acquisition Regulations

**Existing Authority**: 25 C.F.R. Part 151

**Proposed Regulatory Changes**:

- Establish mandatory processing timelines consistent with legislation
- Create categorical exclusions for routine on-reservation acquisitions
- Standardize application requirements to reduce processing burdens
- Require annual reports on processing times by BIA regional office

### Trust Management Compact Regulations

**Existing Authority**: Indian Self-Determination Act (25 U.S.C. 5301 et seq.)

**Proposed Regulatory Changes**:

- Establish standards for trust management compact negotiations
- Define minimum tribal capacity requirements for compact eligibility
- Create model compact provisions for common trust management functions
- Establish oversight and accountability framework

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Plenary power**: Congress has clear constitutional authority to enact all proposed legislation under the Indian Commerce Clause
- **Takings Clause**: The fractionation escheat provision must provide fair market value compensation to survive Fifth Amendment scrutiny (*Babbitt v. Youpee*, 1997)
- **Establishment Clause**: Sacred sites protections must be framed as protecting religious liberty broadly, not establishing a particular religion
- **Equal protection**: All provisions are grounded in the political classification of tribal sovereignty, not racial classification (*Morton v. Mancari*, 1974; *Brackeen v. Haaland*, 2023)

### Preemption Questions

- The Carcieri fix preempts state challenges to trust acquisitions based on the 1934 date limitation
- Sacred sites protections apply to federal land and preempt conflicting federal agency actions
- Trust management compacts do not alter the federal-state jurisdictional framework

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- Sacred Sites Protection Act provides tribal standing and injunctive relief
- Trust acquisition timelines enforceable through mandamus actions
- Trust management compact performance monitored through annual reporting
- Criminal penalties for sacred site desecration enforced by DOJ

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Fractionation Resolution Act reviewed at Year 10 with potential appropriation adjustment
- Trust management compacts renewed on 5-year cycles
- Sacred sites protections permanent but subject to periodic inventory updates

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Carcieri Fix and Trust Land Restoration Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Revenue-sharing bypass | Tribes may structure acquisitions to stay below 500-acre threshold, avoiding revenue-sharing negotiations | Medium |
| Categorical exclusion abuse | Definition of "within existing reservation boundaries" may be contested for reservations with disputed boundaries | Medium |
| Processing timeline enforcement | No penalty for BIA failure to meet 180-day timeline | High |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No funding for tribal land acquisition costs | Tribes must still fund land purchases; no acquisition grants | Budget constraints |
| State litigation not addressed | States can still challenge trust decisions in court, causing delays | Judicial review requirements |
| Contested application timeline of 365 days may be insufficient | Complex cases may require longer processing | Case complexity variation |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Add deemed-approved provision if BIA fails to act within processing timeline
2. Establish tribal land acquisition grant program funded at $50 million annually
3. Provide expedited judicial review for state challenges to trust decisions
4. Allow BIA to extend contested application timeline by 180 days with tribal consent

### Sacred Sites Protection Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| "Maximum extent practicable" standard | Agencies may interpret this narrowly to justify adverse actions | High |
| Confidentiality vs. protection conflict | Keeping site locations confidential may hinder protection efforts | Medium |
| Private land gap | No mandatory protection for sacred sites on private land; only voluntary conservation | High |
| Definition of "sacred site" | Limiting definition to "specific, discrete" locations may exclude sacred landscapes | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No private land mandate | Most land in the U.S. is private; sacred sites on private land remain unprotected | Property rights concerns |
| Limited penalty range | $250,000 maximum fine may be insufficient for large-scale desecration | Political compromise |
| No monument designation authority | Act does not include expedited monument designation for threatened sites | Separation of powers |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Amend the standard to "shall protect sacred sites unless the agency demonstrates by clear and convincing evidence that no practicable alternative exists"
2. Expand the definition to include "sacred landscapes" where tribes identify broader areas of cultural significance
3. Increase criminal penalties to $1 million and 5 years for desecration causing irreversible damage
4. Create a Sacred Sites Emergency Protection provision allowing temporary protection orders

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| BIA capacity constraints | Phase implementation to match hiring and training capacity |
| Tribal capacity variation | Provide graduated compact requirements based on tribal administrative capacity |
| Political vulnerability | Codify reforms in statute rather than regulation or executive order |
| Funding uncertainty | Seek mandatory appropriations for core programs |
| Judicial hostility | Draft legislation to withstand strict scrutiny; build legislative record |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- All programs subject to GAO performance review every 3 years
- Fractionation Resolution Fund reviewed at Year 10 for continuation or adjustment
- Trust management compacts renewed on 5-year cycles with performance evaluation
- Sacred sites inventory updated annually with tribal input
