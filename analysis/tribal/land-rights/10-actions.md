# Tribal Land Rights: Actions

## Overview

Citizens can play a meaningful role in advancing tribal land rights reform through civic engagement, advocacy, education, and direct support for tribal organizations. This document provides specific, actionable steps for individuals and communities to support land rights reform at the federal, state, and local levels.

## Individual Actions

### Educate Yourself

1. **Learn whose ancestral lands you live on**: Use the Native-Land.ca interactive map to identify the Indigenous nations whose territories your community occupies
2. **Read the treaties**: Access original treaty texts at the National Archives (archives.gov) to understand the specific promises made to tribal nations in your region
3. **Understand the allotment legacy**: The Indian Land Tenure Foundation (iltf.org) provides accessible resources on fractionation and its impacts
4. **Follow tribal media**: Read Indian Country Today (indiancountrytoday.com), High Country News Indigenous Affairs coverage, and tribal newspapers

### Contact Elected Officials

Federal legislation is essential for most land rights reforms. Contact your U.S. senators and representative about:

- **Carcieri fix legislation**: Ask them to co-sponsor bills confirming trust acquisition authority for all federally recognized tribes
- **Land Buy-Back reauthorization**: Support permanent, mandatory funding for fractionation resolution
- **Sacred Sites Protection Act**: Urge support for enforceable protections for tribally identified sacred sites
- **Trust process reform**: Advocate for 180-day processing timelines for uncontested trust applications

### Template Email for Federal Legislators

```text
Subject: Support Tribal Land Rights Reform

Dear [Senator/Representative] [Last Name],

I am writing to urge your support for tribal land rights reform,
specifically:

1. Carcieri Fix Legislation: The 2009 Supreme Court decision in
Carcieri v. Salazar created an unjust two-tier system that prevents
some federally recognized tribes from restoring their land bases.
Please co-sponsor legislation confirming the Secretary of the
Interior's authority to take land into trust for all federally
recognized tribes.

2. Land Consolidation Funding: The Cobell Land Buy-Back Program
made significant progress resolving fractionation but expired in
2023 with millions of fractionated interests remaining. Please
support permanent, mandatory funding for continued land
consolidation.

3. Sacred Sites Protection: Current federal law provides no
enforceable protection for tribally identified sacred sites.
Please support legislation giving tribes standing to protect
sacred sites on federal land.

These reforms honor treaty obligations, support tribal
self-determination, and fulfill the federal government's trust
responsibility. They have bipartisan support and would benefit
the 9.7 million Americans who identify as American Indian or
Alaska Native.

Thank you for your consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your name]
[Your address]
```

## Community Actions

### At the Local Level

- **Support land-into-trust applications**: When tribal trust acquisitions are proposed in your area, attend public hearings and submit supportive comments
- **Advocate for Land Acknowledgments**: Encourage local governments, schools, and institutions to adopt Land Acknowledgment statements that recognize Indigenous peoples' connections to the land
- **Support tribal-local cooperation**: Advocate for service delivery agreements, mutual aid compacts, and revenue-sharing arrangements between tribes and local governments
- **Oppose anti-tribal ballot measures**: Anti-sovereignty ballot measures appear periodically in some states; organize opposition through education campaigns

### At the State Level

- Contact state legislators about state-tribal cooperation legislation
- Support state recognition of tribal sovereignty and land rights
- Oppose state litigation against tribal trust acquisitions
- Advocate for state revenue-sharing agreements with tribes

## Talking Points

### For Conversations with Neighbors and Community Members

1. **Treaty obligations are legal commitments**: The U.S. made binding promises in exchange for land cessions totaling over 1.5 billion acres. These are not gifts or charity -- they are legal debts.

2. **Tribal sovereignty is in the Constitution**: The Indian Commerce Clause, the Treaty Clause, and over 200 years of Supreme Court precedent recognize tribal nations as sovereign entities with a government-to-government relationship with the United States.

3. **Trust land benefits communities**: Studies show that tribal enterprises on trust land generate economic activity, employment, and tax revenue for surrounding communities that often exceed any property tax losses.

4. **Fractionation is a federal-created problem**: The federal government's allotment policy deliberately broke up communal tribal lands. The federal government has an obligation to fix the resulting fractionation crisis.

5. **Sacred sites are irreplaceable**: Once destroyed, a sacred site cannot be restored. These places have cultural and spiritual significance comparable to churches, synagogues, and mosques -- and deserve at least equivalent protection.

6. **Self-determination works**: Where tribes have been given management authority over their own lands and programs, outcomes consistently improve. The HEARTH Act and self-governance compacts demonstrate that tribal management is effective.

### For Conversations with Skeptics

- **"Why do tribes get special treatment?"** Tribal programs are based on the political relationship between sovereign nations and the United States, not on race. The Supreme Court has repeatedly affirmed this distinction, most recently in *Brackeen v. Haaland* (2023).

- **"Won't trust land hurt our tax base?"** Revenue-sharing agreements and tribal economic activity can offset tax base concerns. In Wisconsin, tribal enterprises generate $1.5 billion in annual economic activity and $65 million in state tax revenue (Harvard Project, 2017).

- **"The allotment era was a long time ago -- why does it matter now?"** The 4 million fractionated ownership interests that exist today were created by allotment policy. These interests make productive land use impossible on roughly 40% of trust land. The problem grows with each generation.

- **"Can't tribes just buy land on the open market?"** Tribes can buy fee land, but it does not have trust protections, remains subject to state taxation and jurisdiction, and does not carry the same sovereignty implications. The trust process exists precisely to restore the land base that was taken.

## Professional Actions

### For Lawyers

- Volunteer with the Native American Rights Fund or local tribal legal aid programs
- Provide pro bono representation in trust land disputes
- File amicus briefs supporting tribal land rights in federal court cases
- Advocate within bar associations for Indian law education and support

### For Educators

- Incorporate accurate Indigenous history into curricula, including land dispossession
- Use resources from the National Museum of the American Indian (NMAI) and the Smithsonian
- Invite tribal speakers and knowledge keepers to classrooms
- Support tribal college partnerships and exchanges

### For Journalists

- Cover tribal land issues with accuracy and tribal perspective
- Use Native-led media as primary sources
- Investigate federal trust management performance
- Report on the human impact of fractionation and trust land barriers
