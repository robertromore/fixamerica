# Tribal Courts: Current State

## Court Structure and Models

### Types of Tribal Court Systems

Tribal courts vary significantly in structure, reflecting the diversity of tribal governance traditions and the historical circumstances under which each tribe established its judicial system.

| Model | Description | Prevalence | Examples |
|-------|-------------|-----------|----------|
| CFR Courts (Courts of Federal Regulation) | Established and originally operated under BIA regulations (25 CFR Part 11); judges historically appointed by BIA | Declining; approximately 20-25 still operating | Some smaller tribes that have not established their own courts |
| Tribal Code Courts | Established by tribal constitution or ordinance; operate under tribally adopted codes of law | Most common; majority of 400+ tribal courts | Navajo Nation District Courts, Cherokee Nation District Court |
| Traditional/Customary Courts | Use traditional dispute resolution methods as the primary or exclusive judicial process | Small number operate exclusively; many incorporate elements | Peacemaker Courts (Navajo), various pueblo courts |
| Hybrid Courts | Combine elements of code courts with traditional practices | Increasingly common | Many tribes integrate peacemaking into code court framework |
| Intertribal Courts | Serve multiple tribes through cooperative agreements | Growing; particularly useful for smaller tribes | Northwest Intertribal Court System, Plains Indian Court of Appeals |

### Court Organization

Most tribal court systems include:

- **Trial courts**: Courts of general jurisdiction handling criminal, civil, family, and juvenile cases
- **Appellate courts**: Approximately 200 tribes have appellate courts; others use intertribal appellate bodies
- **Specialized courts**: Drug courts, wellness courts, family courts, juvenile courts, elder courts
- **Administrative tribunals**: Handle regulatory, licensing, and employment disputes

## Jurisdiction

### Criminal Jurisdiction

Tribal court criminal jurisdiction is defined by a complex web of federal statutes and Supreme Court decisions:

| Category | Tribal Court Authority | Legal Basis | Sentencing Limit |
|----------|----------------------|-------------|-----------------|
| Misdemeanors by tribal members | Full jurisdiction | Inherent sovereignty | 1 year per offense (ICRA) |
| Felonies by tribal members | Concurrent with federal (Major Crimes Act offenses) | Inherent sovereignty + 18 U.S.C. 1153 | 3 years per offense if TLOA-enhanced; otherwise 1 year |
| Non-Indian defendants (DV, dating violence, stalking, trafficking, child violence) | SDVCJ jurisdiction if tribe qualifies | VAWA 2013/2022; 25 U.S.C. 1304 | 3 years per offense |
| Non-Indian defendants (all other crimes) | No jurisdiction | *Oliphant v. Suquamish*, 435 U.S. 191 (1978) | N/A |
| Non-member Indians | Full jurisdiction | *United States v. Lara*, 541 U.S. 193 (2004) | Subject to ICRA/TLOA limits |

### Civil Jurisdiction

| Area | Tribal Court Authority | Limitations |
|------|----------------------|-------------|
| Member-to-member disputes | Full jurisdiction | None |
| Non-member activity on trust land | Limited jurisdiction | *Montana v. United States* (1981) two-part test |
| Child welfare (ICWA cases) | Transfer and concurrent jurisdiction | *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023) upheld ICWA framework |
| Contracts and commerce | Jurisdiction over activity on tribal land | May be limited for non-member parties |
| Tort claims | Jurisdiction over claims arising on tribal land | Sovereign immunity may apply |
| Regulatory enforcement | Authority over tribal regulatory framework | Limited for non-members on fee land |

### Enhanced Sentencing Under TLOA

The Tribal Law and Order Act (2010) allowed tribal courts to impose sentences of up to 3 years per offense (stackable up to 9 years for multiple offenses) if the tribe provides:

| Requirement | Detail | Implementation Status |
|-------------|--------|----------------------|
| Effective assistance of counsel | Licensed attorney for defendants facing more than 1 year | Many tribes struggle to provide due to attorney shortage |
| Law-trained judge | Presiding judge must have legal training sufficient for the position | Most larger tribes comply; some smaller tribes face challenges |
| Publicly available criminal laws | Tribal code must be published and accessible | Most tribes comply |
| Audio/video recording | Proceedings must be recorded | Implementation varies |
| Right of appeal | Defendants must have access to appellate review | Approximately 200 tribes have appellate courts |

**Implementation**: As of 2025, approximately 100 tribes have formally adopted TLOA enhanced sentencing provisions, though not all have the resources to fully implement them (Tribal Law and Policy Institute, 2025).

### VAWA Special Domestic Violence Criminal Jurisdiction

Approximately 45 tribes have implemented SDVCJ as of 2025:

| VAWA SDVCJ Requirement | Detail |
|------------------------|--------|
| Defendant ties | Non-Indian defendant must reside or be employed in tribal territory, or be spouse/intimate partner of tribal member |
| Effective counsel | Right to licensed defense attorney at tribe's expense if indigent |
| Jury composition | Fair cross-section of community, not limited to tribal members |
| Law-trained judge | Sufficient legal training |
| All ICRA protections | Full Indian Civil Rights Act rights |

**Outcomes**: No SDVCJ conviction has been overturned on due process grounds since implementation began in 2014, demonstrating that tribal courts can effectively exercise jurisdiction over non-Indians while protecting defendants' rights (NCAI SDVCJ Implementation Report, 2025).

### Tribal Self-Funding

Many tribes supplement federal funding with their own revenues:

- Gaming tribes may fund courts from casino revenues, sometimes substantially
- Non-gaming tribes, particularly smaller and more remote tribes, are almost entirely dependent on federal funding
- Court fees and fines provide minimal revenue
- The disparity between gaming and non-gaming tribes creates a two-tier system where some tribal courts are well-resourced and others are barely functional

| State court judge salary (comparison) | $140,000-$220,000 | NCSC Judicial Salary Survey, 2023 |
| Judge retention challenge | High turnover due to low pay and isolation | NAICJA, 2023 |

### Prosecutors and Defense Attorneys

- Many tribal courts lack full-time prosecutors; cases may be handled by tribal attorneys who also handle civil matters
- Public defender systems are rare; TLOA and VAWA require effective assistance of counsel but funding for defense attorneys is inadequate
- Some tribes contract with private attorneys for prosecution and defense services
- The shortage of attorneys willing to practice in remote tribal communities is chronic and worsening

### Court Staff

- Clerks, court reporters, probation officers, and administrative staff are in short supply
- Many tribal courts operate with 2-5 total staff members for all functions
- Record-keeping is often manual or uses outdated systems
- Technology infrastructure (internet, case management software, video conferencing) is lacking in many tribal courts

## Traditional and Customary Law

### Peacemaking and Restorative Justice

Many tribal courts incorporate traditional dispute resolution practices:

| Practice | Description | Notable Examples |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Peacemaking | Community-based mediation using traditional principles; focus on restoration rather than punishment | Navajo Peacemaker Courts (established 1982); most studied and replicated model |
| Talking circles | Structured dialogue involving victim, offender, family, and community | Widely used in tribal juvenile justice |
| Clan-based resolution | Disputes resolved through traditional kinship structures | Some Pueblo nations, Haudenosaunee |
| Elder consultation | Elders serve as advisors or mediators in dispute resolution | Common across many tribal courts |
| Healing to wellness courts | Substance use and mental health courts using culturally grounded treatment | Over 100 tribal wellness courts operating (Tribal Law and Policy Institute, 2023) |

### Customary Law Recognition

- Some tribal courts apply customary (unwritten traditional) law alongside or instead of written tribal codes
- The Navajo Nation has the most developed body of customary law jurisprudence, with published judicial opinions applying traditional Navajo principles
- Challenges: documenting oral legal traditions without distorting them; ensuring consistency and predictability

## Recognition and Enforcement

### Full Faith and Credit Issues

| Context | Status | Legal Basis |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| Federal courts | Must recognize tribal court orders in Indian country matters | 28 U.S.C. 1738; case law |
| State courts | Inconsistent; many states do not recognize tribal court orders | No uniform federal requirement; some states have comity statutes |
| Protective orders | Full faith and credit required under VAWA | 18 U.S.C. 2265 |
| Child custody (ICWA) | Tribal court orders recognized under ICWA | 25 U.S.C. 1911 |
| Civil judgments | Recognition varies by state | No federal requirement; tribal-state compacts in some jurisdictions |
| Criminal convictions | Recognized as predicate offenses in federal DV cases | *United States v. Bryant*, 579 U.S. 140 (2016) |

### The Recognition Problem

The lack of consistent full faith and credit for tribal court orders has practical consequences:

- Protective orders issued by tribal courts may not be enforceable when victims travel off-reservation
- Child custody determinations may not be honored by state courts
- Civil judgments (contracts, torts) may be unenforceable against non-Indian parties who leave tribal territory
- This undermines the practical authority of tribal courts and encourages forum-shopping

## Recent Developments (2024-2025)

### Federal Actions

- **VAWA 2022 SDVCJ expansion**: Extended tribal criminal jurisdiction over non-Indians to additional crime categories including child violence, stalking, sex trafficking, and assault of law enforcement
- **BIA tribal court funding**: Modest increase in FY 2024 (+4%) but remains far below estimated need
- **DOJ CTAS**: Continued competitive grant funding for tribal court improvement projects
- **Haaland v. Brackeen (2023)**: Affirmed congressional authority over Indian affairs and upheld ICWA, reinforcing the legal framework for tribal court jurisdiction

### Tribal Innovations

- **Healing to wellness courts**: Over 100 tribal wellness courts now operating, addressing substance use and mental health through culturally grounded treatment (Tribal Law and Policy Institute, 2023)
- **Technology adoption**: Some tribal courts have adopted case management systems and virtual hearing capabilities, accelerated by the COVID-19 pandemic
- **Intertribal appellate growth**: Regional intertribal appellate courts continue to expand, providing appellate services to tribes that cannot support their own appellate systems
