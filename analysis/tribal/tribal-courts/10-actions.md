# Tribal Courts: Actions

## Overview

This document outlines practical steps that citizens, organizations, and officials can take to support the strengthening of tribal courts. Actions are organized by the actor's position and capacity, from individual citizens to elected officials and legal professionals.

## Individual Citizens

### Learn and Educate

- **Understand tribal courts**: Learn how tribal courts function, the cases they handle, and the constraints they operate under -- particularly the *Oliphant* limitation and chronic underfunding
- **Study the jurisdictional gap**: Learn how the absence of tribal criminal jurisdiction over non-Indians creates a public safety crisis, particularly for Indigenous women and children
- **Challenge misconceptions**: Tribal courts are not inferior courts; they handle over 300,000 cases annually and have demonstrated due process competency through VAWA SDVCJ implementation with zero overturned convictions
- **Follow the issue**: Track tribal court legislation through the Senate Committee on Indian Affairs and House Natural Resources Committee websites

### Contact Elected Officials

- **Write to your congressional representatives** urging support for:
    - Mandatory federal funding for tribal courts at adequate levels
    - Legislation to restore full tribal criminal jurisdiction (overturn *Oliphant*)
    - Full faith and credit requirements for tribal court orders
    - Expanded funding for tribal public defenders and court personnel
    - Enhanced sentencing authority for tribal courts
- **Attend town halls** and raise tribal court funding and jurisdiction as a public safety issue
- **Register for committee hearing notifications** from the Senate Committee on Indian Affairs

### Support Tribal Justice

- **Donate to** organizations supporting tribal court development:
    - National American Indian Court Judges Association (NAICJA): <https://www.naicja.org>
    - Tribal Law and Policy Institute (TLPI): <https://www.tlpi.org>
    - Native American Rights Fund (NARF): <https://www.narf.org>
- **Share information** about tribal court issues in community discussions and on social media
- **Support candidates** who advocate for tribal court strengthening and jurisdiction restoration

### Vote and Advocate

- **Vote for candidates** who support tribal sovereignty and adequate tribal court funding
- **Sign petitions** supporting tribal court jurisdiction expansion
- **Attend public comment periods** for federal rulemaking affecting tribal justice

## Community Organizations

### Build Coalitions

- **Partner with** tribal organizations, civil rights groups, victim advocacy organizations, and law enforcement groups to build tribal court reform coalitions
- **Engage local government** in dialogue about the benefits of tribal-state court cooperation, including reducing jurisdictional gaps that allow crime to go unprosecuted
- **Host educational events** featuring tribal court judges, prosecutors, and community members

### Provide Direct Support

- **Pro bono professional services**: Attorneys, court administrators, IT professionals, and social workers can volunteer expertise to tribal courts
- **Fundraising**: Organize events to support tribal court development organizations
- **Mentorship programs**: Connect legal professionals with tribal court personnel for professional development

### Advocate for Policy Change

- **Submit organizational comments** during federal rulemaking proceedings affecting tribal courts
- **Endorse tribal court legislation** and communicate endorsements to Congress
- **Publish op-eds and articles** educating the public about the tribal court funding gap and jurisdictional limitations

## Tribal Citizens and Communities

### Engage with Your Tribal Court

- **Participate in court proceedings** as jurors, witnesses, and community members when called upon
- **Provide feedback** to tribal council and court administration on court performance and accessibility
- **Support traditional justice programs** such as peacemaking courts, talking circles, and wellness courts
- **Advocate within your tribe** for judicial independence protections, including fixed-term appointments and removal only for cause

### Build Court Capacity

- **Encourage young tribal members** to pursue legal education and careers in tribal court practice
- **Support elder participation** in traditional justice programs
- **Document traditional dispute resolution practices** to preserve and integrate customary law
- **Participate in tribal governance** by attending council meetings where court funding and structure are discussed

### Support VAWA and TLOA Implementation

- **Advocate for your tribe** to implement VAWA SDVCJ if it has not already done so
- **Support TLOA enhanced sentencing** adoption by your tribal court
- **Work with tribal leadership** to ensure adequate funding is allocated to meet TLOA and VAWA requirements

## Legal Professionals

### Provide Legal Support

- **Pro bono representation**: Serve as defense counsel, prosecutor, or advisor in tribal courts, particularly for tribes that cannot afford full-time legal staff
- **Amicus briefs**: File briefs in cases involving tribal court jurisdiction and full faith and credit issues
- **Legal research**: Contribute research on tribal court reform options, comparative jurisdiction models, and constitutional issues
- **Litigation**: Support challenges to state court refusals to recognize tribal court orders

### Develop the Field

- **Teach Indian law**: Law professors should include tribal court systems in constitutional law, criminal procedure, and civil procedure courses
- **Supervise tribal court clinics**: Law schools should establish or expand clinics placing students in tribal courts
- **Publish scholarship**: Research and publish on tribal court effectiveness, traditional justice outcomes, and jurisdictional reform
- **Mentor tribal court practitioners**: Provide mentorship to tribal court judges and attorneys, particularly those new to the field

### Policy Advocacy

- **Draft model legislation** for tribal court funding, full faith and credit, and jurisdiction restoration
- **Provide expert testimony** at congressional hearings on tribal court issues
- **Engage bar associations**: Advocate within state and national bar associations for resolutions supporting tribal court recognition and funding

## Academics and Researchers

### Research Contributions

- **Study tribal court outcomes**: Conduct empirical research comparing tribal and state court outcomes, including recidivism rates, case clearance rates, and participant satisfaction
- **Evaluate traditional justice programs**: Research the effectiveness of peacemaking, wellness courts, and restorative justice programs in tribal courts
- **Analyze jurisdictional gaps**: Document the public safety consequences of the *Oliphant* limitation and other jurisdictional constraints
- **Develop comparative models**: Study court systems in other nations that have successfully integrated Indigenous justice practices

### Expert Services

- **Serve as expert witnesses** in cases involving tribal court competency and jurisdiction
- **Provide training** for tribal court judges and staff through NAICJA, TLPI, and law school programs
- **Peer review** proposed legislation and regulations affecting tribal courts

## Elected Officials

### Federal Level

- **Introduce or cosponsor** legislation for mandatory tribal court funding at levels recommended by the Indian Law and Order Commission
- **Introduce or cosponsor** the Tribal Jurisdiction Restoration Act to overturn *Oliphant*
- **Introduce or cosponsor** the Tribal Court Full Faith and Credit Act
- **Request increased appropriations** for BIA tribal court programs and DOJ CTAS tribal court grants
- **Hold oversight hearings** on tribal court funding adequacy, TLOA implementation progress, and VAWA SDVCJ outcomes
- **Support student loan forgiveness** for attorneys and judges serving in tribal courts

### State Level

- **Enact state full faith and credit statutes** recognizing tribal court orders
- **Establish tribal-state judicial forums** for coordination between state and tribal courts
- **Appropriate state funds** for tribal-state court cooperation programs
- **Pass state resolutions** supporting federal tribal court funding and jurisdiction legislation
- **Require training** for state court judges on tribal court recognition and cooperation

### Local Level

- **Cooperate with tribal courts** on cross-jurisdictional law enforcement and court matters
- **Recognize tribal court protective orders** in local law enforcement operations
- **Support tribal court access** to local resources such as detention facilities through intergovernmental agreements
- **Include tribal court representatives** in local justice planning initiatives
