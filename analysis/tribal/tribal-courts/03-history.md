# Tribal Courts: History

## Overview

The history of tribal courts is a story of Indigenous self-governance enduring through centuries of colonial suppression, federal imposition, and institutional neglect. From pre-contact justice systems to modern hybrid courts blending traditional practices with contemporary legal requirements, tribal courts have continuously evolved in response to -- and often in defiance of -- external pressures that have sought to eliminate, replace, or constrain Indigenous judicial authority.

---

## Pre-Colonial Justice Systems (Before 1492)

### Indigenous Legal Traditions

Before European contact, Indigenous nations maintained sophisticated systems of law and dispute resolution:

- **Customary law**: Unwritten but well-understood rules governing behavior, obligations, and dispute resolution, passed through oral tradition and cultural practice
- **Clan-based justice**: Many nations (including the Haudenosaunee, Cherokee, Creek, and Choctaw) used clan systems to adjudicate disputes and maintain social order
- **Restorative approaches**: Most Indigenous justice traditions emphasized restoring relationships and community harmony rather than punishment
- **Gender and kinship roles**: In matrilineal societies, women often held significant authority in dispute resolution and governance
- **Councils of elders**: Decision-making bodies composed of respected community leaders who resolved disputes through deliberation and consensus

### Key Characteristics

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Oral tradition | Laws and precedent transmitted through storytelling, ceremony, and mentorship |
| Community participation | Justice was a community function, not delegated to specialized institutions |
| Restoration over punishment | Focus on making the victim whole and reintegrating the offender |
| Spiritual dimension | Justice frequently incorporated spiritual practices and beliefs |
| Adaptability | Systems evolved in response to changing conditions while maintaining core principles |

## Colonial and Early Federal Period (1492-1871)

### Imposition of European Legal Systems

European colonizers imposed their legal systems on Indigenous peoples while simultaneously acknowledging tribal authority when convenient:

| Period | Development | Impact on Tribal Justice |
|--------|------------|--------------------------|
| 1600s-1700s | Colonial courts assert jurisdiction over interactions between colonists and Indigenous people | Tribal internal justice systems generally left intact for intra-tribal matters |
| 1778-1871 | Treaty era; 374 treaties ratified | Many treaties recognized tribal judicial authority; others imposed federal requirements |
| 1832 | *Worcester v. Georgia* | Chief Justice Marshall recognized tribal nations as "distinct political communities" with inherent sovereignty |
| 1849 | BIA transferred to Interior Department | Civilian administration replaced military oversight of tribal affairs |

### Early Federal Recognition

- The Marshall Trilogy (*Johnson v. M'Intosh* (1823), *Cherokee Nation v. Georgia* (1831), *Worcester v. Georgia* (1832)) established that tribal nations possess inherent sovereignty predating the Constitution
- This sovereignty included the authority to administer justice within tribal territories
- The practical reality, however, was increasing federal encroachment on tribal judicial authority

## Courts of Indian Offenses Era (1883-1934)

### BIA-Imposed Courts

In 1883, Secretary of the Interior Henry Teller established the Courts of Indian Offenses (also known as CFR Courts) through administrative directive:

**Purpose**: To suppress Indigenous cultural practices and accelerate assimilation

**Structure**:

- Judges appointed by BIA Indian agents
- Applied the Code of Indian Offenses, which criminalized traditional practices including:
    - Sun dances and other religious ceremonies
    - Plural marriage
    - Traditional healing practices
    - Gift-giving ceremonies (potlatch)
    - "Immorality" broadly defined by BIA agents

**Impact**:

| Aspect | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Sovereignty | Undermined traditional justice systems by imposing BIA-controlled courts |
| Culture | Used courts as tools of cultural suppression; criminalized religious and cultural practices |
| Authority | Judges were accountable to BIA, not to tribal communities |
| Legitimacy | Created institutions that many Indigenous people viewed as instruments of colonial control |
| Precedent | Established the template for federally regulated tribal courts that persists in some form today |

### The Major Crimes Act (1885)

Following *Ex parte Crow Dog* (1883), in which the Supreme Court recognized tribal jurisdiction over an intra-Indian murder, Congress passed the Major Crimes Act:

- Asserted federal jurisdiction over seven (later expanded to 16) major crimes committed by Indians in Indian Country
- Removed the most serious criminal cases from tribal authority
- Signaled congressional intent to control tribal criminal justice
- Created the dual jurisdiction system that persists today

## Allotment and Assimilation Era (1887-1934)

### Impact on Tribal Justice

| Policy | Impact on Tribal Courts |
|--------|------------------------|
| Dawes Act (1887) | Allotment shattered communal land base, complicating tribal jurisdiction over checkerboard territory |
| Boarding schools | Removed children from communities, disrupting intergenerational transmission of customary law and justice traditions |
| Suppression of traditional governance | BIA agents actively undermined tribal governing bodies that operated courts |
| Assimilation pressure | Push toward individual property ownership and American citizenship undermined communal justice systems |

### Resistance

Despite intense pressure, many tribal justice traditions survived:

- Traditional practices continued in secret where they had been banned
- Some tribes maintained parallel systems -- one visible to BIA agents, another operating within the community
- Oral transmission of legal traditions continued through families and clans

---

## Indian Reorganization and Self-Governance (1934-1968)

### Indian Reorganization Act (1934)

The IRA (Wheeler-Howard Act) marked a shift toward tribal self-governance:

- Ended allotment and encouraged tribal self-organization
- Many tribes adopted constitutions and established formal tribal court systems under IRA frameworks
- Courts established under IRA constitutions were tribal institutions, though often modeled on American court structures
- 174 tribes adopted constitutions under the IRA (BIA, 2023)

### Impact on Courts

| Development | Significance |
|-------------|-------------|
| Constitutional courts | Tribes established courts under their own constitutions, replacing CFR courts in many cases |
| Written tribal codes | Tribes began codifying their laws, creating more formal legal frameworks |
| Trained judges | Some tribes began requiring legal training for judges |
| Continued BIA oversight | Despite IRA, BIA retained significant influence over tribal court operations and funding |

### Termination Era (1945-1968)

The termination era reversed many IRA gains:

- House Concurrent Resolution 108 (1953) declared intent to terminate federal-tribal relationships
- 109 tribes were terminated, eliminating their court systems along with all governmental functions
- Public Law 280 (1953) transferred criminal jurisdiction to six states without tribal consent, undermining tribal court authority in those states
- Urban relocation (1952-1972) moved Indigenous people away from communities where tribal courts operated

## Modern Tribal Courts Era (1968-Present)

### Indian Civil Rights Act (1968)

The ICRA established the modern framework for tribal court operations:

| ICRA Provision | Impact |
|----------------|--------|
| Applied most Bill of Rights protections to tribal governments | Required tribal courts to provide due process, equal protection, jury trial, and other protections |
| Exempted Establishment Clause and right to appointed counsel | Recognized tribal governmental uniqueness; no requirement for government-funded counsel |
| Habeas corpus | Allowed federal court review of tribal detention through habeas petitions |
| Sentencing limit | Originally capped tribal court sentences at 6 months (later raised to 1 year, then 3 years) |
| Sovereign immunity preserved | Tribal sovereign immunity was not waived |

### Indian Self-Determination Act (1975)

Allowed tribes to contract BIA functions, including court operations:

- Tribes began operating their own court systems with federal funding
- Self-governance compacts gave tribes greater flexibility in court design
- Some tribes expanded courts significantly; others remained resource-constrained

### *Oliphant v. Suquamish Indian Tribe* (1978)

The single most consequential decision for tribal courts:

- Stripped tribal courts of criminal jurisdiction over non-Indians
- Created a class of defendants immune from tribal prosecution regardless of where or what crime was committed
- Has never been overturned; remains the primary limitation on tribal court criminal authority
- VAWA SDVCJ (2013, 2022) created narrow statutory exceptions

### Tribal Justice Acts

| Law | Year | Key Provisions |
|-----|------|---------------|
| Indian Tribal Justice Act | 1993 | Authorized $58.4 million annually for tribal courts; never fully funded (actual: ~$10 million in early years) |
| Indian Tribal Justice Technical and Legal Assistance Act | 2000 | Authorized training and technical assistance for tribal courts |
| Tribal Law and Order Act | 2010 | Increased sentencing authority to 3 years; required federal prosecution accountability; mandated defendant rights protections |
| VAWA 2013 | 2013 | Created Special Domestic Violence Criminal Jurisdiction over non-Indians |
| VAWA 2022 | 2022 | Expanded SDVCJ to additional crime categories |

### Key Supreme Court Decisions Affecting Tribal Courts

| Case | Year | Holding | Impact on Tribal Courts |
|------|------|---------|------------------------|
| *Ex parte Crow Dog* | 1883 | Recognized tribal jurisdiction over intra-Indian crime | Affirmed tribal court authority (led to congressional override via Major Crimes Act) |
| *Talton v. Mayes* | 1896 | Bill of Rights does not apply to tribal governments | Tribal courts not bound by Constitution directly (changed by ICRA 1968) |
| *Oliphant v. Suquamish* | 1978 | Tribal courts lack jurisdiction over non-Indians | Most significant limitation on tribal court criminal authority |
| *Santa Clara Pueblo v. Martinez* | 1978 | ICRA enforcement limited to habeas corpus | Limited federal review of tribal court decisions |
| *National Farmers Union v. Crow Tribe* | 1985 | Federal courts must allow tribal courts to first determine their own jurisdiction | Established tribal court exhaustion doctrine |
| *Iowa Mutual Insurance v. LaPlante* | 1987 | Extended exhaustion doctrine to diversity jurisdiction cases | Strengthened tribal court authority over civil cases |
| *Duro v. Reina* | 1990 | Tribal courts lack jurisdiction over non-member Indians | Overridden by congressional "Duro fix" (1991) |
| *United States v. Lara* | 2004 | Congress can recognize tribal jurisdiction over non-member Indians | Affirmed congressional power to define scope of tribal jurisdiction |
| *Plains Commerce Bank v. Long Family Land* | 2008 | Limited tribal court civil jurisdiction over non-member fee land transactions | Narrowed tribal civil jurisdiction |
| *United States v. Bryant* | 2016 | Tribal court convictions valid as predicate offenses for federal DV charges | Affirmed legitimacy of tribal court proceedings |
| *Haaland v. Brackeen* | 2023 | Congress has authority under Indian Commerce Clause; ICWA upheld | Reinforced congressional power to support tribal court jurisdiction |

## The Evolution of Tribal Court Practice

### From Imposed to Indigenous

| Era | Dominant Model | Tribal Agency |
|-----|---------------|---------------|
| 1883-1934 | BIA-imposed CFR courts | Minimal; judges appointed by BIA |
| 1934-1968 | IRA constitutional courts | Growing; tribes designed courts under federal templates |
| 1968-1990 | ICRA-constrained code courts | Significant; tribes operated their own courts but within federal requirements |
| 1990-2010 | Diversifying models; peacemaking revival | High; tribes experimented with traditional justice integration |
| 2010-present | Enhanced authority courts with traditional elements | Expanding; TLOA and VAWA increase authority; traditional practices increasingly integrated |

### The Peacemaking Revival

Beginning in the 1980s, many tribal courts began reintegrating traditional dispute resolution practices:

- The Navajo Nation Peacemaker Courts (established 1982) became the most studied and replicated model
- Peacemaking emphasizes healing, restoration, and community reintegration rather than adversarial adjudication
- By 2023, over 100 tribal courts incorporate some form of peacemaking or restorative justice (Tribal Law and Policy Institute, 2023)
- Research indicates that peacemaking processes produce higher participant satisfaction and lower recidivism than adversarial proceedings (Zuni Cruz, "Tribal Law as Indigenous Social Reality and Separate Consciousness," *Tribal Law Journal* 1, 2000)
