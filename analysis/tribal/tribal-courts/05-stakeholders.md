# Tribal Courts: Stakeholders

## Overview

Tribal courts occupy a unique position at the intersection of tribal sovereignty, federal Indian law, and the broader American justice system. Their stakeholders range from the Indigenous communities they serve to the federal agencies that fund and regulate them, the state courts with which they interact, and the legal profession that shapes their practice.

---

## Primary Stakeholders

### Tribal Citizens and Community Members

**Who they are**: Enrolled members of federally recognized tribes who are subject to tribal court jurisdiction and use tribal courts to resolve disputes.

**Interests and needs**:

- Access to fair, efficient, and culturally responsive justice
- Courts that incorporate traditional values and practices
- Protection from crime, domestic violence, and exploitation
- Resolution of civil disputes (land, family, contracts) without traveling to distant federal or state courts
- Confidence that court proceedings are independent and impartial

**Power**: As the constituency tribal courts serve, community members exert indirect influence through tribal elections and direct participation in court proceedings. Their engagement and trust are essential to court legitimacy.

---

### Tribal Court Judges

**Who they are**: Approximately 600-700 judges serving in tribal courts across the country, ranging from law-trained jurists to traditional leaders serving in customary court roles (NAICJA, 2023).

**Interests and needs**:

- Adequate compensation to attract and retain qualified judges
- Judicial independence from political pressure by tribal councils
- Professional development and continuing education
- Access to legal resources, technology, and administrative support
- Recognition of tribal court service in the broader legal profession

**Power**: Judges shape tribal court practice through their decisions and procedural choices. The National American Indian Court Judges Association (NAICJA) advocates for judicial interests at the federal level.

---

### Tribal Governments

**Who they are**: The 574 federally recognized tribal nations and their executive and legislative bodies (tribal councils, presidents, chairpersons).

**Interests and needs**:

- Courts that effectively administer justice within tribal territory
- Judicial systems that reinforce tribal sovereignty
- Courts that can handle expanded jurisdiction under VAWA and TLOA
- Balance between judicial independence and governmental accountability
- Courts that are fiscally sustainable within constrained budgets

**Power**: Tribal governments establish, fund (in part), and oversee tribal courts through constitutions and ordinances. They appoint judges in many tribal systems.

---

### Defendants and Litigants

**Who they are**: Individuals -- both Indigenous and non-Indigenous -- who appear before tribal courts as defendants, plaintiffs, respondents, or petitioners.

**Interests and needs**:

- Due process protections consistent with ICRA
- Effective assistance of counsel (particularly when facing incarceration)
- Impartial judges and juries
- Fair and proportional outcomes
- Access to appellate review
- Understanding of tribal court procedures and applicable law

**Power**: Limited individual power; defendants' rights are protected by ICRA and, for enhanced sentencing cases, by TLOA and VAWA requirements.

## Government Stakeholders

### Federal Agencies

| Agency | Role | Key Interests |
|--------|------|---------------|
| Bureau of Indian Affairs (BIA) | Primary federal funder of tribal courts; operates remaining CFR courts | Administering limited funds across 574 tribes; managing trust responsibility |
| Department of Justice (DOJ) | Funds tribal court improvement through CTAS grants; Office on Violence Against Women (OVW) supports VAWA implementation | Supporting effective tribal justice; reducing crime in Indian Country |
| Office of Tribal Justice (DOJ) | Coordinates DOJ tribal affairs; consults with tribal justice systems | Interagency coordination; tribal consultation |
| Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA) | Funds tribal wellness courts and behavioral health treatment courts | Expanding evidence-based treatment court models |
| Administration for Children and Families (HHS) | Supports child welfare proceedings under ICWA | ICWA compliance; child safety |

### Congressional Stakeholders

| Committee | Role |
|-----------|------|
| Senate Committee on Indian Affairs | Primary oversight of tribal court legislation and funding |
| House Natural Resources Committee, Subcommittee for Indian and Insular Affairs | House counterpart; oversight of BIA tribal court programs |
| Senate and House Judiciary Committees | Jurisdiction over federal court interactions with tribal courts |
| Senate and House Appropriations Committees | Fund BIA and DOJ tribal court programs |

### State Court Systems

**Who they are**: State courts in all 50 states, but particularly those in PL 280 states and states with significant tribal populations.

**Interests and needs**:

- Clarity on jurisdictional boundaries between tribal and state courts
- Mechanisms for enforcing tribal court orders (and vice versa)
- Protocols for transferring cases between court systems
- Training for state judges on tribal court interaction

**Tensions**: Some state court systems resist recognizing tribal court authority, viewing it as competing jurisdiction. Others have developed cooperative relationships through tribal-state judicial forums and compacts.

## Legal Profession Stakeholders

### Bar Associations and Legal Organizations

| Organization | Role | Position |
|-------------|------|----------|
| National American Indian Court Judges Association (NAICJA) | Primary professional association for tribal court judges | Advocates for judicial independence, funding, and recognition |
| Tribal Law and Policy Institute (TLPI) | Research, training, and technical assistance for tribal courts | Supports court development and innovation |
| Federal Bar Association Indian Law Section | Federal practitioners in Indian law | Supports tribal court recognition and federal coordination |
| American Bar Association | National legal profession organization | Has adopted resolutions supporting tribal court funding and recognition |
| National Legal Aid and Defender Association | Legal aid and public defense | Advocates for defense counsel funding in tribal courts |
| State bar associations | State-level legal profession organizations | Varied; some have supported tribal court recognition, others have opposed |

### Law Schools

**Who they are**: Law schools with Indian law programs and clinics.

**Interests**: Training future practitioners in tribal law; conducting research on tribal court systems; providing clinical services to tribal communities.

**Key programs**: University of New Mexico, University of Arizona, University of Montana, Sandra Day O'Connor College of Law (ASU), University of Colorado, Michigan State University, University of Oklahoma.

## Power Analysis

### Who Shapes Tribal Court Policy

| Actor | Type of Power | How Used |
|-------|--------------|----------|
| Congress | Plenary power; legislation and appropriations | Defines tribal court jurisdiction, funding, and procedural requirements |
| Supreme Court | Judicial interpretation | *Oliphant* and subsequent cases have constrained tribal court authority |
| BIA | Administrative; funding distribution | Controls base funding; administers CFR courts |
| DOJ | Grants and technical assistance | Shapes court development through competitive funding |
| Tribal councils | Governance authority | Establish courts, appoint judges, adopt codes |
| State courts | Recognition decisions | Determine whether to honor tribal court orders |

### Who Lacks Power

| Group | Barriers |
|-------|----------|
| Tribal court judges | Low compensation, political vulnerability, limited professional recognition |
| Tribal court defendants | May lack counsel; limited appellate options |
| Tribal communities | Dependent on external funding and federal permission for court authority |
| Traditional justice practitioners | Federal requirements may marginalize traditional approaches |
