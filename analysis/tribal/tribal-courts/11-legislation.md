# Tribal Courts: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative landscape for tribal court reform encompasses four main areas: (1) establishing mandatory federal funding at adequate levels, (2) restoring full tribal criminal jurisdiction by overturning *Oliphant v. Suquamish Indian Tribe* (1978), (3) mandating full faith and credit for tribal court orders, and (4) increasing sentencing authority to parity with state courts. This document presents draft legislation for each area along with analysis of potential challenges, regulatory frameworks, and loopholes.

## Constitutional Amendments

No constitutional amendment is strictly required for tribal court reform. Congress possesses plenary power over Indian affairs under the Indian Commerce Clause (Article I, Section 8, Clause 3) and has sufficient authority to enact all proposed reforms through ordinary legislation. The Supreme Court in *United States v. Lara* (2004) affirmed congressional power to recognize tribal jurisdiction over non-member Indians, and *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023) reaffirmed broad congressional authority under the Indian Commerce Clause.

However, a constitutional amendment could permanently protect tribal court authority from future judicial or legislative erosion.

### Tribal Judicial Authority Protection Amendment (Optional)

**Purpose**: Constitutionalize tribal court jurisdiction and prevent future Supreme Court decisions from further diminishing tribal judicial authority.

**Draft Text**:

```text
Section 1. Indian tribes shall retain inherent sovereign authority
to establish and operate judicial systems with jurisdiction over
all persons and causes of action arising within their territories,
subject to the same due process protections required of state
courts under this Constitution.

Section 2. No court of the United States shall construe the
sovereign authority of Indian tribes to have been diminished except
by explicit act of Congress, and no such act shall diminish tribal
judicial authority below the minimum necessary to maintain public
safety and order within tribal territories.

Section 3. Congress shall have the power to enforce this article
by appropriate legislation.

Section 4. This article shall take effect two years after the
date of ratification.
```

**Explanation**: Section 1 establishes tribal courts as co-equal judicial institutions with inherent jurisdiction, addressing the *Oliphant* framework that presumed implicit divestiture. Section 2 reverses the implicit divestiture doctrine by requiring explicit congressional action and setting a floor of public safety authority. Section 4 provides a transition period for implementation.

**Potential Challenges**: Ratification requires supermajority support; states may resist loss of concurrent jurisdiction; defining "due process protections required of state courts" could generate litigation; the scope of "territories" may be contested.

**Refinements**: Clarify that "territories" includes all Indian Country as defined in 18 U.S.C. 1151; specify that due process requirements parallel ICRA as amended by TLOA and VAWA; include a congressional reporting requirement during the transition period.

## Federal Legislation

### Bill 1: Tribal Courts Modernization and Mandatory Funding Act

**Purpose**: Convert tribal court funding from discretionary to mandatory appropriations and establish formula-based distribution at levels sufficient for adequate court operations.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Tribal Courts Modernization and
Mandatory Funding Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) tribal courts are essential institutions of tribal
self-governance, handling over 300,000 cases annually across more
than 400 court systems;
(b) the Indian Tribal Justice Act of 1993 authorized $58.4
million annually for tribal courts but was never fully funded;
(c) Bureau of Indian Affairs base funding for tribal courts
remains approximately $30 million per year, a fraction of the
estimated need of $500 million or more;
(d) tribal courts spend an estimated $15-25 per capita on
judicial services compared to $200 or more per capita in state
court systems;
(e) inadequate funding prevents tribes from implementing the
Tribal Law and Order Act enhanced sentencing provisions and VAWA
Special Domestic Violence Criminal Jurisdiction;
(f) the federal trust responsibility obligates the United States
to provide adequate funding for tribal governmental functions,
including courts; and
(g) mandatory funding is necessary to ensure stable, predictable
resources for tribal court operations.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
(a) ELIGIBLE TRIBE.--The term "eligible tribe" means any
federally recognized Indian tribe or authorized intertribal
consortium.
(b) TRIBAL COURT.--The term "tribal court" means any court
established by an Indian tribe, including appellate courts,
specialized courts, and intertribal courts.
(c) SECRETARY.--The term "Secretary" means the Secretary of
the Interior.

SEC. 4. MANDATORY FUNDING FOR TRIBAL COURTS.

(a) IN GENERAL.--There is appropriated, out of any money in
the Treasury not otherwise appropriated, for each fiscal year
beginning with fiscal year 2027, $500,000,000 for grants to
eligible tribes for the operation and improvement of tribal courts.
(b) ANNUAL ADJUSTMENT.--The amount appropriated under subsection
(a) shall be adjusted annually for inflation as measured by the
Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers.
(c) DISTRIBUTION FORMULA.--
  (1) BASE ALLOCATION.--Each eligible tribe operating a tribal
  court shall receive a base allocation of not less than $100,000.
  (2) FORMULA FACTORS.--Amounts remaining after base allocations
  shall be distributed according to a formula established by the
  Secretary in consultation with tribal governments, incorporating:
    (A) tribal citizen population;
    (B) geographic area of tribal jurisdiction;
    (C) caseload volume;
    (D) remoteness and transportation costs;
    (E) existing court capacity and infrastructure needs; and
    (F) implementation of enhanced sentencing under TLOA or
    SDVCJ under VAWA.
  (3) INTERTRIBAL COURTS.--Tribes that participate in intertribal
  court arrangements may pool their allocations.

SEC. 5. PERMISSIBLE USES OF FUNDS.

Funds provided under this Act may be used for:
(a) judicial salaries and benefits;
(b) prosecution and defense attorney services;
(c) court staff salaries and benefits;
(d) courthouse construction, renovation, and maintenance;
(e) case management systems and technology;
(f) law library and legal research access;
(g) traditional justice programs, including peacemaking courts,
talking circles, and elder councils;
(h) specialized court programs, including wellness courts, drug
courts, family courts, juvenile courts, and veterans courts;
(i) appellate court operations;
(j) detention and alternative sentencing programs;
(k) judicial training and continuing education;
(l) court reporter and recording services; and
(m) any other purpose directly related to tribal court operations
as determined by the tribe.

SEC. 6. JUDICIAL PERSONNEL SUPPORT.

(a) SALARY SUPPLEMENTATION.--Of the funds appropriated under
section 4, not less than $50,000,000 shall be available for
judicial salary supplementation grants to bring tribal court judge
salaries to at least 75 percent of state court judicial salaries
in the state where the tribal court is located.
(b) STUDENT LOAN FORGIVENESS.--
  (1) Any attorney or judge who serves in a tribal court for a
  period of not less than 5 years shall be eligible for forgiveness
  of federal student loan debt in an amount not to exceed $100,000.
  (2) There is authorized to be appropriated $20,000,000 for each
  fiscal year for loan forgiveness under this subsection.
(c) TRIBAL LAW FELLOWSHIPS.--
  (1) The Attorney General shall establish a Tribal Court
  Fellowship Program placing not fewer than 100 fellows per year in
  tribal courts for terms of 2 to 3 years.
  (2) There is authorized to be appropriated $15,000,000 for each
  fiscal year for the fellowship program.
(d) PUBLIC DEFENDER FUNDING.--Of the funds appropriated under
section 4, not less than $75,000,000 shall be available for tribal
public defender offices to ensure effective assistance of counsel
as required under TLOA and VAWA.

SEC. 7. TECHNOLOGY AND INFRASTRUCTURE.

(a) CASE MANAGEMENT SYSTEMS.--The Secretary, in coordination with
the Attorney General, shall provide grants for tribal courts to
adopt modern case management software.
(b) BROADBAND.--Tribal court facilities shall be eligible for
priority broadband deployment under existing federal broadband
programs.
(c) VIRTUAL HEARING CAPABILITY.--Grants shall be available for
video conferencing and virtual hearing technology.
(d) RECORD DIGITIZATION.--Grants shall be available for
digitizing existing paper court records.

SEC. 8. ACCOUNTABILITY AND REPORTING.

(a) TRIBAL REPORTING.--Each tribe receiving funds under this Act
shall submit an annual report to the Secretary describing the use
of funds and court performance metrics.
(b) FEDERAL REPORTING.--The Secretary shall submit an annual
report to Congress on tribal court funding distribution, caseloads,
outcomes, and unmet needs.
(c) GAO REVIEW.--The Comptroller General shall conduct a review
of tribal court funding adequacy and program effectiveness at
years 3, 5, and 8 following enactment.

SEC. 9. SELF-GOVERNANCE.

Funds under this Act shall be available through self-governance
compacts under the Indian Self-Determination and Education
Assistance Act (25 U.S.C. 5301 et seq.).

SEC. 10. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on October 1 of the fiscal year
following the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the single most critical barrier to tribal court effectiveness -- chronic underfunding. By establishing mandatory appropriations with a formula-based distribution, it ensures stable funding independent of annual appropriations battles. The $500 million figure reflects the Indian Law and Order Commission's estimate adjusted for inflation. The bill's personnel provisions address the second most critical barrier: the inability to attract and retain qualified judges, attorneys, and staff.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Mandatory spending faces resistance from fiscal conservatives and budget committees
- The $500 million price tag, while modest relative to the federal budget, will face scrutiny
- Formula design could create winners and losers among tribes
- Self-governance compact provisions may raise concerns about accountability

**Refinements**:

- Phase in mandatory funding over 3 years ($200M, $350M, $500M) to reduce budgetary impact
- Include a tribal consultation period for formula design before implementation
- Add provisions for independent auditing of tribal court fund use
- Include a 10-year reauthorization requirement

### Bill 2: Tribal Court Full Faith and Credit Act

**Purpose**: Require all state and federal courts to give full faith and credit to tribal court orders, paralleling the Full Faith and Credit Clause's application to state court orders.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Tribal Court Full Faith and Credit
Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) tribal courts issue orders in hundreds of thousands of cases
annually, including protective orders, child custody determinations,
civil judgments, and criminal convictions;
(b) the absence of a uniform federal requirement for state court
recognition of tribal court orders undermines the practical
authority of tribal courts;
(c) the inconsistent treatment of tribal court orders creates a
dual system in which tribal court decisions have limited reach
beyond reservation boundaries;
(d) VAWA already requires full faith and credit for tribal
protective orders (18 U.S.C. 2265), and this has been implemented
successfully;
(e) the Supreme Court in United States v. Bryant (2016) recognized
tribal court criminal convictions as valid predicate offenses for
federal prosecution; and
(f) tribal courts are entitled to the same presumption of
regularity and validity afforded to state courts.

SEC. 3. FULL FAITH AND CREDIT REQUIREMENT.

(a) IN GENERAL.--Chapter 115 of title 28, United States Code, is
amended by adding at the end the following:

"Sec. 1738C. Tribal court orders

"(a) Each State, territory, and possession of the United States
and each tribal government shall give full faith and credit to
the judicial proceedings of any Indian tribe, and any court of the
United States and any court of a State, territory, or possession
shall give full faith and credit to any order, judgment, or
decree of a tribal court that is consistent with this section.

"(b) SCOPE.--The requirement under subsection (a) applies to:
  "(1) civil judgments, including contract, tort, and property
  disputes;
  "(2) domestic violence protective orders;
  "(3) child custody and support orders;
  "(4) guardianship and conservatorship orders;
  "(5) criminal convictions and sentences;
  "(6) probation and supervision conditions; and
  "(7) any other final order of a tribal court.

"(c) PRESUMPTION OF VALIDITY.--A tribal court order shall be
entitled to the same presumption of validity as the order of a
court of a State.

"(d) GROUNDS FOR CHALLENGE.--A tribal court order may be
challenged only on the grounds that:
  "(1) the tribal court lacked jurisdiction over the subject
  matter or the parties;
  "(2) the order was obtained by fraud; or
  "(3) the defendant was denied fundamental fairness in the
  proceedings.

"(e) BURDEN OF PROOF.--The party challenging the tribal court
order bears the burden of proof by clear and convincing evidence.

"(f) REGISTRATION.--
  "(1) Any party seeking enforcement of a tribal court order in a
  State or federal court may register the order by filing a
  certified copy with the clerk of the enforcing court.
  "(2) Upon registration, the order shall be enforceable as if it
  were an order of the enforcing court.
  "(3) The clerk shall provide notice of the registration to all
  parties and to the issuing tribal court.

"(g) RECIPROCITY.--As a condition of receiving full faith and
credit under this section, a tribal court shall give full faith
and credit to the orders of State and federal courts on the same
terms."

SEC. 4. TRAINING AND TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE.

(a) The Attorney General shall develop and disseminate training
materials for State court judges on tribal court systems, tribal
court orders, and the requirements of this Act.
(b) The Attorney General shall provide technical assistance to
State and tribal courts to facilitate compliance with this Act.
(c) There is authorized to be appropriated $5,000,000 for each
of the first 5 fiscal years following enactment for training and
technical assistance under this section.

SEC. 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 180 days after the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill fills a critical gap in the American judicial system by extending full faith and credit to tribal court orders. The structure mirrors interstate full faith and credit, with limited challenge grounds and a registration mechanism for enforcement. The reciprocity provision addresses state court concerns about one-directional obligations. The training provision addresses the practical barrier of state judges' unfamiliarity with tribal court systems.

**Potential Challenges**:

- State judicial conferences may resist mandatory recognition
- Concerns about varying procedural standards across tribal courts
- Insurance companies and businesses may oppose recognition of tribal civil judgments
- Constitutional questions about Congress's authority to mandate state court recognition of tribal orders

**Refinements**:

- Phase in by order type, starting with protective orders and child custody (already partially covered by VAWA and ICWA) and expanding to civil judgments
- Include a safe harbor for state courts acting in good faith compliance
- Establish a federal registry of tribal courts that meet minimum procedural standards
- Add dispute resolution mechanisms for conflicts between tribal and state court orders

### Bill 3: Tribal Court Sentencing Parity Act

**Purpose**: Increase tribal court sentencing authority to parity with state courts for comparable offenses, while maintaining appropriate due process protections.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Tribal Court Sentencing Parity Act
of 2026".

SEC. 2. AMENDMENTS TO THE INDIAN CIVIL RIGHTS ACT.

(a) Section 202(a)(7)(B) of the Indian Civil Rights Act of 1968
(25 U.S.C. 1302(a)(7)(B)) is amended by striking "a term of 1
year" and inserting "a term of 2 years".

(b) Section 202(b) of the Indian Civil Rights Act of 1968
(25 U.S.C. 1302(b)) is amended--
  (1) inGENERAL.--by striking "a term of imprisonment of not more
  than 3 years" and inserting "a term of imprisonment of not more
  than 10 years";
  (2) STACKING.--by striking "total of 9 years" and inserting
  "total of 20 years"; and
  (3) by adding at the end the following:
  "(e) ENHANCED DUE PROCESS FOR SENTENCES EXCEEDING 2 YEARS.--
  A tribal court imposing a sentence of imprisonment exceeding 2
  years for any single offense shall provide:
    "(1) effective assistance of counsel by a licensed attorney at
    tribal expense for indigent defendants;
    "(2) a presiding judge with sufficient legal training to
    preside over criminal proceedings, or who is licensed to
    practice law;
    "(3) prior to charging, publicly available criminal laws that
    define the offense and the applicable range of penalties;
    "(4) audio or video recording of the trial proceedings; and
    "(5) a right of appeal to a tribal appellate court or
    intertribal appellate court."

SEC. 3. ALTERNATIVE SENTENCING.

Nothing in this Act shall be construed to limit the authority of
tribal courts to impose alternative sentences, including:
(a) community service;
(b) participation in restorative justice or peacemaking programs;
(c) enrollment in substance abuse or mental health treatment;
(d) restitution to victims;
(e) supervised probation; and
(f) any other culturally appropriate alternative determined by
the tribal court.

SEC. 4. DETENTION CAPACITY.

(a) The Secretary of the Interior shall provide grants for the
construction, renovation, and operation of tribal detention
facilities necessary to support the enhanced sentencing authority
under this Act.
(b) There is authorized to be appropriated $100,000,000 for each
of the first 5 fiscal years following enactment for detention
capacity grants.
(c) Tribes may use funds under this section for alternatives to
incarceration, including treatment facilities, halfway houses,
and electronic monitoring programs.

SEC. 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 1 year after the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the sentencing disparity between tribal and state courts by increasing the standard ICRA maximum from 1 year to 2 years and the enhanced maximum from 3 years to 10 years per offense. These limits bring tribal courts closer to parity with state courts, which can impose sentences of 10 or more years for serious felonies. Enhanced due process requirements apply to any sentence exceeding 2 years, ensuring fair proceedings. The alternative sentencing provision explicitly preserves tribal courts' ability to use culturally grounded responses. The detention capacity provision addresses the practical constraint that most tribes lack adequate detention facilities.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Due process concerns for defendants facing longer sentences in tribal courts
- Detention capacity shortages across Indian Country
- Cost of providing effective assistance of counsel for all enhanced sentencing cases
- Potential for increased federal habeas corpus litigation

**Refinements**:

- Phase in enhanced maximums: 5 years in year 1, 10 years in year 3
- Require BIA certification that a tribe has adequate detention capacity before enhanced sentencing applies
- Establish a tribal court sentencing commission to develop guidelines
- Fund habeas corpus training for tribal court judges to reduce reversal rates

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Tribal Court Recognition Act

**Purpose**: Establish state-level full faith and credit for tribal court orders in advance of or in addition to federal legislation.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Tribal Court Recognition
Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Tribal court" means any judicial body of a federally
recognized Indian tribe, including trial courts, appellate courts,
and specialized courts.
(b) "Tribal court order" means any final order, judgment, or
decree issued by a tribal court.

SECTION 3. RECOGNITION OF TRIBAL COURT ORDERS.

(a) The courts of this State shall give full faith and credit to
any tribal court order, subject to the same defenses that apply
to recognition of orders from courts of other states.
(b) A tribal court order may be registered with any court of
this State by filing a certified copy with the clerk of the court.
(c) Upon registration, the tribal court order shall be
enforceable as if it were an order of the registering court.

SECTION 4. TRIBAL-STATE JUDICIAL FORUM.

(a) The Chief Justice of the [State] Supreme Court shall
establish a Tribal-State Judicial Forum to promote communication,
cooperation, and coordination between state and tribal courts.
(b) The Forum shall meet not less than twice annually and shall
include representatives from each tribal court operating within
the state.

SECTION 5. JUDICIAL TRAINING.

The [State] Judicial Education Commission shall include training
on tribal court systems, tribal court orders, and tribal-state
court cooperation in continuing judicial education requirements.

SECTION 6. RECIPROCITY.

This Act shall apply to tribal courts that provide reciprocal
recognition of orders issued by courts of this State.
```

**Explanation**: This model legislation creates a state-level framework for tribal court recognition that can be enacted without federal action. The tribal-state judicial forum provision promotes ongoing cooperation. The reciprocity requirement addresses concerns about one-directional obligations. The judicial training requirement addresses the practical barrier of unfamiliarity.

**Adaptations**: States should adjust based on the number and location of tribal courts within their borders, existing tribal-state compacts, and PL 280 status.

## Regulatory Framework

### BIA Tribal Court Funding Regulations (Revision)

**Existing Authority**: The Secretary of the Interior has authority to revise tribal court funding regulations under 25 C.F.R. Part 11 and the Indian Tribal Justice Act (25 U.S.C. 3601-3631).

**Draft Regulatory Revisions**:

- Revise funding distribution formula to account for caseload, jurisdiction exercised, geographic remoteness, and infrastructure needs
- Establish minimum funding standards for tribes exercising TLOA enhanced sentencing or VAWA SDVCJ
- Create a streamlined grant application process for tribal court technology and infrastructure
- Mandate annual reporting on tribal court funding adequacy by BIA Office of Justice Services
- Require BIA to publish a tribal court resource gap analysis every 2 years

**Explanation**: These regulatory changes can be implemented without legislation, using existing BIA authority, to improve funding distribution and accountability within current appropriation levels.

### DOJ Tribal Court Technical Assistance Program

**Existing Authority**: The Attorney General has authority under the Tribal Law and Order Act (P.L. 111-211) and VAWA to provide technical assistance to tribal courts.

**Draft Program Elements**:

- Expand CTAS to include dedicated tribal court capacity-building grants
- Establish a Tribal Court Technical Assistance Center within the Office of Tribal Justice
- Develop model tribal criminal codes with TLOA and VAWA compliance
- Create a national tribal court data system for voluntary caseload and outcome reporting
- Fund tribal-state judicial forum development through DOJ grants

**Explanation**: These programmatic expansions use existing DOJ authority to provide immediate support while legislative reforms are pursued.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Indian Commerce Clause authority**: Congress has clear authority to regulate tribal court jurisdiction and funding under the Indian Commerce Clause. *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023) reaffirmed the breadth of this authority.
- **Due Process**: Expanded tribal court jurisdiction and sentencing authority must comply with ICRA due process requirements. The VAWA SDVCJ track record demonstrates that tribal courts can satisfy these requirements.
- **Equal Protection**: Tribal jurisdiction over non-Indians based on location within tribal territory does not raise equal protection concerns because tribal membership classifications are political, not racial (*Morton v. Mancari* (1974); *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023)).
- **Full Faith and Credit**: Congress's authority to mandate state court recognition of tribal orders is supported by the Full Faith and Credit Clause (Article IV, Section 1), which empowers Congress to "prescribe the Manner in which such Acts, Records and Proceedings shall be proved, and the Effect thereof."
- **Habeas Corpus**: Federal habeas review of tribal court detention (25 U.S.C. 1303) provides a constitutional safety valve for enhanced sentencing.

### Preemption Questions

- Federal mandatory funding legislation would preempt inconsistent state policies regarding tribal court funding
- Federal full faith and credit legislation would preempt state laws refusing to recognize tribal court orders
- Tribal jurisdiction restoration would clarify federal-tribal jurisdictional relationships without preempting state concurrent jurisdiction where it exists
- State model legislation complements federal action and is not preempted unless directly conflicting

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- Mandatory funding enforced through automatic appropriation; tribal courts can seek judicial relief if funds are withheld
- Full faith and credit enforced through federal court review of state court refusals to recognize tribal orders
- Sentencing parity enforced through ICRA habeas corpus provisions
- Accountability provisions enforced through GAO oversight, annual reporting, and tribal consultation

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Mandatory funding subject to 10-year reauthorization (funding continues during reauthorization process)
- GAO review of all provisions at years 3, 5, and 8
- Annual reporting by BIA and DOJ to Congress on tribal court funding, caseloads, and outcomes
- 5-year review of full faith and credit implementation by the Federal Judicial Center
- Congressional oversight hearings every 2 years by the Senate Committee on Indian Affairs

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Tribal Courts Modernization and Mandatory Funding Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Formula manipulation | Tribes could inflate caseload or population data to increase formula allocations | Medium |
| Self-governance diversion | Tribes receiving funds through self-governance compacts could redirect court funding to other purposes | High |
| Technology vendor capture | Without procurement standards, technology grants could be captured by overpriced vendors | Medium |
| Judicial salary arbitrage | Tribes could use salary supplementation grants while reducing their own court funding contributions | Medium |
| Reporting noncompliance | Tribes failing to submit annual reports could continue receiving funds without accountability | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Formula design lag | Formula may not adapt quickly to changing tribal needs | Formulaic funding inherently lags behind need |
| No minimum court standards | Funding does not require minimum court quality standards | Tension between funding and sovereignty |
| Capital funding inadequate | $500M annual operating budget may not cover capital needs for courthouse construction | Separating capital and operating needs |
| Small tribe access | Base allocation of $100,000 may be insufficient for the smallest tribes | Difficult to fund 574 courts adequately |
| Detention gap | Operating funding does not address the severe shortage of detention facilities | Detention is a separate capital need |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Anti-fraud auditing**: Require independent biennial audits of tribal court funding use; withhold future funding for tribes that cannot account for expenditures
2. **Self-governance safeguards**: Require that self-governance compacts designate court funding as a separate, non-transferable line item
3. **Procurement standards**: Establish minimum procurement standards for technology purchases exceeding $50,000
4. **Salary supplementation maintenance of effort**: Require tribes to maintain their own court funding at baseline levels to receive federal salary supplementation
5. **Reporting enforcement**: Withhold 10% of subsequent year funding for tribes that fail to submit timely annual reports
6. **Capital investment**: Create a separate capital grant program for courthouse construction and renovation
7. **Formula review**: Require formula review and adjustment every 3 years through tribal consultation

### Tribal Court Full Faith and Credit Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Jurisdictional challenge abuse | Parties could routinely challenge tribal court jurisdiction to delay enforcement | High |
| Fundamental fairness standard | The "fundamental fairness" challenge ground could be interpreted broadly to undermine tribal court orders | High |
| Registration burden | Requirement to file certified copies could burden victims seeking enforcement of protective orders | Medium |
| Reciprocity withdrawal | Tribal courts could withdraw reciprocity after benefiting from state recognition | Low |
| State court delay | State courts could delay registration proceedings indefinitely | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No enforcement mechanism | Act requires recognition but does not create a private right of action for enforcement | Balance between mandate and federalism |
| Varying tribal court procedures | State courts may struggle with unfamiliar tribal court procedures when evaluating challenges | Diversity of tribal court systems |
| No federal database | No centralized system for verifying tribal court orders across jurisdictions | Infrastructure gap |
| Limited training funding | $5M per year for training may be insufficient for all 50 states | Budget constraints |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Challenge time limits**: Impose 30-day time limit for jurisdictional and fundamental fairness challenges
2. **Narrow fairness standard**: Define "fundamental fairness" to include only denial of notice, opportunity to be heard, or impartial tribunal -- not variation in procedure
3. **Emergency registration**: Create expedited registration for protective orders (24-hour processing)
4. **Reciprocity monitoring**: Require annual reciprocity compliance reports from tribal courts
5. **State court mandamus**: Allow parties to seek mandamus relief in federal court if state courts unreasonably delay registration
6. **Federal tribal court order registry**: Establish a national electronic registry of tribal court orders accessible to state courts

### Tribal Court Sentencing Parity Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Counsel quality | "Effective assistance of counsel" could be satisfied with underqualified attorneys | High |
| Judge training standard | "Sufficient legal training" is undefined and could be interpreted minimally | Medium |
| Stacking abuse | Stacking provisions could result in disproportionate sentences for minor offenses | Medium |
| Detention condition avoidance | Enhanced sentences without adequate detention facilities could result in unconstitutional conditions of confinement | High |
| Alternative sentencing undermining | Mandatory minimum requirements in tribal codes could override alternative sentencing provisions | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Detention capacity gap | 46 BIA-funded facilities for 574 tribes cannot support enhanced sentencing | Historical underinvestment in tribal detention |
| Counsel shortage | Not enough licensed attorneys willing to practice in tribal courts | Geographic isolation, low compensation |
| Appellate court access | Not all tribes have appellate courts for enhanced sentencing review | Capacity constraints |
| Federal habeas burden | Enhanced sentencing may increase federal habeas corpus filings | More severe sentences invite more challenges |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Counsel standards**: Define minimum qualifications for defense counsel in enhanced sentencing cases, including criminal trial experience requirements
2. **Judge certification**: Create a tribal judicial certification program through NAICJA with minimum training standards
3. **Sentencing guidelines**: Require tribal courts using enhanced sentencing to adopt published sentencing guidelines
4. **Detention standards**: Require compliance with minimum detention standards before a tribe may impose sentences exceeding 2 years; fund compliance through detention capacity grants
5. **Intertribal appellate access**: Require tribes imposing enhanced sentences to provide appellate review through their own court or an intertribal appellate court
6. **Habeas corpus training**: Fund annual habeas corpus compliance training for tribal court judges

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Transition disruption | Phase in all reforms over 1-3 years with dedicated transition teams |
| Interagency coordination | Establish interagency working group (BIA, DOJ, HHS, SAMHSA) for tribal court reform implementation |
| State court resistance | Phase in full faith and credit by order type; fund tribal-state judicial forums; provide training |
| Political sustainability | Build bipartisan support through public safety framing; demonstrate cost-effectiveness of tribal court investment |
| Tribal capacity variation | Allow smaller tribes to access intertribal court arrangements; provide intensive technical assistance |
| Data gaps | Establish national tribal court data system for caseload and outcome reporting (voluntary initially, mandatory after 5 years) |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Mandatory funding subject to 10-year reauthorization with continued funding during reauthorization
- GAO audit of all programs required at years 3, 5, and 8
- Annual report to Congress on funding distribution, caseloads, outcomes, and unmet needs
- Inspector General oversight of anti-fraud and procurement compliance
- 5-year comprehensive review of full faith and credit implementation
