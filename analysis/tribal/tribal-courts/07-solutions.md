# Tribal Courts: Solutions

## Overview

Strengthening tribal courts requires a comprehensive approach addressing funding, jurisdiction, personnel, recognition, infrastructure, and the integration of traditional justice practices. These solutions are designed to work as a coordinated system, with each reform reinforcing the others. The overarching goal is to ensure that every tribal nation has access to a functional, adequately resourced, and culturally grounded judicial system capable of exercising the full range of jurisdictional authority inherent in tribal sovereignty.

---

## 1. Establish Mandatory Federal Funding for Tribal Courts

### The Reform

Convert tribal court funding from discretionary to mandatory appropriations, with formula-based distribution at levels proportional to caseload and jurisdiction.

### Key Components

| Component | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Base funding | $500 million/year mandatory appropriation for tribal court operations |
| Distribution formula | Based on tribal population, geographic area, caseload, remoteness, and existing capacity |
| Self-governance | Available through self-governance compacts under ISDEAA |
| Inflation adjustment | Annual adjustment for inflation |
| Supplemental grants | Competitive grants for court innovation, technology, and specialized courts |

### Rationale

- The Indian Tribal Justice Act of 1993 authorized $58.4 million annually but was never fully funded; actual BIA tribal court funding remains approximately $30 million per year
- Current per capita tribal court spending (~$15-25 per person served) is a fraction of state court spending (~$200+ per person)
- Mandatory funding ensures stability and removes courts from annual appropriations battles
- The Indian Law and Order Commission (2013) identified adequate court funding as the single most important reform for tribal justice

### Expected Impact

- All 574 tribes would have access to a functional court system
- Courts could hire and retain qualified judges, prosecutors, and defense attorneys
- TLOA enhanced sentencing and VAWA SDVCJ implementation would expand dramatically
- Physical infrastructure (courthouses, technology, record systems) would be modernized

## 2. Restore Full Tribal Criminal Jurisdiction

### The Reform

Legislatively overturn *Oliphant v. Suquamish Indian Tribe* (1978) to restore inherent tribal criminal jurisdiction over all persons within tribal territories.

### How It Works

- See [Missing and Murdered Indigenous People: Legislation](../missing-murdered-indigenous/11-legislation.md) for full draft text of the Tribal Jurisdiction Restoration Act
- Congress exercises plenary power to recognize inherent tribal authority over all persons
- Robust due process protections (effective counsel, law-trained judge, impartial jury, appellate review, habeas corpus)
- Phased implementation with capacity-building support
- Federal concurrent jurisdiction maintained as backstop

### Evidence of Feasibility

- VAWA SDVCJ track record: ~45 tribes implementing since 2014 with zero convictions overturned on due process grounds
- *United States v. Lara* (2004) affirmed congressional power to recognize tribal jurisdiction over non-member Indians
- Congress has plenary power to define the scope of tribal jurisdiction

## 3. Eliminate Sentencing Disparities

### The Reform

Increase tribal court sentencing authority to parity with state courts for comparable offenses, while maintaining appropriate due process protections.

### Key Components

| Component | Current | Proposed |
|-----------|---------|----------|
| Standard ICRA maximum | 1 year per offense | 5 years per offense |
| Enhanced (TLOA-equivalent) maximum | 3 years per offense (stackable to 9) | 10 years per offense (stackable to 20) |
| Due process requirements | TLOA requirements for enhanced sentences | All TLOA/VAWA protections for any sentence exceeding 1 year |
| Alternative sentencing | Available but underutilized | Expanded options including community service, restorative justice, treatment programs |

### Rationale

- Current sentencing limits create a perception that tribal courts cannot handle serious crime
- Parity with state courts reinforces tribal court legitimacy and institutional standing
- Greater sentencing authority paired with alternative sentencing options allows courts to tailor responses to individual cases
- Due process protections ensure fair proceedings regardless of sentence length

## 4. Mandate Full Faith and Credit for Tribal Court Orders

### The Reform

Enact federal legislation requiring all state and federal courts to give full faith and credit to tribal court orders, paralleling the Full Faith and Credit Clause's application to state court orders.

### Key Components

- **Scope**: All final orders of tribal courts, including civil judgments, protective orders, custody determinations, and criminal convictions
- **Standards**: Tribal court orders entitled to same presumption of validity as state court orders
- **Challenges**: Allow limited challenges on grounds of lack of jurisdiction or fundamental procedural unfairness (same standards as for state court orders)
- **Registration**: Create streamlined process for registering tribal court orders with state courts for enforcement
- **Reciprocity**: Require tribal courts to recognize state court orders on the same terms

### Evidence of Feasibility

- VAWA already requires full faith and credit for tribal protective orders (18 U.S.C. 2265), and this has worked without significant problems
- *United States v. Bryant* (2016) recognized tribal court criminal convictions as valid predicate offenses for federal prosecution
- Multiple states already recognize tribal court orders through comity or state statute

## 5. Address Personnel Shortages

### The Reform

Fund comprehensive programs to recruit, train, retain, and compensate tribal court personnel at competitive levels.

### Key Components

| Component | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Judicial salary supplementation | Federal grants to bring tribal court judge salaries to at least 75% of state court levels |
| Loan forgiveness | Student loan forgiveness for attorneys and judges who serve in tribal courts for 5+ years |
| Tribal law fellowships | Funded fellowships placing new attorneys in tribal courts for 2-3 year terms |
| Training programs | Annual judicial conference for tribal court judges; ongoing CLE through NAICJA and TLPI |
| Public defender funding | Dedicated federal funding for tribal public defender offices |
| Court staff pipeline | Training programs for clerks, court reporters, probation officers, and administrators |
| Housing assistance | Housing allowances for court personnel serving in remote tribal communities |

### Implementation

- Partner with law schools with Indian law programs to develop tribal court clinical programs
- Expand NAICJA's judicial training programs with dedicated federal funding
- Create a national tribal court personnel clearinghouse for job postings and recruitment
- Develop online and distance learning options for continuing legal education

## 6. Strengthen Judicial Independence

### The Reform

Support structural reforms that insulate tribal court judges from political pressure while respecting tribal self-governance.

### Key Components

| Reform | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Model judicial independence code | Develop (in consultation with tribes) a model code for tribal judicial independence |
| Tenure protections | Encourage fixed-term judicial appointments (minimum 4 years) with removal only for cause |
| Judicial selection commissions | Support creation of independent judicial selection committees to recommend candidates |
| Ethical standards | Support tribal adoption of judicial canons of ethics |
| Appellate access | Ensure all tribal courts have access to appellate review (own or intertribal) |
| Training on independence | Include judicial independence in NAICJA training curricula |

### Approach

These reforms must be developed in partnership with tribal governments, not imposed externally. The goal is to provide models, resources, and incentives for tribes that choose to strengthen judicial independence, while respecting the sovereign right of each tribe to determine its own governmental structure.

## 7. Support Traditional Justice Integration

### The Reform

Fund and protect the integration of traditional dispute resolution practices into tribal court systems, ensuring that federal requirements do not force tribes to abandon effective customary approaches.

### Key Components

| Component | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Peacemaking programs | Dedicated funding for tribal peacemaking, talking circles, and restorative justice programs |
| Customary law documentation | Support tribally directed efforts to document and codify customary law (where tribes choose to do so) |
| Federal flexibility | Amend federal requirements (ICRA, TLOA) to explicitly accommodate traditional practices alongside adversarial proceedings |
| Research and evaluation | Fund research on outcomes of traditional justice practices compared to adversarial models |
| Knowledge sharing | Support intertribal exchanges of traditional justice practices and innovations |
| Elder involvement | Fund elder councils and community advisory bodies that participate in tribal court proceedings |

### Rationale

- Research consistently shows that peacemaking and restorative justice produce higher participant satisfaction and lower recidivism than adversarial proceedings (Zuni Cruz, 2000; Tribal Law and Policy Institute, 2023)
- Traditional justice practices strengthen cultural identity and community cohesion
- Forcing tribes into purely adversarial models imposes a colonial framework on Indigenous institutions

## 8. Expand Specialized Court Programs

### The Reform

Fund the expansion of tribal wellness courts, drug courts, family courts, juvenile courts, and other specialized court programs that apply evidence-based and culturally grounded approaches.

### Current Status and Goals

| Court Type | Current Number | Proposed Target | Evidence Base |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|---------------|
| Healing to Wellness Courts | 100+ | 200+ | SAMHSA-recognized evidence-based model; demonstrated reduction in recidivism |
| Family courts | Varies | Dedicated family court in every tribe with 1,000+ population | Improved child welfare outcomes; ICWA compliance |
| Juvenile courts | Varies | Specialized juvenile proceedings in every tribal court | Age-appropriate interventions; cultural identity support |
| Elder courts | Small number | Available in every tribe with 500+ elders | Elder abuse prevention; culturally appropriate guardianship |
| Veterans courts | Emerging | In every tribe with significant veteran population | Culturally specific veteran services |

### Funding

- Expand SAMHSA tribal wellness court funding from ~$10 million to $50 million/year
- Dedicate DOJ CTAS funding for specialized court development
- Support cross-tribal court-sharing arrangements for smaller tribes

## 9. Modernize Court Infrastructure and Technology

### The Reform

Invest in physical infrastructure, technology systems, and communication networks for tribal courts.

### Key Investments

| Investment | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Courthouse construction and renovation | Federal capital grants for tribal court facilities |
| Case management systems | Fund adoption of modern case management software across all tribal courts |
| Electronic filing | Support tribal court e-filing systems compatible with federal and state court systems |
| Virtual hearing capability | Video conferencing for remote proceedings, appellate hearings, and expert testimony |
| Record digitization | Digitize existing paper records for preservation and accessibility |
| Broadband infrastructure | Ensure all tribal court facilities have reliable high-speed internet |
| Law library access | Fund electronic legal research access (Westlaw, LexisNexis, or equivalents) for all tribal courts |

---

## 10. Develop Intertribal Court Systems

### The Reform

Support the expansion of intertribal court systems that provide appellate, specialized, and trial court services to multiple tribes through cooperative agreements.

### Key Components

- Fund existing intertribal appellate courts (7 regional systems currently operating) and support creation of new ones
- Develop model intertribal court compacts for tribes to adopt
- Support intertribal judicial training and resource sharing
- Create mechanisms for intertribal case transfer and mutual assistance
- Enable smaller tribes to access court services through regional partnerships rather than building standalone systems

### Benefits

- Economies of scale for smaller tribes
- Broader jury pools
- More independent appellate review (judges from other tribes)
- Shared expertise and resources
- Reduced per-tribe costs for specialized court programs

## Solution Interaction Model

| Solution | Enables | Depends On |
|----------|---------|------------|
| Mandatory funding | All other reforms | Legislative action |
| Jurisdiction restoration | Courts handle all cases; full institutional significance | Funding, personnel, due process capacity |
| Sentencing parity | Proportional justice; court credibility | Funding, detention capacity, due process protections |
| Full faith and credit | Court orders enforceable everywhere; institutional recognition | Court quality, standardized procedures |
| Personnel pipeline | Qualified judges, attorneys, and staff | Funding, competitive compensation |
| Judicial independence | Fair proceedings; public confidence | Structural reforms, tenure protections |
| Traditional justice | Cultural relevance; better outcomes | Federal flexibility, community engagement |
| Specialized courts | Tailored interventions; evidence-based outcomes | Funding, trained personnel |
| Infrastructure | Modern court operations | Capital investment, broadband |
| Intertribal systems | Access for smaller tribes; economies of scale | Cooperative agreements, shared funding |
