# Tribal Courts: Roadmap

## Overview

This roadmap outlines a sequenced implementation strategy for strengthening tribal courts, organized in three phases. Phase 1 focuses on immediate capacity building within existing authority. Phase 2 addresses structural reforms requiring legislation. Phase 3 envisions long-term institutional maturation. Each phase builds on the accomplishments of the previous phase.

---

## Phase 1: Immediate Capacity Building (Years 1-2)

### Objective

Strengthen existing tribal courts with available resources and build the case for structural reform.

### Funding and Resource Actions

| Action | Vehicle | Timeline | Estimated Cost |
|--------|---------|----------|---------------|
| Increase BIA tribal court base funding from $30M to $60M | Appropriations bill | Year 1 | $30M increase |
| Expand DOJ CTAS tribal court grants | Appropriations | Year 1 | $25M increase |
| Fund 50 new tribal public defender positions | DOJ OVW/CTAS grants | Year 1-2 | $15M/year |
| Provide electronic legal research access to all tribal courts | BIA/DOJ grant | Year 1 | $5M |
| Fund case management system adoption for 100 courts | DOJ technology grants | Year 1-2 | $10M |

### Personnel Development

| Action | Timeline | Lead |
|--------|----------|------|
| Launch tribal court judicial fellowship program (50 fellows/year) | Year 1 | DOJ/BIA partnership with law schools |
| Expand NAICJA annual judicial training conference | Year 1 | NAICJA with BIA funding |
| Create tribal court internship pipeline with 10 law school partners | Year 1-2 | DOJ Office of Tribal Justice |
| Establish student loan forgiveness for tribal court service | Year 1 | Congressional action (include in broader student loan legislation) |

### VAWA and TLOA Implementation Support

| Action | Timeline | Lead |
|--------|----------|------|
| Provide intensive technical assistance to 50 additional tribes for SDVCJ implementation | Year 1-2 | DOJ OVW / TLPI |
| Fund TLOA enhanced sentencing capacity building for 100 tribes | Year 1-2 | BIA / DOJ |
| Develop model tribal criminal codes with TLOA/VAWA compliance | Year 1 | TLPI / NAICJA |

### Traditional Justice Support

| Action | Timeline | Lead |
|--------|----------|------|
| Fund 30 new tribal peacemaking programs | Year 1-2 | BIA / DOJ |
| Support 10 intertribal knowledge exchanges on traditional justice | Year 1-2 | NAICJA / TLPI |
| Commission research on peacemaking and wellness court outcomes | Year 1 | NIJ |

### Phase 1 Total Estimated Cost: ~$130 million/year

## Phase 2: Structural Reform (Years 2-5)

### Objective

Enact the legislative changes necessary to establish mandatory funding, restore jurisdiction, and mandate recognition of tribal court orders.

### Legislative Actions

| Action | Vehicle | Timeline |
|--------|---------|----------|
| Tribal Courts Modernization Act (mandatory $500M/year funding) | Standalone bill | Year 2-3 |
| Tribal Jurisdiction Restoration Act (overturn *Oliphant*) | Standalone bill | Year 2-4 |
| Tribal Court Full Faith and Credit Act | Standalone or judicial reform bill | Year 2-3 |
| Enhanced sentencing parity (increase ICRA maximums) | ICRA amendment | Year 3-4 |
| Tribal court public defender mandate with funding | Part of funding act | Year 2-3 |

### Institutional Development

| Action | Timeline | Measure of Success |
|--------|----------|-------------------|
| 150 additional tribes implementing VAWA SDVCJ | Year 2-5 | Total: 195 tribes with SDVCJ |
| 200 additional tribes with TLOA enhanced sentencing | Year 2-5 | Total: 300 tribes with enhanced sentencing |
| 50 new wellness courts | Year 2-5 | Total: 150 wellness courts |
| 3 new regional intertribal appellate courts | Year 3-5 | Total: 10 intertribal appellate courts |
| All tribal courts with case management systems | Year 3-5 | 100% electronic case management |
| Tribal court judge salaries at 75% of state court levels | Year 3-5 | Competitive compensation attracting qualified judges |

### Court Infrastructure

| Investment | Timeline | Estimated Cost |
|-----------|----------|---------------|
| Construct/renovate 50 tribal courthouses | Year 2-5 | $250M (capital) |
| Full broadband connectivity for all tribal courts | Year 2-4 | $50M (part of broader infrastructure) |
| Virtual hearing capability for all tribal courts | Year 2-3 | $20M |
| Record digitization for 200 courts | Year 2-5 | $30M |

### Judicial Independence

| Action | Timeline | Lead |
|--------|----------|------|
| Develop model tribal judicial independence provisions (in consultation with tribes) | Year 2-3 | NAICJA / TLPI |
| Offer technical assistance to 100 tribes on judicial selection reform | Year 2-5 | NAICJA / Vera Institute |
| Establish judicial ethics training requirement for BIA-funded courts | Year 2 | BIA |
| Support creation of tribal judicial conduct commissions | Year 3-5 | Tribal governments with NAICJA support |

### Phase 2 Total Estimated Cost: ~$600 million/year (including mandatory funding)

## Phase 3: Institutional Maturation (Years 5-10)

### Objective

Achieve functional parity between tribal courts and state courts of comparable jurisdiction, with tribal courts recognized as co-equal institutions in the American justice system.

### Maturation Goals

| Goal | Target Year | Measure of Success |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| All 574 tribes with access to a functional court system (own or intertribal) | Year 7 | 100% coverage |
| Full criminal jurisdiction exercised by 400+ tribes | Year 7-10 | Tribes implementing full jurisdiction |
| Tribal court orders universally recognized | Year 5-7 | State and federal courts consistently honoring tribal orders |
| Judge compensation at parity with state courts | Year 7-10 | Average tribal court salary matching state court average |
| Case clearance rates comparable to state courts | Year 7-10 | National data showing parity |
| Wellness courts available in every tribe with substance use challenges | Year 7-10 | 250+ wellness courts operating |
| Published tribal court opinions broadly accessible | Year 5-7 | Online database of tribal court decisions |

### Systemic Improvements

| Area | Actions | Timeline |
|------|---------|----------|
| Legal education | Indian law required in all ABA-accredited law schools; tribal court clerkships available | Year 5-10 |
| Professional recognition | Tribal court experience recognized for state and federal judicial appointments | Year 5-10 |
| Research and evaluation | National tribal court data system for case statistics and outcomes | Year 5-7 |
| International engagement | Share tribal justice innovations with Indigenous peoples globally | Ongoing |

### Phase 3 Incremental Cost: ~$200 million/year above Phase 2 levels

## Milestones and Metrics

### Key Performance Indicators

| Metric | Baseline (2025) | Year 2 Target | Year 5 Target | Year 10 Target |
|--------|----------------|---------------|---------------|----------------|
| BIA base tribal court funding | $30M/year | $60M/year | $500M/year (mandatory) | $500M+/year (inflation-adjusted) |
| Tribes with functional courts | ~400 | 425 | 500 | 574 (all) |
| Tribes with VAWA SDVCJ | 45 | 95 | 195 | 400+ |
| Tribes with TLOA enhanced sentencing | ~100 | 150 | 300 | 450+ |
| Tribal wellness courts | 100+ | 120 | 150 | 250+ |
| Intertribal appellate courts | 7 | 8 | 10 | 12+ |
| Average tribal judge salary | $50-80K | $60-90K | $100-140K | $140-180K |
| Tribal courts with case management systems | ~50% | 65% | 90% | 100% |

### Accountability Mechanisms

- **Annual reporting**: BIA and DOJ report to Congress on tribal court funding distribution, caseloads, and outcomes
- **GAO oversight**: Biennial GAO assessments of tribal court funding adequacy and program effectiveness
- **Tribal consultation**: Semi-annual consultation with tribal leaders on court development priorities
- **NAICJA assessments**: Annual judicial needs survey by NAICJA
- **Tribal court data system**: National reporting system for tribal court statistics (voluntary initially, mandatory after Phase 2)

## Political Strategy

### Coalition Building

| Phase | Strategy | Key Partners |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Phase 1 | Frame as public safety and government efficiency; leverage bipartisan support for existing programs | Law enforcement associations, victim rights groups, bipartisan congressional champions |
| Phase 2 | Build on VAWA SDVCJ success; demonstrate court competency through Phase 1 outcomes | Tribal governments, judicial organizations, bar associations |
| Phase 3 | Frame as institutional maturation and equality; tribal courts as innovation leaders | Legal education community, justice reform organizations, international Indigenous rights networks |

### Sequencing Logic

1. **Phase 1** demonstrates tribal court competency and builds the evidentiary case for structural reform
2. **Phase 2** enacts the legislative changes that close jurisdictional gaps and establish adequate funding
3. **Phase 3** achieves functional parity and positions tribal courts as co-equal institutions in the American judicial landscape

## Risk Assessment

| Risk | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|------------|
| Congressional gridlock prevents mandatory funding | High | Pursue phased increases through appropriations; build bipartisan coalition |
| Supreme Court further restricts tribal jurisdiction | Moderate | Legislative codification of tribal authority; long-term constitutional amendment |
| Tribal capacity constraints slow implementation | Moderate | Phased approach; intertribal partnerships; intensive technical assistance |
| Judicial independence concerns undermine reform case | Low-Moderate | Support voluntary reforms; model codes; NAICJA leadership |
| State court resistance to full faith and credit | Moderate | Phase in through VAWA model; tribal-state judicial forums |
