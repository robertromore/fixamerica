# Tribal Courts: Overview

## Executive Summary

Tribal courts are the judicial institutions through which tribal nations exercise their inherent sovereign authority to resolve disputes, enforce tribal law, and administer justice within their communities. Over 400 tribal court systems operate across the United States, handling an estimated 300,000 or more cases annually -- encompassing criminal, civil, family, juvenile, and administrative matters (National American Indian Court Judges Association, 2023). These courts are the most direct expression of tribal self-governance, and their effectiveness is inseparable from the broader health of tribal sovereignty.

Despite their critical role, tribal courts operate under severe constraints. They are chronically underfunded, receiving only a fraction of the federal support available to state and federal courts. Their criminal jurisdiction has been artificially limited by the Supreme Court's decision in *Oliphant v. Suquamish Indian Tribe* (1978), which stripped tribes of authority over non-Indian defendants. The Indian Civil Rights Act (1968) originally limited tribal court sentencing to one year of imprisonment, a cap that was increased to three years by the Tribal Law and Order Act (2010) but remains far below state court authority for comparable offenses. Tribal court decisions are frequently not recognized by state and federal courts, undermining their practical authority.

At the same time, tribal courts represent one of the most innovative and successful areas of American jurisprudence. Many tribal courts incorporate traditional dispute resolution practices, restorative justice models, and culturally grounded approaches to healing and accountability that produce demonstrably better outcomes for Indigenous communities than the adversarial systems imposed by federal and state courts. Wellness courts, peacemaking processes, and community-based justice models developed by tribal courts are increasingly studied and adopted by non-tribal jurisdictions.

Strengthening tribal courts requires adequate and mandatory funding, restoration of full criminal jurisdiction, recognition of tribal court orders by state and federal courts, capacity building for judges and court personnel, and support for the integration of traditional justice practices with contemporary legal requirements.

## Scope

### What This Subtopic Covers

- **Structure and function**: How tribal courts are organized and what cases they handle
- **Jurisdictional authority**: Criminal, civil, and regulatory jurisdiction, including limitations
- **Funding and resources**: Federal, tribal, and other funding sources for tribal courts
- **Personnel**: Judges, prosecutors, defense attorneys, clerks, and other court staff
- **Traditional and customary law**: Integration of Indigenous legal traditions with contemporary court practice
- **Due process and rights protections**: ICRA requirements, TLOA and VAWA enhancements
- **Recognition and enforcement**: Full faith and credit issues between tribal, state, and federal courts
- **Specialized courts**: Drug courts, wellness courts, family courts, juvenile courts
- **Appellate review**: Tribal appellate systems, including intertribal appellate courts

## Core Tensions

- **Sovereignty vs. imposed limitations**: Tribal courts are expressions of inherent sovereignty, yet their jurisdiction, sentencing authority, and procedural requirements are dictated by federal law
- **Traditional justice vs. adversarial model**: Many tribal justice traditions emphasize restoration, healing, and community accountability, but federal requirements increasingly push tribal courts toward adversarial models
- **Capacity vs. expectations**: Federal law expands tribal court authority (TLOA, VAWA SDVCJ) but does not provide proportional funding to implement those expansions
- **Independence vs. political pressure**: Tribal court judges may face political pressure from tribal councils, but the small scale of tribal governments makes structural separation of powers challenging
- **Recognition vs. marginalization**: Tribal courts handle hundreds of thousands of cases annually but their orders are frequently not recognized by state and federal courts

## Key Questions

1. **How are tribal courts structured and funded?** What are the major models of tribal court organization, and why is funding so inadequate?

2. **What jurisdiction do tribal courts actually exercise?** How do *Oliphant*, the Major Crimes Act, ICRA, TLOA, and VAWA SDVCJ define and limit tribal court authority?

3. **How do traditional justice practices function within tribal courts?** What role do peacemaking, restorative justice, and customary law play in tribal court systems?

4. **Are tribal courts providing due process?** How do ICRA requirements, TLOA enhancements, and VAWA protections ensure fair proceedings?

5. **Why don't state and federal courts consistently recognize tribal court orders?** What are the barriers to full faith and credit, and how can they be removed?

6. **What specialized courts have tribes developed?** How effective are tribal wellness courts, drug courts, family courts, and juvenile courts?

7. **What would adequate tribal court funding look like?** How much investment is needed to bring tribal courts to parity with state courts of comparable jurisdiction?

## Vision of Success

A fully supported tribal court system would feature:

- All 574 federally recognized tribes having access to a functional tribal court system, whether their own or through intertribal agreements
- Full criminal jurisdiction over all persons within tribal territories, supported by adequate detention, prosecution, and defense capacity
- Mandatory federal funding at levels proportional to caseload and jurisdiction, comparable to state court funding formulas
- Full faith and credit recognition of tribal court orders by all state and federal courts
- Integration of traditional justice practices without penalty or limitation from federal requirements
- Qualified judges, prosecutors, and defense attorneys in every tribal court, with competitive compensation
- Modern court infrastructure, including courthouses, technology, record-keeping systems, and communication networks
- Tribal appellate systems providing meaningful review, with intertribal appellate courts available for tribes that need them
