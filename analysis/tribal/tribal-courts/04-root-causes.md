# Tribal Courts: Root Causes

## Overview

The challenges facing tribal courts -- inadequate funding, limited jurisdiction, insufficient personnel, lack of recognition, and institutional constraints -- are not accidents or oversights. They are the predictable consequences of federal policies that have systematically undermined tribal self-governance while assuming obligations that the federal government has failed to fulfill. Understanding these root causes is essential for designing solutions that address the structural problems rather than merely treating symptoms.

---

## 1. Chronic and Structural Underfunding

### The Core Problem

Federal funding for tribal courts has never approached the levels necessary for adequate operations, and the gap between need and investment has widened as tribal court jurisdiction and caseloads have expanded.

### Why It Persists

| Factor | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| Discretionary funding | BIA tribal court funding is discretionary, meaning it must compete annually with other federal priorities |
| No baseline obligation | Unlike state courts that receive guaranteed state funding, there is no mandatory federal funding formula for tribal courts |
| Political marginalization | Indigenous peoples represent approximately 2.9% of the U.S. population (Census, 2020); their needs receive proportionally less political attention |
| Competing tribal needs | Within already-inadequate federal Indian affairs budgets, courts compete with health, education, housing, and infrastructure |
| Historical undervaluation | Federal policy has historically viewed tribal courts as subordinate or supplementary rather than co-equal judicial institutions |

### Scale of the Gap

The Indian Tribal Justice Act of 1993 authorized $58.4 million annually for tribal courts. That amount was never appropriated; actual BIA tribal court funding remains approximately $30 million per year -- a fraction of the authorization, let alone the estimated need of $500 million or more (Indian Law and Order Commission, 2013, inflation-adjusted).

## 2. The *Oliphant* Jurisdiction Limitation

### The Core Problem

The Supreme Court's 1978 decision in *Oliphant v. Suquamish Indian Tribe* stripped tribal courts of criminal jurisdiction over non-Indians, creating a fundamental gap in tribal judicial authority.

### How It Constrains Tribal Courts

- Tribal courts cannot hear the cases that most affect their communities' safety -- crimes committed by non-Indians on tribal land
- Over 90% of sexual violence against Indigenous women is committed by non-Indian perpetrators (NIJ, 2016), yet tribal courts cannot prosecute them
- The jurisdictional limitation reduces tribal courts' institutional significance, making it harder to attract qualified judges and attorneys
- VAWA SDVCJ (2013, 2022) created narrow exceptions but covers only specific crime categories, and only 45 tribes have implemented it

### The Doctrinal Framework

*Oliphant* was based on the premise that tribal sovereignty had been implicitly diminished by tribes' "dependent" status within the United States. This reasoning has been criticized by legal scholars as historically unfounded and inconsistent with the treaty framework that recognized inherent tribal sovereignty (Washburn, "American Indians, Crime, and the Law," *Michigan Law Review* 104, 2006).

## 3. Sentencing Limitations

### The Core Problem

The Indian Civil Rights Act (1968) limits tribal court sentencing authority, creating a disparity between tribal and state courts for comparable offenses.

### How It Constrains Tribal Courts

| Limitation | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| Standard ICRA limit: 1 year per offense | Tribal courts cannot impose sentences proportional to serious crimes |
| TLOA-enhanced limit: 3 years per offense (stackable to 9) | Improvement, but still far below state court authority for comparable offenses; requires expensive procedural protections |
| State court comparison | State courts may impose sentences of 10+ years for felonies |
| Perception of tribal courts | Sentencing limits lead to perception that tribal courts cannot handle serious cases |
| Offender behavior | Knowledge of sentencing limits may embolden offenders |

### The TLOA Enhancement Barrier

The Tribal Law and Order Act (2010) increased sentencing authority to 3 years per offense but conditioned this on tribes providing:

- Effective assistance of counsel (licensed attorney)
- Law-trained judge
- Published criminal laws
- Recorded proceedings
- Appellate review

Many tribes cannot meet these requirements due to funding and personnel shortages, meaning they remain limited to 1-year sentences (Tribal Law and Policy Institute, 2025).

## 4. Lack of Full Faith and Credit

### The Core Problem

There is no uniform federal requirement that state courts recognize and enforce tribal court orders, undermining the practical authority of tribal courts.

### Why It Persists

| Factor | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| No federal statute | Congress has not enacted a general full faith and credit requirement for tribal courts (VAWA protective orders are an exception) |
| State resistance | Some state courts view tribal courts as inferior or question their procedures |
| Unfamiliarity | State judges often lack knowledge of tribal court systems and may distrust unfamiliar institutions |
| Political opposition | State bar associations and judicial organizations have sometimes opposed full faith and credit mandates |
| Forum shopping | The absence of recognition encourages parties to seek state court remedies to avoid tribal court jurisdiction |

### Practical Impact

- Protective orders issued by tribal courts may not be enforceable when victims travel off-reservation
- Child custody orders may be challenged or ignored by state courts
- Contract and tort judgments cannot be enforced against defendants who leave tribal territory
- The result is a dual system in which tribal court orders have limited reach beyond reservation boundaries

## 5. Personnel Shortages

### The Core Problem

Tribal courts cannot attract and retain qualified judges, prosecutors, defense attorneys, and support staff because of inadequate compensation, geographic isolation, and the challenging working conditions created by underfunding.

### Contributing Factors

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Salary disparity | Tribal court judges earn $50,000-$80,000; state judges earn $140,000-$220,000 (NAICJA/NCSC, 2023) |
| Geographic isolation | Many tribal courts are in remote locations with limited housing, services, and amenities |
| Attorney shortage | Very few law schools offer Indian law programs; even fewer graduates choose to practice in tribal communities |
| Defense attorney deficit | TLOA and VAWA require effective assistance of counsel, but funding for public defenders is minimal |
| Burnout | Small court staffs handle large and diverse caseloads with minimal support |
| Career trajectory | Tribal court experience is not always recognized by state and federal courts, limiting career mobility |

## 6. Tension Between Traditional and Adversarial Models

### The Core Problem

Federal requirements (ICRA, TLOA, VAWA) increasingly push tribal courts toward adversarial, Anglo-American court models, potentially at the expense of traditional justice practices that may be more effective for Indigenous communities.

### How This Creates Tension

| Federal Requirement | Traditional Practice It May Conflict With |
|--------------------|------------------------------------------|
| Formal rules of evidence | Oral testimony and community knowledge traditions |
| Adversarial proceedings | Consensus-based and restorative approaches |
| Licensed attorney requirement | Elder and community member representation |
| Written legal codes | Customary (unwritten) law traditions |
| Individual rights framework | Community-oriented and relational justice |
| Incarceration as sentence | Restoration, restitution, and healing as outcomes |

### The Double Bind

Tribal courts face a double bind: if they operate according to traditional practices, they may be criticized as lacking due process and denied full faith and credit by state courts. If they adopt adversarial models, they may lose the cultural legitimacy and community engagement that makes them effective. The most successful tribal courts have found ways to integrate both, but this requires resources and sophistication that many courts lack.

---

## 7. Structural Separation of Powers Challenges

### The Core Problem

In small tribal governments, maintaining judicial independence from the legislative and executive branches is structurally challenging.

### Contributing Factors

- Many tribal constitutions were drafted under BIA guidance and may not clearly establish judicial independence
- Tribal council members may have political incentives to influence court outcomes
- In small communities, judges may have personal relationships with parties, attorneys, and political leaders
- Some tribal constitutions allow tribal councils to remove judges without cause
- The absence of a separate judicial branch in some tribal governance structures creates institutional vulnerability

### Impact

- Perception of political influence undermines confidence in tribal court fairness
- Actual interference, though not universal, occurs in some tribal court systems
- This perception is used by opponents of tribal jurisdiction expansion to argue that tribal courts cannot be trusted to handle non-Indian defendants fairly

---

## 8. Colonial Legacy and Institutional Marginalization

### The Core Problem

Tribal courts exist within a legal and political framework that was designed to control, limit, and ultimately eliminate Indigenous self-governance. Despite the shift toward self-determination, the underlying structure of federal Indian law continues to treat tribal courts as subordinate.

### Manifestations

| Area | Colonial Legacy |
|------|----------------|
| Congressional plenary power | Congress claims unlimited authority over Indian affairs, meaning any expansion of tribal court authority can be reversed |
| Trust relationship | The "trust" framework treats tribes as dependents rather than co-equal sovereigns |
| Federal approval requirements | BIA approval is still required for some aspects of tribal governance and land use |
| Funding dependency | Tribal courts depend on federal funding that the federal government has no enforceable obligation to provide at adequate levels |
| Legal education | Most law schools teach almost no Indian law; tribal courts are invisible in legal education |
| Public perception | Tribal courts are poorly understood by the non-Indigenous public, leading to distrust and opposition to jurisdiction expansion |

---

## Root Cause Interaction

These root causes interact and reinforce one another:

```text
Colonial Legacy and Federal Policy
         |
         v
Oliphant + ICRA Limitations + Underfunding
         |
         v
Limited Jurisdiction + Inadequate Resources
         |
         v
Personnel Shortages + Infrastructure Deficits
         |
         v
Perceived Inadequacy + Lack of Recognition
         |
         v
Reduced Authority + Further Marginalization
         |
         v
CYCLE CONTINUES
```

Breaking this cycle requires simultaneous action on jurisdiction, funding, recognition, and capacity -- any single intervention without the others will be insufficient.
