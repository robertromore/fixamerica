# Native Hawaiians: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to Native Hawaiian recognition and self-determination comes from multiple, sometimes contradictory sources. Constitutional originalists argue that Hawaiian-specific programs are racial preferences; some Native Hawaiians themselves oppose recognition as an inadequate substitute for independence; mainland tribal organizations worry about diluting the federal-tribal relationship; and fiscal conservatives object to expanded federal obligations. Understanding this opposition landscape is essential because it has successfully blocked recognition legislation for over two decades.

## Major Sources of Opposition

### Constitutional/Legal Opposition

**Core argument**: Programs and policies that benefit Native Hawaiians based on ancestry constitute racial classifications that violate the Fourteenth and Fifteenth Amendments.

**Key actors**:

- **Grassroot Institute of Hawaii**: Libertarian think tank that has challenged Hawaiian-specific programs as racial preferences
- **Pacific Legal Foundation**: Has litigated against Hawaiian-specific programs
- **Goldwater Institute**: Constitutional challenges to Hawaiian programs
- **Individual plaintiffs**: Non-Hawaiian residents of Hawaii who challenge programs as discriminatory

**Specific arguments**:

- *Rice v. Cayetano* (2000) established that at least some Hawaiian-ancestry classifications are racial, not political
- Native Hawaiians do not have treaties with the United States like mainland tribes do
- Federal recognition for Native Hawaiians would be creating a racial government, not recognizing a pre-existing one
- The Commerce Clause reference to "Indian Tribes" does not encompass Native Hawaiians
- Programs like Kamehameha Schools' Hawaiian-preference admissions and DHHL leases would not survive strict scrutiny

**Legal strategy**: File lawsuits challenging individual Hawaiian programs to build precedent against the political classification of Native Hawaiians, ultimately threatening all Hawaiian-specific legislation.

**Counterargument**: The Apology Resolution acknowledges the unique political and legal relationship between the United States and Native Hawaiians. The over 150 federal statutes addressing Native Hawaiians demonstrate congressional recognition of a political, not racial, relationship. *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023) reaffirmed that Indigenous classifications are political.

### Sovereignty and Independence Movement Opposition

**Core argument**: Federal recognition within the United States is an inadequate remedy for the overthrow of a sovereign nation and amounts to accepting a subordinate political status.

**Key organizations**:

- Ka Lahui Hawaii
- Ka Pakaukau
- Acting Government of the Hawaiian Kingdom
- Various deoccupation and independence organizations

**Specific arguments**:

- The Hawaiian Kingdom was a sovereign nation recognized by over 90 countries; its legal existence was never properly terminated
- International law, including the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples, supports self-determination including independence
- Federal recognition under the Indian Commerce Clause treats Hawaiians as a domestic minority rather than a colonized nation
- The 1898 annexation was illegal (joint resolution, not treaty; no consent); therefore the U.S. has no legitimate authority
- The proper remedy is deoccupation and restoration of sovereignty, not integration into the Indian affairs framework
- Accepting federal recognition forecloses the independence option

**Impact on policy**: This opposition has been significant because it fractures the Native Hawaiian community, preventing a unified political demand. Congress has used internal disagreements as a reason to delay action.

**Counterargument**: Federal recognition does not necessarily preclude future independence claims. In the interim, recognition would provide constitutional protection for existing programs and establish self-governance capacity. Some recognition proposals include explicit preservation of future self-determination options.

### Mainland Tribal Concerns

**Core argument**: Extending the federal-tribal relationship to Native Hawaiians could dilute tribal sovereignty, strain limited resources, and create problematic precedents.

**Specific concerns**:

| Concern | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Commerce Clause scope | Native Hawaiians are not "Indian Tribes" under the Commerce Clause; extending recognition requires a different legal theory |
| Precedent | If Congress can create a tribal relationship with any Indigenous group, the political classification doctrine may weaken |
| Resource competition | Native Hawaiian programs could reduce appropriations for Indian affairs |
| Structural difference | Native Hawaiians lack reservations, treaties, and traditional tribal governance structures analogous to mainland tribes |
| IGRA implications | Could Native Hawaiian recognition enable gaming? |

**NCAI position**: NCAI has generally supported Hawaiian recognition but with careful attention to ensuring it does not undermine the mainland tribal framework.

**Counterargument**: Congress already funds over 150 Hawaiian-specific programs; recognition would formalize, not expand, this relationship. The legal theories supporting Hawaiian recognition are distinct from the Commerce Clause framework. NCAI's general support demonstrates mainstream tribal acceptance.

### Fiscal and Political Conservatives

**Core argument**: Federal recognition creates new spending obligations, expands federal bureaucracy, and represents government overreach.

**Specific arguments**:

- Recognition would create new trust responsibilities requiring indefinite federal funding
- Ceded lands claims could cost the federal and state governments billions
- New bureaucratic structures are unnecessary and wasteful
- The federal government should not create new political entities within existing states
- Recognition is a solution in search of a problem -- existing programs already serve Native Hawaiians

**Key actors**:

- Heritage Foundation
- Congressional Republicans (particularly mainland members without Native Hawaiian constituents)
- Americans for Tax Reform and similar groups

**Counterargument**: The United States already spends significant sums on Native Hawaiian programs; recognition would make this spending more effective by creating a governance framework. The trust responsibility already exists implicitly; recognition formalizes it.

### State-Level Opposition

**Core argument**: Federal recognition could create a sovereign entity within Hawaii that complicates state governance, reduces the tax base, and creates jurisdictional confusion.

**Specific concerns**:

- Ceded lands: If a Native Hawaiian government claims ceded lands, the state could lose control of 1.8 million acres (approximately 29% of Hawaii's land area)
- Taxation: A sovereign entity could be tax-exempt, reducing state revenue
- Jurisdiction: Criminal and civil jurisdiction questions
- Land use: A separate government could affect zoning, development, and environmental regulation
- Tourism: Any sovereignty arrangement that affects tourist access or activities could harm the economy

**Key actors**:

- Some state legislators (though the Hawaii delegation generally supports recognition)
- Business interests dependent on ceded lands
- Property owners on ceded lands

**Counterargument**: Recognition legislation can be designed to address state concerns, including land jurisdiction, taxation, and existing property rights. The Hawaii delegation has historically supported recognition, indicating state-level acceptance.

## Opposition Strategies

### Legal Challenges

| Strategy | Example | Status |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Equal protection lawsuits | Challenges to Kamehameha Schools admissions | Ongoing |
| Voting rights challenges | *Rice v. Cayetano* | Won (2000) |
| Program-specific litigation | Challenges to DHHL blood quantum | Periodic |
| Standing challenges | Challenge standing of Hawaiian organizations | Various |

### Political Strategies

| Strategy | Description | Effect |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| Senate filibuster | Block Akaka Bill from floor vote | Successfully blocked 2000-2012 |
| Committee opposition | Block bills in committee | Delayed hearings and markups |
| Executive action reversal | Trump withdrawal of 2016 Interior rule | Closed administrative pathway |
| Amendment loading | Attach poison pill amendments to recognition bills | Made passage politically difficult |

### Messaging Strategies

| Strategy | Description | Target Audience |
|----------|-------------|----------------|
| "Racial preference" framing | Characterize recognition as creating a race-based government | General public; courts |
| "Balkanization" narrative | Warn of fragmented sovereignty within Hawaii | Mainland constituents |
| "Special interest" framing | Characterize recognition as benefiting a narrow group | Fiscal conservatives |
| "Gaming tribe" fear | Suggest recognition will create a Hawaiian casino industry | Anti-gambling constituencies |
| Internal division exploitation | Highlight Hawaiian community divisions to argue against action | Congress |

## Effectiveness of Opposition

Opposition has been highly effective:

- The Akaka Bill failed in every Congress from 2000 to 2012
- The 2016 Interior Department rule was withdrawn within a year
- Constitutional challenges have narrowed the basis for Hawaiian-specific programs
- No formal recognition or self-governance mechanism exists despite three decades of advocacy
- The political classification question remains unresolved, leaving all Hawaiian programs vulnerable

## Legitimate Concerns vs. Bad-Faith Opposition

### Legitimate Concerns

- Ensuring constitutional compliance for any recognition framework
- Protecting existing property rights on ceded lands
- Addressing jurisdictional questions clearly
- Preventing resource dilution for mainland tribes
- Ensuring Native Hawaiian community consensus

### Self-Interested or Bad-Faith Opposition

- Using "racial preference" framing to block Indigenous self-determination
- Exploiting community divisions to justify inaction
- Opposing recognition to maintain control over ceded lands revenue
- Using fiscal arguments while ignoring existing spending obligations
- Blocking recognition while simultaneously challenging unrecognized programs as unconstitutional
