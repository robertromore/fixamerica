# Native Hawaiians: Actions

## Overview

This document outlines specific actions that citizens, advocates, policymakers, and organizations can take to support Native Hawaiian self-determination, land justice, and community well-being. Actions are organized by actor type and effort level, with priority given to activities that address the most urgent needs: securing the constitutional foundation of Hawaiian-specific programs, reforming the Department of Hawaiian Home Lands, and advancing the unresolved question of Native Hawaiian political status.

## For All Citizens

### Learn and Understand

- **Study the history of the Hawaiian Kingdom and its overthrow**: Understanding that the United States overthrew a sovereign nation recognized by the international community is foundational to informed advocacy. Start with Noenoe K. Silva's *Aloha Betrayed* or Tom Coffman's *Nation Within*.
- **Understand the distinction between political and racial classification**: The constitutional vulnerability of Native Hawaiian programs hinges on whether Hawaiian identity is classified as political (like American Indian tribal membership) or racial. This distinction is central to every policy debate.
- **Learn about Hawaiian Home Lands**: The 28,700-person waitlist, 20-30+ year wait times, and the 50% blood quantum requirement illustrate institutional failure. DHHL's annual reports and OHA publications are accessible starting points.
- **Follow Native Hawaiian media and scholarship**: Honolulu Civil Beat, Hawaii Public Radio, and Ka Wai Ola (OHA's newspaper) provide regular coverage of Native Hawaiian issues. The University of Hawaii's Kamakakuokalani Center for Hawaiian Studies publishes academic research.
- **Understand the range of self-determination positions**: Native Hawaiians are not unified on what political status to pursue. Learn about federal recognition, free association, and independence positions and their respective arguments.

### Advocate

- **Contact your members of Congress**: Urge support for political classification legislation to protect existing Native Hawaiian programs, increased DHHL federal funding, and reinstatement of the Interior Department recognition pathway
- **Support organizations**: Donate to or volunteer with organizations working on Native Hawaiian issues, including the Native Hawaiian Legal Corporation, Council for Native Hawaiian Advancement, and Papa Ola Lokahi (see [Resources](09-resources.md) for a full directory)
- **Share information**: Native Hawaiian issues receive minimal national media attention despite the extraordinary fact that the United States has never established a political relationship with the Indigenous people of the nation it overthrew. Use social media and personal networks to raise awareness
- **Attend public comment periods**: Federal agencies accept public comments on Native Hawaiian consultation, environmental permits affecting Hawaiian lands, and proposed rule changes

### Consume Responsibly

- **Support Native Hawaiian artists and businesses**: Purchase art, crafts, food products, and services from Native Hawaiian-owned businesses. The Council for Native Hawaiian Advancement maintains directories
- **Practice responsible tourism**: If visiting Hawaii, learn about and respect cultural protocols, support Native Hawaiian-owned tourism operations, and avoid contributing to the commodification of Hawaiian culture
- **Understand the housing crisis connection**: Tourism-driven development, vacation rentals, and foreign investment contribute to the housing crisis that disproportionately displaces Native Hawaiians. Support policies that address this dynamic

## For Hawaii Residents

### Civic Engagement

- **Support political classification legislation**: Advocate for federal legislation declaring that Native Hawaiian is a political, not racial, classification, providing constitutional protection for existing programs
- **Engage with ceded lands revenue debates**: The 1.8 million acres of ceded lands generate revenue that is contested between the state and Native Hawaiian interests. Attend state legislative hearings on ceded lands management and revenue allocation
- **Vote for candidates who support Native Hawaiian self-determination**: Evaluate candidates' positions on Hawaiian Home Lands reform, ceded lands, and Native Hawaiian political status
- **Participate in OHA trustee elections**: After *Rice v. Cayetano*, all Hawaii voters elect OHA trustees. Non-Hawaiian residents should consider candidates' commitment to OHA's Native Hawaiian beneficiary mission
- **Support DHHL reform**: Attend Hawaiian Homes Commission meetings, support legislative proposals to increase DHHL funding and reform blood quantum requirements

### Community Action

- **Support Hawaiian language programs**: Volunteer with or donate to Punana Leo (language nest preschools), Kula Kaiapuni (Hawaiian immersion schools), and Hawaiian language programs at the University of Hawaii
- **Participate in cultural events respectfully**: Attend Makahiki celebrations, Merrie Monarch hula festival, and community cultural events as a respectful learner
- **Support environmental protection of Hawaiian lands and waters**: Advocate for protection of sacred sites (such as Mauna Kea), coral reefs, fishponds, and watersheds that are central to Native Hawaiian cultural practice
- **Volunteer with Native Hawaiian service organizations**: Organizations like Habitat for Humanity Hawaii, Aloha United Way, and Native Hawaiian-focused nonprofits need volunteers

## For Native Hawaiian Community Members

### Governance and Advocacy

- **Participate in self-determination discussions**: Attend community meetings, forums, and consultations on political status. Every Native Hawaiian voice strengthens the community's ability to pursue self-determination
- **Engage with OHA and DHHL**: Attend board meetings, submit testimony, apply for grants and programs, and hold these institutions accountable to their beneficiary mission
- **Document and share your family's connection to Hawaiian Home Lands**: If on the DHHL waitlist, document your experience. Personal stories are powerful advocacy tools
- **Connect with sovereignty and self-determination organizations**: Whether you support federal recognition, free association, or independence, engage with organizations working toward your preferred outcome
- **Participate in governance-building activities**: If a constitutional convention or governance-building process emerges, participate. The Na'i Aupuni experience demonstrated the importance of broad participation

### Community Resilience

- **Support youth cultural education**: Mentor young Native Hawaiians in cultural practices, language, and history. The transmission of cultural knowledge is essential to community continuity
- **Document traditional knowledge**: Work with elders to record oral histories, traditional ecological knowledge, place names, and cultural practices. These records are vital for cultural preservation and environmental management
- **Engage in climate adaptation planning**: Sea level rise, coral reef degradation, and extreme weather threaten Native Hawaiian communities. Participate in community-level resilience planning
- **Support Native Hawaiian health initiatives**: Engage with Native Hawaiian Health Care Systems, reduce stigma around seeking help for mental health and substance use, and support community wellness programs
- **Maintain connections with mainland Hawaiians**: Approximately half of all Native Hawaiians live outside Hawaii. Strengthening these connections ensures that any self-determination process includes the full community

### For Mainland Native Hawaiians

- **Stay connected to community**: Join Hawaiian civic clubs, attend Aloha festivals, and participate in mainland Hawaiian organizations
- **Advocate in your state and congressional district**: Your members of Congress may not be aware of Native Hawaiian issues. Educating them expands the coalition
- **Register your interest in self-determination processes**: Ensure that any future plebiscite or governance-building process includes mainland Hawaiians
- **Support Hawaiian language and cultural education for your children**: Distance from Hawaii makes cultural transmission more challenging but no less important

## For Policymakers

### Federal Policymakers

- **Enact political classification legislation**: The single most important near-term action is establishing by statute that Native Hawaiian is a political classification, securing the constitutional basis for over 150 federal programs
- **Reinstate and strengthen the Interior Department recognition rule**: Restore the 2016 Final Rule (43 C.F.R. Part 50) establishing a pathway for Native Hawaiian government reorganization, with additional protections against future reversal
- **Increase DHHL federal infrastructure funding**: Appropriate dedicated federal funds for roads, water, utilities, and housing on Hawaiian Home Lands to enable the development of land that has sat undeveloped for a century
- **Fund a comprehensive self-determination process**: Appropriate funding for a community-driven process, including voter education and possibly an internationally supervised plebiscite, to determine Native Hawaiian preferences on political status
- **Reform blood quantum requirements**: Amend the Hawaiian Homes Commission Act to move from the 50% blood quantum requirement to a lineal descent model, preventing the gradual extinction of program eligibility
- **Permanently reauthorize the Native Hawaiian Health Care Improvement Act**: End the cycle of periodic reauthorization and provide stable, increased funding
- **Address military environmental contamination**: Fund cleanup of contaminated sites on Hawaiian lands, including the Red Hill fuel storage facility and other military installations
- **Resolve ceded lands claims**: Enact legislation establishing a framework for Native Hawaiian claims to ceded lands revenue and management

### State Policymakers (Hawaii)

- **Increase state DHHL funding and accountability**: Increase appropriations for DHHL infrastructure development and establish performance metrics for waitlist reduction
- **Reform ceded lands revenue allocation**: Increase the share of ceded lands revenue directed to Native Hawaiian programs through OHA
- **Strengthen cultural resource protections**: Enact state legislation protecting sacred sites, burial grounds, and culturally significant landscapes
- **Support Hawaiian language in public education**: Expand Kula Kaiapuni (Hawaiian immersion) programs and require Hawaiian history and culture education in all public schools
- **Address the housing crisis**: Enact policies that prioritize housing for residents over tourist accommodations and foreign investment, recognizing the disproportionate impact on Native Hawaiians

### Local Officials

- **Consult with Native Hawaiian organizations on land use decisions**: Ensure that development approvals, zoning changes, and infrastructure projects consider impacts on Native Hawaiian communities, cultural sites, and traditional practices
- **Support affordable housing**: Prioritize affordable housing development in areas where Native Hawaiians face displacement
- **Protect sacred sites and cultural landscapes**: Use local planning authority to protect culturally significant areas

## For Researchers and Academics

- **Center Native Hawaiian perspectives**: Ensure research on Native Hawaiian communities is community-driven, culturally appropriate, and benefits the communities studied. Follow protocols established by Native Hawaiian organizations and the University of Hawaii
- **Study the constitutional question**: Legal scholarship on whether Native Hawaiians constitute a political classification is directly relevant to the survival of over 150 federal programs. Rigorous legal analysis strengthens the case
- **Document health disparities**: Continued research on Native Hawaiian health disparities, including the 5-7 year life expectancy gap, provides the evidence base for program funding
- **Evaluate DHHL effectiveness**: Independent research on why DHHL has failed to distribute available land despite a century of mandate would inform reform efforts
- **Study self-determination models**: Comparative research on self-determination processes (including free association compacts in the Pacific) would inform Native Hawaiian decision-making
- **Support Native Hawaiian scholars**: Mentor, fund, and collaborate with Native Hawaiian researchers and students

## For Legal Professionals

- **Provide pro bono legal services**: The Native Hawaiian Legal Corporation has significant unmet demand for legal services in areas including land rights, housing, family law, and constitutional challenges
- **Defend Hawaiian-specific programs**: Volunteer or provide pro bono support for the defense of Native Hawaiian programs against equal protection challenges
- **Support governance-building**: Assist with drafting governance documents, constitutional frameworks, and legal codes for potential Native Hawaiian self-governance
- **Litigate ceded lands claims**: Support legal efforts to establish Native Hawaiian rights to ceded lands revenue and management
- **Advocate for DHHL reform**: Support legal challenges to DHHL's failure to fulfill its statutory mandate

## For Philanthropic Organizations

- **Fund Native Hawaiian-led organizations**: Direct grants to organizations led by and serving Native Hawaiians, prioritizing capacity building and general operating support
- **Invest in language revitalization**: Hawaiian language revitalization is a success story that needs continued support. Fund Punana Leo, Kula Kaiapuni, and university-level Hawaiian language programs
- **Support self-determination processes**: Fund community engagement, voter education, and deliberation processes that enable Native Hawaiians to determine their preferred political status
- **Address health disparities**: Fund culturally grounded health programs, traditional healing initiatives, and research on Native Hawaiian health needs
- **Support housing solutions**: Fund affordable housing development and advocacy to address the crisis displacing Native Hawaiians from their homeland
