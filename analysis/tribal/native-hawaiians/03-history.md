# Native Hawaiians: History

## Pre-Contact Hawaii (Pre-1778)

### Hawaiian Kingdom Foundations

Before European contact, Native Hawaiians had developed a sophisticated civilization across the Hawaiian archipelago:

- **Population**: Estimated 400,000 to 1,000,000 at contact (scholars debate)
- **Governance**: Complex hierarchical system of chiefs (ali'i) governing ahupua'a (land divisions from mountain to sea)
- **Economy**: Advanced agricultural and aquacultural systems, including lo'i kalo (taro paddies) and fishponds
- **Navigation**: Among the world's greatest seafaring peoples, having settled the Hawaiian Islands through deliberate long-distance voyaging
- **Culture**: Rich oral tradition, hula, religious practices, and sophisticated understanding of ecology and resource management

### Ahupua'a System

The ahupua'a was both a land management system and a governance structure:

- Land divided into wedge-shaped sections from mountain to sea
- Each ahupua'a contained all resources needed for a self-sustaining community
- Managed by a konohiki (land steward) under the authority of ali'i
- The kapu (taboo) system regulated resource use and social behavior
- This system sustained a large population without ecological degradation

## European Contact and the Hawaiian Kingdom (1778-1893)

### Captain Cook and Early Contact (1778-1810)

- Captain James Cook made European contact in 1778
- Introduction of Western diseases devastated the Native Hawaiian population (declining from perhaps 800,000 to under 40,000 by 1893)
- Trade relationships established with European and American merchants
- Firearms enabled Kamehameha I to unify the islands under a single monarchy by 1810

### The Hawaiian Kingdom (1810-1893)

The Hawaiian Kingdom was a recognized sovereign nation:

| Achievement | Date | Significance |
|-------------|------|-------------|
| Unification under Kamehameha I | 1810 | Established centralized Hawaiian government |
| First Hawaiian constitution | 1840 | Created constitutional monarchy |
| International recognition | 1840s | Recognized by United States, Britain, France, and other nations |
| Treaties signed | Various | Over 100 treaties with foreign nations |
| United Nations member precursor | Pre-annexation | Participated in international postal and other conventions |

### The Great Mahele (1848)

King Kamehameha III enacted land reforms that privatized the traditional land system:

- Divided lands among the Crown, government, chiefs, and commoners
- Intended to protect Hawaiian land rights against foreign claims
- Instead enabled massive land acquisition by foreigners, as commoners were unfamiliar with Western property concepts
- By the 1890s, foreigners owned the majority of Hawaiian land despite being a small minority of the population

### Sugar Plantation Economy

American sugar planters became the dominant economic force:

- Established large-scale sugar plantations on former Hawaiian lands
- Imported labor from China, Japan, the Philippines, and Portugal
- Gained increasing political influence over the monarchy
- The Reciprocity Treaty of 1875 gave Hawaiian sugar duty-free access to U.S. markets, deepening dependence
- Pearl Harbor was ceded to the United States as part of the 1887 treaty renewal

### The Bayonet Constitution (1887)

American and European business interests forced King Kalakaua to sign a new constitution at gunpoint:

- Stripped the monarch of executive power
- Disenfranchised most Native Hawaiians and Asian immigrants through property and literacy requirements
- Gave non-citizen foreign residents the right to vote
- Concentrated power in the hands of the foreign-born planter class
- Often called the "Bayonet Constitution" because it was signed under threat of violence

## The Overthrow and Its Aftermath (1893-1921)

### The Overthrow (January 17, 1893)

The Committee of Safety, a group of American and European businessmen, overthrew Queen Liliuokalani with the support of the United States:

- **U.S. Minister John Stevens** ordered the landing of 162 U.S. Marines from the USS Boston
- The marines' presence was the decisive factor; the Queen surrendered to avoid bloodshed
- Queen Liliuokalani yielded her authority "to the superior force of the United States of America"
- The Committee declared a Provisional Government
- Stevens immediately recognized the new government and declared Hawaii a U.S. protectorate

### Investigation and Condemnation

President Grover Cleveland commissioned an investigation:

- **Blount Report (1893)**: Commissioner James Blount concluded the overthrow was illegal and that the U.S. military presence was the decisive factor
- Cleveland called the overthrow "an act of war" committed without congressional authorization
- Cleveland sought to restore the Queen, but the Provisional Government refused
- The Senate Morgan Report (1894) provided a contradictory account more favorable to the overthrow participants

### Republic of Hawaii (1894-1898)

The Provisional Government reorganized as the Republic of Hawaii:

- Sanford Dole declared president
- Suppressed the 1895 counter-revolution by Hawaiian royalists
- Imprisoned Queen Liliuokalani in Iolani Palace
- Sought annexation by the United States

### Annexation (1898)

The Republic of Hawaii was annexed to the United States through the Newlands Resolution:

- **Not a treaty**: Annexation was accomplished through a joint resolution of Congress, not a treaty, because proponents could not achieve the two-thirds Senate vote required for treaty ratification
- **No Native Hawaiian consent**: Native Hawaiians overwhelmingly opposed annexation; the 1897 Ku'e Petition gathered signatures from 95% of the Native Hawaiian population opposing annexation
- **International law questions**: Whether a joint resolution was legally sufficient for annexation remains debated
- **Ceded lands**: The Republic conveyed approximately 1.8 million acres of Crown and Government lands to the United States
- Hawaii became a U.S. territory

### Organic Act (1900)

The Hawaiian Organic Act established territorial government:

- Made all Hawaiian citizens U.S. citizens
- Established territorial legislature
- Did not address Native Hawaiian rights or self-determination
- Made no provision for Native Hawaiian self-governance

## Hawaiian Homes Commission Act (1921)

### Origins

Prince Jonah Kuhio Kalanianaole, Hawaii's delegate to Congress, advocated for setting aside public lands for Native Hawaiians, who had been largely dispossessed:

### Key Provisions

| Provision | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Land set-aside | ~200,000 acres of public lands for Native Hawaiian homesteading |
| Blood quantum requirement | 50% Native Hawaiian blood quantum for eligibility |
| Lease terms | 99-year homestead leases at $1/year |
| Administration | Hawaiian Homes Commission (later transferred to state) |
| Purpose | Rehabilitate Native Hawaiians through return to the land |

### Problems from the Start

- The best agricultural and coastal lands were excluded (retained by sugar plantations)
- Much of the designated land was remote, arid, or volcanic
- Blood quantum requirement excluded many Native Hawaiians
- Inadequate funding for infrastructure (roads, water, housing)
- Administration was paternalistic and slow

## Statehood and Its Consequences (1959)

### The Statehood Act

The Admission Act of 1959 made Hawaii the 50th state:

- Transferred administration of Hawaiian Home Lands to the State of Hawaii
- The state accepted the HHCA as a trust obligation
- Ceded lands (~1.8 million acres) transferred to the state as a public trust for five purposes:
    1. Support of public education
    2. Betterment of conditions of Native Hawaiians
    3. Development of farm and home ownership
    4. Public improvements
    5. Provision of lands for public use
- No Native Hawaiian plebiscite on statehood was conducted
- The United Nations had listed Hawaii as a non-self-governing territory, and the statehood vote did not include an independence option

### Consequences for Native Hawaiians

- Loss of any special political status
- DHHL administration by state government with inherent conflicts of interest
- Ceded lands revenue disputes between state and Native Hawaiian interests
- No self-governance framework
- Cultural and language suppression continued through the public school system

## Hawaiian Renaissance and Modern Advocacy (1970s-Present)

### Cultural Revival

The 1970s saw a powerful cultural renaissance:

| Development | Date | Significance |
|-------------|------|-------------|
| Protect Kahoolawe Ohana | 1976 | Campaign to stop military bombing of Hawaiian island |
| Hokule'a voyage | 1976 | Traditional double-hulled canoe sailed to Tahiti, reviving navigation traditions |
| Hawaiian language immersion | 1984 | First Punana Leo (language nest) preschool established |
| Hawaiian music and hula revival | 1970s-1980s | Cultural practices moved from suppression to celebration |
| Sovereignty movement emergence | 1980s-1990s | Multiple organizations advocating for self-determination |

### Key Legislative Milestones

| Legislation | Year | Impact |
|-------------|------|--------|
| State constitutional convention | 1978 | Established OHA; recognized Hawaiian as official language; strengthened ceded lands trust |
| Native Hawaiian Health Care Act | 1988 | Established Native Hawaiian health care systems |
| Apology Resolution (P.L. 103-150) | 1993 | Congress apologized for the overthrow; acknowledged illegality |
| Native Hawaiian Education Act | 1994 | Federal funding for Native Hawaiian education |
| NAHASDA amendments | 2000 | Native Hawaiian housing assistance |

### The Akaka Bill (2000-2012)

Senator Daniel Akaka introduced the Native Hawaiian Government Reorganization Act repeatedly:

- Would have established a process for Native Hawaiians to organize a governing entity
- The entity would have a government-to-government relationship with the United States
- Passed the House in 2010 but never cleared the Senate
- Opposed by some Native Hawaiians who viewed it as insufficient, by sovereignty advocates who wanted independence, and by conservatives who viewed it as racial preference

### Obama Administration Rule (2016)

The Interior Department finalized 43 C.F.R. Part 50:

- Established a framework for Native Hawaiian government reorganization
- Required community-driven process to form a government
- The organized government could request federal recognition
- The rule was withdrawn by the Trump administration in 2017

### Na'i Aupuni (2015-2016)

A privately organized effort to hold a Native Hawaiian constitutional convention:

- OHA provided $2.6 million in funding
- Delegates were elected (though the election was challenged in court)
- A constitutional convention was held in February 2016
- Delegates drafted a governing document (the "Constitution of the Native Hawaiian Nation")
- Ratification was attempted but the political status of the resulting entity remained unclear
- No government was formally established
