# Native Hawaiians: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative approach to Native Hawaiian issues must address the fundamental unresolved question of political status while simultaneously protecting existing programs and reforming failing institutions. Unlike mainland tribal policy, Native Hawaiian legislation confronts the unique circumstance that the United States overthrew a recognized sovereign nation and has never established a formal political relationship with its Indigenous people. Reforms require new federal legislation, amendments to the Hawaiian Homes Commission Act, executive action, and potentially international processes. This document presents draft legislation, regulatory proposals, and legal analysis across five priority areas: political classification, federal recognition, Hawaiian Home Lands reform, ceded lands resolution, and program strengthening.

## Constitutional Amendments

### Amendment: Native Hawaiian Political Status Recognition

**Purpose**: Provide the strongest possible constitutional foundation for Native Hawaiian programs and self-determination by establishing that the relationship between the United States and Native Hawaiians is political in nature, arising from the historical circumstances of the overthrow and annexation.

**Draft Text**:

```text
Section 1. The Congress acknowledges the unique political and
legal relationship between the United States and the Native
Hawaiian people, arising from the illegal overthrow of the
Kingdom of Hawaii in 1893 and the subsequent annexation of
Hawaii without the consent of the Native Hawaiian people.

Section 2. The Native Hawaiian people shall have the right to
organize a governing entity and to exercise self-determination
over their internal affairs, cultural preservation, and natural
resources, in a manner consistent with federal law.

Section 3. Programs enacted by Congress for the benefit of Native
Hawaiians shall be deemed political classifications based on
the unique historical and legal relationship described in
Section 1, and shall not be subject to strict scrutiny under
the equal protection guarantee of the Fourteenth Amendment.

Section 4. Congress shall have the power to enforce this article
by appropriate legislation.

Section 5. This article shall take effect one year after
ratification.
```

**Explanation**: Section 1 constitutionalizes the acknowledgment already made in the Apology Resolution (P.L. 103-150). Section 2 establishes the right to self-determination without prescribing its form, preserving all political status options. Section 3 directly resolves the constitutional vulnerability of Hawaiian-specific programs by establishing the political classification as a constitutional matter, removing it from judicial revision. Section 5 provides a transition period.

**Potential Challenges**: Constitutional amendments require two-thirds of both chambers and three-fourths of state legislatures, making passage extremely difficult. Opposition from those who view Hawaiian-specific programs as racial preferences would be intense. The scope of this amendment, particularly Section 3's exemption from strict scrutiny, would generate significant legal debate.

**Refinements**: A narrower amendment addressing only the political classification question (Sections 1 and 3) would be more politically feasible. A broader amendment could include ceded lands provisions. The self-determination provision in Section 2 could be made more specific or left intentionally broad.

## Federal Legislation

### Native Hawaiian Political Classification Act

**Purpose**: Establish by statute that Native Hawaiian is a political classification based on the unique historical and legal relationship between the United States and the Native Hawaiian people, providing immediate constitutional protection for over 150 federal programs.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Native Hawaiian Political
Classification Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) The United States, through the actions of its agents and
citizens, participated in the overthrow of the Kingdom of
Hawaii on January 17, 1893, as acknowledged by the Apology
Resolution (Public Law 103-150);
(b) The Native Hawaiian people are the Indigenous people of
Hawaii, with a unique historical and legal relationship with
the United States arising from the overthrow and subsequent
annexation;
(c) Congress has enacted more than 150 statutes addressing the
conditions of Native Hawaiians, demonstrating a longstanding
recognition of this unique relationship;
(d) The Supreme Court in Morton v. Mancari, 417 U.S. 535
(1974), held that legislation singling out Indians for special
treatment is a political rather than racial classification, and
in Haaland v. Brackeen, 599 U.S. 255 (2023), reaffirmed the
political classification of Indian status;
(e) The unique historical circumstances of the Native Hawaiian
people, including the overthrow of a sovereign nation recognized
by the international community, establish a political basis for
the classification of Native Hawaiian that is analogous to,
though distinct from, the classification of American Indians
and Alaska Natives.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

(a) NATIVE HAWAIIAN.--The term "Native Hawaiian" means any
individual who is a descendant of the aboriginal people who,
prior to 1778, occupied and exercised sovereignty in the area
that now constitutes the State of Hawaii, as evidenced by--
    (1) genealogical records;
    (2) birth records;
    (3) records of the Kingdom of Hawaii; or
    (4) other documentation accepted by the Secretary of the
    Interior in consultation with Native Hawaiian organizations.
(b) NATIVE HAWAIIAN ORGANIZATION.--The term "Native Hawaiian
organization" means any organization that--
    (1) serves and represents the interests of Native Hawaiians;
    (2) has as a primary purpose the provision of services to
    Native Hawaiians; and
    (3) is recognized by the Secretary of the Interior for
    purposes of consultation under this Act.

SEC. 4. POLITICAL CLASSIFICATION.

(a) IN GENERAL.--The Congress hereby declares that the term
"Native Hawaiian," as used in federal law, is a political
classification based on the unique historical and legal
relationship between the United States and the Native Hawaiian
people, and is not a racial classification.
(b) LEGAL EFFECT.--Programs and activities of the Federal
Government, the State of Hawaii, or any political subdivision
thereof that are enacted for the benefit of Native Hawaiians
shall be subject to the rational basis standard of review
applicable to political classifications, consistent with
Morton v. Mancari, 417 U.S. 535 (1974).
(c) CONSTRUCTION.--Nothing in this Act shall be construed to--
    (1) establish a government-to-government relationship
    between the United States and the Native Hawaiian people;
    (2) affect the right of Native Hawaiians to organize a
    governing entity;
    (3) diminish any existing right or benefit of Native
    Hawaiians under federal law;
    (4) affect the legal status or claims of the Kingdom of
    Hawaii under international law; or
    (5) modify the terms of the Hawaiian Homes Commission Act,
    1920, or the Admission Act (Public Law 86-3).

SEC. 5. CONSULTATION.

(a) The Secretary of the Interior, in coordination with other
federal agencies, shall establish procedures for meaningful
consultation with Native Hawaiian organizations on policies
and actions that affect Native Hawaiians.
(b) Such consultation procedures shall be comparable to the
procedures established for consultation with federally
recognized Indian tribes.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the most immediate legal vulnerability of Native Hawaiian programs without resolving the broader political status question. By declaring the political classification by statute, it provides a constitutional defense for existing programs while preserving all self-determination options. The broad definition of Native Hawaiian (lineal descent rather than blood quantum) reflects the political nature of the classification. Section 4(c) carefully avoids foreclosing any future political status outcome.

**Potential Challenges**: Constitutional challenge arguing Congress cannot by statute determine the standard of judicial review. Opponents will argue that congressional findings cannot override the Equal Protection Clause. However, *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023) and *Morton v. Mancari* (1974) provide strong precedent for deference to congressional determination of political classifications in Indigenous affairs.

**Refinements**: Could include a more detailed definition of Native Hawaiian aligned with specific genealogical databases. Could include a sunset provision requiring reauthorization, though this would undermine the goal of constitutional stability.

### Native Hawaiian Government Reorganization Act

**Purpose**: Establish a process for Native Hawaiians to organize a governing entity and, if they choose, seek a formal government-to-government relationship with the United States.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Native Hawaiian Government
Reorganization Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.--Congress finds that--
    (1) The United States participated in the overthrow of the
    Kingdom of Hawaii in 1893;
    (2) The Apology Resolution (P.L. 103-150) acknowledged the
    illegality of the overthrow;
    (3) Native Hawaiians are the only major Indigenous group in
    the United States without a recognized governing entity;
    (4) Native Hawaiians have the right to reorganize a
    governing entity as an expression of self-determination;
    (5) A comprehensive, community-driven process is necessary
    to determine the preferred political status of the Native
    Hawaiian people.
(b) PURPOSE.--The purpose of this Act is to provide a framework
for Native Hawaiians to exercise self-determination through the
reorganization of a governing entity, without prescribing the
form that self-determination shall take.

SEC. 3. NATIVE HAWAIIAN COMMISSION.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--There is established the Native Hawaiian
Commission (the "Commission") to facilitate the self-
determination process.
(b) COMPOSITION.--The Commission shall consist of 9 members,
appointed as follows:
    (1) 5 members appointed by the Secretary of the Interior
    from nominations submitted by Native Hawaiian organizations;
    (2) 2 members appointed by the Governor of Hawaii from
    nominations submitted by Native Hawaiian organizations;
    (3) 2 members appointed by Native Hawaiian organizations
    designated by the Secretary.
(c) QUALIFICATIONS.--Each member of the Commission shall be a
Native Hawaiian as defined in Section 8.
(d) DUTIES.--The Commission shall--
    (1) develop and maintain a roll of Native Hawaiians
    eligible to participate in the self-determination process;
    (2) conduct a comprehensive voter education program on
    political status options, including federal recognition,
    free association, independence, and enhanced status quo;
    (3) organize and administer a constitutional convention of
    Native Hawaiian delegates;
    (4) facilitate the drafting and ratification of a governing
    document;
    (5) administer a self-determination plebiscite if the
    community process so determines.
(e) TIMELINE.--The Commission shall complete its work within 5
years of establishment.

SEC. 4. VOTER EDUCATION.

(a) The Commission shall develop and disseminate educational
materials in Hawaiian and English describing each political
status option and its legal, economic, and practical
implications.
(b) Materials shall present each option neutrally and include
analysis of advantages, disadvantages, and precedents.
(c) The Commission shall hold community meetings across Hawaii
and in major mainland communities with significant Native
Hawaiian populations.

SEC. 5. CONSTITUTIONAL CONVENTION.

(a) The Commission shall organize elections for delegates to a
Native Hawaiian constitutional convention.
(b) All Native Hawaiians on the roll maintained by the
Commission shall be eligible to vote and stand for election.
(c) Delegate apportionment shall ensure representation of
mainland Native Hawaiians proportional to their share of the
total Native Hawaiian population.
(d) The convention shall draft a governing document for a
Native Hawaiian governing entity.

SEC. 6. RATIFICATION AND RECOGNITION.

(a) The governing document shall be submitted to all Native
Hawaiians on the Commission's roll for ratification by majority
vote.
(b) If the governing document is ratified, the Native Hawaiian
governing entity may--
    (1) request a formal government-to-government relationship
    with the United States;
    (2) request negotiations for a compact of free association;
    (3) pursue other political status options through
    appropriate processes.
(c) If the entity requests a government-to-government
relationship, the Secretary of the Interior shall--
    (1) evaluate the request based on criteria established by
    regulation;
    (2) make a recommendation to Congress within 180 days;
    (3) if Congress approves, establish the government-to-
    government relationship by publication in the Federal
    Register.

SEC. 7. NEGOTIATIONS.

(a) Upon establishment of a government-to-government
relationship, the United States, the State of Hawaii, and
the Native Hawaiian governing entity shall enter into
negotiations regarding--
    (1) transfer of programs and services;
    (2) jurisdiction and governance authority;
    (3) ceded lands management and revenue;
    (4) Hawaiian Home Lands administration;
    (5) any other matters the parties agree to address.
(b) Negotiations shall be completed within 3 years, with
extensions as mutually agreed.

SEC. 8. DEFINITIONS.

(a) NATIVE HAWAIIAN.--The term "Native Hawaiian" means any
individual who is a descendant of the aboriginal people who,
prior to 1778, occupied and exercised sovereignty in the area
that now constitutes the State of Hawaii.

SEC. 9. PROTECTION OF EXISTING RIGHTS.

(a) Nothing in this Act shall be construed to--
    (1) diminish any existing right or benefit of Native
    Hawaiians under federal or state law;
    (2) affect existing property rights;
    (3) alter the obligations of the United States under the
    Hawaiian Homes Commission Act or any other statute;
    (4) limit the right of Native Hawaiians to seek any
    political status, including independence, through
    appropriate legal and political processes.
(b) All federal programs for Native Hawaiians shall continue
without interruption during the reorganization process.

SEC. 10. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated $30,000,000 for each
of fiscal years following enactment through the completion of
the Commission's work for the implementation of this Act.

SEC. 11. INTERNATIONAL OBSERVERS.

(a) The Commission may invite international observers from the
United Nations, Pacific Islands Forum, or other international
organizations to observe the self-determination process.
(b) The presence of international observers shall not confer
any legal status on the process under international law unless
separately agreed by the United States.
```

**Explanation**: This bill is an updated and expanded version of the Akaka Bill, with critical additions. Unlike the Akaka Bill, it does not prescribe federal recognition as the sole outcome; instead, it establishes a community-driven process that could result in recognition, free association, independence, or an enhanced status quo. Section 9(a)(4) explicitly preserves the independence option, addressing the sovereignty movement's primary objection to previous recognition legislation. International observer provisions (Section 11) add legitimacy to the process. The lineal descent definition replaces the blood quantum requirement.

**Potential Challenges**: Senate filibuster (historical precedent with the Akaka Bill). Opposition from those who view the independence option as destabilizing. Military concerns about basing rights. Constitutional challenge to the self-determination framework. Internal community divisions about whether to participate.

**Refinements**: Could include explicit military basing protections to neutralize Defense Department opposition. Could include a plebiscite as a mandatory rather than optional step. Could specify the relationship between the Native Hawaiian governing entity and the State of Hawaii during the transition period.

### Hawaiian Home Lands Reform Act

**Purpose**: Address the systemic failures of the Hawaiian Homes Commission Act by reforming blood quantum requirements, providing mandatory infrastructure funding, and strengthening federal oversight.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Hawaiian Home Lands Reform Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) The Hawaiian Homes Commission Act of 1920 set aside
approximately 200,000 acres of land for Native Hawaiian
homesteading;
(b) Over 100 years after enactment, fewer than 10,000 leases
have been issued while over 28,700 applicants remain on the
waitlist, with average wait times of 20 to 30 years;
(c) Applicants regularly die before receiving leases;
(d) The 50 percent blood quantum requirement increasingly
excludes Native Hawaiians of mixed heritage and will, if
unchanged, render the program inoperative within a generation;
(e) Significant acreage remains undeveloped due to lack of
infrastructure;
(f) State administration of the program creates conflicts of
interest between the state's general obligations and its trust
obligations to Native Hawaiian beneficiaries.

SEC. 3. BLOOD QUANTUM REFORM.

(a) The Hawaiian Homes Commission Act, 1920, is amended by
striking "not less than one-half part of the blood of the
races inhabiting the Hawaiian Islands previous to 1778" and
inserting "who is a descendant of not less than one-half part
of the blood of the races inhabiting the Hawaiian Islands
previous to 1778, or who is a lineal descendant of a Native
Hawaiian who was eligible for a lease under this Act".
(b) TRANSITION.--Within 2 years of enactment, DHHL shall--
    (1) update eligibility rolls to include newly eligible
    applicants;
    (2) integrate newly eligible applicants into the existing
    waitlist based on their initial application date or, for
    new applicants, their date of application;
    (3) publish updated waitlist statistics.

SEC. 4. MANDATORY INFRASTRUCTURE FUNDING.

(a) APPROPRIATIONS.--There are appropriated from the Treasury
$100,000,000 annually for 15 fiscal years for infrastructure
development on Hawaiian Home Lands, including--
    (1) roads and transportation;
    (2) water and wastewater systems;
    (3) electrical and energy infrastructure;
    (4) broadband connectivity;
    (5) community facilities.
(b) ADMINISTRATION.--Funds shall be administered by the
Department of the Interior in consultation with DHHL and
Native Hawaiian organizations.
(c) PRIORITY.--Infrastructure funding shall be prioritized
for lands with the largest number of pending waitlist
applicants and the greatest infrastructure deficits.

SEC. 5. WAITLIST ACCELERATION.

(a) Within 1 year of enactment, DHHL shall publish a
comprehensive plan to issue leases to all current waitlist
applicants within 20 years.
(b) DHHL shall issue no fewer than 500 new residential leases
per year, increasing to 1,000 per year within 5 years.
(c) DHHL shall submit annual progress reports to Congress
documenting lease issuance, infrastructure development, and
remaining waitlist.

SEC. 6. LAND EXCHANGE AUTHORITY.

(a) DHHL is authorized to exchange Hawaiian Home Lands that are
unsuitable for homesteading for lands of equal or greater value
that are suitable for development.
(b) Any exchange must--
    (1) be approved by the Hawaiian Homes Commission;
    (2) not reduce the total acreage of Hawaiian Home Lands;
    (3) result in a net improvement in the quality and
    developability of Hawaiian Home Lands;
    (4) be subject to public notice and comment;
    (5) include consultation with Native Hawaiian organizations.

SEC. 7. FEDERAL OVERSIGHT.

(a) INSPECTOR GENERAL.--The Inspector General of the
Department of the Interior shall conduct biennial audits of
DHHL's administration of Hawaiian Home Lands, including--
    (1) compliance with trust obligations;
    (2) lease issuance rates;
    (3) financial management;
    (4) infrastructure development;
    (5) waitlist management.
(b) REPORTS.--The Inspector General shall submit audit findings
to Congress and make them publicly available.
(c) REMEDIES.--If the Inspector General finds that DHHL has
failed to comply with its trust obligations, the Secretary of
the Interior may--
    (1) provide technical assistance;
    (2) require corrective action plans;
    (3) withhold or redirect federal funding;
    (4) in extreme cases, petition a federal court for
    appointment of a receiver.

SEC. 8. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

In addition to the mandatory appropriations in Section 4, there
are authorized to be appropriated $20,000,000 annually for
administrative capacity, planning, and technical assistance.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the three core failures of the HHCA: the blood quantum requirement that is shrinking the eligible population, the lack of infrastructure that prevents land development, and the inadequate oversight that permits continued failure. Mandatory (not discretionary) appropriations ensure stable funding. The federal oversight provisions, including inspector general authority and the possibility of receivership, create accountability mechanisms. Blood quantum reform uses a transitional approach that expands eligibility to lineal descendants of previously eligible individuals rather than eliminating blood quantum entirely, which would require a more fundamental restructuring.

**Potential Challenges**: State of Hawaii may oppose enhanced federal oversight as an intrusion on state authority over DHHL. Blood quantum reform will face opposition from some existing beneficiaries who fear dilution of resources. Mandatory appropriations face resistance from budget hawks. The 500-1,000 leases per year target is ambitious given current capacity.

**Refinements**: Could include provisions for converting DHHL leases to fee simple ownership if a Native Hawaiian governing entity is established. Could include emergency housing provisions for applicants who have been on the waitlist for more than 20 years. Could provide for transfer of DHHL administration to a Native Hawaiian governing entity upon its establishment.

## State Model Legislation

### Model Ceded Lands Revenue Allocation Act

**Purpose**: Establish a guaranteed revenue allocation from ceded lands to Native Hawaiian programs.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Ceded Lands Revenue Fairness
Act".

SECTION 2. FINDINGS.

The Legislature finds that--
(a) Approximately 1.8 million acres of Crown and Government
lands of the former Kingdom of Hawaii were conveyed to the
United States upon annexation and subsequently transferred to
the State of Hawaii upon admission;
(b) The Admission Act requires that ceded lands and their
proceeds be held in trust for five purposes, including the
betterment of conditions of Native Hawaiians;
(c) The State has not consistently fulfilled this trust
obligation;
(d) A guaranteed revenue allocation is necessary to ensure
that Native Hawaiians receive a fair share of proceeds from
their ancestral lands.

SECTION 3. REVENUE ALLOCATION.

(a) Not less than 20 percent of all revenue derived from the
use, sale, lease, or other disposition of ceded lands shall be
transferred to the Office of Hawaiian Affairs for the
betterment of conditions of Native Hawaiians.
(b) This allocation shall apply to revenue from all sources,
including but not limited to--
    (1) lease rents;
    (2) license fees;
    (3) sale proceeds;
    (4) concession income;
    (5) any other revenue generated from ceded lands.
(c) The allocation under this section shall not be subject to
reduction through the annual appropriations process.

SECTION 4. ACCOUNTING AND TRANSPARENCY.

(a) The Department of Land and Natural Resources shall
maintain and publish an annual accounting of all ceded lands
revenue.
(b) The accounting shall be audited by an independent auditor
selected by the Office of Hawaiian Affairs.

SECTION 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on July 1 following enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This model state legislation addresses the longstanding dispute over ceded lands revenue by establishing a guaranteed 20% allocation to OHA for Native Hawaiian programs. The 20% figure is based on one of the five trust purposes in the Admission Act (betterment of conditions of Native Hawaiians = 1/5 = 20%). The non-appropriation provision prevents the legislature from reducing the allocation annually.

**Adaptations**: The percentage could be adjusted based on negotiation. The legislation could direct revenue to a Native Hawaiian governing entity rather than OHA if one is established. Additional provisions could address management authority over culturally significant ceded lands parcels.

### Model Sacred Sites Protection Act

**Purpose**: Provide state-level legal protections for Native Hawaiian sacred sites, burial grounds, and culturally significant landscapes.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Native Hawaiian Sacred Sites
Protection Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Sacred site" means any specific, discrete, or narrowly
delineated location on state or private land that is identified
by a Native Hawaiian organization as sacred by virtue of its
established religious, ceremonial, or cultural significance to
Native Hawaiians.
(b) "Native Hawaiian organization" means any organization that
serves and represents the interests of Native Hawaiians and is
recognized by the Office of Hawaiian Affairs.

SECTION 3. PROTECTIONS.

(a) No state agency shall approve any development, construction,
or land use change that would substantially impair the
religious, ceremonial, or cultural significance of a sacred
site without--
    (1) consultation with affected Native Hawaiian
    organizations;
    (2) a finding that no feasible alternative exists;
    (3) implementation of all practicable measures to minimize
    harm.
(b) Private landowners seeking development permits that would
affect a sacred site shall be subject to the same consultation
and mitigation requirements.

SECTION 4. SACRED SITES REGISTRY.

(a) The Office of Hawaiian Affairs, in consultation with Native
Hawaiian organizations, shall establish and maintain a registry
of sacred sites.
(b) The specific locations of sacred sites may be kept
confidential to protect them from desecration.

SECTION 5. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) Any Native Hawaiian organization may bring an action in
state court to enforce the provisions of this Act.
(b) The court may award injunctive relief, declaratory relief,
and reasonable attorney fees to a prevailing plaintiff.
```

## Regulatory Framework

### Interior Department Recognition Rule (Reinstatement)

**Existing Authority**: 43 C.F.R. Part 50 (finalized 2016; withdrawn 2017)

**Proposed Action**: Reinstate and strengthen the 2016 Final Rule:

- Reissue the rule establishing a process for Native Hawaiian government reorganization
- Add provisions for federal funding of the reorganization process
- Strengthen procedural protections to make future withdrawal more difficult (formal notice-and-comment rulemaking for any modification)
- Coordinate with any legislative recognition pathway to ensure consistency
- Include provisions for international observer participation

**Explanation**: The executive pathway provides a faster route to recognition than legislation, though it is more vulnerable to reversal. Reinstating the rule while pursuing legislative action creates two parallel pathways.

### Native Hawaiian Consultation Procedures

**Existing Authority**: Executive Order 13175 (Consultation and Coordination with Indian Tribal Governments)

**Proposed Action**: Issue a new Executive Order establishing formal consultation procedures for Native Hawaiian organizations:

- Require all federal agencies to consult with designated Native Hawaiian organizations on policies and actions affecting Native Hawaiians, comparable to tribal consultation
- Establish timelines for consultation (minimum 60 days for major actions)
- Require written responses to concerns raised during consultation
- Designate lead agency (Department of the Interior) for coordination
- Annual reporting on consultation compliance

### DHHL Trust Oversight Regulations

**Existing Authority**: Hawaiian Homes Commission Act; Admission Act trust provisions

**Proposed Action**: Promulgate regulations through the Department of the Interior establishing:

- Minimum standards for DHHL administration (lease issuance rates, infrastructure development, financial management)
- Regular compliance reviews and reporting
- Federal technical assistance for DHHL capacity building
- Procedures for addressing trust obligation violations

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Indian Commerce Clause**: The Commerce Clause references "Indian Tribes," and whether this extends to Native Hawaiians is contested. However, Congress has broad authority under the Spending Clause and the Fourteenth Amendment Section 5 to enact Hawaiian-specific legislation. *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023) reaffirmed broad congressional authority in Indigenous affairs.
- **Equal Protection**: The central constitutional question. *Morton v. Mancari* (1974) held that Indian-specific programs are political, not racial, classifications subject to rational basis review. Whether this extends to Native Hawaiians without formal recognition is unresolved. *Rice v. Cayetano* (2000) struck down Hawaiian-only voting but did not resolve the broader classification question. The proposed Political Classification Act directly addresses this issue.
- **Fifteenth Amendment**: *Rice v. Cayetano* applied the Fifteenth Amendment to Hawaiian-only OHA elections. Any self-determination voting process must be carefully structured to avoid Fifteenth Amendment challenge, potentially through federal legislation establishing the process as a political (not electoral) exercise of self-determination.
- **Due Process**: Programs that distinguish between Native Hawaiians by blood quantum (HHCA's 50%) and Native Hawaiians by descent (broader definition) must have a rational basis for the distinction.

### Preemption Questions

- Federal political classification legislation would preempt state equal protection challenges to Hawaiian-specific state programs
- Federal recognition of a Native Hawaiian governing entity would create a new sovereign within the State of Hawaii, requiring negotiation of jurisdictional boundaries
- Ceded lands legislation must address the interaction between federal trust obligations, state trust obligations, and any new Native Hawaiian governmental authority

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- Inspector General oversight of DHHL (proposed in Hawaiian Home Lands Reform Act)
- Federal court jurisdiction for enforcement of trust obligations
- Consultation compliance reporting and accountability
- Congressional oversight through annual progress reports

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- 5-year comprehensive review of political classification legislation effectiveness
- 10-year evaluation of Native Hawaiian Government Reorganization Act implementation
- Annual progress reports on DHHL reform milestones
- Biennial Inspector General audits of DHHL administration

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Native Hawaiian Political Classification Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Judicial override | Courts may hold that Congress cannot determine the standard of judicial review for equal protection claims | High |
| Narrow construction | Courts may interpret "political classification" narrowly, applying it to some programs but not others | Medium |
| Definition challenges | The broad lineal descent definition may be challenged as insufficiently connected to a political community | Medium |
| State program exclusion | Courts may hold that the political classification applies only to federal programs, not state programs like OHA and DHHL | High |
| Future program vulnerability | New programs enacted after this Act may not benefit from the political classification without explicit incorporation | Low |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not establish self-governance | Political classification alone does not create a government or governance authority | Intentional limitation to build political support |
| Depends on congressional findings | The strength of the political classification rests on congressional findings that courts may review de novo | Structural limitation of statutory approach |
| Does not address ceded lands | The fundamental land justice question remains unresolved | Scope limitation |
| No enforcement mechanism for consultation | Consultation procedures without enforcement may be ignored | Political compromise |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Include explicit provision that political classification applies to both federal and state programs enacted for Native Hawaiian benefit
2. Establish a private right of action for enforcement of consultation requirements
3. Include a congressional findings section with detailed historical, legal, and factual support that would be difficult for courts to reject
4. Require periodic review and updating of congressional findings to reflect new legal developments
5. Include severability clause ensuring that if any provision is struck down, remaining provisions survive

### Native Hawaiian Government Reorganization Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Commission composition capture | Political appointment process could result in a Commission unrepresentative of the community | High |
| Voter roll manipulation | Definition of eligible Native Hawaiians and roll maintenance could exclude significant populations | High |
| Mainland exclusion | Despite provisions for mainland participation, practical barriers may exclude mainland Hawaiians | Medium |
| No binding outcome | The plebiscite is advisory, not binding, allowing Congress to ignore results | High |
| Military veto | Defense Department concerns could effectively block implementation regardless of community preference | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| 5-year timeline may be insufficient | Community consensus-building on such fundamental questions may require more time | Complexity of self-determination |
| No guaranteed funding after initial authorization | The reorganization process depends on annual appropriations after initial authorization | Appropriations process limitations |
| Independence option creates political opposition | Including independence as an option makes the bill harder to pass | Genuine political constraint |
| Does not address interim governance | No provision for Native Hawaiian governance during the multi-year reorganization process | Sequential design |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Require Commission appointments to reflect the geographic, political, and demographic diversity of the Native Hawaiian community, including mandatory mainland representation
2. Establish the voter roll using the broadest possible lineal descent definition with community verification
3. Provide dedicated funding for mainland Hawaiian participation (travel, virtual participation, satellite meetings)
4. Include provision that if the plebiscite achieves a supermajority (60%+) for a particular outcome, Congress shall act on the result within 2 years
5. Establish interim consultation and governance-building mechanisms during the reorganization process

### Hawaiian Home Lands Reform Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Land exchange abuse | Land exchanges could result in loss of valuable or culturally significant lands | High |
| Funding diversion | Infrastructure funds could be diverted to non-beneficiary purposes | Medium |
| Oversight evasion | State could resist Inspector General oversight as federal overreach | Medium |
| Blood quantum litigation | Existing beneficiaries below the new standard could challenge expanded eligibility | Medium |
| Lease target gaming | DHHL could meet lease targets by issuing leases on unsuitable land | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not transfer DHHL from state to Native Hawaiian control | State conflicts of interest persist | Political compromise; no governing entity exists |
| Mandatory appropriations face budget resistance | Funding may be delayed or reduced through budget process | Congressional budget dynamics |
| Does not address lease-to-ownership conversion | Beneficiaries remain leaseholders, limiting wealth building | HHCA structural limitation |
| Expanded eligibility may overwhelm existing waitlist | More eligible applicants without proportional resource increase could worsen wait times | Resource constraints |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Require that land exchanges be reviewed by an independent appraisal and that culturally significant lands may not be exchanged without Native Hawaiian organization consent
2. Earmark infrastructure funds exclusively for beneficiary-serving development; prohibit use for general state purposes
3. Establish that Inspector General oversight is a condition of continued federal funding, creating a strong incentive for state cooperation
4. Phase in expanded eligibility over 5 years, with corresponding increases in infrastructure funding and lease issuance targets
5. Authorize conversion from 99-year leases to fee simple ownership upon establishment of a Native Hawaiian governing entity, with appropriate restrictions on alienation

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Constitutional vulnerability | Political Classification Act as first legislative priority; constitutional amendment as long-term goal |
| Community fragmentation | Self-determination process that includes all options; community engagement before legislative action |
| Federal-state jurisdictional complexity | Negotiated frameworks with clear boundaries; phased implementation |
| Funding instability | Mandatory (not discretionary) appropriations for core programs |
| Climate change impacts | Integration of climate adaptation into all Hawaiian Home Lands and ceded lands planning |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- All legislation includes 5-year progress reports to Congress
- 10-year comprehensive program evaluation with Government Accountability Office review
- Automatic funding adjustments tied to inflation and Hawaii cost-of-living indices
- Sunset provisions only for pilot programs, not core authorities
