# Native Hawaiians: Solutions

## Overview

Resolving Native Hawaiian political status requires addressing the interrelated problems of constitutional vulnerability, failed institutions, land justice, and self-determination. The solutions range from near-term program reforms that can be accomplished within the existing legal framework to fundamental restructuring of the political relationship between Native Hawaiians and the United States. All solutions must contend with internal community divisions, constitutional constraints, and entrenched opposition.

## Solution Categories

### 1. Federal Recognition Legislation

#### 1A. Native Hawaiian Government Reorganization Act (Updated Akaka Bill)

**Problem**: Native Hawaiians have no recognized governing entity and no government-to-government relationship with the federal government.

**Proposal**: Enact comprehensive legislation enabling Native Hawaiians to reorganize a government and establish a formal relationship with the United States.

**Key elements**:

- Establish a process for Native Hawaiians to organize a government through a democratic convention
- Authorize the organized government to negotiate with federal and state governments
- Declare that the relationship between the Native Hawaiian government and the United States is political, not racial
- Preserve existing federal programs during the reorganization process
- Protect existing property rights and state jurisdiction during transition
- Include explicit preservation of future self-determination options (including independence)

**Advantages**:

- Provides constitutional protection for Hawaiian-specific programs
- Establishes self-governance authority
- Creates a framework for ceded lands resolution
- Has historical legislative precedent (passed the House in 2010)

**Challenges**:

- Senate opposition (filibuster history)
- Internal community divisions between recognition and independence advocates
- Mainland tribal concerns about precedent
- Constitutional challenges from opponents

#### 1B. Executive Branch Recognition Pathway

**Problem**: Legislative recognition has failed for over two decades.

**Proposal**: Reinstate and strengthen the 2016 Interior Department rule (43 C.F.R. Part 50):

- Reissue the rule establishing a pathway for Native Hawaiian government reorganization
- Provide federal funding and technical assistance for the reorganization process
- Include stronger protections against reversal by future administrations
- Coordinate with Congress for legislative backing

**Advantages**: Can be implemented without legislation; faster than congressional action
**Challenges**: Vulnerable to reversal by subsequent administrations; may face legal challenge

### 2. Self-Determination Process

#### 2A. Comprehensive Self-Determination Plebiscite

**Problem**: The Native Hawaiian community has never been formally asked what political status it prefers.

**Proposal**: Fund and administer a comprehensive, internationally supervised self-determination plebiscite:

**Options on the ballot**:

1. Federal recognition as a nation within a nation
2. Free association with the United States
3. Full independence
4. Integration (maintain current status)

**Process requirements**:

- Comprehensive voter education program in Hawaiian and English
- International observers (UN, Pacific Island Forum)
- All eligible Native Hawaiians (Hawaii and mainland) may participate
- Eligibility based on descent from pre-annexation Hawaiian nationals
- Results are advisory but carry significant moral and political weight
- Supermajority threshold (60%) for any outcome that changes political status

**Precedent**: UN-supervised plebiscites for decolonization have been conducted in dozens of territories.

### 3. Hawaiian Home Lands Reform

#### 3A. DHHL Federal Trust Reform

**Problem**: State administration of DHHL has failed to deliver lands to beneficiaries despite having 200,000 acres and a 28,700-person waitlist.

**Proposals**:

| Reform | Description | Expected Impact |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| Federal infrastructure funding | Dedicated federal appropriations for roads, water, and utilities on HHCA lands | Enable development of undeveloped lands |
| Blood quantum reform | Reduce or eliminate 50% blood quantum; move to lineal descent | Expand eligible population; prevent extinction of program |
| Federal oversight enhancement | Establish federal inspector general oversight of DHHL | Accountability for state administration |
| Waitlist acceleration | Mandate annual targets for lease issuance | Reduce wait times from decades to years |
| Land exchange authority | Authorize DHHL to exchange unsuitable lands for developable parcels | Improve quality of homestead lands |

#### 3B. DHHL Governance Reform

**Proposal**: Transfer DHHL administration from the state to a Native Hawaiian governing entity (if established) or to a federally supervised trust:

- Remove state conflict of interest
- Align management with beneficiary interests
- Provide direct federal-beneficiary trust relationship
- Model on Indian Self-Determination Act contracting

### 4. Ceded Lands Resolution

#### 4A. Ceded Lands Trust Reform

**Problem**: The 1.8 million acres of ceded lands (Crown and Government lands) were taken from the Hawaiian Kingdom without compensation. Revenue from these lands is contested between the state and Native Hawaiian interests.

**Proposal**: Establish a comprehensive ceded lands resolution:

- Acknowledge Native Hawaiian claims to ceded lands in legislation
- Establish a revenue-sharing formula directing a guaranteed percentage of ceded lands revenue to Native Hawaiian programs
- Create a joint management framework for cultural and natural resource sites
- Protect existing property rights while recognizing Native Hawaiian interests
- Establish a claims process for specific parcels of cultural significance

### 5. Constitutional Protections

#### 5A. Political Classification Legislation

**Problem**: Without clear statutory declaration that Native Hawaiian is a political classification, all Hawaiian-specific programs are constitutionally vulnerable.

**Proposal**: Enact standalone legislation declaring that:

- Native Hawaiians have a unique political and legal relationship with the United States
- This relationship is based on the historical circumstances of the overthrow and annexation
- Programs benefiting Native Hawaiians are political, not racial, in nature
- The relationship is analogous to, but distinct from, the federal-tribal relationship with American Indians

**This could be enacted even without full recognition legislation**, providing immediate constitutional protection.

### 6. Program Strengthening

#### 6A. Immediate Program Reforms

Even without recognition, existing programs can be strengthened:

| Program | Reform | Cost |
|---------|--------|------|
| Native Hawaiian Health Care Act | Permanent reauthorization; increased funding | $20-30M/year |
| Native Hawaiian Education Act | Expanded funding; include higher education | $50-75M/year |
| Native Hawaiian Housing | Increased NAHASDA allocation; emergency housing funds | $50-100M/year |
| Hawaiian language programs | Federal immersion program funding | $10-20M/year |
| NAGPRA implementation | Dedicated Hawaiian repatriation funding | $5-10M/year |

#### 6B. Environmental and Cultural Protection

- Strengthen Native Hawaiian consultation under NHPA Section 106
- Establish federal protections for Hawaiian sacred sites (e.g., Mauna Kea)
- Fund traditional ecological knowledge documentation and application
- Address military environmental contamination on Hawaiian lands
- Support coral reef, fishpond, and watershed restoration programs

## Priority Recommendations

1. **Immediate**: Enact political classification legislation to protect existing programs
2. **Short-term**: Increase DHHL federal funding; begin blood quantum reform discussion
3. **Medium-term**: Pass recognition legislation or reinstate executive pathway
4. **Long-term**: Conduct self-determination plebiscite; resolve ceded lands claims
