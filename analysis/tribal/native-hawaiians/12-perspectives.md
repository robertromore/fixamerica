# Native Hawaiians: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes Native Hawaiian policy through nine distinct political perspectives, evaluating current state assessments, root cause analyses, proposed solutions, and legislation. Native Hawaiian issues present unique dynamics that cut across conventional political alignments: the overthrow of a sovereign nation by the United States challenges patriotic narratives; the constitutional vulnerability of Hawaiian-specific programs creates strange bedfellows around the political classification question; and the range of self-determination options -- from federal recognition to independence -- generates unusual internal divisions. Each perspective receives engagement consistency ratings and position scores, culminating in compromise proposals that bridge multiple viewpoints.

## Perspective Definitions

| Perspective | Core Values | Policy Orientation |
|-------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Tradition, limited government, individual responsibility | Incremental change, fiscal restraint, market solutions |
| Liberal | Progress, equality, social responsibility | Government as problem-solver, regulation, safety nets |
| Progressive | Systemic change, economic justice, structural reform | Transformative reform, wealth redistribution, worker power |
| Libertarian | Individual liberty, minimal state, free markets | Deregulation, privatization, voluntary association |
| Constitutionalist | Original intent, enumerated powers, federalism | Strict constitutional limits, states' rights |
| Populist | Anti-elite, pro-worker, economic nationalism | Trade protection, anti-monopoly, immigration restriction |
| Centrist | Pragmatism, compromise, incremental progress | Bipartisan solutions, evidence-based policy |
| Religious Right | Faith-based values, traditional morality | Social conservatism, religious liberty |
| Democratic Socialist | Worker ownership, public goods, democratic control | Public investment, labor rights, universal programs |

## Perspective Analyses

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 3/5 - Mixed Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Stated concern for equal treatment and constitutional fidelity, but selectively applied; opposition to Hawaiian programs as "racial preferences" coexists with support for other ancestry-based programs |
| Principle consistency | Limited government principles should support self-governance for Native Hawaiians as an alternative to federal programs, but conservatives oppose both |
| Goalpost stability | Positions have been stable in opposing recognition legislation, but the underlying rationale shifts between constitutional, fiscal, and political arguments |
| Zero-sum behavior | Frequently frames Hawaiian self-determination as zero-sum with equal treatment of all citizens |

**Justification:** Conservatives apply equal treatment principles selectively in Native Hawaiian policy. They argue that Hawaiian-specific programs are racial preferences while supporting other ancestry-based policies and programs. Limited government principles could support Native Hawaiian self-governance as localized decision-making, but in practice, conservatives oppose both federal programs and self-governance authority. The consistent thread is opposition to expanding Indigenous political status.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Heritage Foundation has characterized the Akaka Bill as creating a "race-based government"
- Congressional Republicans from mainland states consistently opposed the Akaka Bill on equal treatment grounds
- Hawaii Republican Party has taken a more nuanced position given local political dynamics but generally opposes recognition
- *Rice v. Cayetano* was argued by opponents as an equal treatment case, a framing conservatives supported

### Constitutionalist Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Genuine commitment to constitutional text and original meaning; applies this relatively consistently to Hawaiian issues |
| Principle consistency | The Constitution does not mention Native Hawaiians, creating genuine interpretive questions; constitutionalists grapple with this honestly |
| Goalpost stability | Stable positions on enumerated powers and Equal Protection; some evolution after *Haaland v. Brackeen* |
| Zero-sum behavior | Can frame Native Hawaiian claims as conflicting with constitutional principles, but also recognizes congressional authority |

**Justification:** Constitutionalists bring genuine analytical rigor to Native Hawaiian policy. The Indian Commerce Clause references "Indian Tribes" but not Native Hawaiians, creating a real constitutional question. Most constitutionalists acknowledge that Congress has broad authority to legislate for Indigenous peoples but debate whether this extends to a group not historically recognized as a "tribe." The deduction reflects a tendency to resolve ambiguities against Native Hawaiian claims rather than in favor of self-determination, despite the historical context of the overthrow.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Justice Gorsuch's textualist approach to tribal sovereignty (in *McGirt*, *Oklahoma v. Castro-Huerta*) demonstrates that originalism can support Indigenous rights
- Federalist Society scholarship has engaged seriously with the constitutional status of Native Hawaiians
- Some constitutionalist scholars argue the Apology Resolution creates enforceable obligations; others disagree
- The *Haaland v. Brackeen* decision, supported by originalist justices, reinforced congressional authority in Indigenous affairs

**Justification:** Populism's anti-elite, pro-worker orientation should theoretically align with Native Hawaiian interests: the Hawaiian people were dispossessed by a wealthy planter elite backed by the U.S. military, and today face displacement by the tourism industry and real estate speculators. Mainland populism has little engagement with Hawaiian issues.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Populist politicians have framed Hawaiian programs as benefiting a "privileged" group through OHA and Kamehameha Schools

## Compromise Proposals

### Compromise 1: *Native Hawaiian Program Protection Act*

**Bridges:** Liberal + Centrist + Constitutionalist + Progressive + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** Five perspectives agree that the constitutional vulnerability of existing Native Hawaiian programs is an urgent problem that must be addressed regardless of disagreements about broader political status. The Political Classification Act provides protection for programs that all parties acknowledge are currently operating, while avoiding the more controversial questions of recognition, sovereignty, and ceded lands.

**Policy Description:**

Enact the Native Hawaiian Political Classification Act establishing that Native Hawaiian is a political classification subject to rational basis review. Include detailed congressional findings based on the historical circumstances of the overthrow, the Apology Resolution, the over 150 existing federal statutes, and the *Haaland v. Brackeen* precedent. Establish formal consultation procedures between federal agencies and Native Hawaiian organizations. Does not create a government, resolve ceded lands, or establish self-governance.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Liberal | Protects existing programs; establishes consultation; builds foundation for future recognition |
| Centrist | Practical, achievable reform; evidence-based approach; bipartisan potential |
| Constitutionalist | Uses congressional authority properly; detailed findings; respects judicial role |
| Progressive | Provides immediate protection while preserving future self-determination options |
| Democratic Socialist | Secures existing programs as floor; builds toward broader reform |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Liberal | Defers recognition and self-governance; accepts incremental approach |
| Centrist | Accepts that this does not resolve the fundamental political status question |
| Constitutionalist | Accepts congressional determination of political classification |
| Progressive | Accepts insufficient reform as necessary interim step |
| Democratic Socialist | Accepts protection of existing framework rather than transformation |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 5/9 perspectives (excluding Conservative, Libertarian, Populist, Religious Right)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 5/5 coalition members rated 4+ consistency
- **Durability:** High. Once enacted, political classification becomes entrenched and difficult to reverse
- **Implementation Path:** Federal legislation; Hawaii delegation as lead sponsors; build on *Brackeen* precedent

### Compromise 2: *Hawaiian Home Lands Modernization Act*

**Bridges:** Liberal + Centrist + Progressive + Populist + Democratic Socialist + Religious Right

**Core Agreement:** Six perspectives agree that the DHHL waitlist of 28,700 applicants with 20-30+ year wait times represents a clear institutional failure that must be addressed. Housing is a universal concern, and reforming DHHL does not require resolving the broader political status debate. Even some who oppose Native Hawaiian "special status" acknowledge that the existing program should at least function as intended.

**Policy Description:**

Comprehensive DHHL reform including: increased federal infrastructure funding ($75 million annually for 15 years); expanded blood quantum eligibility (lineal descent from original HHCA-eligible individuals); annual lease issuance targets (500 per year minimum); federal Inspector General oversight; land exchange authority for unsuitable parcels; and emergency housing assistance for applicants who have waited more than 20 years.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Liberal | Addresses housing crisis; expands eligibility; strengthens trust obligation fulfillment |
| Centrist | Evidence-based reform; measurable targets; phased implementation |
| Progressive | Expands access; increases resources; begins to address institutional failure |
| Populist | Addresses housing crisis; accountability for government program |
| Democratic Socialist | Major public investment in housing; expands beneficiary class |
| Religious Right | Supports family housing and stability; community preservation |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Liberal | Accepts reform within existing state administration rather than transfer to Hawaiian entity |
| Centrist | Accepts mandatory rather than discretionary funding |
| Progressive | Accepts incremental reform rather than structural transformation |
| Populist | Accepts Hawaiian-specific (rather than universal) housing program |
| Democratic Socialist | Accepts targeted rather than universal public housing |
| Religious Right | Accepts expanded secular program |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 6/9 perspectives (excluding Conservative, Libertarian, Constitutionalist on scope)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 4/6 coalition members rated 4+ consistency
- **Durability:** High. Infrastructure investments create lasting physical assets; expanded eligibility is difficult to reverse
- **Implementation Path:** Bipartisan legislation; amendment to HHCA; Hawaii delegation leadership

### Compromise 3: *Native Hawaiian Self-Determination Process Act*

**Bridges:** Liberal + Progressive + Centrist + Democratic Socialist + Libertarian (partial)

**Core Agreement:** Multiple perspectives agree that the Native Hawaiian community should have a meaningful opportunity to express its preferences on political status. The disagreement is not about whether Native Hawaiians should have a voice but about which outcomes are acceptable. A structured, community-driven process with voter education and professional facilitation could build the consensus that has been elusive for decades.

**Policy Description:**

Fund and administer a comprehensive community engagement and deliberation process, culminating in an advisory (not binding) preference poll on political status options. The process would include voter education in Hawaiian and English, community meetings across Hawaii and on the mainland, expert panels on legal and economic implications of each option, and international observers. The poll results would inform (but not dictate) congressional action.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Liberal | Empowers community voice; democratic process; informed decision-making |
| Progressive | Self-determination in action; includes independence option; international observers |
| Centrist | Evidence-based; deliberative process; advisory nature reduces risk |
| Democratic Socialist | Community empowerment; anti-colonial process; democratic governance |
| Libertarian (partial) | Individual voices counted; consent-based governance; could support independence outcome |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Liberal | Accepts advisory (not binding) outcome; independence option may complicate politics |
| Progressive | Accepts advisory rather than binding plebiscite; process may not produce preferred outcome |
| Centrist | Accepts that process could produce radical outcome (independence) |
| Democratic Socialist | Accepts that process may not produce transformative outcome |
| Libertarian | Accepts government-funded deliberation process |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 5/9 perspectives (plus partial Libertarian support)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 5/5 full coalition members rated 4+ consistency
- **Durability:** Moderate. The process itself is time-limited, but the results create political legitimacy for whatever direction the community chooses
- **Implementation Path:** Federal legislation authorizing and funding the process; commission appointment; 3-5 year timeline

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The program protection coalition (Compromise 1) is the most politically viable, uniting 5 perspectives with the highest collective consistency ratings (all rated 4+) around a targeted reform that addresses the most immediate legal threat. This should be the first legislative priority because it protects the foundation upon which all other reforms depend.

### Key Obstacles

- **Conservative-Libertarian-Populist alignment on "equal treatment"**: These three perspectives share a framing of Hawaiian programs as racial preferences. This coalition has successfully blocked recognition legislation for over two decades and would likely oppose the Political Classification Act.
- **Senate procedural barriers**: The Akaka Bill's history demonstrates that even popular legislation can be blocked by filibuster. Any Hawaiian legislation must either attract 60 Senate votes or be attached to a broader package.
- **Internal community division**: Congress has historically cited Native Hawaiian community disagreement as a reason to delay action. The self-determination process (Compromise 3) addresses this directly but requires upfront political investment.
- **Judicial risk**: Even if enacted, the Political Classification Act faces likely constitutional challenge. Its durability depends on the strength of congressional findings and the composition of the Supreme Court.

### Low Consistency Partners

- **Populist** (2/5 consistency): Populist support is the least reliable. Engagement should focus on housing affordability and opposition to tourism-driven displacement -- framing that aligns with populist economic concerns without requiring support for "special status."
- **Conservative and Religious Right** (3/5 consistency): These perspectives' positions are largely driven by coalition dynamics rather than independent analysis. Housing reform (Compromise 2) may attract some support when framed as fixing a broken program rather than expanding a new one.

### Recommended Approach

1. **Lead with program protection** (Compromise 1): Enact the Political Classification Act to secure the constitutional foundation. This is the most achievable reform with the highest-consistency coalition.
2. **Pursue DHHL reform** (Compromise 2): Reform Hawaiian Home Lands as a practical, tangible improvement that demonstrates institutional competence and addresses housing needs. This builds political capital and benefits the community immediately.
3. **Fund the self-determination process** (Compromise 3): A community-driven process that produces a clear Native Hawaiian preference on political status would transform the political dynamics by replacing congressional excuses about "community division" with a democratic mandate.
4. **Maintain transformative goals**: While pursuing achievable compromises, continue advocacy for comprehensive self-determination, ceded lands resolution, and structural reform as long-term objectives informed by the community process.
