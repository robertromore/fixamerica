# Native Hawaiians: Current State

## Political Status

### The Recognition Gap

Native Hawaiians are the only major Indigenous group in the United States without a formally recognized government-to-government relationship with the federal government. This gap exists despite:

- The United States' acknowledged role in the 1893 overthrow of the Hawaiian Kingdom
- The Apology Resolution (P.L. 103-150, 1993) recognizing the illegality of the overthrow
- Over 150 federal statutes specifically addressing Native Hawaiian needs
- The inclusion of Native Hawaiians in several federal programs alongside American Indians and Alaska Natives

| Status | American Indians/Alaska Natives | Native Hawaiians |
|--------|-------------------------------|-------------------|
| Federal recognition | 574 recognized tribes | No recognized governing entity |
| Government-to-government relationship | Established | Not established |
| Trust responsibility | Recognized | Implicit but not formalized |
| Self-governance authority | Extensive | None |
| Land held in trust | 56.2 million acres | 0 (HHCA lands are state-administered) |
| Gaming authority (IGRA) | Yes | No |

### Current Self-Governance Landscape

Multiple organizations claim to represent Native Hawaiian interests, but none has formal governmental status:

| Entity | Role | Status |
|--------|------|--------|
| Office of Hawaiian Affairs (OHA) | State agency managing ceded lands revenue | State entity; not a sovereign government |
| Department of Hawaiian Home Lands (DHHL) | Administers Hawaiian Home Lands under HHCA | State agency; federal trust oversight |
| Na'i Aupuni | Attempted constitutional convention (2015-2016) | Private; convention held but no government formed |
| Aha Moku Advisory Committee | Cultural resource management advisory | Advisory; no governance authority |
| Various sovereignty organizations | Independence or nation-within-nation advocacy | Unrecognized; politically active |

### Obama Administration Rule (2016)

In October 2016, the Department of the Interior finalized a rule (43 C.F.R. Part 50) establishing a process for Native Hawaiians to reorganize a government and seek a formal government-to-government relationship with the United States. Key features:

- Required Native Hawaiian community to organize a government through a democratic process
- The organized government would request a government-to-government relationship
- The Interior Secretary would evaluate the request based on specified criteria
- The relationship, if established, would be between the United States and the Native Hawaiian government

The Trump administration withdrew the rule in 2017, and it has not been reinstated.

## Federal Programs

### Hawaiian Homes Commission Act (1921)

The HHCA set aside approximately 200,000 acres of public lands for Native Hawaiians with at least 50% blood quantum. Administration was transferred to the State of Hawaii at statehood (1959) with federal oversight.

**Current status**:

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Total HHCA land | ~200,000 acres | DHHL |
| Leases issued (cumulative) | ~9,600 residential | DHHL, 2023 |
| Current waitlist | ~28,700 applicants | DHHL, 2023 |
| Average wait time | 20-30+ years | DHHL; media reports |
| Annual DHHL budget | ~$40 million state + variable federal | DHHL Annual Report |
| Lands in productive use | Less than 40% of available acreage | DHHL; OHA analysis |

**Key problems**:

- Decades-long waitlists with applicants dying before receiving leases
- Blood quantum requirement (50%) increasingly excludes mixed-heritage Native Hawaiians
- Significant acreage remains undeveloped due to infrastructure costs
- Federal oversight is minimal despite trust obligation
- State administration has been criticized as conflicted and underfunded

## Legal Landscape

### Key Constitutional Issues

#### Rice v. Cayetano (2000)

The Supreme Court struck down a Hawaii state constitutional provision limiting voting for Office of Hawaiian Affairs trustees to Native Hawaiians, holding it violated the Fifteenth Amendment. Key implications:

- OHA elections were opened to all Hawaii residents
- Raised questions about the constitutionality of all Hawaiian-specific programs
- Court did not resolve whether Native Hawaiians constitute a political (rather than racial) classification
- Prompted the push for formal federal recognition to establish a political basis for Hawaiian-specific programs

#### Ongoing Litigation

Several legal challenges affect Native Hawaiian programs:

| Case/Issue | Status | Stakes |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| Challenges to Kamehameha Schools admissions | Settled/ongoing | Whether Hawaiian-preference admissions are racial discrimination |
| Challenges to DHHL blood quantum | Periodic litigation | Whether 50% blood quantum is appropriate |
| Ceded lands revenue disputes | State-level | Whether OHA is entitled to revenue from ceded lands |
| Challenges to Native Hawaiian-specific federal programs | Ongoing threats | Whether programs survive strict scrutiny without political classification |

### The Constitutional Vulnerability

Without federal recognition establishing Native Hawaiians as a political classification (like American Indian tribes), every Hawaiian-specific program is vulnerable to challenge under the Equal Protection Clause:

- If Native Hawaiian is a racial classification, strict scrutiny applies
- Under strict scrutiny, programs must serve a compelling government interest and be narrowly tailored
- *Morton v. Mancari* (1974) held that Indian-specific programs are political, not racial -- but this has not been formally extended to Native Hawaiians
- *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023) reaffirmed the political classification of Indian status, potentially supporting extension to Native Hawaiians

## Sovereignty and Self-Determination Movements

### Federal Recognition Movement

**Goal**: Establish a Native Hawaiian governing entity with a government-to-government relationship with the United States, similar to (but distinct from) American Indian tribes.

**Key organizations**: Office of Hawaiian Affairs (historically), some Native Hawaiian civic clubs

**Arguments for**:

- Provides constitutional protection for Hawaiian-specific programs
- Establishes self-governance authority
- Creates framework for ceded lands resolution
- Acknowledges historical injustice within existing political framework

### Independence Movement

**Goal**: Restore the Hawaiian Kingdom as an independent, sovereign nation.

**Key organizations**: Ka Lahui Hawaii, various sovereignty groups

**Arguments for**:

- The overthrow was illegal under international law
- Hawaii was never lawfully annexed (Joint Resolution, not treaty)
- International law supports decolonization and self-determination
- Federal recognition within the U.S. is inadequate redress for the overthrow of a sovereign nation

### Free Association

**Goal**: Establish Hawaii as a freely associated state with the United States (similar to Palau, Marshall Islands, or Micronesia).

**Arguments for**:

- Preserves self-determination while maintaining security and economic relationships
- Has precedent in Pacific Island nations
- Offers a middle path between recognition and independence

## Recent Developments (2024-2025)

- No significant federal legislative action on Native Hawaiian recognition
- Continued litigation challenging the constitutionality of Hawaiian-specific programs
- Growing housing crisis in Hawaii disproportionately affecting Native Hawaiians
- Hawaiian language immersion programs continue to expand
- DHHL waitlist remains largely unchanged; infrastructure development on existing lands is slow
- Climate change impacts (sea level rise, coral reef degradation, extreme weather) threaten Native Hawaiian communities and traditional practices
