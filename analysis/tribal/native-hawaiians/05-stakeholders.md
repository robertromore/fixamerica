# Native Hawaiians: Stakeholders

## Overview

The Native Hawaiian political status question involves a complex array of stakeholders with distinct interests, ranging from Native Hawaiian communities themselves (which are internally divided) to federal and state governments, the military, the tourism industry, and mainland tribal organizations. The stakes are exceptionally high: they encompass sovereignty over 1.8 million acres of ceded lands, the constitutional future of over 150 federal programs, and the self-determination rights of more than 700,000 people.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Native Hawaiian Community

**Interest**: Self-determination, cultural preservation, land justice, and resolution of political status.

**Profile**:

- Approximately 370,000 in Hawaii; 350,000+ on the mainland
- Internally divided on the form self-determination should take
- Experiencing significant health, housing, education, and economic disparities
- Strong cultural identity and revitalization movement despite historical suppression

**Internal divisions**:

| Faction | Position | Estimated Support |
|---------|----------|-------------------|
| Federal recognition advocates | Nation-within-a-nation, like mainland tribes | Moderate; includes OHA, some civic clubs |
| Independence advocates | Full sovereignty restoration | Significant minority; passionate activism |
| Free association advocates | Sovereignty with U.S. relationship | Small but growing academic and policy support |
| Status quo supporters | Maintain current programs without change | Some; fear change could lose existing benefits |
| Integrationists | Oppose special status | Small minority |

**Key concerns**:

- Constitutional vulnerability of existing programs without political classification
- DHHL waitlist and housing crisis
- Ceded lands revenue and management
- Cultural and linguistic survival
- Health disparities
- Incarceration rates
- Climate change impacts on traditional resources and communities

**Influence level**: Moderate within Hawaii; limited at the federal level.

### Office of Hawaiian Affairs (OHA)

**Interest**: Advocacy for Native Hawaiian rights; administration of ceded lands revenue; support for self-governance.

**Profile**:

- State agency created by 1978 constitutional convention
- Governed by 9-member board elected by all Hawaii voters (post-*Rice v. Cayetano*)
- Manages a trust portfolio valued at approximately $700 million
- Provides grants, advocacy, and services to Native Hawaiian community

**Key concerns**:

- Constitutional vulnerability of OHA itself
- Ceded lands revenue disputes with the state
- Maintaining relevance if a separate Native Hawaiian government is established
- Balancing advocacy role with state agency status

**Influence level**: Moderate to high within Hawaii; significant financial resources.

### Department of Hawaiian Home Lands (DHHL)

**Interest**: Administering Hawaiian Home Lands for Native Hawaiian homesteaders.

**Profile**:

- State agency administering ~200,000 acres under HHCA
- Reports to the Hawaiian Homes Commission
- Chronically underfunded and understaffed
- Waitlist of ~28,700 applicants

**Key concerns**:

- Infrastructure funding for undeveloped lands
- Blood quantum requirement excluding many Native Hawaiians
- State vs. federal trust responsibility
- Land exchanges and development pressures

**Influence level**: Moderate within Hawaii; limited federal influence.

### Sovereignty and Independence Organizations

**Interest**: Restoration of Hawaiian independence or sovereignty outside the U.S. political framework.

**Key organizations**:

| Organization | Position |
|-------------|----------|
| Ka Lahui Hawaii | Hawaiian nation-within-nation (historically); some independence advocacy |
| Ka Pakaukau | Full independence |
| Hawaiian Kingdom Government (acting) | Asserts continued legal existence of Hawaiian Kingdom |
| Aloha Aina organizations | Land-based sovereignty; various positions |

**Influence level**: Culturally significant; limited formal political power.

## Secondary Stakeholders

### State of Hawaii Government

**Interest**: Governing the state, managing ceded lands, maintaining tourism economy, and addressing Native Hawaiian needs without ceding state authority.

**Key concerns**:

- Ceded lands revenue allocation (OHA claims vs. state budget)
- Jurisdictional impacts of federal recognition or sovereignty
- Tourism industry protection
- Military relationships and federal funding
- Political dynamics within a state where Native Hawaiians are a significant voting block

**Key actors**:

- Governor and executive branch (DHHL oversight)
- State legislature (appropriations, land management)
- State Attorney General (litigation, constitutional issues)

**Influence level**: High. The state controls DHHL, ceded lands, and much of the policy infrastructure.

### Federal Government

**Interest**: Fulfilling trust obligations while managing competing interests including military presence, national security, and budgetary constraints.

**Key agencies**:

| Agency | Role |
|--------|------|
| Department of the Interior | Historical oversight of Hawaiian affairs; developed the 2016 rule |
| Department of Education | Native Hawaiian Education Act programs |
| HHS | Native Hawaiian Health Care Act programs |
| HUD | Native Hawaiian housing programs |
| Department of Defense | Major military presence in Hawaii |

**Key concerns**:

- Constitutional defense of Native Hawaiian programs against equal protection challenges
- Military basing rights and land access
- Budget constraints on expanding programs
- Political dynamics of recognition legislation

**Influence level**: Very high. Federal government controls recognition, funding, and legal framework.

### U.S. Congress

**Interest**: Exercise of plenary power over Indian affairs; constituent and interest group pressures.

**Key committees**:

- Senate Committee on Indian Affairs
- House Natural Resources Committee
- Hawaii congressional delegation (2 Senators, 2 Representatives)

**Key concerns**:

- Constitutional questions about Hawaiian-specific legislation
- Budget implications of recognition
- Mainland tribal concerns about dilution of Indian affairs framework
- Political dynamics of recognition

**Influence level**: Very high. Congress has sole authority to establish federal recognition legislatively.

### Mainland Tribal Organizations

**Interest**: Protecting the integrity of the federal-tribal relationship and ensuring that Hawaiian recognition does not dilute tribal sovereignty or resources.

**Key organizations**:

- National Congress of American Indians (NCAI)
- Individual tribes with positions on Hawaiian recognition

**Key concerns**:

- Whether Hawaiian recognition creates a precedent that weakens the political classification doctrine
- Whether federal funding for Native Hawaiian programs reduces Indian affairs appropriations
- Whether the Hawaiian model is too different from mainland tribal governance to be analogous

**NCAI position**: Has generally supported Native Hawaiian recognition as consistent with Indigenous self-determination, with some reservations about specific implementation.

**Influence level**: Moderate. NCAI's position carries weight in Congress.

### Tourism Industry

**Interest**: Maintaining Hawaii's tourism economy, which generates approximately $20 billion annually.

**Key concerns**:

- Land use changes that could affect tourist destinations
- Cultural restrictions on tourism activities
- Environmental regulations affecting development
- Potential sovereignty-based tax or regulatory changes

**Influence level**: High within Hawaii; significant lobbying resources.

### U.S. Military

**Interest**: Maintaining military installations and operational capacity in the Pacific.

**Profile**:

- Over 100 military installations in Hawaii
- ~22% of Oahu land area
- Critical strategic location for Pacific operations
- Approximately 50,000 active-duty military and 100,000+ military-affiliated residents

**Key concerns**:

- Any sovereignty arrangement that could affect basing rights
- Environmental cleanup obligations (e.g., Red Hill)
- Cultural site access and management
- Ceded lands that include military installations

**Influence level**: Very high. Military presence is a major federal interest.

### Legal Advocacy Organizations

| Organization | Role |
|-------------|------|
| Native Hawaiian Legal Corporation | Legal representation for Native Hawaiians |
| Native American Rights Fund (NARF) | Has supported Hawaiian recognition |
| ACLU of Hawaii | Has taken varying positions on Hawaiian-specific programs |
| Pacific Legal Foundation | Has challenged Hawaiian-specific programs as racial preferences |
| Goldwater Institute | Has challenged Hawaiian-specific programs |

**Influence level**: Moderate. Litigation shapes the legal landscape.

## Affected Populations

### Native Hawaiians Without DHHL Leases

The 28,700 applicants on the DHHL waitlist represent the most immediately affected group:

- Many have waited 20-30+ years
- Applicants die on the waitlist at significant rates
- Blood quantum requirement increasingly excludes younger generations
- Without leases, they lack access to affordable housing in the state with the highest housing costs

### Native Hawaiians on the Mainland

Approximately half of all Native Hawaiians live outside Hawaii:

- Many left due to cost of living
- Maintain cultural identity but face disconnection from community
- Political participation in Hawaiian affairs is uncertain
- May not benefit from Hawaii-based self-governance arrangements

### Mixed-Heritage Hawaiians Below 50% Blood Quantum

The HHCA's 50% blood quantum requirement excludes a growing population:

- Due to intermarriage, the majority of people with Native Hawaiian ancestry now fall below 50%
- OHA uses 1/256th blood quantum; DHHL uses 50%
- This creates a shrinking eligible population and growing exclusion
- Blood quantum is itself a colonial construct imposed by the HHCA
