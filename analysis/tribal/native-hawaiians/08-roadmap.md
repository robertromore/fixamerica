# Native Hawaiians: Implementation Roadmap

## Strategic Framework

Advancing Native Hawaiian self-determination requires a multi-track strategy that simultaneously protects existing programs, builds community consensus, and pursues political status resolution. Unlike mainland tribal recognition, the Native Hawaiian situation involves a community divided on the fundamental question of what status to pursue. The roadmap must therefore prioritize community engagement and consensus-building alongside legislative and administrative action.

## Phase 1: Protect and Strengthen (Years 1-2)

### Objective

Secure the constitutional foundation for existing Native Hawaiian programs and address the most urgent needs.

### Actions

| Action | Mechanism | Lead Actor | Timeline |
|--------|-----------|------------|----------|
| Enact political classification legislation | Standalone bill | Congress | Year 1 |
| Reauthorize Native Hawaiian Health Care Act permanently | Legislation | Congress | Year 1 |
| Increase DHHL federal infrastructure funding | Appropriations | Congress/Interior | Year 1 |
| Reinstate 2016 Interior Department recognition rule | Rulemaking | Interior Department | Year 1 |
| Fund comprehensive community engagement process | Appropriations | Congress/OHA | Year 1-2 |
| Establish federal inspector general oversight of DHHL | Executive order/legislation | President/Congress | Year 2 |
| Begin blood quantum reform consultation | DHHL/community process | DHHL/Hawaiian community | Year 2 |

### Success Metrics

- Political classification legislation enacted
- NHHCIA permanently reauthorized
- DHHL federal funding increased by at least 50%
- Interior recognition pathway restored
- Community engagement process launched with participation from all major factions

### Political Strategy

- Frame political classification as protecting existing programs, not creating new ones
- Build on *Haaland v. Brackeen* precedent affirming political classification of Indigenous peoples
- Engage Hawaii delegation (all four members) as lead sponsors
- Seek mainland tribal support through NCAI

## Phase 2: Community Consensus and Governance Building (Years 2-5)

### Objective

Facilitate a community-driven process to determine Native Hawaiian preferences on political status and build governance capacity.

### Community Engagement Process

**Structure**:

- Community meetings across Hawaii and in major mainland Hawaiian communities
- Educational materials on all political status options (recognition, free association, independence, status quo)
- Expert presentations on legal, economic, and political implications of each option
- Traditional Hawaiian deliberation processes (ho'oponopono and community aha)
- Online participation for mainland Hawaiians

**Governance capacity building**:

| Activity | Purpose | Timeline |
|----------|---------|----------|
| Constitutional convention | Draft governing document | Year 3-4 |
| Leadership development | Train future government officials | Years 2-5 |
| Institutional design | Develop government structure proposals | Years 3-4 |
| Legal framework development | Draft laws and governance codes | Years 3-5 |
| Fiscal planning | Revenue and budget projections for self-governance | Years 3-5 |

### DHHL Reform

| Action | Timeline |
|--------|----------|
| Begin blood quantum reform (legislative amendment) | Year 2-3 |
| Initiate land exchanges for unsuitable parcels | Year 3 |
| Accelerate infrastructure development | Years 2-5 |
| Establish annual lease issuance targets | Year 2 |
| Waitlist reduction plan | Year 2 (plan); Years 3-5 (execution) |

### Success Metrics

- Community engagement reaching majority of Hawaiian adults in Hawaii and on mainland
- Constitutional convention held with broad participation
- Preliminary preference poll conducted
- DHHL waitlist reduced by 25%
- Blood quantum reform enacted or in progress

## Phase 3: Political Status Resolution (Years 5-8)

### Objective

Implement the community's preferred political status through legislation, negotiation, or international process.

### Scenario Planning

| Scenario | Actions | Lead Actor |
|----------|---------|------------|
| **Federal recognition** | Negotiate government-to-government agreement; transfer programs; ceded lands framework | Congress/Interior/Native Hawaiian Government |
| **Free association** | Negotiate compact of free association; UN oversight; transition plan | Congress/State Department/Native Hawaiian Government |
| **Independence** | International process; UN supervised plebiscite; transition plan | Congress/UN/Native Hawaiian Government |
| **Enhanced status quo** | Strengthen existing programs; formalize political classification; expand self-governance within state framework | Congress/State of Hawaii |

### Ceded Lands Resolution

Regardless of political status outcome:

- Negotiate ceded lands revenue-sharing agreement
- Establish joint management for culturally significant sites
- Create claims process for specific parcels
- Address military land use and environmental remediation
- Protect existing private property rights

### Success Metrics

- Formal self-determination vote conducted
- Political status framework enacted
- Ceded lands agreement negotiated
- Governance transition plan developed

## Phase 4: Implementation and Transition (Years 8-15)

### Objective

Implement the chosen political status and transition governance institutions.

### For Federal Recognition Scenario

| Action | Timeline | Mechanism |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| Establish Native Hawaiian government | Year 8-9 | Community-ratified constitution |
| Transfer DHHL to Native Hawaiian government | Year 9-10 | Federal legislation |
| Negotiate government-to-government agreements | Year 9-11 | Formal negotiations |
| Resolve ceded lands framework | Year 10-12 | Federal/state/tribal negotiation |
| Full self-governance operational | Year 12-15 | Phased transition |

### For Free Association/Independence Scenario

| Action | Timeline | Mechanism |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| International supervision of transition | Year 8-10 | UN/Pacific Forum |
| Constitutional government established | Year 8-9 | Ratification process |
| Security and economic agreements | Year 9-11 | Bilateral negotiation |
| Full sovereignty operational | Year 12-15 | Phased transition |

## Risk Assessment

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|--------|------------|
| Community consensus impossible | Medium | Very High | Design process to accommodate multiple outcomes; phased approach |
| Constitutional challenge to political classification | High | High | Strong legislative findings; *Brackeen* precedent; litigation defense |
| Congressional inaction | High | High | Executive pathways; state-level reforms; international advocacy |
| Administration change reverses progress | Medium | High | Seek bipartisan legislation; build institutional momentum |
| Internal opposition from sovereignty movement | High | Medium | Include independence option in plebiscite; preserve future options |
| Mainland tribal opposition | Low | Medium | NCAI engagement; demonstrate no dilution of tribal framework |
| Military resistance to land changes | Medium | High | Negotiate basing agreements separately; environmental remediation |
| State opposition to ceded lands resolution | Medium | High | Involve Hawaii delegation; negotiate revenue sharing |

## Coalition Map

### Core Coalition

- Hawaii congressional delegation (Senators and Representatives)
- Office of Hawaiian Affairs
- Native Hawaiian civic clubs and community organizations
- NCAI and mainland tribal allies
- Civil rights organizations

### Potential Allies

- Pacific Island nations (free association precedent)
- International Indigenous rights organizations
- Environmental organizations (Hawaiian land stewardship)
- Military (if basing rights are protected)
- State of Hawaii government (with negotiated terms)

### Persuadable Opposition

- Moderate Republicans (with fiscal responsibility framing)
- Mainland tribal organizations (with assurance of non-dilution)
- State agencies (with clear jurisdictional frameworks)

### Likely Persistent Opposition

- Constitutional originalist legal organizations
- Anti-sovereignty political actors
- Some fiscal conservative groups
- Tourism industry (on land and regulatory issues)
