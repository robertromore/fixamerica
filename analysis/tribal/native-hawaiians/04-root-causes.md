# Native Hawaiians: Root Causes

## Overview

The political, legal, and socioeconomic challenges facing Native Hawaiians stem from a chain of events beginning with the illegal overthrow of the Hawaiian Kingdom in 1893 and perpetuated by structural failures in federal and state policy. The root causes fall into six major categories: colonial dispossession, constitutional ambiguity, institutional design failures, political fragmentation, economic displacement, and cultural suppression.

## Root Cause Analysis

### 1. Colonial Dispossession and the Overthrow

**The foundational cause**: The United States overthrew a sovereign nation, took its lands, and then failed to establish any framework for the self-determination of its Indigenous people.

#### Chain of Dispossession

| Event | Land Impact | Governance Impact |
|-------|------------|-------------------|
| Great Mahele (1848) | Enabled foreign land acquisition | Undermined traditional land management |
| Bayonet Constitution (1887) | Secured foreign elite control | Stripped monarch of power |
| Overthrow (1893) | Crown and Government lands seized | Self-governance destroyed |
| Annexation (1898) | 1.8 million acres "ceded" to U.S. | No Native Hawaiian political status |
| HHCA (1921) | 200,000 acres set aside (but poorly selected) | No self-governance authority |
| Statehood (1959) | Ceded lands to state; DHHL to state control | No plebiscite; no independence option |

#### The Unique Nature of Hawaiian Dispossession

Unlike mainland tribes, who retained some recognition of their political status through treaties and the reservation system, Native Hawaiians lost their entire nation:

- The Hawaiian Kingdom was a sovereign nation recognized by the international community
- The overthrow was accomplished with U.S. military support
- Annexation was through a joint resolution, not a treaty, bypassing the consent requirement
- No treaty was ever signed with Native Hawaiians
- No reservation was established
- No government-to-government relationship was created

### 2. Constitutional and Legal Ambiguity

#### The Political vs. Racial Classification Problem

The most significant legal root cause is the unresolved question of whether Native Hawaiians constitute a political classification (like American Indian tribes) or a racial classification:

| If Political Classification | If Racial Classification |
|-----------------------------|-------------------------|
| Programs subject to rational basis review (*Morton v. Mancari*) | Programs subject to strict scrutiny |
| Self-governance is an exercise of inherent sovereignty | Self-governance is racial separatism |
| Land trusts are analogous to Indian Country | Land trusts are race-based preferences |
| Federal programs are fulfilling trust responsibility | Federal programs are racial entitlements |

**Why this is unresolved**:

- Native Hawaiians were never recognized as a "tribe" under federal Indian law
- *Rice v. Cayetano* (2000) struck down a Hawaiian-only voting provision without resolving the broader classification question
- Congress has not enacted recognition legislation that would establish the political classification
- *Morton v. Mancari* and *Haaland v. Brackeen* apply to recognized Indian tribes, not automatically to Native Hawaiians
- Every Hawaiian-specific program exists in constitutional jeopardy

### 3. Institutional Design Failures

#### Hawaiian Home Lands

The HHCA's failures are rooted in its original design and compounded by state administration:

| Design Flaw | Description | Consequence |
|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Land selection | Best agricultural lands excluded; much of HHCA land is lava, remote, or arid | Land unsuitable for homesteading without massive infrastructure investment |
| Blood quantum | 50% Native Hawaiian blood quantum requirement | Excludes growing majority of mixed-heritage Native Hawaiians |
| Infrastructure unfunded | No dedicated federal funding for roads, water, utilities | Lands cannot be developed for homesteading |
| State administration | Transferred to state with inherent conflicts of interest | State has competing priorities for land and resources |
| Lease model | 99-year leases, not fee simple ownership | Limits wealth-building; leases revert to state |
| Federal oversight | Minimal and episodic | State violations go uncorrected |

#### Office of Hawaiian Affairs

OHA was created by the 1978 state constitutional convention with a flawed design:

- **Not a sovereign government**: A state agency, not an Indigenous government
- **Voter base**: After *Rice v. Cayetano*, all Hawaii voters elect OHA trustees, not just Native Hawaiians
- **Revenue source**: Depends on ceded lands revenue controlled by the state legislature
- **Mission confusion**: Combines advocacy, service delivery, and asset management
- **No governance authority**: Cannot exercise sovereignty, jurisdiction, or self-determination

### 4. Political Fragmentation

#### Internal Divisions

The Native Hawaiian community is divided on fundamental political questions:

| Position | Advocates | Arguments |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Federal recognition (nation within a nation) | OHA, some civic clubs | Constitutional protection; self-governance; pragmatic |
| Full independence | Ka Lahui, sovereignty groups | Overthrow was illegal; international law supports decolonization; recognition is insufficient |
| Free association | Some scholars and advocates | Middle path; sovereignty with security; Pacific precedent |
| Status quo | Some Hawaiian community members | Change is risky; current programs could be jeopardized |
| Integration | Some Native Hawaiians | All Americans should be treated equally; oppose special status |

**Why fragmentation matters**:

- Congress has used internal disagreement as a reason to delay action
- No single organization can claim to speak for all Native Hawaiians
- The 2015-2016 Na'i Aupuni process highlighted divisions
- External opponents exploit internal disagreements

#### Mainland vs. Hawaii-Based Hawaiians

Approximately 350,000+ Native Hawaiians live outside Hawaii, raising questions about:

- Who participates in self-determination decisions
- Whether a Hawaii-based government can serve mainland residents
- How voting and membership in a future Native Hawaiian entity would work

### 5. Economic Displacement

#### The Housing Crisis

Hawaii's cost of living -- the highest in the nation -- disproportionately affects Native Hawaiians:

- **Median home price**: Over $800,000 on Oahu (2024)
- **Native Hawaiian homeownership**: ~45%, compared to ~60% statewide
- **DHHL waitlist**: 28,700 applicants, many waiting decades
- **Diaspora**: Many Native Hawaiians leave Hawaii entirely due to cost of living, weakening community bonds and political power

#### Tourism Economy

Hawaii's tourism-dependent economy creates structural problems:

- Tourism generates service-sector jobs that are low-wage and seasonal
- Tourist development consumes land, water, and cultural resources
- Sacred sites and cultural landscapes are commodified
- Housing is diverted to vacation rentals and tourist infrastructure
- Environmental degradation affects traditional food sources and practices

#### Military Occupation

The U.S. military occupies significant Hawaiian land:

- ~22% of Oahu land area is used by the military
- Military installations on former Crown and ceded lands
- Environmental contamination (e.g., Red Hill fuel storage facility near Pearl Harbor water supply)
- Cultural sites damaged or inaccessible due to military use

### 6. Cultural and Linguistic Suppression

#### Historical Suppression

- Hawaiian language was banned in public schools in 1896 (after the overthrow)
- Traditional religious practices were suppressed during the missionary era and after annexation
- Cultural knowledge systems were devalued in favor of Western education
- Population collapse (from 800,000+ to under 40,000) caused catastrophic loss of cultural knowledge holders

#### Ongoing Pressures

- Hawaiian language, though reviving, has only ~24,000 fluent or proficient speakers
- Traditional land and ocean management practices are constrained by state and federal regulations
- Sacred sites face development pressure (e.g., Mauna Kea telescope controversy)
- Cultural commodification through tourism reduces Hawaiian culture to performance
- Public school curriculum gives limited attention to Hawaiian history and culture

## Causal Chain

```text
Overthrow of Hawaiian Kingdom (1893)
        ↓
Annexation without consent (1898) → Loss of sovereignty, land, self-governance
        ↓
No political status established → Constitutional ambiguity
        ↓
HHCA poorly designed (1921) → Land fails to reach beneficiaries
        ↓
Statehood without self-determination (1959) → State controls Hawaiian programs
        ↓
Rice v. Cayetano (2000) → Constitutional vulnerability exposed
        ↓
Recognition legislation fails (2000-2012) → Status quo perpetuated
        ↓
Obama rule withdrawn (2017) → Executive pathway closed
        ↓
Political fragmentation → No unified demand for specific resolution
        ↓
Continued disparity and dispossession → Native Hawaiians in legal limbo
```

## Systemic Reinforcement

These root causes reinforce each other:

- Without political status, Hawaiian programs are constitutionally vulnerable
- Constitutional vulnerability prevents Congress from acting boldly
- Congressional inaction perpetuates institutional design failures
- Institutional failures drive economic displacement
- Economic displacement weakens political power and community cohesion
- Weakened community cohesion deepens political fragmentation
- Political fragmentation provides cover for continued inaction
- The cycle continues
