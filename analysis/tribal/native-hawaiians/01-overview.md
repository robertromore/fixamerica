# Native Hawaiians: Overview

## Executive Summary

Native Hawaiians occupy a unique and contested position in American law and policy. Unlike American Indian and Alaska Native tribes on the mainland, Native Hawaiians have never received formal federal recognition as an Indigenous governing entity, despite the United States' acknowledged role in the 1893 overthrow of the Hawaiian Kingdom -- a sovereign nation recognized by the international community. The 1993 Apology Resolution (P.L. 103-150) formally acknowledged the illegality of the overthrow and the deprivation of Native Hawaiian self-determination, yet no political mechanism has been established to fulfill the resolution's implicit promise of reconciliation.

Approximately 370,000 Native Hawaiians and Pacific Islanders reside in Hawaii, with an additional 350,000+ on the mainland. They experience significant disparities in health, housing, education, and economic well-being compared to non-Native residents of Hawaii. Federal programs serving Native Hawaiians -- including the Department of Hawaiian Home Lands (DHHL), Native Hawaiian Health Care, and Native Hawaiian Education programs -- operate under a patchwork of authorizing statutes without the foundational government-to-government relationship that supports similar programs for American Indian and Alaska Native communities.

The central policy question -- whether and how Native Hawaiians should receive federal recognition or establish a self-governing entity -- has generated decades of debate. The Native Hawaiian Government Reorganization Act (the "Akaka Bill") was introduced in Congress repeatedly from 2000 to 2012 but never enacted. The Obama administration's 2016 Final Rule establishing a pathway for Native Hawaiian government reorganization was withdrawn by the Trump administration in 2017. Meanwhile, a separate sovereignty movement advocates for complete independence from the United States rather than recognition within it.

Resolving Native Hawaiian political status requires confronting fundamental questions about self-determination, colonialism, reconciliation, and the appropriate relationship between an Indigenous people and the nation that overthrew their government.

## Scope

### What This Subtopic Covers

- **Political status**: Federal recognition, self-governance, and sovereignty questions
- **Historical context**: The overthrow of the Hawaiian Kingdom, annexation, and statehood
- **Federal programs**: Hawaiian Homes Commission Act, Native Hawaiian healthcare and education programs
- **Legal framework**: Apology Resolution, Rice v. Cayetano, and the constitutionality of Hawaiian-specific programs
- **Self-determination movements**: Federal recognition, nation-within-a-nation, and independence movements
- **Land rights**: Hawaiian Home Lands, ceded lands (Crown and Government lands), and the Public Land Trust
- **Cultural preservation**: Language revitalization, traditional practices, and sacred sites
- **Socioeconomic disparities**: Health, housing, education, and income gaps

### What This Subtopic Does Not Cover

- **General Hawaii state politics**: Unless directly related to Native Hawaiian policy
- **Pacific Islander issues**: Other Pacific Islander communities (Samoan, Marshallese, etc.)
- **Federal recognition process**: The Part 83 process is covered in [Federal Recognition](../federal-recognition/01-overview.md); this subtopic focuses on the distinct Native Hawaiian pathway
- **Military presence in Hawaii**: Except as it affects Native Hawaiian lands and rights

## Core Tensions

- **Federal recognition vs. independence**: Some Native Hawaiians seek a government-to-government relationship with the United States; others seek full restoration of sovereignty as an independent nation
- **Political classification vs. racial classification**: Federal programs for Native Hawaiians face constitutional challenges arguing they constitute racial preferences (*Rice v. Cayetano*, 2000)
- **Self-determination vs. existing institutions**: Creating a Native Hawaiian governing entity requires balancing new political structures with existing state institutions and land management frameworks
- **Land justice vs. development**: The Hawaiian Home Lands program has failed to distribute available land while ceded lands generate revenue that is contested between the state and Native Hawaiian interests
- **Cultural preservation vs. economic pressure**: Traditional Hawaiian practices, language, and land relationships face pressure from tourism, military, development, and cost-of-living dynamics
- **Mainland Native Hawaiians vs. Hawaii-based community**: Recognition or self-governance raises questions about the political participation of Native Hawaiians living outside Hawaii

## Key Questions

1. **Should Native Hawaiians receive federal recognition?** What form should political status take -- a tribal-like government, a unique sui generis entity, or restored independence?

2. **Is the Apology Resolution enforceable?** What obligations does the U.S. government's acknowledgment of the illegal overthrow create?

3. **Are Hawaiian-specific programs constitutional?** How do *Rice v. Cayetano* and subsequent litigation affect the legal basis for Native Hawaiian programs?

4. **What should happen with ceded lands?** How should the 1.8 million acres of Crown and Government lands be managed, and who should benefit from their revenue?

5. **Can the Hawaiian Home Lands program be fixed?** Why has DHHL failed to fulfill its mandate despite having 200,000 acres, and what reforms would work?

6. **How should self-determination be exercised?** What process would legitimately determine Native Hawaiian preferences on political status?

## Vision of Success

A just resolution of Native Hawaiian political status would feature:

- Native Hawaiians exercising genuine self-determination over their political future, through a democratic process that includes all Native Hawaiians
- An established political entity (whatever its form) with recognized authority over internal affairs, cultural preservation, and resource management
- Resolution of ceded lands claims in a manner that provides resources for Native Hawaiian programs and acknowledges historical injustice
- Functional delivery of Hawaiian Home Lands to eligible applicants without decades-long waits
- Native Hawaiian health, education, housing, and economic indicators approaching or exceeding state averages
- Hawaiian language and cultural practices thriving through adequately funded, community-controlled programs
- A legal framework for Native Hawaiian programs that is constitutionally secure against continued challenge
