# Indian Health Service: Actions

## Overview

Improving Indian health requires action from citizens, communities, health professionals, and political leaders. This document provides specific, actionable steps for individuals and organizations who want to support the transformation of health care for American Indians and Alaska Natives.

## Individual Actions

### Educate Yourself

- **Learn the basics**: IHS serves 2.8 million AI/AN people with a budget of $7.1 billion -- less than one-third of comparable federal health program per-capita spending
- **Read the data**: The U.S. Commission on Civil Rights' *Broken Promises* report (2018) documents the systematic underfunding of IHS
- **Understand the obligation**: Health care for AI/AN people is not welfare -- it is a treaty obligation exchanged for land cessions
- **Follow Indian health news**: National Indian Health Board (nihb.org), Indian Country Today, Native News Online

### Direct Support

- **Donate to tribal health organizations**: National Indian Health Board, National Council of Urban Indian Health, tribal health foundations
- **Support behavioral health initiatives**: Donate to or volunteer with tribal suicide prevention and substance use programs
- **Remote volunteer opportunities**: Some tribal health programs accept tele-volunteer health professionals
- **Support Native health professional students**: Donate to AI/AN health scholarship programs through AAIP or tribal colleges

### Professional Actions (Health Care Workers)

- **Serve in IHS or tribal health**: Consider careers or rotations in IHS, tribal, or urban Indian health programs
- **IHS Loan Repayment Program**: Offers up to $40,000 in student loan repayment for 2-year service commitments
- **National Health Service Corps**: Includes IHS and tribal facilities as eligible placement sites
- **Cultural competency training**: Seek training in Indigenous health, historical trauma, and culturally respectful care
- **Telehealth**: Provide specialty care via telehealth to underserved tribal areas
- **Mentoring**: Mentor AI/AN students pursuing health careers through AAIP or tribal college programs

### Professional Actions (Non-Health-Care)

- **Attorneys**: Provide pro bono support for tribal health policy advocacy; assist with Medicaid enrollment, benefits access
- **Educators**: Incorporate Indigenous health disparities and history into curricula
- **Journalists**: Report on IHS funding gaps and health disparities with accuracy and context
- **Researchers**: Partner with tribal communities and Tribal Epidemiology Centers on community-driven research (respect tribal data sovereignty)

## Community Actions

### Local Government and Institutions

- **Support local-tribal health cooperation**: Encourage county and city health departments to develop cooperative agreements with tribal health programs
- **Advocate for state Medicaid coordination**: Support state policies that maximize Medicaid coverage for AI/AN people (including 100% FMAP for services at IHS/tribal facilities)
- **Hospital partnerships**: Encourage local hospitals to develop referral and partnership agreements with nearby IHS or tribal facilities
- **Emergency services coordination**: Support mutual aid agreements between tribal and non-tribal emergency medical services

### Educational and Research Institutions

- **Health pipeline programs**: Support programs that recruit and support AI/AN students in health professions
- **Research partnerships**: Develop ethical research partnerships with tribal communities that respect data sovereignty
- **Residency rotations**: Establish medical residency rotations in IHS and tribal health facilities
- **Continuing education**: Offer continuing education programs on Indigenous health and cultural competency

### Faith Communities and Nonprofits

- **Health mission trips**: Support (with appropriate cultural sensitivity) health service projects in partnership with tribal health programs
- **Advocacy**: Incorporate IHS funding advocacy into organizational justice and equity agendas
- **Community health worker support**: Fund training and support for Community Health Representatives and Community Health Aides

## Political Actions

### Federal Level

- **Contact your members of Congress**: Call or write to support:
    - Mandatory IHS funding to end chronic underfunding
    - IHS workforce investments (competitive pay, housing, loan repayment expansion)
    - Facility construction funding to replace aging hospitals
    - Expanded behavioral health and suicide prevention programs
    - Urban Indian health program expansion

- **Support annual budget advocacy**: Track the NCAI/NIHB annual budget request and advocate for recommended funding levels
- **Testify or submit public comments**: Participate in congressional hearings, appropriations testimony, and regulatory comment periods on IHS issues
- **Support champions**: Identify and support members of Congress who advocate for Indian health

### State Level

- **Medicaid expansion**: Advocate for Medicaid expansion in states that have not yet expanded (benefits AI/AN people who are eligible)
- **100% FMAP awareness**: Educate state officials about the 100% federal matching rate for Medicaid services at IHS/tribal facilities -- this saves states money while improving AI/AN access
- **State tribal liaison offices**: Support state offices dedicated to government-to-government relations with tribes, including health coordination
- **Scope-of-practice reform**: Support legislation allowing Community Health Aides and traditional healers to practice within tribal health programs

## Talking Points

### For Conservative Audiences

- IHS is a contractual obligation arising from treaties, not a social welfare program. Conservatives honor contracts.
- Self-governance compacts demonstrate that tribal management outperforms federal bureaucracy -- a conservative success story
- Efficient IHS funding prevents expensive emergency care and disability costs downstream
- Advance appropriations (achieved 2024) provide fiscal predictability and planning efficiency

### For Liberal Audiences

- Health care is a human right, and AI/AN people have the worst health outcomes in America
- The 12-year life expectancy gap is a civil rights crisis rooted in systemic underfunding
- IHS underfunding is the legacy of colonialism; adequate funding is a step toward justice
- Behavioral health and suicide prevention are urgent life-and-death priorities

### For General Audiences

- The United States signed 374 treaties promising health care in exchange for land
- IHS spends $4,078 per patient -- less than a third of what Medicare spends
- Native Americans have the lowest life expectancy of any group in the U.S.
- This is not about money -- it's about keeping promises. IHS costs less than 0.12% of the federal budget

## Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Reality |
|--------------|---------|
| "IHS provides free health care to all Native Americans" | IHS is chronically underfunded, often rationing care to life-threatening cases only. Services are not "free" -- they were purchased through land cessions worth trillions. |
| "All Native Americans have access to IHS" | Only 2.8 million of 5.6 million eligible people access IHS services. Urban AI/AN people (78% of the population) have minimal access through only 41 urban Indian programs. |
| "IHS is like socialized medicine" | IHS fulfills a specific treaty and trust obligation -- it is a contractual commitment, not a general government health program. |
| "Tribes should fund their own health care from gaming revenue" | Most tribes have modest or no gaming income. Regardless, treaty obligations exist independently of tribal revenue, just as Medicare exists regardless of state revenue. |
| "More money won't fix IHS" | Where funding has increased -- notably the $6.1 billion American Rescue Plan supplement and programs like SDPI -- outcomes have measurably improved. |
| "Native Americans can just use regular health care" | Many tribal communities are hundreds of miles from the nearest private provider. AI/AN uninsured rates (17.8%) are double the national average. |
| "IHS problems are about management, not money" | Management challenges are real but are themselves products of underfunding. Tribally managed programs with adequate funding consistently outperform underfunded federal direct service. |
