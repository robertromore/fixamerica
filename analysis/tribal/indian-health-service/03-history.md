# Indian Health Service: History

## Overview

The history of health care for American Indians and Alaska Natives mirrors the broader history of federal Indian policy -- oscillating between neglect, paternalism, and self-determination. From treaty-era promises of medical care to the establishment of IHS in 1955 and the self-determination revolution of the 1970s, each era has shaped the system's current strengths and deficiencies. Understanding this history reveals why IHS is structured as it is and why reform has been so difficult.

---

## Treaty and Early Federal Period (1778-1849)

### Health Provisions in Treaties

Many of the 374 treaties between the United States and tribal nations included explicit health care provisions:

- **Treaty of Fort Laramie (1868)**: Required the United States to provide a physician for the Sioux Nation
- **Treaty of Medicine Lodge (1867)**: Promised medical services for the Kiowa, Comanche, and Apache
- **Multiple land cession treaties**: Included provisions for "physicians and medicines" as part of the consideration for tribal land cessions

These provisions were not charity -- they were contractual terms of trade. Tribes ceded hundreds of millions of acres in exchange for specific promises, including health care.

### Early Health Challenges

- European diseases (smallpox, measles, influenza) devastated tribal populations
- Estimated 90% population decline among some tribes due to introduced diseases (1500-1900)
- U.S. Army physicians provided sporadic care on military posts
- No organized health system for tribal communities existed

---

## Bureau of Indian Affairs Health Division (1849-1955)

### BIA Medical Responsibility

When the Bureau of Indian Affairs transferred from the War Department to the newly created Department of the Interior in 1849, it assumed responsibility for whatever health services were being provided:

- Medical services were minimal and administered by Indian agents with no medical training
- The Snyder Act (1921) provided the first general authorization for federal health services to Indians
- BIA health services grew slowly but remained underfunded and poorly organized

### Key Developments

| Year | Development | Significance |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| 1832 | Congress authorizes $12,000 for smallpox vaccination | First federal health expenditure for Indians |
| 1849 | BIA transferred to Interior Department | Medical responsibilities transferred from military |
| 1908 | First dedicated health appropriation | $40,000 for "prevention and treatment of trachoma" |
| 1921 | Snyder Act | General authorization for federal health and welfare services |
| 1928 | Meriam Report | Documented appalling health conditions; called for major reforms |
| 1934 | Indian Reorganization Act | Supported tribal self-governance but health remained BIA-administered |
| 1954 | Transfer Act (P.L. 83-568) | Transferred Indian health from BIA to U.S. Public Health Service |

### The Meriam Report (1928)

The landmark *Problem of Indian Administration* (Meriam Report) documented devastating health conditions:

- Tuberculosis and trachoma were epidemic
- Infant mortality was three times the national average
- Hospitals were overcrowded and unsanitary
- Medical staff were undertrained and underpaid
- Per-capita health spending was a fraction of white population spending
- The report recommended comprehensive reforms but many went unimplemented for decades

## Establishment of IHS (1955-1975)

### Transfer to Public Health Service

The Indian Health Service was formally established in 1955 when health responsibilities transferred from the BIA to the U.S. Public Health Service within the Department of Health, Education, and Welfare (now HHS):

- **Rationale**: BIA was seen as unable to deliver quality health care; PHS had medical expertise
- **Initial structure**: Organized into area offices and service units across Indian Country
- **Staffing**: Commissioned Corps officers provided physician workforce
- **Early focus**: Infectious disease control, sanitation, hospital construction

### Progress and Persistent Gaps

| Era | Achievement | Remaining Gap |
|-----|-------------|--------------|
| 1955-1965 | Constructed 50+ hospitals and clinics | Facilities concentrated in reservation areas; urban AI/AN unserved |
| 1960s | Reduced TB and trachoma rates dramatically | Chronic diseases (diabetes, heart disease) emerged as new threats |
| 1960-1970 | Infant mortality reduced by 50% | Still double the national average |
| 1965-1975 | Sanitation facilities program improved water/sewer | Many communities still lacked basic sanitation |

### Community Health Representative Program

Established in the 1960s, the Community Health Representative (CHR) program trained tribal community members to provide basic health education and outreach:

- Model recognized that Western medical facilities alone were insufficient
- CHRs served as bridges between IHS clinics and communities
- Program continues today with approximately 1,600 CHRs nationally
- Precursor to the community health worker model now widely adopted

## Self-Determination Era (1975-2000)

### Legislative Framework

| Year | Legislation | Impact on Indian Health |
|------|-------------|----------------------|
| 1975 | Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act (P.L. 93-638) | Tribes could contract to operate health programs; beginning of tribal health management |
| 1976 | Indian Health Care Improvement Act (IHCIA) | Comprehensive statutory framework for IHS; declared congressional policy to provide "highest possible health status" |
| 1988 | Self-Governance Demonstration Project | Piloted tribal self-governance compacts; health programs included |
| 1992 | IHCIA Amendments | Expanded urban Indian health; added substance abuse and behavioral health provisions |
| 1994 | Self-Governance Act | Made self-governance compacting permanent; expanded to IHS programs |
| 2000 | IHCIA Reauthorization | Updated provisions; expanded scope |

### Tribal Health Management

The self-determination era transformed health care delivery in Indian Country:

- Tribes increasingly contracted (638 contracts) or compacted (self-governance) to operate their own health programs
- By 2000, tribally operated programs managed over 50% of IHS funding
- Alaska Native Tribal Health Consortium (ANTHC) became a model for comprehensive tribal health management
- Evidence consistently showed that tribally managed programs produced equal or better outcomes at similar cost

### Persistent Challenges

Despite legislative advances:

- Funding remained discretionary and inadequate
- Purchased/referred care (then Contract Health Services) was chronically underfunded
- Workforce shortages grew as Commissioned Corps participation declined
- New health threats (diabetes epidemic, substance abuse, HIV) emerged without commensurate funding
- Urban Indian health programs served a tiny fraction of the off-reservation AI/AN population

## Modern Era (2000-Present)

### Indian Health Care Improvement Act Permanent Reauthorization (2010)

The Affordable Care Act (2010) included permanent reauthorization of IHCIA, ending the cycle of expiration and reauthorization that had created periodic uncertainty:

- **Permanent authority**: IHCIA no longer subject to expiration
- **Expanded provisions**: Behavioral health, long-term care, diabetes prevention, urban Indian health, hospice
- **Third-party billing**: Strengthened IHS ability to bill Medicare, Medicaid, and private insurance
- **CHEF program**: Community Health Aide Program authorized outside Alaska

### COVID-19 Pandemic (2020-2022)

The pandemic exposed and amplified IHS deficiencies:

- AI/AN communities experienced 1.8-3.5x higher infection rates (CDC, 2023)
- Navajo Nation had the highest per-capita infection rate in the U.S. during early pandemic
- Overcrowded housing, lack of running water, and limited health infrastructure exacerbated transmission
- Tribal nations also demonstrated remarkable resilience and self-governance capacity:
    - Many tribes implemented faster, more effective public health measures than surrounding states
    - Tribal health systems achieved some of the highest vaccination rates in the country
    - American Rescue Plan provided $6.1 billion in supplemental tribal health funding

### Advance Appropriations Achievement (2024)

After decades of advocacy, IHS received advance appropriations authority:

- Provides two-year funding certainty
- Protects against government shutdown disruptions
- Does not address overall funding adequacy
- Represents significant political victory and precedent

### Quality and Accreditation Crises

Multiple IHS facilities have faced serious quality and safety issues:

| Facility | Year | Issue |
|----------|------|-------|
| Rosebud IHS Hospital (SD) | 2015-2016 | Emergency department closed due to CMS deficiencies |
| Pine Ridge IHS Hospital (SD) | 2016 | CMS threatened decertification for quality violations |
| Winnebago IHS Hospital (NE) | 2017 | Serious surgical quality concerns |
| Multiple Great Plains facilities | 2015-2018 | Systemic quality failures documented by CMS and OIG |
| Lawton IHS Hospital (OK) | 2019 | Accreditation concerns |

These crises reflect the consequences of chronic underfunding, understaffing, and deferred maintenance rather than inherent problems with the IHS model.

## Key Turning Points

| Year | Event | Impact |
|------|-------|--------|
| 1921 | Snyder Act | First general authorization for Indian health services |
| 1928 | Meriam Report | Documented health crisis; catalyzed reform |
| 1955 | IHS established under PHS | Professionalized Indian health; began systematic care |
| 1975 | Indian Self-Determination Act | Tribes gained control over health program administration |
| 1976 | Indian Health Care Improvement Act | Comprehensive statutory framework for Indian health |
| 1988 | Self-governance compacting begins | Tribes manage health programs with maximum autonomy |
| 2010 | IHCIA permanently reauthorized (via ACA) | Ended reauthorization uncertainty; expanded scope |
| 2020-2022 | COVID-19 pandemic | Exposed underfunding; demonstrated tribal resilience; triggered emergency funding |
| 2024 | Advance appropriations achieved | Two-year budget certainty; protected from shutdowns |

## Lessons from History

1. **Treaty obligations created a binding health care duty**: Health care provisions in treaties were consideration for land cessions. They are not welfare programs; they are contract terms.

2. **Funding has never matched the obligation**: In every era, from the BIA period through today, health funding for AI/AN communities has been a fraction of what comparable populations receive.

3. **Tribal management improves outcomes**: When tribes control their own health programs, outcomes consistently improve. The Alaska model demonstrates this most clearly.

4. **Discretionary funding is the fundamental structural problem**: As long as IHS remains subject to annual appropriations, it cannot plan, recruit, or deliver services effectively.

5. **Crises reveal failures but rarely produce lasting reform**: The Meriam Report (1928), COVID-19 pandemic, and hospital accreditation crises all exposed systemic problems, but structural fixes remain incomplete.

6. **Western medicine alone is insufficient**: Community-based programs (CHRs, Community Health Aides, traditional healing integration) consistently demonstrate value and should be expanded.
