# Indian Health Service: Current State

## System Structure

### Three Delivery Systems

IHS delivers health care through three interrelated systems:

| System | Description | Share of Budget | Facilities/Programs |
|--------|-------------|----------------|-------------------|
| **IHS Direct Service** | Federally operated hospitals, clinics, and health stations | ~37% | 24 hospitals, 51 health centers, 32 health stations |
| **Tribal Programs (638)** | Tribally operated programs under Self-Determination Act contracts and compacts | ~58% | 22 hospitals, 287 outpatient facilities, various community programs |
| **Urban Indian Organizations (UIOs)** | Urban health programs serving off-reservation AI/AN populations | ~1.2% ($82 million) | 41 programs in 22 states |

### Service Areas

- **12 Area Offices**: Portland, Tucson, Oklahoma City, Navajo, Phoenix, Albuquerque, Bemidji, Billings, Great Plains, Nashville, Alaska, California
- **37 states** with IHS presence
- **Service population**: 2.8 million users (FY 2023)
- **Eligible population**: Approximately 5.6 million (enrolled members of federally recognized tribes and their descendants)

### Funding Gaps

The U.S. Commission on Civil Rights' *Broken Promises* report (2018) estimated that IHS is funded at approximately 40% of need. Key gaps include:

| Category | FY 2024 Funding | Estimated Need | Gap |
|----------|----------------|---------------|-----|
| Hospitals and clinics | $2.3 billion | $5.0 billion | -54% |
| Purchased/Referred Care | $1.0 billion | $2.5 billion | -60% |
| Mental health | $115 million | $500 million | -77% |
| Alcohol/substance abuse | $255 million | $600 million | -58% |
| Dental health | $214 million | $500 million | -57% |
| Facilities construction | $259 million | $14+ billion (deferred maintenance) | -98% |
| Urban Indian health | $82 million | $500 million | -84% |

*Sources: IHS Budget Justification FY 2024; NCAI FY 2024 Budget Request; GAO facility assessments*

### Purchased/Referred Care Crisis

When IHS facilities cannot provide a service, patients are referred to outside providers through Purchased/Referred Care (PRC, formerly Contract Health Services). This system is critically underfunded:

- PRC funds are exhausted partway through each fiscal year in most service areas
- Patients are prioritized by medical urgency (Priority I = life-threatening; Priority IV = preventive)
- Many service areas can only fund Priority I cases (immediately life-threatening)
- Patients who are Priority II-IV may wait months or years, or never receive care
- Federal Tort Claims Act coverage for PRC providers has expanded access somewhat

### Recruitment and Retention Challenges

- **Geographic isolation**: Many IHS facilities are in extremely remote locations, hours from urban amenities
- **Compensation**: IHS salary ranges lag private sector and VA by 20-40% for many positions (IHS HR, 2023)
- **Housing**: Many service areas lack adequate housing for health care workers
- **Facilities**: Aging infrastructure and equipment limitations deter providers
- **Burnout**: High patient acuity, understaffing, and limited resources contribute to rapid turnover
- **Loan repayment**: IHS Loan Repayment Program (up to $40,000 for 2-year commitment) helps but cannot close the gap; program is oversubscribed

### Accreditation

- Some IHS hospitals have lost or been threatened with loss of CMS accreditation due to quality and safety deficiencies
- Notable incidents include Rosebud IHS Hospital (South Dakota) emergency department closure (2015-2016) and Great Plains Area persistent quality issues
- CMS Conditions of Participation violations have occurred at multiple facilities

### Telehealth Expansion

- COVID-19 pandemic accelerated telehealth adoption across IHS
- Telehealth visits increased from ~5,000 (FY 2019) to ~700,000 (FY 2021) (IHS, 2022)
- Broadband limitations in many tribal areas constrain telehealth expansion
- IHS Telehealth Program operating in all 12 areas but with inconsistent capacity

## Recent Developments (2024-2025)

### Advance Appropriations

- **Landmark achievement**: IHS received advance appropriations authority beginning FY 2024, providing two-year budget certainty
- This was a top tribal priority for decades, protecting IHS from government shutdowns
- Does not address overall funding adequacy but significantly improves planning and stability

### COVID-19 Impact and Recovery

- AI/AN communities experienced 1.8-3.5x higher COVID-19 infection and mortality rates than the general population (CDC, 2023)
- American Rescue Plan provided $6.1 billion in supplemental tribal health funding (one-time, FY 2021)
- COVID funds enabled temporary capacity expansion, but gains are not sustained by regular appropriations
- Some tribal health programs used COVID funding to develop innovative care models now at risk of defunding

### Bipartisan Infrastructure Law

- $3.5 billion for tribal water and sanitation infrastructure
- Improvements to water and wastewater systems directly affect health outcomes (waterborne illness, hygiene)

### Behavioral Health Initiatives

- 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline expanded to serve tribal communities (FY 2023)
- IHS Behavioral Health programs expanded methamphetamine and opioid treatment
- Tribal Behavioral Health Grant programs (SAMHSA) continue but funding remains below need
- Community Health Aide Program expanded to additional IHS areas beyond Alaska

### Alaska Native Health System

The Alaska Tribal Health System is often cited as a model:

- Alaska Native Tribal Health Consortium (ANTHC) operates most health services under self-governance compacts
- Community Health Aide Program (CHAP) trains local community members to provide primary care in remote villages
- Tribal health system serves 178,000 Alaska Natives across 229 tribes
- Significant improvements in health outcomes attributed to tribal management and community-based care

## The Funding Obligation

The federal obligation to provide health care to AI/AN people arises from multiple sources:

| Source | Nature | Key Provisions |
|--------|--------|---------------|
| Treaties | Contractual obligation | Specific treaties (e.g., Fort Laramie, 1868) include health care provisions in exchange for land cessions |
| Snyder Act (1921) | Statutory authorization | 25 U.S.C. 13: authorizes BIA to "direct, supervise, and expend" funds for "relief of distress and conservation of health" |
| Indian Health Care Improvement Act (1976, permanent reauthorization 2010) | Statutory framework | Comprehensive framework for IHS operations; declared it "the policy of this Nation... to provide the highest possible health status to Indians" |
| Trust responsibility | Legal doctrine | Federal fiduciary duty to tribal nations arising from the unique government-to-government relationship |
| Indian Self-Determination Act (1975) | Statutory authority | Authorizes tribal management of health programs under contracts and compacts |

Despite these obligations, Congress has never funded IHS at levels commensurate with need.
