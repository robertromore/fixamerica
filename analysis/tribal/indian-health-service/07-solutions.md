# Indian Health Service: Solutions

## Overview

Addressing the IHS crisis requires simultaneous action on funding, workforce, facilities, service delivery models, and the social determinants of health. These solutions are designed to work together, with mandatory funding enabling workforce recruitment, which enables facility utilization, which improves outcomes. No single reform is sufficient; the package must be comprehensive.

## Solution 1: Convert IHS to Mandatory Funding

### Description

Reclassify IHS appropriations from discretionary to mandatory, ensuring adequate, predictable, inflation-adjusted funding that fulfills treaty obligations.

### Evidence Base

- IHS advance appropriations (FY 2024) demonstrated feasibility and improved planning
- U.S. Commission on Civil Rights documented 60% underfunding (*Broken Promises*, 2018)
- Medicare, Medicaid, VA health care operate on mandatory funding with superior outcomes
- Government shutdowns disrupted IHS services at least 5 times in the past decade
- Per-capita spending gap: $4,078 (IHS) vs. $13,671 (Medicare) per user (FY 2023)

### Implementation

1. **Legislation**: Amend the Indian Health Care Improvement Act to reclassify IHS as mandatory spending
2. **Baseline**: Set initial mandatory level at the higher of FY 2024 enacted level (adjusted for inflation) or a needs-based formula
3. **Inflation adjustment**: Index to the Medical Care component of CPI (CPI-M), which better reflects health care cost growth
4. **Formula development**: Tribal consultation on allocation formula considering population, geographic cost differential, health status indicators, and historical underfunding
5. **Phase-in**: 5-year phase-in to full mandatory levels, with interim annual increases of 15-20%

### Cost Estimate

- Full mandatory funding at adequate levels: $15-18 billion annually (compared to current $7.1 billion)
- Additional cost above current spending: $8-11 billion annually
- Cost offsets: Reduced emergency care spending, reduced Medicaid costs, improved productivity
- Budget scoring: Requires reconciliation process or waiver from standard CBO scoring

### Timeline

- Legislation: 2-4 years
- Phase-in: 5 years after enactment
- Full impact: 7-10 years

### Challenges

- CBO scoring shows significant mandatory spending increase
- Appropriations committees resist losing control
- May require budget reconciliation to overcome Senate filibuster
- Formula design is politically complex

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline | Target (10-year) |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| Per-user IHS spending | $4,078 (FY 2023) | $10,000+ |
| Funding as % of estimated need | ~40% | 90%+ |
| Government shutdown impact | Service disruption | Zero |
| PRC fund exhaustion | Mid-fiscal year | Never |

## Solution 2: Comprehensive Workforce Transformation

### Description

Implement a multi-pronged strategy to recruit, train, and retain health care providers in Indian Country, including competitive compensation, housing, loan repayment, and pipeline programs.

### Evidence Base

- 25% average vacancy rate across IHS (IHS HR, 2023)
- IHS salaries lag private sector by 20-40%
- IHS Loan Repayment Program is oversubscribed -- more qualified applicants than available slots
- Community Health Aide Program in Alaska demonstrated effectiveness of community-based providers
- Tribal health programs that offer competitive compensation achieve lower vacancy rates

### Implementation

1. **Competitive pay**: Raise IHS salary scales to Medicare-equivalent rates (approximately 40% increase for most positions)
2. **Housing**: Federal investment in provider housing near IHS and tribal facilities ($1 billion over 5 years)
3. **Expanded loan repayment**: Triple the IHS Loan Repayment Program capacity; increase maximum award to $100,000 for 4-year commitment in high-need areas
4. **Pipeline programs**: Expand scholarships for AI/AN students entering health professions; partnerships with tribal colleges
5. **Community Health Aide Program expansion**: Extend Alaska's successful CHAP model to all IHS areas (authorized by IHCIA 2010 reauthorization)
6. **Telehealth integration**: Deploy broadband and telehealth infrastructure to supplement in-person care
7. **Retention incentives**: Retention bonuses for providers who serve beyond initial commitments; sabbatical programs

### Cost Estimate

- Salary increases: $1.5-2 billion annually
- Housing investment: $1 billion over 5 years
- Loan repayment expansion: $300 million annually
- Pipeline programs: $200 million annually
- CHAP expansion: $150 million annually
- Telehealth infrastructure: $500 million over 5 years

### Timeline

- Compensation increases: 1-2 years
- Housing construction: 3-5 years
- Pipeline programs: 5-10 years (educational cycle)
- CHAP expansion: 3-5 years
- Full workforce stabilization: 7-10 years

### Challenges

- Remote locations remain inherently difficult for recruitment regardless of compensation
- Housing construction in remote areas is expensive and slow
- Pipeline programs require a generation to produce AI/AN physicians in significant numbers
- CHAP expansion requires regulatory and scope-of-practice negotiation in each state

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline | Target (5-year) |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| Overall vacancy rate | 25% | 10% |
| Mental health professional vacancies | 35% | 15% |
| Average physician retention | 3-5 years | 7-10 years |
| Community Health Aides (outside Alaska) | ~200 | 2,000+ |
| AI/AN medical school graduates annually | ~200 | 400+ |

## Solution 3: Facility Modernization and Construction

### Description

Invest in modernizing, replacing, and expanding IHS and tribal health facilities to meet current medical standards, with a focus on replacing the oldest and most deficient facilities.

### Evidence Base

- Average IHS hospital age: 40+ years (IHS Facilities Engineering, 2023)
- Deferred maintenance backlog: $14+ billion
- 12 hospitals need replacement; dozens more need major renovation
- IHS facilities construction priority list wait time: 20-30 years (GAO, 2019)
- CMS accreditation threats at multiple facilities due to infrastructure deficiencies

### Implementation

1. **Accelerated construction**: $5 billion in initial capital investment to replace the 12 most critical facilities
2. **Deferred maintenance**: $3 billion to address the deferred maintenance backlog at existing facilities
3. **Equipment modernization**: $1 billion for medical equipment upgrades (imaging, lab, surgical)
4. **Outpatient expansion**: Fund community-based clinics to reduce reliance on hospital-centered care
5. **Green and resilient design**: New facilities built to withstand climate impacts and incorporate energy efficiency
6. **Tribal facility support**: Matching funds for tribal health facility construction and renovation

### Cost Estimate

- Total capital investment: $14-18 billion over 10 years
- Annual maintenance funding increase: $500 million
- Equipment modernization: $1 billion over 5 years

### Timeline

- Emergency replacements (3-5 most critical): 3-5 years
- Full priority list construction: 10-15 years (reduced from 20-30 year current timeline)
- Deferred maintenance: 5-7 years

### Challenges

- Construction in remote locations is expensive (up to 2-3x urban costs)
- Environmental review and permitting add time
- Workforce to staff new facilities must be recruited simultaneously
- Design must accommodate cultural and community needs

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline | Target (10-year) |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| Facilities meeting current building codes | <50% | 90%+ |
| Average facility age | 40+ years | 20 years |
| CMS accreditation compliance | Multiple deficiencies | Full compliance |
| Deferred maintenance backlog | $14+ billion | $2 billion |

## Solution 4: Expand Self-Governance Health Compacting

### Description

Accelerate the transition from IHS direct service to tribally managed health programs through expanded self-governance compacting, with additional flexibility and support for smaller tribes.

### Evidence Base

- Tribally operated programs currently manage ~58% of IHS funding
- Alaska Native Tribal Health Consortium demonstrates that comprehensive tribal health management improves outcomes
- Self-governance compact tribes report 15-20% greater administrative efficiency (SGCETC, 2022)
- Harvard Project research consistently links self-determination to improved outcomes
- Cherokee Nation Health Services operates comprehensive system serving 380,000 citizens

### Implementation

1. **Streamlined compacting process**: Reduce time from application to compact execution to 12 months
2. **Flexible funding**: Allow tribes to reallocate compact funds among health, behavioral health, environmental health, and traditional healing programs
3. **Consortium models**: Enable small tribes to form multi-tribal health consortia for compacting (modeled on Alaska regional corporations)
4. **Technical assistance**: Fund intertribal organizations to provide administrative, clinical, and financial management support
5. **Data sovereignty**: Ensure tribal ownership and control over health data generated by tribal programs
6. **Quality assurance**: Tribal-designed quality metrics that include cultural competency and community-defined outcomes

### Cost Estimate

- Compacting transition support: $300 million over 5 years
- Technical assistance: $100 million annually
- Net savings: 10-15% administrative efficiency improvement

### Timeline

- Streamlined process: 1-2 years
- Expanded participation: 3-5 years
- Full system transformation: 10-15 years

### Challenges

- Not all tribes have or want compacting capacity
- Direct-service IHS facilities must be maintained for tribes that prefer federal operation
- Data sovereignty raises complex privacy and interoperability questions
- Quality measurement must balance standardized metrics with tribal-defined outcomes

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline | Target (5-year) |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| Share of IHS funding managed by tribes | 58% | 70% |
| Tribes in self-governance health compacts | ~130 | 200+ |
| Multi-tribal health consortia | ~15 | 40+ |
| Patient satisfaction (tribal programs vs. direct service) | Already higher in tribal programs | Sustained improvement |

## Solution 5: Behavioral Health Transformation

### Description

Dramatically expand behavioral health services in Indian Country, integrating culturally grounded approaches with evidence-based treatment for trauma, substance use, and suicide prevention.

### Evidence Base

- Mental health professional vacancies: 35% (IHS HR, 2023)
- AI/AN youth suicide rate: 2.7x national average (CDC WISQARS, 2022)
- Substance use disorder prevalence: 1.7x national average (SAMHSA NSDUH, 2022)
- IHS behavioral health funding: $370 million (FY 2024); estimated need: $1.1 billion
- Culturally grounded interventions (e.g., land-based healing, cultural immersion, traditional ceremony) show promise in reducing suicidality and substance use

### Implementation

1. **Funding**: Increase behavioral health funding to $1.1 billion annually (3x current levels)
2. **Cultural healing programs**: Dedicate funding for tribally designed programs incorporating traditional healing practices
3. **Trauma-informed care**: Train all IHS and tribal health providers in historical trauma and trauma-informed approaches
4. **988 Tribal Lifeline**: Establish tribally operated crisis lines with culturally competent responders
5. **Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT)**: Expand MAT access for opioid and alcohol use disorders in all IHS service areas
6. **Youth programs**: Fund culture-based youth resilience programs, mentorship, and peer support
7. **Workforce**: Develop tribal behavioral health professional pipeline, including traditional healers in care teams

### Cost Estimate

- Behavioral health funding increase: $730 million annually (to reach $1.1 billion)
- Cultural healing program grants: $200 million annually
- Workforce development: $100 million annually
- Infrastructure: $300 million over 5 years

### Timeline

- Emergency crisis intervention expansion: Year 1
- Cultural healing program grants: Year 1-2
- MAT expansion: Year 1-3
- Full behavioral health system: 5-7 years

### Challenges

- Mental health professional recruitment in remote areas is extremely difficult
- Cultural healing practices resist standardization and evaluation
- Stigma around behavioral health treatment remains in some communities
- Scope-of-practice laws may restrict traditional healers and community health workers

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline | Target (5-year) |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| AI/AN suicide rate vs. national | 1.6x | 1.2x |
| Youth suicide rate vs. national | 2.7x | 1.8x |
| Behavioral health professional vacancies | 35% | 15% |
| AI/AN substance use disorder treatment rate | 13% receiving treatment | 30%+ |
| Culturally grounded program availability | Limited | Available in all 12 IHS areas |

## Solution 6: Urban Indian Health Expansion

### Description

Dramatically expand health care access for the 78% of AI/AN people living in urban areas through increased Urban Indian Organization (UIO) funding, new program establishment, and portable benefits.

### Evidence Base

- 78% of AI/AN population lives off-reservation (Census, 2020)
- 41 UIOs serve only 71,000 of an estimated 2.6 million eligible urban AI/AN people (IHS, 2023)
- Urban Indian health funding: $82 million (FY 2024) -- approximately 1.2% of IHS budget
- Urban AI/AN uninsured rate: 17.8% vs. 8.3% general population (Census ACS, 2022)
- UIOs are among the most cost-effective programs in the IHS system

### Implementation

1. **Funding**: Increase UIO funding to $500 million annually (6x current levels)
2. **New programs**: Establish 40+ new UIOs in cities with significant AI/AN populations
3. **Portable benefits**: Ensure tribal members can access IHS-funded services regardless of residence
4. **Integration**: Coordinate UIOs with community health centers, Medicaid, and private insurance
5. **Cultural programs**: Fund cultural connection programs linking urban AI/AN people with tribal communities
6. **Data collection**: Improve tracking of urban AI/AN health status and needs

### Cost Estimate

- UIO funding increase: $418 million annually (to reach $500 million)
- New program establishment: $200 million over 5 years
- Administration and coordination: $50 million annually

### Timeline

- Funding increase: 1-2 years
- New program establishment: 3-5 years
- Full urban Indian health system: 5-7 years

### Challenges

- Urban AI/AN populations are dispersed and mobile
- Jurisdictional questions about which tribe's compact covers which urban services
- Competition with community health centers for resources and patients
- Urban AI/AN identity is complex; eligibility verification can be barriers

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline | Target (5-year) |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| UIOs nationally | 41 | 80+ |
| Urban AI/AN people served | 71,000 | 500,000+ |
| Urban AI/AN uninsured rate | 17.8% | 10% |
| Urban Indian health funding as % of IHS | 1.2% | 5%+ |

## International Models

### New Zealand: Maori Health

- **Whanau Ora**: Holistic, family-centered approach to Indigenous health and social services
- **Maori Health Providers**: Tribally operated health services integrated into the national health system
- **He Korowai Oranga**: National Maori health strategy with measurable targets
- **Applicability**: Cultural integration model and community-centered care relevant to IHS reform

### Canada: First Nations and Inuit Health

- **First Nations and Inuit Health Branch (FNIHB)**: Federal agency providing health services (similar to IHS)
- **Health transfer agreements**: Tribes (First Nations) manage health programs under transfer agreements (similar to 638 compacting)
- **Jordan's Principle**: Federal obligation to fund services for First Nations children without jurisdictional disputes
- **Applicability**: Jordan's Principle model of no-denial-of-service relevant to PRC reform; health transfer models parallel compacting

### Australia: Aboriginal Health

- **Closing the Gap**: National framework with measurable targets for reducing Indigenous health disparities
- **Aboriginal Community Controlled Health Services**: Community-governed health organizations (similar to UIOs and tribal programs)
- **Practice Incentives Program -- Indigenous Health**: Financial incentives for providers serving Indigenous communities
- **Applicability**: Measurable targets and community control models relevant to IHS reform

## Sequencing Strategy

### Phase 1: Immediate (Years 1-2)

- Behavioral health crisis response (988 Tribal Lifeline, MAT expansion)
- Workforce compensation increases
- Begin mandatory funding legislative process
- Expand Community Health Aide Program

### Phase 2: Near-Term (Years 2-5)

- Mandatory funding enacted and phased in
- Facility construction begins for most critical replacements
- Self-governance compacting expanded
- Urban Indian health programs doubled

### Phase 3: Full Implementation (Years 5-15)

- Full mandatory funding at adequate levels
- Facility modernization complete for priority list
- Workforce stabilized with sustainable pipeline
- Health disparities measurably reduced
