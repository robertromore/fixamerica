# Indian Health Service: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to IHS reform takes several forms: fiscal arguments against increased funding, ideological opposition to "government-run health care," skepticism about IHS competence, and the quieter but powerful resistance of institutional actors who benefit from the status quo. Understanding these opposition forces is essential for developing effective advocacy strategies.

## Opposition Landscape

| Opposition Source | Primary Motivation | Mechanism | Threat Level |
|-------------------|-------------------|-----------|--------------|
| Congressional budget hawks | Reduce federal spending | Appropriations votes, budget resolutions | High |
| Appropriations committee members | Retain discretionary control | Resist mandatory conversion | High |
| Anti-government-health-care ideologues | Oppose federal health programs generally | Legislative votes, public framing | Medium |
| State governments (some) | Avoid financial responsibility; control health dollars | Lobbying against Medicaid expansion for tribes | Medium |
| IHS institutional resistance (some) | Preserve direct-service model and institutional role | Slow-rolling self-governance expansion | Medium |
| Private providers dependent on PRC | Maintain PRC referral revenue stream | Lobbying against system reforms that redirect referrals | Low-Medium |

## Ideological Opposition

### Government Health Care Opposition

Some opponents view IHS reform as expanding "government-run health care":

- Frame IHS as evidence that government cannot deliver health care effectively
- Use IHS quality failures to argue against single-payer or public option proposals
- Oppose any increase in mandatory health spending
- View tribal health as a state or private sector responsibility

**Reality**: IHS is not a general-purpose government health program. It is the fulfillment of a specific contractual obligation arising from treaties in which tribes ceded land in exchange for services. Comparing IHS to proposed public health insurance programs conflates fundamentally different legal obligations.

### Fiscal Opposition

Budget hawks argue that the federal government cannot afford significant IHS funding increases:

- Frame treaty obligations as historical relics rather than binding commitments
- Emphasize total federal spending numbers without per-capita context
- Argue for "efficiency" reforms rather than funding increases
- Point to waste and mismanagement as reasons to limit rather than increase funding

**Reality**: Total IHS spending ($7.1 billion, FY 2024) is less than 0.12% of the federal budget. Per-capita spending is less than one-third of Medicare. The "efficiency" argument ignores that IHS has been asked to do far more with far less for decades.

## Detailed Opposition Arguments

### Argument 1: "IHS Proves Government Can't Deliver Health Care"

**Claim**: IHS quality failures (hospital closures, accreditation losses, patient deaths) demonstrate that government-run health care is inherently incompetent.

**Evidence cited**: Rosebud Hospital ER closure (2015), Pine Ridge accreditation threats, Great Plains quality failures, inspector general reports on waste and mismanagement.

**Reality**: IHS quality problems are the direct result of chronic underfunding, not inherent government incompetence. Tribally operated programs using the same funding streams consistently perform better than IHS direct-service facilities. The Alaska Native health system, operated almost entirely by tribes, achieves health outcomes approaching national averages in some categories. VA and Medicare -- also government-run -- serve tens of millions with generally high patient satisfaction.

**Rebuttal**: Using IHS quality failures as an argument against government health care is like blaming a firefighter for not extinguishing a fire when given a garden hose instead of a fire truck. The problem is resources, not the model. Where resources are adequate and tribal management is empowered, outcomes improve dramatically.

### Argument 2: "We Can't Afford Mandatory IHS Funding"

**Claim**: Converting IHS to mandatory funding would cost billions and increase the federal deficit at a time of fiscal constraint.

**Evidence cited**: CBO scoring of mandatory conversion; total mandatory spending growth; national debt levels.

**Reality**: The estimated cost of bringing IHS to adequate funding ($8-12 billion additional annually) is approximately 0.15-0.2% of the federal budget. The United States spends $886 billion on defense (FY 2024). IHS funding is a legal obligation arising from treaties that transferred hundreds of millions of acres to the United States -- land worth trillions of dollars. Moreover, inadequate health care generates higher costs through emergency care, disability, lost productivity, and premature mortality.

**Rebuttal**: The "affordability" argument treats treaty obligations as optional expenditures rather than binding commitments. The United States does not ask whether it can "afford" to pay the interest on Treasury bonds. Treaty obligations are equally binding. The cost of fulfilling these obligations is modest in the context of the federal budget.

### Argument 3: "Tribes Should Pay for Their Own Health Care"

**Claim**: Many tribes have gaming revenue and should use it to fund health care rather than relying on federal programs.

**Evidence cited**: Total tribal gaming revenue of $41.4 billion (FY 2023, NIGC); some tribes make per-capita payments to members; tribal enterprises generate significant revenue.

**Reality**: Tribal gaming revenue is governmental revenue used to fund government services. Most tribes have modest or no gaming income -- the top 20 operations generate the majority of total revenue. Regardless of tribal revenue, the federal obligation to provide health care arises from treaties and the trust responsibility, not from tribal ability to pay. No one suggests that wealthy states should forfeit their Medicare entitlement because they can "afford" to self-fund.

**Rebuttal**: This argument applies a standard to tribes that is applied to no other government. States are not required to fund Medicare for their residents regardless of state revenue. Federal obligations exist independently of tribal wealth. Moreover, tribes that have gaming revenue already invest heavily in health care -- supplementing, not replacing, the federal obligation.

### Argument 4: "IHS Should Be Privatized"

**Claim**: AI/AN people would be better served by private health insurance and access to the private health care market rather than a government-run system.

**Evidence cited**: Private sector innovation; consumer choice; IHS quality problems; general anti-government-health-care arguments.

**Reality**: Privatization would require replacing a treaty-based federal obligation with an insurance subsidy model. Many AI/AN communities are in locations where no private health care market exists. The Indian Health Care Improvement Act already allows IHS to bill Medicare, Medicaid, and private insurance -- IHS collects approximately $1.3 billion annually in third-party collections (FY 2023). Full privatization would abandon the federal trust responsibility without evidence that private markets can serve remote, impoverished populations.

**Rebuttal**: Private health care markets have not materialized in most of Indian Country because the population is small, dispersed, and low-income. Privatization would simply end the federal obligation without replacing it with a viable alternative. The self-governance compacting model -- tribes managing their own programs with federal funding -- is a proven alternative that combines tribal autonomy with federal resource commitment.

### Argument 5: "IHS Waste Means More Money Won't Help"

**Claim**: Inspector General and GAO reports show waste, mismanagement, and fraud within IHS, so additional funding would be squandered.

**Evidence cited**: OIG reports on IHS financial management; GAO reports on facility management; media accounts of waste.

**Reality**: IHS administrative challenges are real but are themselves products of underfunding. Under-resourced agencies cannot invest in financial management systems, oversight capacity, or administrative training. Tribal self-governance compacts consistently demonstrate improved administrative performance because they have management flexibility. The proportion of IHS funding consumed by administration is comparable to or lower than other federal health agencies.

**Rebuttal**: Underfunding creates the conditions for mismanagement (staff shortages, outdated systems, deferred maintenance), and then the resulting mismanagement is used to justify continued underfunding. This circular argument can only be broken by adequate investment combined with accountability reforms -- precisely the approach used when the VA faced similar quality crises.

### Argument 6: "Urban Indians Should Use Regular Health Programs"

**Claim**: The 78% of AI/AN people living in urban areas should access health care through Medicaid, marketplace plans, community health centers, and other general programs rather than specialized urban Indian health programs.

**Evidence cited**: Availability of other safety net programs; cost of expanding urban Indian programs; small number of UIOs (41).

**Reality**: Urban AI/AN people face unique health disparities and cultural barriers that general health programs do not address. Many urban AI/AN people are uninsured or underinsured (17.8% uninsured vs. 8.3% general population, Census ACS 2022). Cultural competency, traditional healing integration, and community connection are essential elements of effective care that general programs do not provide. Urban Indian organizations provide these services but are funded at only $82 million (FY 2024) for the entire nation.

**Rebuttal**: The argument that urban AI/AN people should simply use general health programs ignores the trust responsibility, which extends to all eligible AI/AN people regardless of residence. It also ignores the documented health disparities and cultural needs that require specialized programs. Urban Indian health programs are among the most cost-effective in the IHS system.

## Counter-Strategies

### Reframing Strategies

- **From "cost" to "obligation"**: Frame IHS spending as fulfilling contractual and legal obligations, not as discretionary social spending
- **From "government health care" to "treaty health care"**: Distinguish IHS from general health policy debates; IHS is not a welfare program
- **From "failure" to "underfunding"**: Ensure that every mention of IHS quality problems is contextualized with funding data
- **From "special treatment" to "contract performance"**: Tribes exchanged land for services; the U.S. is not performing its end of the contract

### Political Strategies

- **Bipartisan framing**: Emphasize self-governance and efficiency to attract conservative support; emphasize equity and obligation for liberal support
- **Veterans coalition**: Partner with veterans organizations on VA-IHS coordination; veterans are a politically powerful constituency
- **Rural health coalition**: Align with rural health advocates who share challenges of provider shortages and geographic isolation
- **State Medicaid partnership**: Demonstrate financial benefit to states of IHS-Medicaid coordination (100% FMAP for IHS/tribal facility services)

### Evidence Strategies

- **Tribal health success stories**: Document and publicize tribal health programs that achieve excellent outcomes (Alaska Native system, Cherokee Nation health, Southern Plains models)
- **Cost-effectiveness data**: Demonstrate that adequate IHS funding saves money in emergency care, disability, and lost productivity
- **International comparisons**: New Zealand, Canada, and Australia provide models of Indigenous health investment that demonstrate return on investment
- **Provider testimony**: Health care workers who have served in IHS and tribal facilities are powerful witnesses
