# Indian Health Service: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Reforming the Indian Health Service requires legislative action across multiple fronts: converting funding from discretionary to mandatory, transforming the health care workforce, modernizing facilities, expanding self-governance over health delivery, strengthening behavioral health programs, and extending services to urban Indian populations. This document presents draft federal legislation addressing each of these needs, along with regulatory frameworks, legal considerations, and analysis of potential loopholes and shortcomings.

The legal foundation for all proposed legislation rests on the federal trust responsibility, treaty obligations that exchanged health care commitments for land cessions, and the existing statutory framework of the Indian Health Care Improvement Act (25 U.S.C. 1601 et seq.) and Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act (25 U.S.C. 5301 et seq.). These proposals aim to bring federal health care delivery into alignment with existing legal obligations rather than creating new ones.

## Constitutional Amendments

### Treaty Health Care Guarantee Amendment

**Purpose**: Elevate the federal obligation to provide health care to American Indians and Alaska Natives from a statutory and treaty-based duty to a constitutional guarantee, ensuring that treaty-promised health care cannot be eroded through legislative neglect, appropriations failures, or judicial reinterpretation.

**Draft Text**:

```text
Section 1. The United States shall fulfill its treaty obligations
and trust responsibility to provide health care to members of
federally recognized Indian tribal nations. Congress shall
appropriate funds sufficient to provide health care services at
levels no less than the per-capita spending of the Medicare
program, adjusted for geographic cost differentials.

Section 2. No Indian tribal nation or its members shall be denied
health care services on account of residence, whether on or off
reservation or tribal land.

Section 3. Indian tribal nations shall have the right to manage
and administer health care programs serving their members, with
federal funding no less than the amount that would otherwise be
provided through federal direct service.

Section 4. Congress shall have the power to enforce this article
by appropriate legislation.

Section 5. This article shall take effect three years after the
date of ratification.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 1 establishes a constitutional funding floor tied to Medicare per-capita spending, closing the discretionary funding gap
- Section 2 ensures that urban AI/AN populations -- 78% of the total -- cannot be excluded from health services based on residence
- Section 3 constitutionally protects the right to tribal self-governance in health care delivery, building on the Indian Self-Determination Act framework
- Section 4 provides standard enforcement authority
- Section 5 allows a three-year transition for Congress to enact implementing legislation and adjust appropriations

**Potential Challenges**:

- Constitutional amendments require two-thirds vote of both chambers and ratification by 38 states
- Tying funding to Medicare per-capita rates is unprecedented in constitutional text and may generate litigation over calculation methodology
- States with significant AI/AN populations may resist provisions perceived as expanding federal obligations
- "Geographic cost differentials" require ongoing administrative calculation

**Refinements**:

- Consider a narrower amendment addressing only the mandatory funding obligation without specifying Medicare parity, leaving the funding level to statute
- Develop model state ratification resolutions emphasizing that the amendment fulfills existing obligations rather than creating new ones
- Build coalition with veterans organizations by paralleling the VA mandatory funding precedent

## Federal Legislation

### Bill 1: Indian Health Service Mandatory Funding Act

**Purpose**: Reclassify IHS appropriations from discretionary to mandatory spending, establish a needs-based funding formula developed through tribal consultation, and index funding to medical inflation to ensure adequacy over time.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Indian Health Service Mandatory
Funding Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) the United States entered into treaties with Indian tribal
    nations that included express and implied commitments to
    provide health care services in exchange for the cession
    of tribal lands;
(b) the Indian Health Care Improvement Act declares it "the
    policy of this Nation, in fulfillment of its special trust
    responsibilities and legal obligations to Indians, to
    ensure the highest possible health status for Indians and
    urban Indians and to provide all resources necessary to
    effect that policy" (25 U.S.C. 1602);
(c) the United States Commission on Civil Rights found in
    2018 that the Indian Health Service is funded at
    approximately 40 percent of estimated need;
(d) per-capita IHS spending of $4,078 (FY 2023) is less than
    one-third of Medicare per-capita spending of $13,671;
(e) American Indians and Alaska Natives have a life expectancy
    12.3 years shorter than the general population;
(f) discretionary funding subjects IHS to annual uncertainty,
    government shutdowns, and sequestration, undermining
    health care delivery, workforce recruitment, and facility
    planning; and
(g) advance appropriations for IHS, achieved in fiscal year
    2024, demonstrated the feasibility and benefits of
    multi-year funding certainty but do not address overall
    funding adequacy.

SEC. 3. MANDATORY APPROPRIATIONS FOR INDIAN HEALTH SERVICE.

(a) IN GENERAL.--Beginning with the first fiscal year that
    begins 2 years after the date of enactment of this Act,
    all programs, activities, and services of the Indian
    Health Service authorized under the Indian Health Care
    Improvement Act (25 U.S.C. 1601 et seq.) and the Snyder
    Act (25 U.S.C. 13) are hereby reclassified as mandatory
    spending.

(b) FUNDING LEVELS.--
    (1) INITIAL LEVEL.--For the first fiscal year of
        mandatory funding, the appropriation shall be the
        greater of--
        (A) the fiscal year 2026 enacted level, adjusted for
            inflation as described in paragraph (3); or
        (B) the amount determined by the funding formula
            established under subsection (c).
    (2) PHASE-IN.--If the amount determined under paragraph
        (1)(B) exceeds the amount determined under paragraph
        (1)(A), the difference shall be phased in over 5
        fiscal years in equal annual increments.
    (3) ANNUAL ADJUSTMENT.--Funding levels shall be adjusted
        annually based on--
        (A) the Medical Care component of the Consumer Price
            Index for All Urban Consumers (CPI-M);
        (B) changes in the eligible Indian population as
            determined by the Secretary; and
        (C) documented changes in service delivery costs,
            including geographic cost differentials.

(c) NEEDS-BASED FUNDING FORMULA.--
    (1) The Secretary of Health and Human Services, acting
        through the Director of the Indian Health Service and
        in consultation with Indian tribal nations, shall
        develop a transparent, needs-based formula for
        determining the total IHS funding level and
        allocating funds among IHS service areas and programs.
    (2) The formula shall consider--
        (A) user population and eligible population;
        (B) health status indicators, including mortality
            rates, disease prevalence, and behavioral health
            metrics;
        (C) geographic service area, including remoteness
            and transportation barriers;
        (D) facility condition and capacity;
        (E) workforce vacancy rates and recruitment costs;
        (F) purchased/referred care utilization and unmet
            need; and
        (G) comparison to per-capita spending in Medicare,
            Medicaid, and the Veterans Health Administration.
    (3) The formula shall be reviewed and updated every
        5 years through formal tribal consultation.
    (4) The Secretary shall publish the formula methodology
        and all input data in the Federal Register.

(d) PURCHASED/REFERRED CARE.--
    (1) Purchased/Referred Care funding shall be set at
        levels sufficient to ensure that no patient is denied
        medically necessary referred care due to fund
        depletion during any fiscal year.
    (2) An emergency supplemental mechanism shall be
        established to provide additional PRC funding when
        service area allocations are exhausted.

(e) PROTECTION FROM SEQUESTRATION.--Programs funded under
    this Act shall be exempt from sequestration under the
    Balanced Budget and Emergency Deficit Control Act of
    1985 (2 U.S.C. 900 et seq.).

(f) TRIBAL SHARES.--Nothing in this section shall diminish
    the right of Indian tribal nations to receive their
    share of funding through self-determination contracts
    or self-governance compacts under the Indian
    Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act (25
    U.S.C. 5301 et seq.).

SEC. 4. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated such sums as are
necessary to carry out this Act.

SEC. 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment, with
mandatory funding beginning with the first fiscal year that
starts 2 years after such date.
```

**Explanation**: This bill mirrors the approach used for Medicare and VA health care, treating IHS as a mandatory obligation rather than a discretionary program. The needs-based formula ensures that funding is determined by health need rather than political negotiation. The five-year phase-in accommodates budget scoring while ensuring steady progress toward adequate funding. The PRC provision addresses the critical problem of mid-year fund exhaustion that forces patients to go without specialty care.

**Potential Challenges**:

- CBO scoring will show a significant mandatory spending increase ($8-11 billion annually at full implementation)
- Appropriations committees will resist losing control over IHS funding
- May require budget reconciliation to overcome Senate filibuster
- Formula design is complex given the diversity of 574 tribal nations
- Defining "medically necessary" PRC care may generate administrative disputes

**Refinements**:

- If full mandatory reclassification is not achievable, extend advance appropriations to all IHS program lines and establish a mandatory floor at current spending plus medical inflation
- Include a budget offset package to reduce CBO score impact
- Phase implementation by program area, starting with hospitals and clinics, then PRC, then behavioral health
- Include an independent tribal advisory commission for formula oversight

### Bill 2: Indian Health Workforce Transformation Act

**Purpose**: Address the 25% average provider vacancy rate across IHS through competitive compensation, expanded loan repayment, housing investment, pipeline programs, and Community Health Aide Program expansion.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Indian Health Workforce
Transformation Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) the Indian Health Service has an average provider vacancy
    rate of 25 percent, with mental health professional
    vacancies at 35 percent and dentist vacancies at 30
    percent;
(b) IHS salary ranges lag the private sector by 20 to 40
    percent for most health care positions;
(c) inadequate housing in remote service areas is a primary
    barrier to recruitment and retention;
(d) the IHS Loan Repayment Program is oversubscribed, with
    qualified applicants turned away each year;
(e) the Community Health Aide Program, proven effective in
    Alaska, was authorized for expansion to all IHS areas
    by the Indian Health Care Improvement Act reauthorization
    of 2010 but has not been fully implemented; and
(f) American Indian and Alaska Native representation in
    health professions is critically low, with AI/AN
    people comprising 2.9 percent of the population but
    less than 0.5 percent of physicians.

SEC. 3. COMPETITIVE COMPENSATION.

(a) PAY PARITY.--The Secretary of Health and Human Services
    shall establish IHS health care provider compensation at
    rates no less than the equivalent positions in the
    Veterans Health Administration or the 75th percentile
    of regional market rates, whichever is greater.

(b) HARDSHIP DIFFERENTIAL.--Providers serving in designated
    remote or underserved IHS service areas shall receive
    a hardship differential of not less than 15 percent
    above the rate established under subsection (a).

(c) RETENTION BONUSES.--
    (1) Providers who complete their initial service
        commitment and continue serving at an IHS or
        tribal facility shall be eligible for retention
        bonuses of--
        (A) $25,000 for years 3-5 of service;
        (B) $35,000 for years 5-8 of service; and
        (C) $50,000 for years exceeding 8 of service.
    (2) Retention bonus amounts shall be adjusted for
        inflation annually.

SEC. 4. LOAN REPAYMENT EXPANSION.

(a) INCREASED CAPACITY.--The IHS Loan Repayment Program
    under section 108 of the Indian Health Care Improvement
    Act (25 U.S.C. 1616a) shall be expanded to serve not
    fewer than 5,000 participants annually by fiscal year
    2031.

(b) INCREASED AWARD.--The maximum loan repayment award shall
    be increased to--
    (1) $100,000 for a 4-year commitment in a designated
        high-need service area; and
    (2) $60,000 for a 2-year commitment in other IHS or
        tribal service areas.

(c) BEHAVIORAL HEALTH PRIORITY.--Not less than 30 percent
    of loan repayment awards shall be reserved for
    behavioral health professionals, including
    psychiatrists, psychologists, clinical social workers,
    licensed professional counselors, and substance use
    disorder specialists.

(d) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.--There is authorized to
    be appropriated $500,000,000 for each of fiscal years
    2027 through 2036 to carry out the expanded Loan
    Repayment Program.

SEC. 5. PROVIDER HOUSING.

(a) CONSTRUCTION PROGRAM.--The Secretary shall establish a
    program to construct, acquire, or renovate housing for
    health care providers serving at IHS or tribal health
    facilities in areas where adequate housing is
    unavailable.

(b) STANDARDS.--Housing provided under this section shall
    meet quality standards comparable to military housing
    and shall include broadband internet access.

(c) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.--There is authorized
    to be appropriated $250,000,000 for each of fiscal years
    2027 through 2031 for provider housing construction.

SEC. 6. COMMUNITY HEALTH AIDE PROGRAM EXPANSION.

(a) NATIONWIDE IMPLEMENTATION.--The Secretary shall fully
    implement the Community Health Aide Program authorized
    under section 119 of the Indian Health Care Improvement
    Act (25 U.S.C. 1616l) in all 12 IHS service areas not
    later than 5 years after the date of enactment.

(b) TRAINING.--The Secretary, in coordination with tribal
    health programs and educational institutions, shall
    establish regional CHAP training centers in each IHS
    service area.

(c) SCOPE OF PRACTICE.--Community Health Aides and
    Community Health Practitioners trained and certified
    under this program shall be authorized to practice
    within the scope established by the CHAP certification
    board, notwithstanding any State scope-of-practice
    limitations, when providing services at IHS or
    tribally operated facilities.

(d) TARGET.--The Secretary shall work to ensure that not
    fewer than 5,000 Community Health Aides and Community
    Health Practitioners are trained and certified by
    fiscal year 2032.

(e) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.--There is authorized
    to be appropriated $150,000,000 for each of fiscal years
    2027 through 2036 for CHAP expansion.

SEC. 7. NATIVE HEALTH PROFESSIONAL PIPELINE.

(a) SCHOLARSHIP EXPANSION.--The IHS Scholarship Program
    under section 103 of the Indian Health Care Improvement
    Act (25 U.S.C. 1613a) shall be expanded to provide not
    fewer than 1,000 scholarships annually by fiscal year
    2031.

(b) PRE-PROFESSIONAL PROGRAMS.--The Secretary shall
    establish grants for tribal colleges and universities
    to develop pre-medical, pre-nursing, and pre-dental
    programs to prepare AI/AN students for health
    professional education.

(c) RESIDENCY SUPPORT.--The Secretary shall fund residency
    positions at IHS and tribal health facilities, with
    a target of 200 new residency positions by fiscal year
    2032.

(d) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.--There is authorized
    to be appropriated $300,000,000 for each of fiscal years
    2027 through 2036 for pipeline programs under this
    section.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 180 days after the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses workforce shortages through a comprehensive strategy: competitive pay to stop the exodus of providers to higher-paying settings, loan repayment to recruit new graduates, housing to remove a critical barrier in remote areas, CHAP expansion to build a community-based workforce modeled on Alaska's success, and pipeline programs to increase AI/AN representation in health professions. The federal preemption of state scope-of-practice laws for CHAP providers at IHS and tribal facilities is essential because inconsistent state regulations have been a primary barrier to CHAP expansion outside Alaska.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Total cost of $1.2-1.5 billion annually faces appropriations resistance
- State medical boards may challenge federal preemption of scope-of-practice laws
- Remote locations remain inherently difficult for recruitment regardless of compensation
- Pipeline programs require a generation to produce significant numbers of AI/AN physicians
- Provider housing construction in remote areas is expensive and slow

**Refinements**:

- Phase compensation increases over 3 years to reduce initial budget impact
- Include state compact provisions allowing interested states to adopt CHAP scope of practice voluntarily
- Partner with military housing programs for construction expertise in remote areas
- Establish telehealth fellowship programs to supplement in-person workforce

### Bill 3: Indian Health Facilities Modernization Act

**Purpose**: Fund the replacement, renovation, and modernization of IHS and tribal health facilities, eliminating the 20-30 year construction backlog and addressing $14+ billion in deferred maintenance.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Indian Health Facilities
Modernization Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) the average IHS hospital is over 40 years old;
(b) at least 12 IHS hospitals require full replacement;
(c) the deferred maintenance backlog at IHS facilities
    exceeds $14 billion;
(d) the average wait time from placement on the IHS
    health care facilities construction priority list to
    facility completion is 20 to 30 years;
(e) multiple IHS facilities have lost or been threatened
    with loss of CMS accreditation due to infrastructure
    deficiencies; and
(f) inadequate facilities are a primary barrier to
    workforce recruitment and quality care delivery.

SEC. 3. FACILITIES CONSTRUCTION FUND.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--There is established in the Treasury a
    fund to be known as the "Indian Health Facilities
    Modernization Fund".

(b) AUTHORIZATION.--There is authorized to be appropriated
    to the Fund $2,000,000,000 for each of fiscal years
    2027 through 2036, to remain available until expended.

(c) USE OF FUNDS.--Amounts in the Fund shall be available
    for--
    (1) replacement of hospitals and clinics on the IHS
        health care facilities construction priority list;
    (2) construction of new outpatient facilities and
        community health centers;
    (3) renovation and expansion of existing facilities;
    (4) medical equipment acquisition and modernization;
    (5) health information technology infrastructure; and
    (6) matching funds for tribal health facility
        construction.

(d) PRIORITY.--The Secretary shall prioritize projects
    based on--
    (1) facility condition, including accreditation
        deficiencies;
    (2) patient safety risks;
    (3) population served and projected growth;
    (4) geographic access considerations; and
    (5) whether the facility serves as the sole health
        care access point for the community.

SEC. 4. ACCELERATED CONSTRUCTION TIMELINE.

(a) The Secretary shall complete replacement of the 5
    facilities with the most critical deficiencies not
    later than 5 years after the date of enactment.

(b) All facilities currently on the IHS construction
    priority list shall have construction commenced not
    later than 10 years after the date of enactment.

(c) The Secretary shall utilize alternative project
    delivery methods, including design-build contracts,
    to accelerate construction timelines.

SEC. 5. DEFERRED MAINTENANCE.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.--There is authorized to be appropriated
    $1,000,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through
    2033 for deferred maintenance at existing IHS and
    tribal health facilities.

(b) The Secretary shall prioritize deferred maintenance
    projects that address patient safety risks, CMS
    accreditation deficiencies, and infection control
    needs.

SEC. 6. TRIBAL FACILITY SUPPORT.

(a) MATCHING GRANTS.--The Secretary shall establish a
    matching grant program for tribal health facility
    construction, renovation, and equipment acquisition.

(b) FEDERAL SHARE.--The federal share of any project
    under this section shall be not less than 80 percent
    and not more than 100 percent, based on tribal need
    and capacity.

(c) AUTHORIZATION.--There is authorized to be appropriated
    $500,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through
    2036 for tribal facility matching grants.

SEC. 7. DESIGN STANDARDS.

(a) All facilities constructed under this Act shall be
    designed to--
    (1) meet current CMS conditions of participation;
    (2) incorporate culturally appropriate design elements
        developed in consultation with the tribal
        communities served;
    (3) include space for traditional healing practices;
    (4) meet energy efficiency and climate resilience
        standards; and
    (5) support telehealth and health information
        technology integration.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill creates a dedicated, multi-year construction fund modeled on the approach used for military base construction and VA facility modernization. The $20 billion total authorization over 10 years addresses the scale of the facilities crisis. Accelerated timelines with design-build authority reduce the decades-long construction queue. Tribal matching grants enable tribes with capacity to build their own facilities. The design standards ensure new facilities integrate cultural needs and modern technology.

**Potential Challenges**:

- $20 billion total authorization faces significant appropriations resistance
- Construction in remote locations costs 2-3x urban rates
- Environmental review and permitting can add years to timelines
- Workforce to staff new facilities must be recruited concurrently
- Design-build authority may face procurement regulation challenges

**Refinements**:

- Phase authorization with initial focus on the 5 most critical facilities
- Streamline NEPA review for replacement facilities on existing sites
- Partner with Army Corps of Engineers and Naval Facilities Engineering Command for construction in remote areas
- Include workforce planning requirements as a condition of construction funding

### Bill 4: Urban Indian Health Equity Act

**Purpose**: Dramatically expand health care access for the 78% of AI/AN people living in urban areas by increasing Urban Indian Organization funding, establishing new programs, and ensuring portable health benefits regardless of residence.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Urban Indian Health Equity Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) approximately 78 percent of American Indians and Alaska
    Natives live in urban areas;
(b) only 41 Urban Indian Organizations currently operate,
    serving approximately 71,000 of an estimated 2.6
    million eligible urban AI/AN people;
(c) urban Indian health programs receive approximately
    1.2 percent of the total IHS budget ($82 million in
    FY 2024) despite serving the majority population;
(d) urban AI/AN people have an uninsured rate of 17.8
    percent, more than double the national average;
(e) the federal trust responsibility extends to all
    eligible AI/AN people regardless of residence; and
(f) Urban Indian Organizations are among the most
    cost-effective programs in the IHS system.

SEC. 3. FUNDING INCREASE.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.--There is authorized to be appropriated
    for Urban Indian Organizations under title V of the
    Indian Health Care Improvement Act (25 U.S.C. 1651 et
    seq.) not less than--
    (1) $250,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
    (2) $375,000,000 for fiscal year 2028; and
    (3) $500,000,000 for fiscal year 2029 and each fiscal
        year thereafter, adjusted for inflation.

SEC. 4. NEW PROGRAM ESTABLISHMENT.

(a) The Secretary shall issue a competitive grant program
    for the establishment of new Urban Indian Organizations
    in metropolitan areas with significant AI/AN
    populations that are not currently served by a UIO.

(b) The Secretary shall prioritize grants for--
    (1) metropolitan areas with AI/AN populations exceeding
        10,000 that lack an existing UIO;
    (2) areas with documented health disparities among
        urban AI/AN populations; and
    (3) applicants with demonstrated capacity for
        culturally competent health care delivery.

(c) TARGET.--Not fewer than 40 new Urban Indian
    Organizations shall be established by fiscal year 2032.

SEC. 5. PORTABLE BENEFITS.

(a) No eligible AI/AN person shall be denied IHS-funded
    health care services on the basis of residence or
    proximity to a reservation or tribal service area.

(b) The Secretary shall establish a system for IHS-funded
    services to be delivered through Urban Indian
    Organizations, community health centers, or other
    qualified providers when an eligible AI/AN person
    resides outside an IHS or tribal service area.

(c) The Secretary shall develop a uniform eligibility
    verification process that minimizes administrative
    burden on patients while preventing ineligible use.

SEC. 6. SERVICES.

Urban Indian Organizations funded under this Act shall
provide, at minimum--
(a) primary care services;
(b) behavioral health services, including substance use
    disorder treatment;
(c) dental services;
(d) pharmacy services or pharmacy benefit management;
(e) referral coordination for specialty care;
(f) culturally grounded health education and prevention
    programs;
(g) enrollment assistance for Medicaid, Medicare, and
    marketplace health insurance; and
(h) traditional healing referral and integration where
    requested by the community served.

SEC. 7. THIRD-PARTY BILLING.

(a) Urban Indian Organizations shall be eligible for
    Medicaid reimbursement at the 100 percent Federal
    Medical Assistance Percentage rate on the same basis
    as IHS and tribally operated facilities.

(b) Urban Indian Organizations shall be eligible for
    Medicare reasonable cost reimbursement on the same
    basis as IHS and tribally operated facilities.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the most glaring gap in the IHS system: the near-total exclusion of urban AI/AN populations. With 78% of the eligible population living off-reservation but receiving only 1.2% of IHS funding, the current allocation is indefensible. The portable benefits provision ensures that the trust responsibility follows the person, not the geography. The 100% FMAP provision for UIO Medicaid billing eliminates a financial barrier that currently prevents many UIOs from maximizing third-party revenue.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Sixfold funding increase faces appropriations resistance
- Defining urban AI/AN eligibility across 574 tribes is complex
- New UIOs require startup time for facility acquisition, staffing, and accreditation
- State Medicaid programs may resist 100% FMAP extension to UIOs
- Competition with community health centers for patients, providers, and funding

**Refinements**:

- Phase the funding increase over 3 years as proposed in the bill text
- Include cooperative agreement provisions allowing UIOs to co-locate with community health centers
- Establish a UIO accreditation and technical assistance program
- Include data collection requirements to demonstrate cost-effectiveness

### Bill 5: Indian Country Behavioral Health Emergency Act

**Purpose**: Declare a behavioral health emergency in Indian Country and authorize comprehensive funding for tribal behavioral health services, including culturally grounded programs, crisis intervention, and workforce development.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Indian Country Behavioral Health
Emergency Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND DECLARATION.

(a) FINDINGS.--Congress finds that--
    (1) the suicide rate among American Indians and Alaska
        Natives is 1.6 times the national average;
    (2) the youth suicide rate among AI/AN people aged 15
        to 24 is 2.7 times the national average;
    (3) alcohol-related death rates among AI/AN people are
        3.8 times the national average;
    (4) substance use disorder prevalence among AI/AN people
        is 1.7 times the national average;
    (5) IHS behavioral health programs are funded at
        approximately 23 percent of estimated need;
    (6) mental health professional vacancy rates at IHS
        facilities average 35 percent; and
    (7) historical trauma from boarding schools, forced
        removal, and cultural suppression is a documented
        driver of behavioral health disparities.

(b) DECLARATION.--Congress hereby declares that a behavioral
    health emergency exists in Indian Country and among
    urban AI/AN populations requiring immediate and
    sustained federal action.

SEC. 3. BEHAVIORAL HEALTH FUNDING.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.--There is authorized to be appropriated
    for IHS behavioral health programs under the Indian
    Health Care Improvement Act--
    (1) $800,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
    (2) $950,000,000 for fiscal year 2028; and
    (3) $1,100,000,000 for fiscal year 2029 and each
        fiscal year thereafter, adjusted for inflation.

(b) ALLOCATION.--Funds appropriated under this section
    shall be allocated as follows:
    (1) not less than 40 percent for tribal behavioral
        health program grants;
    (2) not less than 20 percent for crisis intervention
        and suicide prevention;
    (3) not less than 15 percent for substance use
        disorder treatment, including medication-assisted
        treatment;
    (4) not less than 10 percent for culturally grounded
        healing programs; and
    (5) the remainder for workforce development and
        program administration.

SEC. 4. TRIBAL CRISIS RESPONSE.

(a) 988 TRIBAL LIFELINE.--The Secretary, in coordination
    with SAMHSA, shall establish tribally operated crisis
    response capacity within the 988 Suicide and Crisis
    Lifeline system, with culturally competent responders
    trained in historical trauma and Indigenous approaches
    to crisis intervention.

(b) MOBILE CRISIS TEAMS.--The Secretary shall fund mobile
    crisis response teams in each IHS service area,
    staffed by behavioral health professionals and
    community health workers with cultural competency
    training.

(c) AUTHORIZATION.--There is authorized to be appropriated
    $100,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through
    2036 for tribal crisis response under this section.

SEC. 5. CULTURALLY GROUNDED PROGRAMS.

(a) GRANT PROGRAM.--The Secretary shall establish a grant
    program for tribally designed behavioral health
    programs incorporating traditional healing practices,
    land-based healing, cultural immersion, ceremony, and
    intergenerational knowledge transfer.

(b) EVALUATION.--Program evaluation shall use
    tribal-defined outcome measures developed in
    consultation with participating tribes, which may
    include cultural connectedness, community belonging,
    and traditional knowledge acquisition alongside
    clinical measures.

(c) TRADITIONAL HEALER INCLUSION.--Traditional healers
    and cultural practitioners may be included as members
    of behavioral health care teams at IHS and tribal
    facilities, with compensation commensurate with their
    role and expertise.

SEC. 6. SUBSTANCE USE DISORDER TREATMENT.

(a) MEDICATION-ASSISTED TREATMENT.--The Secretary shall
    ensure that medication-assisted treatment for opioid
    and alcohol use disorders is available at every IHS
    and tribal hospital and at all IHS service unit
    headquarters not later than 2 years after the date
    of enactment.

(b) RESIDENTIAL TREATMENT.--The Secretary shall fund the
    establishment or expansion of residential substance
    use disorder treatment programs in each IHS service
    area, with priority for culturally grounded treatment
    models.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill treats the behavioral health crisis in Indian Country with the urgency it demands by declaring an emergency and tripling current funding. The culturally grounded programs provision recognizes that Western psychiatric models alone are insufficient for addressing intergenerational trauma. The traditional healer inclusion provision breaks down barriers between Western and Indigenous healing. The 988 Tribal Lifeline ensures that AI/AN people in crisis reach culturally competent responders.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Behavioral health professional recruitment in remote areas remains extremely difficult even with funding
- Traditional healing practices resist standardized evaluation and may face skepticism from clinical establishment
- Scope-of-practice restrictions in some states may limit traditional healer participation
- Residential treatment facility construction takes years
- Emergency declaration may face legal questions about duration and scope

**Refinements**:

- Include telehealth behavioral health provisions to supplement in-person services
- Develop a tribal behavioral health professional credential recognized across IHS
- Phase residential treatment expansion by partnering with existing tribal treatment centers
- Include research funding for evidence development on culturally grounded interventions

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Medicaid-Tribal Coordination Act

**Purpose**: Maximize Medicaid coverage for AI/AN populations and ensure states take advantage of the 100% Federal Medical Assistance Percentage for services at IHS and tribal facilities.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Medicaid-Tribal Health
Coordination Act".

SECTION 2. FINDINGS.

The [State] Legislature finds that--
(a) the State receives 100 percent federal reimbursement
    for Medicaid services provided to American Indians and
    Alaska Natives at IHS or tribally operated facilities;
(b) maximizing AI/AN enrollment in Medicaid and
    coordinating with IHS and tribal health programs saves
    the State money while improving health outcomes; and
(c) current enrollment and coordination barriers result in
    lost federal revenue and avoidable health disparities.

SECTION 3. AUTOMATIC MEDICAID ENROLLMENT.

(a) The State Medicaid agency shall establish procedures
    for automatic or facilitated enrollment of eligible
    AI/AN individuals identified through IHS or tribal
    health program intake.

(b) The State shall accept tribal enrollment documents as
    sufficient proof of identity and residency for
    Medicaid eligibility determination.

SECTION 4. TRIBAL HEALTH PROVIDER COORDINATION.

(a) The State Medicaid agency shall enter into coordination
    agreements with IHS and tribal health programs
    operating within the State.

(b) Such agreements shall include streamlined billing
    procedures, timely reimbursement guarantees, and
    recognition of tribal health program provider
    credentials.

SECTION 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on [date].
```

**Explanation**: This model legislation helps states capture the 100% FMAP benefit for AI/AN services at IHS and tribal facilities while streamlining enrollment for eligible AI/AN individuals. States have a direct financial incentive to adopt this legislation because every AI/AN Medicaid patient served at an IHS or tribal facility is fully federally funded.

**Adaptations**: States should customize eligibility procedures, enrollment timelines, and coordination agreement templates based on their existing Medicaid infrastructure and the number and type of tribal health programs operating within their borders.

## Regulatory Framework

### HHS: IHS Quality and Accreditation Standards

**Existing Authority**: Indian Health Care Improvement Act (25 U.S.C. 1601 et seq.); CMS Conditions of Participation (42 C.F.R. Part 482).

**Draft Regulation**:

The Secretary of Health and Human Services shall promulgate regulations requiring:

1. **Continuous quality monitoring**: IHS and tribal health facilities shall maintain continuous compliance with CMS Conditions of Participation, with quarterly assessments rather than periodic surveys alone.

2. **Corrective action timelines**: When deficiencies are identified, corrective action plans must be submitted within 30 days and implemented within 90 days, with federal support resources deployed immediately.

3. **Quality metrics**: IHS shall report annually on quality metrics comparable to those used by the VA and Medicare, including patient safety indicators, readmission rates, patient satisfaction, and access timeliness.

4. **Tribal quality alternatives**: Tribal health programs may develop alternative quality measures incorporating culturally defined health outcomes, subject to approval by the IHS Director in consultation with the tribal program.

5. **Whistleblower protections**: IHS employees who report quality or safety concerns shall be protected from retaliation under the same standards that apply to VA employees.

### HHS: Tribal Health Data Sovereignty

**Existing Authority**: Indian Health Care Improvement Act; Tribal Epidemiology Center authorities.

**Draft Regulation**:

1. **Tribal data ownership**: Health data generated by tribal health programs shall be owned and controlled by the tribal government operating the program.

2. **Data sharing agreements**: Any sharing of tribal health data with federal agencies, researchers, or other entities shall require a formal data sharing agreement approved by the tribal government.

3. **Tribal Epidemiology Center access**: Tribal Epidemiology Centers designated under the Indian Health Care Improvement Act shall have full access to federal health data systems for purposes of tribal health surveillance and research.

4. **Privacy protections**: Tribal health data shall be subject to HIPAA protections and any additional protections established by tribal law or regulation.

### HHS: Purchased/Referred Care Reform

**Existing Authority**: 42 C.F.R. Part 136; Indian Health Care Improvement Act.

**Draft Regulation**:

1. **Medical priority system reform**: The PRC medical priority system shall be reformed to fund all medically necessary care, not only life-threatening emergencies, when adequate funding is available under the IHS Mandatory Funding Act.

2. **Provider network development**: IHS shall develop and maintain preferred provider networks in each service area to negotiate lower rates and ensure quality.

3. **Referral coordination**: IHS shall establish dedicated referral coordination staff at each service unit to manage PRC referrals, follow up with patients, and track outcomes.

4. **Payment rates**: PRC payment rates shall be set at Medicare rates unless a different rate is negotiated with the provider.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Authority

- **Indian Commerce Clause**: Article I, Section 8, Clause 3 provides Congress clear authority over Indian affairs, as affirmed in *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023)
- **Treaty power**: Treaty obligations to provide health care constitute independent legal authority for all proposed legislation
- **Spending power**: Congress has broad authority to appropriate funds for the general welfare, and IHS falls squarely within this power
- **Trust responsibility**: The judicially recognized trust responsibility supports all proposed legislation as fulfillment of fiduciary duty
- **Political classification**: Under *Morton v. Mancari* (1974), Indian-specific legislation is a political classification subject to rational basis review, not strict scrutiny

### Preemption and Federal-State Relations

- **Scope-of-practice preemption**: Federal preemption of state scope-of-practice laws for CHAP providers at IHS and tribal facilities is supported by the Supremacy Clause and the Indian Commerce Clause; states retain authority over non-IHS, non-tribal settings
- **Medicaid coordination**: The 100% FMAP for AI/AN services at IHS and tribal facilities is established federal law; proposed legislation extends this to UIOs
- **State regulation of tribal facilities**: Under existing law, state health facility regulations generally do not apply to IHS or tribally operated facilities on trust land; this remains unchanged

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- **Mandatory funding**: Self-executing once enacted; does not require annual appropriations action
- **Quality standards**: Enforceable through CMS accreditation process and OIG oversight
- **Workforce provisions**: IHS Director responsible for implementation; Congress exercises oversight through Senate Indian Affairs Committee and House Natural Resources Committee
- **Facility construction**: Timelines enforceable through mandamus actions; annual reporting to Congress provides accountability
- **PRC reform**: Administrative enforcement through IHS service area directors

### Interaction with Existing Law

- **Indian Health Care Improvement Act**: All proposed legislation builds on and supplements the IHCIA framework; no provisions are intended to diminish existing IHCIA authorities
- **Indian Self-Determination Act**: Mandatory funding provisions explicitly protect tribal contract and compact rights; increased funding flows through existing self-determination mechanisms
- **Affordable Care Act**: IHS and tribal facilities remain eligible for all ACA provisions; proposed UIO Medicaid parity builds on ACA's permanent IHCIA reauthorization

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### IHS Mandatory Funding Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Formula manipulation | Future administrations could manipulate formula inputs (population counts, cost estimates, health status data) to reduce calculated funding levels | High |
| Inflation index mismatch | CPI-M may not accurately capture health care cost growth in remote tribal communities, where costs are 2-3x urban rates | Medium |
| Reclassification reversal | A future Congress could reclassify IHS back to discretionary through reconciliation | High |
| Administrative absorption | Federal agencies could absorb disproportionate shares of mandatory funding for headquarters overhead rather than direct services | Medium |
| PRC "medically necessary" definition | Narrow administrative interpretation of "medically necessary" could restrict PRC access despite full funding | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Initial baseline may perpetuate underfunding | Starting from current levels (40% of need) means even with phase-in, full adequacy takes 7+ years | CBO scoring constraints require manageable baseline |
| Formula cannot capture all tribal diversity | 574 tribes with vastly different needs, geographies, and health profiles cannot be perfectly served by a single formula | Inherent limitation of formula-based allocation |
| Does not address social determinants | Health care funding alone cannot close the disparity gap without addressing poverty, housing, and infrastructure | Legislative scope limitation |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Establish an independent Tribal Health Funding Commission with tribal and expert representation to oversee formula development and annual review, with authority to recommend formula adjustments
2. Develop tribal-specific cost indices based on actual cost-of-care data from each IHS service area rather than relying solely on national CPI-M
3. Include a "ratchet" provision preventing future funding reductions below the highest previously enacted mandatory level
4. Require that at least 85% of appropriated funds reach IHS service areas, tribal programs, and UIOs directly, capping headquarters administrative costs
5. Define "medically necessary" by reference to Medicare coverage standards, with additional flexibility for culturally specific services

### Indian Health Workforce Transformation Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Pay parity calculation gaming | Agencies could use narrow market surveys or outdated data to set lower compensation benchmarks | Medium |
| Loan repayment without retention | Expanded loan repayment may attract providers who complete minimum commitment then leave, creating a revolving door | Medium |
| CHAP scope-of-practice challenges | States may challenge federal preemption of scope-of-practice laws, creating legal uncertainty | High |
| Housing standard ambiguity | "Comparable to military housing" could be interpreted narrowly given variation in military housing quality | Low |
| Pipeline program leakage | Scholarship recipients may not return to serve in Indian Country if service obligations are not enforced | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address provider burnout systemically | Even with better pay, high patient acuity and understaffing cause burnout until vacancy rates decline | Workforce transformation takes years; burnout is an interim problem |
| Pipeline programs require a generation | Significant increases in AI/AN physician workforce will take 15-20 years | Educational cycle length |
| Remote location challenges persist | No compensation package fully overcomes extreme geographic isolation for all providers | Structural geographic reality |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Require annual market surveys using BLS and AAMC data to set compensation benchmarks, with tribal consultation on methodology
2. Extend loan repayment service obligation to 4 years for maximum awards and impose proportional repayment requirements for early departure
3. Include explicit Supremacy Clause language in CHAP preemption provisions and provide for DOJ defense of CHAP providers facing state challenges
4. Establish quantitative housing standards (square footage, broadband speed, appliance specifications) rather than qualitative comparisons
5. Enforce scholarship service obligations through IRS debt collection procedures for non-compliant recipients, with hardship exceptions

### Indian Health Facilities Modernization Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Priority list manipulation | Future administrations could reorder the construction priority list for political rather than clinical reasons | Medium |
| Design-build quality risks | Accelerated construction may sacrifice quality; cost overruns in remote areas may consume available funds | Medium |
| Environmental review delays | Even with streamlined processes, NEPA and other environmental reviews can add years | High |
| Tribal matching fund barriers | Tribes without gaming or other revenue may be unable to provide matching funds even at 80/20 | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| $20 billion may be insufficient | Remote construction costs (2-3x urban) and inflation may exhaust the fund before all needs are met | Estimation challenges for multi-decade construction program |
| Facilities without workforce are empty buildings | Construction outpacing workforce recruitment creates underutilized new facilities | Workforce and facilities reform must be synchronized |
| Does not address equipment replacement cycle | Initial equipment modernization does not address ongoing replacement needs | Separate ongoing appropriation needed |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Require that priority list changes receive tribal consultation and congressional notification 90 days before implementation
2. Include performance bonds and quality holdbacks in design-build contracts; require independent quality inspections
3. Establish categorical exclusions from NEPA for replacement facilities on existing sites and for renovations not expanding the facility footprint
4. Allow 100% federal share for tribes demonstrating financial need, eliminating the matching requirement barrier
5. Synchronize facility construction timelines with workforce recruitment in the same service area

### Urban Indian Health Equity Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Eligibility verification barriers | Complex or burdensome verification processes could deter eligible urban AI/AN people from accessing services | High |
| Portable benefit denial | Administrative resistance to serving AI/AN people outside traditional IHS service areas could undermine portability | Medium |
| UIO Medicaid parity challenge | CMS or states may challenge the extension of 100% FMAP to UIOs, creating reimbursement delays | High |
| New UIO startup failures | Newly established UIOs may face governance, staffing, or financial challenges leading to closure | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Cannot serve all 2.6 million eligible urban AI/AN people | Even with 80+ UIOs, geographic coverage will remain incomplete | Dispersed urban AI/AN population |
| Cultural competency varies among new UIOs | Newly established organizations may lack deep community connections | Organizational maturity takes time |
| Does not address urban AI/AN housing, employment, and poverty | Health services alone cannot close the urban AI/AN disparity gap | Legislative scope limitation |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Require streamlined eligibility verification using tribal enrollment documentation with presumptive eligibility pending verification
2. Establish an administrative appeals process for denial of portable benefits, with IHS Director review
3. Include explicit statutory language mandating 100% FMAP for UIOs, preempting any CMS reinterpretation
4. Require new UIOs to partner with established UIOs or tribal health programs for mentorship during a 3-year startup period
5. Coordinate with HUD and DOL programs serving urban AI/AN populations to address social determinants

### Indian Country Behavioral Health Emergency Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Emergency declaration time limit | Without a defined duration, the emergency declaration may lose urgency or be rescinded by a future administration | Medium |
| Traditional healer compensation barriers | Existing federal pay scales and credentialing systems may not accommodate traditional healers | Medium |
| Allocation percentage rigidity | Fixed allocation percentages may not reflect varying needs across IHS service areas | Low |
| Cultural program evaluation challenges | Inability to demonstrate outcomes through Western metrics could jeopardize continued funding | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Cannot compel provider recruitment | Funding for positions does not guarantee providers will accept them in remote locations | Structural workforce shortage |
| Culturally grounded programs are tribe-specific | A national grant program cannot fully account for the cultural diversity of 574 tribal nations | Inherent diversity of Indigenous healing traditions |
| Historical trauma requires generational healing | Behavioral health funding addresses symptoms; full healing requires decades of community rebuilding | Depth of intergenerational trauma |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Tie the emergency declaration to specific measurable targets (e.g., AI/AN suicide rate within 1.5x national average) with automatic renewal until targets are met
2. Create a Traditional Healer federal pay classification outside the General Schedule, developed in consultation with tribal nations
3. Allow IHS Area Directors to reallocate up to 20% of behavioral health funds among categories based on regional need, with tribal consultation
4. Fund a National Institute of Indigenous Health Research to develop culturally appropriate evaluation methodologies

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Federal agency resistance to reform implementation | Mandatory implementation timelines with congressional oversight; annual reporting requirements; OIG audit authority |
| Coordination across multiple bills | Establish an interagency Indian Health Reform Coordination Office within HHS to synchronize implementation |
| Data gaps on tribal health needs and outcomes | Require federal health data systems to collect and report AI/AN-specific data with tribal data sovereignty protections |
| Inconsistent judicial interpretation of trust responsibility | Comprehensive statutory definitions of trust responsibility scope and enforcement mechanisms |
| Political turnover threatening reform continuity | Mandatory funding protects against appropriations reversal; self-governance compacts create tribal ownership of programs |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- The needs-based funding formula shall be reviewed and updated every 5 years through tribal consultation (IHS Mandatory Funding Act, Sec. 3(c)(3))
- Workforce program authorization shall be reviewed after 10 years (Indian Health Workforce Transformation Act, Sec. 4(d))
- The Facilities Modernization Fund shall be assessed for reauthorization in fiscal year 2035, with a comprehensive facilities condition report
- All implementing regulations shall be reviewed every 3 years with tribal consultation
- The behavioral health emergency declaration shall be reviewed every 5 years against measurable health outcome targets
