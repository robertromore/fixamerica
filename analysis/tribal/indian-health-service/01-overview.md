# Indian Health Service: Overview

## Executive Summary

The Indian Health Service (IHS) is the federal agency responsible for providing health care to approximately 2.8 million American Indians and Alaska Natives (AI/AN) across the United States. Established as a division of the U.S. Public Health Service in 1955, IHS fulfills -- in theory -- the federal trust responsibility and treaty obligations that guaranteed health care in exchange for the cession of hundreds of millions of acres of tribal land. In practice, IHS has been chronically underfunded, understaffed, and unable to meet the health needs of the population it serves.

The consequences are staggering. American Indians and Alaska Natives have the lowest life expectancy of any racial or ethnic group in the United States -- 65.2 years, compared to 77.5 years for the general population (IHS, 2024). AI/AN people die at higher rates from diabetes, chronic liver disease, suicide, assault, and unintentional injuries. Infant mortality rates are 1.6 times the national average. These disparities are not the product of individual choices or cultural factors -- they are the predictable result of systematic underfunding of a health system that serves some of the nation's most remote and impoverished communities.

IHS operates through three delivery systems: direct service by IHS-operated facilities, tribally operated programs under Indian Self-Determination Act compacts, and urban Indian health programs. Together, these systems serve a population spread across 37 states, including some of the most remote areas in the country. Yet IHS per-capita spending -- approximately $4,078 per user in FY 2023 -- is less than one-third of Medicare per-capita spending and less than one-quarter of Federal Employees Health Benefit spending. Closing this gap is not a matter of charity; it is a matter of fulfilling legally binding obligations.

Reform requires transforming IHS funding from discretionary to mandatory, expanding the health care workforce in Indian Country, modernizing facilities, strengthening tribal self-governance over health care delivery, and addressing the social determinants of health that perpetuate disparities.

## Core Tensions

- **Treaty obligation vs. discretionary funding**: Health care was promised in perpetuity but is funded annually, subject to budget politics and government shutdowns
- **Self-determination vs. federal control**: Tribes increasingly manage their own health care but remain dependent on inadequate federal funding
- **Direct service vs. purchased/referred care**: IHS facilities cannot provide all services; purchased/referred care funds are exhausted partway through every fiscal year
- **Rural access vs. urban population**: 78% of AI/AN people live in urban areas, but 95% of IHS funding goes to reservation-based programs
- **Workforce needs vs. recruitment challenges**: Remote locations, lower pay, and facility deficiencies make recruitment and retention extremely difficult
- **Cultural competency vs. standard practice**: Integrating traditional healing with Western medicine requires institutional flexibility that bureaucratic systems resist

## Key Questions

1. **Why is IHS so chronically underfunded?** What are the structural and political reasons that treaty-obligated health care receives a fraction of comparable program funding?

2. **How do funding shortfalls translate into health disparities?** What specific services are unavailable or inadequate, and what are the health consequences?

3. **Does tribal management improve health outcomes?** What does the evidence show about tribally operated health programs compared to direct IHS service?

4. **How can the health care workforce in Indian Country be expanded?** What recruitment and retention strategies have worked, and what barriers remain?

5. **What would adequate funding look like?** How much would it cost to bring IHS services to parity with other federal health programs?

6. **How should urban Indian health be expanded?** What models exist for serving the majority of AI/AN people who live off-reservation?

7. **Can IHS facilities be modernized within existing frameworks?** What capital investment is needed, and what are the most efficient delivery models?

## Vision of Success

An adequately funded and effectively administered Indian health system would feature:

- Life expectancy for AI/AN people within 2 years of the national average
- Per-capita spending comparable to Medicare or Federal Employee Health Benefits
- Full staffing at all IHS and tribal facilities, with competitive salaries and recruitment incentives
- Modern, accredited facilities in every service area
- Comprehensive behavioral health services, including culturally grounded programs
- Purchased/referred care funds available throughout the entire fiscal year
- Urban Indian health programs accessible to all AI/AN people regardless of residence
- Meaningful integration of traditional healing with Western medical practice
