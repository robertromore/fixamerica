# Missing and Murdered Indigenous People: Opposition

## Overview

While few political actors openly oppose addressing the MMIP crisis, substantial opposition exists to the structural reforms necessary to meaningfully reduce it. This opposition is directed not at the goal of protecting Indigenous people but at the means -- particularly the restoration of tribal criminal jurisdiction, increased federal spending, and regulation of extractive industries. Understanding these opposition dynamics is essential for designing strategies that can overcome or accommodate them.

---

## Opposition to Tribal Jurisdiction Expansion

### Who Opposes

- **State attorneys general and law enforcement associations**: Particularly in PL 280 states and states with significant tribal lands
- **Criminal defense bar**: Concerns about due process protections in tribal courts
- **Some members of Congress**: Particularly those representing states with jurisdictional conflicts
- **Legal scholars favoring federal supremacy**: Those who view *Oliphant* as correctly decided

### Why They Oppose

| Argument | Stated Concern | Underlying Interest |
|----------|---------------|---------------------|
| Due process | Non-Indians may not receive adequate constitutional protections in tribal courts | Resistance to expanding tribal governmental authority |
| Complexity | Expanding tribal jurisdiction creates additional legal complexity | Preserving state and federal authority primacy |
| Resource concerns | Tribes lack resources to handle expanded caseloads | Avoiding federal obligation to fund tribal courts |
| Precedent | Overturning *Oliphant* would fundamentally alter federal Indian law | Protecting existing jurisdictional arrangements |
| State sovereignty | Tribal jurisdiction expansion is seen as diminishing state authority | Competition for governmental power |

### Strength of Opposition

- Moderate to strong in Congress; tribal jurisdiction bills face significant legislative resistance
- Strong in the legal establishment; *Oliphant* has been reaffirmed and extended in subsequent decisions
- The Supreme Court's decision in *Oklahoma v. Castro-Huerta* (2022) -- holding that states have concurrent jurisdiction in Indian Country -- indicates judicial resistance to tribal jurisdictional expansion

### Counter-Arguments

- VAWA 2013 SDVCJ has been implemented by approximately 45 tribes with no documented due process violations (NCAI, 2025)
- The Indian Civil Rights Act (1968) and the Tribal Law and Order Act (2010) already require due process protections comparable to those in state courts
- The current system demonstrably fails: over 52% declination rate, 90%+ of perpetrators not subject to any jurisdiction that will prosecute them

## Opposition to Increased Federal Funding

### Who Opposes

- **Fiscal conservatives in Congress**: Opposition to discretionary spending increases
- **Budget hawks**: Competition with other federal priorities
- **Some executive branch agencies**: Bureaucratic resistance to programmatic expansion
- **Anti-government organizations**: Ideological opposition to expanded federal programs

### Why They Oppose

| Argument | Stated Concern | Underlying Interest |
|----------|---------------|---------------------|
| Fiscal responsibility | Federal spending must be constrained | Ideological opposition to government expansion |
| Competing priorities | Limited funds should go to highest-impact programs | MMIP does not generate sufficient political return |
| Effectiveness concerns | More money does not necessarily produce better outcomes | Avoiding open-ended commitments |
| Self-determination | Tribes should be economically self-sufficient | Reducing federal trust obligation |

### Strength of Opposition

- Strong in the current Congress (119th); discretionary spending faces cuts across many programs
- MMIP-specific funding has been relatively small and bipartisan, but significant increases face resistance
- The BIA Missing and Murdered Unit has 14 coordinators for 574 tribes, reflecting funding constraints that are politically difficult to overcome

## Opposition to Extractive Industry Regulation

### Who Opposes

- **Oil, gas, and mining industry**: Companies operating near tribal lands
- **Industry trade associations**: American Petroleum Institute, National Mining Association
- **State economic development agencies**: In resource-dependent states
- **Labor unions in extractive industries**: Concerned about job impacts
- **Members of Congress from resource-producing states**: Bipartisan opposition to industry regulation

### Why They Oppose

| Argument | Stated Concern | Underlying Interest |
|----------|---------------|---------------------|
| Economic impact | Regulation will reduce energy production and jobs | Protecting profits and market access |
| Overreach | Government should not regulate temporary worker housing | Minimizing regulatory burden |
| Causation | Connection between man camps and violence is unproven or overstated | Avoiding liability and reputational damage |
| Energy security | Domestic energy production is a national priority | Framing regulation as anti-American |
| Jobs | Workers need housing near work sites | Avoiding costly safety measures |

### Strength of Opposition

- Very strong; extractive industry lobbying is among the most powerful in Washington
- Bipartisan support for domestic energy production makes regulation politically difficult
- Industry has successfully resisted most proposals to regulate man camps or require violence prevention measures

## Opposition to Data Reform

### Who Opposes

- **Some law enforcement agencies**: Resistance to additional reporting requirements
- **States**: Concern about unfunded mandates for data collection
- **Privacy advocates**: Concerns about expanded surveillance and data collection
- **Some tribal governments**: Concerns about data sovereignty and outside control of tribal information

### Why They Oppose

| Argument | Stated Concern | Underlying Interest |
|----------|---------------|---------------------|
| Administrative burden | Additional reporting requirements strain limited resources | Resistance to accountability metrics |
| Privacy | Expanded databases could be misused | Legitimate concerns about surveillance |
| Tribal sovereignty | External data systems may violate tribal authority over citizen information | Protecting self-governance and data sovereignty |
| Accuracy | Forced racial identification could increase errors | Legitimate methodological concern |

### Strength of Opposition

- Moderate; data reform has broader support than jurisdiction expansion
- Data sovereignty concerns from tribal governments are legitimate and must be addressed through co-design
- Law enforcement resistance to reporting requirements is pervasive but manageable with adequate resources

---

## Opposition to Media Coverage Reform

### Who Opposes

- **News media organizations**: Resistance to external pressure on editorial decisions
- **First Amendment advocates**: Opposition to government involvement in media coverage decisions
- **Some journalists**: Professional autonomy concerns

### Why They Oppose

| Argument | Stated Concern | Underlying Interest |
|----------|---------------|---------------------|
| Editorial independence | Media should cover stories based on news value, not government mandates | Professional autonomy and First Amendment |
| Resource constraints | Local newsrooms lack staff for extended coverage | Declining media industry economics |
| Audience interest | Coverage decisions reflect audience demand | Market-driven journalism |

### Strength of Opposition

- Strong constitutional protections for press freedom make mandated coverage reform unlikely
- However, advocacy for voluntary reform and support for Indigenous media has been effective
- Media awareness training and journalism fellowships have increased some coverage

## Institutional Inertia

### Federal Bureaucratic Resistance

Even without active opposition, institutional inertia impedes MMIP reform:

- **FBI culture**: Indian Country cases are not career-enhancing assignments; agents seek transfers to higher-profile programs (GAO, 2023)
- **U.S. Attorney priorities**: Indian Country cases compete with drug, immigration, and national security cases that receive more institutional attention
- **BIA capacity**: The Bureau is chronically understaffed and has competing mandates beyond public safety
- **Interagency coordination**: Multiple federal agencies have overlapping responsibilities with no single point of accountability

### State and Local Resistance

- **PL 280 state law enforcement**: Some agencies have historically refused to respond to calls on reservations, citing resource constraints or jurisdictional ambiguity
- **Local governments near reservations**: Opposition to land-into-trust acquisitions and tribal jurisdiction expansion, often framed as tax base concerns
- **District attorneys**: May decline to prosecute cases involving tribal communities due to cultural unfamiliarity or political calculation

## Opposition Strategies

### Common Tactics

| Tactic | How Used | Example |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Deflection | Acknowledge the problem but oppose specific solutions | "We support MMIP awareness but not tribal jurisdiction expansion" |
| Incremental resistance | Support symbolic measures while opposing structural reform | Support awareness days but oppose funding increases |
| Complexity arguments | Argue the problem is too complex for proposed solutions | "Jurisdiction reform won't solve the underlying issues" |
| Cost framing | Frame reform as too expensive | "We cannot afford to fund tribal law enforcement at requested levels" |
| States' rights | Oppose tribal jurisdiction as federalism concern | "This is a state matter, not a tribal matter" |
| Legal formalism | Cite *Oliphant* and existing precedent as settled law | "The Supreme Court has already decided this question" |

---

## Overcoming Opposition

### Successful Strategies Used by MMIP Advocates

- **Bipartisan framing**: MMIP legislation (Savanna's Act, Not Invisible Act) passed with bipartisan support by emphasizing law enforcement and public safety rather than sovereignty
- **Personal testimony**: Families of MMIP victims have provided compelling testimony that has moved legislators across the political spectrum
- **Data and evidence**: Documenting the scope of the crisis through research (NIJ, Urban Indian Health Institute, Sovereign Bodies Institute) has been essential to building the case for reform
- **VAWA pilot success**: The successful implementation of SDVCJ by tribes under VAWA 2013 demonstrated that tribal jurisdiction expansion works without the due process concerns raised by opponents
- **State-level action**: When federal action stalls, state task forces and legislation create momentum and demonstrate feasibility
- **Coalition building**: Partnerships between Indigenous organizations, law enforcement associations, victim rights groups, and bipartisan congressional champions have been essential
