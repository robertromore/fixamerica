# Missing and Murdered Indigenous People: Solutions

## Overview

Addressing the MMIP crisis requires a comprehensive, multi-layered approach that tackles root causes, removes structural barriers, and invests in both immediate response capacity and long-term prevention. Solutions must be designed in partnership with tribal nations and Indigenous communities, respecting sovereignty while providing the resources and legal authority necessary for effective protection. No single reform will solve the crisis; the solutions below are designed to work together as a reinforcing system.

---

## 1. Restore Tribal Criminal Jurisdiction

### The Reform

Legislatively overturn *Oliphant v. Suquamish Indian Tribe* (1978) to restore inherent tribal criminal jurisdiction over all persons within tribal territories.

### How It Works

- Congress exercises its plenary power to recognize that tribal nations possess inherent criminal jurisdiction over all persons -- Indian and non-Indian -- who commit crimes within tribal territory
- Tribes implementing expanded jurisdiction must provide due process protections consistent with the Indian Civil Rights Act, including effective assistance of counsel, access to a law-trained judge, and a jury drawn from a fair cross-section
- Phased implementation allows tribes to build capacity before assuming full jurisdiction
- Federal concurrent jurisdiction is maintained as a backstop

### Evidence of Feasibility

- VAWA 2013 SDVCJ has been successfully implemented by approximately 45 tribes with no documented due process violations (NCAI, 2025)
- Tribal courts already handle thousands of criminal cases annually under existing jurisdiction
- The Tribal Law and Order Act (2010) established enhanced sentencing provisions that tribes have implemented effectively

### Expected Impact

- Closes the jurisdictional gap that allows over 90% of non-Indian perpetrators to escape tribal prosecution
- Reduces response times by enabling tribal law enforcement to act immediately
- Deters crime by eliminating zones of impunity on tribal lands

### Challenges

- Congressional action required; significant legislative resistance expected
- Tribal courts will need substantial funding to handle expanded caseloads
- Concerns about due process protections must be addressed through robust procedural requirements

## 2. Establish a Comprehensive National MMIP Data System

### The Reform

Create a unified, interoperable national database for tracking missing and murdered Indigenous people across all jurisdictions, with tribal data sovereignty protections.

### Key Components

| Component | Description |
|-----------|------------|
| Unified reporting standards | Common definitions for missing persons, homicides, and unidentified remains across tribal, federal, state, and local jurisdictions |
| Mandatory reporting | Require all law enforcement agencies to enter missing Indigenous persons cases into the system within 24 hours |
| Tribal data access | Grant all tribal law enforcement agencies direct access to NCIC, NamUs, and the new system |
| Racial classification reform | Require proper identification of Indigenous victims using self-identification and tribal enrollment data where available |
| Tribal data sovereignty | Ensure tribes retain ownership and control of data about their citizens; implement governance protocols for data sharing |
| Cold case integration | Incorporate existing unsolved cases into the system for re-examination |
| Public dashboard | Create a publicly accessible (anonymized) dashboard showing case status and trends |

### Evidence of Feasibility

- Canada's National Centre for Missing Persons and Unidentified Remains provides a comparable model
- Savanna's Act (2020) already mandates some of these requirements but implementation has been incomplete
- The FBI's transition to NIBRS provides a framework for standardized reporting

### Expected Impact

- Enables accurate assessment of MMIP scope for the first time
- Facilitates cross-jurisdictional case coordination
- Identifies patterns (geographic, temporal, perpetrator) that can guide prevention

## 3. Increase Law Enforcement Funding and Capacity

### The Reform

Fund tribal and federal law enforcement in Indian Country at levels comparable to non-tribal communities of similar size and geography, with dedicated MMIP investigation capacity.

### Specific Investments

| Investment | Current Level | Proposed Level | Source |
|-----------|--------------|---------------|--------|
| BIA law enforcement officers | ~2,800 | 5,000+ (proportional to population/area) | BIA, 2023 |
| FBI Indian Country agents | ~120 | 300+ (proportional to caseload) | FBI, 2023 |
| Missing and Murdered Unit coordinators | 14 | 75+ (one per region, with tribal liaisons) | BIA MMU, 2024 |
| Tribal victim advocates | Varies (many tribes have none) | At least one per tribe | DOJ OVW, 2024 |
| Forensic investigation capacity | Minimal in most tribal agencies | Forensic units or regional partnerships for all tribal areas | NIJ, 2022 |
| Communication infrastructure | Spotty cell/radio coverage on many reservations | Full coverage including satellite backup | FCC, 2023 |

### Implementation Approach

- Mandatory (not discretionary) federal appropriations for tribal law enforcement
- Formula-based distribution to ensure equitable allocation
- Tribal self-governance compacts allowing tribes to design their own law enforcement programs
- Regional forensic partnerships for smaller tribes

## 4. Reform Federal Prosecution of Indian Country Cases

### The Reform

Reduce federal declination rates through dedicated Indian Country prosecutors, accountability measures, and alternative prosecution pathways.

### Key Components

- **Dedicated Indian Country AUSAs**: Require at least one Assistant U.S. Attorney dedicated to Indian Country cases in every federal district with tribal land
- **Declination accountability**: Require U.S. Attorneys to report declination reasons publicly; establish DOJ oversight of declination patterns
- **Tribal consultation on declinations**: Require consultation with tribal prosecutors before declining cases; offer referral to tribal courts where jurisdiction exists
- **Cross-designation**: Enable tribal prosecutors to serve as Special Assistant U.S. Attorneys (SAUSAs) to handle Indian Country cases in federal court
- **Cold case units**: Establish dedicated federal cold case units for Indian Country homicides and missing persons cases
- **Cultural competency training**: Mandatory training for all federal personnel handling Indian Country cases

### Evidence of Feasibility

- The Tribal Law and Order Act (2010) already requires declination reporting, but enforcement has been inconsistent
- Cross-designation of tribal prosecutors has been used successfully in several districts
- GAO has repeatedly recommended dedicated Indian Country prosecution resources (GAO-23-105442, 2023)

## 5. Expand Victim Services and Support

### The Reform

Fund culturally appropriate victim services -- including shelters, counseling, legal aid, and economic support -- accessible to Indigenous people in both reservation and urban settings.

### Key Investments

| Service | Current Availability | Proposed Target |
|---------|---------------------|----------------|
| Tribally operated DV shelters | 46 for 574 tribes | At least 100; regional shelters serving multiple tribes |
| StrongHearts Native Helpline | Operational 24/7 | Expanded capacity with local follow-up connections |
| Tribal legal aid | Critically underfunded | Dedicated legal aid attorney for every tribe |
| Victim advocates | Many tribes have none | At least one trained advocate per tribal community |
| Urban Indigenous services | Limited; scattered programs | Funded programs in every city with 5,000+ Indigenous residents |
| Trauma-informed mental health | IHS serves fraction of need | Expand IHS behavioral health; fund tribal-specific programs |
| Transitional housing | Minimal | Transitional housing programs in every BIA region |

### Urban Focus

- 71% of Indigenous people live in urban areas (Census, 2020), yet most MMIP resources target reservation communities
- Urban Indian organizations need dedicated MMIP funding for victim services, advocacy, and coordination with local law enforcement
- Urban police departments in cities with significant Indigenous populations should have designated MMIP liaisons

## 6. Address Extractive Industry Impacts

### The Reform

Regulate temporary worker housing ("man camps") and require violence prevention measures for extractive industry operations near tribal lands.

### Key Components

- **Man camp regulation**: Require background checks, substance use policies, and security measures for temporary worker housing within 50 miles of tribal land
- **Impact assessment**: Require companies to assess and mitigate violence risks before beginning operations near tribal communities
- **Tribal consultation**: Mandate meaningful consultation with affected tribes before permitting extractive operations
- **Revenue sharing**: Allocate a portion of extractive industry revenues to fund tribal law enforcement and victim services
- **Reporting requirements**: Require companies to report crimes committed by employees to tribal and federal law enforcement

### Evidence of Need

- The Bakken oil boom in North Dakota and Montana was associated with documented increases in sex trafficking, sexual assault, and drug trafficking near tribal communities (DOJ, 2019; NCAI, 2022)
- Fort Berthold Indian Reservation experienced a tripling of violent crime during the oil boom (MHA Nation, 2019)

## 7. Strengthen Media Coverage and Public Awareness

### The Reform

Increase media coverage of MMIP cases and improve public awareness through investment in Indigenous media, journalism training, and awareness infrastructure.

### Key Components

- **Alert systems**: Expand Amber Alert in Indian Country (AAIC) to all 574 tribes; create MMIP-specific alert system comparable to Silver Alerts
- **Indigenous media investment**: Fund Indigenous news organizations and journalism fellowships focused on MMIP coverage
- **Journalism training**: Partner with journalism schools to develop MMIP coverage curricula
- **Public awareness campaigns**: Fund national awareness campaigns comparable to those for other public safety issues
- **Social media partnerships**: Work with platforms to amplify MMIP alerts and awareness content

## 8. Prevention Through Root Cause Investment

### The Reform

Address the socioeconomic conditions, historical trauma, and community vulnerabilities that create disproportionate MMIP risk.

### Key Investments

| Area | Intervention | Rationale |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| Housing | Expand NAHASDA funding; build shelters and transitional housing | Housing instability increases vulnerability to violence and trafficking |
| Economic development | Fund tribal enterprise development; remove trust land economic barriers | Poverty is the strongest predictor of victimization |
| Education | Fund tribal education programs; address boarding school trauma through healing programs | Education reduces vulnerability and builds community capacity |
| Behavioral health | Expand IHS and tribal behavioral health programs | Substance use disorders increase vulnerability; trauma requires treatment |
| Cultural revitalization | Fund language, ceremony, and cultural programs | Cultural connection is a protective factor against violence and substance use |
| Infrastructure | Build roads, broadband, and emergency communication systems | Isolation delays emergency response and increases vulnerability |

---

## 9. International and Human Rights Mechanisms

### The Reform

Engage international human rights mechanisms to create external accountability for U.S. MMIP failures.

### Key Actions

- Report U.S. MMIP crisis to UN Permanent Forum on Indigenous Issues and UN Committee on the Elimination of Racial Discrimination
- Seek Inter-American Commission on Human Rights review of U.S. MMIP response
- Coordinate with Canadian government and First Nations on cross-border MMIP cases (particularly along Highway of Tears corridor)
- Endorse and implement the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples provisions relevant to safety and justice

## Solution Interaction Model

These solutions are designed to work as a system:

| Solution | Enables | Depends On |
|----------|---------|------------|
| Jurisdiction restoration | Investigation, prosecution, deterrence | Funding, court capacity |
| Data system | Pattern identification, resource allocation | Jurisdiction clarity, reporting standards |
| Law enforcement funding | Investigation capacity, response times | Jurisdiction clarity, data system |
| Prosecution reform | Accountability, deterrence | Law enforcement capacity, data |
| Victim services | Safety, healing, cooperation with prosecution | Funding, cultural competency |
| Extractive industry regulation | Prevention, reduced vulnerability | Political will, enforcement capacity |
| Media reform | Public awareness, political pressure | Indigenous media capacity |
| Root cause investment | Long-term prevention | Sustained funding commitment |
