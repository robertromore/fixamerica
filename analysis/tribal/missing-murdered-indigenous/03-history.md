# Missing and Murdered Indigenous People: History

## Overview

The crisis of missing and murdered Indigenous people is not a modern phenomenon but the continuation of centuries of violence rooted in colonization, dispossession, and federal policies designed to subjugate, remove, and assimilate Indigenous peoples. Understanding this history is essential to comprehending why the crisis persists and why the systems meant to protect Indigenous people so consistently fail.

---

## Pre-Colonial Era (Before 1492)

### Indigenous Self-Governance and Justice

Before European contact, Indigenous nations across North America maintained sophisticated systems of governance, justice, and community safety:

- Hundreds of distinct nations operated their own legal systems, including restorative justice practices, clan-based dispute resolution, and community accountability mechanisms
- Violence against women was addressed through kinship systems, clan responsibilities, and community sanctions
- Matrilineal societies (such as the Haudenosaunee, Cherokee, and many Pueblo nations) afforded women significant political authority, property rights, and personal autonomy (Mann, "The Fire Next Time: New Histories of Violence in Colonial Indian Country," *Ethnohistory* 51, no. 4, 2004)
- Gender-diverse individuals (now often identified under the umbrella term Two-Spirit) held recognized social roles in many nations

### Key Context

- Pre-contact violence against women and children occurred but was regulated through kinship and community accountability systems
- The destruction of these systems by colonization removed Indigenous protective structures without replacing them with functioning alternatives

## Colonial Period (1492-1776)

### Violence as Colonial Tool

European colonization introduced systematic violence against Indigenous peoples:

| Period | Key Events | Impact on Indigenous Safety |
|--------|-----------|---------------------------|
| 1492-1600s | Spanish conquest and mission system | Enslavement, sexual violence, forced labor; California missions documented extensive abuse |
| 1600s-1700s | English colonial expansion | Warfare, bounties on Indigenous people, captive-taking |
| 1700s | French and British imperial competition | Indigenous nations drawn into proxy wars; communities destabilized |

### Sexual Violence as Colonial Policy

- European colonizers engaged in widespread sexual violence against Indigenous women, documented by colonists themselves and in missionary records (Smith, *Conquest: Sexual Violence and American Indian Genocide*, 2005)
- Trafficking and enslavement of Indigenous women was practiced by multiple colonial powers
- Destruction of matrilineal social structures undermined Indigenous women's status and protections

## Early Federal Period (1776-1871)

### Treaty Era and Removal

| Period | Federal Policy | Impact on Indigenous Safety |
|--------|---------------|---------------------------|
| 1778-1871 | Treaty-making (374 treaties ratified) | Promised protection but created dependency on federal enforcement |
| 1830 | Indian Removal Act | Forced removal killed thousands; shattered community structures |
| 1830s-1850s | Trail of Tears and forced relocations | Death, family separation, destruction of social safety systems |
| 1849 | Transfer of Indian affairs to Interior Department | Military management replaced with civilian bureaucracy; neither prioritized Indigenous safety |

### The Trail of Tears and Mass Violence

- The forced removal of the Cherokee, Chickasaw, Choctaw, Creek, and Seminole nations killed an estimated 15,000 people (Perdue and Green, *The Cherokee Nation and the Trail of Tears*, 2007)
- Family separations during removal disrupted kinship systems that had protected community members
- Women and children were particularly vulnerable during forced marches, with documented sexual violence by soldiers and settlers

### Establishment of Federal Responsibility

- Treaties established the federal trust responsibility, including obligations to provide for safety and welfare
- The creation of reservations concentrated Indigenous populations in confined areas under federal control
- The federal government assumed exclusive jurisdiction over crimes between Indians and non-Indians but rarely enforced it

---

## Allotment and Assimilation Era (1871-1934)

### Dawes Act and Land Loss

The General Allotment Act of 1887 (Dawes Act) sought to destroy communal landholding and assimilate Indigenous people:

- 90 million acres of tribal land lost between 1887 and 1934 (National Congress of American Indians, 2023)
- Checkerboard land patterns created jurisdictional confusion that persists today
- Loss of communal land bases undermined tribal governance capacity, including the ability to maintain community safety

### Boarding Schools and Family Destruction

The federal Indian boarding school system (1860s-1960s) constituted one of the most devastating policies affecting Indigenous safety:

| Aspect | Detail | Source |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Schools operated | Over 400 federal and church-run boarding schools | Federal Indian Boarding School Investigative Report, Vol. 1, DOI, 2022 |
| Children removed | Hundreds of thousands over the program's duration | DOI Investigative Report, 2022 |
| Deaths documented | At least 973 children died at boarding schools (known; actual number likely far higher) | DOI Investigative Report, Vol. 2, 2024 |
| Abuse documentation | Widespread physical, sexual, and emotional abuse documented across institutions | DOI Investigative Report, 2022 |

- Children were forcibly removed from families, stripped of language and culture, and subjected to abuse
- The intergenerational trauma from boarding schools is directly linked to elevated rates of violence, substance use, and family disruption in subsequent generations (Brave Heart, "The Historical Trauma Response Among Natives," *Journal of Psychoactive Drugs* 35, no. 1, 2003)
- Destruction of parenting knowledge across generations created cascading effects on family stability

### Major Crimes Act (1885)

- Following *Ex parte Crow Dog* (1883), Congress passed the Major Crimes Act, asserting federal jurisdiction over serious crimes committed by Indians in Indian Country
- This undermined tribal justice systems while providing inadequate federal alternatives
- Created the template for the jurisdictional complexity that persists today

## Termination and Relocation Era (1945-1968)

### Termination Policy

Congress pursued termination of tribal status and federal obligations:

| Policy | Year | Impact |
|--------|------|--------|
| House Concurrent Resolution 108 | 1953 | Declared intent to terminate federal-tribal relationships |
| Public Law 280 | 1953 | Transferred criminal jurisdiction to six states without tribal consent or state resources |
| Urban relocation program | 1952-1972 | Relocated over 100,000 Indigenous people to cities without support systems |
| Termination acts | 1954-1966 | Terminated 109 tribes; eliminated federal services and protections |

### Impact on Indigenous Safety

- Public Law 280 transferred criminal jurisdiction to states that had neither the resources nor the will to serve tribal communities
- In PL 280 states, law enforcement response to crimes on reservations remained chronically inadequate
- Urban relocation placed Indigenous people in cities without community support structures, increasing vulnerability to violence, trafficking, and homelessness
- Termination eliminated federal trust responsibility for over 100 tribes, including law enforcement services

## Self-Determination Era (1968-Present)

### Restoration of Tribal Authority

| Law | Year | Significance for MMIP |
|-----|------|----------------------|
| Indian Civil Rights Act | 1968 | Applied bill of rights protections to tribal governance; limited tribal sentencing to 1 year |
| Indian Self-Determination Act | 1975 | Allowed tribes to contract federal programs, including law enforcement |
| Indian Child Welfare Act | 1978 | Addressed removal of Indigenous children from families |
| *Oliphant v. Suquamish* | 1978 | Stripped tribal criminal jurisdiction over non-Indians -- the single most damaging legal precedent for MMIP |
| Indian Tribal Justice Act | 1993 | Authorized (but chronically underfunded) support for tribal court systems |
| VAWA 2013 | 2013 | Partially restored tribal jurisdiction over non-Indian domestic violence offenders |
| Tribal Law and Order Act | 2010 | Enhanced tribal sentencing authority to 3 years; required federal accountability |

### *Oliphant v. Suquamish Indian Tribe* (1978)

This Supreme Court decision is the most consequential legal development in the MMIP crisis:

- Held that tribal courts lack inherent criminal jurisdiction over non-Indians
- Created a jurisdictional vacuum where non-Indian offenders on tribal land could only be prosecuted by federal or state authorities
- Combined with federal declination rates exceeding 50%, this effectively created a zone of impunity for non-Indian perpetrators of violence against Indigenous people
- The decision has been criticized by legal scholars as historically unfounded and has been repeatedly identified by tribal leaders as the primary legal barrier to addressing MMIP (Washburn, "American Indians, Crime, and the Law," *Michigan Law Review* 104, no. 4, 2006)

### The Rise of MMIP Awareness (2010s-Present)

The MMIP crisis gained significant national visibility beginning in the mid-2010s:

| Year | Development |
|------|------------|
| 2015 | Sovereign Bodies Institute begins systematic MMIP data collection |
| 2016 | National Inquiry into Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women and Girls launched in Canada; heightened U.S. attention |
| 2017 | Urban Indian Health Institute study documents MMIP in 71 urban cities |
| 2018 | Savanna LaFontaine-Greywind murder galvanizes advocacy; red dress movement grows |
| 2019 | BIA Missing and Murdered Unit established; multiple state task forces launched |
| 2019 | Executive Order 13898: Establishing the Task Force on Missing and Murdered American Indians and Alaska Natives |
| 2020 | Savanna's Act and Not Invisible Act signed into law |
| 2020 | National Day of Awareness for MMIWG (May 5) recognized by Congress |
| 2022 | VAWA reauthorization expands tribal jurisdiction to additional crime categories |
| 2022 | DOI Federal Indian Boarding School Investigative Report published |
| 2023 | Not Invisible Act Commission issues initial recommendations |
| 2024 | BIA Missing and Murdered Unit reports reviewing 1,500+ cases |

### The Red Dress Movement

The red dress has become a powerful symbol of the MMIP movement:

- Artist Jaime Black's REDress Project (2010) displayed red dresses in public spaces to represent missing and murdered Indigenous women
- Red Dress Day (May 5) is observed as a day of awareness across North America
- The movement has been instrumental in bringing the MMIP crisis to broader public consciousness

## Historical Patterns and Their Legacy

### Continuity of Violence

The MMIP crisis is not a discrete modern problem but the latest manifestation of a continuous pattern:

| Historical Policy | Modern Consequence |
|------------------|-------------------|
| Colonization and land theft | Geographic isolation, poverty, inadequate infrastructure |
| Boarding schools | Intergenerational trauma, family disruption, substance use |
| Forced removal | Disconnection from homelands and traditional support systems |
| Allotment | Checkerboard jurisdiction, governance capacity reduction |
| Termination | Loss of federal services and protections |
| *Oliphant* | Jurisdictional vacuum enabling impunity for non-Indian offenders |
| Urban relocation | Urban Indigenous populations outside tribal jurisdiction |
| PL 280 | Inadequate state law enforcement on reservations |

### The Federal Trust Failure

Throughout this history, a consistent pattern emerges: the federal government has assumed responsibility for Indigenous safety while systematically failing to provide the resources and authority necessary to fulfill that responsibility. Each era of federal policy has either directly inflicted violence on Indigenous people or created conditions that enable it.
