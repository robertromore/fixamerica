# Missing and Murdered Indigenous People: Current State

## Scale and Scope

### The Magnitude of the Crisis

The MMIP crisis defies precise quantification because of systemic data gaps, but available evidence reveals staggering disparities:

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Indigenous women experiencing violence (lifetime) | 84.3% | National Institute of Justice, 2016 |
| Indigenous women experiencing sexual violence | 56.1% | National Institute of Justice, 2016 |
| Indigenous women stalked in lifetime | 48.8% | National Institute of Justice, 2016 |
| Murder rate for Indigenous women (some reservations) | Up to 10x national average | DOJ Indian Country Investigations, 2022 |
| Missing Indigenous persons in NamUs database | 4,200+ cases | National Missing and Unidentified Persons System, 2023 |
| Unidentified Indigenous remains in NamUs | 628 cases | NamUs, 2023 |
| Homicide clearance rate in Indian Country | Approximately 50% | FBI UCR / DOJ, 2022 |
| National homicide clearance rate (comparison) | 54.4% | FBI UCR, 2022 |
| Indian Country cases declined by federal prosecutors | 52% | DOJ Indian Country Report, 2022 |
| Percentage of perpetrators who are non-Indian | Over 90% (sexual violence against Indigenous women) | National Institute of Justice, 2016 |

### Demographic Patterns

The crisis disproportionately affects specific groups within Indigenous communities:

**Gender and Age Distribution:**

- Indigenous women and girls represent the largest identified category of MMIP victims
- Indigenous men and boys are also affected at elevated rates but receive even less attention and data collection
- Two-Spirit and LGBTQ+ Indigenous people face compounded vulnerabilities, though data collection is minimal
- Adolescents and young adults (ages 15-29) represent the highest-risk age group (CDC WISQARS, 2022)

**Geographic Distribution:**

| Region | Key Characteristics | Source |
|--------|-------------------|--------|
| Northern Plains (MT, ND, SD, WY, NE) | Highest per capita rates; vast distances; extreme weather impedes response | Sovereign Bodies Institute, 2023 |
| Southwest (AZ, NM) | Large reservations; border proximity complicates jurisdiction | Urban Indian Health Institute, 2018 |
| Pacific Northwest (WA, OR, AK) | Significant urban Indigenous populations; Highway of Tears corridor | Washington State MMIWP Report, 2022 |
| Alaska | Extreme remoteness; no road access to many villages; highest per capita violence rates | Alaska Federation of Natives, 2023 |
| Oklahoma | Post-McGirt jurisdictional complexity; large Indigenous population | Oklahoma MMIP Task Force, 2023 |
| Urban areas nationally | 71% of Indigenous people are urban; lack tribal jurisdiction protections | U.S. Census Bureau, 2020 |

### Connection to Trafficking and Extractive Industries

Research increasingly documents links between MMIP and human trafficking:

- Proximity to man camps (temporary housing for extractive industry workers) correlates with increased violence against Indigenous women (National Congress of American Indians, 2022)
- The Bakken oil region in North Dakota and Montana saw a documented increase in sex trafficking and violence during oil booms (DOJ, 2019)
- Indigenous people constitute a disproportionate share of trafficking victims: 40% in Minnesota, 50% in Anchorage (Minnesota Department of Health, 2019; Covenant House Alaska, 2020)
- Interstate highways and transportation corridors near tribal lands serve as trafficking routes (Polaris Project, 2020)

## Jurisdictional Framework

### The Jurisdictional Maze

Criminal jurisdiction in Indian Country is among the most complex in American law, and this complexity directly contributes to the MMIP crisis:

| Scenario | Who Investigates | Who Prosecutes | Legal Authority |
|----------|-----------------|----------------|-----------------|
| Non-Indian assaults Indigenous person | FBI or BIA | U.S. Attorney | General Crimes Act, 18 U.S.C. 1152 |
| Indigenous person assaults Indigenous person (major crime) | FBI or BIA | U.S. Attorney + Tribal | Major Crimes Act, 18 U.S.C. 1153 |
| Non-Indian assaults Indigenous person (DV, dating violence, stalking, trafficking) | FBI/BIA + Tribal | Tribal (if qualifying tribe) + U.S. Attorney | VAWA 2013/2022 Special Domestic Violence Criminal Jurisdiction |
| Non-Indian-on-non-Indian | State or FBI | State | *McBratney* doctrine |
| Crime on fee land within reservation | Varies by state | Varies | *Montana*, PL 280, state law |
| Crime in PL 280 state | State | State | Public Law 280 (1953) |
| Crime in urban area | Local police | Local/state DA | State/local criminal law |

### The *Oliphant* Gap

The Supreme Court's 1978 decision in *Oliphant v. Suquamish Indian Tribe* remains the single most significant legal barrier to addressing MMIP:

- Tribal courts cannot prosecute non-Indians for crimes committed on tribal land (with limited VAWA exceptions)
- Over 90% of sexual violence against Indigenous women is committed by non-Indian perpetrators (NIJ, 2016)
- Federal prosecutors decline more than half of Indian Country referrals, often citing insufficient evidence or resources
- The result: perpetrators of violence against Indigenous people face prosecution at far lower rates than those who commit identical crimes elsewhere in the country

### VAWA 2013 and 2022 Expansions

The Violence Against Women Act reauthorizations partially addressed the *Oliphant* gap:

**VAWA 2013:**

- Authorized Special Domestic Violence Criminal Jurisdiction (SDVCJ) for tribes
- Limited to domestic violence, dating violence, and violations of protective orders involving non-Indians with sufficient ties to the tribe
- Required tribes to provide rights protections: effective counsel, impartial jury pool, qualified judge

**VAWA 2022 Expansion:**

- Extended SDVCJ to cover child violence, stalking, sex trafficking, and assault of tribal law enforcement
- As of 2025, approximately 45 tribes have implemented SDVCJ (National Congress of American Indians, 2025)
- Many tribes lack the resources to implement the required procedural protections

## Federal Response

### Legislative Framework

| Law | Year | Key Provisions | Implementation Status |
|-----|------|---------------|----------------------|
| Savanna's Act | 2020 | Requires DOJ to develop MMIP guidelines; mandates data collection; improves tribal access to federal databases | Guidelines issued 2022; implementation ongoing |
| Not Invisible Act | 2020 | Established joint commission on reducing violent crime against Indigenous people | Commission appointed 2022; initial report issued 2023 |
| VAWA 2022 | 2022 | Expanded tribal SDVCJ to additional crime categories | 45 tribes implementing as of 2025 |
| Tribal Law and Order Act | 2010 | Enhanced sentencing authority for tribal courts (up to 3 years); required federal accountability | Partially implemented; many tribes lack resources for enhanced sentencing |

### BIA Missing and Murdered Unit (MMU)

Established in 2019, the MMU operates within the Bureau of Indian Affairs:

- **Staffing**: Approximately 14 coordinators covering all 574 tribes (as of 2024) (BIA, 2024)
- **Cases reviewed**: Over 1,500 cases examined through 2024 (BIA MMU, 2024)
- **Community engagement**: Conducted outreach in 120+ tribal communities (BIA MMU, 2024)
- **Limitations**: Severely understaffed relative to caseload; no independent investigative authority; relies on FBI and BIA law enforcement for investigation

### Operation Not Forgotten

The U.S. Marshals Service's initiative to locate missing Indigenous children:

- Launched 2020 in partnership with the National Center for Missing and Exploited Children
- Has recovered or located over 225 missing children through 2024 (USMS, 2024)
- Focused primarily on children in foster care and runaways, not abduction cases

### Tribal Community Response Plans (TCRPs)

The DOJ's Office on Violence Against Women funds development of TCRPs:

- Over 80 tribes have developed TCRPs (DOJ OVW, 2024)
- Plans establish protocols for responding to reports of missing or murdered Indigenous persons
- Include coordination with federal, state, and local agencies
- Limitations: funding is inconsistent and plans vary widely in scope and implementation

### Tribal Initiatives

Tribal governments and Indigenous-led organizations have pioneered many of the most effective responses:

- **Sovereign Bodies Institute**: Maintains the most comprehensive MMIP database, documenting over 4,800 cases (Sovereign Bodies Institute, 2024)
- **National Indigenous Women's Resource Center**: Provides technical assistance, training, and advocacy
- **StrongHearts Native Helpline**: Operates 24/7 domestic violence and dating violence helpline for Indigenous people (1-844-762-8483)
- **Tribal community safety plans**: Many tribes have developed their own missing persons protocols where federal systems have failed
- **Amber Alert in Indian Country (AAIC)**: Implemented by 82 tribes as of 2024 (DOJ, 2024)

## Data Systems and Gaps

### Current Data Infrastructure

| System | Coverage | MMIP Utility | Limitations |
|--------|----------|-------------|-------------|
| NamUs (National Missing and Unidentified Persons System) | National | Primary federal database for missing persons | Voluntary reporting; significant undercount of Indigenous cases |
| FBI NCIC (National Crime Information Center) | National | Missing persons and wanted persons | Requires law enforcement entry; tribes lack direct access in many cases |
| FBI UCR/NIBRS | National | Crime statistics | Many tribal law enforcement agencies do not report; Indigenous identity often miscoded |
| Sovereign Bodies Institute database | National (non-governmental) | Most comprehensive MMIP-specific data | Relies on media reports and community submissions; limited resources |
| State databases | Varies by state | Varies | Rarely disaggregate by Indigenous identity |
| Tribal records | Individual tribes | Varies | Lack interoperability; inconsistent formats |

### Racial Misclassification

A persistent problem undermining MMIP data:

- Indigenous victims are frequently misclassified as Hispanic, white, or "other" in death records, missing persons reports, and law enforcement databases (Sovereign Bodies Institute, 2023)
- The National Death Index has documented systematic misclassification of Indigenous decedents (CDC/NCHS, 2022)
- Misclassification rates for Indigenous people in vital statistics range from 30-40% in some states (Jim et al., "Racial Misclassification of American Indians and Alaska Natives," *American Journal of Public Health* 104, no. S3, 2014)
- The result: the crisis is even larger than available data suggest

## Media Coverage Disparities

Research consistently documents that missing and murdered Indigenous people receive dramatically less media coverage than white victims:

- A 2021 study by the Annenberg School found that missing Indigenous women received significantly less coverage than missing white women of comparable circumstances
- The "Missing White Woman Syndrome" phenomenon is well-documented in media studies (Sommers, "Missing White Woman Syndrome," *Journal of Criminal Law and Criminology* 106, no. 2, 2016)
- Local media in areas with significant Indigenous populations provide somewhat better coverage, but national outlets rarely cover MMIP cases
- Social media campaigns (#MMIW, #MMIP, #NoMoreStolenSisters) have increased visibility but cannot substitute for systemic media reform

## The Human Cost

Behind the statistics are individuals, families, and communities:

- Families of missing and murdered Indigenous people frequently report being dismissed, ignored, or blamed by law enforcement when reporting loved ones missing
- Many families conduct their own searches because of slow or absent official response
- Generational trauma compounds as families spend years seeking answers
- Community-level impacts include collective grief, fear, and distrust of law enforcement institutions
- Economic impacts include lost income, funeral costs, and ongoing advocacy expenses borne by families already experiencing poverty
