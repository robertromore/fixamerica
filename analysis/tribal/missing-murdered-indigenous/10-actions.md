# Missing and Murdered Indigenous People: Actions

## Overview

This document outlines concrete actions that individuals, organizations, and communities can take to help address the MMIP crisis. Actions are organized by the type of actor and the level of effort required.

---

## Actions for Individual Citizens

### Learn and Educate

- **Educate yourself** about the MMIP crisis by reading the resources in [09-resources.md](09-resources.md), particularly the NIJ 2016 report and Sovereign Bodies Institute data
- **Learn the history** of federal Indian policy and how it created the conditions for the current crisis (see [03-history.md](03-history.md))
- **Understand jurisdiction**: Learn why *Oliphant v. Suquamish Indian Tribe* (1978) is central to the crisis and why tribal jurisdiction restoration is the most important structural reform
- **Share information** about MMIP with your networks, using accurate data and centering Indigenous voices
- **Attend events**: Participate in Red Dress Day (May 5), National Day of Awareness for MMIWG, and local MMIP awareness events
- **Read Indigenous media**: Follow Indian Country Today, Native News Online, and Indigenous journalists who cover MMIP

### Advocacy and Political Action

- **Contact your members of Congress**: Urge support for:
    - Legislation restoring full tribal criminal jurisdiction (overturning *Oliphant*)
    - Increased appropriations for BIA law enforcement, the Missing and Murdered Unit, and tribal victim services
    - Full implementation and funding of Savanna's Act and the Not Invisible Act
    - Regulation of man camps near tribal lands
- **Vote for candidates** who support tribal sovereignty and MMIP reform
- **Submit public comments** on federal rulemakings affecting tribal jurisdiction, law enforcement, or extractive industries
- **Support state MMIP task forces**: If your state has one, follow its work and advocate for implementation of its recommendations
- **If you serve on a jury**: Be aware that cases involving violence against Indigenous people may come before state and federal courts; take them as seriously as any other case

### Direct Support

- **Donate to Indigenous-led organizations** working on MMIP:
    - Sovereign Bodies Institute (sovereign-bodies.org)
    - National Indigenous Women's Resource Center (niwrc.org)
    - Coalition to Stop Violence Against Native Women (csvanw.org)
    - StrongHearts Native Helpline (strongheartshelpline.org)
    - Native American Rights Fund (narf.org)
- **Volunteer** with local MMIP advocacy organizations or victim services programs
- **Support MMIP families**: Attend vigils, share missing persons flyers, and contribute to family-led search efforts when invited
- **Offer professional skills**: Lawyers, social workers, counselors, and IT professionals can provide pro bono services to tribal communities and MMIP advocacy organizations

## Actions for Media Professionals

- **Cover MMIP cases** with the same urgency and depth as cases involving non-Indigenous victims
- **Avoid stereotypes and victim-blaming**: Do not characterize Indigenous victims through the lens of substance use, homelessness, or criminal history unless directly relevant to the case
- **Use correct terminology**: "Missing and murdered Indigenous people" (MMIP) or "missing and murdered Indigenous women and girls" (MMIWG); avoid reducing individuals to statistics
- **Center family voices**: Seek comment from families and Indigenous-led organizations, not only law enforcement
- **Provide context**: Include information about jurisdictional gaps, underfunding, and historical policies when covering individual cases
- **Follow up**: Continue covering cases beyond the initial report; track investigations and outcomes
- **Hire Indigenous journalists**: Actively recruit Indigenous reporters, editors, and producers
- **Partner with Indigenous media**: Collaborate with Indian Country Today, Native News Online, and tribal media outlets

---

## Actions for Law Enforcement

- **Take every report seriously**: Treat missing Indigenous persons reports with the same urgency as any other missing persons case. There is no waiting period.
- **Enter cases into NCIC immediately**: Do not delay database entry
- **Coordinate across jurisdictions**: Establish working relationships with tribal, federal, and state counterparts before cases arise
- **Implement Savanna's Act guidelines**: Follow DOJ guidelines for responding to MMIP cases
- **Cultural competency training**: Participate in training on working with Indigenous communities and understanding cultural context
- **Community engagement**: Build trust with Indigenous communities through regular, non-enforcement interactions
- **Report to NamUs**: Enter all missing and unidentified persons cases into the National Missing and Unidentified Persons System
- **Properly identify race**: Record Indigenous identity accurately in all databases and reports; ask rather than assume

---

## Actions for Legal Professionals

- **Pro bono representation**: Represent MMIP families in seeking information about cases, filing victim impact statements, and navigating the criminal justice system
- **Support tribal court development**: Provide technical assistance, training, and resources to tribal court systems
- **Advocate for jurisdiction reform**: File amicus briefs supporting tribal jurisdiction; publish scholarship on *Oliphant* reform
- **Cross-designation**: Tribal prosecutors should seek Special Assistant U.S. Attorney (SAUSA) designation to handle Indian Country cases in federal court
- **Public interest litigation**: File civil rights complaints and lawsuits challenging inadequate federal investigation and prosecution of Indian Country cases
- **Legislative drafting**: Assist tribal governments and advocacy organizations in drafting legislation for tribal jurisdiction restoration and related reforms

---

## Actions for Educators

- **Incorporate MMIP into curricula**: Teach about the MMIP crisis in social studies, civics, criminal justice, and Native American studies courses, using age-appropriate materials
- **Teach accurate history**: Include the history of federal Indian policy, boarding schools, and their connection to current crises
- **Invite Indigenous speakers**: Host presentations by Indigenous advocates, scholars, and community members
- **Support Indigenous students**: Be aware that Indigenous students may have personal connections to MMIP; provide culturally responsive support
- **Research**: Conduct and support community-based participatory research on MMIP in partnership with tribal communities

---

## Actions for Healthcare Professionals

- **Screen for violence**: Routinely screen Indigenous patients for domestic violence, sexual assault, and trafficking using validated, culturally appropriate tools
- **Mandatory reporting**: Comply with mandatory reporting laws while being sensitive to the complexities of reporting in Indian Country, including jurisdictional issues
- **Cultural competency**: Participate in training on providing trauma-informed care to Indigenous patients, including understanding historical trauma
- **Document injuries**: Thorough documentation of injuries can be critical evidence if a case is prosecuted
- **Connect to services**: Know and share resources including StrongHearts Native Helpline (1-844-762-8483) and local victim services
- **Support IHS reform**: Advocate for increased funding and improved services at Indian Health Service facilities

---

## Actions for Faith Communities

- **Acknowledge history**: Recognize the role that religious institutions played in boarding schools and other policies that harmed Indigenous communities
- **Support healing**: Fund and participate in healing programs for boarding school survivors and their descendants
- **Host awareness events**: Use your platforms to raise awareness about MMIP
- **Provide sanctuary**: Offer space for MMIP family support groups, vigils, and community gatherings
- **Advocate**: Include MMIP in your social justice advocacy agenda; contact legislators on behalf of reform

---

## Actions for Businesses

- **Extractive industries**: Implement background checks, security measures, and substance use policies for temporary worker housing; fund tribal law enforcement and victim services as part of community benefit agreements
- **All employers near tribal lands**: Develop workplace policies addressing violence, harassment, and trafficking awareness
- **Corporate philanthropy**: Direct charitable giving to Indigenous-led MMIP organizations
- **Media companies**: Implement editorial policies that ensure equitable coverage of missing and murdered Indigenous people
- **Technology companies**: Partner with tribes and advocacy organizations to develop and deploy MMIP alert systems, data tools, and communication infrastructure

---

## Actions for State and Local Government

- **Establish MMIP task forces**: If your state has not created an MMIP task force, advocate for one with bipartisan support
- **Implement task force recommendations**: If your state has issued MMIP recommendations, push for legislative and executive implementation
- **Improve data collection**: Require proper racial identification in law enforcement and vital statistics records
- **Cross-commission agreements**: Enter into intergovernmental agreements with tribes for law enforcement cooperation
- **Fund victim services**: Allocate state funds for victim services accessible to Indigenous residents
- **MMIP liaisons**: Designate MMIP liaisons in state law enforcement agencies
