# Missing and Murdered Indigenous People: Stakeholders

## Overview

The MMIP crisis involves a wide range of stakeholders, from the families and communities directly affected to the governmental institutions responsible for prevention, investigation, and prosecution. Understanding who has power, who is affected, and who influences policy is essential to designing effective solutions.

---

## Primary Stakeholders

### Indigenous Victims and Survivors

**Who they are**: Indigenous women, girls, men, boys, Two-Spirit, and LGBTQ+ individuals who have been or are at risk of being victimized by violence, abduction, or trafficking.

**Interests and needs**:

- Immediate physical safety and access to shelters and protective services
- Law enforcement that responds promptly, takes reports seriously, and investigates thoroughly
- Access to culturally appropriate victim services, counseling, and legal aid
- Justice and accountability for perpetrators
- Not being blamed, dismissed, or stereotyped by institutions meant to protect them

**Power**: Minimal institutional power. Survivors carry moral authority but are often marginalized in policy discussions.

---

### Families of Missing and Murdered Indigenous People

**Who they are**: Parents, siblings, children, extended family, and chosen family members of MMIP victims.

**Interests and needs**:

- Answers about what happened to their loved ones
- Thorough investigation of cases, including cold cases
- Regular communication from law enforcement about case status
- Support services including grief counseling, financial assistance, and legal aid
- A voice in policy design and implementation
- Dignity and respect in interactions with government agencies

**Power**: Families have driven much of the MMIP movement through grassroots advocacy and media engagement. Organizations like the Sovereign Bodies Institute and the National Indigenous Women's Resource Center amplify family voices.

**Key organizations representing families**:

- Sovereign Bodies Institute
- Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women USA (MMIW USA)
- National Indigenous Women's Resource Center (NIWRC)
- Local and regional family-led advocacy groups

---

### Tribal Governments

**Who they are**: The 574 federally recognized tribal nations and their governing bodies, law enforcement agencies, and court systems.

**Interests and needs**:

- Restoration of criminal jurisdiction over all persons within tribal territories
- Adequate funding for tribal law enforcement, courts, and victim services
- Direct access to federal databases and investigative resources
- Respect for tribal sovereignty in the design of MMIP responses
- Data sovereignty -- control over how tribal citizens' information is collected and used

**Power**: Tribal governments have sovereign authority but face severe resource constraints. The National Congress of American Indians, tribal caucuses, and individual tribal leaders are influential in federal policy discussions.

**Key bodies**:

- National Congress of American Indians (NCAI)
- National American Indian Court Judges Association
- Tribal Law and Policy Institute
- Individual tribal councils and police departments

## Government Stakeholders

### Federal Agencies

| Agency | Role in MMIP | Key Interests |
|--------|-------------|---------------|
| Bureau of Indian Affairs (BIA) | Law enforcement, Missing and Murdered Unit, tribal services | Fulfilling trust responsibility; managing limited resources across 574 tribes |
| FBI | Lead federal investigative agency for Indian Country major crimes | Balancing Indian Country caseload with other priorities; maintaining national security focus |
| Department of Justice (DOJ) | Federal prosecution; Office on Violence Against Women (OVW) | Reducing declination rates; implementing Savanna's Act; distributing VAWA grants |
| U.S. Attorneys' Offices | Prosecution of federal Indian Country cases | Managing caseload across all federal crime categories |
| Bureau of Indian Education (BIE) | Schools serving Indigenous children, some of whom are at risk | Student safety; compliance with mandatory reporting |
| Indian Health Service (IHS) | Health care for Indigenous people, including emergency and behavioral health | Identifying and reporting victims; providing trauma-informed care |
| Department of the Interior | Oversees BIA; houses Missing and Murdered Unit | Coordinating MMIP response across Interior bureaus |
| U.S. Marshals Service | Operation Not Forgotten (missing children) | Locating missing Indigenous children |

### Congressional Stakeholders

| Committee/Caucus | Role | Key Members (as of 119th Congress) |
|-----------------|------|-----------------------------------|
| Senate Committee on Indian Affairs | Primary oversight of federal Indian policy | Bipartisan committee; jurisdiction over MMIP legislation |
| House Natural Resources Committee, Subcommittee for Indian and Insular Affairs | House counterpart for Indian affairs | Oversight of BIA and Interior programs |
| Congressional Native American Caucus | Bipartisan caucus advocating for Indigenous issues | Cross-party membership; sponsors MMIP-related legislation |

### State Governments

**Who they are**: Governors, legislatures, attorneys general, and law enforcement agencies in states with significant Indigenous populations.

**Interests**:

- Complying with state MMIP task force mandates
- Coordinating with tribal law enforcement
- Managing jurisdiction in PL 280 states
- Political responsiveness to Indigenous constituents and advocacy groups

**Tensions**: Some states resist expanded tribal jurisdiction (viewing it as erosion of state authority), while others have proactively partnered with tribes on MMIP responses.

## Industry Stakeholders

### Extractive Industries

**Who they are**: Oil, gas, mining, and pipeline companies operating on or near tribal lands.

**Interests**:

- Continued access to energy and mineral resources
- Minimizing regulatory burden and liability
- Avoiding association with violence and trafficking
- Maintaining workforce housing and operations

**Impact on MMIP**: Man camps associated with extractive operations are linked to increased violence, trafficking, and substance availability near tribal communities (NCAI, 2022).

**Power**: Substantial lobbying power; significant campaign contributions; economic leverage in resource-dependent states.

### Media Industry

**Who they are**: National and local news outlets, social media platforms, entertainment media.

**Role**:

- Decisions about which missing persons cases to cover directly affect public awareness and investigative resources
- "Missing White Woman Syndrome" describes the documented disparity in coverage (Sommers, 2016)
- Indigenous-led media outlets (Indian Country Today, Native News Online) provide more consistent coverage but have limited reach

## Power Analysis

### Who Has Power Over MMIP Policy

| Actor | Type of Power | How Used |
|-------|--------------|----------|
| Congress | Legislative; plenary power over Indian affairs | Can restore tribal jurisdiction, fund programs, or neglect the issue |
| Supreme Court | Judicial; interprets Constitution and federal law | *Oliphant* created the jurisdictional gap; could revisit |
| President | Executive orders, agency direction, appointments | Can prioritize MMIP within executive agencies |
| Federal prosecutors | Discretionary; decide which cases to pursue | Declination decisions directly affect accountability |
| State legislatures | Legislative; criminal jurisdiction in PL 280 states | Can establish MMIP task forces, allocate resources |
| Tribal governments | Sovereign authority within legal constraints | Can develop safety plans, implement VAWA jurisdiction |

### Who Lacks Power

| Group | Barriers to Power |
|-------|-------------------|
| MMIP families | Geographic isolation, poverty, institutional marginalization |
| Indigenous women and girls | Intersecting marginalization by race, gender, geography |
| Two-Spirit and LGBTQ+ Indigenous people | Additional marginalization within and outside Indigenous communities |
| Urban Indigenous populations | Outside tribal governance structures; dispersed populations |
| Tribal law enforcement | Jurisdictional limitations, underfunding, understaffing |
