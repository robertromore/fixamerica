# Missing and Murdered Indigenous People: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Addressing the MMIP crisis requires legislation that closes the jurisdictional gap created by *Oliphant v. Suquamish Indian Tribe* (1978), establishes comprehensive data systems, increases law enforcement and victim service resources, and regulates the extractive industry impacts that contribute to violence against Indigenous people. This document presents draft legislation at the federal and state levels, regulatory frameworks, and analysis of legal considerations and potential weaknesses.

---

## Constitutional Amendments

### Recognition of Tribal Criminal Jurisdiction Amendment

**Purpose**: Constitutionally entrench tribal criminal jurisdiction to prevent future Supreme Court decisions from further eroding tribal authority, as *Oliphant* and *Castro-Huerta* have done.

**Draft Text**:

```text
Section 1. The inherent sovereign authority of Indian tribal nations
to exercise criminal jurisdiction over all persons within their
territorial boundaries is hereby recognized and shall not be denied
or abridged by the United States or by any State.

Section 2. Congress shall have the power to enforce this article
by appropriate legislation, which shall include provisions ensuring
that all persons subject to tribal criminal jurisdiction receive
protections consistent with the rights guaranteed by this
Constitution.

Section 3. This article shall take effect two years after the date
of ratification.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 1 constitutionalizes inherent tribal criminal jurisdiction, reversing *Oliphant* at the highest legal level and preventing congressional or judicial reversal
- Section 2 requires Congress to enact implementing legislation with due process protections, addressing the primary concern of opponents
- Section 3 provides a two-year implementation window for tribal court capacity building

**Potential Challenges**:

- Constitutional amendments require two-thirds of both chambers and three-fourths of state legislatures -- an extremely high bar
- Political opposition from states that view tribal jurisdiction expansion as diminishing state authority
- Requires sustained national advocacy campaign

**Refinements**:

- If a constitutional amendment is not achievable, statutory legislation (below) provides the primary path
- The amendment serves as the long-term aspiration while legislation addresses the immediate crisis

## Federal Legislation

### Tribal Jurisdiction Restoration Act

**Purpose**: Legislatively overturn *Oliphant* by recognizing inherent tribal criminal jurisdiction over all persons within tribal territories.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Tribal Jurisdiction Restoration Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(1) Indian tribal nations possess inherent sovereign authority
    predating the Constitution of the United States;
(2) the Supreme Court's decision in Oliphant v. Suquamish Indian
    Tribe, 435 U.S. 191 (1978), erroneously concluded that tribal
    nations lack inherent criminal jurisdiction over non-Indians;
(3) the jurisdictional gap created by Oliphant has directly
    contributed to a crisis of violence against Indigenous people,
    including the epidemic of missing and murdered Indigenous women,
    girls, and Two-Spirit people;
(4) the successful implementation of Special Domestic Violence
    Criminal Jurisdiction under VAWA 2013 and 2022 by approximately
    45 tribal nations demonstrates that tribal courts can effectively
    exercise criminal jurisdiction over non-Indians while providing
    full due process protections; and
(5) restoration of tribal criminal jurisdiction is necessary to
    fulfill the federal trust responsibility and to ensure the safety
    of all persons within Indian Country.

SEC. 3. RECOGNITION OF INHERENT TRIBAL CRIMINAL JURISDICTION.

(a) IN GENERAL.--The inherent sovereign authority of a
participating tribe to exercise criminal jurisdiction over all
persons is hereby recognized and affirmed with respect to criminal
conduct that occurs within the Indian country of the participating
tribe.

(b) CONCURRENT JURISDICTION.--Nothing in this section shall be
construed to limit the concurrent jurisdiction of the United States
or any State over criminal conduct in Indian country.

SEC. 4. RIGHTS OF DEFENDANTS.

(a) IN GENERAL.--A participating tribe exercising jurisdiction
under this Act shall provide to any defendant--
(1) the right to effective assistance of counsel at least equal
    to that guaranteed by the United States Constitution;
(2) the right to a trial by an impartial jury drawn from a fair
    cross-section of the community, which shall include non-Indians
    if the defendant is a non-Indian;
(3) all other rights whose protection is necessary under the
    Constitution of the United States in order for Congress to
    recognize and affirm the inherent power of the participating
    tribe to exercise criminal jurisdiction over the defendant;
(4) the right to a presiding judge who is licensed to practice
    law and has sufficient legal training; and
(5) the right to a record of proceedings sufficient to allow
    for meaningful appellate review.

(b) HABEAS CORPUS.--A person subject to detention by a
participating tribe shall have the right to petition for a writ of
habeas corpus in a court of the United States.

SEC. 5. PARTICIPATING TRIBE.

(a) DEFINITION.--The term "participating tribe" means an Indian
tribe that--
(1) is federally recognized;
(2) has established a tribal court with criminal jurisdiction;
(3) has adopted criminal laws and procedures consistent with
    the requirements of section 4; and
(4) has been certified by the Attorney General as meeting the
    requirements of this Act.

(b) CERTIFICATION.--The Attorney General shall establish a
process for certifying tribes as participating tribes under this
Act. The Attorney General shall certify a tribe within 180 days
of receiving a complete application unless the tribe fails to meet
the requirements of subsection (a).

(c) PHASED IMPLEMENTATION.--
(1) During the first 3 years following enactment, participation
    shall be available to any tribe meeting the requirements of
    subsection (a).
(2) Beginning 3 years after enactment, the Attorney General
    shall actively assist all remaining tribes in meeting the
    requirements for participation.

SEC. 6. TRIBAL COURT CAPACITY BUILDING.

(a) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.--There is authorized to be
appropriated $250,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through
2036 for grants to Indian tribes for--
(1) development and enhancement of tribal court systems;
(2) training of tribal court judges, prosecutors, and defense
    attorneys;
(3) construction and renovation of tribal court and detention
    facilities;
(4) development of tribal criminal codes and procedures; and
(5) recruitment and retention of qualified legal personnel.

SEC. 7. TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE.

The Attorney General, in coordination with the Secretary of the
Interior, shall provide technical assistance to Indian tribes
seeking to implement jurisdiction under this Act.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 3 recognizes inherent tribal authority (congressional recognition, not delegation, ensuring sovereignty-based foundation)
- Section 4 provides robust due process protections modeled on VAWA SDVCJ requirements that have withstood legal challenge
- Section 5 creates a certification process that ensures tribes are prepared before exercising expanded jurisdiction
- Section 6 provides substantial capacity-building funding
- Concurrent federal jurisdiction is maintained as a safety net

**Potential Challenges**:

- Legislative resistance from members who view this as overriding Supreme Court precedent
- Due process concerns from the criminal defense bar
- Resource constraints for tribal court development

**Refinements**:

- If full jurisdiction restoration is not achievable, expand VAWA SDVCJ categories incrementally
- Consider pilot programs in willing jurisdictions before national implementation

### MMIP Data Modernization Act

**Purpose**: Create a comprehensive, interoperable national data system for tracking MMIP cases with tribal data sovereignty protections.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "MMIP Data Modernization Act".

SEC. 2. NATIONAL MMIP DATABASE.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--Not later than 2 years after the date of
enactment, the Attorney General, in consultation with the Secretary
of the Interior and tribal governments, shall establish a national
database for missing and murdered Indigenous people.

(b) REQUIREMENTS.--The database shall--
(1) be interoperable with NCIC, NamUs, NIBRS, and state law
    enforcement databases;
(2) include standardized fields for racial identification,
    tribal enrollment, last known location, case status, and
    investigating agency;
(3) be accessible to all tribal, federal, state, and local law
    enforcement agencies;
(4) include a public-facing dashboard with anonymized case
    data, trends, and outcomes; and
(5) incorporate existing unsolved MMIP cases.

(c) MANDATORY REPORTING.--All federal, state, and local law
enforcement agencies shall enter information regarding missing or
murdered Indigenous persons into the database within 72 hours of
receiving a report.

SEC. 3. TRIBAL DATA SOVEREIGNTY.

(a) TRIBAL OWNERSHIP.--Data relating to members of an Indian
tribe entered into the database shall remain the property of the
relevant tribe.
(b) GOVERNANCE.--The Attorney General shall establish, in
consultation with tribal governments, a data governance protocol
that ensures tribal consent for use of tribally identified data
in research, publication, and policy analysis.

SEC. 4. RACIAL CLASSIFICATION REFORM.

(a) Not later than 1 year after enactment, the Attorney General
shall issue guidance requiring all federal law enforcement agencies
to--
(1) record the self-identified race of all crime victims and
    missing persons;
(2) cross-reference tribal enrollment data where available; and
(3) prohibit classification of Indigenous persons as Hispanic,
    white, or other without verification.

SEC. 5. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There is authorized to be appropriated $50,000,000 for each of
fiscal years 2027 through 2031 for the establishment and operation
of the database under this Act.
```

**Explanation**:

- Creates the unified data system that is universally acknowledged as necessary but does not yet exist
- Mandatory reporting requirement addresses the voluntary reporting gap that causes undercounting
- Tribal data sovereignty provisions address legitimate concerns about outside control of tribal information
- Racial classification reform attacks the misidentification problem that undercounts MMIP cases by 30-40%

### Indian Country Public Safety Funding Act

**Purpose**: Convert BIA law enforcement and tribal victim services funding from discretionary to mandatory appropriations.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Indian Country Public Safety
Funding Act".

SEC. 2. MANDATORY APPROPRIATIONS.

(a) BIA LAW ENFORCEMENT.--There is appropriated, out of any money
in the Treasury not otherwise appropriated, for each fiscal year
beginning with fiscal year 2027--
(1) $800,000,000 for the Bureau of Indian Affairs Office of
    Justice Services for tribal law enforcement, adjusted annually
    for inflation; and
(2) $50,000,000 for the Bureau of Indian Affairs Missing and
    Murdered Unit, adjusted annually for inflation.

(b) TRIBAL VICTIM SERVICES.--There is appropriated, out of any
money in the Treasury not otherwise appropriated, for each fiscal
year beginning with fiscal year 2027, $300,000,000 for the
Department of Justice Office on Violence Against Women for tribal
victim services grants, adjusted annually for inflation.

(c) FORMULA DISTRIBUTION.--The Secretary of the Interior, in
consultation with tribal governments, shall distribute funds under
subsection (a) using a formula that accounts for--
(1) tribal population;
(2) geographic area of Indian country;
(3) remoteness and infrastructure challenges;
(4) violent crime rates; and
(5) existing tribal law enforcement capacity.

SEC. 3. TRIBAL SELF-GOVERNANCE.

Funds appropriated under this Act shall be available for
distribution through self-governance compacts under the Indian
Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act (25 U.S.C. 5301
et seq.).
```

## State Model Legislation

### Model State MMIP Response Act

**Purpose**: Establish state-level infrastructure for addressing MMIP, including task forces, data collection, and law enforcement coordination.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Missing and Murdered
Indigenous People Response Act".

SECTION 2. MMIP TASK FORCE.

(a) There is established within the [Governor's Office/Attorney
General's Office] a Missing and Murdered Indigenous People Task
Force.
(b) The Task Force shall consist of--
(1) representatives of tribal governments within the state;
(2) representatives of state law enforcement agencies;
(3) representatives of Indigenous-led advocacy organizations;
(4) family members of missing and murdered Indigenous persons;
(5) representatives of the state medical examiner's office; and
(6) such other members as the [Governor/Attorney General]
    determines appropriate.
(c) At least 50 percent of Task Force members shall be enrolled
members of federally recognized Indian tribes.

SECTION 3. MMIP LIAISON.

(a) The [state law enforcement agency] shall designate an MMIP
liaison responsible for--
(1) coordinating with tribal law enforcement on MMIP cases;
(2) ensuring proper racial identification in state databases;
(3) maintaining a state registry of MMIP cases; and
(4) providing annual reports to the legislature.

SECTION 4. DATA COLLECTION.

(a) All state and local law enforcement agencies shall--
(1) record the self-identified race and tribal enrollment
    of all missing and murdered persons;
(2) enter missing Indigenous persons into NCIC within 24 hours
    of receiving a report; and
(3) report MMIP data to the MMIP liaison quarterly.

SECTION 5. CROSS-COMMISSIONING.

State law enforcement agencies are authorized to enter into
cross-commissioning agreements with tribal law enforcement
agencies for the purpose of mutual enforcement of criminal laws
within overlapping jurisdictional areas.

SECTION 6. APPROPRIATION.

There is appropriated from the general fund the sum of $[amount]
for fiscal year [year] for the implementation of this Act.
```

## Regulatory Framework

### DOJ MMIP Investigation Guidelines

**Existing Authority**: Savanna's Act (P.L. 116-165) requires the Attorney General to develop guidelines for MMIP response.

**Draft Regulation**:

- Require all FBI field offices with tribal land in their territory to designate at least one agent as Indian Country MMIP specialist
- Establish maximum response time standards: initial response within 4 hours of report; investigative response within 24 hours
- Require quarterly review of MMIP case status by each U.S. Attorney's office with Indian Country jurisdiction
- Mandate declination consultation with tribal prosecutors before declining any MMIP-related case
- Require cultural competency training for all federal personnel handling Indian Country cases

### BIA Man Camp Safety Regulations

**Existing Authority**: BIA authority over activities on trust land; DOI authority over federal land permits.

**Draft Regulation**:

- Require criminal background checks for all workers in temporary housing within 25 miles of tribal land
- Mandate on-site security personnel and lighting standards for man camps
- Require reporting of all criminal incidents to tribal and federal law enforcement within 24 hours
- Establish maximum occupancy and minimum safety standards for temporary worker housing
- Require companies to fund community impact assessments before beginning operations

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Congress's power to restore tribal jurisdiction**: Congress's plenary power over Indian affairs (Art. I, Sec. 8, Cl. 3) provides clear authority to legislatively overturn *Oliphant*, as it did partially in VAWA 2013
- **Due process**: The Supreme Court has not ruled on whether VAWA SDVCJ provisions satisfy due process; robust protections in the Tribal Jurisdiction Restoration Act are designed to preempt such challenges
- **Equal protection**: Opponents may argue tribal jurisdiction creates unequal treatment based on race; *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023) affirmed that tribal membership is a political, not racial, classification

### Preemption Questions

- **Federal-state-tribal**: Concurrent jurisdiction framework avoids preemption conflicts; federal and state jurisdiction is preserved alongside tribal authority
- **State MMIP laws**: State legislation supplements but does not replace federal authority or tribal sovereignty

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- Mandatory funding provisions reduce dependence on annual appropriations battles
- DOJ oversight of declination rates creates accountability for federal prosecution
- Tribal data sovereignty provisions ensure enforcement serves tribal interests

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Five-year review of Tribal Jurisdiction Restoration Act implementation by GAO
- Annual reporting requirements for all federal agencies with MMIP responsibilities
- Not Invisible Act Commission to conduct ongoing monitoring

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Tribal Jurisdiction Restoration Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Certification bottleneck | Attorney General could delay or deny certification to limit tribal participation | High |
| Capacity requirements | Smaller tribes may not meet due process requirements without substantial assistance | High |
| Fee land exclusion | Jurisdiction may be contested on fee land within reservation boundaries | Medium |
| Non-reservation Indians | Tribes whose members live off-reservation remain unprotected by tribal jurisdiction | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Voluntary participation | Not all tribes may participate, leaving gaps in coverage | Resource constraints; political choice |
| Federal concurrent jurisdiction | Federal declination problem persists even with tribal jurisdiction | Separate institutional problem |
| Urban Indigenous populations | Bill does not address violence against urban Indigenous people | Jurisdictional limitation of tribal authority |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Require the Attorney General to certify tribes within 180 days of complete application, with judicial review for denials
2. Provide enhanced technical assistance and funding for smaller tribes to meet certification requirements
3. Clarify that jurisdiction extends to all land within reservation boundaries, including fee land, consistent with *McGirt* reasoning
4. Pair with urban Indigenous safety programs funded through DOJ

### MMIP Data Modernization Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Reporting evasion | Agencies may technically comply by entering minimal information | Medium |
| Tribal opt-out | Data sovereignty provisions could be used to withhold data from the unified system | Medium |
| No enforcement mechanism | No penalty for agencies that fail to report within 72 hours | High |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Historical data gaps | System cannot retroactively fix decades of missing data | Systemic historical failure |
| Interoperability challenges | Different systems use incompatible data formats | Legacy technology |
| Ongoing misclassification | Even with guidance, misclassification may persist at local levels | Training and compliance gaps |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Establish minimum data quality standards with mandatory compliance reviews
2. Work with tribal governments to develop data sharing protocols that protect sovereignty while enabling system-wide analysis
3. Include enforcement provisions: agencies that fail to report face loss of federal law enforcement grants
4. Fund dedicated data entry and quality assurance positions

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Political vulnerability | Mandatory funding reduces dependence on annual appropriations |
| Supreme Court reversal risk | Constitutional amendment (long-term); legislative findings addressing constitutional basis (near-term) |
| Bureaucratic inertia | Dedicated MMIP coordination body with cross-agency authority |
| Tribal capacity variations | Phased implementation with targeted technical assistance |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Five-year GAO review of all legislation enacted under this framework
- Annual reporting to Congress on MMIP case outcomes, funding distribution, and tribal jurisdiction implementation
- Not Invisible Act Commission to serve as standing advisory body
