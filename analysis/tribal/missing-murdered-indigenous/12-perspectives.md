# Missing and Murdered Indigenous People: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes the MMIP crisis through nine distinct political perspectives, evaluating current state assessments, root cause analyses, proposed solutions, and legislation. Each perspective receives engagement consistency ratings and position scores, culminating in compromise proposals that bridge multiple viewpoints. The MMIP issue is notable for generating broader bipartisan sympathy than many tribal policy areas, though significant disagreements persist on structural solutions -- particularly tribal jurisdiction restoration.

## Perspective Definitions

| Perspective | Core Values | Policy Orientation |
|-------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Tradition, limited government, individual responsibility | Incremental change, fiscal restraint, market solutions |
| Liberal | Progress, equality, social responsibility | Government as problem-solver, regulation, safety nets |
| Progressive | Systemic change, economic justice, structural reform | Transformative reform, wealth redistribution, worker power |
| Libertarian | Individual liberty, minimal state, free markets | Deregulation, privatization, voluntary association |
| Constitutionalist | Original intent, enumerated powers, federalism | Strict constitutional limits, states' rights |
| Populist | Anti-elite, pro-worker, economic nationalism | Trade protection, anti-monopoly, immigration restriction |
| Centrist | Pragmatism, compromise, incremental progress | Bipartisan solutions, evidence-based policy |
| Religious Right | Faith-based values, traditional morality | Social conservatism, religious liberty |
| Democratic Socialist | Worker ownership, public goods, democratic control | Public investment, labor rights, universal programs |

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 3/5 - Mixed Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | *Genuine concern for public safety and law-and-order, but resistance to tribal sovereignty expansion reflects broader federalism priorities* |
| Principle consistency | *Supports law enforcement funding increase but opposes tribal jurisdiction expansion that would logically improve law enforcement outcomes* |
| Goalpost stability | *Some shifting: supported Savanna's Act but resists structural jurisdiction reform that would address the same problem more directly* |
| Zero-sum behavior | *Tends to view tribal jurisdiction as coming at the expense of state authority* |

**Justification:** Conservatives genuinely support addressing violence against Indigenous people through increased law enforcement but are conflicted on tribal jurisdiction restoration because it conflicts with their preference for state authority and skepticism of expanding governmental power for non-state entities.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- *Bipartisan support for Savanna's Act (2020) and Not Invisible Act (2020) demonstrates genuine concern*
- *Heritage Foundation has expressed reservations about expanding tribal criminal jurisdiction beyond VAWA (2023)*
- *Conservative senators have supported MMIP law enforcement funding while opposing broader sovereignty measures*

## Compromise Proposals

### Compromise 1: *Bipartisan MMIP Safety Package*

**Bridges:** *Conservative + Liberal + Centrist + Populist + Religious Right*

**Core Agreement:** *Protecting Indigenous people from violence is a shared moral and practical imperative that transcends ideological boundaries.*

**Policy Description:**

A legislative package combining: (1) the MMIP Data Modernization Act; (2) dedicated federal law enforcement funding for Indian Country at $500 million/year; (3) tribal victim services expansion at $200 million/year; and (4) phased expansion of VAWA SDVCJ to cover all violent crimes (not full Oliphant reversal).

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Conservative | *Funds law enforcement, not sovereignty expansion; data-driven approach* |
| Liberal | *Significant new investment in victim services and law enforcement; progress on jurisdiction* |
| Centrist | *Evidence-based, bipartisan, builds on proven VAWA model* |
| Populist | *More cops, better data, government accountability* |
| Religious Right | *Protects families; includes faith-based service provision* |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | *Accepting increased federal spending and incremental jurisdiction expansion* |
| Liberal | *Accepting phased jurisdiction expansion rather than full Oliphant reversal* |
| Centrist | *Accepting mandatory rather than discretionary funding* |
| Populist | *Accepting tribal-specific (rather than universal) law enforcement funding* |
| Religious Right | *Accepting government-funded (not exclusively faith-based) services* |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 5/9 perspectives
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 2/5 coalition members rated 4+ consistency
- **Durability:** *Moderate; law enforcement and data provisions are relatively durable across political cycles*
- **Implementation Path:** *Bipartisan bill modeled on Savanna's Act coalition; appropriations through Interior and DOJ*

**Bridges:** *Liberal + Progressive + Libertarian + Democratic Socialist + Centrist*

**Core Agreement:** *Tribal self-governance is both a right and the most effective means of protecting Indigenous communities.*

**Policy Description:**

Full legislative reversal of *Oliphant* through the Tribal Jurisdiction Restoration Act, with robust due process protections, phased implementation, and substantial capacity-building funding. Combined with tribal data sovereignty protections and mandatory federal funding.

### Compromise 3: *MMIP Prevention and Accountability Act*

**Bridges:** *Liberal + Conservative + Centrist + Progressive + Religious Right + Populist*

**Core Agreement:** *Federal agencies must be held accountable for their failure to investigate and prosecute crimes against Indigenous people.*

**Policy Description:**

Federal prosecution reform requiring: (1) dedicated Indian Country AUSAs in every relevant district; (2) public reporting of declination rates and reasons; (3) tribal consultation before declination; (4) cross-designation of tribal prosecutors as SAUSAs; (5) cold case review program; and (6) mandatory cultural competency training.

**Concern Mapping:**

|-------------|-----------------------------------|

| Liberal | *Addresses prosecution failures that enable impunity* |
| Centrist | *Practical, evidence-based reform with bipartisan appeal* |
| Progressive | *Addresses immediate prosecution gap while broader reforms are pursued* |
| Religious Right | *Holds government accountable for protecting vulnerable populations* |
| Populist | *Anti-elite accountability; government doing its job* |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | *Accepting oversight of federal prosecutors' discretion* |
| Liberal | *Accepting that this does not address the underlying jurisdictional problem* |
| Progressive | *Accepting incremental federal reform rather than structural change* |
| Populist | *Accepting that reform targets federal, not local, institutions* |
| Religious Right | *Accepting government-mandated training programs* |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **High Consistency Ratio:** 3/6 coalition members rated 4+ consistency

- **Implementation Path:** *Most politically feasible; could pass as standalone bill or part of DOJ authorization*

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The broadest viable coalition combines law enforcement funding, data reform, and federal prosecution accountability (Compromise 1 + Compromise 3). This coalition spans 7/9 perspectives and avoids the most contentious issue (tribal jurisdiction expansion) while making meaningful progress.

### Key Obstacles

- Tribal jurisdiction restoration -- the most impactful structural reform -- faces opposition from 4/9 perspectives, requiring either a narrower coalition strategy or reframing that addresses concerns about state authority and due process
- Extractive industry regulation faces the strongest structural opposition (5/9 perspectives opposed), likely requiring regulation through executive action rather than legislation
- Mandatory funding provisions face fiscal conservative opposition that crosses multiple perspectives

### Low Consistency Partners

- **Populist (2/5)**: Engage through law enforcement and government accountability framing rather than sovereignty or racial justice frameworks
- **Conservative, Libertarian, Constitutionalist, Religious Right (all 3/5)**: These perspectives have internal divisions that create opportunities; engage principled factions (e.g., Gorsuch-aligned constitutionalists on treaty obligations, libertarians on tribal self-governance)

### Recommended Approach

1. **Immediate priority**: Pursue Compromise 3 (MMIP Prevention and Accountability Act) as the broadest coalition opportunity with the least political resistance
2. **Parallel track**: Pursue Compromise 1 (Bipartisan MMIP Safety Package) through appropriations and VAWA reauthorization
3. **Long-term strategy**: Build the evidentiary case for full jurisdiction restoration (Compromise 2) by documenting VAWA SDVCJ success and prosecution reform outcomes
4. **Executive action**: Pursue extractive industry regulation through BIA and DOI administrative authority where legislative action is blocked
