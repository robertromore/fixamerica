# Missing and Murdered Indigenous People: Resources

## Overview

This document compiles key resources for understanding, researching, and acting on the MMIP crisis. Resources are organized by type to serve researchers, policymakers, advocates, journalists, and affected families.

## Legal Resources

### Key Court Cases

| Case | Citation | Significance |
|------|----------|-------------|
| *Oliphant v. Suquamish Indian Tribe* | 435 U.S. 191 (1978) | Stripped tribal courts of criminal jurisdiction over non-Indians |
| *Montana v. United States* | 450 U.S. 544 (1981) | Limited tribal civil jurisdiction over non-Indians on fee land |
| *Oklahoma v. Castro-Huerta* | 597 U.S. ___ (2022) | Held states have concurrent jurisdiction over non-Indians in Indian Country |
| *Haaland v. Brackeen* | 599 U.S. 255 (2023) | Upheld ICWA and congressional authority under Indian Commerce Clause |
| *McGirt v. Oklahoma* | 591 U.S. ___ (2020) | Affirmed Muscogee reservation boundaries; significant for jurisdictional analysis |
| *United States v. Bryant* | 579 U.S. 140 (2016) | Upheld use of tribal court convictions as predicate offenses for federal domestic violence prosecution |
| *Ex parte Crow Dog* | 109 U.S. 556 (1883) | Recognized tribal jurisdiction over intra-Indian crime; led to Major Crimes Act |

### Key Statutes

| Statute | Citation | Relevance |
|---------|----------|-----------|
| Major Crimes Act | 18 U.S.C. 1153 | Federal jurisdiction over major crimes by Indians in Indian Country |
| General Crimes Act | 18 U.S.C. 1152 | Federal jurisdiction over interracial crimes in Indian Country |
| Indian Civil Rights Act | 25 U.S.C. 1301-1304 | Constitutional protections in tribal courts; VAWA SDVCJ authority |
| Violence Against Women Act (2013, 2022) | 34 U.S.C. 12291 et seq. | Tribal SDVCJ; victim services funding |
| Savanna's Act | P.L. 116-165 (2020) | MMIP response guidelines; data collection requirements |
| Not Invisible Act | P.L. 116-166 (2020) | Joint commission on violence against Indigenous people |
| Tribal Law and Order Act | P.L. 111-211 (2010) | Enhanced tribal sentencing; federal accountability |

## Reporting a Missing Person

### Steps for Families

1. **Report immediately**: There is no waiting period to report a missing person. Contact local law enforcement and insist on filing a report.
2. **Request NCIC entry**: Ask that the missing person be entered into the National Crime Information Center database immediately.
3. **Contact NamUs**: Submit a case to the National Missing and Unidentified Persons System at namus.nij.ojp.gov.
4. **Contact the BIA Missing and Murdered Unit**: 1-833-560-2065.
5. **Contact tribal law enforcement**: If the person is a tribal member or was last seen on or near tribal land.
6. **Contact the StrongHearts Native Helpline**: 1-844-762-8483 for support and referrals.
7. **Document everything**: Gather recent photos, physical descriptions, medical information, and details about last known location and contacts.
8. **Reach out to advocacy organizations**: Sovereign Bodies Institute, NIWRC, and local MMIP advocacy groups can provide guidance and support.
