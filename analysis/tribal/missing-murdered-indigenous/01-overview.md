# Missing and Murdered Indigenous People: Overview

## Executive Summary

The crisis of missing and murdered Indigenous people (MMIP) represents one of the most severe and persistent human rights failures in the United States. Indigenous women, girls, and Two-Spirit individuals face rates of violence vastly disproportionate to any other demographic group. The National Institute of Justice reports that more than 84% of American Indian and Alaska Native women experience violence in their lifetime, and murder is the third-leading cause of death for Indigenous women aged 10-24 (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, 2022). Despite the severity of this crisis, systemic failures in data collection, jurisdictional authority, law enforcement response, and media coverage have rendered many of these cases invisible.

The MMIP crisis is not a new phenomenon. It is rooted in centuries of colonization, forced removal, and federal policies that systematically undermined tribal sovereignty, fractured Indigenous communities, and created the jurisdictional gaps that allow violence against Indigenous people to go uninvestigated and unprosecuted. The convergence of poverty, geographic isolation, inadequate law enforcement resources, and the *Oliphant v. Suquamish Indian Tribe* (1978) decision -- which stripped tribal courts of criminal jurisdiction over non-Indians -- has created an environment where perpetrators operate with near impunity.

Federal attention to the crisis has increased since the late 2010s, with the passage of Savanna's Act (2020), the Not Invisible Act (2020), and the establishment of the federal Missing and Murdered Unit within the Bureau of Indian Affairs. However, these measures remain inadequately funded and insufficiently implemented. Meaningful progress requires restoring tribal jurisdiction, establishing comprehensive data systems, increasing law enforcement resources in Indian Country, addressing root causes of vulnerability, and centering Indigenous communities in the design and implementation of solutions.

## Scope

### What This Subtopic Covers

- **Scope and scale**: Data on missing and murdered Indigenous people across the United States
- **Jurisdictional failures**: How fragmented authority between tribal, federal, and state systems enables impunity
- **Law enforcement gaps**: Inadequate investigation, response times, and resources in Indian Country
- **Data deficiencies**: Absence of comprehensive national databases and inconsistent reporting
- **Victim demographics**: Disproportionate impact on women, girls, Two-Spirit, and LGBTQ+ Indigenous people

## Core Tensions

- **Tribal sovereignty vs. jurisdictional gaps**: Tribal nations have inherent authority to protect their citizens, yet federal law strips their jurisdiction over non-Indian perpetrators who commit the majority of violence against Indigenous people
- **Federal responsibility vs. chronic underfunding**: The federal government bears treaty-based and trust obligations for public safety in Indian Country but funds law enforcement at a fraction of comparable non-tribal communities
- **Data collection vs. sovereignty**: Comprehensive data systems are needed to understand the crisis, but data sovereignty concerns require that tribes control how their citizens' information is collected, stored, and used
- **Immediate safety vs. systemic change**: Families need immediate investigative resources, while long-term solutions require fundamental restructuring of jurisdiction, funding, and authority
- **Urban vs. reservation focus**: The majority of Indigenous people live in urban areas, but most federal MMIP efforts focus on reservation communities

## Key Questions

1. **Why are Indigenous people disproportionately victimized?** What structural, historical, and socioeconomic factors create vulnerability?

2. **How do jurisdictional gaps enable violence?** Why can't tribal governments prosecute non-Indian offenders, and what are the consequences?

3. **Why are so many cases unsolved?** What systemic failures in law enforcement investigation and prosecution contribute to impunity?

4. **Is the current data adequate?** Why are MMIP cases systematically undercounted, and what would a comprehensive system look like?

5. **What have tribal communities done independently?** How have grassroots organizations and tribal governments responded where federal systems have failed?

6. **Are recent federal laws working?** Have Savanna's Act, the Not Invisible Act, and the BIA Missing and Murdered Unit produced measurable improvements?

7. **What would an effective response look like?** What combination of jurisdictional reform, funding, data systems, and community engagement would meaningfully reduce MMIP cases?

## Vision of Success

An effective response to the MMIP crisis would feature:

- Tribal nations with full criminal jurisdiction over all persons within their territories, ending the impunity created by *Oliphant*
- A comprehensive, interoperable data system that accurately tracks every missing and murdered Indigenous person, with tribal data sovereignty protections
- Law enforcement staffing in Indian Country at levels comparable to non-tribal communities of similar size and geography
- Federal prosecutors who prioritize Indian Country cases with dedicated resources and culturally competent staff
- Rapid response protocols that treat missing Indigenous persons cases with the same urgency as any other missing persons case
- Victim services -- including shelters, counseling, and legal aid -- accessible to Indigenous people in both reservation and urban settings
- Media coverage that reflects the scale of the crisis and treats Indigenous victims with the dignity afforded to others
- Root causes of vulnerability -- poverty, housing instability, substance use disorders, historical trauma -- addressed through sustained investment
