# Missing and Murdered Indigenous People: Roadmap

## Overview

This roadmap outlines a sequenced implementation strategy for addressing the MMIP crisis, organized in three phases. Phase 1 focuses on immediate actions that can be taken under existing authority or with bipartisan legislative support. Phase 2 addresses structural reforms requiring more significant legislative action. Phase 3 envisions long-term systemic changes that address root causes. The phases are designed so that earlier actions create the foundation for later reforms.

---

## Phase 1: Immediate Actions (Years 1-2)

### Objective

Maximize impact within existing legal authority and build the bipartisan coalition needed for structural reform.

### Legislative Actions

| Action | Vehicle | Timeline | Lead |
|--------|---------|----------|------|
| Full funding for Savanna's Act implementation | Appropriations bill | Year 1 | Senate Committee on Indian Affairs |
| Full funding for Not Invisible Act Commission | Appropriations bill | Year 1 | Senate/House Indian Affairs |
| Expand VAWA SDVCJ tribal jurisdiction to additional crimes | VAWA reauthorization or standalone bill | Year 1-2 | Congressional Native American Caucus |
| Mandate declination reporting with reasons | DOJ authorization bill | Year 1 | Senate Judiciary Committee |

### Executive Actions

| Action | Authority | Timeline | Lead Agency |
|--------|-----------|----------|-------------|
| Expand BIA Missing and Murdered Unit from 14 to 40 coordinators | DOI budget request / reprogramming | Year 1 | Department of the Interior |
| Issue executive order requiring all federal agencies to prioritize MMIP | Presidential authority | Year 1 | White House |
| Direct FBI to assign dedicated Indian Country agents in all districts with tribal land | DOJ directive | Year 1 | DOJ / FBI |
| Grant all tribal law enforcement direct access to NCIC and NamUs | DOJ/FBI administrative action | Year 1 | DOJ |
| Issue updated Savanna's Act implementation guidelines | DOJ administrative authority | Year 1 | DOJ |
| Establish interagency MMIP coordination body | Executive order | Year 1 | White House Domestic Policy Council |

### Tribal and Community Actions

| Action | Timeline | Lead |
|--------|----------|------|
| Expand Amber Alert in Indian Country to all 574 tribes | Year 1-2 | DOJ / Tribal governments |
| Fund development of 100 additional Tribal Community Response Plans | Year 1-2 | DOJ OVW |
| Establish cold case review partnerships between FBI and tribal law enforcement | Year 1 | FBI / BIA |
| Expand StrongHearts Native Helpline capacity | Year 1 | DOJ OVW / NIWRC |

## Phase 2: Structural Reform (Years 2-5)

### Objective

Enact the legislative changes necessary to close jurisdictional gaps, build institutional capacity, and create sustainable funding mechanisms.

### Legislative Actions

| Action | Vehicle | Timeline | Lead |
|--------|---------|----------|------|
| Restore full tribal criminal jurisdiction (overturn *Oliphant*) | Tribal Jurisdiction Restoration Act (standalone) | Year 2-4 | Senate Committee on Indian Affairs |
| Establish mandatory (non-discretionary) funding for tribal law enforcement | Indian Country Public Safety Act | Year 2-3 | Senate/House Appropriations |
| Create national MMIP data system with tribal data sovereignty | MMIP Data Act | Year 2-3 | Senate Indian Affairs / Judiciary |
| Regulate man camps and extractive industry operations near tribal lands | Energy Impact Safety Act | Year 3-4 | Senate Energy / Indian Affairs |
| Fund tribal court system capacity building | Tribal Courts Modernization Act | Year 2-3 | Senate Indian Affairs |
| Cross-designate tribal prosecutors as Special AUSAs | Indian Country Prosecution Reform Act | Year 2-3 | Senate Judiciary |

## Phase 3: Systemic Transformation (Years 5-10)

### Objective

Address the root causes of vulnerability through sustained investment in Indigenous communities, achieving measurable reductions in MMIP cases and closing the violence gap between Indigenous and non-Indigenous populations.

### Root Cause Investments

| Area | Actions | Timeline |
|------|---------|----------|
| Housing | Increase NAHASDA funding by 300%; build transitional housing in all BIA regions | Year 5-10 |
| Economic development | Remove trust land economic barriers; fund tribal enterprise development; expand broadband | Year 5-10 |
| Education | Fully fund BIE schools; expand tribal college programs; create MMIP prevention curricula | Year 5-10 |
| Behavioral health | Expand IHS behavioral health capacity; fund tribal healing programs addressing historical trauma | Year 5-10 |
| Cultural revitalization | Fund language preservation, ceremony, and cultural programs as protective factors | Year 5-10 |
| Infrastructure | Complete reservation road, broadband, and emergency communication infrastructure | Year 5-10 |

### Institutional Maturation

| Goal | Timeline | Measure of Success |
|------|----------|-------------------|
| Tribal courts handling full criminal caseloads | Year 5-7 | Case clearance rates comparable to state courts |
| Law enforcement staffing at national average ratios | Year 5-7 | Officer-to-population ratio at 2.4 per 1,000 |
| MMIP data system fully operational | Year 5 | All jurisdictions reporting; public dashboard updated monthly |
| Federal declination rate below 20% | Year 5-7 | DOJ annual reporting |
| Victim services accessible to all Indigenous communities | Year 7-10 | Shelter within 60-minute drive of every reservation |
| Media coverage parity | Year 5-10 | Documented reduction in coverage disparity |

### International Engagement

| Establish cross-border MMIP coordination with Canada | Year 5-6 |

## Milestones and Metrics

### Key Performance Indicators

| Metric | Baseline (2025) | Year 2 Target | Year 5 Target | Year 10 Target |
|--------|----------------|---------------|---------------|----------------|
| Federal declination rate | 52% | 40% | 20% | 10% |
| Average violent crime response time on reservations | 1+ hour | 45 minutes | 30 minutes | 15 minutes |
| Tribes implementing SDVCJ | 45 | 100 | 250 | 400+ |
| BIA MMU coordinators | 14 | 40 | 75 | 100 |
| Tribally operated DV shelters | 46 | 60 | 106 | 150 |
| MMIP cases in unified database | Incomplete | 80% of known cases | 95% | 99% |
| Homicide clearance rate in Indian Country | ~50% | 55% | 65% | 75% |

### Accountability Mechanisms

- **Annual MMIP progress report**: Submitted to Congress by the interagency MMIP coordination body
- **Tribal consultation**: Semi-annual consultation sessions with tribal leaders on MMIP progress
- **GAO oversight**: Biennial GAO assessments of MMIP program effectiveness
- **Not Invisible Act Commission**: Ongoing monitoring and recommendations
- **Public dashboard**: Real-time metrics accessible to the public, media, and advocacy organizations

## Political Strategy

### Coalition Building

| Phase | Strategy | Key Partners |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Phase 1 | Frame as law enforcement and public safety issue; leverage bipartisan support | Law enforcement associations, victim rights groups, bipartisan congressional champions |
| Phase 2 | Build on Phase 1 successes; leverage VAWA SDVCJ track record to support broader jurisdiction reform | Tribal governments, civil rights organizations, justice reform advocates |
| Phase 3 | Frame root cause investments as economic development and self-determination | Economic development organizations, education advocates, health care coalitions |

### Sequencing Logic

The roadmap is sequenced so that:

1. **Phase 1** actions demonstrate feasibility, build trust, and create data that supports the case for Phase 2
2. **Phase 2** structural reforms close the jurisdictional and resource gaps that are the immediate causes of MMIP impunity
3. **Phase 3** root cause investments address the conditions that create vulnerability, aiming for long-term, sustained reduction in MMIP rates

## Risk Assessment

| Risk | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|------------|
| Congressional gridlock prevents jurisdiction reform | High | Pursue executive actions and state-level reforms in parallel |
| Funding cuts reduce capacity | Moderate | Make key funding mandatory rather than discretionary |
| Supreme Court further restricts tribal authority | Moderate | Legislative codification of tribal jurisdiction; Constitutional amendment long-term |
| Tribal capacity constraints delay implementation | Moderate | Phased approach; technical assistance; regional partnerships |
| Data sovereignty concerns slow data system | Moderate | Co-design with tribal governments from the outset |
| Extractive industry lobbying blocks regulation | High | Build coalition with environmental and public safety advocates |
