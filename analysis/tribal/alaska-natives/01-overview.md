# Alaska Natives: Overview

## Executive Summary

Alaska Natives occupy a unique position in American Indigenous policy, shaped by geography, federal legislation, and governance structures that differ fundamentally from those of tribes in the contiguous United States. The Alaska Native Claims Settlement Act of 1971 (ANCSA) extinguished Aboriginal land claims and replaced the reservation model with a corporate framework -- establishing 12 regional corporations and over 200 village corporations to manage 44 million acres and nearly $1 billion in settlement funds. This corporate model, while generating significant economic activity, created a governance structure that often exists in tension with the traditional tribal authority of Alaska's 229 federally recognized tribes.

Alaska Natives face compounding challenges rooted in extreme geographic isolation, a subarctic and arctic climate that drives up the cost of every public service, and a federal-state relationship that has historically denied Alaska tribes the same sovereign authority afforded to tribes in the lower 48 states. Subsistence hunting and fishing -- central to Alaska Native culture, nutrition, and economy -- are governed by a complex and often contradictory federal-state framework that threatens food security and cultural continuity. Meanwhile, villages confront infrastructure crises including lack of running water, failing sanitation systems, coastal erosion accelerated by climate change, and some of the highest energy costs in the nation.

Approximately 113,000 Alaska Natives live in rural communities, many of which are accessible only by air or water. These communities experience poverty rates exceeding 25%, suicide rates among the highest in the world, and epidemic-level substance abuse and domestic violence. The Indian Health Service and tribal health organizations provide care through a system that, despite innovations like the Alaska Tribal Health Compact, remains underfunded relative to the scope of need.

Addressing these challenges requires reforms that reconcile the corporate structure of ANCSA with tribal sovereignty, secure subsistence rights against competing commercial and sport interests, deliver infrastructure and services to remote communities, and confront the climate crisis that threatens the physical existence of dozens of Alaska Native villages.

## Core Tensions

- **Corporate model vs. tribal governance**: ANCSA created for-profit corporations to manage land and resources, but tribal governments exercise political sovereignty -- these two structures sometimes conflict over priorities, accountability, and authority
- **Subsistence vs. commercial interests**: Alaska Native subsistence rights face competition from commercial fishing, sport hunting, and resource extraction industries with substantial political influence
- **Federal vs. state authority**: Alaska has historically resisted acknowledging tribal sovereignty and Indian Country status, creating jurisdictional uncertainty that undermines tribal authority
- **Climate urgency vs. bureaucratic timelines**: Villages facing imminent destruction from erosion and flooding confront relocation processes that take decades and cost hundreds of millions of dollars per village
- **Cultural preservation vs. economic necessity**: Young Alaska Natives face pressure to leave villages for education and employment, threatening the intergenerational transmission of language and traditional knowledge
- **Shareholder model vs. community welfare**: ANCSA corporations serve shareholders (who hold stock), not necessarily community residents, creating misalignment between corporate decisions and village needs

## Key Questions

1. **How should ANCSA's corporate model be reformed?** Can the corporate and tribal governance structures be reconciled to better serve Alaska Native communities?

2. **Are subsistence rights adequately protected?** How do federal and state frameworks compare, and what reforms would secure subsistence access for future generations?

3. **What is the status of Alaska tribal sovereignty?** How has the legal understanding of tribal authority evolved since ANCSA, and what gaps remain?

4. **How can threatened villages be relocated?** What institutional, financial, and legal mechanisms are needed to move communities facing climate-driven destruction?

5. **Why are social conditions so severe in rural Alaska?** What are the root causes of the suicide, substance abuse, and violence epidemics, and what interventions have shown promise?

6. **How can infrastructure gaps be closed?** What would it cost to provide basic water, sanitation, energy, and broadband services to all Alaska Native communities?

## Vision of Success

A reformed system for Alaska Natives would feature:

- Tribal governments exercising full sovereignty over their territories and citizens, with clear Indian Country status for Alaska Native villages
- ANCSA corporations operating in partnership with tribal governments rather than as competing power centers
- Subsistence rights secured through federal law with meaningful Alaska Native participation in wildlife and fisheries management
- All Alaska Native communities having access to safe drinking water, reliable sanitation, affordable energy, and broadband connectivity
- A funded, streamlined process for relocating villages threatened by climate change, with community self-determination driving relocation decisions
- Alaska Native languages stabilized through immersion programs, technology, and intergenerational transmission
- Economic development opportunities that allow Alaska Natives to thrive in their home communities without choosing between culture and livelihood
- Health outcomes approaching national averages, with culturally appropriate behavioral health services addressing the suicide and substance abuse crises
