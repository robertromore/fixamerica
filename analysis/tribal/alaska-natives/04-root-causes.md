# Alaska Natives: Root Causes

## Overview

The challenges facing Alaska Native communities are not random misfortunes but the predictable results of structural decisions made over 150 years of federal policy. The root causes fall into five interconnected categories: the fundamental design flaws of ANCSA, the denial of full tribal sovereignty, the failure to protect subsistence rights, geographic isolation compounded by federal neglect, and the intergenerational effects of colonization and forced assimilation.

## Root Cause 1: ANCSA's Corporate-Over-Sovereignty Design

### The Problem

ANCSA deliberately chose a corporate model over tribal governance as the mechanism for settling Alaska Native land claims. This choice prioritized economic integration into the American capitalist system over political self-determination, creating an institutional framework that systematically disadvantages Alaska Native communities.

### How It Operates

- **Land held by corporations, not governments**: Unlike reservations in the lower 48 states, ANCSA lands are owned by for-profit corporations. Corporate boards are legally obligated to maximize shareholder value, not to provide governmental services or preserve cultural practices.
- **Shareholder vs. citizen model**: ANCSA created shareholders, not citizens of a political entity. Corporate decisions are driven by fiduciary duty to shareholders, not by democratic accountability to a community.
- **Split estate**: Village corporations hold surface rights while regional corporations hold subsurface (mineral) rights. This split creates conflicts over resource extraction, environmental protection, and revenue distribution.
- **No Indian Country status**: The Supreme Court's *Venetie* decision (1998) held that ANCSA lands do not constitute "Indian Country" under federal law. Without Indian Country status, tribes lack the territorial jurisdiction that underpins tribal governance in the lower 48.
- **Extinguishment of aboriginal title**: ANCSA extinguished all claims based on aboriginal title, including -- arguably -- the underlying basis for subsistence rights. This eliminated the foundational land relationship that supports tribal sovereignty elsewhere.

### Why It Persists

- Oil and resource extraction industries benefit from the corporate model, which facilitates commercial transactions on ANCSA lands
- Some ANCSA corporations have become powerful economic entities (combined revenues exceeding $15 billion) with institutional interest in maintaining the current structure
- Reforming ANCSA would require congressional action, and Alaska's congressional delegation has generally prioritized resource development over tribal governance
- The legal complexity of unwinding corporate land holdings to establish Indian Country is daunting

## Root Cause 2: Denial of Full Tribal Sovereignty

### The Problem

Despite federal recognition of 229 Alaska tribes, the practical exercise of tribal sovereignty in Alaska is severely constrained compared to tribes in the contiguous United States. The absence of a land base with Indian Country status is the most significant limitation.

### How It Operates

- **No territorial jurisdiction**: Without Indian Country, Alaska tribes cannot exercise the territorial jurisdiction that is the foundation of tribal governance elsewhere. They can govern their members but not their territory.
- **State encroachment**: Alaska has historically resisted acknowledging tribal sovereignty. The state did not formally recognize tribal governmental authority until *John v. Baker* (1999), and state agencies continue to resist tribal jurisdiction in many areas.
- **Limited criminal jurisdiction**: The VAWA 2013 tribal jurisdiction provisions have been extremely difficult to implement in Alaska due to the absence of Indian Country, meaning Alaska Native women -- who experience domestic violence and sexual assault at the highest rates in the nation -- lack the jurisdictional protections available to women in the lower 48 tribal communities.
- **Consultation failures**: Federal and state agencies routinely fail to conduct meaningful government-to-government consultation with Alaska tribes, treating them as stakeholders rather than sovereign governments.
- **Self-governance limitations**: While Alaska tribes can compact for federal services under the Self-Determination Act, their governmental authority remains constrained by the lack of a territorial base.

### Why It Persists

- The state of Alaska has historically opposed tribal sovereignty as a threat to state jurisdiction and resource management authority
- The resource extraction industry opposes Indian Country designations that could subject their operations to tribal regulation
- Federal reluctance to create new Indian Country, despite the 2017 Secretarial Order allowing land-into-trust applications
- The *Venetie* precedent remains controlling law; overturning it would require either a Supreme Court reversal or congressional legislation

## Root Cause 3: Subsistence Rights Insecurity

### The Problem

Subsistence hunting, fishing, and gathering are not recreational activities for Alaska Natives -- they are the economic, nutritional, cultural, and spiritual foundation of community life. Yet subsistence rights rest on fragile legal foundations that are perpetually contested.

### How It Operates

- **Dual management chaos**: Federal and state subsistence management systems operate independently with different eligibility criteria, different regulations, and different enforcement priorities. A harvest legal under federal law may be illegal under state law.
- **Constitutional barrier**: The Alaska Constitution's common-use clause prohibits a rural subsistence priority, making it impossible for the state to implement ANILCA's rural preference. Multiple attempts to amend the constitution have failed.
- **Commercial and sport competition**: Subsistence users compete with commercial fishing fleets and sport hunting/fishing operations that generate significant revenue and political influence. When resources decline, subsistence users often bear disproportionate restrictions.
- **Climate-driven resource decline**: Declining salmon populations (particularly Yukon and Kuskokwim chinook), changing caribou migration patterns, and reduced sea ice for marine mammal hunting threaten subsistence resources regardless of regulatory protections.
- **Regulatory complexity**: Alaska Natives must navigate a labyrinth of federal, state, and international regulations to conduct traditional harvest activities that their communities have practiced for millennia.

### Why It Persists

- Commercial fishing is a $6 billion industry in Alaska with enormous political influence
- Sport hunting and fishing tourism generates significant revenue for the state
- Amending the Alaska Constitution requires a two-thirds vote of the legislature and majority voter approval; urban voters significantly outnumber rural voters
- Federal agencies lack sufficient staff and funding to enforce subsistence priorities in remote areas
- Climate change is accelerating resource decline faster than management systems can adapt

## Root Cause 4: Geographic Isolation Compounded by Federal Neglect

### The Problem

Alaska Native communities are among the most geographically isolated in the United States, with over 200 communities accessible only by air or water. This isolation dramatically increases the cost of every public service -- infrastructure, healthcare, education, law enforcement -- and the federal government has never funded these services at levels that account for Alaska's unique cost factors.

### How It Operates

- **Infrastructure cost multiplier**: Building and maintaining infrastructure in rural Alaska costs 3-10 times more than comparable projects in the contiguous United States due to permafrost, lack of road access, short construction seasons, and the need to barge or fly in all materials.
- **Energy poverty**: Communities dependent on diesel generators pay electricity rates 3-4 times the national average and heating fuel costs that consume 25-40% of household income.
- **Food insecurity**: The cost of store-bought food in rural Alaska is 2-3 times Anchorage prices, making subsistence harvests an economic necessity, not merely a cultural preference.
- **Healthcare access**: Many communities are served only by Community Health Aides with limited training, and medical evacuations to hub communities or Anchorage can cost $10,000-$50,000 per trip.
- **Federal funding formulas**: Most federal programs use population-based funding formulas that do not account for the dramatically higher per-capita costs of serving small, remote communities.

### Why It Persists

- Alaska's small population (approximately 730,000) limits its congressional delegation and political influence
- Federal cost-benefit analyses systematically undervalue infrastructure investments in small, remote communities
- The extreme costs create a self-reinforcing cycle: lack of infrastructure drives out population, which reduces political justification for investment, which drives further decline
- Climate change is increasing costs by damaging existing infrastructure (permafrost thaw, erosion) and requiring adaptation investments

## Root Cause 5: Intergenerational Trauma and Cultural Disruption

### The Problem

Alaska Natives have experienced successive waves of colonization, cultural suppression, and forced assimilation whose effects cascade across generations. The social crises in Alaska Native communities -- suicide, substance abuse, domestic violence, child welfare involvement -- are downstream consequences of this historical trauma.

### How It Operates

- **Boarding school legacy**: Children removed from families for federal and territorial boarding schools experienced cultural dislocation, physical and sexual abuse, and the suppression of language and identity. These experiences disrupted parenting, attachment, and cultural transmission.
- **Rapid cultural change**: Within a single generation (roughly 1950-1980), many Alaska Native communities transitioned from semi-nomadic subsistence lifestyles to sedentary village life with a cash economy, Western education, and television. The psychological and social dislocation was profound.
- **Alcohol and substance abuse**: Introduced alcohol has had devastating effects on communities with no prior history of alcohol use. Many communities have voted to go "dry" or "damp," but bootlegging and substance importation continue.
- **Language loss**: Of the 20 Alaska Native languages, only two (Central Yup'ik and Siberian Yupik) are still being learned by children as a first language. Language loss severs the connection to traditional knowledge, governance, and identity.
- **ACE accumulation**: Adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) compound across generations: traumatized parents are more likely to struggle with parenting, increasing ACE exposure for the next generation.

### Why It Persists

- Behavioral health services are grossly insufficient in rural Alaska; many communities have no resident mental health professional
- Treatment models often fail to incorporate cultural healing practices
- Economic conditions (poverty, unemployment, lack of housing) create stress that perpetuates substance abuse and family dysfunction
- Federal and state agencies historically prioritized assimilation over cultural preservation
- Language revitalization efforts are underfunded relative to the urgency of language loss

## Root Cause Interconnections

The five root causes are deeply interconnected and mutually reinforcing:

| Root Cause | Exacerbated By | Exacerbates |
|-----------|----------------|-------------|
| ANCSA design flaws | Denial of sovereignty; geographic isolation | Subsistence insecurity; cultural disruption |
| Sovereignty denial | ANCSA design; state opposition | Infrastructure neglect; subsistence insecurity |
| Subsistence insecurity | Climate change; sovereignty denial; ANCSA extinguishment | Food insecurity; cultural disruption; economic hardship |
| Geographic isolation + federal neglect | Funding formulas; small population | Health crises; education gaps; economic marginalization |
| Intergenerational trauma | All of the above | Substance abuse; suicide; family dysfunction; language loss |

## The Feedback Loop

The root causes create a reinforcing cycle:

1. ANCSA's corporate model and the denial of tribal sovereignty weaken the governmental capacity of Alaska Native communities
2. Weakened governance reduces the ability to secure resources, enforce regulations, and deliver services
3. Underfunded, underserved communities experience declining social conditions (poverty, substance abuse, violence)
4. Social crises further erode governance capacity and cultural continuity
5. Cultural disruption weakens the community bonds and traditional knowledge that historically sustained resilience
6. Weakened communities have less political power to advocate for structural reforms, perpetuating the cycle

## Comparison: Alaska Natives vs. Lower 48 Tribes

| Dimension | Lower 48 Tribes | Alaska Natives | Consequence |
|-----------|----------------|----------------|-------------|
| Land status | Reservations (Indian Country) | ANCSA corporate lands (not Indian Country) | Alaska tribes lack territorial jurisdiction |
| Governance | Tribal government exercises authority over territory | Tribal government exercises authority over members only | Limited regulatory and law enforcement capacity |
| Subsistence | Treaty-protected hunting/fishing rights | ANCSA extinguished aboriginal claims; ANILCA provides limited federal protection | Subsistence rights are legally fragile |
| Federal funding | Direct federal-tribal relationship; trust responsibility | Same legal trust responsibility but complicated by ANCSA corporate structure | Funding often flows to corporations, not tribal governments |
| Criminal jurisdiction | VAWA 2013 expanded tribal jurisdiction | VAWA barely applicable due to no Indian Country | Alaska Native women have least legal protection |
