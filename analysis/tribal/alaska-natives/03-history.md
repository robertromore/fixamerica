# Alaska Natives: History

## Pre-Contact Era (Before 1741)

### Indigenous Civilizations

Alaska Natives developed sophisticated civilizations adapted to some of the most demanding environments on Earth, with continuous habitation spanning at least 15,000 years:

- **Inupiaq and Yup'ik peoples** developed marine mammal hunting technologies -- including the umiak and kayak -- that enabled survival in Arctic and subarctic coastal environments. Whaling communities on the North Slope organized complex cooperative hunts requiring extensive social coordination.
- **Athabascan peoples** of the Interior adapted to boreal forest environments, developing seasonal migration patterns that followed caribou, salmon, and other resources across vast territories.
- **Tlingit, Haida, and Tsimshian peoples** of Southeast Alaska created one of the most complex non-agricultural societies in the world, with elaborate social hierarchies, monumental art traditions, and resource management systems based on clan ownership of fishing streams and hunting territories.
- **Aleut/Unangan peoples** mastered the maritime environment of the Aleutian Chain, developing exceptional navigational skills and some of the earliest known marine resource management practices.
- **Alutiiq/Sugpiaq peoples** of the Gulf of Alaska coast combined marine and terrestrial subsistence strategies with sophisticated woodworking and weaving traditions.

### Governance and Social Organization

Pre-contact Alaska Native societies exhibited diverse governance structures:

- **Tlingit and Haida**: Matrilineal clan systems with hereditary leaders (*hít s'aatí* or house chiefs) governing through consensus and potlatch ceremonies that redistributed wealth
- **Inupiaq**: Band-level organization centered on *umialik* (whaling captains) who led through demonstrated competence and generosity
- **Yup'ik**: Village-based governance through *qasgiq* (communal men's houses) where elders made collective decisions
- **Athabascan**: Band-level organization with leadership through consensus; seasonal gathering and dispersal patterns
- **Aleut/Unangan**: Hierarchical village organization with hereditary chiefs and social classes

## Russian Colonial Period (1741-1867)

### Initial Contact and Exploitation

Russian fur traders arrived in Alaska in 1741, initiating a period of brutal colonization:

- **1741**: Vitus Bering's expedition reaches Alaska; Georg Steller documents first European contact with Alaska Natives
- **1745-1799**: *Promyshlenniki* (Russian fur hunters) established temporary outposts throughout the Aleutian Islands, enslaving Aleut men as sea otter hunters
- **1784**: Grigory Shelikhov established the first permanent Russian settlement at Three Saints Bay (Kodiak Island), subjugating the Alutiiq population through military force
- **1799**: Russian-American Company chartered, given monopoly over Alaskan fur trade

### Impact on Alaska Native Populations

| Group | Pre-Contact Population (est.) | Post-Russian Population (est.) | Decline | Primary Causes |
|-------|------------------------------|-------------------------------|---------|----------------|
| Aleut/Unangan | 15,000-25,000 | 2,500 | 80-90% | Enslavement, disease, violence |
| Alutiiq/Sugpiaq | 10,000-20,000 | 3,000 | 70-85% | Disease, forced labor, warfare |
| Tlingit | 15,000 | 10,000 | 33% | Limited contact (resisted Russian control) |

### Russian Orthodox Church

The Russian Orthodox Church played a dual role:

- Missionaries learned Native languages and created writing systems (notably for Aleut/Unangan and Yup'ik)
- Church officials sometimes advocated for Native welfare against company abuses
- Conversion was often coerced or incentivized through material goods
- Russian Orthodoxy became deeply integrated into Aleut, Alutiiq, and Yup'ik cultures, where it persists today

## Early American Period (1867-1936)

### Purchase and Neglect (1867-1884)

- **1867**: United States purchased Alaska from Russia for $7.2 million. The Treaty of Cession (Article III) promised that "uncivilized tribes" would be "subject to such laws and regulations as the United States may, from time to time, adopt in regard to aboriginal tribes"
- **1867-1884**: Alaska had no territorial government; administered by the military, then the Treasury Department, with minimal governance infrastructure
- **No treaties**: Unlike tribes in the contiguous United States, Alaska Natives never entered into treaties with the United States, creating a distinct legal framework
- **Aboriginal title**: Alaska Natives retained aboriginal title to their traditional lands, a status that would remain legally ambiguous for over a century

### Organic Act and Early Governance (1884-1912)

- **1884**: First Organic Act established minimal civil government; Section 8 stated that "Indians or other persons in said district shall not be disturbed in the possession of any lands actually in their use or occupation or now claimed by them"
- **1905**: Nelson Act established public schools for white children and "children of mixed blood who lead a civilized life"; Bureau of Education operated separate schools for Native children
- **1912**: Second Organic Act created territorial legislature; Alaska Natives excluded from meaningful political participation

### Assimilation and Education

- **1885-1960s**: Federal and territorial boarding schools separated Alaska Native children from families to impose English and Euro-American culture
- **Sheldon Jackson** (General Agent of Education in Alaska, 1885-1907) established schools, introduced reindeer herding, and pursued aggressive assimilation policies
- **Jesse Lee Home** and similar institutions served as orphanages and boarding schools where Native children experienced cultural suppression and, in many cases, abuse

### Alaska Native Brotherhood and Sisterhood

- **1912**: Alaska Native Brotherhood (ANB) founded in Sitka by Tlingit and Tsimshian leaders; Alaska Native Sisterhood (ANS) followed in 1915
- These organizations fought for citizenship, voting rights, desegregation, and land claims
- **1924**: Indian Citizenship Act granted citizenship to all Native Americans, including Alaska Natives, though practical barriers to voting persisted
- **1945**: Alaska's Anti-Discrimination Act -- the first civil rights legislation in the nation -- passed largely due to ANB/ANS advocacy, championed by Elizabeth Peratrovich (Tlingit)

## Statehood and Land Claims (1936-1971)

### Indian Reorganization Act in Alaska (1936)

- **1934**: Indian Reorganization Act (IRA) applied generally to Indian affairs
- **1936**: Alaska amendment to the IRA allowed Alaska Native villages to organize as federally recognized tribal governments
- Six reservations established under the IRA: Venetie, Karluk, Akutan, Diomede, Unalakleet, and Wales (most later revoked under ANCSA)

### Alaska Statehood (1959)

- **1958**: Alaska Statehood Act granted Alaska state status, authorizing the state to select 104 million acres from the federal public domain
- **Section 4**: The Statehood Act disclaimed "all right and title" to land "that may be held by any Indians, Eskimos, or Aleuts" -- but did not define or resolve those land rights
- **Conflict**: State land selections began to overlap with Alaska Native traditional territories, generating protests and legal challenges

### The Land Claims Movement (1960s)

- **1961**: Tundra Times newspaper founded by Howard Rock (Inupiaq), providing a platform for land claims advocacy
- **1963**: First statewide meeting of Alaska Native leaders organized to address land claims
- **1966**: Alaska Federation of Natives (AFN) founded, unifying regional Native organizations to pursue a comprehensive land settlement
- **1966**: Secretary of the Interior Stewart Udall imposed a "land freeze," halting state land selections pending resolution of Native claims
- **1968**: Oil discovered at Prudhoe Bay; oil companies needed a pipeline across Native lands, creating powerful economic incentives to resolve claims

## ANCSA and Its Aftermath (1971-1990)

### Alaska Native Claims Settlement Act (1971)

ANCSA (43 U.S.C. 1601-1629h) was signed into law on December 18, 1971. Its key provisions:

| Provision | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Land conveyance | 44 million acres to Native corporations (approximately 12% of Alaska) |
| Monetary settlement | $962.5 million ($462.5 million from federal treasury; $500 million from mineral revenues) |
| Corporate structure | 12 regional corporations and over 200 village corporations |
| Enrollment | Persons with at least one-quarter Alaska Native blood quantum born on or before December 18, 1971 |
| Stock restrictions | Shares non-transferable for 20 years (later extended) |
| Extinguishment | All aboriginal title and claims extinguished, including hunting and fishing rights based on aboriginal title |
| Reservations | Existing reservations (except Metlakatla Annette Island Reserve) revoked |

### Why a Corporate Model

Several factors drove the choice of corporations over reservations:

- **Oil industry lobbying**: Companies wanted clear title to pipeline and drilling rights; corporate ownership was seen as more compatible with commercial transactions
- **Anti-reservation sentiment**: Both Native and non-Native Alaskans opposed the reservation model, viewing it as paternalistic and economically limiting
- **Assimilation ideology**: Policymakers believed a corporate structure would integrate Alaska Natives into the capitalist economy
- **Alaska Native preferences**: Some leaders, particularly in urban areas and Southeast Alaska, saw corporations as vehicles for economic self-determination
- **Political compromise**: The corporate model avoided the politically contentious creation of large reservation lands

### Implementation Challenges (1972-1990)

- **Land selection**: Complex and contested process of identifying which 44 million acres would be conveyed; some selections remain incomplete
- **Corporate mismanagement**: Several corporations experienced financial losses, mismanagement, and shareholder suits in their early decades
- **Section 7(i) revenue sharing**: Regional corporations required to share 70% of natural resource revenues from ANCSA lands; disputes over calculations led to decades of litigation
- **"1991 provisions"**: Original law provided that stock restrictions and land protections would expire in 1991, potentially allowing corporate takeovers and loss of Native-owned land; addressed by 1987-1988 amendments
- **Subsistence extinguishment debate**: Whether ANCSA extinguished subsistence rights based on aboriginal title became a major legal and political question

## ANILCA and Modern Era (1980-Present)

### Alaska National Interest Lands Conservation Act (1980)

ANILCA (16 U.S.C. 3101-3233) designated over 100 million acres of federal conservation lands and included Title VIII, which:

- Established a rural subsistence priority on federal public lands
- Recognized that "the continuation of the opportunity for subsistence uses by rural residents of Alaska" is "essential to Native physical, economic, traditional, and cultural existence"
- Created a framework for federal management of subsistence when the state failed to comply

### State Non-Compliance

- **1989**: Alaska Supreme Court ruled in *McDowell v. State* that the Alaska Constitution's common-use clause prohibited a rural subsistence priority, putting Alaska out of compliance with ANILCA
- **1990**: Federal government assumed management of subsistence on federal public lands after the state failed to enact a conforming law
- **Dual management**: Since 1990, Alaska has operated under two separate subsistence management systems -- federal (with rural priority) and state (without) -- creating confusion and conflict

### 1991 Amendments and Beyond

- **1987-1988 amendments**: Extended stock alienation restrictions indefinitely; allowed corporations to issue new shares to "after-born" Natives (those born after December 18, 1971); maintained land protections
- **1998**: *Alaska v. Native Village of Venetie Tribal Government*, 522 U.S. 520 -- Supreme Court ruled that ANCSA lands are not "Indian Country" under federal law, severely limiting tribal jurisdiction
- **2000s-2010s**: Gradual federal recognition of Alaska tribal sovereignty, including:
    - **2017**: Secretarial Order allowing Alaska tribes to apply for land-into-trust (previously denied based on *Venetie*)
    - **2021**: Updated solicitor's opinion affirming Alaska tribes' eligibility for land-into-trust
- **2013**: Violence Against Women Act reauthorization included limited tribal criminal jurisdiction, but implementation in Alaska was delayed and remains limited due to the absence of Indian Country

### Ongoing Legal and Policy Developments

- **Tribal sovereignty recognition**: Alaska tribes are increasingly asserting governmental authority, including through compacting for federal services and exercising child welfare jurisdiction under ICWA
- **Climate adaptation**: Federal and state agencies are beginning to fund village relocation, but no comprehensive framework exists
- **ANCSA reform discussions**: Periodic proposals to amend ANCSA to address shareholder dilution, land protection, and governance issues
- **Subsistence reform**: Multiple failed attempts to amend the Alaska Constitution to allow a rural subsistence priority
