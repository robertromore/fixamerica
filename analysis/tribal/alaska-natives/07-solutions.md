# Alaska Natives: Solutions

## Overview

Addressing the systemic challenges facing Alaska Natives requires reforms across five domains: strengthening tribal sovereignty, securing subsistence rights, closing infrastructure gaps, building climate resilience, and healing intergenerational trauma. Each solution is assessed for its feasibility, impact, and the political conditions necessary for implementation.

## Solution 1: Establish Indian Country in Alaska

### Problem Addressed

Alaska tribes lack territorial jurisdiction because ANCSA lands are not "Indian Country" under federal law (*Venetie*, 1998). This prevents tribes from exercising the same governmental authority as tribes in the lower 48.

### Proposal

Enact federal legislation defining Alaska Native village areas as Indian Country for jurisdictional purposes.

### Key Components

- **Statutory Indian Country designation**: Amend 18 U.S.C. 1151 to include Alaska Native villages within the definition of Indian Country
- **Jurisdictional scope**: Grant Alaska tribes criminal and civil jurisdiction comparable to tribes in the lower 48, including VAWA special domestic violence criminal jurisdiction
- **Implementation timeline**: Phase in over 5 years with federal technical assistance and training for tribal courts and law enforcement
- **Land-into-trust acceleration**: Streamline BIA processing of land-into-trust applications for Alaska tribes, with a target of completing pending applications within 3 years
- **State coordination**: Establish cross-jurisdictional agreements between tribal, state, and federal authorities

### Evidence of Effectiveness

- Tribes in the lower 48 with Indian Country status exercise effective governance, including law enforcement, courts, and environmental regulation
- The VAWA tribal jurisdiction pilot (lower 48) has demonstrated that tribal courts can fairly and effectively prosecute non-Indian defendants
- The Metlakatla Indian Community -- the only Alaska reservation -- exercises Indian Country jurisdiction successfully

### Feasibility Assessment

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| Political viability | Low-moderate. Strong state and industry opposition; would require dedicated congressional champions |
| Legal basis | Solid. Congress has plenary power to define Indian Country |
| Implementation cost | Moderate. $500 million-$1 billion over 5 years for tribal court and law enforcement development |
| Timeline | 5-10 years for legislation and implementation |
| Impact | High. Would transform tribal governance capacity in Alaska |

## Solution 2: Comprehensive Subsistence Reform

### Problem Addressed

The dual federal-state subsistence management system creates confusion, fails to prioritize Alaska Native access, and cannot adapt to rapid climate-driven resource decline.

### Proposal

Enact comprehensive federal subsistence legislation that supersedes state management and establishes a clear Alaska Native subsistence priority.

### Key Components

- **Federal preemption**: Extend federal subsistence management to all lands and waters in Alaska, eliminating the dual management system
- **Alaska Native priority**: Establish a subsistence priority for Alaska Native communities based on historical and customary use, not merely rural residency
- **Co-management boards**: Create regional co-management boards with majority Alaska Native representation for each major resource (salmon, caribou, marine mammals, migratory birds)
- **Climate adaptation**: Build adaptive management provisions that can rapidly adjust harvest levels and methods in response to climate-driven population changes
- **Customary trade protection**: Legalize and protect traditional exchange of subsistence foods among Alaska Native communities
- **Enforcement reform**: Create tribal fish and game enforcement authority with cross-deputization agreements

### Evidence of Effectiveness

- New Zealand's Treaty of Waitangi settlements provide indigenous fishing rights and co-management that have improved both resource sustainability and indigenous livelihoods
- Canadian co-management boards (e.g., Nunavut Wildlife Management Board) have successfully integrated indigenous knowledge into resource management
- The Alaska Eskimo Whaling Commission demonstrates effective co-management of marine mammals

### Feasibility Assessment

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| Political viability | Low. Would require overcoming commercial fishing and sport hunting opposition |
| Legal basis | Solid. Congress has authority over federal lands and can preempt state management |
| Implementation cost | Moderate. $200-$300 million for co-management infrastructure |
| Timeline | 5-8 years for legislation and implementation |
| Impact | Very high. Would resolve the most contested issue in Alaska Native policy |

## Solution 3: ANCSA Governance Reform

### Problem Addressed

The corporate model creates tensions between shareholder interests and community welfare, between corporate boards and tribal councils, and between economic development and cultural preservation.

### Proposal

Amend ANCSA to create formal mechanisms for coordination between ANCSA corporations and tribal governments, and to allow communities to restructure their governance arrangements.

### Key Components

- **Tribal-corporate coordination requirement**: Mandate formal consultation and coordination between ANCSA corporations and tribal governments on decisions affecting community welfare
- **Opt-in tribal land transfer**: Allow village corporations to voluntarily transfer surface estate lands to tribal government ownership (as trust land) with shareholder approval
- **Revenue-sharing reform**: Clarify and simplify Section 7(i) revenue-sharing formulas to reduce litigation
- **After-born enrollment**: Standardize and guarantee enrollment for Alaska Natives born after December 18, 1971
- **Corporate governance reforms**: Require transparency standards comparable to publicly traded companies, including executive compensation disclosure and conflict-of-interest policies
- **Cultural purpose recognition**: Amend ANCSA to recognize cultural and community purposes alongside economic purposes in corporate charters

### Evidence of Effectiveness

- Several ANCSA corporations have voluntarily partnered with tribal governments (e.g., CIRI and Cook Inlet tribal entities)
- The 1991 amendments successfully addressed stock alienation concerns, demonstrating that ANCSA can be reformed
- Tribal entities that manage their own lands (e.g., Metlakatla) demonstrate effective integration of governance and economic development

### Feasibility Assessment

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| Political viability | Moderate. Some corporate support; requires careful stakeholder engagement |
| Legal basis | Solid. Congress can amend ANCSA |
| Implementation cost | Low. Primarily governance reforms, not new programs |
| Timeline | 3-5 years for legislation; ongoing implementation |
| Impact | Moderate-high. Would reduce tribal-corporate conflict and improve governance |

## Solution 4: Alaska Village Infrastructure Initiative

### Problem Addressed

Alaska Native villages lack basic infrastructure -- safe water, sanitation, reliable energy, broadband, and transportation -- at levels that would be considered a humanitarian crisis anywhere else in the United States.

### Proposal

Establish a dedicated, mandatory-funded federal program to bring all Alaska Native villages to minimum infrastructure standards within 15 years.

### Key Components

- **Water and sanitation**: Invest $3 billion over 15 years to provide piped water and sewer service or equivalent to all Alaska Native communities; eliminate honey bucket sanitation
- **Energy transition**: Invest $2 billion to transition 180+ diesel-dependent communities to renewable energy (wind, solar, hydrokinetic, biomass) with battery storage
- **Broadband**: Extend broadband service (minimum 100/20 Mbps) to all Alaska Native communities through a combination of satellite (LEO), fixed wireless, and fiber technologies; $1.5 billion over 10 years
- **Transportation**: Upgrade rural airports and establish year-round surface transportation options where feasible; $2 billion over 15 years
- **Housing**: Build and renovate 10,000 housing units in rural Alaska at a cost of $4 billion over 15 years (accounting for Alaska construction cost multipliers)
- **Workforce development**: Train Alaska Natives for infrastructure construction and maintenance jobs, ensuring community capacity to maintain new systems

### Evidence of Effectiveness

- The Village Safe Water program has successfully delivered water/sanitation to dozens of communities, demonstrating technical feasibility
- The Alaska Energy Authority's renewable energy programs have reduced diesel consumption in participating communities by 30-50%
- Canada's Northern Strategy investments in Arctic infrastructure demonstrate scalable approaches to similar challenges

### Feasibility Assessment

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| Political viability | Moderate-high. Infrastructure has bipartisan appeal; Alaska delegation supports |
| Legal basis | Solid. Federal trust responsibility; existing program authority |
| Implementation cost | $12.5 billion over 15 years ($830 million/year) |
| Timeline | 15 years for full implementation |
| Impact | Very high. Would transform quality of life in Alaska Native communities |

## Solution 5: Climate Adaptation and Village Relocation Program

### Problem Addressed

At least 31 Alaska Native villages face imminent threats from erosion, flooding, and permafrost thaw. No comprehensive federal program exists to relocate threatened communities.

### Proposal

Establish a federal Alaska Native Village Relocation Program with dedicated funding, streamlined permitting, and community self-determination in relocation decisions.

### Key Components

- **Dedicated program**: Create an Alaska Native Village Relocation Program within the Denali Commission or as a standalone entity, with authority to coordinate across federal agencies
- **Mandatory funding**: Appropriate $500 million annually for 20 years ($10 billion total) for village relocation and climate adaptation
- **Community-driven process**: Communities decide whether to relocate, where to relocate, and how to design their new village, with technical assistance from federal agencies
- **Streamlined permitting**: Create expedited environmental review and permitting for village relocation projects, with categorical exclusions for standard relocation activities
- **Cultural continuity**: Ensure relocation plans preserve community cohesion, subsistence access, and cultural continuity
- **Adaptation alternatives**: For communities that choose not to relocate or are not yet at imminent risk, fund erosion protection, flood mitigation, and infrastructure hardening

### Evidence of Effectiveness

- The Newtok-to-Mertarvik relocation, though underfunded and delayed, demonstrates that community-driven relocation is feasible
- International models (Pacific Island nations, Bangladesh climate migration programs) offer lessons for managed relocation
- The Army Corps of Engineers has identified cost-effective erosion and flood protection measures for many at-risk communities

### Feasibility Assessment

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| Political viability | Moderate. Climate framing is polarizing, but physical destruction of villages creates bipartisan urgency |
| Legal basis | Solid. Federal trust responsibility; disaster mitigation authority |
| Implementation cost | $10 billion over 20 years ($500 million/year) |
| Timeline | 20 years for full implementation; priority villages within 5-10 years |
| Impact | Critical. Without action, dozens of communities face physical destruction |

## Solution 6: Behavioral Health and Healing Initiative

### Problem Addressed

Alaska Natives experience suicide, substance abuse, domestic violence, and adverse childhood experiences at rates far exceeding national averages, rooted in intergenerational trauma from colonization and cultural disruption.

### Proposal

Establish a comprehensive, culturally grounded behavioral health system for Alaska Native communities that integrates traditional healing practices with clinical care.

### Key Components

- **Community-based behavioral health**: Place licensed behavioral health professionals in every Alaska Native community with a population over 100 (approximately 150 communities), using a combination of permanent staff and telehealth
- **Traditional healing integration**: Fund and recognize traditional healing practices alongside clinical approaches; establish cultural healing camps in each region
- **Suicide prevention**: Implement the Alaska Native Community Resilience model in all communities, combining cultural connectedness, social support, and crisis intervention
- **Substance abuse treatment**: Expand residential and outpatient treatment capacity, with culturally specific programs; fund regional treatment centers
- **Trauma-informed systems**: Train all village-based service providers (health aides, teachers, public safety officers) in trauma-informed care
- **Youth programs**: Expand Alaska Native youth leadership, cultural immersion, and mentorship programs; connect youth to elders for cultural transmission

### Evidence of Effectiveness

- The Qungasvik (Toolbox) program in Yup'ik communities demonstrated significant reductions in suicide ideation and substance abuse through culturally grounded prevention
- The Alaska Native Tribal Health Consortium's traditional healing programs have shown positive outcomes in patient satisfaction and treatment engagement
- The Indian Health Service's Zero Suicide Initiative has reduced suicide rates in participating tribal communities

### Feasibility Assessment

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| Political viability | High. Behavioral health has broad bipartisan support |
| Legal basis | Solid. IHS authority; tribal self-governance |
| Implementation cost | $500 million annually ongoing |
| Timeline | 3-5 years to build workforce; ongoing service delivery |
| Impact | High. Would address the most acute social crises in Alaska Native communities |
