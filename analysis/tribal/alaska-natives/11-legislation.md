# Alaska Natives: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative approach to Alaska Native issues must address the unique legal framework created by ANCSA, which replaced the reservation model with a corporate structure, extinguished aboriginal title, and created a governance landscape unlike any other in Indian Country. Reforms require both new federal legislation and amendments to ANCSA, ANILCA, and other existing statutes. This document presents draft legislation, regulatory proposals, and legal analysis across five priority areas: tribal sovereignty, subsistence rights, infrastructure, village relocation, and ANCSA reform.

## Constitutional Amendments

### Amendment: Alaska Native Tribal Sovereignty Recognition

**Purpose**: While tribal sovereignty is inherent and does not require constitutional recognition, a constitutional amendment would provide the strongest possible legal foundation against future erosion by Congress or the courts.

**Draft Text**:

```text
Section 1. The inherent sovereignty of federally recognized Indian
tribes, including Alaska Native tribes, is hereby recognized and
shall not be diminished by Congress except pursuant to a treaty
ratified by the Senate.

Section 2. Indian Country, as defined by Congress, shall include
the lands and territories of Alaska Native tribes as designated
by federal law.

Section 3. Congress shall have the power to enforce this article
by appropriate legislation.

Section 4. This article shall take effect two years after
ratification.
```

**Explanation**: Section 1 limits congressional plenary power by requiring treaty-based consent for sovereignty diminishment. Section 2 explicitly includes Alaska within the Indian Country framework. This amendment would supersede *Lone Wolf v. Hitchcock* and *Venetie*.

**Potential Challenges**: Constitutional amendments require two-thirds of both chambers and three-fourths of state legislatures. Resistance from states with significant tribal populations is likely. The scope of this amendment extends beyond Alaska to all of Indian law.

**Refinements**: A narrower amendment focused solely on Alaska Indian Country status would be more politically feasible but less transformative.

## Federal Legislation

### Alaska Native Sovereignty and Self-Governance Act

**Purpose**: Establish Indian Country status for Alaska Native villages, enabling full tribal jurisdiction.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Alaska Native Sovereignty and
Self-Governance Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) The 229 federally recognized Alaska Native tribes possess
inherent sovereignty as domestic dependent nations;
(b) The Supreme Court's decision in Alaska v. Native Village of
Venetie Tribal Government (1998) held that ANCSA lands do not
constitute Indian Country, creating a jurisdictional gap that
undermines tribal governance in Alaska;
(c) Alaska Native communities experience rates of violent crime,
domestic violence, and sexual assault far exceeding national
averages, in part due to jurisdictional limitations;
(d) The absence of Indian Country status in Alaska prevents
Alaska Native tribes from exercising governmental authority
comparable to tribes in the contiguous United States.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

(a) ALASKA NATIVE VILLAGE.--The term "Alaska Native village" means
any community in which the weights recognized tribal government is
organized under the Act of June 18, 1934 (25 U.S.C. 5123), as
amended for Alaska, or is otherwise federally recognized.
(b) VILLAGE AREA.--The term "village area" means the geographic
area encompassing the village site, including contiguous lands
held by the village corporation, tribal government, or individual
Alaska Native allottees, as determined by the Secretary in
consultation with the tribal government.

SEC. 4. INDIAN COUNTRY DESIGNATION.

(a) IN GENERAL.--Notwithstanding the decision of the Supreme Court
in Alaska v. Native Village of Venetie Tribal Government, 522
U.S. 520 (1998), the village areas of federally recognized Alaska
Native tribes are hereby designated as Indian Country within the
meaning of section 1151 of title 18, United States Code.
(b) BOUNDARIES.--The Secretary of the Interior, in consultation
with each tribal government, shall determine the boundaries of
each village area within 3 years of enactment.
(c) EXPANSION.--Tribal governments may petition to expand village
area boundaries to include additional trust lands acquired through
the land-into-trust process.

SEC. 5. TRIBAL JURISDICTION.

(a) CRIMINAL JURISDICTION.--Alaska Native tribes shall exercise
criminal jurisdiction over all Indians within their village areas
to the same extent as tribes in the contiguous United States.
(b) SPECIAL DOMESTIC VIOLENCE JURISDICTION.--Alaska Native tribes
shall exercise the special tribal criminal jurisdiction authorized
by section 904 of the Violence Against Women Reauthorization Act
of 2013 (34 U.S.C. 1304) over all persons within their village
areas.
(c) CIVIL JURISDICTION.--Alaska Native tribes shall exercise civil
jurisdiction within their village areas to the same extent as
tribes in the contiguous United States.

SEC. 6. IMPLEMENTATION.

(a) TRANSITION PERIOD.--This Act shall be implemented over a
5-year transition period, during which the Secretary shall provide
technical assistance and funding for tribal court development,
law enforcement training, and jurisdictional agreements.
(b) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.--There are authorized to be
appropriated $100,000,000 annually for 5 years for implementation
of this Act.
(c) CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL AGREEMENTS.--The Secretary shall
facilitate agreements between Alaska Native tribal governments,
the State of Alaska, and federal agencies to ensure coordinated
law enforcement and judicial operations.

SEC. 7. SAVINGS CLAUSE.

Nothing in this Act shall be construed to--
(a) Alter the corporate status, land ownership, or shareholder
rights of any ANCSA corporation;
(b) Diminish any existing right or authority of an Alaska Native
tribe;
(c) Affect the subsistence rights of any person under ANILCA.
```

**Explanation**: This bill directly overturns *Venetie* by statute, using Congress's plenary power over Indian affairs to define Alaska village areas as Indian Country. The 5-year phase-in allows for capacity building. The savings clause protects ANCSA corporate interests to reduce opposition.

**Potential Challenges**: Strong opposition from the State of Alaska, resource extraction industry, and some ANCSA corporate interests. Questions about the interaction between Indian Country status and existing state jurisdiction.

**Refinements**: Could be enacted as a pilot program for willing tribes, building a track record before full implementation.

### Alaska Subsistence Protection Act

**Purpose**: Unify subsistence management under federal authority with meaningful Alaska Native participation.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Alaska Subsistence Protection Act".

SEC. 2. EXTENSION OF FEDERAL SUBSISTENCE MANAGEMENT.

(a) IN GENERAL.--Title VIII of the Alaska National Interest Lands
Conservation Act (16 U.S.C. 3111-3126) is amended to extend
federal subsistence management authority to all lands and waters
in the State of Alaska.
(b) PRIORITY.--Subsistence uses of fish and wildlife by Alaska
Native communities with historical and customary dependence on
such resources shall have priority over all other consumptive
uses.

SEC. 3. REGIONAL CO-MANAGEMENT BOARDS.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--The Secretary of the Interior and the
Secretary of Commerce shall establish Regional Subsistence
Co-Management Boards for each of the following regions: Arctic,
Western, Interior, Southcentral, Southwest, and Southeast Alaska.
(b) COMPOSITION.--Each Board shall consist of--
    (1) 5 members appointed by Alaska Native tribal governments
    in the region;
    (2) 2 members representing federal agencies;
    (3) 2 members representing the State of Alaska;
    (4) 1 member representing commercial users;
    (5) 1 member representing sport users.
(c) AUTHORITY.--Each Board shall--
    (1) set subsistence harvest seasons and limits;
    (2) allocate resources among user groups consistent with the
    subsistence priority;
    (3) integrate traditional ecological knowledge into management
    decisions;
    (4) develop climate adaptation strategies for subsistence
    resources.

SEC. 4. CUSTOMARY TRADE PROTECTION.

(a) Traditional exchange of subsistence-harvested foods among
Alaska Native communities is hereby recognized as a protected
cultural practice.
(b) No federal or state regulation shall prohibit or restrict
the non-commercial customary trade of subsistence foods among
Alaska Native individuals and communities.

SEC. 5. TRIBAL FISH AND GAME ENFORCEMENT.

(a) Alaska Native tribal governments may establish tribal fish
and game enforcement programs.
(b) Tribal fish and game officers shall have authority to enforce
federal subsistence regulations within their village areas.
(c) Authorization of $50,000,000 annually for tribal fish and
game programs.
```

**Explanation**: This bill resolves the dual management problem by extending federal authority statewide and establishing a clear Alaska Native subsistence priority. Co-management boards ensure meaningful Native participation. Customary trade protection addresses a persistent regulatory conflict.

**Potential Challenges**: Extremely controversial in Alaska. The commercial fishing industry will strongly oppose. Constitutional questions about federal preemption of state fish and game authority. Alaska's congressional delegation may not support.

**Refinements**: Could be implemented incrementally, starting with species in crisis (e.g., Yukon chinook salmon) before expanding.

### Alaska Native Village Infrastructure Act

**Purpose**: Provide mandatory federal funding for water, sanitation, energy, broadband, and housing in Alaska Native communities.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Alaska Native Village Infrastructure
Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) Congress finds that Alaska Native villages lack basic
infrastructure at levels constituting a humanitarian crisis, with
32 communities lacking piped water, 180+ communities dependent
on diesel generators, and housing deficits exceeding 10,000 units.
(b) The purpose of this Act is to bring all Alaska Native
communities to minimum federal infrastructure standards within
15 years.

SEC. 3. MANDATORY APPROPRIATIONS.

(a) WATER AND SANITATION.--There are appropriated from the
Treasury $200,000,000 annually for 15 fiscal years for Alaska
Native village water and sanitation projects administered through
the Indian Health Service.
(b) ENERGY.--There are appropriated $133,000,000 annually for
15 fiscal years for Alaska Native village energy transition
projects administered through the Alaska Energy Authority and
Denali Commission.
(c) BROADBAND.--There are appropriated $150,000,000 annually for
10 fiscal years for Alaska Native village broadband deployment
administered through NTIA.
(d) HOUSING.--There are appropriated $267,000,000 annually for
15 fiscal years for Alaska Native housing construction and
renovation administered through HUD NAHASDA.
(e) TRANSPORTATION.--There are appropriated $133,000,000 annually
for 15 fiscal years for Alaska rural airport and transportation
improvements administered through FAA and Alaska DOT.

SEC. 4. TRIBAL WORKFORCE DEVELOPMENT.

(a) $25,000,000 annually for Alaska Native workforce training in
infrastructure construction, maintenance, and operations.
(b) Preference for Alaska Native hire in all projects funded
under this Act.

SEC. 5. STANDARDS AND ACCOUNTABILITY.

(a) The Secretary of the Interior shall establish minimum
infrastructure standards for Alaska Native communities.
(b) Annual progress reports to Congress documenting infrastructure
improvements and remaining deficiencies.
```

**Explanation**: Mandatory (not discretionary) appropriations ensure funding stability and remove the annual uncertainty of the appropriations process. The 15-year timeline is aggressive but achievable with sustained investment.

**Potential Challenges**: Mandatory appropriations face resistance from appropriations committees. Total cost of approximately $13 billion over 15 years is significant. Implementation capacity in remote Alaska communities is limited.

**Refinements**: Could include a "trigger" that releases additional funding if climate change accelerates infrastructure degradation.

## State Model Legislation

### Model Alaska Subsistence Constitutional Amendment

**Purpose**: Amend the Alaska Constitution to allow a rural subsistence priority.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. Article VIII of the Constitution of the State of
Alaska is amended by adding a new section to read:

Section [X]. Subsistence Use Priority.

(a) The legislature shall provide for a prior right of
subsistence use of fish and wildlife resources by rural residents
of the state.

(b) For purposes of this section, "subsistence use" means the
customary and traditional uses of wild renewable resources for
direct personal or family consumption as food, shelter, fuel,
clothing, tools, or transportation; for the making and selling
of handicraft articles out of nonedible byproducts of fish and
wildlife resources taken for personal or family consumption; and
for customary trade.

(c) This section does not create an prior right based on race,
national origin, or ancestry.

SECTION 2. This amendment shall take effect upon ratification.
```

**Explanation**: This is modeled on the failed 1999 proposed amendment but clarified to address "racial preference" arguments by explicitly basing the priority on rural residency and customary use, not race.

**Adaptations**: The Alaska Legislature must pass this by two-thirds vote in both chambers before it goes to voters. Multiple versions have been proposed; the key variable is whether the priority is framed as rural-based, need-based, or culturally-based.

## Regulatory Framework

### BIA Land-into-Trust Regulations for Alaska

**Existing Authority**: 25 C.F.R. Part 151; 2017 Secretarial Order

**Draft Regulation**:

Amend 25 C.F.R. Part 151 to:

- Establish expedited processing timelines for Alaska land-into-trust applications (120-day initial review, 1-year decision)
- Reduce documentation requirements for uncontested applications
- Create a presumption in favor of trust acquisition for lands within or adjacent to Alaska Native village areas
- Establish a dedicated Alaska land-into-trust processing unit within BIA

**Explanation**: Current processing times for Alaska land-into-trust applications average 3-5 years. Expedited processing would accelerate tribal jurisdiction development.

### Federal Subsistence Board Regulations

**Existing Authority**: 50 C.F.R. Part 100; ANILCA Title VIII

**Draft Regulation**:

- Formalize integration of traditional ecological knowledge (TEK) into subsistence management decisions
- Establish emergency harvest provisions that allow rapid responses to climate-driven resource declines
- Create community subsistence fishing permits that replace individual permits for village-level harvest management

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Indian Commerce Clause**: Congress has broad authority under the Indian Commerce Clause (Art. I, Sec. 8, Cl. 3) to define Indian Country, regulate tribal jurisdiction, and manage subsistence resources on federal land. *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023) reaffirmed this authority.
- **Equal Protection**: Subsistence priority based on rural residency (not race) has been upheld as a political classification. *Morton v. Mancari*, 417 U.S. 535 (1974), established that legislation benefiting Indians is a political, not racial, classification.
- **Tenth Amendment**: Federal preemption of state fish and game management would face Tenth Amendment challenges, but federal authority over federal lands is well-established.
- **Due Process**: Indian Country designation affecting non-Native landowners within village areas would require clear jurisdictional boundaries and procedural protections.

### Preemption Questions

- Federal subsistence legislation would preempt state management on state lands, an unprecedented step that would face significant legal challenge
- Indian Country designation would create concurrent federal, state, and tribal jurisdiction requiring coordination agreements
- ANCSA corporate land rights must be preserved alongside tribal governmental authority

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- Tribal courts with properly trained judges and adequate resources
- Cross-deputization agreements between tribal, state, and federal law enforcement
- Federal prosecution backstop for cases tribal courts cannot handle
- Annual compliance reporting to Congress

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- 10-year comprehensive review of Indian Country implementation
- Annual progress reports on infrastructure investment
- 5-year review of subsistence co-management board effectiveness

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Alaska Native Sovereignty and Self-Governance Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Boundary manipulation | Village area boundaries could be drawn narrowly, excluding important community lands | High |
| Corporate land exclusion | ANCSA corporate lands within village areas may resist tribal jurisdiction | High |
| State concurrent jurisdiction | State could claim concurrent jurisdiction, undermining tribal authority | Medium |
| Federal prosecution declination | Federal prosecutors may continue declining cases despite tribal jurisdiction | Medium |
| Capacity gaps | Tribes without resources to establish courts could have jurisdiction in name only | High |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| 5-year phase-in delay | Leaves communities without jurisdiction during transition | Political compromise requirement |
| Voluntary boundary expansion | Tribes cannot unilaterally expand village areas | Limits on tribal authority to acquire land |
| Non-Indian fee land | Jurisdiction over non-Indian fee land within village areas is contested | *Montana v. United States* limitations |
| Savings clause breadth | May be interpreted to limit tribal authority in unforeseen ways | Drafting caution to reduce opposition |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Require boundary determination to include all contiguous tribal, corporate, and allotted lands unless the tribal government requests otherwise
2. Establish that Indian Country status applies to all lands within village area boundaries regardless of ownership, consistent with lower 48 precedent
3. Include mandatory federal funding floors for tribal court operations ($200,000 minimum per tribe annually)
4. Create federal ombudsman to resolve state-tribal jurisdictional disputes
5. Require annual reporting on federal prosecution declination rates within Alaska Indian Country

### Alaska Subsistence Protection Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| "Historical and customary" ambiguity | Definition of which communities qualify for priority could be contested | Medium |
| Co-management board capture | Non-Native representatives could block subsistence-favorable decisions | Medium |
| Climate override | Emergency conservation closures could suspend subsistence priority indefinitely | High |
| Customary trade limits | "Non-commercial" restriction on customary trade could be interpreted narrowly | Medium |
| Enforcement gaps | Tribal enforcement authority may be insufficient without federal support | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| State opposition | State may refuse to cooperate with federal management extension | Alaska Constitution conflict |
| Commercial fishing disruption | Economic impacts on commercial fleet from subsistence priority | Genuine resource competition |
| Data gaps | Insufficient data to set scientifically sound subsistence harvest levels for all species | Historical underinvestment in research |
| Climate uncertainty | Management frameworks may not adapt quickly enough to rapid environmental change | Scientific uncertainty |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Define "historical and customary" using existing ADF&G Division of Subsistence community use data, with tribal government certification
2. Require co-management board decisions by simple majority with at least 3 Alaska Native members voting in favor
3. Prohibit conservation closures from disproportionately affecting subsistence users; any closure must reduce all user groups proportionally
4. Define customary trade to include exchange of quantities consistent with documented historical practices
5. Fund annual subsistence resource assessments integrating traditional ecological knowledge with scientific monitoring

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Federal-state conflict | Establish permanent federal-state-tribal coordination committee |
| Funding instability | Mandatory (not discretionary) appropriations for core programs |
| Workforce shortages | Alaska Native workforce development programs with retention incentives |
| Climate acceleration | Built-in adaptive management triggers tied to environmental indicators |
| ANCSA-tribal coordination | Formal coordination requirements in ANCSA amendments |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- All legislation includes 5-year progress reports to Congress
- 10-year comprehensive program evaluation with Government Accountability Office review
- Automatic funding adjustments tied to inflation and cost-of-living indices for rural Alaska
- Sunset provisions only for pilot programs, not core authorities
