# Alaska Natives: Stakeholders

## Overview

The stakeholder landscape for Alaska Native issues is unusually complex, reflecting the intersection of tribal sovereignty, corporate governance, federal trust responsibility, state authority, and resource extraction interests. The ANCSA corporate model created a set of institutional actors -- regional and village corporations -- that exist alongside tribal governments, producing a governance ecosystem unlike any other in Indian Country.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Alaska Native Tribal Governments

**229 federally recognized tribes** exercise governmental authority over their members and, to a limited extent, within their communities.

| Characteristic | Detail |
|----------------|--------|
| Authority | Self-governance, membership, child welfare (ICWA), some regulatory authority |
| Limitations | No Indian Country; limited territorial jurisdiction; underfunded |
| Organization | Most operate with small staffs (1-10 employees); some have no paid staff |
| Key priorities | Sovereignty recognition, subsistence, healthcare, infrastructure |

**Key representatives:**

- Individual tribal councils and first chiefs
- Tanana Chiefs Conference (42 Interior villages)
- Association of Village Council Presidents (56 Yukon-Kuskokwim villages)
- Kawerak, Inc. (20 Bering Strait villages)
- Maniilaq Association (11 Northwest Arctic villages)
- Central Council of Tlingit and Haida Indian Tribes of Alaska (30,000+ members)

### Alaska Native Regional Corporations

**12 regional corporations** (plus a 13th for non-resident Natives) manage ANCSA lands and funds.

| Characteristic | Detail |
|----------------|--------|
| Authority | Land ownership, business operations, subsurface resource rights |
| Combined revenue | $15+ billion annually |
| Shareholders | Approximately 140,000 Alaska Natives |
| Governance | Elected boards of directors; annual shareholder meetings |
| Key priorities | Economic returns, land management, government contracting |

**Interests and tensions:**

- Corporate fiduciary duty to shareholders may conflict with broader community welfare
- Corporations lobby for resource development that some tribal governments oppose
- Corporations provide significant employment, dividends, and scholarships
- Some corporations have diversified into government contracting (8(a) program), reducing dependence on Alaska resource extraction

### Alaska Native Village Corporations

**Over 200 village corporations** manage surface estate lands.

| Characteristic | Detail |
|----------------|--------|
| Authority | Surface land ownership, local economic development |
| Financial status | Ranges from profitable to insolvent; many are marginal |
| Governance | Elected boards; often small organizations |
| Key tensions | Overlap with tribal council authority; surface/subsurface conflicts with regional corporations |

### Alaska Native Individuals and Families

**Approximately 148,000 Alaska Natives** (alone or in combination) are the ultimate stakeholders.

| Subgroup | Key Concerns |
|----------|-------------|
| Rural village residents (~79,000) | Subsistence, infrastructure, healthcare, jobs, cultural preservation |
| Urban Alaska Natives (~69,000) | Services, identity, shareholder rights, cultural connection |
| Elders | Language preservation, cultural transmission, elder care, subsistence |
| Youth | Education, suicide prevention, employment, cultural identity |
| Women | Domestic violence, sexual assault, healthcare, ICWA |
| "After-born" shareholders | Stock rights, corporate governance, enrollment equity |

## Organizational Stakeholders

### Alaska Federation of Natives (AFN)

- **Role**: Statewide umbrella organization representing Alaska Natives on policy issues
- **Membership**: Tribal governments, regional corporations, village corporations, and tribal health organizations
- **Influence**: Annual convention is the largest gathering of Alaska Natives; resolutions guide statewide Native policy advocacy
- **Key positions**: Subsistence protection, infrastructure investment, climate adaptation, tribal sovereignty

### Tribal Health Organizations

- **Alaska Native Tribal Health Consortium (ANTHC)**: Statewide organization providing specialty care, hospital management (ANMC), water/sanitation construction, and health research
- **Regional tribal health organizations**: Seven regional entities providing primary care, behavioral health, and dental services under the Alaska Tribal Health Compact
- **Community Health Aide Program**: Over 550 health aides serving 170+ village clinics; a model for remote healthcare delivery

### Alaska Inter-Tribal Council (AITC)

- Consortium of tribal governments focusing on governance capacity, sovereignty, and inter-tribal coordination
- Advocates for tribal jurisdiction and Indian Country recognition

## Federal Stakeholders

### Bureau of Indian Affairs (BIA)

| Role | Assessment |
|------|------------|
| Trust responsibility | Administers trust lands, provides services to 229 Alaska tribes |
| Alaska Region | Regional office in Juneau; field offices in Anchorage, Fairbanks, Nome, Bethel |
| Funding | Chronic underfunding; Alaska Region receives proportionally less than lower 48 |
| Performance | Bureaucratic delays in land-into-trust applications; consultation often inadequate |

### Indian Health Service (IHS)

| Role | Assessment |
|------|------------|
| Healthcare funding | Provides funding through Alaska Tribal Health Compact |
| Structure | Most IHS services in Alaska are tribally operated (not direct service) |
| Funding adequacy | Estimated at 50% of need; advance appropriations since FY 2024 provide stability |
| Innovation | Alaska's tribal health system is a national model for self-governance |

### Other Federal Agencies

| Agency | Role in Alaska Native Issues |
|--------|------------------------------|
| U.S. Army Corps of Engineers | Erosion protection, village relocation studies |
| Federal Subsistence Board | Manages subsistence on 230 million acres of federal land |
| U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service | Manages National Wildlife Refuges; subsistence co-management |
| Environmental Protection Agency | Village water/sanitation funding; climate adaptation |
| USDA Rural Development | Housing, infrastructure, and economic development programs |
| Denali Commission | Federal-state partnership for rural Alaska infrastructure |
| FEMA | Disaster response; limited applicability to slow-onset climate threats |
| Department of Education | Bureau of Indian Education (BIE) schools; Impact Aid |

### Congressional Delegation

- **Alaska's delegation** (2 Senators, 1 Representative) has outsized influence through seniority and committee assignments
- Historically prioritizes resource development, military, and infrastructure
- Bipartisan support for tribal health funding and infrastructure, with disagreements on subsistence and sovereignty
- Sen. Lisa Murkowski (R) served as chair/ranking member of Senate Energy and Natural Resources Committee and Senate Appropriations Interior subcommittee, making her among the most influential members for Alaska Native issues

## State Stakeholders

### State of Alaska

| Role | Position |
|------|----------|
| Sovereignty | Historically resisted tribal sovereignty; gradually accommodating |
| Subsistence | Cannot implement rural priority due to constitutional prohibition |
| Revenue | Dependent on oil/gas revenue; interests aligned with resource extraction |
| Services | Provides education, public safety, transportation to rural areas |

### Alaska Department of Fish and Game

- Manages fish and wildlife on state lands and navigable waters
- Sets subsistence, sport, and commercial hunting and fishing regulations
- Has been criticized for prioritizing commercial and sport users over subsistence
- Operates Board of Game and Board of Fisheries, which set regulations through public processes where Alaska Natives are underrepresented

### Alaska State Legislature

- Urban majority; rural/Native representation declining with redistricting
- Constitutional amendment for subsistence has repeatedly failed
- Controls state budget, including rural education, transportation, and public safety funding

## Industry Stakeholders

### Oil and Gas Industry

| Company/Interest | Stake |
|-----------------|-------|
| ConocoPhillips, ExxonMobil, Hilcorp | Major operators on North Slope; some operations on ANCSA corporate lands |
| Revenue impact | Oil industry generates ~60% of state general fund revenue |
| Relationship with Alaska Natives | Complex; provides employment and royalties through ANCSA corporations but opposes expanded tribal jurisdiction |
| Political influence | Dominant force in Alaska politics; lobbies against regulations that could affect operations |

### Commercial Fishing Industry

| Interest | Stake |
|----------|-------|
| Revenue | $6 billion industry; largest private employer in Alaska |
| Subsistence conflict | Competes directly with subsistence users for declining salmon stocks |
| Political power | Well-organized lobby; significant campaign contributions |
| Position | Generally opposes expanded subsistence priority; supports "equal access" framing |

### Mining Industry

| Interest | Stake |
|----------|-------|
| Key projects | Donlin Gold (Yukon-Kuskokwim), Pebble Mine (Bristol Bay -- rejected), Ambler Mining District |
| ANCSA connection | Mining on or near ANCSA lands generates revenue for regional corporations |
| Tribal opposition | Many tribal governments oppose mining projects that threaten subsistence resources |
| Regulatory environment | Federal permitting process is the primary review mechanism due to federal land ownership |

## Power Analysis

### Most Powerful Stakeholders

1. **Alaska's congressional delegation**: Controls federal funding, legislation, and policy direction
2. **Oil and gas industry**: Dominates state politics through revenue dependence and lobbying
3. **ANCSA regional corporations**: Combined economic power, lobbying capacity, and 8(a) government contracting influence
4. **State of Alaska**: Regulatory authority, land management, and political opposition to tribal sovereignty

### Least Powerful Stakeholders

1. **Rural village residents**: Small populations, geographic isolation, limited resources for advocacy
2. **Village corporations**: Many are financially marginal with limited political influence
3. **Alaska Native youth**: Highest-need population with least political voice
4. **Alaska Native women**: Experience highest rates of violence with least legal protection
