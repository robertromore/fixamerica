# Alaska Natives: Current State

### Corporation Performance Issues

Despite aggregate revenue exceeding $15 billion annually, significant disparities exist:

- **Dividend distribution**: Annual dividends range from under $500 to over $3,000 per shareholder depending on the corporation
- **Shareholder employment**: Corporations employ only a fraction of their shareholders; many jobs are located outside the region
- **Land management**: Village corporations control surface estate while regional corporations hold subsurface rights, creating conflicts over resource extraction
- **Governance concerns**: Some corporations face shareholder lawsuits over transparency, executive compensation, and fiduciary duties
- **Non-Native enrollment**: ANCSA originally limited enrollment to Natives born before December 18, 1971; the 1991 amendments allowed new shares for "Natives born after" (after-borns), but implementation varies

### Village Corporations

Over 200 village corporations manage surface estate lands and local economic development:

- Many village corporations are financially marginal, with limited revenue streams
- Several have merged with their regional corporation
- Land conveyances to village corporations remain incomplete in some regions
- Conflict between village corporation (economic) and tribal council (governmental) authority is common

## Subsistence Rights Framework

### Legal Structure

| Authority | Coverage | Key Provisions |
|-----------|----------|---------------|
| Alaska National Interest Lands Conservation Act (ANILCA), Title VIII (1980) | Federal public lands in Alaska | Priority for rural residents for subsistence uses of fish and wildlife |
| Alaska Constitution, Art. VIII | State lands and waters | Common-use doctrine; no rural preference permitted |
| Federal Subsistence Board | Federal public lands and waters | Manages subsistence on 230 million acres of federal land |
| Alaska Board of Game / Board of Fisheries | State lands and navigable waters | Sets seasons, bag limits; no rural priority |
| Katie John decisions | Navigable waters | Extended federal subsistence jurisdiction to navigable waters within/adjacent to federal lands |

### Current Conflicts

- **Dual management**: The federal and state systems operate independently with different rules, seasons, and priorities, creating confusion and enforcement problems
- **Rural vs. urban divide**: Federal law provides a rural subsistence priority, but the Alaska Constitution prohibits preferential treatment, meaning the state cannot implement a rural preference
- **Declining fish and wildlife**: Climate change, commercial overharvesting, and habitat loss reduce subsistence resources
- **Enforcement**: Alaska State Troopers and federal agents sometimes cite Alaska Natives for subsistence harvests that are legal under federal law but not state law
- **Customary trade**: Traditional exchange of subsistence foods faces regulatory restrictions

## Recent Developments (2024-2025)

- **Newtok relocation**: Approximately 40% of Newtok residents have relocated to the new site at Mertarvik; construction continues but funding gaps persist
- **Yukon River salmon crisis**: Federal and state emergency closures of chinook and chum salmon subsistence fishing continued through 2024; Alaska Federation of Natives declared food security emergency
- **Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act**: Approximately $3.5 billion in tribal infrastructure funding, with Alaska receiving a disproportionate share for broadband, water, and transportation
- **ANCSA 7(i) revenue-sharing disputes**: Ongoing litigation over how regional corporations share natural resource revenues
- **Donlin Gold Mine**: Proposed gold mine in Yukon-Kuskokwim region continues environmental review; significant opposition from downstream villages over water quality concerns
