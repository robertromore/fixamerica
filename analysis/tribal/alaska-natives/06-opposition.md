# Alaska Natives: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to reforms benefiting Alaska Natives comes from multiple directions, often reflecting genuine competing interests rather than simple hostility. The most significant opposition centers on three axes: resistance to expanded tribal sovereignty, defense of commercial and sport interests against subsistence priority, and protection of resource extraction from tribal regulation. Understanding these opposition forces is essential for crafting viable reform strategies.

## Opposition to Tribal Sovereignty Expansion

### State of Alaska

**Position**: The state has historically opposed recognizing Alaska tribes as having the same sovereign authority as lower 48 tribes, particularly regarding territorial jurisdiction and Indian Country status.

**Key arguments:**

- Alaska's unique geography and settlement patterns make Indian Country designations impractical; there are no traditional reservations (except Metlakatla)
- Recognizing Indian Country across Alaska would create jurisdictional chaos by subjecting non-Native residents and businesses to tribal authority
- State regulatory authority -- particularly over natural resources, land use, and public safety -- would be fragmented
- The state has a legitimate interest in uniform application of its laws across all communities

**Actual motivations:**

- Tribal jurisdiction could subject oil, gas, and mining operations to tribal environmental regulation and taxation
- Indian Country recognition would reduce state authority over land management and resource extraction
- Political calculation: rural Alaska voters are a small minority; opposing tribal sovereignty carries little electoral cost in urban Alaska

**Key actors:**

- Governor's office (positions vary by administration but generally resistant)
- Alaska Department of Law (has litigated against tribal sovereignty)
- Alaska Legislature (urban majority generally unsympathetic)

**Effectiveness**: High. The state's opposition has been a significant factor in the *Venetie* decision, resistance to land-into-trust applications, and failure to amend the constitution for subsistence.

### Resource Extraction Industry

**Position**: Oil, gas, and mining companies oppose expanded tribal sovereignty that could subject their operations to tribal regulation, taxation, or environmental review.

**Key arguments:**

- Tribal regulation would add a layer of bureaucracy to already complex permitting processes
- Inconsistent tribal standards could create uncertainty for investment
- Existing federal and state regulation is adequate to protect Alaska Native interests

**Actual motivations:**

- Tribal environmental regulation could impose stricter standards than federal or state requirements
- Tribal taxation of resource extraction would reduce profits
- Tribal consent requirements could delay or block projects
- The current system, where ANCSA corporations can negotiate directly with industry without tribal governmental oversight, is more favorable to extraction interests

**Key actors:**

- Alaska Oil and Gas Association (AOGA)
- Resource Development Council of Alaska (RDC)
- Individual operators (ConocoPhillips, Hilcorp, ExxonMobil)
- Alaska Miners Association

**Effectiveness**: Very high. The resource extraction industry is the most powerful lobbying force in Alaska and has significant influence over the state's congressional delegation.

## Opposition to Subsistence Priority

### Commercial Fishing Industry

**Position**: Opposes a rural or Native subsistence priority, arguing for equal access to fish and wildlife resources.

**Key arguments:**

- The Alaska Constitution guarantees equal access to fish and wildlife for all Alaskans
- A subsistence priority would restrict commercial fishing, threatening livelihoods and the state economy
- Modern management can balance all uses without giving preference to any group
- Subsistence needs can be met through allocation within the existing regulatory framework

**Actual motivations:**

- Commercial fishing generates $6 billion annually; any reduction in commercial allocation directly affects revenue
- A legal subsistence priority would shift the burden of conservation closures to commercial and sport users
- The industry's political influence depends on framing fisheries as a shared resource, not a Native right

**Key actors:**

- United Fishermen of Alaska (UFA)
- Alaska Trollers Association
- North Pacific Fisheries Association
- Individual processors (Trident Seafoods, Peter Pan Seafoods)

**Effectiveness**: High. The commercial fishing industry has successfully blocked constitutional amendments for subsistence priority and maintains strong influence on the Board of Fisheries.

### Sport Hunting and Fishing Organizations

**Position**: Oppose subsistence priority as discriminatory; advocate for equal access.

**Key arguments:**

- All Alaskans should have equal rights to harvest fish and wildlife regardless of where they live
- Rural preference is a "racial preference" that violates equal protection principles (despite subsistence being based on residency, not race)
- Conservation can be achieved without prioritizing one user group

**Actual motivations:**

- Sport hunting and fishing tourism generates significant revenue ($1.5 billion annually)
- Guides and outfitters would lose access and revenue if subsistence users receive priority during shortages
- Urban and suburban sport users are a large voting bloc

**Key actors:**

- Safari Club International (Alaska chapter)
- Alaska Outdoor Council
- Alaska Professional Hunters Association
- Individual guides and outfitters

**Effectiveness**: Moderate to high. These organizations are well-organized, well-funded, and represent a significant voting constituency in Anchorage and Fairbanks.

## Opposition to ANCSA Reform

### ANCSA Corporate Leadership

**Position**: Many (not all) ANCSA corporate leaders oppose structural reforms that would shift authority from corporations to tribal governments.

**Key arguments:**

- ANCSA corporations have generated billions in revenue and created thousands of jobs
- The corporate model provides economic benefits that tribal governments cannot replicate
- Shareholders chose the corporate model and should determine its future through corporate governance, not congressional legislation
- Reforms risk destabilizing successful corporations and harming shareholders

**Actual motivations:**

- Corporate boards and executives have institutional interest in maintaining their authority and compensation
- Shifting land or authority to tribal governments could reduce corporate asset bases
- Some corporate leaders genuinely believe the corporate model serves Alaska Natives better than governmental alternatives
- The 8(a) government contracting program provides billions in revenue; changes to corporate structure could jeopardize 8(a) eligibility

**Key actors:**

- ANCSA Regional Association (represents 12 regional corporations)
- Individual regional corporation boards and executives
- Some village corporation leaders

**Effectiveness**: Moderate. Corporate leaders have significant political influence but do not represent a unified position -- some corporations support expanded tribal authority.

### Political Conservatives

**Position**: Oppose creating new Indian Country or expanding tribal sovereignty in Alaska as an expansion of federal power and a threat to private property rights.

**Key arguments:**

- Indian Country creates "separate nations" within the United States, undermining national unity
- Tribal sovereignty reduces individual property rights and economic freedom
- Federal trust land removes property from the tax base
- Self-determination is best achieved through private enterprise (the ANCSA model), not government expansion

**Actual motivations:**

- Philosophical opposition to collective governance and communal land ownership
- Concern that tribal jurisdiction could restrict resource extraction
- Political alignment with state's rights advocates who view tribal sovereignty as a federal encroachment on state authority

**Effectiveness**: Moderate. Conservative opposition is strongest in Congress, where it can block legislation, and in the Alaska Legislature.

## Opposition to Climate Adaptation and Village Relocation

### Fiscal Conservatives

**Position**: Oppose the scale of federal spending required for village relocation (estimated at $200-$400 million per village).

**Key arguments:**

- Relocating small communities (populations of 100-600) is not cost-effective
- Residents should relocate individually to existing communities
- Federal spending priorities should focus on larger populations
- Climate adaptation can be achieved through incremental measures short of full relocation

**Actual motivations:**

- General opposition to federal spending increases
- Reluctance to establish precedent for climate relocation that could expand to other communities
- Cost-benefit analysis that values efficiency over community preservation

**Key actors:**

- Heritage Foundation, Cato Institute (national policy organizations)
- Some members of Congress on appropriations committees
- Taxpayer advocacy groups

**Effectiveness**: Moderate. Has contributed to the failure to establish a comprehensive village relocation program.

### Climate Change Skeptics

**Position**: Oppose framing village threats as climate-related, arguing that erosion and flooding are natural processes.

**Key arguments:**

- Coastal erosion has always occurred in Alaska
- Attributing village threats to climate change is politically motivated
- Villages were poorly sited originally

**Effectiveness**: Declining, as the physical evidence of accelerated erosion and permafrost thaw is overwhelming. However, this position still influences some members of Congress and state legislators.

## Opposition Tactics

| Tactic | Used By | Example |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Litigation | State of Alaska, industry | *Venetie* case; challenges to subsistence regulations |
| Constitutional framing | Sport/commercial users | "Equal access" framing of subsistence as racial preference |
| Lobbying | Oil/gas, commercial fishing | Campaign contributions, revolving door with regulatory agencies |
| Regulatory capture | Industry | Board of Game and Board of Fisheries appointments favoring commercial/sport interests |
| Cost-benefit arguments | Fiscal conservatives | Arguing village relocation is too expensive per capita |
| Divide and conquer | Various | Exploiting tensions between tribal governments and ANCSA corporations |
| Delay | Federal/state agencies | Prolonging land-into-trust applications, environmental reviews, relocation studies |
| Narrative control | Multiple | Framing subsistence as a "lifestyle choice" rather than a right; framing sovereignty as "separatism" |

### Vulnerabilities

- **Public opinion**: National public opinion generally supports Native rights and climate adaptation; opposition relies on keeping Alaska Native issues below the national radar
- **Corporate fractures**: Not all ANCSA corporations oppose tribal sovereignty; some have partnered with tribal governments
- **Resource decline**: As fish and wildlife populations decline, the "no priority needed" argument weakens
- **Climate urgency**: Physical destruction of villages makes opposition to relocation funding increasingly untenable
- **Legal evolution**: Federal court decisions and administrative actions (land-into-trust) are gradually expanding tribal sovereignty regardless of state opposition
