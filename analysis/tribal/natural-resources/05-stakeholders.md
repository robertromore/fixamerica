# Natural Resources: Stakeholders

## Overview

Tribal natural resource management involves a complex web of stakeholders with competing interests. Tribal nations assert sovereignty over resources on their lands; the federal government holds trust responsibility; state governments claim jurisdiction over water and wildlife; energy and extraction companies seek access; and environmental organizations pursue conservation goals that may or may not align with tribal priorities. Understanding these relationships is essential for effective resource policy.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Tribal Nations

**Role**: Sovereign resource owners with treaty-reserved rights

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Number affected | All 574 federally recognized tribes have some resource interests |
| Energy-producing tribes | ~85 tribes with significant energy or mineral resources (DOE, 2023) |
| Water-dependent tribes | All tribes, with ~100+ having unresolved water rights claims (CRS, 2024) |
| Forest-managing tribes | ~100 tribes with commercial forestry operations (BIA, 2023) |
| Treaty fishing tribes | ~50 tribes with treaty-reserved fishing rights |

**Interests**:

- Sovereignty over resource development decisions
- Fair revenue from resource extraction
- Environmental protection of lands, water, and culturally significant areas
- Economic development and self-sufficiency
- Free, prior, and informed consent (FPIC) for projects affecting tribal resources
- Climate adaptation resources

### Tribal Members Living on Reservations

**Role**: Direct beneficiaries and those most affected by resource decisions

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Population | Approximately 1.1 million people live on reservations (Census, 2020) |
| Poverty rate | 25.4% on reservations vs. 11.5% nationally (Census ACS, 2022) |
| Water access | 48% of tribal homes lack reliable clean water (IHS, 2023) |
| Subsistence dependence | Many communities depend on fish, game, and gathered foods for subsistence |

**Interests**:

- Clean water, air, and land
- Employment from resource-related industries
- Revenue sharing from resource development
- Protection of subsistence resources (fish, wildlife, traditional foods)
- Health protections from environmental contamination
- Infrastructure development funded by resource revenue

### Bureau of Indian Affairs (BIA)

**Role**: Federal trustee for tribal natural resources

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Trust Natural Resources budget | $245 million (FY 2024) |
| Staff | Approximately 2,500 natural resource staff (BIA, 2023) |
| Responsibilities | Lease approval, forestry, water, agriculture, minerals management |
| Limitations | Chronic understaffing, outdated regulations, bureaucratic inertia |

**Interests**:

- Fulfill trust obligations within budget constraints
- Maintain regulatory authority over resource decisions
- Avoid litigation and liability
- Balance tribal interests with broader federal policy objectives

### Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) -- Tribal Programs

**Role**: Environmental regulator in Indian Country

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Tribal program budget | $120 million (FY 2024) |
| Treatment as State (TAS) tribes | ~100 tribes with TAS authority for at least one program (EPA, 2023) |
| Cleanup sites | 900+ contaminated sites on or near tribal lands |
| Staff dedicated to tribal programs | ~178 (EPA, FY 2024) |

**Interests**:

- Environmental protection and compliance
- Building tribal regulatory capacity
- Cleanup of contaminated sites
- Water quality standards enforcement

## Secondary Stakeholders

### Energy and Extraction Industries

| Industry | Presence on Tribal Lands | Key Companies |
|----------|------------------------|---------------|
| Oil and gas | ~4,200 active leases | Continental Resources, Slawson Exploration, various independents |
| Coal | Major mines on Navajo, Crow lands | Peabody Energy, Navajo Transitional Energy Company (tribal-owned) |
| Uranium | Legacy contamination; limited current activity | Historical: Kerr-McGee, now Tronox Incorporated |
| Renewable energy | Growing presence | Various developers partnering with tribes |

**Interests**:

- Access to tribal resources at competitive rates
- Regulatory certainty and expedited permitting
- Long-term lease stability
- Favorable royalty rates

### State Governments

**Role**: Competing resource claimants, particularly for water and wildlife

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Water rights conflicts | States allocate water under prior appropriation; tribal reserved rights may supersede state allocations |
| Wildlife management | States manage fish and game on non-tribal lands; treaty rights create shared jurisdiction |
| Tax competition | States seek to tax resource activity on tribal lands; tribes resist |
| Environmental regulation | States may lack jurisdiction on tribal land but have authority on adjacent lands |

**Interests**:

- Protect state water allocations from tribal claims
- Maintain state wildlife management authority
- Tax resource activity within state borders
- Control environmental regulation

### Environmental Organizations

| Organization | Focus |
|-------------|-------|
| Sierra Club | Conservation, clean energy, anti-extraction |
| Earthjustice | Legal advocacy for environmental protection |
| Natural Resources Defense Council | Water quality, clean energy, environmental justice |
| Indigenous Environmental Network | Indigenous-led environmental justice |
| NDN Collective | Indigenous rights, environmental justice, climate |
| Honor the Earth | Indigenous environmental advocacy (Winona LaDuke) |

**Interests**:

- Environmental protection on and near tribal lands
- Opposition to fossil fuel extraction
- Support for renewable energy development
- Environmental justice for frontline communities
- May sometimes conflict with tribal economic development priorities when tribes choose resource extraction

| Agency | Role | Relationship to Tribes |
|--------|------|----------------------|
| Bureau of Reclamation | Water settlement implementation, dam operations | Implements water settlements; operates dams affecting tribal water |
| Army Corps of Engineers | Water infrastructure, permitting (Section 404) | Issues permits for projects affecting tribal water; consultation required |
| DOE Office of Indian Energy | Technical assistance, grants for tribal energy | Supports tribal energy development; relatively small budget |

| U.S. Forest Service / BLM | Adjacent federal land management | Manages lands surrounding reservations; affects tribal resources |

| Stakeholder | Role |
|------------|------|
| Tribal colleges and universities | Natural resource management training, local research |
| Land-grant universities | Extension services, forestry, and agricultural research |
| National laboratories (DOE) | Energy research, environmental monitoring |

| User Type | Conflict Area |
|-----------|--------------|
| Agricultural irrigators | Compete for water allocated under state systems that may conflict with tribal reserved rights |

## Stakeholder Power Analysis

| Stakeholder | Interest Level | Power Level | Relationship |
|------------|---------------|-------------|--------------|
| Tribal nations | Very high | Moderate (sovereignty + limited resources) | Resource owners with treaty rights but limited enforcement capacity |
| BIA | Moderate | High | Trustee with regulatory authority |
| Congress | Variable | Very high | Controls appropriations, authorization, and can modify tribal rights |
| State governments | High | High | Control water allocation, wildlife management; political influence |
| Energy industry | High | High | Financial resources, lobbying power, market influence |
| Environmental organizations | High | Moderate | Legal expertise, public advocacy, but may conflict with tribal priorities |
| EPA | Moderate | Moderate | Environmental authority but limited tribal-specific resources |
| DOE | Moderate | Low-Moderate | Technical assistance and grants; small budget |
| General public | Low | Low (individually) | Broad but diffuse interest in environmental and energy issues |

## Key Stakeholder Conflicts

### Tribal Sovereignty vs. Federal Trusteeship

The central conflict in tribal natural resources: tribes seek control over their own resources, while the federal government retains management authority under the trust relationship. This conflict plays out in:

- Lease approval processes (BIA vs. tribal authority)
- Resource management standards (federal regulations vs. tribal codes)
- Revenue management (federal collection and distribution vs. tribal self-management)
- Environmental regulation (EPA vs. tribal regulatory programs)

### Tribal Rights vs. State Claims

Water rights represent the most contentious area of tribal-state conflict:

- State prior appropriation systems allocated tribal water to non-Indian users for over a century before *Winters* rights were seriously enforced
- States resist tribal water settlements that reduce allocations to existing non-Indian users
- Wildlife management conflicts arise when treaty-reserved hunting and fishing rights extend to off-reservation lands managed by states

### Economic Development vs. Environmental Protection

This tension exists both between tribes and outside actors and within tribal communities:

- Some tribal members want resource extraction jobs and revenue; others want environmental protection
- Environmental organizations sometimes oppose tribal resource development decisions, creating tension with sovereignty principles
- The energy transition creates both threats (to fossil fuel-dependent tribal economies) and opportunities (renewable energy development)

### Industry vs. Tribal Consent

Energy and extraction companies seek access to tribal resources, while tribes increasingly demand FPIC:

- Companies prefer regulatory certainty and expedited permitting; FPIC introduces uncertainty
- Historical pattern of below-market leases creates distrust
- Standing Rock (Dakota Access Pipeline) epitomized this conflict; pipeline was rerouted from Bismarck, ND (majority white) to near tribal land
