# Natural Resources: Overview

## Executive Summary

Natural resource management on tribal lands represents a critical intersection of sovereignty, economic development, environmental justice, and cultural preservation. Indigenous peoples of the United States hold reserved rights to water, minerals, timber, fish, wildlife, and other natural resources through treaties, executive orders, and federal law. These rights, affirmed by centuries of legal precedent, are essential to tribal self-determination and economic self-sufficiency. Yet the federal trust responsibility for managing tribal natural resources has been marked by chronic mismanagement, underfunding, and exploitation that has cost tribal nations billions of dollars in lost revenue and environmental degradation.

Tribal lands contain extraordinary natural wealth. Indian Country encompasses an estimated 30% of the nation's coal reserves west of the Mississippi, 20% of known oil and gas reserves, significant deposits of uranium and other minerals, and vast timber, water, and renewable energy resources (Department of the Interior, 2023). The 56.2 million acres of trust land include some of the most ecologically significant landscapes in North America, from old-growth forests to critical watersheds to biodiversity hotspots. Despite this wealth, tribal communities experience some of the highest poverty rates in the nation -- 25.4% compared to the national average of 11.5% (Census ACS, 2022) -- in part because federal management of tribal resources has historically prioritized outside interests over tribal benefit.

Water rights represent the most consequential and contested resource issue in Indian Country. The *Winters* doctrine, established in *Winters v. United States*, 207 U.S. 564 (1908), reserved water rights for tribes sufficient to fulfill the purposes of their reservations. Yet quantifying and enforcing these rights has proven extraordinarily difficult, with negotiated settlements often requiring decades to complete. The Supreme Court's 2023 decision in *Arizona v. Navajo Nation* -- ruling that the federal government has no affirmative duty to assess the Navajo Nation's water needs under the 1868 treaty -- demonstrated the continuing vulnerability of tribal water rights.

Addressing tribal natural resource issues requires honoring treaty-reserved resource rights, reforming the trust management system to give tribes greater control, adequately funding resource management programs, ensuring free, prior, and informed consent for resource development on and near tribal lands, and confronting the disproportionate environmental burdens imposed on Indigenous communities.

## Core Tensions

- **Economic development vs. environmental protection**: Tribes need revenue but resource extraction can damage land, water, and cultural sites
- **Tribal sovereignty vs. federal management**: The trust responsibility gives the federal government management authority that often overrides tribal preferences
- **Treaty rights vs. state interests**: Reserved water and resource rights frequently conflict with state-granted rights to non-Indian users
- **Energy transition vs. tribal economies**: Some tribes depend on fossil fuel revenue; the energy transition threatens these economies while creating renewable energy opportunities
- **Free, prior, and informed consent vs. federal project approval**: Tribes demand the right to consent to or reject resource development, but federal law does not always guarantee this right
- **Cultural preservation vs. commercial use**: Some resources (water, certain landscapes, wildlife) have cultural and spiritual significance that may conflict with commercial development

## Key Questions

1. **How should tribal water rights be quantified and enforced?** What legal and political mechanisms can ensure tribes receive their reserved water, and what does *Arizona v. Navajo Nation* mean for future claims?

2. **Is the trust management system serving tribal interests?** How has federal management of tribal resources performed, and what reforms would give tribes greater control?

3. **How can tribes benefit from the energy transition?** What is the renewable energy potential on tribal lands, and how can tribes participate equitably in the transition?

4. **What is the scope of environmental contamination in Indian Country?** How many sites are contaminated, what are the health effects, and who bears responsibility for cleanup?

5. **Are treaty-reserved fishing and hunting rights secure?** How do treaty rights interact with state wildlife management, and what threats do they face?

6. **What does free, prior, and informed consent mean in practice?** Should tribes have veto power over resource development on and near their lands, and how should this right be implemented?

7. **How is climate change affecting tribal resources?** What are the specific impacts, and what adaptation and mitigation resources are available?

## Vision of Success

A reformed tribal natural resource system would feature:

- Tribes exercising full sovereign authority over natural resource development decisions on their lands
- All tribal water rights quantified, adjudicated, and delivered, with infrastructure to use them
- Federal trust management reformed to give tribes direct control over their resources, with adequate technical and financial support
- Renewable energy development providing sustainable revenue streams for tribal communities
- Environmental contamination on tribal lands fully cleaned up, with responsible parties bearing the costs
- Treaty-reserved hunting and fishing rights honored and protected by federal and state governments
- Free, prior, and informed consent recognized as the standard for any development affecting tribal lands or resources
- Climate adaptation funding commensurate with the disproportionate impacts on tribal communities
