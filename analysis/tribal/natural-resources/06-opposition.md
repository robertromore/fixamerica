# Natural Resources: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to tribal natural resource sovereignty is more organized and consequential than opposition to many other tribal policy issues. Control of water, minerals, energy, and land involves billions of dollars in economic value and directly affects powerful interest groups: energy companies, state governments, agricultural water users, and development interests. Unlike language preservation, where opposition is largely passive, natural resource issues generate active, well-funded resistance from stakeholders who stand to lose economically or politically from recognition of tribal resource rights.

## Organized Opposition

### State Governments

State governments are among the most consistent opponents of expanded tribal resource sovereignty, particularly on water rights:

**Key Organizations and Actors**:

- Western Governors' Association (water policy)
- State attorneys general (water litigation)
- State fish and wildlife agencies (treaty fishing/hunting)
- State legislatures (taxation, regulation)

**Arguments**:

- Tribal water rights threaten existing state water allocations
- State-granted water rights create reliance interests that should not be disrupted
- States should have concurrent jurisdiction over environmental regulation on tribal lands
- Tribal tax exemptions create unfair competition for resource-related businesses

**Activities**:

- Litigation challenging tribal water rights quantification (ongoing in multiple states)
- *Oklahoma v. Castro-Huerta* (2022): Oklahoma successfully argued for state criminal jurisdiction in Indian Country, with implications for regulatory jurisdiction over resources
- State opposition to land-into-trust applications that would expand tribal resource jurisdiction
- Lobbying against federal legislation that would preempt state authority

**Impact**: State opposition is particularly significant because water settlements require state participation and political support, and because states control regulatory frameworks that affect resource development near tribal lands.

### Energy and Extraction Industry

**Key Organizations**:

- American Petroleum Institute (API)
- National Mining Association
- Independent Petroleum Association of America
- State-level oil and gas associations

**Arguments**:

- Federal approval processes for tribal energy leases are too slow and should be streamlined (in industry's favor, not necessarily tribal sovereignty's)
- Tribal royalty rates should remain competitive (i.e., not increased to market rates)
- FPIC requirements would create regulatory uncertainty and deter investment
- Environmental regulations on tribal lands should not exceed those on comparable private or federal lands

**Activities**:

- Lobbying against increased tribal royalty rates
- Supporting BIA lease approval processes that maintain federal (rather than tribal) control
- Opposing tribal environmental regulations that exceed federal standards
- In some cases, supporting ITEDSA reforms that expedite development (aligning with tribal economic interests but not necessarily environmental interests)

### Agricultural and Water Users

**Key Organizations**:

- National Water Resources Association
- Western Growers Association
- State farm bureaus
- Irrigation districts

**Arguments**:

- Senior state water rights should not be subordinated to tribal reserved rights
- Water settlements should not reduce existing allocations
- Agricultural users have invested in infrastructure based on state water rights
- Municipal growth requires water security that tribal claims threaten

**Activities**:

- Opposing water settlement legislation that reduces non-Indian allocations
- Lobbying for legislative protections of existing state water rights
- Intervening in water rights adjudications to minimize tribal allocations
- Seeking to limit the application of the homeland purpose standard for water quantification

### Property Rights Organizations

**Key Organizations**:

- Citizens Equal Rights Alliance (CERA)
- Pacific Legal Foundation
- Mountain States Legal Foundation

**Arguments**:

- Expanding tribal jurisdiction over resources violates equal protection
- Land-into-trust acquisitions remove property from state and local tax rolls
- Tribal sovereignty should not extend to regulate non-Indian fee land within reservations
- FPIC requirements infringe on private property rights of landowners within and adjacent to reservations

**Activities**:

- Litigation challenging land-into-trust acquisitions (*Carcieri v. Salazar* type challenges)
- Opposing tribal environmental regulations that affect non-Indian fee land
- Lobbying against expansion of tribal regulatory jurisdiction
- Supporting state jurisdiction over resources on fee land within reservations

## Ideological Opposition

### Anti-Regulatory Conservatives

**Arguments**:

- Federal trust management should be reduced, not reformed
- Tribes should manage their own resources without federal involvement (may overlap with pro-sovereignty position but for different reasons)
- Environmental regulations on tribal lands should be minimized
- Market forces, not government programs, should determine resource development

**Tension**: Some anti-regulatory conservatives simultaneously oppose federal management (which restricts tribal autonomy) and tribal regulation (which might be more stringent than federal standards). This creates inconsistency: they support tribal autonomy when it means less regulation but oppose it when tribes impose stronger environmental protections.

### Fiscal Conservatives

**Arguments**:

- Water settlement funding represents new federal spending obligations
- Trust management reform should reduce costs, not increase them
- Climate adaptation funding for tribal communities should come from existing programs
- Resource management should be self-sustaining through resource revenue

**Impact**: Fiscal opposition to water settlement funding is a primary reason that $2.2 billion in authorized settlements remain unfunded (CRS, 2024).

## Implicit Opposition

### Institutional Inertia

| Institution | Form of Opposition | Effect |
|------------|-------------------|--------|
| BIA bureaucracy | Slow processing, outdated regulations, resistance to tribal self-governance | Delays resource development; maintains federal control |
| ONRR | Inadequate auditing, delayed payments | Tribes receive less revenue than owed |
| EPA | Slow TAS approvals, insufficient tribal program funding | Tribal environmental capacity remains limited |
| Army Corps of Engineers | Infrastructure permits delayed, consultation perfunctory | Water infrastructure projects stalled |
| OMB | Treats resource management as discretionary spending | Annual budget uncertainty |

### Legal Precedent Erosion

Recent Supreme Court decisions have narrowed tribal resource rights:

| Case | Year | Impact |
|------|------|--------|
| *Arizona v. Navajo Nation* | 2023 | No federal duty to assess or secure tribal water needs |
| *Sackett v. EPA* | 2023 | Narrowed Clean Water Act wetland protections |
| *Oklahoma v. Castro-Huerta* | 2022 | State jurisdiction in Indian Country; potential resource regulation implications |
| *Montana v. United States* | 1981 | Limited tribal civil jurisdiction over non-Indians on fee land; affects resource regulation |

## Opposition Arguments and Responses

| Argument | Response |
|----------|----------|
| "Tribal water rights threaten existing users" | Tribal rights are legally senior; settlements provide compensation for junior rights holders through federal funding |
| "FPIC will stop energy development" | FPIC ensures community consent; projects with community support proceed more smoothly than contested projects |
| "Tribal regulations create uncertainty" | Regulatory certainty requires clear jurisdictional rules, which tribal authority can provide; the current patchwork is more uncertain |
| "Water settlements are too expensive" | Unfunded settlements generate ongoing litigation costs; settlements are far cheaper than judicial adjudication |
| "Tribes should develop resources through the market" | Trust land restrictions prevent market-based development; reform must address structural barriers |
| "Environmental cleanup should follow normal EPA processes" | Normal processes have failed -- 523 unremediated uranium mines on Navajo Nation alone demonstrate the inadequacy of standard approaches |

## Political Dynamics

### Congressional Landscape

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Western delegation influence | Senators from water-scarce states face constituent pressure to protect existing allocations |
| Energy state delegations | Members from energy-producing states balance industry and tribal interests |
| Appropriations resistance | Water settlement funding competes with other priorities |
| Bipartisan potential | Water infrastructure has bipartisan support; energy development has cross-party appeal |
| Committee jurisdiction | Senate Indian Affairs is supportive; Energy and Natural Resources has broader stakeholder pressures |

### Industry Campaign Contributions

| Sector | Annual Lobbying (2023) | Key Targets | Source |
|--------|----------------------|-------------|--------|
| Oil and gas | $124 million | Energy committee members, appropriators | OpenSecrets, 2023 |
| Mining | $31 million | Interior, EPA appropriations | OpenSecrets, 2023 |
| Agriculture/water | $168 million | Agriculture, water committees | OpenSecrets, 2023 |

The disparity between industry lobbying resources and tribal advocacy capacity is significant. NCAI's total annual budget is approximately $12 million (NCAI, 2023), a fraction of any single industry's lobbying expenditure.

## Who Benefits from the Status Quo

| Beneficiary | How |
|-------------|-----|
| Energy companies | Below-market lease rates; slow federal process maintains status quo favorable to existing leases |
| State water users | Continue using water allocated under state systems that did not account for tribal rights |
| State governments | Retain jurisdiction and tax authority that would be reduced by expanded tribal sovereignty |
| BIA bureaucracy | Maintains institutional relevance and regulatory authority |
| Adjacent landowners | Avoid tribal environmental regulation and tax jurisdiction |
