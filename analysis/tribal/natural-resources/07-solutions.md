# Natural Resources: Solutions

## Overview

Reforming tribal natural resource management requires addressing the legal, institutional, and structural root causes identified in this analysis. Solutions must simultaneously honor treaty-reserved resource rights, transfer management authority from federal agencies to tribal governments, adequately fund resource programs, ensure environmental protection, and support tribes in navigating the energy transition. The guiding principle is resource sovereignty: tribal nations should exercise the same authority over their natural resources that other governments exercise over theirs.

## Solution Categories

### 1. Resolve Tribal Water Rights

**Problem Addressed**: Over 100 tribes have unresolved water rights claims; $2.2 billion in authorized settlements remain unfunded; *Arizona v. Navajo Nation* limited judicial enforcement

**Proposal**: Complete all tribal water rights settlements within 15 years and fully fund existing authorized settlements

| Component | Detail | Estimated Cost |
|-----------|--------|---------------|
| Fund authorized settlements | Appropriate $2.2 billion for existing unfunded settlement provisions | $2.2 billion (one-time) |
| Expedite pending settlements | Establish a Federal-Tribal Water Settlement Commission to facilitate negotiations | $50 million/year |
| Water infrastructure | Fund delivery systems so that tribes can put water rights to beneficial use | $5 billion over 10 years |
| Groundwater protections | Extend *Winters* doctrine protections to groundwater sources on tribal lands | Legislation (minimal cost) |
| Settlement completion target | All viable settlements completed by 2040 | Variable |

**Evidence**: Completed settlements have delivered tangible benefits: the Navajo-Gallup Water Supply Project (authorized 2009) will provide clean water to 250,000 people when completed (Bureau of Reclamation, 2023). The cost of settlements is far less than indefinite litigation.

### 2. Transfer Resource Management Authority to Tribes

**Problem Addressed**: Federal trust management has been chronically inadequate; BIA approval processes delay development; *Cobell* documented systemic mismanagement

**Proposal**: Expand HEARTH Act and ITEDSA models to give tribes direct control over all resource management decisions

| Component | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Universal HEARTH authority | Extend HEARTH Act surface lease authority to all tribes (currently 56 have approved regulations) |
| Expanded ITEDSA | Simplify Tribal Energy Resource Agreements to increase uptake; currently underutilized |
| Forestry self-governance | Allow tribes to manage forest resources under self-governance compacts without BIA approval for individual sales |
| Mineral self-governance | Enable tribes to negotiate and approve mineral leases without BIA review |
| Environmental self-regulation | Support tribes in developing and enforcing their own environmental codes as alternatives to federal regulation |
| Technical assistance | Fund tribal resource management staff and training to build capacity for self-governance |

**Evidence**: Tribes with HEARTH Act authority process leases in 49 days compared to 227 days under BIA (GAO, 2022). The Confederated Salish and Kootenai Tribes have managed their forest more sustainably than the adjacent national forest under self-governance compacts.

### 3. Dramatically Increase Resource Management Funding

**Problem Addressed**: BIA manages tribal forests at one-quarter the per-acre investment of the Forest Service; water infrastructure has a $3.4 billion backlog; environmental enforcement is grossly underfunded

**Proposal**: Increase federal resource management funding to parity with comparable non-tribal programs

| Program | Current Funding | Proposed Funding | Increase |
|---------|----------------|-----------------|----------|
| BIA Trust Natural Resources | $245 million (FY 2024) | $500 million | +$255 million |
| BIA Forestry | $62 million | $180 million | +$118 million |
| EPA Tribal Programs | $120 million | $300 million | +$180 million |
| DOE Office of Indian Energy | $45 million | $150 million | +$105 million |
| IHS Water Infrastructure | $300 million | $600 million | +$300 million |
| **Total increase** | | | **+$958 million/year** |

**Evidence**: The $3.4 billion water infrastructure backlog alone demonstrates the scale of underinvestment. Forest management at $1.47/acre vs. $5.81/acre for national forests means tribal forests receive less than one-quarter of comparable federal investment.

### 4. Implement Free, Prior, and Informed Consent

**Problem Addressed**: Federal law requires consultation with tribes but does not require consent; projects like the Dakota Access Pipeline proceeded over tribal objections

**Proposal**: Codify FPIC as the standard for all federal actions significantly affecting tribal lands and resources

| Component | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Legislative standard | Amend NEPA and relevant resource statutes to require tribal consent (not just consultation) for projects with significant impacts on tribal lands, water, or resources |
| Scope | Applies to projects on tribal land, projects on federal land within 50 miles of tribal boundaries that affect tribal resources, and projects requiring federal permits that affect treaty-reserved resources |
| Consent process | Tribal governments determine their own consent procedures through tribally adopted protocols |
| Override mechanism | Congressional override only (not executive or agency), requiring specific legislation identifying the project and overriding interest |
| Retroactivity | Existing permits that proceeded without adequate consultation may be reopened for review |

**Evidence**: International precedent (UNDRIP, ILO Convention 169) supports FPIC. Studies show that projects with community consent experience fewer delays, legal challenges, and social conflicts than contested projects (World Bank, 2019).

### 5. Remediate Environmental Contamination

**Problem Addressed**: 900+ contaminated sites on or near tribal lands; 523 abandoned uranium mines on Navajo Nation alone; cleanup has been slow and underfunded

**Proposal**: Create a dedicated Tribal Environmental Remediation Program with mandatory funding

| Component | Detail | Estimated Cost |
|-----------|--------|---------------|
| Navajo uranium cleanup | Complete remediation of all 523 abandoned mines within 15 years | $1.7 billion |
| Superfund tribal sites | Accelerate cleanup of 70+ Superfund sites in Indian Country | $2 billion over 15 years |
| Brownfield remediation | Clean up 800+ brownfield sites | $1 billion over 15 years |
| Health monitoring | Long-term health monitoring for communities affected by contamination | $50 million/year |
| Responsible party recovery | Aggressive pursuit of responsible parties for cleanup costs | Revenue recovery |
| Tribal capacity | Train tribal environmental professionals for cleanup oversight | $25 million/year |

**Evidence**: The Navajo uranium legacy -- over 40 years of contamination affecting thousands of people -- demonstrates the consequences of delayed remediation. Completed cleanups at other sites have documented health improvements and economic benefits.

### 6. Support Tribal Energy Transition

**Problem Addressed**: Some tribes depend on fossil fuel revenue; the energy transition threatens these economies while tribal lands hold vast renewable energy potential

**Proposal**: Create a comprehensive Tribal Energy Transition Program

| Component | Detail | Estimated Cost |
|-----------|--------|---------------|
| Renewable energy development grants | Fund tribal solar, wind, and geothermal projects | $200 million/year |
| Transition assistance | Revenue replacement for tribes transitioning from fossil fuels | $100 million/year for 10 years |
| Grid connection | Fund transmission infrastructure connecting tribal renewable projects to the grid | $500 million over 10 years |
| Workforce training | Train tribal members for renewable energy careers | $50 million/year |
| Tribal energy authority | Support tribes in establishing energy regulatory authority | $20 million/year |
| Tax equity solutions | Create mechanisms for tribes (tax-exempt entities) to access renewable energy tax credits | Legislation (revenue impact varies) |

**Evidence**: Tribal lands hold an estimated 5.1% of total U.S. renewable energy potential (NREL, 2023). The Moapa Band of Paiutes' 250 MW solar project demonstrates that large-scale renewable energy on tribal land is viable and profitable.

### 7. Protect Treaty Fishing and Hunting Rights

**Problem Addressed**: Salmon populations at historic lows; habitat loss threatens treaty-guaranteed resources; state-tribal conflicts over allocation and management

**Proposal**: Strengthen federal enforcement of treaty fishing and hunting rights and invest in habitat restoration

| Component | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Habitat restoration | Dedicated funding for fish and wildlife habitat restoration on and near tribal lands |
| Dam removal | Continue and accelerate dam removal where feasible to restore anadromous fish habitat (building on Klamath success) |
| Co-management expansion | Formalize tribal co-management authority for fish and wildlife through federal-tribal-state agreements |
| ESA coordination | Ensure Endangered Species Act enforcement complements rather than undermines treaty rights |
| Subsistence protections | Strengthen federal protections for Alaska Native subsistence rights |

### 8. Address Climate Change Impacts on Tribal Communities

**Problem Addressed**: Tribal communities are disproportionately affected by climate change; 31+ Alaska Native villages face imminent threats; drought, wildfire, and species loss affect tribal resources

**Proposal**: Create a dedicated Tribal Climate Adaptation Fund

| Component | Detail | Estimated Cost |
|-----------|--------|---------------|
| Climate adaptation grants | Fund tribal climate resilience planning and implementation | $200 million/year |
| Village relocation | Complete relocation of imminently threatened Alaska Native villages | $5 billion over 15 years |
| Wildfire management | Increase tribal fuels management and fire suppression capacity | $100 million/year |
| Traditional ecological knowledge | Fund integration of Indigenous ecological knowledge into climate adaptation | $25 million/year |
| Water security | Drought-proof tribal water systems in vulnerable areas | $500 million over 10 years |

**Evidence**: The cost of Alaska Native village relocation ($5 billion) is a fraction of the cost of repeated disaster response. Traditional ecological knowledge has been shown to improve climate adaptation outcomes when integrated with Western science (Voggesser et al., 2013).
