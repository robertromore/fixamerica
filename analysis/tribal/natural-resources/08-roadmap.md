# Natural Resources: Implementation Roadmap

## Overview

This roadmap outlines a phased approach to reforming tribal natural resource management over 15 years. The strategy sequences legislative, administrative, and programmatic actions to build momentum, address the most urgent needs first, and create the institutional capacity for long-term systemic reform. Each phase builds on the preceding one, with early wins in water settlement funding and trust management transfer creating political support for more ambitious later actions on environmental remediation, energy transition, and climate adaptation.

---

## Phase 1: Foundation and Immediate Reforms (Years 1-3)

### Objectives

- Fund all authorized but unfunded tribal water rights settlements
- Begin transferring resource management authority to tribes
- Launch accelerated environmental remediation on the most contaminated tribal sites
- Establish FPIC consultation framework for federal actions affecting tribal resources
- Increase baseline resource management funding to parity levels

### Action Table

| Action | Type | Lead | Timeline | Cost Estimate | Priority |
|--------|------|------|----------|---------------|----------|
| Appropriate $2.2 billion for unfunded water settlements | Legislative | Congress (Appropriations) | Year 1 | $2.2 billion (one-time) | Critical |
| Enact Indian Water Rights Settlement Completion Act | Legislative | Congress (Indian Affairs/Energy & Natural Resources) | Year 1 | Authorization (minimal) | Critical |
| Expand HEARTH Act to all federally recognized tribes | Legislative | Congress | Year 1 | $30 million/year (capacity) | Critical |
| Increase BIA Trust Natural Resources budget to $500 million | Legislative | Congress (Appropriations) | Year 1 | +$255 million/year | Critical |
| Establish Federal-Tribal Water Settlement Commission | Administrative | DOI | Year 1-2 | $50 million/year | High |
| Launch Navajo uranium mine accelerated remediation | Administrative | EPA/DOE | Years 1-3 | $500 million over 3 years | High |
| Issue FPIC executive order for all federal resource actions | Executive | President | Year 1 | Minimal | High |
| Expand DOE Office of Indian Energy to $150 million | Legislative | Congress | Year 2 | +$105 million/year | High |
| Simplify ITEDSA Tribal Energy Resource Agreements | Administrative | DOI | Year 1-2 | $15 million/year | Medium |
| Establish tribal climate vulnerability assessment program | Administrative | EPA/DOI | Years 1-3 | $25 million/year | Medium |

### Phase 1 Success Metrics

## Phase 2: Scaling and Capacity Building (Years 4-8)

### Objectives

- Complete water rights settlements for the majority of pending claims
- Transfer mineral and forestry management to tribes through self-governance compacts
- Build tribal renewable energy capacity at scale
- Complete Navajo uranium remediation and accelerate other Superfund cleanups
- Codify FPIC as a statutory standard
- Establish tribal co-management of treaty fisheries and wildlife

## Phase 3: Full Implementation and Systemic Reform (Years 9-15)

### Objectives

- Complete all viable water rights settlements
- Achieve full tribal resource sovereignty for all interested tribes
- Complete environmental remediation of all priority sites in Indian Country
- Establish tribal lands as a major renewable energy hub
- Ensure all imminently threatened Alaska Native villages are relocated or protected
- Integrate traditional ecological knowledge into federal climate adaptation

### Action Table

| Action | Type | Lead | Timeline | Cost Estimate | Priority |
|--------|------|------|----------|---------------|----------|
| Enact Tribal Resource Sovereignty Act | Legislative | Congress | Year 9-10 | $100 million/year | Critical |
| Complete all viable water rights settlements | Programmatic | Water Settlement Commission | Years 9-15 | Variable (settlement-specific) | Critical |
| Complete Navajo uranium remediation | Programmatic | EPA/DOE | Years 9-12 | $700 million over 4 years | High |
| Achieve 10 GW tribal renewable energy capacity | Programmatic | DOE/Tribes | Years 9-15 | $150 million/year (declining) | High |
| Complete Alaska Native village relocations (most urgent) | Programmatic | FEMA/BIA | Years 9-15 | $3 billion over 7 years | High |

| Establish permanent tribal wildfire management capacity | Administrative | BIA/USFS | Years 9-12 | $100 million/year | Medium |
| Create national tribal traditional ecological knowledge center | Administrative | DOI/Tribes | Year 10 | $20 million/year | Medium |

## Risk Mitigation

### Political Risks

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation Strategy |
|------|-----------|--------|---------------------|
| Congressional resistance to water settlement funding | Medium | Very High | Frame as legal obligation; document litigation costs of non-settlement; seek bipartisan champions |
| Supreme Court further narrows tribal water rights | Low-Medium | Very High | Codify Winters doctrine protections in statute; build legislative record |
| Administration change reverses FPIC executive order | Medium | High | Prioritize statutory FPIC over executive order; seek bipartisan codification |
| State opposition to tribal resource sovereignty | High | Medium | Revenue-sharing provisions; demonstrate economic benefits to surrounding communities |
| Energy industry opposition to tribal royalty reform | High | Medium | Phase increases; demonstrate that tribal-controlled leasing is faster and more efficient |
| Reduced appropriations during economic downturn | Medium | High | Seek mandatory appropriations for core programs; build constituency support |

### Implementation Risks

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation Strategy |
|------|-----------|--------|---------------------|
| Tribal capacity gaps for self-governance | Medium | Medium | Multi-year transition funding; technical assistance; peer mentoring from experienced tribes |
| Environmental remediation costs exceed estimates | Medium | Medium | Aggressive responsible party recovery; phased prioritization by health risk |
| Renewable energy grid connection delays | High | Medium | Early investment in transmission planning; coordinate with state utility commissions |
| Alaska Native village relocation community resistance | Low | Medium | Community-driven planning; respect for cultural preferences in relocation decisions |
| Climate impacts accelerate faster than adaptation | Medium | High | Build flexibility into climate adaptation fund; prioritize most vulnerable communities |

### Legal Risks

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation Strategy |
|------|-----------|--------|---------------------|
| FPIC legislation challenged as exceeding Commerce Clause authority | Low | High | Ground in Indian Commerce Clause; build on existing consultation requirements |
| State challenges to tribal environmental regulations | Medium | Medium | Ensure tribal regulations meet or exceed federal standards; seek EPA support |
| Energy company challenges to increased royalty rates | Medium | Medium | Phase implementation; provide market-rate justification |
| Takings claims from FPIC veto of approved projects | Low-Medium | Medium | Provide compensation mechanisms for documented reliance interests |

## Funding Strategy

### Total Estimated Costs by Phase

| Phase | Total Cost | Annual Average | Primary Funding Source |
|-------|-----------|----------------|----------------------|
| Phase 1 (Years 1-3) | $5.6 billion | $1.87 billion | Mixed: one-time settlement funding + discretionary increases |
| Phase 2 (Years 4-8) | $7.9 billion | $1.58 billion | Mandatory appropriations + dedicated funds |
| Phase 3 (Years 9-15) | $8.4 billion | $1.20 billion | Mandatory appropriations (declining as settlements and remediation complete) |
| **Total** | **$21.9 billion** | **$1.46 billion** | **Mixed** |

### Funding Justification

- Total 15-year cost of $21.9 billion compares to the $3.4 billion Cobell settlement alone (which addressed only individual trust fund mismanagement, not resource management failures)
- Annual cost of $1.46 billion represents approximately 6% of total federal spending on tribal programs ($24.7 billion, FY 2024)
- Water settlement costs are far lower than indefinite litigation: a single contested adjudication can cost tens of millions of dollars and take decades
- Environmental remediation costs are recoverable in part from responsible parties
- Renewable energy investments will generate long-term tribal revenue streams that reduce ongoing federal support needs
- Alaska Native village relocation costs are a fraction of repeated disaster response costs

### Revenue Offsets

| Source | Estimated Revenue | Timeline |
|--------|------------------|----------|
| Responsible party recovery (remediation) | $1-2 billion | Years 1-15 |
| Increased tribal royalty collections (market-rate reform) | $200-400 million/year | Years 3-15 |
| BIA administrative savings from self-governance transfers | $50-100 million/year | Years 5-15 |
| Tribal renewable energy tax revenue (federal) | Growing | Years 8-15 |

## Accountability Framework

### Reporting Requirements

- Annual progress report to Congress from the Secretary of the Interior covering all program areas
- Federal-Tribal Water Settlement Commission annual status report
- EPA annual report on tribal environmental remediation progress
- DOE annual report on tribal energy development
- GAO audit of program effectiveness every 3 years
- Tribal advisory committee with authority to recommend program modifications

### Course Correction Triggers

- If water settlement negotiations stall for more than 2 years for any tribe, the Commission shall report to Congress with recommendations for legislative resolution
- If environmental remediation falls behind schedule, increase funding by 25% and prioritize sites with documented health impacts
- If renewable energy installation targets are not met by Phase 2, review grid connection barriers and adjust transmission investment
- If tribal self-governance compacts show performance issues, provide additional technical assistance for 12 months before considering reassumption
- If climate impacts accelerate beyond projections, activate emergency adaptation provisions and increase Alaska Native village relocation funding
