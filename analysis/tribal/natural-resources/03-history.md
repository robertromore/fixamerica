# Natural Resources: History

## Pre-Colonial Resource Management

### Indigenous Environmental Stewardship

Before European contact, Indigenous peoples managed natural resources across the continent through sophisticated systems developed over thousands of years:

- **Controlled burning**: Native peoples used prescribed fire to manage forests, grasslands, and wildlife habitat across much of North America, creating productive landscapes that early European observers often mistook for "wilderness" (Pyne, *Fire in America*, 1982)
- **Fisheries management**: Pacific Northwest tribes developed sustainable salmon harvest systems, including weirs, traps, and seasonal closures, that maintained fish populations for millennia (Taylor, *Making Salmon*, 1999)
- **Water management**: Pueblo peoples of the Southwest constructed extensive irrigation systems (acequias) that sustained agriculture in arid environments for over a thousand years
- **Forest management**: Eastern woodland tribes practiced selective harvest and cultivation of forest resources, creating diverse and productive forest ecosystems
- **Wildlife management**: Hunting practices included seasonal restrictions, taboos against overkill, and habitat management that maintained animal populations

### Resource Governance

Indigenous natural resource management was embedded in broader governance and cultural systems:

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Territorial rights | Tribes maintained defined territories with recognized boundaries; resource access was governed by inter-tribal agreements |
| Seasonal rounds | Many tribes followed seasonal migration patterns that allowed resources to regenerate |
| Spiritual relationships | Natural resources were understood as relatives or gifts requiring reciprocal care, not merely commodities |
| Customary law | Detailed legal traditions governed resource access, harvest, and sharing |
| Ecological knowledge | Thousands of years of observation produced detailed environmental knowledge transmitted through oral tradition |

## Colonial and Early American Period (1600s-1860s)

### Resource Dispossession

| Period | Development | Resource Impact |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| 1600s-1700s | Colonial land grants and treaties | Timber, farmland, and water resources transferred to colonists |
| 1778-1871 | Treaty period (374 treaties) | Treaties reserved specific resource rights (fishing, hunting, water); vast land cessions transferred most resources |
| 1803-1848 | Territorial expansion (Louisiana Purchase, Mexican Cession) | Indigenous resource territories absorbed into U.S. claims |
| 1830 | Indian Removal Act | Southeastern tribes forcibly relocated from resource-rich homelands to Indian Territory |

### Treaty-Reserved Resource Rights

Treaties explicitly or implicitly reserved resource rights for tribes:

- **Stevens Treaties (1854-1855)**: Pacific Northwest treaties reserved "the right of taking fish, at all usual and accustomed grounds and stations" -- a right later interpreted as guaranteeing 50% of harvestable fish
- **Fort Laramie Treaty (1868)**: Reserved vast territory and resources for the Sioux Nation, including the Black Hills (later seized after gold was discovered)
- **Treaty of 1855 with Yakama**: Reserved fishing, hunting, and gathering rights on ceded lands
- **Winters doctrine implication**: Treaties that established reservations implicitly reserved water sufficient for the reservation's purpose, even when not explicitly mentioned

## Allotment and Exploitation Era (1887-1934)

### The Dawes Act and Resource Fragmentation

The General Allotment Act of 1887 had devastating effects on tribal resource management:

| Impact | Detail | Source |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Land loss | 90 million acres of tribal land lost (approximately two-thirds of total) | NCAI, 2023 |
| Resource fragmentation | Allotment broke up contiguous resource areas into individual parcels | Indian Land Tenure Foundation, 2024 |
| Fractionation | Over time, allotted parcels subdivided through inheritance, creating millions of fractional interests that prevent coherent resource management | BIA, 2023 |
| Surplus land sales | "Surplus" lands not allotted were opened to non-Indian settlement, often including the most resource-rich areas | Hoxie, *A Final Promise*, 1984 |

### Mineral and Timber Exploitation

| Resource | Period | Pattern |
|----------|--------|---------|
| Timber | 1880s-1930s | Federal agents leased tribal timber at below-market rates to non-Indian companies; *United States v. Cook* (1873) limited tribal control |
| Oil | 1920s-1930s | Osage oil boom; federal management of Osage mineral rights led to Osage Reign of Terror as non-Indians murdered Osage allottees for headrights (Grann, *Killers of the Flower Moon*, 2017) |
| Minerals | 1890s-1940s | Mining on tribal lands approved by federal agents often without meaningful tribal consent or fair compensation |
| Water | 1890s-1930s | Western water appropriation systems allocated tribal water to non-Indian users without acknowledging reserved rights |

### The Osage Murders

The Osage Nation's experience illustrates the deadly consequences of resource exploitation:

- The Osage retained subsurface mineral rights when relocated to Oklahoma Territory
- Oil discovery in the early 1900s made the Osage "the richest people per capita in the world"
- Federal guardianship system placed Osage finances under non-Indian "guardians"
- A conspiracy of murders targeting Osage headright holders killed dozens (estimated 60+) of Osage people in the 1920s
- FBI investigation revealed systematic fraud and murder; few perpetrators were convicted
- The episode demonstrated the lethal risks of federal management that prioritized outside access to tribal resources

## Indian Reorganization and Resource Recovery (1934-1945)

### Indian Reorganization Act (1934)

The IRA ended allotment and began restoring tribal resource management:

- Prohibited further allotment of tribal lands
- Authorized restoration of surplus lands to tribal ownership
- Permitted tribal constitutions and charters enabling economic development
- Created revolving credit fund for tribal enterprises, including resource development
- However, the IRA model of tribal government was often imposed rather than chosen, creating governance structures that did not always align with traditional resource management practices

## Termination and Resource Loss (1945-1968)

### Termination's Impact on Resources

| Tribe | Termination Year | Resource Impact |
|-------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Menominee | 1954 (restored 1973) | 234,000 acres of forest converted to private corporation; near-bankruptcy and forest mismanagement resulted |
| Klamath | 1954 (restored 1986) | 862,000 acres of prime timberland liquidated; environmental and economic devastation |
| Various Oregon tribes | 1954-1956 | Fishing and resource rights extinguished; partially restored later |
| Alabama-Coushatta (TX) | 1954 (restored 1987) | Lost federal resource management support and protections |

### Energy Exploitation (1950s-1980s)

The post-war period saw intensive exploitation of tribal energy resources:

- **Uranium mining on Navajo Nation**: Over 500 uranium mines operated between 1944 and 1986, primarily to supply the nuclear weapons program. Navajo miners worked without protective equipment; mines were abandoned without remediation. Legacy contamination persists across the reservation (Brugge et al., *The Navajo People and Uranium Mining*, 2006)
- **Coal strip mining**: Large-scale coal mining began on Navajo and Hopi lands in the 1960s under leases negotiated by federal officials at below-market rates
- **CERT formation**: In 1975, 25 energy-producing tribes formed the Council of Energy Resource Tribes (CERT) to gain leverage in energy negotiations, modeled after OPEC

## Self-Determination and Resource Sovereignty (1968-Present)

### Key Legislation

| Law | Year | Significance |
|-----|------|-------------|
| National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) | 1969 | Required environmental review of federal actions, including those affecting tribal lands; tribes often not adequately consulted |
| Clean Water Act | 1972 | EPA began treating tribes as states for regulatory purposes; tribes gained authority over water quality standards |
| Indian Self-Determination Act | 1975 | Enabled tribes to contract federal resource management programs |
| American Indian Religious Freedom Act | 1978 | Protected ceremonial use of natural resources (eagles, peyote, sacred water) |
| IGRA (Indian Gaming Regulatory Act) | 1988 | Gaming revenue enabled some tribes to fund independent resource management |
| ITEDSA (Indian Tribal Energy Development and Self-Determination Act) | 2005 | Allowed tribal energy resource agreements without BIA approval; amended 2018 |
| HEARTH Act | 2012 | Allowed tribes to approve surface leases without BIA; expedited development |
| Inflation Reduction Act | 2022 | $720 million for tribal climate resilience |
| Bipartisan Infrastructure Law | 2021 | $13 billion for tribal infrastructure, including $3.5 billion for water |

### Water Rights Settlements

| Period | Key Settlements |
|--------|----------------|
| 1978-1990 | Ak-Chin (1978), Tohono O'odham (1982), Salt River Pima (1988) -- early model settlements |
| 1990-2010 | Navajo-San Juan (2009), Crow (2010) -- larger, more complex settlements |
| 2010-2024 | White Mountain Apache (2010), Blackfeet (2016), Navajo-Utah (2020) -- continued progress but persistent funding gaps |

### The Cobell Litigation and Trust Reform

*Cobell v. Salazar* (filed 1996, settled 2009) was the largest class-action lawsuit against the federal government, arising from mismanagement of Individual Indian Money trust accounts:

| Element | Detail | Source |
|---------|--------|--------|
| Claim | Federal mismanagement of royalties and other income from Indian trust lands | *Cobell v. Norton*, 240 F.3d 1081 (D.C. Cir. 2001) |
| Duration | 1996-2009 (13 years of litigation) | Court records |
| Settlement | $3.4 billion total | *Cobell v. Salazar* Settlement Agreement, 2009 |
| Accounting fund | $1.5 billion to individual account holders | Settlement terms |
| Land consolidation | $1.9 billion for Land Buy-Back Program to address fractionation | Settlement terms |
| Systemic finding | Federal government could not account for billions in trust fund transactions | District court findings |

### Environmental Justice Movement

The environmental justice movement brought attention to the disproportionate environmental burdens on tribal lands:

- **1982**: Warren County, NC PCB landfill protests launched the environmental justice movement
- **1991**: First National People of Color Environmental Leadership Summit; Indigenous environmental justice emerged as distinct issue
- **1994**: Executive Order 12898 directed federal agencies to address environmental justice, including in Indian Country
- **2016**: Standing Rock Sioux protest against Dakota Access Pipeline drew global attention to tribal environmental rights and the limits of federal consultation
- **2023-2024**: Klamath River dam removal -- largest in U.S. history -- represented a major environmental justice victory for Yurok, Karuk, and Hoopa Valley tribes

## Lessons from History

1. **Resource extraction has been the primary motive for dispossession**: From the gold rush in the Black Hills to uranium mining on Navajo land, the desire for Indigenous resources has driven federal Indian policy
2. **Trust management has consistently failed tribes**: The *Cobell* litigation documented systemic federal mismanagement of tribal resources spanning over a century
3. **Treaties reserved resource rights that remain legally binding**: Despite repeated attempts to extinguish them, treaty-reserved resource rights have been upheld by federal courts
4. **Tribal resource sovereignty requires institutional capacity**: Tribes that have gained control of their resources (through self-governance compacts, HEARTH Act, ITEDSA) have generally managed them more effectively than the BIA
5. **Environmental contamination is a direct consequence of exploitation**: The uranium legacy on Navajo Nation and contaminated sites across Indian Country demonstrate the human and environmental costs of resource extraction without tribal consent or adequate regulation
