# Natural Resources: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative approach to tribal natural resource reform requires action in six areas: (1) completing tribal water rights settlements and codifying water protections, (2) transferring resource management authority from federal agencies to tribal governments, (3) codifying free, prior, and informed consent for federal actions affecting tribal resources, (4) establishing mandatory environmental remediation for contaminated tribal lands, (5) supporting the tribal energy transition, and (6) protecting treaty-reserved fishing and hunting rights. This document provides draft legislation, regulatory frameworks, and legal analysis for each area.

## Federal Legislation

### The Indian Water Rights Settlement Completion Act

**Purpose**: Fund all authorized but unfunded tribal water rights settlements, establish a permanent commission to facilitate future settlements, and codify Winters doctrine protections against judicial erosion.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Indian Water Rights Settlement
Completion Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) the Winters doctrine, established in Winters v. United
States, 207 U.S. 564 (1908), reserved water rights for Indian
reservations sufficient to fulfill the purposes of each
reservation;
(b) 38 tribal water rights settlements have been enacted since
1978, representing a successful model for resolving tribal water
claims through negotiation rather than litigation;
(c) approximately $2.2 billion in authorized settlement
provisions remain unfunded, undermining the trust relationship
and denying tribes the water they were promised;
(d) over 100 tribes have unresolved water rights claims that
require resolution;
(e) 48 percent of tribal homes lack access to reliable clean
water, a crisis requiring urgent federal action;
(f) the Supreme Court's decision in Arizona v. Navajo Nation,
599 U.S. ___ (2023), limited judicial enforcement of the trust
responsibility for water, making legislative action essential.

SEC. 3. MANDATORY FUNDING FOR AUTHORIZED SETTLEMENTS.

(a) APPROPRIATION.--There is appropriated, out of any money in
the Treasury not otherwise appropriated, $2,200,000,000 for
fiscal year 2027 to fulfill all authorized but unfunded
provisions of enacted tribal water rights settlements.

(b) ALLOCATION.--The Secretary of the Interior shall allocate
funds under subsection (a) in accordance with the authorization
provisions of each enacted settlement, with priority given to
settlements with the longest unfunded periods.

(c) DEADLINE.--All funds appropriated under this section shall
be obligated within 3 fiscal years of enactment.

SEC. 4. FEDERAL-TRIBAL WATER SETTLEMENT COMMISSION.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--There is established an independent
Federal-Tribal Water Settlement Commission (the "Commission").

(b) MEMBERSHIP.--The Commission shall consist of--
    (1) 3 members appointed by the President, including at least
    1 member with expertise in tribal water rights;
    (2) 3 members appointed by tribal nations through a process
    determined by the National Congress of American Indians;
    (3) 1 member appointed jointly by the Chairs of the Senate
    Committee on Indian Affairs and the House Natural Resources
    Committee.

(c) DUTIES.--The Commission shall--
    (1) facilitate negotiations between tribes, states, and the
    federal government for new water rights settlements;
    (2) provide technical and legal assistance to tribes engaged
    in water rights negotiations;
    (3) develop model settlement frameworks to expedite
    negotiations;
    (4) submit annual reports to Congress on the status of all
    pending tribal water claims;
    (5) recommend legislative action where negotiations have
    stalled for more than 3 years.

(d) GOAL.--The Commission shall seek to achieve resolution of
all viable tribal water rights claims within 15 years of
enactment.

SEC. 5. CODIFICATION OF WINTERS DOCTRINE PROTECTIONS.

(a) RESERVED RIGHTS AFFIRMED.--Congress affirms that every
federally recognized Indian reservation, whether established by
treaty, executive order, or act of Congress, carries reserved
water rights sufficient to fulfill the purposes of the
reservation, including but not limited to agricultural,
domestic, municipal, industrial, cultural, and environmental
uses.

(b) GROUNDWATER.--Reserved water rights under subsection (a)
include groundwater sources hydrologically connected to
reservation lands or necessary for the purposes of the
reservation.

(c) PRIORITY DATE.--The priority date for reserved water rights
is the date of establishment of the reservation, or time
immemorial for reservations that encompass aboriginal homelands.

(d) FEDERAL TRUST DUTY.--The United States has an affirmative
obligation to assess, quantify, and take reasonable steps to
secure water for Indian reservations sufficient to fulfill the
purposes of each reservation.

SEC. 6. WATER INFRASTRUCTURE.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.--There is authorized to be appropriated
$500,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2037 for
tribal water infrastructure, including--
    (1) drinking water treatment and distribution systems;
    (2) wastewater collection and treatment;
    (3) water storage and delivery for agricultural and municipal
    use;
    (4) dam safety improvements on tribal facilities.

SEC. 7. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There is authorized to be appropriated to the Commission
$50,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2042 to carry
out its duties under this Act.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the most urgent tribal natural resource issue -- water -- through three mechanisms: immediate mandatory funding for authorized settlements, a permanent commission to resolve remaining claims, and statutory codification of the Winters doctrine to prevent further judicial erosion after *Arizona v. Navajo Nation*. The groundwater provision closes a gap that several states have exploited. The affirmative trust duty provision directly responds to the *Arizona v. Navajo Nation* holding.

**Potential Challenges**: The $2.2 billion mandatory appropriation will face budget process opposition. Western state delegations may resist codification of broad reserved rights. The affirmative trust duty provision directly contradicts *Arizona v. Navajo Nation* and may generate litigation about its scope.

**Refinements**: If mandatory appropriation for the full amount is not achievable, a phased approach with $500 million/year for 5 years could be substituted. The affirmative trust duty could be narrowed to apply only to reservations established by treaty or where specific resource promises were made.

### The Tribal Resource Sovereignty Act

**Purpose**: Transfer natural resource management authority from BIA to tribal governments through expanded self-governance compacts, covering minerals, forestry, water management, and environmental regulation.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Tribal Resource Sovereignty Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.--Congress finds that--
    (1) the federal trust management of tribal natural resources
    has been marked by chronic mismanagement, as documented in
    Cobell v. Salazar and numerous GAO reports;
    (2) tribes with self-governance authority over resource
    functions consistently outperform BIA management, including
    faster lease processing, more sustainable forest management,
    and higher revenue generation;
    (3) the BIA manages tribal forests at $1.47 per acre
    compared to $5.81 per acre for the U.S. Forest Service on
    national forests;
    (4) federal energy lease approval on tribal land takes 4-7
    years compared to 2-3 years on private land;
    (5) the HEARTH Act and ITEDSA demonstrate that tribal
    resource management produces superior results when tribes
    have authority.

(b) PURPOSE.--The purpose of this Act is to transfer natural
resource management functions from the Bureau of Indian Affairs
to tribal governments while maintaining trust land protections.

SEC. 3. COMPREHENSIVE RESOURCE SELF-GOVERNANCE COMPACTS.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.--Any federally recognized Indian tribe may
enter into a Comprehensive Resource Self-Governance Compact
with the Secretary of the Interior to assume all natural
resource management functions for trust land within its
reservation or jurisdiction.

(b) SCOPE.--A compact under this section may include authority
to--
    (1) negotiate, approve, and administer surface and
    subsurface leases, including oil, gas, coal, and mineral
    leases;
    (2) manage forest resources, including timber sales, fire
    management, and reforestation;
    (3) administer water resource management, including water
    quality monitoring, irrigation systems, and water
    distribution;
    (4) develop and enforce tribal environmental regulations
    meeting or exceeding applicable federal standards;
    (5) conduct appraisals and valuations of trust resources;
    (6) collect, manage, and distribute royalties and lease
    revenues;
    (7) issue permits for resource use and development;
    (8) administer rights-of-way across trust land.

(c) TRUST STATUS.--Land and resources managed under a compact
shall retain full trust status. No compact provision shall be
construed to authorize alienation of trust land or resources.

(d) FUNDING TRANSFER.--Upon execution of a compact, the
Secretary shall transfer to the tribe the full amount of
federal funds that would otherwise have been expended by the
BIA or other federal agencies to perform the compacted
functions, plus a contract support cost amount of not less than
15 percent of the transferred funds.

SEC. 4. UNIVERSAL HEARTH AUTHORITY.

(a) EXTENSION.--The authority granted by the HEARTH Act (Public
Law 112-151) for tribal surface leasing is hereby extended to
all federally recognized Indian tribes.

(b) SUBSURFACE EXTENSION.--A tribe that has operated under
HEARTH Act authority for surface leases for not less than 2
years may apply to extend that authority to subsurface leases,
including mineral leases, under regulations promulgated by the
Secretary.

SEC. 5. TRIBAL ENVIRONMENTAL REGULATORY AUTHORITY.

(a) TREATMENT AS STATE.--The process for tribal applications
for Treatment as State (TAS) authority under the Clean Water
Act, Clean Air Act, and Safe Drinking Water Act shall be
streamlined. The EPA Administrator shall--
    (1) establish a single consolidated application for TAS
    authority under all applicable environmental statutes;
    (2) act on TAS applications within 180 days;
    (3) provide technical assistance grants of not less than
    $500,000 per tribe to develop environmental regulatory
    capacity.

(b) TRIBAL ENVIRONMENTAL STANDARDS.--Tribes with TAS authority
or operating under resource self-governance compacts may
establish environmental standards that are more stringent than
applicable federal standards.

SEC. 6. TRIBAL BONDING AND FINANCE AUTHORITY.

(a) A tribe operating under a resource self-governance compact
may issue revenue bonds secured by the revenue stream from
trust land resource activities under the compact.

(b) Such bonds shall not create a lien on the underlying trust
land or resources.

(c) The Secretary of the Treasury is authorized to provide
credit enhancement for tribal resource revenue bonds.

SEC. 7. TRANSITION SUPPORT.

(a) There is authorized to be appropriated $200,000,000 for
each of fiscal years 2027 through 2032 to support tribal
capacity building for compact implementation, including--
    (1) training for tribal natural resource management staff;
    (2) information technology systems for resource management;
    (3) legal and technical assistance for compact negotiation;
    (4) peer mentoring from experienced self-governance tribes.

SEC. 8. OVERSIGHT AND ACCOUNTABILITY.

(a) ANNUAL REPORTING.--Tribes operating under compacts shall
submit annual reports to the Secretary on resource management
activities, revenues, and environmental compliance.

(b) PERFORMANCE REVIEW.--The Secretary shall conduct performance
reviews of compacts every 5 years.

(c) REASSUMPTION.--The Secretary retains authority to reassume
resource management functions if a tribe fails to meet compact
requirements, after providing 12 months' notice and an
opportunity for corrective action.
```

**Explanation**: This bill creates a comprehensive framework for transferring resource management from BIA to tribes, building on the proven HEARTH Act and self-governance compact models. It addresses the four major resource areas (minerals, forests, water, environment) and provides funding transfers, capacity building, and financing authority. The trust status preservation provision ensures that self-governance does not risk land alienation.

**Potential Challenges**: BIA institutional resistance to transferring functions is likely. Energy industry may oppose tribal environmental standards exceeding federal levels. Capacity concerns for smaller tribes will be raised. The bonding authority may face opposition from those concerned about creating pathways to land alienation.

**Refinements**: A phased rollout beginning with 30 pilot tribes could demonstrate effectiveness. The subsurface HEARTH extension could require a 3-year rather than 2-year track record. Bonding authority could be capped at a percentage of projected revenue to manage risk.

### The Tribal Free, Prior, and Informed Consent Act

**Purpose**: Codify the requirement for tribal consent -- not merely consultation -- for federal actions that significantly affect tribal lands, water, and natural resources.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Tribal Free, Prior, and Informed
Consent Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous
Peoples (UNDRIP) establishes free, prior, and informed consent
as the standard for actions affecting Indigenous lands and
resources;
(b) current federal law requires only "consultation" with
tribes before federal actions that may affect tribal resources,
a standard that has proven inadequate to protect tribal
interests;
(c) major infrastructure projects, including the Dakota Access
Pipeline, have proceeded over tribal objections, causing
environmental damage and violating the trust relationship;
(d) projects with community consent experience fewer delays,
legal challenges, and social conflicts than contested projects.

SEC. 3. FPIC REQUIREMENT.

(a) FEDERAL ACTIONS.--No federal agency shall approve, fund,
or authorize any action that will have a significant impact on
tribal lands, waters, or natural resources without first
obtaining the free, prior, and informed consent of each
affected Indian tribe.

(b) SCOPE.--This section applies to--
    (1) any action on trust land or within reservation
    boundaries;
    (2) any action on federal land within 50 miles of tribal
    boundaries that the affected tribe demonstrates will have
    a significant impact on tribal resources, treaty rights, or
    cultural resources;
    (3) any action requiring a federal permit, license, or
    authorization that will have a significant impact on
    treaty-reserved resources, including water, fish, wildlife,
    and gathering rights;
    (4) any federal funding decision that will result in
    significant environmental impacts on tribal communities.

(c) CONSENT PROCEDURES.--
    (1) Each tribe shall determine its own procedures for
    granting or withholding consent through a tribally adopted
    consent protocol.
    (2) The federal agency shall provide all relevant
    information to the tribe in sufficient time for meaningful
    review, including environmental assessments, economic
    analyses, and alternatives.
    (3) The consent process shall begin no later than the
    scoping phase of any environmental review and shall not be
    limited to a single consultation meeting.

(d) WITHHOLDING CONSENT.--If a tribe withholds consent, the
federal action shall not proceed unless--
    (1) the action is required by an Act of Congress
    specifically identifying the project and finding an
    overriding national interest; or
    (2) the President certifies that the action is necessary
    to address an imminent threat to public health or national
    security, subject to judicial review.

SEC. 4. TRIBAL STANDING.

(a) Any tribe may bring a civil action in United States
district court to enforce the provisions of this Act.

(b) Courts may grant temporary restraining orders, preliminary
injunctions, or permanent injunctions to prevent federal
actions that proceed without required consent.

(c) In any action under this section, the burden of proof is
on the federal agency to demonstrate that consent was obtained
or that an exception under Section 3(d) applies.

SEC. 5. EXISTING PROJECTS.

(a) Federal permits, licenses, or authorizations issued before
the date of enactment of this Act shall not be retroactively
invalidated.

(b) However, upon renewal, modification, or extension of any
such permit, license, or authorization, the FPIC requirement
shall apply.

SEC. 6. REGULATIONS.

The Secretary of the Interior, in consultation with tribal
nations, shall promulgate regulations implementing this Act
within 18 months of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill establishes FPIC as the legal standard for federal actions significantly affecting tribal resources, replacing the inadequate "consultation" standard. The consent process is tribally controlled, and the override mechanism requires Congressional action or a Presidential certification subject to judicial review -- a high threshold that ensures FPIC is meaningful. The existing-projects provision avoids retroactivity problems while ensuring FPIC applies at the next regulatory touchpoint.

**Potential Challenges**: Industry will oppose the veto power as creating regulatory uncertainty. Some agencies will argue it would paralyze federal decision-making. The 50-mile radius for off-reservation actions will be challenged as overly broad. The override mechanism may face separation-of-powers concerns.

**Refinements**: The 50-mile radius could be reduced to 25 miles or replaced with a case-by-case significance determination. The override could include an expedited Congressional review process. A safe harbor provision for projects with documented tribal support could reduce industry concerns.

### The Tribal Environmental Remediation Act

**Purpose**: Establish a dedicated, mandatorily funded program to remediate environmental contamination on and near tribal lands, with priority on the Navajo uranium legacy and Superfund sites.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Tribal Environmental Remediation
Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) over 900 contaminated sites exist on or near tribal lands,
including 523 abandoned uranium mines on the Navajo Nation;
(b) environmental contamination on tribal lands resulted
primarily from federal actions or federally permitted
activities, including uranium mining for the nuclear weapons
program, coal mining under federally negotiated leases, and
industrial activities on adjacent federal lands;
(c) cleanup of contaminated tribal sites has proceeded at a
pace far slower than cleanup of comparable non-tribal sites;
(d) contamination causes documented health impacts, including
elevated rates of cancer, kidney disease, and respiratory
illness in affected tribal communities;
(e) the federal government bears primary responsibility for
remediation as the trustee of tribal lands.

SEC. 3. TRIBAL ENVIRONMENTAL REMEDIATION FUND.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--There is established in the Treasury a
fund to be known as the "Tribal Environmental Remediation
Fund".

(b) MANDATORY APPROPRIATION.--There is appropriated to the
Fund $350,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2042.

(c) USE OF FUNDS.--Amounts in the Fund shall be available
to--
    (1) remediate abandoned uranium mines on the Navajo Nation
    and other tribal lands;
    (2) accelerate cleanup of Superfund sites on or adjacent
    to tribal lands;
    (3) remediate brownfield sites on tribal lands;
    (4) fund health monitoring and treatment for communities
    affected by contamination;
    (5) train tribal environmental professionals for cleanup
    oversight;
    (6) support tribal environmental monitoring and assessment
    capacity.

SEC. 4. NAVAJO URANIUM REMEDIATION PRIORITY.

(a) The Administrator of the EPA, in coordination with the
Secretary of Energy and the Navajo Nation, shall complete
remediation of all 523 abandoned uranium mines on the Navajo
Nation within 15 years of enactment.

(b) There is allocated from the Fund not less than
$120,000,000 per year for the first 10 years for Navajo
uranium mine remediation.

(c) The Navajo Nation shall have co-management authority over
remediation activities, including the right to approve cleanup
plans and select remediation methods.

SEC. 5. RESPONSIBLE PARTY RECOVERY.

(a) The Attorney General shall pursue cost recovery from all
identified responsible parties for contamination on tribal
lands.

(b) Recovered amounts shall be deposited in the Fund.

(c) The statute of limitations for cost recovery actions under
this section shall be 25 years from the date of discovery of
contamination.

SEC. 6. HEALTH MONITORING.

(a) The Indian Health Service, in coordination with the
Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, shall establish
a long-term health monitoring program for tribal communities
affected by environmental contamination.

(b) There is authorized to be appropriated $50,000,000 for
each fiscal year for health monitoring under this section.

SEC. 7. REPORTING.

The Administrator of the EPA shall submit annual reports to
Congress on remediation progress, including sites completed,
funds expended, health monitoring results, and responsible
party cost recovery.
```

**Explanation**: This bill creates a dedicated, mandatorily funded program for tribal environmental remediation, addressing the most egregious environmental justice failures in Indian Country. The Navajo uranium priority reflects the scale and urgency of that crisis. The responsible party recovery provision ensures that polluters, not just taxpayers, bear cleanup costs. Health monitoring addresses the documented health impacts of contamination.

**Potential Challenges**: The $350 million/year mandatory appropriation will face budget opposition. Industry may oppose the extended statute of limitations for cost recovery. The Navajo co-management provision may set precedent that some agencies resist.

**Refinements**: If mandatory appropriation is reduced, prioritization criteria should ensure the highest-health-risk sites are addressed first. The cost recovery statute of limitations could be reduced from 25 to 15 years. Health monitoring could be integrated with existing IHS programs to reduce administrative costs.

### The Tribal Energy Transition and Sovereignty Act

**Purpose**: Support tribal participation in the energy transition through renewable energy development funding, fossil fuel transition assistance, grid connection investment, and workforce training.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Tribal Energy Transition and
Sovereignty Act".

SEC. 2. TRIBAL RENEWABLE ENERGY DEVELOPMENT FUND.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--There is established in the Department of
Energy a Tribal Renewable Energy Development Fund.

(b) APPROPRIATION.--There is authorized to be appropriated to
the Fund $200,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through
2037.

(c) USE OF FUNDS.--
    (1) Grants for tribal solar, wind, geothermal, and biomass
    energy development projects;
    (2) Feasibility studies and resource assessments;
    (3) Technical assistance for project development;
    (4) Community-scale energy projects for tribal communities
    lacking reliable electricity.

SEC. 3. TRIBAL CLEAN ENERGY TAX CREDIT EQUIVALENCY.

(a) DIRECT PAY.--Tribal governments and tribally owned
entities shall be eligible for direct payment equivalents of
all federal renewable energy tax credits, including the
Investment Tax Credit (ITC) and Production Tax Credit (PTC).

(b) TRIBAL CLEAN ENERGY BONDS.--There is established a Tribal
Clean Energy Bond program allowing tribes to issue tax-credit
bonds for renewable energy projects, with federal credit
support.

SEC. 4. FOSSIL FUEL TRANSITION ASSISTANCE.

(a) TRANSITION FUND.--There is authorized to be appropriated
$100,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2037 for
transition assistance to tribes with economies dependent on
fossil fuel extraction.

(b) ELIGIBLE USES.--
    (1) Revenue replacement grants during transition periods;
    (2) Economic diversification planning and implementation;
    (3) Worker retraining and placement services;
    (4) Infrastructure repurposing for post-fossil fuel
    economic activities;
    (5) Community development grants.

SEC. 5. TRIBAL GRID CONNECTION PROGRAM.

(a) There is authorized to be appropriated $500,000,000 over
10 fiscal years to fund transmission infrastructure connecting
tribal renewable energy projects to regional electricity grids.

(b) The Secretary of Energy shall coordinate with state public
utility commissions and regional transmission organizations to
ensure non-discriminatory interconnection for tribal energy
projects.

SEC. 6. WORKFORCE TRAINING.

(a) There is authorized to be appropriated $50,000,000 for
each fiscal year for tribal energy workforce training,
including--
    (1) renewable energy installation, maintenance, and
    operations training;
    (2) energy management and regulatory training;
    (3) partnerships with tribal colleges and universities;
    (4) apprenticeship programs with energy companies operating
    on tribal lands.

SEC. 7. TRIBAL ENERGY AUTHORITY.

(a) REGULATORY SUPPORT.--The Department of Energy shall provide
technical assistance to tribes establishing energy regulatory
authority, including rate-setting, interconnection standards,
and energy efficiency standards.

(b) There is authorized to be appropriated $20,000,000 for
each fiscal year for regulatory capacity building under this
section.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the unique barriers tribes face in the energy transition. The tax credit equivalency provision solves the fundamental problem that tax-exempt tribal entities cannot use standard renewable energy tax incentives. The transition assistance addresses the economic vulnerability of fossil fuel-dependent tribes. The grid connection program overcomes the infrastructure barrier that prevents tribal renewable projects from reaching markets.

**Potential Challenges**: The tax credit direct-pay provision has budget scoring implications. Fossil fuel interests may oppose transition assistance that accelerates mine and well closures. Grid connection costs may be higher than estimated in remote areas.

**Refinements**: Tax credit equivalency could be phased in over 5 years. Transition assistance eligibility could require a tribe-approved transition plan. Grid connection funding could prioritize projects with the highest megawatt-per-dollar ratio.

## State Model Legislation

### Model Tribal Water Compact Act

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Tribal Water Compact
Act".

SECTION 2. PURPOSE.

The purpose of this Act is to establish a framework for
negotiating and implementing water sharing agreements between
the State and Indian tribes with reserved water rights within
the State.

SECTION 3. STATE TRIBAL WATER COMPACT COMMISSION.

(a) There is established a [State] Tribal Water Compact
Commission consisting of representatives of the State, each
federally recognized tribe with water interests in the State,
and the federal government.

(b) The Commission shall facilitate water sharing agreements
that respect tribal reserved rights while providing certainty
for state water users.

SECTION 4. COMPACT PROVISIONS.

Any compact negotiated under this Act shall include--
(a) quantification of tribal water rights;
(b) water delivery schedules and infrastructure provisions;
(c) drought-sharing protocols;
(d) environmental flow requirements;
(e) dispute resolution mechanisms.
```

## Regulatory Framework

### BIA Resource Management Regulations

**Existing Authority**: 25 C.F.R. Parts 211-227 (Minerals); 25 C.F.R. Part 163 (Forestry)

**Proposed Regulatory Changes**:

- Establish mandatory timelines for resource lease processing (90 days for tribal-initiated leases)
- Create categorical exclusions for routine resource management actions on tribal land
- Require market-rate valuation for all new mineral and energy leases
- Update royalty calculation methods to reflect current market conditions
- Establish tribal co-management protocols for resource management decisions
- Streamline ITEDSA Tribal Energy Resource Agreement approval process

### ONRR Royalty Reform Regulations

**Existing Authority**: 30 C.F.R. Parts 1201-1243

**Proposed Regulatory Changes**:

- Require annual auditing of all tribal royalty accounts
- Establish real-time royalty tracking systems accessible to tribes
- Impose penalties for late or inaccurate royalty payments
- Require independent appraisals for royalty valuation on tribal leases
- Establish tribal royalty advisory committees

### EPA Tribal Program Regulations

**Existing Authority**: Various provisions of the Clean Water Act, Clean Air Act, and SDWA authorizing TAS

**Proposed Regulatory Changes**:

- Consolidate TAS applications into a single process
- Establish 180-day processing timelines for TAS applications
- Increase technical assistance funding for tribal environmental programs
- Recognize tribal environmental standards that exceed federal standards
- Create a tribal environmental compliance assistance program

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Indian Commerce Clause**: Congress has clear authority to enact all proposed legislation under Article I, Section 8, Clause 3, as affirmed in *Brackeen v. Haaland* (2023)
- **Treaty Clause**: Water rights and fishing/hunting rights legislation implements existing treaty obligations under Article VI
- **Spending Clause**: Mandatory appropriations for remediation and settlements are within Congressional spending authority
- **Due Process**: FPIC provisions must provide adequate process for project applicants; the override mechanism and judicial review provisions address this
- **Takings Clause**: FPIC's effective veto over projects on non-tribal land may raise takings concerns; the limitation to "significant impacts" and the availability of alternative locations mitigate this
- **Commerce Clause**: Energy legislation regulates interstate commerce in energy resources

### Preemption Questions

- The Water Settlement Completion Act preempts state water law to the extent of codified reserved rights, under the Supremacy Clause and *Winters* doctrine
- The FPIC Act preempts federal agency regulations that allow tribal consultation without consent
- The Tribal Resource Sovereignty Act preempts BIA regulations for tribes operating under self-governance compacts
- Tribal environmental standards that exceed federal standards preempt less stringent state standards on tribal land

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- FPIC Act provides tribal standing and injunctive relief in federal court
- Environmental Remediation Act imposes mandatory deadlines enforceable through citizen suits
- Resource Sovereignty Act compacts are enforceable as government-to-government agreements
- ONRR royalty reforms enforceable through administrative penalties and tribal audit rights
- Water settlements enforceable through specific performance actions by tribes

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Environmental Remediation Fund reviewed at Year 10 for continuation
- Resource self-governance compacts renewed on 5-year cycles with performance evaluation
- Energy Transition Act authorized for 10 years with reauthorization review
- Water Settlement Commission reviewed every 5 years
- All programs subject to GAO audit every 3 years

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Indian Water Rights Settlement Completion Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| "Viable" settlement qualifier | The goal of resolving "all viable" claims allows indefinite deferral of difficult negotiations | Medium |
| State non-participation | States can delay or refuse settlement negotiations, and the Act does not compel state participation | High |
| Groundwater definition | "Hydrologically connected" groundwater may be narrowly interpreted to exclude important aquifers | Medium |
| Priority date disputes | "Time immemorial" priority date for aboriginal homelands will generate litigation over which reservations qualify | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No mechanism to compel state participation | Settlements stall without state cooperation | Federal structure limits; cannot require state consent |
| Infrastructure funding authorized but not appropriated | Water rights without delivery systems are "paper water" | Separation of authorization and appropriation |
| Commission has no binding authority | Recommendations can be ignored by Congress | Advisory body limitations |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Add provision requiring states receiving federal water infrastructure funding to participate in good-faith settlement negotiations
2. Convert infrastructure authorization to mandatory appropriation for tribes with completed settlements
3. Give the Commission authority to recommend judicial adjudication when negotiations fail after 5 years
4. Clarify groundwater definition to include any aquifer within reservation boundaries or within 10 miles of reservation boundaries that provides water to reservation users

### Tribal Free, Prior, and Informed Consent Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| "Significant impact" threshold | Agencies may narrowly define significance to avoid FPIC | High |
| 50-mile radius boundary | Precise boundary creates incentives to locate projects at 51 miles | Medium |
| Consent protocol manipulation | External pressure on tribal consent processes could undermine FPIC | Medium |
| Emergency exception abuse | Presidential "imminent threat" certification could be used for non-emergency projects | Medium |
| Existing projects grandfathering | Long-lived permits may not come up for renewal for decades | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No compensation for tribes that consent but later experience harm | Consent assumed to waive future claims | Gap in protection |
| Multiple affected tribes may disagree on consent | No mechanism for inter-tribal disputes | Collective action problem |
| Federal agencies lack FPIC implementation experience | Slow, inconsistent implementation likely | Institutional capacity |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Require agencies to develop significance criteria through tribal consultation, with a presumption that actions affecting water quality, fish habitat, or air quality are significant
2. Replace the fixed 50-mile radius with a watershed-based boundary for water impacts and an airshed-based boundary for air quality impacts
3. Establish an independent FPIC ombudsman to investigate complaints of interference with tribal consent processes
4. Limit Presidential emergency exception to 180 days, requiring Congressional action for extension
5. Add provision preserving tribal right to seek damages for harms not reasonably foreseeable at the time of consent

### Tribal Environmental Remediation Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Cost recovery limitations | Some responsible parties may be defunct or judgment-proof | Medium |
| Remediation standard ambiguity | No defined cleanup standard could allow agencies to declare sites "clean" prematurely | High |
| Co-management scope | Navajo co-management may not extend to EPA decisions about prioritization | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| 15-year timeline may be insufficient for 523 mines | Complex sites may require longer remediation | Technical complexity |
| Health monitoring funding authorized but not mandatory | May not be appropriated | Authorization vs appropriation gap |
| Does not address off-reservation contamination migrating onto tribal land | Contamination from adjacent sites not covered | Jurisdictional boundaries |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Establish specific cleanup standards requiring remediation to background levels or health-based standards, whichever is more protective
2. Convert health monitoring authorization to mandatory appropriation
3. Add provision requiring EPA to address off-reservation contamination sources that affect tribal land water, air, or soil quality
4. Extend the timeline to 20 years if EPA certifies that technical constraints prevent 15-year completion, with annual progress reports

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| BIA institutional resistance to self-governance transfers | Phase implementation; create incentives for BIA cooperation; legislative mandate for transfer |
| Tribal capacity variation across 574 tribes | Graduated requirements; multi-year capacity building; peer mentoring networks |
| Political vulnerability to administration changes | Codify reforms in statute; use mandatory appropriations; create bipartisan constituencies |
| State opposition to expanded tribal authority | Revenue-sharing provisions; document economic benefits to surrounding communities; facilitate cooperative agreements |
| Judicial uncertainty under current Supreme Court | Build strong legislative records; draft statutes to survive strict scrutiny; consider constitutional amendments as backup |
| Multiple agency coordination challenges | Designate DOI as lead agency; establish inter-agency tribal resource coordinating committee |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- All programs subject to GAO performance review every 3 years
- Environmental Remediation Fund reviewed at Year 10 for continuation or adjustment
- Resource self-governance compacts renewed on 5-year cycles with performance evaluation
- Water Settlement Commission reviewed every 5 years with reauthorization required
- Energy Transition Act authorized for 10 years with Congressional reauthorization
- FPIC implementation regulations reviewed and updated every 5 years
