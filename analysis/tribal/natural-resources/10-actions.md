# Natural Resources: Actions

## Overview

Citizens can play a meaningful role in advancing tribal natural resource rights through civic engagement, advocacy, education, and direct support for tribal organizations. This document provides specific, actionable steps for individuals and communities to support reform at the federal, state, and local levels across all major tribal natural resource issues: water rights, energy development, environmental justice, treaty fishing and hunting rights, and climate adaptation.

## Individual Actions

### Educate Yourself

1. **Learn about tribal resource rights in your region**: Identify which tribes hold treaty-reserved resource rights in your area and what resources are at stake (water, fish, minerals, timber)
2. **Understand the Winters doctrine**: Learn how tribal water rights work and why they matter. The Congressional Research Service report "Indian Water Rights Settlements" (R44148) is freely available and provides a clear overview
3. **Follow environmental justice in Indian Country**: Read Indian Country Today (indiancountrytoday.com), the Indigenous Environmental Network (ienearth.org), and High Country News for ongoing coverage
4. **Know your watershed**: Understand how water in your region connects to tribal water rights -- many western water systems involve tribal claims that affect all users
5. **Learn about contaminated tribal sites**: EPA maintains public data on Superfund and brownfield sites; identify contaminated sites near tribal communities in your area

### Contact Elected Officials

Federal legislation and appropriations are essential for most tribal natural resource reforms. Contact your U.S. senators and representative about:

- **Water settlement funding**: Urge full appropriation of all authorized tribal water rights settlements
- **Indian Water Rights Settlement Completion Act**: Support legislation to fund remaining settlement provisions
- **FPIC legislation**: Ask them to support codifying free, prior, and informed consent for federal actions affecting tribal resources
- **Environmental remediation funding**: Advocate for mandatory funding to clean up contaminated tribal sites, particularly the 523 abandoned uranium mines on Navajo Nation
- **Tribal energy transition**: Support increased funding for the DOE Office of Indian Energy and tribal renewable energy programs
- **Climate adaptation**: Urge adequate funding for the Tribal Climate Adaptation Fund and Alaska Native village relocation

### Template Email for Federal Legislators

```text
Subject: Support Tribal Natural Resource Rights

Dear [Senator/Representative] [Last Name],

I am writing to urge your support for tribal natural resource
reform, specifically:

1. Water Settlement Funding: Over $2.2 billion in authorized
tribal water rights settlements remain unfunded. These
settlements represent legally binding obligations -- 48% of
tribal homes still lack reliable clean water. Please support
full appropriation of all authorized settlements and passage
of the Indian Water Rights Settlement Completion Act.

2. Environmental Remediation: Over 900 contaminated sites exist
on or near tribal lands, including 523 abandoned uranium mines
on the Navajo Nation. Cleanup has been unconscionably slow.
Please support mandatory funding for tribal environmental
remediation with accelerated timelines.

3. Tribal Energy Transition: Tribal lands hold an estimated 5.1%
of total U.S. renewable energy potential, yet development has
barely begun. Please support increased funding for the DOE
Office of Indian Energy and programs that help tribes develop
renewable energy resources on their own terms.

4. Free, Prior, and Informed Consent: Current law requires only
"consultation" with tribes before federal actions that may
affect their resources. Please support legislation requiring
tribal consent for federal actions that significantly affect
tribal lands and resources.

These reforms honor treaty obligations, protect public health,
advance clean energy, and fulfill the federal trust
responsibility. They benefit not only the 9.7 million Americans
who identify as American Indian or Alaska Native, but all
communities that share water and environmental resources with
tribal nations.

Thank you for your consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your name]
[Your address]
```

## Community Actions

### At the Local Level

- **Support tribal water infrastructure projects**: When federal water infrastructure funding is allocated to your region, advocate for tribal projects to receive equitable consideration
- **Participate in NEPA comment periods**: When federal environmental reviews involve projects affecting tribal resources, submit comments supporting meaningful tribal consultation and FPIC
- **Attend public hearings on resource development**: When energy, mining, or water projects are proposed near tribal lands, attend public hearings and support tribal concerns
- **Advocate for collaborative watershed management**: Encourage local water districts and municipalities to include tribes in watershed planning and management
- **Support tribal co-management of local fisheries and wildlife**: When state agencies manage fish and wildlife in areas with treaty rights, advocate for tribal co-management agreements
- **Reduce your environmental footprint**: Tribal communities are disproportionately affected by climate change; individual and community action on emissions matters

### At the State Level

- Contact state legislators about state-tribal water compacts and cooperative agreements
- Support state funding for environmental cleanup on or near tribal lands
- Advocate for state recognition of tribal water quality standards
- Oppose state litigation against tribal water rights claims
- Support state renewable energy policies that include tribal participation
- Advocate for state fish and wildlife agencies to honor treaty co-management obligations

### Support Environmental Justice Campaigns

- **Navajo uranium cleanup**: Support accelerated remediation of the 523 abandoned uranium mines. Contact your representatives and support Navajo Nation EPA advocacy efforts
- **Standing Rock and pipeline resistance**: Support tribal rights to consent to or reject pipeline and energy infrastructure projects near their lands
- **Klamath River restoration**: Support continued dam removal and habitat restoration for treaty fisheries
- **Alaska Native village relocation**: Advocate for federal funding to relocate the 31+ Alaska Native villages imminently threatened by climate change

## Talking Points

### For Conversations with Neighbors and Community Members

1. **Treaty rights are legal obligations**: Tribal water, fishing, and resource rights were reserved by treaties -- legal contracts between sovereign nations. Honoring them is not charity; it is fulfilling legal debts in exchange for land cessions totaling over 1.5 billion acres.

2. **Environmental contamination on tribal lands is a federal responsibility**: The federal government permitted uranium mining, coal extraction, and other hazardous activities on tribal lands, often without tribal consent. The 523 abandoned uranium mines on Navajo Nation alone represent a clear federal cleanup obligation.

3. **Tribal water rights benefit everyone**: Water settlements bring legal certainty to overallocated western water systems. Resolving tribal claims through negotiated settlements is far cheaper and more efficient than decades of litigation.

4. **Tribal renewable energy is a national asset**: Tribal lands hold an estimated 5.1% of total U.S. renewable energy potential. Developing these resources benefits tribal economies and national energy security.

5. **Tribal resource management works**: Where tribes have gained control of their resources, outcomes improve. Tribes with HEARTH Act authority process leases in 49 days compared to 227 days under BIA management. Tribal forests managed under self-governance compacts are often healthier than adjacent national forests.

6. **Climate change hits tribal communities first and worst**: Alaska Native villages are being washed away. Southwest tribal water supplies are shrinking. Pacific salmon are declining. Supporting tribal climate adaptation protects frontline communities.

### For Conversations with Skeptics

- **"Why should tribes get water that others are already using?"** Tribal water rights are legally senior to most state-granted water rights. The Winters doctrine (1908) established that reservations carry reserved water rights with priority dates of the reservation's creation. Settlements provide a negotiated solution that is fairer to all parties than judicial adjudication.

- **"Environmental cleanup costs too much."** The federal government permitted the activities that created the contamination. Leaving 523 abandoned uranium mines unremediated on Navajo Nation costs lives through elevated cancer rates and kidney disease. Cleanup costs are a fraction of the health and environmental costs of inaction.

- **"Tribes should just develop their resources through the market."** Trust land cannot be mortgaged, federal lease approval takes 4-7 years, and tax-exempt tribal entities cannot access standard renewable energy tax credits. The market barriers are structural -- created by federal policy -- and require policy solutions.

- **"FPIC gives tribes veto power over everything."** FPIC applies to projects that significantly affect tribal lands and resources. It ensures that communities most affected by resource development have a voice. Projects with community consent face fewer legal challenges and social conflicts.

- **"Climate change adaptation should not get special funding for tribes."** Tribal communities are disproportionately affected by climate change due to geographic location, resource dependence, and infrastructure deficits created by federal underinvestment. Equitable adaptation requires targeted investment.

## Professional Actions

### For Lawyers

- Volunteer with the Native American Rights Fund, Earthjustice, or tribal legal departments on resource cases
- Provide pro bono representation in water rights negotiations and environmental enforcement actions
- File amicus briefs supporting tribal resource rights in federal court cases
- Advise tribal governments on energy development agreements and environmental regulation
- Advocate within bar associations for Indian law and environmental justice education

### For Scientists and Engineers

- Participate in tribal climate vulnerability assessments and adaptation planning
- Provide technical assistance for tribal water quality monitoring and environmental assessment
- Support tribal renewable energy feasibility studies and project development
- Collaborate with tribal colleges on natural resource research and training programs
- Assist with environmental contamination assessment and remediation planning

### For Educators

- Incorporate accurate Indigenous environmental history into curricula, including the uranium legacy, treaty fishing rights, and environmental justice
- Use resources from the National Museum of the American Indian and the Smithsonian
- Partner with tribal colleges on environmental science and natural resource management programs
- Invite tribal natural resource managers and environmental justice advocates to speak
- Teach students about the Winters doctrine, treaty rights, and FPIC as part of civics education

### For Journalists

- Cover tribal natural resource issues with accuracy, depth, and tribal perspective
- Use Native-led media and tribal spokespeople as primary sources
- Investigate federal trust management performance and environmental remediation delays
- Report on the human health impacts of environmental contamination in Indian Country
- Cover the tribal energy transition and renewable energy development stories
- Document climate change impacts on tribal communities, especially Alaska Native villages

### For Business Professionals

- Support tribal enterprises and tribal-owned businesses involved in resource management
- If working in energy or extractives, advocate for fair tribal lease terms and FPIC practices
- Invest in tribal renewable energy projects through impact investment vehicles
- Encourage corporate supply chains to respect tribal resource rights and FPIC
- Support tribal workforce development in natural resource and energy fields
