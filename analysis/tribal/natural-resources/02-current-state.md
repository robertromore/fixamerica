# Natural Resources: Current State

## Water Rights

### Legal Framework

Tribal water rights rest on the *Winters* doctrine and related case law:

| Legal Basis | Description | Status |
|-------------|-------------|--------|
| *Winters v. United States*, 207 U.S. 564 (1908) | Reserved water rights for tribal reservations sufficient to fulfill reservation purposes | Foundation of tribal water law |
| Practicably irrigable acreage (PIA) standard | Quantification based on irrigation potential | Primary quantification method; being expanded |
| Homeland purpose standard | Water for all reservation purposes, not just agriculture | Emerging standard in some jurisdictions |
| *Arizona v. Navajo Nation*, 599 U.S. ___ (2023) | Federal government has no affirmative duty to assess or secure tribal water needs | Narrowed trust responsibility for water |
| State adjudications and federal settlements | Bilateral negotiated agreements | 38 settlements enacted since 1978 |

### Recent Water Developments

- **Arizona v. Navajo Nation (2023)**: Supreme Court held 5-4 that the 1868 treaty does not require the federal government to take affirmative steps to secure water for the Navajo Nation, significantly limiting judicial enforcement of the trust responsibility for water
- **Bipartisan Infrastructure Law**: Provided $3.5 billion for tribal water infrastructure, the largest single investment in history
- **Colorado River crisis**: Prolonged drought has intensified competition for Colorado River water, directly affecting tribal water rights in the basin, particularly for the Navajo, Ute, and several Pueblo communities

### Trust Management Issues

The federal government's management of tribal energy and mineral resources has been criticized for decades:

| Issue | Detail | Source |
|-------|--------|--------|
| Below-market lease rates | Historical leases often negotiated at rates below market value | *Cobell v. Salazar* findings; GAO reports |
| Missing royalty payments | ONRR has faced persistent auditing gaps for tribal royalties | GAO-22-105167, 2022 |
| Slow lease approval | Federal approval process for energy leases averages 4-7 years (compared to 2-3 years for private land) | DOE Office of Indian Energy, 2023 |
| Limited tribal control | BIA retains approval authority over most resource decisions on trust land | 25 CFR Part 211-227 |
| HEARTH Act (2012) | Allows tribes to negotiate and approve surface leases without BIA approval | 56 tribes have approved HEARTH regulations (BIA, 2024) |
| ITEDSA (2005) | Indian Tribal Energy Development and Self-Determination Act allows tribal energy resource agreements | Limited uptake due to complexity; amended 2018 |

### Forestry Management Challenges

- BIA manages tribal forests at a fraction of the per-acre investment that the Forest Service applies to national forests ($1.47/acre for tribal forests vs. $5.81/acre for national forests, BIA vs. USFS budget data, FY 2024)
- Wildfire risk is increasing due to climate change; tribal forests lack adequate fuels management funding
- Timber harvest revenue is often used to offset general tribal government costs rather than reinvested in forest management
- The Indian Forest Management Assessment Team (IFMAT) has conducted three assessments (1993, 2003, 2013) documenting persistent underfunding

### The Gap Between Trust Responsibility and Practice

| Dimension | Legal Standard | Reality |
|-----------|---------------|---------|
| Resource management | Fiduciary obligation to manage for tribal benefit | Below-market leases; slow processing; inadequate oversight |
| Royalty collection | Accurate accounting and timely payment | Historical mismanagement; *Cobell* documented billions in losses |
| Environmental protection | Protect tribal lands and resources from degradation | 900+ contaminated sites; legacy contamination unremediated |
| Water rights | Reserved rights under *Winters* doctrine | 100+ tribes with unresolved water claims; *Arizona v. Navajo Nation* limited enforcement |
| Consent | Consultation required for federal actions | Consultation often perfunctory; FPIC not legally required |

## Recent Developments (2024-2025)

### Supreme Court

- **Arizona v. Navajo Nation (2023)**: Limited federal trust duty for water; dissent by Justice Gorsuch argued the 1868 treaty compels federal action
- **Sackett v. EPA (2023)**: Narrowed Clean Water Act jurisdiction over wetlands, potentially reducing protections for tribal water resources

### Congressional Action

- **Bipartisan Infrastructure Law implementation**: $3.5 billion in tribal water infrastructure projects under construction or design (2022-2025)
- **Inflation Reduction Act**: $720 million for tribal climate resilience being distributed through competitive grants
- **Indian Water Rights Settlement Completion Act**: Would fund remaining authorized settlement provisions; pending committee action

### Administrative Actions

- **Klamath River dam removal**: Largest dam removal in U.S. history completed (2023-2024), restoring 400+ miles of salmon habitat for Yurok, Karuk, and Hoopa Valley tribes
- **ONRR royalty reforms**: New regulations improving transparency in tribal royalty calculations (2024)
- **HEARTH Act expansion**: Additional tribes approved for self-governance of surface leases
