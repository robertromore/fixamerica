# Language Preservation: Overview

## Executive Summary

Indigenous language preservation represents one of the most urgent cultural crises in the United States. Of the estimated 300 or more Indigenous languages spoken in North America at the time of European contact, only approximately 175 remain in use today, and linguists project that without significant intervention, fewer than 20 will survive to the end of the 21st century (Endangered Languages Project, 2023). The loss of Indigenous languages is not merely a linguistic concern -- it represents the erasure of entire knowledge systems, governance traditions, ecological wisdom, and cultural identities that have developed over thousands of years.

The decline of Indigenous languages is a direct consequence of federal policy. For over a century, the United States government systematically suppressed Native languages through the boarding school system (1879-1969), which forcibly removed children from their families and punished them for speaking their mother tongues. The 2022 Federal Indian Boarding School Initiative Report documented at least 408 federal boarding schools across 37 states and identified marked or unmarked burial sites at approximately 53 of these schools (Department of the Interior, 2022). This deliberate campaign of linguistic and cultural destruction severed intergenerational language transmission in hundreds of communities, creating a crisis that persists today.

Federal policy has shifted since the passage of the Native American Languages Act of 1990 (NALA, 25 U.S.C. 2901-2906), which declared it federal policy to "preserve, protect, and promote the rights and freedom of Native Americans to use, practice, and develop Native American languages." The Esther Martinez Native American Languages Programs Reauthorization Act -- first enacted in 2006 and most recently reauthorized in 2019 -- provides competitive grants for language immersion and restoration programs. However, funding remains grossly inadequate relative to the scale of the crisis: approximately $15.7 million was appropriated for Native language programs through the Administration for Native Americans in FY 2024, a fraction of what is needed to reverse decades of systematic suppression (Administration for Native Americans, FY 2024).

Successful language revitalization requires a comprehensive approach: immersion schools and language nests for young children, master-apprentice programs to transfer fluency from elders, technology-assisted documentation and learning tools, training of language teachers, and sustained federal funding that matches the scale of the government's historical responsibility for language loss. The revitalization of Hawaiian and several Pueblo languages demonstrates that decline can be reversed when communities receive adequate resources and autonomy.

## Core Tensions

- **Urgency vs. resources**: The window to save many languages is measured in years (as elder speakers pass), but funding and trained personnel are chronically insufficient
- **Community control vs. standardization**: Effective revitalization must be community-driven, but federal programs often impose standardized metrics and approaches
- **Documentation vs. revitalization**: Linguistic documentation preserves records of languages but does not necessarily produce new speakers; communities debate where limited resources should be directed
- **Technology vs. tradition**: Digital tools can expand access to language learning, but some communities view recording and digitizing sacred or ceremonial language as culturally inappropriate
- **Education policy vs. language needs**: State educational standards and testing requirements can crowd out time for language instruction in tribal schools
- **Individual rights vs. community needs**: Parents may prioritize English proficiency for economic opportunity while communities need new speakers to survive

## Key Questions

1. **How many Indigenous languages can realistically be saved?** What criteria and resources determine whether a language can be revitalized, and what obligations exist to those that cannot?

2. **Is federal funding adequate?** How does the approximately $15.7 million in annual Esther Martinez Act funding compare to the estimated cost of comprehensive language revitalization?

3. **What makes immersion programs successful?** What common factors characterize the most effective language revitalization efforts, and how can they be replicated?

4. **How should technology be used?** What role should digital tools, apps, and AI-assisted language learning play in preservation, and what cultural protocols should govern their use?

5. **What is the federal government's obligation?** Given that federal policy caused language loss, what level of remediation is owed, and how should it be structured?

6. **How can language revitalization support broader tribal sovereignty?** What is the relationship between linguistic self-determination and political self-governance?

7. **How should language preservation intersect with education policy?** Can tribal immersion schools meet state educational standards while prioritizing language acquisition?

## Vision of Success

A successful language preservation system would feature:

- All Indigenous languages with living speakers supported by adequately funded revitalization programs
- At least one immersion school or language nest program available for every endangered language with a viable speaker community
- Federal funding at a level commensurate with the government's historical responsibility for language destruction -- estimated at $200-500 million annually
- Master-apprentice programs and language teacher training producing new fluent speakers in every generation
- Digital archives and learning tools developed under tribal control and cultural protocols
- State and federal education policy that recognizes and supports Indigenous language instruction, including bilingual certification pathways
- Language use normalized in tribal governance, media, commerce, and daily life
- No additional Indigenous languages becoming extinct
