# Language Preservation: Root Causes

## Overview

The decline of Indigenous languages in the United States is the product of interconnected historical, structural, and institutional factors. While the boarding school era represents the most visible and deliberate cause of language loss, the crisis is sustained by ongoing systemic conditions that continue to impede revitalization. Understanding these root causes is essential to designing interventions adequate to the scale of the problem.

## Historical Root Causes

### Deliberate Federal Suppression

The single most important root cause of Indigenous language decline is the systematic campaign of linguistic suppression carried out by the federal government:

| Policy | Period | Mechanism | Impact |
|--------|--------|-----------|--------|
| Boarding school system | 1879-1969 | Forced child removal, punishment for language use | Severed intergenerational transmission in hundreds of communities |
| English-only education mandates | 1880s-1960s | Federal schools prohibited Indigenous language instruction | Generations educated without access to their languages |
| Termination policy | 1953-1968 | Dissolved tribal governments and community structures | Eliminated institutional support for language maintenance |
| Urban relocation | 1952-1972 | Relocated 750,000+ Native people to cities | Separated speakers from language communities (Fixico, 1986) |

**Why this matters today**: The effects of these policies are not merely historical. The intergenerational trauma they caused continues to inhibit language transmission. Adults who attended boarding schools, or whose parents attended, often did not teach their languages to their children, creating gaps in transmission that are now 2-3 generations deep.

### Forced Population Displacement

The removal and confinement of Indigenous peoples disrupted the territorial and social conditions necessary for language maintenance:

- **Indian Removal Act (1830)**: Forced relocation of southeastern tribes to Oklahoma separated communities from homelands where place-based language and ecological knowledge were embedded
- **Reservation confinement**: Consolidation of linguistically distinct groups onto shared reservations sometimes forced the adoption of English or a dominant Indigenous language at the expense of smaller languages
- **Allotment (Dawes Act, 1887)**: Broke up communal lands and introduced English-speaking settlers into reservation communities, diluting language environments

### Demographic Collapse

Disease, warfare, and dispossession reduced Indigenous populations by an estimated 90-95% between 1492 and the late 19th century (Thornton, 1987). This demographic catastrophe:

- Eliminated entire speech communities
- Reduced populations below the threshold needed for natural language maintenance
- Destroyed the density of daily interactions that sustain language use

## Structural Root Causes

### English-Dominant Economy

The dominance of English in the American economy creates persistent structural pressure against Indigenous language maintenance:

| Factor | Description | Effect on Language |
|--------|-------------|-------------------|
| Employment requirements | Nearly all employment requires English; Indigenous language fluency has little economic value | Parents prioritize English for children's economic prospects |
| Higher education | Colleges and universities operate almost exclusively in English | Language shift accelerates as youth pursue higher education |
| Media environment | Television, internet, social media are overwhelmingly English-language | Children are immersed in English from infancy |
| Government services | Federal, state, and most tribal government operations are conducted in English | English becomes the language of power and administration |

### Small Speaker Populations

Many Indigenous languages have always had relatively small speaker populations compared to colonial languages, making them inherently vulnerable:

- Languages with fewer than 1,000 speakers are at significant risk even without external pressures
- Geographic isolation of small communities can be a double-edged sword: it protects against language shift but limits the pool of speakers and potential learners
- When speaker numbers drop below a critical threshold (estimated at approximately 100-500 active daily users), natural transmission becomes extremely difficult (Fishman, *Reversing Language Shift*, 1991)

### Urbanization

The migration of Indigenous people to urban areas has been a major driver of language shift:

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Urban Indigenous population | 78% of AI/AN population lives off-reservation | Census Bureau, 2020 |
| Urban language use | Significantly lower rates of Indigenous language fluency in urban areas | NCAI, 2023 |
| Community density | Urban Indigenous populations are dispersed across metropolitan areas, making community language use difficult | Census data |

### Lack of Language Environments (Domains)

Language survival requires that speakers use the language across multiple social domains:

| Domain | Status for Most Indigenous Languages | Comparison with English |
|--------|--------------------------------------|------------------------|
| Home/family | Declining; many families use English at home | Dominant in virtually all homes |
| Education | Limited immersion programs; most schools are English-only | Default language of instruction |
| Government | Most tribal governments operate primarily in English | All levels of government |
| Commerce | Almost no commercial activity conducted in Indigenous languages | All commerce |
| Media | Minimal Indigenous language media; some radio and online | Overwhelmingly dominant |
| Religion/ceremony | Strongest remaining domain for many languages | N/A |

## Institutional Root Causes

### Inadequate Federal Funding

Federal funding for language preservation is grossly inadequate given the scale of the crisis and the federal government's responsibility for causing it:

| Comparison | Amount | Source |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| Total ANA language funding (FY 2024) | $15.7 million | ANA, FY 2024 |
| Estimated need for 175 languages | $200-500 million annually | NCAI, 2023 |
| Per-language average funding | ~$90,000 per language (if divided equally) | Calculated |
| Cost of one comprehensive immersion school | $1-3 million annually | GAO, 2022 |
| Federal spending on foreign language education | $71 million (Title VI, FY 2024) | Department of Education |

The comparison with federal spending on foreign language education is instructive: the government spends approximately 4.5 times more on foreign language education than on preserving the languages it actively destroyed.

### Competitive Grant Structure

The current funding model relies on competitive grants administered through the Administration for Native Americans:

- **Short duration**: Grants are typically 3 years, insufficient for long-term language programs
- **Low funding rates**: Only 22.5% of applicants receive funding (ANA, 2023)
- **Grant-writing burden**: Small tribal communities must compete with larger, better-resourced tribes for the same limited pool
- **Discontinuity**: Programs often cannot sustain themselves between grant cycles, losing staff, students, and momentum
- **Metrics mismatch**: Federal reporting requirements may not align with community-defined language goals

### Education System Barriers

The broader education system creates structural obstacles to language preservation:

| Barrier | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| State standards and testing | State-mandated standardized tests in English create pressure against immersion approaches |
| Teacher certification | Most states do not recognize Indigenous language teaching credentials |
| No Child Left Behind / ESSA legacy | Accountability frameworks prioritize English-language metrics |
| Curriculum requirements | Required subjects leave limited time for language instruction |
| BIE schools | Bureau of Indian Education schools historically did not prioritize Indigenous language instruction |

### Lack of Institutional Infrastructure

Most Indigenous languages lack the institutional infrastructure that supports language maintenance:

| Infrastructure Element | Languages That Have It | Languages That Lack It |
|-----------------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Standardized writing system | Approximately 50-60 | 100+ |
| Published grammar and dictionary | Approximately 80 | 90+ |
| Teaching curriculum (K-12) | Approximately 20-30 | 140+ |
| Dedicated immersion school | Approximately 30-40 | 130+ |
| University-level study | Fewer than 15 | 160+ |
| Regular media production | Fewer than 10 | 165+ |

## Cultural and Psychological Root Causes

### Internalized Language Stigma

The boarding school experience and broader assimilation pressures created deeply internalized negative attitudes toward Indigenous languages:

- Boarding school survivors were taught that their languages were "primitive" and obstacles to success
- Some survivors actively chose not to transmit their languages to protect their children from the punishment they experienced
- Internalized stigma can manifest as reluctance to speak the language in public, devaluation of language skills, or active opposition to language programs within communities
- These attitudes can persist across generations even after the original coercive context has ended

### Intergenerational Trauma

The boarding school experience produced intergenerational trauma that affects language transmission:

- **First generation**: Direct survivors experienced punishment for language use, creating fear and shame associations
- **Second generation**: Children of survivors often grew up without the language, sometimes without understanding why their parents did not teach them
- **Third generation**: Grandchildren may experience the language loss as an absence without fully understanding its historical causes
- **Healing and language**: Language revitalization itself can be a component of intergenerational healing, but it can also trigger difficult emotions connected to historical trauma

### Generational Disconnect

The gap between elder fluent speakers and younger learners creates practical and social challenges:

- Elder speakers may use language varieties, vocabularies, and ceremonial registers that younger learners find difficult to access
- Younger learners may be more comfortable with technology-mediated learning, while elders may prefer oral and relational methods
- Different generations may have different priorities for language use (ceremonial preservation vs. everyday communication)

## Systemic Interactions

### Feedback Loops

Language decline is characterized by self-reinforcing negative cycles:

1. **Fewer speakers leads to fewer domains of use**: As speaker numbers decline, opportunities for daily language use shrink
2. **Fewer domains leads to reduced motivation**: When a language cannot be used for practical purposes, the incentive to learn it decreases
3. **Reduced motivation leads to fewer learners**: Without motivated learners, revitalization programs struggle to sustain enrollment
4. **Fewer learners leads to program closure**: Programs with declining enrollment lose funding and close
5. **Program closure leads to fewer speakers**: Without institutional support, the speaker population continues to decline

### Intersecting Disadvantages

Language loss intersects with other forms of structural disadvantage in Indian Country:

- **Poverty**: Communities with the highest poverty rates often have the fewest resources for language programs; poverty rate in Indian Country is 25.4% compared to 11.5% nationally (Census ACS, 2022)
- **Infrastructure**: Remote communities lacking broadband cannot access technology-based language tools; only 67% of tribal areas have broadband (FCC, 2023)
- **Health**: Poor health outcomes among elders reduce the time and energy available for language teaching; life expectancy for AI/AN is 5.5 years lower than for the general population (IHS, 2023)
- **Education**: Underfunded BIE schools lack resources for language programs; BIE schools receive 40% less per-pupil funding than comparable public schools (NIEA, 2023)

## Summary of Root Causes

| Category | Root Cause | Severity | Reversibility |
|----------|-----------|----------|---------------|
| Historical | Boarding school suppression | Critical | Partially -- through revitalization investment |
| Historical | Forced population displacement | Critical | Partially -- through land restoration and community rebuilding |
| Historical | Demographic collapse | Critical | Irreversible for extinct languages; manageable for surviving ones |
| Structural | English-dominant economy | High | Difficult -- requires creating economic value for language skills |
| Structural | Urbanization | High | Moderate -- urban language programs can help |
| Structural | Small speaker populations | High | Variable -- depends on community commitment and resources |
| Institutional | Inadequate federal funding | Critical | Highly reversible -- requires political will |
| Institutional | Competitive grant structure | High | Highly reversible -- shift to formula/mandatory funding |
| Institutional | Education system barriers | High | Reversible -- policy changes at state and federal level |
| Cultural | Internalized stigma | High | Slowly reversible -- through community healing and positive language environments |
| Cultural | Intergenerational trauma | High | Slowly reversible -- through trauma-informed approaches |
