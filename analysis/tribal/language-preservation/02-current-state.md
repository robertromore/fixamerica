# Language Preservation: Current State

## Language Endangerment Status

### Overall Vitality

The Indigenous languages of the United States exist on a spectrum of endangerment:

| Vitality Level | Description | Estimated Count | Examples |
|---------------|-------------|----------------|----------|
| Vigorous | All generations speak; community language | 5-10 | Navajo (Dine Bizaad), some Pueblo languages |
| Shifting | Children may speak but declining use | 15-20 | Ojibwe, Lakota, Cherokee |
| Endangered | Mainly older adults speak; limited child transmission | 50-60 | Crow, Arapaho, Choctaw |
| Severely endangered | Only grandparent generation speaks | 40-50 | Potawatomi, Comanche, many California languages |
| Critically endangered | Fewer than 10 speakers remain | 50+ | Siletz Dee-ni, Wichita (dormant since 2016), many others |
| Dormant/Extinct | No living speakers | 50+ | Catawba, Mohegan-Pequot (reclamation underway) |

*Source: Endangered Languages Catalogue (ElCat), 2023; UNESCO Atlas of the World's Languages in Danger, 2024*

### Speaker Demographics

The age distribution of fluent speakers reveals the urgency of the crisis:

| Age Cohort | Percentage of Fluent Speakers | Implication |
|-----------|------------------------------|-------------|
| Over 70 | Approximately 40% | Critical knowledge holders; limited time |
| 50-69 | Approximately 25% | Often last generation with childhood fluency |
| 30-49 | Approximately 20% | Many are second-language learners, not native speakers |
| Under 30 | Approximately 15% | Mostly immersion school graduates or heritage learners |

*Source: Indigenous Language Institute, 2023; National Indian Education Association survey data, 2023*

### Regional Variation

| Region | Languages Remaining | Relative Vitality | Key Languages |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|---------------|
| Alaska | 20+ | Mixed; Yup'ik relatively strong, many critically endangered | Yup'ik, Inupiaq, Tlingit, Haida |
| Southwest | 30+ | Strongest in U.S.; Navajo has largest speaker base | Navajo, Zuni, Hopi, O'odham, Keres |
| Northern Plains | 10-15 | Endangered to severely endangered | Lakota, Dakota, Crow, Blackfoot |
| Great Lakes | 8-10 | Endangered; active revitalization | Ojibwe, Menominee, Potawatomi |
| Pacific Northwest | 15-20 | Many critically endangered | Salish languages, Sahaptin, Nez Perce |
| Oklahoma | 15-20 | Many severely endangered | Cherokee, Choctaw, Chickasaw, Muscogee |
| California | 40+ | Most critically endangered region | Karuk, Yurok, many with single-digit speakers |
| Southeast | 5-8 | Small speaker communities | Eastern Cherokee, Mississippi Choctaw |
| Northeast | 5-8 | Several dormant; reclamation efforts | Mohawk, Seneca, Wampanoag (reclamation) |
| Hawaii | 1 | Successful revitalization model | Hawaiian ('Olelo Hawai'i) |

*Source: UNESCO Atlas, 2024; Endangered Languages Catalogue, 2023*

### The Navajo Language: Largest but Declining

Navajo (Dine Bizaad) remains the most widely spoken Indigenous language in the United States, but its vitality is declining:

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Total speakers | Approximately 170,000 | Census ACS, 2022 |
| Speakers under 18 | Approximately 30% of Navajo youth speak Dine Bizaad | Navajo Nation Division of Dine Education, 2023 |
| Fluency decline | 1980: 93% of Navajo children spoke the language; 2020: approximately 51% | Census data, 1980 and 2020 |
| Immersion schools | 7 Navajo immersion programs operating | NIEA, 2023 |

## Revitalization Programs

### Immersion Schools

| Program Type | Estimated Number | Key Examples |
|-------------|-----------------|--------------|
| Full immersion schools (K-8 or K-12) | 25-30 | Punana Leo (Hawaiian), Dine Bi'olta' (Navajo), Akwesasne Freedom School (Mohawk) |
| Language nests (pre-K immersion) | 30-40 | Punana Leo preschools, Piegan Institute Cuts Wood School (Blackfoot) |
| Partial immersion / bilingual programs | 40-50 | Various tribal schools with language components |
| **Total immersion-oriented programs** | **~80-120** | Across approximately 40-50 languages |

*Source: National Indian Education Association, 2023; Indigenous Language Institute, 2023*

### Master-Apprentice Programs

The Master-Apprentice Language Learning Program (MALLP), pioneered by the Advocates for Indigenous California Language Survival, pairs elder fluent speakers with younger learners for intensive one-on-one immersion:

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Programs using MALLP model | 40+ across the U.S. | AICLS, 2023 |
| Languages served by MALLP | Approximately 30 | AICLS, 2023 |
| Average hours of immersion per pair | 10-20 hours per week | AICLS program data |
| New speakers produced | Hundreds of advanced learners; dozens of near-fluent speakers | AICLS, 2023 |

### Hawaiian Language Revitalization: A Model

Hawaiian offers the most successful U.S. language revitalization case:

| Metric | 1980 | 2024 | Source |
|--------|------|------|--------|
| Native speakers | Fewer than 50 children | 3,000+ immersion students; 18,000+ speakers | Ka Haka 'Ula O Ke'elikolani, 2023; Census ACS, 2022 |
| Immersion schools | 0 | 26 Punana Leo preschools and Kula Kaiapuni schools | 'Aha Punana Leo, 2024 |
| University program | None | Full BA and MA programs at UH Hilo | University of Hawaii, 2024 |
| Constitutional status | Not recognized | Official state language since 1978 | Hawaii State Constitution |

## Legal Framework

### Key Federal Statutes

| Law | Year | Key Provisions |
|-----|------|---------------|
| Native American Languages Act (NALA) | 1990 | Declared federal policy to preserve and promote Native languages; reversed assimilation policy |
| Esther Martinez Native American Languages Programs Act | 2006, reauth. 2014, 2019 | Authorized grants for immersion and restoration programs |
| Native American Languages Reauthorization Act (proposed) | Pending | Would increase authorized funding levels and create new program categories |
| Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA) | 2015 | Included provisions for Native language instruction in schools |

### Tribal Language Policies

Many tribal nations have enacted their own language policies:

| Tribe/Nation | Policy | Year |
|-------------|--------|------|

| Cherokee Nation | Cherokee Language Master Plan | 2019 |

## Recent Developments (2024-2025)

### Federal Policy

- **Boarding School Initiative Volume 2**: DOI released second volume of the Federal Indian Boarding School Initiative report in July 2024, documenting additional schools and burial sites, reinforcing the historical case for language revitalization investment
- **FY 2025 Budget Request**: Administration requested $18.2 million for ANA language programs, a $2.5 million increase over FY 2024 (Office of Management and Budget, FY 2025)
- **Durbin Feeling Native American Languages Act**: Introduced in 2023 to create a national survey of Native language vitality and establish a grant program for language teacher training; pending committee action

### State Actions

- **New Mexico**: Enacted the Indigenous Language Revitalization Act in 2024, providing state matching funds for tribal language programs
- **Washington State**: Approved Indigenous language teacher certification pathways in 2023
- **Montana**: Continued funding for Indian Language Preservation Program through the state university system

### Program Developments

- **Cherokee Nation Immersion Charter School**: Graduated its first class of K-6 students fully educated in Cherokee syllabary and language (2024)
- **Wampanoag Language Reclamation Project**: Continued producing new speakers of a language that had no speakers for over a century, demonstrating that even dormant languages can be reclaimed
- **AI Language Tools**: Several tribal communities began developing AI-assisted language learning tools, raising questions about data sovereignty and cultural protocols for language technology

## Challenges and Gaps

### Workforce

| Challenge | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Teacher shortage | Fewer than 500 certified Indigenous language teachers nationally (NIEA, 2023) |
| Certification barriers | Most states do not offer Indigenous language teaching certification |
| Elder speaker health | Fluent speakers are aging; many face health challenges that limit participation |
| Linguist availability | Limited number of trained linguists willing to work long-term with communities |

### Institutional

| Challenge | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Funding instability | ANA grants are competitive, short-term (3 years), and insufficient |
| No entitlement funding | Language programs compete for discretionary dollars rather than receiving dedicated mandatory funding |
| Assessment conflicts | State-mandated English-language testing creates pressure against immersion approaches |
| Curriculum gaps | Lack of standardized curricula and teaching materials in most Indigenous languages |

### Structural

| Challenge | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Intergenerational rupture | Boarding school trauma severed language transmission in many communities |
| Geographic isolation | Many language communities are in remote areas with limited infrastructure |
| Language attitudes | Internalized stigma from boarding school era can discourage language use |
| Economic pressure | English dominance in the labor market creates incentives against language maintenance |
