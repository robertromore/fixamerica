# Language Preservation: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to Indigenous language preservation is less organized and overt than opposition to many other policy areas. Few political actors openly argue against preserving endangered languages. However, significant obstacles arise from ideological opposition to federal spending, English-only movements, institutional inertia, competing budgetary priorities, and structural barriers embedded in education policy. Understanding these forms of opposition -- both explicit and implicit -- is essential for designing effective advocacy strategies.

## Explicit Opposition

### English-Only / Official English Movement

**Key Organizations**:

- U.S. English (founded 1983)
- ProEnglish (founded 1994)
- English First

**Arguments**:

- English should be the sole official language of the United States to promote national unity
- Government resources should not be spent on "foreign" or minority language programs
- Multilingualism fragments society and impedes assimilation
- Immigrants and minorities should be encouraged to learn English rather than maintain heritage languages

**Activities**:

- Lobbied for Official English legislation at federal and state levels
- 32 states have adopted Official English laws or constitutional amendments as of 2024 (U.S. English, 2024)
- Some state Official English laws have been interpreted to restrict government support for non-English language programs

**Rebuttal**:

- Indigenous languages are not foreign languages -- they are the original languages of this continent
- The Native American Languages Act (1990) specifically recognized that Indigenous languages occupy a unique legal status distinct from immigrant languages
- Research consistently shows that bilingualism produces cognitive benefits and does not impede English acquisition (Bialystok, *Bilingualism in Development*, 2001)
- Treaty obligations and the trust relationship create legal duties distinct from general immigration policy

### Fiscal Conservatives

**Key Organizations**:

- Heritage Foundation
- Cato Institute
- Americans for Tax Reform

**Arguments**:

- Federal language programs represent discretionary spending that should be reduced
- Language preservation is a matter for private philanthropy and community initiative, not federal funding
- The federal government should not be in the business of cultural preservation
- Cost-benefit analysis does not justify spending on languages spoken by small populations

**Rebuttal**:

- The federal government directly caused language loss through deliberate policy; remediation is an obligation, not charity
- Federal trust responsibility extends to cultural preservation
- The cost of comprehensive language funding ($200-500 million annually) is a negligible fraction of the federal budget ($6.1 trillion in FY 2024)
- Language programs generate economic activity in tribal communities and reduce other social service costs

## Implicit Opposition

### Institutional Inertia

The most significant barriers to language preservation often come not from active opponents but from institutional systems that passively obstruct revitalization:

| Institution | Form of Opposition | Effect |
|------------|-------------------|--------|
| State education departments | Rigid curriculum standards and testing requirements in English | Crowd out time for language instruction; penalize immersion schools |
| Teacher certification boards | No pathway for Indigenous language certification | Unable to hire fluent speakers as credentialed teachers |
| Bureau of Indian Education | Historical emphasis on English-medium instruction; bureaucratic slowness | BIE schools have been slow to integrate language programs |
| Office of Management and Budget | Treats language funding as discretionary rather than mandatory | Annual budget battles threaten funding stability |
| Federal grant administration | Complex application processes, reporting requirements, and short grant cycles | Disadvantages small tribal programs with limited administrative capacity |

### Competing Budget Priorities

Within tribal communities and at the federal level, language preservation competes for limited resources:

| Competing Priority | Budget Context | Effect on Language Funding |
|-------------------|---------------|--------------------------|
| Health care (IHS) | IHS is chronically underfunded by an estimated $12 billion annually (NCAI, 2023) | Language programs seen as lower priority than life-and-death health needs |
| Law enforcement | Crime rates in Indian Country are 2.5x the national average (DOJ, 2022) | Public safety demands divert attention from cultural programs |
| Housing | 40% of on-reservation housing is overcrowded or substandard (HUD, 2023) | Physical needs often take precedence over cultural programs |
| Infrastructure | 1 in 10 reservation households lacks clean water (EPA, 2023) | Basic infrastructure competes with language investment |
| Economic development | Tribal unemployment averages 11.7% (Census ACS, 2022) | Job creation is often a higher immediate priority |

### Academic Gatekeeping

Academic institutions can inadvertently obstruct community-controlled language preservation:

- **Credentialing requirements**: Universities set standards for who qualifies as a language expert that may exclude community members without formal degrees
- **Research extraction**: Historically, linguists have documented languages primarily for academic publications rather than community use, sometimes restricting community access to materials
- **Intellectual property disputes**: Questions about who owns language data collected by outside researchers
- **Methodological disagreements**: Academic approaches to language documentation may conflict with community preferences for how language should be taught and used

### Technology Industry Indifference

While some technology companies have supported Indigenous language efforts, the industry as a whole has not prioritized these communities:

- **AI language models**: Major AI systems (speech recognition, translation, text generation) do not support most Indigenous languages because training data is insufficient and the user base is too small for commercial viability
- **Platform design**: Social media platforms, operating systems, and keyboards often do not support Indigenous language characters and diacritical marks
- **Data sovereignty concerns**: Cloud-based language tools raise questions about who controls the data and how it might be used

## Opposition Arguments and Responses

| Argument | Response |
|----------|----------|
| "These languages are dying naturally; it's a waste of resources to try to save them" | Language death is not natural when it results from deliberate government suppression; the federal government has a remediation obligation |
| "English proficiency is more important for economic success" | Research shows bilingual education does not impair English acquisition and produces cognitive, cultural, and professional benefits |
| "Language preservation is a tribal responsibility, not a federal one" | The federal government caused language loss through its own policies; the trust responsibility extends to cultural remediation |
| "Small language communities don't justify large investments" | Every language represents a unique knowledge system; the loss is irreversible and the cost of preservation is minimal relative to the federal budget |
| "Standardized testing requires English instruction" | Testing policy can be modified to accommodate immersion education; states like Hawaii and New Mexico have done so successfully |
| "Federal money would be wasted -- you can't force people to learn a language" | Successful programs like Hawaiian immersion demonstrate high demand; the problem is insufficient program capacity, not lack of interest |

## Political Dynamics

### Congressional Landscape

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Bipartisan support | Language preservation legislation has historically received bipartisan support (Esther Martinez Act passed by voice vote) |
| Low salience | Language preservation is a low-profile issue that rarely generates significant political opposition |
| Appropriations gap | Support in authorization is often not matched by adequate appropriations |
| Committee jurisdiction | Indian Affairs committees are generally supportive, but Appropriations committees face competing demands |
| Native American caucus | Congressional caucus on Native American affairs supports expanded funding but has limited influence on appropriations |

### State-Level Opposition

| Form | Examples |
|------|----------|
| Official English laws | May be interpreted to restrict state funding for Indigenous language programs; varies by state |
| Testing mandates | State standardized testing requirements create implicit opposition to immersion education |
| Certification barriers | State refusal to create Indigenous language teaching certificates limits program growth |

## Who Benefits from the Status Quo

| Beneficiary | How |
|-------------|-----|
| English-only education establishment | No need to accommodate non-English instruction; existing curricula and teacher training remain unchanged |
| State testing companies | Standardized English-language tests continue without modification |
| Budgetary status quo | Language programs remain small and underfunded; no new mandatory spending required |
| Academic institutions | Research access to endangered languages continues on academic terms |
