# Language Preservation: Solutions

## Overview

Reversing the decline of Indigenous languages requires a comprehensive strategy that addresses the root causes identified in this analysis: historical suppression, inadequate funding, institutional barriers, and the structural dominance of English. The solutions presented here draw on successful models from the United States (particularly Hawaiian revitalization), international examples (Maori in New Zealand, Welsh in the United Kingdom), and the expertise of Indigenous language communities themselves. The overarching principle is that language revitalization must be community-driven, adequately funded, and supported by a policy environment that removes barriers rather than creating them.

## Solution Categories

### 1. Dramatically Increase Federal Funding

**Problem Addressed**: Current funding ($15.7 million annually) is a fraction of estimated need ($200-500 million annually)

**Proposal**: Increase federal appropriations for Indigenous language preservation to at least $250 million annually within 5 years

| Component | Proposed Funding | Purpose |
|-----------|-----------------|---------|
| Expanded ANA grants | $50 million | Increase grant pool and funding rate |
| Mandatory formula funding | $100 million | Base funding for every federally recognized tribe with a language program |
| Immersion school grants | $40 million | Dedicated funding for immersion schools and language nests |
| Teacher training | $25 million | Language teacher preparation and certification programs |
| Technology and documentation | $15 million | Digital tools, archives, and community-controlled databases |
| Elder speaker support | $10 million | Stipends, health support, and travel for elder speakers serving as teachers |
| Research and evaluation | $10 million | Community-directed research on language vitality and program effectiveness |

**Evidence**: The Hawaiian language revitalization, which reversed near-extinction, was supported by sustained state funding of approximately $20-30 million annually for a single language. Scaling this to 175 languages requires proportionally larger investment.

**Implementation**: Legislation authorizing mandatory spending through the Indian Self-Determination Act framework, bypassing the annual appropriations process.

### 2. Shift from Competitive to Formula-Based Funding

**Problem Addressed**: Competitive grants disadvantage small communities, create instability, and fund only 22.5% of applicants

**Proposal**: Create a formula-based funding mechanism that provides base support to every tribe with a language preservation program

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Base allocation | Minimum $100,000 annually for any federally recognized tribe with an endangered language |
| Supplemental factors | Additional funding based on number of languages, speaker population, endangerment level, and geographic isolation |
| Competitive tier | Retain a competitive grant program for large-scale, innovative, or multi-tribal projects |
| Duration | 5-year funding cycles with streamlined renewal |
| Reporting | Community-defined metrics rather than one-size-fits-all federal requirements |

**Evidence**: Self-governance compacts under the Indian Self-Determination Act demonstrate that formula-based, tribally controlled funding produces better outcomes than competitive grant programs (GAO, 2017).

### 3. Establish a National Indigenous Language Preservation Trust

**Problem Addressed**: Language programs are vulnerable to annual budget politics and changing administrations

**Proposal**: Create a permanently endowed trust fund for Indigenous language preservation

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Initial capitalization | $5 billion (comparable to the Cobell Settlement's $3.4 billion) |
| Annual distribution | Approximately $200-250 million annually at 4-5% distribution rate |
| Governance | Board of Indigenous language experts, tribal representatives, and linguists, with tribal majority |
| Funding source | Congressional appropriation as remediation for boarding school-era language destruction |
| Tax status | Tax-exempt trust with investment managed independently of federal budget |

**Evidence**: Endowment models (university endowments, the Alaska Permanent Fund) provide long-term funding stability. New Zealand's Te Matawai fund provides a partial international model for dedicated language revitalization funding.

### 4. Expand and Support Immersion Education

**Problem Addressed**: Only 25-30 full immersion schools exist nationally; immersion is the only proven method for producing fluent speakers from non-speaking communities

**Proposal**: Fund the creation of at least 100 new immersion programs within 10 years

| Component | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Language nests (pre-K) | Fund 50+ new language nest programs modeled on Punana Leo |
| Immersion schools (K-8) | Fund 30+ new immersion schools with full operational support |
| Immersion tracks in existing schools | Support 50+ existing BIE and tribal schools in developing immersion tracks |
| Teacher pipeline | Train 500+ new Indigenous language teachers over 10 years |
| Curriculum development | Fund comprehensive K-12 curricula for at least 50 languages |

**Evidence**: Hawaiian immersion schools have produced over 3,000 students fluent in Hawaiian; Cherokee immersion school in Oklahoma has graduated fully bilingual students; Maori-medium education in New Zealand has stabilized Maori language decline.

### 5. Create Indigenous Language Teacher Certification Pathways

**Problem Addressed**: Most states do not recognize Indigenous language teaching credentials; fluent speakers often cannot teach because they lack conventional education degrees

**Proposal**: Federal legislation requiring states to create Indigenous language teacher certification pathways

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Competency-based certification | Certification based on demonstrated language proficiency rather than academic degrees |
| Cultural knowledge recognition | Credit for traditional knowledge, cultural authority, and community endorsement |
| Federal incentive | Tie education funding to states that create certification pathways |
| Model policy | Develop federal model certification standards that states can adopt |
| Reciprocity | Enable certification portability across state lines within language communities |

**Evidence**: Washington State created an Indigenous language teaching certification pathway in 2023; New Mexico has allowed tribal certification of language teachers. These models demonstrate feasibility.

### 6. Reform Education Policy to Support Language Immersion

**Problem Addressed**: State testing mandates, curriculum requirements, and accountability frameworks create barriers to immersion education

**Proposal**: Amend federal and state education policy to accommodate and support language immersion

| Reform | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Testing flexibility | Allow Indigenous language immersion students to meet English proficiency standards on a delayed timeline (e.g., grade 3 rather than grade 1) |
| Curriculum waivers | Waive specific curriculum requirements for immersion programs that meet equivalent academic outcomes |
| ESSA amendment | Amend Every Student Succeeds Act to explicitly protect and support Indigenous language immersion programs |
| BIE reform | Require Bureau of Indian Education to integrate Indigenous language instruction into all BIE-funded schools where communities desire it |
| Dual-language models | Support dual-language (Indigenous language/English) models that meet both language preservation and academic goals |

**Evidence**: Hawaii's immersion students perform at or above state averages on English-language assessments by grade 5, despite receiving instruction primarily in Hawaiian in early grades (Wilson and Kamana, 2006).

### 7. Support Elder Speaker Programs

**Problem Addressed**: Elder fluent speakers are the irreplaceable source of linguistic knowledge; they are aging and often lack support

**Proposal**: Create a comprehensive support program for elder speakers who participate in language teaching

| Component | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Language Keeper stipends | Monthly stipends ($1,000-$2,000) for elders who commit to regular language teaching |
| Health support | Enhanced health care access for elder speakers, including home-based care that enables continued teaching |
| Recording and documentation | Professional documentation of elder speakers with community-controlled archiving |
| Travel support | Transportation for elders to reach programs, or for programs to reach elders |
| Intergenerational housing | Support for multigenerational living arrangements that facilitate daily language use |

### 8. Develop Community-Controlled Language Technology

**Problem Addressed**: Technology can accelerate language learning but raises data sovereignty concerns

**Proposal**: Fund tribally controlled technology development for language preservation

| Component | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Tribal data sovereignty | All language data collected with tribal consent and stored under tribal control |
| Open-source platforms | Fund development of open-source language learning tools that tribes can customize |
| AI language models | Support development of AI tools for Indigenous languages with community ownership of training data |
| Social media integration | Work with platforms to support Indigenous language characters, keyboards, and content |
| Broadcasting | Fund Indigenous language radio, podcast, and video content |

**Evidence**: FirstVoices (British Columbia) demonstrates a successful model of community-controlled language technology, serving 100+ communities with tribally owned data.

### 9. Support Urban Indigenous Language Programs

**Problem Addressed**: 78% of Indigenous people live off-reservation, often disconnected from language communities

**Proposal**: Extend language programming to urban Indigenous populations

| Component | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Urban language centers | Fund language programs at urban Indian centers and organizations |
| Weekend and evening immersion | Programs designed for working families in urban areas |
| Distance learning | Technology-mediated connection between urban learners and reservation-based speakers |
| Cultural events | Language-focused gatherings, storytelling events, and community activities in urban areas |

### 10. International Cooperation and Best Practices

**Problem Addressed**: The United States lags behind other countries with Indigenous populations in language revitalization policy

**Proposal**: Learn from and adopt proven international models

| Country | Model | Applicable Lesson |
|---------|-------|------------------|
| New Zealand | Maori Language Act, Te Matawai fund, Kohanga Reo (language nests) | Government-funded language nests and dedicated language authority |
| Wales (UK) | Welsh Language Act, S4C Welsh-language television | Government-mandated bilingual services and dedicated media |
| Canada | Indigenous Languages Act (2019), Office of the Commissioner of Indigenous Languages | Federal language commissioner and dedicated legislation |
| Norway | Sami Language Act, Sami Parliament language authority | Regional bilingual governance model |
