# Language Preservation: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative landscape for Indigenous language preservation has evolved from active suppression to formal protection, but the gap between stated policy and actual resource commitment remains vast. The Native American Languages Act of 1990 reversed over a century of federal assimilation policy by declaring it the policy of the United States to "preserve, protect, and promote" Indigenous languages. The Esther Martinez Act (2006, reauthorized 2014, 2019) created the first dedicated funding mechanism for immersion programs. However, total federal appropriations remain a small fraction of what is needed. This document proposes comprehensive legislation to match federal investment to the scale of the crisis and the government's historical responsibility.

## Constitutional Amendments

### Amendment: Indigenous Cultural Rights Protection

**Purpose**: Constitutionally protect Indigenous peoples' rights to maintain and revitalize their languages and cultures, preventing future legislative suppression.

**Draft Text**:

```text
Section 1. The rights of Indigenous peoples of the United States to
use, maintain, develop, and transmit their languages, cultures, and
traditional knowledge to future generations shall not be abridged
by the United States or by any State.

Section 2. The United States shall take affirmative measures,
including the provision of adequate resources, to support the
preservation and revitalization of Indigenous languages endangered
as a consequence of federal policy.

Section 3. Congress shall have the power to enforce this article
by appropriate legislation.

Section 4. This article shall take effect two years after the
date of ratification.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 1 establishes an affirmative constitutional right to linguistic and cultural preservation, preventing any future government from reimposing suppression policies
- Section 2 creates a constitutional basis for federal investment in language revitalization, grounded in remediation for past harm
- Section 3 provides standard enforcement authority
- Section 4 allows time for implementing legislation

**Potential Challenges**:

- Constitutional amendments require supermajority support (two-thirds of both chambers, three-fourths of state legislatures)
- May be seen as creating a special right for one group, though the political (not racial) classification of tribal membership (per *Morton v. Mancari*, 1974) supports differential treatment
- Scope of "adequate resources" is open to interpretation

**Refinements**:

- Include language clarifying that "Indigenous peoples" refers to members of federally and state-recognized tribes, Native Hawaiians, and Alaska Natives
- Consider combining with a broader Indigenous rights amendment

## Federal Legislation

### National Indigenous Language Preservation Act

**Purpose**: Create a comprehensive federal framework for Indigenous language preservation, including mandatory funding, institutional support, and education policy reform.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "National Indigenous Language
Preservation Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) Findings.—Congress finds that—
  (1) the United States operated at least 408 Indian boarding
  schools across 37 States that systematically suppressed
  Indigenous languages through forced child removal, punishment,
  and cultural destruction;
  (2) as a direct consequence of federal policy, the number
  of Indigenous languages spoken in the United States has
  declined from over 300 to approximately 175, with the majority
  critically endangered;
  (3) current federal funding of approximately $15,700,000
  annually for Indigenous language programs is grossly inadequate
  relative to the estimated need of $200,000,000 to $500,000,000
  annually;
  (4) the federal government bears primary responsibility for
  language loss and has a trust obligation to support
  revitalization; and
  (5) successful models of language revitalization, including
  Hawaiian and Cherokee immersion programs, demonstrate that
  language decline can be reversed with adequate resources.

(b) Purpose.—The purpose of this Act is to—
  (1) provide mandatory, sustained federal funding for
  Indigenous language preservation and revitalization;
  (2) create institutional infrastructure to support language
  programs nationwide;
  (3) remove educational and regulatory barriers to language
  revitalization; and
  (4) honor the federal government's trust responsibility to
  Indigenous peoples whose languages were suppressed by federal
  policy.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
  (1) INDIGENOUS LANGUAGE.—The term "Indigenous language"
  means any language that is indigenous to the United States
  or its territories, including languages of American Indian
  tribes, Alaska Native groups, and Native Hawaiians.
  (2) ELIGIBLE ENTITY.—The term "eligible entity" means a
  federally recognized Indian tribe, a tribal organization,
  a Native Hawaiian organization, an Alaska Native village
  or corporation, or a consortium thereof.
  (3) LANGUAGE NEST.—The term "language nest" means an
  early childhood program in which the Indigenous language
  is the primary or sole medium of instruction.
  (4) IMMERSION PROGRAM.—The term "immersion program" means
  an educational program in which an Indigenous language is
  used as the primary medium of instruction for at least 50
  percent of the instructional day.

SEC. 4. INDIGENOUS LANGUAGE PRESERVATION FUND.

(a) Establishment.—There is established in the Treasury of the
United States a fund to be known as the "Indigenous Language
Preservation Fund."
(b) Mandatory Funding.—There is appropriated to the Fund, out
of any money in the Treasury not otherwise appropriated—
  (1) $100,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
  (2) $175,000,000 for fiscal year 2028;
  (3) $250,000,000 for fiscal year 2029 and each fiscal year
  thereafter, adjusted annually for inflation.
(c) Formula Grants.—
  (1) IN GENERAL.—Sixty percent of amounts in the Fund shall
  be distributed as formula grants to eligible entities.
  (2) BASE AMOUNT.—Each eligible entity with an endangered
  Indigenous language shall receive a base amount of not less
  than $100,000 annually.
  (3) SUPPLEMENTAL FACTORS.—Additional amounts shall be
  allocated based on—
    (A) the number of endangered languages within the
    entity's jurisdiction;
    (B) the number of remaining fluent speakers;
    (C) the endangerment classification of each language;
    (D) geographic isolation; and
    (E) demonstrated community commitment to revitalization.
(d) Competitive Grants.—Thirty percent of amounts in the Fund
shall be available for competitive grants for—
  (1) immersion schools and language nests;
  (2) master-apprentice programs;
  (3) language technology development;
  (4) curriculum and materials development;
  (5) teacher training programs; and
  (6) multi-tribal or regional language projects.
(e) Administration.—Ten percent of amounts in the Fund shall
be available for administration, technical assistance, and
program evaluation.

SEC. 5. INDIGENOUS LANGUAGE PRESERVATION TRUST.

(a) Establishment.—There is established the Indigenous Language
Preservation Trust, a trust fund capitalized at $5,000,000,000.
(b) Capitalization.—Congress shall appropriate $1,000,000,000
annually for five fiscal years beginning in fiscal year 2027.
(c) Investment.—The Trust shall be invested in accordance with
the investment policies applicable to the Thrift Savings Fund.
(d) Distribution.—Beginning in fiscal year 2032, the Trust
shall distribute not more than 5 percent of its corpus annually
for Indigenous language preservation programs.
(e) Governance.—The Trust shall be governed by a Board of
Trustees consisting of—
  (1) nine members appointed by the President, of whom at
  least six shall be enrolled members of federally recognized
  tribes with expertise in language preservation;
  (2) two members recommended by the National Congress of
  American Indians; and
  (3) two members recommended by the National Indian
  Education Association.

SEC. 6. EDUCATION POLICY REFORM.

(a) Testing Flexibility.—The Secretary of Education shall
grant waivers to Indigenous language immersion programs from
English-language proficiency testing requirements through grade
3, provided that students demonstrate English proficiency by
grade 5.
(b) Teacher Certification.—
  (1) FEDERAL STANDARD.—The Secretary of Education shall
  establish, in consultation with tribal nations, a model
  Indigenous language teacher certification pathway based on
  demonstrated language proficiency and cultural competency
  rather than academic degree requirements.
  (2) STATE INCENTIVE.—Any State that fails to create an
  Indigenous language teacher certification pathway within
  3 years of enactment shall be ineligible for a 2 percent
  increase in Title I education funding.
(c) BIE Integration.—The Director of the Bureau of Indian
  Education shall integrate Indigenous language instruction
  into all BIE-funded schools where the local tribal community
  requests such instruction.

SEC. 7. ELDER SPEAKER SUPPORT.

(a) Language Keeper Program.—The Administration for Native
Americans shall establish a Language Keeper Program to provide
monthly stipends of not less than $1,500 to elder fluent
speakers who participate in language teaching for not fewer
than 20 hours per month.
(b) Health Support.—Elder speakers participating in the
Language Keeper Program shall be eligible for enhanced health
services through the Indian Health Service, including home-based
care and transportation assistance.

SEC. 8. DATA SOVEREIGNTY.

(a) Tribal Ownership.—All language data collected, recorded,
or archived with federal funding under this Act shall be the
property of the tribal nation or community from which the
language originates.
(b) Access Controls.—Eligible entities shall determine the
terms of access to language data, including restrictions on
recording, publication, and digital distribution of language
materials.
(c) Federal Compliance.—Federal agencies and federally funded
researchers shall comply with tribal data sovereignty
determinations.

SEC. 9. OFFICE OF INDIGENOUS LANGUAGES.

There is established within the Department of the Interior an
Office of Indigenous Languages, headed by a Director appointed
by the Secretary, to—
  (1) coordinate federal language preservation activities;
  (2) provide technical assistance to eligible entities;
  (3) conduct a biennial survey of Indigenous language
  vitality;
  (4) administer grants under this Act; and
  (5) report annually to Congress on the status of
  Indigenous language preservation.

SEC. 10. AUTHORIZATION AND EFFECTIVE DATE.

(a) This Act is authorized to be carried out beginning in
fiscal year 2027.
(b) The Secretary shall promulgate implementing regulations
within 180 days of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- Creates the first mandatory federal funding for Indigenous language preservation, removing programs from annual appropriations uncertainty
- Combines formula-based and competitive grant mechanisms to ensure both baseline support for all communities and additional resources for innovative programs
- Addresses key institutional barriers (teacher certification, testing, BIE integration)
- Protects tribal data sovereignty over language materials
- Establishes a permanent trust fund for long-term sustainability

**Potential Challenges**:

- $5 billion trust capitalization is a significant appropriation
- Mandatory spending proposals face resistance from fiscal conservatives
- State education policy is traditionally a state prerogative; federal certification mandates may face pushback
- Trust governance requires careful design to maintain tribal control

**Refinements**:

- Phased capitalization of trust over 5 years reduces annual budget impact
- Frame as remediation for documented federal harm (boarding school reports) rather than new spending
- Include opt-in rather than mandatory state participation, with funding incentives

### Durbin Feeling Native American Languages Act (Enhancement)

**Purpose**: Create a national survey of language vitality and teacher training programs

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Durbin Feeling Native American
Languages Act".

SEC. 2. NATIONAL SURVEY.

(a) The Administration for Native Americans shall conduct a
comprehensive national survey of Indigenous language vitality
every 5 years, in partnership with tribal nations.
(b) The survey shall include—
  (1) speaker population by age cohort;
  (2) intergenerational transmission rates;
  (3) domains of language use;
  (4) existing program capacity; and
  (5) community-identified priorities.

SEC. 3. TEACHER TRAINING GRANTS.

(a) The Secretary of Education, in consultation with tribal
nations, shall award grants to tribal colleges and universities
for Indigenous language teacher training programs.
(b) Authorization.—There is authorized to be appropriated
$25,000,000 annually for fiscal years 2027 through 2032.
```

**Explanation**: Provides the data infrastructure necessary for evidence-based language policy and addresses the critical teacher shortage.

**Potential Challenges**: Requires tribal consent for survey participation; some communities may prefer not to share vitality data publicly.

**Refinements**: Include provisions for tribes to control the dissemination of survey data about their languages.

## State Model Legislation

### Model Indigenous Language Teacher Certification Act

**Purpose**: Create a pathway for fluent speakers to become certified teachers without conventional degree requirements.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Indigenous Language Teacher
Certification Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Indigenous language" means a language indigenous to the
territory of the present-day United States that is spoken by
members of a federally or state-recognized Indian tribe, Alaska
Native group, or Native Hawaiian community.
(b) "Tribal certification" means a determination by a tribal
government or its designee that an individual possesses
sufficient language proficiency and cultural knowledge to serve
as a language instructor.

SECTION 3. CERTIFICATION PATHWAY.

(a) The [State Board of Education / Department of Education]
shall create an Indigenous Language Teaching Certificate
available to individuals who—
  (1) possess fluency in an Indigenous language as certified
  by the relevant tribal government; and
  (2) complete a teacher preparation program of not fewer
  than 120 hours, which may include culturally specific
  pedagogy training provided by tribal education departments.
(b) The Indigenous Language Teaching Certificate shall authorize
the holder to teach the certified language in any public school,
charter school, or tribally controlled school within the State.
(c) Holders of Indigenous Language Teaching Certificates shall
be compensated at rates equivalent to other certified teachers
with comparable experience.

SECTION 4. RECIPROCITY.

The [State] shall recognize Indigenous Language Teaching
Certificates issued by other States for the same language.
```

**Explanation**: Removes one of the most significant barriers to language program expansion by allowing fluent speakers to teach without requiring a bachelor's degree, while maintaining quality standards through tribal certification and pedagogical training.

**Adaptations**: States may adjust the pedagogical training hours, add mentorship requirements, or create tiered certification levels.

### Model Indigenous Language Revitalization Fund Act

**Purpose**: Create state-level matching funds for tribal language programs.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Indigenous Language
Revitalization Fund Act".

SECTION 2. FUND ESTABLISHMENT.

There is established in the [State Treasury] the Indigenous
Language Revitalization Fund.

SECTION 3. APPROPRIATION.

The Legislature shall appropriate [$X million] annually to
the Fund.

SECTION 4. USE OF FUNDS.

(a) Funds shall be distributed as matching grants to tribal
nations and Indigenous language organizations operating within
the State.
(b) Eligible uses include—
  (1) language immersion programs;
  (2) master-apprentice programs;
  (3) language teaching materials development;
  (4) technology-based language learning tools; and
  (5) elder speaker support programs.
(c) Matching requirement: Applicants must demonstrate a
1:1 match from tribal, federal, or private sources.
```

**Explanation**: Creates sustained state-level investment in language preservation, complementing federal funding and tribal investment.

**Adaptations**: States may adjust appropriation levels, matching ratios, and eligible activities based on the number and needs of Indigenous language communities within their borders.

## Regulatory Framework

### Administration for Native Americans (ANA)

**Existing Authority**: Native American Languages Act (25 U.S.C. 2901-2906); Esther Martinez Act (P.L. 109-394)

**Proposed Regulatory Reforms**:

| Reform | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Extend grant cycles | Increase maximum grant duration from 3 years to 5 years |
| Simplify applications | Reduce application length and complexity for grants under $250,000 |
| Streamline reporting | Allow community-defined metrics in addition to federal requirements |
| Increase flexibility | Allow grantees to modify program activities without re-application when community needs change |
| Pre-award technical assistance | Provide grant-writing workshops and application support for small tribes |

### Bureau of Indian Education (BIE)

**Existing Authority**: Indian Self-Determination Act (P.L. 93-638); Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA)

**Proposed Regulatory Reforms**:

| Reform | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Language integration mandate | Require all BIE-funded schools to offer Indigenous language instruction where communities request it |
| Immersion school standards | Develop standards for immersion schools that measure language outcomes alongside academic outcomes |
| Bilingual hiring preference | Prioritize hiring of bilingual staff at BIE schools |
| Curriculum flexibility | Allow BIE schools to substitute Indigenous language instruction for foreign language requirements |

### Department of Education

**Existing Authority**: ESSA, Title VI (Indian Education)

**Proposed Regulatory Reforms**:

| Reform | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Testing waivers | Issue guidance clarifying that immersion schools may receive waivers from English-language testing through grade 3 |
| Certification guidance | Issue model standards for Indigenous language teacher certification |
| Title VI alignment | Ensure Indian Education formula grants can be used for language programming |

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Indian Commerce Clause**: Congress has plenary authority over Indian affairs under Art. I, Sec. 8, Cl. 3, providing a constitutional basis for language legislation. *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023) affirmed this authority.
- **Political classification**: *Morton v. Mancari* (1974) established that classifications based on tribal membership are political, not racial, and therefore not subject to strict scrutiny under the Equal Protection Clause. This supports differential treatment for Indigenous language programs.
- **First Amendment**: Language rights are implicitly protected under freedom of speech and free exercise of religion (for ceremonial language).
- **Tenth Amendment**: Federal education policy requirements may face state sovereignty objections, though the spending power provides leverage.

### Preemption Questions

- Federal language policy (NALA, Esther Martinez Act) preempts conflicting state English-only laws as applied to Indigenous language programs
- State education standards remain primarily a state function, but federal funding conditions can incentivize compliance
- Tribal sovereignty over education on tribal land is recognized under the Self-Determination Act

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- Formula funding provides automatic enforcement through distribution
- State certification incentives use funding conditions rather than mandates
- Data sovereignty provisions enforceable through federal grant conditions
- Office of Indigenous Languages provides monitoring and reporting

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Trust Fund: Permanent with annual audit and 5-year board review
- Formula grants: 10-year authorization with mandatory reauthorization
- Competitive grants: 5-year authorization cycles
- National survey: Mandatory every 5 years

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### National Indigenous Language Preservation Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Formula gaming | Entities could overstate language endangerment to increase formula allocations | Medium |
| Certification bypass | States could create minimal-quality certification pathways that technically comply but do not produce effective teachers | Medium |
| Data sovereignty evasion | Researchers could argue that pre-existing data collections are exempt from tribal ownership provisions | High |
| Trust fund diversion | Future Congresses could redirect trust fund corpus to other purposes | Critical |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address languages with no living speakers | Dormant language reclamation is not specifically funded | Focus on revitalization assumes living speakers |
| Limited private-sector engagement | No mechanisms to encourage technology companies to support Indigenous languages | Government-centric approach |
| No international coordination | Transborder languages (e.g., languages shared with Canada or Mexico) are not addressed | Domestic legislation scope |
| Trust capitalization timeline | $5 billion over 5 years may face appropriations resistance | Political feasibility |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Add a dedicated category for dormant language reclamation projects in competitive grants, with evaluation criteria appropriate to archival-based language work
2. Create tax incentives for technology companies that develop Indigenous language tools under tribal data sovereignty protocols
3. Authorize the Office of Indigenous Languages to enter into agreements with Canadian and Mexican counterpart agencies for transborder language coordination
4. Include sequestration protections for the Trust Fund to prevent future diversion
5. Require the national language survey to include independent verification of endangerment classifications to prevent formula gaming

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Administrative capacity in small tribes | Provide technical assistance and allow consortium applications |
| Brain drain from language programs | Require competitive compensation for language teachers |
| Technology obsolescence | Fund platform maintenance, not just development |
| Cultural sensitivity of standardized metrics | Allow community-defined success measures alongside federal reporting |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Office of Indigenous Languages shall conduct a comprehensive program review every 5 years
- Trust Fund Board shall report annually to Congress on investment performance and program outcomes
- Formula allocation factors shall be reviewed and adjusted every 10 years based on the national language survey
- Competitive grant categories shall be updated every 5 years to reflect evolving needs and best practices
