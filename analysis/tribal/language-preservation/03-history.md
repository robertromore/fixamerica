# Language Preservation: History

## Pre-Colonial Linguistic Diversity

### The Original Landscape

Before European contact, the territory that would become the United States was home to extraordinary linguistic diversity:

| Metric | Detail | Source |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Estimated language families | 60+ distinct language families in North America | Campbell, *American Indian Languages*, 1997 |
| Estimated individual languages | 300+ in the territory of the present-day U.S. | Mithun, *The Languages of Native North America*, 1999 |
| Linguistic density | Greater language diversity per square mile than Europe | Goddard, *Handbook of North American Indians*, vol. 17, 2001 |
| Estimated speakers | 5-15 million people across hundreds of language communities | Thornton, *American Indian Holocaust and Survival*, 1987 |

### Language Families of North America

Indigenous North American languages represent dozens of unrelated language families, a level of diversity that reflects tens of thousands of years of independent development:

- **Algonquian**: Largest family by geographic range; includes Ojibwe, Cree, Blackfoot, Cheyenne, Arapaho, and dozens of eastern languages
- **Siouan-Catawban**: Lakota, Dakota, Crow, Osage, Hidatsa, and related languages of the Plains and Southeast
- **Iroquoian**: Mohawk, Seneca, Cherokee, Oneida, and related languages
- **Uto-Aztecan**: Comanche, Shoshone, Hopi, O'odham, and languages extending into Mexico
- **Athabaskan (Dene)**: Navajo, Apache languages, Tlingit, and numerous languages across Alaska, Canada, and the Southwest
- **Salishan**: Languages of the Pacific Northwest plateau and coast
- **Muskogean**: Choctaw, Chickasaw, Muscogee (Creek), and related southeastern languages
- **Language isolates**: Languages with no known relatives, such as Zuni, Haida, and Kutenai

### Functions of Language in Indigenous Societies

Indigenous languages served roles that extended far beyond communication:

- **Governance**: Treaty negotiations, intertribal diplomacy, and political oratory were conducted in Indigenous languages, with translation requiring deep cultural understanding
- **Ecological knowledge**: Thousands of years of environmental observation were encoded in language, including terms for ecological relationships, seasonal patterns, and land management practices with no English equivalents
- **Spiritual practice**: Ceremonial language, songs, prayers, and oral histories carried sacred knowledge transmitted across generations
- **Legal systems**: Customary law, conflict resolution processes, and social norms were expressed and transmitted through language
- **Identity**: Language was and remains central to tribal identity, kinship systems, and community belonging

## Colonial and Early American Period (1500s-1860s)

### Early Contact and Multilingualism

| Period | Development | Impact |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| 1500s-1600s | European explorers and traders learn Indigenous languages | Multilingualism is pragmatic necessity; interpreters are valued |
| 1600s-1700s | Missionaries develop writing systems for some Indigenous languages | Mixed effects: documentation but with assimilationist intent |
| 1700s | Trade pidgins and lingua francas develop | Functional communication but not language preservation |
| 1778-1871 | Treaty period (374 treaties ratified) | Treaties negotiated across languages; translation failures contributed to unjust terms |

### Missionary Linguistics

European missionaries were among the first to document Indigenous languages, but their motives were assimilationist:

- **John Eliot**: Translated the Bible into Massachusett (1663), creating the first Bible printed in the Americas; the language later became dormant
- **Sequoyah**: Developed the Cherokee syllabary (1821), enabling Cherokee literacy independent of missionary control -- a notable exception to the missionary pattern
- **Franciscan missions (California)**: Documented California languages while simultaneously disrupting the communities that spoke them
- **Catholic and Protestant schools**: Some early mission schools taught in Indigenous languages, but increasingly shifted to English-only instruction

### Early Federal Policy

The federal government's earliest approaches to Indigenous languages were inconsistent:

- **Civilization Fund Act (1819)**: Provided $10,000 annually to religious organizations to "civilize" Indians, including English instruction, but did not explicitly ban Indigenous languages
- **Indian Removal Act (1830)**: Forced relocation disrupted language communities, separating speakers from their linguistic homelands
- **Treaty provisions**: Some treaties included provisions for education, which gradually became English-only education

## The Boarding School Era (1879-1969)

### Origins and Philosophy

The Indian boarding school system represents the most deliberate and destructive federal assault on Indigenous languages:

| Event | Date | Significance |
|-------|------|-------------|
| Carlisle Indian Industrial School founded | 1879 | Captain Richard Henry Pratt established the model: "Kill the Indian, save the man" |
| Peak of boarding school system | 1900-1930 | Over 350 schools operating; 83% of school-age Native children enrolled (Adams, *Education for Extinction*, 1995) |
| Total schools documented | 1879-1969 | At least 408 federal boarding schools across 37 states (DOI, 2022) |
| Burial sites identified | As of 2024 | Approximately 53 schools with marked or unmarked burial sites (DOI, 2024) |

### Language Suppression Methods

Boarding schools employed systematic methods to eradicate Indigenous languages:

- **Forced separation**: Children as young as 4-5 were removed from families, severing the primary context for language transmission
- **Punishment**: Students were beaten, had their mouths washed with soap, were denied food, or were placed in solitary confinement for speaking their languages (Lomawaima, *They Called It Prairie Light*, 1994)
- **English-only rules**: Schools enforced strict English-only policies, with continuous surveillance by staff
- **Cultural humiliation**: Students' names were changed to English names, hair was forcibly cut, and traditional clothing was confiscated
- **Peer pressure systems**: Schools sometimes used older students to enforce English-only rules among younger students

### Scale and Impact

| Metric | Detail | Source |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Total children enrolled | Estimated 100,000+ at system's peak | Adams, *Education for Extinction*, 1995 |
| Duration of removal | Typically 4-8 years; some children were away from home for entire childhoods | Lomawaima, 1994 |
| Children who returned speaking their language | A rapidly declining percentage over time; many children lost fluency entirely | Reyhner & Eder, *American Indian Education*, 2004 |
| Intergenerational effect | Parents who attended boarding schools were significantly less likely to speak Indigenous languages to their own children | Debenport, *Fixing the Books*, 2015 |

### Psychological and Cultural Impact

The boarding school experience created lasting trauma that continues to affect language transmission:

- **Language shame**: Many boarding school survivors internalized the message that their languages were inferior, refusing to speak them even after returning home
- **Interrupted parenting**: Children raised in institutions did not learn parenting practices that included language transmission
- **Community disruption**: The absence of entire generations of children disrupted community language use patterns
- **Cultural association**: Some survivors associated their languages with punishment and trauma, creating psychological barriers to transmission

## Termination and Relocation Era (1945-1968)

### Policy Impact on Languages

| Policy | Dates | Language Impact |
|--------|-------|-----------------|
| Indian Termination Policy | 1953-1968 | 109 tribes terminated; loss of land base and community cohesion further weakened language use |
| Urban Relocation Program | 1952-1972 | Relocated 750,000+ Native people to cities (Fixico, *Termination and Relocation*, 1986); severed speakers from language communities |
| Public Law 280 | 1953 | Transferred jurisdiction to states, imposing English-language legal systems |
| House Concurrent Resolution 108 | 1953 | Declared intent to end federal-tribal relationship, undermining cultural programs |

### Urban Relocation and Language Loss

The Bureau of Indian Affairs' relocation program moved hundreds of thousands of Native people from reservations to cities between the 1950s and 1970s:

- Relocated families were deliberately placed away from other Native people to encourage assimilation
- Urban environments provided no institutional support for Indigenous language use
- Children raised in cities were educated exclusively in English
- Over time, urban Native populations became predominantly English-speaking
- Today approximately 78% of American Indians and Alaska Natives live in urban areas (Census Bureau, 2020), many disconnected from language communities

## Self-Determination and Revitalization (1968-Present)

### Policy Reversal

| Event | Date | Significance |
|-------|------|-------------|
| Indian Civil Rights Act | 1968 | Began shift away from termination policy |
| Indian Education Act | 1972 | First federal support for culturally appropriate education |
| Indian Self-Determination Act | 1975 | Enabled tribal control of education programs |
| American Indian Religious Freedom Act | 1978 | Protected ceremonial language and practice |
| Native American Languages Act (NALA) | 1990 | Declared federal policy to preserve and promote Indigenous languages |
| Esther Martinez Act | 2006 | Authorized grants for immersion programs and language nests |
| Esther Martinez Reauthorization | 2014, 2019 | Extended and expanded language preservation funding |

### The Native American Languages Act of 1990

NALA (25 U.S.C. 2901-2906) was a landmark policy reversal:

- Acknowledged the "unique status" of Indigenous languages and the federal government's "responsibility" for their decline
- Declared it federal policy to "preserve, protect, and promote" Indigenous languages
- Recognized the right of Native Americans to use their languages in education, government, and daily life
- Did not, however, include any funding mechanism -- a critical limitation

### Grassroots Revitalization Movements

Community-driven language efforts often preceded federal policy support:

- **Punana Leo (Hawaiian, 1983)**: Founded Hawaiian-language preschools when fewer than 50 children spoke Hawaiian; now enrolls 3,000+ students in immersion programs
- **Piegan Institute (Blackfoot, 1987)**: Founded the Cuts Wood School, a Blackfoot immersion school in Montana
- **Akwesasne Freedom School (Mohawk, 1979)**: Mohawk immersion school operating independently of BIA funding
- **Advocates for Indigenous California Language Survival (1992)**: Developed the Master-Apprentice Language Learning Program (MALLP) model
- **Cherokee Nation Immersion School (2001)**: Cherokee language immersion charter school in Tahlequah, Oklahoma
- **Wampanoag Language Reclamation Project (1993)**: Jessie Little Doe Baird began reclaiming Wampanoag from historical documents; the language had no living speakers since the mid-19th century

### Esther Martinez and the Push for Immersion

The Esther Martinez Native American Languages Preservation Act (P.L. 109-394, 2006) was named for a Tewa storyteller and linguist from Ohkay Owingeh Pueblo:

- Authorized competitive grants specifically for language immersion programs (survival schools) and language nests (early childhood immersion)
- Required programs to achieve fluency goals, not merely introduce vocabulary
- First reauthorization (2014): Extended program through 2019
- Second reauthorization (2019): Extended through 2024; expanded eligibility

## Lessons from History

### What the Historical Record Demonstrates

1. **Language loss was deliberate policy**: The decline of Indigenous languages was not natural attrition but the result of conscious federal policy aimed at cultural destruction
2. **Intergenerational trauma compounds language loss**: The effects of boarding school policies extend across generations, creating psychological as well as practical barriers to language transmission
3. **Community-led efforts are most effective**: The most successful revitalization programs -- Hawaiian, Mohawk, Cherokee -- were initiated by communities, not by federal agencies
4. **Immersion produces speakers**: Exposure-based approaches and vocabulary instruction do not produce fluent speakers; intensive immersion does
5. **It is never too late**: The Wampanoag reclamation project demonstrates that even languages with no living speakers can be revived from historical records
6. **Federal support matters but must follow community leadership**: The Esther Martinez Act has funded critical programs, but success depends on community autonomy and culturally appropriate approaches
