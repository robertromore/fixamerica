# Language Preservation: Stakeholders

## Overview

Indigenous language preservation involves a diverse set of stakeholders with varying degrees of power, investment, and proximity to the issue. The most important stakeholders are Indigenous communities themselves -- the speakers, learners, and cultural knowledge holders whose languages are at risk. However, federal agencies, educational institutions, linguists, technology companies, and state governments all play significant roles in shaping the conditions under which revitalization succeeds or fails.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Elder Fluent Speakers

**Role**: The irreplaceable holders of linguistic knowledge

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Estimated number | Fewer than 25,000 fluent elder speakers across all U.S. Indigenous languages (Indigenous Language Institute, 2023) |
| Age profile | Approximately 65% are over 60 years old (Indigenous Language Institute, 2023) |
| Urgency | Each elder's passing represents permanent loss of unique linguistic knowledge |
| Challenges | Health limitations, geographic isolation, insufficient compensation for teaching |

**Interests**:

- Transmission of language to younger generations before it is too late
- Respect for cultural protocols regarding what knowledge can be shared and how
- Appropriate compensation and recognition for their role as language authorities
- Health and wellness support that extends their capacity to teach

### Indigenous Language Learners

**Role**: The next generation of speakers essential for language survival

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Immersion school students | Approximately 3,000-5,000 across all programs (NIEA, 2023) |
| Adult learners | Thousands more in community classes, master-apprentice programs, and self-study |
| Motivations | Cultural identity, family connection, community responsibility, professional goals |
| Challenges | Limited program availability, competing demands, geographic access |

**Interests**:

- Access to high-quality immersion programs and learning opportunities
- Certification pathways that recognize language skills professionally
- Community contexts where language use is valued and practiced daily
- Career opportunities that utilize and reward language abilities

### Tribal Governments

**Role**: Sovereign authorities with responsibility for cultural preservation

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Number with language programs | Approximately 150-200 tribes operate some form of language program (NCAI, 2023) |
| Funding sources | Federal grants, tribal revenue, gaming proceeds, philanthropy |
| Policy authority | Can declare official languages, mandate language instruction, allocate resources |
| Challenges | Competing priorities (health, housing, infrastructure), limited revenue |

**Interests**:

- Sovereignty over language policy and educational approaches
- Adequate federal funding without burdensome reporting requirements
- Control over how languages are documented, archived, and shared
- Integration of language into tribal governance and services

### Tribal Language Programs and Schools

**Role**: Institutional vehicles for language transmission

| Program Type | Estimated Number | Key Organizations |
|-------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Immersion schools | 25-30 | Punana Leo, Cherokee Immersion School, Akwesasne Freedom School |
| Language nests | 30-40 | Various tribal pre-K programs |
| Community programs | 100+ | Tribal language departments, cultural centers |
| Master-apprentice pairs | 200+ | AICLS and adaptations nationwide |

**Interests**:

- Stable, long-term funding rather than short-term competitive grants
- Flexibility to design culturally appropriate curricula
- Teacher training and certification recognition
- Assessment methods that value immersion outcomes over standardized English-language testing

### Native Language Teachers

**Role**: Educators who deliver language instruction

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Estimated number | Fewer than 500 certified Indigenous language teachers nationally (NIEA, 2023) |
| Certification barriers | Most states do not offer Indigenous language teaching certificates |
| Compensation | Often paid less than English-language teachers; many serve as volunteers |
| Training | Limited university programs that train Indigenous language teachers |

**Interests**:

- State certification pathways that recognize language competency without requiring conventional education degrees
- Competitive compensation on par with other teachers
- Professional development and peer networking opportunities
- Autonomy to teach according to community cultural norms

## Secondary Stakeholders

### Federal Agencies

#### Administration for Native Americans (ANA)

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Budget (language programs) | $15.7 million (FY 2024) |
| Role | Primary federal grant-making body for language preservation |
| Programs | Native American Languages (NAL) and Esther Martinez Immersion (EMI) grants |
| Limitations | Small budget, competitive grants only, 3-year funding cycles |

#### Bureau of Indian Education (BIE)

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Schools operated/funded | 183 schools serving approximately 46,000 students (BIE, 2023) |
| Role | Operates and funds schools on reservations; can integrate language instruction |
| Limitations | Historically underinvested in language programs; administrative challenges |

#### National Science Foundation (NSF)

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| DEL Program budget | $4.8 million (FY 2024) |
| Role | Funds linguistic documentation research through Documenting Endangered Languages program |
| Focus | Academic documentation rather than community revitalization |

#### National Endowment for the Humanities (NEH)

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Language-related funding | $2.3 million (FY 2024) |
| Role | Supports humanities-oriented language documentation and archival projects |

### Academic and Research Institutions

| Stakeholder | Role | Key Institutions |
|------------|------|-----------------|
| Linguistics departments | Language documentation, grammatical analysis, training linguists | MIT, University of Arizona, University of Hawaii |
| Language archives | Preserving recordings, texts, and other language materials | Endangered Languages Archive (ELAR), Survey of California Languages (UC Berkeley) |
| Tribal colleges and universities | Community-based language education and research | 37 tribal colleges across the U.S. (AIHEC, 2023) |

**Interests**:

- Access to language data for research (may conflict with community data sovereignty)
- Publication and academic recognition
- Collaborative relationships with communities
- Funding for fieldwork and documentation

### Philanthropic Organizations

|-------------|-------------|

### Technology Developers

| Organization | Role |
|-------------|------|
| Duolingo | Free language courses (Navajo, Hawaiian) |
| FirstVoices (First Peoples' Cultural Council) | Open-source platform for language archiving and learning |
| Rosetta Stone | Free Endangered Languages program (7 Indigenous languages) |

- Positive social impact branding
- Technology development and testing

## Tertiary Stakeholders

### State Governments

| Role | Current Status |
|------|---------------|
| Education policy | Control teacher certification, curriculum standards, and testing requirements that affect language instruction |
| Funding | Some states (New Mexico, Montana, Washington) provide matching funds for language programs |
| Language recognition | States can officially recognize Indigenous languages (Hawaii is the only current example at constitutional level) |

### General Public

| Interest | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| Cultural heritage | Many Americans value linguistic diversity as part of the nation's heritage |
| Educational innovation | Immersion education models can inform broader education policy |
| Historical accountability | Growing awareness of boarding school history creates support for remediation |
| Taxpayer concerns | Some question government spending on language programs serving small communities |

### International Organizations

| Organization | Role |
|-------------|------|
| UNESCO | Maintains Atlas of the World's Languages in Danger; declared 2022-2032 International Decade of Indigenous Languages |
| United Nations | UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP) affirms language rights |
| International Labour Organization | ILO Convention 169 protects Indigenous cultural and linguistic rights |

## Stakeholder Power Analysis

| Stakeholder | Interest Level | Power Level | Relationship |
|------------|---------------|-------------|--------------|
| Elder speakers | Very high | Low (individually) | Essential knowledge holders with limited political power |
| Tribal governments | High | Moderate | Sovereignty provides authority but resources are limited |
| Language programs | Very high | Low | Dependent on external funding and policy decisions |
| ANA/federal agencies | Moderate | High | Control funding but have limited budgets |
| Congress | Variable | Very high | Controls appropriations and authorization |
| State governments | Low to moderate | High | Control education policy that affects language instruction |
| Academic institutions | Moderate | Moderate | Research capacity but may not align with community priorities |
| Technology companies | Low | Moderate | Resources and platform reach but limited commitment |

## Stakeholder Conflicts

### Community Control vs. Academic Research

Tensions exist between tribal communities seeking to preserve their languages on their own terms and academic linguists seeking to document and analyze languages for scholarly purposes:

- **Data sovereignty**: Communities increasingly assert ownership over linguistic data; some restrict recording and sharing of language materials
- **Research priorities**: Academic linguists may prioritize grammatical analysis over practical teaching materials
- **Credit and authorship**: Questions about who benefits from language research and documentation
- **Cultural protocols**: Some language elements (ceremonial, sacred) are not appropriate for outside documentation

### Immersion vs. Exposure Approaches

Within communities, stakeholders may disagree on the best approach to language revitalization:

- **Immersion advocates**: Argue that only full immersion produces fluent speakers; cite Hawaiian and Maori models
- **Exposure proponents**: Favor integrating language into existing English-medium education; easier to implement but less effective at producing fluency
- **Practical constraints**: Many communities lack the teachers and resources for full immersion, making exposure-based approaches the only realistic option

### Federal Funding Priorities

Disagreement exists over how limited federal funds should be allocated:

- **Large vs. small programs**: Should funding prioritize languages with the most speakers (larger impact) or those most critically endangered (greatest urgency)?
- **Documentation vs. revitalization**: Should resources go to recording languages for posterity or to producing new speakers?
- **Competitive vs. formula**: Should funding remain competitive (rewarding strong proposals) or shift to formula-based distribution (ensuring all communities receive support)?
