# Language Preservation: Actions

## Overview

Citizens -- both Indigenous and non-Indigenous -- can take meaningful action to support Indigenous language preservation. The actions below range from individual advocacy to systemic engagement, organized by the level of effort and commitment required.

## For All Citizens

### Learn and Educate

- **Educate yourself about boarding school history**: Read the Department of the Interior's Federal Indian Boarding School Initiative reports (2022, 2024) to understand the federal government's role in language destruction
- **Learn basic words and phrases**: If you live in an area with Indigenous language communities, learn basic greetings and phrases as a sign of respect and to normalize language visibility
- **Use available resources**: Access free courses like Duolingo's Navajo and Hawaiian courses, or explore FirstVoices and 7000 Languages platforms
- **Share accurate information**: Counter misinformation about Indigenous languages being "dead" or unimportant by sharing facts about language vitality and revitalization successes
- **Attend cultural events**: Support community language events, storytelling sessions, and cultural gatherings that are open to the public

### Advocate for Policy Change

- **Contact your congressional representatives**: Urge support for:
    - Increased appropriations for ANA language programs (at least $250 million annually)
    - The Durbin Feeling Native American Languages Act
    - Reauthorization and expansion of the Esther Martinez Act
    - Creation of a National Indigenous Language Preservation Trust
- **Support state-level reforms**: Advocate for:
    - Indigenous language teacher certification pathways in your state
    - State matching funds for tribal language programs (following New Mexico's 2024 model)
    - Testing flexibility for immersion school students
- **Oppose English-only legislation**: Contact legislators to oppose Official English bills that could restrict support for Indigenous language programs

## For Indigenous Community Members

### Individual Actions

- **Speak your language daily**: Even limited use helps normalize the language and creates learning opportunities for others
- **Learn from elders**: Seek out elder speakers in your community; participate in master-apprentice programs if available
- **Teach your children**: Prioritize language use at home, even if you are not fully fluent; partial transmission is better than none
- **Use language on social media**: Post in your Indigenous language to increase its visibility and normalize digital use
- **Record family members**: With permission and appropriate cultural protocols, record elder relatives speaking, telling stories, and teaching language
- **Attend language programs**: Enroll in community classes, evening programs, or summer immersion camps

### Community Actions

- **Advocate for tribal language policy**: Support tribal government resolutions declaring official languages and mandating language instruction
- **Start or support a language nest**: Organize pre-K immersion programs, even informally, with elder speakers and young children
- **Create language domains**: Establish language-only spaces at community gatherings, in tribal offices, and at cultural events
- **Form language learning groups**: Organize regular speaking groups for adult learners at various levels
- **Engage tribal government**: Advocate for allocation of tribal revenue to language programs; support language requirements for tribal government proceedings
- **Develop curricula**: Work with educators to create age-appropriate teaching materials in your language

### For Tribal Governments

| Action | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Declare official tribal language | Formal resolution establishing the Indigenous language as the tribe's official language |
| Fund language programs | Allocate tribal revenue to language immersion, master-apprentice, and community programs |
| Establish language authority | Create a tribal language commission or department with dedicated staff |
| Adopt language requirements | Require some level of language instruction in tribally controlled schools |
| Support teacher training | Fund community members to become certified language teachers |
| Assert data sovereignty | Establish protocols for how language data is collected, stored, and shared |
| Partner with BIE | Work with Bureau of Indian Education to integrate language into BIE-funded schools |
| Apply for federal grants | Submit applications to ANA, NSF, NEH, and IMLS for language funding |

## For Educators

### K-12 Teachers

- **Learn about Indigenous languages in your area**: Understand which tribal nations are local and what their language status is
- **Incorporate Indigenous language awareness**: Include age-appropriate information about Indigenous languages and their history in curricula
- **Support Indigenous language students**: Value bilingualism; never discourage students from speaking their heritage languages
- **Advocate for school policy**: Push for Indigenous language instruction and cultural curriculum in your school or district
- **Collaborate with tribal educators**: Build relationships with tribal education departments and language programs

### Higher Education

- **Create Indigenous language courses**: Universities can offer courses in regional Indigenous languages
- **Develop language teacher training programs**: Establish programs that prepare Indigenous language teachers for certification
- **Support collaborative research**: Ensure that linguistic research benefits the language communities it studies
- **Respect data sovereignty**: Follow tribal protocols for research involving language data
- **Hire Indigenous language faculty**: Create positions for Indigenous language specialists

## For Linguists and Researchers

- **Prioritize community needs**: Design research that serves community revitalization goals, not just academic publication
- **Share materials**: Ensure that documentation materials are accessible to and controlled by the language community
- **Train community linguists**: Build community capacity for ongoing documentation and analysis rather than creating dependency on outside researchers
- **Respect cultural protocols**: Some language elements may not be appropriate for recording, publication, or outside access
- **Advocate for funding**: Support increased NSF, NEH, and ANA funding for language programs
- **Produce practical materials**: Create dictionaries, grammars, and teaching materials that are useful for community learners, not just specialists

## For Technology Professionals

- **Support open-source projects**: Contribute to open-source language learning and documentation platforms
- **Build under tribal direction**: Any technology work should be guided by and accountable to the language community
- **Respect data sovereignty**: Language data belongs to the community; design systems that keep communities in control
- **Address keyboard and character support**: Help ensure Indigenous language characters and diacritical marks are supported across platforms
- **Develop AI tools responsibly**: AI language tools should be developed with community consent and ownership of training data

## Advocacy Timeline

| Timeframe | Priority Actions |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Immediate | Contact Congress about language funding; donate to language organizations; learn about local Indigenous languages |
| 1-3 months | Write to state legislators about teacher certification; volunteer professional skills |
| 3-6 months | Organize community awareness events about boarding school history and language preservation |
| 6-12 months | Support ballot initiatives or legislative campaigns for state language funding |
| Ongoing | Maintain advocacy pressure on federal and state legislators; sustain financial support; continue learning |
