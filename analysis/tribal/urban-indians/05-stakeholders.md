# Urban Indians: Stakeholders

## Overview

The urban Indian policy landscape involves a complex web of stakeholders with overlapping interests, competing priorities, and varying degrees of power. Understanding these relationships is essential for identifying pathways to reform.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Urban American Indian and Alaska Native Individuals and Families

**Interest**: Access to healthcare, housing, education, employment, cultural community, and recognition of their ongoing relationship with tribal nations and the federal government.

**Power Level**: Low individually, moderate when organized through urban Indian organizations.

**Key Concerns**:

- Healthcare access through UIHPs or alternative providers
- Affordable housing in urban areas
- Cultural programming and community connection
- Educational opportunities for children, including culturally relevant curricula
- Accurate enumeration and data representation
- Maintaining tribal enrollment and connection to home communities

**Internal Diversity**: This is not a monolithic group. It includes recent reservation migrants, multi-generational urban families, members of hundreds of different tribes, individuals with varying degrees of cultural connection, and people of mixed heritage with diverse identity orientations.

### Urban Indian Health Programs (UIHPs)

**Interest**: Adequate funding, expanded capacity, recognition as essential components of the IHS delivery system, and ability to serve the full eligible population.

**Power Level**: Moderate through the National Council of Urban Indian Health and congressional relationships.

**Key Concerns**:

- Funding parity with IHS direct service and tribal health programs
- Medicaid and Medicare reimbursement rates
- Workforce recruitment and retention
- Facility maintenance and expansion
- Telehealth infrastructure
- Data collection and reporting requirements

### Urban Indian Centers and Organizations

**Interest**: Sustainable funding, institutional recognition, and capacity to serve as community hubs for urban Native populations.

**Power Level**: Low to moderate, depending on local political relationships and organizational capacity.

**Key Concerns**:

- Core operating funding rather than project-specific grants
- Facility space for cultural, social, and service programming
- Recognition as legitimate service providers by federal and state agencies
- Capacity to address growing and diverse needs
- Staffing and administrative infrastructure

### Tribal Governments

**Interest**: Maintaining sovereignty, serving enrolled members (including those living off-reservation), and ensuring federal Indian policy does not diminish tribal authority or resources.

**Power Level**: High through government-to-government relationship, treaty rights, and organized advocacy (National Congress of American Indians).

**Key Concerns**:

- Urban Indian funding should not come at the expense of tribal program funding
- Tribal authority over enrollment and service eligibility must be preserved
- Urban Indian services should complement, not replace, tribal services
- Circular migration patterns mean reservation and urban services are interconnected
- Data collection should distinguish between tribal-specific and pan-Indian needs

**Tension**: Some tribal governments view urban Indian advocacy as potentially diverting resources from reservation-based needs. Others actively support urban service delivery for their off-reservation members.

## Federal Stakeholders

### Indian Health Service (IHS)

**Interest**: Fulfilling its mission to "raise the physical, mental, social, and spiritual health of American Indians and Alaska Natives to the highest level" within budget constraints.

**Power Level**: High as the primary federal agency for AI/AN health.

**Key Concerns**:

- Balancing funding across direct service, tribal, and urban delivery systems
- Meeting Congressional Budget Justification requirements
- Addressing provider shortages across all delivery systems
- Expanding telehealth and alternative delivery models
- Managing competing demands from tribal and urban constituencies

### Bureau of Indian Affairs (BIA)

**Interest**: Administering federal Indian programs and fulfilling trust responsibility obligations.

**Power Level**: Moderate as the primary administrative agency for Indian affairs.

**Key Concerns**:

- The BIA's mandate is largely reservation-focused; urban Indian services are not a primary function
- Tribal enrollment verification for urban service delivery
- Coordination with other federal agencies serving urban populations
- Housing, education, and social service delivery through tribal rather than urban channels

### Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD)

**Interest**: Addressing housing needs across all populations, including AI/AN populations in urban and tribal areas.

**Power Level**: Moderate in urban Indian housing policy through NAHASDA and mainstream housing programs.

**Key Concerns**:

- NAHASDA funds flow to tribally designated housing entities, not urban Indian organizations
- Section 184 loan program utilization rates among urban Indians
- Coordination between tribal housing programs and mainstream urban housing programs
- Homelessness among urban AI/AN populations

### Department of Education

**Interest**: Educational outcomes for all students, including AI/AN students in urban school systems.

**Power Level**: Moderate through Title VI (Indian Education) and other federal education programs.

**Key Concerns**:

- AI/AN student identification and data collection in urban districts
- Culturally relevant curricula and teacher training
- Dropout prevention and college readiness
- Johnson-O'Malley Act funding eligibility in urban settings

### Census Bureau

**Interest**: Accurate enumeration of all populations, including AI/AN populations in urban areas.

**Power Level**: High in terms of data that drives funding and redistricting.

**Key Concerns**:

- Reducing AI/AN undercount in future Census operations
- Improving racial classification methods
- Outreach strategies for hard-to-count urban AI/AN populations
- American Community Survey methodology for small populations

## Congressional Stakeholders

### Congressional Native American Caucus

**Interest**: Advancing legislation to address Native American issues, including urban Indian needs.

**Power Level**: Moderate through legislative influence and issue visibility.

**Key Members**: Historically bipartisan; includes members from states with significant AI/AN populations.

### Relevant Committees

- **Senate Committee on Indian Affairs**: Primary jurisdiction over federal Indian policy
- **House Natural Resources Committee, Subcommittee on Indian and Insular Affairs**: House jurisdiction over Indian legislation
- **Appropriations Subcommittees on Interior**: Control IHS and BIA funding levels

## State and Local Stakeholders

### State Governments

**Interest**: Service delivery to all residents, including urban AI/AN populations; managing state Medicaid programs that interact with IHS/UIHP services.

**Power Level**: Moderate through state funding decisions, Medicaid administration, and regulatory authority.

**Key Concerns**:

- Medicaid reimbursement for services to AI/AN individuals
- State-funded social services for urban Native populations
- K-12 education for AI/AN students in public school systems
- State-tribal relations that affect urban service delivery

### Municipal Governments

**Interest**: Serving all city residents equitably, including AI/AN populations.

**Power Level**: Low to moderate, depending on local AI/AN population size and political engagement.

**Key Concerns**:

- Limited awareness of urban Indian needs in many cities
- Homeless services and housing programs
- Cultural recognition and inclusion
- Police-community relations with Native populations

## Advocacy and Research Stakeholders

### National Congress of American Indians (NCAI)

**Interest**: Advocating for tribal sovereignty and the rights of all AI/AN people, including urban Indians.

**Power Level**: High as the largest and most influential national AI/AN advocacy organization.

### National Council of Urban Indian Health (NCUIH)

**Interest**: Advocating specifically for Urban Indian Health Programs and the health needs of urban AI/AN populations.

**Power Level**: Moderate through congressional testimony, policy research, and coalition building.

### National Urban Indian Family Coalition (NUIFC)

**Interest**: Strengthening urban Indian families and communities through advocacy and networking.

**Power Level**: Low to moderate.

### Academic Institutions

**Interest**: Research on urban Indian health, education, and social outcomes; training Native professionals.

**Key Institutions**: University of Arizona, University of New Mexico, University of Minnesota, UCLA American Indian Studies Center, tribal colleges and universities.
