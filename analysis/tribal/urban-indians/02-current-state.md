# Urban Indians: Current State

## Demographics

### Population Distribution

The American Indian and Alaska Native population has become overwhelmingly urban. According to the 2020 Census, approximately 71% of the 9.7 million people who identify as AI/AN (alone or in combination) live in metropolitan areas. This represents a continuation of a decades-long trend: in 1970, only about 45% of AI/AN people lived in urban areas; by 1990 it was 56%; and by 2010 it had reached 67% (U.S. Census Bureau; Norris, Vines, and Hoeffel, 2012).

### Socioeconomic Conditions

Urban AI/AN populations face significant disparities compared to both the general urban population and, in some cases, reservation populations:

- **Poverty**: The urban AI/AN poverty rate is approximately 22.1%, nearly double the national average of 12.4% (U.S. Census Bureau ACS, 2022)
- **Unemployment**: Urban AI/AN unemployment runs approximately 9.4%, compared to 5.3% nationally (Bureau of Labor Statistics, 2023)
- **Homeownership**: Urban AI/AN homeownership rate is approximately 47%, compared to 65% nationally (U.S. Census Bureau ACS, 2022)
- **Health insurance**: Approximately 21% of urban AI/AN adults lack health insurance, compared to 10% nationally, despite eligibility for IHS services (National Council of Urban Indian Health, 2023)
- **Educational attainment**: Approximately 16% of urban AI/AN adults hold a bachelor's degree or higher, compared to 33% nationally (U.S. Census Bureau ACS, 2022)

### Diversity of Urban Indian Experience

The urban AI/AN population is not monolithic. It includes:

- Members of federally recognized tribes who maintain active enrollment and connection to their home communities
- Descendants of relocated families who may be multiple generations removed from reservation life
- Members of state-recognized tribes or tribes seeking federal recognition
- Individuals with mixed heritage who identify as AI/AN
- Recent arrivals from reservations seeking employment, education, or healthcare
- Families who cycle between urban and reservation settings ("circular migration")

## Service Delivery

### Urban Indian Health Programs

The Indian Health Service operates through three delivery systems: IHS direct service facilities, tribal health programs operated under the Indian Self-Determination Act (P.L. 93-638), and Urban Indian Health Programs (UIHPs). Of these three, UIHPs receive the smallest share of funding despite serving the largest share of the AI/AN population.

**UIHP Funding and Capacity**

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Number of UIHPs | 41 programs in 33 states | IHS, 2024 |
| Annual UIHP budget | Approximately $100 million | IHS Congressional Justification, FY 2025 |
| Share of total IHS budget | Approximately 1.5% | National Council of Urban Indian Health, 2023 |
| Estimated urban AI/AN population served | Approximately 600,000 per year | NCUIH, 2023 |
| Eligible but unserved urban AI/AN | Estimated 3-4 million | NCUIH, 2023 |

UIHPs provide a range of services depending on their size and funding, including:

- Outpatient medical and dental care
- Behavioral health and substance abuse treatment
- HIV/AIDS prevention and treatment
- Health education and disease prevention
- Community health outreach
- Referral services to other providers

However, most UIHPs cannot offer the full range of services available at IHS or tribal facilities. Only a handful operate as full ambulatory care centers; many function primarily as referral and outreach organizations.

### Housing

Urban AI/AN populations face severe housing challenges:

- **HUD programs**: The Native American Housing Assistance and Self-Determination Act (NAHASDA) primarily funds tribally designated housing entities (TDHEs) that operate on or near reservations. Urban Natives generally must rely on mainstream HUD programs, where they compete with the general population
- **Section 184 loans**: The Indian Home Loan Guarantee Program (Section 184) is available to tribal members purchasing homes in any location, but awareness and utilization rates among urban Indians remain low
- **Homelessness**: AI/AN people are disproportionately represented among the urban homeless population, comprising approximately 4% of homeless individuals despite being about 2.9% of the total population (HUD Annual Homeless Assessment Report, 2023)

### Education

Urban AI/AN students attend mainstream public schools where:

- Curricula rarely include Native history, culture, or perspectives
- Teachers and administrators may lack cultural competency training
- AI/AN students are often too few in number to trigger Title VI or Johnson-O'Malley Act support
- Dropout rates remain elevated, with approximately 28% of urban AI/AN students failing to complete high school on time (National Indian Education Association, 2023)
- Access to Native language instruction is extremely limited in urban school districts

### Employment and Economic Opportunity

- Urban AI/AN workers are concentrated in service, retail, and construction sectors
- Access to federal Indian preference hiring programs (under the Indian Self-Determination Act and other authorities) is limited in urban settings
- Small business development resources through tribal programs are generally unavailable to urban Indians
- Discrimination in hiring remains a documented concern, with AI/AN job applicants receiving fewer callbacks than comparably qualified white applicants in audit studies (Quillian et al., 2017)

## Urban Indian Organizations

### Structure and Role

Urban Indian organizations serve as the primary institutional infrastructure for urban Native communities. These include:

- **Urban Indian Health Programs**: The 41 IHS-funded UIHPs providing healthcare services
- **Urban Indian centers**: Community-based organizations providing social services, cultural programming, and gathering space
- **National Urban Indian Family Coalition (NUIFC)**: Network of urban Indian organizations advocating at the national level
- **National Council of Urban Indian Health (NCUIH)**: Advocacy organization for UIHPs
- **Local cultural organizations**: Dance groups, language classes, art collectives, and spiritual communities

### Capacity Challenges

Urban Indian organizations face persistent challenges:

- **Funding instability**: Most rely on a patchwork of federal, state, local, and private grants with short funding cycles
- **Staffing shortages**: Competitive urban labor markets make it difficult to recruit and retain qualified staff at nonprofit salary levels
- **Space constraints**: Many urban Indian centers operate in inadequate facilities with limited room for programming
- **Jurisdictional complexity**: Navigating federal, state, and local funding streams requires administrative capacity that small organizations often lack
- **Population diversity**: Serving members of dozens or hundreds of different tribes requires cultural sensitivity and flexibility

## Data and Enumeration

### Census Undercount

The AI/AN population has been undercounted in every modern Census. The 2020 Census Post-Enumeration Survey estimated that the AI/AN population living on reservations was undercounted by 5.64%, the highest undercount of any racial group (U.S. Census Bureau, 2022). Urban AI/AN undercount rates are less precisely measured but are believed to be significant due to:

- Complex living situations (doubling up, transient housing)
- Distrust of government surveys
- Racial misclassification (AI/AN individuals classified as "some other race" or Hispanic)
- Language barriers for some Alaska Native populations
- Low response rates in areas without targeted outreach

### Consequences of Data Gaps

Inaccurate population data has cascading effects:

- **Reduced federal funding**: Many formula-based programs allocate funds based on Census population counts
- **Political underrepresentation**: Legislative districts may not accurately reflect AI/AN population concentrations
- **Inadequate service planning**: Health systems, schools, and social services cannot plan effectively without accurate demographic data
- **Invisibility**: Low population counts reinforce the perception that urban Indians are a negligible population

## Recent Developments

### Legislative

- The **Advancing Health Equity for Native Americans Act** (introduced 2023) would increase UIHP funding authorization levels
- The **Urban Indian Health Parity Act** (introduced 2023) would establish funding parity between UIHPs and other IHS delivery systems
- Reauthorization discussions for the **Indian Health Care Improvement Act** have included provisions for expanded urban services

### Administrative

- IHS has expanded telehealth services accessible to urban AI/AN populations since the COVID-19 pandemic
- The Biden administration's Executive Order 13985 on advancing racial equity included tribal consultation provisions
- Several UIHPs have expanded their service areas and added behavioral health capacity with supplemental COVID-era funding

### Judicial

- *Brackeen v. Haaland* (2023): Supreme Court upheld the Indian Child Welfare Act, affirming Congress's broad authority over Indian affairs, with implications for the legal framework supporting urban Indian services
- Ongoing litigation over Medicaid reimbursement rates for UIHPs continues in several states
