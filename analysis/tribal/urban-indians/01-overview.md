# Urban Indians: Overview

## Executive Summary

More than 70% of the American Indian and Alaska Native (AI/AN) population now lives in urban areas, a dramatic reversal from the mid-twentieth century when the vast majority resided on reservations or in rural communities. This shift -- driven partly by federal relocation policies of the 1950s and 1960s, and partly by the pursuit of economic opportunity -- has created a population that often falls through the cracks of both federal Indian policy and mainstream urban social services. Urban Indians frequently lack access to Indian Health Service (IHS) facilities, tribal social programs, cultural community, and the political infrastructure that reservation-based governance provides, while simultaneously facing discrimination, poverty, and invisibility in the cities where they live.

The federal trust responsibility does not diminish when a Native person moves to a city. Yet federal programs have historically been structured around reservation geography, leaving urban Indian populations underserved. Urban Indian Health Programs (UIHPs) serve only a fraction of the eligible population and receive a small share of IHS funding. Housing, education, employment, and cultural programming for urban Natives remain chronically underfunded and fragmented across federal, state, and local agencies that often do not recognize or prioritize this population.

Addressing the needs of urban Indians requires expanding service delivery beyond reservation boundaries, strengthening the network of urban Indian organizations, ensuring accurate Census enumeration, and building policy frameworks that follow people rather than geography.

## Core Tensions

- **Reservation-centric policy vs. urban reality**: Federal Indian programs are designed around reservation geography, but most Native people live in cities
- **Tribal enrollment vs. urban identity**: Urban Indians may maintain tribal enrollment but lack access to tribal services; some urban Natives lack enrollment entirely
- **Federal responsibility vs. jurisdictional ambiguity**: The trust responsibility follows individuals, but federal agencies often disclaim responsibility for urban Natives
- **Cultural preservation vs. assimilation pressures**: Urban environments offer economic opportunity but can erode tribal language, ceremony, and community bonds
- **Data invisibility vs. need for resources**: Census undercounts and racial misclassification reduce the political visibility and funding allocations for urban Indians
- **Pan-Indian community vs. tribal specificity**: Urban Indian centers serve members of many tribes, creating pan-Indian community but sometimes at the expense of tribe-specific cultural practices

## Key Questions

1. **How did federal policy create the urban Indian population?** What were the goals and consequences of the Indian Relocation Act and termination-era policies?

2. **Why are urban Indians underserved?** How do federal funding formulas and program structures exclude urban Native populations?

3. **What role do urban Indian organizations play?** How effective are urban Indian centers, and what capacity do they need?

4. **How can cultural identity be maintained in urban settings?** What programs and institutions support urban Native cultural life?

5. **How does the Census undercount affect urban Indians?** What are the consequences for political representation and federal funding?

6. **What policy reforms would close the service gap?** How can federal programs be restructured to serve Native people wherever they live?

## Vision of Success

A system that adequately serves urban Indians would feature:

- Federal programs structured to follow Native individuals rather than being tied exclusively to reservation geography
- Fully funded Urban Indian Health Programs with capacity to serve the entire eligible urban AI/AN population
- Robust networks of urban Indian centers providing cultural programming, social services, employment assistance, and community connection
- Accurate Census enumeration that captures the full urban AI/AN population
- Strong political representation and advocacy infrastructure for urban Native communities
- Cultural preservation programs -- including language, ceremony, and arts -- accessible in major urban areas
- Coordinated federal, state, local, and tribal service delivery that eliminates jurisdictional gaps
