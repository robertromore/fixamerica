# Urban Indians: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative landscape for urban Indians is defined by a fundamental gap: the trust responsibility exists but the delivery infrastructure does not match where the population lives. Federal Indian law -- treaties, statutes, court decisions -- establishes a government-to-government relationship that inherently applies to individuals, not just geographic areas. Yet the vast majority of statutes, appropriations, and regulations are structured around reservation geography. Closing this gap requires both new legislation and amendments to existing law.

## Constitutional Considerations

### Existing Constitutional Authority

No constitutional amendment is necessary to serve urban Indians. Congress's authority over Indian affairs is well-established:

- **Article I, Section 8, Clause 3** (Indian Commerce Clause): Congress has the power "to regulate Commerce with foreign Nations, and among the several States, and with the Indian Tribes"
- **Treaty Clause** (Article II, Section 2): Treaties with tribal nations are the "supreme Law of the Land" under Article VI
- **Plenary Power Doctrine**: The Supreme Court has recognized Congress's broad authority over Indian affairs, most recently affirmed in *Brackeen v. Haaland* (2023)
- **Trust Responsibility**: A judicially recognized obligation arising from treaties and the course of dealing between the United States and tribal nations

The political classification of Indian status -- upheld in *Morton v. Mancari* (1974) and *Brackeen v. Haaland* (2023) -- provides constitutional protection for legislation targeting AI/AN populations, distinguishing Indian law from race-based classifications subject to strict scrutiny.

## Federal Legislation

### Bill 1: Urban Indian Health Parity Act

**Purpose**: Establish funding parity between Urban Indian Health Programs and other IHS delivery systems, ensuring that the approximately 71% of AI/AN people living in urban areas have equitable access to IHS-quality healthcare.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Urban Indian Health Parity Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) Findings.--Congress finds that--
    (1) approximately 71 percent of the American Indian and Alaska
        Native population resides in urban areas;
    (2) Urban Indian Health Programs receive approximately 1.5 percent
        of the Indian Health Service budget;
    (3) the federal trust responsibility to American Indian and Alaska
        Native people is not diminished by residence in an urban area;
    (4) the disparity between UIHP funding and the urban AI/AN
        population constitutes a failure to fulfill the trust
        responsibility; and
    (5) UIHPs have demonstrated cost-effective delivery of culturally
        competent healthcare services.

(b) Purpose.--The purpose of this Act is to establish equitable
    funding for Urban Indian Health Programs commensurate with the
    urban AI/AN population served.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
    (1) INDIAN.--The term "Indian" has the meaning given in section
        4 of the Indian Health Care Improvement Act (25 U.S.C. 1603).
    (2) URBAN CENTER.--The term "urban center" has the meaning given
        in section 4 of the Indian Health Care Improvement Act.
    (3) URBAN INDIAN ORGANIZATION.--The term "urban Indian
        organization" has the meaning given in section 4 of the Indian
        Health Care Improvement Act.
    (4) UIHP.--The term "UIHP" means an Urban Indian Health Program
        operated by an urban Indian organization under a contract or
        grant pursuant to title V of the Indian Health Care
        Improvement Act.

SEC. 4. FUNDING PARITY.

(a) In General.--Beginning with fiscal year 2027, the Secretary of
    Health and Human Services shall ensure that the per-capita
    allocation for UIHPs is not less than 50 percent of the per-capita
    allocation for the Indian Health Service direct service program,
    increasing by 10 percentage points annually until parity is
    achieved.

(b) Calculation.--For purposes of subsection (a), the per-capita
    allocation shall be determined by dividing total program funding
    by the eligible service population as determined by the most
    recent Census data and IHS user population estimates.

(c) Authorization of Appropriations.--There are authorized to be
    appropriated such sums as may be necessary to carry out this
    section, including--
    (1) $300,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
    (2) $500,000,000 for fiscal year 2028;
    (3) $700,000,000 for fiscal year 2029;
    (4) $900,000,000 for fiscal year 2030; and
    (5) such sums as necessary for each fiscal year thereafter.

(d) Supplement Not Supplant.--Funds appropriated under this section
    shall supplement and not supplant existing IHS appropriations for
    direct service and tribal health programs.

SEC. 5. EXPANSION OF URBAN INDIAN HEALTH PROGRAMS.

(a) New Programs.--The Secretary shall establish not fewer than 15
    new Urban Indian Health Programs in metropolitan statistical areas
    with significant AI/AN populations that do not currently have a
    UIHP, prioritizing areas with the greatest unmet need.

(b) Application Process.--The Secretary shall establish a
    competitive application process for new UIHPs, developed in
    consultation with existing UIHPs, tribal governments, and the
    National Council of Urban Indian Health.

(c) Facility Construction.--The Secretary may provide grants for
    construction, renovation, or lease of facilities for UIHPs
    authorized under this section.

SEC. 6. UIHP SELF-DETERMINATION AUTHORITY.

(a) Contracting Authority.--Urban Indian organizations operating
    UIHPs shall be eligible to enter into self-determination
    contracts under the Indian Self-Determination and Education
    Assistance Act (25 U.S.C. 5301 et seq.) for the operation of
    health programs serving urban AI/AN populations.

(b) Compacting Authority.--Urban Indian organizations meeting the
    requirements of section 403 of the Indian Self-Determination
    and Education Assistance Act may negotiate self-governance
    compacts for the delivery of health services.

SEC. 7. REPORTING.

Not later than 2 years after the date of enactment of this Act, and
annually thereafter, the Secretary shall submit to Congress a report
on--
    (1) per-capita spending comparisons across IHS delivery systems;
    (2) urban AI/AN health outcomes and disparities;
    (3) UIHP service utilization and unmet need; and
    (4) progress toward funding parity.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the most critical and measurable disparity in urban Indian services. By establishing a statutory funding formula and phased parity requirement, it removes the need for annual appropriations battles over UIHP funding levels. The self-determination authority provision recognizes UIHPs as capable partners in healthcare delivery, analogous to tribal health programs.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Appropriations: Achieving actual funding at authorized levels requires sustained political support
- CBO scoring: The significant cost increase will require offsets or deficit spending authorization
- Tribal opposition: Some tribes may view expanded UIHP authority as competition; the supplement-not-supplant provision addresses this
- Defining eligible population: Census undercount makes the denominator for per-capita calculations uncertain

**Refinements**:

- Include mandatory appropriations language to insulate funding from annual appropriations politics
- Add tribal consultation requirements for new UIHP establishment
- Include workforce development provisions (loan repayment, scholarships) to ensure staffing capacity

### Bill 2: Urban Indian Trust Responsibility Codification Act

**Purpose**: Codify the principle that the federal trust responsibility applies to all AI/AN individuals regardless of residence and require federal agencies to serve urban AI/AN populations.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Urban Indian Trust Responsibility
Codification Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
    (1) the federal trust responsibility arises from treaties,
        statutes, executive orders, and the course of dealing between
        the United States and tribal nations;
    (2) this responsibility attaches to individual American Indians
        and Alaska Natives as well as to tribal nations;
    (3) the trust responsibility is not diminished or extinguished
        by the residence of an AI/AN individual in an urban area;
    (4) federal agencies have inconsistently applied the trust
        responsibility to urban AI/AN populations; and
    (5) clarification of this obligation is necessary to ensure
        equitable service delivery.

SEC. 3. CODIFICATION OF TRUST RESPONSIBILITY.

(a) Declaration.--It is the policy of the United States that the
    trust responsibility of the federal government to American
    Indian and Alaska Native people applies regardless of the
    geographic residence of the individual, including in urban areas.

(b) Agency Obligations.--Each federal agency that administers
    programs serving American Indian and Alaska Native populations
    shall--
    (1) ensure that program design, eligibility criteria, and
        delivery mechanisms do not exclude or disadvantage AI/AN
        individuals residing in urban areas;
    (2) within 2 years of enactment, submit to Congress an
        assessment of how its programs serve urban AI/AN populations
        and a plan to address any identified gaps; and
    (3) include urban AI/AN service delivery in its annual
        performance reporting.

SEC. 4. OFFICE OF URBAN INDIAN AFFAIRS.

(a) Establishment.--There is established within the Department of
    the Interior an Office of Urban Indian Affairs.

(b) Director.--The Office shall be headed by a Director appointed
    by the Secretary of the Interior in consultation with urban
    Indian organizations and tribal governments.

(c) Functions.--The Office shall--
    (1) coordinate federal agency efforts to serve urban AI/AN
        populations;
    (2) provide technical assistance to urban Indian organizations;
    (3) consult with tribal governments on urban service delivery;
    (4) advise the Secretary on urban Indian policy matters; and
    (5) submit an annual report to Congress on the state of urban
        Indian affairs.

SEC. 5. INTERAGENCY COORDINATION.

(a) Council.--There is established an Interagency Council on Urban
    Indian Affairs, chaired by the Director of the Office of Urban
    Indian Affairs and including representatives from--
    (1) the Indian Health Service;
    (2) the Bureau of Indian Affairs;
    (3) the Department of Housing and Urban Development;
    (4) the Department of Education;
    (5) the Department of Labor;
    (6) the Department of Justice; and
    (7) such other agencies as the Director determines appropriate.

(b) Duties.--The Council shall--
    (1) identify and address jurisdictional gaps in urban AI/AN
        service delivery;
    (2) develop interagency data-sharing agreements;
    (3) coordinate outreach to urban AI/AN communities; and
    (4) recommend legislative and administrative actions.

SEC. 6. TRIBAL CONSULTATION.

Nothing in this Act shall be construed to--
    (1) diminish the government-to-government relationship between
        the United States and tribal nations;
    (2) alter tribal authority over enrollment, membership, or
        service eligibility;
    (3) reduce funding for tribal programs; or
    (4) create urban Indian governance structures that compete with
        tribal sovereignty.
```

**Explanation**: This bill establishes the legal principle that has been implicit but unenforced: the trust responsibility follows the person. The institutional mechanism (Office of Urban Indian Affairs, Interagency Council) provides accountability and coordination that currently do not exist. The tribal protection provisions are essential for securing tribal support.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Scope: The bill's broad mandate could create implementation challenges across multiple agencies
- Tribal sovereignty concerns: Careful drafting and consultation are needed to address concerns about parallel governance structures
- Enforcement: Without mandatory funding, agency compliance may be aspirational rather than substantive
- Political will: Creating a new federal office requires sustained political support

**Refinements**:

- Add specific enforcement mechanisms, including congressional oversight hearings and GAO audits
- Include urban Indian advisory committees at the agency level
- Specify minimum staffing and funding for the Office of Urban Indian Affairs

## State Model Legislation

### Model Urban Indian Services Act

**Purpose**: Establish state-level recognition of urban Indian populations and require state agencies to address their needs.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "[State] Urban Indian Services Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.
(a) "American Indian or Alaska Native" means a person who is a
    member of a federally recognized tribe, a state-recognized
    tribe, or who meets the eligibility criteria of the Indian
    Health Care Improvement Act.
(b) "Urban Indian organization" means a nonprofit organization
    that serves the health, social service, or cultural needs of
    urban AI/AN populations.
(c) "Urban area" means a metropolitan statistical area as defined
    by the Office of Management and Budget.

SECTION 3. STATE INDIAN AFFAIRS COMMISSION -- URBAN COMPONENT.
(a) The [State] Indian Affairs Commission shall include at least
    two members representing urban AI/AN communities.
(b) The Commission shall prepare a biennial report on the status
    of urban AI/AN populations in the state, including
    recommendations for policy and funding changes.

SECTION 4. MEDICAID REIMBURSEMENT.
(a) The state Medicaid agency shall ensure that services provided
    to AI/AN individuals at Urban Indian Health Programs are
    reimbursed at the 100 percent Federal Medical Assistance
    Percentage rate authorized by 42 U.S.C. 1396d(b).
(b) The agency shall conduct outreach to facilitate Medicaid
    enrollment among urban AI/AN populations.

SECTION 5. EDUCATION.
(a) The state Department of Education shall develop and make
    available curricula on Native American history, culture, and
    contemporary issues for use in public schools.
(b) School districts with 50 or more AI/AN students shall
    designate an Indian education liaison.

SECTION 6. DATA COLLECTION.
State agencies shall include AI/AN racial and ethnic identifiers
in data collection systems and report AI/AN-specific data in
annual reports to the legislature.
```

**Explanation**: This model legislation addresses state-level gaps in urban Indian services. The Medicaid provision is particularly important because many states fail to claim the enhanced FMAP rate for services to AI/AN individuals at UIHPs, leaving federal money on the table and increasing costs for UIHPs and their patients.

**Adaptations**: States with larger AI/AN populations may add more robust provisions, including state-funded housing programs, workforce development initiatives, and cultural preservation grants. States with state-recognized tribes should ensure definitions are inclusive.

## Regulatory Framework

### IHS Administrative Action: UIHP Telehealth Expansion

**Existing Authority**: Indian Health Care Improvement Act, Title V (25 U.S.C. § 1651 et seq.)

**Draft Regulation**:

The Indian Health Service shall:

1. Establish telehealth as a standard modality of care for all UIHPs
2. Provide technology infrastructure grants to UIHPs for telehealth implementation
3. Authorize UIHPs to serve AI/AN patients outside their immediate geographic service area via telehealth
4. Develop reimbursement policies for telehealth services that are equivalent to in-person visit rates
5. Create interoperability standards for electronic health records across IHS, tribal, and urban delivery systems

**Explanation**: Telehealth represents the most efficient way to expand UIHP reach without the capital costs of new facilities. IHS has existing authority to implement these changes administratively but has not done so comprehensively.

### Census Bureau Administrative Action: AI/AN Enumeration Improvement

**Existing Authority**: 13 U.S.C. § 141 (Census authority)

**Draft Administrative Guidance**:

1. Allocate dedicated funding for AI/AN outreach in the 2030 Census operational plan
2. Partner with urban Indian organizations as Complete Count Committee partners
3. Hire AI/AN enumerators in metropolitan areas with significant AI/AN populations
4. Revise racial classification question format to reduce AI/AN misclassification
5. Develop supplemental enumeration procedures for AI/AN populations in complex housing situations

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Political Classification**: Federal Indian law is based on a political classification (tribal membership and the federal-tribal relationship), not a racial classification. This was affirmed in *Morton v. Mancari* (1974) and *Brackeen v. Haaland* (2023). Urban Indian legislation should explicitly connect eligibility to tribal enrollment or IHS eligibility criteria rather than racial identity
- **Spending Power**: Congress has broad authority to appropriate funds for programs fulfilling the trust responsibility, including urban service delivery
- **Commerce Clause**: The Indian Commerce Clause provides independent authority for legislation affecting AI/AN populations

### Preemption Questions

- State laws cannot diminish federal trust responsibility obligations
- State Medicaid programs must comply with federal FMAP requirements for AI/AN populations
- State education laws operate alongside, not in lieu of, federal Indian education authorities
- UIHPs that receive both federal and state funding must navigate potentially conflicting regulatory requirements

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- Congressional oversight through the Senate Committee on Indian Affairs and House Subcommittee on Indian and Insular Affairs
- GAO auditing and reporting on agency compliance
- Federal court jurisdiction for claims based on trust responsibility violations
- Administrative appeals within IHS, BIA, and other agencies
- Ombudsman function within the proposed Office of Urban Indian Affairs

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Proposed legislation includes biennial reporting requirements enabling congressional oversight
- UIHP funding parity progress reports create accountability benchmarks
- Interagency Council mandated to produce annual assessments
- 10-year reauthorization cycle for the Office of Urban Indian Affairs

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Urban Indian Health Parity Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Authorization vs. appropriation gap | Congress may authorize funding levels but fail to appropriate them, a persistent pattern in federal Indian programs | Critical |
| Population denominator manipulation | IHS could use narrow definitions of "eligible service population" to reduce per-capita calculations and claim parity at lower absolute funding | High |
| Supplement-not-supplant evasion | Future Congresses could reduce baseline IHS funding and redirect it to UIHPs, technically complying while cutting total Indian health spending | High |
| New UIHP establishment delays | The competitive application process could be slow-walked by unsympathetic administrations, delaying expansion indefinitely | Medium |
| Self-determination contract terms | Federal contracting requirements could impose administrative burdens that offset the benefits of self-determination authority for small UIHPs | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Workforce shortage unaddressed | Funding increases without workforce may not translate to expanded services | Medical workforce pipeline for AI/AN health is underdeveloped |
| Facility limitations | New funding without facility construction creates bottlenecks | Capital funding is separate from operational funding |
| Eligibility verification complexity | Some urban AI/AN individuals may face barriers establishing eligibility | IHS eligibility criteria require tribal enrollment or descent documentation |
| Data dependency on Census | Funding formula depends on Census data that undercounts AI/AN population | Census methodology has not been reformed |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Include mandatory appropriations language or a dedicated funding mechanism (e.g., Indian Health Service Trust Fund) to close the authorization-appropriation gap
2. Define "eligible service population" in statute using the broadest defensible criteria, including Census-based estimates supplemented by IHS user population data
3. Establish a baseline protection: total IHS funding (direct service + tribal + urban) must increase by at least the UIHP parity increment each year
4. Set statutory deadlines for new UIHP establishment with judicial review for unreasonable delays
5. Create simplified contracting procedures for UIHPs with annual budgets below $5 million
6. Include loan repayment, scholarship, and training provisions to address workforce shortages
7. Authorize facility construction grants as part of the parity framework

### Urban Indian Trust Responsibility Codification Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Agency compliance without substance | Agencies could submit assessments and plans without meaningfully changing programs | High |
| Office underfunding | The Office of Urban Indian Affairs could be established with insufficient staff and budget to fulfill its mandate | High |
| Tribal consultation as veto | The tribal consultation requirement could be used to delay or block urban service expansion if framed as opposition | Medium |
| Reporting without accountability | Annual reports may document problems without creating obligations to fix them | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No enforcement mechanism | Agencies face no consequences for noncompliance | Bill emphasizes coordination over mandates |
| No dedicated funding | Trust responsibility codification without funding is declaratory | Funding requires separate legislation |
| Scope breadth | Covering all federal agencies creates diffuse accountability | Urban Indian issues are genuinely cross-cutting |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Add specific performance metrics and congressionally mandated timelines for agency compliance
2. Authorize appropriations for the Office of Urban Indian Affairs at levels sufficient for its mandate (minimum $10 million annually)
3. Specify that tribal consultation is advisory, not a veto, while preserving tribal sovereignty protections
4. Establish a private right of action for urban Indian organizations to challenge agency noncompliance
5. Require agencies to designate senior officials responsible for urban AI/AN service delivery

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Census undercount undermines formula funding | Conduct supplemental AI/AN population surveys between Census years; adjust formulas using multiple data sources |
| Tribal-urban political tension | Ensure all new urban funding is additive; create joint tribal-urban advisory mechanisms |
| Administrative capacity of UIHPs and urban Indian organizations | Provide multi-year capacity-building grants before expanding service mandates |
| Cross-jurisdictional complexity | Establish clear MOUs between federal, state, local, and tribal entities |
| Political vulnerability to changing administrations | Use mandatory funding and permanent authorization to insulate programs from political cycles |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- All programs should include 5-year performance reviews with public reporting
- The Interagency Council should produce a comprehensive assessment every 3 years
- Congress should hold oversight hearings on urban Indian issues at least annually through the Senate Committee on Indian Affairs
- GAO should be directed to audit urban Indian program implementation every 4 years
