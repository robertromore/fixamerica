# Urban Indians: History

## Pre-Colonial and Early Contact Period

### Indigenous Urban Traditions

The narrative that Native peoples are inherently rural or nomadic is historically inaccurate. Prior to European contact, Indigenous peoples built and inhabited substantial urban centers:

- **Cahokia** (near present-day St. Louis): At its peak around 1100 CE, Cahokia housed an estimated 20,000 people, making it larger than London at the time
- **Pueblo communities**: Multi-story apartment complexes in the Southwest supported concentrated populations for centuries
- **Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) towns**: Large palisaded towns with longhouses housing hundreds of people
- **Southeastern Mississippian centers**: Moundville, Etowah, and Spiro supported complex urban-scale societies

European colonization destroyed these urban traditions, pushing Native peoples onto increasingly marginal lands and disrupting the economic systems that supported concentrated populations.

## Nineteenth Century: Removal and Concentration

### Forced Relocation to Reservations (1830s-1880s)

The Indian Removal Act of 1830 and subsequent policies forcibly relocated tribal nations from their homelands to designated territories. The reservation system that emerged concentrated Native populations in rural, often resource-poor areas:

- **Trail of Tears** (1830s): Cherokee, Muscogee, Seminole, Chickasaw, and Choctaw nations forced to Indian Territory
- **Great Plains removals** (1860s-1880s): Lakota, Cheyenne, Arapaho, and other nations confined to reservations
- **Western relocations**: Navajo Long Walk (1864), Apache campaigns (1870s-1880s)

### Early Urban Migration

Even in the nineteenth century, some Native individuals migrated to cities:

- Native workers participated in industrial labor in eastern cities
- Boarding school graduates sometimes settled in urban areas near their schools
- Some tribes maintained traditional presence near emerging cities (e.g., Ojibwe communities near Minneapolis)

## Early Twentieth Century: World Wars and Economic Shifts

### World War I and Interwar Period

World War I brought approximately 12,000 Native Americans into military service, many experiencing urban life for the first time. After the war:

- Some veterans settled in cities rather than returning to reservations
- The Meriam Report (1928) documented dire conditions on reservations, implicitly creating incentives for out-migration
- The Indian Citizenship Act of 1924 granted citizenship to all Native Americans, theoretically enabling fuller participation in urban civic life

### Indian New Deal (1934)

The Indian Reorganization Act (IRA) of 1934 ended the allotment era and encouraged tribal self-governance, but its focus on reservation-based tribal governments established the template that would marginalize urban Indians in future policy:

- Tribal constitutions and governments were organized around reservation geography
- Federal services were delivered through reservation-based agencies
- Urban Indians were not part of the policy framework

### World War II

World War II marked a turning point in Native urbanization:

- Approximately 25,000 Native Americans served in the military
- An additional 40,000 Native workers left reservations for wartime industrial jobs in cities like Los Angeles, San Francisco, Seattle, and Chicago
- Many veterans and wartime workers chose to remain in cities after the war, establishing the first substantial urban Indian communities

## The Relocation Era (1950s-1970s)

### Indian Relocation Act of 1956

The single most important driver of Native urbanization was the federal Indian Relocation Program, formally authorized by Public Law 959 in 1956 but operating informally since 1952. The program was part of the broader termination-era agenda to dissolve the federal-tribal relationship.

**Program Mechanics**:

- The Bureau of Indian Affairs recruited reservation residents to relocate to designated cities
- Participants received one-way transportation, temporary housing assistance (typically 30 days), job placement assistance, and a small living stipend
- Designated relocation cities included: Los Angeles, San Francisco, San Jose, Oakland, Dallas, Denver, Chicago, Cleveland, Cincinnati, and St. Louis

**Scale**:

- Between 1952 and 1972, the BIA relocated over 100,000 Native Americans to cities
- By some estimates, including family members who accompanied program participants, the total affected population exceeded 160,000

### Promises vs. Reality

The relocation program promised a path to economic self-sufficiency but frequently delivered:

- **Inadequate job training**: Participants were often placed in low-wage, unskilled positions regardless of their abilities
- **Housing in poor neighborhoods**: Relocatees were typically housed in inner-city neighborhoods with high poverty, crime, and substandard housing
- **Cultural isolation**: Individuals from different tribes were scattered across cities with no community infrastructure
- **Abandoned support**: The 30-day support period was grossly insufficient; many relocatees exhausted their assistance before finding stable employment
- **No return assistance**: Those who wanted to return to reservations received no help doing so

### Connection to Termination Policy

The relocation program cannot be understood apart from the broader termination agenda:

- **House Concurrent Resolution 108** (1953) declared the intent to end the federal-tribal relationship "as rapidly as possible"
- **Public Law 280** (1953) transferred criminal jurisdiction over tribal lands to states in several states without tribal consent
- Between 1953 and 1968, Congress terminated federal recognition of 109 tribes
- Relocation served termination goals by dispersing tribal populations, weakening reservation communities, and creating a narrative that Native people were voluntarily leaving reservations

### Return Migration and Circular Movement

Despite the program's one-directional intent, many relocatees returned:

- Estimated return rates ranged from 30% to 75% depending on the city and time period (Fixico, 2000)
- Many families established circular migration patterns, moving between city and reservation
- Returnees often brought urban organizational skills and political awareness back to reservations

## Emergence of Urban Indian Communities (1960s-1970s)

### Community Formation

As urban Indian populations grew, distinct communities emerged:

- **Indian bars and gathering places**: Informal social institutions that provided community but also contributed to substance abuse problems
- **Indian churches**: Both mainstream denominations with Native congregations and traditional spiritual practices adapted to urban settings
- **Powwow circuits**: Intertribal gatherings that became central to urban Indian social life
- **Athletic leagues and social clubs**: Community-building activities organized along pan-Indian rather than tribal lines

### Institutional Development

The 1960s and 1970s saw the creation of the organizational infrastructure that still serves urban Indians:

- **1956**: Chicago American Indian Center founded (one of the first urban Indian centers)
- **1960**: National Indian Youth Council formed in Gallup, New Mexico
- **1961**: American Indian Chicago Conference brought together over 500 tribal representatives
- **1968**: American Indian Movement (AIM) founded in Minneapolis by urban Indians concerned about police brutality and discrimination
- **1970s**: Urban Indian Health Programs established through the Indian Health Care Improvement Act (1976)

### The Red Power Movement

Urban Indian communities played a central role in the Red Power movement:

- **Alcatraz Occupation** (1969-1971): Urban Indians from the San Francisco Bay Area occupied Alcatraz Island, galvanizing national attention
- **Trail of Broken Treaties** (1972): A cross-country caravan from urban Indian communities to Washington, D.C., resulting in the occupation of BIA headquarters
- **Wounded Knee Occupation** (1973): While primarily involving AIM (an urban-founded organization) and Pine Ridge Reservation residents, it drew urban Indian support nationally
- Urban Indian organizing influenced the passage of the **Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act** (1975) and the **Indian Health Care Improvement Act** (1976)

## Self-Determination Era (1975-2000)

### Policy Framework

The Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act of 1975 (P.L. 93-638) marked a fundamental shift in federal Indian policy, but its benefits primarily accrued to reservation-based tribal governments:

- Self-governance compacts allowed tribes to assume control of federal programs, but only for their reservation-based operations
- Urban Indians remained largely outside the self-determination framework
- The Indian Health Care Improvement Act (1976) authorized Urban Indian Health Programs but at minimal funding levels

### Growth of Urban Indian Organizations

Despite policy marginalization, urban Indian organizations expanded:

- The number of urban Indian centers grew from fewer than 20 in 1970 to over 60 by 2000
- UIHPs expanded from 36 in 1976 to 41 by the 2000s
- National advocacy organizations -- the National Council of Urban Indian Health (NCUIH) and the National Urban Indian Family Coalition (NUIFC) -- gained policy influence
- Urban Indian education programs, including Title VII (now Title VI) Indian education programs, began serving urban students

### Challenges

The self-determination era also brought challenges for urban Indians:

- **Tribal government focus**: As tribal governments gained capacity and resources, the policy conversation increasingly centered on reservation-based needs
- **Sovereignty discourse**: The emphasis on tribal sovereignty sometimes positioned urban Indians as having voluntarily left the tribal community
- **Funding competition**: Urban Indian organizations competed for limited federal funds against tribal governments with stronger legal standing
- **Identity politics**: Questions about who counts as "Indian" in urban settings became politically charged

## Twenty-First Century (2000-Present)

### Post-2000 Developments

- **2010 Affordable Care Act**: Permanently reauthorized the Indian Health Care Improvement Act and included provisions for urban Indian health
- **2013 Violence Against Women Act reauthorization**: Extended protections relevant to urban Native women
- **2020 Census**: Counted 9.7 million AI/AN people (alone or in combination), confirming the continued growth and urbanization of the population
- **COVID-19 pandemic (2020-2022)**: UIHPs served as critical testing and vaccination sites; pandemic exposed severe capacity limitations
- **2022 Urban Indian Health Parity Act**: Introduced to address funding disparities between UIHPs and other IHS delivery systems

### Continuing Issues

Urban Indian communities continue to grapple with:

- Persistent poverty and economic inequality
- Healthcare access gaps despite UIHP expansion
- Cultural preservation challenges as younger generations grow up in urban settings
- Data invisibility and Census undercount
- Housing instability and disproportionate homelessness
- Intergenerational trauma from relocation, boarding schools, and family separation
