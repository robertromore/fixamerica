# Urban Indians: Solutions

## Overview

Solutions for urban Indian issues must address the structural exclusion of urban Natives from federal programs, strengthen the organizational capacity of urban Indian institutions, improve data visibility, and support cultural preservation in urban settings. The following proposals are organized from most to least comprehensive.

## Solution 1: Urban Indian Health Funding Parity

### Description

Restructure IHS funding to provide equitable per-capita allocations to Urban Indian Health Programs, ending the current disparity in which UIHPs receive approximately 1.5% of the IHS budget while serving the geographic area where 71% of AI/AN people live.

### Key Components

- Establish a statutory formula tying UIHP funding to the urban AI/AN population
- Phase in funding increases over 5-7 years to reach per-capita parity with IHS direct service and tribal programs
- Transition UIHP funding from discretionary to mandatory appropriations
- Expand the number of UIHPs from 41 to at least 70, covering all metropolitan areas with significant AI/AN populations
- Include facility construction and renovation funding
- Authorize UIHPs to enter into self-determination-style contracts and compacts

### Expected Impact

- 3-4 million additional urban AI/AN individuals would gain meaningful access to culturally competent healthcare
- Preventive care expansion would reduce emergency room utilization and downstream costs
- Behavioral health and substance abuse treatment capacity would increase significantly
- Health disparities between urban AI/AN and general populations would narrow

### Implementation Considerations

- Funding increases must be additive to existing IHS appropriations, not redirected from tribal or direct service programs
- New UIHPs should be established through a competitive application process with tribal consultation
- Quality standards and performance metrics should be developed in partnership with NCUIH
- Telehealth infrastructure should be included to extend reach beyond physical facility locations

### Evidence Base

- NCUIH analyses demonstrate that UIHPs deliver cost-effective care with strong patient satisfaction
- The Government Accountability Office has documented urban Indian health disparities attributable to underfunding (GAO-19-74)
- Comparable programs (community health centers, FQHC expansions) have demonstrated that targeted funding increases reduce health disparities

## Solution 2: Person-Based Federal Trust Responsibility Framework

### Description

Codify the principle that the federal trust responsibility attaches to individual Native Americans regardless of their residence, and restructure federal programs accordingly.

### Key Components

- Enact legislation declaring that the trust responsibility follows individual AI/AN people, not geographic boundaries
- Require all federal agencies with Indian programs to serve eligible AI/AN individuals in urban areas
- Create interagency coordination mechanisms for urban Indian service delivery
- Establish an Office of Urban Indian Affairs within the Department of the Interior
- Develop urban Indian service delivery standards across federal agencies
- Authorize urban Indian organizations to receive direct federal funding under trust responsibility authorities

### Expected Impact

- The legal framework for urban Indian services would shift from permissive to mandatory
- Federal agencies would be required to demonstrate how they serve urban AI/AN populations
- Jurisdictional gaps between federal, state, local, and tribal responsibility would be addressed
- Urban Indians would gain formal standing in federal Indian policy

### Implementation Considerations

- Must be carefully drafted to avoid diminishing tribal sovereignty or the government-to-government relationship
- Tribal consultation is essential to ensure tribal governments support the framework
- Enrollment verification processes must respect tribal authority while enabling urban service access
- Phased implementation with pilot programs would reduce risk

### Evidence Base

- Legal scholars have long argued that the trust responsibility is individual, not geographic (Wilkins and Lomawaima, 2001)
- The Indian Health Care Improvement Act already recognizes urban service delivery as part of the trust framework
- International models (Canada's urban Indigenous service framework, New Zealand's Whanau Ora) demonstrate person-based approaches

## Solution 3: Comprehensive Census Reform for AI/AN Enumeration

### Description

Implement reforms to the Census and other federal data collection systems to eliminate the AI/AN undercount and improve data quality for urban Indian populations.

### Key Components

- Fund targeted outreach programs for urban AI/AN populations in advance of the 2030 Census
- Partner with urban Indian organizations to conduct outreach, providing funding for their participation
- Allow tribal enrollment verification as a Census response tool
- Revise racial classification methodology to reduce misclassification of AI/AN individuals
- Conduct supplemental surveys specifically designed for urban AI/AN populations between Census years
- Improve American Community Survey sampling for small AI/AN populations in urban areas
- Create AI/AN population estimates at the metropolitan statistical area level

### Expected Impact

- More accurate population counts would increase formula-based funding to areas with urban AI/AN populations
- Improved data would support better service planning and program design
- Political representation would more accurately reflect AI/AN population distribution
- Research on urban Indian issues would have a stronger empirical foundation

### Implementation Considerations

- Must address trust barriers that discourage AI/AN participation in government surveys
- Tribal data sovereignty concerns must be respected -- tribes control their own enrollment data
- Classification methodology changes should be developed with AI/AN community input
- Cost of enhanced enumeration efforts must be weighed against funding gains from more accurate counts

### Evidence Base

- The 2020 Census Post-Enumeration Survey documented the 5.64% AI/AN undercount
- Research shows that targeted outreach (Complete Count Committees, community partnerships) reduces undercount rates for hard-to-count populations
- Improved AI/AN data from the 2000 and 2010 Censuses correlate with increased program funding

## Solution 4: Urban Indian Cultural Infrastructure Investment

### Description

Create a dedicated federal funding stream for cultural preservation programming in urban Indian communities, including language revitalization, traditional arts, ceremonial support, and intergenerational cultural transmission.

### Key Components

- Establish an Urban Indian Cultural Preservation Grant Program within the Administration for Native Americans
- Fund urban language nests and immersion programs for endangered Native languages
- Support urban powwow grounds, ceremonial spaces, and cultural centers
- Create urban elder-youth mentorship programs
- Fund digital cultural preservation projects (language apps, oral history archives, virtual museum exhibits)
- Support urban Indian artist residency and exhibition programs
- Establish urban Indian cultural education curricula for public schools

### Expected Impact

- Urban AI/AN youth would have greater access to cultural knowledge and identity resources
- Language revitalization efforts would extend beyond reservations to reach the majority of language learners
- Urban Indian communities would have physical and institutional spaces for cultural practice
- Intergenerational cultural transmission would be supported rather than left to chance

### Implementation Considerations

- Cultural programming must respect tribal-specific traditions; pan-Indian approaches should supplement, not replace, tribal cultural practices
- Tribes must be consulted on the sharing of culturally sensitive materials
- Programs should connect urban cultural activities to reservation-based cultural institutions
- Funding should be flexible enough to accommodate diverse tribal traditions

### Evidence Base

- The Native American Languages Act (1990) and Esther Martinez Native American Languages Programs have demonstrated effective language revitalization models
- Research shows that cultural connection is a protective factor against suicide, substance abuse, and depression among AI/AN youth (Chandler and Lalonde, 2008)
- Successful urban cultural programs (e.g., Heart of the Earth Survival School in Minneapolis, American Indian Community House in New York) provide replicable models

## Solution 5: Urban Indian Housing Initiative

### Description

Expand housing assistance for urban AI/AN populations through a combination of NAHASDA reform, targeted urban housing programs, and homelessness prevention services.

### Key Components

- Amend NAHASDA to allow tribally designated housing entities to serve enrolled members living in urban areas
- Create a dedicated Urban Indian Housing Block Grant to fund urban Indian housing organizations
- Expand Section 184 Indian Home Loan Guarantee Program outreach and utilization
- Fund urban Indian homelessness prevention programs, including rapid rehousing and rental assistance
- Support urban Indian transitional housing programs for individuals relocating from reservations
- Develop homeownership counseling and financial literacy programs for urban AI/AN populations

### Expected Impact

- Reduced homelessness among urban AI/AN populations
- Increased homeownership rates
- Stable housing as a platform for improved health, education, and employment outcomes
- Reduced housing cost burden for urban AI/AN families

### Implementation Considerations

- Housing assistance must be coordinated with local housing authorities and Continuum of Care organizations
- Tribal housing entities need capacity building to serve off-reservation members
- Urban Indian housing organizations need organizational development support
- Fair housing enforcement must address discrimination against AI/AN renters and homebuyers

## Solution 6: Urban Indian Education Reform

### Description

Improve educational access and outcomes for urban AI/AN students through targeted funding, cultural curricula, and institutional support.

### Key Components

- Increase Title VI (Indian Education) funding and lower the student count threshold for urban district eligibility
- Fund Native language instruction in urban school districts with significant AI/AN enrollment
- Develop and distribute culturally relevant curricula addressing Native history, governance, and contemporary issues
- Establish urban Indian education liaison positions in major school districts
- Create urban Indian scholarship and college readiness programs
- Expand Johnson-O'Malley Act eligibility to urban school districts
- Support urban Indian charter schools and alternative education programs

### Expected Impact

- Reduced dropout rates among urban AI/AN students
- Improved academic achievement and college enrollment
- Greater cultural competency among teachers serving AI/AN students
- Stronger educational pipeline for AI/AN professionals
