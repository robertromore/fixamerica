# Urban Indians: Root Causes

## Overview

The challenges facing urban Indians are not the result of individual choices or cultural deficiencies. They are the predictable consequences of deliberate federal policies, structural design flaws in the federal-tribal relationship, and systemic failures in how American institutions account for and serve Indigenous populations. This analysis identifies five interconnected root causes.

## Root Cause 1: Federal Relocation Policy Without Support Infrastructure

### Description

The federal government actively relocated over 100,000 Native Americans to cities between 1952 and 1972 but failed to create the institutional infrastructure necessary to support them. This was not an oversight -- it was consistent with the termination-era goal of dissolving tribal communities and assimilating Native people into the general population.

### Mechanism

- The BIA recruited reservation residents with promises of jobs, housing, and a better life
- Participants received minimal transitional support (typically 30 days of housing and a bus ticket)
- No urban Indian service infrastructure was established alongside the relocation program
- Relocatees were deliberately scattered across cities to prevent community formation
- The program was designed as a one-way process with no return assistance

### Consequences

- Tens of thousands of Native people were deposited in unfamiliar cities without community, cultural support, or adequate resources
- High rates of poverty, unemployment, substance abuse, and homelessness resulted
- Families were separated from extended kinship networks that traditionally provided social safety nets
- Intergenerational trauma compounded as children of relocatees grew up disconnected from tribal communities

### Evidence

- BIA internal reports from the 1960s acknowledged return rates of 30-75%, suggesting the program was failing by its own metrics (Fixico, 2000)
- The relocation program's budget was a fraction of the cost of building adequate urban support infrastructure
- Congress never authorized companion legislation to create urban Indian service systems

## Root Cause 2: Reservation-Centric Federal Policy Architecture

### Description

Federal Indian policy is built on a geographic model that assumes Native people live on reservations. This structural assumption excludes the majority of the AI/AN population from the programs designed to fulfill the federal trust responsibility.

### Mechanism

- The Indian Health Service, Bureau of Indian Affairs, and tribal programs are organized around reservation geography
- Funding formulas use reservation population counts and service area boundaries
- Self-determination contracting and compacting (P.L. 93-638) is available to tribal governments, not urban Indian organizations
- Urban Indian Health Programs were added as an afterthought to the IHS delivery system and receive only 1.5% of the budget despite serving the geographic area where 71% of AI/AN people live
- NAHASDA housing funds flow to tribally designated housing entities, not urban Indian organizations

### Consequences

- Urban Indians are systematically underserved by the programs intended to fulfill the trust responsibility
- UIHPs lack the funding, facilities, and capacity to serve more than a fraction of the eligible urban population
- Urban Natives must often choose between accessing their tribal community's services (by relocating back to the reservation) or pursuing economic opportunity in cities
- The policy structure creates a false choice between sovereignty and service access

### Evidence

- IHS per-capita spending for urban AI/AN populations is a fraction of per-capita spending for reservation populations (NCUIH, 2023)
- The Government Accountability Office has repeatedly documented urban Indian service gaps (GAO-19-74, 2019)
- UIHPs serve approximately 600,000 people annually while an estimated 3-4 million urban AI/AN people are eligible

## Root Cause 3: Census Undercount and Data Invisibility

### Description

The AI/AN population is the most undercounted racial group in the U.S. Census, and data collection systems across federal agencies fail to accurately capture urban Indian demographics. This invisibility reduces political power, funding allocations, and service capacity.

### Mechanism

- Census enumeration methods are not well-suited to AI/AN populations, particularly those in complex housing situations, with distrust of government, or who identify with multiple racial categories
- The 2020 Census undercounted the AI/AN on-reservation population by 5.64%; urban undercount is believed to be significant but less precisely measured
- Federal data systems often use inconsistent racial classification, making it difficult to aggregate AI/AN data across agencies
- Small AI/AN populations in individual urban areas fall below reporting thresholds, making them statistically invisible
- The Office of Management and Budget's racial classification standards allow "multiracial" identification that can fragment the AI/AN count

### Consequences

- Formula-based federal programs allocate less funding to areas with undercounted AI/AN populations
- Congressional districts do not reflect true AI/AN population concentrations
- Health, education, and social service agencies cannot plan effectively without accurate data
- Research on urban Indian issues is hampered by inadequate baseline data
- Policy makers can ignore urban Indian needs because the population appears smaller than it is

### Evidence

- The 2020 Census Post-Enumeration Survey documented the 5.64% AI/AN undercount (U.S. Census Bureau, 2022)
- Research suggests that many AI/AN individuals in urban areas are classified as "some other race" or are counted under a different primary racial category (Liebler and Halpern-Manners, 2008)
- The National Congress of American Indians has documented systematic data gaps across federal agencies

## Root Cause 4: Jurisdictional Ambiguity and Responsibility Gaps

### Description

No level of government has clear, unambiguous responsibility for urban Indian populations. The federal government considers urban Indians partially outside its trust responsibility framework; state and local governments view Native services as a federal responsibility; and tribal governments lack resources and jurisdiction to serve members who live off-reservation.

### Mechanism

- The federal trust responsibility is legally recognized as applying to individual Native people, not just to tribes or reservations, but this principle is inconsistently applied in practice
- IHS and BIA define their service populations largely through reservation geography and tribal affiliation
- State and local governments receive federal Indian program funds through different channels and may not direct them to urban Indian populations
- Tribal governments face resource constraints and may prioritize on-reservation members
- Urban Indian organizations lack the governmental authority of tribes and the funding streams available to state and local agencies

### Consequences

- Urban Indians fall through jurisdictional cracks, unable to access federal Indian programs, tribal services, or mainstream urban services adequately
- No single entity is accountable for urban Indian outcomes
- Service fragmentation means that urban Indians must navigate multiple bureaucracies to meet basic needs
- Crisis response (health emergencies, natural disasters, homelessness) often overlooks urban Indian populations

### Evidence

- The U.S. Commission on Civil Rights documented jurisdictional gaps affecting urban Indians in its 2018 report *Broken Promises*
- GAO reports have consistently identified coordination failures between federal, tribal, and urban Indian service providers
- During COVID-19, urban Indians were initially excluded from both tribal and general population vaccine distribution plans in some states

## Root Cause 5: Cultural Disconnection and Identity Erosion

### Description

Urbanization disrupts the cultural institutions, kinship networks, and community structures that sustain tribal identity. Without deliberate investment in urban cultural infrastructure, urban Indians face pressures toward cultural assimilation that compound the effects of boarding school policies, relocation, and family separation.

### Mechanism

- Traditional ceremonies, language use, and cultural practices depend on community density and intergenerational transmission that are difficult to maintain in dispersed urban settings
- Urban school systems rarely offer Native language instruction or culturally relevant curricula
- Mainstream media representation of Native people reinforces stereotypes and erases contemporary urban Native life
- Intermarriage and multicultural environments may weaken tribal-specific cultural practices
- Urban Indian centers provide some cultural programming but lack the funding for comprehensive language immersion, ceremonial support, or cultural education

### Consequences

- Language loss accelerates in urban settings: of the approximately 170 surviving Native languages in the United States, most are spoken primarily by elders on reservations
- Urban-raised Native youth may struggle with identity and belonging, contributing to mental health challenges
- Cultural disconnection can reduce participation in tribal governance and weaken the political voice of urban Indians
- Loss of traditional knowledge -- ecological, medicinal, artistic, spiritual -- that has community-wide value

### Evidence

- UNESCO classifies the vast majority of Native American languages as "endangered" or "critically endangered"
- Studies document higher rates of identity-related distress among urban Native youth compared to reservation-based peers (Whitbeck et al., 2014)
- National Indian Education Association data show that less than 5% of urban school districts offer any Native language instruction

## Interaction of Root Causes

These root causes are deeply interconnected:

```
Relocation Policy (RC1) ──> Created urban population without support
         │
         ├──> Reservation-Centric Policy (RC2) ──> Excluded urban Natives from services
         │              │
         │              └──> Jurisdictional Gaps (RC4) ──> No entity takes responsibility
         │
         ├──> Data Invisibility (RC3) ──> Reduced funding and political power
         │              │
         │              └──> Reinforces exclusion from policy (RC2)
         │
         └──> Cultural Disconnection (RC5) ──> Weakened identity and community
                        │
                        └──> Reduces political organizing capacity ──> Reinforces RC3 and RC4
```

The relocation program created the urban Indian population. Reservation-centric policy ensured they would be underserved. Data invisibility reduced their political power to change these policies. Jurisdictional ambiguity meant no one was accountable. And cultural disconnection weakened the community bonds needed to organize for change.
