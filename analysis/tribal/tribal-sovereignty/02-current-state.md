# Tribal Sovereignty: Current State

## Legal Framework Today

### Constitutional and Treaty Foundations

Tribal sovereignty rests on multiple legal pillars:

| Legal Basis | Description | Status |
|-------------|-------------|--------|
| Inherent sovereignty | Pre-constitutional self-governance authority | Recognized by Supreme Court since *Worcester v. Georgia* (1832) |
| Treaty rights | 374 ratified treaties (1778-1871) | Binding obligations; "supreme law of the land" under Article VI |
| Commerce Clause | Art. I, Sec. 8, Cl. 3: Congress may regulate commerce "with the Indian Tribes" | Active basis for federal Indian law |
| Trust responsibility | Fiduciary duty arising from land cessions and treaties | Judicially recognized but imprecisely defined |
| Indian Self-Determination Act (1975) | P.L. 93-638; tribes contract to run federal programs | 284 self-governance compacts as of 2023 |
| Tribal sovereignty doctrine | *Worcester v. Georgia*, *Williams v. Lee*, *Santa Clara Pueblo v. Martinez* | Foundational but subject to congressional modification |

### The Plenary Power Doctrine

Congress claims virtually unlimited authority over Indian affairs under the plenary power doctrine established in *Lone Wolf v. Hitchcock*, 187 U.S. 553 (1903). This power has been used to:

- Unilaterally abrogate treaties
- Terminate tribal status (Termination Era, 1953-1968)
- Allot reservation lands to individuals (*Dawes Act*, 1887)
- Impose criminal jurisdiction on tribes (*Major Crimes Act*, 1885)
- Transfer jurisdiction to states (Public Law 280, 1953)

While recent Supreme Court decisions have recognized some limits -- notably in *McGirt v. Oklahoma*, 591 U.S. ___ (2020), which affirmed that the Muscogee (Creek) Nation reservation was never disestablished -- the plenary power doctrine remains the greatest legal threat to tribal sovereignty.

## Jurisdictional Landscape

### Criminal Jurisdiction

Criminal jurisdiction in Indian Country is among the most complex areas of American law:

| Category | Jurisdiction | Legal Authority |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Indian-on-Indian crime (minor) | Tribal | Inherent sovereignty |
| Indian-on-Indian crime (major) | Federal + Tribal | Major Crimes Act (18 U.S.C. 1153) |
| Non-Indian-on-Indian crime | Federal | General Crimes Act (18 U.S.C. 1152); VAWA 2013 (limited) |
| Indian-on-non-Indian crime | Federal + Tribal | General Crimes Act; Major Crimes Act |
| Non-Indian-on-non-Indian crime | Federal or State | *McBratney* doctrine |
| Victimless crimes by non-Indians | Generally state | *McBratney*; *Draper v. United States* |
| PL 280 states (6 mandatory + optional) | State (concurrent with tribal) | Public Law 280 (1953) |

### The Non-Indian Jurisdiction Gap

The Supreme Court's decision in *Oliphant v. Suquamish Indian Tribe*, 435 U.S. 191 (1978), stripped tribes of criminal jurisdiction over non-Indians. This created a persistent public safety crisis:

- Non-Indian offenders cannot be prosecuted by tribal courts (with narrow VAWA exceptions)
- Federal prosecutors decline approximately 52% of Indian Country cases (DOJ, 2022)
- U.S. Attorney offices are often hundreds of miles from tribal communities
- Result: crimes against Native people go unprosecuted at alarming rates

### Civil Jurisdiction

| Area | Tribal Authority | Limitations |
|------|-----------------|-------------|
| Tribal member disputes | Full jurisdiction | None for member-to-member |
| Non-member activity on trust land | Limited | *Montana v. United States* (1981) two-part test |
| Taxation | Tribal authority over tribal land | Cannot tax non-Indian fee land within reservation |
| Regulation | Authority over trust land activities | Contested for non-Indian fee land |
| Child welfare | ICWA provides tribal jurisdiction | *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023) upheld ICWA |
| Water rights | *Winters* doctrine reserved rights | Quantification disputes ongoing in many basins |

## Recent Developments (2024-2025)

### Supreme Court

- **Haaland v. Brackeen (2023)**: Upheld the Indian Child Welfare Act, affirming Congress's authority under the Indian Commerce Clause and validating the political classification of tribal membership
- **Oklahoma v. Castro-Huerta (2022)**: Held that states have concurrent jurisdiction to prosecute non-Indians who commit crimes against Indians in Indian Country -- a significant departure from prior understanding
- **Arizona v. Navajo Nation (2023)**: Ruled the federal government has no affirmative duty to assess the Navajo Nation's water needs under the 1868 Treaty, limiting trust responsibility enforcement

### Congressional Action

- **VAWA Reauthorization (2022)**: Expanded tribal criminal jurisdiction to cover non-Indian defendants in cases of child violence, stalking, sex trafficking, and assault of tribal law enforcement
- **Bipartisan Infrastructure Law**: Included $13 billion for tribal infrastructure projects
- **Inflation Reduction Act**: $720 million for tribal climate resilience and adaptation
- **Advance Appropriations**: IHS received advance appropriations authority in FY 2024, providing funding stability

### Administrative Actions

- **Federal recognition**: Ongoing petitions from multiple groups; process remains lengthy (average 15-25 years)
- **Land-into-trust**: Biden administration processed over 78,000 acres into trust (2021-2024)
- **Consultation**: Executive orders and secretarial orders reinforcing consultation requirements, though implementation remains inconsistent
- **NAGPRA enforcement**: Updated regulations strengthening tribal authority over repatriation of remains and cultural items (2024)

### Alaska Native Distinctions

Alaska presents unique sovereignty issues:

- 229 federally recognized tribes but no traditional reservations (except Metlakatla)
- Alaska Native Claims Settlement Act (ANCSA, 1971) created for-profit regional and village corporations instead of reservations
- Subsistence rights governed by complex federal-state framework
- Geographic remoteness intensifies service delivery challenges
- 2017 Secretarial Order enabled Alaska land-into-trust applications

## The Gap Between Recognition and Reality

| Legal Principle | On Paper | In Practice |
|----------------|----------|-------------|
| Government-to-government relationship | Affirmed by every president since Nixon | Consultation often perfunctory; tribal input frequently ignored |
| Treaty obligations as "supreme law" | Article VI, U.S. Constitution | Congress routinely underfunds treaty obligations |
| Trust responsibility | Recognized by courts and agencies | No enforceable standard; *Arizona v. Navajo Nation* narrowed scope |
| Self-determination | Self-governance compacts expand tribal control | Still operates within federal framework; funding remains discretionary |
| Tribal court authority | Over 400 courts operating | *Oliphant* strips jurisdiction over non-Indians; underfunded |
| Criminal jurisdiction | Federal responsibility for major crimes | 52% declination rate leaves crimes unprosecuted |
