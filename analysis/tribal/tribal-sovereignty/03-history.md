# Tribal Sovereignty: History

## Overview

The history of tribal sovereignty in the United States is a history of oscillation between recognition and destruction, self-determination and forced assimilation. Federal Indian policy has swung through radically different eras, each leaving lasting scars on tribal governance capacity. Understanding this history is essential because many of today's challenges -- jurisdictional confusion, underfunding, land fragmentation -- are direct products of past policy choices that were imposed on tribes without their consent.

---

## Pre-Contact and Early Contact (Pre-1776)

### Indigenous Self-Governance

Before European contact, Indigenous nations across North America maintained sophisticated systems of self-governance:

- **Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) Confederacy**: A multi-nation democratic federation predating the U.S. Constitution by centuries, with a Great Law of Peace that influenced American founders
- **Pueblo governance**: Theocratic and democratic systems governing agriculture, water, and community life in the Southwest for over 1,000 years
- **Cherokee Nation**: A constitutional government with written laws, courts, and a bicameral legislature established before forced removal
- **Tribal diversity**: Hundreds of distinct political systems ranging from centralized chiefdoms to decentralized band societies

### European Colonial Period

European powers recognized tribal sovereignty through treaty-making:

- **Doctrine of Discovery**: Gave European nations claims against each other but acknowledged Indigenous occupancy and governmental authority (*Johnson v. M'Intosh*, 1823)
- **Treaty diplomacy**: Britain, France, Spain, and the Netherlands all negotiated treaties with tribal nations as sovereign entities
- **Trade relationships**: Economic alliances built on government-to-government negotiations
- **Military alliances**: Tribal nations served as critical military partners in colonial conflicts

## Treaty Era (1776-1871)

### Constitutional Recognition

The Constitution recognized tribal sovereignty in three key provisions:

- **Commerce Clause** (Art. I, Sec. 8, Cl. 3): Grants Congress power to "regulate Commerce with foreign Nations, and among the several States, and with the Indian Tribes" -- placing tribes alongside foreign nations and states
- **Treaty Clause** (Art. II, Sec. 2): The treaty-making power used for Indian treaties
- **Supremacy Clause** (Art. VI): Makes treaties the "supreme Law of the Land"

### Major Treaty Periods

| Period | Key Treaties | Significance |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| 1778-1800 | Treaty of Fort Pitt (Delaware, 1778), Treaty of Hopewell (Cherokee, 1785) | First U.S.-tribal treaties; mutual recognition of sovereignty |
| 1800-1830 | Numerous land cession treaties | Tribes ceded millions of acres in exchange for guarantees of remaining lands and services |
| 1830-1850 | Removal treaties (Treaty of New Echota, 1835) | Forced relocation of southeastern tribes; Trail of Tears |
| 1850-1871 | Reservation treaties (Fort Laramie, 1851 and 1868) | Established reservation system; major land cessions in exchange for perpetual rights |

### The Marshall Trilogy

Chief Justice John Marshall's three decisions established foundational principles:

1. **Johnson v. M'Intosh (1823)**: Established the Doctrine of Discovery in U.S. law; tribes retained occupancy rights but not full title
2. **Cherokee Nation v. Georgia (1831)**: Tribes are "domestic dependent nations" -- not foreign nations, not states, but a unique political status
3. **Worcester v. Georgia (1832)**: State laws have no force within tribal boundaries; federal-tribal relationship is exclusive. Marshall wrote that tribal nations are "distinct political communities, having territorial boundaries, within which their authority is exclusive"

### End of Treaty-Making

In 1871, Congress unilaterally ended treaty-making with tribes through a rider on the Indian Appropriations Act (25 U.S.C. 71). Existing treaties remained in force, but future agreements would be by statute or executive order rather than treaty.

## Allotment and Assimilation Era (1871-1934)

### The Dawes Act (1887)

The General Allotment Act (Dawes Act) represented the most destructive assault on tribal land and governance:

- **Mechanism**: Divided communal tribal lands into individual allotments (typically 160 acres per family head, 80 per single adult)
- **"Surplus" land**: Remaining land declared "surplus" and opened to non-Indian settlement
- **Land loss**: Tribal land base shrank from 138 million acres (1887) to 48 million acres (1934) -- a loss of 90 million acres (65%)
- **Fractionation**: Through inheritance, allotments became divided among hundreds of heirs, making land use nearly impossible
- **Citizenship**: Allottees granted citizenship; full citizenship extended to all Native Americans in 1924

### Assimilation Policies

- **Boarding schools**: Children forcibly removed from families and sent to off-reservation boarding schools designed to "kill the Indian, save the man" (Captain Richard Henry Pratt, 1879)
- **Religious suppression**: Traditional ceremonies banned; Sun Dance prohibited (1883-1934)
- **Language prohibition**: Native languages forbidden in schools; children punished for speaking them
- **Courts of Indian Offenses**: Federal courts imposed on reservations to enforce assimilation

### Impact

The allotment era devastated tribal governance:

- Tribal governments stripped of authority over allotted lands
- Economic base destroyed through land loss
- Social fabric torn by family separation and cultural suppression
- Population decline from approximately 600,000 (1800) to 237,000 (1900 census) due to disease, warfare, and deprivation

---

## Indian Reorganization Era (1934-1953)

### The Indian Reorganization Act (1934)

The Wheeler-Howard Act (IRA) reversed allotment policy:

- **Ended allotment**: Halted further division of tribal lands
- **Tribal constitutions**: Encouraged tribes to adopt written constitutions and establish formal governmental structures
- **Land restoration**: Authorized purchase of new lands for tribes
- **Economic development**: Created revolving credit fund for tribal enterprises
- **Self-governance**: Permitted tribes to organize and incorporate

### Limitations

The IRA imposed a Western governmental model on tribes:

- Boilerplate constitutions often overrode traditional governance systems
- Secretarial approval required for many tribal decisions, embedding paternalism
- Some tribes (notably the Navajo Nation) rejected the IRA by referendum
- 174 tribes adopted IRA constitutions; 78 rejected them (BIA records)

### Indian Claims Commission (1946)

Congress established the Indian Claims Commission to adjudicate tribal claims for past land takings:

- Heard over 600 claims between 1946 and 1978
- Awarded approximately $818 million in settlements
- Claims resolved through monetary compensation only -- no land returns
- Many settlements valued land at historical prices, not current market value

---

## Termination Era (1953-1968)

### House Concurrent Resolution 108 (1953)

Congress declared its intent to end the federal-tribal relationship "as rapidly as possible":

- **109 tribes terminated**: Federal recognition withdrawn from over 100 tribes and bands
- **Approximately 1.4 million acres removed from trust**: Land lost federal protection
- **Services ended**: Health care, education, and other federal services cut off
- **State jurisdiction imposed**: Public Law 280 (1953) transferred criminal and some civil jurisdiction to six mandatory states (California, Minnesota, Nebraska, Oregon, Wisconsin, Alaska) without tribal consent

### Notable Terminations

| Tribe | Year | Consequences |
|-------|------|-------------|
| Menominee (Wisconsin) | 1961 | Lost 235,000 acres of forest; poverty rate skyrocketed; restored 1973 |
| Klamath (Oregon) | 1961 | Lost 862,000 acres of timber land; community decimated; restored 1986 |
| Mixed-blood Ute (Utah) | 1961 | Lost land and services; ongoing litigation |
| Western Oregon tribes (60+) | 1954-1956 | Lost federal recognition; many later restored |
| Alabama-Coushatta (Texas) | 1954 | Restored 1987 |

### Relocation Program

The Bureau of Indian Affairs operated an urban relocation program (1952-1972):

- Encouraged (and sometimes coerced) reservation residents to move to cities
- Promised job training and housing assistance
- Often delivered neither, leaving relocatees in urban poverty
- Over 100,000 Native Americans relocated to cities including Los Angeles, Chicago, Denver, and Minneapolis

## Self-Determination Era (1968-Present)

### Legislative Milestones

| Year | Legislation | Significance |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| 1968 | Indian Civil Rights Act | Applied most Bill of Rights protections to tribal governments; limited sentencing authority |
| 1970 | Nixon's Special Message on Indian Affairs | Formally repudiated termination; called for self-determination |
| 1975 | Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act (P.L. 93-638) | Tribes could contract to operate federal programs themselves |
| 1978 | Indian Child Welfare Act (ICWA) | Tribal jurisdiction over custody of Indian children |
| 1978 | American Indian Religious Freedom Act (AIRFA) | Protected traditional religious practices |
| 1988 | Indian Gaming Regulatory Act (IGRA) | Authorized tribal gaming; required state compacts for Class III gaming |
| 1990 | Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act (NAGPRA) | Required return of cultural items and human remains |
| 1994 | Self-Governance Act | Made self-governance compacting permanent and expanded it beyond BIA |
| 2000 | Indian Tribal Justice Act (reauthorized) | Supported tribal court development |
| 2010 | Tribal Law and Order Act | Expanded tribal sentencing authority to 3 years per offense |
| 2013 | Violence Against Women Reauthorization Act | Restored limited tribal jurisdiction over non-Indian domestic violence offenders |
| 2022 | VAWA Reauthorization | Further expanded tribal jurisdiction to additional crime categories |

### The Gaming Revolution

The Indian Gaming Regulatory Act of 1988 transformed tribal economies:

- Tribal gaming revenue reached $41.4 billion in FY 2023 (National Indian Gaming Commission)
- Over 500 gaming facilities operated by 245 tribes
- Gaming funded tribal government services, infrastructure, and per capita payments
- However, revenue concentrated among a small number of tribes near urban markets; most tribal gaming operations are modest

### Ongoing Challenges

Despite the self-determination era's advances, structural problems persist:

- Federal funding remains discretionary and inadequate
- *Oliphant* jurisdiction gap still not fully resolved
- *Oklahoma v. Castro-Huerta* (2022) expanded state authority over Indian Country, partly reversing *McGirt*
- Climate change threatens tribal lands, water rights, and subsistence economies
- Urban Native populations (78% of AI/AN population lives off-reservation) often lack access to tribal services and federal Indian programs

## Key Turning Points

| Year | Event | Impact |
|------|-------|--------|
| 1823 | *Johnson v. M'Intosh* | Established Doctrine of Discovery; limited tribal property rights |
| 1831-1832 | Marshall Trilogy completed | Defined tribes as "domestic dependent nations" with inherent sovereignty |
| 1871 | End of treaty-making | Shifted power to Congress; treaties replaced by statutes |
| 1887 | Dawes Act | Destroyed tribal land base; 90 million acres lost |
| 1934 | Indian Reorganization Act | Reversed allotment; began rebuilding tribal governance |
| 1953 | Termination policy adopted | 109 tribes terminated; federal relationship severed |
| 1975 | Indian Self-Determination Act | Tribes gained control over federal program administration |
| 1978 | *Oliphant v. Suquamish* | Stripped tribal criminal jurisdiction over non-Indians |
| 1988 | Indian Gaming Regulatory Act | Created tribal gaming industry; economic transformation |
| 2013 | VAWA tribal jurisdiction | First partial restoration of jurisdiction over non-Indians |
| 2020 | *McGirt v. Oklahoma* | Affirmed treaty reservation boundaries; landmark sovereignty ruling |
| 2022 | *Castro-Huerta* | State concurrent jurisdiction in Indian Country; sovereignty setback |

## Lessons from History

1. **Policy oscillation is the core problem**: Every generation of reform has been followed by retrenchment. Structural protections -- not just policy preferences -- are needed to make gains permanent.

2. **Land is sovereignty**: The destruction of the tribal land base through allotment was the most effective attack on tribal governance. Land restoration must be central to sovereignty strengthening.

3. **Imposed solutions fail**: Whether allotment, termination, or boilerplate IRA constitutions, policies designed without tribal input and consent produce harmful outcomes.

4. **Self-determination works**: When tribes control their own programs and governance, outcomes improve. Self-governance compacts demonstrate this consistently.

5. **Treaty obligations are non-negotiable**: Treaties represent binding obligations exchanged for millions of acres of land. They are not welfare programs -- they are the purchase price of the United States.

6. **Courts alone cannot protect sovereignty**: Judicial protections have been unreliable. Legislative and constitutional protections are needed to secure tribal sovereignty against future erosion.
