# Tribal Sovereignty: Roadmap

## Overview

This roadmap outlines a phased strategy for strengthening tribal sovereignty over a 15-year period. The sequencing prioritizes urgent public safety and funding needs first, builds toward comprehensive structural reform, and culminates in constitutional protection. Each phase builds on the accomplishments of the previous one.

## Phase 1: Foundation (Years 1-3)

### Priority: Public Safety and Funding Stability

The first phase addresses the most urgent needs -- the criminal jurisdiction gap that endangers lives and the funding instability that undermines every tribal program.

### Action Items

| Action | Lead Actor | Supporting Actors | Timeline | Priority |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|----------|----------|
| Pass Tribal Criminal Jurisdiction Act restoring full tribal jurisdiction over non-Indians | Congress (Senate Indian Affairs Committee) | NCAI, NARF, DOJ, tribal leaders | Year 1-2 | Critical |
| Expand VAWA tribal jurisdiction implementation | Tribes, DOJ | BIA, tribal courts | Year 1 (ongoing) | Critical |
| Convert BIA and IHS funding to mandatory appropriations | Congress (Appropriations, Budget Committees) | NCAI, tribal governments, CBO | Year 1-3 | Critical |
| Fund tribal court expansion and modernization | Congress | BIA, NAICJA, tribal court systems | Year 1-2 | High |
| Codify government-to-government consultation requirements | Congress | Executive agencies, NCAI | Year 1-2 | High |
| Increase tribal law enforcement funding by 50% | Congress | BIA Office of Justice Services | Year 1-2 | High |
| Establish Office of Tribal Affairs in the White House | President | Cabinet agencies | Year 1 | Medium |
| Launch federal employee tribal consultation training program | OPM, agencies | Tribal trainers, NCAI | Year 1-2 | Medium |

### Phase 1 Success Criteria

- Full criminal jurisdiction legislation enacted
- At least 50 tribes exercising expanded jurisdiction
- BIA and IHS reclassified as mandatory spending
- Consultation codified in statute
- Tribal court funding increased by 100%

---

## Phase 2: Expansion (Years 3-7)

### Priority: Self-Governance, Land, and Economic Sovereignty

The second phase builds on Phase 1's legal and funding foundations to expand tribal control over programs, consolidate land bases, and strengthen economic sovereignty.

### Action Items

| Action | Lead Actor | Supporting Actors | Timeline | Priority |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|----------|----------|
| Extend self-governance compacting to all federal agencies (DOT, EPA, HUD, Education, etc.) | Congress | Self-Governance Tribal Consortium, federal agencies | Year 3-4 | Critical |
| Pass Carcieri fix legislation | Congress | NCAI, NARF, BIA | Year 3 | Critical |
| Establish $2 billion Tribal Land Acquisition Fund | Congress | BIA, tribal governments | Year 3-5 | High |
| Mandate 180-day land-into-trust processing | BIA / Congress | Interior, tribal applicants | Year 3 | High |
| Legislate tribal civil jurisdiction over all reservation land | Congress | NCAI, tribal courts, NARF | Year 4-5 | High |
| Expand Urban Indian Health Programs to 75+ organizations | Congress, IHS | UIOs, tribal governments | Year 3-5 | High |
| Reform IGRA to reduce state compact leverage | Congress | NIGA, tribal gaming commissions | Year 4-6 | Medium |
| Develop tribal broadband infrastructure initiative | Congress, FCC | Tribal utilities, USDA | Year 3-5 | Medium |
| Establish tribal water rights settlement fund ($5 billion) | Congress | Interior, tribal water commissions | Year 4-7 | High |
| Create Tribal Infrastructure Bank | Congress | Treasury, tribal governments | Year 4-6 | Medium |

### Phase 2 Success Criteria

- Self-governance compacting available through 8+ federal agencies
- 400+ tribes in self-governance compacts
- 5 million acres restored to trust status
- Land-into-trust processing time reduced to 6 months average
- Urban Indian programs doubled in number and funding
- 5+ water rights settlements completed

---

## Phase 3: Structural Protection (Years 7-15)

### Priority: Constitutional Security and Full Sovereignty

The third phase pursues the most ambitious goal -- constitutional protection for tribal sovereignty -- while continuing to build tribal governance capacity and economic self-sufficiency.

### Action Items

| Action | Lead Actor | Supporting Actors | Timeline | Priority |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|----------|----------|
| Launch constitutional amendment campaign | NCAI, tribal coalition | Civil rights organizations, law schools, faith communities | Year 7-10 | Critical |
| Draft and introduce constitutional amendment in Congress | Congressional champions | NCAI, NARF, constitutional scholars | Year 8-10 | Critical |
| State ratification campaign (38 states needed) | National coalition | Tribal governments, state allies, grassroots organizations | Year 10-15 | Critical |
| Formally repudiate Doctrine of Discovery | Congress, President | Religious institutions, academia | Year 7-8 | High |
| Establish treaty review commission | Congress | Tribal governments, historians, legal scholars | Year 7-9 | High |
| Develop tribal appellate court system (inter-tribal) | Tribal governments, NAICJA | NARF, DOJ | Year 7-10 | Medium |
| Achieve tribal economic parity targets | Tribal governments | Federal agencies, private sector | Year 7-15 | High |
| Establish permanent Indigenous representation in U.S. government | Congress | Tribal governments | Year 10-15 | Medium |
| Complete comprehensive land consolidation within major reservations | BIA, tribal governments | Interior | Year 7-15 | Medium |

### Phase 3 Success Criteria

- Constitutional amendment proposed (two-thirds vote in both chambers)
- Amendment ratified by 38 states
- Doctrine of Discovery formally repudiated
- Treaty review commission completed and reported
- Reservation poverty rate reduced to within 5 percentage points of national average
- Inter-tribal appellate court system operational

## Risk Mitigation

### Political Risks

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|--------|------------|
| Administration change reverses executive actions | High | High | Prioritize legislation over executive orders; pursue mandatory funding |
| Supreme Court further erodes sovereignty | Medium | Very High | Seek legislative overrides of adverse decisions; constitutional amendment as ultimate protection |
| Congressional opposition blocks legislation | Medium | High | Build bipartisan coalition; frame sovereignty as self-governance and self-reliance |
| State backlash to expanded tribal jurisdiction | High | Medium | Demonstrate mutual benefits; engage state-tribal compact relationships |

### Implementation Risks

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|--------|------------|
| Tribal capacity varies widely | Certain | Medium | Phased opt-in; technical assistance; intertribal support networks |
| Federal agency resistance to self-governance expansion | High | Medium | Legislative mandates with implementation timelines; political accountability |
| Land-into-trust backlash from local communities | Medium | Medium | Community engagement; economic impact demonstrations; willing-seller requirements |
| Funding levels remain inadequate despite mandatory status | Medium | High | Formula design with adequate baselines; automatic inflation adjustment |

### Legal Risks

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|--------|------------|
| Constitutional challenges to expanded tribal jurisdiction | High | High | Design legislation with robust due process protections; precedent from VAWA implementation |
| *Carcieri*-related litigation continues | Certain | Medium | Legislative fix eliminates issue |
| States challenge mandatory funding reclassification | Low | Medium | Strong constitutional basis in Commerce Clause and treaty obligations |
