# Tribal Sovereignty: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to tribal sovereignty comes from multiple directions: state governments seeking to expand their authority, industries preferring weaker regulation, ideological opponents who view tribal sovereignty as inconsistent with equal citizenship, and fiscal conservatives who resist the costs of fulfilling treaty obligations. Understanding these opposition forces and their arguments is essential for developing effective counter-strategies.

## Opposition Landscape

| Opposition Source | Primary Motivation | Mechanism | Threat Level |
|-------------------|-------------------|-----------|--------------|
| State governments (select) | Expand jurisdiction, tax authority | Litigation, lobbying Congress | High |
| Extractive industries | Reduce tribal regulatory authority | Lobbying, campaign contributions, legal challenges | High |
| Anti-sovereignty groups (CERA, One Nation United) | Ideological opposition to "special rights" | Media campaigns, congressional testimony, litigation support | Medium |
| Congressional fiscal conservatives | Reduce discretionary spending | Appropriations votes, budget resolutions | High |
| Local non-Indian communities | Property rights, taxation concerns | Local political pressure, state legislative lobbying | Medium |
| Some state attorneys general | Assert state criminal/civil authority | Supreme Court litigation (*Castro-Huerta*) | High |

## Ideological Opposition

### Strict Equality Argument

Some opponents frame tribal sovereignty as fundamentally inconsistent with equal citizenship:

- Tribes are seen as having "extra" rights that other Americans do not
- Sovereign immunity is viewed as unfair to non-Indian plaintiffs
- Gaming operations are portrayed as unfair competitive advantages
- Tax-exempt status characterized as "freeloading"

**Reality**: Tribal sovereignty is not a grant of special privileges but a recognition of pre-existing governmental authority. Treaties were negotiated between sovereigns; the rights tribes retain were exchanged for vast land cessions. As the Supreme Court noted in *Morton v. Mancari* (1974), classifications based on tribal membership are political, not racial.

### State Sovereignty Argument

Some states argue that tribal sovereignty creates "jurisdictional voids" and that states should fill gaps:

- *Castro-Huerta* (2022) reflected this argument: Justice Kavanaugh wrote that Indian Country is "part of the State" and subject to state jurisdiction
- States assert they are better positioned to provide services and enforce laws
- State taxation claims argue that non-Indian activity within reservations should generate state revenue

**Reality**: States did not create Indian Country, have no treaty relationship with tribes, and historically used jurisdiction over Indian Country to exploit rather than serve tribal communities. PL 280 states that assumed jurisdiction without funding consistently produced worse outcomes than federal-tribal systems.

## Detailed Opposition Arguments

### Argument 1: "Tribal Sovereignty Creates a Lawless Zone"

**Claim**: Jurisdictional gaps leave crimes unpunished and make Indian Country unsafe.

**Evidence cited**: High crime rates on reservations; *Oliphant* prevents tribal prosecution of non-Indians; federal declination rates are high.

**Reality**: The jurisdictional gaps are real, but they were *created* by federal law -- specifically *Oliphant* (stripping tribal jurisdiction) and the Major Crimes Act (imposing federal jurisdiction). The solution is restoring tribal authority, not expanding state jurisdiction. Tribes with adequate resources and jurisdiction produce comparable or better public safety outcomes than comparable rural areas.

**Rebuttal**: The "lawless zone" argument uses problems created by sovereignty erosion to justify further erosion. States like Oklahoma that gained concurrent jurisdiction through *Castro-Huerta* have not demonstrated improved outcomes for Native victims. Restoring tribal jurisdiction -- as VAWA 2013 and 2022 partially did -- is the evidence-based solution.

### Argument 2: "Tribal Governments Are Unaccountable"

**Claim**: Tribal governments lack democratic accountability, due process, and transparency, making sovereignty expansion dangerous.

**Evidence cited**: Tribal sovereign immunity shields governments from suit; some tribal constitutions lack separation of powers; disenrollment disputes suggest arbitrary governance.

**Reality**: Over 500 tribes operate under constitutions that provide for elected leadership, separation of powers, and civil rights protections. The Indian Civil Rights Act (1968) applies most Bill of Rights protections to tribal governments. Tribal courts number over 400 and provide due process. Like any governmental system, tribal governance is imperfect, but the solution is supporting tribal institutional development, not stripping sovereignty.

**Rebuttal**: This argument applies a double standard -- no one suggests stripping state sovereignty because of state government corruption or dysfunction. The appropriate response to governance challenges is capacity building, not sovereignty removal. Moreover, tribal sovereign immunity is functionally identical to state sovereign immunity under the 11th Amendment.

### Argument 3: "Tribal Gaming Creates Unfair Competition"

**Claim**: Tribal casinos have unfair advantages because they are exempt from state taxation and regulation, harming non-tribal competitors.

**Evidence cited**: Tribal gaming revenue of $41.4 billion (FY 2023, NIGC); some tribes near urban markets generate enormous revenue; gaming funds political influence.

**Reality**: Tribal gaming is regulated by the National Indian Gaming Commission, tribal gaming commissions, and (for Class III gaming) state compacts. Tribes pay for their own regulation and often make substantial revenue-sharing payments to states. Gaming revenue is tribal governmental revenue used to fund services that the federal government has failed to provide adequately. Most tribal gaming operations are small; the top 20 tribal operations account for the majority of total revenue.

**Rebuttal**: Tribal gaming exists because Congress authorized it (IGRA, 1988) as an exercise of tribal sovereignty. State compacts require significant concessions. The "unfair competition" argument ignores that tribes use gaming revenue to fund government services, health care, education, and infrastructure that other governments fund through taxation.

### Argument 4: "Land-Into-Trust Harms Local Communities"

**Claim**: When the federal government takes land into trust for tribes, it removes property from the local tax base and subjects it to different jurisdictional rules, harming non-Indian neighbors.

**Evidence cited**: Municipal and county revenue impacts from trust acquisitions; jurisdictional confusion when parcels shift status; environmental and land-use concerns.

**Reality**: The land-into-trust process includes community impact assessment and public comment periods. Trust land supports tribal economic development that generates employment and spending in local economies. The Carcieri decision (*Carcieri v. Salazar*, 2009) restricted land-into-trust for tribes recognized after 1934, creating inequity. Revenue impacts are typically offset by economic development.

**Rebuttal**: Land restoration is necessary to reverse the effects of allotment, which stripped 90 million acres from tribes. The process includes safeguards. Moreover, tribal economic development on trust land creates jobs and economic activity that benefit surrounding communities.

### Argument 5: "Federal Indian Programs Are Costly and Inefficient"

**Claim**: Federal spending on tribal programs is substantial and produces poor outcomes, suggesting the current system is wasteful.

**Evidence cited**: $24.7 billion in federal tribal-related spending (FY 2024); persistent poverty and health disparities despite decades of spending; BIA administrative costs.

**Reality**: Federal spending on tribal obligations is not charity -- it represents partial fulfillment of treaty obligations. Per-capita spending remains far below comparable federal programs: IHS spends $4,078 per user compared to $13,671 for Medicare (FY 2023). The U.S. Commission on Civil Rights found the federal government underfunds tribal programs by approximately 60% (*Broken Promises*, 2018). Poor outcomes reflect underfunding, not overspending.

**Rebuttal**: The cost argument ignores the legal basis for federal Indian spending (treaty obligations), the massive value of land ceded by tribes, and the documented underfunding relative to need. Where tribes control their own programs through self-governance compacts, efficiency improves.

### Argument 6: "Sovereignty Impedes Integration and Economic Progress"

**Claim**: Maintaining separate tribal governmental systems inhibits economic integration and social progress for Native Americans.

**Evidence cited**: High reservation poverty rates; limited economic opportunities on reservations; rural isolation; brain drain to urban areas.

**Reality**: Economic challenges on reservations result primarily from chronic underfunding, geographic isolation, regulatory barriers, and legacy effects of allotment and termination -- not from sovereignty itself. Tribes that exercise robust sovereignty with adequate resources -- such as the Mississippi Band of Choctaw Indians, the Confederated Tribes of the Umatilla Indian Reservation, and various gaming tribes -- have achieved significant economic development.

**Rebuttal**: This argument confuses cause and effect. Sovereignty does not cause poverty; the erosion of sovereignty causes poverty. Self-determination is correlated with improved economic outcomes (Harvard Project on American Indian Economic Development, ongoing research). Tribes need more sovereignty and resources, not less.

## Counter-Strategies

### Legal Strategies

- **Support tribal court development**: Counter the "unaccountable" narrative by showcasing tribal judicial competence
- **Document jurisdiction outcomes**: Gather data showing that tribal jurisdiction produces better outcomes than federal or state jurisdiction
- **Challenge colonial doctrines**: Advocate for formal repudiation of the Doctrine of Discovery and the "plenary power" doctrine's most extreme formulations
- **Defend ICWA and VAWA provisions**: Protect recent legislative gains through litigation support

### Political Strategies

- **Build bipartisan coalitions**: Frame tribal sovereignty as consistent with conservative values of limited government and self-reliance
- **Engage state-tribal compact relationships**: Demonstrate mutual benefit through gaming compacts, environmental agreements, and service-sharing
- **Expand tribal voter participation**: Increase Native American voter registration and turnout
- **Develop relationships with key committee members**: Focus on Senate Indian Affairs and House Natural Resources committees

### Narrative Strategies

- **Reframe from "special rights" to "governmental authority"**: Tribes are governments, not interest groups
- **Emphasize treaty obligations**: Frame federal Indian spending as fulfilling contractual obligations, not providing welfare
- **Tell success stories**: Highlight tribes that have used sovereignty effectively to improve community outcomes
- **Educate on history**: Public understanding of the history of treaty-making, allotment, and termination is essential for shifting opinion
- **Use economic impact data**: Tribal economic activity generates billions in taxes, employment, and economic output for surrounding communities

### Institutional Strategies

- **Strengthen self-governance compacts**: Demonstrate that tribal management outperforms federal management
- **Support intertribal organizations**: NCAI, NARF, and regional organizations provide collective advocacy capacity
- **Develop tribal media capacity**: Indigenous-owned media can counter misinformation
- **Engage international forums**: UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples provides moral authority
