# Tribal Sovereignty: Actions

## Overview

Strengthening tribal sovereignty requires action at every level -- from individual education and advocacy to organized political campaigns. This document provides specific, actionable steps that citizens, communities, and organizations can take to support the recognition and protection of tribal sovereignty.

## Individual Actions

### Educate Yourself

- **Learn whose land you are on**: Use resources like native-land.ca to identify the Indigenous nations whose traditional territory you occupy
- **Read foundational works**: Start with *The Heartbeat of Wounded Knee* by David Treuer (2019) for contemporary context and *An Indigenous Peoples' History of the United States* by Roxanne Dunbar-Ortiz (2014) for historical foundations
- **Understand treaties**: Research the specific treaties that affect the tribal nations in your region through the National Archives or the Tribal Treaty Database
- **Follow Indigenous media**: Indian Country Today, High Country News (Indigenous Affairs), Native News Online, and Indianz.com provide ongoing coverage

### Direct Support

- **Support tribal businesses**: Purchase from Native-owned businesses; the National Center for American Indian Enterprise Development maintains directories
- **Attend public events**: Powwows, cultural events, and tribal community days are often open to the public (respect protocols and ask before photographing)
- **Donate to tribal organizations**: NARF, NCAI, First Nations Development Institute, and specific tribal programs accept donations
- **Volunteer with Urban Indian Centers**: 41 Urban Indian Organizations operate in cities across the country

### Professional Actions

- **Legal professionals**: Provide pro bono support to tribal legal departments or NARF
- **Educators**: Incorporate accurate Indigenous history into curricula; consult the National Indian Education Association for resources
- **Journalists**: Cover tribal sovereignty issues with accuracy; consult with tribal communications offices
- **Business leaders**: Develop partnerships with tribal enterprises; ensure supply chain inclusion

## Community Actions

### Local Government

- **Support land acknowledgments**: Encourage local governments, schools, and institutions to adopt meaningful land acknowledgments that go beyond symbolic statements to include concrete commitments
- **Advocate for local-tribal cooperation**: Encourage municipal and county governments to develop cooperative agreements with tribal nations on service delivery, land use, and public safety
- **Oppose anti-sovereignty ballot measures**: Monitor state and local ballot measures that would undermine tribal authority and organize opposition
- **Support state-tribal compacts**: Advocate for fair compact agreements on gaming, taxation, and environmental protection

### Educational Institutions

- **Curriculum reform**: Support inclusion of tribal history, treaty rights, and contemporary tribal governance in K-12 and college curricula
- **Tribal partnerships**: Encourage universities to develop formal partnerships with tribal colleges and universities
- **Research ethics**: Support research protocols that respect tribal data sovereignty and require tribal approval for research involving tribal communities
- **Support Native student organizations**: Ensure Native American student groups receive institutional support

### Faith Communities

- **Boarding school accountability**: Support efforts to document and acknowledge the role of religious institutions in the boarding school system
- **Sacred sites advocacy**: Advocate for protection of Indigenous sacred sites
- **Repatriation support**: Support NAGPRA compliance and the return of cultural items and remains
- **Interfaith solidarity**: Build relationships with tribal spiritual leaders while respecting the distinctiveness of Indigenous spiritual traditions

## Political Actions

### Federal Level

- **Contact your members of Congress**: Call or write to support:
    - Restoration of full tribal criminal jurisdiction
    - Mandatory funding for tribal treaty obligations
    - Carcieri fix legislation
    - Self-governance compacting expansion
    - Strengthened consultation requirements

- **Support the Senate Committee on Indian Affairs**: Monitor committee hearings and submit public comments on relevant legislation
- **Engage in presidential campaigns**: Ask candidates about their tribal sovereignty positions during town halls, debates, and campaign events
- **Support tribal candidates**: Native Americans running for office at all levels need campaign support

### State Level

- **Oppose state encroachment on tribal sovereignty**: Monitor state legislation that seeks to expand state jurisdiction into Indian Country
- **Support retrocession in PL 280 states**: Advocate for states to return jurisdiction to tribal nations where tribes request it
- **Advocate for state tribal liaison offices**: Support creation of state-level offices dedicated to government-to-government relations with tribes
- **Support state recognition**: For tribes without federal recognition, support state recognition as an interim step

## Template: Email to Member of Congress

> Subject: Support Tribal Sovereignty Legislation
>
> Dear [Senator/Representative] [Last Name],
>
> As your constituent in [City, State], I am writing to urge your support for legislation strengthening tribal sovereignty, including:
>
> 1. **Full criminal jurisdiction**: Restoring tribal authority to prosecute all offenders within tribal boundaries, building on the successful VAWA implementation
>
> 2. **Mandatory funding**: Converting tribal treaty obligations from discretionary to mandatory appropriations, ensuring consistent funding regardless of budget politics
>
> 3. **Carcieri fix**: Restoring the Secretary of the Interior's authority to take land into trust for all federally recognized tribes
>
> The federal government has binding treaty obligations to tribal nations. These treaties exchanged vast land cessions for promises of protection and services. Honoring these obligations is not discretionary -- it is a matter of national integrity.
>
> I urge you to support these measures and to oppose any legislation that would further erode tribal sovereignty.
>
> Thank you for your attention to this important matter.
>
> Sincerely,
> [Your Name]
> [Your Address]

## Talking Points

### For Conservative Audiences

- Tribal sovereignty is about self-governance and reducing government dependency -- core conservative values
- Treaties are contracts; honoring contracts is fundamental to the rule of law
- Self-governance compacts reduce federal bureaucracy and improve efficiency
- Tribal governments are the original federalism -- local governments closest to the people

### For Liberal Audiences

- Tribal sovereignty is a civil rights and social justice imperative
- Treaty obligations represent America's oldest debt to marginalized communities
- Environmental and climate justice requires partnership with tribal nations
- Closing the opportunity gap requires honoring commitments

### For General Audiences

- The United States signed 374 treaties with tribal nations, exchanging land for specific promises
- Those treaties are the "supreme Law of the Land" under the Constitution (Article VI)
- The federal government has not kept its promises: tribal programs are underfunded by approximately 60%
- Strengthening tribal sovereignty produces better outcomes for everyone -- lower crime, better health, stronger communities

## Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Reality |
|--------------|---------|
| "Native Americans don't pay taxes" | Tribal members pay federal income tax, FICA, and state taxes (except on income earned on tribal land in some states). Tribal governments, like state governments, are tax-exempt as governmental entities. |
| "Tribes receive unlimited federal funding" | Federal tribal programs are underfunded by approximately 60% relative to need. IHS per-capita spending is less than one-third of Medicare. (USCCR, 2018) |
| "Tribal sovereignty is a special privilege" | Sovereignty is inherent governmental authority predating the United States. Treaty rights were exchanged for the cession of millions of acres of land. |
| "Casinos make all Indians rich" | Only about 40% of tribes operate gaming facilities, and the top 20 operations generate most of the revenue. Many tribes have modest or no gaming income. (NIGC, 2023) |
| "Tribes are not real governments" | Tribal nations operate courts, police forces, social services, education systems, and infrastructure -- all functions of government. Over 574 tribes are recognized as sovereign nations. |
| "Reservation problems are caused by sovereignty" | Reservation challenges result from chronic underfunding, historical dispossession, and jurisdictional gaps -- not from self-governance. Tribes with strong governance institutions produce better outcomes. (Harvard Project) |
| "We can't afford to fulfill treaty obligations" | The entire federal tribal spending ($24.7 billion, FY 2024) represents less than 0.4% of the federal budget. The value of land ceded by tribes is incalculable. |
