# Tribal Sovereignty: Root Causes

## Overview

The persistent erosion of tribal sovereignty is not accidental. It results from structural features of American law and politics that systematically disadvantage tribal nations. Understanding these root causes reveals why decades of good-faith reform efforts have produced incomplete results and why more fundamental changes are necessary.

## Structural Root Causes

### 1. The Plenary Power Doctrine

**The problem**: Congress claims virtually unlimited authority over Indian affairs, meaning any tribal right exists only at Congress's sufferance.

**Evidence**: The Supreme Court in *Lone Wolf v. Hitchcock* (1903) held that Congress could unilaterally abrogate treaties with tribes without their consent and without compensation. While subsequent cases have modestly limited this power, the core doctrine remains intact.

**Mechanism**:

- Treaties guarantee rights, but Congress can override treaties by statute
- Tribal sovereignty depends on congressional forbearance, not constitutional protection
- No constitutional amendment specifically protects tribal sovereignty
- Unlike state sovereignty (protected by the 10th Amendment and structural federalism), tribal sovereignty has no structural constitutional defense

**Why it persists**: Plenary power benefits Congress by preserving maximum flexibility. Tribes lack the political power to force a constitutional amendment, and courts have declined to limit the doctrine meaningfully.

### 2. Colonial Legal Foundations

**The problem**: American Indian law is built on colonial-era doctrines that assume Indigenous inferiority.

**Evidence**: The foundational cases of federal Indian law rest on explicitly colonial reasoning:

- *Johnson v. M'Intosh* (1823): Discovery gave European nations title to Indigenous lands based on "the character and habits of the people whose rights have been wrested from them"
- *Cherokee Nation v. Georgia* (1831): Tribes are "domestic dependent nations" in a relationship "resembl[ing] that of a ward to his guardian"
- *United States v. Kagama* (1886): Federal authority over tribes justified by their "weakness and helplessness"

These doctrines have never been formally repudiated. They continue to inform judicial reasoning and public perception.

### 3. Jurisdictional Fragmentation

**The problem**: Criminal and civil jurisdiction in Indian Country is divided among federal, state, and tribal authorities in ways that create gaps, confusion, and injustice.

**Evidence**:

| Jurisdictional gap | Consequence | Source |
|-------------------|-------------|--------|
| *Oliphant* (no tribal jurisdiction over non-Indians) | Non-Indian offenders go unprosecuted | DOJ declination data, 2022 |
| Major Crimes Act (federal jurisdiction for serious crimes) | Distant U.S. Attorneys decline 52% of Indian Country cases | DOJ, 2022 |
| Public Law 280 (state jurisdiction without consent) | States assumed jurisdiction without tribal consent or funding | P.L. 83-280, 1953 |
| *Montana* test (limits on civil authority over non-members) | Tribes cannot regulate non-member conduct on fee land within reservations | *Montana v. United States*, 1981 |

**Why it persists**: Resolving jurisdictional fragmentation requires cooperation among Congress, tribes, states, and federal agencies. Each stakeholder has different interests, and the status quo -- while harmful to tribes -- distributes benefits (or avoids costs) for non-tribal actors.

### 4. Discretionary Funding of Trust Obligations

**The problem**: The federal government's trust obligations to tribes are funded through annual discretionary appropriations rather than mandatory funding, making them vulnerable to budget politics.

**Evidence**:

| Comparison | Funding Type | Annual Uncertainty |
|------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Social Security | Mandatory | None |
| Medicare | Mandatory | None |
| Tribal treaty obligations | Discretionary | Subject to annual appropriations, sequestration, government shutdowns |
| IHS per-capita spending vs. other federal health programs | $4,078 per user (IHS, FY 2023) vs. $13,671 (Medicare) | -- |

The U.S. Commission on Civil Rights estimated in 2018 that the federal government spends 60% less per capita on services for Native Americans than for comparable populations receiving federal services (*Broken Promises* report, 2018).

**Why it persists**: Mandatory funding would remove tribal programs from annual appropriations leverage. Appropriators in both parties resist converting discretionary programs to mandatory because it reduces their power. Tribes lack the voting bloc size to force the change.

### 5. The Checkerboard Problem

**The problem**: The allotment era created a "checkerboard" pattern of trust land, fee land, and state-owned land within reservation boundaries, making tribal governance extraordinarily difficult.

**Evidence**:

- Within many reservations, trust land (under tribal/federal jurisdiction) is interspersed with fee land (often under state jurisdiction)
- A tribal police officer may have jurisdiction on one parcel but not the adjacent one
- Zoning, environmental regulation, and taxation vary parcel by parcel
- The HEARTH Act (2012) partially addressed leasing on trust land, but the underlying fragmentation remains

**Why it persists**: Restoring the checkerboard to consolidated tribal land requires either purchasing fee land from willing sellers or legislating jurisdictional solutions, both of which face political and legal obstacles.

## Political Economy: Who Benefits from the Status Quo

| Beneficiary | How They Benefit | Mechanism |
|-------------|-----------------|-----------|
| State governments | Exercise jurisdiction and taxation authority over Indian Country | PL 280, *Castro-Huerta*, checkerboard jurisdiction |
| Extractive industries | Access tribal resources with limited tribal regulatory authority | Federal leasing authority, jurisdictional gaps, trust land restrictions |
| Congressional appropriators | Retain control over tribal funding levels | Discretionary funding structure |
| Non-Indian residents on reservation fee land | Avoid tribal taxation and regulation | *Montana* limitations on tribal civil authority |
| Federal bureaucracy (BIA) | Retains management authority over tribal affairs | Trust responsibility interpreted as federal control |
| Politically connected interests | Influence federal Indian policy without tribal accountability | Lobbying, campaign contributions, regulatory capture |

## Perverse Incentives

### For Congress

- **Incentive**: Maintain plenary power because it preserves legislative flexibility and appropriations leverage
- **Effect**: Tribes cannot rely on any right that Congress might later revoke
- **Example**: *McGirt v. Oklahoma* (2020) affirmed treaty boundaries, but Congress immediately faced pressure to legislate around the decision

### For Federal Agencies

- **Incentive**: Interpret "trust responsibility" as authority to manage rather than obligation to serve
- **Effect**: BIA acts as gatekeeper rather than facilitator of tribal self-governance
- **Example**: Land-into-trust applications average 2-4 years to process; some take decades (BIA records)

### For State Governments

- **Incentive**: Expand state jurisdiction into Indian Country to increase tax base and regulatory authority
- **Effect**: States lobby Congress and file lawsuits to erode tribal sovereignty
- **Example**: Oklahoma's successful campaign in *Castro-Huerta* to gain concurrent criminal jurisdiction

### For Courts

- **Incentive**: Resolve individual cases rather than restructure the jurisdictional framework
- **Effect**: Piecemeal decisions create additional complexity without systemic reform
- **Example**: The *Montana* test for civil jurisdiction has spawned decades of case-by-case litigation with inconsistent results

## Why Reform Has Failed

### 1. No Constitutional Protection

Unlike states, tribes have no structural constitutional protection. The 10th Amendment reserves powers to states; no equivalent provision exists for tribes. Any legislative gain can be reversed by a future Congress.

### 2. Political Marginalization

American Indians and Alaska Natives constitute approximately 2.9% of the U.S. population (Census, 2020). They are geographically concentrated in states with small Electoral College representation. Tribal interests rarely determine election outcomes, giving Congress little electoral incentive to prioritize them.

### 3. Complexity Breeds Inaction

Federal Indian law is so complex that few members of Congress, judges, or policymakers understand it. The Senate Committee on Indian Affairs and the House Natural Resources Subcommittee on Indian and Insular Affairs handle Indian issues, but neither has the political weight of major committees.

### 4. Internal Diversity

With 574 federally recognized tribes ranging from the Navajo Nation (400,000 enrolled members) to small California rancherias (fewer than 10 members), tribal interests are diverse and sometimes conflicting. This diversity makes unified political action difficult.

### 5. Anti-Sovereignty Backlash

Successful exercises of sovereignty -- particularly gaming wealth and jurisdictional assertions -- generate backlash from non-Indian neighbors, state governments, and political actors who frame tribal sovereignty as "special rights" rather than inherent governmental authority.

### 6. Federal Paternalism

Decades of federal management have created bureaucratic inertia. BIA's role as trustee gives it institutional interests in maintaining its gatekeeping function, even as self-determination policy pushes toward tribal control.

## Root Cause Summary

| Root Cause | Category | Severity | Tractability |
|-----------|----------|----------|-------------|
| Plenary power doctrine | Constitutional/Legal | Critical | Low (requires constitutional amendment or major judicial shift) |
| Colonial legal foundations | Legal/Cultural | Critical | Medium (doctrinal reform possible through legislation and litigation) |
| Jurisdictional fragmentation | Legal/Structural | High | Medium (legislative solutions exist but require political will) |
| Discretionary funding | Fiscal/Political | High | Medium (precedent exists with IHS advance appropriations) |
| Checkerboard land pattern | Historical/Structural | High | Low (requires decades of land consolidation) |
| Political marginalization | Political | High | Medium (coalition-building and voting rights can help) |
| Federal paternalism/BIA inertia | Institutional | Medium | Medium (self-governance compacts demonstrate alternative) |
