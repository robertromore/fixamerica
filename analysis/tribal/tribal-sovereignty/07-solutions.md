# Tribal Sovereignty: Solutions

## Overview

Strengthening tribal sovereignty requires action on multiple fronts: restoring criminal jurisdiction, securing adequate and reliable funding, resolving the checkerboard land problem, building tribal governance capacity, and ultimately providing constitutional protection against future erosion. These solutions draw on evidence from successful self-governance compacts, international models of Indigenous rights, and the lived experience of tribal nations that have leveraged sovereignty for community improvement.

## Solution 1: Restore Full Tribal Criminal Jurisdiction

### Description

Restore tribal criminal jurisdiction over all persons who commit crimes within tribal boundaries, overturning the *Oliphant* doctrine. This would close the most dangerous gap in Indian Country public safety.

### Evidence Base

- VAWA 2013 restored limited tribal jurisdiction over non-Indian domestic violence offenders; participating tribes (currently 30+) have achieved a 36% reduction in non-Indian domestic violence (NCAI, 2023)
- VAWA 2022 expanded jurisdiction to additional crime categories with similar success
- DOJ data shows 52% declination rate for Indian Country cases, demonstrating federal prosecution failure (DOJ, 2022)
- Tribal courts successfully adjudicate cases involving non-Indian defendants under VAWA with full due process protections

### Implementation

1. **Congressional legislation**: Amend the Indian Civil Rights Act to restore full criminal jurisdiction, with due process protections including:
    - Right to counsel for defendants facing imprisonment
    - Jury drawn from community (not exclusively tribal members)
    - Right of appeal to federal court (habeas corpus)
    - Judicial qualifications for tribal court judges hearing these cases
2. **Tribal court capacity building**: Federal funding for tribal court infrastructure, training, and technology
3. **Phased implementation**: Allow tribes to opt in as they develop capacity

### Cost Estimate

- Tribal court expansion: $500 million-$1 billion over 5 years (extrapolated from VAWA implementation costs; TLPI, 2023)
- Defense counsel costs: $100-$200 million annually
- Revenue offset: Reduced federal prosecution costs and improved public safety reduce long-term costs

### Timeline

- Legislation: 1-2 years
- Implementation: 3-5 years (phased tribal opt-in)
- Full impact: 5-10 years

### Challenges

- Supreme Court precedent (*Oliphant*) can be overridden legislatively but faces political opposition
- Due process concerns require careful constitutional drafting
- Tribal court capacity varies widely among 574 tribes
- State opposition, particularly from states that gained concurrent jurisdiction under *Castro-Huerta*

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline | Target (5-year) |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| Federal declination rate for Indian Country cases | 52% (2022) | N/A (tribal jurisdiction renders metric obsolete) |
| Violent crime rate on reservations vs. national average | 2.5x national average | 1.5x national average |
| Tribes exercising full criminal jurisdiction | 30+ (VAWA only) | 150+ |

## Solution 2: Convert Treaty Obligations to Mandatory Funding

### Description

Reclassify federal funding for treaty-based tribal obligations from discretionary to mandatory appropriations, ensuring consistent, adequate funding regardless of annual budget politics.

### Evidence Base

- IHS advance appropriations (achieved FY 2024) demonstrated that mandatory-style funding is feasible and improves service delivery
- Social Security and Medicare provide precedent for mandatory funding of federal obligations
- U.S. Commission on Civil Rights found 60% underfunding of tribal programs (*Broken Promises*, 2018)
- Government shutdowns have disproportionately harmed tribal communities dependent on federal services

### Implementation

1. **Legislation**: Reclassify BIA, IHS, tribal education, and housing programs as mandatory spending
2. **Formula-based allocation**: Develop transparent formulas based on tribal population, land area, service need, and treaty provisions
3. **Inflation adjustment**: Build in automatic annual adjustments indexed to actual cost increases
4. **Tribal consultation**: Engage tribes in formula design and program priority-setting

### Cost Estimate

- Estimated additional annual cost: $8-$12 billion to bring tribal program funding to adequate levels (based on U.S. Commission on Civil Rights estimates, adjusted for inflation)
- This represents fulfillment of existing obligations, not new spending

### Timeline

- Legislation: 2-4 years (requires overcoming appropriations committee resistance)
- Implementation: 1-2 years after enactment
- Full impact: Immediate upon implementation

### Challenges

- Appropriations committees resist losing control over spending
- Budget scoring under CBO rules may show significant cost increases
- Formula design is politically complex given tribal diversity
- May require budget reconciliation process to overcome Senate filibuster

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline | Target (5-year) |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| Per-capita IHS spending vs. Medicare | $4,078 vs. $13,671 (FY 2023) | $8,000+ |
| Government shutdown impact on tribal services | Service disruptions during every shutdown | Zero disruption |
| Tribal program funding as % of estimated need | ~40% (USCCR, 2018) | 80%+ |

## Solution 3: Resolve the Checkerboard Through Land Consolidation

### Description

Accelerate the consolidation of tribal land bases through expanded land-into-trust processing, a Tribal Land Acquisition Fund, and legislation resolving fractionation and jurisdictional complexity.

### Evidence Base

- The Indian Land Consolidation Act and Cobell settlement funded purchase of fractionated interests, reducing over 3 million fractional interests (BIA, 2023)
- Land-into-trust processing averages 2-4 years; faster processing is administratively feasible
- *Carcieri v. Salazar* (2009) restricted land-into-trust for tribes recognized after 1934; legislative fix would restore equity
- HEARTH Act (2012) empowered tribes to approve their own leases on trust land, demonstrating that tribal management improves efficiency

### Implementation

1. **Carcieri fix**: Legislation affirming the Secretary's authority to take land into trust for all federally recognized tribes regardless of recognition date
2. **Expedited processing**: Mandate 180-day processing timeline for uncontested applications
3. **Tribal Land Acquisition Fund**: $2 billion revolving fund for tribes to purchase fee land within reservation boundaries from willing sellers
4. **Jurisdictional clarity**: Legislation establishing tribal civil jurisdiction over all land within reservation boundaries, regardless of trust/fee status

### Cost Estimate

- Land Acquisition Fund: $2 billion (revolving fund, initial capitalization)
- BIA processing capacity expansion: $100 million over 5 years
- Administrative costs: $50 million annually

### Timeline

- Legislation: 1-3 years
- Land acquisition: Ongoing (10-20 year program)
- Jurisdictional clarity: Immediate upon enactment

### Challenges

- Fee land within reservations is often owned by non-Indians who may resist sale
- Non-Indian communities within reservation boundaries raise concerns about change of governance
- Property rights advocates will oppose jurisdictional changes
- Cost of land acquisition is substantial

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline | Target (10-year) |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| Trust land acreage | 56.2 million acres (2023) | 62 million acres |
| Average land-into-trust processing time | 2-4 years | 6 months |
| Fractionated ownership interests | 5 million+ | 2 million |

## Solution 4: Expand Self-Governance Compacting

### Description

Expand self-governance compacting authority to all federal agencies serving tribal communities, allowing tribes to design and administer programs tailored to their specific needs.

### Evidence Base

- Self-governance compacts with BIA and IHS have demonstrated improved outcomes: tribes operating under compacts report 15-20% greater program efficiency (Self-Governance Communication and Education Tribal Consortium, 2022)
- 284 tribes currently participate in self-governance compacts
- The Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act (P.L. 93-638) provides the statutory framework
- Harvard Project on American Indian Economic Development research consistently shows self-determination correlates with improved socioeconomic outcomes

### Implementation

1. **Government-wide self-governance**: Extend compacting authority to all federal agencies (DOT, EPA, HUD, Education, etc.)
2. **Flexible funding**: Allow tribes to reallocate compact funds among programs based on community priorities
3. **Reduced reporting burden**: Streamline accountability requirements while maintaining fiscal integrity
4. **Technical assistance**: Fund intertribal organizations to support tribes developing compact capacity

### Cost Estimate

- Administrative expansion: $200 million over 5 years
- Net savings: Self-governance compacts typically reduce administrative overhead by 10-15%

### Timeline

- Legislation: 1-2 years
- Agency implementation: 2-3 years
- Full tribal participation: 5-10 years

### Challenges

- Federal agencies resist ceding program control
- Not all tribes have capacity for immediate compacting
- Accountability frameworks must balance flexibility with fiscal responsibility
- Smaller tribes may lack administrative infrastructure

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline | Target (5-year) |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| Tribes in self-governance compacts | 284 (2023) | 400+ |
| Agencies offering compacting | 2 (BIA, IHS) | 8+ |
| Administrative overhead reduction | Baseline | 15% reduction |

## Solution 5: Strengthen Consultation Requirements

### Description

Codify and strengthen the requirement for meaningful government-to-government consultation before any federal action affecting tribal interests, with enforceable standards and consequences for non-compliance.

### Evidence Base

- Executive orders requiring consultation (Clinton EO 13175, reaffirmed by subsequent presidents) have been inconsistently implemented
- GAO has documented repeated failures of meaningful consultation (GAO-19-22, 2019)
- Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) is the international standard under the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP, 2007)
- Tribes report that consultation often consists of perfunctory notification rather than genuine engagement

### Implementation

1. **Legislation codifying consultation**: Replace executive orders with statutory requirements
2. **FPIC standard for actions directly affecting treaty rights**: Require tribal consent, not merely input, for actions affecting core treaty rights
3. **Judicial review**: Allow tribes to challenge inadequate consultation in court
4. **Agency-specific protocols**: Each federal agency develops tribal consultation protocols with tribal input
5. **Training requirements**: All federal employees interacting with tribes receive consultation training

### Cost Estimate

- Federal agency implementation: $100-$200 million over 5 years
- Judicial review will generate some litigation costs
- Improved consultation reduces costly project delays and litigation

### Timeline

- Legislation: 1-2 years
- Agency implementation: 2-3 years
- Cultural change: 5-10 years

### Challenges

- Agencies resist constraints on decision-making authority
- Defining "meaningful" consultation is inherently contested
- FPIC standard faces strong opposition from agencies and industries that require rapid permitting
- 574 tribes means 574 potential consultation requirements per action

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline | Target (5-year) |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| Federal actions with documented tribal consultation | Estimated 40-50% | 95%+ |
| Tribal satisfaction with consultation quality (survey) | Low (NCAI surveys) | Moderate-to-high |
| Consultation-related litigation | Frequent | Reduced by 50% |

## Solution 6: Protect Tribal Sovereignty Constitutionally

### Description

Amend the U.S. Constitution to explicitly recognize tribal sovereignty, limit the plenary power doctrine, and provide structural protections comparable to those states enjoy.

### Evidence Base

- The plenary power doctrine allows Congress to unilaterally override tribal sovereignty, making all legislative gains potentially temporary
- States have structural constitutional protections (10th Amendment, equal state suffrage in the Senate, Electoral College representation) that tribes lack
- Self-determination era gains have been partially reversed by judicial decisions (*Castro-Huerta*, *Arizona v. Navajo Nation*) and could be reversed legislatively
- International models (New Zealand, Bolivia, Ecuador) have incorporated Indigenous rights into their constitutions

### Implementation

1. **Constitutional amendment**: Require two-thirds vote of both chambers of Congress and ratification by three-fourths of state legislatures
2. **Amendment text**: Explicitly recognize inherent tribal sovereignty, limit congressional plenary power to regulation consistent with tribal consent, guarantee treaty rights, and require adequate funding
3. **Tribal ratification**: Include a requirement that a majority of federally recognized tribes approve the amendment

### Cost Estimate

- Campaign and ratification costs: $50-$100 million (comparable to other amendment campaigns)
- Implementation costs depend on amendment specifics

### Timeline

- Campaign development: 2-5 years
- Congressional passage: Uncertain (historically difficult)
- State ratification: 3-7 years after passage

### Challenges

- Constitutional amendments are extremely difficult to pass (only 27 in history)
- Requires broad national coalition building
- State legislatures must ratify; many are hostile to tribal sovereignty
- Defining appropriate constitutional language requires extensive negotiation

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline | Target |
|--------|----------|--------|
| Constitutional protection for tribal sovereignty | None | Explicit recognition |
| Congressional ability to unilaterally abrogate treaties | Unlimited | Constitutionally limited |
| Policy stability across administrations | Subject to reversal | Structurally protected |

## Solution 7: Address Urban Native American Needs

### Description

Extend federal Indian program eligibility and services to urban Native American populations, which now constitute 78% of the AI/AN population.

### Evidence Base

- Urban Indian Health Program serves only 71,000 of the estimated 2.6 million urban AI/AN people eligible for IHS services (IHS, 2023)
- Urban Indian organizations (UIOs) number only 41 nationally, compared to need
- Urban AI/AN populations face significant health, economic, and social disparities
- Title V of IHCIA authorizes urban Indian programs but funding is a fraction of IHS overall budget ($82 million of $7.1 billion in FY 2024)

### Implementation

1. **Expand Urban Indian Health Programs**: Increase funding for UIOs and establish new programs in cities with large AI/AN populations
2. **Portable benefits**: Ensure tribal members can access services regardless of residence
3. **Urban Indian Centers**: Fund comprehensive service centers providing health, social, cultural, and legal services
4. **Data collection**: Improve tracking of urban AI/AN populations and needs

### Cost Estimate

- Urban program expansion: $1-2 billion over 5 years
- Revenue offset: Improved health outcomes reduce emergency and Medicaid costs

### Timeline

- Legislation: 1-2 years
- Program expansion: 3-5 years
- Full implementation: 5-7 years

### Challenges

- Jurisdictional questions about which tribe provides services to multi-tribal urban populations
- Tension between reservation-based and urban priorities within tribal politics
- Limited existing infrastructure in many cities
- Data gaps make need assessment difficult

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline | Target (5-year) |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| Urban Indian Health Program users | 71,000 (2023) | 300,000+ |
| Urban Indian organizations | 41 | 75+ |
| Urban AI/AN health disparity gap vs. general population | Significant | Reduced by 25% |

## International Models

### Canada: First Nations Self-Government

- Section 35 of the Constitution Act (1982) recognizes and affirms Aboriginal and treaty rights
- Modern treaties and self-government agreements provide comprehensive governance authority
- First Nations fiscal arrangements include formula-based transfers
- Truth and Reconciliation Commission (2015) produced 94 Calls to Action
- **Applicability**: Constitutional protection and formula-based funding models directly relevant

### New Zealand: Treaty of Waitangi Framework

- Treaty of Waitangi (1840) serves as foundational constitutional document
- Waitangi Tribunal adjudicates historical and contemporary treaty claims
- Co-governance arrangements give Maori formal role in resource management
- Maori seats in Parliament ensure political representation
- **Applicability**: Treaty-based governance and dedicated political representation models relevant

### Australia: Self-Determination Reforms

- Native Title Act (1993) recognized Indigenous land rights after *Mabo* decision
- Indigenous Land Use Agreements provide negotiated frameworks
- Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Voice to Parliament proposed (referendum failed 2023)
- Closing the Gap framework sets measurable targets for reducing disparities
- **Applicability**: Target-setting frameworks and land rights legislation offer lessons

### Bolivia: Plurinational State

- 2009 Constitution declared Bolivia a "plurinational state" recognizing Indigenous nations
- Indigenous Autonomy established within the constitutional framework
- 36 Indigenous languages recognized as official
- Prior consultation requirement for resource extraction
- **Applicability**: Plurinational framework demonstrates how sovereignty can be constitutionally embedded

## Sequencing Strategy

### Phase 1: Immediate (Years 1-2)

- Restore full tribal criminal jurisdiction (Solution 1)
- Strengthen consultation requirements (Solution 5)
- Begin legislative process for mandatory funding (Solution 2)

### Phase 2: Near-Term (Years 2-5)

- Convert treaty obligations to mandatory funding (Solution 2)
- Expand self-governance compacting to all agencies (Solution 4)
- Launch land consolidation fund (Solution 3)
- Expand urban Indian programs (Solution 7)

### Phase 3: Long-Term (Years 5-15)

- Constitutional amendment campaign (Solution 6)
- Continued land consolidation (Solution 3)
- Full implementation and evaluation of all reforms
