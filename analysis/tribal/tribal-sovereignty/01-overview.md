# Tribal Sovereignty: Overview

## Executive Summary

Tribal sovereignty is the inherent authority of Indigenous tribal nations to govern themselves within the borders of the United States. Rooted in centuries of pre-colonial self-governance and recognized through hundreds of treaties, the U.S. Constitution, federal statutes, and Supreme Court precedent, tribal sovereignty represents a unique legal and political relationship unlike any other in American law. The federal government's trust responsibility to tribes -- arising from the exchange of vast land cessions for promises of protection, services, and self-determination -- remains the foundational obligation shaping federal Indian policy.

Despite its legal recognition, tribal sovereignty has been persistently eroded through congressional plenary power, inconsistent federal policies that have oscillated between self-determination and termination, jurisdictional confusion among federal, state, and tribal authorities, and chronic underfunding of treaty obligations. The result is a patchwork system where tribal nations exercise governmental authority over their territories and citizens but face constant legal challenges, resource constraints, and political uncertainty that undermine their capacity for effective self-governance.

The path forward requires strengthening the legal foundations of tribal sovereignty, honoring treaty obligations with adequate and mandatory funding, resolving jurisdictional conflicts that create public safety gaps, and empowering tribal nations to exercise the full range of governmental powers inherent in their sovereign status. These reforms would not only fulfill longstanding obligations but also produce better outcomes for the 9.7 million Americans who identify as American Indian or Alaska Native (U.S. Census Bureau, 2020).

## Scope

### What This Subtopic Covers

- **Legal foundations**: Treaties, constitutional recognition, federal trust responsibility
- **Governmental authority**: Tribal jurisdiction over territory, citizens, and civil/criminal matters
- **Federal-tribal relations**: Government-to-government relationship, consultation requirements
- **Jurisdictional issues**: Criminal and civil jurisdiction, Public Law 280, cross-boundary disputes
- **Self-determination**: Indian Self-Determination Act, self-governance compacts, tribal autonomy
- **Land and territory**: Trust land, fee land, reservation boundaries, land-into-trust processes
- **Economic sovereignty**: Tribal enterprises, taxation, regulatory authority
- **Cultural sovereignty**: Language preservation, sacred sites, religious freedom

## Core Tensions

- **Sovereignty vs. plenary power**: Tribes possess inherent sovereignty, yet Congress claims plenary (complete) authority over Indian affairs
- **Self-determination vs. paternalism**: Federal policy oscillates between empowering tribal self-governance and imposing federal control
- **Jurisdiction vs. public safety**: Gaps in criminal jurisdiction -- particularly over non-Indians on tribal land -- create law enforcement vacuums
- **Trust responsibility vs. funding**: The federal government acknowledges trust obligations but chronically underfunds them through discretionary appropriations
- **Cultural preservation vs. assimilation pressures**: Tribal sovereignty includes the right to maintain distinct cultural practices, but economic and social pressures drive assimilation
- **Economic development vs. regulatory barriers**: Tribes seek economic self-sufficiency but face unique regulatory, taxation, and infrastructure obstacles

## Key Questions

1. **What are the legal foundations of tribal sovereignty?** How do treaties, the Constitution, and federal law define the government-to-government relationship?

2. **Why has Congress's plenary power been so destructive?** How has the doctrine been used to unilaterally alter tribal rights, and what limits exist?

3. **How do jurisdictional gaps endanger public safety?** Why can't tribes prosecute non-Indian offenders, and what has been done to address this?

4. **Is the trust responsibility adequately funded?** How do current appropriations compare to treaty obligations and demonstrated need?

5. **What does meaningful self-determination look like?** How have self-governance compacts succeeded, and where do they fall short?

6. **How can tribal sovereignty be strengthened?** What legislative, administrative, and constitutional changes would secure tribal governance authority?

7. **What role do state governments play?** How do state-tribal relations affect sovereignty, and where do conflicts arise?

## Vision of Success

A system in which tribal sovereignty is fully recognized would feature:

- Tribal nations exercising complete governmental authority over their territories and citizens without constant legal challenge
- Full criminal and civil jurisdiction, including authority over all persons within tribal boundaries
- Mandatory, formula-based funding that fulfills treaty obligations at levels commensurate with need
- Genuine government-to-government consultation before any federal action affecting tribal interests
- Tribal courts recognized as co-equal with state and federal courts
- Economic conditions on reservations approaching national averages
- Cultural and linguistic heritage thriving through tribal-controlled education and cultural programs
