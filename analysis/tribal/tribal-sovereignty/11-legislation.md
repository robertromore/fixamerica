# Tribal Sovereignty: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Strengthening tribal sovereignty requires legislative action across multiple fronts: restoring criminal jurisdiction, securing adequate and mandatory funding for treaty obligations, resolving land issues, expanding self-governance authority, and codifying consultation requirements. This document presents draft federal legislation addressing each of these needs, along with analysis of legal considerations, potential loopholes, and implementation challenges.

The federal trust responsibility to tribal nations -- arising from treaties, statutes, and the historic exchange of vast land cessions for promises of protection and services -- provides the legal foundation for all proposed legislation. These proposals aim to bring federal policy into alignment with existing obligations rather than creating new ones.

## Constitutional Amendments

### Tribal Sovereignty Protection Amendment

**Purpose**: Provide explicit constitutional recognition of tribal sovereignty, limiting the plenary power doctrine and ensuring that tribal governmental authority cannot be unilaterally diminished by Congress without tribal consent.

**Draft Text**:

```text
Section 1. The inherent sovereignty of Indian tribal nations is
hereby recognized and affirmed. The United States acknowledges its
government-to-government relationship with each federally recognized
tribal nation.

Section 2. The right of Indian tribal nations to self-governance,
including the authority to determine their own membership, govern
their territory, administer justice, and manage their resources,
shall not be diminished by Congress, the States, or any subdivision
thereof, except with the free, prior, and informed consent of the
affected tribal nation or nations.

Section 3. Treaty obligations of the United States to Indian tribal
nations, whether arising from ratified treaties, executive orders,
or acts of Congress, shall be honored and adequately funded. Congress
shall appropriate funds sufficient to meet these obligations.

Section 4. Indian tribal nations shall have criminal and civil
jurisdiction over all persons and activities within their territorial
boundaries, subject to such limitations as are agreed upon by the
affected tribal nation and the United States.

Section 5. Congress shall have the power to enforce this article
by appropriate legislation, provided that such legislation shall
not diminish the rights recognized herein without the consent of
the affected tribal nation or nations.

Section 6. This article shall take effect two years after the date
of ratification.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 1 provides explicit constitutional recognition absent from the current text
- Section 2 limits plenary power by requiring tribal consent for sovereignty diminishment
- Section 3 elevates treaty funding from discretionary to constitutional obligation
- Section 4 resolves the *Oliphant* jurisdiction gap at the constitutional level
- Section 5 maintains congressional authority but constrains it with a consent requirement
- Section 6 provides a transition period for implementation

**Potential Challenges**:

- Constitutional amendments require two-thirds vote of both chambers and ratification by 38 states
- State legislatures may resist provisions that limit state authority in Indian Country
- Defining "adequate funding" constitutionally is unprecedented and may generate litigation
- "Free, prior, and informed consent" standard is contested even in international law

**Refinements**:

- Consider a narrower amendment addressing only jurisdiction and plenary power if full version is politically infeasible
- Develop model state ratification resolutions
- Build bipartisan coalition emphasizing self-governance values

## Federal Legislation

### Bill 1: Tribal Criminal Jurisdiction Restoration Act

**Purpose**: Restore full tribal criminal jurisdiction over all persons who commit crimes within tribal territorial boundaries, overriding the *Oliphant v. Suquamish Indian Tribe* (1978) decision.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Tribal Criminal Jurisdiction
Restoration Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) Indian tribal nations possess inherent sovereign authority
    to govern their territory and protect their communities;
(b) the decision of the Supreme Court in Oliphant v. Suquamish
    Indian Tribe (1978) divested tribes of criminal jurisdiction
    over non-Indian defendants, creating a persistent public
    safety crisis in Indian Country;
(c) the Violence Against Women Reauthorization Acts of 2013
    and 2022 partially restored tribal jurisdiction over
    non-Indian defendants in specific categories, demonstrating
    that tribal courts can effectively exercise such jurisdiction
    with appropriate due process protections;
(d) the federal government declines to prosecute approximately
    52 percent of criminal cases referred from Indian Country,
    leaving crimes against Native people unpunished; and
(e) restoring tribal criminal jurisdiction is necessary to
    fulfill the federal trust responsibility and protect the
    safety of all persons in Indian Country.

SEC. 3. RESTORATION OF TRIBAL CRIMINAL JURISDICTION.

(a) IN GENERAL.--Notwithstanding any other provision of law,
    in addition to all powers of self-governance recognized and
    affirmed by this Act, the powers of self-governance of a
    participating tribe include the inherent power of that tribe
    to exercise criminal jurisdiction over all persons.

(b) PARTICIPATING TRIBE.--A tribe may exercise jurisdiction
    under this section if the tribe--
    (1) is a federally recognized Indian tribe;
    (2) has established a criminal justice system that provides
        the protections described in subsection (c); and
    (3) has notified the Attorney General of its election to
        exercise jurisdiction under this section.

(c) RIGHTS OF DEFENDANTS.--In a criminal proceeding in which a
    tribe exercises jurisdiction under this section over a
    defendant who is not a member of that tribe, the tribe
    shall provide to the defendant--
    (1) all rights described in the Indian Civil Rights Act
        of 1968 (25 U.S.C. 1301 et seq.);
    (2) if the defendant is subject to a possible sentence of
        imprisonment, the right to effective assistance of
        counsel at least equal to that guaranteed by the
        United States Constitution;
    (3) the right to a trial by an impartial jury drawn from
        sources that reflect a fair cross section of the
        community and do not systematically exclude any
        distinctive group, including non-Indians;
    (4) all other rights whose protection is necessary under
        the Constitution of the United States in order for
        Congress to recognize and affirm the inherent power of
        the participating tribe to exercise criminal jurisdiction;
    (5) the right to petition a Federal court for a writ of
        habeas corpus; and
    (6) the right to file an appeal in a tribal appellate
        court or, where no tribal appellate court exists, in
        an intertribal appellate court.

(d) CONCURRENT JURISDICTION.--Nothing in this section shall
    be construed to diminish the jurisdiction of the United
    States or any State under applicable law.

SEC. 4. TRIBAL COURT CAPACITY BUILDING.

(a) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.--There is authorized to
    be appropriated $200,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027
    through 2031 to support tribal court capacity building.

(b) USE OF FUNDS.--Funds appropriated under this section may
    be used for--
    (1) tribal court facilities construction and improvement;
    (2) judicial training and certification programs;
    (3) public defender and appointed counsel programs;
    (4) court technology and case management systems;
    (5) detention and alternatives-to-incarceration programs;
    (6) victim services and advocacy programs; and
    (7) intertribal appellate court development.

SEC. 5. REPORTING.

Not later than 2 years after the date of enactment and annually
thereafter, the Attorney General, in consultation with participating
tribes, shall submit to Congress a report on the implementation
of this Act, including data on cases prosecuted, conviction rates,
sentencing, and defendant rights compliance.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 180 days after the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill uses the VAWA model -- which has been upheld in practice and has precedent through *United States v. Bryant* (2016) -- and extends it to all criminal matters. The due process protections in Section 3(c) are modeled on the requirements that have made VAWA special tribal jurisdiction constitutionally sound.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Opposition from states that gained concurrent jurisdiction under *Castro-Huerta*
- Due process challenges in federal court (habeas petitions)
- Tribal capacity varies; some tribes may need years to develop qualifying court systems
- Political opposition framing this as a rights issue for non-Indian defendants

**Refinements**:

- Phase implementation to allow tribal capacity development
- Include technical assistance provisions for smaller tribes
- Consider intertribal court agreements for tribes unable to maintain independent systems

### Bill 2: Tribal Treaty Obligations Funding Act

**Purpose**: Convert federal funding for treaty-based tribal obligations from discretionary to mandatory appropriations, ensuring adequate and predictable funding.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Tribal Treaty Obligations
Funding Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(a) the United States entered into 374 treaties with Indian
    tribal nations, creating binding obligations in exchange
    for the cession of hundreds of millions of acres of land;
(b) Article VI of the Constitution declares that treaties
    are the "supreme Law of the Land";
(c) the United States Commission on Civil Rights found in
    2018 that the federal government underfunds tribal
    programs by approximately 60 percent relative to need;
(d) discretionary funding subjects tribal programs to the
    uncertainty of annual appropriations, government
    shutdowns, and sequestration; and
(e) mandatory funding is appropriate for obligations that
    are binding, ongoing, and legally required.

SEC. 3. MANDATORY APPROPRIATIONS.

(a) IN GENERAL.--The following programs are hereby reclassified
    as mandatory spending:
    (1) Bureau of Indian Affairs programs under 25 U.S.C. 5301
        et seq., including--
        (A) tribal government support;
        (B) trust land management;
        (C) public safety and justice;
        (D) education programs administered by the Bureau of
            Indian Education; and
        (E) social services.
    (2) Indian Health Service programs under the Indian Health
        Care Improvement Act (25 U.S.C. 1601 et seq.).
    (3) Tribal housing programs under the Native American
        Housing Assistance and Self-Determination Act (25
        U.S.C. 4101 et seq.).
    (4) Such other programs as serve to fulfill treaty
        obligations to Indian tribal nations.

(b) FUNDING LEVELS.--
    (1) INITIAL LEVEL.--For the first fiscal year beginning
        after the date of enactment, the mandatory funding
        level for each program described in subsection (a)
        shall be not less than the greater of--
        (A) the fiscal year 2024 enacted level, adjusted for
            inflation; or
        (B) the amount determined by the funding formula
            established under subsection (c).
    (2) ANNUAL ADJUSTMENT.--Funding levels shall be adjusted
        annually based on--
        (A) the Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers;
        (B) changes in the eligible tribal population; and
        (C) documented changes in service delivery costs.

(c) FUNDING FORMULA.--
    (1) The Secretary of the Interior, in consultation with
        tribal nations, shall develop a transparent,
        needs-based formula for allocating funds among
        eligible tribal programs.
    (2) The formula shall consider--
        (A) tribal population;
        (B) geographic service area;
        (C) demonstrated need for services;
        (D) specific treaty provisions; and
        (E) cost of service delivery.
    (3) The formula shall be reviewed and updated every
        5 years in consultation with tribal nations.

SEC. 4. ADVANCE APPROPRIATIONS.

For any program not reclassified under Section 3, advance
appropriations shall be provided as authorized under the
Indian Health Service advance appropriations precedent
established in fiscal year 2024.

SEC. 5. PROTECTION FROM SEQUESTRATION.

Programs funded under this Act shall be exempt from
sequestration under the Balanced Budget and Emergency
Deficit Control Act of 1985 (2 U.S.C. 900 et seq.).

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect beginning with the first fiscal
year that begins after the date that is 1 year after the
date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill treats tribal treaty obligations the same way the federal government treats Social Security and Medicare -- as mandatory obligations that must be funded. The formula-based approach ensures transparency and prevents arbitrary allocation.

**Potential Challenges**:

- CBO scoring will show substantial mandatory spending increase
- Appropriations committees will resist losing control
- May require budget reconciliation to overcome Senate filibuster
- Formula design is complex given tribal diversity

**Refinements**:

- Phase the transition to mandatory over 3-5 years
- Include a budget offset package
- Start with IHS and BIA core programs, then expand

### Bill 3: Tribal Land Consolidation and Restoration Act

**Purpose**: Resolve the checkerboard land pattern, fix the *Carcieri* problem, and accelerate land-into-trust processing.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Tribal Land Consolidation and
Restoration Act".

SEC. 2. CARCIERI FIX.

(a) Section 19 of the Indian Reorganization Act (25 U.S.C.
    5129) is amended by striking "now under Federal
    jurisdiction" and inserting "now or hereafter under
    Federal jurisdiction, including any Indian tribe that
    has been federally recognized at any time".

(b) Any land-into-trust application denied or delayed
    solely on the basis of the decision in Carcieri v.
    Salazar (2009) shall be reconsidered under the
    standard established by subsection (a).

SEC. 3. EXPEDITED LAND-INTO-TRUST PROCESSING.

(a) TIMELINE.--The Secretary of the Interior shall process
    all uncontested land-into-trust applications within
    180 days of receipt of a complete application.

(b) CONTESTED APPLICATIONS.--For applications that receive
    objections, the Secretary shall complete processing
    within 365 days of receipt.

(c) STAFFING.--The Secretary shall ensure that the Bureau
    of Indian Affairs Office of Indian Services has
    sufficient staff to meet the timelines established
    in this section.

SEC. 4. TRIBAL LAND ACQUISITION FUND.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--There is established in the Treasury
    of the United States a fund to be known as the "Tribal
    Land Acquisition Fund".

(b) AUTHORIZATION.--There is authorized to be appropriated
    to the Fund $2,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2027, to
    remain available until expended.

(c) USE OF FUNDS.--Amounts in the Fund shall be available
    to Indian tribal nations for the acquisition of land
    within or adjacent to their reservations from willing
    sellers, to be taken into trust by the Secretary.

(d) PRIORITY.--Priority shall be given to acquisitions
    that--
    (1) consolidate the tribal land base within
        reservation boundaries;
    (2) restore land lost through allotment or
        administrative action;
    (3) support tribal economic development; or
    (4) protect culturally significant sites.

SEC. 5. RESERVATION BOUNDARY CLARIFICATION.

(a) For purposes of Federal law, the boundaries of each
    Indian reservation shall be those established by
    treaty, statute, or executive order, unless Congress
    has explicitly and unambiguously disestablished such
    boundaries.

(b) The standard for disestablishment shall require clear
    and unambiguous congressional intent, consistent with
    the holding in McGirt v. Oklahoma (2020).

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses three related land issues: the *Carcieri* gap that prevents some tribes from acquiring trust land, the slow pace of land-into-trust processing, and the need for a dedicated funding mechanism for tribal land restoration.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Local opposition to trust acquisitions (loss of property tax base)
- Willing-seller requirement may limit available land in some areas
- $2 billion authorization may face appropriations resistance
- *Carcieri* fix has failed in previous Congresses despite bipartisan support

**Refinements**:

- Include payments-in-lieu-of-taxes provisions for affected local governments
- Phase the fund over multiple fiscal years
- Pair with economic development provisions demonstrating local community benefit

## Regulatory Framework

### Bureau of Indian Affairs: Consultation Regulations

**Existing Authority**: Executive Order 13175 (2000); various secretarial orders.

**Draft Regulation**:

The Department of the Interior shall promulgate regulations requiring:

1. **Meaningful consultation**: All federal agencies shall engage in good-faith, government-to-government consultation with tribal nations before taking any action that may affect tribal interests, lands, resources, or rights.

2. **FPIC for treaty-affecting actions**: For any federal action that directly affects rights established by treaty, the affected tribal nation's free, prior, and informed consent shall be sought.

3. **Documentation**: All consultation processes shall be documented, and tribal input shall be incorporated into the administrative record.

4. **Timelines**: Consultation shall begin no later than 90 days before any proposed action; tribal nations shall have no fewer than 60 days to respond.

5. **Accountability**: Each federal agency shall designate a senior official responsible for consultation compliance and shall report annually to Congress on consultation activities.

**Explanation**: This regulation codifies and strengthens the consultation requirements that have been established by executive orders but inconsistently implemented across agencies.

### Department of Justice: Indian Country Prosecution Standards

**Existing Authority**: 18 U.S.C. 1152, 1153; 28 C.F.R. Part 0.

**Draft Regulation**:

The Attorney General shall establish minimum standards for Indian Country prosecution, including:

1. **Declination reporting**: All declination decisions shall be reported to the affected tribal nation with written explanation within 30 days.

2. **Coordination**: U.S. Attorney offices with Indian Country jurisdiction shall maintain dedicated tribal liaisons and coordinate with tribal law enforcement and prosecutors.

3. **Performance metrics**: U.S. Attorney offices shall be evaluated on Indian Country prosecution outcomes, including declination rates, disposition times, and victim service provision.

4. **Cross-deputization**: The Department of Justice shall facilitate cross-deputization agreements between federal, state, and tribal law enforcement.

## Legal Considerations

### Tribal Sovereignty and Constitutional Authority

- **Commerce Clause**: Congress has clear authority under the Indian Commerce Clause (Art. I, Sec. 8, Cl. 3), as affirmed in *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023)
- **Treaty power**: Treaty obligations provide independent legal authority
- **Trust responsibility**: Judicially recognized duty supporting legislation to protect tribal interests
- **Political classification**: *Morton v. Mancari* (1974) established that Indian classifications are political, not racial, and thus subject to rational basis review rather than strict scrutiny

### Plenary Power Implications

- Congress may restore tribal jurisdiction that courts have stripped (*Oliphant* was based on implicit congressional intent, which Congress can explicitly override)
- Mandatory funding is within congressional spending power
- Land-into-trust is an established exercise of trust responsibility
- The constitutional amendment would formally limit plenary power going forward

### State Authority Conflicts

- *Castro-Huerta* created concurrent state jurisdiction; legislation can define the boundaries of state, federal, and tribal authority
- PL 280 retrocession provisions exist but require state consent; legislation could authorize tribal-initiated retrocession
- Gaming compacts under IGRA remain governed by the federal framework

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- Tribal courts serve as primary enforcement mechanism for jurisdiction restoration
- Mandatory funding is self-executing once enacted
- Land-into-trust processing timelines can be enforced through mandamus actions
- Consultation requirements enforceable through judicial review under the Administrative Procedure Act

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Tribal Criminal Jurisdiction Restoration Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Capacity opt-in delay | Tribes that cannot immediately meet capacity requirements may face prolonged jurisdiction gaps | High |
| Federal declination continues | Concurrent federal jurisdiction means U.S. Attorneys may continue declining cases, creating confusion about which system handles which cases | Medium |
| Habeas corpus challenges | Defendants may flood federal courts with habeas petitions, creating delays and undermining tribal court authority | Medium |
| Cross-section jury requirement | Requiring juries to reflect a "fair cross section" of the community may be difficult in small, homogeneous tribal communities | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address civil jurisdiction gaps (*Montana*) | Tribes still lack civil regulatory authority over non-members on fee land | Bill focuses on criminal jurisdiction only |
| Capacity requirements may exclude smaller tribes | Smaller tribes with limited resources may be unable to establish qualifying court systems | One-size-fits-all standards |
| Does not address PL 280 retrocession | PL 280 states retain concurrent jurisdiction regardless of this bill | PL 280 reform is a separate issue |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Establish intertribal court compact provisions allowing smaller tribes to participate through shared judicial systems
2. Create a dedicated federal-tribal coordination protocol to prevent cases from falling through jurisdictional cracks
3. Develop tiered capacity standards appropriate for different tribal sizes and resources
4. Include PL 280 retrocession provisions in companion legislation

### Tribal Treaty Obligations Funding Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Formula manipulation | Future administrations could manipulate formula inputs (population data, cost estimates) to reduce funding | High |
| Inflation indexing gaps | CPI-U may not accurately reflect costs in remote tribal communities | Medium |
| Reclassification of programs | Future Congresses could reclassify programs back to discretionary | High |
| Administrative costs | Federal agencies could absorb disproportionate shares of funding for administrative overhead | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Initial funding baseline may be too low | Starting from current underfunded levels perpetuates inadequacy | CBO scoring constraints |
| Does not address non-treaty obligations | Programs not directly tied to specific treaties may be excluded | Legislative scope limitation |
| Formula cannot capture all tribal diversity | 574 tribes have vastly different needs and circumstances | Inherent limitation of formula-based allocation |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Include a tribal advisory commission for formula development and ongoing review
2. Use tribal-specific cost indices rather than national CPI-U
3. Include a "ratchet" provision preventing future funding reductions below the highest previously enacted level
4. Require that at least 85% of appropriated funds reach tribal programs directly

### Tribal Land Consolidation and Restoration Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Willing-seller constraint | Land may not be available from willing sellers in critical areas | Medium |
| 180-day timeline evasion | BIA could find applications "incomplete" to reset the clock | High |
| Fund depletion | $2 billion may be inadequate given land values in some regions | Medium |
| Boundary clarification litigation | Despite codifying *McGirt* standard, extensive litigation may follow | High |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address fractionation comprehensively | Fractional interests continue to accumulate through inheritance | Requires separate inheritance reform |
| Local government revenue impacts | Trust acquisitions reduce local tax base | Structural feature of trust land status |
| Does not address Alaska Native land issues | ANCSA created a unique land regime not addressed by this bill | Alaska requires separate legislation |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Include payments-in-lieu-of-taxes for affected local governments
2. Establish "completeness" standards that prevent BIA from using technical deficiencies to evade timelines
3. Authorize additional Fund appropriations based on documented need
4. Include an Alaska-specific title addressing ANCSA land issues

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Federal agency resistance to tribal authority expansion | Mandatory implementation timelines with congressional oversight |
| Inconsistent judicial interpretation of tribal sovereignty | Comprehensive statutory definitions; constitutional amendment as ultimate solution |
| Data gaps on tribal needs and outcomes | Require federal agencies to collect and report data in coordination with tribal data sovereignty protocols |
| Tribal capacity variation | Tiered implementation with technical assistance for smaller tribes |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- The funding formula shall be reviewed and updated every 5 years through tribal consultation (Sec. 3(c)(3) of the Funding Act)
- The Attorney General shall report annually on jurisdiction implementation (Sec. 5 of the Jurisdiction Act)
- The Tribal Land Acquisition Fund shall be reviewed every 10 years for adequacy and reauthorization
- All implementing regulations shall be reviewed every 3 years with tribal consultation
