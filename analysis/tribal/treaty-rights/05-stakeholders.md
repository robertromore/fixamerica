# Treaty Rights: Stakeholders

## Overview

Treaty rights involve a complex web of stakeholders with varying degrees of power, interest, and influence. This analysis identifies key groups, their positions, and their capacity to affect reform outcomes.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Tribal Nations (574 Federally Recognized)

**Interest**: Direct beneficiaries of treaty obligations; exercise treaty-reserved rights

**Position**: Overwhelmingly support strengthening treaty rights enforcement and increasing compliance funding

**Power and Influence**:

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|------------|
| Legal standing | Strong -- recognized as sovereign governments with treaty rights |
| Political influence | Growing but limited by small population and fragmented advocacy |
| Economic resources | Varies enormously -- from gaming-wealthy tribes to impoverished nations |
| Organizational capacity | Strong through NCAI, NTCA, and regional intertribal organizations |
| Public sympathy | Moderate; growing awareness but limited understanding of treaty obligations |

**Key Organizations**:

- National Congress of American Indians (NCAI) -- primary national advocacy organization representing tribal governments (est. 1944; over 500 tribal government members)
- National Tribal Contract Support Cost Coalition
- Affiliated Tribes of Northwest Indians
- United South and Eastern Tribes (USET)
- Great Plains Tribal Chairmen's Association

**Internal Diversity**: Tribes are not monolithic. Differences exist based on:

- Treaty history (some tribes have strong treaty positions; others were never party to treaties)
- Economic capacity (gaming tribes vs. non-gaming tribes)
- Geographic location (reservation-based vs. landless tribes)
- Size (Navajo Nation with 400,000+ enrolled members vs. tribes with fewer than 100)
- Federal recognition status (574 federally recognized vs. 60+ state-recognized only)

### Tribal Citizens

**Interest**: Direct recipients of treaty-promised services and rights holders

**Population**: 9.7 million Americans identify as American Indian or Alaska Native alone or in combination (U.S. Census Bureau, 2020)

**Impact of Treaty Noncompliance**:

| Area | Impact |
|------|--------|
| Health care | IHS serves 2.6 million; per capita spending one-third of national average |
| Education | BIE schools in poor condition; 72% graduation rate vs. 87% nationally |
| Housing | Overcrowding rate on reservations is 8 times the national average |
| Public safety | Homicide rate 4 times national average; 40% law enforcement understaffing |
| Water and sanitation | 30% of reservation homes lack adequate plumbing (IHS, 2023) |

**Vulnerabilities**: Geographic isolation, poverty, limited political representation, historical trauma

### Federal Government

**Role**: Treaty counterparty with trust responsibility

**Relevant Entities**:

| Entity | Role | Position |
|--------|------|----------|
| Bureau of Indian Affairs | Trust land management, governance support | Supports trust responsibility; constrained by budget |
| Indian Health Service | Treaty-based health care delivery | Acknowledges underfunding; requests larger budgets |
| Bureau of Indian Education | Treaty-based education services | Acknowledges facility crisis; seeks funding |
| Department of Justice | Treaty rights litigation and enforcement | Mixed; represents both tribal and federal interests |
| Congress (Indian Affairs Committees) | Oversight, appropriations, legislation | Variable; depends on committee composition and political climate |
| White House | Executive policy, consultation orders | Administration-dependent; recent trend toward stronger consultation |

**Internal Tensions**: Federal agencies simultaneously represent the trust obligation to tribes and the fiscal interests of the federal government. The Department of Justice has argued both for and against treaty rights in different cases, depending on the administration's priorities.

## Secondary Stakeholders

### State Governments

**Interest**: Jurisdiction, taxation, and regulatory authority that may conflict with treaty rights

**Position**: Generally resist expansion of tribal treaty rights, particularly:

- Criminal jurisdiction on tribal lands
- Treaty-reserved hunting and fishing that conflicts with state regulations
- Tax-exempt tribal enterprises competing with state-taxed businesses
- Water rights claims that affect state water allocation schemes

**Key Conflicts**:

| State | Treaty Issue | Status |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| Oklahoma | Reservation boundaries (*McGirt*, *Castro-Huerta*) | Active litigation; state seeking to narrow tribal jurisdiction |
| Washington | Fishing rights (Boldt Decision legacy) | Ongoing management disputes |
| Montana | Flathead water compact | Settled 2021 ($1.9 billion) |
| Wisconsin | Ojibwe off-reservation hunting and gathering | Rights affirmed; ongoing enforcement tensions |
| Minnesota | Mille Lacs Band hunting and fishing | Rights affirmed by Supreme Court (1999) |
| Arizona/Nevada | Colorado River water rights | Active negotiations |

### Extractive Industries

**Interest**: Access to resources on or near treaty lands

**Position**: Generally oppose expansion of treaty rights that could restrict resource access

**Specific Concerns**:

- Mining operations near treaty-protected lands (e.g., copper mining near sacred Apache sites at Oak Flat)
- Oil and gas development on allotted lands and within ceded territories
- Pipeline construction crossing treaty lands (e.g., Dakota Access Pipeline controversy)
- Timber harvesting in treaty-reserved gathering areas

**Influence**: Substantial lobbying resources; estimated $130 million in federal lobbying spending by oil, gas, and mining industries annually (Center for Responsive Politics, 2024)

### Commercial Fishing and Agricultural Industries

**Interest**: Resource access that may conflict with treaty-reserved rights

**Position**: Varies by region and specific treaty

**Key Issues**:

- Pacific Northwest commercial fishing industry has historically opposed treaty fishing allocations
- Agricultural water users in the West compete with tribal *Winters* rights
- Great Lakes commercial fishing interests intersect with treaty-reserved fishing rights

### Conservation and Environmental Organizations

**Interest**: Environmental protection that often aligns with treaty rights enforcement

**Position**: Generally supportive of treaty rights, particularly where treaty enforcement protects natural resources

**Key Organizations**:

- Earthjustice (provides legal representation in treaty rights cases)
- Natural Resources Defense Council
- Sierra Club (has formal partnerships with tribal nations)
- National Wildlife Federation

**Alignment**: Treaty rights enforcement often serves conservation goals -- treaty-reserved fishing rights, for example, require sustainable fish populations. However, tensions can arise when tribal resource use conflicts with environmental group priorities.

### Legal Advocacy Organizations

**Interest**: Treaty rights enforcement and Indian law reform

**Key Organizations**:

| Organization | Role | Focus |
|--------------|------|-------|
| Native American Rights Fund (NARF) | Legal representation for tribes | Treaty rights litigation, voting rights |
| National Indian Law Library | Legal research and resources | Treaty research, case tracking |
| Tribal Supreme Court Project | Supreme Court advocacy | Amicus briefs, case monitoring |
| Indian Law Resource Center | International human rights advocacy | UN mechanisms, OAS proceedings |

### Academic and Research Institutions

**Interest**: Historical research, legal analysis, and policy development

**Key Institutions**:

- Harvard Project on American Indian Economic Development
- University of Arizona Indigenous Governance Program
- Michigan State University Federal Indian Law and Policy program
- University of New Mexico Indian Law Center

## Key Dynamics

### Coalition Opportunities

- **Tribal-environmental alliances**: Treaty rights enforcement aligns with conservation in many cases, creating natural coalition potential
- **Bipartisan support for trust responsibility**: Some conservative lawmakers support treaty compliance as a matter of honoring federal commitments
- **State-tribal compacts**: Where mutual interests align (gaming, taxation, resource management), state-tribal cooperation has proven effective
- **Veteran-tribal alliances**: Native Americans serve in the military at the highest per capita rate of any ethnic group; veteran advocacy groups may support treaty compliance

### Coalition Barriers

- **State-tribal jurisdiction conflicts**: Zero-sum dynamics over criminal jurisdiction, taxation, and resource allocation
- **Industry opposition**: Well-funded lobbying against expanded treaty rights enforcement
- **Federal budget constraints**: Treaty compliance competes with all other discretionary spending
- **Public ignorance**: Most Americans are unaware of treaty obligations, limiting grassroots pressure
