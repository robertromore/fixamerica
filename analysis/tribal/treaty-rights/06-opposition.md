# Treaty Rights: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to treaty rights enforcement comes from identifiable groups with specific interests, arguments, and strategies. Understanding this opposition is essential for designing reforms that can survive political and legal challenge.

## Primary Opposition Sources

### State Governments

**Motivation**: Preservation of state jurisdiction, taxation authority, and regulatory control

**Key Arguments**:

| Argument | Response |
|----------|----------|
| "Treaties are outdated relics" | Treaties are constitutionally co-equal with federal statutes and remain binding law |
| "Equal treatment requires ending special rights" | Treaty rights are not "special" -- they were purchased by tribes through land cessions worth hundreds of billions in modern value |
| "State sovereignty is threatened" | Tribal sovereignty predates state sovereignty; states were created subject to existing treaty obligations |
| "Divided jurisdiction creates confusion" | Jurisdictional clarity can be achieved through cooperation, not by eliminating tribal authority |

**Strategies**:

- Lobbying Congress to limit tribal jurisdiction (e.g., Oklahoma's response to *McGirt*)
- State legislation asserting jurisdiction over Indian Country
- Litigation challenging treaty boundaries and reserved rights
- Ballot initiatives targeting specific treaty rights (e.g., Washington State anti-treaty fishing measures in the 1970s-1980s)
- Amicus briefs in Supreme Court cases seeking to narrow treaty protections

**Key Examples**:

- Oklahoma's aggressive legislative response to *McGirt v. Oklahoma* (2020), seeking to reassert state criminal jurisdiction over tribal lands
- Wisconsin's historical resistance to Ojibwe spearfishing rights affirmed in *Lac du Flambeau Band v. State of Wisconsin* (1991)
- Washington State's prolonged resistance to the Boldt Decision (1974), including state court rulings attempting to limit the decision's scope

### Extractive Industries

**Motivation**: Access to resources on or near treaty lands without tribal regulatory oversight

**Key Arguments**:

| Argument | Response |
|----------|----------|
| "Development benefits everyone, including tribes" | Benefits should be determined by tribes under treaty rights, not imposed by outside interests |
| "Tribal regulations delay projects" | Tribal regulatory authority is a treaty right; delays reflect legitimate review |
| "Energy independence requires access to all resources" | Energy policy must comply with treaty law, just as it must comply with other constitutional requirements |

**Strategies**:

- Lobbying for streamlined permitting that bypasses tribal consultation
- Campaign contributions to lawmakers opposed to treaty rights expansion
- Legal challenges to tribal regulatory authority over non-Indian activities on treaty lands
- Public relations campaigns framing treaty rights as obstacles to economic development

**Spending**: The oil and gas industry alone spent approximately $130 million on federal lobbying in 2023 (Center for Responsive Politics). Mining and natural resource extraction companies spent an additional $30 million.

### Anti-Treaty Rights Organizations

**Motivation**: Opposition to what they characterize as "race-based" privileges

**Key Organizations**:

| Organization | Position | Tactics |
|--------------|----------|---------|
| Citizens Equal Rights Alliance (CERA) | Opposes "special rights" for tribes | Litigation, lobbying, public advocacy |
| Protect Americans' Rights and Resources (PARR) | Opposes treaty fishing rights | Founded in response to Boldt Decision; lobbying, public campaigns |
| Interstate Congress for Equal Rights and Responsibilities (ICERR) | Challenges tribal sovereignty | Legal briefs, congressional testimony |
| Various property rights groups | Oppose tribal land-into-trust acquisitions | Litigation, administrative challenges |

**Key Arguments**:

- Treaty rights are "race-based preferences" that violate equal protection
- Tribal sovereignty creates "governments within governments" that are unaccountable
- Non-Indians on tribal lands lack adequate legal protections
- Treaty rights should be terminated through settlement or legislation

**Response to "Race-Based" Argument**: The Supreme Court has consistently held that the federal-tribal relationship is political, not racial. *Morton v. Mancari*, 417 U.S. 535 (1974), upheld Indian preference in BIA hiring as a political classification based on the unique government-to-government relationship, not race.

### Fiscal Conservatives

**Motivation**: Opposition to increased federal spending on treaty obligations

**Key Arguments**:

| Argument | Response |
|----------|----------|
| "We can't afford to fund treaty obligations" | Treaty obligations are binding commitments, not optional programs; the cost ($50 billion/year) is 0.8% of the federal budget |
| "Tribes should become self-sufficient" | Treaty obligations were the purchase price for land cessions worth trillions; self-sufficiency requires adequate initial investment |
| "Mandatory funding removes congressional oversight" | Mandatory funding with oversight mechanisms (as with Medicare or veterans' benefits) preserves accountability while honoring commitments |

### Commercial Fishing and Agricultural Interests

**Motivation**: Economic competition with treaty-reserved resource access

**Key Arguments**:

- Treaty fishing allocations (50% of harvestable fish under the Boldt Decision) reduce commercial harvest
- Tribal water rights threaten agricultural water allocations in the arid West
- Treaty-reserved gathering rights limit timber harvesting and land development

**Historical Context**: The most intense opposition to treaty fishing rights occurred in the Pacific Northwest in the 1960s-1980s, when non-Indian fishers engaged in violent confrontations with tribal fishers, culminating in the "Fish Wars" that preceded the Boldt Decision. While overt violence has subsided, economic competition remains a source of friction.

## Opposition Strategies

### Legal Strategies

| Strategy | Example | Effectiveness |
|----------|---------|---------------|
| Challenge treaty validity | Claims that treaties were abrogated by statehood or subsequent legislation | Mixed; *Herrera* (2019) rejected statehood abrogation theory |
| Narrow treaty interpretation | Argue treaty terms should be read restrictively | Generally rejected under canons of construction |
| Assert state jurisdiction | *Castro-Huerta* (2022) expanded state criminal jurisdiction | Successful in current Supreme Court |
| Equal protection challenges | Argue treaty rights violate 14th Amendment | Rejected; *Mancari* (1974) established political classification doctrine |
| Administrative challenges | Contest land-into-trust, environmental reviews | Delays but rarely blocks tribal rights |

### Political Strategies

| Strategy | Example | Effectiveness |
|----------|---------|---------------|
| Appropriations riders | Attach treaty-limiting provisions to spending bills | Effective; difficult to remove in must-pass legislation |
| State preemption | State laws asserting jurisdiction over Indian Country | Variable; depends on federal preemption analysis |
| Ballot initiatives | State measures targeting specific treaty rights | Rarely successful in modern era |
| Confirmation battles | Block nominees sympathetic to treaty rights | Effective at slowing policy change |

### Public Relations Strategies

- Frame treaty rights as "special privileges" rather than bargained-for rights
- Emphasize isolated cases of tribal government mismanagement
- Portray treaty enforcement as economically harmful to non-Indian communities
- Exploit public ignorance about treaty history and legal obligations

## Countering Opposition

### Legal Responses

- Invoke canons of construction requiring pro-tribal interpretation
- Document treaty history and tribal understanding at time of signing
- Challenge standing of non-Indian parties to contest treaty rights
- Build record of federal treaty noncompliance for trust responsibility claims
- Engage international human rights mechanisms

### Political Responses

- Build bipartisan coalitions framing treaty compliance as a matter of national honor
- Develop economic analyses showing benefits of treaty compliance (e.g., resource management outcomes)
- Engage veterans' organizations (Native Americans serve in the military at the highest per capita rate)
- Educate public about treaty history through curriculum reform and public awareness campaigns

### Narrative Responses

| Opposition Frame | Counter-Frame |
|------------------|---------------|
| "Special rights" | Purchased rights -- tribes paid with 1.5 billion acres of land |
| "Outdated treaties" | Living law -- treaties are as binding as the Constitution itself |
| "Divided jurisdiction" | Government-to-government cooperation, as with federal-state relations |
| "Fiscal burden" | Honoring commitments -- the same principle that applies to national debt |
| "Race-based policy" | Political relationship between sovereign governments |

## Assessment of Opposition Strength

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|------------|
| Legal foundation | Weak -- treaty rights are well-established law |
| Political influence | Strong -- well-funded, well-organized, concentrated in key states |
| Public support | Moderate -- most Americans are uninformed but sympathetic when educated |
| Institutional support | Mixed -- some court support (*Castro-Huerta*), but trend favors treaty rights |
| Long-term trajectory | Declining -- public awareness growing, legal precedent strengthening |
