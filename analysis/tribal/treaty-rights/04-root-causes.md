# Treaty Rights: Root Causes

## Overview

The persistent violation of treaty obligations is not random or accidental. It results from identifiable structural, legal, political, and economic forces that systematically disadvantage tribal nations. Understanding these root causes is essential to designing reforms that address the problem at its source rather than treating symptoms.

## Structural Causes

### The Plenary Power Doctrine

**The most fundamental root cause** of treaty rights violations is the judicially created doctrine that Congress has virtually unlimited power over Indian affairs.

- *Lone Wolf v. Hitchcock* (1903) held that Congress can unilaterally abrogate treaty provisions, analogizing the power to the political question doctrine in foreign affairs
- Unlike foreign treaties, which require Senate consent to modify, Indian treaties can be abrogated by ordinary legislation or even appropriations riders
- The doctrine lacks meaningful constitutional basis -- the Indian Commerce Clause grants regulatory authority, not sovereignty over tribal nations
- No other constitutional relationship permits one party to unilaterally rewrite bilateral agreements without the other's consent

**Why it persists**: The plenary power doctrine benefits Congress by preserving maximum flexibility. Tribal nations lack the political leverage to challenge it legislatively, and the Supreme Court has been reluctant to overrule over a century of precedent (Cohen, *Handbook of Federal Indian Law*, 2012 ed., ch. 5).

### Discretionary Appropriations for Mandatory Obligations

Treaty obligations are legally binding promises, yet Congress funds them through the discretionary appropriations process:

| Obligation Type | Funding Mechanism | Protection Level |
|-----------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Social Security | Mandatory entitlement | Cannot be cut without new legislation |
| Medicare | Mandatory entitlement | Cannot be cut without new legislation |
| Veterans benefits | Mandatory entitlement | Cannot be cut without new legislation |
| Treaty-based tribal health care | Annual discretionary appropriation | Subject to annual budget fights, sequestration, government shutdowns |
| Treaty-based tribal education | Annual discretionary appropriation | Subject to annual budget fights, sequestration, government shutdowns |

This structural mismatch means that treaty obligations compete annually with every other discretionary program for limited funding, despite being constitutionally binding commitments (U.S. Commission on Civil Rights, "Broken Promises," 2018).

### Fragmented Federal Responsibility

Treaty obligations are scattered across dozens of federal agencies with no central accountability:

| Agency | Treaty-Related Responsibility |
|--------|------------------------------|
| Bureau of Indian Affairs (Interior) | Trust lands, governance, law enforcement |
| Indian Health Service (HHS) | Health care delivery |
| Bureau of Indian Education (Interior) | Schools and education |
| Department of Justice | Criminal prosecution, litigation |
| Environmental Protection Agency | Environmental regulation |
| Department of Housing and Urban Development | Tribal housing (NAHASDA) |
| Department of Agriculture | Food assistance, forestry |
| Department of Transportation | Road infrastructure |

No single entity is responsible for ensuring that the totality of treaty obligations is met. The result is that no agency is accountable for overall compliance (GAO, "Indian Issues: Federal Funding for Non-Federally Recognized Tribes," GAO-12-348, 2012).

## Legal Causes

### Judicial Erosion of Treaty Rights

Courts have created doctrines that limit treaty rights beyond what treaty text or tribal understanding would support:

- **Implicit divestiture** (*Oliphant v. Suquamish Indian Tribe*, 435 U.S. 191 (1978)): Tribes lost criminal jurisdiction over non-Indians through a judicially invented doctrine not found in any treaty or statute
- **Congressional intent test**: Courts require tribes to demonstrate that Congress did not intend to abrogate treaty rights, placing the burden on the disadvantaged party
- **Balancing tests**: Some courts balance treaty rights against state regulatory interests, introducing a weighing process foreign to treaty text
- **Conservation necessity exception**: State conservation regulations may limit treaty hunting and fishing rights, creating a basis for state interference (*Puyallup Tribe v. Department of Game*, 391 U.S. 392 (1968))

### Sovereign Immunity and Enforcement Barriers

Tribes face significant barriers to enforcing treaty rights in court:

- The federal government must consent to be sued for treaty violations, limiting available remedies
- The Tucker Act (28 U.S.C. Sec. 1491) channels many treaty claims to the Court of Federal Claims, where remedies are limited to monetary damages rather than injunctive relief
- Standing requirements may limit individual tribal members' ability to enforce collective treaty rights
- Statutes of limitations and laches defenses may bar long-standing claims
- Litigation costs are prohibitive for many tribes, particularly smaller nations with limited resources

### Absence of International Accountability

Unlike treaties with foreign nations, Indian treaty violations face no international enforcement mechanism:

- The UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP, 2007) supports treaty rights but is non-binding
- No international tribunal has jurisdiction over U.S. domestic treaty compliance
- The Inter-American Commission on Human Rights has heard cases but lacks enforcement authority
- The United States does not recognize the jurisdiction of the International Court of Justice over domestic Indian affairs

## Political Causes

### Structural Underrepresentation

Indigenous peoples are systematically underrepresented in the political process:

| Factor | Detail | Source |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Population share | American Indians/Alaska Natives are 2.9% of the U.S. population | U.S. Census Bureau, 2020 |
| Congressional representation | 6 Native American members of Congress in the 118th Congress (2023-2025) | Congressional Research Service |
| Voting barriers | Distance to polling places, voter ID requirements, and language barriers disproportionately affect Native voters | Native American Rights Fund, 2023 |
| Lobbying resources | Tribal lobbying spending is a fraction of major industry groups | Center for Responsive Politics, 2024 |
| Electoral college impact | Native populations are distributed across many states, reducing concentrated electoral influence | Tribal voting analysis, NARF |

### Invisibility in National Politics

- Treaty rights issues rarely feature in presidential campaigns or congressional debates
- Media coverage of tribal issues is minimal compared to other policy areas
- Public education about treaties and Indigenous history is inadequate -- most Americans have never read a U.S.-Indian treaty
- The general public is largely unaware that treaties are legally binding or that the government has obligations to fulfill them

### Interest Group Dynamics

Powerful interest groups benefit from treaty noncompliance:

| Interest Group | Benefit from Noncompliance |
|----------------|---------------------------|
| Extractive industries | Access to resources on or near treaty lands without tribal regulatory oversight |
| State governments | Avoid sharing jurisdiction, tax revenue, or regulatory authority with tribes |
| Agricultural interests | Access to water and land originally reserved for tribes |
| Development interests | Reduced environmental and cultural review requirements |
| Commercial fishing industry | Competition reduction when treaty fishing rights are not enforced |

## Economic Causes

### Resource Extraction Incentives

The economic incentive to violate treaties has remained constant across eras:

- **Gold Rush (1849-1860s)**: California treaties promising reservation lands were never ratified; an estimated 100,000 Indigenous people were killed or displaced (Madley, *An American Genocide*, 2016)
- **Black Hills (1877)**: Congress unilaterally seized the Black Hills from the Sioux after gold was discovered, violating the 1868 Fort Laramie Treaty
- **Oil and gas (1900s-present)**: Oklahoma allotment-era fraud allowed non-Indian interests to acquire mineral rights on treaty lands
- **Water (ongoing)**: Western water is overallocated, and tribal *Winters* rights are often the first to be curtailed in practice despite legal priority

### Cost of Compliance

Full treaty compliance would be expensive by federal budget standards:

- The U.S. Commission on Civil Rights estimated in 2018 that the funding gap for treaty-obligated programs exceeds $40 billion annually (in 2018 dollars; approximately $50 billion adjusted for inflation to 2024)
- This amount, while substantial, represents approximately 0.8% of the federal budget and approximately 0.2% of GDP
- By comparison, the United States spent $886 billion on defense in FY 2024 (USAspending.gov)
- The cost of compliance is not prohibitive in absolute terms but requires political will that has been absent

### Perverse Incentive Structure

The current system creates incentives to continue noncompliance:

- Breaking treaties is free -- there is no penalty for failing to meet treaty obligations
- Compliance is expensive -- fulfilling obligations requires sustained spending
- The beneficiaries of noncompliance (resource extractors, state governments, federal budget writers) are politically powerful
- The victims of noncompliance (tribal nations) are politically marginalized
- There is no independent body empowered to audit and enforce compliance

## Cultural Causes

### Racial Hierarchy and Historical Dehumanization

The history of treaty violation is inseparable from the history of anti-Indigenous racism:

- Treaties were often negotiated in English with non-English-speaking signatories
- Tribal negotiators were frequently coerced through military threats, starvation, or alcohol
- Courts applied racialized reasoning in early Indian law decisions, characterizing Indigenous peoples as "savages" and "wards"
- The Dawes Act was explicitly designed to destroy tribal cultures and impose Western property norms
- Termination-era policy was framed as "freeing" Indians from federal "dependency"

### Erasure from Public Consciousness

- Most Americans learn little or nothing about treaties in school
- Popular culture portrays treaties as historical curiosities rather than binding law
- The "vanishing Indian" narrative suggests treaty obligations are anachronistic
- Urban Indians (approximately 70% of the Native population) are often invisible to their non-Native neighbors
- The legal complexity of Indian law discourages public engagement

## Root Cause Interaction

These causes do not operate independently. They form a self-reinforcing system:

```text
Political marginalization
       |
       v
Inadequate funding -----> Poor outcomes on reservations
       |                            |
       v                            v
Treaty noncompliance          "Dependency" narrative
       |                            |
       v                            v
No enforcement mechanism     Reduced political will
       |                            |
       +----------------------------+
       |
       v
Continued marginalization
```

The cycle can only be broken by addressing multiple root causes simultaneously, particularly the structural ones: plenary power, discretionary funding, and enforcement mechanisms.
