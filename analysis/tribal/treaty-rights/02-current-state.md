# Treaty Rights: Current State

## Legal Framework Today

### Constitutional Status of Indian Treaties

Indian treaties occupy a unique position in American law:

| Legal Principle | Description | Key Authority |
|-----------------|-------------|---------------|
| Supreme law of the land | Treaties rank equally with federal statutes under Article VI | *Reid v. Covert*, 354 U.S. 1 (1957) |
| Last-in-time rule | When a treaty and statute conflict, the later enactment prevails | *Lone Wolf v. Hitchcock*, 187 U.S. 553 (1903) |
| Canons of construction | Ambiguities in treaties resolved in favor of tribes | *Winters v. United States*, 207 U.S. 564 (1908) |
| Reserved rights doctrine | Treaties reserve all rights not explicitly ceded | *United States v. Winans*, 198 U.S. 371 (1905) |
| Trust responsibility | Treaty promises create fiduciary obligations | *Seminole Nation v. United States*, 316 U.S. 286 (1942) |
| Plenary power | Congress may unilaterally abrogate treaty provisions | *Lone Wolf v. Hitchcock*, 187 U.S. 553 (1903) |

### Treaty Canons of Construction

Courts apply specific interpretive rules to Indian treaties, recognizing the disparity in bargaining power:

- Treaties must be interpreted as the tribal signatories would have understood them at the time of signing
- Ambiguities are resolved in favor of the tribes
- Treaty rights are not granted by the United States but reserved by the tribes from their pre-existing sovereignty
- Treaties should be construed liberally in favor of tribes (*Carpenter v. Shaw*, 280 U.S. 363 (1930))

### Categories of Treaty Rights

| Right Category | Examples | Current Enforcement Status |
|----------------|----------|---------------------------|
| Reserved lands | Reservation boundaries, trust lands | Generally enforced; *McGirt v. Oklahoma* (2020) reaffirmed reservation status |
| Hunting and fishing | Off-reservation hunting, fishing at usual and accustomed grounds | Actively litigated; generally upheld under *Boldt Decision* (1974) and subsequent cases |
| Water rights | *Winters* rights to sufficient water for reservation purposes | Underenforced; major adjudications ongoing in multiple western states |
| Gathering rights | Plant harvesting, wild rice gathering, timber rights | Inconsistently enforced; varies by treaty and jurisdiction |
| Annuity payments | Cash, goods, and services promised in exchange for land cessions | Largely discontinued; replaced by programmatic funding |
| Health care | Medical services promised in numerous treaties | Delivered through IHS at severely inadequate funding levels |
| Education | Schools and educational support | Partially fulfilled through BIE and tribally controlled schools |

## Impact Assessment

### On Tribal Nations

**Health Care Deficiencies**

The IHS, the primary vehicle for fulfilling treaty health care obligations, serves approximately 2.6 million American Indians and Alaska Natives (IHS, 2023). Despite treaty-based entitlements:

- IHS per capita spending ($4,078) is less than one-third of national average ($13,185)
- Life expectancy for American Indians/Alaska Natives is 5.5 years less than the U.S. average (65.2 vs. 77.5 years, CDC, 2021)
- Infant mortality rate is 1.6 times the national average (NIHB, 2023)
- 33% of IHS facilities are in poor or failing condition (IHS Facilities Engineering Program, 2023)

**Education Gaps**

- Bureau of Indian Education schools serve approximately 41,000 students across 183 schools (BIE, 2024)
- 80% of BIE school buildings need repair or replacement (GAO, 2024)
- BIE graduation rate is 72% compared to 87% nationally (NCES, 2023)
- Per-pupil spending at BIE schools averages $16,500 vs. $18,600 nationally, but facility conditions are far worse (BIE Annual Report, 2024)

**Natural Resource Access**

- Treaty-reserved fishing rights generate an estimated $500 million annually for Pacific Northwest tribes (NWIFC, 2023)
- Water rights remain unadjudicated for many tribes, particularly in the West
- Climate change threatens treaty-reserved species and ecosystems, including salmon, wild rice, and culturally significant plants

### On Federal-Tribal Relations

- Chronic underfunding erodes trust between tribal nations and the federal government
- Unilateral abrogation undermines the government-to-government relationship
- Inconsistent enforcement creates uncertainty for tribal economic planning
- Discretionary appropriations allow political manipulation of treaty obligations

## Recent Developments (2024-2025)

### Legislative Developments

- **Advance Appropriations for IHS**: Congress enacted advance appropriations for the Indian Health Service beginning in FY 2024, providing two-year funding stability for the first time (Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2023, P.L. 117-328)
- **Tribal Treaty Rights Protection Act**: Introduced in the 118th Congress to codify treaty canons of construction; did not advance past committee
- **RESPECT Act** (Reaffirming and Safeguarding Promises, Ensuring Compliance with Treaties): Introduced in the 119th Congress to establish mandatory funding for treaty-obligated programs

### Judicial Developments

- *Oklahoma v. Castro-Huerta* (2022) narrowed the implications of *McGirt* by allowing state criminal jurisdiction over non-Indians on tribal lands, a decision widely criticized by tribal nations and legal scholars
- Multiple pending cases in the Ninth and Tenth Circuits address treaty-reserved water rights, including the Klamath Basin and Colorado River allocations
- The Supreme Court's 2024-2025 term includes cases with potential implications for treaty-based regulatory authority

### Executive Actions

- Biden Administration Executive Order 14112 (December 2023) directed federal agencies to strengthen tribal consultation and ensure treaty rights are considered in federal decision-making
- The Department of the Interior initiated a comprehensive review of treaty obligation compliance across federal agencies in 2024

## The Gap Between Promise and Reality

| Treaty Promise | Current Reality | Gap |
|----------------|-----------------|-----|
| Health care for tribal citizens | IHS funded at 31% of need | 69% shortfall |
| Education and schools | BIE facilities deteriorating; 80% need repair | $3.5 billion deferred maintenance |
| Reserved lands protected | Allotment era reduced tribal land base by 90 million acres | Land loss largely uncompensated |
| Hunting and fishing rights | Rights affirmed but under constant legal challenge | Ongoing litigation costs; state interference |
| Water for reservation purposes | Rights recognized but unadjudicated in many basins | Decades-long adjudication processes |
| Law enforcement and protection | Tribal police forces understaffed by estimated 40% | Public safety gaps, especially for non-Indian crime |
