# Treaty Rights: History

## Overview

The history of U.S.-tribal treaty relations is a story of bilateral agreements between sovereign nations, followed by systematic unilateral violation by the more powerful party. Understanding this history is essential because treaty obligations remain legally binding, the patterns of violation illuminate present-day challenges, and the historical record provides the factual foundation for reform.

## Timeline of Major Eras

### Pre-Constitutional Period (1778-1789)

**Context**: The nascent United States, fighting the Revolutionary War and struggling under the Articles of Confederation, needed Indigenous alliances and feared Indigenous military power.

**Key Events**:

- **1778**: Treaty of Fort Pitt with the Lenape (Delaware) Nation -- the first U.S.-Indian treaty, promising mutual defense and even contemplating a future Indian state with congressional representation (National Archives, Treaty Series)
- **1783**: Treaty of Paris ends the Revolutionary War; Britain cedes territory occupied by dozens of Indigenous nations without their consent
- **1784-1789**: Congress negotiates treaties under the Articles of Confederation, establishing the principle that Indian affairs are a federal responsibility

**Significance**: Early treaties were genuine diplomatic instruments reflecting the military and political reality that the United States could not simply impose its will on Indigenous nations.

### Constitutional Era and Early Republic (1789-1830)

**Context**: The Constitution (Art. I, Sec. 8, Cl. 3) gives Congress power to regulate commerce "with the Indian Tribes," placing tribes alongside foreign nations and the states. President Washington adopted a treaty-making approach to Indian relations.

**Key Events**:

- **1789**: The Indian Trade and Intercourse Acts begin regulating commerce with tribes, requiring federal licenses for traders
- **1790**: First Indian Trade and Intercourse Act prohibits purchase of Indian land without federal approval (1 Stat. 137)
- **1803**: Louisiana Purchase -- the United States acquires French claims to vast territories occupied by hundreds of Indigenous nations, without their knowledge or consent
- **1823**: *Johnson v. McIntosh*, 21 U.S. 543 -- Chief Justice Marshall articulates the "discovery doctrine," holding that European "discovery" granted exclusive right to purchase Indian land
- **1831**: *Cherokee Nation v. Georgia*, 30 U.S. 1 -- Marshall describes tribes as "domestic dependent nations" in a relationship resembling "that of a ward to his guardian"
- **1832**: *Worcester v. Georgia*, 31 U.S. 515 -- Marshall affirms tribal sovereignty; state laws have no force within Cherokee territory

**Significance**: The Marshall Trilogy established the doctrinal framework that persists today: tribes are sovereign, but their sovereignty is subject to federal authority. Treaties were the primary mechanism for defining the federal-tribal relationship.

### Removal Era (1830-1850)

**Context**: President Andrew Jackson and Congress prioritized white settlement of the Southeast over treaty obligations and Supreme Court rulings.

**Key Events**:

- **1830**: Indian Removal Act authorizes the President to negotiate removal treaties exchanging southeastern homelands for western territory (4 Stat. 411)
- **1831-1850**: Approximately 100,000 Indigenous people forcibly relocated from the Southeast to Indian Territory (present-day Oklahoma)
- **1835**: Treaty of New Echota -- signed by an unauthorized faction of the Cherokee Nation, used to justify removal despite opposition from the elected Cherokee government
- **1838**: Trail of Tears -- forced removal of the Cherokee Nation; an estimated 4,000-8,000 die during the march (Smithsonian National Museum of the American Indian)
- **1840s**: Additional removal treaties signed with tribes across the Midwest and Great Lakes regions

**Significance**: The Removal Era demonstrated that the United States would enforce treaties when convenient and break them when profitable. "Voluntary" treaties were coerced through military pressure and fraud.

### Treaty Era Peak (1850-1871)

**Context**: Westward expansion and the discovery of gold intensified pressure on Indigenous lands. The United States negotiated treaties at a rapid pace, often under duress.

**Key Events**:

- **1851**: Treaty of Fort Laramie with Plains nations -- establishes boundaries for tribal territories, promises annuity payments and protection from white encroachment
- **1854-1856**: Stevens Treaties in the Pacific Northwest -- Governor Isaac Stevens negotiates a series of treaties with tribes in present-day Washington, reserving fishing rights at "usual and accustomed grounds and stations"
- **1855**: Treaty of Point Elliott, Treaty of Medicine Creek, Treaty of Point No Point -- key Pacific Northwest treaties that would be central to fishing rights litigation a century later
- **1861-1865**: Civil War; some tribes ally with Confederacy, leading to post-war treaty penalties
- **1868**: Fort Laramie Treaty (second) -- establishes the Great Sioux Reservation, promising the Black Hills would remain Sioux territory "in perpetuity"
- **1868**: Treaty of Fort Bridger with the Eastern Shoshone -- reserves Wind River Reservation
- **1871**: Indian Appropriations Act ends treaty-making with tribes, though existing treaties remain in effect (16 Stat. 544)

**Significance**: The most intensive period of treaty-making produced the bulk of rights that tribes enforce today. The 1871 end of treaty-making reflected Congress's growing assertion of unilateral power over Indian affairs.

### Allotment and Assimilation (1871-1934)

**Context**: Federal policy shifted from treaty-based diplomacy to imposed assimilation, systematically dismantling treaty protections.

**Key Events**:

- **1871**: End of treaty-making; Congress begins legislating unilaterally on Indian affairs
- **1883**: *Ex parte Crow Dog*, 109 U.S. 556 -- Supreme Court affirms tribal criminal jurisdiction; Congress responds by passing the Major Crimes Act (1885) to impose federal jurisdiction
- **1887**: General Allotment Act (Dawes Act) -- breaks up communal tribal lands into individual allotments, opening "surplus" lands to white settlement. Tribal land base shrinks from 138 million acres to 48 million acres by 1934 (Cohen, *Handbook of Federal Indian Law*, 2012)
- **1903**: *Lone Wolf v. Hitchcock*, 187 U.S. 553 -- Supreme Court holds that Congress has plenary power to abrogate Indian treaties, comparing it to power over foreign treaties
- **1906**: Burke Act amends the Dawes Act, accelerating allotment and trust-to-fee conversion
- **1924**: Indian Citizenship Act grants U.S. citizenship to all Native Americans, though voting rights remain restricted in many states

**Significance**: The allotment era caused the single greatest loss of tribal land and treaty rights. The plenary power doctrine established in *Lone Wolf* remains the legal foundation for treaty abrogation.

### Indian Reorganization and Partial Recovery (1934-1953)

**Context**: The New Deal brought a shift toward tribal self-governance, though treaty rights remained subordinated to federal discretion.

**Key Events**:

- **1934**: Indian Reorganization Act (Wheeler-Howard Act) ends allotment, restores some lands to tribal ownership, and encourages tribal constitutions (48 Stat. 984)
- **1934-1945**: Federal policy supports tribal governance, though within a paternalistic framework that falls short of treaty-based self-determination
- **1946**: Indian Claims Commission established to adjudicate historical treaty violations -- awards monetary compensation but not land restoration (60 Stat. 1049)

**Significance**: The IRA halted the worst excesses of allotment but did not restore treaty rights or the treaty-based government-to-government relationship.

### Termination Era (1953-1968)

**Context**: Cold War-era assimilationism led Congress to attempt to eliminate tribal sovereignty entirely.

**Key Events**:

- **1953**: House Concurrent Resolution 108 announces the policy of terminating federal recognition of tribes "at the earliest possible time"
- **1953**: Public Law 280 transfers federal criminal jurisdiction over Indian Country to certain states without tribal consent (67 Stat. 588)
- **1953-1966**: 109 tribes terminated; lands sold, trust relationships dissolved, treaty obligations repudiated
- **1954**: Menominee Termination Act -- one of the most devastating examples; the Menominee Nation loses federal services, land is taxed, and the tribe nearly collapses economically

**Significance**: Termination represented the most extreme form of treaty abrogation: complete unilateral repudiation of the government-to-government relationship. Its devastating effects built the political case for self-determination.

### Self-Determination Era (1968-Present)

**Context**: Beginning with President Johnson and accelerating under Nixon, federal policy shifted toward tribal self-governance, though without fully restoring treaty-based obligations.

**Key Events**:

- **1968**: Indian Civil Rights Act applies most Bill of Rights protections to tribal governments (25 U.S.C. Sec. 1301-1303)
- **1970**: President Nixon's Special Message to Congress repudiates termination and endorses self-determination
- **1973**: Menominee Restoration Act reverses termination for the Menominee Tribe
- **1974**: *United States v. Washington* (Boldt Decision) -- Judge George Boldt rules that Stevens Treaty tribes are entitled to 50% of the harvestable salmon and steelhead in western Washington (384 F. Supp. 312)
- **1975**: Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act (P.L. 93-638) allows tribes to contract to administer federal programs
- **1978**: *Oliphant v. Suquamish Indian Tribe*, 435 U.S. 191 -- Supreme Court strips tribes of criminal jurisdiction over non-Indians, creating a public safety gap not contemplated by any treaty
- **1980**: *United States v. Sioux Nation*, 448 U.S. 371 -- Supreme Court rules the taking of the Black Hills violated the Fifth Amendment; awards $105 million. The Sioux Nation refuses the award, demanding land return. The fund has grown to over $2 billion (as of 2024) and remains unclaimed
- **1988**: Indian Gaming Regulatory Act establishes framework for tribal gaming (25 U.S.C. Sec. 2701-2721)
- **1999**: *Minnesota v. Mille Lacs Band of Chippewa Indians*, 526 U.S. 172 -- affirms treaty-reserved hunting, fishing, and gathering rights in ceded territory
- **2013**: Violence Against Women Act reauthorization restores limited tribal criminal jurisdiction over non-Indians for domestic violence
- **2019**: *Herrera v. Wyoming*, 587 U.S. ___ -- affirms Crow Treaty hunting rights were not extinguished by Wyoming statehood
- **2020**: *McGirt v. Oklahoma*, 591 U.S. ___ -- affirms that the Muscogee (Creek) Nation reservation was never disestablished
- **2022**: *Oklahoma v. Castro-Huerta*, 597 U.S. ___ -- allows state criminal jurisdiction over non-Indians on tribal lands, narrowing *McGirt*

**Significance**: The self-determination era has produced significant treaty rights victories in court but has not resolved the fundamental problem: treaty obligations remain underfunded, and Congress retains the power to abrogate treaty provisions unilaterally.

## Patterns and Lessons

### Recurring Patterns of Treaty Violation

| Pattern | Historical Examples | Current Manifestation |
|---------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Resource-driven abrogation | Gold in Black Hills (1876); oil in Oklahoma (1900s) | Energy development on treaty lands; water diversion |
| Coerced consent | Removal treaties (1830s); starvation-era treaties (1860s) | Funding conditions, regulatory pressure |
| Unilateral modification | Dawes Act (1887); termination (1953) | Riders on appropriations bills; regulatory changes |
| Selective enforcement | Enforcing cession provisions but not protection promises | Collecting resource revenues but underfunding services |
| Jurisdiction stripping | Major Crimes Act (1885); *Oliphant* (1978) | State assertion of jurisdiction without tribal consent |

### Key Legal Doctrines

| Doctrine | Established | Effect on Treaty Rights |
|----------|-------------|------------------------|
| Discovery doctrine | *Johnson v. McIntosh* (1823) | Undermines Indigenous title despite treaties |
| Plenary power | *Lone Wolf v. Hitchcock* (1903) | Allows unilateral treaty abrogation |
| Reserved rights | *Winters v. United States* (1908) | Protects rights not explicitly ceded |
| Canons of construction | *Carpenter v. Shaw* (1930) | Requires pro-tribal interpretation |
| Trust responsibility | *Seminole Nation v. United States* (1942) | Imposes fiduciary duty on federal government |
| Implicit divestiture | *Oliphant v. Suquamish* (1978) | Strips powers not explicitly addressed in treaties |
