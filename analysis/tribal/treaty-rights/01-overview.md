# Treaty Rights: Overview

## Executive Summary

Between 1778 and 1871, the United States ratified 374 treaties with Indigenous nations, exchanging vast land cessions for promises of reserved lands, hunting and fishing rights, annuity payments, education, health care, and federal protection. These treaties are not historical artifacts -- they are the "supreme law of the land" under Article VI of the U.S. Constitution, carrying the same legal weight as treaties with foreign nations. The Supreme Court has consistently affirmed their binding force, most recently in *McGirt v. Oklahoma*, 591 U.S. ___ (2020), and *Herrera v. Wyoming*, 587 U.S. ___ (2019).

Despite their constitutional status, treaty obligations have been systematically dishonored. Congress has unilaterally abrogated treaty provisions, the executive branch has failed to deliver promised services and funding, courts have narrowed treaty rights through hostile doctrines, and state governments have infringed on rights reserved to tribal nations. The U.S. Commission on Civil Rights estimated in 2018 that the federal government underfunds its treaty and trust obligations to tribal nations by tens of billions of dollars annually -- a level of noncompliance that would constitute an international law violation if applied to any foreign treaty partner.

Addressing the treaty rights crisis requires mandatory funding tied to treaty obligations, statutory protections against unilateral abrogation, robust enforcement mechanisms, and genuine government-to-government negotiation when treaty modifications are necessary.

## Scope

### What This Subtopic Covers

- **Treaty framework**: The legal status of Indian treaties under U.S. constitutional law
- **Reserved rights doctrine**: Rights retained by tribes through treaties, including hunting, fishing, gathering, and water rights
- **Trust responsibility**: The fiduciary obligations arising from treaty promises
- **Treaty abrogation**: Congressional power to unilaterally modify or break treaties, and limits on that power
- **Funding obligations**: Federal duty to fund services promised in treaties, including health care, education, and law enforcement
- **Modern treaty interpretation**: How courts apply treaty canons of construction
- **International context**: How U.S. treaty compliance compares to international standards

## Core Tensions

- **Supreme law vs. plenary power**: Treaties are constitutionally co-equal with federal statutes, yet Congress claims unilateral power to abrogate them
- **Retained rights vs. state authority**: Treaty-reserved hunting, fishing, and gathering rights conflict with state regulatory schemes
- **Trust obligation vs. discretionary funding**: Treaty promises are treated as obligations, but funding is appropriated at Congress's discretion
- **Original understanding vs. modern interpretation**: Courts apply canons of construction favoring tribes, but also impose limitations not contemplated by treaty signatories
- **Bilateral agreements vs. unilateral modification**: Treaties were negotiated as bilateral agreements between sovereigns, but Congress can modify them without tribal consent
- **Individual rights vs. collective rights**: Treaty rights belong to tribal nations collectively, creating tensions with individual-rights frameworks

## Key Questions

1. **What is the legal status of Indian treaties today?** How do treaties interact with federal statutes, and what happens when they conflict?

2. **What rights do treaties reserve?** How are hunting, fishing, gathering, water, and other reserved rights defined and enforced?

3. **Why has the federal government broken so many treaties?** What historical, legal, and political forces have driven treaty abrogation?

4. **How are treaty obligations funded?** Why is there such a large gap between obligations and appropriations?

5. **Can treaty abrogation be reversed?** What legal and legislative mechanisms exist to restore treaty rights?

6. **How do courts interpret treaties?** What canons of construction apply, and how effectively do they protect tribal interests?

7. **What would full treaty compliance look like?** How much would it cost, and what would it require?

## Vision of Success

A system that honors treaty obligations would feature:

- Treaty obligations treated as mandatory spending, comparable to Social Security or Medicare, rather than subject to annual appropriations discretion
- A statutory prohibition on unilateral treaty abrogation, requiring tribal consent or fair compensation before modification
- Treaty-reserved hunting, fishing, gathering, and water rights fully enforced against state interference
- IHS funding at parity with comparable federal health programs, reflecting treaty-based health care entitlements
- An independent treaty compliance commission with authority to audit federal performance against treaty obligations
- Meaningful government-to-government consultation before any federal action affecting treaty rights
- International accountability mechanisms aligned with the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples
