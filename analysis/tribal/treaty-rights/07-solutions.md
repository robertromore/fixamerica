# Treaty Rights: Solutions

## Overview

Solutions to the treaty rights crisis must address the structural root causes -- plenary power, discretionary funding, fragmented responsibility, and enforcement failures -- rather than treating individual symptoms. The proposals below are organized from most achievable to most transformative.

## Solution Categories

### Solution 1: Mandatory Funding for Treaty Obligations

**Problem Addressed**: Treaty obligations funded through discretionary appropriations, resulting in chronic underfunding

**Proposal**: Convert treaty-obligated programs from discretionary to mandatory spending, modeled on Social Security, Medicare, and veterans' benefits

**Key Components**:

- Establish a Treaty Obligation Trust Fund with mandatory appropriations
- Fund IHS, BIA, BIE, tribal law enforcement, and NAHASDA at levels determined by a needs-based formula
- Index funding to inflation and population growth
- Provide advance appropriations (extending the model enacted for IHS in FY 2024) to all treaty-obligated programs
- Protect treaty funding from sequestration and government shutdowns

**Estimated Cost**: $50-58 billion annually in additional spending to close the current gap (U.S. Commission on Civil Rights, "Broken Promises," 2018, adjusted for inflation)

**Precedent**: The Snyder Act of 1921 (25 U.S.C. Sec. 13) authorizes appropriations for Indian affairs but does not mandate specific levels. The Indian Health Care Improvement Act (25 U.S.C. Sec. 1601 et seq.) was permanently reauthorized in 2010 but remains subject to discretionary appropriations.

**Feasibility**: Moderate. Requires congressional action to reclassify spending. The advance appropriations precedent for IHS (enacted 2023) demonstrates incremental progress is achievable.

### Solution 2: Treaty Rights Enforcement Commission

**Problem Addressed**: No independent body monitors treaty compliance or enforces obligations

**Proposal**: Establish an independent Treaty Rights Enforcement Commission modeled on the U.S. Commission on Civil Rights but with enhanced authority

**Key Components**:

- Independent commission with five commissioners: two appointed by tribal nations (through NCAI nomination), one by the President, one by the Senate, one by the House
- Authority to audit federal agency compliance with treaty obligations
- Subpoena power for documents and testimony
- Authority to issue binding compliance orders, subject to judicial review
- Annual public report on treaty compliance status
- Authority to receive and investigate complaints from tribal nations
- Mandatory federal agency response to commission findings within 90 days

**Precedent**: The Indian Claims Commission (1946-1978) adjudicated historical treaty claims but was limited to monetary awards. The proposed commission would focus on prospective compliance.

**Feasibility**: Moderate. Congress has created similar independent commissions. Political support depends on framing as accountability, not expansion of federal bureaucracy.

---

### Solution 3: Statutory Limits on Treaty Abrogation

**Problem Addressed**: Congress can unilaterally abrogate treaty provisions through ordinary legislation

**Proposal**: Enact a Federal Indian Treaty Protection Act requiring enhanced procedural protections before any treaty modification

**Key Components**:

- Require a recorded supermajority vote (two-thirds of both chambers) for any legislation that abrogates or modifies treaty provisions
- Prohibit treaty abrogation through appropriations riders or omnibus legislation
- Mandate government-to-government consultation with affected tribes before any legislation affecting treaty rights
- Require a Treaty Impact Statement for any proposed legislation affecting Indian Country, analogous to environmental impact statements
- Establish a right of action for tribes to challenge unauthorized treaty abrogation in federal court

**Limitations**: Congress cannot permanently bind future Congresses, so these protections could be repealed. However, creating procedural hurdles significantly raises the cost of casual treaty abrogation.

**Feasibility**: Low-Moderate. Requires Congress to voluntarily limit its own power. Historical precedent includes the War Powers Resolution and the Congressional Budget Act, which imposed procedural constraints on congressional action.

---

### Solution 4: Full Adjudication of Treaty-Reserved Water Rights

**Problem Addressed**: Treaty-reserved water rights remain unadjudicated for many tribes, leaving critical resources unprotected

**Proposal**: Accelerated federal adjudication of tribal water rights with adequate funding for tribal participation

**Key Components**:

- Establish a dedicated federal water rights adjudication program within the Department of Justice
- Provide $500 million over 10 years for tribal water rights litigation and technical support
- Prioritize settlements over litigation, with federal funding for settlement implementation
- Ensure that adjudicated rights include infrastructure funding for water delivery systems
- Require that any federal water project affecting tribal rights comply with treaty obligations before proceeding

**Precedent**: The Navajo-Hopi Little Colorado River Water Rights Settlement Act of 2012 provided $180 million for water infrastructure. The Montana Flathead Water Compact (2021) settled water rights claims for $1.9 billion.

**Feasibility**: Moderate. Water settlements have bipartisan support when structured to benefit all parties. Major barrier is cost.

---

### Solution 5: Treaty Rights Education Mandate

**Problem Addressed**: Public ignorance of treaty obligations enables political noncompliance

**Proposal**: Mandate treaty and Indigenous history education in all public schools receiving federal funding

**Key Components**:

- Require age-appropriate curriculum on U.S.-Indian treaties, sovereignty, and federal trust responsibility at elementary, middle, and high school levels
- Develop curriculum in consultation with tribal nations and Indigenous education experts
- Fund teacher training through existing Department of Education programs
- Include treaty history in civics and government courses at the collegiate level
- Support tribal-developed curricula for use in tribally controlled schools

**Precedent**: Washington State (2005), Montana (2005), Oregon (2017), and several other states have enacted "Indian Education for All" or similar mandates requiring Indigenous history and culture in K-12 curricula. These mandates have been shown to increase student knowledge and reduce stereotyping (Montana Office of Public Instruction, 2019).

**Feasibility**: High. State-level precedents exist. Federal mandate could be attached to education funding reauthorization.

---

### Solution 6: Restoration of Full Criminal Jurisdiction

**Problem Addressed**: *Oliphant v. Suquamish* (1978) stripped tribal criminal jurisdiction over non-Indians, creating a public safety gap not contemplated by any treaty

**Proposal**: Legislatively restore tribal criminal jurisdiction over all persons within treaty-defined territories

**Key Components**:

- Amend the Indian Civil Rights Act to authorize tribal criminal jurisdiction over non-Indians for all offenses
- Require tribal courts exercising expanded jurisdiction to provide constitutional protections (right to counsel, jury trial, appellate review)
- Provide federal funding for tribal court systems to handle expanded caseloads
- Establish a federal appellate review process for non-Indian defendants who contest tribal court jurisdiction

**Precedent**: The Violence Against Women Act reauthorization (2013, 2022) partially restored tribal jurisdiction over non-Indian domestic violence and sexual assault offenders. Data from the first decade of VAWA jurisdiction shows no due process violations and effective prosecution (National Congress of American Indians, 2023).

**Feasibility**: Moderate. VAWA precedent demonstrates viability. Opposition centered on due process concerns can be addressed through procedural protections.

---

### Solution 7: International Accountability Mechanisms

**Problem Addressed**: No international mechanism enforces U.S. compliance with Indian treaties

**Proposal**: Voluntary submission to international review of treaty compliance

**Key Components**:

- Accept periodic review of Indian treaty compliance by the UN Permanent Forum on Indigenous Issues
- Report on UNDRIP implementation, including treaty compliance, as part of UN Universal Periodic Review
- Support establishment of an Inter-American treaty compliance mechanism through the Organization of American States
- Invite international observers to monitor treaty rights enforcement in the United States

**Limitations**: International mechanisms are advisory, not binding. Value lies in transparency and reputational pressure.

**Feasibility**: Low. U.S. resistance to international oversight is strong. However, voluntary engagement can be initiated by executive action without congressional approval.

## Implementation Synergies

Several solutions reinforce each other:

- **Mandatory funding + enforcement commission**: Guaranteed funding is meaningless without compliance monitoring; the commission ensures funds are properly directed
- **Abrogation limits + criminal jurisdiction restoration**: Procedural protections against abrogation protect restored jurisdiction from future rollback
- **Education mandate + all reforms**: Public understanding of treaty obligations creates political support for all other reforms
- **Water adjudication + mandatory funding**: Adjudicated water rights require infrastructure funding to become meaningful
