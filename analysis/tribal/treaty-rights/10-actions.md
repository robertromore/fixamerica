# Treaty Rights: Actions

## Overview

This document outlines concrete actions that individuals, organizations, and communities can take to support treaty rights enforcement and reform. Actions are organized by commitment level and type.

## Individual Actions

### Learn

- **Read at least one U.S.-Indian treaty**: Full treaty texts are available at the National Archives (archives.gov). Start with a treaty relevant to the Indigenous nations whose traditional territory you live on
- **Take a course**: Dozens of universities offer free or low-cost online courses on federal Indian law and Indigenous history. The Smithsonian's Native Knowledge 360 provides accessible introductory materials
- **Learn whose land you are on**: Use native-land.ca to identify the traditional territories and treaty areas where you live and work
- **Read Indigenous perspectives**: Follow Indigenous journalists and scholars. Indian Country Today (indiancountrytoday.com) provides daily coverage of treaty rights issues
- **Understand the legal framework**: Read the overview of treaty law in Canby's *American Indian Law in a Nutshell* or Pevar's *The Rights of Indians and Tribes*

### Speak

- **Talk about treaty obligations with friends and family**: Most Americans are unaware that treaties are binding law. Share what you learn
- **Write letters to editors**: When treaty rights issues appear in the news, write informed letters to local and national publications
- **Use social media**: Share credible information about treaty rights from reputable sources
- **Correct misinformation**: When you hear treaty rights described as "special privileges," explain that they were purchased through land cessions and are constitutionally protected
- **Support Indigenous voices**: Amplify rather than speak over Indigenous advocates

### Act

- **Contact your representatives**: Call or write your U.S. senators and representative to express support for treaty rights legislation. Specific bills to mention include:
    - Advance appropriations for all treaty-obligated programs
    - The Treaty Rights Enforcement Commission Act
    - Expansion of tribal criminal jurisdiction under VAWA
- **Vote**: Support candidates who endorse treaty compliance. Ask candidates at town halls about their position on federal treaty obligations
- **Attend public hearings**: When treaty issues are discussed at state or federal hearings, attend and submit public comments
- **Participate in consultation processes**: Federal agencies are required to consult with tribal nations on actions affecting Indian Country. Public comment periods often accompany these consultations

## Organizational Actions

### For Educational Institutions

- **Implement treaty education**: Adopt curricula covering U.S.-Indian treaties at all grade levels
- **Train teachers**: Provide professional development on Indigenous history and federal Indian law
- **Partner with tribal nations**: Develop educational materials in consultation with local tribal governments and educators
- **Host speakers**: Invite tribal leaders, attorneys, and scholars to discuss treaty rights
- **Support research**: Fund academic research on treaty rights and federal trust responsibility compliance

### For Legal Professionals

- **Provide pro bono services**: Treaty rights litigation is resource-intensive; tribal nations need legal support
- **File amicus briefs**: Support treaty rights cases before federal and state courts
- **Mentor Indigenous law students**: Support the next generation of Indian law attorneys
- **Educate the bar**: Present CLE programs on federal Indian law and treaty rights
- **Volunteer with NARF or tribal legal programs**: Treaty rights enforcement requires sustained legal capacity

### For Faith Communities

- **Study the moral dimensions of treaty compliance**: Treaty obligations are a matter of national integrity and justice
- **Pass resolutions supporting treaty rights**: Many denominations have existing statements on Indigenous rights that can be updated and amplified
- **Advocate through denominational networks**: Use existing advocacy infrastructure to lobby for treaty legislation
- **Support reconciliation efforts**: Participate in truth and reconciliation processes where they exist
- **Host educational events**: Use community spaces to educate congregations about treaty history

### For Businesses

- **Understand treaty impacts on your industry**: Businesses operating near or on tribal lands should understand applicable treaty rights
- **Engage in meaningful consultation**: When business activities may affect treaty rights, consult with affected tribal nations early and genuinely
- **Support tribal enterprises**: Procurement and partnership with tribal businesses supports treaty-based economic self-determination
- **Advocate for compliance**: Business associations can support treaty compliance as a matter of legal certainty and stable investment environments

### For State and Local Governments

- **Enact treaty education mandates**: Follow the examples of Montana, Washington, and Oregon
- **Negotiate state-tribal cooperation compacts**: Address jurisdiction, taxation, and resource management through bilateral agreements
- **Respect treaty boundaries**: Ensure state regulatory actions do not infringe on treaty-reserved rights
- **Fund tribal consultation**: Dedicate state resources to meaningful government-to-government engagement
- **Recognize treaty rights in land use planning**: Incorporate treaty rights analysis into environmental review and land use decisions

## Community Actions

### Build Local Coalitions

- **Form treaty rights committees**: Within existing civic organizations, create committees focused on treaty compliance
- **Connect with local tribal governments**: Build ongoing relationships with tribal nations in your area
- **Organize community forums**: Host educational events on treaty history and current issues
- **Develop joint advocacy platforms**: Work with tribal nations to identify shared priorities

### Engage in Civic Processes

- **Monitor federal agency actions**: Track BIA, IHS, and DOJ actions that affect treaty rights
- **Submit public comments**: Participate in federal rulemaking processes related to tribal consultation, environmental review, and resource management
- **Attend congressional hearings**: When the Senate Indian Affairs Committee or House Subcommittee for Indigenous Peoples holds hearings, attend or watch and engage
- **Support treaty-related ballot measures**: Where treaty-related measures appear on ballots, organize voter education campaigns

| November | National Native American Heritage Month; educational events |

| Attending hearings | Testimony submitted; representative awareness demonstrated |
