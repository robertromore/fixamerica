# Treaty Rights: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

This document presents draft legislation to address the systemic failure to honor treaty obligations to tribal nations. The legislative approach focuses on three pillars: mandatory funding for treaty-obligated programs, institutional enforcement mechanisms, and procedural protections against unilateral abrogation. Each proposal includes draft text, explanation, challenges analysis, and refinements.

## Constitutional Amendments

### Treaty Rights Protection Amendment

**Purpose**: Constitutionalize protections against unilateral treaty abrogation and guarantee tribal access to courts for treaty enforcement.

**Draft Text**:

```text
Section 1. No treaty between the United States and any Indian
nation or tribe shall be abrogated, modified, or rendered
unenforceable by any Act of Congress or executive action except
with the free, prior, and informed consent of the affected
Indian nation or tribe, or upon payment of just compensation
as determined by an independent tribunal.

Section 2. Any Indian nation or tribe that is party to a treaty
with the United States shall have standing to enforce the
provisions of such treaty in the courts of the United States,
and sovereign immunity of the United States shall not bar
such action.

Section 3. The trust responsibility of the United States to
Indian nations and tribes, arising from treaties and the course
of dealing between them, shall be a judicially enforceable
obligation.

Section 4. Congress shall have the power to enforce this article
by appropriate legislation.

Section 5. This article shall take effect two years after the
date of ratification.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 1 ends unilateral abrogation by requiring tribal consent or just compensation, paralleling the Fifth Amendment's taking clause
- Section 2 eliminates sovereign immunity as a barrier to treaty enforcement, giving tribes the same access to courts that any contract party would have
- Section 3 makes the trust responsibility judicially enforceable, resolving decades of ambiguity about whether tribes can sue for trust violations
- The two-year effective date allows Congress to enact implementing legislation

**Potential Challenges**:

- Ratification requires two-thirds of both chambers and three-fourths of state legislatures
- Opposition from states concerned about expanded tribal court access
- Fiscal implications of guaranteed treaty compliance
- Federalism objections to constraining congressional power

**Refinements**:

- If full amendment fails, pursue statutory protections (see Federal Legislation below)
- Consider limiting Section 1 to prospective application (future abrogation only) to reduce political opposition
- Include a negotiation and mediation requirement before litigation under Section 2

## Federal Legislation

### Treaty Obligation Trust Fund Act

**Purpose**: Convert treaty-obligated programs from discretionary to mandatory spending, closing the chronic funding gap.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Treaty Obligation Trust Fund Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) The United States has entered into 374 ratified treaties
    with Indian nations and tribes.
(2) These treaties are the supreme law of the land under
    Article VI of the Constitution.
(3) Treaty obligations include, but are not limited to,
    health care, education, law enforcement, housing, and
    resource protection.
(4) Federal funding for treaty-obligated programs falls
    short of treaty requirements by an estimated
    $50,000,000,000 annually.
(5) Discretionary appropriations subject treaty obligations
    to annual political uncertainty, government shutdowns,
    and sequestration.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
(1) TREATY-OBLIGATED PROGRAM.—The term "treaty-obligated
    program" means any federal program that fulfills, in
    whole or in part, obligations arising from ratified
    treaties between the United States and Indian nations
    or tribes, including—
    (A) the Indian Health Service;
    (B) the Bureau of Indian Affairs;
    (C) the Bureau of Indian Education;
    (D) tribal law enforcement programs;
    (E) tribal housing programs under the Native American
        Housing Assistance and Self-Determination Act; and
    (F) any other program identified by the Treaty Rights
        Enforcement Commission established under section 5.
(2) COMMISSION.—The term "Commission" means the Treaty
    Rights Enforcement Commission.
(3) INDIAN NATION OR TRIBE.—The term "Indian nation or
    tribe" means any Indian tribe, band, nation, pueblo,
    or other organized group or community that is recognized
    as eligible for the special programs and services provided
    by the United States to Indians because of their status
    as Indians.

SEC. 4. TREATY OBLIGATION TRUST FUND.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established in the Treasury of
    the United States a trust fund to be known as the "Treaty
    Obligation Trust Fund".
(b) MANDATORY APPROPRIATIONS.—There are authorized to be
    appropriated to the Fund, and there are appropriated,
    for each fiscal year beginning with the first fiscal year
    that begins after the date of enactment of this Act,
    such sums as are necessary to fund treaty-obligated
    programs at levels determined under subsection (c).
(c) FUNDING LEVELS.—
    (1) IN GENERAL.—The Commission, in consultation with
        Indian nations and tribes, shall determine the
        funding level necessary to fulfill treaty obligations
        for each treaty-obligated program.
    (2) FACTORS.—In determining funding levels, the
        Commission shall consider—
        (A) the specific obligations contained in applicable
            treaties;
        (B) the trust responsibility of the United States;
        (C) the demonstrated need of Indian nations and
            tribes;
        (D) comparability with funding levels for similar
            non-Indian federal programs; and
        (E) inflation and population growth.
    (3) INITIAL LEVELS.—Until the Commission issues its
        first determination under paragraph (1), funding
        shall be not less than 150 percent of the amounts
        appropriated for each treaty-obligated program in
        the fiscal year preceding enactment.
(d) ADVANCE APPROPRIATIONS.—Amounts appropriated under this
    section shall be available for obligation for a period of
    not less than 2 fiscal years.
(e) EXEMPTION FROM SEQUESTRATION.—Amounts in the Fund shall
    be exempt from sequestration under the Balanced Budget and
    Emergency Deficit Control Act of 1985.

SEC. 5. TREATY RIGHTS ENFORCEMENT COMMISSION.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established an independent
    commission to be known as the "Treaty Rights Enforcement
    Commission".
(b) MEMBERSHIP.—
    (1) The Commission shall be composed of 5 members—
        (A) 2 members nominated by the National Congress
            of American Indians and appointed by the
            President;
        (B) 1 member appointed by the President;
        (C) 1 member appointed by the President pro tempore
            of the Senate; and
        (D) 1 member appointed by the Speaker of the House
            of Representatives.
    (2) At least 3 members shall be enrolled members of
        federally recognized Indian nations or tribes.
    (3) Members shall serve 6-year staggered terms.
(c) DUTIES.—The Commission shall—
    (1) conduct annual audits of federal agency compliance
        with treaty obligations;
    (2) determine funding levels for treaty-obligated
        programs under section 4(c);
    (3) receive and investigate complaints from Indian
        nations and tribes regarding treaty noncompliance;
    (4) issue compliance orders to federal agencies, subject
        to judicial review;
    (5) publish an annual Treaty Compliance Report Card;
    (6) recommend legislative and administrative actions to
        improve treaty compliance.
(d) SUBPOENA POWER.—The Commission shall have the power to
    issue subpoenas for documents and testimony necessary
    to carry out its duties.
(e) MANDATORY RESPONSE.—Any federal agency that receives a
    compliance order under subsection (c)(4) shall respond
    in writing within 90 days, either implementing the order
    or stating specific reasons for noncompliance, subject
    to judicial review.

SEC. 6. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated to the Commission
$25,000,000 for each fiscal year.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date that is 180 days after
the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- The mandatory funding mechanism mirrors Social Security and Medicare, treating treaty obligations as binding commitments rather than discretionary choices
- The 150% initial funding floor provides immediate relief while the Commission develops full needs assessments
- Tribal representation on the Commission ensures that the entities to whom obligations are owed have a voice in determining compliance
- Subpoena power and mandatory agency response create meaningful enforcement teeth

**Potential Challenges**:

- CBO scoring will show significant mandatory spending increase
- Budget hawks will oppose reclassification of discretionary programs
- Existing agency bureaucracies may resist oversight by an independent commission
- Political opposition to tribal-majority commission membership

**Refinements**:

- If full mandatory funding fails, pursue phased implementation over 10 years
- Alternative: index treaty funding to a percentage of GDP or federal revenue
- Consider starting with the best-documented treaty obligations (IHS, BIE) before expanding

### Federal Indian Treaty Protection Act

**Purpose**: Establish procedural protections against unilateral treaty abrogation.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Federal Indian Treaty Protection
Act".

SEC. 2. SUPERMAJORITY REQUIREMENT FOR TREATY ABROGATION.

(a) IN GENERAL.—No Act of Congress shall be construed to
    abrogate, modify, or diminish any right secured by a
    treaty between the United States and any Indian nation
    or tribe unless—
    (1) the Act expressly states that it is intended to
        abrogate, modify, or diminish such right;
    (2) the Act is passed by a vote of not less than
        two-thirds of the members present in each House
        of Congress; and
    (3) the affected Indian nation or tribe has been
        consulted in accordance with subsection (b).
(b) CONSULTATION REQUIREMENT.—
    (1) Before any legislation that would abrogate, modify,
        or diminish treaty rights is considered on the floor
        of either House of Congress, the relevant committee
        shall conduct government-to-government consultation
        with each affected Indian nation or tribe.
    (2) Consultation shall include—
        (A) written notice to each affected Indian nation
            or tribe not less than 180 days before committee
            action;
        (B) an opportunity for testimony by tribal leaders;
        (C) a written response from each affected Indian
            nation or tribe; and
        (D) a Treaty Impact Statement prepared by the
            Congressional Research Service.
(c) PROHIBITION ON APPROPRIATIONS RIDERS.—No provision that
    abrogates, modifies, or diminishes any treaty right may
    be included in any general or special appropriations Act,
    continuing resolution, or omnibus spending measure.

SEC. 3. TREATY IMPACT STATEMENTS.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—The Congressional Research Service, in
    consultation with the Treaty Rights Enforcement Commission,
    shall prepare a Treaty Impact Statement for any proposed
    legislation that may affect treaty rights.
(b) CONTENTS.—A Treaty Impact Statement shall include—
    (1) identification of all treaties potentially affected;
    (2) analysis of how the proposed legislation would modify
        treaty obligations;
    (3) assessment of the impact on affected Indian nations
        and tribes;
    (4) alternatives that would achieve the legislative
        purpose without treaty modification; and
    (5) estimated fiscal impact of any treaty modification.

SEC. 4. JUDICIAL REVIEW.

Any Indian nation or tribe may bring an action in the United
States District Court for the District of Columbia to challenge
any Act of Congress that the Indian nation or tribe alleges was
enacted in violation of the procedures required by this Act. The
court shall have the power to declare such Act void and
unenforceable to the extent it abrogates, modifies, or diminishes
treaty rights.
```

**Explanation**:

- The express statement rule prevents "drive-by" abrogation through ambiguous legislation, codifying a stronger version of the existing clear statement rule
- The supermajority requirement raises the political cost of abrogation without making it constitutionally impossible
- The consultation requirement ensures tribal voices are heard before any modification
- The appropriations rider prohibition prevents the most common method of casual abrogation

**Potential Challenges**:

- Congress cannot permanently bind future Congresses; a future simple majority could repeal these protections
- The supermajority requirement may face constitutional questions about whether one Congress can impose procedural requirements on future Congresses
- Political opposition from legislators who view procedural constraints as infringing on congressional prerogatives

**Refinements**:

- Frame as a rule of construction (weaker but more durable) rather than a binding procedural requirement
- Pair with the constitutional amendment for long-term protection
- Include a sunset provision requiring affirmative reauthorization to maintain political engagement

## State Model Legislation

### Model Treaty Education Act

**Purpose**: Mandate education on U.S.-Indian treaty history, obligations, and contemporary relevance in public schools.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Treaty and Indigenous
History Education Act".

SECTION 2. FINDINGS.

The Legislature finds that:
(a) The United States has entered into 374 ratified treaties
    with Indian nations and tribes, many of which affect
    lands within this State.
(b) Understanding treaty history and obligations is essential
    to informed citizenship.
(c) Educational curricula in this State have historically
    provided inadequate instruction on Indigenous history
    and treaty obligations.

SECTION 3. CURRICULUM REQUIREMENTS.

(a) The [State Department of Education] shall develop and
    implement age-appropriate curriculum on:
    (1) The history of treaties between the United States
        and Indian nations and tribes, with emphasis on
        treaties affecting this State;
    (2) The legal status of treaties as the supreme law
        of the land;
    (3) The history and contemporary status of Indian nations
        and tribes within or historically connected to
        this State;
    (4) The federal trust responsibility and its
        implications; and
    (5) Contemporary issues in federal Indian law and policy.
(b) The curriculum shall be developed in consultation with
    Indian nations and tribes located within or historically
    connected to this State.
(c) The curriculum shall be integrated into existing social
    studies, history, and government courses at elementary,
    middle school, and high school levels.

SECTION 4. TEACHER TRAINING.

(a) The [State Department of Education] shall provide
    professional development programs for teachers to
    implement the curriculum required under Section 3.
(b) Professional development programs shall be developed
    in consultation with Indian nations and tribes and
    Indigenous educators.

SECTION 5. APPROPRIATION.

There is appropriated from the general fund $[amount] for
the purpose of implementing this Act.

SECTION 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on [date], with full implementation
required no later than [date + 2 years].
```

**Explanation**:

- Modeled on Montana's Indian Education for All (2005) and Washington State's Since Time Immemorial curriculum (2005)
- Tribal consultation requirement ensures curricula are developed with input from Indigenous communities
- Integration into existing courses avoids creating an unfunded standalone mandate
- Teacher training addresses the primary implementation barrier identified in state-level evaluations

**Adaptations**:

- States with large tribal populations may include more extensive requirements
- States without federally recognized tribes can focus on national treaty history
- Funding mechanisms may vary (general fund, education trust fund, federal grants)

## Regulatory Framework

### Department of the Interior -- Treaty Compliance Regulations

**Existing Authority**: 25 U.S.C. Sec. 2 (BIA organization); 25 U.S.C. Sec. 13 (Snyder Act); Executive Order 13175 (Consultation and Coordination with Indian Tribal Governments)

**Draft Regulation**:

```text
25 C.F.R. Part 900 (Proposed)

Sec. 900.1 Purpose.
These regulations establish procedures for ensuring compliance
with treaty obligations and implementing the trust responsibility
of the United States to Indian nations and tribes.

Sec. 900.2 Treaty Compliance Review.
(a) Each bureau within the Department of the Interior that
    administers programs affecting Indian nations or tribes
    shall conduct an annual treaty compliance review.
(b) The review shall assess—
    (1) The extent to which the bureau's programs and
        expenditures fulfill applicable treaty obligations;
    (2) Any gaps between treaty obligations and program
        delivery;
    (3) Plans to address identified compliance gaps.
(c) The results of each review shall be published in the
    Federal Register and transmitted to the Senate Committee
    on Indian Affairs and the House Subcommittee for
    Indigenous Peoples.

Sec. 900.3 Consultation Requirements.
(a) Before taking any action that may affect treaty rights,
    the Department shall conduct government-to-government
    consultation with each affected Indian nation or tribe.
(b) Consultation shall be initiated not less than 120 days
    before the proposed action.
(c) The Department shall provide a written response to
    tribal concerns raised during consultation.
```

**Explanation**: These regulations would codify existing executive order requirements and add treaty-specific compliance review. They can be implemented without new legislation under existing statutory authority.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Plenary power**: Congress's plenary power over Indian affairs has been recognized for over a century but lacks clear constitutional basis. Legislative reform is more achievable than judicial overruling of *Lone Wolf*
- **Spending Clause**: Mandatory treaty funding is constitutionally permissible; the question is political will, not legal authority
- **Separation of powers**: The Treaty Rights Enforcement Commission must be structured to avoid Appointments Clause issues; commissioners exercising executive authority must be appointed by the President with Senate confirmation

### Preemption Questions

- Federal treaty rights preempt conflicting state law under the Supremacy Clause
- State regulatory authority over treaty-reserved activities (hunting, fishing, water use) must yield to federal treaty obligations
- *Castro-Huerta* (2022) created new ambiguity regarding state jurisdiction on tribal lands that legislation should clarify

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- Treaty Rights Enforcement Commission provides administrative enforcement
- Waiver of sovereign immunity enables judicial enforcement
- Mandatory funding eliminates the appropriations process as a bottleneck
- Treaty Impact Statements create transparency and political accountability

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- The Commission should undergo reauthorization every 10 years
- Funding formulas should be reviewed and updated every 5 years
- Treaty compliance benchmarks should be assessed annually

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Treaty Obligation Trust Fund Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Definition gaming | Future Congresses could narrowly define "treaty-obligated program" to exclude programs | High |
| Commission capture | Political appointments could produce a commission that underestimates funding needs | Medium |
| Repeal risk | A future Congress could repeal the mandatory funding designation | High |
| Accounting manipulation | Agencies could claim existing spending satisfies treaty obligations through creative accounting | Medium |
| Implementation delay | Bureaucratic processes could delay actual fund disbursement | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Cannot bind future Congresses | Mandatory funding could be reclassified | Constitutional structure |
| Commission independence | Political pressure may affect Commission decisions | Appointment politics |
| Needs assessment complexity | Determining "full funding" is inherently difficult | Varying tribal needs and treaty provisions |
| Inter-tribal equity | Funding formulas may not equitably serve all 574 tribes | Varying treaty provisions and tribal circumstances |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Anti-circumvention clause**: Include language preventing reclassification of treaty-obligated programs without supermajority vote
2. **Independent inspector general**: Appoint an IG for the Commission to prevent capture
3. **Tribal appeals process**: Allow tribes to appeal Commission funding determinations
4. **Annual compliance audit**: Require GAO audit of fund disbursement against treaty obligations
5. **Automatic escalator**: Index funding to inflation and population growth to prevent erosion

### Federal Indian Treaty Protection Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Implicit abrogation | Congress could pass laws that effectively abrogate treaties without expressly stating intent | High |
| Procedural workaround | Future Congresses could repeal the supermajority requirement with a simple majority | Critical |
| Consultation as box-checking | Consultation requirements could be met pro forma without meaningful engagement | Medium |
| Narrow interpretation | Courts could interpret "abrogate, modify, or diminish" narrowly | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Non-entrenchment principle | One Congress cannot bind future Congresses | Constitutional structure |
| No constitutional backing | Statutory protections are inherently weaker than constitutional ones | Political feasibility vs. legal durability |
| Retroactivity gap | Does not address past abrogations | Political compromise |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Constitutional amendment**: Pursue the Treaty Rights Protection Amendment for permanent protection
2. **Broad interpretation clause**: Include legislative history and findings supporting expansive interpretation
3. **Meaningful consultation standards**: Define specific requirements for "meaningful" consultation, including tribal veto power for direct treaty modifications
4. **Restoration provision**: Include a process for tribes to petition for restoration of previously abrogated treaty provisions

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Federal agency resistance to compliance orders | Mandatory response requirements; judicial enforcement; appropriations consequences |
| Inter-agency coordination failures | Designate a White House-level treaty compliance coordinator |
| Data quality for needs assessment | Fund tribal data collection infrastructure |
| State government opposition | Federal preemption provisions; incentive structures through federal funding conditions |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- The Treaty Rights Enforcement Commission shall be reauthorized every 10 years
- Funding formulas shall be reviewed and updated every 5 years based on Commission recommendations
- Congress shall hold oversight hearings on treaty compliance annually
- GAO shall conduct a comprehensive review of treaty compliance every 5 years
