# Sacred Sites: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to stronger sacred site protections comes from a diverse coalition of economic interests, political ideologies, and institutional perspectives. Understanding the arguments, motivations, and strategies of opponents is essential for crafting reforms that can navigate political resistance while achieving meaningful protection.

## Major Opposition Groups

### 1. Extractive Industries

#### Mining Industry

**Key actors**: National Mining Association, American Exploration and Mining Association, Resolution Copper (Rio Tinto/BHP), Lithium Nevada (formerly Lithium Americas), Freeport-McMoRan

**Arguments**:

- Mining is essential for national security and the domestic supply chain for critical minerals
- Sacred site protections create regulatory uncertainty that deters investment
- Existing environmental review processes (NEPA, Section 106) already adequately address cultural concerns
- The 1872 Mining Law establishes a right to access minerals on federal land
- Transitioning to clean energy requires massive increases in domestic mineral production

**Underlying motivations**:

- Billions of dollars in mineral value at stake (Resolution Copper estimates $70+ billion in copper at Oak Flat)
- Investor and shareholder expectations for return on exploration investments
- Industry consolidation makes individual project outcomes more significant
- Critical minerals framing provides powerful political cover

**Strategies**:

- Lobbying for expedited permitting and reduced review timelines
- Attaching land exchanges and project authorizations to must-pass legislation (e.g., NDAA)
- Funding state and local advocacy for mining projects
- Framing opposition to mining as opposition to clean energy and national security

#### Oil and Gas Industry

**Key actors**: American Petroleum Institute, Independent Petroleum Association of America, various operators in the Permian Basin and Greater Chaco region

**Arguments**:

- Energy independence requires access to federal mineral resources
- Lease rights represent property interests that cannot be taken without compensation
- Sacred site claims are subjective and could be applied to any area to block development
- Federal leasing generates significant revenue for the Treasury

**Underlying motivations**:

- Protection of existing lease investments and future access to federal minerals
- Revenue preservation in a transitioning energy market
- Maintaining political influence in energy-producing states

### 2. Property Rights and Anti-Regulation Advocates

**Key actors**: Pacific Legal Foundation, Mountain States Legal Foundation, American Legislative Exchange Council (ALEC), various Wise Use movement organizations

**Arguments**:

- Sacred site protections constitute government overreach and regulatory taking
- Property owners have a right to develop their land regardless of cultural significance to others
- Federal land designations (monuments, withdrawals) remove land from productive use
- The Antiquities Act has been used beyond its original intent
- Sacred site claims are unfalsifiable and could be applied to unlimited areas

**Underlying motivations**:

- Philosophical commitment to limited government and private property rights
- Economic interests of ranchers, developers, and resource extractors
- Opposition to federal land ownership and management in Western states
- Strategic interest in limiting the regulatory state

**Strategies**:

- Litigation challenging monument designations and land withdrawals
- State legislation asserting state authority over federal lands
- Lobbying for transfer of federal lands to state or private ownership
- Public messaging framing protections as government "land grabs"

### 3. Western State Political Leaders

**Key actors**: Governors, state legislators, and congressional delegations from Utah, Nevada, Arizona, Wyoming, Montana, and other Western states with large federal landholdings

**Arguments**:

- Federal land designations override local decision-making
- State and local communities should control land use decisions
- Monument designations harm local economies dependent on ranching, mining, and energy
- Federal agencies are unresponsive to local needs

**Underlying motivations**:

- Political constituencies in resource-dependent communities
- State revenue interests (severance taxes, lease payments)
- Ideological commitment to federalism and state sovereignty
- Electoral dynamics in conservative-leaning Western states

**Case study -- Utah and Bears Ears**:

- Utah's congressional delegation unanimously opposed the original Bears Ears designation
- The state filed litigation challenging the monument
- Governor Cox and the legislature have continued to advocate for reduced protections
- The Utah delegation facilitated the 85% reduction under the Trump administration
- State officials argue that existing state protections are adequate

### 4. Recreational Interests

**Key actors**: Off-road vehicle associations, rock climbing organizations, mountain biking groups, recreational mining clubs

**Arguments**:

- Sacred site restrictions limit public access to public lands
- Recreation is a legitimate use of federal land
- Specific restrictions are unnecessary when general-use rules apply
- Access restrictions based on religious significance violate the Establishment Clause

**Underlying motivations**:

- Desire for unrestricted access to public lands
- Economic interests in recreation-dependent businesses and tourism
- Cultural attachment to outdoor recreation as an American value
- Resistance to being excluded from areas they have historically used

### 5. Development and Infrastructure Interests

**Key actors**: Construction and engineering firms, state departments of transportation, utility companies, telecommunications companies

**Arguments**:

- Infrastructure projects serve the public interest and cannot always avoid culturally significant areas
- Section 106 review already provides adequate cultural resource consideration
- Stronger protections would delay critical infrastructure improvements
- Mitigation measures can address most cultural concerns

**Underlying motivations**:

- Project timelines and cost control
- Contractual obligations and deadlines
- Avoiding precedent-setting delays that could affect future projects

## Common Arguments Against Stronger Protections

### The "Everywhere Is Sacred" Argument

**Claim**: If tribes can designate any area as sacred, they could effectively veto any development anywhere.

**Analysis**: This argument mischaracterizes sacred site claims. Sacred sites are specific places with documented cultural and spiritual significance, not arbitrary designations. The argument reflects a fundamental misunderstanding of Indigenous sacred geography and confuses the potential scope of sacred landscapes with the claim that all land is equally sacred. However, the concern about overbroad claims has influenced courts, as seen in the *Navajo Nation v. U.S. Forest Service* dissent.

### The Establishment Clause Argument

**Claim**: Protecting sacred sites based on their religious significance to specific groups amounts to government establishment of religion.

**Analysis**: Courts have generally rejected this argument. The protection of sacred sites does not establish a religion; it accommodates the exercise of existing religious practices. Analogies to the protection of historic churches and cemeteries -- which has never been considered an Establishment Clause violation -- demonstrate that cultural and religious site protection is a neutral, legitimate government interest.

### The Economic Necessity Argument

**Claim**: The economic value of resources at sacred sites is too great to forgo, particularly for critical minerals needed for clean energy.

**Analysis**: This argument treats sacred sites as obstacles to be overcome rather than values to be protected. It does not account for the costs of sacred site destruction to tribal communities. The framing assumes that extraction is the only path to economic value and ignores alternatives such as recycling, substitution, international sourcing, and the economic benefits of cultural tourism and landscape preservation.

### The Federal Overreach Argument

**Claim**: Sacred site protections represent federal government overreach, particularly when they restrict state and private land use.

**Analysis**: This argument has force regarding private land, where federal authority is indeed limited. However, on federal land -- which constitutes 28% of the U.S. land area and contains most of the sacred sites at issue -- the federal government has clear authority and, through its trust responsibility, a specific obligation to protect tribal interests. The argument also overlooks the fact that tribal sovereignty predates state and federal sovereignty.

### The Process Adequacy Argument

**Claim**: Existing laws (NHPA, NEPA, AIRFA, RFRA) already provide adequate consideration of sacred site concerns.

**Analysis**: The historical record demonstrates conclusively that existing processes do not prevent sacred site destruction. Section 106 is procedural only; AIRFA has no enforcement mechanism; RFRA has been interpreted narrowly in land-use contexts. The argument conflates process (which exists) with protection (which does not).

## Counter-Strategies for Reform Advocates

### Addressing Economic Arguments

- Quantify the economic value of cultural tourism and landscape preservation
- Document the costs of sacred site destruction (mental health, community disruption, cultural loss)
- Propose economic alternatives for resource-dependent communities
- Support domestic mineral recycling and supply chain diversification

### Addressing Legal Arguments

- Legislative fixes can address *Lyng* without raising Establishment Clause concerns
- RFRA amendments can clarify the substantial burden standard for sacred sites
- Co-management models demonstrate that protection and public access can coexist
- International law (UNDRIP) provides a framework for FPIC that is compatible with American legal principles

### Addressing Political Arguments

- Build bipartisan coalitions around religious freedom, which has cross-partisan appeal
- Frame sacred site protection as consistent with property rights (protecting tribal property rights)
- Engage local communities in the benefits of sacred site tourism and preservation
- Demonstrate that co-management creates local jobs and economic activity

### Building Unusual Alliances

- Religious freedom advocates (Becket Fund, First Liberty Institute) share the principle that government should not destroy places of worship
- Conservation organizations share the goal of protecting landscapes from extractive development
- Historic preservation advocates share the framework of protecting irreplaceable cultural resources
- Veterans and military families may connect with the honor and duty dimensions of treaty obligations
