# Sacred Sites: History

## Pre-Colonial Era

### Indigenous Sacred Landscapes

Before European contact, the North American continent contained an intricate geography of sacred places maintained by hundreds of distinct Indigenous nations. Sacred sites were not isolated locations but interconnected elements of living cultural landscapes:

- **Creation and origin sites**: Places where nations understood their people to have emerged or been created, forming the foundation of identity and cosmology
- **Ceremonial grounds**: Locations for seasonal ceremonies, vision quests, sundances, and other spiritual practices maintained across generations
- **Burial grounds**: Resting places of ancestors, often containing grave goods and maintained with specific protocols
- **Gathering places**: Sites for harvesting medicinal plants, ceremonial foods, and other culturally essential materials
- **Pilgrimage routes**: Paths connecting sacred locations across vast distances, used for spiritual journeys
- **Natural features**: Mountains, springs, caves, rock formations, rivers, and other geographic features understood as spiritually alive and powerful

### Characteristics of Indigenous Sacred Geography

- Sacred sites were not "owned" in the European property sense but were maintained through reciprocal relationships between people and place
- Knowledge of sacred sites was often restricted to specific individuals, clans, or societies within a nation
- Many sacred practices were place-specific: they could not be relocated to alternative sites
- Sacred landscapes often crossed the territories of multiple nations, requiring inter-tribal protocols for access and protection
- The concept of sacred sites encompassed entire ecosystems, not just discrete points on a map

## Colonial Era (1492-1776)

### Early Contact and Disruption

European colonization immediately threatened Indigenous sacred landscapes:

- **Spanish missions (1500s-1800s)**: Deliberately constructed on or near sacred sites to replace Indigenous religion with Christianity. Mission San Juan Capistrano and many California missions were built on village and ceremonial sites
- **English colonization**: Land clearing and settlement displaced tribes from sacred areas throughout the eastern seaboard
- **French relations**: Fur trade relationships sometimes preserved access to sacred sites, but expansion gradually restricted tribal territories
- **Doctrine of Discovery**: The papal bulls of 1452 and 1493, later adopted into American law in *Johnson v. M'Intosh* (1823), treated Indigenous land as available for European claim, with no recognition of sacred geography

### Religious Suppression

Colonial powers actively suppressed Indigenous religious practices:

- Spanish Inquisition extended to the Americas; Pueblo spiritual leaders were imprisoned and executed
- The Pueblo Revolt of 1680 was partly a response to religious persecution and desecration of sacred sites
- English colonial laws prohibited "idolatry" and "heathen" practices
- Conversion efforts systematically targeted the destruction of Indigenous spiritual systems

## Early American Period (1776-1871)

### Treaty Era

During the treaty-making period (1778-1871), some treaties recognized tribal rights to access sacred sites:

- Treaties occasionally reserved rights to hunt, fish, and gather at traditional locations, which implicitly included access to sacred sites
- The Treaty of Fort Laramie (1868) recognized the Black Hills (Paha Sapa) as part of the Great Sioux Reservation -- a deeply sacred landscape
- However, no treaties explicitly used the term "sacred sites" or created specific protections for religious places
- Treaty lands were subsequently reduced through unilateral congressional action, opening sacred areas to settlement and resource extraction

### Removal Era (1830-1850s)

The Indian Removal Act of 1830 and subsequent forced relocations severed tribes from sacred landscapes:

- The Cherokee, Muscogee (Creek), Seminole, Chickasaw, and Choctaw nations were removed from ancestral territories containing thousands of sacred sites
- Removed tribes lost access to burial grounds, ceremonial sites, and sacred geographic features
- The Trail of Tears and other removal marches themselves became sacred places of memory and mourning
- Sacred sites left behind were developed, plowed, looted, and destroyed with no legal recourse

### Gold Rush and Western Expansion

The California Gold Rush (1849) and subsequent westward expansion devastated sacred sites throughout the West:

- Mining operations destroyed sacred rivers, mountains, and gathering sites
- California's Indigenous population declined from approximately 150,000 in 1848 to 30,000 by 1870, partly due to the destruction of culturally essential landscapes
- The Homestead Act (1862) opened sacred lands to individual settlement claims

## Allotment and Assimilation Era (1871-1934)

### The Dawes Act (1887)

The General Allotment Act fundamentally altered the relationship between tribes and sacred lands:

- Communally held reservation lands were divided into individual allotments of 80-160 acres
- "Surplus" lands after allotment were opened to non-Indian settlement
- Tribes lost approximately 90 million acres (nearly two-thirds of their remaining land base) between 1887 and 1934
- Sacred sites on unallotted "surplus" land passed out of tribal control entirely
- Fractionated ownership made communal management of sacred landscapes nearly impossible

### Religious Suppression Policies

The federal government actively banned Indigenous religious practices:

- **Code of Indian Offenses (1883)**: Commissioner of Indian Affairs Hiram Price established Courts of Indian Offenses that criminalized sundances, "medicine men" practices, and other ceremonial activities
- **Penalties**: Practitioners faced imprisonment and loss of rations
- **Boarding schools (1879-1960s)**: Children were forcibly removed from communities, severing transmission of knowledge about sacred sites and spiritual practices
- **Sun Dance ban**: Explicitly prohibited from 1883 until 1934 in most jurisdictions
- These policies targeted the cultural knowledge necessary to identify, maintain, and protect sacred sites

### The Black Hills (Paha Sapa)

The seizure of the Black Hills exemplifies sacred site destruction during this era:

- The 1868 Treaty of Fort Laramie recognized the Black Hills as part of the Great Sioux Reservation
- Following the discovery of gold in 1874, the federal government unilaterally seized the Black Hills in the Act of February 28, 1877
- The Black Hills are central to Lakota, Dakota, and Nakota spiritual practice and cosmology
- The Supreme Court ruled in *United States v. Sioux Nation* (1980) that the taking was illegal and awarded $105 million in compensation, but the Sioux have refused the money (now over $1 billion with interest), demanding the return of the land
- Mount Rushmore (1927-1941) was carved into sacred granite formations, compounding the desecration

## Indian Reorganization and Termination (1934-1968)

### Indian Reorganization Act (1934)

The IRA ended allotment and partially reversed assimilation policies:

- Land loss was halted, and some lands were returned to tribal control
- Religious freedom was partially restored as the Code of Indian Offenses was gradually relaxed
- However, no specific provisions addressed sacred site protection
- The IRA's tribal government structures sometimes created internal conflicts over sacred site management

### Termination Era (1953-1968)

The termination policy dissolved the government-to-government relationship with over 100 tribes:

- Terminated tribes lost all federal recognition and associated land protections
- Sacred sites on terminated reservation lands became subject to state law with no tribal protections
- The Menominee, Klamath, and dozens of other tribes lost access to sacred landscapes
- Public Law 280 (1953) transferred jurisdiction to states in several states, further marginalizing tribal authority over sacred places

### Dam Construction

Federal dam projects flooded extensive sacred landscapes:

- **Pick-Sloan Missouri Basin Program (1944-1966)**: Flooded over 200,000 acres of the most productive and culturally significant lands of the Mandan, Hidatsa, Arikara, Standing Rock Sioux, and other tribes along the Missouri River
- **Kinzua Dam (1965)**: Flooded 10,000 acres of Seneca Nation land, including the grave of Cornplanter and ceremonial sites, despite the 1794 Treaty of Canandaigua
- **Glen Canyon Dam (1963)**: Submerged sacred sites of the Navajo, Hopi, and other tribes under Lake Powell
- These projects proceeded with no consultation, no consent, and no recognition of sacred site impacts

## Religious Freedom and Legal Development (1968-2000)

### American Indian Religious Freedom Act (1978)

AIRFA represented the first federal acknowledgment of Native religious rights:

- Directed federal agencies to evaluate their policies for impacts on Native religious practices
- Recognized the right to access sacred sites, use peyote, and possess sacred objects
- However, the Act contained no enforcement mechanism and created no judicially enforceable rights
- Courts quickly rendered it toothless: *Badoni v. Higginson* (1980), *Crow v. Gullet* (1983), and ultimately *Lyng* (1988)

### *Lyng v. Northwest Indian Cemetery Protective Association* (1988)

The Supreme Court's decision in *Lyng* was the single most damaging ruling for sacred site protection:

- The U.S. Forest Service proposed building a road through the "high country" of the Six Rivers National Forest in northern California, an area central to the spiritual practices of the Yurok, Karuk, and Tolowa peoples
- The Forest Service's own study found the road would "virtually destroy" the ability to practice religion in the area
- The Court held 5-3 that the government's land management decisions, even when they effectively destroyed sacred sites, did not violate the Free Exercise Clause because they did not coerce religious practice or penalize religious belief
- Justice Brennan's dissent warned the decision would leave sacred sites "at the mercy of land managers"
- *Lyng* remains controlling precedent and has effectively foreclosed First Amendment challenges to sacred site destruction

### Archaeological Resources Protection Act (1979)

ARPA strengthened protections for archaeological sites on federal and tribal land:

- Criminalized unauthorized excavation, removal, or damage to archaeological resources
- Required permits for excavation on federal land
- Required tribal consultation for permits on tribal land
- However, ARPA focused on archaeological rather than sacred value and did not protect living sacred landscapes

### Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act (1990)

NAGPRA addressed the specific issue of burial sites and cultural items:

- Required federal agencies and museums to inventory Native American remains and cultural items
- Established a process for repatriation to affiliated tribes
- Criminalized trafficking in Native remains and cultural items
- Protected burial sites on federal and tribal land from unauthorized excavation
- Significant limitation: did not apply to private land and left enforcement primarily to tribes

### Religious Freedom Restoration Act (1993)

RFRA was enacted partly in response to *Employment Division v. Smith* (1990):

- Required the government to demonstrate a compelling interest and use the least restrictive means before substantially burdening religious exercise
- Initially applied to both federal and state governments; *City of Boerne v. Flores* (1997) limited it to federal actions
- Sacred site advocates hoped RFRA would provide stronger protection than AIRFA
- The Ninth Circuit's en banc decision in *Navajo Nation v. U.S. Forest Service* (2008) dashed those hopes, holding that making recycled wastewater snow on the sacred San Francisco Peaks did not substantially burden Navajo religion

### Executive Order 13007 (1996)

President Clinton's EO 13007 directed federal agencies to:

- Accommodate access to and ceremonial use of Indian sacred sites on federal lands
- Avoid adversely affecting the physical integrity of sacred sites
- Maintain the confidentiality of sacred site information

The order was purely advisory, created no enforceable rights, and agencies were permitted to decline protection for "compelling government interests."

## Modern Era (2000-Present)

### Bears Ears National Monument

Bears Ears represents the most significant sacred site protection effort of the modern era:

- **2016**: President Obama designated Bears Ears National Monument (1.35 million acres) in Utah, following a campaign led by the Bears Ears Inter-Tribal Coalition representing five tribes
- **2017**: President Trump reduced the monument by 85% to approximately 201,876 acres, exposing sacred sites to potential development
- **2021**: President Biden restored and expanded the monument to approximately 1.36 million acres
- The monument's political vulnerability -- being drastically reduced and then restored by successive presidents -- demonstrated the fragility of executive-action-based protections

### Standing Rock and the Dakota Access Pipeline (2016-2017)

The Dakota Access Pipeline (DAPL) conflict brought sacred site issues to national attention:

- The pipeline route crossed near the Standing Rock Sioux Tribe's sacred sites and water source at Lake Oahe
- The Army Corps of Engineers' Section 106 consultation was challenged as inadequate
- Thousands of Water Protectors gathered in opposition, resulting in a major confrontation
- Courts found NEPA deficiencies but allowed the pipeline to operate while additional review was completed
- The episode highlighted the gap between consultation requirements and meaningful protection

### Oak Flat and *Apache Stronghold*

The ongoing fight over Oak Flat (Chi'chil Bildagoteel) represents the current frontier of sacred site law:

- The 2014 NDAA land exchange bypassed normal environmental and cultural review
- Apache Stronghold's RFRA challenge failed in the Ninth Circuit (2022), which held that *Lyng* controlled
- The Supreme Court's denial of certiorari in 2024 left the *Lyng* framework intact
- The case has galvanized advocacy for legislative reform

### Thacker Pass and Renewable Energy Conflicts

The emergence of renewable energy development has created new threats:

- Lithium, cobalt, and other minerals essential for batteries and clean energy technology are often found in areas sacred to Indigenous peoples
- The Thacker Pass project in Nevada highlighted the tension between climate goals and sacred site protection
- Environmental justice advocates have argued that Indigenous communities should not bear disproportionate costs of the energy transition

## Patterns and Lessons

### Recurring Themes

1. **Procedural rights without substantive protection**: Every major legal development has created process requirements (consultation, review, assessment) without prohibiting the destruction of sacred sites
2. **Executive vulnerability**: Protections established by executive action (monuments, withdrawals, executive orders) can be reversed by subsequent administrations
3. **Religious freedom doctrines do not apply**: Courts have consistently held that government destruction of sacred places does not burden religious freedom in a constitutionally cognizable way
4. **Economic interests prevail**: When sacred sites conflict with mining, energy, or infrastructure development, economic interests have prevailed in virtually every case
5. **Knowledge loss compounds**: Boarding schools, religious suppression, and displacement destroyed the cultural knowledge necessary to identify and advocate for sacred site protection
