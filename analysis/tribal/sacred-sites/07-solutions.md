# Sacred Sites: Solutions

## Overview

Effective sacred site protection requires action across multiple domains: legislative reform to close the legal gap created by *Lyng*, administrative improvements to strengthen consultation and co-management, judicial strategies to develop more protective case law, and community-based approaches to preserve sacred site knowledge. No single reform is sufficient; a comprehensive strategy must address the interconnected root causes identified in the analysis.

## Legislative Solutions

### 1. Sacred Sites Protection Act

**Problem addressed**: No federal law provides enforceable, substantive protections for sacred sites on federal land.

**Proposal**: Enact comprehensive federal legislation that:

- Establishes a rebuttable presumption against federal actions that would substantially harm documented sacred sites
- Creates a confidential sacred site registry administered by a tribal-majority board
- Requires Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) from affected tribes before federal actions that would damage or destroy registered sacred sites
- Provides a right of action for tribes to challenge sacred site destruction in federal court
- Codifies and strengthens Executive Order 13007's access and protection directives
- Establishes criminal penalties for intentional desecration of registered sacred sites

**Evidence of effectiveness**: New Zealand's *Te Awa Tupua (Whanganui River Claims Settlement) Act 2017* and Australia's *Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Heritage Protection Act 1984* demonstrate that enforceable sacred site protections are legally and practically workable in common-law systems.

### 2. RFRA Amendment for Sacred Sites

**Problem addressed**: Courts have interpreted the "substantial burden" standard under RFRA to exclude government destruction of sacred sites.

**Proposal**: Amend RFRA to clarify that:

- Government actions that damage, destroy, or substantially impair access to documented sacred sites constitute a "substantial burden" on religious exercise
- The analysis does not require a finding of government coercion or penalty
- Sacred site claims may be brought by tribal nations as well as individuals
- Confidential treatment of sacred site information does not defeat a RFRA claim

**Rationale**: This approach works within the existing RFRA framework, which already commands bipartisan support and has been applied robustly in other religious freedom contexts (*Burwell v. Hobby Lobby*, 2014).

### 3. Repeal of the Resolution Copper Land Exchange

**Problem addressed**: Section 3003 of the FY 2015 NDAA mandated a land exchange that will destroy Oak Flat (Chi'chil Bildagoteel).

**Proposal**: Repeal Section 3003 and designate Oak Flat as a protected area. Multiple bills have been introduced, including the Save Oak Flat Act (introduced in multiple sessions).

### 4. 1872 Mining Law Reform

**Problem addressed**: The General Mining Law allows private mining claims on federal land with minimal cultural resource review.

**Proposal**: Reform the 1872 Mining Law to:

- Require cultural resource survey and tribal consultation before claims are validated
- Establish a royalty system that generates revenue for sacred site protection
- Allow administrative withdrawal of areas containing sacred sites from mineral entry
- Eliminate the "patenting" provision that allows private ownership of public mineral lands

### 5. NAGPRA Strengthening

**Problem addressed**: Incomplete compliance with NAGPRA and gaps in burial site protection.

**Proposal**: Strengthen NAGPRA by:

- Establishing enforceable deadlines for institutional compliance with inventory and repatriation requirements
- Increasing penalties for non-compliance
- Extending protections to private land where burial sites are discovered during development
- Increasing NAGPRA grant funding to $50 million annually (from current $3 million)
- Requiring proactive surveys on federal land before ground-disturbing activities

## Administrative Solutions

### 6. Government-Wide Sacred Sites Policy

**Problem addressed**: Fragmented agency approaches to sacred site protection.

**Proposal**: Issue an executive order or presidential memorandum that:

- Establishes uniform sacred sites consultation and protection standards across all federal agencies
- Requires each agency to designate a senior-level Sacred Sites Coordinator
- Mandates pre-decisional consultation (before alternatives are developed)
- Requires agencies to explain in writing any decision that overrides tribal sacred site concerns
- Directs the development of confidential sacred site documentation protocols
- Requires annual reporting on sacred site actions and consultation outcomes

### 7. Strengthened Consultation Standards

**Problem addressed**: Consultation is often perfunctory and occurs too late to influence decisions.

**Proposal**:

- **Timing**: Consultation must begin before project alternatives are developed, not after a preferred alternative is selected
- **Level**: Consultation must involve senior decision-makers, not only staff-level cultural resource specialists
- **Duration**: Adequate time must be provided for tribal internal deliberation, including across multiple tribal councils
- **Information**: Full project information, alternatives, and environmental data must be shared with consulting tribes
- **Outcomes**: Agencies must document how tribal concerns were addressed or explain why they were not adopted
- **Follow-up**: Consultation must continue through project implementation, not end at the decision point

### 8. Expanded Co-Management

**Problem addressed**: Tribes lack management authority over federal lands containing sacred sites.

**Proposal**: Expand co-management by:

- Establishing co-management agreements for all national monuments and conservation areas designated for cultural resource protection
- Creating a formal framework for shared decision-making (not merely advisory participation)
- Integrating Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) into land management planning
- Funding tribal co-management capacity through dedicated appropriations
- Using Bears Ears as a model while addressing its limitations (tribal advisory authority should be upgraded to co-decision-making authority)

**Models**: The Kativik Environmental Quality Commission (Canada) and the Kakadu National Park Board of Management (Australia) provide examples of formal co-management with Indigenous decision-making authority.

### 9. Expanded Use of the Antiquities Act and Administrative Withdrawals

**Problem addressed**: Sacred landscapes need protection from resource extraction.

**Proposal**:

- Use the Antiquities Act to designate national monuments protecting threatened sacred landscapes
- Issue 20-year mineral withdrawals for areas under review for permanent protection
- Pursue legislative codification of monument boundaries to insulate against future presidential action
- Prioritize tribal-initiated monument proposals

**Limitations**: Monument designations and withdrawals are executive actions vulnerable to reversal by subsequent administrations, as demonstrated by the Bears Ears reduction. Legislative codification is essential for permanence.

### 10. THPO Capacity Building

**Problem addressed**: Tribal Historic Preservation Offices are chronically underfunded.

**Proposal**:

- Increase annual THPO funding from $20 million to $100 million
- Establish a sacred site survey grant program ($25 million annually)
- Fund tribal cultural resource professional development and training
- Support inter-tribal coordination for sacred sites spanning multiple tribal territories
- Provide technology and GIS tools for confidential sacred site documentation

## Legal and Judicial Solutions

### 11. Test Case Litigation Strategy

**Problem addressed**: *Lyng* and subsequent case law provide no judicial protection for sacred sites.

**Proposal**: Develop a coordinated litigation strategy to:

- Build factual records that demonstrate the devastating impact of sacred site destruction
- Present sacred site claims under multiple legal theories simultaneously (RFRA, NHPA, NEPA, trust responsibility, treaty rights)
- Pursue cases in circuits that may be receptive to distinguishing or limiting *Lyng*
- Coordinate with amicus strategies from religious liberty, environmental, and civil rights organizations
- Build the record for potential Supreme Court reconsideration of *Lyng*

### 12. Treaty-Based Protection

**Problem addressed**: Treaty-reserved rights to access and use sacred sites are underutilized.

**Proposal**:

- Identify treaty provisions that protect access to sacred sites (including reserved rights to hunt, fish, gather, and travel through ceded territories)
- Bring treaty-based claims alongside or instead of religious freedom claims
- Argue that the destruction of sacred sites within ceded territories violates treaty-reserved rights
- Build on the *McGirt v. Oklahoma* (2020) framework for recognizing that treaty promises remain enforceable

## Community-Based Solutions

### 13. Sacred Site Knowledge Preservation

**Problem addressed**: Boarding school policies, removal, and language loss have damaged transmission of sacred site knowledge.

**Proposal**:

- Fund tribally controlled programs for documenting sacred site knowledge (with appropriate confidentiality protocols)
- Support intergenerational knowledge transfer programs pairing elders with youth
- Invest in language revitalization as inseparable from sacred site knowledge
- Develop culturally appropriate archival methods controlled by tribal communities

### 14. Inter-Tribal Coordination

**Problem addressed**: Many sacred sites are significant to multiple tribes, requiring coordinated advocacy.

**Proposal**:

- Support inter-tribal coalitions modeled on the Bears Ears Inter-Tribal Coalition
- Establish inter-tribal sacred site monitoring networks
- Create shared databases (with appropriate access controls) for sacred sites spanning tribal territories
- Develop coordinated advocacy strategies for shared sacred landscapes

### 15. Public Education and Alliance Building

**Problem addressed**: Public understanding of sacred site issues remains limited.

**Proposal**:

- Develop public education campaigns that explain sacred site issues within the broader framework of religious freedom and cultural heritage
- Partner with religious communities of all faiths on shared religious freedom principles
- Engage conservation and environmental organizations as allies
- Support Indigenous-led storytelling and media projects about sacred sites
- Build relationships with the historic preservation community
