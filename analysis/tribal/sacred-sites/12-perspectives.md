# Sacred Sites: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes sacred site protection through nine distinct political perspectives, evaluating current state assessments, root cause analyses, proposed solutions, and legislation. Each perspective receives engagement consistency ratings and position scores, culminating in compromise proposals that bridge multiple viewpoints.

## Perspective Definitions

| Perspective | Core Values | Policy Orientation |
|-------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Tradition, limited government, individual responsibility | Incremental change, fiscal restraint, market solutions |
| Liberal | Progress, equality, social responsibility | Government as problem-solver, regulation, safety nets |
| Progressive | Systemic change, economic justice, structural reform | Transformative reform, wealth redistribution, worker power |
| Libertarian | Individual liberty, minimal state, free markets | Deregulation, privatization, voluntary association |
| Constitutionalist | Original intent, enumerated powers, federalism | Strict constitutional limits, states' rights |
| Populist | Anti-elite, pro-worker, economic nationalism | Trade protection, anti-monopoly, immigration restriction |
| Centrist | Pragmatism, compromise, incremental progress | Bipartisan solutions, evidence-based policy |
| Religious Right | Faith-based values, traditional morality | Social conservatism, religious liberty |
| Democratic Socialist | Worker ownership, public goods, democratic control | Public investment, labor rights, universal programs |

## Perspective Analyses

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 3/5 - Mixed Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | *Stated: property rights and limited government. Actual: often aligned with extractive industry interests that support the party financially* |
| Principle consistency | *Property rights principles are applied inconsistently -- tribal property and treaty rights receive less vigorous defense than non-tribal property rights* |
| Goalpost stability | *Positions are moderately stable but shift when religious freedom framing is emphasized* |
| Zero-sum behavior | *Some tendency to view sacred site protections as zero-sum with economic development* |

**Justification:** Conservatives apply property rights and religious freedom principles inconsistently when tribal interests are involved. The same property rights arguments used to defend private landowners are rarely deployed for tribal land and treaty rights. However, the religious freedom dimension creates genuine internal tension, as conservative religious freedom principles logically extend to Indigenous sacred site claims.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- *Republican members have supported RFRA broadly but not sponsored sacred site amendments to RFRA*
- *Conservative media coverage of Oak Flat has been limited compared to other religious freedom cases (e.g., Hobby Lobby, Masterpiece Cakeshop)*
- *Western Republican delegations have consistently opposed Bears Ears and Chaco protections*

#### Position Scores

| Dimension | Score | Reasoning |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Current State Assessment | 4/10 | *Largely acknowledge factual conditions but underweight the severity of sacred site destruction and attribute it to different causes* |
| Root Cause Agreement | 3/10 | *Reject the analysis of property law and regulatory frameworks as root causes; attribute problems to federal overreach rather than insufficient protection* |

**Source references:**

- *Heritage Foundation position papers on federal land management emphasize state control over tribal co-management*
- *National Review editorials have framed Bears Ears as federal overreach rather than sacred site protection*

#### Solution Evaluations

| Solution | Score | Stated Position | Underlying Concerns |
|----------|-------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Sacred Sites Protection Act | 2/10 | *Excessive regulation; creates tribal veto over development* | *Impact on extractive industry campaign donors; precedent for expanded federal regulation* |
| RFRA Sacred Sites Amendment | 5/10 | *Religious freedom is important but RFRA should not be expanded to land use* | *Genuine tension between religious freedom commitment and industry alliance* |
| Co-management expansion | 3/10 | *Federal land should be returned to states, not co-managed with tribes* | *Preference for state control over federal land; skepticism of tribal governance capacity* |
| THPO funding increase | 4/10 | *Fiscal responsibility requires restraint in new spending* | *Not a priority spending area; limited political constituency benefit* |

#### Legislation Evaluations

| Bill | Score | Stated Position | Underlying Concerns |
|------|-------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Sacred Sites Protection Act | 2/10 | *Regulatory overreach; would harm economic development* | *Industry opposition; anti-regulation ideology* |
| Save Oak Flat Act | 3/10 | *Repealing a lawful land exchange undermines property rights and investment certainty* | *Mining industry support; Arizona political dynamics* |
| RFRA Sacred Sites Clarification | 4/10 | *RFRA should be maintained as written; targeted amendments risk unintended consequences* | *Discomfort with applying RFRA to cases that benefit non-Christian religious practice* |

#### Alternative Proposals

Conservatives would prefer:

- Voluntary partnerships between industry and tribes to minimize impacts
- State-level management of sacred sites rather than federal regulation
- Market-based solutions such as conservation easements purchased by willing parties
- Limiting federal land ownership through transfer to states

#### Coalition Potential

- **Natural allies:** Libertarian (property rights), Constitutionalist (treaty obligations as binding law)
- **Potential bridges:** Religious Right (shared religious freedom principles could bridge to sacred site support)
- **Unlikely partners:** Progressive, Democratic Socialist

### Libertarian Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | *Genuine commitment to individual liberty and religious freedom; principled opposition to government overreach* |

| Goalpost stability | *Stable positions on principles; flexibility on application* |
| Zero-sum behavior | *Generally oppose government intervention but acknowledge government as the problem when it authorizes destruction of others' sacred sites* |

**Justification:** Libertarians face a genuine dilemma on sacred sites. Their property rights and religious freedom principles support tribal claims, but their opposition to regulation and government land management creates tension. The most consistent libertarian position would support tribal property rights and oppose government facilitation of sacred site destruction.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- *Cato Institute has published pieces critical of the 1872 Mining Law as corporate welfare*
- *Libertarian scholars have argued that tribal sovereignty is consistent with libertarian principles*
- *Reason magazine has covered sacred site issues with some sympathy for tribal property claims*

| Root Cause Agreement | 4/10 | *Agree that government plenary power and property law frameworks are problematic but propose different solutions (privatization vs. regulation)* |

**Source references:**

- *Cato Institute papers on federal land policy and tribal sovereignty*
- *Reason Foundation analysis of 1872 Mining Law as crony capitalism*

#### Solution Evaluations

| Solution | Score | Stated Position | Underlying Concerns |
|----------|-------|-----------------|---------------------|

| 1872 Mining Law reform | 5/10 | *Ending subsidized mining on public land is consistent with free-market principles* | *Oppose replacing one regulatory regime with another* |

#### Legislation Evaluations

| RFRA Sacred Sites Clarification | 6/10 | *RFRA should apply robustly to all religious exercise* | *Genuinely supportive of religious freedom dimension* |

#### Alternative Proposals

Libertarians would prefer:

- Transfer federal land containing sacred sites to tribal ownership (land back through property rights)
- Privatize federal lands generally, with tribal nations given first right of purchase for sacred sites
- Eliminate subsidies and special access for extractive industries (1872 Mining Law reform)
- Rely on property rights and voluntary agreements rather than regulatory mandates

#### Coalition Potential

- **Natural allies:** Constitutionalist (treaty obligations), Conservative (religious freedom dimension)

## Compromise Proposals

### Compromise 1: *Religious Freedom Restoration for Sacred Sites*

**Bridges:** Liberal + Conservative + Religious Right + Libertarian + Constitutionalist + Centrist

**Core Agreement:** Religious freedom is a foundational American value, and government should not be permitted to destroy places of worship -- regardless of the faith tradition.

**Policy Description:**

Amend RFRA to clarify that destruction of documented sacred sites constitutes a substantial burden on religious exercise. This does not prohibit all actions affecting sacred sites; it requires the government to demonstrate a compelling interest and use the least restrictive means. Pair with strengthened confidentiality protections and increased THPO funding ($50 million annually).

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Conservative | Framed as religious freedom, not regulation; builds on bipartisan RFRA |
| Liberal | Provides enforceable legal protection for sacred sites |
| Religious Right | Strengthens RFRA framework for all faiths; protects places of worship |
| Libertarian | Limits government power to destroy religious property |
| Constitutionalist | Exercises Congress's enumerated power; restores RFRA's original purpose |
| Centrist | Pragmatic, targeted reform with bipartisan track record |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Accept some limitation on federal land management discretion |
| Liberal | Accept RFRA framework rather than comprehensive legislation |
| Progressive | Accept incremental reform rather than structural change |
| Religious Right | Accept equal treatment of Indigenous and Christian religious freedom claims |
| Libertarian | Accept a legislative rather than market-based approach |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 7/9 perspectives (Conservative at 5, Liberal 8, Libertarian 6, Constitutionalist 6, Centrist 7, Religious Right 7, Democratic Socialist 6)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 5/7 coalition members rated 4+ consistency
- **Durability:** Strong; religious freedom has sustained bipartisan support for decades
- **Implementation Path:** Introduction in Senate Indian Affairs Committee with bipartisan sponsors; religious freedom coalition lobbying; floor vote within 2 sessions

### Compromise 3: *Federal Lands Sacred Sites Co-Management Program*

**Bridges:** Liberal + Centrist + Constitutionalist + Progressive + Democratic Socialist

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The RFRA Sacred Sites Amendment commands the broadest coalition (7/9 perspectives), making it the most viable legislative pathway. The religious freedom framing transcends traditional left-right divisions and allows conservatives and the Religious Right to support sacred site protections without abandoning their broader ideological commitments.

### Key Obstacles

- **Extractive industry lobbying** is the single greatest obstacle, cutting across multiple perspectives and concentrating opposition in key Western congressional delegations
- **The *Lyng* precedent** provides courts with a framework for rejecting sacred site claims even under strengthened statutory protections
- **Populist inconsistency** (rated 2/5) means this constituency is unreliable and responsive to framing rather than principle
- **Conservative mixed consistency** (rated 3/5) means industry alignment may override religious freedom principles in practice

### Low Consistency Partners

- **Populists** (2/5): Frame sacred site protection as opposing foreign corporate exploitation of American resources. Economic nationalism and anti-elite messaging may mobilize populist support on a case-by-case basis, but sustained engagement is unlikely.
- **Conservatives** (3/5): Religious freedom framing is the key lever. Engage through Religious Right intermediaries and emphasize that weakening religious freedom for one faith weakens it for all faiths.

### Recommended Approach

1. Lead with the RFRA Sacred Sites Amendment as the primary legislative vehicle, leveraging the religious freedom coalition
2. Simultaneously pursue the Save Oak Flat Act as a high-profile, concrete case that builds public awareness
3. Use administrative actions (co-management, mineral withdrawals, strengthened consultation) to build institutional capacity and demonstrate effectiveness
4. Build toward comprehensive Sacred Sites Protection Act as the long-term goal, using RFRA amendment and co-management success as proof of concept
5. Invest in public education and inter-faith coalition building to shift the political environment
