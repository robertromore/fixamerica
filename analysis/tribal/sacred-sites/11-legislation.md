# Sacred Sites: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative landscape for sacred site protection is defined by a fundamental gap: no federal law provides enforceable, substantive protections against the destruction of sacred sites. Existing statutes create procedural requirements (consultation, review, assessment) but allow agencies and Congress to authorize sacred site destruction regardless of the outcome of those processes. The proposals below address this gap through multiple legislative strategies, from targeted amendments to existing law to comprehensive new legislation.

## Constitutional Amendments

No constitutional amendment is proposed for sacred site protection specifically. The First Amendment's Free Exercise Clause should, in principle, protect Indigenous sacred sites, but the Supreme Court's interpretation in *Lyng v. Northwest Indian Cemetery Protective Association* (1988) has foreclosed this avenue. Legislative solutions that override *Lyng* are more feasible than constitutional amendment and can be crafted to avoid Establishment Clause concerns.

## Federal Legislation

### Sacred Sites Protection Act

**Purpose**: Establish enforceable federal protections for Indigenous sacred sites on federal and tribal land, create a confidential sacred site registry, and require tribal consent before federal actions that would damage or destroy registered sacred sites.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Sacred Sites Protection Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSES.

(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that—
    (1) Indigenous peoples of the United States maintain profound
    spiritual, cultural, and historical connections to specific
    places on the landscape;
    (2) sacred sites are essential to the continuation of
    Indigenous religious practices, cultural identity, and
    community well-being;
    (3) existing federal law does not provide enforceable
    protections against the destruction of sacred sites;
    (4) the Supreme Court's decision in Lyng v. Northwest Indian
    Cemetery Protective Association, 485 U.S. 439 (1988), held
    that the Free Exercise Clause does not prevent the government
    from authorizing destruction of sacred sites;
    (5) the United States has a trust responsibility to protect
    the cultural and religious heritage of tribal nations; and
    (6) the destruction of sacred sites causes irreparable harm
    to Indigenous communities and constitutes a violation of
    fundamental principles of religious freedom.

(b) PURPOSES.—The purposes of this Act are to—
    (1) establish enforceable protections for sacred sites on
    federal land;
    (2) create a confidential process for identifying and
    registering sacred sites;
    (3) require Free, Prior, and Informed Consent from affected
    tribal nations before federal actions that would damage or
    destroy registered sacred sites;
    (4) provide a right of action for tribal nations to
    challenge sacred site destruction in federal court; and
    (5) strengthen tribal participation in the management of
    federal lands containing sacred sites.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
(a) SACRED SITE.—The term "sacred site" means any specific,
discrete, narrowly delineated location on federal land that is
identified by an Indian tribe or Native Hawaiian organization as
sacred by virtue of its established religious or cultural
significance to, or ceremonial use by, an Indian tribe or Native
Hawaiian organization.
(b) INDIAN TRIBE.—The term "Indian tribe" has the meaning given
in section 4 of the Indian Self-Determination and Education
Assistance Act (25 U.S.C. 5304).
(c) REGISTERED SACRED SITE.—The term "registered sacred site"
means a sacred site that has been entered in the Sacred Sites
Registry established under section 5.
(d) FEDERAL LAND.—The term "federal land" means any land or
interest in land owned by the United States, including land
managed by the Bureau of Land Management, Forest Service, National
Park Service, Bureau of Reclamation, U.S. Army Corps of Engineers,
Department of Defense, and any other federal agency.
(e) FREE, PRIOR, AND INFORMED CONSENT.—The term "Free, Prior,
and Informed Consent" means the collective affirmative agreement of
an affected Indian tribe, obtained through culturally appropriate
consultation processes, before the initiation of any federal action
that would substantially affect a registered sacred site.

SEC. 4. PROHIBITION ON DESTRUCTION OF SACRED SITES.

(a) IN GENERAL.—No federal agency shall authorize, fund, carry
out, or permit any action that would—
    (1) destroy or substantially damage the physical integrity
    of a registered sacred site;
    (2) substantially impair access to a registered sacred site
    for traditional religious or cultural purposes; or
    (3) introduce elements to a registered sacred site that
    would substantially diminish its sacred character;
unless the affected Indian tribe or tribes have provided Free,
Prior, and Informed Consent to the action.

(b) APPLICABILITY.—This section applies to—
    (1) all federal agencies;
    (2) all actions on federal land requiring federal
    authorization, funding, or permitting; and
    (3) all land exchanges, disposals, or transfers of federal
    land containing registered sacred sites.

(c) EXCEPTION.—The prohibition in subsection (a) may be waived
only upon a finding by the President that a specific action is
necessary to prevent imminent threat to human life or national
security, and only to the minimum extent necessary to address such
threat.

SEC. 5. SACRED SITES REGISTRY.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary of the Interior shall establish
a Sacred Sites Registry within 180 days of enactment.

(b) ADMINISTRATION.—The Registry shall be administered by the
Sacred Sites Commission established under section 8.

(c) REGISTRATION PROCESS.—
    (1) Any Indian tribe may submit a nomination for
    registration of a sacred site.
    (2) Nominations shall include such information as the
    Commission determines necessary to evaluate the nomination,
    provided that the Commission shall not require disclosure
    of information that the nominating tribe considers
    culturally restricted.
    (3) The Commission shall act on nominations within 180 days.
    (4) Registration shall be granted upon a finding that the
    site has established religious or cultural significance
    to the nominating tribe.

(d) CONFIDENTIALITY.—
    (1) All information submitted in connection with a
    registration nomination shall be confidential.
    (2) The location and description of registered sacred sites
    shall not be disclosed to any person outside the Commission,
    the affected tribe, and the land management agency with
    jurisdiction, except as necessary to implement this Act.
    (3) Information in the Registry shall be exempt from
    disclosure under section 552 of title 5, United States Code
    (Freedom of Information Act).
    (4) Any person who knowingly discloses confidential Registry
    information shall be subject to penalties under section 7.

SEC. 6. CONSULTATION AND CONSENT.

(a) PRE-DECISIONAL CONSULTATION.—Before any federal agency
develops alternatives for an action that may affect a registered
sacred site, the agency shall initiate consultation with the
affected tribe or tribes.

(b) CONSENT PROCESS.—
    (1) Consultation shall be conducted through culturally
    appropriate processes determined in coordination with the
    affected tribe.
    (2) Adequate time shall be provided for tribal internal
    deliberation, including consideration by tribal councils
    and traditional authorities.
    (3) Full project information, alternatives, and
    environmental data shall be shared with the affected tribe.
    (4) Consent shall be documented in writing signed by
    authorized tribal officials.

SEC. 7. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) RIGHT OF ACTION.—Any Indian tribe may bring a civil action
in United States district court to enforce the provisions of
this Act.

(b) INJUNCTIVE RELIEF.—The court may issue temporary restraining
orders, preliminary injunctions, and permanent injunctions to
prevent violations of this Act.

(c) CRIMINAL PENALTIES.—
    (1) Any person who knowingly destroys, damages, or desecrates
    a registered sacred site shall be fined not more than
    $250,000 or imprisoned not more than 2 years, or both.
    (2) Any person who knowingly discloses confidential Registry
    information in violation of section 5(d) shall be fined
    not more than $100,000 or imprisoned not more than 1 year,
    or both.

(d) ATTORNEY FEES.—The court may award reasonable attorney fees
and costs to a prevailing tribal plaintiff.

SEC. 8. SACRED SITES COMMISSION.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established the Sacred Sites
Commission as an independent commission within the Department
of the Interior.

(b) MEMBERSHIP.—The Commission shall consist of 9 members:
    (1) 5 members appointed by the President from nominations
    submitted by Indian tribes and Native Hawaiian organizations;
    (2) 2 members appointed by the President; and
    (3) 2 members appointed by the President from nominations
    submitted by the majority and minority leaders of the Senate
    and the Speaker and minority leader of the House.

(c) FUNCTIONS.—The Commission shall—
    (1) administer the Sacred Sites Registry;
    (2) issue guidance to federal agencies on sacred site
    identification and protection;
    (3) mediate disputes between tribes and agencies regarding
    sacred site designations;
    (4) submit an annual report to Congress on the status of
    sacred site protection; and
    (5) recommend legislative and regulatory improvements.

SEC. 9. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated—
(a) $25,000,000 annually for the Sacred Sites Commission;
(b) $100,000,000 annually for Tribal Historic Preservation
Office grants; and
(c) $25,000,000 annually for sacred site survey and monitoring
grants to Indian tribes.
```

**Explanation**: This comprehensive legislation addresses the core protection gap by establishing a substantive prohibition on sacred site destruction, creating a confidential registry process, requiring FPIC, and providing enforcement mechanisms. The tribal-majority Commission ensures that sacred site determinations are made by those with the relevant cultural expertise.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Industry opposition will be intense, particularly from mining and energy sectors
- Western state delegations may oppose restrictions on federal land use
- The FPIC requirement will be characterized as giving tribes "veto power" over development
- Appropriations levels are ambitious given current budget politics
- Questions about the scope of "sacred site" definition may arise

**Refinements**:

- Consider a phased implementation beginning with the most threatened sites
- Establish a dispute resolution mechanism for contested registration decisions
- Include transition provisions for projects already in the pipeline
- Address the intersection with existing Section 106 process to avoid duplication

### RFRA Sacred Sites Clarification Act

**Purpose**: Amend the Religious Freedom Restoration Act to clarify that government actions that destroy sacred sites substantially burden religious exercise.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "RFRA Sacred Sites Clarification Act".

SEC. 2. AMENDMENT TO RFRA.

Section 3 of the Religious Freedom Restoration Act of 1993
(42 U.S.C. 2000bb-1) is amended by adding at the end the
following:

"(d) SACRED SITES.—
    (1) For purposes of this section, a government action that
    destroys, substantially damages, or substantially impairs
    access to a location that has established significance as a
    sacred site for the religious exercise of any person shall be
    deemed to impose a substantial burden on the religious exercise
    of such person.
    (2) The term 'sacred site' means a specific, discrete location
    on federal land that has established religious significance to
    an Indian tribe, Native Hawaiian organization, or members
    thereof.
    (3) A determination of established religious significance
    may be supported by tribal government attestation, historical
    documentation, ethnographic evidence, or any combination
    thereof.
    (4) Information submitted in support of a sacred site claim
    under this subsection shall be subject to the confidentiality
    protections of section 304 of the National Historic
    Preservation Act (54 U.S.C. 307103)."
```

**Explanation**: This targeted amendment overrides *Lyng* and *Navajo Nation v. U.S. Forest Service* by establishing that sacred site destruction constitutes a substantial burden under RFRA. Once the substantial burden is established, the government must demonstrate a compelling interest and use the least restrictive means -- the existing RFRA framework. This approach is more politically feasible than comprehensive legislation because it builds on bipartisan RFRA support.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Industry will argue this gives tribes veto power over any project near sacred sites
- Courts may still set a high bar for "established religious significance"
- The compelling interest test gives government significant flexibility to override protections
- Some constitutional scholars may raise Establishment Clause concerns

**Refinements**:

- Define "established significance" with sufficient specificity to guide courts
- Address the interaction with RFRA's application to land-use decisions more broadly
- Consider whether to extend protections beyond federal land

### Save Oak Flat Act

**Purpose**: Repeal the Resolution Copper land exchange and protect Chi'chil Bildagoteel (Oak Flat) from mining.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Save Oak Flat Act".

SEC. 2. REPEAL OF SOUTHEAST ARIZONA LAND EXCHANGE.

Section 3003 of the Carl Levin and Howard P. "Buck" McKeon
National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2015 (Public
Law 113-291) is repealed.

SEC. 3. WITHDRAWAL.

Subject to valid existing rights, the approximately 2,422 acres
of National Forest System land in the Tonto National Forest in
the State of Arizona known as "Oak Flat" or "Chi'chil
Bildagoteel" are hereby withdrawn from all forms of—
(a) entry, appropriation, or disposal under the public land laws;
(b) location, entry, and patent under mining laws; and
(c) disposition under all laws relating to mineral and geothermal
leasing or mineral materials.
```

**Explanation**: This straightforward repeal and withdrawal addresses the most immediate sacred site threat in the United States. Multiple versions of this legislation have been introduced in Congress.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Resolution Copper (Rio Tinto/BHP) will lobby intensely against repeal
- National security and critical minerals arguments will be deployed (copper supply)
- Arizona delegation is divided
- May be framed as anti-mining precedent

**Refinements**:

- Include economic transition support for communities that anticipated mining jobs
- Address Resolution Copper's claim to compensation for expenditures

## State Model Legislation

### State Sacred Sites Protection Act

**Purpose**: Provide state-level protections for sacred sites on state-managed land and during state-permitted activities.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "[State] Sacred Sites Protection Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.
(a) "Sacred site" means a specific, discrete location on state
land or affected by state-permitted activity that has established
religious or cultural significance to a federally recognized
Indian tribe with historical ties to the state.
(b) "State land" includes all land owned or managed by the state,
any state agency, or any political subdivision of the state.

SECTION 3. CONSULTATION REQUIREMENT.
Before any state agency authorizes, funds, or carries out an
action that may affect a sacred site, the agency shall consult
with the affected Indian tribe or tribes in a manner consistent
with government-to-government relations.

SECTION 4. PROTECTION STANDARD.
No state agency shall authorize, fund, or carry out an action
that would destroy or substantially damage a sacred site unless
the agency makes a written finding that:
(a) there is no feasible alternative that would avoid or
minimize harm to the sacred site; and
(b) the action is necessary to serve a compelling state interest.

SECTION 5. CONFIDENTIALITY.
Information about the location and character of sacred sites
submitted by tribes to state agencies shall be confidential
and exempt from state public records laws.

SECTION 6. ENFORCEMENT.
An affected Indian tribe may bring a civil action to enforce
this Act in state court.
```

**Explanation**: This model legislation extends sacred site protections to state land and state-permitted activities, filling a gap in federal law which applies primarily to federal actions.

**Adaptations**: States with large Indigenous populations (Arizona, New Mexico, Oklahoma, South Dakota, Montana, Alaska) should consider enhanced protections. States with strong public records laws may need specific exemption language.

## Regulatory Framework

### Department of the Interior Sacred Sites Rule

**Existing Authority**: NHPA (54 U.S.C. 300101 et seq.), AIRFA (42 U.S.C. 1996), EO 13007

**Draft Regulation**:

- Establish uniform standards for sacred site identification and protection across all Interior bureaus
- Require pre-decisional consultation with tribes before developing alternatives for any action on lands potentially containing sacred sites
- Create confidential sacred site database accessible only to authorized federal and tribal officials
- Require written explanation when an agency decision overrides tribal sacred site concerns
- Establish training requirements for all land management agency staff

**Explanation**: This regulation would standardize sacred site protection practices across Interior bureaus, addressing the current inconsistency between BLM, NPS, BIA, and other agencies.

### Forest Service Sacred Sites Directive

**Existing Authority**: National Forest Management Act (16 U.S.C. 1600 et seq.), NHPA, EO 13007

**Draft Regulation**:

- Integrate sacred site protection into forest planning process
- Require sacred site surveys before timber sales, road construction, and recreation development
- Establish co-management protocols for national forests containing sacred sites
- Provide for temporary closures to protect sacred site integrity during ceremonies

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Free Exercise Clause**: Legislative protections for sacred sites do not raise Establishment Clause concerns. The Supreme Court has recognized that government accommodation of religious practice -- including specific religious groups -- is permissible and does not constitute establishment. See *Corporation of the Presiding Bishop v. Amos*, 483 U.S. 327 (1987).
- **Takings Clause**: Restrictions on private land development near sacred sites could raise takings concerns, but restrictions on federal land use do not.
- **Indian Commerce Clause**: Congress has broad authority under Art. I, Sec. 8, Cl. 3 to legislate regarding Indian tribes. *Haaland v. Brackeen* (2023) affirmed this authority.
- **Political Classification**: Tribal-specific protections are based on the political, not racial, classification of tribal membership. *Morton v. Mancari*, 417 U.S. 535 (1974).

### Preemption Questions

- Federal sacred site legislation would preempt state law to the extent of any conflict
- State legislation can provide supplementary protections beyond the federal floor
- Tribal law governs sacred sites on tribal land; federal legislation would apply to federal land

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- Private right of action for tribes in federal court
- Criminal penalties for intentional desecration
- Administrative enforcement through Sacred Sites Commission
- Injunctive relief available through federal courts

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Sacred Sites Commission reauthorization every 10 years
- Annual reporting requirement to Congress
- GAO review of implementation effectiveness every 5 years

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Sacred Sites Protection Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Definition scope | "Specific, discrete, narrowly delineated location" may exclude sacred landscapes spanning large areas | High |
| Federal land limitation | Act applies only to federal land; private and state land sacred sites unprotected | High |
| Pipeline exemption | Existing valid rights and pipeline permits may be grandfathered | Medium |
| Registration burden | Tribes must affirmatively register sites; unregistered sites receive no protection | High |
| Commission capture | Political appointment process could result in a Commission hostile to tribal interests | Medium |
| National security exception | Presidential waiver for "national security" could be applied broadly | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No private land coverage | Many sacred sites are on private land and remain unprotected | Constitutional limits on regulating private property |
| Retroactivity gap | Existing projects and leases may proceed despite sacred site impacts | Concerns about takings liability and contract law |
| Confidentiality tension | Registry system requires some documentation, creating disclosure risks | Inherent tension between protection (requires identification) and confidentiality |
| Appropriations dependence | Commission and THPO funding subject to annual appropriations | Congressional appropriations process |
| Single-tribe focus | Sites sacred to multiple tribes may face coordination challenges in registration | Diversity of tribal governance structures |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Expand the definition of "sacred site" to include "sacred landscapes" with graduated protections for core and buffer areas
2. Create a voluntary private land sacred site conservation easement program with tax incentives
3. Require assessment of existing projects and leases for sacred site impacts, with modification or cancellation authority where impacts are severe
4. Authorize advance appropriations for the Commission (modeled on IHS advance appropriations authority)
5. Establish inter-tribal coordination protocols for multi-tribe sacred sites
6. Narrow the national security exception to require specific, documented findings reviewed by the Commission

### RFRA Sacred Sites Clarification Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Compelling interest flexibility | Government may still override protections by asserting compelling interests (national security, critical minerals) | High |
| "Established significance" burden | Courts may require extensive historical documentation that tribes cannot provide without compromising confidentiality | Medium |
| Individual vs. tribal standing | RFRA was designed for individual claims; tribal government claims may face procedural challenges | Medium |
| Narrow definition | "Specific, discrete location on federal land" may exclude landscape-scale sacred areas | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Compelling interest test allows overrides | Government can still authorize destruction if it demonstrates compelling interest | Structure of RFRA's balancing test |
| Court interpretation uncertainty | Courts may interpret the amendment narrowly | Judicial discretion in statutory interpretation |
| Federal land only | No protection for sacred sites on state or private land | RFRA applies to federal government only |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Include legislative history specifying that "compelling interest" in the sacred site context requires a showing beyond general economic benefit or project convenience
2. Clarify that tribal government attestation is sufficient to establish "established significance" without additional corroboration
3. Explicitly authorize tribal government standing under RFRA for sacred site claims
4. Pair with state model legislation to extend protections beyond federal land

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Agency resistance to new requirements | Mandatory training; performance metrics tied to sacred site compliance |
| Underfunded THPOs cannot participate effectively | Dedicated appropriations increase; capacity building grants |
| Interagency coordination failures | Sacred Sites MOU enforcement; Commission oversight authority |
| Political vulnerability of administrative protections | Legislative codification of key protections |
| Litigation delay tactics by project proponents | Expedited judicial review provisions; preliminary injunction standards |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Annual Commission report to Congress on sacred site protection outcomes
- GAO review every 5 years on implementation effectiveness
- Reauthorization of Commission every 10 years with mandatory congressional hearings
- Automatic review triggered when a registered sacred site is damaged or destroyed
