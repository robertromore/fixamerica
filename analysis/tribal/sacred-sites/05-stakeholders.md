# Sacred Sites: Stakeholders

## Overview

Sacred site protection involves a complex web of stakeholders with varying levels of power, interest, and influence. The most affected communities -- Indigenous peoples whose sacred sites are at risk -- often have the least institutional power to protect them. Understanding stakeholder dynamics is essential for developing effective reform strategies.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Tribal Nations and Indigenous Communities

**Role**: Rights-holders whose sacred sites are at stake

**Key interests**:

- Protection of sacred sites from destruction and desecration
- Access to sacred sites for ceremonial and spiritual purposes
- Confidentiality of sacred site locations and cultural information
- Co-management or management authority over lands containing sacred sites
- Repatriation of sacred objects and ancestral remains
- Self-determination in defining what constitutes a sacred site

**Power and influence**:

- 574 federally recognized tribal nations possess sovereign governmental authority
- Treaty rights provide legal basis for some sacred site claims
- Growing political influence through coalition-building (e.g., Bears Ears Inter-Tribal Coalition)
- Limited financial resources relative to industry opponents
- Moral authority and growing public sympathy

**Key organizations**:

| Organization | Role |
|-------------|------|
| National Congress of American Indians (NCAI) | Largest inter-tribal advocacy organization; coordinates sacred site policy positions |
| National Association of Tribal Historic Preservation Officers (NATHPO) | Represents 208 THPOs; technical expertise in cultural resource protection |
| Bears Ears Inter-Tribal Coalition | Five-tribe coalition advocating for Bears Ears co-management |
| Apache Stronghold | Nonprofit leading Oak Flat protection effort |
| Association on American Indian Affairs (AAIA) | Oldest Indian rights organization; sacred site litigation and advocacy |
| Native American Rights Fund (NARF) | Legal advocacy; represents tribes in sacred site litigation |

### Traditional Knowledge Holders and Spiritual Leaders

**Role**: Custodians of sacred site knowledge and practice

**Key interests**:

- Continuing sacred practices at specific places
- Transmitting sacred knowledge to future generations
- Maintaining confidentiality of restricted knowledge
- Preventing disturbance of sacred landscapes

**Power and influence**:

- Essential authority on the significance and boundaries of sacred sites
- Cultural authority within tribal communities
- Limited direct access to legal and political processes
- Knowledge may be restricted by cultural protocols, limiting advocacy capacity

### Tribal Historic Preservation Officers (THPOs)

**Role**: Tribal officials designated to carry out historic preservation functions

**Key interests**:

- Identifying and documenting sacred sites (within cultural protocols)
- Participating in Section 106 consultations
- Securing resources for survey, monitoring, and protection
- Building tribal capacity for cultural resource management

**Power and influence**:

- 208 THPOs designated under NHPA as of 2024
- Direct role in federal consultation processes
- Chronic underfunding limits effectiveness (average $96,000 per office)
- Expertise and institutional knowledge are critical to protection efforts

## Federal Government Stakeholders

### Bureau of Land Management (BLM)

**Role**: Manages 245 million acres of federal land, much of it containing sacred sites

**Key interests**:

- Multiple-use mandate balancing recreation, grazing, mining, conservation
- Processing mining claims and energy leases
- Complying with NHPA, NEPA, and EO 13007
- Managing national monuments (including Bears Ears)

**Power and influence**:

- Vast land management authority; decisions directly affect sacred sites
- Permitting authority for mineral extraction and energy development
- Budget incentivized by fee revenue from leasing and permitting

### U.S. Forest Service (USFS)

**Role**: Manages 193 million acres of national forests and grasslands

**Key interests**:

- Timber management, recreation, and conservation
- Multiple-use sustained yield mandate
- Tribal consultation obligations

**Power and influence**:

- Major land manager; forests contain numerous sacred sites
- Forest plans determine land use for 15-20 year cycles
- Co-management partner at Bears Ears and other sites

### National Park Service (NPS)

**Role**: Manages 85 million acres; administers NHPA and NAGPRA programs

**Key interests**:

- Cultural resource preservation and interpretation
- NHPA program administration (including Section 106)
- NAGPRA compliance and oversight
- Tribal partnerships in park management

**Power and influence**:

- Administers the National Register of Historic Places
- Runs the National NAGPRA Program
- Conservation-oriented mandate provides some alignment with tribal interests
- Limited authority beyond NPS-managed lands

### Advisory Council on Historic Preservation (ACHP)

**Role**: Independent federal agency that oversees Section 106 review process

**Key interests**:

- Ensuring federal agencies comply with NHPA consultation requirements
- Mediating disputes between agencies, tribes, and project proponents
- Issuing guidance on sacred site treatment in Section 106

**Power and influence**:

- Advisory authority; cannot compel outcomes
- Comments carry weight in federal decision-making
- Limited staff and budget

### Bureau of Indian Affairs (BIA)

**Role**: Primary federal agency for government-to-government relations with tribes

**Key interests**:

- Trust responsibility to tribal nations
- Land-into-trust decisions
- Supporting tribal self-governance

**Power and influence**:

- Can take land into trust, providing stronger protections for sacred sites
- Limited authority over actions by other federal agencies
- Chronic underfunding of trust responsibilities

## Industry Stakeholders

### Mining and Mineral Extraction Industry

**Role**: Major source of threats to sacred sites on federal land

**Key interests**:

- Access to mineral resources on federal and private land
- Predictable permitting timelines
- Minimizing regulatory burden and project delays
- Critical mineral development for national security and clean energy

**Key entities**:

| Company/Entity | Sacred Site Conflict |
|----------------|---------------------|
| Resolution Copper (Rio Tinto/BHP) | Oak Flat (Chi'chil Bildagoteel), Arizona |
| Lithium Americas (now Lithium Nevada) | Thacker Pass, Nevada |
| Freeport-McMoRan | Various southwestern mining operations |
| National Mining Association | Industry lobbying on permitting and sacred site regulations |

**Power and influence**:

- Substantial lobbying expenditures (mining sector: $30+ million annually)
- Political influence through campaign contributions
- 1872 Mining Law provides strong legal position on federal lands
- "Critical minerals" framing aligns industry interests with national security discourse

### Oil and Gas Industry

**Role**: Energy development on federal lands threatens sacred landscapes

**Key interests**:

- Access to federal mineral leases
- Predictable regulatory environment
- Pipeline and infrastructure development

**Key entities**:

| Company/Entity | Sacred Site Conflict |
|----------------|---------------------|
| Energy Transfer Partners | Dakota Access Pipeline (Standing Rock) |
| Enbridge | Line 3 Pipeline (Anishinaabe treaty lands) |
| Various operators | Greater Chaco landscape drilling |
| American Petroleum Institute | Industry lobbying on federal leasing |

**Power and influence**:

- Oil and gas lobbying: over $100 million annually at the federal level
- Strong political influence in resource-dependent states
- Federal royalty revenue creates government financial interest in leasing

### Renewable Energy and Critical Minerals Industry

**Role**: Emerging source of sacred site threats driven by energy transition

**Key interests**:

- Lithium, cobalt, copper, and rare earth mineral development
- Solar and wind project siting
- Clean energy mandates and production targets

**Power and influence**:

- Growing political support across party lines for domestic mineral production
- Climate urgency framing complicates opposition
- Inflation Reduction Act and other legislation incentivize rapid development

|-------------|------|----------------|

| Earthjustice | Environmental law firm | Sacred site cases as part of environmental litigation |

| First Liberty Institute | Religious freedom advocacy | Supportive of Native religious freedom claims |

### Environmental and Conservation Organizations

| Organization | Role | Stance on Sacred Sites |
|-------------|------|----------------------|
| Sierra Club | Environmental advocacy | Supports sacred site protections; led Bears Ears campaign |
| Natural Resources Defense Council (NRDC) | Environmental law and policy | Supports protections; engages in litigation |
| Wilderness Society | Federal land conservation | Supports monument designations and withdrawals |
| National Trust for Historic Preservation | Historic preservation advocacy | Supports sacred site listings and protections |
| Society for American Archaeology | Archaeological professional organization | Supports NAGPRA compliance and archaeological site protection |

### Academic and Research Institutions

**Role**: Research, documentation, and expert testimony

**Key interests**:

- Archaeological and anthropological research
- Cultural resource documentation
- Expert witness roles in litigation
- Ethical standards for research involving sacred sites

**Power and influence**:

- Provide evidence base for legal and policy arguments
- NAGPRA compliance obligations for institutions holding collections
- Can either support or complicate tribal interests depending on research approach

## State and Local Government

### State Governments

**Role**: Limited direct authority over federal sacred site decisions; significant authority over state lands and private land use

**Key interests**:

- Economic development and tax revenue from extractive industries
- State historic preservation (SHPOs coordinate with THPOs)
- State-tribal relations

**Key dynamics**:

- Western states with large federal land holdings (Utah, Nevada, Arizona) often oppose monument designations and land withdrawals
- Some states have enacted supplementary sacred site protections (e.g., California's AB 275, 2024)
- State legislative delegations influence federal policy significantly

### Local Governments and Communities

**Role**: Affected by land management decisions in their jurisdictions

**Key interests**:

- Local employment and economic development
- Property tax revenue
- Community identity and quality of life

**Power and influence**:

- Local opposition to sacred site protections can be significant, particularly in resource-dependent communities
- Local support can also bolster protection efforts (e.g., San Juan County, Utah, on Bears Ears)
- County commissioners and local elected officials influence federal delegations

## General Public

### Supportive Public

- Growing public awareness and sympathy for sacred site issues, particularly after Standing Rock
- Environmental and cultural heritage constituencies overlap with sacred site protection
- Religious freedom advocates increasingly support Indigenous religious claims
- Tourism interests sometimes align with sacred site preservation

### Opposing or Indifferent Public

- Communities dependent on extractive industries may view protections as threatening livelihoods
- Recreational users may oppose access restrictions at sacred sites
- General public awareness remains limited outside high-profile cases
- Cultural unfamiliarity with Indigenous sacred practices can limit sympathy
