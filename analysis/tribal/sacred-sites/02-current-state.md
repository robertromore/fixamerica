# Sacred Sites: Current State

## Legal Framework Today

### Federal Statutory Protections

The legal framework for sacred site protection is fragmented across multiple statutes, none of which provide comprehensive protection:

| Law | Year | Key Provisions | Limitations |
|-----|------|----------------|-------------|
| National Historic Preservation Act (NHPA) | 1966 | Section 106 review for federal undertakings; Traditional Cultural Properties eligible for National Register | Advisory only; does not prohibit destruction; applies only to federal actions |
| American Indian Religious Freedom Act (AIRFA) | 1978 | Policy statement supporting Native religious freedom; directs agencies to evaluate policies | No enforceable rights; courts treat as purely aspirational (*Lyng*, 1988) |
| Archaeological Resources Protection Act (ARPA) | 1979 | Criminalizes unauthorized excavation on federal/tribal land | Focuses on archaeological resources, not living sacred sites |
| Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act (NAGPRA) | 1990 | Protects burial sites; requires repatriation of remains and objects | Limited to burial sites and cultural items; compliance gaps remain |
| Religious Freedom Restoration Act (RFRA) | 1993 | Requires compelling government interest and least restrictive means for substantial burdens on religion | Courts inconsistently apply to sacred site cases; *Navajo Nation v. U.S. Forest Service* (2008) rejected RFRA claim |
| National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) | 1969 | Environmental impact statements must consider cultural resources | Procedural only; does not mandate protection outcomes |

### Executive Orders

| Executive Order | Year | Directive | Effect |
|-----------------|------|-----------|--------|
| EO 13007 (Indian Sacred Sites) | 1996 | Directs agencies to accommodate access and avoid adversely affecting sacred sites | No enforcement mechanism; agencies retain discretion |
| EO 13175 (Consultation and Coordination) | 2000 | Requires meaningful consultation before actions with tribal implications | Compliance is inconsistent; no consent requirement |
| EO 14112 (Reforming Federal Funding and Support for Tribal Nations) | 2023 | Strengthens consultation; directs best practices for sacred sites | Implementation ongoing; relies on agency follow-through |

### Key Supreme Court Decisions

| Case | Year | Holding | Impact |
|------|------|---------|--------|
| *Lyng v. Northwest Indian Cemetery Protective Association* | 1988 | Government may build a road through sacred forest even if it virtually destroys religious practice | Established that the Free Exercise Clause does not prevent government actions that incidentally burden sacred sites |
| *Employment Division v. Smith* | 1990 | Neutral, generally applicable laws need not be justified by compelling interest even if they burden religion | Narrowed religious freedom claims generally; partly addressed by RFRA |
| *Navajo Nation v. U.S. Forest Service* (9th Cir., en banc) | 2008 | Recycled wastewater on San Francisco Peaks does not substantially burden Navajo religion under RFRA | High bar for "substantial burden" in land-use contexts |
| *Standing Rock Sioux Tribe v. U.S. Army Corps of Engineers* | 2017-2020 | NEPA/NHPA procedural challenges to Dakota Access Pipeline partially succeeded | Demonstrated procedural tools but did not establish substantive sacred site protection |

## Active Threats to Sacred Sites

### Oak Flat (Chi'chil Bildagoteel), Arizona

The Resolution Copper project threatens Chi'chil Bildagoteel (Oak Flat), a site sacred to the San Carlos Apache and other tribes. Section 3003 of the National Defense Authorization Act for FY 2015 mandated a land exchange transferring 2,422 acres of the Tonto National Forest to Resolution Copper Mining, a joint venture of Rio Tinto and BHP. The planned copper mine would create a crater approximately two miles wide and 1,000 feet deep, destroying the sacred site. Apache Stronghold, a nonprofit, has challenged the exchange on religious freedom grounds; the Ninth Circuit ruled against the challenge in 2022 (*Apache Stronghold v. United States*, 38 F.4th 742), and the Supreme Court declined to hear the case in 2024.

### Thacker Pass, Nevada

The Thacker Pass lithium mine on BLM land in Humboldt County, Nevada, threatens a site sacred to the Fort McDermitt Paiute and Shoshone Tribe and other tribes, including the location of a nineteenth-century massacre site. The project received a Record of Decision in 2021 despite tribal opposition. Litigation challenging NEPA and NHPA compliance was partially successful but did not halt the project.

### Chaco Canyon Region, New Mexico

The greater Chaco landscape surrounding Chaco Culture National Historical Park contains thousands of archaeological and sacred sites important to Navajo, Pueblo, and other tribal nations. Oil and gas drilling has expanded rapidly in the region. In 2022, the Biden administration imposed a 20-year mineral withdrawal on approximately 336,000 acres around the park, but existing leases remain active.

### Mauna Kea, Hawaii

Mauna Kea, the highest peak in Hawaii, is profoundly sacred to Native Hawaiians. The Thirty Meter Telescope (TMT) project, approved for construction near the summit, has generated sustained opposition. As of 2025, construction has not proceeded; the Hawaii Supreme Court upheld the permit in 2018, but protest and political pressure have delayed the project.

### Line 3 Pipeline, Minnesota

The Line 3 Replacement project by Enbridge crossed treaty territories and wild rice waters sacred to the Anishinaabe people. Despite sustained opposition including legal challenges, the pipeline was completed in 2021. The case highlighted the inadequacy of existing consultation and environmental review processes.

## Federal Agency Practices

### Consultation Quality

Federal agencies are required to consult with tribes on actions affecting sacred sites, but the quality of consultation varies dramatically:

| Quality Indicator | Best Practice | Typical Practice |
|-------------------|---------------|------------------|
| Timing of consultation | Before alternatives are developed | After preferred alternative is selected |
| Level of official involved | Senior decision-maker | Staff-level cultural resources specialist |
| Duration | Multiple meetings over months | Single letter or meeting |
| Information sharing | Full project details and alternatives | Summary of preferred action |
| Outcome | Decision modified based on tribal input | Tribal concerns noted but not addressed |
| Follow-up | Ongoing engagement through implementation | Consultation record closed at decision point |

### Confidentiality Protections

Section 304 of the NHPA allows agencies to withhold information about the location, character, or ownership of historic resources when disclosure could result in harm. However:

- Tribes are often reluctant to share sacred site information even with confidentiality assurances
- Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) exemptions for sacred site information are narrow
- Project applicants and their consultants may gain access to sensitive information through Section 106 review
- No standardized protocol exists across agencies for handling sacred site information

## Co-Management Models

### Bears Ears National Monument

Bears Ears, designated in 2016 and restored at 1.36 million acres in 2021, includes the Bears Ears Inter-Tribal Coalition -- representing the Hopi Tribe, Navajo Nation, Ute Mountain Ute Tribe, Ute Indian Tribe, and Pueblo of Zuni -- in collaborative management with the Bureau of Land Management and U.S. Forest Service. This is the most prominent co-management model for sacred sites on federal land:

- Tribes participate in land management planning
- Traditional ecological knowledge is integrated into resource management
- Coalition has advisory authority but not decision-making power
- Monument management plan under development as of 2025

### Other Co-Management Arrangements

| Site | Arrangement | Status |
|------|-------------|--------|
| Canyon de Chelly National Monument | NPS managed; Navajo residents maintain homes and farms | Operating since 1931 |
| Kasha-Katuwe Tent Rocks National Monument | BLM co-management with Pueblo de Cochiti | Reopened 2023 after closure |
| San Gabriel Mountains National Monument | USFS partnership with Tongva and other tribes | Advisory participation |
| Badger-Two Medicine, Montana | 20-year mineral withdrawal protecting Blackfeet sacred area | Withdrawal renewed; permanent protection pending |

## Recent Developments (2024-2025)

### NAGPRA Final Rule (2024)

The Department of the Interior issued updated NAGPRA regulations effective January 12, 2024, that significantly strengthen tribal authority:

- Institutions must obtain consent from lineal descendants and tribes before allowing access to or research on holdings
- Duty to consult applies to all cultural items, not just those with established cultural affiliation
- Disposition of culturally unidentifiable remains prioritizes tribal and Native Hawaiian organization input
- Compliance deadlines tightened for inventory completion

### Sacred Sites Memorandum of Understanding (2022)

In November 2022, the Departments of the Interior, Agriculture, Defense, Energy, and the Environmental Protection Agency signed a Sacred Sites MOU committing to improve inter-agency coordination, strengthen consultation, and develop best practices for sacred site protection.

### Apache Stronghold Litigation

The Ninth Circuit's 2022 en banc decision in *Apache Stronghold v. United States* rejected religious freedom claims against the Resolution Copper land exchange, holding that transferring federal land to a private mining company did not impose a "substantial burden" on Apache religious exercise under RFRA. The Supreme Court declined certiorari in 2024. This decision reinforced the *Lyng* framework and highlighted the need for legislative action.

### Chaco Region Mineral Withdrawal

The 20-year mineral withdrawal protecting approximately 336,000 acres around Chaco Culture National Historical Park, finalized in 2023, was the most significant protective action for a sacred landscape in recent years. However, the withdrawal does not affect existing valid leases and can be reversed by future administrations.

## The Protection Gap

| What Tribes Need | What the Law Provides | Gap |
|------------------|-----------------------|-----|
| Enforceable right to prevent destruction | Procedural review requirements only | No substantive right to protection |
| Consent before actions affecting sacred sites | Consultation without consent requirement | Agency retains final decision-making authority |
| Confidential identification processes | Limited NHPA Section 304 protections | Information often disclosed through project review |
| Permanent protection | Monument designations, withdrawals (reversible) | Political vulnerability to future administrations |
| Co-management authority | Advisory participation in most cases | Tribes lack decision-making power |
| Adequate funding for THPOs | $20 million annually for 208 THPOs | Average of $96,000 per THPO; insufficient for survey and monitoring |
| Protection on private land | Virtually none unless NHPA-listed | No mechanism for protecting privately-owned sacred sites |
