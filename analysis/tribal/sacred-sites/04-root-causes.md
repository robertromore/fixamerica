# Sacred Sites: Root Causes

## Overview

The persistent failure to protect Indigenous sacred sites in the United States stems from deeply embedded legal, cultural, political, and economic structures. These root causes are not isolated failures but interconnected systems that reinforce one another, creating a protection gap that has endured despite decades of incremental legal reform.

## Legal and Constitutional Root Causes

### 1. The *Lyng* Doctrine: Government Discretion Over Sacred Lands

The Supreme Court's 1988 decision in *Lyng v. Northwest Indian Cemetery Protective Association* is the single most important legal root cause of sacred site vulnerability. By holding that government actions destroying sacred sites do not violate the Free Exercise Clause -- because they neither coerce religious conduct nor penalize religious belief -- the Court created a framework in which Indigenous sacred site claims receive no constitutional protection.

- **Effect**: The government may knowingly destroy sacred sites as long as the destruction is incidental to a "neutral" land management decision
- **Why it persists**: Overturning *Lyng* requires either a Supreme Court reversal or congressional legislation; neither has occurred
- **Downstream impact**: Lower courts consistently apply *Lyng* to reject sacred site claims, including RFRA claims (*Navajo Nation v. U.S. Forest Service*, 2008; *Apache Stronghold v. United States*, 2022)

### 2. The Substantial Burden Standard Under RFRA

Even where RFRA applies, courts have imposed an extremely high threshold for what constitutes a "substantial burden" on religious exercise in the sacred site context:

- Courts distinguish between government actions that *coerce* religious practice and those that merely make religious practice more difficult or impossible
- Destroying a sacred site has been held not to impose a "substantial burden" because the government is not forcing individuals to act contrary to their beliefs
- This interpretation treats sacred site destruction as a lesser burden than, for example, requiring a business owner to provide insurance coverage for contraception (*Burwell v. Hobby Lobby*, 2014)

### 3. Property Law Frameworks

American property law does not recognize Indigenous sacred relationships with land:

- **Fee simple ownership**: The dominant property paradigm treats land as a commodity with no inherent spiritual or cultural value
- **Mineral rights**: Subsurface rights can be separated from surface rights, allowing mining beneath or through sacred sites
- **Federal public land doctrine**: Federal land is managed for "multiple use," which subordinates sacred site protection to resource extraction, recreation, and other uses
- **No equivalent of easement or covenant for sacred value**: There is no property law mechanism to permanently protect the sacred character of a place across ownership changes

### 4. Consultation Without Consent

Federal law requires agencies to consult with tribes but never requires obtaining tribal consent:

- **Consultation as procedural compliance**: Agencies treat consultation as a box-checking exercise rather than a meaningful exchange
- **Information asymmetry**: Agencies control the process timeline, scope, and format of consultation
- **No veto power**: Even when tribes provide detailed objections, agencies retain unilateral decision-making authority
- **International comparison**: The United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP) calls for Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC), which the United States has endorsed in principle but not implemented in law

## Cultural and Epistemological Root Causes

### 5. The Western Sacred/Secular Divide

Western legal and philosophical traditions draw a sharp distinction between the sacred and the secular that does not map onto Indigenous worldviews:

- **Buildings vs. landscapes**: Western religion tends to be associated with built structures (churches, temples), while Indigenous sacred practice is often embedded in natural landscapes
- **Portable vs. place-specific**: Western religious practice can generally be relocated; Indigenous sacred practice is often inseparable from specific places
- **Documentary vs. oral**: Western legal systems rely on written documentation; Indigenous sacred knowledge is often transmitted orally and maintained through practice
- **Exclusive vs. integrated**: Western law treats religion as a separate domain from land use, economics, and governance; Indigenous worldviews integrate the sacred into all aspects of life

### 6. Confidentiality Paradoxes

The structure of American administrative and legal processes creates an impossible bind for tribes seeking to protect sacred sites:

- **Disclosure requirement**: To receive legal protection, tribes must identify sacred sites and explain their significance -- often before agency officials, project proponents, and courts
- **Desecration risk**: Public disclosure of sacred site locations and practices can itself constitute desecration under tribal protocols
- **Knowledge restrictions**: Many sacred sites are known only to specific individuals or societies within a tribe; compelling disclosure may violate internal cultural norms
- **Weaponized information**: Disclosed information can be used by opponents to challenge the significance or authenticity of sacred site claims
- **Insufficient confidentiality protections**: NHPA Section 304 and FOIA exemptions provide limited protection, but litigation discovery and project review processes can force disclosure

### 7. Assimilation Legacy and Knowledge Loss

Centuries of deliberate assimilation policy have damaged the transmission of sacred site knowledge:

- Boarding school policies (1879-1960s) removed children from communities where sacred knowledge was transmitted
- The Code of Indian Offenses (1883-1934) criminalized the ceremonies that maintained sacred site relationships
- Forced relocations (removal, allotment, termination) physically separated communities from sacred landscapes
- Language loss has eroded the vocabulary and narratives essential to sacred site knowledge
- The cumulative effect is that some sacred sites have been lost to living memory, even as they face active threats

## Political and Structural Root Causes

### 8. Congressional Plenary Power

Congress claims virtually unlimited authority over Indian affairs, including the power to:

- Override any administrative sacred site protection
- Dispose of federal land containing sacred sites (as in the Resolution Copper land exchange)
- Modify or abrogate treaties that protected access to sacred lands
- Preempt state or tribal protections
- This power means that even strong executive-branch protections can be reversed by a single legislative rider

### 9. Political Underrepresentation

Indigenous peoples lack the political power to protect their interests in the legislative and regulatory process:

- Native Americans constitute approximately 2.9% of the U.S. population (alone or in combination: 5.9%)
- Only a handful of Native Americans have served in Congress (currently 6 members in the 119th Congress)
- Tribal nations lack formal representation in federal decision-making beyond consultation
- Campaign finance systems favor extractive industries over tribal interests
- Geographic dispersion and rural location of many sacred sites reduce political visibility

### 10. Fragmented Federal Land Management

Federal lands containing sacred sites are managed by multiple agencies with competing mandates:

| Agency | Primary Mandate | Sacred Site Priority |
|--------|----------------|---------------------|
| Bureau of Land Management | Multiple use and sustained yield | Low; resource extraction and grazing prioritized |
| U.S. Forest Service | Multiple use; timber, recreation, conservation | Variable; depends on forest plan and regional leadership |
| National Park Service | Conservation and public enjoyment | Moderate; cultural resource mandate but limited co-management |
| Bureau of Reclamation | Water delivery and power generation | Low; infrastructure operation prioritized |
| U.S. Army Corps of Engineers | Navigation and flood control | Low; infrastructure mandate |
| Department of Defense | Military readiness | Low; security mission dominant |

- No single agency has lead authority for sacred site protection
- Agency cultures prioritize their primary mandates over cultural resource protection
- Staff turnover means institutional knowledge of sacred site obligations is inconsistent
- Budget pressures incentivize permitting activities (which generate revenue) over protective actions

## Economic Root Causes

### 11. Extractive Industry Influence

Mining, energy, and development interests systematically oppose sacred site protections:

- **Lobbying**: The mining and energy sectors spend hundreds of millions annually on federal lobbying; tribal nations cannot match this spending
- **Revenue dependence**: Federal and state governments collect royalties, lease payments, and taxes from resource extraction on federal lands
- **Job creation narratives**: Industry frames extraction projects as essential for local and national economic well-being
- **1872 Mining Law**: The General Mining Law of 1872 allows private claims on federal lands with minimal environmental or cultural review, effectively prioritizing mineral access over all other values
- **National security framing**: Critical mineral projects (lithium, copper, rare earths) are increasingly framed as national security imperatives, overriding cultural considerations

### 12. Cost Externalization

The current system externalizes the costs of sacred site destruction onto tribal communities:

- Environmental and cultural impact assessments undervalue or ignore spiritual harm
- Economic analyses do not account for the cultural, psychological, and community health impacts of sacred site destruction
- The benefits of extraction (profits, royalties, employment) accrue to non-tribal entities, while the costs (cultural loss, psychological harm, community disruption) are borne by tribes
- No compensation mechanism exists for the destruction of sacred sites -- unlike other property takings, there is no "just compensation" for spiritual and cultural loss

## Interconnection of Root Causes

These root causes do not operate independently. They form a reinforcing system:

1. **Legal doctrine** (*Lyng*) removes constitutional protection, leaving tribes dependent on legislative and administrative remedies
2. **Political underrepresentation** prevents tribes from securing legislative fixes
3. **Congressional plenary power** allows legislative overrides of any administrative protections that are achieved
4. **Extractive industry influence** shapes both legislative and administrative outcomes
5. **Cultural epistemological gaps** mean that decision-makers do not understand or value what is being destroyed
6. **Confidentiality paradoxes** prevent tribes from fully presenting their case within the legal system
7. **Knowledge loss** from assimilation policies means some claims cannot be made at all

The result is a system in which sacred sites are protected only when their destruction would not conflict with any significant economic or political interest -- a standard that provides almost no protection in practice.
