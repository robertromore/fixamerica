# Sacred Sites: Overview

## Executive Summary

Sacred sites are places of profound spiritual, cultural, and historical significance to Indigenous peoples across the United States. These include mountains, rivers, burial grounds, ceremonial areas, plant-gathering locations, and landscapes integral to creation narratives, religious practices, and cultural identity. Despite their central importance to the more than 9.7 million Americans who identify as American Indian or Alaska Native, sacred sites receive inconsistent and inadequate legal protection under federal, state, and tribal law.

The current legal framework is a patchwork of statutes -- the American Indian Religious Freedom Act (AIRFA, 1978), the National Historic Preservation Act (NHPA, 1966), the Religious Freedom Restoration Act (RFRA, 1993), the Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act (NAGPRA, 1990), and various executive orders -- none of which provide comprehensive, enforceable protections against the destruction of sacred sites. Federal courts have repeatedly held that the First Amendment and RFRA do not prevent the government from authorizing activities that destroy sacred lands, most notably in *Lyng v. Northwest Indian Cemetery Protective Association*, 485 U.S. 439 (1988), where the Supreme Court ruled that the government could build a road through an area central to tribal religious practice even if it would "virtually destroy" the ability to practice that religion.

The consequences of this legal gap are severe. Sacred sites continue to be damaged or destroyed by mining, energy development, highway construction, recreational development, and federal land management decisions. The desecration of these places inflicts deep cultural and psychological harm on tribal communities, undermining their ability to maintain spiritual practices, transmit cultural knowledge, and sustain their identity as distinct peoples.

Reform requires strengthening legal protections, establishing meaningful tribal consultation and consent processes, supporting tribal co-management of federal lands containing sacred sites, and creating enforceable mechanisms for identifying and protecting sacred places before development decisions are finalized.

## Core Tensions

- **Property rights vs. sacred rights**: Private property and mineral rights law conflicts with the protection of sacred landscapes
- **Economic development vs. cultural preservation**: Energy, mining, and infrastructure projects threaten sacred sites that stand in the way of extractable value
- **Religious freedom vs. government discretion**: The Supreme Court has held that the government may destroy sacred sites without violating the Free Exercise Clause
- **Confidentiality vs. documentation**: Tribes often cannot disclose the location or nature of sacred sites without risking their desecration, yet legal protections require documentation
- **Federal land management vs. tribal sovereignty**: Most sacred sites on federal land are managed by agencies with mandates that do not prioritize cultural preservation
- **Consultation vs. consent**: Federal law requires consultation with tribes but does not require obtaining their consent before acting

## Key Questions

1. **Why do existing laws fail to protect sacred sites?** What are the specific legal gaps that allow destruction of places central to Indigenous religious practice?

2. **How does the *Lyng* decision shape sacred site law?** Why has the Supreme Court held that destroying sacred sites does not burden religious freedom?

3. **What is the difference between consultation and consent?** How would requiring Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) change outcomes?

4. **Can co-management work?** What do models like Bears Ears and other co-management arrangements reveal about balancing federal and tribal authority?

5. **How do sacred site protections intersect with energy and mineral development?** What happens when sacred sites sit atop valuable resources?

6. **What role does NAGPRA play?** How effective is the law at protecting burial sites and ensuring repatriation?

7. **How can confidentiality be maintained?** What mechanisms protect sacred site information while still enabling legal protection?

## Vision of Success

A system that adequately protects sacred sites would feature:

- Enforceable federal law prohibiting the destruction of documented sacred sites absent tribal consent
- Meaningful tribal co-management authority over federal lands containing sacred sites
- Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) as the standard for federal actions affecting sacred sites
- Confidential processes for identifying and protecting sacred sites that do not require public disclosure of sensitive cultural information
- Full NAGPRA compliance by all federal agencies and institutions receiving federal funds
- Adequate funding for tribal historic preservation offices and sacred site monitoring
- Reversal of the *Lyng* doctrine through legislation recognizing that government destruction of sacred sites substantially burdens religious freedom
- Integration of Indigenous knowledge and values into federal land management planning
