# Emerging Technologies: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Comprehensive emerging technology governance requires new institutional capacity, adaptive regulatory frameworks, and technology-specific rules that can evolve with technological development. This document provides draft legislation for key reforms.

## Federal Legislation

### Emerging Technologies Governance Act

**Purpose**: Establish a comprehensive framework for anticipatory governance of emerging technologies.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Emerging Technologies Governance
Act of 2025".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
    (1) emerging technologies present significant opportunities
    and risks for society;
    (2) current governance frameworks are inadequate to address
    the pace and scope of technological change;
    (3) anticipatory and adaptive governance is needed;
    (4) institutional capacity for technology assessment must
    be restored; and
    (5) multi-stakeholder engagement is essential.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

(a) EMERGING TECHNOLOGY.—The term "emerging technology" means
any technology that—
    (1) is in early stages of development or deployment;
    (2) has potential for significant societal impact; and
    (3) presents governance challenges not adequately addressed
    by existing frameworks.

(b) DESIGNATED EMERGING TECHNOLOGY.—The term "designated emerging
technology" means a technology designated under section 5.

SEC. 4. OFFICE OF TECHNOLOGY ASSESSMENT.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established in the legislative branch
the Office of Technology Assessment.

(b) DIRECTOR.—The Office shall be headed by a Director appointed
by the Technology Assessment Board.

(c) BOARD.—The Technology Assessment Board shall consist of—
    (1) six members of the Senate, three from each party; and
    (2) six members of the House, three from each party.

(d) FUNCTIONS.—The Office shall—
    (1) conduct assessments of emerging technologies at the
    request of congressional committees;
    (2) provide horizon scanning and early warning of emerging
    technology issues;
    (3) develop analytical frameworks for technology governance;
    (4) maintain expertise on major technology areas; and
    (5) provide briefings and support to members.

(e) INDEPENDENCE.—The Director shall ensure assessments are
objective, nonpartisan, and based on best available evidence.

(f) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
$50,000,000 annually for the Office.

SEC. 5. EMERGING TECHNOLOGY DESIGNATION.

(a) PROCESS.—The President, through the Office of Science and
Technology Policy, shall designate emerging technologies requiring
coordinated governance attention.

(b) CRITERIA.—Designation shall be based on—
    (1) potential for significant beneficial or harmful impacts;
    (2) pace of development;
    (3) adequacy of existing governance;
    (4) dual-use potential; and
    (5) international developments.

(c) INITIAL DESIGNATIONS.—Not later than 1 year after enactment,
the following shall be designated:
    (1) artificial intelligence;
    (2) quantum computing;
    (3) biotechnology and synthetic biology;
    (4) autonomous systems;
    (5) brain-computer interfaces; and
    (6) other technologies as appropriate.

(d) REVIEW.—Designations shall be reviewed annually.

SEC. 6. GOVERNANCE FRAMEWORKS.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—For each designated emerging technology, OSTP
shall coordinate development of a governance framework.

(b) ELEMENTS.—Each framework shall include—
    (1) risk assessment methodology;
    (2) oversight responsibilities and authorities;
    (3) safety and security requirements;
    (4) ethical guidelines;
    (5) public engagement processes;
    (6) international coordination approach; and
    (7) update mechanisms.

(c) ADAPTIVE DESIGN.—Frameworks shall be designed for—
    (1) regular review and update;
    (2) response to emerging risks;
    (3) incorporation of new knowledge; and
    (4) international harmonization.

SEC. 7. INTERAGENCY COORDINATION.

(a) NATIONAL EMERGING TECHNOLOGY COUNCIL.—There is established
the National Emerging Technology Council, chaired by the OSTP
Director, to coordinate emerging technology governance across
the executive branch.

(b) MEMBERSHIP.—The Council shall include representatives from
agencies with significant emerging technology responsibilities.

(c) FUNCTIONS.—The Council shall—
    (1) coordinate development of governance frameworks;
    (2) identify gaps in authority and capacity;
    (3) facilitate information sharing;
    (4) coordinate international engagement; and
    (5) report annually to Congress.

SEC. 8. AGENCY CAPACITY.

(a) TECHNICAL EXPERTISE.—Agencies with emerging technology
responsibilities shall develop and maintain technical expertise.

(b) HIRING AUTHORITY.—Such agencies shall have direct hire
authority for technical positions.

(c) TRAINING.—OPM shall develop training programs for emerging
technology governance.

(d) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
$100,000,000 annually for agency capacity building.

SEC. 9. PUBLIC ENGAGEMENT.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Development of governance frameworks shall
include meaningful public engagement.

(b) MECHANISMS.—Engagement shall include—
    (1) public comment processes;
    (2) stakeholder forums;
    (3) citizen deliberation processes; and
    (4) public reporting.

SEC. 10. INTERNATIONAL COORDINATION.

(a) POLICY.—It is the policy of the United States to pursue
international coordination on emerging technology governance.

(b) ELEMENTS.—International engagement shall include—
    (1) bilateral technology dialogues;
    (2) multilateral forum participation;
    (3) standards coordination; and
    (4) research cooperation.

SEC. 11. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect upon enactment.
```

### Technology-Specific Framework Acts

#### Quantum Computing Security Act

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Quantum Computing Security Act
of 2025".

SEC. 2. CRYPTOGRAPHIC TRANSITION.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—The Director of CISA shall coordinate the
transition of Federal information systems to quantum-resistant
cryptography.

(b) TIMELINE.—
    (1) Within 1 year, agencies shall inventory cryptographic
    systems vulnerable to quantum attacks.
    (2) Within 3 years, agencies shall develop transition plans.
    (3) Within 7 years, critical systems shall be transitioned.

(c) STANDARDS.—NIST shall develop and update post-quantum
cryptographic standards.

SEC. 3. EXPORT CONTROLS.

(a) REVIEW.—The Bureau of Industry and Security shall review
export controls on quantum computing technology.

(b) CRITERIA.—Controls shall balance—
    (1) national security;
    (2) international cooperation; and
    (3) economic competitiveness.

SEC. 4. WORKFORCE.

(a) There is authorized $100,000,000 for quantum computing
workforce development.
```

#### Advanced Biotechnology Governance Act

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Advanced Biotechnology Governance
Act of 2025".

SEC. 2. COORDINATED FRAMEWORK UPDATE.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—The President shall update the Coordinated
Framework for the Regulation of Biotechnology.

(b) ELEMENTS.—The update shall address—
    (1) synthetic biology;
    (2) gene drives;
    (3) human germline modification;
    (4) dual-use research; and
    (5) emerging applications.

SEC. 3. DUAL-USE RESEARCH.

(a) OVERSIGHT.—NIH shall strengthen oversight of dual-use
research of concern.

(b) REPORTING.—Institutions shall report dual-use research.

(c) INTERNATIONAL.—The State Department shall pursue international
coordination on dual-use research governance.

SEC. 4. HUMAN GERMLINE.

(a) MORATORIUM.—There is a moratorium on clinical application
of human germline modification until comprehensive governance
is established.

(b) RESEARCH.—Basic research may continue under NIH oversight.

SEC. 5. BIOSECURITY.

(a) CAPACITY.—There is authorized $200,000,000 annually for
biosecurity capacity.

(b) SCREENING.—DNA synthesis providers shall screen orders
against select agents.
```

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Emerging Technology Advisory Council Act

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Emerging Technology
Advisory Council Act".

SECTION 2. ESTABLISHMENT.

There is established the [State] Emerging Technology Advisory
Council within the [Governor's office/designated agency].

SECTION 3. MEMBERSHIP.

(a) The Council shall consist of—
    (1) representatives of state agencies;
    (2) academic experts;
    (3) industry representatives;
    (4) civil society; and
    (5) public members.

SECTION 4. DUTIES.

The Council shall—
    (1) advise the Governor and legislature on emerging technology;
    (2) identify emerging technology opportunities and risks;
    (3) recommend state policies;
    (4) facilitate public engagement; and
    (5) coordinate with federal and other state efforts.

SECTION 5. REPORTS.

The Council shall report annually on—
    (1) emerging technology developments;
    (2) state readiness; and
    (3) recommended actions.

SECTION 6. APPROPRIATION.

There is appropriated $[X] for the Council.

SECTION 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect [date].
```

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Definition gaming** | Technologies that avoid designation | High |
| **Jurisdictional gaps** | Falls between agencies | High |
| **Offshore development** | Move research abroad | Medium |
| **Self-assessment** | Inadequate oversight | Medium |
| **Rapid iteration** | Outpace framework updates | High |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| **Expertise gaps** | Inadequate oversight | Workforce |
| **Pace mismatch** | Law lags technology | Inherent |
| **International gaps** | Regulatory arbitrage | Sovereignty |
| **Industry capture** | Weak rules | Politics |
| **Enforcement** | Limited capacity | Resources |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Functional definitions** capturing technology effects
2. **Gap-filling authority** for new technologies
3. **International coordination** reducing arbitrage
4. **Mandatory updates** at regular intervals
5. **Independent assessment** of compliance
6. **Whistleblower protections** for technology concerns
7. **Adequate funding** for oversight
8. **Expert hiring** authorities
9. **Public engagement** requirements
10. **Adaptive design** with trigger mechanisms
