# Data Privacy: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Comprehensive privacy legislation must establish meaningful individual rights, require corporate accountability, provide strong enforcement mechanisms, and preserve state authority to exceed federal minimums. This document provides draft legislation for federal and state privacy reform.

## Federal Legislation

### Comprehensive Privacy Act

**Purpose**: Establish comprehensive federal privacy protections for all Americans.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "American Data Privacy Act of 2025".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that—
    (1) personal data is collected, used, and sold in ways that most
    Americans neither understand nor consent to;
    (2) this data is used to make consequential decisions about
    employment, credit, housing, and other opportunities;
    (3) data collection creates security risks through breaches;
    (4) the United States lacks comprehensive privacy protection;
    (5) state laws provide important protections but create
    inconsistency; and
    (6) strong federal protections that do not diminish state
    authority are needed.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to—
    (1) establish individual privacy rights;
    (2) require data minimization and purpose limitation;
    (3) provide strong enforcement; and
    (4) set a floor, not a ceiling, for privacy protection.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

(a) PERSONAL DATA.—The term "personal data" means information that
identifies or is reasonably linkable to an identified or identifiable
individual.

(b) SENSITIVE DATA.—The term "sensitive data" means personal data
that includes—
    (1) government identifiers (SSN, driver's license, passport);
    (2) financial account information;
    (3) precise geolocation information;
    (4) biometric information;
    (5) genetic information;
    (6) health information;
    (7) sexual orientation or behavior;
    (8) religious beliefs;
    (9) race or ethnicity;
    (10) immigration status;
    (11) contents of communications; and
    (12) data concerning individuals under 17.

(c) COVERED ENTITY.—The term "covered entity" means any person that
collects, processes, or transfers personal data, except—
    (1) government entities (covered separately);
    (2) individuals acting in a personal capacity;
    (3) entities processing only employee data (separate provisions).

(d) SERVICE PROVIDER.—A person that processes personal data on behalf
of a covered entity pursuant to a contract.

SEC. 4. INDIVIDUAL RIGHTS.

(a) RIGHT TO ACCESS.—An individual has the right to obtain from a
covered entity—
    (1) confirmation of whether their personal data is processed;
    (2) a copy of their personal data;
    (3) the categories of sources;
    (4) the purposes of processing;
    (5) categories of third parties with whom data is shared.

(b) RIGHT TO CORRECTION.—An individual has the right to correct
inaccurate personal data.

(c) RIGHT TO DELETION.—An individual has the right to delete their
personal data, subject to limited exceptions for—
    (1) completing a transaction;
    (2) security purposes;
    (3) legal obligations;
    (4) journalistic purposes;
    (5) internal research consistent with expectations.

(d) RIGHT TO PORTABILITY.—An individual has the right to receive
their personal data in a portable, commonly used format.

(e) RIGHT TO OPT OUT.—An individual has the right to opt out of—
    (1) the transfer of personal data to third parties;
    (2) targeted advertising;
    (3) profiling in furtherance of decisions that produce legal or
    similarly significant effects.

(f) NON-DISCRIMINATION.—A covered entity may not discriminate against
an individual for exercising rights under this section, including by—
    (1) denying goods or services;
    (2) charging different prices;
    (3) providing a different level of quality.

(g) EXERCISE OF RIGHTS.—
    (1) A covered entity shall provide at least two methods to submit
    requests, including a toll-free number and online mechanism.
    (2) A covered entity shall respond to requests within 45 days.
    (3) Rights may be exercised free of charge.

SEC. 5. COVERED ENTITY OBLIGATIONS.

(a) DATA MINIMIZATION.—A covered entity shall limit the collection
of personal data to that which is reasonably necessary and
proportionate to provide a product or service requested by the
individual.

(b) PURPOSE LIMITATION.—A covered entity may not process personal
data for purposes materially different from those disclosed at the
time of collection without obtaining consent.

(c) TRANSPARENCY.—A covered entity shall provide a clear, accessible,
and accurate privacy notice that includes—
    (1) categories of data collected;
    (2) purposes of processing;
    (3) categories of third parties;
    (4) retention periods;
    (5) individual rights and how to exercise them.

(d) DATA SECURITY.—A covered entity shall implement and maintain
reasonable security practices appropriate to the nature of the
data and the size of the entity.

(e) CONSENT FOR SENSITIVE DATA.—A covered entity may not process
sensitive data without obtaining the affirmative express consent
of the individual.

(f) CHILDREN'S DATA.—
    (1) A covered entity may not process the personal data of an
    individual the entity knows or should know is under 17 for
    targeted advertising.
    (2) Enhanced consent requirements apply to data of individuals
    under 13 (COPPA preserved).

SEC. 6. DATA TRANSFER RESTRICTIONS.

(a) PROHIBITION ON SALE.—A covered entity shall not sell personal
data unless the individual has provided affirmative consent or the
individual has received clear notice and an opportunity to opt out.

(b) THIRD-PARTY OBLIGATIONS.—A third party that receives personal
data shall—
    (1) use data only for the purposes disclosed;
    (2) comply with individual rights requests;
    (3) maintain appropriate security;
    (4) notify the covered entity of breaches.

(c) SERVICE PROVIDER CONTRACTS.—Contracts with service providers
shall—
    (1) specify the purposes of processing;
    (2) prohibit processing for other purposes;
    (3) require compliance with this Act;
    (4) require data return or deletion.

SEC. 7. DATA BROKER REGISTRATION.

(a) REGISTRATION.—A data broker shall register annually with the
Federal Trade Commission.

(b) DEFINITION.—"Data broker" means a business that collects and
sells personal data of individuals with whom it has no direct
relationship.

(c) DISCLOSURE.—Registration shall include—
    (1) categories of data collected;
    (2) sources of data;
    (3) categories of data sold;
    (4) categories of purchasers;
    (5) opt-out mechanism.

(d) CONSUMER ACCESS.—Data brokers shall provide individuals with
access to their data upon request.

(e) DO-NOT-SELL REGISTRY.—The Commission shall establish a central
mechanism for individuals to opt out of data broker sales.

SEC. 8. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) FTC ENFORCEMENT.—The Federal Trade Commission shall enforce this
Act under Section 5 of the FTC Act.

(b) CIVIL PENALTIES.—
    (1) A violation of this Act is subject to civil penalties of
    $50,000 per violation.
    (2) Knowing violations are subject to penalties of $100,000 per
    violation.
    (3) Pattern violations are subject to penalties of up to 4 percent
    of annual global revenue.

(c) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—
    (1) Any individual whose rights under this Act have been violated
    may bring a civil action.
    (2) A prevailing plaintiff may recover—
        (A) actual damages;
        (B) statutory damages of $100 to $1,000 per violation;
        (C) punitive damages for knowing violations;
        (D) injunctive relief; and
        (E) reasonable attorney's fees and costs.
    (3) Class actions are permitted.

(d) STATE ENFORCEMENT.—State attorneys general may bring actions to
enforce this Act on behalf of state residents.

SEC. 9. PREEMPTION.

(a) FLOOR, NOT CEILING.—This Act establishes minimum standards and
shall not preempt State laws that provide greater protection for
personal data.

(b) PRESERVED STATE AUTHORITY.—States retain authority to—
    (1) enact privacy laws exceeding these requirements;
    (2) enforce this Act and state laws;
    (3) regulate specific sectors not preempted.

SEC. 10. RULEMAKING.

The Commission shall issue regulations to implement this Act within
18 months of enactment.

SEC. 11. EFFECTIVE DATE.

(a) This Act takes effect 24 months after enactment.
(b) Small businesses (under $25 million revenue) have an additional
12 months for compliance.
```

### Data Broker Registration Act

**Purpose**: Require comprehensive data broker registration and transparency.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Data Broker Registration Act of 2025".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) DATA BROKER.—The term "data broker" means any person that
knowingly collects, sells, or licenses personal information of
individuals with whom it has no direct relationship for
consideration.

SEC. 3. REGISTRATION.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Each data broker shall register annually with the
Federal Trade Commission.

(b) REGISTRATION CONTENTS.—Registration shall include—
    (1) legal name and contact information;
    (2) categories of personal information collected;
    (3) sources of personal information;
    (4) categories of personal information sold;
    (5) categories of purchasers;
    (6) description of opt-out mechanism;
    (7) description of individual access mechanism;
    (8) data retention policies;
    (9) security measures.

SEC. 4. CONSUMER RIGHTS.

(a) ACCESS.—Individuals have the right to obtain from any data
broker all personal information the broker holds about them.

(b) DELETION.—Individuals have the right to request deletion of
their personal information from any data broker.

(c) OPT-OUT.—Individuals have the right to opt out of the sale of
their personal information.

(d) UNIVERSAL OPT-OUT.—The Commission shall establish a central
registry enabling individuals to opt out of data broker sales
through a single mechanism.

SEC. 5. PROHIBITED PRACTICES.

(a) SENSITIVE DATA.—Data brokers shall not collect, sell, or license
sensitive personal information unless the individual has provided
affirmative consent.

(b) LOCATION DATA.—Data brokers shall not sell precise geolocation
data without affirmative consent.

(c) GOVERNMENT SALES.—Data brokers shall not sell personal information
to government entities except pursuant to a court order.

SEC. 6. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) FTC ENFORCEMENT.—Violations subject to civil penalties of $50,000
per day per violation.

(b) PRIVATE RIGHT.—Individuals may bring actions for violations with
statutory damages of $500-$5,000 per violation.

(c) STATE ENFORCEMENT.—State attorneys general may bring actions.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect 1 year after enactment.
```

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Comprehensive Privacy Act

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Privacy Act".

SECTION 2. CONSUMER RIGHTS.

(a) A consumer has the right to—
    (1) know what personal data is collected;
    (2) access their personal data;
    (3) correct inaccurate data;
    (4) delete their data;
    (5) obtain data in portable format;
    (6) opt out of sale, targeted advertising, and profiling.

SECTION 3. CONTROLLER DUTIES.

(a) DATA MINIMIZATION.—Controllers shall limit collection to what
is reasonably necessary for disclosed purposes.

(b) PURPOSE LIMITATION.—Controllers shall not process data for
incompatible purposes without consent.

(c) SECURITY.—Controllers shall maintain reasonable security.

(d) SENSITIVE DATA.—Controllers shall obtain consent before
processing sensitive data.

SECTION 4. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) ATTORNEY GENERAL.—The Attorney General may bring actions for
violations, with civil penalties of $[X] per violation.

(b) PRIVATE RIGHT.—Consumers may bring actions for—
    (1) actual damages or $[X] statutory damages;
    (2) injunctive relief;
    (3) attorney fees.

SECTION 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect [date].
```

## Constitutional Considerations

### First Amendment

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| **Data as speech** | Commercial data regulation constitutional |
| **Compelled disclosure** | Transparency requirements permissible |
| **Commercial speech** | Lower scrutiny for commercial |
| **Editorial discretion** | Not implicated by data rules |

### Commerce Clause

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| **Federal authority** | Clear commerce power |
| **State action** | Some dormant clause limits |
| **Preemption** | Floor approach preserves states |

### Due Process

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| **Vagueness** | Terms sufficiently clear |
| **Fair notice** | Transition period provides notice |
| **Property** | Data not traditional property |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **De-identification** | Claim data is anonymized | High |
| **Consent manipulation** | Dark patterns for consent | High |
| **First-party exception** | Shift to first-party data | Medium |
| **B2B carve-out** | Business data exemption | Medium |
| **Employee data gap** | Worker data excluded | Medium |
| **Small business threshold** | Structure to stay under | Low |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| **Enforcement resources** | Limited capacity | Budget |
| **Technical complexity** | Verification difficult | Expertise |
| **Global data flows** | Cross-border issues | Jurisdiction |
| **Consent fatigue** | Undermines choice | Design |
| **Power asymmetry** | Company advantages | Structural |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Functional de-identification test** requiring verification
2. **Dark pattern prohibition** with specific examples
3. **First-party limits** applying minimization broadly
4. **Worker privacy provisions** in separate title
5. **Small business scaling** rather than exemption
6. **Technical standards** for consent interfaces
7. **International mechanisms** for data transfers
8. **Regular review** and update procedures
9. **Adequacy assessments** for enforcement resources
10. **Private enforcement** to supplement agencies
