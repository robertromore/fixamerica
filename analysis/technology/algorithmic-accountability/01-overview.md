# Algorithmic Accountability: Overview

## Executive Summary

Automated decision-making systems increasingly determine access to employment, credit, housing, healthcare, criminal justice outcomes, and government services. These algorithmic systems often operate without transparency, accountability, or meaningful oversight, perpetuating and amplifying discrimination while denying individuals the ability to understand or challenge decisions affecting their lives. Comprehensive algorithmic accountability reform requires transparency requirements, impact assessments, anti-discrimination standards, and meaningful enforcement mechanisms.

## Scope

This analysis covers:

- **Automated decision systems** in high-stakes contexts
- **Algorithmic discrimination** in employment, credit, housing
- **Government use** of algorithms in benefits, criminal justice
- **Transparency** and explainability requirements
- **Impact assessments** and auditing frameworks
- **Redress mechanisms** for algorithmic harms
- **Regulatory approaches** to algorithmic governance

## Core Problems

### Opacity and Lack of Transparency

| Problem | Impact |
|---------|--------|
| Black box systems | Decisions unexplainable |
| Trade secret claims | Shield from scrutiny |
| No disclosure requirements | Subjects unaware |
| Technical complexity | Meaningful review difficult |

### Discrimination and Bias

| Problem | Impact |
|---------|--------|
| Training data bias | Historical discrimination encoded |
| Proxy discrimination | Facially neutral inputs correlate with protected classes |
| Disparate impact | Disproportionate harm to protected groups |
| Feedback loops | Bias amplified over time |

### Lack of Accountability

| Problem | Impact |
|---------|--------|
| No impact assessments | Harm not evaluated pre-deployment |
| No auditing requirements | Ongoing bias undetected |
| Diffuse responsibility | No one accountable |
| Inadequate redress | Victims cannot challenge |

### Government Use

| Problem | Impact |
|---------|--------|
| Benefits denial | Eligible people cut off |
| Criminal justice | Biased risk assessments |
| Child welfare | Families wrongly flagged |
| Immigration | Inconsistent, discriminatory |

## Stakeholder Landscape

| Stakeholder | Interest |
|-------------|----------|
| Affected individuals | Fair treatment, ability to challenge |
| Civil rights organizations | Anti-discrimination |
| Technology companies | Flexibility, limited liability |
| Academic researchers | Study access, transparency |
| Government agencies | Efficiency, accuracy |
| Regulators | Enforcement tools |

## Reform Approaches

| Approach | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Transparency** | Disclosure and notice requirements |
| **Impact assessments** | Pre-deployment evaluation |
| **Auditing** | Ongoing bias testing |
| **Anti-discrimination** | Apply civil rights laws |
| **Redress** | Right to challenge, human review |
| **Procurement** | Government purchasing standards |
