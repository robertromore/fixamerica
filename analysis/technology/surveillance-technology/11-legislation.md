# Surveillance Technology: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Comprehensive surveillance technology reform requires federal legislation establishing baseline protections, state laws providing additional safeguards, and local ordinances ensuring community control. This document provides draft legislation for key reforms.

## Federal Legislation

### Facial Recognition and Biometric Technology Moratorium Act

**Purpose**: Establish a moratorium on government use of facial recognition pending comprehensive regulation.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Facial Recognition and Biometric
Technology Moratorium Act of 2025".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
    (1) facial recognition technology has proliferated without
    adequate legal framework or oversight;
    (2) the technology has demonstrated significant accuracy
    disparities across racial and gender groups;
    (3) wrongful arrests resulting from facial recognition
    misidentification have been documented;
    (4) mass use of facial recognition creates a pervasive
    surveillance infrastructure incompatible with democratic values;
    (5) a moratorium is necessary pending development of appropriate
    safeguards.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

(a) FACIAL RECOGNITION TECHNOLOGY.—The term "facial recognition
technology" means an automated or semi-automated process that
assists in identifying an individual or capturing information
about an individual based on the physical characteristics of the
individual's face.

(b) BIOMETRIC SURVEILLANCE.—The term "biometric surveillance"
means the use of biometric data to identify specific individuals
or track specific individuals' movements in public or semi-public
spaces.

(c) FEDERAL AGENCY.—The term "Federal agency" has the meaning
given in 5 U.S.C. § 551.

SEC. 4. MORATORIUM.

(a) PROHIBITION.—No Federal agency may—
    (1) acquire or use facial recognition technology;
    (2) use information derived from facial recognition technology
    operated by another entity; or
    (3) use biometric surveillance systems in public or semi-public
    spaces.

(b) DURATION.—The moratorium under subsection (a) shall remain
in effect until Congress enacts legislation—
    (1) establishing standards for the use of facial recognition
    and biometric surveillance;
    (2) requiring accuracy and bias testing;
    (3) establishing meaningful oversight and accountability
    mechanisms; and
    (4) providing remedies for misuse.

SEC. 5. FEDERAL FUNDING.

(a) PROHIBITION.—No Federal funds may be used by a State or
local government entity to acquire or use facial recognition
technology or biometric surveillance during the moratorium.

(b) GRANT CONDITIONS.—Any Federal grant to a State or local
government for law enforcement purposes shall include a condition
prohibiting use of grant funds for facial recognition or biometric
surveillance.

SEC. 6. EXCEPTIONS.

(a) NARROW EXCEPTIONS.—The moratorium does not apply to—
    (1) use of biometric technology for user authentication with
    consent (e.g., device unlock);
    (2) use for federal employee identity verification in secure
    facilities; or
    (3) the National Institute of Standards and Technology for
    research and testing purposes.

(b) NO LAW ENFORCEMENT EXCEPTION.—There is no exception for
law enforcement use during the moratorium.

SEC. 7. EXISTING SYSTEMS.

(a) INVENTORY.—Within 90 days of enactment, each Federal agency
shall provide to Congress an inventory of facial recognition and
biometric surveillance systems in use.

(b) CESSATION.—Use of such systems shall cease within 180 days
of enactment.

(c) DATA DELETION.—Biometric data collected by such systems
shall be deleted within 1 year of enactment, except as required
for pending litigation.

SEC. 8. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—Any individual subjected to facial
recognition or biometric surveillance in violation of this Act
may bring a civil action for—
    (1) declaratory and injunctive relief;
    (2) actual damages or $10,000 per violation, whichever is
    greater; and
    (3) reasonable attorney's fees.

(b) GOVERNMENT WAIVER.—The United States waives sovereign immunity
for actions under this section.

(c) SUPPRESSION.—Evidence obtained in violation of this Act shall
be suppressed in any proceeding.

SEC. 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect immediately upon enactment.
```

### Electronic Communications Privacy Act Modernization

**Purpose**: Update ECPA for modern technology and establish stronger protections.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "ECPA Modernization Act of 2025".

SEC. 2. STORED COMMUNICATIONS.

Section 2703 of title 18, United States Code, is amended—

(a) by striking subsection (a) and inserting the following:

"(a) WARRANT REQUIRED.—A governmental entity may require the
disclosure of the contents of any wire, oral, or electronic
communication, whether in electronic storage or otherwise held
by an electronic communication service or remote computing
service, only pursuant to a warrant issued using the procedures
described in the Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure (or, in
the case of a State court, issued using State warrant procedures).";

(b) by striking subsection (b);

(c) by striking subsection (c) and inserting:

"(b) NON-CONTENT RECORDS.—A governmental entity may require
disclosure of a record or other information pertaining to a
subscriber or customer (not including the contents of communications)
only pursuant to—
    "(1) a warrant; or
    "(2) a court order based on specific and articulable facts
    showing reasonable grounds to believe the records are relevant
    and material to an ongoing criminal investigation.".

SEC. 3. LOCATION INFORMATION.

(a) WARRANT REQUIRED.—Section 2703 of title 18, United States
Code, is amended by adding at the end:

"(h) LOCATION INFORMATION.—
    "(1) A governmental entity may not obtain location information
    from an electronic communication service, a remote computing
    service, or by any other means without a warrant.
    "(2) The warrant shall describe with particularity—
        "(A) the person whose location is sought;
        "(B) the time period covered; and
        "(C) the purpose of the collection.
    "(3) DEFINITION.—'Location information' means any information
    concerning the location of an electronic device, including
    real-time location, historical location, cell site location
    information, and GPS coordinates.".

SEC. 4. GEOFENCE PROHIBITION.

(a) PROHIBITION.—Section 2703 of title 18, United States Code,
is amended by adding:

"(i) GEOFENCE WARRANTS PROHIBITED.—
    "(1) No warrant may be issued requiring a provider to disclose
    the identities of all users or devices located within a
    specified geographic area during a specified time period
    ('geofence warrant').
    "(2) A warrant for location information must identify the
    specific person or device whose location is sought.".

SEC. 5. CELL SITE SIMULATORS.

Section 2511 of title 18, United States Code, is amended by adding:

"(5) CELL SITE SIMULATORS.—
    "(a) Use of a cell site simulator or similar device to intercept
    communications, obtain location information, or collect device
    identifiers requires a warrant based on probable cause.
    "(b) A warrant shall—
        "(1) identify the specific person or device targeted;
        "(2) minimize collection of third-party information; and
        "(3) require deletion of third-party information within 48 hours.
    "(c) Any interception of communications content requires a
    Title III order.".

SEC. 6. DATA BROKER PURCHASES.

(a) PROHIBITION.—Section 2703 of title 18, United States Code,
is amended by adding:

"(j) DATA PURCHASES.—
    "(1) A governmental entity may not purchase, receive, or
    otherwise obtain from a private entity any data that the
    governmental entity would require a warrant to collect directly.
    "(2) This prohibition applies to location information, contents
    of communications, and other protected data regardless of
    whether the private entity obtained such data with consent.".

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect 180 days after enactment.
```

### Community Control Over Police Surveillance (Federal)

**Purpose**: Require community approval and oversight of surveillance technology acquisition.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Community Control Over Police
Surveillance Act of 2025".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
    (1) surveillance technology has proliferated in communities
    across America without meaningful public input;
    (2) affected communities, particularly communities of color,
    have historically been subject to over-surveillance;
    (3) democratic oversight of surveillance is essential; and
    (4) community control ensures accountability.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

(a) SURVEILLANCE TECHNOLOGY.—The term "surveillance technology"
means any electronic device, system, or software designed or
primarily used to collect, retain, process, or share audio,
electronic, visual, location, thermal, biometric, olfactory,
or similar information about individuals, including—
    (1) facial recognition technology;
    (2) cell site simulators;
    (3) automatic license plate readers;
    (4) gunshot detection systems;
    (5) social media monitoring software;
    (6) predictive policing software;
    (7) body-worn cameras;
    (8) unmanned aerial vehicles (drones);
    (9) biometric identification technology; and
    (10) other technology designated by regulation.

SEC. 4. CONDITION ON FEDERAL FUNDING.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—As a condition of receiving Federal law enforcement
assistance funds, a State or local government shall—
    (1) maintain a public inventory of all surveillance technology
    used by law enforcement;
    (2) publish use policies for each technology;
    (3) obtain legislative body approval before acquiring new
    surveillance technology;
    (4) conduct and publish annual reports on surveillance
    technology use; and
    (5) establish a process for community input.

(b) LEGISLATIVE APPROVAL.—Before acquiring surveillance technology,
a law enforcement agency must—
    (1) publish a proposed use policy;
    (2) provide a civil liberties impact assessment;
    (3) hold a public hearing with community input; and
    (4) obtain approval from the governing legislative body.

(c) ANNUAL REPORTS.—Annual reports shall include—
    (1) how the technology was used;
    (2) compliance with use policies;
    (3) costs and funding sources;
    (4) complaints received; and
    (5) outcomes from use.

SEC. 5. CIVIL RIGHTS IMPACT ASSESSMENT.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Before approving surveillance technology, the
legislative body shall receive a civil rights impact assessment
addressing—
    (1) potential disparate impacts on protected groups;
    (2) First Amendment implications;
    (3) Fourth Amendment implications;
    (4) data security measures; and
    (5) proposed safeguards.

SEC. 6. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) FUND WITHHOLDING.—Failure to comply with this Act shall
result in withholding of Federal law enforcement assistance funds.

(b) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—Individuals may bring actions to
enforce the transparency provisions of this Act.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect 1 year after enactment.
```

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Facial Recognition Act

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Facial Recognition Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Facial recognition technology" means technology that analyzes
facial features to identify specific individuals.

(b) "Government entity" means any state or local government agency.

SECTION 3. PROHIBITION.

(a) No government entity shall use facial recognition technology.

(b) No government entity shall—
    (1) request facial recognition searches from other entities;
    (2) use information derived from facial recognition; or
    (3) contract for facial recognition services.

SECTION 4. EXCEPTIONS.

(a) This prohibition does not apply to—
    (1) personal device authentication with consent; or
    (2) redaction of faces to protect privacy.

SECTION 5. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) Violations are subject to civil penalties of $[X] per incident.

(b) Individuals subjected to facial recognition may bring civil
actions for actual damages or $[X] per violation, plus attorney's
fees.

(c) Evidence obtained through facial recognition is inadmissible.

SECTION 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect immediately upon enactment.
```

### Model State Location Privacy Act

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Location Privacy Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Location information" means data concerning the past or
present location of an electronic device.

(b) "Government entity" means any state or local government agency.

SECTION 3. WARRANT REQUIREMENT.

(a) A government entity may not obtain location information
without a warrant based on probable cause.

(b) The warrant shall specify—
    (1) the person or device;
    (2) the time period; and
    (3) the basis for probable cause.

SECTION 4. GEOFENCE PROHIBITION.

(a) No warrant may be issued for the location information of
all persons within a geographic area (geofence warrant).

SECTION 5. DATA PURCHASE PROHIBITION.

(a) A government entity may not purchase location information
from any private entity.

SECTION 6. LICENSE PLATE READERS.

(a) Data from automated license plate readers shall be deleted
within 30 days unless part of an active investigation.

(b) Access to license plate reader data requires articulable
suspicion documented in writing.

SECTION 7. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) Violations result in suppression of evidence.

(b) Individuals may bring civil actions for $[X] per violation.

SECTION 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect [date].
```

## Constitutional Considerations

### Fourth Amendment

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| **Facial recognition** | Public use may lack search protection |
| **Location tracking** | *Carpenter* requires warrant |
| **Aggregation** | Mosaic theory supports protection |
| **Third-party doctrine** | Eroding but still relevant |

### First Amendment

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| **Chilling effect** | Surveillance inhibits expression |
| **Assembly** | Protest monitoring concerns |
| **Association** | Mapping relationships |
| **Anonymity** | May have First Amendment dimension |

### Equal Protection

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| **Disparate impact** | Facial recognition accuracy disparities |
| **Targeted communities** | Over-surveillance |
| **Discriminatory enforcement** | Selective deployment |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Third-party workaround** | Use private vendors | High |
| **Data purchase** | Buy what can't collect | High |
| **Parallel construction** | Hide surveillance use | High |
| **Federal preemption claims** | Block state/local action | Medium |
| **Definition gaming** | Technology not covered | Medium |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| **Enforcement gaps** | Violations unpunished | Resources |
| **Secrecy** | Hidden from courts | Culture |
| **Technology evolution** | Law lags | Speed |
| **Political will** | Weak implementation | Politics |
| **Interstate differences** | Inconsistent protection | Federalism |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Third-party coverage** explicitly including vendors
2. **Data purchase prohibition** closing warrant bypass
3. **Anti-parallel construction** requiring disclosure
4. **Federal floor** preserving state/local authority
5. **Functional definitions** capturing new technologies
6. **Private enforcement** supplementing government
7. **Mandatory exclusion** for violations
8. **Damages provisions** creating incentives
9. **Reporting requirements** enabling oversight
10. **Regular review** updating for technology
