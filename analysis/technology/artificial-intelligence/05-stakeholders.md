# Artificial Intelligence: Stakeholders

## Overview

AI development and governance involves diverse stakeholders with varying interests, power levels, and positions on key issues. Understanding this landscape is essential for building coalitions, anticipating opposition, and designing politically viable solutions.

## Stakeholder Map

### Industry Actors

#### Foundation Model Companies

| Company | Position | Resources | Influence |
|---------|----------|-----------|-----------|
| **OpenAI** | Balanced rhetoric, fast deployment | $10B+ Microsoft backing | Very high |
| **Anthropic** | Safety-focused, supports regulation | $7B+ from Google/Amazon | High |
| **Google/DeepMind** | Innovation focus, cautious on regulation | Enormous internal resources | Very high |
| **Meta AI** | Open source strategy, limited regulation | Large internal resources | High |
| **Microsoft** | Responsible AI rhetoric, OpenAI partner | Enormous resources | Very high |

**Common interests:**
- Favorable regulatory environment
- Access to compute and data
- Talent recruitment
- IP protection
- International competitiveness

**Differences:**
- Open vs. closed models
- Pace of deployment
- Safety investment level
- Regulation specifics

## Coalition Possibilities

### Safety-Focused Coalition

**Members**: Safety orgs, concerned researchers, some companies (Anthropic), think tanks

**Agreement on:**
- Testing requirements for frontier models
- Reporting of dangerous capabilities
- Safety research investment

**Tensions:**
- Open vs. closed models
- Pace of regulation
- Definition of "frontier"

### Civil Rights Coalition

**Members**: Civil rights groups, labor, community organizations, some academics

**Agreement on:**
- Algorithmic discrimination prohibition
- Transparency requirements
- Private right of action
- Worker protections

**Tensions:**
- Technology specifics
- Enforcement mechanisms
- Industry engagement

### Innovation Coalition

**Members**: Tech companies, VC, some think tanks, some academics

**Agreement on:**
- Avoid overregulation
- Federal preemption
- Self-regulation role
- R&D investment

**Tensions:**
- Safety requirements
- Liability
- Competition policy

| Stakeholder | Primary Demand | Secondary Demands |
|-------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Big Tech | Light regulation | Preemption, subsidies |
| Safety orgs | Testing requirements | Transparency, oversight |
| Civil rights | Discrimination prohibition | Transparency, enforcement |
| Labor | Worker protections | Transition support, voice |
| Academics | Research access | Funding, independence |
| State AGs | Enforcement authority | No preemption |
| Defense | Capability development | Less restriction |
| EU | US alignment with AI Act | Mutual recognition |

## Engagement Strategies

### For Advocates

**Building power:**
1. Demonstrate public concern
2. Document harms with evidence
3. Build unlikely coalitions
4. Develop policy expertise
5. Support affected communities

**Pressure points:**
- Congressional hearings
- Shareholder resolutions
- Public campaigns
- State-level action
- International standards

### For Policymakers

**Stakeholder management:**
1. Engage diverse voices
2. Resist capture
3. Seek independent expertise
4. Include affected communities
5. Balance interests transparently
