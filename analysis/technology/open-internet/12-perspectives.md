# Open Internet: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes open internet policy -- specifically net neutrality regulation, broadband competition, platform transparency and accountability, Section 230 reform, and infrastructure investment -- through nine distinct political perspectives. The core questions are: should broadband providers be regulated as common carriers or left to market competition, how should the tension between free expression online and platform content moderation power be managed, what role should government play in preserving an open internet versus allowing private actors to set the terms of access, and how should competing visions of internet freedom -- freedom from government regulation versus freedom from corporate gatekeeping -- be reconciled? Each perspective receives engagement-consistency ratings and position scores, culminating in compromise proposals that bridge multiple viewpoints.

The open internet occupies a uniquely contested political space because it combines elements of telecommunications regulation, free speech law, antitrust policy, and infrastructure investment. The fundamental tension is between those who view the open internet as requiring government protection from corporate gatekeepers and those who view government regulation as itself the primary threat to internet freedom. The U.S. has oscillated between regulatory approaches -- the FCC adopted net neutrality rules in 2015 under Title II, repealed them in 2017, and attempted to restore them in 2024 amid ongoing litigation. Meanwhile, approximately 50% of American households have access to only one or two broadband providers, creating the market power that makes net neutrality protections necessary. Platform content moderation has become a flashpoint as well, with conservatives alleging political bias while progressives demand stronger action against misinformation and hate speech. Section 230 of the Communications Act, which shields platforms from liability for user-generated content, faces reform pressure from both parties for entirely different reasons. The combination of regulatory instability, market concentration, platform power, and partisan polarization creates conditions where political perspectives diverge sharply on both the diagnosis of problems and the appropriate solutions.

### Engagement Consistency (1-5)

This metric assesses how reliably a perspective's stated positions align with its underlying principles, and how stable those positions are when concerns are addressed. Higher scores indicate more predictable negotiating partners.

| Level | Label | Indicators |
|-------|-------|------------|
| 5 | Highly Consistent | Stated positions reflect underlying principles; positions stable when concerns addressed |
| 4 | Mostly Consistent | Principles generally align with positions; minor strategic flexibility |
| 3 | Mixed Consistency | Some positions principled, others reflect coalition pressures or unstated concerns |
| 2 | Low Consistency | Positions shift frequently; stated concerns differ from underlying priorities |
| 1 | Unpredictable | Positions appear driven primarily by opposition dynamics rather than principles |

**Coalition-Building Considerations:**

- Alignment gap: When stated concerns differ from underlying priorities, addressing stated concerns may not secure support
- Position stability: Perspectives with shifting positions require ongoing negotiation
- Principle consistency: Perspectives that apply principles uniformly are more predictable partners
- Internal diversity: Some perspectives contain factions with different priorities

## Perspective Analyses

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 2/5 - Low Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Stated commitment to "free markets" and "light-touch regulation" should produce opposition to ISP monopoly power and support for competition, but conservatives have generally defended incumbent ISPs against both regulation and competitive entry from municipal broadband; the recent shift toward demanding government intervention against Big Tech content moderation directly contradicts the anti-regulation principle applied to ISPs |
| Principle consistency | "Free market" principles are applied to oppose net neutrality and Title II regulation of ISPs but abandoned when demanding government intervention in platform content moderation decisions; "free speech" is invoked to oppose platform moderation of conservative content but not to defend platforms' editorial discretion; "limited government" opposes FCC broadband regulation but supports state attorneys general investigating social media companies |
| Goalpost stability | Low; conservative positions on internet regulation have shifted substantially in the past decade -- from broadly opposing all internet regulation (including net neutrality and platform regulation) to demanding government regulation of platform content moderation while still opposing ISP regulation; the shift correlates with perceived political disadvantage on platforms rather than a principled reassessment |
| Zero-sum behavior | High; internet policy is increasingly framed as a battleground in the culture war; willingness to support or oppose regulation depends heavily on whether the target is a perceived ally (ISPs) or perceived adversary (Big Tech platforms) |

**Justification:** Conservatives face a deep contradiction on open internet policy: the same "limited government" and "free market" principles that they invoke to oppose net neutrality regulation of ISPs should also counsel against government regulation of platform content moderation -- yet the political dynamics of perceived Big Tech bias have produced a sharp reversal. The score of 2 reflects the fact that stated principles no longer reliably predict conservative positions; whether a given internet regulation is supported or opposed depends primarily on whether the target is an ISP (oppose regulation) or a content platform (support regulation), which is a political calculation rather than a principled one.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Republican FCC commissioners consistently opposed Title II classification and net neutrality while Republican state officials sued platforms over content moderation
- Conservative position shifted from opposing the Fairness Doctrine (government should not regulate media speech) to supporting laws requiring platform "neutrality" in content moderation
- Heritage Foundation opposes net neutrality as government overreach but supports government intervention against platform content decisions
- Republican-led states enacted laws (Texas HB 20, Florida SB 7072) mandating platform carriage of content -- a direct content regulation that conflicts with anti-regulation principles

#### Alternative Proposals

- Repeal or significantly reform Section 230 to increase platform liability for content moderation decisions
- State laws requiring platforms to carry political speech without viewpoint discrimination
- Tax incentives for private broadband deployment in unserved areas
- Antitrust action against Big Tech platforms (but not ISPs)
- Streamlined permitting and deregulation to encourage ISP deployment
- FTC enforcement of platform neutrality rather than FCC regulation

#### Coalition Potential

- **Natural allies:** Religious Right (on platform content concerns), Populist (on anti-Big Tech sentiment)
- **Potential bridges:** Libertarian (on ISP deregulation but not platform regulation), Centrist (on targeted platform transparency)
- **Unlikely partners:** Progressive, Democratic Socialist (on net neutrality, Title II, public broadband)

**Justification:** Liberals have developed a coherent open internet framework that applies regulatory tools consistently: net neutrality for ISPs, transparency for platforms, competition for markets, and consumer protection for users. **Justification:** Progressives have the most internally consistent open internet framework because their structural analysis -- corporate control of essential communications infrastructure is the root problem -- produces clear, predictable positions across all dimensions. The score of 5 reflects both the coherence of the analytical framework and the transparency of progressive positions.

### Compromise 1: Internet Transparency and Consumer Protection Act

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Progressive + Populist + Centrist + Constitutionalist + Religious Right + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** Both broadband providers and online platforms wield significant power over Americans' internet experience, and transparency about how that power is exercised -- including ISP network practices, platform content moderation decisions, and algorithmic systems -- is a necessary prerequisite for informed consumer choice and effective public accountability.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation establishes comprehensive internet transparency requirements for both broadband providers and online platforms. ISPs with more than 100,000 subscribers must publish standardized broadband nutrition labels disclosing actual speeds, prices (all-in with no hidden fees), data caps, and network management practices. Covered online platforms (50 million+ monthly U.S. users) must publish quarterly transparency reports on content moderation actions, including volume removed by category, removal methods (automated vs. human), appeals and outcomes, and government requests. Platforms must disclose the use of algorithmic ranking systems and provide users the option to use chronological feeds. Meaningful appeals processes with human review are required for content moderation decisions. Qualified researcher access to platform data is authorized with appropriate privacy protections. The FTC enforces platform provisions and the FCC enforces ISP provisions, with state attorneys general authorized to bring supplementary enforcement actions. Consumer complaint processes are established at both agencies with publicly reported resolution data. An annual report to Congress tracks ISP and platform transparency compliance and identifies emerging concerns.

### Compromise 2: Broadband Competition and Local Choice Act

**Bridges:** Liberal + Progressive + Populist + Centrist + Constitutionalist + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** Broadband competition benefits consumers through lower prices and better service, and local communities should have the authority to pursue broadband alternatives -- including municipal networks, cooperatives, and public-private partnerships -- without state-level restrictions enacted at the behest of incumbent ISPs.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation establishes that no state law shall prohibit or effectively prohibit any municipality, county, public utility district, or cooperative from providing broadband service. States retain authority to impose reasonable, nondiscriminatory requirements applicable to all broadband providers. Pole attachment reform ensures timely access to utility infrastructure for all broadband providers with one-touch make-ready for simple attachments. Dig-once requirements mandate conduit installation during federally funded highway projects. A $3 billion federal revolving loan fund provides low-interest financing for community broadband projects. Municipal and cooperative networks receiving federal support must provide open access on wholesale terms. Franchise reform streamlines local approval processes with 120-day decision deadlines. Technical assistance is provided to communities considering broadband alternatives. Annual reporting tracks community broadband deployment, pricing, and performance relative to incumbent providers.

| Centrist | Accept federal preemption of state laws; accept that some community projects may fail |
| Constitutionalist | Accept federal preemption of state restrictions on local authority |
| Democratic Socialist | Accept open access that allows private providers on public networks |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 6/9 perspectives (Conservative opposes federal preemption and public broadband; Libertarian opposes government ownership; Religious Right defers to conservative coalition)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 5/6 coalition members rated 4+ consistency (Liberal, Progressive, Centrist, Constitutionalist, Democratic Socialist)
- **Durability:** High; once communities build broadband networks, the infrastructure creates permanent constituencies; open access provisions create competitive markets; revolving loan fund becomes self-sustaining
- **Implementation Path:** Frame as "local choice" and "community empowerment" rather than "government broadband"; build on successful municipal broadband examples (Chattanooga, Wilson, Longmont); electric cooperative model provides rural framing; bipartisan co-sponsorship from rural and urban legislators

### Compromise 3: Net Neutrality Codification Act

**Bridges:** Liberal + Progressive + Centrist + Constitutionalist + Populist + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** The oscillating FCC classification of broadband -- from information service to telecommunications service and back -- has created harmful regulatory instability that serves neither consumers nor providers, and Congress should resolve this uncertainty by enacting statutory net neutrality protections that do not depend on which party controls the FCC.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation codifies bright-line net neutrality rules: broadband providers may not block lawful content, throttle or degrade lawful traffic, or engage in paid prioritization. A general conduct standard prohibits unreasonable interference with users' internet access. Transparency requirements mandate standardized disclosure of network management practices. Reasonable network management is permitted for legitimate technical purposes. The FCC receives explicit statutory authority to enforce these provisions through investigation, penalties (up to $100,000 per violation per day), and expedited complaint procedures. State attorneys general may bring supplementary enforcement actions. States may adopt additional protections consistent with the federal floor. Interconnection oversight authority enables the FCC to review peering disputes under a just-and-reasonable standard. The legislation does not reclassify broadband under Title II but provides standalone statutory authority for net neutrality enforcement, avoiding the broader common carrier framework. Judicial review is available in federal appeals courts with a 60-day filing deadline.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Liberal | Permanent statutory net neutrality ends regulatory oscillation; bright-line rules with enforcement teeth; state authority preserved; interconnection oversight addresses backbone-level discrimination |
| Progressive | Codified protections with meaningful penalties; interconnection authority addresses often-overlooked choke point; state authority enables stronger state-level protections |
| Centrist | Congressional resolution of regulatory instability; avoids full Title II while codifying core protections; bipartisan structure; enforceable rules with practical flexibility |
| Constitutionalist | Congressional action resolves the administrative authority question; clear statutory delegation replaces contested FCC reclassification; judicial review protects due process |
| Populist | ISPs cannot block, throttle, or charge extra for access; regular people get equal treatment online; corporations face real penalties |
| Democratic Socialist | Net neutrality protections are codified permanently; interconnection authority prevents backbone-level discrimination; state floor allows stronger state protections |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Liberal | Accept standalone statutory authority rather than full Title II classification |
| Progressive | Accept net neutrality codification without broader utility regulation or rate authority |
| Centrist | Accept the scope of enforcement penalties and state enforcement authority |
| Constitutionalist | Accept federal preemption establishing a floor for state net neutrality laws |
| Populist | Accept a regulatory framework rather than direct antitrust breakup |
| Democratic Socialist | Accept net neutrality without public ownership provisions or rate regulation |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 6/9 perspectives (Conservative opposes net neutrality legislation; Libertarian opposes all internet regulation; Religious Right defers to conservative coalition on ISP regulation)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 5/6 coalition members rated 4+ consistency (Liberal, Progressive, Centrist, Constitutionalist, Democratic Socialist)
- **Durability:** High; statutory codification cannot be reversed by FCC reclassification and requires congressional action to repeal; the approach has survived multiple court challenges when properly authorized; state enforcement creates distributed accountability
- **Implementation Path:** Frame as resolving uncertainty and providing stability for investment; emphasize that this codifies rules that were in place for years and enjoyed broad public support; standalone statutory authority avoids the politically charged Title II debate; bipartisan support available from members who supported net neutrality but opposed full Title II classification

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The most effective open internet reform coalition operates on three tracks. The broadest coalition (8/9 perspectives) can advance transparency -- both ISP and platform -- because every perspective benefits from more information about corporate practices, even if they disagree about what to do with that information. A center-left coalition with populist support (6/9 perspectives) can advance net neutrality codification through standalone legislation that avoids the politically charged Title II classification, attracting constitutionalist support through proper congressional authorization. Competition policy also draws 7/9 perspectives through anti-monopoly framing that resonates across the ideological spectrum. Platform accountability is the wild card: while dissatisfaction with platforms is nearly universal, the specific reforms each perspective demands are often mutually contradictory (conservatives want less moderation, progressives want more accountability for harmful content), making specific legislation difficult despite shared discontent.

### Key Obstacles

- **ISP-platform asymmetry in conservative positions:** Conservatives apply fundamentally different standards to ISPs (oppose regulation) and platforms (demand regulation), making comprehensive open internet legislation that addresses both extremely difficult; any bill that regulates ISPs will lose conservative support, and any bill that regulates platforms will need to navigate contradictory demands
- **Section 230 as political quicksand:** Despite near-universal dissatisfaction with Section 230, every perspective wants different reforms -- conservatives want to punish moderation, progressives want to address algorithmic amplification, populists want to increase liability, and libertarians want to preserve immunity; specific legislation inevitably alienates more perspectives than it attracts
- **Regulatory oscillation:** The FCC's broadband classification has changed with every administration change, creating policy instability that can only be resolved by congressional action; but congressional action requires bipartisan support that has been elusive on internet regulation
- **Industry lobbying:** The broadband and technology industries collectively spend hundreds of millions annually on lobbying and campaign contributions, maintaining the regulatory fragmentation that serves their interests; ISPs lobby against net neutrality while platforms lobby against Section 230 reform, effectively blocking comprehensive legislation
- **First Amendment complexity:** Platform content regulation faces genuine First Amendment constraints that limit the scope of achievable reform; compelled carriage requirements (demanded by conservatives) are constitutionally suspect, as are content-specific mandates

### Low Consistency Partners

Conservative (2/5) and Religious Right (2/5) perspectives rate at low consistency on open internet policy, reflecting contradictory positions on government regulation of ISPs versus platforms. Conservative support is most reliable on platform transparency (which exposes perceived anti-conservative bias) and least reliable on ISP regulation (where industry alliance interests dominate); addressing stated concerns about "internet freedom" may not secure support because the underlying priority is differential treatment of ISPs and platforms. Religious Right support is most genuine on content safety and religious expression protection and least independently motivated on infrastructure and competition questions where positions are imported from the conservative coalition. Populist (3/5) support is most accessible through anti-monopoly framing and least accessible when solutions are framed as regulatory expansion.

### Recommended Approach

1. **Lead with transparency:** The Internet Transparency and Consumer Protection Act (Compromise 1) commands 8/9 perspective support and creates the informational foundation for all subsequent reforms; ISP broadband labels and platform transparency reports are practical, popular, and constitutionally defensible; frame as consumer empowerment rather than regulation

2. **Codify net neutrality through standalone legislation:** The Net Neutrality Codification Act (Compromise 3) commands 6/9 perspective support by resolving the classification debate through targeted congressional action; avoids the politically toxic Title II label while achieving the same practical protections; constitutionalist support strengthens the coalition

3. **Enable broadband competition and community choice:** The Broadband Competition and Local Choice Act (Compromise 2) commands 6/9 perspective support and addresses the market structure conditions that make net neutrality necessary; "local choice" framing neutralizes the "government broadband" objection; successful community broadband examples provide the evidence base

4. **Pursue platform accountability incrementally:** Given the contradictory demands that different perspectives place on platform reform, pursue transparency and research access first (which enjoy broad support), then use the resulting evidence to build coalitions for specific accountability measures; avoid comprehensive Section 230 reform until the contradictory demands can be reconciled

5. **Use accountability to build trust for regulation:** Transparency programs with strong compliance mechanisms demonstrate that government can effectively oversee internet policy, building the political foundation for stronger regulatory action over time; each successful reform creates the institutional capacity and public support for the next step

6. **Separate ISP and platform tracks legislatively:** Given the asymmetric treatment that conservatives apply to ISPs and platforms, pursuing separate legislation for each domain avoids the problem of trading ISP regulation (which conservatives oppose) for platform regulation (which conservatives support); separate bills can each attract their natural coalition
