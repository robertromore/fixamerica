# Gig Economy: Stakeholders

## Overview

The gig economy involves a complex web of stakeholders with varying interests, power, and resources. Understanding these stakeholders is essential for designing reforms that can build winning coalitions and address opposition.

## Gig Workers

### Demographics and Categories

**Primary Gig Workers** (gig work is main income)

| Characteristic | Profile |
|----------------|---------|
| Estimated number | 2-4 million workers |
| Average hours | 30-50 hours/week |
| Demographics | Disproportionately Black, Latino, immigrant |
| Education | Less likely to have college degree |
| Motivation | Lack of other employment options |
| Dependency | High; need platform income to survive |

**Supplemental Gig Workers** (gig work alongside other income)

| Characteristic | Profile |
|----------------|---------|
| Estimated number | 10-20 million workers |
| Average hours | 5-20 hours/week |
| Demographics | More diverse, including students, retirees |
| Education | More likely to have college degree |
| Motivation | Extra income, flexibility |
| Dependency | Lower; can survive without gig income |

**Platform-Specific Demographics**

| Platform | Typical Worker Profile |
|----------|----------------------|
| Uber/Lyft | Male, 30-50, immigrant/minority, full-time |
| DoorDash | Younger, more diverse, often supplemental |
| Instacart | More female, broader age range |
| TaskRabbit | Skilled trades, varied ages |
| Care.com | Predominantly female, varied ages |
| Upwork | Professional, educated, global |

### Worker Interests

**Primary Interests**

| Interest | Priority Level |
|----------|---------------|
| Higher, stable earnings | Highest |
| Access to benefits (health, retirement) | Very High |
| Protection from arbitrary deactivation | High |
| Transparency in pay and algorithms | High |
| Workplace safety protections | High |
| Right to organize and bargain | Medium-High |
| Schedule flexibility (genuine) | Medium |
| Unemployment insurance access | Medium |

**Internal Divisions**

| Divide | Groups |
|--------|--------|
| Full-time vs. part-time | Different needs for benefits, stability |
| By platform | Competition rather than solidarity |
| By geography | Urban vs. suburban vs. rural experiences |
| By status | Those who can exit vs. those who can't |
| Ideological | Some embrace contractor identity |

### Worker Power Resources

**Strengths**

| Resource | Application |
|----------|-------------|
| Numbers | Millions of workers |
| Essential services | Cities depend on delivery, rideshare |
| Public sympathy | Stories of exploitation generate support |
| Diverse coalition potential | Connect with traditional labor, civil rights |
| Electoral potential | Large voting bloc in key states |

**Weaknesses**

| Limitation | Effect |
|------------|--------|
| Geographic dispersion | Hard to organize |
| High turnover | Unstable organizing base |
| Legal status | NLRA doesn't protect contractor organizing |
| Arbitration clauses | Block collective legal action |
| Platform surveillance | Fear of retaliation |
| Divided interests | Full-time vs. part-time; multi-apping |

### Worker Organizations

**Major Groups**

| Organization | Focus |
|--------------|-------|

| Rideshare Drivers United | LA-based, driver organizing |
| Los Deliveristas Unidos | NYC delivery workers |

| National Domestic Workers Alliance | Care workers, including platform |

### Platform Interests

**Primary Objectives**

| Interest | Priority |
|----------|----------|
| Maintain contractor classification | Existential |
| Preserve algorithmic control | Very High |
| Block organizing rights for workers | Very High |
| Prevent wage floor mandates | High |
| Avoid benefits mandates | High |
| Maintain arbitration clauses | High |
| Shape favorable regulation | Medium-High |
| Public image as innovators | Medium |

**Business Model Dependencies**

| Dependency | Why It Matters |
|------------|----------------|
| Low labor costs | Core to profitability |
| Flexible labor supply | Surge capacity without commitment |
| Externalized costs | Workers bear vehicle, benefits costs |
| Scale | Network effects require mass of workers |
| Regulatory moat | Once established, hard to dislodge |

### Platform Resources

**Financial**

| Resource | Amount |
|----------|--------|
| Lobbying (annual) | $100+ million (all major platforms) |
| Ballot measure spending | $200+ million (Prop 22) |
| Legal teams | Top firms on retainer |
| Cash reserves | Billions available |

**Political**

| Resource | Application |
|----------|-------------|
| Consumer mobilization | In-app advocacy against regulation |
| Executive relationships | Tech-government revolving door |
| Media access | Friendly coverage, advertising dollars |
| Research funding | Academic studies supporting positions |
| Trade associations | TechNet, Internet Association, Chamber |

**Operational**

| Resource | Use |
|----------|-----|
| Data/algorithms | Understand workers better than regulators |
| App control | Can adjust practices rapidly |
| Geographic flexibility | Threaten to exit hostile jurisdictions |
| Worker recruitment | Endless supply of replacements |

## Traditional Employers and Industries

### Affected Industries

**Transportation**

| Affected Party | Impact |
|----------------|--------|
| Taxi companies | Decimated by rideshare competition |
| Taxi drivers | Lost value of medallions, employment |
| Limousine services | Lost high-end market |
| Delivery companies (traditional) | Undercut on price |
| Trucking companies | Facing platform competition |

**Service Industries**

| Affected Party | Impact |
|----------------|--------|
| Restaurants | Dependent on delivery apps; high fees |
| Retail stores | Competing with gig-enabled delivery |
| Home service companies | Undercut by task platforms |
| Care agencies | Competing with Care.com model |

### Traditional Employer Interests

**Concerns**

| Interest | Explanation |
|----------|-------------|
| Level playing field | Don't want to compete against misclassification |
| Enforcement of existing law | Unfair competition if laws not enforced |
| No race to bottom | Pressure to misclassify own workers |
| Industry standards | Preserve quality/safety standards |

**Alignment with Reform**

| Issue | Position |
|-------|----------|
| ABC test adoption | Generally supportive |
| Enforcement funding | Supportive |
| Portable benefits | Mixed (may raise all labor costs) |
| Platform regulation | Supportive |

### Traditional Employer Resources

**Strengths**

| Resource | Use |
|----------|-----|
| Established lobbying | Trade associations, chamber memberships |
| Regulatory relationships | Existing ties to transportation regulators |
| Legal expertise | Experience with labor law compliance |
| Worker relationships | Employees as advocates |

**Limitations**

| Limitation | Effect |
|------------|--------|
| Smaller scale | Less money than platform giants |
| Fragmented | Many small businesses vs. unified platforms |
| Conflicted | Some want to adopt platform model |
| Defensive | Fighting to survive, not expand |

## Government and Regulators

### Federal Agencies

**Department of Labor**

| Interest | Position |
|----------|----------|
| Enforce labor law | Classification enforcement |
| Protect workers | Support employee classification |
| Administrative efficiency | Clearer rules easier to enforce |
| Political constraints | Subject to administration changes |

**National Labor Relations Board**

| Interest | Position |
|----------|----------|
| Protect organizing rights | Gig workers currently excluded |
| Expand jurisdiction | Include gig workers as employees |
| Precedent setting | Amazon/Uber cases |

**Federal Trade Commission**

| Interest | Position |
|----------|----------|
| Consumer protection | Algorithmic fairness |
| Competition | Platform market power |
| Unfair practices | Deceptive worker classification |

**Internal Revenue Service**

| Interest | Position |
|----------|----------|
| Tax compliance | Misclassification loses revenue |
| Enforcement | Classification audits |
| Administrative burden | Contractor model complex |

### State Regulators

**State Labor Departments**

| Interest | Position |
|----------|----------|
| Enforce state labor law | Varies by administration |
| Collect unemployment insurance | Platforms don't pay |
| Workers' compensation | Platforms don't contribute |
| Budget constraints | Limited enforcement capacity |

**State Attorneys General**

| Interest | Position |
|----------|----------|
| Enforce consumer/worker protection | Active in several states |
| Build political profile | High-visibility cases |
| State revenue | Tax implications |
| Coalition building | Multi-state actions |

**Public Utility/Transportation Commissions**

| Interest | Position |
|----------|----------|
| Regulate transportation | Rideshare oversight |
| Safety standards | Vehicle, driver requirements |
| Consumer protection | Price, service standards |
| Industry stability | Balance innovation and fairness |

## Consumers

### Consumer Interests

**Immediate Interests**

| Interest | Priority |
|----------|----------|
| Low prices | High |
| Convenience | High |
| Service availability | High |
| Quality/safety | Medium |
| Worker treatment | Lower (often invisible) |

**Latent Interests**

| Interest | When Relevant |
|----------|---------------|
| Worker welfare | When stories emerge |
| Fair treatment | When personally affected |
| Community impact | When visible |
| Long-term sustainability | Economic security concerns |

### Consumer Behavior

| Behavior | Implication |
|----------|-------------|
| Price-sensitive | Platforms subsidize with worker exploitation |
| Convenience-driven | Value speed over worker conditions |
| Tip variably | Significant income for workers |
| Low awareness | Don't know business model details |
| Potential for change | Education can shift behavior |

### Consumer Advocacy

**Potential Allies**

Some consumer groups support worker protections:

- Recognition that exploited workers provide worse service
- Safety concerns (tired, overworked drivers)
- Community impact concerns
- Values-based consumption

**Challenges**

- Immediate price/convenience often wins
- Limited visibility into worker conditions
- Platform narratives about "disruption" and choice
