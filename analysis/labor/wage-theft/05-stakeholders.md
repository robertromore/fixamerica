# Wage Theft: Stakeholders

## Overview

Wage theft involves a complex ecosystem of stakeholders with varying levels of power, interest, and influence. Understanding these stakeholders is essential for building effective coalitions for reform and anticipating opposition.

## Workers Affected by Wage Theft

### Low-Wage Workers

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Data |
|----------------|------|
| Number earning under $15/hour | 52 million |
| Minimum wage violation rate | 17% of low-wage workers |
| Average theft per victim | $2,634/year |
| As percentage of income | 17% |

**Key Concerns**
- Immediate financial survival
- Fear of retaliation/job loss
- Limited knowledge of rights
- No resources for legal action
- Often multiple jobs, can't take time off

**Political Power**
- Low individual power
- Potential collective power through organizing
- Often don't vote or participate politically
- Increasingly organized through worker centers

### Immigrant Workers

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Data |
|----------------|------|
| Foreign-born workers | 29 million |
| Undocumented workers | 8 million |
| Violation rate (undocumented) | 37% |
| Reporting rate | Near zero |

**Key Concerns**
- Fear of deportation
- Employer threats to call ICE
- Language barriers
- Unfamiliarity with U.S. law
- No path to legal residency
- Desperate need for employment

**Political Power**
- Minimal direct political power
- Cannot vote if undocumented
- Organized through immigrant rights groups
- Coalition building potential

### Workers of Color

**Profile**

| Group | Minimum Wage Violation Rate |
|-------|----------------------------|
| Latino workers | 26% |
| Black workers | 21% |
| Asian workers | 18% |
| White workers | 15% |

**Key Concerns**
- Disproportionate violation rates
- Concentration in high-violation industries
- Compounding effects of other discrimination
- Historical exclusion from protections

**Political Power**
- Significant voting bloc
- Civil rights organization connections
- Racial justice framing resonates
- Coalition potential with broader movements

### Women Workers

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Data |
|----------------|------|
| Women in workforce | 75 million |
| Women violation rate | 18% |
| Men violation rate | 15% |
| Concentration in tip-theft industries | Higher |

**Key Concerns**
- Higher rates in restaurant, retail, domestic work
- Intersecting with gender discrimination
- Tip theft particularly affects women
- Sexual harassment compounds vulnerability

**Political Power**
- Major voting bloc
- Women's organizations as allies
- MeToo movement raised workplace issues
- Intersection with gender equity movement

### Tipped Workers

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Data |
|----------------|------|
| Tipped workers | 4.3 million |
| Federal tipped minimum | $2.13/hour |
| Tip theft victims | 1.6 million/year |
| Annual theft | $2.1 billion |

**Key Concerns**
- Employer tip theft
- Invalid tip credit claims
- Forced tip pooling with managers
- Sub-minimum wage dependency
- Income instability

**Political Power**
- Organized through ROC United
- High visibility in policy debates
- Media attention to restaurant industry

### Gig Workers

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Data |
|----------------|------|
| Gig economy workers | 10+ million |
| Independent contractor classification | Near universal |
| FLSA coverage | None (misclassified) |

**Key Concerns**
- Misclassification denies all protections
- No minimum wage, overtime, benefits
- Platform algorithmic control
- No unemployment insurance
- No workers' compensation

**Political Power**
- Growing organization (Gig Workers Rising, etc.)
- High media profile
- Tech industry attention
- Ballot measure battleground (Prop 22)

## Employers

### High-Violation Industry Employers

**Restaurant Industry**

| Characteristic | Data |
|----------------|------|
| Establishments | 1 million+ |
| Workers | 14.7 million |
| Violation rate (investigated) | 84% |
| Key violations | Tip theft, overtime, minimum wage |

**Position**: Generally oppose increased enforcement, support tip credit, oppose increases to minimum wage.

**Construction Industry**

| Characteristic | Data |
|----------------|------|
| Establishments | 750,000+ |
| Workers | 7.7 million |
| Misclassification rate | 10-30% |
| Key violations | Misclassification, overtime |

**Position**: Mixed - union contractors support enforcement; non-union contractors often oppose.

**Retail Industry**

| Characteristic | Data |
|----------------|------|
| Establishments | 1+ million |
| Workers | 15.8 million |
| Violation rate (investigated) | 68% |
| Key violations | Off-the-clock, overtime |

**Position**: Oppose class actions, support arbitration, oppose wage increases.

### Franchise Systems

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Data |
|----------------|------|
| Franchise businesses | 750,000+ |
| Franchise workers | 8+ million |
| Model | Franchisor sets conditions, denies employer status |

**Franchisor Position**
- Deny joint employer status
- Claim franchisee independence
- Oppose joint employer rules
- Lobby against enforcement

**Franchisee Position**
- Squeezed by franchisor requirements
- Low profit margins
- Often commit violations to survive
- Some support reform

### Gig Economy Platforms

**Major Platforms**

| Company | Workers | Classification | Political Activity |
|---------|---------|---------------|-------------------|
| Uber | 5+ million | Independent contractor | $200M+ for Prop 22 |
| Lyft | 2+ million | Independent contractor | Major Prop 22 funder |
| DoorDash | 1+ million | Independent contractor | Major Prop 22 funder |
| Instacart | 600,000+ | Independent contractor | Prop 22 supporter |

**Position**: Aggressively defend independent contractor model, fund ballot measures, lobby against ABC test.

### Temp Agencies and Staffing Firms

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Data |
|----------------|------|
| Temp agency workers | 3.2 million |
| Industry revenue | $150+ billion |
| Model | Triangle employment relationship |

**Position**: Support current joint employer rules, oppose expanded liability.

### Compliant Employers

**Who They Are**
- Union employers
- Companies with strong HR compliance
- Some large corporations with brand exposure
- Employers in low-violation industries

**Position**: May support stronger enforcement that creates level playing field and removes competitive disadvantage from compliance.

## Government Agencies

### Department of Labor - Wage and Hour Division

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Data |
|----------------|------|
| Staff | ~2,000 total |
| Investigators | ~750 |
| Budget | ~$280 million |
| Investigations/year | ~21,000 |
| Back wages collected | $225 million |

**Key Interests**
- Adequate funding and staffing
- Clear legal authority
- Judicial support for enforcement
- Political support from administration
- Morale and retention

**Challenges**
- Chronic underfunding
- Impossible workload
- Political interference
- Court decisions limiting authority
- Retaliation against complainants

### State Labor Agencies

**Strong Enforcement States**

| State | Agency | Investigators | Notable Features |
|-------|--------|---------------|------------------|
| California | Labor Commissioner | 350+ | PAGA, private AG suits |
| New York | DOL | 200+ | Wage theft task force |
| Massachusetts | AG Office | 80+ | Criminal prosecution |
| Washington | L&I | 60+ | Strong penalties |

**Position**: Generally support federal floor with state ability to exceed.

**Weak Enforcement States**

| State | Status |
|-------|--------|
| Mississippi | No state wage/hour agency |
| Alabama | No dedicated enforcement |
| Louisiana | Minimal resources |

**Position**: Often oppose federal enforcement as "overreach."

### NLRB

**Role in Wage Theft**
- Protects workers who organize against wage theft
- Addresses retaliation for concerted activity
- No direct wage enforcement authority

### EEOC

**Role in Wage Theft**
- Equal Pay Act enforcement (gender wage discrimination)
- Intersection with race/sex discrimination in wages
- Coordinate with WHD on some issues

### IRS

**Role in Wage Theft**
- Worker classification affects tax treatment
- Misclassification involves tax fraud
- SS-8 process for classification disputes
- Limited coordination with DOL

## Legal Community

### Plaintiffs' Employment Attorneys

**Profile**
- Represent workers in wage claims
- Typically contingency fee basis
- Specialize in class actions
- Network through NELA (National Employment Law Association)

**Key Interests**
- Access to courts (oppose arbitration)
- Class action availability
- Meaningful penalties
- Clear liability standards
- Reasonable statutes of limitations

**Political Activity**
- Lobbying against forced arbitration
- Supporting increased penalties
- Advocating for fee-shifting
- Testifying in legislative hearings

### Defense-Side Employment Attorneys

**Profile**
- Represent employers
- Hourly billing
- "Wage and hour compliance" practice
- Management-side bar associations

**Key Interests**
- Maintain current system
- Defend arbitration
- Limit class actions
- Narrow liability standards

**Political Activity**
- Lobbying for employer-friendly rules
- Opposing penalty increases
- Supporting arbitration

### Legal Aid Organizations

**Profile**
- Represent low-income workers
- Cannot recover fees in same way
- Limited capacity
- Focus on most vulnerable

**Key Interests**
- Worker protections
- Access to justice
- Enforcement funding
- Reduced barriers to claims

### Worker Centers

**Major Organizations**

| Organization | Focus | Location |
|--------------|-------|----------|
| ROC United | Restaurant workers | National |

| National Day Laborer Organizing Network | Day laborers | National |

- Support policy reform

**Position**: Support wage theft enforcement with immigration protections.

## Business Organizations

### National Restaurant Association

**Profile**
- Primary restaurant industry lobby
- 500,000+ member companies
- Major political donor

**Position on Wage Theft**
- Oppose minimum wage increases
- Support tip credit
- Oppose joint employer rules
- Support arbitration
- Oppose class actions

### U.S. Chamber of Commerce

**Profile**
- Largest business federation
- 3 million member businesses
- Major political influence

**Position on Wage Theft**
- Oppose enforcement increases
- Support employer-friendly classification
- Oppose PRO Act
- Support arbitration

### International Franchise Association

**Profile**
- Franchise industry lobby
- 1,400 franchise brands

**Position on Wage Theft**
- Strongly oppose joint employer
- Defend franchise model
- Oppose accountability to franchisors

### National Retail Federation

**Profile**
- Retail industry lobby
- World's largest retail trade association

**Position on Wage Theft**
- Support arbitration
- Oppose class actions
- Oppose wage increases
- Support current enforcement levels

### State Restaurant Associations

**Role**
- State-level lobbying
- Often more extreme than national
- Block local wage increases
- Oppose state enforcement

### National Employment Law Project

### Center for Popular Democracy

## Power Analysis

**Relative Power of Stakeholders**

| Stakeholder | Economic Power | Political Power | Organizing Capacity |
|-------------|---------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Workers | Low individual | Low-Medium | Growing |
| Industry lobbies | Very high | Very high | Well-organized |
| Unions | Medium | Medium | Established |
| Worker centers | Low | Growing | Growing |
| Government agencies | N/A | Depends on administration | Bureaucratic |

**Key Insight**: The stakeholders most affected by wage theft (workers) have the least political power, while those who benefit from wage theft (some employers) have significant political influence.
