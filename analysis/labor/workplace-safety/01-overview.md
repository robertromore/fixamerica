# Workplace Safety: Overview

## Executive Summary

Every year, more than 5,000 American workers die on the job and nearly 3 million suffer serious injuries or illnesses. These are not accidents but preventable tragedies resulting from systemic failures in workplace safety enforcement, inadequate standards, and employer prioritization of profits over worker protection. The Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA), created in 1970 to ensure safe working conditions, has been systematically weakened through understaffing, underfunding, and regulatory capture. With fewer than 2,000 federal inspectors responsible for 8 million workplaces, the average employer can expect an OSHA inspection once every 165 years.

The burden of workplace danger falls disproportionately on vulnerable workers: immigrants, people of color, temporary and gig workers, and those in industries like construction, agriculture, and meatpacking where hazards are endemic and protections scarce. As climate change intensifies, heat-related illness and death represent a growing crisis, yet federal heat standards remain nonexistent. The COVID-19 pandemic exposed catastrophic gaps in workplace safety, with workers forced to choose between their health and their livelihoods.

Reform requires a fundamental reimagining of workplace safety: dramatically expanding OSHA's capacity, strengthening penalties to deter violations, protecting whistleblowers who report hazards, and extending protections to all workers regardless of immigration status or employment classification.

## Scope

### What This Analysis Covers

- **Occupational injuries and fatalities**: The scope, causes, and distribution of workplace harm
- **OSHA structure and limitations**: How the agency is organized and why it fails to protect workers
- **Enforcement gaps**: Inadequate inspection rates, weak penalties, and compliance failures
- **High-hazard industries**: Construction, agriculture, meatpacking, warehousing, and healthcare
- **Emerging hazards**: Heat illness, COVID-19 and infectious disease, algorithmic management
- **Worker rights**: Whistleblower protections, right to refuse unsafe work, anti-retaliation
- **Ergonomic and repetitive stress injuries**: The invisible epidemic of musculoskeletal disorders
- **State OSHA programs**: How state plans compare to federal enforcement
- **Regulatory process**: How safety standards are developed and delayed

### What This Analysis Does Not Cover

- **Workers' compensation**: Covered in [Labor: Workers' Compensation](../workers-compensation/) (when created)
- **Occupational disease**: Long-latency illnesses like occupational cancers covered separately
- **Environmental hazards**: Community environmental justice in [Environment Overview](../../environment/)
- **Transportation safety**: NHTSA, FAA, and other modal safety agencies
- **Mine safety**: MSHA and mining-specific issues

## Key Questions

This analysis addresses the following questions:

1. **Why do American workplaces remain so dangerous despite OSHA's existence for over 50 years?**
   - What structural limitations prevent effective enforcement?
   - How have employers successfully weakened standards and enforcement?

2. **Who bears the burden of workplace danger?**
   - How are hazards distributed by industry, occupation, race, and immigration status?
   - Why do certain workers face elevated risks?

3. **What are the emerging threats to worker safety?**
   - How is climate change creating new workplace hazards?
   - What COVID-19 lessons remain unlearned?
   - How does algorithmic management affect worker safety?

4. **What reforms would actually reduce workplace harm?**
   - How many inspectors would be needed for effective enforcement?
   - What penalty levels would deter violations?
   - How can whistleblower protections be strengthened?

5. **How can workers protect themselves in the current system?**
   - What rights do workers have?
   - How can workers organize for safety improvements?

### The Enforcement Gap

| Metric | Current Reality |
|--------|-----------------|
| Federal OSHA inspectors | ~1,850 |
| Workplaces covered | 8 million |
| Years between average inspections | 165 years |
| Maximum willful violation penalty | $161,323 |

| Criminal prosecution rate | <1% of worker deaths |
