# Labor Unions: Stakeholder Analysis

## Workers

### Non-Union Private Sector Workers

**Profile**

- 112 million workers
- 6% unionized
- Most would benefit from unionization
- Many unaware of organizing rights

**Interests**

| Interest | Union Impact |
|----------|--------------|
| Higher wages | Union premium 10-20% |
| Better benefits | Health, retirement, leave |
| Job security | Just cause protections |
| Voice at work | Grievance procedures |
| Safer workplaces | Union shops 30% fewer violations |

**Barriers to Organizing**

| Barrier | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Fear of retaliation | 57% of employers threaten plant closure |
| Lack of information | Many don't know their rights |
| Employer opposition | Captive audience meetings, anti-union campaigns |
| Turnover | Leave before campaigns conclude |
| Part-time/contingent status | Less attachment to workplace |

**Potential for Mobilization**: High but suppressed. Surveys show 48% of non-union workers would vote for a union, representing about 58 million workers. This latent demand is blocked by legal and employer obstacles.

### Union Members

**Profile**

- 14.4 million workers
- 7.3 million private sector
- 7.1 million public sector
- Concentrated in specific industries

**Interests**

| Interest | Priority |
|----------|----------|
| Maintaining existing gains | High |
| Expanding to new workers | Variable |
| Political advocacy | High |
| Job security | High |
| Contract improvements | Ongoing |

**Internal Divisions**

| Division | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Skilled vs. unskilled | Different organizing approaches |
| Private vs. public | Different legal frameworks |
| Old guard vs. new | Strategy disagreements |
| Service vs. organizing | Resource allocation |

### Public Sector Workers

**Profile**

- 21.9 million total
- 7.1 million unionized (32.5%)
- Teachers, police, firefighters, other government employees

**Special Circumstances**

| Factor | Implication |
|--------|-------------|
| More favorable legal environment | Higher organizing success |
| Political nature of employer | Can lobby for funding |
| No profit motive to oppose unions | But budget constraints |
| *Janus* decision | No mandatory fees |
| Essential services | Strike restrictions |

**Key Public Sector Unions**

| Union | Members | Sector |
|-------|---------|--------|
| NEA (National Education Association) | 3 million | K-12 education |
| AFSCME | 1.4 million | State/local government |
| AFT (American Federation of Teachers) | 1.7 million | Education |
| SEIU (public sector portion) | 1+ million | Healthcare, government |

### Gig and Platform Workers

**Profile**

- 4-5 million app-based workers
- Classified as independent contractors
- No traditional labor law protections
- Growing rapidly

**Interests**

| Interest | Challenge |
|----------|-----------|
| Employee classification | Would gain NLRA rights |
| Minimum earnings | Currently no floor |
| Benefits access | None currently provided |
| Deactivation protections | No due process |
| Expense reimbursement | Bear all costs currently |

**Organizing Challenges**

- Not covered by NLRA as contractors
- Dispersed, don't share workplace
- High turnover
- Algorithm management
- No clear employer in some cases

## Unions and Labor Organizations

### AFL-CIO

**Profile**

- Federation of 60 affiliated unions
- 12.5 million members
- Primary labor political voice
- Founded 1955 (merger of AFL and CIO)

**Structure**

| Level | Function |
|-------|----------|
| National | Policy, politics, coordination |
| State federations | State-level advocacy |
| Central labor councils | Local coordination |
| Affiliated unions | Bargaining, organizing |

**Resources and Capabilities**

| Resource | Amount/Description |
|----------|-------------------|
| Annual budget | ~$200 million |
| Political spending | $50+ million/cycle |
| Staff | ~500 at national level |
| Member mobilization | Door-to-door, phone banking |

### Major Individual Unions

**Largest Unions by Membership**

| Union | Members | Key Sectors |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| NEA | 3 million | K-12 education |
| SEIU | 2 million | Healthcare, public sector, janitorial |
| AFT | 1.7 million | Education, healthcare |
| Teamsters | 1.4 million | Trucking, warehousing, misc. |
| AFSCME | 1.4 million | Public sector |
| UFCW | 1.3 million | Retail, food processing |
| UAW | 400,000 | Auto, higher education |
| IBEW | 775,000 | Electrical |
| CWA | 700,000 | Communications, tech |

**Strategic Differences**

| Approach | Unions | Strategy |
|----------|--------|----------|
| Organizing-focused | SEIU, UNITE HERE | Resource-intensive campaigns |
| Traditional | Building trades | Maintain existing density |
| Social movement | Some CWA, SEIU locals | Broad coalitions |
| Business unionism | Many craft unions | Focus on members |

### New and Independent Unions

**Recent Organizing**

| Organization | Focus | Approach |
|--------------|-------|----------|
| Amazon Labor Union | Amazon warehouses | Independent, worker-led |
| Starbucks Workers United | Starbucks stores | SEIU affiliate |
| Alphabet Workers Union | Google/Alphabet | CWA affiliate, minority union |
| Apple retail unions | Apple stores | IAM, CWA |

These new efforts often bypass traditional union structures, using social media and worker-to-worker organizing.

### Worker Centers

**Profile**

- Community-based organizations
- Not unions (don't bargain contracts)
- Serve immigrant, low-wage workers
- ~200 organizations nationally

**Examples**

| Organization | Focus |
|--------------|-------|
| Restaurant Opportunities Centers | Restaurant workers |
| National Domestic Workers Alliance | Domestic workers |
| Warehouse Workers for Justice | Warehouse workers |
| Coalition of Immokalee Workers | Agricultural workers |

Worker centers organize workers excluded from NLRA or difficult to organize traditionally.

## Employers

### Large Corporations

**Profile**

- Fortune 500 and other major employers
- Generally hostile to unionization
- Sophisticated anti-union capabilities
- Political influence

**Interests**

| Interest | Approach |
|----------|----------|
| Minimize labor costs | Oppose wage increases |
| Maintain management control | Oppose collective bargaining |
| Flexibility | Oppose work rules |
| Avoid strikes | Union avoidance |
| Shareholder returns | Suppress labor share |

**Anti-Union Capabilities**

| Capability | Use |
|------------|-----|
| In-house labor relations | Campaign against organizing |
| Law firms | Legal strategies, delays |
| Consultants | Union avoidance campaigns |
| Political spending | Fund anti-labor candidates |
| Industry associations | Coordinate opposition |

### Small Businesses

**Profile**

- 33 million small businesses
- Most have no employees or few employees
- Some genuinely cannot afford union wages
- Often used as front for larger business interests

**Variation**

| Type | Relationship to Unions |
|------|----------------------|
| Mom-and-pop | Rarely face organizing |
| Small franchises | May be dominated by franchisor |
| Growing companies | May face first organizing |
| Government contractors | May be required to pay prevailing wage |

### Union Avoidance Industry

**Key Players**

| Type | Examples |
|------|----------|
| Consulting firms | Burke Group, Labor Relations Institute, IRI Consultants |
| Law firms | Littler Mendelson, Jackson Lewis, Ogletree Deakins |
| Trade associations | Chamber of Commerce, Associated Builders and Contractors |

**Industry Size**

- $340+ million annually in disclosed spending
- Actual spending likely much higher (disclosure limited)
- 75% of organizing campaigns involve consultants

## Government

### National Labor Relations Board

**Profile**

- Independent federal agency
- Administers NLRA
- Five board members (presidential appointments)
- Fluctuates based on administration

**Structure**

| Component | Function |
|-----------|----------|
| Board (5 members) | Decides cases, sets policy |
| General Counsel | Prosecutes cases |
| Regional offices | Conduct elections, investigate charges |
| Administrative law judges | Hear cases |

**Political Variation**

| Administration | NLRB Tendency |
|----------------|---------------|
| Obama | More worker-friendly |
| Trump | More employer-friendly |
| Biden | More worker-friendly |

The Board's composition determines how labor law is interpreted and enforced.

### Department of Labor

**Relevant Functions**

| Division | Role |
|----------|------|
| Office of Labor-Management Standards | Union financial reporting |
| Wage and Hour Division | Minimum wage, overtime |
| OSHA | Workplace safety |
| Women's Bureau | Working women issues |

The DOL does not directly regulate union organizing but affects related worker issues.

### Congress

**Key Committees**

| Committee | Jurisdiction |
|-----------|-------------|
| House Education and Labor | NLRA, labor standards |
| Senate HELP | Labor policy |
| Appropriations | NLRB/DOL funding |

**Current Alignment**

Labor law reform requires overcoming Senate filibuster (60 votes) or eliminating it. Neither party consistently supports labor.

### State Governments

**Roles**

| Role | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Right-to-work laws | 27 states have them |
| Public sector bargaining | State laws govern |
| Minimum wage | Many states above federal |
| Worker classification | State enforcement varies |

## Political Actors

### Democratic Party

**Official Position**: Pro-labor

**Reality**

| Faction | Position |
|---------|----------|
| Progressive wing | Strong labor support, PRO Act |
| Mainstream | Rhetoric supportive, action limited |
| Moderate/corporate wing | Hesitant on major reform |

**Labor Political Spending**

- Overwhelmingly supports Democrats
- 90%+ of union contributions go to Democrats
- But declining share of Democratic funding

### Republican Party

**Official Position**: Skeptical of unions

**Policy Positions**

| Issue | Position |
|-------|----------|
| Right-to-work | Support |
| PRO Act | Oppose |
| Public sector unions | Often hostile |
| Minimum wage | Generally oppose increases |
| NLRB | Favor employer-friendly appointments |

**Working-Class Base**

Some tension between anti-union policy and working-class voters, especially in Rust Belt. Some Republicans have adopted pro-worker rhetoric without supporting pro-union policy.

## International Actors

### Foreign Union Federations

**Key Organizations**

| Organization | Scope |
|--------------|-------|
| International Trade Union Confederation | Global coordination |
| European Trade Union Confederation | European unions |
| IndustriALL Global Union | Manufacturing |

**Relevance**

- Provide models of alternative approaches
- Support cross-border organizing
- Limited direct influence in U.S.

### Multinational Corporations

**Challenge**

- Can relocate production to avoid unions
- Play workers in different countries against each other
- Concentrated power vs. fragmented labor

## Stakeholder Interest Summary

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Position on Reform |
|-------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Non-union workers | Better wages/conditions | Would benefit |
| Union members | Maintain/expand gains | Support |
| AFL-CIO | Labor power | Strong support |
| Large employers | Labor cost control | Strong opposition |
| Small business | Varies | Often used as cover |
| Union avoidance industry | Revenue | Strong opposition |
| NLRB (Biden) | Enforcement | Supportive |
| Congressional Democrats | Varies by faction | Mixed |
| Congressional Republicans | Employer interests | Generally oppose |
