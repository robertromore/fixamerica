# Paid Leave: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

This document provides draft legislation for comprehensive paid leave reform, including federal legislation for paid family and medical leave, federal paid sick leave, expanded FMLA protections, and model state legislation. Each section includes draft text, explanation, and analysis of potential loopholes and shortcomings.

## Federal Legislation

### Family and Medical Insurance Leave Act (FAMILY Act)

**Purpose**: Establish a national paid family and medical leave program through social insurance, providing partial wage replacement for workers taking leave for qualifying family and medical reasons.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Family and Medical Insurance
Leave Act" or the "FAMILY Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) The United States is the only developed nation without
    a national paid family and medical leave program.
(2) Only 27 percent of private sector workers have access
    to paid family leave through their employers.
(3) The Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993, while an
    important protection, provides only unpaid leave and
    covers only 60 percent of workers.
(4) Approximately 46 percent of workers eligible for FMLA
    cannot afford to take unpaid leave.
(5) Women disproportionately bear the burden of inadequate
    leave, contributing to the gender wage gap.
(6) States with paid leave programs have demonstrated
    positive effects on worker retention, employer
    satisfaction, and family economic security.
(7) Paid leave improves health outcomes for mothers,
    infants, and workers recovering from serious illness.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
(a) "COVERED INDIVIDUAL" means any individual who—
    (1) has earned income from employment during the base
        period; and
    (2) has paid Family and Medical Leave Insurance
        contributions.

(b) "QUALIFYING FAMILY MEMBER" means—
    (1) a spouse, including a domestic partner;
    (2) a child, including an adopted child, stepchild,
        foster child, or child for whom the individual
        stands in loco parentis;
    (3) a parent, including an adoptive parent, stepparent,
        or foster parent, or parent-in-law;
    (4) a grandparent or grandchild;
    (5) a sibling; or
    (6) any other individual related by blood or affinity
        whose close association with the individual is
        equivalent to a family relationship.

(c) "SERIOUS HEALTH CONDITION" means an illness, injury,
    impairment, or physical or mental condition that
    involves—
    (1) inpatient care in a hospital, hospice, or
        residential medical care facility; or
    (2) continuing treatment by a health care provider.

(d) "BASE PERIOD" means the first four of the last five
    completed calendar quarters before the benefit year.

SEC. 4. ESTABLISHMENT OF FAMILY AND MEDICAL LEAVE INSURANCE
PROGRAM.

(a) IN GENERAL.—There is established in the Social Security
    Administration the Office of Paid Family and Medical
    Leave to administer the Family and Medical Leave
    Insurance program established under this Act.

(b) BENEFITS.—
    (1) ENTITLEMENT.—A covered individual shall be entitled
        to receive a Family and Medical Leave Insurance
        benefit for any week in which the individual—
        (A) is on leave from employment for a qualifying
            reason; and
        (B) meets the eligibility requirements of this Act.

    (2) QUALIFYING REASONS.—A qualifying reason for leave
        includes—
        (A) the birth of a child of the individual and to
            care for such child;
        (B) the placement of a child with the individual
            for adoption or foster care;
        (C) to care for a qualifying family member who has
            a serious health condition;
        (D) a serious health condition that makes the
            individual unable to perform the functions of
            the individual's position;
        (E) any qualifying exigency arising from the
            deployment of a qualifying family member in
            the Armed Forces; or
        (F) to care for a servicemember who is a
            qualifying family member.

(c) BENEFIT DURATION.—
    (1) FAMILY LEAVE.—An individual may receive up to 12
        weeks of benefits in any 12-month period for
        qualifying reasons described in subsection
        (b)(2)(A), (B), (C), (E), and (F).
    (2) MEDICAL LEAVE.—An individual may receive up to 12
        weeks of benefits in any 12-month period for the
        qualifying reason described in subsection (b)(2)(D).
    (3) COMBINED MAXIMUM.—The total benefits received under
        paragraphs (1) and (2) may not exceed 24 weeks in
        any 12-month period.

SEC. 5. BENEFIT AMOUNT.

(a) WEEKLY BENEFIT AMOUNT.—The weekly benefit amount shall
    be calculated as follows—
    (1) for wages up to 50 percent of the State average
        weekly wage, 90 percent of such wages;
    (2) for wages above 50 percent and up to 100 percent of
        the State average weekly wage, 70 percent of such
        wages; and
    (3) for wages above 100 percent of the State average
        weekly wage, 50 percent of such wages.

(b) MAXIMUM BENEFIT.—The maximum weekly benefit shall not
    exceed 100 percent of the national average weekly wage.

(c) MINIMUM BENEFIT.—The minimum weekly benefit shall not be
    less than $100 or the individual's average weekly wage,
    whichever is less.

SEC. 6. FUNDING.

(a) FAMILY AND MEDICAL LEAVE INSURANCE TRUST FUND.—
    (1) There is established in the Treasury of the United
        States a trust fund to be known as the Family and
        Medical Leave Insurance Trust Fund.
    (2) The Trust Fund shall consist of—
        (A) contributions deposited pursuant to subsection
            (b); and
        (B) interest earned on investments of the Trust
            Fund.

(b) CONTRIBUTIONS.—
    (1) RATE.—There is imposed on each employer and
        employee a contribution of 0.2 percent of wages,
        for a total combined contribution of 0.4 percent.
    (2) EMPLOYER CONTRIBUTION.—Each employer shall pay a
        contribution equal to 0.2 percent of each employee's
        wages.
    (3) EMPLOYEE CONTRIBUTION.—Each employee shall pay a
        contribution equal to 0.2 percent of wages, to be
        withheld by the employer.
    (4) SELF-EMPLOYED.—Self-employed individuals may opt
        into the program by paying a contribution equal to
        0.4 percent of net self-employment income.
    (5) WAGE BASE.—Contributions shall be collected on
        wages up to the Social Security wage base.

SEC. 7. JOB PROTECTION.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Any employer that employs 15 or more
    employees shall restore an employee returning from leave
    under this Act to—
    (1) the position held by the employee when the leave
        commenced; or
    (2) an equivalent position with equivalent employment
        benefits, pay, and other terms and conditions of
        employment.

(b) COORDINATION WITH FMLA.—Job protection under this section
    shall run concurrently with any applicable job protection
    under the Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993.

SEC. 8. ANTI-RETALIATION.

(a) PROHIBITION.—It shall be unlawful for any employer to
    discharge, fine, suspend, expel, discipline, or in any
    other manner discriminate against any individual because
    such individual—
    (1) has filed a claim for benefits under this Act;
    (2) has testified or is about to testify in any
        proceeding under this Act;
    (3) has exercised any right provided under this Act; or
    (4) has provided information to any party regarding any
        suspected violation of this Act.

(b) REMEDIES.—An individual who is discharged or discriminated
    against in violation of this section may bring a civil
    action to recover—
    (1) reinstatement;
    (2) back pay with interest;
    (3) compensatory damages;
    (4) punitive damages not to exceed $100,000; and
    (5) reasonable attorney's fees and costs.

SEC. 9. COORDINATION WITH STATE PROGRAMS.

(a) STATE PROGRAMS.—Nothing in this Act shall be construed to
    diminish or supplant any law of any State that provides
    greater family or medical leave rights than those
    provided under this Act.

(b) COORDINATION OF BENEFITS.—An individual receiving
    benefits under a State paid family or medical leave
    program may receive benefits under this Act only to the
    extent that such benefits, combined with State benefits,
    do not exceed 100 percent of the individual's regular
    weekly wages.

SEC. 10. EFFECTIVE DATE.

(a) CONTRIBUTIONS.—The contribution requirements of section 6
    shall take effect on the first day of the second
    calendar year following the date of enactment.

(b) BENEFITS.—Benefit payments under this Act shall begin
    on the first day of the third calendar year following
    the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 4**: Establishes program within SSA, defines qualifying reasons
- **Section 5**: Progressive benefit formula favoring low-wage workers
- **Section 6**: Modest payroll tax (0.4% total) funds program
- **Section 7**: Job protection for employers with 15+ employees
- **Section 8**: Strong anti-retaliation protections
- **Section 9**: Allows state programs to continue, coordinate benefits

### Healthy Families Act

**Purpose**: Require employers to provide paid sick leave to all employees.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Healthy Families Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that—
    (1) approximately 34 million workers in the United
        States lack access to paid sick days;
    (2) workers without paid sick days are more likely to
        report going to work while sick, spreading illness;
    (3) the COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated the public health
        importance of paid sick leave;
    (4) low-wage, part-time, and service sector workers are
        least likely to have paid sick days;
    (5) lack of paid sick days disproportionately affects
        women, workers of color, and immigrants.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to ensure that all
    workers have access to paid sick time to address their
    own health needs and those of their family members.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
(a) "EMPLOYEE" means any individual employed by an employer,
    including part-time employees and temporary employees.

(b) "EMPLOYER" means any person engaged in commerce or in any
    industry or activity affecting commerce who employs 1 or
    more employees.

(c) "FAMILY MEMBER" means—
    (1) a child, regardless of age;
    (2) a parent;
    (3) a spouse or domestic partner;
    (4) a grandparent or grandchild;
    (5) a sibling; or
    (6) any individual related by blood or affinity whose
        close association with the employee is equivalent
        to a family relationship.

(d) "PAID SICK TIME" means time that is compensated at the
    same hourly rate and with the same benefits as the
    employee normally earns during hours worked.

SEC. 4. ACCRUAL OF PAID SICK TIME.

(a) IN GENERAL.—An employer shall provide each employee with
    paid sick time accrued as follows—
    (1) an employee shall accrue not less than 1 hour of
        paid sick time for every 30 hours worked; and
    (2) an employee who is exempt from overtime requirements
        shall be deemed to work 40 hours per week unless
        the employee's normal work week is less than 40
        hours.

(b) ACCRUAL CAP.—An employer may limit the total amount of
    paid sick time an employee may accrue to 56 hours.

(c) CARRYOVER.—Accrued paid sick time shall carry over from
    year to year, except that an employer may limit the
    amount of accrued paid sick time an employee may have
    at any time to 56 hours.

(d) USE CAP.—An employer may limit the use of paid sick time
    in any year to 56 hours.

(e) EXISTING POLICIES.—An employer with a paid leave policy
    that provides an amount of paid leave sufficient to meet
    the requirements of this section and allows such leave
    to be used for the same purposes and under the same
    conditions as required by this Act shall be deemed to
    be in compliance.

SEC. 5. PERMITTED USES.

(a) USES.—An employee may use paid sick time for—
    (1) the employee's own physical or mental illness,
        injury, or health condition;
    (2) obtaining preventive medical care for the employee
        or a family member;
    (3) caring for a family member with an illness, injury,
        or health condition;
    (4) obtaining medical attention, services from a victim
        services organization, counseling, relocation, or
        legal services for the employee or a family member
        related to domestic violence, sexual assault, or
        stalking; or
    (5) closure of the employee's workplace by order of a
        public official due to a public health emergency,
        or the need to care for a child whose school or
        place of care has been closed due to a public
        health emergency.

(b) MINIMUM INCREMENT.—Paid sick time may be used in the
    smallest increment that the employer's payroll system
    uses to account for absences.

SEC. 6. EMPLOYER REQUIREMENTS.

(a) NOTICE REQUIREMENT.—Each employer shall post and keep
    posted a notice of employee rights under this Act.

(b) RECORDS.—Each employer shall retain records documenting
    hours worked, paid sick time accrued, and paid sick time
    used for a period of 3 years.

(c) NO RETALIATION.—An employer shall not discharge, fine,
    suspend, or discriminate against any employee who—
    (1) requests or uses paid sick time;
    (2) files a complaint under this Act;
    (3) testifies in a proceeding under this Act; or
    (4) exercises any right under this Act.

SEC. 7. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) AGENCY ENFORCEMENT.—The Secretary of Labor shall have
    authority to investigate violations and impose penalties.

(b) CIVIL PENALTIES.—An employer who violates this Act shall
    be liable for a civil penalty of up to $10,000 for each
    affected employee.

(c) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—An employee aggrieved by a
    violation may bring a civil action to recover—
    (1) damages equal to three times the amount of unpaid
        sick time;
    (2) compensatory damages;
    (3) punitive damages; and
    (4) reasonable attorney's fees and costs.

SEC. 8. PREEMPTION.

Nothing in this Act shall be construed to preempt, limit, or
otherwise affect the applicability of any State or local law
that provides greater paid sick time rights than those
provided under this Act.

SEC. 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 6 months after the date of
enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 4**: 1 hour per 30 hours worked, 56-hour cap
- **Section 5**: Broad permitted uses including safe leave
- **Section 6**: Notice, records, anti-retaliation
- **Section 7**: Both agency and private enforcement
- **Section 8**: Preserves stronger state/local laws

### FMLA Expansion Act

**Purpose**: Strengthen and expand the Family and Medical Leave Act.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "FMLA Expansion Act".

SEC. 2. EXPANSION OF EMPLOYER COVERAGE.

Section 101(4) of the Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993
(29 U.S.C. 2611(4)) is amended by striking "50" and inserting
"1".

SEC. 3. EXPANSION OF ELIGIBLE EMPLOYEES.

Section 101(2) of the Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993
(29 U.S.C. 2611(2)) is amended—
(1) in subparagraph (A)(i), by striking "12 months" and
    inserting "90 days"; and
(2) on subparagraph (A)(ii), by striking "1,250 hours"
    and inserting "500 hours".

SEC. 4. EXPANSION OF FAMILY DEFINITION.

Section 101(7) of the Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993
(29 U.S.C. 2611(7)) is amended to read as follows:

"(7) SPOUSE; PARENT; SON OR DAUGHTER; FAMILY MEMBER.—
    "(A) SPOUSE.—The term 'spouse' means the other person
        with whom an individual entered into marriage as
        defined or recognized under State law, or a domestic
        partner as defined under applicable State law or as
        designated by the employee.
    "(B) PARENT.—The term 'parent' includes a biological,
        adoptive, or foster parent, stepparent, parent-in-law,
        or any individual who stood in loco parentis to the
        employee when the employee was a child.
    "(C) SON OR DAUGHTER.—The term 'son or daughter' includes
        a biological, adopted, or foster child, stepchild,
        legal ward, or any individual to whom the employee
        stands in loco parentis, regardless of age.
    "(D) QUALIFYING FAMILY MEMBER.—The term 'qualifying
        family member' includes—
        "(i) a spouse or domestic partner;
        "(ii) a parent or parent-in-law;
        "(iii) a son or daughter;
        "(iv) a grandparent or grandchild;
        "(v) a sibling;
        "(vi) any individual related by blood or affinity
            whose close association with the employee is
            equivalent to a family relationship."

SEC. 5. LEAVE FOR DOMESTIC VIOLENCE, SEXUAL ASSAULT, OR
STALKING.

Section 102(a)(1) of the Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993
(29 U.S.C. 2612(a)(1)) is amended by adding at the end:

"(F) To address issues arising from domestic violence,
    sexual assault, or stalking affecting the employee or
    a qualifying family member, including to obtain medical
    attention, counseling, services from a victim services
    organization, legal services, or to relocate."

SEC. 6. BEREAVEMENT LEAVE.

Section 102(a)(1) of the Family and Medical Leave Act of 1993
(29 U.S.C. 2612(a)(1)) is further amended by adding:

"(G) Because of the death of a qualifying family member,
    for a period of up to 10 days."

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 180 days after the date of
enactment.
```

## State Model Legislation

### State Paid Family and Medical Leave Act

**Purpose**: Model legislation for states to establish comprehensive paid family and medical leave programs.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Paid Family and Medical
Leave Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "COVERED INDIVIDUAL" means any individual who has earned
    wages in covered employment during the base period.

(b) "FAMILY MEMBER" means—
    (1) a spouse or domestic partner;
    (2) a child, regardless of age;
    (3) a parent, including in-law;
    (4) a grandparent or grandchild;
    (5) a sibling; or
    (6) any individual with whom the covered individual has
        a significant personal bond equivalent to a family
        relationship.

(c) "SERIOUS HEALTH CONDITION" means an illness, injury, or
    condition requiring inpatient care or continuing treatment.

SECTION 3. ESTABLISHMENT OF PROGRAM.

(a) There is established within the [Department of Labor/
    Employment Security] the Paid Family and Medical Leave
    Program.

(b) QUALIFYING REASONS.—Benefits shall be payable for leave
    taken for—
    (1) bonding with a new child within 12 months of birth,
        adoption, or foster placement;
    (2) caring for a family member with a serious health
        condition;
    (3) the individual's own serious health condition;
    (4) qualifying military exigency; or
    (5) safe leave related to domestic violence, sexual
        assault, or stalking.

SECTION 4. BENEFIT DURATION.

(a) FAMILY LEAVE.—Up to 12 weeks in a 12-month period.

(b) MEDICAL LEAVE.—Up to 12 weeks in a 12-month period.

(c) COMBINED MAXIMUM.—Up to 16 weeks combined; up to 18 weeks
    for serious pregnancy-related health conditions.

SECTION 5. BENEFIT AMOUNT.

(a) WEEKLY BENEFIT.—
    (1) 90 percent of wages up to 50 percent of the state
        average weekly wage;
    (2) 50 percent of wages above 50 percent of the state
        average weekly wage.

(b) MAXIMUM.—The maximum weekly benefit shall be [$ amount]
    or [percentage] of the state average weekly wage.

(c) MINIMUM.—The minimum weekly benefit shall be $100 or the
    individual's average weekly wage, whichever is less.

SECTION 6. FUNDING.

(a) CONTRIBUTIONS.—
    (1) Total contribution rate: [0.8-1.0] percent of wages.
    (2) Employer share: [40-50] percent of total.
    (3) Employee share: [50-60] percent of total.

(b) SMALL EMPLOYER EXEMPTION.—Employers with fewer than [25]
    employees are not required to pay the employer share but
    their employees remain covered.

(c) WAGE BASE.—Contributions shall be collected on wages up
    to the Social Security wage base.

SECTION 7. JOB PROTECTION.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Employers with [25] or more employees shall
    restore an employee to the same or equivalent position.

(b) RETALIATION PROHIBITED.—No employer shall discriminate
    against any individual for exercising rights under this
    Act.

SECTION 8. ADMINISTRATION.

(a) The [Department] shall—
    (1) process claims within [14] days;
    (2) establish an appeals process;
    (3) conduct outreach and education;
    (4) enforce compliance.

SECTION 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

(a) CONTRIBUTIONS.—Collection begins [18 months] after
    enactment.

(b) BENEFITS.—Benefit payments begin [12 months] after
    contributions begin.
```

### State Paid Sick Leave Act

**Purpose**: Model legislation for state paid sick leave requirements.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Healthy Workplaces Act".

SECTION 2. ACCRUAL.

(a) RATE.—Employees shall accrue paid sick time at a rate of
    not less than 1 hour for every 30 hours worked.

(b) CAP.—Employers may cap accrual at 48 hours per year.

(c) CARRYOVER.—Unused sick time shall carry over, with an
    employer cap of 80 hours total accrued.

(d) FRONTLOADING.—Employers may frontload the annual amount
    at the beginning of each year.

SECTION 3. PERMITTED USES.

Paid sick time may be used for—
(a) the employee's own illness, injury, or medical care;
(b) caring for a family member's illness or medical care;
(c) preventive care;
(d) reasons related to domestic violence, sexual assault,
    or stalking;
(e) public health emergencies.

SECTION 4. EMPLOYER REQUIREMENTS.

(a) All employers with employees in this State shall comply.

(b) Notice of rights shall be posted and provided to
    employees.

(c) Records shall be maintained for 3 years.

SECTION 5. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) The [Department of Labor] shall enforce this Act.

(b) Civil penalties up to $1,000 per violation.

(c) Private right of action with treble damages.

SECTION 6. NO PREEMPTION.

Local governments may enact more protective ordinances.

SECTION 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect [6 months/1 year] after enactment.
```

(a) MENTAL HEALTH.—A serious health condition includes mental
    health conditions that require treatment by a health care
    provider.

(b) CHRONIC CONDITIONS.—Conditions requiring periodic treatment
    shall be covered regardless of whether treatment is
    required during leave.

(a) PRESUMPTION.—Where an employee is terminated within 90

    prioritize investigation of retaliation claims.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| Commerce Clause | Strong precedent for federal labor standards |
| Spending power | Alternative basis for some provisions |
| State sovereignty | *Garcia* permits application to state employees |
| ERISA preemption | Careful drafting can avoid preemption issues |

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| Agency enforcement | Expert, proactive | Resource-limited |
| Private right of action | Worker-initiated | Costly, risky |
| State enforcement | Local presence | Variable capacity |
| Criminal penalties | Deterrent | Rarely used |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### FAMILY Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Independent contractor exclusion | Misclassified workers lack coverage | High |
| Small employer job protection | No job protection under 15 employees | High |
| Waiting period | Delay in receiving benefits | Medium |
| Wage calculation | Base period may not reflect current wages | Medium |
| Intermittent leave gaming | Employer manipulation of intermittent leave | Low |
| Family definition | Some relationships may not qualify | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| 12 weeks insufficient | Many conditions require longer | Political compromise |
| Wage replacement not 100% | Workers still lose income | Cost constraints |
| Phase-in period | Workers wait years for benefits | Implementation needs |
| Job protection gaps | Small employer workers vulnerable | Small business concerns |
| Enforcement capacity | DOL understaffed | Budget limitations |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Misclassification**: Include ABC test for coverage; presume employment status; joint employer liability
2. **Small employer job protection**: Phase in job protection for smaller employers; tax credit offset
3. **Waiting period**: Eliminate or minimize waiting period; allow immediate benefits for childbirth
4. **Wage calculation**: Allow alternative period if base period not representative
5. **Family definition**: Explicit inclusive language; self-designation option
6. **Duration**: Increase to 16 weeks; allow extension for complications
7. **Wage replacement**: Target 80% average; 100% for low wage
8. **Enforcement**: Dedicated funding; private right of action

### Healthy Families Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Documentation abuse | Excessive requirements for sick leave | High |
| Retaliation difficulty | Hard to prove, easy to disguise | High |
| Small increment requirement | May discourage use | Low |
| Frontloading manipulation | Not actually providing required days | Medium |
| Definition of employee | Exclusion of some workers | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| 56 hours may be insufficient | Serious illness needs more | Compromise |
| No job protection | Only anti-retaliation | Scope limitation |
| Accrual for new employees | Must wait to accrue | Program design |
| No wage replacement guarantee | Uses accrued time | Not insurance model |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Documentation**: Limit documentation requirements; prohibit requiring doctor's note for short absences
2. **Retaliation**: Presumption of retaliation if adverse action within 90 days; burden shifting
3. **Frontloading**: Clear requirements for frontloaded policies; audit provisions
4. **Definition**: Explicitly include gig workers, domestic workers, agricultural workers
5. **Duration**: Consider higher cap for larger employers; unlimited carryover
6. **Job protection**: Add explicit job protection provisions

### State Model Legislation

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Small employer exemptions | Creates coverage gaps | Medium |
| Opt-out provisions | Employers may circumvent | Medium |
| Benefit coordination | Confusion with federal program | Low |
| Self-employed exclusion | Gig workers may not opt in | High |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| State-only coverage | Workers in other states unprotected | Federalism |
| Variable implementation | Quality varies by state | State capacity |
| Preemption risk | Federal action could override | Legal uncertainty |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Coverage gaps**: Minimize employer exemptions; include all workers
2. **Opt-out**: Strict requirements for private plan equivalency
3. **Coordination**: Clear federal-state coordination rules
4. **Self-employed**: Automatic opt-in with opt-out option; outreach

### Cross-Cutting Issues

#### Systemic Vulnerabilities

| Vulnerability | Affects | Mitigation |
|---------------|---------|------------|
| Political reversal | All programs | Build durable coalition; constitutional protection |
| Employer evasion | All provisions | Strong enforcement; private right of action |
| Underfunding | Administration | Dedicated, protected funding |
| Awareness gaps | All workers | Robust outreach requirements |
| Technology barriers | Low-income workers | Multiple access methods |

#### Monitoring Requirements

| Metric | Frequency | Entity |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Benefit claims | Monthly | Administering agency |
| Employer compliance | Annual | DOL/State agency |
| Retaliation complaints | Quarterly | Enforcement agency |
| Worker awareness | Annual | Survey research |

#### Amendment Procedures

1. **Benefit inadequacy**: Automatic inflation adjustment; periodic adequacy review
2. **Coverage gaps**: Expand covered relationships; add qualifying reasons
3. **Enforcement gaps**: Increase penalties; add enforcement capacity
4. **Funding shortfalls**: Contribution rate adjustment mechanism

| Worker awareness | Mandatory notice, outreach campaign, multilingual materials |
| Administrative capacity | Adequate staffing, technology investment |
| Appeals backlog | Timely processing requirements, adequate hearing officers |
| Fraud prevention | Verification systems, audits, penalties |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Program effectiveness review every 5 years
- GAO evaluation of implementation within 3 years
- Annual reporting on claims, denials, appeals
- Automatic benefit adjustments tied to wage growth
