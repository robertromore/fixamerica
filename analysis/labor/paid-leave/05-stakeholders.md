# Paid Leave: Stakeholders

## Overview

Paid leave policy involves a complex landscape of stakeholders with varying degrees of power, interest, and positioning. Understanding these dynamics is essential for building coalitions and developing effective advocacy strategies.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Workers and Families

**Workers Without Paid Leave**

| Category | Estimated Number |
|----------|-----------------|
| Workers without paid family leave | 100+ million |
| Workers without paid sick leave | 34 million |
| Workers who cannot afford FMLA | ~35 million |
| New parents lacking paid leave | 3+ million annually |

**Interests**

| Interest | Priority |
|----------|----------|
| Paid time for family needs | Critical |
| Job protection during leave | Critical |
| Adequate wage replacement | High |
| Accessible programs | High |
| Inclusive family definitions | Moderate |

**Power Assessment**

| Power Source | Level |
|--------------|-------|
| Voting power | High (large numbers) |
| Organizing potential | Moderate |
| Financial resources | Low |
| Political access | Low |
| Current influence | Limited |

**Vulnerabilities**

| Vulnerability | Impact |
|---------------|--------|
| Fear of retaliation | Reduces advocacy |
| Economic precarity | Cannot afford to take unpaid leave |
| Information gaps | Do not know options |
| Fragmented | Difficult to organize |

### Employers

**Small Businesses (Under 50 Employees)**

| Characteristic | Detail |
|----------------|--------|
| Number | ~6 million businesses |
| Workers employed | ~50 million |
| Current leave provision | Lower than large employers |
| Primary concern | Cost and administrative burden |

**Interests**

| Interest | Priority |
|----------|----------|
| Minimize mandates | High |
| Level playing field | Moderate (some want) |
| Predictable costs | High |
| Administrative simplicity | High |
| Employee retention | Moderate |

**Large Employers (500+ Employees)**

| Characteristic | Detail |
|----------------|--------|
| Number | ~20,000 businesses |
| Workers employed | ~55 million |
| Current leave provision | Higher than small |
| Primary concern | Competitive advantage/cost |

**Interests**

| Interest | Priority |
|----------|----------|
| Maintain competitive advantage | High (for those with good leave) |
| Level playing field | Moderate (some support mandates) |
| Predictable regulatory environment | High |
| Employee recruitment/retention | High |

### Government

**Federal Government**

| Actor | Role |
|-------|------|
| Congress | Legislative authority |

**State Governments**

|-------|------|

| Governors | Signing or vetoing legislation |
| State labor departments | Administering programs |
| State insurance departments | Overseeing TDI/paid leave programs |

## Economic Stakeholders

### Who Bears Costs and Benefits

**Under Social Insurance Model**

| Stakeholder | Contribution | Benefit |
|-------------|--------------|---------|
| Employees | Payroll tax | Leave benefits |
| Employers | Payroll tax (share varies) | Reduced turnover |
| Government | Administration | Healthier workforce |
| Taxpayers | General revenue (some models) | Reduced public assistance |

**Current System Costs**

| Stakeholder | Cost Borne |
|-------------|------------|
| Workers | Lost wages, job loss, health consequences |
| Families | Financial hardship, caregiving burden |
| Employers | Turnover, absenteeism |
| Healthcare system | Worse health outcomes |
| Taxpayers | Public assistance for those without leave |

## Key Stakeholder Dynamics

### The Small Business Debate

**Opposition Claims**

| Claim | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| Cannot afford leave | Some truth for smallest businesses |
| Will close businesses | No evidence from state programs |
| Unfair burden | Social insurance spreads costs |

**Support Arguments**

| Argument | Evidence |
|----------|----------|
| Level playing field | Currently disadvantaged competing with large employers |
| Reduces turnover | Small businesses have high turnover costs |
| Employee loyalty | Benefits improve retention |
| Social insurance | Costs shared, not borne alone |

**Small Business for Paid Leave**

| Organization | Position |
|--------------|----------|
| Main Street Alliance | Supports paid leave |
| Small Business Majority | Supports paid leave |
| Individual small businesses | Many support |

### The Gender Dimension

**Women as Key Stakeholders**

| Aspect | Significance |
|--------|--------------|
| Primary caregivers | 70% of family caregiving |
| Workforce participation | Leave affects decisions |
| Wage impact | Lack of leave widens gap |
| Political power | Significant voting bloc |

**Men as Emerging Stakeholders**

| Trend | Implication |
|-------|-------------|
| More men want paternity leave | Broadens coalition |
| Shared caregiving increasing | More men affected |
| Economic necessity | Two-income families need both parents working |

### Inter-Stakeholder Conflicts

| Conflict | Tension |
|----------|---------|
| Workers vs. small business | Mandate costs vs. worker needs |
| Large vs. small employers | Large can absorb costs, small struggle |
| State vs. federal | Federalism debates |
| Universal vs. targeted | Who should be covered |

## Stakeholder Engagement Strategies

| Strategy | Approach |
|----------|----------|

| Coalition participation | Join advocacy organizations |
| Voter mobilization | Make leave a voting issue |

| Strategy | Approach |
|----------|----------|

| Policy input | Shape program design |

| Strategy | Approach |
|----------|----------|
