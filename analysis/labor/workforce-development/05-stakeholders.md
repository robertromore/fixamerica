# Workforce Development: Stakeholders

## Workers and Job Seekers

### Unemployed and Displaced Workers

**Profile**:
- 6+ million unemployed at any time
- 2+ million displaced annually by trade, technology, recession
- Many long-term unemployed exhausted UI benefits
- Older workers face age discrimination

**Interests**:
- Access to quality training
- Income support during training
- Credentials that lead to jobs
- Career counseling and guidance
- Support services (child care, transportation)

### Incumbent Workers

**Profile**:
- 150+ million employed workers
- Many underemployed or in declining occupations
- Limited access to employer training
- Cannot afford to stop working for training

**Interests**:
- Upskilling opportunities while employed
- Credentials recognized by employers
- Advancement pathways
- Portable skills

### Young Adults

**Profile**:
- 4 million turn 18 annually
- Many don't attend four-year college
- Limited career guidance
- High unemployment rates (youth)

**Interests**:
- Apprenticeship opportunities
- Career pathways alternatives to college
- Quality credentials
- Entry-level job access

## Employers

### Large Employers

**Profile**:
- Significant training budgets
- Recruitment challenges in skilled positions
- Often partner with community colleges
- Can influence policy

**Interests**:
- Pipeline of skilled workers
- Training that meets specific needs
- Tax incentives for training investment
- Reduce recruitment costs

**Concerns**:
- Quality and relevance of public training
- Poaching by competitors
- Regulatory burden in apprenticeships
- Credential proliferation

### Small and Medium Businesses

**Profile**:
- 99% of employers
- Limited training capacity
- Cannot absorb apprenticeship costs alone
- Most affected by skills shortages

**Interests**:
- Shared training infrastructure
- Consortia approaches
- Pre-screened job candidates
- Reduced hiring costs

### Industry Associations

**Organizations**: NAM, sector-specific associations, local chambers

**Role**:
- Articulate industry skill needs
- Develop industry credentials
- Organize sector partnerships
- Lobby on workforce policy

## Education and Training Providers

### Community Colleges

**Profile**:
- 1,000+ institutions
- Primary workforce training providers
- Dependent on state funding and tuition
- Serve multiple missions

**Interests**:
- Adequate funding
- Flexibility to serve workforce needs
- Recognition of non-credit programs
- Employer partnerships

**Challenges**:
- Completion rates
- Connecting graduates to jobs
- Faculty currency
- Serving diverse populations

### Private Training Providers

**Profile**:
- For-profit and nonprofit
- Specialized occupational training
- Variable quality
- WIOA-eligible providers

**Interests**:
- Access to public funding
- Fair competition with colleges
- Outcome-based contracts
- Regulatory clarity

### Union Training Programs

**Organizations**: Building trades apprenticeships, SEIU training funds

**Role**:
- High-quality apprenticeships
- Joint labor-management governance
- Industry-validated standards
- Member skill development

## Government Actors

### Department of Labor

**Role**:
- WIOA administration
- Apprenticeship registration
- Employment service
- Performance oversight

**Offices**:
- Employment and Training Administration
- Office of Apprenticeship
- Bureau of Labor Statistics (data)

### Department of Education

**Role**:
- Community college funding
- Pell grants
- Career and technical education
- Accreditation oversight

### State Workforce Agencies

**Role**:
- WIOA plan development
- American Job Center oversight
- State funding allocation
- Performance management

### Local Workforce Development Boards

**Role**:
- Local service delivery oversight
- Employer engagement
- Training provider selection
- Regional planning

**Challenges**:
- Limited authority
- Funding constraints
- Variable capacity
- Political interference

## Advocacy and Research Organizations

### Workforce Policy Organizations

**Organizations**: National Skills Coalition, Jobs for the Future, Aspen Institute

**Activities**:
- Policy research
- Best practice dissemination
- Coalition building
- Technical assistance

### Labor-Aligned Groups

**Organizations**: AFL-CIO, Working America, worker centers

**Positions**:
- Increased public investment
- Quality standards
- Worker voice in program design
- Living wage jobs as outcome

### Business-Aligned Groups

**Organizations**: Business Roundtable, U.S. Chamber, SHRM

**Positions**:
- Employer-responsive training
- Credential standardization
- Reduced regulation
- Tax incentives

## International Models

### German Model

**Actors**:
- Employer associations (funding, standards)
- Trade unions (governance, apprentice advocacy)
- Government (regulation, partial funding)
- Schools (classroom component)

**Relevance**: Model for U.S. reform but requires different institutional infrastructure

### UK Apprenticeship Levy

**Model**:
- 0.5% payroll tax on large employers
- Funds transferred to employer levy accounts
- Use for apprenticeship training
- Mandatory employer contribution
