# Workforce Development: Current State

## Federal Workforce Programs

### Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act (WIOA)

The primary federal workforce law (enacted 2014):

**Funding Levels (FY2023)**:

| Program | Funding | Participants |
|---------|---------|--------------|
| Adult Program | $900M | 1.5M |
| Dislocated Worker | $1.1B | 800K |
| Youth Program | $940M | 500K |
| Wagner-Peyser (Employment Service) | $730M | 5M |

**American Job Centers**:
- 2,400+ centers nationwide
- One-stop access to employment services
- Career counseling, job search assistance
- Training referrals and support services

**Outcomes**:
- 60% employed at exit
- Median quarterly earnings: $7,500
- Training completion rate: 15-20%
- Return on investment contested

### Trade Adjustment Assistance (TAA)

For workers displaced by trade:

- $450 million annually
- Covers retraining, job search, relocation
- Extended unemployment benefits
- Healthcare tax credits
- Program has been repeatedly reauthorized with modifications

## Apprenticeship System

### Current Scale

- 593,000 active registered apprentices
- 27,000 registered programs
- 0.3% of workforce (vs. 3% in Germany, 2.5% in UK)
- Growing slowly (~10% over past decade)

### Industry Distribution

| Industry | % of Apprentices |
|----------|-----------------|
| Construction | 48% |
| Manufacturing | 8% |
| Healthcare | 3% |
| IT | 2% |
| Other | 39% |

### Program Characteristics

- Average duration: 4 years
- 87% completion rate (much higher than other training)
- Average wage premium: 40% over similar non-apprentices
- Employer-sponsored with DOL registration
- Combines on-the-job learning with classroom instruction

### Recent Expansion Efforts

- Apprenticeship grants ($200M+ annually 2015-2020)
- Expansion to non-traditional sectors (IT, healthcare)
- Industry-Recognized Apprenticeship Programs (IRAPs) created under Trump, rescinded under Biden
- State apprenticeship expansion initiatives

## Community College Workforce Programs

### Scale

- 1,000+ community colleges
- 7 million students
- 40%+ enrollment in workforce programs
- Primary provider of short-term credentials

### Types of Programs

**Credit Programs**:
- Associate degrees (2 years)
- Certificates (1+ year)
- Articulation to four-year institutions

**Non-Credit Programs**:
- Short-term credentials
- Industry certifications
- Customized employer training
- Often faster, lower-cost

### Challenges

- Funding favors degree programs over short-term training
- Low completion rates (25% for associate degrees)
- Weak connections to employers
- Student support services inadequate
- Remediation needs consume resources

## Sector Partnerships

### Model

- Regional collaboratives of employers, educators, workforce agencies
- Focus on specific industries
- Develop industry-validated curricula
- Direct pathways to employment
- Evidence of effectiveness from rigorous evaluations

### Examples

**Project QUEST** (San Antonio):
- Healthcare and IT focus
- 2+ years average earnings gains of $5,000+
- Model replicated nationally

**Year Up**:
- IT and business training for young adults
- 6-month training + 6-month internship
- 100% wage gains at 3 years

### Limitations

- Small scale (thousands, not millions)
- Intensive case management expensive
- Dependent on employer engagement
- Difficult to sustain funding

## Employer-Sponsored Training

### Scale

- $100+ billion annually (private sector)
- Average 30 hours per employee per year
- Concentrated in large firms
- Professional/technical workers receive most

### Distribution

| Employer Size | % Offering Training |
|---------------|-------------------|
| 500+ employees | 99% |
| 100-499 | 96% |
| 50-99 | 88% |
| <50 | 56% |

### Types

- On-the-job training (most common)
- External courses and conferences
- Tuition reimbursement
- Online learning platforms
- Management development

### Underinvestment

- U.S. firms train less than European counterparts
- Short-term focus due to worker mobility
- Poaching concerns limit investment
- Quarterly earnings pressure

## Career Pathways and Credentials

### Stackable Credentials

- Short-term credentials building to larger qualifications
- Industry certifications with labor market value
- Articulation agreements between institutions
- Competency-based progression

### Current Landscape

- 4,000+ industry certifications
- Highly variable quality and recognition
- Limited labor market data on credential value
- Credential Engine attempting to map ecosystem

### Quality Concerns

- Many credentials lack labor market value
- Students can't assess credential worth
- Employers confused by proliferation
- Accreditation varies
