# Minimum Wage: Stakeholders

## Overview

The minimum wage debate involves a complex web of stakeholders with varying interests, resources, and influence. Understanding who stands to gain or lose from minimum wage policy, and their relative political power, is essential for developing effective reform strategies.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Low-Wage Workers

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Details |
|----------------|---------|
| Number affected by $15 minimum | ~32 million |
| Current federal minimum wage workers | ~1.3 million |
| Median demographics | Female, 30 years old, no college degree |
| Industries | Food service, retail, care work |
| Family status | 28% have children |

**Interests**

| Interest | Current Status |
|----------|----------------|
| Living wage | Far below living wage in most areas |
| Predictable income | Highly variable for tipped workers |
| Dignity at work | Often stigmatized as unskilled |
| Career advancement | Limited mobility in low-wage jobs |
| Work-life balance | Many work multiple jobs |

**Political Power**

| Resource | Assessment |
|----------|------------|
| Voting numbers | High potential, low turnout |
| Organization | Growing (Fight for $15) |
| Campaign contributions | Minimal |
| Public sympathy | Moderate to high |
| Coalition allies | Unions, faith groups |

**Key Organizations**

| Organization | Focus |
|--------------|-------|
| Fight for $15 / SEIU | Fast food and retail workers |
| Restaurant Opportunities Center | Restaurant workers |
| National Domestic Workers Alliance | Domestic workers |
| United Farm Workers | Agricultural workers |
| Jobs with Justice | Coalition building |

### Tipped Workers

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Details |
|----------------|---------|
| Number | ~5.5 million |
| Industries | Restaurants (70%), hospitality, personal services |
| Gender | 66% women |
| Average hourly earnings | $12.50 (including tips) |
| Poverty rate | 12.8% (vs. 6.5% all workers) |

**Unique Circumstances**

| Issue | Impact |
|-------|--------|
| Income volatility | Tips vary by shift, day, season |
| Customer harassment | "Emotional labor" for tips |
| Employer dependence | Tip credit system |
| Wage theft | Common violations |
| Subminimum wage | $2.13 federal floor |

**Divided Perspectives**

| Viewpoint | Argument |
|-----------|----------|
| Support One Fair Wage | Stable income, less harassment, employer accountability |
| Prefer tip system | Higher earnings possible, flexibility |

### Workers with Disabilities

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Details |
|----------------|---------|
| Number on subminimum wage | ~40,000 |
| Certificate holders | ~1,200 employers |
| Wage range | $0.25 to full minimum |
| Average subminimum | ~$3.50/hour |
| Settings | Sheltered workshops |

**Competing Interests**

| Position | Advocates |
|----------|-----------|
| Eliminate subminimum | Disability rights organizations, ASAN |
| Preserve subminimum | Some parents, sheltered workshop operators |

**Key Organizations**

| Organization | Position |
|--------------|----------|
| Autistic Self Advocacy Network | Eliminate 14(c) |
| National Disability Rights Network | Phase out subminimum |
| ACCSES | Mixed (some members prefer current system) |
| Goodwill Industries | Transitioning away from 14(c) |

### Young Workers

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Details |
|----------------|---------|
| Workers 16-24 | 48% of minimum wage workers |
| Student status | Many, but not majority |
| Full-time status | 35% work full-time |
| Family responsibilities | 15% are parents |

**Interests**

| Interest | Status |
|----------|--------|
| Entry-level opportunity | Available at any wage |
| Skill development | Often limited in minimum wage jobs |
| Education funding | Many work to pay for school |
| Fair treatment | Often exploited as "dispensable" |

## Employer Stakeholders

### Small Businesses

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Details |
|----------------|---------|
| Businesses with <50 employees | 98% of all businesses |
| Small business employment | ~48% of private workforce |
| Small business minimum wage workers | ~40% of total |
| Average profit margin (restaurant) | 3-5% |

**Concerns**

| Concern | Assessment |
|---------|------------|
| Higher labor costs | Real but varies by business model |
| Competitive disadvantage | Against large competitors paying same wage |
| Price increases | Can pass through but may lose customers |
| Automation pressure | Limited options for small businesses |
| Staffing reduction | May not be possible to serve customers |

**Range of Positions**

| Position | Representative |
|----------|----------------|
| Oppose any increase | NFIB members |
| Support moderate increase | Business for a Fair Minimum Wage |
| Support $15+ | Some progressive business owners |

**Key Organizations**

| Organization | Position |
|--------------|----------|
| National Federation of Independent Business | Oppose |
| Business for a Fair Minimum Wage | Support $15+ |
| American Sustainable Business Network | Support |
| Main Street Alliance | Support |
| Small Business Majority | Support moderate increase |

### Large Corporations

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Details |
|----------------|---------|
| Fortune 500 employment | ~30 million workers |
| Average minimum wage worker tenure | 3+ years |
| Labor as % of costs | 20-30% (service industries) |
| Profit margins | Generally higher than small business |

**Major Low-Wage Employers**

| Company | Employees | Current Starting Wage |
|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| Walmart | 1.6 million | $14-17 |
| Amazon | 1.5 million | $15+ |
| McDonald's (corporate) | 200,000 direct | $15+ (company stores) |
| Starbucks | 400,000 | $15+ |
| Target | 440,000 | $15+ |

**Corporate Positions**

| Position | Companies |
|----------|-----------|
| Publicly support $15 | Amazon, Costco, Ben & Jerry's |
| Quietly oppose federal increase | Many retail, fast food |
| Actively oppose | Restaurant associations, franchise owners |

### Restaurant Industry

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Details |
|----------------|---------|
| Total employment | ~12 million |
| Median hourly wage | $12.80 |
| Tipped workers | ~4 million |
| Business model dependence on tips | High |

**Unique Considerations**

| Factor | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| Tip credit system | Core to business model |
| Thin margins | 3-5% typical |
| Labor intensity | 30%+ of costs |
| Price sensitivity | Customers notice increases |
| Competition | Many substitutes |

**Industry Organization**

| Organization | Position | Resources |
|--------------|----------|-----------|
| National Restaurant Association | Oppose federal increase | $10M+ lobbying |
| International Franchise Association | Oppose | $5M+ lobbying |
| Restaurant Opportunities Center | Support One Fair Wage | Worker organizing |

### Retail Industry

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Details |
|----------------|---------|
| Total employment | ~16 million |
| Median hourly wage | $14.00 |
| Part-time workers | 30% |
| Turnover rate | 60%+ annually |

**Industry Trends**

| Trend | Implication |
|-------|-------------|
| E-commerce competition | Pressure on brick-and-mortar |
| Self-checkout | Automation already underway |
| Tight labor market (recent) | Many already paying above minimum |
| Scheduling technology | Unpredictable hours |

## Who Benefits and Who Loses

### From Current System ($7.25)

**Winners**

| Group | Benefit |
|-------|---------|
| Low-wage employers | Lower labor costs |
| Shareholders | Higher profits |
| Consumers | Marginally lower prices |

**Losers**

| Group | Cost |
|-------|------|
| Low-wage workers | Poverty wages |
| Taxpayers | Public assistance subsidies |
| High-road employers | Undercut by low-wage competitors |
| Local economies | Reduced consumer spending |

### From $15 Federal Minimum

**Winners**

| Group | Benefit |
|-------|---------|
| Low-wage workers (~32 million) | Higher income |
| Local economies | Increased spending |
| Government | Reduced assistance costs, higher tax revenue |
| High-road employers | Level playing field |

**Potential Losers**

| Group | Risk | Mitigation |
|-------|------|------------|
| Some small businesses | Higher costs | Phase-in period |
| Some workers (CBO estimate) | Job losses | Evidence suggests minimal |
| Consumers | Price increases | Generally modest (4-10%) |

## Key Takeaways

1. **Power imbalance**: Business interests have more political influence than workers
2. **Divided workers**: Tipped workers and disability community have internal debates
3. **Small business complexity**: Genuine concerns mixed with industry rhetoric
4. **State/local variation**: Many stakeholders focused on state-level action
5. **Coalition potential**: Broad support exists across faith, business, and labor
6. **Research role**: Dueling research organizations influence debate
