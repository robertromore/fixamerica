# Immigration Detention: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Detention reform legislation addresses mandatory detention repeal, detention standards, alternatives expansion, private prison phase-out, and oversight mechanisms. This document provides draft legislation for key reforms and analysis of existing legal frameworks.

## Dignity in Immigration Detention Act

**Purpose**: Establish enforceable detention standards and oversight.

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Dignity in Immigration
Detention Act".

SEC. 2. DETENTION STANDARDS.

(a) IN GENERAL.—The Secretary shall promulgate
regulations establishing minimum standards for the
detention of individuals in immigration proceedings.

(b) REQUIRED STANDARDS.—Standards shall include:
    (1) MEDICAL CARE.—
        (A) Initial health screening within 12 hours;
        (B) Access to licensed medical professionals;
        (C) Mental health services;
        (D) Chronic disease management;
        (E) Emergency care protocols.
    (2) CONDITIONS.—
        (A) Adequate food, water, and sanitation;
        (B) Clean and temperature-appropriate housing;
        (C) Access to recreation and outdoor time;
        (D) Personal hygiene supplies.
    (3) COMMUNICATION.—
        (A) Telephone access at reasonable cost;
        (B) Video visitation capability;
        (C) Mail privileges;
        (D) Legal call confidentiality.
    (4) LEGAL ACCESS.—
        (A) Private attorney-client meeting space;
        (B) Access to legal materials;
        (C) Legal orientation programs.
    (5) SOLITARY CONFINEMENT.—
        (A) Prohibited for more than 15 consecutive days;
        (B) Prohibited for vulnerable populations;
        (C) Documented justification required;
        (D) Mental health check every 24 hours.

(c) ENFORCEMENT.—Standards shall be legally
enforceable, and detained individuals shall have
a private right of action for violations.

SEC. 3. OVERSIGHT.

(a) INDEPENDENT MONITORS.—The Secretary shall
contract with independent organizations to conduct
unannounced inspections of all detention facilities
not less than quarterly.

(b) REPORTING.—
    (1) Inspection reports shall be published within
    30 days;
    (2) The Secretary shall report annually on
    conditions, deaths, and complaints.

(c) COMPLAINT MECHANISM.—The Secretary shall
establish an accessible complaint process with
external review of grievances.

SEC. 4. AUTHORIZATION.

There is authorized to be appropriated $100,000,000
annually for oversight and standards implementation.
```

## Mandatory Detention Reform Act

**Purpose**: Repeal mandatory detention and establish presumption of release.

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Mandatory Detention
Reform Act".

SEC. 2. REPEAL OF MANDATORY DETENTION.

(a) AMENDMENT.—Section 236(c) of the Immigration
and Nationality Act (8 U.S.C. 1226(c)) is amended
to read as follows:

"(c) DETENTION OF CERTAIN ALIENS.—
    (1) IN GENERAL.—The Attorney General may detain
    an alien described in paragraph (2) pending a
    decision on whether the alien is to be removed,
    subject to the release provisions of this section.
    (2) COVERED ALIENS.—An alien described in this
    paragraph is an alien who—
        (A) has been convicted of an offense involving
        violence against a person; or
        (B) is determined to pose a significant flight
        risk or danger to public safety based on
        individualized assessment.
    (3) RELEASE.—An alien described in paragraph (2)
    shall be released unless the government demonstrates
    by clear and convincing evidence that no conditions
    of release will reasonably assure appearance and
    safety."

(b) PRESUMPTION OF RELEASE.—Section 236(a) is
amended by adding:
    "(3) PRESUMPTION.—There shall be a presumption
    that an alien in removal proceedings should be
    released, subject to appropriate conditions,
    unless the government demonstrates that detention
    is necessary."

SEC. 3. BOND REFORM.

(a) BOND HEARINGS.—Any detained alien shall receive
a bond hearing within 48 hours of detention.

(b) BOND AMOUNTS.—Bond amounts shall be based on
ability to pay and set at the minimum necessary
to assure appearance.

(c) SUBSEQUENT REVIEW.—Detained aliens shall
receive bond review hearings every 90 days.

SEC. 4. TIME LIMITS.

(a) GENERAL LIMIT.—No alien shall be detained
pending removal proceedings for longer than 6
months unless:
    (1) the alien has been convicted of a crime
    involving violence; and
    (2) the government demonstrates that continued
    detention is necessary for public safety.

(b) POST-ORDER DETENTION.—Section 241(a)(6) is
amended to provide that no alien may be detained
for more than 6 months following a final order
of removal if removal is not reasonably foreseeable.

SEC. 5. JUDICIAL REVIEW.

All detention decisions shall be subject to judicial
review by immigration judges with de novo
consideration of release conditions.
```

## Alternatives to Detention Expansion Act

**Purpose**: Expand and fund community-based alternatives to detention.

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Community Supervision
and Support Act".

SEC. 2. ALTERNATIVES TO DETENTION PROGRAM.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary shall establish
a comprehensive alternatives to detention program
providing community-based supervision and support.

(b) COMPONENTS.—The program shall include:
    (1) CASE MANAGEMENT.—
        (A) Assigned case managers for all participants;
        (B) Assistance with court appearances;
        (C) Connection to legal services;
        (D) Social support services.
    (2) COMMUNITY SUPPORT.—
        (A) Partnerships with community organizations;
        (B) Housing assistance;
        (C) Employment support;
        (D) Mental health services.
    (3) MONITORING OPTIONS.—
        (A) Check-in requirements appropriate to risk;
        (B) Electronic monitoring only when necessary
        and least intrusive option;
        (C) Technology that respects dignity.
    (4) SPECIALIZED PROGRAMS.—
        (A) Family case management;
        (B) Asylum seeker support;
        (C) Vulnerable population services.

SEC. 3. LEGAL ORIENTATION.

(a) UNIVERSAL ACCESS.—All individuals in removal
proceedings shall have access to legal orientation
programs explaining their rights and the process.

(b) EXPANSION.—The Secretary shall expand the
Legal Orientation Program to all locations.

SEC. 4. PRIORITIZATION.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Alternatives to detention shall
be the default for immigration enforcement, with
detention used only when alternatives are
insufficient.

(b) VULNERABLE POPULATIONS.—Asylum seekers,
families, children, pregnant women, and individuals
with medical or mental health conditions shall
not be detained except in extraordinary
circumstances.

SEC. 5. EVALUATION.

(a) METRICS.—The Secretary shall track and report:
    (1) Appearance rates;
    (2) Final case outcomes;
    (3) Cost per participant;
    (4) Participant satisfaction.

(b) ANNUAL REPORT.—The Secretary shall report
annually to Congress on program effectiveness.

SEC. 6. AUTHORIZATION.

There is authorized to be appropriated $500,000,000
annually for alternatives to detention programs.
```

## End Private Immigration Detention Act

**Purpose**: Phase out private prison contracts for immigration detention.

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "End Private
Immigration Detention Act".

SEC. 2. PROHIBITION ON NEW CONTRACTS.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Beginning on the date of enactment,
the Secretary may not enter into, renew, or extend
any contract with a private entity for the
operation of a facility to detain individuals
in immigration proceedings.

(b) EXISTING CONTRACTS.—
    (1) Existing contracts shall not be renewed;
    (2) The Secretary shall develop a plan to
    transition away from private facilities within
    5 years of enactment.

SEC. 3. TRANSITION.

(a) PLAN.—Within 180 days of enactment, the
Secretary shall submit to Congress a plan for:
    (1) Phasing out private detention contracts;
    (2) Reducing overall detention capacity through
    expanded alternatives;
    (3) Addressing workforce impacts.

(b) PRIORITY.—In phasing out contracts, the
Secretary shall prioritize terminating contracts
with facilities that have documented patterns of:
    (1) Medical care deficiencies;
    (2) Abuse or mistreatment;
    (3) Deaths in custody;
    (4) Civil rights violations.

SEC. 4. IF DETENTION CONTINUES.

(a) FEDERAL OPERATION.—Any detention that continues
following phase-out shall be operated by the
federal government, not private contractors.

(b) STANDARDS.—Federal facilities shall comply
with all detention standards under the Dignity
in Immigration Detention Act.

(c) SIZE.—Federal detention capacity shall be
limited to the minimum necessary.

SEC. 5. PROHIBITION ON BED QUOTAS.

(a) IN GENERAL.—The Secretary may not enter into
any contract or agreement that requires or
incentivizes maintaining a minimum number of
individuals in detention.

(b) APPROPRIATIONS.—Appropriations Acts shall
not include language requiring maintenance of
detention bed levels.
```

## Immigration Right to Counsel Act

**Purpose**: Guarantee legal representation for detained individuals.

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Immigration Right
to Counsel Act".

SEC. 2. RIGHT TO APPOINTED COUNSEL.

(a) DETAINED INDIVIDUALS.—Any individual detained
in immigration proceedings shall be provided
appointed counsel at government expense if the
individual cannot afford counsel.

(b) CHILDREN.—Any individual under 18 in
immigration proceedings shall be provided
appointed counsel regardless of detention status.

(c) VULNERABLE INDIVIDUALS.—Appointed counsel
shall be provided for individuals with:
    (1) Mental health conditions affecting capacity;
    (2) Cognitive disabilities;
    (3) Serious medical conditions.

SEC. 3. IMPLEMENTATION.

(a) CONTRACTING.—The Attorney General may contract
with qualified legal services organizations,
bar associations, and private attorneys.

(b) STANDARDS.—The Attorney General shall establish
standards for competent representation.

(c) COMPENSATION.—Appointed counsel shall be
compensated at rates comparable to Criminal
Justice Act panel attorneys.

(d) TRAINING.—The Attorney General shall provide
training for appointed counsel.

SEC. 4. LEGAL ORIENTATION.

(a) UNIVERSAL ACCESS.—All individuals in removal
proceedings shall have access to legal orientation
programs.

(b) KNOW YOUR RIGHTS.—Programs shall explain
rights, available relief, and the court process.

SEC. 5. AUTHORIZATION.

There is authorized to be appropriated $500,000,000
annually for appointed counsel under this Act.
```

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Private Prison Prohibition Act

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Private
Prison Prohibition Act".

SECTION 2. PROHIBITION.

(a) IN GENERAL.—No state or local government
entity shall enter into or renew a contract
with a private entity for the incarceration
or detention of individuals, including
immigration detention.

(b) EXISTING CONTRACTS.—Existing contracts
shall not be renewed upon expiration.

SECTION 3. LOCAL DETENTION.

(a) PROHIBITION.—No local jail or detention
facility shall contract with federal immigration
authorities to detain individuals.

(b) EXCEPTION.—This section shall not prohibit
detention pursuant to a valid judicial warrant.

SECTION 4. TRANSPARENCY.

Any existing contract for private detention
shall be disclosed publicly.
```

### Model State Detention Accountability Act

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Detention
Accountability Act".

SECTION 2. STATE INSPECTION AUTHORITY.

(a) ACCESS.—State health and safety inspectors
shall have authority to inspect any detention
facility located in [State].

(b) STANDARDS.—Facilities shall comply with
state health, safety, and sanitation standards.

SECTION 3. REPORTING.

Any detention facility in [State] shall report
to the state annually on:
    (1) Population and demographics;
    (2) Deaths and serious injuries;
    (3) Medical staffing;
    (4) Complaints filed.

SECTION 4. ENFORCEMENT.

The Attorney General may bring action against
facilities that fail to comply with inspection
requirements or state standards.
```

## Constitutional Considerations

### Congressional Authority

| Basis | Application |
|-------|-------------|
| **Naturalization Clause** | Immigration detention authority |
| **Necessary and Proper** | Detention conditions |
| **Spending Power** | Funding conditions |
| **Commerce Clause** | Private prison regulation |

### Constitutional Constraints

| Provision | Application |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Due Process** | Conditions, hearing rights |
| **Eighth Amendment** | Conditions, medical care |
| **Fifth Amendment** | Liberty interest |
| **First Amendment** | Access to counsel, communication |

### Key Precedents

| Case | Holding |
|------|---------|
| **Zadvydas v. Davis** (2001) | Limits on indefinite detention |
| **Jennings v. Rodriguez** (2018) | No constitutional bond hearing requirement |
| **Demore v. Kim** (2003) | Mandatory detention constitutional |
| **Johnson v. Arteaga-Martinez** (2022) | No 6-month bond hearing requirement |
| **Flores Settlement** (1997) | Children detention standards |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Contract restructuring** | Reclassify private contracts | High |
| **Subcontracting** | Private services within federal | Medium |
| **Definition gaming** | Avoid "detention" classification | High |
| **Enforcement discretion** | Non-enforcement of standards | High |
| **Funding cuts** | Undermine alternatives | Medium |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| **Transition period** | Continued detention | Phase-out time |
| **Litigation delays** | Years to enforce | Court timelines |
| **Political vulnerability** | Policy reversal | Administrative authority |
| **Funding dependence** | Appropriations risk | Congressional control |
| **Geographic disparities** | Uneven implementation | Local variation |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Comprehensive definitions** to prevent reclassification
2. **Anti-subcontracting provisions** for private prison phase-out
3. **Binding standards** with private right of action
4. **Dedicated funding streams** to protect alternatives
5. **Expedited litigation procedures** for enforcement
6. **Statutory codification** to reduce political vulnerability
7. **Geographic equity requirements** in implementation
8. **Regular reporting** and oversight mechanisms
9. **Whistleblower protections** for facility workers
10. **Independent oversight** with enforcement authority

### Implementation Concerns

| Concern | Mitigation |
|---------|------------|
| **Transition capacity** | Phased approach with benchmarks |
| **Legal services availability** | Build-up period, fellowship programs |
| **Contractor litigation** | Careful contract drafting |
| **Public safety concerns** | Data on appearance rates |
| **Political opposition** | Coalition building |
