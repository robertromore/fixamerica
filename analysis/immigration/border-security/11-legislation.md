# Border Security: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Border security legislation addresses infrastructure, technology, personnel, processing, and enforcement authority. This document provides draft legislation for key reforms and analysis of existing legal frameworks.

## Border Security and Processing Act

**Purpose**: Establish a functional border management system with adequate infrastructure, personnel, technology, and processing capacity.

### Title I: Infrastructure Investment

```text
SECTION 101. SHORT TITLE.

This title may be cited as the "Border Infrastructure
Modernization Act".

SEC. 102. PORT OF ENTRY MODERNIZATION.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be
appropriated $5,000,000,000 over 5 fiscal years
for port of entry modernization.

(b) PRIORITIES.—Funds shall be used for:
    (1) expansion of existing land ports of entry;
    (2) construction of new ports of entry;
    (3) technology deployment at ports;
    (4) pedestrian and vehicle lane expansion.

(c) SPECIFIC PROJECTS.—Priority projects include:
    (1) San Ysidro Port of Entry—$1,000,000,000
    (2) Laredo Port of Entry—$500,000,000
    (3) El Paso Port of Entry—$400,000,000
    (4) Other designated ports—$2,100,000,000

SEC. 103. PROCESSING FACILITIES.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be
appropriated $1,500,000,000 for processing
facility construction and improvement.

(b) STANDARDS.—All facilities shall meet:
    (1) humanitarian standards for housing;
    (2) adequate space per individual;
    (3) access to medical care;
    (4) separate areas for families, children;
    (5) climate control and sanitation.

(c) CAPACITY.—Total capacity shall accommodate
peak processing needs without overcrowding.

SEC. 104. TECHNOLOGY DEPLOYMENT.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be
appropriated $3,000,000,000 over 5 years for
border technology.

(b) SYSTEMS.—Funds shall be used for:
    (1) integrated fixed towers;
    (2) remote video surveillance;
    (3) ground sensor networks;
    (4) unmanned aerial systems;
    (5) data integration systems.

(c) COVERAGE.—Technology shall provide detection
capability for the entire southern border.
```

### Title II: Personnel

```text
SEC. 201. BORDER PATROL STAFFING.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—The Border Patrol is authorized
to maintain not fewer than 22,000 agents.

(b) RECRUITMENT.—The Secretary shall:
    (1) establish recruitment incentives;
    (2) streamline hiring process;
    (3) expand training capacity;
    (4) provide retention bonuses for remote
    locations.

(c) TIMELINE.—Full staffing shall be achieved
within 3 years of enactment.

SEC. 202. CBP OFFICER STAFFING.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—CBP Office of Field Operations
is authorized to maintain not fewer than
28,000 officers.

(b) ALLOCATION.—Officers shall be allocated to
reduce wait times at ports of entry to not
more than 30 minutes on average.

SEC. 203. ASYLUM OFFICERS.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—USCIS shall hire sufficient
asylum officers to adjudicate asylum claims
within 180 days.

(b) ESTIMATE.—Not fewer than 3,000 asylum
officers shall be hired within 2 years.

SEC. 204. PROCESSING PERSONNEL.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—CBP may hire 5,000 processing
assistants to handle administrative functions.

(b) PURPOSE.—Processing assistants shall perform
non-law enforcement functions to allow agents
to focus on enforcement duties.
```

### Title III: Enforcement Authority

```text
SEC. 301. EXPEDITED REMOVAL.

(a) APPLICATION.—Section 235(b)(1) of the INA
shall be applied consistent with statutory
requirements.

(b) EXCEPTIONS.—Expedited removal shall not
apply to:
    (1) individuals expressing fear of persecution;
    (2) unaccompanied children;
    (3) victims of trafficking.

(c) DUE PROCESS.—Individuals shall have
opportunity to express fear before removal.

SEC. 302. CONSEQUENCE DELIVERY.

(a) POLICY.—The Secretary shall establish
consequence delivery policies that impose
meaningful consequences for illegal entry.

(b) OPTIONS.—Consequences may include:
    (1) formal removal with entry bar;
    (2) criminal prosecution for repeat offenders;
    (3) expedited removal;
    (4) voluntary return with record.

(c) CONSISTENCY.—Consequences shall be applied
consistently to similarly situated individuals.

SEC. 303. SMUGGLING PROSECUTION.

(a) PRIORITY.—The Department of Justice shall
prioritize prosecution of smugglers over
migrants.

(b) RESOURCES.—Additional prosecutors and
judges shall be assigned to border districts.

(c) PENALTIES.—Section 274 penalties shall be
enhanced for commercial smuggling operations.
```

### Title IV: Regional Cooperation

```text
SEC. 401. REGIONAL PROCESSING.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—The Secretary of State, in
coordination with DHS, is authorized to
establish regional processing centers.

(b) LOCATIONS.—Centers may be established in:
    (1) Mexico;
    (2) Guatemala;
    (3) Colombia;
    (4) other appropriate locations.

(c) FUNCTION.—Centers shall process:
    (1) refugee resettlement applications;
    (2) temporary worker applications;
    (3) parole applications;
    (4) other immigration benefits.

SEC. 402. ROOT CAUSE INVESTMENT.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be
appropriated $2,000,000,000 annually for
Central American development and security.

(b) PRIORITIES.—Funds shall address:
    (1) economic development;
    (2) security and rule of law;
    (3) governance and anti-corruption;
    (4) climate resilience.

SEC. 403. MEXICO COOPERATION.

(a) FRAMEWORK.—The Secretary of State shall
negotiate a comprehensive migration management
framework with Mexico.

(b) ELEMENTS.—The framework shall include:
    (1) joint enforcement operations;
    (2) information sharing;
    (3) repatriation cooperation;
    (4) humanitarian standards.
```

## Technology Accountability Act

**Purpose**: Ensure border technology investments are effective and accountable.

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Border Technology
Accountability Act".

SEC. 2. ACQUISITION REQUIREMENTS.

(a) TESTING.—All border technology systems shall
undergo operational testing before full deployment.

(b) METRICS.—Each system shall have defined
performance metrics including:
    (1) detection rate;
    (2) false positive rate;
    (3) response time capability;
    (4) integration with other systems.

(c) PILOT REQUIREMENT.—Systems over $100,000,000
shall have successful pilot programs before
full deployment.

SEC. 3. OVERSIGHT.

(a) GAO REVIEW.—The GAO shall review major
technology acquisitions annually.

(b) REPORTING.—DHS shall report to Congress
semi-annually on technology performance.

(c) COST TRACKING.—Full lifecycle costs shall
be tracked and reported.

SEC. 4. INTEGRATION REQUIREMENTS.

(a) INTEROPERABILITY.—All systems shall be
interoperable with existing CBP systems.

(b) DATA SHARING.—Systems shall share data with
relevant DHS components in real time.
```

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Border Support Act

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Border
Support Act".

SECTION 2. NATIONAL GUARD AUTHORIZATION.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—The Governor may deploy
National Guard personnel to support federal
border security operations.

(b) FUNCTIONS.—National Guard personnel may
perform support functions including:
    (1) surveillance and monitoring;
    (2) logistics and transportation;
    (3) administrative support;
    (4) construction and maintenance.

(c) LIMITATIONS.—National Guard personnel shall
not perform direct law enforcement functions
unless federalized.

SECTION 3. STATE FUNDING.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—There is appropriated $[amount]
for state border security activities.

(b) COORDINATION.—State activities shall be
coordinated with federal agencies.

SECTION 4. LOCAL ASSISTANCE.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—The state shall provide
assistance to border communities for:
    (1) law enforcement support;
    (2) humanitarian services;
    (3) infrastructure impact;
    (4) economic development.
```

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **False asylum claims** | Claims made to avoid removal | High |
| **Document fraud** | False identity documents | High |
| **Reentry after removal** | Return despite bars | High |
| **Smuggling adaptation** | New routes, methods | Medium |
| **Technology circumvention** | Defeat surveillance | Medium |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| **Processing capacity** | Releases continue | Insufficient resources |
| **Detention limits** | Cannot hold long | Legal constraints |
| **Removal barriers** | Countries won't accept | Diplomatic failure |
| **Root causes** | Migration continues | Insufficient investment |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Fraud detection** enhanced screening
2. **Biometric identification** prevent document fraud
3. **Repatriation agreements** for all nationalities
4. **Technology updates** adapt to smuggling
5. **Capacity investment** adequate processing
6. **Regional processing** reduce border pressure
7. **Diplomatic engagement** removal cooperation
8. **Root cause investment** reduce push factors
9. **Monitoring systems** track outcomes
10. **Adaptive management** respond to changes

### Implementation Concerns

| Concern | Mitigation |
|---------|------------|
| **Construction delays** | Multiple contractors |
| **Hiring challenges** | Enhanced incentives |
| **Technology failure** | Testing requirements |
| **Political reversal** | Statutory mandates |
