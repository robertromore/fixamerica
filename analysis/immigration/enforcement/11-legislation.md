# Immigration Enforcement: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Immigration enforcement legislation addresses priorities, procedures, due process, detention, employer accountability, and local cooperation. This document provides draft legislation for key reforms and analysis of existing legal frameworks.

## Enforcement Priorities Act

**Purpose**: Establish clear, consistent enforcement priorities that focus resources on genuine threats while protecting vulnerable populations.

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Immigration Enforcement
Priorities Act".

SEC. 2. ENFORCEMENT PRIORITIES.

(a) PRIORITY CATEGORIES.—The Secretary shall
prioritize enforcement resources on:
    (1) PRIORITY 1.—Individuals who pose a threat
    to national security.
    (2) PRIORITY 2.—Individuals who have been
    convicted of serious violent felonies.
    (3) PRIORITY 3.—Individuals who are leaders
    of criminal organizations.
    (4) PRIORITY 4.—Individuals who have been
    convicted of other felonies.

(b) DISCRETION.—For individuals not in priority
categories, the Secretary shall exercise
prosecutorial discretion considering:
    (1) length of presence in the United States;
    (2) family ties to US citizens or LPRs;
    (3) community ties and contributions;
    (4) immigration history;
    (5) humanitarian considerations.

SEC. 3. SENSITIVE LOCATIONS.

(a) PROHIBITION.—Immigration enforcement actions
shall not occur at:
    (1) schools, including early childhood through
    post-secondary;
    (2) medical facilities, including hospitals,
    clinics, and doctors' offices;
    (3) places of worship;
    (4) courthouses, except in exigent circumstances;
    (5) domestic violence shelters;
    (6) disaster relief sites.

(b) EXCEPTION.—The prohibition may be waived
only with approval of a senior official for
exigent circumstances.

SEC. 4. COLLATERAL ARRESTS.

(a) LIMITATION.—Officers shall not arrest
individuals not named in a warrant unless:
    (1) the individual poses an immediate threat;
    (2) the individual has a prior removal order;
    (3) the individual is a priority under
    section 2.

(b) DOCUMENTATION.—All collateral arrests shall
be documented and reported.

SEC. 5. ACCOUNTABILITY.

(a) REPORTING.—The Secretary shall publish
quarterly reports on enforcement actions by
priority category.

(b) TRAINING.—All enforcement officers shall
receive training on priorities and sensitive
locations.

(c) OVERSIGHT.—The DHS Office of Inspector General
shall review compliance annually.
```

## Due Process Protection Act

**Purpose**: Ensure due process for individuals in immigration proceedings.

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Immigration Due
Process Protection Act".

SEC. 2. RIGHT TO COUNSEL.

(a) DETAINED INDIVIDUALS.—The Secretary shall
provide appointed counsel for detained individuals
who cannot afford representation.

(b) PRIORITY POPULATIONS.—The Attorney General
shall ensure appointed counsel for:
    (1) unaccompanied children;
    (2) individuals with mental disabilities;
    (3) individuals facing expedited removal.

(c) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be
appropriated $500,000,000 annually for immigration
legal representation.

SEC. 3. EXPEDITED REMOVAL LIMITS.

(a) APPLICATION.—Section 235(b)(1) expedited
removal shall apply only to individuals:
    (1) encountered within 14 days of entry;
    (2) within 100 miles of the border.

(b) EXCLUSIONS.—Expedited removal shall not
apply to:
    (1) individuals expressing fear of persecution;
    (2) unaccompanied children;
    (3) individuals present more than 2 years.

SEC. 4. BOND HEARINGS.

(a) RIGHT TO HEARING.—Detained individuals shall
have a bond hearing within 48 hours of arrest.

(b) STANDARD.—Bond shall be granted unless the
government demonstrates:
    (1) flight risk; or
    (2) danger to the community.

(c) AMOUNT.—Bond shall be set at an amount the
individual can reasonably afford.

SEC. 5. DETENTION LIMITS.

(a) TIME LIMIT.—Individuals shall not be detained
pending proceedings for more than 6 months
absent exceptional circumstances.

(b) REVIEW.—Detention beyond 6 months shall be
reviewed by an immigration judge every 30 days.
```

## Employer Accountability Act

**Purpose**: Establish meaningful employer accountability while protecting workers.

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Employer Accountability
Act".

SEC. 2. MANDATORY E-VERIFY.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—All employers shall verify work
authorization through E-Verify.

(b) PHASE-IN.—
    (1) Federal contractors—Year 1
    (2) Employers with 500+ employees—Year 2
    (3) Employers with 50+ employees—Year 3
    (4) All employers—Year 4

(c) EXISTING EMPLOYEES.—Reverification of existing
employees shall be completed within 5 years.

SEC. 3. PENALTIES.

(a) FIRST VIOLATION.—Civil penalty of $2,500 to
$5,000 per unauthorized worker.

(b) SECOND VIOLATION.—Civil penalty of $5,000 to
$10,000 per unauthorized worker.

(c) PATTERN VIOLATION.—Civil penalty of $10,000
to $25,000 per unauthorized worker and criminal
prosecution.

(d) KNOWING HIRE.—Criminal penalty for knowing
hire of unauthorized workers.

SEC. 4. WORKER PROTECTIONS.

(a) NO RETALIATION.—Workers reporting labor
violations shall not face retaliation based
on immigration status.

(b) LABOR RIGHTS.—Immigration status shall not
affect labor rights including:
    (1) minimum wage;
    (2) workplace safety;
    (3) right to organize;
    (4) workers' compensation.

(c) WITNESS PROTECTION.—Workers cooperating in
investigations may be granted deferred action.

SEC. 5. ENFORCEMENT FOCUS.

(a) AUDITS.—ICE shall conduct systematic I-9
audits.

(b) EMPLOYER FOCUS.—Enforcement shall prioritize
employer accountability over worker arrest.

(c) NO RAIDS.—Mass worksite raids targeting
workers shall not be conducted.
```

## Local Cooperation Reform Act

**Purpose**: Reform federal-local cooperation to focus on serious crimes while respecting local discretion.

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Local Cooperation
Reform Act".

SEC. 2. 287(g) REFORM.

(a) SCOPE.—287(g) agreements shall be limited to:
    (1) identification of individuals convicted
    of serious violent felonies;
    (2) identification of individuals subject
    to final orders of removal.

(b) TRAINING.—All 287(g) officers shall complete
training on civil rights and profiling.

(c) OVERSIGHT.—DHS shall conduct annual compliance
reviews.

(d) TERMINATION.—Agreements may be terminated for
violations.

SEC. 3. DETAINER REFORM.

(a) PROBABLE CAUSE.—Detainers shall be issued
only upon probable cause of removability.

(b) TIME LIMIT.—Detainers shall request detention
for not more than 48 hours.

(c) LIABILITY.—The federal government shall
indemnify local jurisdictions for compliance.

SEC. 4. ANTI-COMMANDEERING.

(a) NO MANDATE.—State and local governments shall
not be required to:
    (1) enforce federal immigration law;
    (2) honor detainers;
    (3) share information beyond federal records.

(b) NO RETALIATION.—Federal funds shall not be
withheld based on sanctuary policies.

SEC. 5. COMMUNICATION.

(a) PERMITTED.—Local jurisdictions may communicate
with ICE regarding:
    (1) individuals convicted of serious felonies;
    (2) public safety threats.

(b) PROHIBITED.—Local jurisdictions shall not
be required to inquire about immigration status
during routine encounters.
```

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Sensitive Locations Act

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Sensitive
Locations Protection Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

"Sensitive location" means:
    (a) public and private schools;
    (b) healthcare facilities;
    (c) places of worship;
    (d) courthouses;
    (e) domestic violence shelters;
    (f) public benefits offices.

SECTION 3. PROHIBITION.

State and local law enforcement shall not:
    (a) assist in immigration enforcement at
    sensitive locations;
    (b) provide information to ICE regarding
    individuals at sensitive locations;
    (c) allow ICE access to sensitive locations
    under state control.

SECTION 4. EXCEPTIONS.

This section shall not apply when:
    (a) required by court order;
    (b) exigent circumstances exist.

SECTION 5. PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.

Individuals harmed by violations may bring civil
action for damages and injunctive relief.
```

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Priority gaming** | Classification disputes | Medium |
| **E-Verify fraud** | Identity theft | High |
| **Document fraud** | Circumvent verification | High |
| **Reentry** | Return after removal | High |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| **Resource limits** | Cannot enforce all | Scale |
| **Country cooperation** | Cannot remove all | Diplomatic |
| **Court backlog** | Delayed consequences | Capacity |
| **Employer compliance** | Limited effect | Enforcement |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Biometric E-Verify** for identity verification
2. **Employer audits** systematic and regular
3. **Country cooperation** diplomatic pressure
4. **Court resources** adequate judges
5. **Priority training** consistent application
6. **Oversight** independent monitoring
7. **Data collection** track outcomes
8. **Regular review** assess effectiveness
9. **Fraud detection** prevent circumvention
10. **Accountability** for violations
