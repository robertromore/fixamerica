# Employment-Based Immigration: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Employment-based immigration legislation addresses green card backlogs, H-1B reform, worker protections, and new pathways. This document provides draft legislation for key reforms and analysis of existing legal frameworks.

## Per-Country Cap Elimination Act

**Purpose**: Eliminate employment-based per-country caps to address backlog.

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Employment-Based
Immigrant Fairness Act".

SEC. 2. ELIMINATION OF PER-COUNTRY CAPS.

(a) EMPLOYMENT-BASED.—Section 202(a)(2) of the
Immigration and Nationality Act (8 U.S.C.
1152(a)(2)) is amended by striking "7 percent"
and inserting "15 percent" for the first 5
fiscal years after enactment, and eliminating
the limitation entirely thereafter.

(b) FAMILY PROTECTION.—During the transition
period, not less than 15 percent of employment-
based immigrant visas shall be reserved for
countries other than the two countries with
the largest backlogs.

SEC. 3. DEPENDENT EXEMPTION.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Spouses and minor children of
employment-based immigrants shall not be counted
against the numerical limitations under section
201(d).

(b) CALCULATION.—The numerical limitations shall
apply only to principal beneficiaries.

SEC. 4. VISA RECAPTURE.

(a) RECAPTURE.—All employment-based immigrant
visas that were authorized but not issued from
fiscal year 1992 through the fiscal year
preceding enactment shall be available for
issuance.

(b) AVAILABILITY.—Recaptured visas shall be
available in addition to annual allocations.

SEC. 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the first day of
the first fiscal year beginning after enactment.
```

## H-1B Reform Act

**Purpose**: Reform H-1B selection, wages, and worker protections.

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "H-1B Reform Act".

SEC. 2. WAGE-BASED SELECTION.

(a) SELECTION CRITERIA.—When petitions exceed the
numerical limitation, USCIS shall select petitions
in descending order by offered wage relative to
the prevailing wage for the occupation.

(b) FACTORS.—Selection shall prioritize:
    (1) Ratio of offered wage to prevailing wage;
    (2) Advanced degree from US institution;
    (3) Prior compliance record of employer.

SEC. 3. WAGE REQUIREMENTS.

(a) MINIMUM WAGE.—The wage paid to an H-1B worker
shall be not less than the greater of:
    (1) the Level III prevailing wage for the
    occupation; or
    (2) the actual wage paid to US workers in
    similar positions.

(b) DEPENDENT EMPLOYERS.—Employers with more than
15 percent H-1B workers shall pay not less than
Level IV prevailing wage.

SEC. 4. PORTABILITY.

(a) IMMEDIATE PORTABILITY.—An H-1B worker may
begin employment with a new employer upon filing
of a petition, without waiting for approval.

(b) GRACE PERIOD.—An H-1B worker whose employment
ends shall have 180 days to find new employment
and file a new petition.

(c) STATUS RETENTION.—An H-1B worker in grace
period maintains lawful status.

SEC. 5. WORKER PROTECTIONS.

(a) NON-DISPLACEMENT.—Employers shall not displace
US workers within 90 days before or after filing
an H-1B petition for the same or similar position.

(b) WHISTLEBLOWER.—Workers who report violations
shall be protected from retaliation.

(c) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—Workers may bring
civil action for violations.

SEC. 6. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) AUDITS.—DOL shall conduct random audits of
H-1B employers.

(b) SITE VISITS.—USCIS shall conduct site visits
for all new employer petitions.

(c) PENALTIES.—Penalties for violations shall
include:
    (1) Back pay to affected workers;
    (2) Civil fines of $10,000-$50,000 per
    violation;
    (3) Debarment for pattern violations.
```

## STEM Immigration Act

**Purpose**: Exempt STEM graduates from employment-based caps.

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "STEM Immigration Act".

SEC. 2. STEM EXEMPTION.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Aliens shall not be subject to
the numerical limitations under section 201(d)
if the alien:
    (1) has earned a doctoral degree in a STEM
    field from a US institution of higher
    education; or
    (2) has earned a master's degree in a STEM
    field from a US institution and has an offer
    of employment in that field.

(b) STEM FIELDS.—The Secretary shall define STEM
fields to include at minimum:
    (1) computer science and engineering;
    (2) physical sciences;
    (3) life sciences;
    (4) mathematics and statistics;
    (5) engineering disciplines.

SEC. 3. REQUIREMENTS.

(a) EMPLOYMENT.—The alien must have an offer of
employment in the STEM field.

(b) WAGE.—The offered wage must be at least the
prevailing wage.

(c) DEGREE.—The degree must be from an accredited
institution.

SEC. 4. LABOR CERTIFICATION EXEMPTION.

Aliens qualifying under this section shall not
require labor certification under section
212(a)(5)(A).
```

## Startup Visa Act

**Purpose**: Create visa category for entrepreneurs.

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Startup Visa Act".

SEC. 2. STARTUP VISA.

(a) NEW CATEGORY.—Section 101(a)(15) of the INA
is amended by adding:
    "(W) an alien who seeks to enter the United
    States to develop a startup enterprise with
    high growth potential."

(b) REQUIREMENTS.—An alien is eligible if:
    (1) has received $250,000 or more in financing
    from qualified US investors;
    (2) has at least 10 percent ownership in the
    startup; and
    (3) plays a central role in startup operations.

SEC. 3. DURATION.

(a) INITIAL.—Startup visas shall be valid for
3 years.

(b) EXTENSION.—May be extended for 2 years if:
    (1) raised additional $500,000; or
    (2) created 5 full-time US jobs; or
    (3) achieved $500,000 in revenue.

SEC. 4. PATH TO PERMANENT RESIDENCE.

(a) ELIGIBILITY.—A startup visa holder may adjust
status if within 5 years:
    (1) created 10 full-time US jobs;
    (2) raised $1,000,000 in financing; or
    (3) achieved $1,000,000 in revenue.

(b) CATEGORY.—Adjustment shall be in the EB-2
category.
```

## Green Card Increase Act

**Purpose**: Increase employment-based green card numbers.

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Employment-Based
Green Card Expansion Act".

SEC. 2. INCREASED ALLOCATION.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 201(d)(1) of the INA is
amended by striking "140,000" and inserting
"290,000".

(b) CATEGORY ALLOCATION.—The increased visas
shall be distributed:
    (1) EB-1: 58,000 (20%)
    (2) EB-2: 87,000 (30%)
    (3) EB-3: 87,000 (30%)
    (4) EB-4: 29,000 (10%)
    (5) EB-5: 29,000 (10%)

SEC. 3. UNUSED VISA ROLLOVER.

Unused visas in any category shall roll over
to the next fiscal year and remain in that
category.

SEC. 4. DEPENDENT TREATMENT.

Spouses and children of employment-based
immigrants shall not count against numerical
limitations.
```

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Immigrant Entrepreneur Act

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Immigrant
Entrepreneur Act".

SECTION 2. STATE ENTREPRENEUR PROGRAM.

The State shall establish an immigrant entrepreneur
program to:
    (a) provide resources and support to
    immigrant-founded businesses;
    (b) advocate for federal startup visa
    legislation;
    (c) connect entrepreneurs with investors.

SECTION 3. BUSINESS LICENSING.

Immigrant entrepreneurs in lawful status shall
be eligible for state business licenses regardless
of visa type.

SECTION 4. ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENT.

The State economic development agency shall
prioritize outreach to immigrant entrepreneurs
in growth sectors.
```

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Wage gaming** | Inflate then reduce | Medium |
| **Specialty occupation** | Broad definition | High |
| **Third-party** | Placement evasion | High |
| **Multiple filing** | Lottery gaming | Medium |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| **Transition bump** | Countries displaced | Per-country elimination |
| **Backlog persistence** | Years to clear | Scale |
| **Enforcement gaps** | Violations continue | Resources |
| **Portability limits** | Worker dependence | System design |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Transition protection** for smaller countries
2. **Adequate numbers** to clear backlog
3. **Enforcement resources** for compliance
4. **Worker-initiated** portability options
5. **Wage verification** systematic audits
6. **Specialty occupation** clearer standards
7. **Third-party** enhanced scrutiny
8. **Filing limits** prevent gaming
9. **Whistleblower** protection strengthening
10. **Regular reporting** on compliance

### Implementation Concerns

| Concern | Mitigation |
|---------|------------|
| **USCIS capacity** | Phased implementation |
| **DOL resources** | Enforcement funding |
| **Employer compliance** | Clear guidance |
| **Transition** | Protect all parties |
