# Employment-Based Immigration: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes employment-based immigration reform -- specifically eliminating per-country green card caps, reforming H-1B selection and wages, strengthening worker protections, creating new pathways such as startup visas, and increasing overall employment-based green card numbers -- through nine distinct political perspectives. The core questions are: how many employment-based immigrants should the United States admit, how should they be selected, what protections should temporary workers receive, who benefits and who is harmed by the current system, and how should the tension between employer demand for talent, worker protection, and domestic labor market effects be resolved? Each perspective receives engagement-consistency ratings and position scores, culminating in compromise proposals that bridge multiple viewpoints.

Employment-based immigration is a distinctive policy domain because the system's dysfunction is widely acknowledged -- with 1+ million pending green card applications, wait times exceeding 50 years for Indian EB-3 applicants, and an H-1B lottery that selects randomly rather than by merit -- yet the proposed remedies divide sharply along lines that do not map neatly onto the traditional left-right spectrum. Business interests and progressive immigration advocates both support expanding legal immigration, while labor unions and immigration restrictionists both express concern about wage effects. The employer-tied structure of the system creates a power imbalance that progressives and libertarians both criticize from different angles. This cross-cutting alignment pattern means that employment-based immigration reform has unusual coalition potential but also unusual vulnerability to being bundled with more contentious immigration topics, which has repeatedly blocked standalone reform.

### Engagement Consistency (1-5)

This metric assesses how reliably a perspective's stated positions align with its underlying principles, and how stable those positions are when concerns are addressed. Higher scores indicate more predictable negotiating partners.

| Level | Label | Indicators |
|-------|-------|------------|
| 5 | Highly Consistent | Stated positions reflect underlying principles; positions stable when concerns addressed |
| 4 | Mostly Consistent | Principles generally align with positions; minor strategic flexibility |
| 3 | Mixed Consistency | Some positions principled, others reflect coalition pressures or unstated concerns |
| 2 | Low Consistency | Positions shift frequently; stated concerns differ from underlying priorities |
| 1 | Unpredictable | Positions appear driven primarily by opposition dynamics rather than principles |

**Coalition-Building Considerations:**

- Alignment gap: When stated concerns differ from underlying priorities, addressing stated concerns may not secure support
- Position stability: Perspectives with shifting positions require ongoing negotiation
- Principle consistency: Perspectives that apply principles uniformly are more predictable partners
- Internal diversity: Some perspectives contain factions with different priorities

## Perspective Analyses

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 2/5 - Low Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Stated commitment to free markets and economic competitiveness should produce strong support for expanding legal employment-based immigration, yet conservative positions have shifted toward restriction across all immigration categories including employment-based; business-wing conservatives genuinely support expansion while the populist base demands reduction |
| Principle consistency | "Free market" and "merit-based" rhetoric coexists with support for caps and restrictions that prevent market-clearing allocation of talent; conservatives claim to support legal immigration while opposing most bills that would expand it; the distinction between legal and illegal immigration, central to conservative framing, dissolves when legal pathways are blocked |
| Goalpost stability | Low; conservative positions on employment-based immigration have shifted dramatically -- Reagan signed the 1990 Immigration Act expanding employment visas, while recent Republican platforms have called for reducing legal immigration; "merit-based" reform has been redefined from expanding skilled immigration to reducing overall numbers |
| Zero-sum behavior | Significant; employment-based immigration debates are frequently treated as proxy battles in the broader immigration culture war rather than evaluated on economic competitiveness merits |

**Justification:** Conservatives face a deep internal contradiction on employment-based immigration. The business wing -- represented by the Chamber of Commerce, tech industry, and traditional pro-growth Republicans -- genuinely supports expanding H-1B visas, eliminating green card backlogs, and creating startup visa categories. The populist-restrictionist wing -- now dominant in much of the Republican base -- views all immigration expansion with suspicion and frames employment-based immigration as wage competition for American workers. The score of 2 reflects the instability of conservative positions: stated support for "legal immigration" and "merit-based" systems frequently collapses when specific expansion bills are introduced, with concerns about wage depression and cultural change overriding free-market principles.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Republican "Reforming American Immigration for a Strong Economy (RAISE) Act" would have cut legal immigration by 50%, including employment-based categories
- Business Roundtable and Chamber of Commerce consistently lobby for H-1B expansion and green card backlog relief, often opposed by Republican caucus members
- Heritage Foundation has published competing analyses -- some supporting skilled immigration expansion, others warning of labor market displacement
- Trump administration imposed significant restrictions on H-1B through executive action despite no legislative mandate to reduce the program
- Republican support for the Fairness for High-Skilled Immigrants Act (per-country cap elimination) has been inconsistent, with members alternately supporting and blocking floor votes

#### Alternative Proposals

- Merit-based point system replacing employer sponsorship with government-scored selection
- Mandatory E-Verify for all employers with H-1B or green card workers
- Prevailing wage set at 110% of median for the occupation and area
- Reduction of family-based immigration to redirect numbers to employment-based categories
- Sunset provisions requiring congressional reauthorization of expanded categories
- Enhanced fraud investigation and debarment enforcement
- Limit total immigration to a fixed percentage of the existing population

#### Coalition Potential

- **Natural allies:** Populist (on enforcement and wage protections), Constitutionalist (on congressional authority over immigration numbers)
- **Potential bridges:** Centrist (on market-responsive reforms), Liberal (on per-country cap elimination if paired with enforcement), Libertarian (on reducing bureaucracy)
- **Unlikely partners:** Progressive, Democratic Socialist (on expanding immigration numbers and weakening employer control)

**Justification:** Liberals have developed a comprehensive employment-based immigration platform that integrates expansion (more green cards, higher H-1B caps) with worker protection (portability, wage floors, anti-retaliation) and equity concerns (per-country cap elimination, family unity). The slight reduction from a perfect score reflects the tension between the tech industry donors who prioritize talent access and the labor constituents who prioritize wage protection -- a tension that occasionally produces ambiguity about the appropriate scale of expansion.

**Justification:** Progressives apply a consistent labor justice framework to employment-based immigration: the current system creates a subordinate class of workers who cannot bargain effectively because their immigration status depends on employer goodwill, and this vulnerability depresses wages and conditions for all workers. ### Libertarian Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 5/5 - Highly Consistent**

|-----------|------------|

| Principle consistency | Individual liberty and free market principles are uniformly applied: oppose caps (government rationing of labor), oppose employer-tied visas (government restriction of worker mobility), oppose prevailing wage requirements (government price-fixing in labor markets), and support open immigration as the natural companion to free trade |
| Goalpost stability | Highly stable; libertarian support for immigration liberalization has been consistent for decades and has not shifted with populist pressures; the Cato Institute and Reason Foundation have maintained pro-immigration positions even as the Republican Party shifted restrictionist |
| Zero-sum behavior | None; explicitly rejects the premise that immigration is zero-sum; argues that voluntary employment contracts benefit both parties and that restricting labor mobility harms economic efficiency |

**Justification:** Libertarians have the most internally consistent position on employment-based immigration: government should not restrict voluntary employment contracts between workers and employers regardless of national origin. Caps, lotteries, per-country limits, employer-tied visas, and wage mandates are all government interference in free labor markets. This framework is intellectually coherent and consistently applied -- libertarians support open immigration even when it is politically unpopular within the broader right-of-center coalition. Every position follows logically from the core principles of individual liberty and free markets.

## Compromise Proposals

### Compromise 1: Employment-Based Immigration Modernization Act

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Progressive + Libertarian + Centrist + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** The employment-based immigration system has not been updated since 1990, producing irrational outcomes -- a random lottery for H-1B selection, 30-50+ year backlogs for some countries, and worker vulnerability that depresses wages -- and targeted modernization can address the most egregious dysfunctions without requiring agreement on overall immigration levels.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation replaces the H-1B lottery with wage-based selection, prioritizing petitions where the offered wage is highest relative to prevailing wages for the occupation and area. The minimum wage for all H-1B workers is set at the Level III prevailing wage (the median), eliminating the entry-level wage pathway that body shop firms exploit. Per-country caps on employment-based green cards are raised from 7% to 15% for a 5-year transition period and eliminated entirely thereafter, with a 15% floor reserved for countries other than the top two to protect diversity during transition. Dependents (spouses and minor children) of employment-based green card recipients are exempted from the numerical cap, with the 140,000 annual limit applying only to principal applicants. H-1B workers may begin employment with a new employer upon filing a transfer petition (immediate portability), and workers whose employment ends receive a 180-day grace period to find new employment while maintaining lawful status. Enhanced enforcement includes random DOL audits, USCIS site visits for all new employer petitions, civil fines of $10,000-$50,000 per violation, and debarment for pattern violators. Whistleblower protections ensure that workers who report violations maintain their immigration status during investigation.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Conservative | Wage-based selection eliminates body shop abuse; Level III minimum ensures no cheap labor; enforcement provisions hold employers accountable; per-country cap transition protects diversity; no increase in overall annual numbers for principal applicants |
| Liberal | Per-country cap elimination addresses discriminatory backlogs; dependent exemption reunites families; portability protects workers; enforcement prevents abuse; whistleblower protections empower workers |
| Progressive | Portability attacks employer dependence; higher wage floors reduce exploitation; whistleblower protections empower workers; per-country cap elimination addresses racial discrimination in the system |
| Libertarian | Wage-based selection is more rational than a lottery; portability increases labor market mobility; per-country cap elimination reduces government rationing; dependent exemption reduces government counting of family members |
| Centrist | Evidence-based reforms targeting documented problems; wage-based selection improves efficiency; enforcement addresses fraud; bipartisan appeal across multiple provisions |
| Democratic Socialist | Portability empowers workers against employers; higher wages reduce exploitation; whistleblower protections enable worker agency; per-country cap elimination addresses structural discrimination |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Accept dependent exemption that effectively increases total green card recipients; accept federal portability mandate |
| Liberal | Accept that overall cap remains at 140,000 principals; accept transition rather than immediate per-country cap elimination |
| Progressive | Accept wage-based selection that privileges highest bidders; accept reform within existing system rather than structural transformation |
| Libertarian | Accept wage mandates (Level III minimum); accept enforcement expansion; accept that caps remain rather than being eliminated |
| Centrist | Accept that some provisions (dependent exemption) expand total numbers beyond 140,000; accept transition timeline |
| Democratic Socialist | Accept employer-based system reform rather than worker-controlled immigration; accept wage-based selection that favors large corporations |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 6/9 perspectives (Constitutionalist neutral; Populist and Religious Right may join selectively on enforcement and wage provisions)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 5/6 coalition members rated 4+ consistency (Liberal, Progressive, Libertarian, Centrist, Democratic Socialist)
- **Durability:** High; wage-based selection and portability create constituencies of workers and employers who benefit; per-country cap elimination addresses an injustice with vocal affected communities; enforcement provisions satisfy both labor and anti-fraud constituencies; reforms are self-reinforcing once implemented
- **Implementation Path:** Bipartisan sponsorship leveraging the multiple prior Congress sessions where per-country cap and H-1B reform bills received significant support; frame as modernization rather than expansion; separate from comprehensive immigration debates that trigger broader opposition; build on existing administrative reforms (Biden-era H-1B modernization rule) to demonstrate feasibility

### Compromise 2: American Talent Competitiveness Act

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Centrist + Libertarian + Religious Right + Constitutionalist

**Core Agreement:** The United States is losing the global competition for talent to countries with more efficient immigration systems, and retaining STEM graduates from US universities, creating an entrepreneur pathway, and recapturing unused visas are narrowly targeted reforms that strengthen economic competitiveness without fundamentally altering immigration levels.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation exempts from employment-based numerical caps any immigrant who has earned a doctoral degree in a STEM field from an accredited US institution and has an offer of employment in that field at or above the prevailing wage. A new startup visa category allows foreign entrepreneurs who have received $250,000 or more in financing from qualified US investors to remain for 3 years, with extension for 2 additional years upon meeting performance benchmarks (raising $500,000 additional capital, creating 5 full-time US jobs, or achieving $500,000 in revenue), and a path to permanent residence through EB-2 upon creating 10 jobs, raising $1 million, or achieving $1 million in revenue within 5 years. All employment-based immigrant visas authorized but not issued from fiscal year 1992 through the current year (estimated 200,000+) are recaptured and made available in addition to annual allocations. Unused visas in any category roll over to the next fiscal year rather than being lost. State immigrant entrepreneur programs receive $50 million annually in federal matching grants for technical assistance, business licensing support, and investor connection services.

| Libertarian | STEM exemption reduces government rationing; startup visa enables entrepreneurship; visa recapture eliminates government waste; state programs leverage rather than replace markets |
| Religious Right | STEM graduates contribute to communities; startup visa requires investment in American economy; recaptured visas address existing authorization; state programs support integration |
| Constitutionalist | All provisions within congressional power; recaptured visas were previously authorized; STEM field definition set by statute; startup visa criteria established legislatively |

| Centrist | Accept that STEM exemption is not capped and total impact is uncertain; accept startup visa thresholds as set |
| Libertarian | Accept government-defined STEM fields; accept investment thresholds for startup visa; accept prevailing wage requirements |

| Constitutionalist | Accept delegation to Secretary for STEM field definitions within statutory parameters |

- **Coalition Size:** 6/9 perspectives (Progressive and Democratic Socialist moderately supportive but would prefer broader reform; Populist opposes STEM exemption and new pathways)

- **Implementation Path:** Build on the long history of bipartisan STEM immigration bills; package visa recapture (no-cost recovery of already-authorized visas) with STEM exemption and startup visa as a competitiveness package; frame as responding to Canadian Express Entry and UK talent visa competition; business community provides lobbying support

### Compromise 3: Worker Protection and Employer Accountability Act

**Bridges:** Liberal + Progressive + Populist + Centrist + Religious Right + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** Regardless of disagreements about how many employment-based immigrants to admit, there is broad agreement that workers already in the system deserve protection from exploitation, that employer accountability is inadequate, and that the body shop outsourcing model abuses both immigrant and domestic workers.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation establishes immediate H-1B portability allowing workers to begin employment with a new employer upon filing a transfer petition. Workers whose employment ends receive a 180-day grace period to find new employment while maintaining lawful status. The minimum H-1B wage is raised to the Level III prevailing wage (median) for all employers, and to Level IV for H-1B-dependent employers (15%+ of workforce). Non-displacement requirements prohibit employers from laying off US workers within 90 days before or after filing an H-1B petition for the same or similar position. Enhanced enforcement includes mandatory random audits of H-1B employers, USCIS site visits for all new employer petitions and third-party placement arrangements, civil penalties of $10,000-$50,000 per violation, and debarment from all visa programs for employers with patterns of violations. Whistleblower protections guarantee that workers who report violations maintain their immigration status during investigation and receive protection from retaliation. Workers receive a private right of action to pursue violations in federal court with fee-shifting for prevailing plaintiffs. A $50 million annual employer compliance fund finances DOL enforcement staff, audit infrastructure, and worker outreach programs. Annual public reporting on H-1B employer compliance, wages, and workforce composition creates transparency and accountability.

|-------------|-----------------------------------|

| Progressive | Portability attacks employer dependence; higher wages reduce exploitation; whistleblower protections empower workers; non-displacement protects domestic workers; private right of action enables worker agency |
| Populist | Higher wages mean employers cannot use H-1B for cheap labor; non-displacement protects American workers; enforcement cracks down on body shops; audit requirements increase accountability; wage transparency exposes abusers |
| Centrist | Evidence-based enforcement targeting documented problems; wage reform addresses body shop model; portability improves labor market efficiency; transparency enables accountability; bipartisan worker protection framing |
| Religious Right | Worker dignity protected through portability and wage floors; exploitation prevention aligns with human dignity values; whistleblower protections prevent retaliation against vulnerable workers; non-displacement protects existing workers |
| Democratic Socialist | Portability empowers workers against employers; higher wages reduce exploitation; whistleblower protections enable worker agency; private right of action provides legal tools; enforcement resources create accountability infrastructure |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Liberal | Accept that the bill does not expand visa numbers or address the green card backlog |
| Progressive | Accept reform within existing employer-based system rather than structural transformation; accept that self-petition is not included |
| Populist | Accept portability provisions that make it easier for foreign workers to stay in the labor market; accept that the bill does not reduce H-1B numbers |
| Centrist | Accept $50 million enforcement spending; accept private right of action that may increase litigation |

- **High Consistency Ratio:** 4/6 coalition members rated 4+ consistency (Liberal, Progressive, Centrist, Democratic Socialist)
- **Durability:** High; worker protections once enacted create beneficiary constituencies; enforcement infrastructure becomes self-sustaining through penalties; wage floors indexed to prevailing wage adjust automatically; transparency requirements create accountability that is difficult to reverse; the enforcement-focused framing makes repeal politically costly
- **Implementation Path:** Frame as anti-fraud and worker protection rather than immigration expansion; populist "protect American workers" narrative provides political cover; labor union support provides organizational capacity; business support from compliant employers who face unfair competition from body shops; enforcement-only framing avoids triggering comprehensive immigration debate

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The most effective coalition for employment-based immigration reform operates on two parallel tracks. First, a broad modernization coalition (6-7 perspectives) can advance per-country cap elimination, H-1B wage-based selection, and worker portability -- reforms with strong evidence bases, cross-ideological framing potential (deregulation for libertarians, worker empowerment for progressives, competitiveness for centrists, merit for conservatives), and established legislative momentum from prior Congresses. Second, an enforcement-and-protection coalition (Liberal, Progressive, Populist, Centrist, Religious Right, Democratic Socialist) can advance worker protections and employer accountability reforms that do not require agreement on immigration levels. These two tracks can proceed simultaneously, building reform incrementally rather than depending on comprehensive legislation that has repeatedly failed.

### Key Obstacles

- **Bundling with broader immigration:** Employment-based immigration reform has repeatedly been blocked by being linked to more contentious immigration topics (unauthorized immigration, asylum, border security); standalone legislation has broader coalition support but is difficult to bring to a floor vote when comprehensive immigration debate absorbs political energy
- **Populist opposition:** The populist perspective is the most consistent source of structural opposition, viewing all employment-based immigration expansion as a threat to American workers regardless of evidence; this perspective has significant influence within the Republican caucus and can block bipartisan legislation
- **Business-labor tension:** Employers want more visas with fewer conditions; labor wants more protections with fewer visas; bridging this gap requires reforms that simultaneously expand access and strengthen protections (the modernization approach) rather than trading one against the other
- **Affected communities cannot vote:** Immigrants in the green card backlog, H-1B holders, and their families are directly affected by the system's dysfunction but cannot vote; this creates a political accountability gap where congressional inaction has no electoral consequences
- **Administrative capacity:** USCIS is fee-funded and chronically backlogged; any reform that changes processing requirements needs enforcement funding and implementation planning to avoid worsening processing delays

### Low Consistency Partners

Conservative (2/5) and Populist (2/5) perspectives rate at low consistency on employment-based immigration, reflecting internal contradictions within both perspectives. Conservative support is most reliable on H-1B wage reform and enforcement (which address fraud concerns) and least reliable on expanding numbers or portability (which conflict with the restrictionist base). Engaging conservative partners requires framing reform as merit-based selection, anti-fraud enforcement, and economic competitiveness rather than immigration expansion. Populist support is most accessible when reform is framed as cracking down on body shops, raising wages, and protecting American workers, and least accessible when framed as increasing immigration numbers or benefiting tech companies. Religious Right (3/5) support is most accessible through family unity and human dignity framing and least accessible when reform is perceived as primarily serving employer economic interests.

### Recommended Approach

1. **Separate employment-based reform from comprehensive immigration:** Standalone employment-based bills have broader coalition support than comprehensive packages; avoid triggering border and unauthorized immigration debates that collapse coalitions

2. **Lead with per-country cap elimination and H-1B wage reform:** These reforms command the broadest support (7-8 perspectives at 5+) and address the most widely acknowledged system failures; per-country cap elimination has passed the House as standalone legislation; H-1B wage reform addresses fraud concerns that unite enforcement and worker protection constituencies

3. **Build the worker protection bridge:** Worker portability and employer accountability attract the unusual coalition of progressives (worker empowerment), libertarians (labor market mobility), populists (body shop crackdowns), and centrists (efficiency); frame as protecting both immigrant and American workers

4. **Use competitiveness as the unifying narrative:** Framing employment-based immigration as talent competitiveness -- the US is losing to Canada, UK, and Australia -- accesses support from business conservatives, centrists, and liberals without triggering the cultural anxieties that restrictionists exploit

5. **Advance STEM and startup pathways as economic growth:** "Staple a green card to a diploma" and startup visas have decade-long bipartisan track records; package as innovation and job creation rather than immigration expansion

6. **Build enforcement credibility first:** For perspectives resistant to expansion, demonstrating that existing programs are effectively enforced reduces the argument that expansion will bring more abuse; the Worker Protection and Employer Accountability Act (Compromise 3) builds enforcement infrastructure that makes subsequent expansion politically feasible

7. **Engage affected communities through proxy advocacy:** Since immigrants in the queue cannot vote, reform depends on proxy constituencies -- employers, universities, state and local governments, and American family members -- who can provide political motivation for congressional action
