# School-Based Mental Health: Stakeholders

The school mental health system is shaped by the interactions of students, families, educators, mental health professionals, policymakers, advocacy organizations, and the private sector, each with distinct interests, resources, and levels of power.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| K-12 public school students | Direct recipients of (or denied) mental health services; academic, behavioral, and emotional outcomes depend on service availability | 49.6 million (NCES, 2024) | Access to timely, confidential, culturally responsive mental health support |
| Students with disabilities (IEPs) | Entitled to mental health "related services" under IDEA; face eligibility determination, service adequacy, and transition challenges | 7.5 million (~15% of students) (NCES, 2024) | Adequate and consistent delivery of IEP-mandated mental health services |
| LGBTQ+ students | Disproportionately affected by mental health challenges; 65% report persistent sadness/hopelessness (CDC YRBS, 2024); face unique barriers to accessing supportive services | ~3.5 million (est. 7% of students, Williams Institute, 2023) | Safe, affirming school environments; confidential access to mental health services without mandatory parental disclosure |
| Students in poverty (Title I eligible) | Higher rates of adverse childhood experiences (ACEs), trauma exposure, and unmet mental health needs; least access to community alternatives | ~26 million (~52% of students qualify for free/reduced lunch, NCES, 2024) | Free, accessible mental health services at school; wraparound support services |
| Parents and families | Responsible for children's health decisions; affected by parental consent/notification policies; diverse values regarding mental health intervention | ~37 million households with school-age children (Census, 2023) | Informed about and involved in their child's mental health care; alignment of school programming with family values |

### Secondary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|---------------|
| Classroom teachers (~3.7 million) | Bear the daily burden of student mental health crises in instructional settings; 77% report spending time weekly addressing student mental health issues (RAND, 2023); directly impacted by student behavioral disruption and their own vicarious trauma and burnout | Adequate mental health staffing to reduce teacher burden; training in recognizing mental health needs; protection from being expected to serve as untrained counselors |
| School administrators (~130,000 principals) | Responsible for allocating building-level resources, managing staff, navigating community politics around mental health programming; liable for safety failures; face competing mandates from state and federal authorities | Clear guidance on role boundaries; sufficient resources to meet mental health mandates; legal protection; flexibility to adapt programs to community context |
| School resource officers (SROs) (~20,000 nationally) | Deployed in ~45% of schools; interact directly with students in behavioral crisis; enforcement role can conflict with therapeutic approach | Training in mental health crisis response; clear protocols distinguishing law enforcement and mental health functions; alternatives to arrest for behavioral incidents |
| Higher education institutions (~600 school psychology programs, ~800 school counseling programs) | Train the school mental health workforce; their capacity determines the pipeline of new professionals; accreditation processes set quality standards | Adequate funding for training program expansion; clinical placement availability; ability to recruit diverse student cohorts |
| Community mental health providers | Receive referrals from schools; partner through SBHCs and community schools; provide Tier 3 services beyond school capacity | Clear referral pathways; FERPA/HIPAA coordination frameworks; sustainable reimbursement for school-linked services |
| Juvenile justice system | Receives referrals from schools for behavioral issues; overlaps with school mental health through school-to-prison pipeline dynamics; court-ordered mental health services affect school re-entry | Diversion programs that keep students in school; adequate school-based services that reduce justice system referrals; coordinated re-entry planning |
| Health insurers and managed care organizations | Bear costs when school-based services are unavailable and students present in emergency departments or community clinics; Medicaid plans are primary payers for school-based services for eligible students | Cost-effective school-based service delivery; reduced ED utilization for non-emergency mental health needs; coordination with school-based providers |
| Pediatricians and primary care providers | Often the first point of contact for parents concerned about children's mental health; prescribe psychotropic medications; provide referrals to school and community services | Efficient referral pathways to school-based services; communication with school mental health staff; shared care planning for students on medication |
| Employers (long-term) | Workforce readiness depends on mental health outcomes; untreated youth mental health conditions reduce future economic productivity; estimated economic burden of $247 billion annually from youth mental health conditions (Dieleman et al., 2020) | Workforce pipeline that produces psychologically healthy, resilient young adults ready for employment |

### Tertiary Stakeholders

- **Technology companies**: Develop AI monitoring tools (Bark, Gaggle, GoGuardian), SEL curriculum platforms, telemental health platforms, and data management systems used in school mental health; significant financial interest in school contracts
- **Pharmaceutical companies**: Manufacture psychotropic medications prescribed to the 8.9% of children on such medications; influence clinical guidelines and provider prescribing patterns
- **Media organizations**: Shape public perception of school mental health issues, youth mental health crises, and school safety; coverage patterns influence political will
- **Research institutions and foundations**: Fund school mental health research, pilot programs, and evaluations; influence evidence base and policy recommendations
- **Faith-based organizations**: Provide alternative counseling services in some communities; influence parental perspectives on school mental health programming; can be allies or opponents depending on program content

## Who Has Power

### Decision Makers

| Actor | Role | Authority | Resources | Influence Level |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| U.S. Congress | Authorizes and funds federal school mental health programs (IDEA, ESSA, BSCA) | Legislative and appropriations power | Federal budget | High |
| U.S. Department of Education | Administers BSCA grants, ESSA Title IV-A, IDEA Part B; issues guidance on FERPA, mental health services | Regulatory and grant-making authority | ~$16 billion in relevant programs (FY2024) | High |
| State legislatures | Set school staffing mandates, SEL curriculum requirements, parental consent laws, mental health education requirements | Legislative authority over K-12 education | State education budgets (varies widely) | High |
| State education agencies (SEAs) | Implement federal programs, distribute state funds, set certification standards for school mental health professionals | Regulatory and administrative authority | Varies by state | Medium-High |
| Local school boards | Adopt mental health policies, approve budgets, hire superintendents, respond to community concerns about programming | Local governance authority over public schools | Local property tax revenue and per-pupil allocations | Medium-High |
| Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) | Sets rules for school-based Medicaid billing; 2023 rule expanded reimbursement eligibility | Federal regulatory authority over Medicaid | ~$5.6 billion in school Medicaid (FY2023 est.) | Medium |
| SAMHSA | Administers Project AWARE, mental health block grants, and technical assistance programs | Grant-making and program development | $131 million (Project AWARE, FY2024) | Medium |

### Key Organizations and Institutions

- **American School Counselor Association (ASCA)**: Sets the 250:1 recommended student-to-counselor ratio and the ASCA National Model for school counseling programs; primary professional association for ~128,800 school counselors; lobbies Congress for school counseling funding; annual federal lobbying expenditures of approximately $800,000 (OpenSecrets, 2024); publishes annual ratio data that drives state-level advocacy campaigns
- **National Association of School Psychologists (NASP)**: Sets the 500:1 recommended student-to-psychologist ratio; produces the most comprehensive workforce studies in the field; advocates for graduate program expansion and school mental health legislation; represents ~44,000 school psychologists; publishes the *Communique* and maintains the most detailed dataset on school psychology workforce demographics and training pipeline
- **School Social Work Association of America (SSWAA)**: Advocates for school social workers; sets 250:1 recommended ratio; provides training and professional development for ~30,100 school social workers; smaller than ASCA or NASP but plays a critical role in advocating for the "whole child" approach that integrates family and community context
- **National Association of School Nurses (NASN)**: Advocates for school nurse staffing (recommending 750:1 for healthy student populations); increasingly involved in mental health screening, medication management, and behavioral health crisis response; represents ~73,000 school nurses who are often the first point of contact for students experiencing somatic symptoms of mental health distress
- **Collaborative for Academic, Social, and Emotional Learning (CASEL)**: The leading research and policy organization promoting SEL; develops frameworks, evaluates curricula, and provides technical assistance; target of anti-SEL opposition
- **School-Based Health Alliance**: National organization supporting school-based health centers; advocates for SBHC funding and Medicaid billing expansion; represents ~3,000 SBHCs
- **National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI)**: Largest grassroots mental health advocacy organization; supports school mental health legislation; provides family education programs
- **Mental Health America (MHA)**: Advocacy organization supporting school mental health screening, prevention, and early intervention; publishes annual state mental health rankings
- **National Center for School Mental Health (NCSMH)**: University of Maryland-based research and technical assistance center; operates the national School Mental Health Census

### Influencers

- **Parents' rights organizations (Parents Defending Education, Moms for Liberty)**: Influence school board elections and state legislation on SEL, mental health screening, and parental consent; significant media presence since 2021; oppose perceived government overreach into family mental health decisions. Parents Defending Education ($4.5 million annual budget) uses FOIA requests and targeted media campaigns. Moms for Liberty (285+ chapters in 45 states, $2.2 million budget) recruits and endorses school board candidates. These organizations have been effective at shifting the political landscape in conservative-leaning districts and states
- **Everytown for Gun Safety / Sandy Hook Promise**: Advocate for school safety measures including threat assessment programs and school mental health funding; influence BSCA implementation and state legislation. Sandy Hook Promise's "Know the Signs" program has trained over 18 million people in recognizing warning signs of violence and mental health crises. Everytown maintains a database tracking school shooting incidents and state school safety legislation
- **American Federation of Teachers (AFT) and National Education Association (NEA)**: Representing ~4.6 million educators, the teachers' unions advocate for school mental health staffing to reduce teacher burden; AFT passed resolutions supporting school mental health investment; NEA advocates for counselor ratio mandates. Combined federal lobbying expenditures exceed $40 million annually across all education issues, with school mental health as a growing priority. Union advocacy is particularly effective at the state level, where collective bargaining agreements can include provisions for mental health staffing ratios
- **Heritage Foundation and other conservative think tanks**: Influence Republican legislative positions on school mental health; have published critiques of SEL curricula and federal school mental health mandates. Heritage's Project 2025 included proposals to redirect federal school mental health funding toward parental choice and private providers. The American Enterprise Institute (AEI) has taken a more moderate position, publishing analysis on finding "common ground" on school mental health
- **Children's Hospital Association**: Represents 220+ children's hospitals that see the downstream effects of inadequate school mental health in their emergency departments and inpatient units; co-declared the national emergency in children's mental health (2021); powerful advocacy voice for prevention-based approaches that reduce acute care demand
- **American Psychological Association (APA)**: Influences research standards, training requirements, and public discourse on youth mental health; issued influential reports on social media and youth mental health
- **American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP)**: Co-declared the national emergency in children's mental health (2021); influences pediatric mental health screening guidelines and referral patterns; advocates for school-based mental health as part of child health infrastructure
- **Children's Defense Fund**: Advocates for comprehensive children's services including school mental health; publishes annual "State of America's Children" report
- **National PTA**: Largest parent organization with 3.5 million members; has supported school mental health investment and SEL implementation while emphasizing parental engagement

## Stakeholder Interests and Alignment

### Aligned Interests (Potential Coalition Partners)

| Coalition | Shared Interest | Strength |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| Professional associations (ASCA, NASP, SSWAA, NASN) + teachers' unions (AFT, NEA) | Increased school mental health staffing and protected role definitions | Strong -- consistent advocacy and aligned membership interests |
| Mental health advocacy orgs (NAMI, MHA) + school safety orgs (Sandy Hook Promise, Everytown) | Expanded school mental health services and funding | Strong -- overlapping policy agendas post-Uvalde |
| Bipartisan legislators (those who supported BSCA) + school districts + professional associations | Sustainable federal funding for school mental health | Moderate -- agreement on need but divergence on scope and approach |
| Parents + school boards + educators | Student safety and wellbeing | Moderate -- broad agreement on goals but disagreement on methods (SEL, screening, consent) |
| CMS + school districts + school-based health centers | Expanded Medicaid school-based billing | Strong -- shared financial interest in maximizing reimbursement |

### Conflicting Interests

- **Parents' rights advocates vs. school mental health professionals**: Parents' rights groups demand opt-in consent for all mental health services and full parental notification; clinicians argue that mandatory parental notification can endanger students at risk of abuse or in crisis
- **Academic accountability advocates vs. school counselors**: Administrators focused on test scores prefer to use counselors for test coordination and scheduling; counselors and professional associations insist on protected time for counseling services
- **Privacy advocates vs. threat assessment teams**: FERPA protections and student privacy principles conflict with the information-sharing needs of behavioral threat assessment teams charged with preventing school violence
- **SEL supporters vs. SEL opponents**: CASEL and evidence-based SEL proponents argue for universal implementation; conservative parents' organizations view SEL as ideological programming and demand parental opt-out or curriculum restrictions
- **Federal mandates vs. local control advocates**: Federal funding comes with compliance requirements that some state officials and school boards view as overreach into local education governance
- **Technology companies vs. privacy advocates**: AI monitoring companies promote their tools as essential safety measures, while privacy advocates warn of surveillance overreach, discriminatory impacts, and inadequate data governance
- **School districts vs. community mental health providers**: Schools and community providers sometimes compete for the same limited pool of mental health professionals and Medicaid reimbursements, rather than collaborating on integrated service delivery
- **Medicaid-eligible vs. non-eligible students**: The 2023 CMS rule expanded Medicaid billing to all eligible students, but students who are not Medicaid-eligible (approximately 40% of the student population) receive no reimbursement-backed services, creating a potential two-tier system
- **Urban vs. rural stakeholders**: Urban stakeholders tend to prioritize staffing ratios and programmatic expansion, while rural stakeholders prioritize telehealth infrastructure, recruitment incentives, and geographic access -- sometimes leading to competing policy priorities

### The School Mental Health Iron Triangle

The school mental health policy space features an informal iron triangle connecting:

1. **Congressional education and health committees**: Authorize and fund school mental health programs
2. **Department of Education and SAMHSA**: Administer grants and issue guidance
3. **Professional associations (ASCA, NASP, SSWAA) and advocacy organizations (NAMI, School-Based Health Alliance)**: Lobby for expanded funding and provide data to support legislative action

This triangle has been effective at securing incremental funding increases (e.g., BSCA, ESSA Title IV-A) but has been unable to achieve transformative funding levels or overcome the fragmented multi-stream structure.

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on policy:

- **Lobbying**: ASCA, NASP, and NAMI collectively spend approximately $3.2 million annually on federal lobbying for school mental health legislation (OpenSecrets, 2024). Teachers' unions (AFT, NEA) spend significantly more ($40+ million combined across all education issues) but include school mental health in their broader education agenda
- **Campaign Contributions**: Education-related PACs contribute modestly to congressional campaigns; school mental health is rarely a top-tier campaign issue but has gained salience since Uvalde
- **Public Advocacy**: Sandy Hook Promise and Everytown for Gun Safety run high-profile media campaigns linking school safety to mental health; anti-SEL organizations use social media and parent networks to mobilize opposition at school board meetings
- **Expertise and Data**: NASP and NCSMH produce workforce studies and program evaluations that directly shape legislative findings sections; CASEL's SEL evidence base informs state curriculum standards; the CDC's YRBS provides the most-cited data on youth mental health prevalence
- **School Board Elections**: Since 2021, school mental health programming (particularly SEL) has become a factor in school board races, with organized campaigns to elect candidates who oppose or support specific mental health programming approaches
- **Litigation**: Disability rights organizations and individual families use IDEA due process hearings and federal court litigation to enforce mental health service obligations, creating case law that shapes school district practices. The *Endrew F. v. Douglas County School District* (2017) Supreme Court decision raising the FAPE standard has increased litigation pressure on schools to provide more substantive mental health services under IDEA
- **State-Level Ballot Initiatives**: In some states, ballot initiatives have been used to increase education funding that includes mental health staffing. California's Proposition 63 (Mental Health Services Act, 2004) created a 1% millionaire tax for mental health services, portions of which fund school-based programs
- **Media and Narrative Power**: Student-led movements (March for Our Lives, 2018) and parent advocacy have demonstrated the power of personal narrative in shaping school mental health policy. Post-Parkland student activism shifted the political calculation on school safety legislation, contributing directly to the BSCA's passage four years later
- **Professional Standards and Accreditation**: Professional associations exert influence through accreditation standards for training programs (CACREP for counseling, NASP for school psychology, CSWE for social work). These standards shape workforce competencies, ethical guidelines, and practice expectations. Changes to accreditation standards (e.g., requiring cultural competency training) ripple through the entire profession within a few years

## Student Voice and Youth Advocacy

Students themselves have become increasingly powerful stakeholders in school mental health policy, particularly since the Parkland shooting:

- **March for Our Lives / Students Demand Action**: The post-Parkland student movement expanded from gun control to include mental health advocacy, with student-led campaigns in multiple states advocating for increased school counselor staffing and mental health services
- **Student advisory councils**: Several states (California, Colorado, Massachusetts) have established student advisory councils that provide input on school mental health policy to state education agencies
- **Youth-led mental health organizations**: Organizations like Bring Change to Mind, the JED Foundation's student programs, and NAMI on Campus empower students to advocate for school mental health resources and reduce stigma
- **Social media advocacy**: Students use social media platforms to share mental health experiences, demand better school services, and organize advocacy campaigns, creating public pressure on school boards and legislators

The growing power of student voices has created a new dynamic in school mental health politics: elected officials who might dismiss advocacy from professional associations or advocacy organizations face greater political risk when ignoring students speaking from personal experience about mental health needs.

## State-Level Stakeholder Dynamics

School mental health stakeholder dynamics vary significantly by state political context:

### Progressive States (e.g., California, New York, Colorado)

In progressive-leaning states, the primary dynamic is between advocacy coalitions pushing for expanded services and fiscal constraints limiting implementation. Professional associations, teachers' unions, and mental health advocacy organizations form strong coalitions that have secured dedicated state funding. Opposition tends to focus on cost and implementation feasibility rather than ideological objections to school mental health programming. Parental rights concerns exist but are subordinated to broader support for expanded services.

### Conservative States (e.g., Texas, Florida, Georgia)

In conservative-leaning states, the stakeholder dynamic is more complex. Post-Parkland and post-Uvalde, school safety concerns have created openings for mental health investment when framed as violence prevention (Florida's $140 million post-Parkland investment, Texas's $100 million program). However, SEL opposition is stronger, parental consent requirements are more restrictive, and concerns about government overreach into family mental health decisions carry more political weight. Faith-based organizations are more influential in shaping the acceptable scope of school mental health programming.

### Rural and Frontier States (e.g., Montana, Wyoming, Alaska)

In rural states, the primary stakeholder challenge is capacity rather than ideology. Geographic isolation, small school districts, and limited community mental health infrastructure make traditional staffing models impractical. Telemental health has emerged as the primary solution, with rural schools adopting telehealth at higher rates (46%) than urban (38%) or suburban (29%) schools. Stakeholder coalitions in these states tend to focus on workforce recruitment incentives and technology infrastructure rather than programmatic debates.

## The School Board Dynamic

School boards have become the most contested arena for school mental health stakeholder conflicts, particularly since 2021:

- **Board Composition Shifts**: Organized campaigns by parents' rights groups have successfully elected school board members opposed to SEL and expanded mental health programming in multiple districts. Conversely, mental health advocacy organizations have supported candidates committed to expanding services. The result is increasing polarization at the board level, mirroring national political dynamics
- **Budget Authority**: School boards hold direct authority over mental health staffing budgets, making them a critical leverage point. When boards cut counselor or psychologist positions during budget reductions, they face advocacy from professional associations, parents, and students -- but also pressure from fiscal conservatives to reduce spending
- **Policy Adoption**: Boards adopt or reject SEL curricula, mental health screening protocols, parental consent policies, and AI monitoring tool contracts. Each decision can become a flashpoint for community debate. In high-profile cases (Loudoun County, VA; Moms for Liberty-contested boards in Florida and North Carolina), school mental health policy has become a proxy for broader political identity conflicts

## Federal Agency Stakeholder Roles

### Department of Education

The Department of Education serves multiple roles: grant-maker (BSCA, Title IV-A, School-Based Mental Health Services grants), regulator (FERPA, IDEA compliance), and technical assistance provider. The Department's Office of Safe and Supportive Schools coordinates school mental health grant programs. Under different administrations, the Department has emphasized different priorities -- the Biden administration prioritized BSCA implementation and school mental health hiring, while previous administrations focused more narrowly on school safety and threat assessment.

### Department of Health and Human Services

HHS operates through multiple sub-agencies:

- **SAMHSA**: Administers Project AWARE grants, mental health block grants, and the National Child Traumatic Stress Network (NCTSN). SAMHSA provides technical assistance on evidence-based school mental health practices
- **CMS**: Sets Medicaid reimbursement rules for school-based services. The 2023 rule expanding school-based billing authority was the most consequential HHS action for school mental health in recent history
- **HRSA**: Manages Health Professional Shortage Area designations that affect school access to mental health providers, and operates the National Health Service Corps that could serve as a model for school mental health workforce programs
- **CDC**: Conducts the Youth Risk Behavior Survey (YRBS), the most-cited data source on adolescent mental health, and operates the WISQARS injury data system. CDC's data products are foundational to school mental health advocacy and policy development

### Department of Justice

DOJ plays a role through its community-oriented policing services (COPS) program, which funds school resource officers, and through its involvement in threat assessment training under BSCA. The FBI's behavioral analysis unit provides threat assessment consultation. DOJ's School Safety Clearinghouse (SchoolSafety.gov) provides resources on comprehensive school safety including mental health components.

## Technology Industry Stakeholders

The technology sector has become an increasingly important stakeholder in school mental health, with both constructive and concerning dimensions:

### AI Monitoring Companies

- **Bark Technologies**: Monitors student activity across 30+ platforms; used in ~5,000 school districts; reports flagging over 6.5 million instances of potential self-harm or violence (2023). Revenue model depends on continued school adoption
- **Gaggle**: Monitors student emails and documents in ~1,500 districts; employs human safety specialists to review AI-flagged content. Claims to have helped save 1,562 student lives in 2023. Faced criticism for higher false-positive rates among minority students
- **GoGuardian**: Provides both web filtering and student safety monitoring; used in ~14,000 schools. Offers both administrative monitoring and voluntary student wellness check-in tools

### Telemental Health Platforms

- **Hazel Health**: Partners with school districts to provide telemental health services; serves ~2 million students in 800+ school districts across 16 states. Revenue model based on Medicaid reimbursement and district contracts
- **Talkiatry and others**: Provide direct telepsychiatry services to students in school settings, addressing the psychiatric prescriber shortage that limits medication management for students with serious mental health conditions

### SEL Curriculum Technology

- **Panorama Education**: Provides SEL assessment and survey tools used in ~1,500 districts; generates data on student social-emotional competencies. Faced controversy when parent groups obtained survey questions through FOIA requests and characterized them as invasive
- **Committee for Children**: Develops the Second Step SEL curriculum, the most widely used elementary SEL program (~30,000 schools). Provides digital curriculum platforms, teacher training, and family engagement tools

### Stakeholder Concerns About Technology

The technology industry's growing role in school mental health raises several stakeholder concerns:

- **Privacy**: AI monitoring of student communications creates detailed behavioral profiles that can follow students through their educational careers. The Center for Democracy and Technology (CDT, 2024) found that few districts have adequate data governance policies for AI monitoring tools
- **Equity**: Studies show higher false-positive rates for Black and LGBTQ+ students in AI monitoring systems, raising concerns about discriminatory surveillance (Cardona-Rivera et al., 2024)
- **Conflicts of interest**: Technology companies that profit from monitoring have financial incentives to emphasize risk and recommend continued or expanded monitoring, potentially inflating the perceived scope of threats
- **Efficacy evidence**: The evidence base for AI monitoring tools' effectiveness in preventing student harm is limited and largely derived from company-reported data rather than independent evaluation
