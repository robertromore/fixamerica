# Mental Health Crisis Services: History

## Historical Development

The history of mental health crisis services in America is inseparable from the broader arc of deinstitutionalization, the community mental health movement, and the gradual recognition that people in psychiatric distress need something other than a jail cell or an emergency room. The crisis system as it exists today -- fragmented, underfunded, and incomplete -- is the product of decades of policy decisions, many well-intentioned but poorly executed, that dismantled one system of care without fully building its replacement.

---

### The Institutional Era (Pre-1955): Asylum and Confinement

- **State hospital system**: By 1955, state psychiatric hospitals housed approximately 559,000 patients -- the peak of institutional census. These facilities ranged from humane to horrifying, but they constituted the de facto crisis response system: families and police brought people in acute distress to the state hospital
- **No community crisis services**: Outside of hospitals, virtually no organized mental health crisis response existed. Police served as informal gatekeepers, transporting people to hospitals or jails
- **Conditions and reform pressure**: Investigative journalism (Albert Deutsch's *The Shame of the States*, 1948) and conscientious objectors who served as attendants during World War II exposed deplorable conditions, building political will for reform. Life magazine's 1946 photo exposé of Byberry Hospital in Philadelphia showed patients lying naked on concrete floors, generating public outrage
- **Early pharmaceutical breakthroughs**: The introduction of chlorpromazine (Thorazine) in 1954 provided the first effective antipsychotic medication, making it conceivable that seriously mentally ill individuals could be treated outside institutions
- **The Joint Commission on Mental Illness and Health (1955-1961)**: Congress established this commission to study the nation's mental health needs. Its 1961 report, *Action for Mental Health*, recommended replacing large state hospitals with community-based treatment, providing the intellectual foundation for the Community Mental Health Act
- **Psychiatric emergency rooms emerge**: During the 1940s-1950s, a small number of urban hospitals (Bellevue in New York, San Francisco General, Massachusetts General) established dedicated psychiatric emergency services. These early PES units represented the first specialized crisis intervention settings, but they remained rare exceptions to the general pattern of police-to-state-hospital transport

---

### Deinstitutionalization and Community Mental Health (1955-1980): The Promise and the Gap

- **Community Mental Health Act (1963)**: President Kennedy signed the Community Mental Health Centers Construction Act, envisioning a network of 2,000 community mental health centers (CMHCs) that would replace state hospitals. The act authorized federal funding for construction and initial staffing
- **Rapid hospital closures**: State hospital populations plummeted from 559,000 (1955) to 215,000 (1974) to 72,000 (1994). Discharges outpaced community service development by a wide margin
- **Centers never built**: Fewer than 800 of the planned 2,000 CMHCs were ever constructed. Federal funding was cut during the Nixon administration, and many centers that were built focused on outpatient therapy rather than crisis services
- **Medicaid and the IMD exclusion**: The 1965 creation of Medicaid included the Institutions for Mental Diseases (IMD) exclusion, barring federal Medicaid payment for most care in psychiatric facilities with more than 16 beds. This created a perverse incentive for states to close large hospitals without investing in alternatives, as they could shift costs to the federal government through smaller settings
- **Crisis intervention theory develops**: Gerald Caplan's 1964 *Principles of Preventive Psychiatry* and Donna Aguilera and Janice Messick's 1970 *Crisis Intervention: Theory and Methodology* established the theoretical framework for modern crisis services -- the idea that crisis is time-limited, that early intervention prevents deterioration, and that crisis represents both danger and opportunity for change. These concepts remain the intellectual basis of the SAMHSA crisis continuum
- **Community mental health center crisis services**: Some of the CMHCs that were built included 24-hour crisis units, creating the first community-based (non-hospital) crisis intervention settings. However, most CMHCs focused on outpatient therapy and medication management rather than acute crisis response, and their crisis services were often limited to daytime hours
- **Homelessness and criminalization**: By the late 1970s, the consequences of deinstitutionalization without adequate community services were visible in rising homelessness and the criminalization of mental illness. The number of homeless individuals with serious mental illness grew from an estimated 25,000 in 1960 to over 150,000 by 1980 (Torrey, *Out of the Shadows*, 1997)

---

### The Reagan Era and Federal Retreat (1980-1990): Block Grants and Neglect

- **Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act (1981)**: The Reagan administration replaced categorical federal mental health funding with the Alcohol, Drug Abuse, and Mental Health (ADAMH) Block Grant, reducing federal funding by 25% and eliminating the requirement to build community mental health centers
- **State responsibility without resources**: States assumed primary responsibility for mental health services but lacked adequate funding, leading to further erosion of community crisis capacity
- **Continued hospital closures**: Between 1980 and 1990, an additional 40,000 state hospital beds were eliminated without commensurate community investment
- **EMTALA (1986)**: Congress passed the Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act, requiring hospitals to screen and stabilize all emergency patients regardless of ability to pay. While not specifically about mental health, EMTALA established the legal obligation for EDs to address psychiatric emergencies -- inadvertently reinforcing the emergency department as the default crisis intervention setting
- **Rise of jails as psychiatric facilities**: Los Angeles County Jail, Cook County Jail (Chicago), and Rikers Island (New York) became the three largest psychiatric facilities in the country -- a status they retain today. By 1990, more people with serious mental illness were in jails and prisons than in all state psychiatric hospitals combined (Treatment Advocacy Center, 2010)
- **First crisis hotlines**: The first suicide prevention telephone hotlines emerged in the 1960s and 1970s, primarily staffed by volunteers. The Los Angeles Suicide Prevention Center (founded 1958 by Norman Farberow and Edwin Shneidman) and the Samaritans (founded in the UK in 1953, with U.S. branches beginning in the 1970s) established the model of anonymous, telephone-based crisis counseling that would eventually evolve into the National Suicide Prevention Lifeline and then 988
- **The Protection and Advocacy Act (1986)**: Congress established Protection and Advocacy organizations in every state to investigate abuse and neglect of people with mental illness. These organizations would later become important advocates for crisis service alternatives to institutionalization and incarceration
- **Stewart B. McKinney Homeless Assistance Act (1987)**: The first major federal response to homelessness, including funding for mental health services for homeless individuals. The act recognized the link between deinstitutionalization and homelessness, authorizing the Projects for Assistance in Transition from Homelessness (PATH) program that continues to fund street outreach and crisis services for homeless individuals with mental illness
- **The crack epidemic and dual diagnosis (1985-1995)**: The crack cocaine epidemic of the late 1980s and early 1990s dramatically increased the number of people presenting in crisis with co-occurring mental health and substance use disorders. Emergency departments and police were overwhelmed by a population that existing psychiatric and substance abuse systems were not designed to serve simultaneously, highlighting the need for integrated crisis response

---

### Early Crisis Models and the Memphis Model (1988-2000): Seeds of Innovation

- **Memphis CIT program (1988)**: Following the police shooting of Joseph Dewayne Robinson, a man with mental illness wielding a knife, the Memphis Police Department partnered with the University of Memphis and the National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI) to develop the first Crisis Intervention Team (CIT) training program. The 40-hour training model, emphasizing de-escalation and diversion, became the national standard
- **Mobile crisis team pilots**: Several states (New York, Georgia, Arizona) began experimenting with mobile crisis teams in the 1990s, dispatching mental health professionals to crisis scenes rather than relying solely on police transport. New York City's mobile crisis teams, operated by community mental health agencies under contract with the city's Health and Hospitals Corporation, became one of the first urban mobile crisis models. Arizona's Maricopa County developed a crisis system that would later become the foundation for the national Crisis Now model
- **The rise of crisis stabilization units (1990s)**: States began building purpose-designed crisis stabilization units as alternatives to hospital emergency departments. Georgia established the first statewide network of CSUs, demonstrating that short-term (1-3 day) stays in therapeutic environments could divert 60-70% of individuals from more costly inpatient hospitalizations. The success of Georgia's CSUs became the evidence base for SAMHSA's recommendation of crisis stabilization as the "Safe Place to Be" component of the crisis continuum
- **Suicide prevention as public health priority (1990s-2000s)**: The U.S. Surgeon General's 1999 *Call to Action to Prevent Suicide* elevated suicide prevention from a clinical concern to a public health priority. The National Strategy for Suicide Prevention (2001, revised 2012) included crisis services as a core component, framing suicide as preventable through system-level interventions including crisis hotlines, means restriction, and follow-up care
- **National Suicide Prevention Lifeline (1998-2005)**: The Garrett Lee Smith Memorial Act (2004) and related federal investments established the National Suicide Prevention Lifeline (1-800-273-8255), creating the first national crisis hotline infrastructure, administered by the non-profit Link2Health (later Vibrant Emotional Health). Senator Gordon Smith (R-OR) introduced the legislation after his son Garrett died by suicide in 2003, demonstrating how personal tragedy among policymakers drives reform
- **Kendra's Law (New York, 1999)**: New York enacted Assisted Outpatient Treatment (AOT) legislation after Kendra Webdale was pushed in front of a subway by a man with untreated schizophrenia. The law authorized court-ordered outpatient treatment for individuals with a history of treatment non-compliance who posed a danger. AOT laws, adopted in various forms by most states, became intertwined with crisis services because crisis encounters often trigger AOT petitions. The AOT debate highlights the persistent tension between treatment access and civil liberties in crisis policy
- **Georgia crisis system development**: Georgia began building what would become the most comprehensive state crisis system in the country, establishing crisis stabilization units and a statewide mobile crisis network in the 1990s. Georgia's investment was driven in part by the 1999 *Olmstead v. L.C.* Supreme Court decision, which required states to provide community-based mental health services when appropriate
- **CAHOOTS founding (1989)**: The Crisis Assistance Helping Out On The Streets program was founded in Eugene, Oregon, by the White Bird Clinic. Initially operating with a single van, one medic, and one crisis worker, CAHOOTS responded to calls that police and EMS were ill-equipped to handle -- intoxication, mental health distress, homelessness. The program would operate for three decades in relative obscurity before gaining national attention following the police reform movement of 2020
- **The Olmstead decision (1999)**: The Supreme Court ruled in *Olmstead v. L.C.* (527 U.S. 581) that the Americans with Disabilities Act requires states to provide community-based services to people with mental disabilities when appropriate, rather than confining them to institutions. This landmark ruling provided a legal framework for expanding community crisis services and reducing reliance on hospitalization and incarceration as default crisis responses

### The 2000s: Parity, Integration, and Incremental Progress (2000-2015)

- **Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act (2008)**: Required health insurers to cover mental health and substance use disorders at parity with medical/surgical benefits. While not directly a crisis services law, parity expanded the theoretical coverage base for crisis interventions
- **Affordable Care Act (2010)**: Expanded Medicaid eligibility, bringing millions of previously uninsured adults with mental illness into coverage. Several states used Medicaid expansion to fund crisis services. ACA marketplace plans were required to include mental health as an Essential Health Benefit, theoretically expanding crisis coverage for the commercially insured population
- **Jail diversion programs**: The Sequential Intercept Model, developed by Mark Munetz and Patricia Griffin (2006), provided a framework for identifying diversion opportunities at five points in the criminal justice process, including pre-arrest crisis response. The model became the standard conceptual framework for jail diversion, adopted by SAMHSA's GAINS Center and implemented in hundreds of communities
- **Stepping Up Initiative (2015)**: The National Association of Counties, the Council of State Governments Justice Center, and the American Psychiatric Association launched the Stepping Up Initiative, committing over 500 counties to reduce the number of people with mental illness in jails. The initiative highlighted crisis intervention as the primary diversion point, driving county investment in mobile crisis teams and co-responder programs
- **CAHOOTS model emergence**: Eugene, Oregon's Crisis Assistance Helping Out On The Streets (CAHOOTS) program, operating since 1989, gained national recognition as proof that unarmed civilian crisis response could work safely at scale, handling over 20,000 calls annually by 2015
- **Veterans Crisis Line**: The VA launched the Veterans Crisis Line in 2007, providing specialized crisis support for military service members, veterans, and their families. The line was established in response to a CBS News investigation revealing that 6,256 veterans died by suicide in 2005 alone
- **Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinics (CCBHCs)**: The Excellence in Mental Health Act (2014) created the CCBHC model, requiring participating clinics to provide 24-hour crisis services, among other comprehensive behavioral health services. By 2023, more than 500 CCBHCs were operational in 46 states, representing the largest federal investment in community behavioral health infrastructure since the original Community Mental Health Act (SAMHSA, 2023)
- **Sandy Hook and school crisis response (2012)**: The Sandy Hook Elementary School shooting in Newtown, Connecticut, intensified focus on youth mental health crisis response. Subsequent federal investments, including Project AWARE (Advancing Wellness and Resilience in Education), funded school-based mental health teams in over 2,000 schools, though these primarily focused on prevention rather than acute crisis intervention
- **Crisis Now model development (2014-2018)**: Dr. Margie Balfour and colleagues at RI International formalized the Crisis Now model based on Arizona's crisis system experience. The model codified the three-pillar framework (call center, mobile teams, stabilization facilities) with specific operational standards, performance metrics, and a "no wrong door" philosophy. SAMHSA adopted the Crisis Now framework as the basis for its 2020 National Guidelines

### The 988 Era (2016-Present): A New Vision

- **FCC investigation (2016-2019)**: The Federal Communications Commission studied the feasibility of a three-digit number for mental health crises. In 2016, Congress directed the FCC and SAMHSA to conduct a joint study. The resulting 2019 FCC report recommended 988, finding that a dedicated three-digit number would increase access and reduce barriers to help-seeking. The FCC analyzed call routing logistics, telecom industry costs, and the experience of other three-digit codes (211, 311, 411, 511, 611, 711, 811) in building public awareness
- **The Elijah McClain case (2019)**: The death of Elijah McClain in Aurora, Colorado, during a police encounter that began as a "suspicious person" report became a national catalyst for alternative crisis response after gaining attention in 2020. McClain, a 23-year-old massage therapist, was walking home wearing a ski mask when officers restrained him with a carotid hold and paramedics administered ketamine. His death highlighted the lethal consequences of armed responses to situations that do not involve criminal behavior and fueled the movement toward civilian crisis alternatives
- **National Suicide Hotline Designation Act (2020)**: Signed into law by President Trump in October 2020, this bipartisan legislation designated 988 as the universal number for suicide prevention and mental health crisis, requiring all phone carriers to route 988 calls to the existing Lifeline network by July 16, 2022
- **SAMHSA Crisis Care Guidelines (2020)**: SAMHSA published *National Guidelines for Behavioral Health Crisis Care*, articulating the three-pillar crisis continuum: Someone to Call, Someone to Respond, A Safe Place to Be
- **American Rescue Plan Act (2021)**: Section 9813 provided an enhanced 85% federal Medicaid matching rate for qualifying community-based mobile crisis intervention services, the single largest financial incentive for mobile crisis expansion in U.S. history
- **988 launch (July 16, 2022)**: The three-digit number went live nationwide. First-month contacts increased 45% over the prior July. The launch was accompanied by a $150 million federal marketing and awareness campaign, the largest public health communications effort for a phone number since the original 911 rollout. In the months following launch, call volume surged as awareness spread, straining local crisis center capacity in many regions
- **Bipartisan Safer Communities Act (2022)**: Passed in response to the Uvalde, Texas school shooting, this legislation included $500 million for pediatric mental health services and additional crisis infrastructure funding. While primarily focused on gun safety, the mental health provisions reflected the growing consensus that crisis services for youth were critically inadequate
- **Surgeon General's youth mental health advisory (2021, 2023)**: U.S. Surgeon General Vivek Murthy issued two landmark advisories -- on youth mental health (2021) and on social media's impact on youth (2023) -- that identified the youth mental health crisis as a national emergency and called for expanded crisis services for children and adolescents
- **Post-launch scaling (2023-2024)**: 988 contacts reached 5.4 million in 2023 (SAMHSA, 2024). Federal funding for 988 and crisis services exceeded $1 billion across FY2023-FY2024. States began implementing the Medicaid mobile crisis benefit. Over 100 communities launched civilian alternative response programs
- **George Floyd and police reform (2020-2024)**: The national reckoning over policing following the murder of George Floyd accelerated interest in alternative crisis response models, with dozens of cities piloting or expanding non-police mental health response teams. The phrase "reimagine public safety" became associated with redirecting some police funding toward mental health crisis response, though political backlash against "defund the police" rhetoric complicated the policy conversation
- **COVID-19 pandemic and mental health crisis (2020-2022)**: The pandemic triggered an unprecedented mental health crisis, with anxiety and depression rates roughly tripling during 2020 (CDC, 2021). Youth mental health deteriorated sharply: pediatric mental health ED visits increased by 24% for children ages 5-11 and 31% for ages 12-17 between 2019 and 2020 (CDC Vital Signs, 2022). The pandemic demonstrated the inadequacy of existing crisis infrastructure while simultaneously accelerating telehealth adoption and remote crisis counseling
- **Biden administration mental health strategy (2022-2024)**: President Biden identified mental health as a key domestic priority, with the 2022 State of the Union address calling for a "transformation" of mental health services. The administration's mental health strategy included 988 investment, crisis services expansion, and mental health parity enforcement. Combined federal investment in 988 and crisis services exceeded $1 billion across FY2023-FY2024

## Lessons from History

### Pattern 1: Deinstitutionalization Without Reinvestment

The most consequential lesson is that closing institutional capacity without simultaneously building community alternatives produces catastrophic outcomes. Between 1955 and 2000, the United States eliminated over 480,000 state hospital beds while building fewer than 800 community mental health centers. The result was predictable: homelessness, incarceration, and emergency departments filled the gap left by hospitals. Every subsequent crisis services reform effort has been an attempt to repair this original failure.

### Pattern 2: Unfunded Mandates and Incomplete Implementation

Repeatedly, federal legislation has created a vision for mental health crisis services without providing the resources to realize it. The Community Mental Health Act promised 2,000 centers; fewer than half were built. EMTALA required hospitals to treat psychiatric emergencies but did not fund the capacity to do so. The 988 designation created a phone number but did not guarantee the mobile teams or crisis beds behind it. Each iteration follows the same pattern: bold policy design, inadequate appropriations, and systems that exist on paper but not in practice.

The ARP Section 9813 enhanced FMAP for mobile crisis is the most recent example of this pattern in potential. The 85% federal match is the strongest financial incentive ever offered for mobile crisis expansion -- but it was authorized for only three years. If Congress does not make it permanent, states that built mobile crisis programs on this funding may face a fiscal cliff that forces program contraction, repeating the cycle of build-up and collapse that has characterized crisis policy for six decades.

### Pattern 3: Innovation from Tragedy

Many of the most important crisis services innovations were born from preventable deaths. The Memphis CIT program was created after a police shooting. The Garrett Lee Smith Act was named for a senator's son who died by suicide. The national conversation about alternative crisis response accelerated after high-profile police killings of people in mental health crisis -- including the deaths of Elijah McClain (Aurora, CO, 2019), Daniel Prude (Rochester, NY, 2020), and Walter Wallace Jr. (Philadelphia, 2020). This pattern suggests that political will for crisis reform is typically reactive rather than proactive, and that sustained advocacy is needed to maintain momentum between crises.

The COVID-19 pandemic broke this pattern partially, creating sustained political attention to mental health not triggered by a single incident but by a population-wide crisis. The pandemic-driven mental health emergency may represent the first time that crisis services investment received broad political support based on need rather than tragedy.

### Pattern 4: States as Laboratories

The most effective crisis systems have been built by individual states -- Georgia, Arizona, Colorado, Oregon -- that invested in comprehensive models ahead of federal action. These states now serve as proof-of-concept models for national scaling. Federal policy has historically followed rather than led state innovation in crisis services.

### Pattern 5: Technology as Catalyst

Each major advance in crisis services has been enabled by communications technology. Telephone hotlines (1960s-1970s) created the first remote crisis intervention capability. The internet enabled crisis chat services (2000s). Mobile phones made 988 feasible. Telehealth expanded crisis consultation to rural areas during the pandemic. Real-time data systems now promise to connect the entire crisis continuum. However, technology adoption has consistently outpaced workforce development, creating systems that can receive contacts faster than they can respond to them.

## International Historical Context

The United States is not alone in grappling with mental health crisis response, but it lags behind several peer nations that built community crisis systems earlier:

- **United Kingdom**: The UK mandated Crisis Resolution and Home Treatment (CRHT) teams in every NHS Trust area beginning in 2000, establishing a national community crisis infrastructure more than two decades before the U.S. launched 988. CRHT teams reduced inpatient admissions by 20-30% (NHS England, 2023)
- **Australia**: Began building regional crisis assessment and treatment teams in the 1990s. Launched Safe Haven Cafes (peer-staffed, walk-in crisis support centers) in 2019, with over 20 sites operational by 2023 (AIHW, 2023)
- **Netherlands**: Introduced the Psycholance (dedicated psychiatric ambulance) in Amsterdam in 2010. The model has since expanded to multiple Dutch cities, achieving 70% diversion from police transport and 85% transport to crisis facilities rather than EDs (Mulder et al., *BMC Psychiatry*, 2023)
- **Israel**: Operates a network of community-based psychiatric first-aid stations and mobile crisis teams integrated with the national Magen David Adom emergency medical service since the 1990s

The common thread across successful international models is that crisis services were built as a deliberate, funded component of the health system rather than left as an afterthought to emergency departments and police departments.
