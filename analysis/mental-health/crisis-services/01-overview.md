# Mental Health Crisis Services: Overview

## Overview

Every year, tens of millions of Americans experience a mental health or substance use crisis -- a moment when symptoms overwhelm a person's ability to cope and immediate intervention is needed to prevent harm, hospitalization, or death. In 2022, the United States recorded 49,449 deaths by suicide (CDC, 2023), and an estimated 12.3 million adults reported serious thoughts of suicide (SAMHSA, 2023). Emergency departments treated approximately 7.8 million visits involving a primary mental health diagnosis in 2021 (HCUP, 2023), while law enforcement responded to an estimated 10 million mental health-related calls annually (Treatment Advocacy Center, 2022). These encounters frequently result in tragic outcomes: people in mental health crisis account for at least 25% of fatal police shootings (Washington Post Fatal Force database, 2023).

The crisis response system that is supposed to serve these individuals is deeply fragmented. The July 2022 launch of the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline -- replacing the old 10-digit National Suicide Prevention Lifeline number -- marked a watershed moment, creating a mental health equivalent of 911. In its first full calendar year (2023), the 988 Lifeline fielded 5.4 million contacts via calls, texts, and chats, a 40% increase from the prior year (SAMHSA, 2024). Yet the infrastructure behind 988 remains incomplete. The crisis continuum model endorsed by SAMHSA calls for three core components: a regional crisis call center (Someone to Call), mobile crisis teams that dispatch to the person in crisis (Someone to Respond), and short-term crisis stabilization facilities (A Safe Place to Be). As of 2024, fewer than half of U.S. communities have access to all three components (NASMHPD, 2024).

The gap between 988 as a phone number and 988 as a functioning crisis system has enormous human and fiscal consequences. Without adequate mobile teams and stabilization capacity, people in crisis continue to cycle through emergency departments -- where the median wait for a psychiatric bed exceeds 6 hours and can stretch past 24 hours (ACEP, 2023) -- or encounter armed police officers as first responders. The result is preventable deaths, unnecessary incarceration, costly ED boarding, and deepening distrust of public systems among people who most need help.

Building a complete crisis continuum is both a moral imperative and a fiscal opportunity. Research consistently shows that investing in crisis services reduces ED utilization, jail bookings, and inpatient admissions while improving clinical outcomes and saving money. Georgia's crisis system, one of the most developed in the nation, saves an estimated $2,264 per crisis episode compared to hospitalization (Georgia DBHDD, 2022). The challenge is scaling proven models to a nation where mental health workforce shortages, inconsistent Medicaid coverage, and fragmented governance have left most communities without the services they need.

## Scope

This analysis covers the key aspects of mental health crisis services in the United States, with international comparisons where relevant:

- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline**: Call center infrastructure, answer rates, workforce, technology, specialized sub-networks (veterans, Spanish-language, LGBTQI+, deaf/hard-of-hearing), and integration with dispatch systems
- **Mobile Crisis Teams**: Deployment models (clinician-led, co-response, peer-led, telehealth-assisted), staffing, response times, clinical protocols, geographic coverage gaps, and the Medicaid mobile crisis benefit under ARP Section 9813
- **Crisis Stabilization and Receiving Centers**: Short-term (under 24-hour) crisis stabilization units, 23-hour observation beds, crisis receiving centers, and the Living Room peer-staffed model as alternatives to emergency departments and inpatient hospitalization
- **Law Enforcement Interactions**: Crisis Intervention Training (CIT), co-response models pairing officers with clinicians, civilian alternative response programs (CAHOOTS, STAR, B-HEARD), and efforts to divert crisis calls from 911 to 988
- **Emergency Department Psychiatric Care**: EMTALA obligations, ED boarding, psychiatric consultation models, racial disparities in restraint use, and strategies to reduce unnecessary ED utilization
- **Follow-Up and Care Transitions**: Post-crisis stabilization follow-up, warm handoffs to outpatient care, peer support services, and the Zero Suicide model
- **Workforce**: The 105,000-person gap in crisis workforce positions, compensation disparities, turnover, training pipelines, peer specialist certification, and the proposed Crisis Corps
- **Financing**: Medicaid, Medicare, and commercial insurance coverage of crisis services; the IMD exclusion; 988 telecom fees; federal appropriations; and the case for permanent funding
- **Governance and Data**: Fragmented governance across federal, state, and local agencies; the need for real-time crisis data infrastructure; quality measurement; and accountability mechanisms

## The Crisis Continuum Model

SAMHSA's 2020 *National Guidelines for Behavioral Health Crisis Care* established the framework that now guides federal, state, and local crisis system development. The model, based on the Crisis Now framework developed by Dr. Margie Balfour and RI International, defines three essential components that must work together:

- **Someone to Call**: A regional crisis call center (integrated with 988) that provides 24/7 telephone, text, and chat-based crisis counseling. Call centers serve as the entry point and coordination hub, assessing callers and dispatching mobile teams or connecting to stabilization facilities as needed
- **Someone to Respond**: Mobile crisis teams that respond in person to individuals in crisis, providing on-site assessment, de-escalation, and connection to appropriate services. Teams typically include a licensed clinician and a peer specialist, with law enforcement involvement only when safety risks are present
- **A Safe Place to Be**: Short-term crisis stabilization facilities (crisis stabilization units, crisis receiving centers, 23-hour observation beds) where individuals can stabilize in a therapeutic environment without the need for ED boarding or full inpatient psychiatric hospitalization

No single component is sufficient on its own. A 988 call center without mobile teams cannot respond in person. Mobile teams without crisis stabilization facilities have nowhere to bring people who need more than a field response. Stabilization facilities without call centers and mobile teams lack referral pathways. The three components form an integrated system, and the failure of the current U.S. crisis infrastructure is precisely that most communities have only one or two components -- or none at all.

## Core Tensions and Tradeoffs

- **Health System vs. Public Safety System**: Crisis response currently straddles two systems -- behavioral health and law enforcement -- with different missions, training, funding streams, and legal authorities. Shifting responses toward health-first models requires building clinical capacity that does not yet exist in most communities, while abruptly withdrawing police from crisis calls raises safety concerns.
- **Federal Standards vs. State Flexibility**: The 988 Lifeline is federally funded, but crisis services are primarily state and locally operated. Imposing national standards (answer rates, response times, staffing ratios) could improve quality but may conflict with local conditions and governance traditions.
- **Voluntary vs. Involuntary Treatment**: Crisis services must balance respect for personal autonomy with the obligation to protect people who may be unable to recognize their need for help. Involuntary commitment laws vary widely by state, and overuse of coercive intervention drives people away from seeking help.
- **Medicaid Funding vs. IMD Exclusion**: The Medicaid Institutions for Mental Diseases (IMD) exclusion prohibits federal Medicaid payment for most inpatient psychiatric care in facilities with more than 16 beds, creating a financing barrier for crisis stabilization units and larger psychiatric facilities that could otherwise provide needed capacity.
- **Workforce Investment vs. Immediate Demand**: Building the crisis workforce -- licensed clinicians, peer specialists, crisis counselors -- requires years of training pipeline development, but the demand for crisis response is urgent today. Interim solutions (telehealth, cross-training, volunteer models) may sacrifice quality for speed.

## Key Questions

1. How can the United States build a complete crisis continuum -- someone to call, someone to respond, a safe place to be -- in every community within the next decade?
2. What governance, funding, and accountability structures are needed to sustain 988 as a system rather than merely a phone number?
3. How should crisis services balance clinical best practices with the realities of workforce shortages, particularly in rural and underserved areas?
4. What role should law enforcement play in mental health crisis response as civilian alternatives are built, and how can the transition be managed safely?
5. How can the crisis system address racial and ethnic disparities in access, quality, and outcomes -- particularly the disproportionate rate of fatal police encounters involving Black Americans in mental health crisis?
6. What federal, state, and private insurance reforms are needed to create a sustainable payment model for crisis services that does not depend on time-limited grants?
7. How should the crisis system serve special populations -- including children and adolescents, veterans, older adults, people with co-occurring substance use disorders, and people experiencing homelessness -- whose needs differ from the general adult population?

## Scale of the Problem

The mental health crisis challenge extends beyond the headline statistics to encompass a broad range of populations and settings:

- **Youth crisis surge**: Suicide is the second leading cause of death for Americans ages 10-14 and the third leading cause for ages 15-24 (CDC, 2023). Pediatric mental health ED visits increased by 31% between 2019 and 2022 (AHRQ, 2023), and many children's hospitals report psychiatric boarding times exceeding 48 hours
- **Substance use overlap**: Approximately 9.2 million adults have co-occurring mental health and substance use disorders (SAMHSA, 2023). The fentanyl crisis has dramatically increased the complexity of behavioral health emergencies, requiring crisis workers trained in both psychiatric de-escalation and overdose response
- **Veterans**: The veteran suicide rate is 72% higher than the non-veteran adult rate (VA, 2023). The Veterans Crisis Line (988, press 1) handled over 700,000 contacts in 2023, but coordination between VA crisis services and community crisis systems remains inconsistent
- **Racial disparities**: Black Americans are 2.5 times more likely to be killed by police during mental health encounters than white Americans (Saleh et al., *JAMA Network Open*, 2021). Language barriers, cultural mistrust, and geographic access gaps compound the problem for Hispanic, Asian, Indigenous, and immigrant communities
- **Rural access**: Approximately 65% of non-metropolitan counties have no psychiatrist and 47% have no psychologist (HRSA, 2023). In frontier areas, the nearest crisis stabilization unit may be more than 60 miles away, and mobile crisis teams are effectively nonexistent

## Economic Dimensions

The fiscal case for crisis services reform is compelling. The current system does not save money by failing to invest in crisis infrastructure -- it spends money inefficiently through default systems designed for other purposes:

- **Emergency department costs**: Psychiatric ED visits average $2,264 per day, and psychiatric patients occupy ED beds three times longer than medical patients (ACEP, 2023). Annual psychiatric ED boarding costs hospitals an estimated $5.4 billion in unreimbursed care and lost revenue (AHA, 2023)
- **Incarceration costs**: Incarcerating people with serious mental illness costs $35,000-$60,000 per person per year. An estimated 2 million jail bookings annually involve people with serious mental illness (Treatment Advocacy Center, 2022)
- **Crisis system cost-effectiveness**: Georgia's crisis system saves an estimated $2,264 per episode compared to hospitalization (Georgia DBHDD, 2022). A RAND Corporation analysis found that every $1 invested in mobile crisis services saves $2.16 in avoided ED visits, hospitalizations, and incarceration (RAND, 2022)
- **Full build-out cost**: SAMHSA estimates that completing the national crisis continuum would require $3-5 billion annually in new federal and state spending -- a fraction of the $15+ billion currently spent through EDs, jails, and police response

## Vision of Success

A successful mental health crisis services system in the 21st century would feature:

- **Universal Access**: Every person in the United States can reach a trained crisis counselor within minutes by calling, texting, or chatting 988, and a mobile crisis team can respond in person within 60 minutes in urban areas and 90 minutes in rural areas
- **No Wrong Door**: People in crisis are connected to appropriate care regardless of how they enter the system -- through 988, 911, walk-in, or law enforcement encounter -- with seamless warm handoffs between services
- **Health-First Response**: The default first responder to a mental health crisis is a behavioral health professional, not an armed police officer, with law enforcement involvement limited to situations with imminent physical danger
- **Crisis Stabilization Capacity**: Every region has sufficient crisis stabilization beds and 23-hour observation units to prevent unnecessary ED boarding and avoidable psychiatric hospitalizations, with no one waiting more than 2 hours for placement
- **Equitable Access**: Crisis services are culturally responsive, linguistically accessible, and available to all populations regardless of race, ethnicity, age, disability, sexual orientation, gender identity, or geographic location
- **Sustained Follow-Up**: Every person who contacts the crisis system receives a follow-up within 24 hours and a warm handoff to ongoing care, with peer support integrated throughout the recovery process
- **Data-Driven Improvement**: A national crisis data infrastructure enables real-time bed tracking, coordinated dispatch, quality measurement, and continuous system improvement across the entire crisis continuum
