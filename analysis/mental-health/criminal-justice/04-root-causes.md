# Criminal Justice and Mental Health: Root Causes

## Systemic Analysis

The current crisis of mental illness in the criminal justice system is not the result of a single policy failure but of several long-term, interlocking trends. The closure of state psychiatric hospitals without building community alternatives, the simultaneous expansion of the criminal justice system, fragmented governance across health and justice agencies, and the absence of a right to mental health treatment have converged to make jails and prisons the nation's default psychiatric facilities. These root causes are structural, financial, political, and cultural, and they reinforce each other in ways that make reform exceptionally difficult.

## Failure of Community Mental Health Infrastructure

### The Unfulfilled Promise of Deinstitutionalization

Deinstitutionalization was never completed as envisioned. The 1963 Community Mental Health Act projected 2,000 community mental health centers; only 789 were built. Federal funding was converted to block grants in 1981 under the Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act, and states reduced their own mental health spending as hospital costs declined. The result was a massive net reduction in mental health capacity: state hospital beds declined from 559,000 in 1955 to approximately 37,000 in 2022 (Treatment Advocacy Center, 2022), but community services were never funded at a level sufficient to absorb the discharged population.

**Evidence**: The United States has 11.7 state psychiatric beds per 100,000 population, far below the minimum of 40-60 beds per 100,000 recommended by the Treatment Advocacy Center as necessary to prevent criminalization and homelessness. Forty-three states have fewer than 25 beds per 100,000 (Treatment Advocacy Center, 2022).

### The Medicaid IMD Exclusion

The Institutions for Mental Diseases (IMD) exclusion, embedded in Medicaid since 1965, prohibits federal reimbursement for care in psychiatric facilities with more than 16 beds for people aged 21-64. This single provision has distorted the entire architecture of American mental health care. It incentivizes states to close large psychiatric facilities (which cannot receive Medicaid) and shift care to community settings (which can), even when community capacity is inadequate. It also prevents the development of medium-sized psychiatric facilities that could serve as step-down alternatives between hospitalization and outpatient care.

**Evidence**: A 2023 analysis by the National Association of State Mental Health Program Directors (NASMHPD) estimated that the IMD exclusion reduces federal mental health spending by $3-5 billion annually, leaving states to bear costs that would otherwise be shared with the federal government.

### Workforce Shortage

Community mental health centers report 25-30% vacancy rates for clinical positions (National Council for Mental Wellbeing, 2023). The shortage is particularly acute for psychiatrists willing to serve Medicaid populations, forensic psychologists, and licensed clinical social workers in rural areas. Without staff, programs cannot accept referrals from the justice system, and diversion becomes impossible even where it is authorized.

**Evidence**: There are approximately 30,000 practicing psychiatrists in the United States, but the Health Resources and Services Administration (HRSA) estimates a shortfall of 14,280 psychiatrists needed to meet current demand, with the gap projected to grow to 31,100 by 2030 (HRSA, 2023).

The workforce shortage has multiple dimensions:

| Workforce Category | Current Supply | Estimated Shortfall | Key Barriers | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Psychiatrists | ~30,000 | 14,280 | Low Medicaid reimbursement; burnout; maldistribution | HRSA, 2023 |
| Psychologists (clinical) | ~106,000 | ~15,000 | Training pipeline; student debt; rural scarcity | APA, 2023 |
| Licensed clinical social workers | ~275,000 | ~25,000 | Low pay; high caseloads; burnout | NASW, 2023 |
| Psychiatric nurse practitioners | ~21,000 | ~8,000 | Limited training programs; scope-of-practice restrictions | AANP, 2023 |
| Peer support specialists | ~35,000 | ~20,000+ | Low wages; inconsistent certification standards | SAMHSA, 2023 |
| Forensic evaluators (competency) | ~3,500 | ~2,000 | Specialized training required; limited academic programs | Gowensmith et al., 2023 |

The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated the workforce crisis. A 2023 National Council for Mental Wellbeing survey found that 93% of community mental health organizations reported difficulty recruiting staff, and 73% reported increased staff turnover compared to pre-pandemic levels. Median salaries for community mental health clinicians ($48,000-$55,000) are approximately 40% lower than comparable positions in private practice or hospital settings, creating a structural drain of talent away from the populations most in need.

### Housing as a Treatment Barrier

The shortage of affordable and supportive housing is a root cause of criminalization that is frequently overlooked in mental health policy discussions. Without stable housing, outpatient treatment is ineffective -- a person cannot maintain a medication regimen, keep therapy appointments, or achieve psychiatric stability while sleeping on the street or cycling through shelters.

**Evidence**: HUD reports an estimated 6-month waitlist for supportive housing for people with SMI leaving jail (HUD, 2023). The National Low Income Housing Coalition found that there is a shortage of 7.3 million affordable rental homes for extremely low-income renters (NLIHC, 2024). A 2022 study in *American Journal of Public Health* found that access to permanent supportive housing reduced rearrest rates among people with SMI by 40% compared to those who were released without housing placement.

## Criminal Justice System Expansion

### Mass Incarceration and Low-Level Offending

The total incarcerated population in the United States grew from approximately 500,000 in 1980 to over 2.3 million by 2010. This expansion was driven by mandatory minimum sentencing, three-strikes laws, the War on Drugs, and broken-windows policing. People with untreated mental illness -- who are more likely to commit minor offenses like trespassing, disorderly conduct, and petty theft, and less likely to navigate the bail and court systems -- were disproportionately swept into the system.

**Evidence**: A 2023 Bureau of Justice Statistics report found that 64% of jail inmates with mental illness were incarcerated for nonviolent offenses, compared to 47% of inmates without mental illness.

### Criminalization of Survival Behaviors

Many of the behaviors associated with untreated mental illness and homelessness -- sleeping in public, trespassing, public urination, disorderly conduct, panhandling -- have been increasingly criminalized. Laws targeting these behaviors function as a de facto mechanism for removing people with mental illness from public spaces, channeling them into jails rather than treatment.

**Evidence**: The National Law Center on Homelessness and Poverty found that between 2006 and 2019, citywide bans on camping increased by 92%, bans on sitting/lying in public increased by 78%, and bans on panhandling increased by 103%. A 2022 study in the *Journal of Urban Health* found that people with serious mental illness are 40 times more likely to be arrested for "quality of life" offenses than the general population.

### Bail System and Pretrial Detention

The cash bail system is a significant driver of the criminalization of mental illness. People with serious mental illness are less likely to have the financial resources or family support to post bail, resulting in longer pretrial detention even for minor charges. Pretrial detention -- which averages 26 days for people with mental illness versus 14 days for others (Vera Institute, 2023) -- disrupts treatment, housing, and employment, increasing the likelihood of future justice involvement.

**Evidence**: A 2023 study by the Pretrial Justice Institute found that people with SMI detained pretrial for more than 72 hours had a 30% higher rate of subsequent arrest compared to those released within 72 hours, after controlling for offense severity and criminal history. The study concluded that pretrial detention itself functions as a criminogenic factor for people with mental illness. States that have reformed or eliminated cash bail (Illinois, New Jersey, New York) have not seen the public safety deterioration predicted by opponents, though data on mental health-specific outcomes remains limited.

### Law Enforcement Training Gaps

Despite the growth of CIT programs, the vast majority of police training on mental health remains inadequate. Most police academies dedicate fewer than 8 hours to mental health topics -- compared to 60+ hours on firearms and use of force. Officers who do receive CIT training represent a small fraction of any given department, and CIT-trained officers are not always dispatched to mental health calls.

**Evidence**: A 2022 Police Executive Research Forum survey found that the median police academy curriculum includes 6 hours of mental health instruction. By contrast, the Memphis Model CIT program requires 40 hours. Only 15% of U.S. law enforcement agencies have CIT-trained officers (PERF, 2023). Even in CIT-trained departments, a 2021 study in *Criminal Justice and Behavior* found that CIT officers were dispatched to mental health calls only 52% of the time -- meaning nearly half of mental health calls were handled by officers without specialized training.

## Structural and Governance Failures

### Fragmented Authority

Mental health and criminal justice systems operate under entirely separate governance structures. Mental health services are administered by state and county behavioral health authorities, funded through Medicaid, state general funds, and federal block grants. Criminal justice is governed by police departments, district attorneys, judges, and corrections agencies, funded through state and local criminal justice budgets. There is no structural mechanism requiring coordination between these systems, and the incentives of each system often conflict.

**Evidence**: A 2021 Council of State Governments Justice Center analysis found that only 12 states had formal interagency agreements between their behavioral health and corrections agencies, and only 7 states had shared data systems allowing tracking of individuals across systems.

### No Right to Mental Health Treatment

Unlike criminal defendants, who have a constitutional right to counsel, people with mental illness have no enforceable right to treatment in the community. The Supreme Court's 1999 *Olmstead v. L.C.* decision established a right to community-based services for people with disabilities, but enforcement has been slow and piecemeal. Without a legal right to treatment, the mental health system can simply run out of capacity, leaving law enforcement as the only entity obligated to respond.

**Evidence**: The federal government funds 50% of Medicaid-covered mental health services but provides no entitlement to community mental health care for the uninsured. In 2022, SAMHSA estimated that only 46.2% of adults with any mental illness received mental health services, and only 65.4% of adults with serious mental illness received treatment (NSDUH, 2023).

The asymmetry between the legal obligations of the criminal justice and mental health systems is a structural driver of criminalization. The criminal justice system is legally required to respond to every call, process every arrest, house every detainee, and provide minimally adequate health care to every prisoner (under *Estelle v. Gamble*). The mental health system has no comparable obligation to treat every person who needs care. When a person in crisis cannot access treatment, the only system that is legally obligated to respond is law enforcement. This structural asymmetry -- not the preferences of any individual actor -- is what drives police to become the de facto first responders for mental health emergencies.

### Inadequate Insurance Coverage and Parity Enforcement

Although the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act of 2008 requires insurers to cover mental health services on terms comparable to medical services, enforcement has been inconsistent. Many insurers use prior authorization requirements, narrow provider networks, and lower reimbursement rates to effectively limit access to mental health care.

**Evidence**: A 2023 report by the U.S. Department of Labor found that 40% of mental health claims reviewed involved potential parity violations. Out-of-network utilization rates for mental health services are 5.2 times higher than for medical/surgical services, indicating that in-network mental health provider networks are significantly narrower (Milliman Research Report, 2022). For the 27 million Americans who are uninsured (Census Bureau, 2023), parity is irrelevant -- they have no coverage to which parity applies.

### Medicaid Coverage Gap at Incarceration

Federal Medicaid policy terminates or suspends coverage for people during incarceration, creating immediate treatment disruption at arrest and a gap in coverage upon release. People with mental illness who lose Medicaid at arrest often go without medication and treatment during detention. Upon release, the average time to regain Medicaid coverage is 45-90 days (Bazelon Center, 2023), during which the risk of crisis, re-arrest, and death peaks.

**Evidence**: An estimated 70% of Medicaid-eligible people leaving jails are not enrolled in Medicaid on the day of release (Bazelon Center, 2023). A landmark New England Journal of Medicine study found that formerly incarcerated people have a 12.7 times higher risk of death in the first two weeks after release, with overdose and suicide as leading causes (Binswanger et al., 2007).

## Political Economy

### Who Benefits from the Status Quo

| Actor | Benefit | Economic Value | Incentive to Block Reform |
|-------|---------|----------------|---------------------------|
| Private prison and jail operators | Per-diem payments increase with population; mental health inmates have longer stays | $5.6 billion industry (2023) | Diversion reduces census; fewer inmates means less revenue |
| Private correctional healthcare companies | Contracts to provide mental health care inside facilities | $12 billion+ market (2023) | Community-based care reduces their captive market |
| Bail bond industry | People with mental illness are less likely to make bail, generating bond revenue | $2 billion annually | Pretrial diversion reduces bail-eligible population |
| Police unions | Mental health calls justify staffing levels and overtime | Varies by jurisdiction | Alternative response models reduce law enforcement role and funding |
| Forensic psychiatry providers | Competency evaluations and restoration generate revenue | ~$1.5 billion market | Reforms reducing IST referrals shrink the market |
| Local governments reliant on fines and fees | People with mental illness cycle through courts generating revenue | Varies; see justice/fines-and-fees analysis | Diversion reduces court-generated revenue |

### Perverse Incentives

The current system creates multiple perverse incentives that perpetuate criminalization:

- **For law enforcement**: Arrest is the path of least resistance. Officers can transport someone to jail in 30 minutes but may wait 6-8 hours in an emergency department for a psychiatric evaluation. The structure of the response system makes criminalization the easiest option.

- **For hospitals**: Emergency departments may board psychiatric patients for hours or days, creating financial losses. Refusing to accept patients (leading to jail) has no legal consequence for the hospital.

- **For states**: The IMD exclusion means states pay 100% of state hospital costs but only 25-50% of community Medicaid costs. This creates incentive to shift care to community settings, but when those settings lack capacity, the cost falls to the criminal justice system -- a different budget line.

- **For counties**: Jails are funded through county budgets, but community mental health is funded through state Medicaid and behavioral health allocations. Counties that invest in diversion programs save their own jail costs but may not recoup equivalent state mental health funding to sustain alternatives.

- **For prosecutors**: Prosecutorial discretion creates a structural incentive to process cases efficiently. Diversion programs require prosecutors to divert defendants to treatment tracks that take 12-24 months, involve coordination with treatment providers, and may not result in conviction. Traditional prosecution -- particularly plea bargaining -- resolves cases faster and produces measurable "accountability" outcomes that prosecutors can cite during reelection campaigns.

- **For public defenders**: Overburdened public defenders may inadvertently contribute to criminalization. With average caseloads of 200-400 cases per year (far exceeding the American Bar Association's recommended maximum of 150), public defenders often lack the time to investigate mental health issues, request competency evaluations, or identify diversion eligibility for their clients. The right to counsel, while constitutionally guaranteed, is functionally inadequate for defendants with mental illness who require specialized advocacy.

### The Budget Silo Problem

One of the most insidious root causes is the separation of health and justice budgets at every level of government. Jail costs come from county budgets. Community mental health is funded through state Medicaid and behavioral health allocations. Federal funding flows through separate agencies (SAMHSA for treatment, BJA for justice programs, CMS for insurance coverage). No single budget authority encompasses both the cost of incarceration and the cost of treatment, making it impossible for any single decision-maker to realize the savings from diversion.

**Evidence**: A 2021 Vera Institute analysis found that the combined cost of incarcerating one person with SMI for one year ($55,000 jail + $15,000 correctional mental health) exceeds the cost of community treatment with supportive housing ($35,000-$45,000), but the savings accrue to a different budget line than the investment. Counties that invest in diversion save jail costs but do not receive increased state mental health funding. States that expand community treatment reduce jail populations but do not directly recoup the county-level savings. This budget silo problem creates a collective action failure where reform is cost-effective in aggregate but no single actor captures sufficient savings to justify the investment.

## The Juvenile Justice Pipeline

The criminalization of mental illness often begins in childhood and adolescence. Youth with untreated mental health conditions are disproportionately represented in the juvenile justice system, and early justice involvement dramatically increases the likelihood of adult incarceration.

**Evidence**: An estimated 65-70% of youth in the juvenile justice system meet criteria for at least one mental health disorder, compared to approximately 20% of the general youth population (Office of Juvenile Justice and Delinquency Prevention, 2023). The "school-to-prison pipeline" -- whereby schools respond to behavioral issues with suspension, expulsion, and school-based arrests rather than mental health services -- funnels youth with mental illness into the justice system. Black youth are 3 times more likely to be referred to the juvenile justice system for the same behaviors as white youth (OJJDP, 2023).

Key pipeline factors:

- **Zero-tolerance school policies**: Schools that rely on suspension and referral to law enforcement for behavioral incidents involving youth with mental illness create early justice contacts that begin the criminalization process.
- **Lack of school-based mental health services**: Only 49% of U.S. public schools have a full-time school counselor, and only 14% have a school-based mental health clinic (National Center for Education Statistics, 2023). When youth in crisis have no access to school-based treatment, the default response is discipline and, increasingly, arrest.
- **Foster care and child welfare crossover**: Youth in foster care have rates of mental illness 3-5 times higher than the general youth population and are disproportionately represented in the juvenile justice system. An estimated 25% of youth in juvenile detention have been in foster care (Annie E. Casey Foundation, 2022).
- **Trauma exposure**: The vast majority of justice-involved youth have experienced multiple adverse childhood experiences (ACEs). A 2022 study found that 90% of youth in juvenile detention had experienced at least one traumatic event, and the average number of ACEs was 4.7, compared to a population average of 1.5 (Journal of Traumatic Stress, 2022).

Addressing the juvenile justice pipeline is essential to reducing the adult criminalization of mental illness. Early intervention with evidence-based youth mental health treatment can prevent the initial justice contact that begins the trajectory.

## Cultural and Behavioral Factors

### Stigma and Fear

Public attitudes toward mental illness remain shaped by stigma and fear, particularly regarding perceived dangerousness. Media coverage disproportionately links mental illness to violence, despite consistent research showing that people with mental illness commit only 3-5% of violent acts and are 10 times more likely to be victims than perpetrators of violence (NIMH, 2022). This fear drives support for punitive responses and resistance to community-based treatment facilities ("not in my backyard" opposition to group homes, crisis centers, and supportive housing).

**Evidence**: A 2023 American Psychiatric Association poll found that 46% of Americans said they would be uncomfortable having a person with serious mental illness as a neighbor, and 67% believed people with serious mental illness are "somewhat" or "very" dangerous.

### Substance Use Comorbidity and Moral Framing

Seventy-two percent of incarcerated people with mental health conditions also have co-occurring substance use disorders (Bureau of Justice Statistics, 2023). Public discourse frequently treats substance use as a moral failing rather than a health condition, and the co-occurrence of mental illness and substance use undermines sympathy for treatment-based approaches. The result is a political environment where "treatment" and "accountability" are framed as opposing concepts.

**Evidence**: A 2023 Pew Research Center survey found that 54% of Americans believe people addicted to drugs are "mostly to blame for their own condition," while only 26% attribute addiction primarily to circumstances beyond the individual's control. When mental illness and substance use co-occur, the moral stigma of substance use tends to dominate public perception, reducing support for treatment-based interventions. This dynamic is particularly pronounced in discussions of fentanyl and methamphetamine, where the perceived association with crime and social disorder drives punitive responses.

### Racial Bias in Perception of Dangerousness

Research consistently demonstrates that racial bias distorts perceptions of mental illness and dangerousness in ways that compound the criminalization of people of color with mental health conditions.

**Evidence**: A 2022 study in *Law and Human Behavior* found that police officers rated hypothetical suspects described as Black and mentally ill as significantly more dangerous than identical descriptions of white suspects with mental illness. The same study found that officers recommended arrest for Black suspects with mental illness at rates 35% higher than for white suspects with identical presentations. A separate 2023 analysis by the Vera Institute found that Black defendants with mental illness were 40% less likely to be referred to diversion programs by prosecutors, even when controlling for charge severity and criminal history. These findings suggest that the disparities in mental health-criminal justice outcomes are not solely the product of structural factors but also reflect implicit and explicit bias in decision-making at every system point.

### Emergency Department Bottlenecks and the "Path of Least Resistance"

A critical but underappreciated root cause of criminalization is the dysfunction of the psychiatric emergency system. When officers encounter a person in mental health crisis, the alternatives to arrest are often impractical: emergency departments routinely "board" psychiatric patients for 8-24 hours (and sometimes days) while waiting for an available psychiatric bed, during which time the officer is required to remain at the hospital. This creates an enormous practical incentive for officers to choose arrest over hospitalization.

**Evidence**: The American College of Emergency Physicians reported in 2023 that the average psychiatric boarding time in U.S. emergency departments was 10.4 hours, with 23% of psychiatric patients boarding for more than 24 hours. A 2022 study in *Annals of Emergency Medicine* found that officers spent an average of 4.2 hours on each mental health call that resulted in an emergency department transport, compared to 30 minutes for a jail booking. This 8:1 time differential makes arrest the "path of least resistance" for officers responding to mental health crises, regardless of their training or intentions.

## Data and Information Gaps

A significant root cause of the problem is the lack of consistent, real-time data on mental illness in the criminal justice system. Most jails do not systematically screen for mental illness. Those that do use inconsistent instruments. There is no national database tracking the number of people with mental illness arrested, detained, diverted, or released each year. Without data, jurisdictions cannot identify the scope of their problem, target interventions, or measure progress.

**Evidence**: The Stepping Up Initiative found that only 62% of participating counties screen jail inmates for mental illness, and participation is voluntary. Outside of Stepping Up counties, screening rates are far lower -- as low as 15-25% in some states (CSG Justice Center, 2023). The Bureau of Justice Statistics conducts periodic surveys, but the most recent comprehensive data on mental illness in jails comes from surveys with multi-year lag times. Real-time dashboards like those created by Stepping Up "Innovator" counties represent best practice but exist in fewer than 30 of the nation's 3,000+ counties.

## Why Reform Has Failed

Reform efforts to date have been characterized by a recurring pattern: identification of the problem, development of evidence-based models, pilot implementation, and then failure to scale. Understanding why each reform fell short illuminates the structural barriers to systemic change.

### Attempted Reforms and Their Limitations

1. **Community Mental Health Act (1963)**: Created the vision of community-based care but was never adequately funded. Only 789 of 2,000 planned centers were built, and federal funding was converted to block grants in 1981.

2. **Mentally Ill Offender Treatment and Crime Reduction Act (2004)**: Authorized grants for mental health courts and diversion programs, but annual appropriations ($30 million in 2023) cover a fraction of national need.

3. **Affordable Care Act Mental Health Parity (2010)**: Expanded Medicaid coverage for mental health services, but the IMD exclusion limits institutional care, 12 states have not expanded Medicaid, and workforce shortages prevent access even where coverage exists.

4. **Stepping Up Initiative (2015)**: Generated political commitment in 530+ counties, but most have not achieved measurable reductions in jail mental illness prevalence because resolutions are not binding and do not come with funding.

5. **988 Lifeline (2022)**: Created national crisis line infrastructure, but only 7 states have dedicated funding for 988, and the system cannot dispatch mobile crisis teams to most communities because those teams do not yet exist.

Each reform addresses one piece of the problem -- crisis response, diversion, treatment access, insurance coverage -- without transforming the underlying system. The fundamental failure is the absence of a comprehensive, adequately funded national strategy that addresses all intercept points simultaneously. Until policymakers treat the criminalization of mental illness as a systemic crisis requiring a systemic response -- rather than a collection of discrete problems to be addressed incrementally -- the pattern of promising but insufficient reform will continue.

### The Self-Reinforcing Cycle

The root causes described above operate as a self-reinforcing system. Insufficient community treatment leads to crisis. Crisis leads to police contact. Police contact leads to arrest because alternatives are unavailable or impractical. Arrest leads to jail detention, which worsens mental health. Jail detention disrupts medication, housing, and employment. Release without treatment planning leads to decompensation. Decompensation leads to new crisis. Each cycle deepens the individual's involvement with the criminal justice system, reduces their connection to community supports, and increases the cost to the public. Breaking this cycle requires simultaneous intervention at multiple points -- not sequential, piecemeal reform.

The following table summarizes the relative contribution of each root cause to the overall problem:

| Root Cause | Contribution to Criminalization | Tractability (Ease of Reform) | Investment Required |
|---|---|---|---|
| Psychiatric bed shortage | Very High | Medium (requires capital + operating funds) | $5-10 billion |
| Community mental health underfunding | Very High | Medium (requires sustained appropriations) | $6-10 billion/year |
| IMD exclusion | High | Medium (legislative change needed) | Revenue-neutral if replaced |
| Workforce shortage | Very High | Low-Medium (3-7 year pipeline) | $1-2 billion/year |
| Housing shortage | Very High | Low (structural national crisis) | $10+ billion/year |
| Bail system | Medium | Medium-High (state-level reform possible) | $500 million-$1 billion |
| Police training gaps | Medium | Medium-High (scalable models exist) | $500 million/year |
