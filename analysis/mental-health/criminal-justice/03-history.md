# Criminal Justice and Mental Health: History

## Historical Development

The relationship between mental illness and the criminal justice system in the United States has undergone dramatic transformations over three centuries. The story is fundamentally one of institutional transfer: from poorhouses to asylums, from asylums to communities, and -- when communities failed to provide care -- from communities to jails and prisons. Understanding this history is essential to grasping why the current crisis exists and why reform efforts keep falling short.

---

### The Asylum Era (1773-1950): Institutionalization as Default

- **Colonial Period (1600s-1840)**: People with mental illness were treated as the responsibility of local communities under the English poor law tradition. Those who were not cared for by family were confined in poorhouses, almshouses, or jails -- often alongside criminals, orphans, and the destitute. There was no meaningful distinction between mental illness and criminal behavior in many communities. Virginia established the first public psychiatric institution in 1773, the Eastern State Hospital in Williamsburg, but for most of the colonial and early republic period, the jail and the poorhouse were the primary institutions housing people with mental illness. This foundational pattern -- jails as default psychiatric facilities -- is the same pattern that persists today, having completed a 250-year circle.

- **Moral Treatment Movement (1840s-1870s)**: Dorothea Dix led a national campaign to build state psychiatric hospitals after documenting the horrific conditions in jails and poorhouses where people with mental illness were held. In her 1843 "Memorial to the Legislature of Massachusetts," Dix described finding "men and women in cages, closets, cellars, stalls, pens; chained, naked, beaten with rods, and lashed into obedience." Between 1843 and 1880, she persuaded states to build or expand 32 psychiatric hospitals. The movement was grounded in the belief that humane, structured environments could cure mental illness. Thomas Story Kirkbride developed an influential architectural plan for psychiatric hospitals, emphasizing light, ventilation, and orderly design as therapeutic elements. The Kirkbride Plan hospitals -- many of which still stand as historical landmarks -- represented the first systematic effort to build a national infrastructure for treating mental illness outside the criminal justice system.

- **Growth of the State Hospital System**: By 1900, every state operated at least one psychiatric hospital. The number of state hospital patients grew from approximately 150,000 in 1903 to 559,000 in 1955 -- the peak of American institutionalization. At that point, state hospitals housed approximately 340 patients per 100,000 population (Treatment Advocacy Center, 2022).

- **Overcrowding and Deterioration**: The state hospital system became a victim of its own growth. Institutions designed for hundreds housed thousands. Treatment gave way to custodial care. Conditions deteriorated to the point that journalist Albert Deutsch described state hospitals as "little more than concentration camps" in his 1948 expose *The Shame of the States*. Staff-to-patient ratios in some facilities reached 1:100. The 1946 novel *The Snake Pit* by Mary Jane Ward and its 1948 film adaptation brought public attention to the abuses inside state hospitals, as did a series of Life magazine photographs from Byberry State Hospital in Philadelphia showing patients lying naked in their own filth.

- **The Eugenics Connection (1900-1945)**: The state hospital system was deeply intertwined with the American eugenics movement. Many state hospitals served as custodial warehouses intended to segregate people with mental illness from the general population. At least 30 states enacted compulsory sterilization laws targeting people in psychiatric institutions. An estimated 60,000 people in state hospitals were forcibly sterilized between 1907 and 1963 (Lombardo, 2008). The U.S. Supreme Court upheld compulsory sterilization in *Buck v. Bell* (1927), with Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes infamously declaring "three generations of imbeciles are enough." This history of institutional abuse provided powerful moral ammunition for the deinstitutionalization movement that followed.

- **Criminal Justice During the Asylum Era**: During this period, the criminal justice system's involvement with mental illness was relatively limited. People exhibiting signs of mental illness were typically diverted to state hospitals through civil commitment rather than prosecuted. Civil commitment standards were loose -- in most states, a family member's affidavit and a single physician's certification were sufficient for indefinite confinement. Jails housed a small fraction of people with mental illness -- the "revolving door" had not yet been created because the institutional system, however flawed, absorbed this population. However, the asylum system itself operated as a form of incarceration: patients had no right to refuse treatment, no meaningful right to judicial review, and no timeline for release.

### Deinstitutionalization (1955-1985): The Great Emptying

- **Thorazine and the Psychopharmacological Revolution (1954)**: The introduction of chlorpromazine (Thorazine) -- the first effective antipsychotic medication -- convinced policymakers that people with serious mental illness could be treated outside institutional settings. Thorazine dramatically reduced psychotic symptoms in many patients, and state hospital administrators reported significant improvements in ward conditions. The drug transformed the landscape of possibility, even as its limitations were not yet understood. The severe side effects of early antipsychotics -- including tardive dyskinesia, akathisia, and metabolic syndrome -- would become apparent only decades later. Policymakers seized on the new medications as evidence that community-based treatment was feasible, without fully appreciating that medication alone, without housing, income support, and clinical follow-up, would prove insufficient for many people with severe mental illness.

- **Community Mental Health Act (1963)**: President John F. Kennedy signed the Community Mental Health Centers Construction Act on October 31, 1963, three weeks before his assassination. The legislation envisioned a network of 2,000 community mental health centers (CMHCs) to replace state hospitals, organized into catchment areas of 75,000-200,000 people. Kennedy's message to Congress called for "a bold new approach" that would move care from "the cold mercy of custodial isolation" to "the open warmth of community concern and capability." Congress authorized construction funding but provided only limited operating funds. Kennedy's assassination, the escalation of the Vietnam War, and the competing priorities of the Great Society programs shifted national attention away from implementation. The Act remains one of the most consequential -- and most incompletely executed -- pieces of mental health legislation in American history.

- **Medicaid and the IMD Exclusion (1965)**: The creation of Medicaid included the "Institutions for Mental Diseases" (IMD) exclusion, which prohibited federal Medicaid reimbursement for care in psychiatric facilities with more than 16 beds. This created a powerful financial incentive for states to discharge patients from state hospitals (which received no federal funding) into smaller settings or the community (where Medicaid would pay). The IMD exclusion remains in effect and continues to distort mental health financing.

- **Civil Rights Movement and Patient Rights (1960s-1970s)**: A series of landmark court decisions established constitutional protections for people in psychiatric institutions:

    - *Rouse v. Cameron* (1966): The D.C. Circuit Court established a right to treatment for involuntarily committed patients, holding that commitment without treatment amounted to imprisonment.
    - *Wyatt v. Stickney* (1971): An Alabama federal court established a right to treatment in the least restrictive setting and specified minimum staffing ratios, physical conditions, and individualized treatment plans.
    - *Jackson v. Indiana* (1972): The Supreme Court held that a defendant found incompetent to stand trial could not be held indefinitely solely on the basis of incompetency, requiring periodic review and a reasonable probability of restoration.
    - *O'Connor v. Donaldson* (1975): The Supreme Court held that non-dangerous mentally ill people could not be confined against their will if they could survive safely in the community, effectively requiring states to demonstrate dangerousness as a condition of involuntary commitment.
    - *Addington v. Texas* (1979): The Supreme Court required clear and convincing evidence for civil commitment, raising the procedural bar for involuntary hospitalization.

    These decisions, while advancing civil liberties, accelerated the discharge of patients without ensuring adequate community services existed. The courts dismantled the legal framework for institutional confinement faster than legislatures built the infrastructure for community care.

- **The Numbers**: State psychiatric hospital populations dropped from 559,000 in 1955 to 272,000 by 1970, 132,000 by 1980, and 72,000 by 1994 (Treatment Advocacy Center, 2022). Hundreds of hospitals closed entirely. Between 1955 and 2000, the number of state psychiatric beds fell from 340 per 100,000 population to approximately 22 per 100,000.

- **The Broken Promise of Community Care**: Of the 2,000 CMHCs envisioned in Kennedy's 1963 legislation, only 789 were ever built. Federal block grant funding replaced categorical CMHC funding in 1981 under the Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act, and many centers shifted their focus toward less severely ill populations who were easier and cheaper to serve. People with serious mental illness -- the very population discharged from state hospitals -- were systematically underserved.

- **Reagan-Era Retrenchment (1981-1989)**: President Ronald Reagan's Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1981 converted federal community mental health funding into block grants with a 25% reduction, giving states broad discretion to allocate funds. Many states redirected money away from services for people with severe mental illness. Simultaneously, the Reagan administration tightened Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI) eligibility, removing an estimated 500,000 people from disability rolls between 1981 and 1983, many of whom had psychiatric disabilities. The combination of reduced community mental health funding and loss of disability income pushed tens of thousands of people with mental illness into homelessness and, increasingly, into the criminal justice system.

- **Homelessness Emerges as National Crisis (1980s)**: The visible homelessness crisis of the 1980s was inextricably linked to deinstitutionalization. The U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development estimated that approximately 30-40% of homeless individuals had serious mental illness. The case of Rebecca Smith, a formerly institutionalized woman who froze to death on a New York City heating grate in 1982, became a national symbol of the failure of deinstitutionalization. Mayor Ed Koch responded by implementing involuntary hospitalization for visibly mentally ill homeless people -- a coercive approach that foreshadowed the tension between civil liberties and treatment that continues to define the field.

### Criminalization and the Rise of "Trans-Institutionalization" (1970-2000)

- **Penrose's Law in Action**: In 1939, British psychiatrist Lionel Penrose observed an inverse relationship between psychiatric bed capacity and prison population. As state hospitals emptied, American jails and prisons filled with people with mental illness -- a phenomenon scholars would later call "trans-institutionalization." A 2010 analysis by Steven Raphael and Michael Stoll found that the relationship was causal, not merely correlational: for every state psychiatric bed closed, the prison and jail population increased by approximately 0.3 to 0.4 inmates, after controlling for other factors.

- **The War on Crime (1970s-1990s)**: The simultaneous expansion of criminal justice and contraction of mental health services created a perfect storm. Key developments included:

    - The Comprehensive Drug Abuse Prevention and Control Act (1970) and the Anti-Drug Abuse Acts of 1986 and 1988 established mandatory minimum sentences for drug offenses, sweeping people with co-occurring mental illness and substance use into the prison system.
    - New York's Rockefeller Drug Laws (1973) established some of the nation's harshest mandatory minimums, becoming a model for other states. People with mental illness who self-medicated with illegal substances were among those most frequently caught in these laws.
    - California's "three strikes" law (1994) and similar laws in 24 other states imposed life sentences for third felony convictions, regardless of the offender's mental health status.
    - The "broken windows" policing theory, popularized by James Q. Wilson and George Kelling in 1982 and implemented aggressively in New York City under Commissioner William Bratton, led to mass arrests for minor quality-of-life offenses that disproportionately targeted people with visible mental illness.

    The total U.S. incarcerated population grew from approximately 500,000 in 1980 to over 2.3 million by 2010. Homelessness among people with mental illness surged as the safety net contracted and policing expanded.

- **Jail as Mental Hospital**: By the late 1980s, researchers began documenting the growing presence of mental illness in jails. Linda Teplin's landmark 1990 study in Cook County Jail found that 9% of male detainees and 18.5% of female detainees had severe psychiatric disorders. The proportion of jail inmates with mental illness has continued to grow in every subsequent study. By 2023, those rates had roughly doubled: 17% of jail inmates had serious mental illness and 44% had any mental health condition (BJS, 2023). County sheriffs -- most of whom had no training in mental health and no budget for psychiatric care -- found themselves running the largest de facto mental health facilities in their communities. In 1995, the National Alliance on Mental Illness published a landmark report documenting that the Los Angeles County Jail, Cook County Jail, and Rikers Island held more people with mental illness than any psychiatric hospital in the country -- a finding that shocked the public and became one of the most frequently cited statistics in the field.

- **Larry Hogue and the Media Narrative**: The case of Larry Hogue, a homeless man with mental illness and substance use disorder who terrorized an Upper West Side neighborhood in New York City in the early 1990s, became national news and crystallized public fear. Hogue was arrested dozens of times, briefly hospitalized, released without follow-up, and returned to the same neighborhood in a repeating cycle that epitomized the failure of the revolving door. The "violent mentally ill person" narrative -- despite evidence that people with mental illness are far more likely to be victims than perpetrators of violence -- drove punitive policy responses. Similar high-profile cases, including Andrew Goldstein's fatal pushing of Kendra Webdale onto New York subway tracks in 1999, led directly to Kendra's Law, which authorized assisted outpatient treatment (court-ordered community treatment) in New York. Kendra's Law became a model for AOT legislation in other states, illustrating how individual tragedies drive policy in this field.

- **The Rise of Private Corrections (1983-2000)**: The Corrections Corporation of America (now CoreCivic) was founded in 1983, and the GEO Group entered the corrections market in 1984. The private corrections industry grew rapidly as mass incarceration strained state budgets. By 2000, private prisons held approximately 87,000 inmates. The industry's business model depended on maintaining high incarceration rates -- creating an economic interest directly opposed to mental health diversion. Private correctional healthcare companies, including Correctional Healthcare Solutions (later Corizon, now YesCare) and Prison Health Services (later Wexford Health Sources), developed a parallel industry providing medical and mental health services inside jails and prisons, often at the lowest cost rather than the highest quality.

- **Willowbrook and the Rights Revolution**: The 1972 television expose of Willowbrook State School on Staten Island, led by journalist Geraldo Rivera, revealed conditions of extreme neglect and abuse in a facility for people with intellectual disabilities. The resulting class-action lawsuit (*New York State Association for Retarded Children v. Rockefeller*, 1973) and consent decree became a model for institutional reform litigation. Similar cases targeting psychiatric hospitals -- including *Wyatt v. Stickney* in Alabama and *Halderman v. Pennhurst* in Pennsylvania -- established that institutionalized people had a right to treatment in the least restrictive environment.

- **The Insanity Defense Backlash (1982)**: John Hinckley Jr.'s acquittal by reason of insanity for the attempted assassination of President Reagan in 1981 provoked a national backlash against the insanity defense. Congress passed the Insanity Defense Reform Act of 1984, narrowing the federal insanity standard and shifting the burden of proof to the defendant. Many states followed with their own restrictive reforms. Four states -- Idaho, Kansas, Montana, and Utah -- effectively abolished the insanity defense. This backlash reflected and reinforced the public association of mental illness with violence, making it politically harder to advocate for treatment-based alternatives.

- **Competency to Stand Trial**: As more people with serious mental illness entered the criminal justice system, the number of competency evaluations began to grow. Annual competency evaluations increased from approximately 25,000 in 1990 to 60,000-70,000 by 2023 -- a nearly threefold increase (Gowensmith et al., 2023). The landmark Supreme Court decision in *Jackson v. Indiana* (1972) held that a defendant committed solely on the basis of incompetency to stand trial could not be held more than the reasonable period necessary to determine whether there is a substantial probability of attaining competency in the foreseeable future. Despite this ruling, defendants routinely spend years awaiting competency restoration.

### Emergence of Alternatives (1988-2010): Building the Reform Infrastructure

- **Memphis CIT Model (1988)**: Following the fatal police shooting of Joseph DeWayne Robinson, a 27-year-old man with a history of mental illness who was wielding a knife during a psychotic episode, the Memphis Police Department partnered with the University of Memphis, the Memphis chapter of NAMI, and local mental health providers to create the Crisis Intervention Team (CIT) model. The program's core elements included: a 40-hour training curriculum for volunteer officers covering mental health diagnosis, de-escalation techniques, and community resources; a designated drop-off center where officers could transport people in crisis directly to treatment; and a no-refusal policy ensuring immediate clinical assessment. The Memphis Model spread slowly at first -- by 2000, only about 400 departments had adopted it -- but growth accelerated in the 2000s and 2010s. By 2023, approximately 2,700 agencies and 130,000 officers had received CIT training. The model's emphasis on voluntary officer participation, community partnership, and clinical collaboration became the standard framework for law enforcement mental health response nationwide.

- **Drug Courts as Precursor (1989-1997)**: The first drug court was established in Miami-Dade County, Florida, in 1989 by Judge Herbert Klein. The drug court model -- which offered treatment as an alternative to incarceration for substance use-related offenses, with judicial monitoring and graduated sanctions -- demonstrated that therapeutic courts could reduce recidivism and save money. By the mid-1990s, hundreds of drug courts were operating, providing the conceptual and political foundation for mental health courts. The drug court movement proved that the criminal justice system could be used as a platform for treatment, not just punishment.

- **First Mental Health Court (1997)**: Broward County, Florida, established the nation's first mental health court, modeled on the drug court movement. Judge Ginger Lerner-Wren presided over the nation's first dedicated mental health docket, connecting defendants with mental illness to treatment as an alternative to traditional prosecution. The concept spread rapidly: by 2005, there were 125 mental health courts; by 2023, approximately 450. Lerner-Wren's model emphasized voluntary participation, clinical assessment, and judicial empathy -- principles that remain central to mental health court practice.

- **Sequential Intercept Model (2006)**: Researchers Mark Munetz and Patricia Griffin, working at the Munroe-Meyer Institute, published the Sequential Intercept Model (SIM) in *Psychiatric Services*. The model identified five intercept points where people with mental illness could be redirected from deeper criminal justice involvement: (0) community services, (1) law enforcement/emergency services, (2) initial detention/court hearings, (3) jails/courts, (4) reentry, and (5) community corrections. The SIM provided the first comprehensive conceptual framework for organizing the fragmented landscape of diversion programs and became the standard organizing tool for policy development, program planning, and cross-system mapping. SAMHSA adopted the SIM as its primary framework for criminal justice-mental health technical assistance, and by 2024, over 400 communities had conducted SIM mapping workshops to identify gaps and priorities in their local systems.

- **Mentally Ill Offender Treatment and Crime Reduction Act (2004)**: Congress passed the first federal legislation specifically addressing the criminalization of mental illness, authorizing grants for mental health courts, jail diversion programs, and reentry services. The Act was sponsored by Senators Mike DeWine (R-OH) and Ted Kennedy (D-MA), reflecting the bipartisan nature of the issue. It authorized $50 million annually, though actual appropriations consistently fell short. The Act was reauthorized in 2008 as the Mentally Ill Offender Treatment and Crime Reduction Reauthorization and Improvement Act, adding provisions for veterans' treatment courts and crisis intervention training. While groundbreaking in principle, the legislation's impact was limited by chronic underfunding -- annual appropriations averaged approximately $30 million, sufficient to support only a fraction of the programs that communities sought to establish.

- **CAHOOTS Program Founded (1989)**: The White Bird Clinic in Eugene, Oregon, launched the Crisis Assistance Helping Out On The Streets (CAHOOTS) program, dispatching two-person civilian teams -- a medic and a crisis worker -- to behavioral health calls. The program operated for decades in relative obscurity, handling approximately 24,000 calls annually. It would not gain national prominence until the 2020 police reform movement, but it demonstrated that civilian crisis response was safe and effective long before most cities considered the concept.

- **Olmstead v. L.C. (1999)**: The Supreme Court ruled in *Olmstead v. L.C.* that the unjustified segregation of people with disabilities in institutions constitutes discrimination under the Americans with Disabilities Act. The decision established a right to community-based services and became a foundational legal tool for advocates seeking to move people with mental illness out of both psychiatric hospitals and jails into community settings. However, enforcement has been inconsistent, and the decision did not create a right to community services where none existed.

- **Miami-Dade County: A Case Study in Comprehensive Reform (2000-2024)**: Miami-Dade County, under the leadership of Judge Steve Leifman, developed the most comprehensive county-level mental health-criminal justice reform program in the nation. Starting in 2000 with a mental health court, the program expanded to include CIT training for all patrol officers, pre-arrest diversion with a centralized crisis stabilization center, a forensic assertive community treatment team, and a reentry coordination program. By 2023, the program had reduced the county jail population by approximately 4,000, closed an entire jail facility, and saved an estimated $12 million annually. Leifman secured a $37 million county bond in 2017 to build a crisis stabilization and treatment campus as an alternative to jail, which opened in 2021. The Miami model demonstrated that comprehensive, sustained, locally led reform could achieve transformative results -- but it also showed that such reform depends on exceptional individual leadership and years of persistent advocacy.

- **Bexar County, Texas: The Restoration Center Model (2003-2024)**: Under former Sheriff Susan Pamerleau and Judge Nelson Wolff, Bexar County (San Antonio) created a 48-bed crisis center called the Restoration Center, providing a direct drop-off point for law enforcement officers as an alternative to jail or the emergency department. The center handles over 27,000 contacts annually, operates 24/7, and accepts anyone brought by police regardless of insurance status. Officers can complete a drop-off in approximately 15 minutes, compared to 4-8 hours waiting in an emergency department. The Restoration Center has been credited with reducing jail bookings of people with mental illness by 50% and avoiding an estimated $50 million annually in jail and emergency department costs (Bexar County, 2023). The model has been visited and studied by delegations from hundreds of jurisdictions seeking to replicate it.

- **Stepping Up Initiative (2015)**: The National Association of Counties, the Council of State Governments Justice Center, and the American Psychiatric Association launched the Stepping Up Initiative, challenging counties to reduce the number of people with mental illness in jails. Over 530 counties have passed Stepping Up resolutions as of 2024. The initiative's "Innovator County" designation recognized counties that moved beyond resolutions to measurable data-driven reductions.

### Contemporary Era (2010-Present): Crisis, Litigation, and Incremental Reform

- **Competency Restoration Litigation Wave**: Beginning with *Trueblood v. DSHS* in Washington State (2015), federal courts began ordering states to provide timely competency evaluations and restoration. The *Trueblood* case found that Washington State routinely held defendants found incompetent to stand trial in jails for months while they waited for state hospital beds, violating the Fourteenth Amendment's due process protections. The court imposed a 7-day evaluation deadline and significant contempt fines -- more than $70 million in sanctions by 2024. Similar cases followed rapidly: *Decker v. Oregon State Hospital* (Oregon), *State v. McElrath* (Tennessee), and *In re Mental Health Services* (California). By 2024, at least 23 states faced active litigation over competency restoration delays (Treatment Advocacy Center, 2024). The litigation wave demonstrated both the severity of the crisis and the limitations of judicial intervention -- courts can order compliance, but building the clinical infrastructure to achieve it requires sustained investment.

- **Affordable Care Act and Mental Health Parity (2010)**: The ACA designated mental health and substance use treatment as essential health benefits, and its Medicaid expansion extended coverage to millions of low-income adults with mental illness who previously had no insurance. The Act also strengthened enforcement of the 2008 Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act. However, the ACA's impact on criminal justice-involved populations was limited by three factors: (1) twelve states declined to expand Medicaid, leaving millions uninsured, (2) the IMD exclusion remained in place, limiting institutional treatment options, and (3) the "inmate exception" to Medicaid continued to bar coverage for incarcerated individuals. Despite these limitations, states that expanded Medicaid saw measurable improvements: a 2019 study in *Health Affairs* found that Medicaid expansion was associated with a 16% reduction in recidivism among people with mental illness in expansion states compared to non-expansion states.

- **Solitary Confinement Reform**: Growing evidence of the psychiatric harm of solitary confinement led to a wave of state-level restrictions. The UN Special Rapporteur on Torture classified prolonged solitary confinement (more than 15 consecutive days) as cruel, inhuman, or degrading treatment in 2011. By 2024, 18 states and D.C. had enacted laws or policies restricting solitary for people with mental illness (Vera Institute, 2024).

- **988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (2022)**: The activation of the 988 three-digit crisis number on July 16, 2022, represented the most significant federal investment in crisis response infrastructure in decades. The transition from the 10-digit National Suicide Prevention Lifeline to a three-digit number was modeled on the success of 911 in creating a universal emergency access point. The system handled 5.6 million contacts in its first 18 months (SAMHSA, 2024). However, the 988 system exposed the gap between crisis line capacity and community response capacity: most call centers could receive calls but could not dispatch mobile crisis teams because those teams did not yet exist in their service areas. Only 7 states had dedicated 988 funding by 2024 (NAMI, 2024). The 988 system represented an infrastructure investment that, like the Community Mental Health Act of 1963, laid the groundwork for transformation but required sustained funding and community-level implementation to deliver on its promise.

- **Medicaid Reentry Waiver (2023)**: CMS issued a final rule allowing Medicaid reimbursement for services provided to incarcerated people in the 90 days before release, potentially covering 500,000+ people annually (Kaiser Family Foundation, 2024). This represented the first federal policy explicitly addressing the Medicaid coverage gap at reentry.

- **CAHOOTS and Alternative Response Movement**: The CAHOOTS (Crisis Assistance Helping Out On The Streets) program in Eugene, Oregon, which had operated since 1989, became a national model after the 2020 protests over police violence. By 2023, over 20 cities had launched or were developing CAHOOTS-inspired civilian crisis response programs (White Bird Clinic, 2023).

- **COVID-19 Pandemic (2020-2023)**: The pandemic dramatically worsened the mental health crisis in correctional settings. Lockdowns inside jails and prisons -- with people confined to cells for 23-24 hours per day for months -- caused widespread psychiatric deterioration. Visitation was suspended, eliminating a key source of social support. Community mental health services were disrupted, reducing options for diversion. Mental health crisis calls to 911 increased by 35% between 2019 and 2023 (SAMHSA, 2024). The pandemic also accelerated the behavioral health workforce crisis, with community mental health centers reporting a 25-30% increase in staff turnover between 2020 and 2022 (National Council for Mental Wellbeing, 2023).

- **George Floyd Protests and Police Reform Movement (2020)**: The murder of George Floyd and the subsequent nationwide protests brought unprecedented attention to police encounters with people in mental health crisis. Calls to "defund the police" often specifically referenced the need for alternative mental health response systems. Cities including Denver, San Francisco, Olympia, Portland, and Austin launched or expanded alternative response programs in direct response to the movement. While the political intensity of the defund movement faded, the concrete alternative response programs it catalyzed continued to operate and expand.

- **Fentanyl and the Polysubstance Crisis (2019-present)**: The escalation of the fentanyl crisis dramatically increased the complexity of behavioral health presentations in jails and the criminal justice system. Co-occurring opioid use disorder among incarcerated people with mental illness rose from 38% in 2018 to 52% in 2023 (NCCHC, 2023). The need for integrated treatment of mental illness and substance use disorder strained already overwhelmed correctional mental health systems and complicated diversion planning, as few community programs offered simultaneous treatment for both conditions.

- **State-Level Innovation (2020-2024)**: Several states pursued significant reforms during this period:

    - **California**: Governor Gavin Newsom signed the CARE Act in 2023, creating a new civil court process to compel treatment for people with serious mental illness who meet specific criteria. The state also passed a $4.4 billion behavioral health infrastructure bond (Proposition 1, 2024) to fund treatment facilities and housing.
    - **Texas**: The legislature authorized outpatient competency restoration in 2023, allowing defendants found incompetent for nonviolent offenses to receive restoration services in the community rather than waiting for a state hospital bed.
    - **Colorado**: Enacted comprehensive crisis system legislation requiring all communities to have access to mobile crisis teams by 2025, funded through a behavioral health administration created in 2022.
    - **Oregon**: Established the nation's first statewide behavioral health resource network in 2023, creating a centralized system for matching people in crisis with available services across the state.
    - **Illinois**: Passed the Safety, Accountability, Fairness, and Equity (SAFE-T) Act in 2022, eliminating cash bail and requiring mental health assessments as part of pretrial services for all detained defendants.

| 1981 | Reagan block grant conversion; SSDI cuts | Reduced community mental health funding; pushed thousands into homelessness |
| 1982 | Hinckley insanity defense acquittal | Triggered backlash against insanity defense; reinforced mental illness-violence link |
| 1988 | Memphis CIT Model created | Established the dominant law enforcement mental health response model |
| 1989 | CAHOOTS program founded in Eugene, Oregon | Pioneered civilian crisis response; became national model 30 years later |
| 1990 | Teplin study documents mental illness in Cook County Jail | First rigorous epidemiological study of mental illness in jails |
| 1997 | First mental health court established (Broward County, FL) | Created judicial alternative to incarceration for defendants with mental illness |
| 2004 | Mentally Ill Offender Treatment and Crime Reduction Act | First federal legislation targeting criminalization of mental illness |

| 2015 | Stepping Up Initiative launched; *Trueblood v. DSHS* filed | County-level movement to reduce jail mental illness; beginning of competency litigation wave |

## The Role of Media and Public Perception

Media coverage of mental illness and criminal justice has been a powerful force shaping public opinion and policy throughout this history. Key inflection points include:

- **1946-1948**: Albert Deutsch's *The Shame of the States*, Mary Jane Ward's *The Snake Pit*, and Life magazine's Byberry photographs exposed asylum conditions and built public support for reform.
- **1972**: Geraldo Rivera's Willowbrook expose galvanized the deinstitutionalization movement and disability rights litigation.
- **1982-1999**: Cases like John Hinckley Jr., Larry Hogue, and Andrew Goldstein reinforced the public association of mental illness with violence, driving punitive legislation (Insanity Defense Reform Act, Kendra's Law).
- **2014-2015**: The *New York Times* Rikers Island series and the *Tampa Bay Times* Pulitzer Prize-winning investigation "Insane. Invisible. In Danger." brought sustained attention to the crisis of mental illness in jails.
- **2019-2020**: The HBO documentary *Ernie & Joe: Crisis Cops* humanized CIT officers and crisis response, while the George Floyd protests created a national conversation about alternatives to police response.
- **2020-present**: The CAHOOTS model received extensive national media coverage, shifting the narrative from "should we divert people from jail?" to "how do we build alternative response systems?" Popular podcasts, including *Ear Hustle* (produced inside San Quentin) and *Serial* (Season 3, covering the Cleveland criminal justice system), brought the voices of incarcerated people with mental illness to broad audiences.

Research consistently shows that media coverage of mental illness disproportionately emphasizes violence: a 2019 Johns Hopkins analysis found that 55% of news stories about mental illness mentioned violence, despite people with mental illness committing only 3-5% of violent acts. This framing directly shapes public attitudes toward criminalization versus treatment.

## Lessons from History

### Pattern 1: Institutional Transfer Without Adequate Alternatives

The most persistent pattern in this history is the transfer of responsibility for people with mental illness from one institution to another without building the community infrastructure needed to support them. Dorothea Dix moved people from jails to asylums. Deinstitutionalization moved them from asylums to communities. But when communities lacked resources, people ended up back in jails -- completing a 175-year circle. Each transfer was motivated by genuine reform impulses, but each was implemented without adequate funding for the receiving system. The lesson is clear: closing institutions without building alternatives does not reform care; it relocates suffering.

### Pattern 2: Reform Through Crisis and Litigation

Virtually every major reform in this area has been driven by crisis, tragedy, or litigation rather than proactive policy. The CIT model emerged from a fatal police shooting. Competency restoration reform is being driven by federal lawsuits, not legislative initiative. Solitary confinement restrictions have been imposed by courts, not voluntarily adopted. This pattern suggests that policy change in this area requires sustained external pressure and that reforms achieved through litigation alone may lack the political durability and funding commitments needed for long-term success.

### Pattern 3: The Gap Between Policy Innovation and Scale

The United States has developed numerous effective models for addressing mental illness in the criminal justice system -- CIT, mental health courts, CAHOOTS, the Sequential Intercept Model, forensic assertive community treatment. The problem has never been a lack of evidence-based programs; it has been a failure to fund and scale them. Mental health courts exist in only 7% of U.S. counties. CIT training reaches only 15% of law enforcement agencies. The gap between innovation and implementation has been the defining failure of reform efforts.

### Pattern 4: Racial Injustice as a Persistent Undercurrent

The history of mental illness and criminal justice in the United States is inseparable from the history of racial injustice. State hospitals were segregated; Black patients received worse care and were more likely to be subjected to forced sterilization. Deinstitutionalization disproportionately affected Black communities, where the loss of institutional services was not offset by community alternatives. The War on Drugs and broken-windows policing disproportionately targeted communities of color, sweeping Black and Hispanic people with mental illness into the criminal justice system at rates far exceeding those of white Americans. Contemporary diversion programs -- mental health courts, CIT, co-responder models -- have been shown to benefit white defendants at higher rates than defendants of color (Redlich et al., 2022). Any historical account that ignores race misses a fundamental dimension of how and why the current crisis was created.

### Pattern 5: The International Contrast

The United States is an outlier among wealthy democracies in its reliance on the criminal justice system to manage mental illness. Most European countries maintained robust community mental health systems during and after deinstitutionalization. Italy closed all psychiatric hospitals under the Basaglia Law of 1978 but simultaneously built a comprehensive network of community mental health centers, residential facilities, and crisis services. The United Kingdom created the National Health Service mental health trusts, ensuring universal access to psychiatric care. Scandinavian countries never experienced mass criminalization of mental illness because their social welfare systems provided housing, income support, and treatment as entitlements. The American experience is distinctive not because deinstitutionalization occurred -- it occurred in every Western country -- but because the United States uniquely failed to build the community infrastructure to replace institutional care while simultaneously embarking on mass incarceration. The result is a system unlike any other in the developed world: one that spends more per capita on corrections than most nations spend on mental health, while achieving worse outcomes for people with mental illness.

### Pattern 6: The Political Economy of Institutional Inertia

Each era has created economic interests invested in the status quo. State hospital staff unions resisted deinstitutionalization. Private prison companies and correctional healthcare corporations benefit from high incarceration rates. Bail bond companies profit from the pretrial detention of people who cannot afford to post bond, disproportionately people with mental illness. Forensic psychiatry providers generate revenue from competency evaluations. These economic interests do not typically oppose mental health reform explicitly, but they fund campaigns and lobby for policies that maintain the conditions under which criminalization persists. Breaking this pattern requires understanding reform not only as a clinical or legal challenge but as a political economy challenge.

### Pattern 7: The Persistence of Underfunding

Perhaps the most consistent lesson across 175 years of history is that every major reform initiative in this space has been undermined by inadequate funding. Dorothea Dix's asylums became overcrowded because states did not invest in adequate staffing. Kennedy's community mental health centers were never built to scale because Congress did not sustain operating funding. The Mentally Ill Offender Treatment and Crime Reduction Act was authorized at $50 million but appropriated at $30 million. The 988 Lifeline launched without sustained state funding. The Stepping Up Initiative generated resolutions without resources. The pattern is clear: American policymakers consistently authorize programs to address the criminalization of mental illness and then fail to fund them at the level required for meaningful impact. The result is a landscape littered with promising pilots that never scale, demonstration projects that end when grants expire, and reform frameworks that exist on paper but not in practice.

## Looking Forward: Unresolved Historical Questions

Several historical questions remain unresolved and continue to shape contemporary policy debates:

1. **Was deinstitutionalization a mistake, or just an incomplete reform?** Defenders argue that closing state hospitals advanced civil liberties and that the failure was in not building community alternatives -- not in the principle of community care itself. Critics argue that some people with severe mental illness need long-term structured care that community settings cannot provide, and that the elimination of psychiatric beds has left no safe option between outpatient treatment and incarceration.

2. **Can the criminal justice system be reformed from within, or does it require external transformation?** Mental health courts and CIT programs attempt to reform how the criminal justice system handles mental illness. Alternative response models like CAHOOTS attempt to remove mental health from the criminal justice system entirely. The historical record suggests that internal reforms (mental health courts, CIT) can improve outcomes for those they reach but have never scaled sufficiently to transform the system, while external alternatives (crisis response, community treatment) have the potential for systemic change but require infrastructure that does not yet exist at scale.

3. **What is the proper role of coercion in mental health treatment?** From the asylum era to assisted outpatient treatment, the use of legal authority to compel treatment has been the most contested issue in this field. The historical pendulum has swung from virtually unlimited coercive authority (civil commitment with minimal procedural protections) to constitutional restrictions that critics argue went too far (making involuntary treatment nearly impossible even for people in acute psychosis). Finding a sustainable balance between individual liberty and the duty to protect people who cannot protect themselves remains the central ethical challenge.
