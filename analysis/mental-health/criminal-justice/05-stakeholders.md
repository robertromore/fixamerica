# Criminal Justice and Mental Health: Stakeholders

The intersection of mental illness and criminal justice is shaped by the interests and actions of a wide range of stakeholders -- from the individuals directly affected to the government agencies, professional organizations, and advocacy groups that influence policy. Understanding who has power, who is affected, and where interests align or conflict is essential to building viable reform coalitions.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| People with mental illness in jails and prisons | Inadequate treatment, worsened conditions, trauma, extended detention | ~717,000 on any given day (BJS, 2023) | Access to treatment, humane conditions, diversion from incarceration |
| People with SMI booked into jails annually | Cycle of arrest, detention, release without treatment, re-arrest | ~2 million annually (Treatment Advocacy Center, 2023) | Community-based treatment, crisis alternatives to police, stable housing |
| Families of incarcerated people with mental illness | Emotional distress, financial burden, navigation of fragmented systems | ~5-8 million family members (NAMI estimate, 2023) | Access to information, treatment for loved ones, system navigation support |
| People found incompetent to stand trial (IST) | Extended detention awaiting competency restoration, forced medication | ~30,000-35,000 annually (Treatment Advocacy Center, 2023) | Timely evaluation, community-based restoration, charge dismissal for minor offenses |
| People with mental illness in solitary confinement | Psychiatric deterioration, self-harm, suicide risk | ~23,000-29,000 on any given day (BJS, 2021) | Elimination of solitary for people with SMI, access to programming and treatment |

### Secondary Stakeholders

- **Communities of color**: African Americans are 2.4 times more likely to be arrested than white Americans for the same offenses and are less likely to be diverted to mental health services (Vera Institute, 2023). The intersection of racial disparities in policing and mental health creates compounded disadvantage. Hispanic and Latino communities face additional barriers, including immigration status concerns that deter help-seeking, language access limitations, and lower rates of mental health insurance coverage.
- **People experiencing homelessness**: An estimated 30% of people with SMI in jails were homeless in the year before arrest (Treatment Advocacy Center, 2023). Criminalization of homelessness is a primary pathway to justice involvement for people with mental illness. An estimated 250,000 people experiencing homelessness on any given night have serious mental illness (HUD, 2023).
- **Children of incarcerated parents with mental illness**: An estimated 2.7 million children have a parent in jail or prison (Annie E. Casey Foundation, 2022). Parental mental illness combined with incarceration creates compounded adverse childhood experiences. Children of incarcerated parents are 6 times more likely to be incarcerated themselves (National Institute of Justice, 2022).
- **Taxpayers**: The current system costs approximately $15 billion annually in correctional mental health care alone (Vera Institute, 2023), in addition to the broader economic costs of lost productivity, emergency services, and recidivism. The total economic burden of the criminalization of mental illness -- including law enforcement time, court costs, correctional costs, emergency department visits, and lost productivity -- is estimated at $30-50 billion annually (RAND Corporation, 2022).
- **Veterans**: An estimated 55% of incarcerated veterans report mental health conditions, many stemming from combat-related PTSD and traumatic brain injury (Bureau of Justice Statistics, 2023). Veterans Treatment Courts, modeled on mental health courts, serve approximately 600 jurisdictions but reach only a fraction of justice-involved veterans.
- **Emergency department staff and first responders**: Nurses, physicians, and paramedics who manage psychiatric emergencies in emergency departments and in the field experience high rates of burnout, assault, and secondary trauma. The American College of Emergency Physicians reported in 2023 that psychiatric boarding is the number one cause of emergency department crowding and staff burnout.
- **Correctional officers**: Officers working in jails and prisons with high concentrations of people with mental illness face elevated rates of PTSD, substance use, and suicide. A 2022 study in the *Journal of Criminal Justice* found that correctional officers in facilities with high mental illness prevalence had 3 times the rate of PTSD symptoms compared to officers in facilities with lower prevalence.

## Who Has Power

### Decision Makers

| Actor | Role | Authority | Resources | Influence Level |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| State legislatures | Set criminal codes, authorize diversion programs, appropriate mental health funding | Legislative authority over both criminal justice and health budgets | Varies by state; total state MH spending ~$44 billion (2023) | High |
| County commissions/boards | Fund jails, local mental health services; implement Stepping Up resolutions | Local budget authority; jail oversight | County budgets; ~$25 billion on jails nationally (2023) | High |
| Governors | Appoint state MH directors, set executive priorities, issue executive orders | Executive authority; budget proposals | State budget authority; appointment power | High |
| District attorneys | Decide whether to prosecute, divert, or dismiss cases involving mental illness | Prosecutorial discretion | Office budgets; charging authority | High |
| Judges | Preside over mental health courts; order competency evaluations; set bail | Judicial authority; sentencing power | Court budgets; contempt authority | High |
| SAMHSA (federal) | Administers federal mental health block grants and programs | Grant-making; guidance; data collection | $9.5 billion budget (FY2024) | Medium-High |
| CMS (federal) | Sets Medicaid policy including IMD exclusion and reentry waivers | Regulatory authority over Medicaid | Oversees $800+ billion in Medicaid spending | High |
| Bureau of Justice Assistance (DOJ) | Administers justice-related mental health grants | Grant-making; technical assistance | ~$150 million in MH-related grants (FY2024) | Medium |

### Key Organizations and Institutions

- **National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI)**: The largest grassroots mental health advocacy organization, with 600+ state and local affiliates. NAMI has been a leading voice for CIT training, mental health court expansion, and the 988 Lifeline. Influence is primarily through public advocacy, grassroots organizing, and legislative testimony.

- **Treatment Advocacy Center (TAC)**: Focused specifically on policy barriers to treatment for people with serious mental illness. TAC produces widely cited data on psychiatric bed capacity, criminalization, and the consequences of untreated mental illness. Has advocated for assisted outpatient treatment (AOT) laws, which are controversial among civil liberties organizations.

- **Council of State Governments (CSG) Justice Center**: The primary technical assistance provider for the Stepping Up Initiative and state-level criminal justice-mental health reform. CSG provides data, research, and policy guidance to states and counties implementing diversion programs. Influence is through expert authority and direct engagement with policymakers.

- **National Association of Counties (NACo)**: Co-sponsor of the Stepping Up Initiative. NACo represents 3,069 county governments that operate most of the nation's 3,000+ jails. NACo has been influential in framing jail mental health as a county governance issue, not just a clinical one.

- **National Commission on Correctional Health Care (NCCHC)**: Sets voluntary accreditation standards for health care in jails and prisons, including mental health care. Only ~8% of jails and ~22% of state prisons meet NCCHC accreditation standards (NCCHC, 2023). Influence is through standard-setting and accreditation.

- **American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU)**: Active in litigation challenging solitary confinement of people with mental illness, competency restoration delays, and inadequate correctional mental health care. The ACLU's National Prison Project has been involved in major class-action lawsuits in multiple states.

- **Mental Health America (MHA)**: The oldest mental health advocacy organization in the country (founded 1909), MHA focuses on public education, screening tools, and advocacy. MHA's online screening program has reached over 10 million people since 2014, providing a gateway to identification of mental health conditions.

- **Disability Rights organizations (state Protection and Advocacy agencies)**: Federally mandated in every state, these agencies have legal authority to investigate conditions in jails and prisons and advocate for people with mental illness in custody. They have been plaintiffs or intervenors in major litigation on solitary confinement, competency restoration, and correctional mental health care.

- **National Sheriffs' Association (NSA) and Major County Sheriffs of America (MCSA)**: These organizations represent the sheriffs who operate most U.S. jails. Both have taken increasingly progressive positions on mental health in recent years. The MCSA's 2022 position paper acknowledged that "jails have become the nation's largest psychiatric facilities" and called for expanded community treatment alternatives.

### Influencers

- **CIT International**: The national organization promoting the Memphis Model CIT program. Provides training standards, certification, and technical assistance to the ~2,700 CIT-trained agencies. Influence is through training infrastructure and program fidelity standards.

- **Vera Institute of Justice**: Produces research on incarceration costs, jail conditions, and diversion alternatives. The Vera Institute's cost-of-incarceration data is widely used to argue for diversion. Influence is through research credibility and policy analysis.

- **National Sheriffs' Association (NSA)**: Represents the sheriffs who operate most U.S. jails. The NSA has increasingly acknowledged the burden of mental illness on jails and has supported CIT training and jail diversion, though positions vary among individual sheriffs.

- **Police Executive Research Forum (PERF)**: Conducts research on policing and mental health, including response time data and alternative response models. PERF studies are frequently cited by reformers and policymakers.

- **Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law**: A leading legal advocacy organization focused on the rights of people with mental disabilities. The Bazelon Center has been involved in major litigation on community integration (*Olmstead*), Medicaid access, and correctional mental health care.

## Stakeholder Interests and Alignment

### Aligned Interests (Potential Coalition Partners)

| Coalition | Shared Interest | Strength |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| NAMI, TAC, families, mental health providers | Treatment access and diversion from incarceration | Strong |
| Sheriffs, NACo, county commissioners | Reducing jail populations and costs associated with mental illness | Moderate-Strong |
| ACLU, Bazelon Center, disability rights organizations | Civil rights protections, ending solitary confinement, due process for IST defendants | Strong |
| CIT International, PERF, progressive police chiefs | Alternative crisis response models that improve officer safety and outcomes | Moderate |
| Medicaid advocates, public health organizations, CMS | Expanding Medicaid coverage during and after incarceration | Strong |
| Fiscal conservatives, libertarians, progressive reformers | Reducing incarceration costs through more effective community-based alternatives | Moderate |

### Conflicting Interests

- **Treatment advocacy vs. civil liberties**: The Treatment Advocacy Center supports expanded assisted outpatient treatment (mandatory community treatment) and broader involuntary commitment criteria. The ACLU and Bazelon Center argue these approaches violate individual autonomy and disproportionately affect people of color. This is the most divisive intra-reform conflict. California's CARE Act (2023), which created a new civil court process to compel treatment, became a flashpoint for this debate, with disability rights organizations filing legal challenges while treatment advocates praised it as a lifesaving intervention.

- **Law enforcement unions vs. alternative response advocates**: Police unions generally oppose models like CAHOOTS that dispatch civilian teams to mental health calls, arguing that officer safety requires police presence. The Fraternal Order of Police has opposed legislation that would redirect 911 calls from police to civilian teams, arguing this creates "zones of unaccountability." Alternative response advocates argue police presence escalates situations and that civilian models have strong safety records, citing CAHOOTS' 30+ years of safe operations with zero team member deaths.

- **District attorneys vs. diversion advocates**: Many prosecutors support mental health courts but oppose broad pretrial diversion that removes prosecutorial leverage. The National District Attorneys Association has expressed concern that mandatory diversion referrals "undermine prosecutorial discretion," which it considers a cornerstone of the adversarial system. Defense attorneys and civil liberties organizations argue that prosecutorial control over diversion eligibility perpetuates disparities, pointing to research showing that prosecutors refer white defendants to diversion at higher rates than Black defendants with comparable charges.

- **State hospital staff unions vs. community care advocates**: Unions representing state hospital workers (AFSCME, SEIU in some states) have historically opposed hospital closures and the shift to community-based care, which threatens union jobs. In Oregon, AFSCME Local 3295 lobbied against community competency restoration legislation in 2023, arguing that patient safety required institutional settings. Community mental health advocates argue that institutional care is more expensive and less effective, and that union opposition reflects economic self-interest rather than clinical judgment.

- **Rural vs. urban priorities**: Rural law enforcement agencies and mental health providers often have different reform priorities than urban ones. Rural areas lack the population density to sustain specialized programs like mental health courts, CIT units, or crisis stabilization centers. Rural stakeholders tend to prioritize telehealth, regional service models, and law enforcement training, while urban stakeholders focus on alternative response models, facility-based diversion, and court reform. National policy frameworks that assume urban program models can be applied universally often fail in rural contexts.

- **Emergency departments vs. crisis centers**: Hospital systems have complex and sometimes conflicting interests. Emergency departments are overburdened by psychiatric boarding, creating incentive to support crisis stabilization centers that could divert patients. However, crisis centers that divert patients also divert revenue, and some hospital systems have opposed crisis center development in their service areas as competitive threats. The Emergency Medical Treatment and Active Labor Act (EMTALA) requires emergency departments to screen and stabilize all patients, including psychiatric patients, but provides no additional funding for psychiatric care -- creating an unfunded mandate that hospitals have lobbied to reform.

### Key Coalition Opportunities

The most promising reform coalitions combine unexpected allies:

1. **Fiscal conservative + progressive treatment advocate**: Shared interest in reducing incarceration costs. The Stepping Up Initiative demonstrates this coalition, pairing the National Association of Counties (conservative-leaning elected officials) with mental health advocates. The message "treatment saves money" bridges ideological divides.

2. **Sheriff + family advocate + peer specialist**: Sheriffs who are vocal about the burden of mental illness on jails provide political cover for reform. Pairing sheriff voices with family stories and peer testimony creates a powerful advocacy trio that appeals to both conservative and progressive audiences.

3. **Emergency department + crisis service provider + insurer**: All three have financial incentive to reduce psychiatric boarding. Emergency departments want to free bed capacity. Crisis providers want referrals. Insurers want to reduce ER costs. This business-case coalition can operate independently of political ideology.

4. **Public defender + mental health clinician + researcher**: This coalition brings legal expertise, clinical authority, and evidence together to advocate for specific reforms like screening requirements, diversion eligibility expansion, and competency restoration reform.

5. **Veterans organizations + mental health advocates**: Veterans with PTSD and TBI are a sympathetic population that bridges political divides. Veterans Treatment Courts, supported by groups like the National Alliance on Mental Illness and the Veterans of Foreign Wars, have achieved bipartisan support in nearly every state. Success with veterans' programs creates political space for expanding similar models to the broader population.

### Stakeholder Engagement Timeline

Effective reform strategies engage stakeholders in a deliberate sequence:

1. **Build law enforcement buy-in first**: Sheriffs and police chiefs who publicly support reform provide essential political cover. Without law enforcement credibility, reform proposals are vulnerable to "soft on crime" attacks.
2. **Engage fiscal conservatives with cost data**: Before introducing legislation, present the cost comparison between incarceration ($40,000-$60,000/year) and community treatment ($8,000-$15,000/year) to fiscal hawks on appropriations committees.
3. **Involve families and people with lived experience**: Personal testimony from families and peers humanizes the issue and counters the "dangerous mentally ill person" narrative. Advocacy organizations like NAMI can mobilize these voices.
4. **Negotiate with prosecutors and judges early**: Rather than bypassing prosecutors, involve them in program design. Mental health courts and diversion programs that prosecutors help design are more politically durable than those imposed over their objection.
5. **Address correctional officer and union concerns directly**: Acknowledge that working with people with untreated mental illness is dangerous and stressful. Frame reform as improving officer safety and working conditions, not just reducing incarceration.

### The Correctional-Industrial Triangle

The criminal justice response to mental illness is sustained by a political economy in which multiple actors benefit financially from the status quo:

```text
Private Corrections Industry ←→ Political Campaign Contributions
         ↓                                    ↓
    Contract Revenue ←→ Legislature → Criminal Justice Budgets
         ↓                                    ↓
    Jail/Prison Census ←→ Arrests ← Law Enforcement Budgets
```

Private prison operators (CoreCivic, GEO Group), correctional healthcare companies (Wellpath, YesCare), and electronic monitoring firms benefit from high incarceration rates. These companies spent $13 million on lobbying and $8 million on campaign contributions in the 2022 election cycle (OpenSecrets, 2023). While these interests do not explicitly oppose mental health diversion, their business models depend on maintaining a large incarcerated population.

**Key Private Sector Stakeholders in Detail**:

| Company | Revenue (2023) | Role | Political Activity | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| CoreCivic | $1.9 billion | Operates 44 facilities; houses ~44,000 inmates | $5.2M lobbying (2022) | OpenSecrets, 2023 |
| GEO Group | $2.4 billion | Operates 93 facilities globally; ~73,000 beds | $4.8M lobbying (2022) | OpenSecrets, 2023 |
| Wellpath | $2.8 billion | Correctional healthcare in 350+ facilities | Contracts with 38 states | Company filings, 2023 |
| YesCare (formerly Corizon) | $1.5 billion | Correctional healthcare; largest private provider | Contracts in 31 states | Company filings, 2023 |
| Attenti/3M (electronic monitoring) | $500M+ | Ankle monitors for 150,000+ people | Growing market | Industry reports, 2023 |

The electronic monitoring industry represents a growing stakeholder with ambiguous reform implications. As diversion programs expand, more people with mental illness are placed on electronic monitoring as a condition of community supervision. This shifts revenue from jail operators to monitoring companies but does not necessarily improve treatment outcomes -- and monitoring itself can become a barrier to treatment engagement, employment, and housing stability.

**State-Level Stakeholder Dynamics: Case Examples**

- **Texas**: The Texas Criminal Justice Coalition and the conservative Texas Public Policy Foundation have formed an unusual alliance, both arguing that community treatment is more cost-effective than incarceration. This bipartisan coalition has successfully advocated for outpatient competency restoration (2023) and expanded mental health court funding. The Texas Sheriff's Association has been a supportive partner, with multiple sheriffs publicly advocating for diversion.

- **California**: Stakeholder dynamics in California are among the most complex in the nation. The California District Attorneys Association opposed the CARE Act's initial formulation but negotiated amendments that preserved prosecutorial discretion. AFSCME opposed community competency restoration. Disability Rights California filed suit challenging involuntary treatment provisions. The California Hospital Association supported crisis center development as a way to reduce emergency department boarding. Governor Newsom navigated these competing interests by framing reform as both a compassionate and a fiscal imperative.

- **New York**: The closure of Rikers Island -- planned for 2027 -- has created intense stakeholder conflict. The Correctional Officers' Benevolent Association opposes closure, arguing it will reduce jobs. The Legal Aid Society and public defender offices support closure but demand that alternative treatment and housing be in place first. NAMI-NYC and the National Alliance on Mental Illness have advocated for crisis centers and community treatment to absorb the population currently held at Rikers.

- **Florida**: Florida's Baker Act, which allows involuntary psychiatric examination for up to 72 hours, creates unique stakeholder dynamics. Law enforcement agencies use the Baker Act extensively (over 200,000 involuntary examinations annually), but receiving facilities often lack capacity, resulting in emergency department boarding or, increasingly, jail as the alternative. Judge Steve Leifman's Miami-Dade program has become a national model, but it depends on exceptional individual leadership and philanthropic support that other Florida counties have not replicated.

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on mental health and criminal justice policy:

- **Lobbying**: Private corrections companies and their trade association (the American Legislative Exchange Council has promoted model legislation favorable to incarceration). Conversely, NAMI and the National Council for Mental Wellbeing lobby for increased mental health funding and diversion programs.

- **Campaign Contributions**: District attorney and sheriff races are influenced by contributions from bail bond companies, police unions, and law-and-order political action committees. Mental health advocacy organizations have historically spent less on electoral politics.

- **Litigation**: The ACLU, Bazelon Center, Disability Rights organizations, and legal aid societies use litigation to enforce constitutional standards in jails and prisons, compel competency restoration within reasonable timeframes, and challenge solitary confinement practices. Litigation has been the single most effective mechanism for forcing systemic change.

- **Public Advocacy**: NAMI's grassroots network, the 988 Lifeline campaign, and media coverage of jail deaths have been powerful tools for building public support for reform. The National Sheriffs' Association's public statements about jail mental health burden have been particularly influential in building bipartisan support.

- **Expertise and Data**: Organizations like the CSG Justice Center, Treatment Advocacy Center, and Vera Institute of Justice shape policy through research, data collection, and technical assistance. Their reports are frequently cited in legislation and executive orders.

- **Media and Public Narrative**: Investigative journalism -- including the *New York Times* Rikers Island series, the *Tampa Bay Times* "Insane. Invisible. In Danger." investigation, and sustained coverage by the *Marshall Project* -- has been a powerful tool for building public support for reform. These stories humanize the crisis and provide the emotional foundation that data alone cannot supply. Conversely, sensationalized coverage of violence by people with mental illness can set reform efforts back by years.

- **Professional Association Standards**: The American Psychiatric Association, American Psychological Association, and National Commission on Correctional Health Care establish standards of care that become benchmarks in litigation and accreditation. When these organizations issue position statements -- such as the APA's 2012 position opposing solitary confinement for people with SMI -- they provide authoritative support for reform.

## Federal Agency Stakeholder Roles

The fragmented federal role in mental health and criminal justice reform involves multiple agencies with overlapping but uncoordinated responsibilities:

| Agency | Role | Annual MH-CJ Budget | Key Programs | Influence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| SAMHSA | Behavioral health block grants, 988, crisis services | $9.5 billion (total) | Community MH Block Grant, 988, GAINS Center | High -- primary funder |
| Bureau of Justice Assistance (DOJ) | Justice system grants, mental health courts | ~$150M MH-related | JAG, JMHCP, Second Chance Act | Medium-High |
| CMS | Medicaid policy, IMD exclusion, reentry waivers | Oversees $800B+ Medicaid | Medicaid Reentry waiver | Very High -- policy setter |
| HRSA | Workforce development, health professional shortage areas | ~$500M behavioral health | NHSC, behavioral health workforce | Medium |
| NIMH | Research funding | $2.3 billion | Research grants | Low-Medium (indirect) |
| VA | Veterans' mental health and justice programs | $15.6B for MH (2024) | Veterans Treatment Courts, HUD-VASH | Medium -- veteran-specific |

The lack of a single coordinating body across these agencies is itself a stakeholder issue. Each agency optimizes for its own mission, and no agency has both the authority and the resources to address the full continuum from community prevention to reentry.

## Emerging Stakeholders

### Peer Support Movement

People with lived experience of mental illness and justice involvement are increasingly organized as a stakeholder group. Certified peer specialists -- individuals in recovery who provide support, mentoring, and system navigation for others -- are now employed in diversion programs, mental health courts, crisis centers, and reentry programs in all 50 states. The peer movement brings a perspective that clinical professionals and policymakers cannot replicate and has been shown to improve engagement and outcomes. A 2022 SAMHSA meta-analysis found that programs incorporating peer specialists achieved 22% lower recidivism and 18% fewer crisis service contacts.

### Technology Companies

Technology companies are emerging as stakeholders through several mechanisms: (1) crisis technology platforms that power 988 call centers and dispatch systems, (2) electronic health record companies that manage clinical data in correctional settings, (3) telehealth providers that offer psychiatric services via video to jails and rural communities, and (4) artificial intelligence companies developing risk assessment tools used in pretrial and sentencing decisions. These companies shape the infrastructure of the system, often with minimal public oversight. The use of algorithmic risk assessment tools in particular raises concerns about bias against people with mental illness.

### Philanthropic Funders

Major philanthropic organizations have become significant stakeholders, providing funding for pilot programs, research, and advocacy that government budgets do not cover:

| Funder | Focus Area | Investment |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| MacArthur Foundation | Safety and Justice Challenge (jail reduction) | $246 million (2015-2024) |
| Arnold Ventures | Pretrial justice reform, bail reform | $100+ million |
| Pew Charitable Trusts | State criminal justice reform | Ongoing technical assistance |
| Open Society Foundations | Criminal justice reform, bail reform | $50+ million |
| Laura and John Arnold Foundation | Risk assessment tools, pretrial services | $50+ million |

These funders have significantly influenced the direction of reform, but their investments also raise questions about democratic accountability when private philanthropy shapes public policy. Programs funded by time-limited grants often lack sustainability when philanthropic priorities shift.

### Local Government Officials Beyond Law Enforcement

County commissioners, city council members, mayors, and public health directors are increasingly central stakeholders as the financial burden of incarcerating people with mental illness falls primarily on local government budgets. The National Association of Counties' co-sponsorship of the Stepping Up Initiative reflects the growing recognition among local elected officials that jail mental health is fundamentally a governance problem, not just a clinical one. Local public health departments, which administer community mental health services in many jurisdictions, serve as a crucial bridge between health and justice systems.
