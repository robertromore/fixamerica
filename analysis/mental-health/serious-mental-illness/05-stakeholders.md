# Serious Mental Illness: Stakeholders

The landscape of serious mental illness policy is shaped by the interactions of people directly affected by SMI, their families, healthcare providers, government agencies, advocacy organizations, law enforcement, and the insurance industry. Each group has distinct interests, resources, and influence, and their alignment or conflict determines the pace and direction of reform.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Adults with SMI | Direct experience of illness, treatment access, housing, employment, civil liberties | 14.1 million (SAMHSA, 2023) | Access to effective treatment, housing stability, protection from coercion, employment opportunity |
| Family caregivers of people with SMI | Emotional, financial, and physical burden of caregiving; navigating fragmented systems | 8.4 million (NAMI, 2023) | Information access, respite services, legal authority to participate in care, crisis intervention |
| People with SMI in jails/prisons | Inadequate psychiatric treatment in correctional settings, longer sentences | ~383,000 on any given day (TAC, 2022) | Treatment in lieu of incarceration, diversion programs, transition services upon release |
| People with SMI experiencing homelessness | Exposure, victimization, untreated illness, barriers to housing | ~43,000-57,000 chronically homeless (HUD, 2024) | Permanent supportive housing, low-barrier shelter, outreach services |
| People with first-episode psychosis | Critical window for early intervention; average 74-week delay to treatment | ~100,000 new cases/year (NIMH, 2023) | Rapid access to coordinated specialty care, education/employment support |

### Secondary Stakeholders

- **Children of parents with SMI**: Approximately 2.7 million children in the U.S. have a parent with SMI, facing increased risks of neglect, trauma, and developing mental illness themselves (SAMHSA, 2023). These children are 2-3 times more likely to develop a mental health condition compared to children of parents without mental illness (Rasic et al., *Canadian Journal of Psychiatry*, 2014). Child welfare systems often intervene in families affected by SMI, but the interventions frequently address child safety without connecting the parent to psychiatric treatment, leaving the underlying cause unaddressed.
- **Emergency department staff**: Emergency department physicians and nurses bear the brunt of the psychiatric bed shortage. Psychiatric patients boarded in EDs for 24-48 hours consume staff time, reduce bed availability for other patients, and contribute to burnout. The American College of Emergency Physicians (ACEP) has declared psychiatric boarding a public health emergency, with 90% of emergency physicians reporting that they regularly board psychiatric patients and 42% reporting acts of violence by psychiatric patients in their EDs (ACEP, 2023).
- **Employers**: People with SMI who receive effective treatment can work, but employers face accommodation requirements and may harbor stigma. Supported employment programs using the Individual Placement and Support (IPS) model achieve competitive employment rates of 40-60% for people with SMI (Bond et al., 2022). The cost to employers of untreated SMI among their workforce -- including disability claims, absenteeism, presenteeism, and health insurance costs -- is estimated at $193 billion annually in lost earnings (NAMI, 2023).
- **Neighbors and communities**: Communities hosting supportive housing or treatment facilities experience NIMBYism, but research consistently shows no increase in crime or property value decline (Galster et al., 2022). A 2022 study of 154 supportive housing sites found no statistically significant effect on neighboring property values and a modest decrease in police calls within a 500-foot radius compared to before the housing was built (Furman Center, NYU, 2022).
- **Taxpayers**: The economic cost of untreated SMI -- including emergency services, incarceration, homelessness services, and lost productivity -- is estimated at $317 billion annually (NAMI, 2023). Of this, approximately $15 billion is attributable to criminal justice costs, $10 billion to emergency department and acute hospitalization, $5 billion to homelessness services, and the remainder to lost productivity and disability payments.
- **Landlords and housing providers**: Private landlords often refuse to rent to people with SMI due to stigma, credit history, or prior evictions. Housing discrimination based on disability status is illegal under the Fair Housing Act, but enforcement is difficult when landlords use facially neutral criteria (credit scores, rental history) that have a disparate impact on people with SMI.
- **Veterans with SMI**: An estimated 700,000 veterans live with SMI, including schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, and severe PTSD (VA, 2023). The VA operates its own parallel mental health system, which is in some ways more integrated than civilian services but suffers from its own access challenges, particularly in rural areas. Veterans with SMI who receive a less-than-honorable discharge -- often directly attributable to symptoms of their illness -- may be barred from VA services entirely.

## Who Has Power

### Decision Makers

| Actor | Role | Authority | Resources | Influence Level |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Congress | Federal legislation, appropriations | Mental health funding, Medicaid rules, parity enforcement | Annual budget authority | High |
| SAMHSA | Federal mental health program administration | Block grant distribution, CCBHC certification, 988 oversight | $7.5 billion discretionary (FY2024) | High |
| CMS (Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services) | Medicaid/Medicare policy | IMD exclusion waivers, parity enforcement, reimbursement rates | ~$116 billion in behavioral health spending | High |
| State Mental Health Authorities | State psychiatric hospital operation, community program oversight | State hospital capacity, Medicaid plan design, civil commitment implementation | $44.5 billion total state spending (FY2022) | High |
| State Legislatures | State mental health laws | Civil commitment standards, AOT laws, state budgets, housing policy | Varies by state | High |
| Federal courts | Constitutional interpretation | Civil commitment standards, due process requirements | Legal authority | Medium-High |
| County/local governments | Crisis services, jails, housing | 911/988 dispatch, jail diversion, zoning for supportive housing | Varies widely | Medium |

### Key Organizations and Institutions

- **National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI)**: The largest grassroots mental health advocacy organization in the U.S. with over 600 state and local affiliates. NAMI represents both people with mental illness and family members, advocating for treatment access, parity, research funding, and caregiver support. Annual budget approximately $50 million.
- **Treatment Advocacy Center (TAC)**: A nonprofit focused specifically on people with the most severe mental illnesses, advocating for AOT, civil commitment reform, and psychiatric bed capacity. TAC publishes widely cited data on beds, criminalization, and treatment gaps.
- **Mental Health America (MHA)**: A community-based nonprofit focused on prevention, early identification, and expanding access. MHA publishes an annual "State of Mental Health in America" report ranking states.
- **National Council for Mental Wellbeing**: Represents 3,300 mental health and substance use treatment organizations, including community mental health centers. Leads the CCBHC initiative.
- **American Psychiatric Association (APA)**: The professional organization representing 38,000+ psychiatrists. Publishes clinical practice guidelines, advocates for workforce expansion and reimbursement increases.
- **Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA)**: The lead federal agency for mental health programs, administering block grants, CCBHC certification, 988 oversight, and the National Survey on Drug Use and Health (NSDUH).

### Influencers

- **Disability rights organizations (DREDF, Bazelon Center, ACLU Disability Rights)**: Advocate for community integration, least restrictive alternatives, and against involuntary treatment expansion. Oppose most AOT expansion as coercive. Significant influence through litigation. The Bazelon Center has been involved in major Olmstead enforcement cases that have required states to expand community-based services while opposing laws that expand involuntary treatment authority.
- **Law enforcement organizations (IACP, Major Cities Chiefs Association, National Sheriffs' Association)**: Increasingly advocate for mental health diversion, CIT training, and 988 alternatives to 911. Support keeping people with SMI out of jails. Major Cities Chiefs Association published a 2023 position paper calling for a national investment in crisis infrastructure, noting that up to 20% of patrol time in major cities is consumed by mental health calls. Sheriffs, who operate county jails, are among the most vocal advocates for treatment alternatives -- Sheriff Thomas Dart of Cook County, Illinois, has become a national spokesperson for diverting people with SMI from jails to treatment.
- **Pharmaceutical industry (PhRMA, individual companies)**: Fund research, advocacy, and lobbying. Support treatment access expansion generally but have mixed incentives regarding generic clozapine vs. branded LAIs. Janssen (manufacturer of paliperidone palmitate LAI), Otsuka (aripiprazole LAI), and Teva (haloperidol decanoate) have significant revenue stakes in LAI prescribing. Clozapine, as a generic medication ($50-200/month), has no corporate champion -- a key reason for its underutilization compared to branded alternatives costing $1,000+/month.
- **Academic researchers (NIMH-funded investigators, university departments)**: Generate the evidence base for policy. Key researchers include Jeffrey Swanson (Duke) on AOT outcomes and violence risk, Lisa Dixon (Columbia) on first-episode psychosis and coordinated specialty care, Marvin Swartz (Duke) on involuntary treatment policy, Robert Drake (Dartmouth) on supported employment and recovery, T. Scott Stroup (Columbia) on clozapine utilization, and Christoph Correll (Zucker School of Medicine) on antipsychotic effectiveness and physical health comorbidity.
- **Peer support movement**: People with lived experience of SMI who serve as peer specialists, advocates, and recovery coaches. Growing influence on program design and policy. Approximately 30,000 certified peer specialists now work in the U.S. mental health system (National Practice Guidelines, 2023), and Medicaid reimburses peer support services in 42 states. The peer movement has been instrumental in shifting the conversation from "managing symptoms" to "supporting recovery," though tensions exist between peer advocates who emphasize choice and autonomy and treatment advocates who emphasize medication adherence and AOT.
- **Faith-based organizations**: Churches, mosques, synagogues, and other religious institutions provide substantial informal support to people with SMI and their families. Some denominations operate mental health ministries, respite programs, and housing. The NAMI FaithNet initiative connects mental health advocacy with religious communities. Religious organizations are particularly influential in rural and minority communities where formal mental health services are scarce.
- **State governors and budget offices**: Governors control state mental health budgets and appoint the state mental health authority directors who shape policy. In recent years, governors from both parties have championed mental health initiatives -- California Governor Gavin Newsom's CARE Act, Colorado Governor Jared Polis's crisis system investment, and Indiana Governor Eric Holcomb's 988 implementation -- though the depth of investment varies enormously. State budget offices often view mental health spending as discretionary and vulnerable to cuts during economic downturns.

## The Family Caregiver Experience

Family caregivers deserve particular attention as stakeholders because they bear an enormous burden with minimal systemic support:

- **Financial Impact**: Families of people with SMI spend an average of $8,000-12,000 annually in out-of-pocket costs for treatment, medications, housing support, and crisis management (NAMI, 2023). Many family caregivers reduce work hours or leave employment entirely to provide care, with estimated annual lost wages of $15,000-25,000 per caregiver.
- **Emotional and Physical Toll**: A 2023 NAMI survey found that 65% of family caregivers reported symptoms of depression or anxiety, 40% reported physical health decline related to caregiving stress, and 30% reported experiencing violence or threats from their family member during psychotic episodes. Caregiver burnout is pervasive but largely invisible in policy discussions.
- **HIPAA Barriers**: The single most common complaint from family caregivers is the inability to access information about their loved one's treatment. While HIPAA permits (but does not require) providers to share information with identified caregivers in certain circumstances, many providers interpret HIPAA as prohibiting all communication, leaving families unable to monitor medication compliance, recognize warning signs, or coordinate with treatment teams. A 2023 NAMI survey found that 48% of family caregivers reported being denied information by providers citing HIPAA.
- **Navigation Burden**: Families must navigate fragmented systems -- Medicaid eligibility, Social Security disability applications, housing waitlists, court proceedings for commitment or AOT, and crisis response -- with little guidance. NAMI's Family-to-Family program and Helpline provide critical support, but demand far exceeds capacity.
- **Legal Limitations**: In most states, adult children with SMI are legally autonomous even if they are experiencing active psychosis or anosognosia. Parents and spouses have no legal authority to consent to treatment, access medical records, or make housing decisions on behalf of an adult family member unless they obtain guardianship or conservatorship -- a costly and adversarial legal process that most families cannot navigate without legal assistance.

## Stakeholder Interests and Alignment

### Aligned Interests (Potential Coalition Partners)

| Coalition | Shared Interest | Strength |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| NAMI + TAC + APA | Expanded treatment access, clozapine utilization, AOT implementation | Strong |
| Law enforcement + NAMI + TAC | Jail diversion, CIT expansion, 988 crisis infrastructure | Strong |
| Peer specialists + MHA + disability rights | Community-based services, recovery orientation, voluntary engagement | Strong |
| Families + NAMI + religious organizations | Caregiver support, family psychoeducation, HIPAA reform | Moderate |
| CCBHCs + National Council + SAMHSA | Comprehensive community behavioral health clinic expansion | Strong |
| Housing advocates + HUD + supportive housing providers | Permanent supportive housing expansion | Moderate |

### Conflicting Interests

- **Autonomy vs. Treatment**: Disability rights organizations (Bazelon Center, ACLU) oppose AOT expansion and involuntary treatment, while family advocates (NAMI) and treatment-focused organizations (TAC) support it for people who meet specific criteria. This is the deepest and most persistent divide in SMI advocacy.
- **Community vs. Institutional**: Community advocates push for community-only models, while treatment advocates argue that some level of institutional capacity (crisis stabilization, longer-term residential) is necessary. The Olmstead framework further complicates this by requiring community integration.
- **Medication-Focused vs. Recovery-Focused**: Psychiatric professionals emphasize medication adherence (including clozapine and LAIs), while some peer advocates and recovery-oriented organizations emphasize non-pharmacological approaches, shared decision-making, and minimizing medication.
- **Federal vs. State Control**: Some stakeholders push for federal mandates (parity enforcement, IMD exclusion repeal), while others (especially conservative state officials) prefer state flexibility and block grant approaches.

### The Treatment-Liberty Iron Triangle

The most entrenched power relationship in SMI policy is the tension among three groups:

1. **Treatment advocates** (TAC, NAMI family members, APA) who prioritize getting people into treatment, including through court orders when necessary
2. **Civil liberties advocates** (Bazelon Center, ACLU, some peer organizations) who prioritize protecting people from involuntary treatment and state coercion
3. **Fiscal conservatives** (state budget offices, some legislators) who prioritize cost control and resist the funding increases that both voluntary and involuntary treatment expansion would require

No reform can succeed without addressing all three legs of this triangle.

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on SMI policy:

- **Lobbying**: NAMI, APA, PhRMA, and the National Council all maintain active lobbying operations in Washington and state capitals. Mental health lobbying spending totals approximately $30-40 million annually at the federal level (OpenSecrets, 2023). The pharmaceutical industry's mental health-related lobbying is embedded in its broader $350+ million annual federal lobbying operation, making it difficult to separate. The American Hospital Association, which represents emergency departments struggling with psychiatric boarding, spends over $20 million annually on federal lobbying and has increasingly advocated for IMD exclusion reform and crisis system investment.
- **Campaign Contributions**: The pharmaceutical industry is among the largest political donors, contributing over $100 million per election cycle. Mental health-specific PACs are small by comparison -- NAMI's PAC spending is minimal, and the Treatment Advocacy Center does not operate a PAC. The relative absence of mental health-specific campaign contributions means that SMI policy competes for attention with better-funded interests.
- **Litigation**: Disability rights organizations (Bazelon Center, DREDF) use litigation under the ADA, Olmstead, and constitutional law to shape policy. Court orders have driven much of the transition from institutional to community-based care. The Bazelon Center was involved in the original *Olmstead* litigation and continues to file amicus briefs in cases involving involuntary treatment, community integration, and Medicaid coverage. On the other side, families and treatment advocates have begun using litigation to compel states to provide treatment -- including a 2023 lawsuit in California arguing that the state's failure to provide adequate treatment for people with SMI violates the ADA.
- **Public Advocacy**: NAMI's grassroots network, including NAMIWalks events and Congressional briefings, raises public awareness. High-profile media coverage of tragedies involving untreated SMI creates short-term political windows. The Treatment Advocacy Center's "Grading the States" reports, which rank states on their treatment systems, generate media coverage and create competitive pressure among state mental health authorities.
- **Personal Testimony**: Family members, people in recovery, and law enforcement officers who testify at legislative hearings provide the most compelling advocacy. The combination of a parent describing their child's experience with untreated psychosis and a sheriff describing the cost to their jail is extremely effective in state legislative contexts. NAMI trains family members to testify through its Advocacy Ambassador program.
- **Expertise and Data**: Organizations like TAC, NASMHPD, and NIMH-funded researchers provide the data and analysis that inform legislative debates. The SAMHSA NSDUH survey is the authoritative prevalence data source. TAC's data on psychiatric bed counts and criminalization statistics are cited in virtually every federal hearing on SMI. The National Council for Mental Wellbeing provides the primary outcome data from CCBHC demonstrations that supports expansion legislation.
- **Revolving Door**: Movement between SAMHSA, CMS, state mental health authorities, and advocacy organizations is common. Former SAMHSA administrators have moved to leadership roles at NAMI, the National Council, and private mental health companies. This revolving door facilitates communication between government and advocacy but also raises questions about regulatory capture and mission alignment.

## Power Analysis: Who Wins and Who Loses

The current system is not simply broken -- it produces outcomes that serve certain interests:

- **Insurance companies benefit** from the status quo because parity enforcement remains weak, allowing them to limit psychiatric coverage and shift costs to public systems. The 2024 MHPAEA final rule threatens this arrangement, and industry lobbying to weaken enforcement is ongoing.
- **Private prison and jail operators benefit** from the incarceration of people with SMI, who tend to serve longer sentences and cycle through facilities more frequently than other inmates. The four largest private prison companies collectively hold federal and state contracts worth over $3 billion annually.
- **Emergency departments benefit financially** from psychiatric boarding -- each ED visit generates revenue -- even though the practice is clinically inappropriate and operationally disruptive. The misalignment between financial incentives and clinical appropriateness is a structural feature, not a bug.
- **People with SMI, their families, and communities lose** in every dimension: health outcomes, housing stability, safety, financial security, and quality of life. The current system produces the worst outcomes at the highest cost, a combination that persists because the people who bear the costs lack political power relative to the people who benefit from inaction.
