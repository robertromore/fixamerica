# Serious Mental Illness: History

## Historical Development

The treatment of serious mental illness in the United States has undergone dramatic transformations, shaped by scientific discovery, moral reform movements, economic pressures, civil rights advocacy, and political ideology. Understanding this history is essential for grasping why the current system is so deeply fragmented and why millions of Americans with SMI remain untreated, incarcerated, or homeless.

---

### The Institutional Era (1773-1945): Asylums, Reform, and Decline

- **Colonial Period**: People with serious mental illness were confined in almshouses, jails, or kept at home by families. Those deemed "lunatic" were often placed in cellars, attics, or outbuildings. The first public psychiatric hospital in America opened in Williamsburg, Virginia, in 1773, followed by the Pennsylvania Hospital's psychiatric wing (1752, originally for general patients) which admitted its first patients with mental illness in the late 18th century.
- **Dorothea Dix's Campaign (1840s-1880s)**: Dorothea Dix documented appalling conditions in jails and poorhouses where people with mental illness were chained and abused. She traveled over 60,000 miles across the United States, visiting jails, almshouses, and hospitals. Her advocacy led to the creation of 32 state-funded psychiatric hospitals between 1843 and 1880 and the expansion of many existing facilities. Dix's work established the principle that the state bears responsibility for the care of people with severe mental illness -- a principle that would be tested and abandoned repeatedly over the next two centuries.
- **The Kirkbride Plan**: Architect Thomas Kirkbride designed hospitals intended to be therapeutic environments with natural light, fresh air, and organized daily routines. The "Kirkbride buildings" featured a distinctive bat-wing floor plan with staggered wings to maximize light and ventilation. Over 70 Kirkbride hospitals were built between 1845 and 1910. Many survive today as repurposed or abandoned structures.
- **The "Moral Treatment" Movement**: Early asylum reformers believed that mental illness could be treated through structured environment, meaningful activity, and compassionate care. The theory held that removing patients from the stresses of society and placing them in orderly, humane institutions would promote recovery. Initial outcomes were promising -- some institutions reported "cure" rates of 40-60% in the mid-19th century -- but these early successes were overwhelmed by overcrowding as populations grew faster than institutional capacity.
- **Overcrowding and Deterioration (1880s-1945)**: State hospitals quickly became overcrowded. By 1900, there were over 150,000 people in state institutions. Funding failed to keep pace with population growth, and hospitals deteriorated into custodial warehouses. Abuses included forced sterilization (32 states enacted eugenics laws, with over 60,000 people with mental illness sterilized by the 1970s), lobotomies (performed on an estimated 40,000-50,000 Americans between 1936 and 1967, including Rosemary Kennedy in 1941), and physical restraints. The "hydrotherapy" of the early 20th century subjected patients to prolonged immersion in ice baths as punishment disguised as treatment. Insulin coma therapy, introduced in the 1930s, induced repeated comas with a 1-5% mortality rate and no proven efficacy.
- **World War II and the Conscientious Objectors**: Approximately 3,000 conscientious objectors were assigned to work in state psychiatric hospitals during World War II as an alternative to military service. What they witnessed -- patients lying in filth, physical abuse by staff, massive overcrowding, and near-total absence of treatment -- radicalized many of them. Several became journalists, filmmakers, and advocates who would later drive the movement for reform.
- **Peak Institutionalization**: State psychiatric hospital populations peaked at 559,000 in 1955, representing approximately 339 beds per 100,000 population. At this point, state hospitals consumed over half of all state health spending in many states, creating enormous fiscal pressure that would later drive the political case for deinstitutionalization alongside the moral case.

### The Deinstitutionalization Era (1945-1980): Promise and Betrayal

- **Post-War Exposés**: Journalists and conscientious objectors who had served as attendants during World War II published exposés of horrific asylum conditions, including Albert Deutsch's *The Shame of the States* (1948) and Life magazine's photo essay "Bedlam 1946." These accounts described patients in overcrowded wards, lying naked on floors, subjected to physical restraints for days, and receiving no meaningful treatment. The public reaction was shock and revulsion, creating the political preconditions for reform.
- **Chlorpromazine (1954)**: The introduction of chlorpromazine (Thorazine) in 1954 -- the first effective antipsychotic medication -- made it conceivable for many people with schizophrenia to live outside hospitals. Developed in France by Henri Laborit and tested at Sainte-Anne Hospital in Paris, chlorpromazine was approved by the FDA in 1954 and rapidly adopted in state hospitals. Within two years, hospital census began to decline. This was a genuine pharmacological breakthrough, but it was quickly co-opted as justification for closing hospitals before community alternatives existed. Chlorpromazine did not cure schizophrenia; it suppressed positive symptoms (hallucinations, delusions) while causing significant side effects including sedation, weight gain, and tardive dyskinesia.
- **The Joint Commission on Mental Illness and Health (1961)**: Congress created the Joint Commission in 1955 to study the nation's mental health needs. Its 1961 report, *Action for Mental Health*, recommended transforming large custodial institutions into community-based treatment networks with smaller, treatment-oriented facilities. The report provided the intellectual foundation for the community mental health movement.
- **Community Mental Health Act (1963)**: President Kennedy signed the Community Mental Health Centers Construction Act on October 31, 1963 -- the last bill he signed before his assassination. The law envisioned 1,500 federally funded community mental health centers to replace state hospitals. Kennedy's personal connection to the issue -- his sister Rosemary had been lobotomized in 1941 at age 23, leaving her permanently incapacitated -- lent moral urgency to the initiative. However, only 789 centers were ever built, and most focused on outpatient counseling for people with anxiety, depression, and adjustment disorders rather than intensive services for people with schizophrenia or bipolar disorder. The centers were never adequately funded: the original legislation authorized construction but not ongoing operating costs.
- **Medicaid and the IMD Exclusion (1965)**: The creation of Medicaid included the Institution for Mental Diseases (IMD) exclusion, which barred federal Medicaid payment for care in psychiatric facilities with more than 16 beds for adults ages 21-64. This was intended to prevent states from simply shifting their existing state hospital costs to the new federal-state Medicaid program. Senator Russell Long of Louisiana, a key architect of the exclusion, argued that states should not receive a federal windfall for patients they were already serving. The unintended but lasting consequence was to create a permanent financial disincentive against maintaining or building psychiatric inpatient capacity.
- **Supplemental Security Income (SSI, 1972)**: The creation of SSI provided income support for people with disabilities, including those with SMI, outside of institutions. This was intended to support community living, but in practice it meant that people discharged from hospitals had a modest income ($914/month maximum federal benefit in 2024) that was insufficient for housing in most markets. SSI eligibility also facilitated the transfer of patients from state hospitals to nursing homes and board-and-care facilities, which were eligible for federal reimbursement but often provided no psychiatric treatment.
- **The Great Emptying**: Between 1955 and 1980, state hospital populations fell from 559,000 to approximately 130,000 -- a 77% reduction in 25 years. Patients were discharged into communities that lacked housing, treatment, and support services. Many ended up homeless or in nursing homes that served as de facto warehouses. An estimated 200,000-300,000 former hospital patients were transferred to nursing homes by 1980, where they received custodial care but little psychiatric treatment (Lamb and Bachrach, 2001). Others moved into single-room occupancy hotels (SROs) in inner cities -- a housing stock that was itself being demolished in the urban renewal era, removing the last affordable option for many discharged patients.

### The Legal Rights Revolution (1970-1990): Liberty Without Services

- **Wyatt v. Stickney (1971)**: A federal court in Alabama established the first constitutional right to treatment for people involuntarily committed to state psychiatric hospitals. Judge Frank Johnson ruled that if the state confined someone against their will, it was constitutionally obligated to provide adequate treatment -- not merely custodial care. The decision established detailed minimum standards including staffing ratios, individual treatment plans, and living conditions. While enforcement was inconsistent, Wyatt established the principle that confinement without treatment is unconstitutional.
- **O'Connor v. Donaldson (1975)**: The Supreme Court ruled that a state cannot constitutionally confine a non-dangerous person with mental illness who is capable of surviving safely in freedom. Kenneth Donaldson had been confined in Florida State Hospital for nearly 15 years despite repeated requests for release. This landmark case established that mental illness alone does not justify involuntary confinement, but it said nothing about the obligation to provide treatment in the community. The practical effect was to shift the legal burden: the state had to justify continued confinement, not the patient their release.
- **Least Restrictive Alternative Doctrine**: Courts across the country adopted the principle that people with mental illness must be treated in the least restrictive setting appropriate to their needs, further accelerating discharge from hospitals. This doctrine, drawn from constitutional law principles applied in *Shelton v. Tucker* (1960), became a powerful tool for civil liberties attorneys seeking to reduce hospital populations.
- **Lake v. Cameron (1966)**: The D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals ruled that a court must consider less restrictive alternatives before ordering involuntary commitment, establishing that the state must explore outpatient options first. This early case set the precedent that would be expanded nationally in the following decades.
- **Addington v. Texas (1979)**: The Supreme Court required "clear and convincing evidence" (not merely a preponderance) for civil commitment, raising the evidentiary standard for involuntary hospitalization. This standard is lower than "beyond a reasonable doubt" used in criminal cases but higher than the "preponderance" standard used in most civil cases, reflecting the Court's recognition that commitment involves a significant deprivation of liberty.
- **Rennie v. Klein (1981)**: The Third Circuit ruled that involuntarily committed patients retain a qualified constitutional right to refuse medication, requiring judicial or administrative review before forced medication can be administered. This decision, and similar rulings in other circuits, made involuntary medication in hospitals significantly more procedurally complex.
- **Narrowing of Commitment Standards**: States rewrote civil commitment laws to require proof of imminent danger to self or others, effectively making it impossible to hospitalize someone who was deteriorating but had not yet reached crisis. By the mid-1980s, the standard in most states was "imminent dangerousness." The practical consequence was a system that could only respond after someone had made a suicide attempt, committed a violent act, or was found in life-threatening conditions -- a reactive posture that ensured the worst possible outcomes before intervention was legally permissible.
- **Olmstead v. L.C. (1999)**: The Supreme Court ruled that unjustified institutionalization is discrimination under the Americans with Disabilities Act, requiring states to provide community-based services when appropriate. The case was brought by two women, Lois Curtis and Elaine Wilson, who were confined in a Georgia state hospital despite their treatment professionals agreeing they could be served in the community. While a victory for community integration, Olmstead was often used to justify further hospital closures without commensurate community investment. The decision's caveat -- that states must provide community services when they can "reasonably accommodate" the individual -- gave states latitude to claim insufficient resources as a defense against providing services.

### The Criminalization Era (1980-2010): From Hospital to Jail

- **Homelessness Crisis (1980s)**: The combination of hospital closures, failure to build community mental health infrastructure, loss of low-cost housing (SRO demolition, Section 8 cuts), and the Reagan-era cuts to federal mental health funding (the Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1981 replaced categorical mental health funding with block grants at 25% lower funding levels) produced a visible homelessness crisis. The Mental Health Systems Act of 1980, which Jimmy Carter signed as the culmination of his President's Commission on Mental Health, was essentially repealed by OBRA 1981, replacing its targeted programs with a block grant that gave states broad discretion and less money. By 1990, an estimated 200,000 people with SMI were homeless. The emergence of large encampments in cities like New York, Los Angeles, San Francisco, and Washington, D.C., forced public attention to the consequences of deinstitutionalization in ways that abstract policy debate had not.
- **Penrose's Law in Action**: British psychiatrist Lionel Penrose observed in 1939 that there is an inverse relationship between psychiatric hospital beds and prison populations. This pattern played out in the United States: as hospital beds decreased, the incarceration of people with mental illness increased dramatically. By 2000, there were approximately 10 times more people with SMI in jails and prisons than in state psychiatric hospitals. The Los Angeles County Jail, Cook County Jail in Chicago, and Rikers Island in New York became the three largest de facto psychiatric facilities in the country -- a distinction they hold to this day.
- **War on Drugs Impact**: The War on Drugs disproportionately swept people with co-occurring SMI and substance use disorders into the criminal justice system, where they received little or no psychiatric treatment. Mandatory minimum sentencing laws, three-strikes provisions, and the crack cocaine sentencing disparity meant that people self-medicating psychotic symptoms with street drugs faced years or decades in prison rather than treatment. By 2010, approximately 26% of people with SMI had a co-occurring substance use disorder (SAMHSA, 2023), and the criminal justice system had become the primary point of contact for many.
- **Rise of Mental Health Courts (1997-present)**: The first mental health court was established in Broward County, Florida, in 1997 as an alternative to traditional criminal prosecution for defendants with SMI. By 2023, approximately 450 mental health courts were operating across the United States, providing supervised treatment in lieu of incarceration for eligible defendants. Studies show these courts reduce recidivism by approximately 20% (SAMHSA, 2023), though they are available in only a fraction of jurisdictions and typically handle only misdemeanor offenses.
- **Kendra's Law (New York, 1999)**: Following the death of Kendra Webdale, pushed in front of a New York City subway by Andrew Goldstein, a man with untreated schizophrenia who had been repeatedly hospitalized and released without follow-up, New York enacted the first comprehensive assisted outpatient treatment (AOT) law. It allowed courts to order outpatient treatment for people with SMI who met specific criteria, including a history of hospitalization or violent behavior. Kendra's Law was controversial from its inception, with disability rights advocates arguing it represented a return to coercive control and treatment advocates arguing it was the minimum necessary intervention for people who could not engage in voluntary care.
- **Laura's Law (California, 2002)**: California enacted its own AOT law following the murder of Laura Wilcox by a man with untreated SMI at a mental health clinic in Nevada County. Implementation was left to individual counties, and uptake was slow -- only 6 of 58 counties implemented the law in its first decade. In 2022, California Governor Gavin Newsom signed SB 1338 (the CARE Act), creating a new civil court framework for connecting people with schizophrenia spectrum and other psychotic disorders to housing and treatment, further expanding the state's involuntary treatment toolkit.
- **Assertive Community Treatment (ACT) Growth**: The ACT model, originally developed by Leonard Stein and Mary Ann Test at the University of Wisconsin in the 1970s, expanded significantly during this period. ACT teams provide intensive, multidisciplinary, community-based services to people with the most severe SMI, with low caseload ratios (approximately 10 clients per team member). By 2010, over 600 ACT teams were operating nationally, though coverage remained far below need (SAMHSA, 2023).

### The Modern Reform Era (2010-Present): Incremental Progress

- **Affordable Care Act (2010)**: The ACA designated mental health and substance use disorder services as essential health benefits, expanded Medicaid coverage (in states that opted in), and strengthened parity protections. However, the impact on SMI was limited because many people with the most severe conditions were already covered by Medicaid or Medicare, and the ACA's mental health provisions focused more on general mental health access than on the intensive services needed for schizophrenia and bipolar disorder.
- **Sandy Hook and the Mental Health Debate (2012)**: The mass shooting at Sandy Hook Elementary School in Newtown, Connecticut, reignited national debate about the relationship between mental illness and violence. While the debate was often misframed -- mental illness is responsible for only 3-5% of violent crime -- it produced political energy for mental health legislation. The Helping Families in Mental Health Crisis Act, introduced by Representative Tim Murphy (R-PA), a psychologist, became the basis for the mental health provisions of the 21st Century Cures Act.
- **CCBHC Demonstration Program (2014-2017)**: Section 223 of the Protecting Access to Medicare Act of 2014 authorized SAMHSA to certify Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinics in 8 states as a demonstration program. The 66 original CCBHC sites showed remarkable results: 60% reduction in emergency department visits, 41% reduction in jail bookings, and 62% reduction in homelessness among clients (National Council for Mental Wellbeing, 2023). The demonstration's success led to expansion through the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act.
- **21st Century Cures Act (2016)**: Created the position of Assistant Secretary for Mental Health and Substance Use, authorized the creation of an Interdepartmental Serious Mental Illness Coordinating Committee (ISMICC), and provided new grant programs. It represented the first federal legislation to specifically prioritize SMI as distinct from broader mental health concerns. The ISMICC, which includes representatives from HHS, DOJ, HUD, VA, and the Department of Education, published recommendations on improving SMI treatment but has limited enforcement authority.
- **988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (2022)**: The transition from the 10-digit National Suicide Prevention Lifeline to the 3-digit 988 number, authorized by the National Suicide Hotline Designation Act of 2020, represented a fundamental shift in crisis infrastructure, modeled on 911. In its first full year, 988 handled 5.5 million contacts -- a 40% increase from the prior system (SAMHSA, 2024). However, 988 launched with significant capacity constraints: approximately 15% of calls went unanswered in the first year, and many local call centers lacked the staffing to meet demand.
- **Bipartisan Safer Communities Act (2022)**: Following the Uvalde school shooting, Congress passed the most significant mental health legislation in years, including expanded CCBHC funding ($430 million), school-based mental health, pediatric mental health access, and a historic provision allowing Medicaid to cover services in IMD facilities for children and adolescents experiencing acute psychiatric crises. The law demonstrated that bipartisan mental health legislation is possible, even in a deeply polarized Congress.
- **California CARE Act (2022-2024)**: Governor Gavin Newsom signed SB 1338 creating a civil court framework -- CARE Courts -- to connect people with schizophrenia spectrum disorders to housing and treatment. The program, which began implementation in 2023, represents the most ambitious state-level SMI initiative in decades, though early results have been mixed due to limited housing availability and court capacity.
- **MHPAEA Final Rule (2024)**: The Departments of Labor, HHS, and Treasury issued a final rule strengthening mental health parity enforcement, requiring insurers to conduct and disclose comparative analyses of non-quantitative treatment limitations. The rule requires that restrictions on mental health treatment -- such as prior authorization, step therapy, and network adequacy standards -- be no more restrictive than those applied to medical/surgical benefits. Enforcement remains a challenge, but the rule provides new tools for state insurance commissioners and federal regulators.

## The Pharmacological Revolution: A Timeline

Understanding the history of SMI treatment requires tracking the development of psychiatric medications, which have driven both therapeutic progress and systemic change:

| Year | Medication/Class | Significance |
|------|-----------------|--------------|
| 1949 | Lithium | Australian psychiatrist John Cade discovers lithium's antimanic properties; FDA approval delayed until 1970 due to safety concerns |
| 1954 | Chlorpromazine (Thorazine) | First antipsychotic; enables discharge from hospitals but does not cure schizophrenia |
| 1958 | Haloperidol | More potent first-generation antipsychotic; widely used for decades despite severe side effects |
| 1971 | Clozapine (initial use) | First "atypical" antipsychotic; withdrawn in some markets after agranulocytosis deaths |
| 1990 | Clozapine (U.S. re-approval) | FDA re-approves with mandatory blood monitoring (REMS); remains the only drug approved for treatment-resistant schizophrenia |
| 1993 | Risperidone | First widely prescribed second-generation antipsychotic; fewer extrapyramidal side effects but metabolic risks |
| 1996 | Olanzapine | Effective but associated with significant weight gain and metabolic syndrome |
| 2002 | Aripiprazole | First "partial agonist" antipsychotic; lower metabolic risk |
| 2006 | Paliperidone palmitate LAI | First monthly long-acting injectable second-generation antipsychotic |
| 2015 | Aripiprazole lauroxil (3-month LAI) | Extended-interval LAI reduces visit frequency |
| 2021 | Paliperidone palmitate (6-month LAI) | Longest-acting injectable antipsychotic available |

Each pharmacological advance expanded the toolkit for treating SMI but also revealed the gap between what is scientifically possible and what the healthcare system actually delivers. The most striking example remains clozapine: approved in 1990, proven to reduce suicide by 80% and all-cause mortality by 26%, yet prescribed to only 4% of eligible patients more than 30 years later.

## The Housing Dimension: A Parallel History

The history of SMI intersects critically with the history of housing policy:

- **1950s-1960s**: State hospital patients had guaranteed shelter, however poor the conditions. Urban renewal programs demolished SRO hotels that housed discharged patients, removing affordable housing stock.
- **1970s**: SSI payments enabled some community living, but Section 8 housing vouchers (created in 1974) were never adequate in number. People with SMI competed with the general low-income population for limited vouchers.
- **1980s**: The Stewart B. McKinney Homeless Assistance Act (1987) was the first major federal response to homelessness, but it focused on shelters rather than permanent housing. The "continuum of care" model assumed people would progress from shelter to transitional to permanent housing -- a model poorly suited to people with chronic SMI.
- **1992**: Sam Tsemberis founded Pathways to Housing in New York City, establishing the Housing First model -- the principle that stable housing should come first, with treatment services offered but not required as a condition of housing. This paradigm shift eventually reshaped federal homelessness policy.
- **2000s-2010s**: HUD adopted Housing First as federal policy, and permanent supportive housing became the evidence-based standard for chronic homelessness. However, production never kept pace with need: approximately 381,000 PSH beds exist nationally, far short of the estimated 500,000-600,000 needed.
- **2020s**: Rising rents, COVID-era eviction backlogs, and the expiration of pandemic relief funds have intensified the housing crisis for people with SMI, with supportive housing waitlists exceeding 2-5 years in many metropolitan areas.

## Lessons from History

### Pattern 1: Promises Without Funding

The most consistent pattern in SMI history is the gap between legislative promise and actual implementation. The Community Mental Health Act of 1963 promised 1,500 centers; only 789 were built. The 21st Century Cures Act authorized new programs but left funding to annual appropriations. The 988 system launched with inadequate call center capacity. Repeatedly, the political system generates ambitious legislation but fails to sustain the funding necessary to deliver on commitments. Any new reform effort must include mandatory, sustained funding mechanisms rather than discretionary appropriations.

### Pattern 2: Shifting the Burden Without Solving the Problem

Deinstitutionalization did not eliminate the need for intensive care; it merely shifted where that care (or non-care) occurred. State hospitals gave way to jails, emergency rooms, homeless shelters, and family homes. Each shift was framed as progress -- from "asylum reform" to "least restrictive environment" to "community integration" -- but without adequate services, each was merely a transfer of responsibility from one inadequate setting to another. Meaningful reform must ensure that closing one door opens another, with verified capacity before transitions occur.

### Pattern 3: Rights Without Resources

The legal rights revolution of the 1970s was essential in ending genuine abuses, but "liberty" without treatment services is not freedom -- it is abandonment. The narrow focus on preventing involuntary confinement, without equal attention to ensuring access to voluntary and court-ordered outpatient treatment, left millions of people with SMI with the right to refuse treatment but no real access to it. Future policy must pair civil liberties protections with affirmative obligations to provide treatment.

### Pattern 4: Crisis-Driven Legislation

Major SMI legislation has almost always been triggered by high-profile tragedies -- Kendra's Law after a subway pushing, Laura's Law after a shooting, the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act after Uvalde. While these events create political windows, they also skew policy toward violence prevention rather than the far more common outcomes of untreated SMI: homelessness, premature death, and quiet suffering. Sustained advocacy outside of crisis moments is essential.

### Pattern 5: The Pharmacological Promise and Its Limits

Each generation of psychiatric medication -- chlorpromazine in the 1950s, lithium in the 1970s, clozapine in the 1990s, second-generation antipsychotics in the 2000s -- was initially greeted with hope that it would "solve" serious mental illness. Each advance was real and significant, but each was also oversold as a sufficient response, used to justify reducing investments in psychosocial supports, housing, and institutional capacity. The lesson is that medication is necessary but not sufficient: pharmacological treatment must be embedded in a comprehensive system of housing, employment support, case management, and crisis services to produce sustainable recovery.

### Pattern 6: The Federalism Shell Game

Throughout the history of SMI policy, federal and state governments have shifted costs and responsibilities back and forth. States originally bore the full cost of asylums. Medicaid (1965) offered federal matching funds but excluded the IMD setting. SSI (1972) provided federal income support that incentivized discharge from state hospitals. OBRA 1981 pushed funding back to state block grants. The ACA expanded federal Medicaid coverage but left implementation to states. At each transition, gaps opened and people with SMI fell through them. Any durable reform must clearly allocate responsibilities and funding streams between federal and state governments, with accountability mechanisms to prevent cost-shifting.
