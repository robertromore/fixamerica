# Veterans Mental Health: Solutions

Veterans mental health requires a multi-faceted approach that aims to close access gaps, reduce suicide, strengthen the clinical workforce, integrate care across VA and community systems, and accelerate the adoption of evidence-based and emerging treatments. The proposals below address the structural, clinical, and cultural barriers identified in the root causes analysis.

---

## Reform Framework

### Guiding Principles

- The VA must remain the center of gravity for veteran mental health care, but community partnerships are essential for universal access
- Suicide prevention must shift upstream from clinical treatment to population-level interventions that reach veterans outside the VA system
- The workforce crisis cannot be solved by the VA alone; it requires a national mental health workforce pipeline strategy
- Evidence-based treatments must be the standard of care wherever veterans receive treatment, including in Community Care
- Veterans themselves must be partners in system design, not merely beneficiaries of top-down programs
- Moral injury, which does not fit neatly within the PTSD framework, requires dedicated clinical attention and potentially its own diagnostic and treatment infrastructure
- Solutions must address both the clinical dimension (treatment) and the social dimension (community, purpose, connection) of veteran mental health

### Theory of Change

Veteran mental health outcomes will improve when: (1) every veteran can access a qualified, military-competent mental health professional within 14 days, regardless of location; (2) suicide prevention efforts reach the 60.6% of at-risk veterans who are not engaged with VA care; (3) evidence-based treatments are delivered with fidelity across both VA and community settings; and (4) the transition from military to civilian mental health care is seamless. Achieving these conditions requires simultaneous action on workforce supply, system integration, outreach infrastructure, and clinical quality.

## Proposed Solutions

### Solution 1: National Veterans Mental Health Workforce Initiative

**Description**: Establish a comprehensive, multi-year initiative to recruit, train, and retain 10,000 additional mental health professionals for the VA and community care networks within five years. The initiative would combine expanded educational pipeline programs, competitive compensation, loan repayment, and geographic incentive programs to eliminate the 4,600-position VA vacancy gap and build community provider capacity.

**Evidence Base**: The VA's existing Education Debt Reduction Program (EDRP) has shown that loan repayment of up to $200,000 is effective at recruiting clinicians to hard-to-fill positions. The Health Resources and Services Administration (HRSA) National Health Service Corps (NHSC) model, which places providers in underserved areas in exchange for loan repayment, has decades of evidence. The VA's own Mental Health Residential Rehabilitation Treatment Program (MH RRTP) expansion in 2007-2012 demonstrated that targeted hiring initiatives can rapidly increase capacity when adequately funded. Additionally, the VA's academic affiliation network -- partnerships with 131 medical schools and 1,800 colleges -- provides a direct training-to-employment channel that no other health care system can replicate.

**Implementation**:

1. Increase EDRP loan repayment cap from $200,000 to $300,000 for mental health positions in designated shortage areas
2. Create 2,000 new VA-funded psychiatry, psychology, and social work training slots at VA-affiliated academic medical centers, with a service obligation of 3 years of VA employment post-graduation
3. Establish a "Military Mental Health" specialization track in graduate psychology and social work programs, with VA-funded stipends of $30,000 per year
4. Streamline VA hiring from an average of 95 days to 45 days through pre-approved credentialing, expedited background checks, and reduced onboarding bureaucracy
5. Implement geographic incentive pay of 15-25% above base for positions in rural and frontier areas
6. Expand the VA's peer specialist workforce from 2,200 to 5,000 positions, with competitive salaries and career advancement pathways
7. Create a VA-HRSA joint recruitment portal that allows mental health graduates to apply simultaneously for VA positions, NHSC placement, and EDRP eligibility

**Cost/Resources**: $2.5 billion over 5 years ($500 million/year), including expanded loan repayment ($800 million), new training slots ($600 million), hiring process reform ($200 million), geographic incentives ($600 million), and program administration ($300 million).

**Timeline**: Legislative authorization in Year 1; first training cohort enrolled in Year 2; 5,000 new positions filled by Year 3; 10,000 by Year 5.

**Challenges**: National mental health workforce shortage means recruitment is zero-sum absent pipeline expansion. Academic programs take 4-8 years to produce graduates. Rural recruitment requires sustained incentives, not one-time bonuses. Expanding the peer specialist workforce requires developing training infrastructure and professional standards that do not currently exist at scale.

**Impact Metrics**: VA mental health vacancy rate (target: <10% by Year 5); average wait time for new mental health appointment (target: <14 days); community provider military competency training completion rate (target: >50%).

### Solution 2: Upstream Suicide Prevention Through Community-Based Outreach

**Description**: Shift the primary focus of veteran suicide prevention from clinical intervention (treating veterans already in VA care) to community-based, population-level outreach that reaches the 60.6% of veteran suicide decedents who were not recent VA users. This includes expanding the Staff Sergeant Parker Gordon Fox Suicide Prevention Grants program, deploying lethal means safety initiatives, and establishing veteran suicide prevention coordinators in every county with significant veteran population.

**Evidence Base**: The Commander John Scott Hannon Act (2020) created the Staff Sergeant Parker Gordon Fox Suicide Prevention Grant Program, awarding approximately $174 million in grants to community organizations in 2022-2023. Early program evaluations show increased outreach contacts and referrals. Lethal means safety interventions (gun locks, safe storage counseling, means restriction during crisis) have strong evidence: a 2022 Annals of Internal Medicine review found that means restriction reduces suicide by 30-50% without increasing other-method suicides. The VA's "Together With Veterans" pilot program in rural Montana and Alaska demonstrated that community-level coalitions addressing social determinants of veteran suicide can increase help-seeking behavior and reduce social isolation.

**Implementation**:

1. Triple the Fox Suicide Prevention Grant Program from $174 million to $500 million annually, with priority funding for rural and tribal communities with elevated veteran suicide rates
2. Establish veteran suicide prevention coordinator positions in every county with 5,000+ veterans (approximately 800 counties), funded jointly by VA and state veteran agencies
3. Partner with the National Shooting Sports Foundation (NSSF), firearm retailers, and ranges for voluntary safe storage promotion and free gun lock distribution (target: 2 million gun locks distributed in 5 years)
4. Create a VA-State data sharing compact to identify veterans at risk using state health, criminal justice, and social service data (with privacy protections modeled on HIPAA's public health exception)
5. Fund veteran peer support networks in rural communities, houses of worship, and veteran-frequented businesses (VFW posts, American Legion halls)
6. Expand the VA's Solid Start program, which currently contacts all recently separated veterans at 90, 180, and 365 days post-separation, to include warm referrals to community-based services and mental health resources

**Cost/Resources**: $1.8 billion over 5 years ($360 million/year), including expanded Fox grants ($1.5 billion), county coordinators ($150 million), lethal means programs ($100 million), and data infrastructure ($50 million).

**Timeline**: Grant expansion in Year 1; county coordinators recruited by Year 2; full lethal means program operational by Year 3.

**Challenges**: Reaching veterans who are not connected to any institutional system is inherently difficult. Data sharing raises privacy concerns. Lethal means safety faces political opposition from firearm rights groups. Community-based programs depend on local organizational capacity that varies dramatically across regions.

**Impact Metrics**: Veteran suicides per day (target: reduce from 17.5 to below 14 by Year 5); proportion of veteran suicide decedents not in VA care (target: reduce from 60.6% to below 45%); gun locks distributed (target: 2 million).

### Solution 3: Mandatory Military Cultural Competency for Community Care Providers

**Description**: Require all mental health providers who treat veterans through VA Community Care to complete standardized military cultural competency training and demonstrate proficiency in evidence-based treatments for combat-related PTSD, MST, and TBI. Establish a VA certification program with periodic recertification.

**Evidence Base**: A 2023 RAND study found that fewer than 10% of community mental health providers had completed military cultural competency training. Research demonstrates that providers who understand military culture achieve better therapeutic alliance, treatment engagement, and outcomes with veteran patients (Tanielian et al., RAND, 2018). The VA's TRAIN program and Community Provider Toolkit exist but are voluntary and underutilized. The Star Behavioral Health Providers (SBHP) program, developed at the Center for Deployment Psychology, has trained over 5,000 community providers in military cultural competency and provides a working model for scaling certification.

**Implementation**:

1. Develop a standardized 40-hour VA Military Mental Health Certification curriculum covering military culture, combat-related PTSD (CPT and PE protocols), MST, TBI screening, suicide risk assessment, and moral injury
2. Require certification as a condition of Community Care provider enrollment for mental health services
3. Fund free, online and in-person training through VA academic affiliates and the Center for Deployment Psychology
4. Establish 3-year recertification cycle with continuing education requirements (15 hours per cycle)
5. Create a public provider directory indicating certification status, enabling veterans to choose certified providers
6. Offer CEU credits that satisfy state licensing requirements in all 50 states, reducing the perception of additional burden

**Cost/Resources**: $150 million over 5 years ($30 million/year) for curriculum development, training delivery, certification administration, and provider directory.

**Timeline**: Curriculum developed in Year 1; training available in Year 2; mandatory certification phased in over Years 3-5 (18-month grace period for existing providers).

**Challenges**: Community providers may resist mandatory training as burdensome. Some may exit the VA network rather than comply, temporarily reducing access. Training alone does not guarantee treatment fidelity. State licensing boards may not recognize VA certification for CEU purposes without specific agreements.

**Impact Metrics**: Percentage of Community Care mental health providers with VA certification (target: 100% by Year 5); veteran satisfaction with Community Care mental health services (target: parity with VA in-house by Year 5).

### Solution 4: Seamless DoD-VA Mental Health Transition

**Description**: Eliminate the gap in mental health care that occurs when service members separate from the military and transition to VA care. Require a "warm handoff" from a DoD mental health provider to a specific VA mental health provider for every separating service member who has received mental health treatment, is at elevated suicide risk, or requests transition support.

**Evidence Base**: The first year after military separation carries the highest suicide risk. The current Transition Assistance Program (TAP) provides information briefings but no clinical handoff. The VA-DoD Joint Executive Committee has identified care continuity as a priority, but implementation has been slow. Research on care transitions in civilian health systems shows that warm handoffs (direct clinician-to-clinician transfer) reduce emergency department visits and hospitalizations by 20-30% (Naylor et al., *Annals of Internal Medicine*, 2011). The VA's Solid Start program, which calls recently separated veterans at 90, 180, and 365 days, has demonstrated that proactive outreach during the transition period increases VA engagement, but it lacks the clinical handoff component needed to ensure continuity of care.

**Implementation**:

1. Mandate that DoD mental health providers schedule a VA mental health intake appointment for every at-risk separating service member before separation date
2. Establish interoperability between DoD (MHS Genesis) and VA (VistA/Cerner) electronic health records for mental health treatment histories, using FHIR standards for defined data elements
3. Create a 12-month "dual enrollment" period where separating service members can access both DoD and VA mental health services simultaneously, eliminating the coverage gap
4. Assign VA transition care coordinators at every major military installation (estimated 75 installations)
5. Implement a 30-day and 90-day post-separation check-in protocol for all separating service members with documented mental health conditions
6. Integrate mental health transition planning into the Transition Assistance Program as a mandatory clinical module, not merely an informational briefing

**Cost/Resources**: $400 million over 5 years ($80 million/year) for transition coordinators, EHR interoperability, dual enrollment administration, and check-in protocols.

**Timeline**: Transition coordinators deployed in Year 1; EHR interoperability achieved by Year 3; full warm handoff protocol operational by Year 2.

**Challenges**: DoD and VA are separate departments with separate budgets, cultures, and IT systems. EHR interoperability has been a challenge for over 20 years. DoD may resist diverting resources to transition support. The dual enrollment concept requires legislative authorization and inter-departmental budget agreement.

**Impact Metrics**: Percentage of at-risk separating service members with a scheduled VA mental health appointment before separation (target: 95%); suicide rate in first year post-separation (target: 25% reduction from baseline).

### Solution 5: Evidence-Based Treatment Fidelity and Outcomes Monitoring

**Description**: Implement a system-wide clinical quality program that tracks treatment fidelity (whether evidence-based protocols are being followed) and patient outcomes (whether veterans are getting better) across all VA and Community Care mental health services. Use these data for performance management, provider feedback, and resource allocation.

**Evidence Base**: Studies of VA clinician practice find that only 30-50% of PTSD treatment sessions follow evidence-based manualized protocols (Finley et al., 2015). The VA's evidence-based psychotherapy dissemination initiative successfully trained clinicians but has not systematically monitored ongoing fidelity. Measurement-based care -- regular symptom tracking with validated instruments like the PCL-5 for PTSD -- improves outcomes by 20-30% in clinical trials (Fortney et al., *JAMA Psychiatry*, 2017). The VA Behavioral Health Outcomes Program (BHOP) provides a framework for outcome tracking that could be expanded system-wide.

**Implementation**:

1. Mandate routine, standardized outcome measurement (PCL-5 for PTSD, PHQ-9 for depression, C-SSRS for suicide risk) at every mental health encounter, with data captured electronically
2. Develop a national VA mental health quality dashboard with facility-level and provider-level metrics, accessible to VA leadership, congressional oversight, and the public
3. Implement annual clinical fidelity audits for evidence-based psychotherapy (CPT, PE, EMDR) at all VAMCs, using both chart review and session recording with clinician consent
4. Tie facility leadership performance evaluations to mental health outcome metrics, not just access metrics
5. Extend outcome monitoring requirements to Community Care mental health providers as a condition of network participation
6. Create a rapid feedback loop: provide clinicians with quarterly reports comparing their patient outcomes to facility and national benchmarks, with clinical consultation available for below-benchmark performance

**Cost/Resources**: $200 million over 5 years ($40 million/year) for measurement infrastructure, dashboard development, audit programs, and training.

**Timeline**: Standardized measurement implemented in Year 1; dashboard operational by Year 2; fidelity audits begin Year 2; Community Care extension by Year 3.

**Challenges**: Clinician resistance to additional documentation burden. "Teaching to the test" risk where providers focus on measured symptoms rather than holistic recovery. Data infrastructure limitations in Community Care settings. Privacy concerns about provider-level outcome data.

**Impact Metrics**: EBP treatment completion rate (target: increase from 35-40% to 55%); proportion of PTSD patients achieving clinically significant improvement (target: increase from 50-60% to 70%); measurement-based care adoption (target: >90% of encounters).

### Solution 6: VA Psychedelic-Assisted Therapy Research and Clinical Integration

**Description**: Accelerate VA research into psychedelic-assisted therapy (MDMA, psilocybin, ketamine) for treatment-resistant PTSD and depression, and establish a pathway for clinical integration if research results warrant. Create a network of VA Psychedelic-Assisted Therapy Centers of Excellence.

**Evidence Base**: Phase 3 trials of MDMA-assisted therapy showed 67% of participants no longer met PTSD diagnostic criteria after three sessions (Mitchell et al., *Nature Medicine*, 2021). VA-funded psilocybin research for treatment-resistant depression is underway. Ketamine and esketamine (Spravato) are already FDA-approved for treatment-resistant depression and are available at a growing number of VA facilities. The Commander John Scott Hannon Act (2020) directed VA to study these therapies. However, FDA's 2024 advisory committee raised concerns about MDMA trial methodology, delaying clinical approval. Separately, the Lykos Therapeutics MDMA therapy protocol, while not yet FDA-approved, has generated sufficient clinical data to inform VA protocol development.

**Implementation**:

1. Fund $500 million over 5 years for VA clinical trials of MDMA-assisted therapy (PTSD), psilocybin-assisted therapy (depression, PTSD), and combination approaches
2. Establish 10 VA Psychedelic-Assisted Therapy Centers of Excellence at VA medical centers with existing research infrastructure (candidates include White River Junction VT, Bronx NY, San Diego CA, Portland OR, Houston TX)
3. Develop VA-specific clinical protocols, training curricula, and safety monitoring systems, drawing on the MAPS protocol and adapting for the VA clinical environment
4. Create a Compassionate Use pathway allowing veterans with treatment-resistant PTSD who have failed at least two evidence-based treatments to access psychedelic-assisted therapy under research protocols
5. Prepare regulatory and clinical infrastructure for rapid scaling if Phase 3 results support approval
6. Train a cohort of 200 VA clinicians in psychedelic-assisted therapy delivery (each session requires 8-12 hours of direct clinical support from two trained therapists)

**Cost/Resources**: $500 million over 5 years ($100 million/year) for clinical trials, Centers of Excellence, training, and regulatory preparation.

**Timeline**: Expanded trials begin Year 1; Centers of Excellence operational by Year 2; Compassionate Use pathway by Year 3; clinical integration decision by Year 5.

**Challenges**: FDA regulatory hurdles; Schedule I classification limits research logistics; political opposition to psychedelic use; need for specialized therapist training (each session requires 8-12 hours of direct support); the resource-intensive nature of the therapy model (2 therapists per patient per session) makes scaling expensive.

**Impact Metrics**: Veterans enrolled in psychedelic-assisted therapy trials (target: 5,000 by Year 5); treatment-resistant PTSD remission rate (target: >50% in trial participants); safety adverse event rate (target: comparable to standard care).

### Solution 7: National Guard and Reserve Mental Health Access Expansion

**Description**: Eliminate the access disparities facing National Guard and Reserve veterans by extending VA mental health eligibility to all Guard/Reserve members who deployed in combat zones, regardless of activation status, and establishing mobile mental health units that serve geographically dispersed Guard/Reserve populations.

**Evidence Base**: Approximately 800,000 National Guard and Reserve members deployed post-9/11, often returning to communities with no military installation or VA facility nearby. Guard/Reserve members face unique barriers: they lose TRICARE coverage shortly after demobilization, they may not identify as "veterans" eligible for VA care, and their employers and communities may not understand their experiences. Research shows Guard/Reserve suicide rates rival or exceed active-duty rates (DoD DSPO Annual Report, 2023). The Yellow Ribbon Reintegration Program, mandated by the National Defense Authorization Act of 2008, provides 90 days of pre- and post-deployment support but does not extend to long-term mental health care.

**Implementation**:

1. Extend VA mental health eligibility to all Guard/Reserve members who served in a combat zone for at least 30 days, regardless of current activation or discharge status
2. Deploy 100 mobile mental health units (telehealth-equipped vans with satellite connectivity) to serve Guard/Reserve populations in areas without VA facilities
3. Integrate VA mental health screening into annual Guard/Reserve drill weekends, using validated brief screening instruments (PC-PTSD-5, PHQ-2, C-SSRS)
4. Fund Yellow Ribbon Reintegration Program expansion with dedicated mental health modules at 30, 60, and 90 days post-mobilization
5. Partner with state National Guard bureaus to establish state-level Guard mental health coordinators (50 positions)
6. Create a Guard/Reserve-specific peer support network, drawing on the experience of the National Guard Bureau's Resilience, Risk Reduction, and Suicide Prevention program

**Cost/Resources**: $600 million over 5 years ($120 million/year) for eligibility expansion ($300 million), mobile units ($150 million), screening programs ($50 million), Yellow Ribbon expansion ($50 million), and state coordinators ($50 million).

**Timeline**: Eligibility expansion in Year 1; mobile units deployed by Year 2; screening integration by Year 2; full program by Year 3.

**Challenges**: Eligibility expansion increases VA caseload without automatic workforce expansion. Mobile units require maintenance and staffing infrastructure. Guard/Reserve identity barriers are cultural, not just structural. State-level coordination requires 50 separate agreements.

**Impact Metrics**: Guard/Reserve mental health engagement rate (target: increase from estimated 25% to 50%); Guard/Reserve suicide rate (target: 20% reduction); mobile unit utilization rate (target: >80% capacity).

### Solution 8: Intensive PTSD Treatment Program Expansion

**Description**: Scale the VA's intensive outpatient PTSD treatment programs -- which compress months of standard therapy into 2-3 week residential or daily programs -- from approximately 20 sites to 75 sites nationwide. These programs achieve treatment completion rates of 80-90% compared to 35-40% for standard weekly therapy.

**Evidence Base**: Programs such as the Road Home Program at Rush University, the Emory Healthcare Veterans Program, and the VA's own intensive outpatient programs have demonstrated completion rates of 80-90% and PTSD remission rates of 55-65%, far exceeding standard outpatient outcomes. The intensive model removes logistical barriers (transportation, scheduling, work conflicts), creates an immersive therapeutic environment, and leverages the veteran preference for "getting it done" rather than months of weekly sessions. The VA's Clinical Resource Hub model has shown that intensive programs can be implemented at mid-size VA facilities with appropriate staffing and infrastructure.

**Implementation**:

1. Expand intensive PTSD treatment programs from ~20 sites to 75 sites, ensuring at least one program per VISN (Veterans Integrated Service Network)
2. Develop standardized intensive program protocols based on the evidence from existing programs, incorporating CPT, PE, or both
3. Fund travel and lodging for veterans who must travel to intensive program sites, using the VA's existing Beneficiary Travel program
4. Integrate intensive programs with the VA's telehealth infrastructure for post-program follow-up and relapse prevention
5. Establish employer partnership agreements to facilitate veterans taking 2-3 weeks of leave for intensive treatment (modeled on the FMLA framework)

**Cost/Resources**: $350 million over 5 years ($70 million/year) for program development ($150 million), staffing ($120 million), veteran travel support ($50 million), and facility modifications ($30 million).

**Timeline**: 15 new sites operational by Year 2; 75 total sites by Year 5.

**Challenges**: Intensive programs require veterans to be away from work and family for 2-3 weeks. Employer cooperation is necessary but not guaranteed. Not all veterans are clinically appropriate for intensive formats. Staffing intensive programs requires experienced clinicians who are already in short supply.

**Impact Metrics**: Number of veterans completing intensive PTSD programs annually (target: 15,000 by Year 5); treatment completion rate (target: >85%); PTSD remission rate (target: >55%).

## International Models

### Australia: Open Arms Veterans and Families Counselling

**Description**: Australia's Open Arms (formerly VVCS) provides free, confidential mental health counseling to all Australian veterans and their families through a national network of counseling centers, a 24/7 crisis line, and group programs. It operates independently from the main veterans hospital system, reducing stigma.

**Results**: Open Arms serves approximately 25,000 clients annually with high satisfaction ratings (>85%). Australia's veteran suicide rate, while still elevated, has declined more consistently than the U.S. rate. The independent counseling structure has been credited with reducing help-seeking barriers. Open Arms' family counseling component is particularly robust, recognizing that veteran mental health is a family-system issue.

**Applicability to U.S.**: The Vet Center model is analogous but serves only 300 locations. Expanding Vet Centers to 600+ locations and enhancing their independence from the VA medical bureaucracy would replicate the Australian model's strengths.

### Canada: Veterans Affairs Canada Operational Stress Injury Clinics

**Description**: Canada operates 10 specialized Operational Stress Injury (OSI) clinics that provide evidence-based treatment for combat-related PTSD and related conditions, staffed by clinicians with mandatory military cultural competency training. The clinics are geographically distributed and accept self-referrals.

**Results**: OSI clinics achieve PTSD treatment completion rates of approximately 65%, significantly higher than the 35-40% rate in the U.S. VA system. The specialized, small-scale model fosters high therapeutic alliance and reduced stigma. Canada's clinics also integrate family therapy and occupational rehabilitation into the treatment model.

**Applicability to U.S.**: The U.S. could create a network of 50-100 specialized PTSD treatment centers (beyond existing Residential Rehabilitation Treatment Programs) modeled on the Canadian approach, emphasizing self-referral and military competency.

### Israel: IDF Mental Health Services and Continuity of Care

**Description**: Israel's military provides mental health services that continue seamlessly from active service through reserve duty (which extends to age 40-45 for most men). Mental health screening occurs at entry, during service, and at regular intervals through reserve duty. The system explicitly treats mental health as a force readiness issue, not a personal failure.

**Results**: Israel's approach to combat stress -- front-line treatment, immediate debriefing, and long-term monitoring -- has produced return-to-duty rates of 60-80% for acute combat stress reactions. The continuity of care model reduces the transition gap that plagues the U.S. system.

**Applicability to U.S.**: The Israeli model of continuous, destigmatized mental health monitoring from enlistment through post-service life is difficult to replicate in the U.S. all-volunteer force structure, but its principles -- normalization, continuity, and integration -- should guide reform.

### United Kingdom: Combat Stress and NHS Veteran Mental Health Services

**Description**: The UK's Combat Stress charity operates a network of residential treatment centers and community outreach programs for UK veterans with PTSD and related conditions. The NHS has also established a Veteran Mental Health Transition, Intervention and Liaison Service (TILS) and a Complex Treatment Service (CTS) to provide specialized veteran mental health care within the public health system.

**Results**: The UK model demonstrates both the potential and the limitations of integrating veteran care into a general public health system. While the NHS veteran services have improved access, outcomes have been mixed, with veterans reporting that NHS providers often lack military cultural understanding. Combat Stress's specialized residential programs achieve better outcomes but serve a limited number of veterans.

**Applicability to U.S.**: The UK experience reinforces the argument that veteran mental health requires specialized, culturally competent care, not merely integration into the general health system. It supports maintaining the VA as a specialized system rather than privatizing veteran care into the general health care market.

## Pilot Programs and Demonstrations

- **VA Intensive PTSD Programs**: The VA has piloted 2-3 week intensive outpatient PTSD treatment programs at approximately 20 sites (e.g., Road Home Program at Rush University, Emory Healthcare Veterans Program). These programs compress months of weekly therapy into intensive daily sessions, achieving treatment completion rates of 80-90% and PTSD remission rates of 55-65% -- far exceeding standard outpatient completion rates.
- **REACH VET (Recovery Engagement and Coordination for Health -- Veterans Enhanced Treatment)**: An AI-driven predictive analytics program that identifies veterans at highest statistical risk of suicide or adverse outcomes and alerts clinicians to proactively reach out. Early evaluation suggests increased contact rates and referrals, though suicide reduction data are still maturing. REACH VET analyzes electronic health record data for 27 predictive variables and has flagged approximately 30,000 veterans for enhanced outreach since its launch.
- **Together With Veterans**: A community-based, upstream suicide prevention program implemented in rural Montana, Alaska, and other high-risk areas. Uses town-level coalitions to address social determinants of suicide. Initial results show increased help-seeking and community engagement. The program is being expanded to additional rural and tribal communities through the Fox Suicide Prevention Grant Program.
- **ATLAS (Accessing Telehealth through Local Area Stations)**: A VA program that places telehealth access points in community locations like VFW posts, American Legion halls, and Walmart stores, enabling veterans in rural areas to connect with VA mental health providers via video without traveling to a VA facility. ATLAS currently operates at approximately 50 locations and has demonstrated that convenient, community-based access points increase telehealth utilization among rural veterans.

## Sequencing and Prioritization

### Quick Wins (0-2 years)

- Triple the Fox Suicide Prevention Grant Program ($500 million annually)
- Increase EDRP loan repayment to $300,000 for shortage areas
- Launch mandatory military cultural competency training for Community Care providers
- Deploy first 50 mobile mental health units for Guard/Reserve populations
- Expand Vet Center eligibility and hours
- Implement standardized outcome measurement at all VA mental health encounters
- Scale ATLAS telehealth access points from 50 to 200 locations

### Medium-term Reforms (2-5 years)

- Hire 10,000 new VA mental health professionals through the National Workforce Initiative
- Achieve DoD-VA EHR interoperability for mental health records
- Establish 10 Psychedelic-Assisted Therapy Centers of Excellence
- Implement outcome-based quality monitoring across VA and Community Care
- Deploy veteran suicide prevention coordinators in 800 counties
- Expand intensive PTSD treatment programs to 75 sites

### Long-term Transformation (5-10 years)

- Reduce veteran suicide rate by 40% from 2021 baseline
- Achieve <14-day mental health access standard nationwide
- Integrate psychedelic-assisted therapy into standard VA care (if evidence supports)
- Eliminate the DoD-VA transition gap through full warm handoff protocol
- Establish a permanent mental health workforce pipeline that prevents future shortage cycles
- Achieve formal diagnostic recognition of moral injury and establish dedicated treatment protocols

## Complementary Policies

- **Housing**: Expanding HUD-VASH (Housing and Urban Development-VA Supportive Housing) vouchers, as housing instability is a major risk factor for veteran suicide and mental health crisis. An estimated 50-70% of homeless veterans have a diagnosable mental health or substance use condition.
- **Employment**: Strengthening veteran employment programs and employer education about mental health accommodation. The Department of Labor's Veterans' Employment and Training Service (VETS) and programs like Hiring Our Heroes should integrate mental health awareness into employer engagement.
- **Criminal justice**: Expanding veteran treatment courts, which divert veterans with mental health or substance use conditions from incarceration to treatment. Over 600 veteran treatment courts currently operate, but coverage is uneven and many jurisdictions lack them entirely.
- **Disability compensation reform**: Addressing the perception that clinical improvement threatens disability ratings, potentially through a "total and permanent" designation that removes re-evaluation anxiety for veterans with chronic conditions, or through a "recovery-positive" rating system that rewards treatment engagement without reducing compensation.
- **Firearm policy**: Supporting voluntary lethal means safety programs as a complement to, not substitute for, broader firearm safety policy. The VA's partnership with the NSSF and American Foundation for Suicide Prevention provides a model for culturally appropriate engagement.
- **Social connection**: Funding community-based programs (Team Red White & Blue, The Mission Continues, Team Rubicon) that provide veterans with purpose, community, and social connection -- upstream protective factors against suicide that clinical treatment alone cannot provide.
