# Veterans Mental Health: Opposition to Reform

Proposals to expand and reform veterans mental health services -- while broadly popular across the political spectrum -- face resistance from those who believe that the VA system is inherently flawed and should be privatized, that government mental health programs are wasteful or counterproductive, or that specific reform proposals (such as psychedelic-assisted therapy or lethal means safety) conflict with ideological or cultural values.

---

## Who Opposes Reform

### Organized Opposition

| Actor | Interest | Resources | Tactics |
|-------|----------|-----------|---------|
| Privatization advocates (e.g., Concerned Veterans for America, Koch Network) | Replace VA health care with private-sector alternatives funded by vouchers | Tens of millions in annual advocacy spending | Policy papers, political advertising, congressional lobbying, grassroots mobilization |
| Pharmaceutical industry (selective opposition) | Protect medication-first treatment paradigm; resist psychedelic-assisted therapy legalization | $374 million in total lobbying (2023, OpenSecrets) | Lobbying against psychedelic rescheduling, funding for medication studies, influence on clinical guidelines |
| Firearm rights organizations (NRA, GOA, NAGR) | Prevent any linkage between mental health policy and firearm access | NRA: ~$15 million in lobbying (2023); GOA: ~$2 million | Oppose lethal means safety provisions, VA firearms counseling, red flag laws for veterans |
| Fiscal conservatives (selected members of Congress, Heritage Foundation) | Contain or reduce VA spending growth | Congressional voting power, policy research | Budget amendments, means-testing proposals, cost-benefit analysis critiques |
| Community care industry (Optum, TriWest, private hospital chains) | Expand private-sector share of veteran health care spending | Lobbying, campaign contributions, industry associations | Advocate for broader MISSION Act access standards, resist VA quality mandates for community providers |
| VA institutional bureaucracy (selective resistance) | Preserve existing organizational structures, leadership positions, and operational patterns | Control over implementation, data reporting, and internal policy | Slow-walk implementation of reforms that threaten existing arrangements, shape data narratives |

### Ideological Opposition

- **Libertarian/Free-Market**: Government-run health care, including the VA, is inherently inefficient. Veterans would be better served by vouchers or health savings accounts that let them choose private providers in a competitive market. The VA's monopoly suppresses innovation and insulates poor-performing facilities from accountability. Organizations like the Cato Institute and Reason Foundation publish analyses arguing that the VA should be restructured into a payer rather than a provider.
- **Anti-Government Conservative**: The VA bureaucracy is too large, too slow, and too resistant to reform. Expanding its budget rewards failure. Better to shrink the VA and let the private market deliver care more efficiently. This view gained particular prominence after the 2014 Phoenix VA scandal and has been institutionalized in organizations like Concerned Veterans for America, which has received substantial funding from the Koch brothers' political network.
- **Traditionalist/Cultural Conservative**: Some mental health interventions -- particularly psychedelic-assisted therapy, progressive approaches to moral injury, and gender-responsive care for LGBTQ+ veterans -- conflict with traditional values. Opposition also extends to mental health approaches perceived as "medicalizing" normal responses to combat stress. Within military culture itself, some senior leaders and veteran communities resist the expansion of mental health programs because they view psychological resilience as a matter of character, not medicine.
- **Fiscal Hawk**: VA mental health spending has more than doubled in the past decade without a proportional reduction in veteran suicide. Additional spending without structural reform is throwing good money after bad. This argument is advanced by the Congressional Budget Office's cost analyses and by think tanks like the American Enterprise Institute, which call for performance-based budgeting that ties VA mental health appropriations to measurable outcome improvements.
- **Anti-Regulation Conservative**: Mandatory military cultural competency certification for community care providers represents government overreach and will drive providers out of the VA network, reducing access rather than improving quality. The administrative burden of certification, documentation, and outcome reporting will fall disproportionately on small practices and rural providers who are already in short supply.

## Opposition Arguments and Rebuttals

### Argument 1: The VA Is Unfixable -- Privatize Veteran Care

**Claim**: The VA is a government-run monopoly that consistently fails veterans. The 2014 Phoenix scandal, persistent wait times, and high suicide rates demonstrate that the system cannot be reformed from within. Veterans should receive vouchers to access private health care.

**Evidence Cited**: Phoenix VA scandal (2014); VA OIG reports on wait times; veteran satisfaction surveys showing frustration; 17.5 veteran suicides per day. Concerned Veterans for America's 2016 report "Fixing Veterans Health Care" argued for converting the VA into a government-funded insurer that pays private providers.

**Reality**: The VA consistently outperforms the private sector on clinical quality measures. A 2018 RAND study comparing VA to non-VA health care across 9 dimensions found that the VA performed "the same or significantly better" in nearly all categories, including mental health (Tanielian et al., RAND, 2018). The VA's integrated mental health model -- with primary care-mental health integration, specialized PTSD clinics, and a dedicated suicide prevention infrastructure -- has no private-sector equivalent. The 2014 scandal was a genuine failure, but it resulted in major reforms including the MISSION Act community care expansion. A 2023 VA Survey of Healthcare Experiences of Patients (SHEP) found that veteran satisfaction with VA mental health care averaged 4.1 out of 5, comparable to or higher than private-sector benchmarks.

**Rebuttal**: Privatization would destroy the only health care system in the nation with deep expertise in combat-related PTSD, TBI, MST, and military sexual trauma. Community providers who lack military cultural competency training (fewer than 10% have it, per RAND 2023) would be treating the most complex mental health patients in America. The experience of countries that have experimented with privatizing military/veteran health care -- most notably the United Kingdom's outsourcing of veteran mental health to the National Health Service without specialized veteran programs -- has produced worse outcomes for veterans with combat-related conditions. The real solution is targeted reform -- not demolition -- of a system that, when working properly, delivers superior mental health care.

### Argument 2: More Spending Has Not Reduced Veteran Suicide

**Claim**: VA mental health spending has increased dramatically -- from approximately $6.2 billion in FY2014 to $12.5 billion in FY2024 -- yet veteran suicide rates have remained essentially flat. This proves that additional funding is not the answer.

**Evidence Cited**: VA budget data showing spending growth; VA NSPAR data showing stable suicide rates (~17.5 per day); RAND and GAO reports on VA program effectiveness. Heritage Foundation analyses have argued that the VA's suicide prevention programs lack rigorous outcome evaluation.

**Reality**: Veteran suicide is driven by complex, interacting factors -- social isolation, chronic pain, substance use, housing instability, firearm access, economic distress -- that clinical mental health treatment alone cannot fully address. The spending increase has funded genuine improvements: the Veterans Crisis Line now answers over 803,000 calls annually (FY2023), dispatching approximately 47,000 active rescues; evidence-based PTSD treatment has been disseminated to all VAMCs; telehealth now accounts for 37% of mental health encounters. The stabilization of suicide rates during a period when civilian suicide rates also increased may itself represent a partial success. Additionally, 60.6% of veteran suicide decedents were not using VA care -- spending on the VA system, by definition, cannot directly prevent suicides among people the system does not serve.

**Rebuttal**: The argument conflates spending on VA clinical care with comprehensive suicide prevention. The most impactful suicide prevention interventions -- lethal means safety, community-based outreach to non-VA-enrolled veterans, upstream social determinants (housing, employment, social connection) -- have received far less investment than clinical treatment. The Staff Sergeant Parker Gordon Fox Suicide Prevention Grant Program, the first major federal investment in community-based veteran suicide prevention, was not funded until 2022, and its initial allocation of $174 million is modest relative to the scale of the crisis. Spending more on the right interventions, targeted at the right populations, is the answer. Spending less would worsen outcomes.

### Argument 3: Psychedelic-Assisted Therapy Is Dangerous and Unproven

**Claim**: Proposals to use MDMA, psilocybin, and other psychedelics in veteran mental health treatment are premature, scientifically unproven, and morally problematic. These are Schedule I controlled substances with high potential for abuse, and the VA should not be experimenting on vulnerable veterans.

**Evidence Cited**: DEA scheduling classification; limited sample sizes in clinical trials; historical association of psychedelics with counterculture; FDA concerns about MDMA trial methodology (2024 advisory committee vote). Some religious conservative organizations have specifically objected to the use of mind-altering substances in government-funded treatment programs.

**Reality**: Phase 3 clinical trials of MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD have shown substantial clinical improvement in treatment-resistant cases. The Multidisciplinary Association for Psychedelic Studies (MAPS) published results showing that 67% of participants no longer met PTSD diagnostic criteria after three MDMA-assisted therapy sessions, compared to 32% in the placebo group (Mitchell et al., *Nature Medicine*, 2021). Psilocybin has shown significant promise for treatment-resistant depression. The FDA granted MDMA "Breakthrough Therapy" designation in 2017, and Congress directed the VA to study psychedelic-assisted therapy through the Commander John Scott Hannon Act (2020). However, the FDA's 2024 advisory committee raised legitimate concerns about trial methodology and risk of abuse, and MDMA was not approved for general clinical use.

**Rebuttal**: The "unproven" framing ignores the distinction between Schedule I classification (a legal/political designation dating from the Controlled Substances Act of 1970) and clinical evidence. Schedule I classification means the DEA has determined the substance has "no currently accepted medical use" -- but that determination is a regulatory conclusion, not a scientific one, and it has been challenged by the very FDA that oversees drug approval. Thousands of veterans with treatment-resistant PTSD have not responded to CPT, PE, or medication, and dismissing a promising treatment modality because of drug scheduling politics denies them access to potentially life-saving therapy. The appropriate response is rigorous, VA-supervised research and, if results warrant, phased clinical integration -- not blanket prohibition. Bipartisan congressional support for veteran psychedelic research, including from conservative members like Rep. Dan Crenshaw (R-TX) and Sen. Dan Sullivan (R-AK), demonstrates that this issue transcends traditional political alignments.

### Argument 4: Lethal Means Safety Is Gun Control by Another Name

**Claim**: VA suicide prevention programs that address firearm access -- including safe storage counseling, voluntary gun lock distribution, and "means restriction" language -- are backdoor gun control. The government should not be counseling veterans about their firearms or encouraging them to surrender or lock up their weapons.

**Evidence Cited**: Second Amendment rights; VA "firearms questions" in clinical encounters; government distrust; slippery slope concerns about red flag laws. The NRA and Gun Owners of America have specifically opposed VA provisions related to firearms counseling and have lobbied for legislation prohibiting VA clinicians from asking patients about firearm ownership.

**Reality**: Lethal means safety is the single most evidence-based suicide prevention intervention at the population level. Time between suicidal impulse and attempt averages less than 10 minutes in many cases, and 90% of people who survive a suicide attempt do not go on to die by suicide. Reducing access to the most lethal method during a crisis period saves lives without permanently removing firearms. The VA's approach -- counseling veterans about voluntary safe storage, distributing free gun locks, partnering with firearm retailers -- is explicitly voluntary and does not involve confiscation, mandatory storage, or reporting to any database.

**Rebuttal**: With 62.8% of veteran suicides involving firearms, any suicide prevention strategy that ignores this reality is willfully incomplete. The VA's lethal means safety programs are designed by and with veterans and firearm owners, use culturally appropriate language, and are entirely voluntary. Partnering with organizations like the National Shooting Sports Foundation (NSSF) and the American Foundation for Suicide Prevention demonstrates that firearm safety and suicide prevention are compatible goals. The NSSF itself has embraced suicide prevention messaging through its "#GunShopProject" and voluntary retailer training programs, recognizing that reducing firearm suicide protects Second Amendment rights by demonstrating that the firearms community takes the issue seriously. The alternative -- ignoring the single largest risk factor in veteran suicide -- is a policy of willful negligence.

### Argument 5: Mental Health Treatment Medicalizes Normal Stress

**Claim**: Not every veteran who experienced combat stress has a mental disorder. Expanding mental health services and aggressive screening pathologizes normal human responses to difficult experiences. Many veterans are resilient and do not need or want clinical intervention.

**Evidence Cited**: Resilience research showing that 70-80% of combat veterans do not develop PTSD; concerns about over-diagnosis; veteran testimonials about being treated as "broken." Some military leaders have argued that the emphasis on PTSD and mental health injury undermines warrior culture and combat readiness.

**Reality**: This argument contains a kernel of truth that is often weaponized to justify inaction. The majority of veterans are indeed resilient, and not every combat veteran needs mental health treatment. However, the 11-20% of post-9/11 veterans who do develop PTSD, the 11% with major depression, and the 11% with substance use disorder represent millions of people with real clinical needs. The goal is not to diagnose every veteran but to ensure that those who need care can access it without barriers or stigma.

**Rebuttal**: Acknowledging resilience and providing access to care are not contradictory. A well-designed system screens universally, treats those who need it, and leaves others alone. The greater danger is a system that fails to identify and treat veterans in crisis -- as evidenced by the 60.6% of veteran suicide decedents who were not using VA care. The claim that screening "medicalizes" normal stress ignores the documented clinical reality: PTSD, TBI, and MST are biological injuries with measurable neurological correlates, not labels applied to normal stress responses. Failing to screen because some individuals are resilient is equivalent to refusing cancer screening because most people do not have cancer.

### Argument 6: Community Care Quality Standards Will Reduce Access

**Claim**: Requiring community mental health providers to complete military cultural competency certification as a condition of VA Community Care enrollment will drive providers out of the network, reducing access for veterans who already face long wait times.

**Evidence Cited**: Provider burden concerns; shortage of community mental health providers in rural areas; precedent of providers declining Medicaid contracts due to administrative requirements.

**Reality**: The concern about provider attrition has some empirical basis. When certification or credentialing requirements are imposed on provider networks, some providers choose to leave rather than comply. In Medicaid, for example, burdensome prior authorization and documentation requirements have contributed to provider shortages. However, the current situation -- where fewer than 10% of community mental health providers have any military cultural training -- is producing care that is clinically inadequate for veteran patients. An untrained provider delivering generic talk therapy to a veteran with combat-related PTSD is not providing meaningful access; it is providing the appearance of access while delivering substandard care.

**Rebuttal**: The solution is to design certification requirements that are rigorous but not burdensome: free online training, CEU credits that satisfy state licensing requirements, reasonable completion timelines (18-month grace period), and tiered certification options. Making the training free and professionally valuable reduces the disincentive to participate. The alternative -- continuing to refer veterans to providers who do not understand military culture -- is a form of access theater that substitutes volume for quality.

## Historical Patterns of Opposition

Opposition to veterans mental health reform has followed a consistent pattern across American history:

1. **"Not a real condition"**: Every generation of combat veterans has faced skepticism about the legitimacy of their psychological injuries. Civil War soldiers were told they had weak hearts. WWI soldiers were accused of cowardice. Vietnam veterans were dismissed as drug addicts. Post-9/11 veterans with TBI were told their symptoms were psychosomatic. Each time, clinical evidence eventually overcame skepticism, but not before years of unnecessary suffering. Today, the emerging recognition of moral injury faces similar skepticism: because it is not yet a formal DSM diagnosis, some argue it is not a "real" condition requiring treatment.

2. **"Too expensive"**: Budget objections to VA mental health expansion recur with every authorization cycle, despite broad rhetorical support for veterans. The fiscal argument is most effective during periods of deficit concern and least effective in the immediate aftermath of scandal or crisis. The irony is that inadequate mental health treatment is far more expensive than adequate treatment when downstream costs -- emergency services, incarceration, homelessness, lost productivity, disability compensation -- are included. RAND estimated the total societal cost of PTSD and depression among post-9/11 veterans at $4-6.2 billion over two years, far exceeding the cost of evidence-based treatment.

3. **"Private sector does it better"**: The push to privatize VA services intensified after the 2014 scandal and has been advanced primarily by free-market organizations with broader anti-government agendas. The evidence does not support the premise, but the argument resonates with general anti-government sentiment. The privatization movement's funding base -- heavily concentrated in political networks that oppose government programs across all domains -- suggests that veteran care is a vehicle for a broader ideological project rather than a genuine effort to improve veteran outcomes.

4. **"Veterans are being used as political pawns"**: Both proponents and opponents of reform accuse the other side of exploiting veterans for political purposes. Privatization advocates claim the VA defends its turf at veterans' expense. VA defenders claim privatizers use veteran suffering to advance anti-government ideology. Both accusations contain elements of truth, and the resulting cynicism about political motives can paralyze reform efforts.

5. **"The real problem is upstream"**: Some opponents of VA mental health expansion argue that the real solution lies in ending unnecessary wars, reducing deployment tempo, or reforming military culture -- not in expanding treatment for injuries that should have been prevented. While there is truth in the prevention argument, it is used to deflect from the immediate need to treat millions of veterans who are already injured.

## Counter-Strategies

### Political Strategies

- **Bipartisan framing**: Veterans mental health is one of the few remaining bipartisan policy areas. Reform proposals should be framed in terms of honoring service, supporting military families, and fulfilling the nation's obligation -- language that resonates across party lines. The Commander John Scott Hannon Act's bipartisan passage in 2020 provides a recent model for successful coalition-building.
- **VSO mobilization**: Veteran Service Organizations are the most credible messengers. When the VFW, American Legion, and DAV jointly oppose privatization, it carries more political weight than any policy analysis. The "Independent Budget" -- jointly produced by DAV, PVA, and VFW -- provides a unified advocacy platform that is difficult for Congress to dismiss.
- **Veteran voices**: Personal testimony from veterans and families is the most effective tool for overcoming opposition. Members of Congress who are veterans themselves (a shrinking but still significant group) are natural champions. The increasing willingness of high-profile veterans to speak publicly about mental health -- including Medal of Honor recipients, senior officers, and public figures -- has begun to shift the cultural conversation.
- **State-level advocacy**: When federal reform stalls, state-level action can build momentum and create models. States like Texas, California, and Virginia have established state-funded veteran mental health programs that supplement VA services. State-level success creates political pressure for federal action and demonstrates that expanded programs work.

### Communication Strategies

- **Data-driven messaging**: Lead with specific statistics (17.5 per day, 62.8% firearms, 60.6% not in VA care) that convey urgency without partisan framing. These numbers are powerful because they are produced by the VA itself and are not subject to partisan dispute.
- **Reframe "spending" as "investment"**: Position VA mental health funding as an investment that reduces downstream costs in homelessness, criminal justice, emergency services, and disability compensation. The cost-of-inaction framing is supported by RAND economic analyses showing that adequate treatment is cheaper than the consequences of inadequate treatment.
- **Lethal means as veteran safety**: Frame firearm safe storage not as "gun control" but as "veteran safety" and "protecting our brothers and sisters in arms." The VA's partnership with the NSSF demonstrates that this framing is viable even within the firearms community.
- **Innovation narrative**: Frame psychedelic-assisted therapy and other emerging treatments not as cultural experiments but as cutting-edge medical innovation driven by the urgent needs of treatment-resistant veterans. The language of "breakthrough therapy" (the FDA's own designation for MDMA) and "treatment-resistant" (which implies that conventional approaches have failed, not that the patient is difficult) is clinically precise and politically effective.

### Policy Design Strategies

- **Hybrid models**: Combine VA excellence with community access through robust quality requirements for community providers, rather than forcing a binary choice between VA-only and privatization. The MISSION Act framework already embodies this hybrid approach; the task is to add quality accountability to the existing access framework.
- **Pilot-then-scale**: For controversial interventions like psychedelic-assisted therapy, begin with VA-supervised research and clinical trials, building an evidence base that makes opposition untenable. The Commander John Scott Hannon Act's research mandate creates the institutional pathway for this approach.
- **Voluntary frameworks**: Design lethal means safety, screening, and outreach programs as opt-in and veteran-centered, removing the basis for "government overreach" objections. Voluntary programs that are well-designed and culturally appropriate achieve high participation rates while preserving individual autonomy.
- **Sunset and review clauses**: Include mandatory review and reauthorization requirements in reform legislation, reassuring fiscal conservatives that programs will be evaluated for effectiveness rather than perpetuated indefinitely. Time-limited authorizations create built-in accountability and provide opportunities to adjust programs based on evidence.
