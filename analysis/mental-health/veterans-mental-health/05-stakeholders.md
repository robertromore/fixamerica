# Veterans Mental Health: Stakeholders

The veterans mental health landscape is shaped by a complex ecosystem of federal agencies, congressional committees, veteran service organizations, health care providers, researchers, and -- most importantly -- the veterans and families whose lives are directly affected.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Veterans with mental health conditions | Direct recipients of care (or lack thereof); bear the clinical, social, and economic consequences of inadequate treatment | ~3.5 million with diagnosable conditions; 1.7 million in VA MH care (FY2023) | Timely access, quality treatment, reduced stigma, disability compensation |
| Veteran families and caregivers | Secondary trauma, caregiving burden, family disruption, economic strain from untreated or undertreated conditions | ~5.5 million family members of veterans with MH conditions | Caregiver support, family therapy access, respite care, financial stability |
| Veterans not engaged with VA | The 60.6% of veteran suicide decedents who were not recent VA users; highest-risk population least connected to support | Estimated 10+ million veterans not enrolled or not active in VA | Outreach that meets them where they are; removal of enrollment and eligibility barriers |
| Post-9/11 veterans | Highest rates of PTSD, TBI, and MST; youngest cohort with decades of care needs ahead | ~4.1 million | Evidence-based PTSD/TBI treatment, transition support, career services |
| Women veterans | Fastest-growing veteran demographic; disproportionate MST burden; rising suicide rates | ~2 million (10% of veteran population, growing) | MST treatment, gender-responsive care, privacy, childcare during appointments |
| National Guard and Reserve | Deployed at unprecedented rates post-9/11 but lack full-time military health benefits; geographic dispersion compounds access challenges | ~800,000 deployed post-9/11 | Equal access to VA MH services, TRICARE continuity, employer support |
| Veterans with OTH discharges | Historically excluded from VA care despite often being discharged for behaviors symptomatic of untreated MH conditions | ~500,000 post-9/11 | Access to VA mental health care, discharge upgrade assistance, removal of stigma |

### Secondary Stakeholders

- **Active-duty service members**: Future veterans whose mental health needs are shaped by current military service conditions. Approximately 1.3 million active-duty personnel and 800,000 reservists. The mental health care they receive -- or do not receive -- during service directly determines the severity of conditions they bring to the VA system at separation.
- **Veteran communities (peer networks)**: Informal support structures including veteran-led nonprofits, peer support groups, and online communities that often serve as first points of contact. Organizations like Team Red White & Blue, The Mission Continues, and Team Rubicon provide purpose, community, and social connection that function as upstream mental health supports even when they are not explicitly clinical programs.
- **Employers of veterans**: Workplace accommodation, understanding of mental health conditions, and willingness to hire veterans are directly affected by the quality of veteran mental health systems. The Department of Labor's Veterans' Employment and Training Service (VETS) and programs like the Hiring Our Heroes initiative work to connect veterans with employment, but many employers lack understanding of how PTSD, TBI, or MST may affect workplace performance and what accommodations are available.
- **Taxpayers**: Fund the $12.5 billion VA mental health budget (FY2024) and bear the economic costs of inadequate treatment through homelessness, criminal justice involvement, and lost productivity. RAND estimated the total economic cost of PTSD and depression among post-9/11 veterans at $4-6.2 billion over two years (Tanielian & Jaycox, 2008), a figure that has likely grown as the veteran population has expanded.
- **Criminal justice system**: Veterans are overrepresented in jails and prisons, with an estimated 8% of the incarcerated population being veterans. Approximately 55-75% of incarcerated veterans have a diagnosable mental health or substance use condition. Veteran treatment courts, operating in over 600 jurisdictions, divert veterans from incarceration to treatment, but their availability is uneven and their funding uncertain.

### Key Organizations and Institutions

- **Department of Veterans Affairs (VA)**: The central institutional player, operating the nation's largest integrated health care system. The VA's mental health programs are led by the Office of Mental Health and Suicide Prevention (OMHSP) and the National Center for PTSD, headquartered in White River Junction, Vermont. OMHSP is responsible for the National Strategy for Preventing Veteran Suicide, the Veterans Crisis Line, REACH VET, and the Staff Sergeant Parker Gordon Fox Suicide Prevention Grants program.
- **Department of Defense (DoD)**: Responsible for active-duty mental health care and the transition process. The Defense Health Agency (DHA) and Military Health System (MHS) provide mental health services to active-duty personnel and their families. The DoD's Defense Suicide Prevention Office (DSPO) tracks active-duty and reserve suicide data and coordinates military suicide prevention programs.
- **Veterans Health Administration (VHA)**: The health care arm of the VA, operating 171 medical centers and over 1,150 Community-Based Outpatient Clinics. VHA's Academic Affiliations program partners with 131 medical schools and 1,800 colleges, making the VA the largest provider of health care training in the United States. This affiliation network is the primary mechanism through which the VA recruits and trains its mental health workforce.
- **SAMHSA**: The Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration administers the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline and provides grant funding for veteran-specific mental health programs at the state and community level. SAMHSA's Community Mental Health Services Block Grant funds state mental health authorities that serve many veterans who do not use the VA.
- **National Center for PTSD**: Established by Congress in 1989 and housed within the VA, the National Center for PTSD is the world's leading research and educational institution for PTSD and trauma-related conditions. Its seven divisions conduct research, develop clinical guidelines, train clinicians, and produce public education materials. The Center's PTSD consultation program provides expert clinical guidance to VA and community providers.
- **VA Office of Inspector General (OIG)**: The OIG provides independent oversight of VA operations, including mental health programs. OIG reports have been among the most influential drivers of reform, documenting wait time manipulation (2014), staffing shortages (annually), and clinical quality concerns. The OIG's annual determination of occupational staffing shortages is the authoritative source for VA vacancy data.

### Influencers

- **Veteran Service Organizations (VSOs)**: The most powerful advocacy force in veteran policy. Major VSOs include:

| Organization | Membership | Founded | Key Focus | Annual Budget |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Veterans of Foreign Wars (VFW) | ~1.5 million | 1899 | Benefits, health care, legislation | ~$200 million |
| American Legion | ~1.6 million | 1919 | Advocacy, community service, VA oversight | ~$350 million |
| Disabled American Veterans (DAV) | ~1 million | 1920 | Disability benefits, health care access | ~$285 million |
| Iraq and Afghanistan Veterans of America (IAVA) | ~425,000 | 2004 | Post-9/11 veteran policy, suicide prevention | ~$15 million |
| Vietnam Veterans of America (VVA) | ~85,000 | 1978 | Agent Orange, PTSD, aging veteran care | ~$30 million |
| Wounded Warrior Project (WWP) | N/A (service org) | 2003 | Mental health programs, peer support, direct services | ~$350 million |
| Paralyzed Veterans of America (PVA) | ~18,000 | 1946 | Spinal cord injury, disability rights | ~$100 million |
| Student Veterans of America (SVA) | ~1,500 chapters | 2008 | Education, transition, student veteran support | ~$10 million |

- **Nonprofit Mental Health Providers**: Several nonprofit organizations provide direct mental health services to veterans outside the VA system, reaching populations that the VA cannot. The Cohen Veterans Network operates 22 free mental health clinics for post-9/11 veterans and families. Give an Hour coordinates a network of volunteer clinicians providing free sessions. The Headstrong Project offers stigma-free PTSD treatment. These organizations collectively serve tens of thousands of veterans annually and often reach veterans who will not or cannot access the VA.
- **Academic researchers**: VA-affiliated researchers at institutions such as the National Center for PTSD, VA Health Services Research & Development (HSR&D), and leading universities (e.g., Boston University CTE Center, STRONG STAR consortium at UT Health San Antonio, Emory Healthcare Veterans Program) generate the evidence base that drives policy. The VA's research enterprise funds approximately $2 billion annually across all disciplines, with mental health consistently among the top-funded areas.
- **Media and journalism**: Investigative reporting has been a primary driver of reform. *CBS News* (2007 suicide investigation), *ProPublica* and *The Arizona Republic* (2014 Phoenix scandal), and ongoing coverage by *Military Times*, *Stars and Stripes*, and *The War Horse* shape public awareness. The War Horse, a nonprofit newsroom founded by Marine veteran Thomas Brennan in 2018, has become a particularly important voice in veteran mental health journalism, producing investigations that have prompted congressional action.

## Stakeholder Interests and Alignment

### Aligned Interests (Potential Coalition Partners)

| Coalition | Shared Interest | Strength |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| VSOs + Congressional Veterans' Affairs Committees + VA | Increased VA mental health funding and staffing | Strong |
| Veterans + families + mental health professional associations (APA, NASW, AMA) | Workforce expansion (training pipeline, loan repayment, competitive pay) | Strong |
| Bipartisan congressional members + VSOs + DoD | Improved DoD-VA transition and shared electronic health records | Moderate |
| Veteran advocates + firearm safety organizations + VA | Lethal means safety (voluntary safe storage) for suicide prevention | Moderate |
| Research institutions + VA + pharmaceutical/biotech companies | Clinical trials for psychedelic-assisted therapy and other emerging treatments | Moderate |
| Nonprofit mental health providers (Cohen Veterans Network, Headstrong) + VA | Expanding access to military-competent care outside VA facilities | Moderate |
| Rural health advocates + VA Office of Rural Health + telehealth companies | Closing the rural mental health access gap through technology | Moderate |
| Veteran treatment courts + VA + state criminal justice systems | Diverting veterans from incarceration to mental health treatment | Moderate |

### Conflicting Interests

- **VA institutional leadership vs. Community Care advocates**: The VA has an institutional interest in maintaining its role as the primary provider of veteran health care, while some advocates and legislators push for expanding community care and even privatizing VA services. The VA's institutional survival incentive can lead it to resist community care expansion even when VA capacity is genuinely insufficient.
- **VSOs vs. each other**: Different VSOs represent different eras and demographics of veterans. The American Legion and VFW tend toward more traditional advocacy, while IAVA and newer organizations push for innovative approaches like psychedelic therapy and technology-enabled care. Generational differences in advocacy style and policy priorities sometimes produce competing legislative agendas.
- **Disability compensation advocates vs. treatment-focused reformers**: Some stakeholders prioritize increasing disability compensation and expanding eligibility, while others argue that the system should invest more in treatment that reduces symptoms and improves functioning, regardless of compensation status. This tension is particularly acute in discussions of PTSD disability ratings, where the perception that clinical improvement could reduce compensation creates a disincentive for treatment engagement.
- **Firearm rights groups vs. suicide prevention advocates**: Lethal means safety -- the most evidence-based approach to reducing firearm suicide -- faces opposition from Second Amendment organizations that view any discussion of firearms in a mental health context as a potential pathway to gun confiscation. This opposition has blocked legislative provisions that would fund VA safe storage programs and limited the VA's ability to counsel veterans about firearm safety during crisis periods.
- **DoD vs. VA on transition responsibility**: Both departments acknowledge the transition gap but disagree on which department bears primary responsibility for closing it. The DoD tends to view its obligation as ending at separation, while the VA expects service members to initiate contact. Neither department has sufficient incentive to absorb the cost and administrative burden of a seamless transition system.
- **Third-party administrators vs. VA direct care**: Optum and TriWest, the companies that manage the VA's community care network under the MISSION Act, have a financial interest in expanding community care utilization because their revenue is tied to the volume of referrals they process. This creates a structural incentive that may not align with the VA's interest in keeping care in-house when VA capacity is available.

### The Iron Triangle

The veterans mental health policy space features a classic iron triangle:

1. **Congressional Veterans' Affairs Committees**: Control authorization and appropriations
2. **VA bureaucracy**: Implements programs, controls data, and shapes policy options
3. **Veteran Service Organizations**: Mobilize political pressure, provide testimony, and monitor implementation

This triangle can be highly effective at securing funding increases but can also resist structural reform that threatens existing institutional arrangements. Outside actors -- academic researchers, emerging veteran organizations, community providers, and technology companies -- often struggle to break into this closed policy-making loop.

The iron triangle is reinforced by a revolving door: former congressional staff move to VSOs or VA leadership, former VA officials join VSOs or lobbying firms, and VSO leaders are appointed to VA advisory positions. This interconnection creates shared institutional knowledge but can also produce groupthink that resists disruptive innovation.

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on veterans mental health policy:

- **Lobbying**: VSOs maintain permanent lobbying offices in Washington, D.C. The American Legion and VFW collectively spend over $5 million annually on lobbying (OpenSecrets, 2023). These organizations have unmatched access to the Senate and House Veterans' Affairs Committees. The Independent Budget, a joint publication of DAV, PVA, and VFW, presents a unified VSO budget proposal that Congress uses as a benchmark for VA appropriations.
- **Congressional testimony**: VSO leaders testify at nearly every VA oversight hearing. VA officials testify regularly on budget and performance. Academic experts are occasionally invited but have less routine access. The "Independent Budget" hearing, where DAV, PVA, and VFW jointly present their recommended VA budget to Congress, is one of the most influential annual events in veteran policy.
- **Campaign engagement**: VSOs do not endorse candidates but issue voter guides and grade legislators on veteran-related votes. Individual veterans are highly engaged voters, particularly in swing states with large veteran populations (Florida, Arizona, North Carolina, Georgia, Texas). The veteran vote, while not monolithic, is politically significant: an estimated 18% of voters in the 2020 presidential election were veterans or military family members.
- **Public advocacy and media**: High-profile veteran advocates, military families, and media investigations have been the most effective mechanism for catalyzing reform. The 2014 Phoenix VA scandal, driven by media reporting, produced more legislative change than years of policy advocacy. Individual veteran stories -- publicized through social media, documentaries, and congressional testimony -- create the emotional urgency that moves legislators to act.
- **Expertise and data**: The VA's research enterprise and the National Center for PTSD control much of the clinical evidence base. Academic affiliates conduct the studies that validate (or challenge) VA treatment protocols. This creates both a knowledge advantage and a potential conflict of interest: the VA funds the research that evaluates its own programs, and researchers whose careers depend on VA funding may be reluctant to publish findings that are deeply critical of VA operations.
- **Direct service provision**: Nonprofit organizations that provide direct mental health services to veterans (Cohen Veterans Network, Headstrong, Give an Hour) accumulate operational data and clinical experience that inform policy advocacy. Their position as service providers gives them credibility that pure advocacy organizations may lack.
- **Legal action**: Litigation has occasionally driven reform. The 2011 case *Veterans for Common Sense v. Shinseki* (644 F.3d 845, 9th Cir.) challenged VA mental health care delays and, while ultimately not fully decided on the merits, increased public awareness and congressional pressure. Pro bono legal organizations like the National Veterans Legal Services Program pursue discharge upgrade cases that create precedents affecting mental health care eligibility.
