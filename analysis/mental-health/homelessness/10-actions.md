# Mental Illness and Homelessness: What You Can Do

The crisis of mental illness and homelessness affects every American community. While systemic solutions require federal, state, and local policy reform, individual citizens, professionals, and community organizations play essential roles in building political will, providing direct support, and shifting public understanding. This guide provides specific, actionable steps for people at every level of engagement.

---

## Individual Actions

### Learn and Stay Informed

- **Read the UCSF Benioff Homelessness and Housing Initiative study** (*Toward a New Understanding*, 2023) to understand who becomes homeless and why -- the data counters many common myths about homelessness and mental illness
- **Track your local Continuum of Care's performance** at HUD Exchange (<https://www.hudexchange.info/>) -- each community's point-in-time count, housing inventory, and system performance data are publicly available
- **Follow the National Alliance to End Homelessness** (<https://endhomelessness.org/>) for monthly policy updates and data analyses on homelessness trends
- **Subscribe to the Treatment Advocacy Center newsletter** (<https://www.treatmentadvocacycenter.org/>) for updates on psychiatric bed availability, involuntary treatment reform, and criminalization data

### Make Personal Changes

- **Volunteer with a local outreach team or shelter**: Organizations such as PATH outreach programs, Health Care for the Homeless centers, and local CoC agencies consistently need volunteers. Direct contact with people experiencing homelessness builds understanding and combats stigma. Most communities have volunteer orientations that can be found through your local CoC or United Way 211 service.
- **Become a trained Mental Health First Aider**: The 8-hour Mental Health First Aid certification course (<https://www.mentalhealthfirstaid.org/>) teaches how to recognize and respond to signs of mental illness and substance use, including among people experiencing homelessness. Over 2.6 million Americans have been trained since 2008.
- **Donate to effective organizations**: Prioritize organizations that fund permanent supportive housing and evidence-based mental health services over those that fund only emergency shelter. See the Organizations table below for specific recommendations.
- **Carry resource cards**: Keep small cards with local crisis hotline numbers (988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, local shelter intake lines, mobile crisis team numbers) to hand to people who appear to be in distress. This provides a concrete, respectful way to offer help without making assumptions.
- **Challenge stigmatizing language**: When friends, family, or coworkers use dehumanizing language about people experiencing homelessness ("bums," "crazies," "junkies"), offer alternative framing. Language shapes policy: research by Knoll et al. (*Journal of Social Distress and the Homeless*, 2017) found that framing homelessness as a systemic failure rather than a personal failing increases public support for housing investment by 15-20 percentage points.
- **Support local businesses that hire people with lived experience**: Some social enterprises and businesses specifically recruit from homeless and formerly homeless populations. Supporting these businesses through patronage reinforces pathways out of homelessness.

### Use Your Voice

- **Contact Your Representatives**: Call or write your U.S. Representative and Senators when mental health and homelessness legislation is pending. Key ask: full funding for the CoC program, CCBHC expansion, and 988 crisis infrastructure.
- **Submit Public Comments**: When HUD, CMS, or SAMHSA publish proposed rules affecting homelessness and mental health policy, submit public comments. The Federal Register (<https://www.federalregister.gov/>) lists open comment periods. Agencies are required to read and respond to all public comments.
- **Write Letters to the Editor**: When your local newspaper covers homelessness, respond with evidence-based perspective. Key messages: permanent supportive housing saves taxpayer money ($20,000-$25,000 per year vs. $30,000-$50,000 in emergency costs), Housing First achieves 80-90% housing retention, and 90% of homeless people became homeless in the same community.
- **Speak at Local Government Meetings**: Attend city council, county board, and zoning commission meetings when supportive housing proposals or homelessness policy are on the agenda. Your testimony as a community member carries weight.

## Community Actions

### Organize Locally

- **Form a "Neighbors for Supportive Housing" group**: When supportive housing is proposed in your community, organize proactive support. Research from the National Alliance to End Homelessness shows that well-managed PSH does not reduce property values and reduces 911 calls in surrounding neighborhoods. Counter NIMBY opposition with data and coalition-building.
- **Host a community screening of documentaries on homelessness**: Films like *Lost Angels* (2010) or *The Invisible People* web series (<https://invisiblepeople.tv/>) humanize the experience of mental illness and homelessness. Follow screenings with facilitated discussion.
- **Partner with local faith communities**: Churches, synagogues, mosques, and temples are often the first point of contact for people in crisis. Faith communities can host outreach events, provide meal programs, and advocate for supportive housing in their neighborhoods.
- **Engage local business owners**: Business improvement districts often drive enforcement-first policies. Engage business owners with the evidence that permanent housing solutions reduce street homelessness more effectively than encampment sweeps. Houston's business community supported Housing First after seeing 63% reductions in homelessness.

### Build Coalitions

- **Connect mental health advocates with housing advocates**: NAMI chapters, local housing authorities, and CoC boards often operate in parallel. Facilitating connections between these groups strengthens advocacy for integrated solutions. Host joint meetings between NAMI affiliate members and CoC governance board members in your community.
- **Engage law enforcement as partners**: Many police officers are frustrated with the revolving door of arresting people with mental illness for low-level offenses. The Major Cities Chiefs Association has endorsed mental health co-response teams. Invite local police leaders to join conversations about diversion and crisis response alternatives. The Crisis Intervention Team (CIT) model provides a structured framework for police-mental health collaboration.
- **Support peer-led organizations**: Groups led by people with lived experience of homelessness and mental illness bring essential perspective. Support their inclusion in local planning processes, CoC governance, and public forums.
- **Engage faith communities strategically**: Religious congregations are among the most trusted institutions in many communities and can play a critical role in both direct service and political advocacy. National networks including Catholic Charities, Lutheran Services in America, and Volunteers of America are major homeless service providers. Local congregations can host warming centers, provide meals, advocate for supportive housing in their neighborhoods, and push back against NIMBY opposition.
- **Build relationships with local media**: Identify reporters who cover homelessness and mental health issues in your community. Offer to connect them with experts, service providers, and people with lived experience who can provide nuanced perspectives. Media coverage that focuses on systemic causes and evidence-based solutions -- rather than crime and encampment aesthetics -- shifts public opinion toward investment rather than enforcement.

### Participate in Local Governance

- **Attend Continuum of Care meetings**: CoC governance meetings are open to the public in most communities. Attending these meetings provides direct insight into how federal homeless assistance funds are allocated locally, which agencies receive funding, and what performance metrics are being tracked. Your CoC's lead agency can provide meeting schedules and agendas.
- **Join or monitor your local Homeless Count**: The annual Point-in-Time (PIT) count, conducted in late January, relies on volunteers to enumerate sheltered and unsheltered individuals. Volunteering for the PIT count provides firsthand experience with the scope of homelessness in your community and the challenges of counting unsheltered individuals.
- **Serve on advisory boards**: Many cities and counties have appointed advisory bodies on homelessness, mental health, or housing. These positions are often unfilled due to lack of applicants. Serving on an advisory board provides direct influence on local policy and budget priorities.
- **Monitor supportive housing proposals**: Track zoning applications and development proposals for supportive housing in your community. When projects face NIMBY opposition, attend public hearings and speak in support. Your presence and testimony can counter organized opposition.

## Political Actions

### Contact Elected Officials

**When to Contact**:

- During federal budget and appropriations season (typically February-September), when HUD, SAMHSA, and VA funding levels are being set
- When the CoC program reauthorization or HEARTH Act amendments are under consideration
- When your state legislature considers involuntary treatment reform, Medicaid expansion, or housing policy
- When your city council considers supportive housing zoning, encampment policies, or homelessness service contracts
- Whenever you see news coverage of homelessness policy debates in your community

**What to Say -- Template Email**:

```text
Subject: Support Permanent Supportive Housing and Mental Health Services for People Experiencing Homelessness

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

I am a constituent from [City, State], writing to urge your support for
federal investments in permanent supportive housing and community mental
health services for people experiencing homelessness with serious mental
illness.

The 2023 HUD point-in-time count documented 653,100 people experiencing
homelessness -- the highest number since counting began. An estimated
163,000 of these individuals have a serious mental illness.
Research demonstrates that permanent supportive housing achieves
80-90% housing retention and saves taxpayers $30,000-$50,000 per
person per year in avoided emergency room, jail, and shelter costs.

I urge you to support:
1. Full funding for the HUD Continuum of Care program
2. Nationwide expansion of Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinics
3. Adequate funding for 988 crisis response infrastructure
4. A permanent Supportive Housing Trust Fund

Thank you for your service and consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your City, State]
```

**What to Say -- Phone Call Script**:

```text
"Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [City]. I'm
calling about federal funding for homelessness and mental health
services. I'd like [Representative/Senator] [Name] to support full
funding for the HUD Continuum of Care program and expansion of
Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinics. Permanent supportive
housing has been proven to reduce homelessness and save taxpayer
money. Can you please note my position? Thank you."
```

### Support Legislation

Current or proposed legislation to support:

- **CCBHC Nondiscrimination and Parity Act**: Would make CCBHCs a permanent, nationwide program rather than a state-by-state demonstration. Contact your Senators and Representative to cosponsor. The bill has bipartisan support.
- **Continuum of Care (CoC) Reauthorization**: Full reauthorization of the HEARTH Act with expanded authorization levels. Contact members of the House Financial Services Committee and Senate Banking Committee.
- **988 Implementation Funding**: Sustained appropriations for the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, including mobile crisis teams and crisis stabilization centers. Contact members of the Senate HELP Committee and House Energy and Commerce Committee.
- **State-level supportive housing zoning reform**: In your state, advocate for by-right zoning that permits supportive housing in residential zones without case-by-case approval (modeled on California AB 2162).

### Electoral Engagement

**Questions to Ask Candidates**:

1. "What is your plan to address homelessness among people with serious mental illness in our community? Do you support permanent supportive housing expansion?"
2. "Do you support full federal funding for the Continuum of Care program, Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinics, and 988 crisis services?"
3. "How do you balance enforcement of public space laws with the need to provide housing and treatment for people experiencing homelessness? Do you support engagement-first approaches?"
4. "Will you support Medicaid coverage of housing-related services to help people with mental illness transition out of homelessness?"
5. "Do you support by-right zoning for supportive housing in residential neighborhoods, as California has done through AB 2162?"
6. "What is your position on involuntary treatment reform, including assisted outpatient treatment? How do you balance civil liberties with the need to engage people who cannot recognize their illness?"

**Voting Considerations**:

- Look for candidates who distinguish between enforcement and engagement strategies and who reference evidence-based approaches (Housing First, permanent supportive housing, ACT teams)
- Pay attention to city council and county board races, where zoning and local homelessness policy are decided. These down-ballot races often have the most direct impact.
- Check whether candidates have endorsed positions from NAEH, NAMI, or local homelessness advocacy coalitions
- Evaluate whether candidates have visited supportive housing programs, shelters, or outreach operations in their district -- firsthand experience significantly affects policy positions
- Be wary of candidates who promise to "end homelessness" through enforcement alone without addressing housing supply, mental health treatment, and workforce shortages

### State and Local Ballot Measures

Ballot measures related to homelessness and mental health appear with increasing frequency at the state and local level. Examples of recent measures include:

| Jurisdiction | Measure | Year | Focus | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| California | Proposition 1 (Behavioral Health Infrastructure Bond) | 2024 | $6.4 billion for behavioral health facilities and housing | Passed narrowly |
| California | Proposition 63/MHSA (Mental Health Services Act) | 2004 | 1% tax on incomes over $1 million for mental health | Passed; generates ~$3B/year |
| San Francisco | Proposition C (Homelessness Gross Receipts Tax) | 2018 | Tax on large businesses to fund homeless services | Passed; generated $300M/year |
| Los Angeles | Measure HHH (Homeless Housing Bond) | 2016 | $1.2 billion bond for supportive housing | Passed; implementation criticized for cost overruns |
| Denver | Initiated Ordinance 307 (Right to Survive) | 2019 | Would have prohibited enforcement against sleeping outdoors | Defeated |
| Portland | Metro Supportive Housing Services Measure | 2020 | Income tax to fund supportive housing services | Passed; generates $250M/year |

When these measures appear on your ballot, evaluate them using the evidence base presented in this analysis: Does the measure fund permanent solutions (PSH, community mental health) or only emergency responses? Does it include accountability metrics? Is the funding mechanism sustainable?

## Professional Actions

- **For Health Care Professionals**: Learn about Health Care for the Homeless (HCH) programs at federally qualified health centers. Volunteer or practice at an HCH site. If you prescribe long-acting injectable antipsychotics, make them available to patients experiencing homelessness who have difficulty with daily medication adherence. The National Health Care for the Homeless Council's *Adapting Your Practice* guidelines (<https://nhchc.org/clinical-practice/>) provide evidence-based protocols for treating homeless patients with mental illness, including medication management in unstable living situations.
- **For Social Workers and Counselors**: Pursue specialized training in homeless-specific interventions including motivational interviewing, trauma-informed care, and harm reduction. The National Health Care for the Homeless Council offers clinical practice guidelines. Consider employment in PATH programs or ACT teams. The SAMHSA Treatment Improvement Protocol (TIP) 55, *Behavioral Health Services for People Who Are Homeless*, provides a comprehensive clinical framework.
- **For Attorneys**: Provide pro bono representation to people experiencing homelessness who face eviction, benefits denial, or civil rights violations. Organizations like the National Homelessness Law Center (formerly National Law Center on Homelessness and Poverty) coordinate legal advocacy. Key areas of pro bono need include SSI/SSDI disability appeals (which have a 50%+ denial rate for initial applications among people with mental illness), expungement of criminal records that create barriers to housing, and civil rights complaints related to encampment sweeps.
- **For Architects and Urban Planners**: Advocate for supportive housing design that integrates into residential neighborhoods. Support zoning reform that permits higher-density development and by-right supportive housing. Design dignified spaces that combat stigma. The Corporation for Supportive Housing's design guidelines emphasize that PSH should be indistinguishable from market-rate housing in exterior appearance, reducing community opposition and resident stigma.
- **For Educators and Researchers**: Conduct and publicize research on homelessness and mental health outcomes. Incorporate housing stability and social determinants of health into clinical training curricula. Disseminate evidence-based findings to policymakers. Advocate for research funding that includes homeless populations in clinical trials -- a population that is routinely excluded from mental health research due to the difficulty of maintaining contact for follow-up.
- **For Business Leaders**: Support supportive housing developments in your community. Offer employment opportunities to people transitioning from homelessness through supported employment programs. Fund workforce development for homeless services professionals. The Individual Placement and Support (IPS) model of supported employment, which places people with SMI directly into competitive jobs with ongoing support, achieves employment rates of 55-65% compared to 20-25% for traditional vocational programs (Bond et al., *Psychiatric Services*, 2008).
- **For Landlords and Property Managers**: Participate in Housing Choice Voucher and rapid re-housing programs. HUD-funded risk mitigation funds cover property damage and loss of rent, and case managers provide ongoing support to tenants. In many markets, these programs offer guaranteed rent payment at or above market rate with minimal vacancy risk. Contact your local housing authority or CoC lead agency to learn about landlord incentive programs in your area.
- **For Technology Professionals**: Volunteer technical skills with organizations building data systems for coordinated entry, by-name lists, and cross-system data integration. The Built for Zero initiative and local HMIS (Homeless Management Information System) implementations need data analysts, software developers, and systems architects. Organizations like DataKind and Code for America have active projects in the homeless services space.

## For Families of People with SMI Who Are Homeless

Families of individuals with serious mental illness who are experiencing homelessness face unique challenges. The following actions are specifically for family members:

- **Contact your local NAMI affiliate** (<https://www.nami.org/Find-Your-Local-NAMI>) for support groups, education programs (Family-to-Family), and assistance navigating the mental health and homelessness systems. NAMI's Family-to-Family is a free 8-session course for family members of adults with mental illness.
- **Learn about assisted outpatient treatment (AOT)** in your state. If your family member meets AOT criteria (history of treatment non-compliance leading to hospitalization or dangerousness), the court may be able to order community-based treatment. The Treatment Advocacy Center's state-by-state guide (<https://www.treatmentadvocacycenter.org/grading-the-states>) identifies your state's AOT law and process.
- **Request a CARE Act referral** if you are in California and your family member has a schizophrenia spectrum or other psychotic disorder. The CARE Act (effective 2024) allows family members, first responders, clinicians, and others to petition the court for a community-based treatment plan.
- **Document your family member's history**: If involuntary treatment or AOT becomes necessary, having a documented history of treatment non-compliance, hospitalizations, dangerous incidents, and homelessness episodes strengthens the legal case. Keep records of dates, facilities, and outcomes.
- **Connect with an attorney experienced in mental health law**: The Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law, Protection and Advocacy organizations (P&As) in each state, and local legal aid societies can provide guidance on guardianship, conservatorship, and treatment options.
- **Take care of yourself**: The emotional burden of having a family member with SMI who is homeless is immense. Support groups (NAMI, Al-Anon, local caregiver groups) provide essential peer support. Consider individual therapy with a clinician experienced in family systems and mental illness.

## Advocacy Toolkit

### Key Talking Points

When discussing mental illness and homelessness with others, emphasize:

1. **Permanent supportive housing works and saves money**: PSH achieves 80-90% housing retention and saves $30,000-$50,000 per person per year in avoided emergency costs. The current approach of cycling people through ERs, jails, and shelters costs more than housing them.
2. **Homelessness is a local problem with local roots**: 90% of people experiencing homelessness became homeless in the community where they currently live (UCSF, 2023). The "magnet effect" is a myth -- people do not move to cities for homeless services.
3. **We know how to solve this**: The United States reduced veteran homelessness by 55.3% using Housing First plus intensive services. The same model can be applied to all people with serious mental illness experiencing homelessness.
4. **Mental illness is a cause, not a choice**: Approximately 25% of people experiencing homelessness have a serious mental illness. Deinstitutionalization without community alternatives -- a government policy failure -- created this crisis.
5. **Both housing and treatment are needed**: The debate between "housing first" and "treatment first" is a false choice. The evidence supports providing housing immediately while making voluntary treatment readily available.

### Common Misconceptions to Counter

| Misconception | Reality | Source |
|---------------|---------|--------|
| "Homeless people choose to live on the streets" | The vast majority of homeless individuals cite economic factors (rent increases, job loss, eviction) or health crises (including mental health) as the reason. Only 1% cite "choice" as the primary reason. | UCSF Benioff Homelessness Study, 2023 |
| "Housing First means giving people housing with no services" | Housing First provides housing without preconditions, but offers voluntary services including psychiatric care, substance use treatment, and case management. Studies show Housing First participants access more services over time. | Tsemberis et al., 2004; Padgett et al., 2016 |
| "Homeless people are mostly from somewhere else" | 90% of people experiencing homelessness became homeless in the community where they are currently living. Migration for services is rare. | UCSF, 2023; HUD AHAR, 2024 |
| "Supportive housing lowers property values" | Multiple studies show that well-managed permanent supportive housing has no negative impact on surrounding property values and may increase them. | Furman Center, NYU, 2008; Denver study, 2016 |
| "We cannot afford to house everyone" | The current system costs $30,000-$50,000 per person per year in emergency services. PSH costs $20,000-$25,000 per person per year. We cannot afford not to house people. | Culhane et al., 2002; Economic Roundtable, 2018 |
| "Encampment sweeps solve homelessness" | Research shows that 69% of people swept from encampments return within 30 days. Sweeps disperse people, disrupt service connections, and increase emergency utilization. | Herring et al., 2020; San Francisco DPH, 2019 |
| "Mental illness alone causes homelessness" | Mental illness is a significant risk factor, but the primary drivers of homelessness are structural: housing unaffordability, poverty, and lack of social support. Countries with similar mental illness rates but stronger housing safety nets have far lower homelessness rates. | OECD, 2023; Fazel et al., 2014 |
| "Homeless people with mental illness are dangerous" | People with SMI are far more likely to be victims than perpetrators of violence. Research shows that SMI alone increases violence risk only marginally; the primary risk factors are substance use and prior victimization. | Elbogen & Johnson, *Archives of General Psychiatry*, 2009 |

### Seasonal and Time-Sensitive Actions

Certain actions are most effective at specific times of year:

| Time Period | Action | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| January | Volunteer for the Point-in-Time Count | PIT count data determines federal funding allocations for your community |
| February-March | Contact Congress during budget formulation | HUD, SAMHSA, and VA budget requests are being finalized |
| April-June | Attend CoC governance meetings on NOFO strategy | CoCs develop their annual application to HUD during this period |
| July-September | Contact Congress during appropriations season | Final funding levels for HUD CoC, PATH, and 988 are being set |
| October-November | Engage with state and local candidates | Local election candidates make commitments on homelessness policy |
| November-January | Support winter emergency shelter efforts | Hypothermia deaths peak; warming center volunteers are needed |
| Any time | Respond to NIMBY opposition at zoning hearings | Supportive housing proposals can arise at any time |
