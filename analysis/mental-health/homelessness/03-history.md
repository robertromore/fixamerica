# Mental Illness and Homelessness: History

## Historical Development

The relationship between mental illness and homelessness in the United States has been shaped by three major forces: the rise and fall of state psychiatric institutions, the failure to build a community-based mental health system, and the evolution of federal homelessness policy. Understanding this history is essential because today's crisis is not an accident -- it is the predictable result of specific policy choices made over six decades.

---

### The Institutional Era (1840s-1955): Asylum as Default Housing

- **Dorothea Dix's Crusade (1840s-1880s)**: Dorothea Dix's advocacy documented the confinement of mentally ill people in jails, almshouses, and poorhouses under horrific conditions. Her campaign led to the construction of more than 30 state psychiatric hospitals, establishing the principle that the state bore responsibility for housing and treating people with severe mental illness.
- **Growth of the State Hospital System**: By 1900, every state had at least one public psychiatric hospital. Populations grew steadily as states used these institutions to house not only people with psychotic disorders but also those with intellectual disabilities, epilepsy, dementia, and chronic alcoholism. The institutions served a dual function: medical treatment (however rudimentary) and social control, removing people deemed "unfit" from public life.
- **Peak Institutionalization (1955)**: State psychiatric hospitals reached their maximum population of 558,922 patients in 1955. At that point, the hospitalization rate was approximately 340 per 100,000 population. Conditions in many facilities were overcrowded and dehumanizing, with patients warehoused in locked wards with minimal treatment. State spending on mental hospitals consumed roughly 40% of state health budgets.
- **The Institutional Model's Failures**: Exposés by journalists including Albert Deutsch (*The Shame of the States*, 1948) and photographers documented filthy, violent, and neglectful conditions. Life magazine's 1946 photo essay "Bedlam 1946" compared state hospitals to Nazi concentration camps, catalyzing public outrage. Burton Blatt's *Christmas in Purgatory* (1966) further documented the dehumanization of residents in state institutions for people with intellectual disabilities, extending the critique beyond psychiatric hospitals.
- **The Implicit Bargain**: For all their failures, state hospitals provided something that no subsequent policy adequately replaced: a guaranteed place to live for people with severe mental illness who could not live independently. When this implicit housing guarantee was withdrawn through deinstitutionalization, no new guarantee took its place.

---

### Deinstitutionalization (1955-1990): The Great Emptying

- **Pharmacological Revolution (1950s)**: The introduction of chlorpromazine (Thorazine) in 1954 made it possible to manage psychotic symptoms outside hospital settings for the first time. This pharmacological breakthrough provided the medical rationale for discharge, though it did not address patients' needs for housing, income, or social support. Within a decade, a range of antipsychotic medications became available, and state hospital administrators embraced the possibility of community-based treatment. However, the medications had significant side effects (including tardive dyskinesia), and many patients discontinued them after discharge.

- **Community Mental Health Act of 1963**: President Kennedy signed the Community Mental Health Centers Construction Act on October 31, 1963, authorizing federal funding for 1,500 community mental health centers (CMHCs) to replace state hospitals. The law envisioned a network of outpatient clinics, partial hospitalization programs, and emergency services. Only 789 CMHCs were ever built, and few served people with the most severe conditions. The CMHCs that were built tended to serve higher-functioning patients with anxiety, depression, and adjustment disorders -- people who could come to an office, keep appointments, and pay fees. The most severely ill were left underserved from the beginning.

- **Medicare and Medicaid (1965)**: The creation of Medicare and Medicaid inadvertently accelerated deinstitutionalization through a structural financial incentive. The Medicaid "IMD exclusion" (Institution for Mental Diseases exclusion) prohibited federal Medicaid reimbursement for psychiatric care in facilities with more than 16 beds. This created a powerful financial incentive for states to discharge patients from state hospitals (which were entirely state-funded) to nursing homes, board-and-care homes, or the community (where federal Medicaid dollars could pay for their care). The IMD exclusion remains in effect today and continues to distort the financing of psychiatric care.
- **Civil Rights and Legal Reform (1960s-1970s)**: A series of landmark court decisions established patients' rights to treatment, least restrictive environment, and due process before involuntary commitment. Key cases included:
    - *Wyatt v. Stickney*, 325 F. Supp. 781 (M.D. Ala. 1971) -- established a constitutional right to adequate treatment for involuntarily committed patients
    - *O'Connor v. Donaldson*, 422 U.S. 563 (1975) -- held that states cannot confine non-dangerous mentally ill individuals who are capable of surviving safely in freedom
    - *Addington v. Texas*, 441 U.S. 418 (1979) -- required clear and convincing evidence for involuntary civil commitment
    - *Lessard v. Schmidt*, 349 F. Supp. 1078 (E.D. Wis. 1972) -- applied due process protections to civil commitment proceedings
    - These decisions were legally sound and morally necessary, but they assumed that community alternatives existed to absorb the people who would no longer be confined. When those alternatives failed to materialize, the net effect was to accelerate discharge without ensuring a safe landing.
- **The Emptying Accelerates (1970s-1980s)**: State hospital populations fell from 558,922 in 1955 to approximately 130,000 by 1980. Between 1965 and 1980, the number of state hospital beds dropped by more than 60%. States embraced deinstitutionalization because it shifted costs from state general funds to federal programs (Medicaid, SSI, SSDI). Many discharged patients were transferred to nursing homes, board-and-care homes, or adult residential facilities that provided minimal psychiatric care -- a phenomenon researchers termed "transinstitutionalization."
- **What Did Not Happen**: The community mental health system envisioned in 1963 was never fully built. Federal funding for CMHCs was converted to block grants under Reagan in 1981 (through the Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act), reducing federal support by approximately 30%. States used the savings from hospital closures for other purposes rather than reinvesting in community services. A 1981 GAO study found that fewer than half of discharged state hospital patients received any follow-up mental health care within 90 days of discharge. The National Institute of Mental Health, which had championed community mental health, was folded into the Alcohol, Drug Abuse, and Mental Health Administration (ADAMHA) in 1973, diminishing its policy influence.
- **SSI and SSDI as Deinstitutionalization Enablers (1972-1980s)**: The creation of Supplemental Security Income (SSI) in 1972 provided a federal income floor for disabled individuals, including those with severe mental illness. SSI benefits, combined with Medicaid eligibility, were intended to support community living. However, benefits were (and remain) insufficient to afford housing in any U.S. market. The Reagan administration's 1981-1983 review of SSI/SSDI disability rolls resulted in approximately 500,000 people being dropped from benefits, many of whom had psychiatric disabilities -- directly contributing to the surge in visible homelessness in the early 1980s. Following litigation and congressional intervention (the 1984 Social Security Disability Benefits Reform Act), most were reinstated, but the disruption had lasting effects.

### The Emergence of Mass Homelessness (1980-1995): From Hospitals to Streets

- **Visible Homelessness Appears (Early 1980s)**: For the first time since the Great Depression, large numbers of visibly mentally ill people appeared on American streets in the early 1980s. Advocates estimated that one-third to one-half of the new homeless population had serious mental illness. The image of the "bag lady" or "street person" talking to themselves became a cultural touchstone.
- **Confluence of Factors (1980-1985)**: Homelessness among mentally ill people resulted from the convergence of deinstitutionalization without community alternatives, cuts to the social safety net (including SSI/SSDI eligibility restrictions in 1981), the loss of low-cost housing (SRO hotels declined from 1 million units in 1970 to fewer than 400,000 by 1990), and the crack cocaine epidemic. The scale of visible homelessness was unprecedented in post-war America: in New York City alone, the shelter population grew from 2,000 in 1980 to over 10,000 by 1985 (Coalition for the Homeless, NYC).
- **Federal Response Debates (1983-1986)**: The initial federal response was characterized by denial and minimization. The Reagan administration contested advocates' estimates of 2-3 million homeless people, with HUD producing a 1984 report estimating only 250,000-350,000 -- a figure advocates and researchers fiercely disputed. The debate over the "real" number of homeless people delayed policy action for years. Advocates including Mitch Snyder staged high-profile protests, including a 51-day hunger strike in 1984, to pressure the federal government to act.
- **McKinney-Vento Homeless Assistance Act (1987)**: The first comprehensive federal homelessness legislation, named after Representatives Stewart McKinney and Bruce Vento, authorized programs for emergency shelter, transitional housing, health care for the homeless, education for homeless children, and mental health services (through what became the PATH program). Initial authorization was $1 billion over two years. The act also established the Interagency Council on the Homeless (now USICH) to coordinate the federal response.
- **Health Care for the Homeless Program (1987)**: Section 340 of the McKinney-Vento Act established the Health Care for the Homeless (HCH) program, providing grants to federally qualified health centers to deliver primary care, mental health, and substance use services to homeless individuals. By 2023, approximately 300 HCH grantees served more than 1 million patients annually (HRSA, 2023).
- **PATH Program Established (1990)**: The Projects for Assistance in Transition from Homelessness program was authorized as part of the McKinney-Vento Act amendments, providing formula grants to states specifically for outreach and services to homeless individuals with serious mental illness. PATH filled a critical niche by funding the outreach and engagement work that other programs did not cover.
- **Criminalization Begins**: As visible homelessness grew, cities began enacting ordinances against panhandling, loitering, and sleeping in public -- effectively criminalizing the condition of homelessness. Jails increasingly became the de facto housing for mentally ill people who could not access treatment or shelter. The National Law Center on Homelessness and Poverty documented a 73% increase in anti-camping ordinances and a 56% increase in anti-panhandling laws between 1990 and 2010 across major U.S. cities.
- **Early Litigation (1988-1995)**: Advocates began using the courts to challenge the criminalization of homelessness. *Pottinger v. City of Miami*, 810 F. Supp. 1551 (S.D. Fla. 1992), found that arresting homeless people for performing life-sustaining activities in public (sleeping, eating) violated the Eighth Amendment. This case established an early precedent, though its reach was limited to Miami until the broader Ninth Circuit ruling in *Martin v. Boise* decades later.

### The Policy Response Era (1995-2015): Housing First and Federal Strategy

- **Pathways to Housing and Housing First (1992-2000)**: Dr. Sam Tsemberis founded Pathways to Housing in New York City in 1992, developing the Housing First model that provided immediate access to independent apartments for chronically homeless individuals with mental illness, without requiring sobriety or treatment compliance as preconditions. The first published research results (2000, 2004) showed 80-88% housing retention rates, dramatically outperforming traditional "treatment first" programs (47% retention).
- **Continuum of Care Framework (1994)**: HUD reorganized its homeless assistance programs into the Continuum of Care model, requiring communities to submit coordinated applications for funding. This framework remains the primary federal mechanism for homeless services planning and funding.
- **The "Treatment First" Era (1990s-2000s)**: Before Housing First became dominant, most programs for homeless individuals with mental illness followed a "continuum of care" or "staircase" model, requiring progression through shelters, transitional housing, and eventually permanent housing, with sobriety and treatment compliance as prerequisites at each step. These programs had retention rates of only 40-50% because they excluded the most severely ill individuals -- precisely those with the greatest need (Tsemberis, *Housing First*, 2010).
- **Federal Strategic Plans**: USICH issued the first federal strategic plan to end homelessness, "Opening Doors," in 2010. The plan prioritized ending chronic and veteran homelessness and embraced Housing First as the primary strategy. Subsequent plans (2015, 2022) maintained these priorities. The 2010 plan was notable for setting explicit timelines: end chronic homelessness by 2015, end veteran homelessness by 2015, and end homelessness among families and youth by 2020. None of these targets were met, but the plans provided a framework for accountability and resource allocation.
- **The Olmstead Decision (1999)**: The Supreme Court's ruling in *Olmstead v. L.C.*, 527 U.S. 581 (1999), held that unjustified institutional isolation of people with disabilities violates the Americans with Disabilities Act. The decision required states to provide community-based services when appropriate, which had significant implications for homeless individuals with mental illness who were unnecessarily confined in institutions or, conversely, unable to access community services. *Olmstead* became the legal foundation for community integration advocacy.
- **HUD-VASH Launch (2008)**: Congress authorized the HUD-Veterans Affairs Supportive Housing program, combining Housing Choice vouchers with VA case management. By 2023, approximately 110,000 vouchers had been allocated, contributing to a 55.3% reduction in veteran homelessness from 2010 to 2023.
- **HEARTH Act (2009)**: The Homeless Emergency Assistance and Rapid Transition to Housing Act reauthorized and updated the McKinney-Vento programs, expanding the definition of homelessness, adding prevention funding, establishing a performance measurement system, and codifying the CoC planning process.
- **HUD Housing First Mandate (2013-2015)**: HUD began prioritizing CoC applications that embraced Housing First principles, effectively making Housing First adoption a condition for competitive federal funding. This policy accelerated Housing First adoption nationwide but also generated backlash from providers committed to abstinence-based or treatment-first models.

### The Current Crisis (2015-Present): Record Numbers and Legal Shifts

- **Rising Homelessness Resumes (2017-Present)**: After modest declines from 2010 to 2016, total homelessness began rising again, driven by escalating housing costs, the opioid/fentanyl crisis, and the economic disruptions of the COVID-19 pandemic. The 2023 PIT count of 653,100 was the highest on record.
- **COVID-19 Pandemic (2020-2023)**: The pandemic created both dangers and opportunities. Shelters became transmission risks, prompting cities to open hotel-based non-congregate shelter (Project Roomkey in California housed approximately 42,000 people in hotels and motels). Congress provided unprecedented one-time funding including $5 billion through HOME-ARP (2021), $4.4 billion in expanded Emergency Solutions Grants (ESG-CV), and $26.4 billion in Emergency Rental Assistance across two rounds. The CDC eviction moratorium (September 2020-August 2021) temporarily prevented evictions but created a cliff effect upon expiration. The pandemic demonstrated that non-congregate shelter and rapid housing interventions were feasible at scale when political will and funding aligned.
- **Kendra's Law Expansion and the CARE Act Movement (2017-2023)**: New York strengthened its Assisted Outpatient Treatment (AOT) law (Kendra's Law, originally enacted in 1999) through multiple extensions and expanded funding. California followed with the Community Assistance, Recovery, and Empowerment (CARE) Act in 2022, creating a new civil court process for individuals with psychotic disorders. Several other states explored similar frameworks, reflecting growing bipartisan interest in expanding the tools available to engage people with severe mental illness who are unable to seek treatment voluntarily.
- **California CARE Act (2022)**: California enacted the Community Assistance, Recovery, and Empowerment Act, creating a civil court process to develop community-based treatment plans for individuals with schizophrenia spectrum or other psychotic disorders who are unable to provide for their basic needs. The CARE Act represented a significant shift toward state-level compelled treatment frameworks.
- **Grants Pass v. Johnson (2024)**: The Supreme Court's 6-3 ruling reversed nearly a decade of Ninth Circuit precedent that had constrained cities from enforcing anti-camping laws when shelter was unavailable. Writing for the majority, Justice Gorsuch held that the Eighth Amendment prohibits cruel and unusual punishments, not cruel and unusual conditions, and that generally applicable camping ordinances are not "punishments" for the status of being homeless. The dissent, written by Justice Sotomayor, warned that the decision authorized "criminalizing homelessness."

## State-Level Historical Variation

The history of mental illness and homelessness has played out differently across states, reflecting differences in deinstitutionalization pace, housing markets, political culture, and service system development.

### New York

New York has the longest history of policy innovation in this space. The state's "right to shelter" mandate, established through litigation in *Callahan v. Carey* (1981), requires New York City to provide shelter to anyone who requests it -- a unique legal obligation that has produced the nation's largest shelter system (~60,000 beds) but has also been criticized for prioritizing shelter over permanent housing. New York enacted Kendra's Law in 1999 following the subway pushing death of Kendra Webdale, establishing AOT for individuals with a history of treatment non-compliance leading to hospitalization or dangerousness. The law has been extended multiple times and is considered a national model for AOT.

### California

California has the nation's largest homeless population and has been the epicenter of policy experimentation. The state's Mental Health Services Act (Proposition 63, 2004) imposed a 1% tax on incomes over $1 million to fund mental health services, generating approximately $3 billion annually. However, the funds have been criticized for being diffused across too many programs with insufficient focus on the most severely ill. The CARE Act (2022) and Governor Newsom's executive orders on encampment clearances represent a significant shift toward more assertive intervention. California's AB 2162 (2018) established by-right zoning for supportive housing, reducing NIMBY barriers to PSH development.

### Texas

Texas illustrates the potential of community-level action within a state that has not expanded Medicaid. Houston's 63% reduction in homelessness (2011-2023) was achieved through system coordination, landlord engagement, and Housing First adoption, demonstrating that significant progress is possible even in politically conservative environments. The Houston model has attracted bipartisan attention as a replicable approach.

### Oregon

Oregon's experience illustrates the complexity of drug policy reform intersecting with homelessness. Measure 110 (2020) decriminalized small amounts of all drugs and funded expanded treatment services. However, implementation challenges -- including slow deployment of treatment capacity -- contributed to concerns about open drug use in homeless encampments, and the measure was partially repealed in 2024. The CAHOOTS crisis response model (operating since 1989 in Eugene) remains a national model for non-police crisis response.

## Lessons from History

### Pattern 1: Deinstitutionalization Without Reinvestment

The most consequential pattern in this history is the repeated failure to build community-based systems before closing institutional ones. State hospitals were shut down, but the savings were not redirected to community mental health centers, supportive housing, or outreach programs. This same pattern -- closing one system without adequately funding the replacement -- recurs in discussions about reforming the criminal justice system, child welfare, and other institutional responses to mental illness.

### Pattern 2: Cost-Shifting Rather Than Problem-Solving

At every stage, governments have responded to mental illness and homelessness by shifting costs rather than solving problems. States shifted costs from hospitals to federal Medicaid. The federal government shifted mental health funding from categorical programs to block grants. Cities shifted the burden from social services to law enforcement. Each shift reduced costs for the shifting entity while worsening outcomes for people with mental illness.

### Pattern 3: Evidence-Adoption Lag

The Housing First model was developed in 1992 and demonstrated effectiveness by 2000, but it did not become federal policy until 2010-2015 -- a 15-20 year lag. Assertive Community Treatment was shown effective in the 1970s but remains unavailable in most communities. This pattern suggests that even when evidence-based solutions exist, political, ideological, and institutional barriers delay adoption by one to two decades.

### Pattern 4: Crisis-Driven Policy, Not Prevention

Federal homelessness policy has been overwhelmingly reactive. The McKinney-Vento Act (1987) responded to the visible crisis of the 1980s. The HEARTH Act (2009) added prevention but still primarily funded emergency and transitional responses. Major funding increases (HOME-ARP, ESG-CV) came in response to the COVID-19 emergency. Sustained investment in prevention -- mental health treatment, affordable housing, discharge planning -- has never been politically viable outside of a crisis.

### Pattern 5: Fragmentation Across Government

At every historical stage, responsibility for people with mental illness who are homeless has been divided across multiple levels of government and multiple agencies. State governments operated the hospitals but sought to shift costs to federal programs. The federal government funded categorical programs but gave states discretion over implementation. Local governments managed the street-level consequences but lacked the authority to address root causes. This fragmentation has meant that no single entity has ever been accountable for outcomes, enabling each actor to blame the others for failure.

### Pattern 6: Success Is Possible When Systems Align

The history is not entirely one of failure. The 55.3% reduction in veteran homelessness since 2010 demonstrates that when the federal government provides targeted funding (HUD-VASH vouchers), a single agency coordinates services (VA), and a clear accountability framework exists (Built for Zero), significant progress is achievable even within the existing fragmented system. Houston's 63% reduction in total homelessness from 2011 to 2023 shows that community-level coordination can produce dramatic results. The challenge is replicating these successes at national scale for non-veteran populations.
