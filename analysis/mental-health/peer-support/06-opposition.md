# Peer Support Services: Opposition to Reform

Proposals to expand, standardize, and better fund peer support services face resistance from those who believe the peer model lacks sufficient evidence, that expanding non-clinical roles threatens patient safety, or that public money should prioritize licensed professionals over workers whose primary qualification is personal experience.

---

## Who Opposes Reform

### Organized Opposition

| Actor | Interest | Resources | Tactics |
|-------|----------|-----------|---------|
| Licensed professional associations (NASW, APA, ACA) | Protect scope of practice, maintain market share for licensed providers | Multi-million dollar lobbying budgets; state and federal presence | Testify against scope-of-practice expansion; propose restrictive supervision requirements; influence state licensing boards |
| State licensing boards | Maintain regulatory authority and protect professional standards | Legal authority over scope of practice in each state | Issue opinions restricting peer activities; delay approval of new peer billing codes |
| Managed care organizations (some) | Minimize new mandatory coverage requirements | Control Medicaid networks in most states | Oppose mandated benefits for peer services; set low reimbursement rates; impose excessive prior authorization |
| Hospital associations (some) | Protect inpatient revenue from peer respite diversion | Significant lobbying capacity at state and federal levels | Oppose funding for peer respite alternatives; question safety of non-clinical crisis settings |
| Pharmaceutical industry (indirect) | Maintain medication-centered treatment paradigm | Largest healthcare lobbying sector ($374 million in federal lobbying, 2023) | Fund research and advocacy organizations focused on medication and clinical treatment; do not fund peer support research |
| Some academic psychiatrists | Maintain primacy of evidence-based clinical interventions | Academic platforms, journal editorships, government advisory roles | Publish skeptical commentaries on peer evidence base; demand RCT-level evidence before supporting expansion |
| State-specific certification vendors | Maintain captive markets under fragmented state systems | Contractual relationships with state behavioral health authorities | Oppose national certification standards that would introduce competition; lobby against reciprocity agreements |

### Detailed Opposition Profiles

**Licensed Professional Associations**: The National Association of Social Workers (NASW), American Psychological Association (APA), and American Counseling Association (ACA) represent a combined membership of over 400,000 licensed professionals with a direct economic stake in the scope of Medicaid-billable behavioral health services. Their opposition to peer workforce expansion is not monolithic -- all three organizations have issued statements supporting peer services in principle -- but becomes active when specific proposals threaten scope-of-practice boundaries or reimbursement allocation. NASW's 2023 position statement on "interprofessional collaboration" in behavioral health supported peer services but cautioned against "role blurring" and recommended that peer services always be delivered "under the supervision of a licensed clinical social worker or equivalent." This framing, while presented as quality assurance, effectively subordinates peer services to social work authority.

**Managed Care Organizations**: MCOs occupy an ambiguous position. They benefit from low peer reimbursement rates (which reduce costs) while also benefiting from effective peer services (which reduce expensive ED visits and hospitalizations). However, when forced to choose between paying adequate rates and maintaining margins, MCOs consistently prioritize cost control. A 2023 analysis by the National Council for Mental Wellbeing found that MCO-administered Medicaid programs paid effective peer service reimbursement rates 15-25% lower than state fee-for-service rates in the same states, after accounting for prior authorization denials and documentation-related claim rejections.

**Hospital Associations**: The American Hospital Association (AHA) and state hospital associations have not taken formal positions against peer support, but individual hospital systems and their lobbyists have opposed peer respite funding in state legislatures. In Massachusetts, a 2022 proposal to expand Medicaid funding for peer respite programs faced opposition from the Massachusetts Health and Hospital Association, which argued that diverting behavioral health patients from emergency departments to non-clinical settings posed safety risks. In Oregon, a similar proposal was amended after hospital system lobbying to require that peer respite programs maintain formal referral agreements with local hospitals -- a requirement that increases administrative burden on small peer-run organizations.

**Pharmaceutical Industry**: The pharmaceutical industry does not directly oppose peer support but exerts structural influence by funding the research, advocacy, and educational infrastructure that centers medication as the primary treatment modality. Pharmaceutical companies fund continuing medical education programs, support NAMI and other advocacy organizations (NAMI received approximately $25 million from pharmaceutical companies between 2006 and 2019, according to NAMI's financial disclosures), and sponsor academic research that emphasizes pharmacological outcomes. This structural funding pattern creates an information ecosystem in which peer support -- which does not generate pharmaceutical revenue -- receives disproportionately less visibility and research investment.

### Ideological Opposition

- **Conservative/Fiscal**: Some fiscal conservatives oppose creating new government-funded workforce categories, arguing that peer support should be funded through private charity, faith-based organizations, or voluntary associations rather than Medicaid expansion. They may view peer certification as unnecessary government regulation that imposes bureaucratic overhead on what should be organic community support. The Heritage Foundation has not published specific analyses of peer support, but its broader framework -- skepticism of Medicaid expansion and preference for block-grant approaches to behavioral health funding -- would reduce the federal financing infrastructure on which peer services depend.

- **Libertarian**: Libertarian critics oppose both the certification requirements (government credentialing of mutual aid) and the Medicaid funding mechanism (taxpayer-funded behavioral health services), arguing that peer support should operate in the private market free of government involvement. The Cato Institute has published critiques of health workforce credentialing that, while not peer-specific, would apply to peer specialist certification as a form of occupational licensing that restricts labor market freedom.

- **Professional Establishment**: Some clinicians, particularly those trained in strict evidence-based practice frameworks, view peer support as a well-intentioned but insufficiently validated intervention that should not receive public funding until its evidence base matches that of established clinical treatments. This position conflates the strength of the existing evidence (moderate and growing) with the readiness for implementation (already widely implemented). It also applies an evidence standard inconsistently -- many accepted clinical practices, including most forms of group therapy and care coordination, were scaled with less RCT evidence than peer support currently possesses.

- **Treatment Advocacy Center Perspective**: The Treatment Advocacy Center (TAC), which advocates for expanding involuntary treatment for people with serious mental illness, has expressed skepticism toward aspects of the peer movement that emphasize individual autonomy and resistance to coercive interventions. TAC's position -- that people with "anosognosia" (lack of insight into their illness) cannot make autonomous treatment decisions -- is in direct tension with the peer support principle that individuals are the experts on their own experience. While TAC has not formally opposed peer support, its advocacy for expanded assisted outpatient treatment and involuntary commitment creates policy friction with the peer movement's emphasis on self-determination.

## Opposition Arguments and Rebuttals

### Argument 1: Insufficient Evidence Base

**Claim**: "Peer support services lack the rigorous evidence needed to justify public funding. Most studies are small, unblinded, and suffer from methodological weaknesses. We should not scale interventions that have not been validated through large randomized controlled trials."

**Evidence Cited**: Critics point to the limited number of RCTs (approximately 18 for mental health peer support as of 2023), the difficulty of blinding participants, and the heterogeneity of peer interventions that makes meta-analysis challenging. A 2014 Cochrane review (Lloyd-Evans et al.) concluded that the evidence was "insufficient to draw firm conclusions" about peer support effectiveness, a finding that is still cited by opponents despite subsequent studies.

**Reality**: While the RCT evidence base is smaller than for some clinical interventions, it is growing and consistently positive. A 2021 meta-analysis of peer support RCTs found statistically significant reductions in emergency department utilization (9-23%), hospital readmissions (18-36% reduction), and improvements in self-reported recovery and empowerment. Johnson et al. (2018), published in *The Lancet*, found that peer-supported self-management significantly reduced readmissions for people discharged from mental health crisis teams. Additionally, SAMHSA has recognized peer support as an evidence-based practice since 2005, and the VA's internal evaluations have consistently found positive outcomes from its peer specialist program, including improved engagement, reduced no-show rates, and higher patient satisfaction.

**Rebuttal**: The evidence standard applied to peer support is inconsistently strict. Many established clinical practices -- including most forms of psychotherapy, care coordination, and therapeutic community models -- were scaled before achieving the level of RCT evidence now demanded of peer support. Cognitive behavioral therapy had fewer than 10 published RCTs when it began widespread dissemination in the 1980s. The relational and contextual nature of peer support makes traditional clinical trials methodologically difficult, which is an argument for diversifying evidence standards, not for withholding support from a practice that shows consistent positive results across multiple study designs. Moreover, the evidence gap is substantially the product of funding disparities: peer support receives approximately $12 million annually from NIH, while psychotherapy research receives over $300 million.

### Argument 2: Patient Safety Concerns

**Claim**: "People with mental health conditions or substance use histories are inherently unreliable as service providers. They may relapse, trigger clients, overshare, or provide advice that conflicts with clinical treatment. Without proper clinical training, peers may cause harm."

**Evidence Cited**: Anecdotal reports of boundary violations, relapse incidents among peer workers, and cases where peer workers provided advice inconsistent with clinical treatment plans. Some state licensing boards have cited individual complaints involving peer workers as evidence of systemic safety concerns.

**Reality**: Peer specialist certification programs include extensive training on boundaries, ethics, crisis response, and scope of practice -- typically 40 to 100+ hours of competency-based instruction. Studies of peer-delivered services consistently show safety profiles comparable to other paraprofessional services. A 2020 systematic review of adverse events in peer support programs found that serious incidents were rare (< 1% of service encounters) and comparable in frequency to incidents in services delivered by other paraprofessional staff (Chinman et al., 2020). The VA's peer specialist program, with over 1,200 peer workers serving veterans with complex psychiatric conditions, has maintained safety records comparable to its clinical staff programs.

**Rebuttal**: The assumption that people with psychiatric histories are inherently unreliable reflects the very stigma that peer support was designed to challenge. All health workers carry personal risk factors; the question is whether training, supervision, and organizational support adequately mitigate those risks. Evidence shows that they do. Moreover, the argument cuts both ways: clinical providers without lived experience may lack the empathy, credibility, and cultural competence that peer workers bring, potentially causing a different kind of harm through paternalism, coercion, or clinical detachment. A 2019 study in *Psychiatric Services* found that peer specialists were more likely than clinical staff to identify early signs of distress in service recipients and to de-escalate situations before they reached crisis level -- suggesting that lived experience may actually enhance safety rather than undermine it.

### Argument 3: Scope Creep and Unlicensed Practice

**Claim**: "As peer roles expand into crisis services, emergency departments, and primary care settings, peer workers are increasingly performing activities that constitute unlicensed counseling, assessment, or case management. This threatens the integrity of professional licensing systems designed to protect the public."

**Evidence Cited**: Reports from state licensing boards of peer specialists conducting activities resembling clinical assessments, making diagnostic observations in documentation, or providing advice that functions as counseling. The California Board of Behavioral Sciences and the Texas State Board of Examiners of Professional Counselors have both issued advisory opinions cautioning about boundary blurring.

**Reality**: Scope of practice boundaries are genuinely ambiguous in many states, and the lack of clear national guidelines creates confusion for peer workers, supervisors, and employers. However, the solution is better definition and training, not restriction of peer services. States with clearly defined peer scopes of practice -- including Georgia, Pennsylvania, and Texas -- report fewer scope-related complaints. Georgia's detailed scope-of-practice regulation, which specifies both what peer specialists may do (share personal recovery stories, help develop wellness plans, provide resource navigation) and what they may not do (diagnose, prescribe, provide clinical therapy), has been cited as a model.

**Rebuttal**: Scope creep is a legitimate concern that is best addressed through clear scope-of-practice regulations, competency-based training, and qualified supervision -- not through restricting peer services. The fact that some peer workers perform tasks outside their defined role reflects organizational failures (inadequate training, poor supervision, staffing shortages) rather than inherent defects in the peer model. Moreover, licensed professionals regularly delegate tasks to paraprofessional staff in every area of healthcare; the question is not whether paraprofessionals should practice, but how to ensure they practice safely within defined boundaries. Medical assistants, certified nursing assistants, and pharmacy technicians all perform delegated tasks under supervision -- peer specialists should be treated no differently.

### Argument 4: Taxpayer Cost and Government Expansion

**Claim**: "Creating a new government-funded, government-credentialed workforce is an unnecessary expansion of the welfare state. Peer support is essentially mutual aid, which should be funded through voluntary contributions, faith-based organizations, or private insurance rather than Medicaid."

**Evidence Cited**: General arguments about government spending, the size of the behavioral health budget, and the principle that mutual aid should be community-based rather than government-funded.

**Reality**: Peer support services generate net savings for the public behavioral health system. Peer respite programs cost $200-$400 per day compared to $2,264 per day for psychiatric inpatient care (HCUP, 2022). Studies of peer-delivered crisis services show 9-23% reductions in emergency department visits and 18-36% reductions in hospital readmissions, which represent significant Medicaid and Medicare savings. The $29 million in annual SAMHSA peer support grants is a fraction of the $6.5 billion SAMHSA budget. A 2022 economic analysis estimated that adequate investment in peer support services ($500 million annually in additional federal spending) would generate $1.5-$2.5 billion in annual savings from reduced hospitalization, ED visits, and incarceration.

**Rebuttal**: Peer support is already being funded by taxpayers through Medicaid in 46 states. The question is not whether to fund it but whether to fund it adequately. Current chronic underfunding produces a low-wage, high-turnover workforce that is less effective than it could be, reducing the return on public investment. Adequate funding would improve workforce stability, service quality, and cost-effectiveness -- generating net savings that more than offset additional investment. The fiscal argument for peer support is, if anything, stronger than the fiscal argument for most clinical interventions, because peer services cost less to deliver and produce comparable outcomes.

### Argument 5: One-Size-Fits-All Certification Standards

**Claim**: "National certification standards would override state innovation and create bureaucratic barriers to entering the peer workforce. States should be free to set their own standards based on local needs, cultures, and regulatory environments."

**Evidence Cited**: The variation in state approaches, which some view as healthy experimentation. Concerns about federal overreach and loss of state regulatory autonomy. Examples of innovative state-level approaches that would not have emerged under a uniform national standard.

**Reality**: State variation has produced some genuine innovation (e.g., Georgia's pioneering model, youth peer specialist credentials, California's statewide overhaul). However, it has also produced incoherent standards that prevent workforce portability, create confusion about competencies, and allow some states to maintain minimal standards that undermine public confidence in peer services. The variation is not primarily the result of intentional experimentation; it largely reflects the idiosyncratic decisions of individual state behavioral health authorities and the influence of local certification vendors.

**Rebuttal**: A national minimum standard with state flexibility for additional requirements would address both concerns. This approach -- used for social workers (ASWB exam) and counselors (NCE) -- preserves state regulatory authority while establishing a floor of competency that ensures public safety, enables workforce portability, and builds confidence among payers and employers. The current system is not healthy experimentation; it is a patchwork that harms peer workers (who cannot transfer credentials), consumers (who face inconsistent quality), and employers (who cannot assess out-of-state candidates' qualifications).

### Argument 6: Peer Support Undermines Clinical Authority

**Claim**: "Encouraging people in recovery to challenge clinical recommendations or assert personal preferences over professional judgment could lead to treatment non-adherence, relapse, or harm. The emphasis on 'self-determination' may be inappropriate for people with serious mental illness who lack insight into their conditions."

**Evidence Cited**: Arguments from the Treatment Advocacy Center and some academic psychiatrists who emphasize the role of anosognosia in serious mental illness. Cases where individuals who refused recommended treatment experienced negative outcomes.

**Reality**: The peer support model does not oppose clinical treatment; it complements it by supporting individuals in making informed decisions. SAMHSA's core competencies explicitly state that peer specialists should "support the recovery plan, including clinical and non-clinical activities" and should not "contradict or replace clinical advice." The concept of self-determination does not mean rejecting all professional guidance; it means ensuring that individuals are active participants in decisions about their own care rather than passive recipients of imposed treatment.

**Rebuttal**: The assumption that people with serious mental illness cannot participate in treatment decisions is contradicted by extensive evidence. Psychiatric advance directives, shared decision-making models, and person-centered treatment planning are all endorsed by the American Psychiatric Association itself. Peer specialists support these processes by helping individuals understand their options, articulate their preferences, and advocate for themselves within the clinical system. Far from undermining clinical authority, effective peer support improves treatment engagement: VA evaluations show that veterans who work with peer specialists have lower no-show rates and higher treatment completion rates than those without peer support.

## Historical Patterns of Opposition

Opposition to peer support has followed a consistent pattern since the 1970s consumer/survivor movement:

1. **Denial of expertise** (1970s-1990s): Psychiatric professionals dismissed the idea that people with mental illness could provide meaningful services. This opposition was rooted in the medical model's assumption that patients lack insight into their conditions. Early consumer/survivor organizations faced ridicule, hostility, and institutional obstruction.

2. **Safety and liability concerns** (1990s-2000s): As peer programs gained traction, opponents shifted to concerns about patient safety, boundary violations, and liability exposure for organizations employing peer workers. These concerns prompted the development of certification and training requirements that addressed safety while enabling growth.

3. **Evidence demands** (2000s-2010s): As safety data accumulated showing peer services were safe, opposition shifted to demanding RCT-level evidence before supporting expansion -- a standard not applied to many established clinical practices. This period saw the development of SAMHSA's evidence-based practice recognition and the beginning of systematic research.

4. **Scope of practice turf protection** (2010s-present): As peer services became billable through Medicaid, licensed professional guilds increasingly framed their opposition in terms of protecting professional boundaries and preventing unlicensed practice -- concerns that overlap with legitimate quality issues but are also driven by economic competition. This phase coincides with the expansion of peer roles into crisis services, emergency departments, and primary care.

5. **Standards and control** (2020s-present): The most recent phase involves efforts to control the terms of peer support professionalization -- who sets standards, who provides supervision, who determines scope of practice. Licensed professional associations increasingly seek to shape peer support frameworks rather than oppose them outright, positioning themselves as the appropriate supervisory authority over peer workers.

## Counter-Strategies

### Political Strategies

- **Build bipartisan coalitions**: Peer support has genuine bipartisan appeal. Conservatives value its community-based, non-governmental origins; liberals value its empowerment focus; both sides value cost savings. Frame peer support as a fiscally responsible, community-based alternative to expensive institutional care. The VA's peer specialist program provides a bipartisan touchstone that both parties support.
- **Engage veterans' organizations**: The VA's peer specialist program is popular across the political spectrum. Using VA success as a model for civilian expansion can neutralize opposition. Organizations like the Veterans of Foreign Wars (VFW), Disabled American Veterans (DAV), and the American Legion can be powerful allies.
- **Partner with law enforcement**: Police departments that use peer crisis response report reduced use-of-force incidents and repeat 911 calls. Law enforcement endorsement of peer services undermines claims that peers are unsafe. Programs like the Crisis Assistance Helping Out On The Streets (CAHOOTS) model in Eugene, Oregon, and the Tucson Police Department's Mental Health Support Team demonstrate law enforcement support for peer-involved alternatives.
- **Engage employers and business groups**: The economic case for peer support -- reduced absenteeism, improved productivity, lower healthcare costs -- can engage business organizations that are otherwise uninvolved in behavioral health policy.

### Communication Strategies

- **Lead with personal stories**: The most compelling argument for peer support is the testimony of people whose lives have been transformed by peer workers. Personal narratives counter abstract objections about evidence and safety. Peer storytelling events, legislative testimonials, and media features humanize the policy debate in ways that data alone cannot.
- **Emphasize cost savings**: Frame peer support as a fiscally responsible investment that reduces expensive emergency department visits, hospitalizations, and incarceration. Peer respite at $200-$400/day vs. inpatient at $2,264/day is a compelling comparison that resonates across the political spectrum.
- **Reframe the evidence debate**: Acknowledge evidence limitations while contextualizing them -- peer support has more evidence than many accepted clinical practices had at equivalent stages of development. The demand for "more evidence" before expansion should be met with the counter-question: "What level of evidence would you require before expanding access to any other service that shows these results?"
- **Use the VA as proof of concept**: The VA's 1,200+ peer specialist program, operating within the largest integrated health system in the country, provides institutional evidence that peer support works at scale in complex organizational environments.

### Policy Design Strategies

- **Minimum standards, not mandates**: Design national standards as floors with state flexibility, reducing opposition from states'-rights advocates and local innovators.
- **Sunset and evaluation clauses**: Include mandatory evaluation requirements in peer support legislation, addressing evidence concerns while providing data to support future expansion.
- **Collaborative scope-of-practice models**: Develop scope-of-practice guidelines jointly with licensed professional associations, turning potential opponents into co-designers with a stake in the outcome. The Pennsylvania model -- where the peer scope of practice was developed in consultation with social workers, counselors, and peer organizations -- reduced subsequent opposition.
- **Phased expansion**: Start with areas of strongest evidence and broadest support (SUD recovery support, crisis follow-up, veteran services) and expand to other populations as evidence accumulates.
- **Rate adequacy tied to outcomes**: Link Medicaid rate increases to measurable outcomes (reduced hospitalization, improved engagement, lower turnover), giving fiscal conservatives evidence that investment produces returns.
- **Peer supervisor credentials**: Establishing peer supervisor credentials addresses the supervision quality concern while creating career pathways and reducing dependence on clinical professionals for peer oversight.
