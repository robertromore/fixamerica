# Peer Support Services: Solutions

Strengthening peer support services requires a multi-faceted approach that addresses workforce sustainability, financing, certification, integration, and research simultaneously. Piecemeal reforms have produced incremental gains over the past two decades, but a coordinated federal-state strategy is needed to realize peer support's full potential as a component of the behavioral health system.

---

## Reform Framework

### Guiding Principles

- Peer support must remain grounded in lived experience, mutuality, and self-determination -- not become a low-cost substitute for clinical care
- The peer workforce deserves living wages, career pathways, and professional respect comparable to other paraprofessional health workers
- National standards should establish a quality floor while preserving state flexibility for innovation and adaptation
- Financing reform must include Medicaid rate adequacy, Medicare expansion, and private insurance coverage mandates
- Research investment should match the growing role of peer support in the behavioral health system
- Equity must be centered: certification pathways, training programs, and workforce recruitment must actively address racial, geographic, and socioeconomic disparities in the peer workforce

### Theory of Change

Federal legislation establishing national minimum certification standards and mandatory Medicaid/Medicare coverage, combined with appropriations for workforce development and research, will create sustainable financing and quality infrastructure. This infrastructure will reduce turnover, improve service quality, strengthen the evidence base, and build payer confidence -- creating a virtuous cycle that replaces the current vicious cycle of underfunding, high turnover, weak evidence, and payer skepticism.

```text
Current vicious cycle:
  Low funding --> Low wages --> High turnover --> Weak outcomes --> Weak evidence --> Payer skepticism --> Low funding

Proposed virtuous cycle:
  Federal investment --> Rate adequacy --> Living wages --> Workforce stability -->
  Better outcomes --> Stronger evidence --> Payer confidence --> Expanded coverage --> Sustained investment
```

## Proposed Solutions

### Solution 1: National Minimum Certification Standards

**Description**: Establish federal minimum standards for peer specialist certification while preserving state authority to add requirements. The national standard would include: minimum 75 hours of competency-based training aligned with SAMHSA's core competencies; a standardized written examination developed by a national credentialing body; 250 hours of supervised practical experience; 20 hours of continuing education annually; and reciprocity agreements enabling certified specialists to practice across state lines. States could add specialized credentials (forensic, youth, family, veteran) and additional requirements beyond the national floor.

**Evidence Base**: The International Credentialing and Reciprocity Consortium (IC&RC) and National Association of Peer Supporters (N.A.P.S.) have developed competency frameworks that provide a foundation for national standards. Georgia's certification model, the most replicated in the country, uses 75 hours of training and a written exam. A 2022 study found that states with more rigorous certification requirements reported lower rates of scope-of-practice complaints and higher employer satisfaction (C4 Innovations, 2022). The model already exists in comparable health workforces: the Association of Social Work Boards (ASWB) administers a national licensure exam used by all 50 states while each state retains authority over licensure criteria beyond the exam.

**Implementation**: Congress directs SAMHSA to convene a national certification standards committee including peer workers (minimum 50% representation), employers, state authorities, credentialing organizations, and consumer advocates. The committee develops standards within 18 months; states have 3 years to align certification programs with the national minimum. SAMHSA provides technical assistance grants to states requiring program modifications.

**Equity Safeguards**: The national standard must include provisions to prevent demographic narrowing of the workforce:

- Criminal background review processes (individualized assessment rather than blanket exclusions) with mandatory waiver procedures for convictions unrelated to peer work
- Training accessibility requirements (evening/weekend options, online/hybrid formats, stipends for trainees)
- Recognition of prior learning and experience to reduce duplicative training for experienced peers
- Language accessibility for non-English-speaking trainees

**Cost/Resources**: $15 million over 3 years for standards development, stakeholder engagement, and technical assistance to states.

**Timeline**: Standards development (18 months), state implementation (3 years), full compliance (5 years).

**Challenges**: State resistance to federal standards; opposition from certification vendors in states with lower current requirements; potential exclusion of currently certified peers who do not meet new standards (grandfather provisions needed); tension between standardization and the diversity of peer support models.

**Impact Metrics**: Interstate credential reciprocity rate (target: 90%+ within 5 years); employer satisfaction with peer preparation (target: 80%+); scope-of-practice complaint rate (target: 50% reduction); workforce demographic diversity (target: maintain or improve current diversity levels).

### Solution 2: Medicaid Rate Adequacy and Coverage Mandates

**Description**: Require all state Medicaid programs to cover peer support services and establish a federal floor for reimbursement rates. The rate floor would be set at 75% of the state's substance abuse counselor rate, ensuring that peer services are reimbursed at levels sufficient to fund living wages, benefits, supervision, and administrative costs. CMS would issue updated guidance requiring states to conduct annual rate adequacy reviews for peer services and adjust rates when they fall below the cost of delivering services. The guidance would also eliminate common billing restrictions that limit peer service effectiveness, including prohibitions on group service billing, restrictions on telephonic and telehealth delivery, and requirements for licensed clinician co-signature.

**Evidence Base**: A 2023 C4 Innovations analysis found that Medicaid rates in 14 states were insufficient to cover the fully loaded cost of employing peer specialists. States with higher reimbursement rates (Connecticut, Massachusetts, New York) report lower peer workforce turnover and higher service quality metrics. Georgia's rate increase from $12 to $22 per 15-minute unit in 2019 was followed by a 23% reduction in peer turnover within two years. The Medicaid and CHIP Payment and Access Commission (MACPAC) has recommended rate adequacy reviews for behavioral health services, and applying this framework to peer services would be consistent with existing policy direction.

**Implementation**: CMS issues updated State Medicaid Director Letter requiring coverage. Congress legislates the rate floor through Medicaid amendment. States have 2 years to comply. CMS provides technical assistance to states in developing rate methodologies that account for the full cost of peer service delivery, including the hidden costs of high turnover.

**Cost/Resources**: Estimated $200-$350 million annually in additional federal Medicaid matching funds, offset by reductions in emergency department utilization and hospitalization.

**Timeline**: CMS guidance (6 months), legislative action (1-2 years), state implementation (2-3 years).

**Challenges**: Opposition from states concerned about Medicaid budget impact; managed care organizations may resist rate increases; need to ensure rate increases flow to worker wages rather than organizational overhead. Wage pass-through provisions, similar to those used in home care worker rate increases, should be considered.

**Impact Metrics**: Peer specialist median wage (target: $25/hour within 5 years); annual turnover rate (target: below 20%); states meeting rate adequacy standard (target: 100%).

### Solution 3: Medicare Expansion for Peer Services

**Description**: Expand Medicare coverage of peer support services beyond the current opioid-specific authorization under the SUPPORT Act to cover peer services for all behavioral health conditions. This would make peer services available to Medicare beneficiaries with depression, anxiety, PTSD, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, and other mental health conditions -- not just those with opioid use disorders. The expansion would include both individual and group peer services, telephonic and telehealth delivery modalities, and peer-delivered transition support for beneficiaries discharged from psychiatric inpatient settings.

**Evidence Base**: The SUPPORT Act's peer support provision for opioid treatment has been implemented successfully, demonstrating that Medicare can effectively cover peer services. VA peer specialist programs serve veterans with the full range of mental health conditions with positive outcomes. There is no clinical or policy rationale for restricting Medicare peer support to a single condition. Medicare beneficiaries with serious mental illness have disproportionately high rates of hospitalization and ED utilization -- precisely the outcomes that peer support is most effective at reducing. A 2022 analysis estimated that peer support services for Medicare beneficiaries with SMI could reduce 30-day psychiatric readmission rates by 18-25%, saving Medicare an estimated $1.2-$2.0 billion annually.

**Implementation**: Congress amends the Social Security Act to add peer support services as a covered Medicare benefit for behavioral health conditions. CMS develops billing codes and provider enrollment standards. SAMHSA and CMS collaborate on quality standards for Medicare-enrolled peer service providers.

**Cost/Resources**: Estimated $150-$250 million annually in additional Medicare spending, offset by reductions in hospital readmissions and emergency department visits among Medicare beneficiaries with mental health conditions.

**Timeline**: Legislative action (1-2 years), CMS rulemaking (1 year), full implementation (3 years).

**Challenges**: Congressional Budget Office scoring may overestimate costs if savings from reduced hospitalization are not fully captured; Medicare physician payment rules may require adaptation for peer service billing; the Medicare program's clinical orientation may create documentation and medical necessity requirements that are more restrictive than Medicaid.

**Impact Metrics**: Medicare beneficiaries receiving peer services (target: 500,000 within 5 years); hospital readmission rates for Medicare beneficiaries with SMI (target: 15% reduction); Medicare spending on psychiatric hospitalization (target: $1+ billion reduction within 5 years).

### Solution 4: Private Insurance Coverage Requirements

**Description**: Require private health insurance plans to cover peer support services as an essential health benefit under the ACA. Peer services would be subject to the same mental health parity requirements as other behavioral health services under the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act (MHPAEA). HHS would issue guidance clarifying that exclusion of peer support from behavioral health benefit designs constitutes a non-quantitative treatment limitation (NQTL) subject to parity review.

**Evidence Base**: Fewer than 10% of commercial insurance plans currently cover peer services (National Council for Mental Wellbeing, 2023). Mental health parity law requires that insurers not impose more restrictive coverage limitations on behavioral health services than on medical/surgical services, but the exclusion of peer support from benefit designs has not been challenged under parity. Economic analyses of peer support consistently show positive or neutral cost impacts. The actuarial firm Milliman estimated that adding peer support to commercial behavioral health benefits would increase premiums by $0.50-$1.50 per member per month, an amount well within the range of other mandated benefits.

**Implementation**: HHS issues guidance clarifying that peer support qualifies as a behavioral health benefit subject to parity. Congress includes peer services in essential health benefit updates. State insurance departments enforce compliance for state-regulated plans. Federal enforcement applies to self-insured plans through ERISA.

**Cost/Resources**: Minimal federal cost; private insurance premium impacts estimated at $0.50-$1.50 per member per month.

**Timeline**: HHS guidance (1 year), legislative action if needed (2-3 years), plan compliance (1 year after regulation).

**Challenges**: Insurance industry opposition to new mandated benefits; definitional questions about which peer services qualify; need for credentialing standards before private payers will cover services; ERISA preemption may limit applicability to self-insured employer plans without federal action.

**Impact Metrics**: Commercial plans covering peer services (target: 80%+ within 5 years); privately insured individuals accessing peer support (target: 250,000 annually).

### Solution 5: Peer Workforce Development Investment

**Description**: Create a dedicated federal peer support workforce development program, funded at $150 million annually, to support training program expansion, career ladder development, supervision training, loan forgiveness for peer specialists pursuing advanced credentials, and targeted recruitment in underserved communities. The program would include competitive grants to states and organizations, scholarships for peer specialist training, and a national peer specialist mentorship initiative. A dedicated funding stream for peer workforce diversity initiatives would ensure that training and recruitment reach communities of color, rural areas, tribal communities, and LGBTQ+ populations.

**Evidence Base**: The Health Resources and Services Administration (HRSA) operates workforce development programs for physicians (National Health Service Corps), nurses (Nurse Corps), and other health professionals that have demonstrated effectiveness in expanding supply and improving distribution. No equivalent program exists for peer specialists. The peer workforce needs targeted investment comparable to other behavioral health workforce categories. The HRSA behavioral health workforce development program (funded at approximately $400 million annually) includes training grants for psychologists, social workers, and counselors but does not include a dedicated peer specialist track.

**Implementation**: Congress authorizes and appropriates funding through HRSA. SAMHSA provides technical assistance. Grants distributed competitively to states and organizations with emphasis on underserved areas and populations.

**Career Ladder Framework**:

| Level | Title | Requirements | Median Wage Target | Role |
|-------|-------|-------------|-------------------|------|
| 1 | Peer Support Specialist | National certification (75 hours + exam + practicum) | $22-$25/hour | Direct peer support services |
| 2 | Senior Peer Specialist | 3+ years experience + advanced training (40 hours) | $25-$28/hour | Complex cases, mentoring new peers |
| 3 | Peer Supervisor | Supervisor credential + supervisory training (60 hours) | $28-$32/hour | Supervision of peer specialists |
| 4 | Peer Program Manager | 5+ years experience + management training | $32-$38/hour | Program administration and development |
| 5 | Peer Workforce Director | Advanced credential + leadership development | $38-$45/hour | Organizational leadership, policy, training |

**Cost/Resources**: $150 million annually ($50 million for training grants, $30 million for career ladder programs, $25 million for supervision training, $25 million for scholarships and loan forgiveness, $20 million for recruitment, mentorship, and diversity initiatives).

**Timeline**: Authorization (1-2 years), first grant cycle (1 year after appropriation), full program operation (3 years).

**Challenges**: Competition for limited appropriations; need to design programs that reach the most marginalized potential peer workers, not only those with existing educational advantages; coordination with existing HRSA and SAMHSA workforce programs.

**Impact Metrics**: New peer specialists certified annually (target: 10,000); peer specialist diversity (target: workforce demographics match community demographics within 5 years); career advancement rate (target: 25% of peers advance to supervisory/leadership roles within 5 years); retention rate for program participants (target: 80%+ at 2 years).

### Solution 6: Peer Respite and Peer-Run Services Expansion

**Description**: Create a dedicated federal grant program for peer respite centers and peer-run organizations, funded at $75 million annually for 10 years. The program would support the development of 200 new peer respite programs (quadrupling the current number from ~50), expansion of warmlines to all 50 states, and capacity building for peer-run drop-in centers and recovery community organizations. Funding would include capital grants for facility development and operating grants for the first 5 years, after which programs would transition to Medicaid and insurance reimbursement. A technical assistance component would support new programs in developing sustainable business models, governance structures, and quality improvement processes.

**Evidence Base**: Existing peer respite programs divert 60-70% of guests from psychiatric hospitalization (peer respite outcome studies, 2021). At a daily cost of $200-$400 compared to $2,264 for psychiatric inpatient care (HCUP, 2022), peer respite generates $10,000-$18,000 in savings per diverted hospitalization. Warmlines reduce crisis escalation and emergency service utilization, with 70-80% of callers not requiring crisis services after warmline contact. The challenge is that only ~50 peer respite programs and ~70 warmlines currently operate nationally -- far below the level needed to serve as a genuine systemic alternative to hospitalization.

Notable program models to replicate:

- **Afiya House** (Massachusetts): Long-operating model demonstrating sustainability and effectiveness in a Medicaid-funded environment
- **Rose House** (New York): Urban model demonstrating viability in high-cost settings
- **Second Story** (California): West Coast model with strong outcome data showing 70% hospital diversion rate
- **Peer Wellness Center** (Colorado): Integrated model combining respite, warmline, and drop-in services

**Implementation**: SAMHSA administers competitive grants. States must provide 25% match. Programs must be governed by people with lived experience (minimum 51% of board members). Technical assistance provided for program development, Medicaid billing transition, and quality improvement.

**Cost/Resources**: $75 million annually for 10 years ($750 million total). Capital grants average $500,000 per site; operating grants average $300,000 annually.

**Timeline**: First grants awarded (Year 1), 50 new programs (Year 3), 200 new programs (Year 7), full transition to insurance billing (Year 10).

**Challenges**: Zoning and community opposition to residential programs; recruiting qualified peer leadership for new programs; transitioning from grant funding to sustainable reimbursement; maintaining peer-governed culture as programs scale.

**Impact Metrics**: Operating peer respite programs (target: 250 by Year 10); annual hospitalizations diverted (target: 50,000); cost savings per year (target: $500 million in avoided inpatient costs); warmlines operating in all 50 states.

### Solution 7: Dedicated Federal Peer Support Research Program

**Description**: Establish a dedicated NIH/SAMHSA joint research program for peer support services, funded at $50 million annually. The program would support large-scale multi-site randomized trials, implementation science research, health economics studies, and the development of peer-specific outcome measures. A research advisory committee including peer workers and consumer researchers (minimum 40% lived experience representation) would guide priorities. The program would explicitly fund research on peer support effectiveness across diverse populations, including communities of color, rural areas, LGBTQ+ populations, and justice-involved individuals -- populations underrepresented in the existing evidence base.

**Research Priority Areas**:

- Large-scale, multi-site RCTs of peer support in crisis services, primary care integration, and forensic settings
- Implementation science research on organizational factors that support or undermine effective peer integration
- Health economics studies on the cost-effectiveness and return on investment of peer services across settings
- Development and validation of peer-specific outcome measures capturing empowerment, hope, social connection, and self-determination
- Comparative effectiveness research on different peer support models (clinical integration vs. autonomous peer-run vs. hybrid)
- Longitudinal studies on peer specialist career trajectories, retention factors, and workforce sustainability
- Research on the impact of peer workforce diversity on outcomes for communities of color

**Evidence Base**: NIH spending on peer support research totaled approximately $12 million in FY2023, compared to over $300 million for psychotherapy research (NIH RePORTER, 2024). Only 18 RCTs of mental health peer support have been published. The evidence gap is the most frequently cited justification for limiting peer service expansion and coverage.

**Implementation**: NIH creates a peer support research initiative within NIMH. SAMHSA funds practice-based research and implementation studies. Annual funding allocated through competitive grants with emphasis on community-based participatory research approaches.

**Cost/Resources**: $50 million annually ($30 million NIH, $20 million SAMHSA).

**Timeline**: Program established (Year 1), first major trials initiated (Year 2), initial results (Year 5), comprehensive evidence base (Year 10).

**Challenges**: Academic culture that undervalues peer-delivered services as a research topic; difficulty recruiting principal investigators with peer support expertise; methodological challenges of studying relational interventions; need to develop and validate new outcome measures.

**Impact Metrics**: Peer support RCTs published (target: 50+ within 10 years); peer-specific outcome measures validated (target: 5+); research-to-practice translation (target: evidence-based guidelines updated every 3 years); funding portfolio diversity (target: 40%+ of funded studies include non-White populations).

### Solution 8: Peer Supervision Model Reform

**Description**: Establish a national framework for peer support supervision that values peer expertise alongside clinical oversight. The framework would create a dedicated peer supervisor credential recognized across states, require that peer specialists receive supervision from someone trained in peer support competencies (whether a licensed clinician with peer training or a certified peer supervisor), and develop training curricula for both peer and clinical supervisors of peer workers. The framework would also address the supervision access gap in rural areas by authorizing telehealth supervision and regional supervision cooperatives.

**Evidence Base**: The 2022 National Peer Workforce Survey found that 41% of peer specialists reported supervisors with no training in peer support competencies, and 28% reported being asked to perform tasks outside the peer scope of practice by supervisors. States with peer supervisor credentials (Pennsylvania, Connecticut, Georgia, among 15 total) report higher peer worker satisfaction, lower turnover, and fewer scope-of-practice complaints. The N.A.P.S. National Practice Guidelines for Supervisors (2019) provide a comprehensive framework for peer-competent supervision that could serve as the foundation for a national model.

**Implementation**: SAMHSA publishes a national supervision framework. States align their supervision requirements within 3 years. HRSA workforce development funds support supervisor training program development and delivery.

**Cost/Resources**: $10 million annually for supervision training development and delivery (included in workforce development program).

**Timeline**: Framework development (Year 1), supervisor training program launch (Year 2), state adoption (Years 2-4).

**Impact Metrics**: States with peer supervisor credential (target: 40+ within 5 years); peer specialists receiving peer-competent supervision (target: 80%+); supervisor training completions (target: 5,000 within 5 years).

## International Models

### United Kingdom: NHS Peer Support Worker Programme

**Description**: The UK's National Health Service has integrated peer support workers into mental health trusts (regional health authorities) across England since 2010. Peers receive standardized training through the Implementing Recovery through Organizational Change (ImROC) framework and are employed directly by NHS trusts at wages comparable to other healthcare support workers (Band 3-4 on the NHS pay scale, approximately $24,000-$28,000/year). The ImROC program, led by Julie Repper and colleagues, developed a comprehensive organizational change model that addresses not only peer worker training but also the culture, policies, and practices of the organizations that employ them.

**Results**: A 2019 NHS England evaluation found that trusts employing peer workers reported 20% reductions in inpatient bed days, improved patient satisfaction scores, and positive impacts on organizational recovery orientation. By 2023, over 2,000 peer workers were employed across NHS mental health services. Retention rates exceed 70% annually -- significantly higher than U.S. averages.

**Applicability to U.S.**: The NHS model demonstrates that peer workers can be integrated into a large, complex health system with standardized roles, wages, and career pathways. The U.S. would need to adapt this to its multi-payer system, but the VA's peer specialist program already demonstrates similar integration in a large government health system. The ImROC organizational change framework is directly adaptable to U.S. health systems and CCBHCs.

### Australia: Peer Workforce Development Framework

**Description**: Australia's National Mental Health Commission developed a national peer workforce framework in 2019 that defines competencies, career pathways, and organizational standards for peer work. States and territories align their certification programs with the national framework while retaining implementation flexibility. The framework was developed through extensive consultation with peer workers, consumers, and service providers, and includes specific provisions for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peer workers.

**Results**: Australia's peer workforce grew from approximately 1,500 in 2015 to over 4,000 in 2023, with annual retention rates above 80% in states that adopted living wage standards. The national framework enabled workforce portability across states, resolving a portability problem similar to that facing the U.S. peer workforce.

**Applicability to U.S.**: Australia's federated model -- national standards with state implementation -- is directly relevant to the U.S. federal system. The Australian approach of separating competency standards (national) from certification administration (state) could be replicated. Australia's attention to Indigenous peer workforce development offers a model for U.S. approaches to Native American/Alaska Native peer services.

### New Zealand: Peer Support and Te Whare Tapa Wha

**Description**: New Zealand integrates peer support with its indigenous Maori health framework (Te Whare Tapa Wha), recognizing cultural and spiritual dimensions of wellbeing alongside mental, physical, and social health. Peer workers are trained in culturally responsive practices and serve diverse populations. The "Let's get real" framework establishes competencies for all mental health and addiction workers, including peers, within a culturally grounded context.

**Results**: New Zealand's peer workforce demonstrates above-average retention rates and high consumer satisfaction, attributed partly to the holistic, culturally grounded framework that aligns well with peer support values of whole-person wellness.

**Applicability to U.S.**: The New Zealand model offers lessons for integrating peer support with cultural responsiveness, particularly relevant for serving Native American/Alaska Native, Latino, and African American communities in the U.S. where culturally specific peer approaches may be more effective than standardized models.

### Canada: Provincial Peer Support Integration

**Description**: Canadian provinces have developed peer support systems within their single-payer health systems, with Ontario and British Columbia leading. Ontario's Peer Support Canada organization provides a national certification that is recognized across provinces, achieving the portability that the U.S. has not yet accomplished. British Columbia's Peer Support Worker Training provided through the Justice Institute of British Columbia includes specialized tracks for Indigenous, LGBTQ+, and youth populations.

**Results**: Canada's national certification model has achieved cross-provincial recognition with fewer than 10 years of operation, demonstrating that national standards and state/provincial flexibility can coexist. Retention rates in provinces with living wage standards exceed 75%.

**Applicability to U.S.**: Canada's experience demonstrates that a national certification can coexist with provincial implementation in a federated system, and its rapid implementation timeline suggests the U.S. could achieve similar results if political will exists.

## Pilot Programs and Demonstrations

- **Georgia Peer Support Program**: The nation's first comprehensive state peer specialist program (2001-present) has served as the model for programs nationwide. Georgia's experience demonstrates that a well-designed state program can achieve workforce stability when reimbursement rates are adequate. Georgia's 2019 rate increase produced measurable improvements in retention and service quality.
- **VA Peer Specialist Program**: The VA's program (1,200+ peer specialists) provides the largest-scale demonstration of peer integration into a complex health system. Internal evaluations show reduced hospitalization, improved engagement, and higher satisfaction among veterans served by peers. The VA's Whole Health initiative has expanded peer roles into health coaching and wellness planning.
- **Pathways to Housing (New York)**: This Housing First program integrates peer specialists into assertive community treatment teams serving people experiencing chronic homelessness and serious mental illness. Peer-delivered services are associated with improved housing retention and reduced emergency department utilization.
- **CCBHC Demonstration**: Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinics (500+ sites in 46 states) are required to provide peer support services, creating the largest mandatory integration of peer services into community behavioral health care. The CCBHC Prospective Payment System provides more sustainable funding for peer services than fee-for-service Medicaid.
- **CAHOOTS (Eugene, Oregon)**: While not a peer-only program, CAHOOTS integrates peer support workers with medics on mobile crisis response teams that respond to behavioral health 911 calls in lieu of police. The program diverts approximately 5-8% of total 911 call volume and has been replicated in over 20 cities. CAHOOTS demonstrates the viability of peer-involved crisis response at the municipal level.
- **Peer Bridger Program (New York)**: Developed at the Howie the Harp Advocacy Center, this program uses peer specialists to support individuals during the transition from psychiatric inpatient care to community living. The program has demonstrated reduced readmission rates and improved community tenure among participants.

## Sequencing and Prioritization

### Quick Wins (0-2 years)

- CMS issues updated State Medicaid Director Letter requiring all state Medicaid programs to cover peer support services
- HHS issues guidance clarifying peer services as a behavioral health benefit subject to mental health parity
- SAMHSA publishes national core competency framework for peer specialist certification
- Congress authorizes first-year appropriation for peer workforce development program
- SAMHSA publishes national peer supervision framework

### Medium-term Reforms (2-5 years)

- Congress legislates Medicaid rate floor for peer services at 75% of substance abuse counselor rate
- Congress expands Medicare peer support coverage to all behavioral health conditions
- States align certification programs with national minimum standards
- First wave of new peer respite programs opens (50 new sites)
- NIH/SAMHSA peer support research program launches first multi-site trials
- 40+ states adopt peer supervisor credentials
- BLS initiates process to establish peer specialist as distinct occupational category

### Long-term Transformation (5-10 years)

- All states achieve certification reciprocity and rate adequacy compliance
- Peer workforce grows to 75,000+ with annual turnover below 20%
- 250 peer respite programs operating nationally, diverting 50,000+ hospitalizations annually
- Comprehensive evidence base from 50+ RCTs guides practice and policy
- Private insurance coverage of peer services reaches 80%+ of commercial plans
- Peer specialist median wage reaches $25/hour
- BLS publishes separate occupational data for peer specialists

## Complementary Policies

- **Behavioral health workforce expansion**: Peer support solutions must be coordinated with broader workforce strategies, including clinician training, loan forgiveness, and telehealth expansion. See [Workforce Shortage](../workforce-shortage/README.md).
- **988 Crisis Lifeline integration**: Peer support expansion should align with 988 implementation, ensuring peers are part of crisis response teams. See [Crisis Services](../crisis-services/README.md).
- **Insurance parity enforcement**: Stronger enforcement of the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act would support peer service coverage mandates. See [Insurance Parity](../insurance-parity/README.md).
- **Community mental health investment**: CCBHC expansion and community mental health center funding directly support peer service infrastructure. See [Community Mental Health](../community-mental-health/README.md).
- **Criminal justice reform**: Forensic peer specialist expansion depends on broader reforms in diversion, reentry, and alternatives to incarceration. See [Justice: Reentry](../../justice/reentry/README.md).
- **Housing policy**: Peer support is most effective when basic needs are met; housing instability undermines recovery. See [Housing](../../housing/README.md).
