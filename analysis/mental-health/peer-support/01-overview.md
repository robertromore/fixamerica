# Peer Support Services: Overview

## Overview

Peer support services represent a paradigm shift in behavioral health care delivery, centering the expertise of individuals with personal lived experience of mental health conditions, substance use disorders, or co-occurring disorders. Certified peer specialists, recovery coaches, and peer navigators use their own recovery journeys to provide emotional support, share knowledge, model self-advocacy, and help others navigate complex systems of care. Unlike clinical interventions, peer support operates through mutuality, shared experience, and the demonstration that recovery is possible -- a form of evidence that no textbook or credential can replicate.

The United States now employs an estimated 30,000 to 35,000 peer support workers across behavioral health settings, a figure that has grown rapidly since the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) issued its 2007 letter authorizing Medicaid reimbursement for peer support services. As of 2024, 49 states and the District of Columbia have established peer specialist certification programs, and 46 states have approved Medicaid billing codes for peer-delivered services. The Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA) has identified peer support as an evidence-based practice and a core element of recovery-oriented systems of care.

Yet the promise of peer support remains substantially unfulfilled. The peer workforce suffers from annual turnover rates of 30-50%, driven by wages that average $17.65 per hour nationally, limited career advancement, and organizational cultures that too often treat peer workers as subordinate to clinical staff. Medicaid reimbursement rates vary wildly across states -- from under $15 per unit in some states to over $70 in others -- and many private insurers do not cover peer services at all. Peer-run organizations, which embody the most autonomous model of peer support, operate on shoestring budgets and remain excluded from most insurance billing structures.

The peer support field also faces a fundamental tension between professionalization and authenticity. As states impose increasingly stringent certification requirements -- some demanding 75 or more hours of training, background checks, and continuing education -- the field risks excluding the very individuals whose marginalized experiences make them most effective: people with criminal justice involvement, people in early recovery, and people from communities of color disproportionately affected by the mental health system's failures. Balancing quality standards with accessibility to the workforce is perhaps the defining challenge of the next decade.

SAMHSA's 2015 publication of core competencies for peer workers in behavioral health services identified twelve domains of expertise -- including recovery-oriented, person-centered, and culturally responsive practice -- that distinguish peer support from clinical services. These competencies have been adopted, in whole or in part, by the majority of state certification programs. However, their implementation varies widely: some states use the competencies as aspirational guidelines, while others have embedded them into required training curricula, examination standards, and continuing education mandates. The gap between competency frameworks and actual practice conditions remains a persistent challenge for the field.

## Scope

This analysis covers the key aspects of peer support services in the United States:

- **Certified Peer Specialists**: Training, certification standards, scope of practice, and workforce demographics across states, including specialty credentials for forensic, youth, family, and veteran peer specialists
- **Medicaid and Insurance Reimbursement**: CMS guidelines, state plan amendments, billing codes, reimbursement rates, private payer coverage, and the impact of managed care administration on peer service access
- **Peer-Run Organizations and Services**: Peer respite centers, warmlines, drop-in centers, peer-run crisis alternatives, Wellness Recovery Action Plan (WRAP) programs, and recovery community organizations
- **Workforce Development and Sustainability**: Wages, turnover, career ladders, supervision models, integration into multidisciplinary teams, and the impact of organizational culture on peer worker retention
- **Evidence Base and Research**: Randomized controlled trials, quasi-experimental studies, SAMHSA toolkit findings, implementation science, health economics analyses, and research gaps
- **Equity and Access**: Racial disparities in the peer workforce, cultural responsiveness, rural access, the impact of criminal background exclusions, and the intersection of peer support with other social determinants of health
- **Crisis Services Integration**: Peer roles within the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, mobile crisis response teams, emergency department diversion programs, and law enforcement co-response models

## Core Tensions and Tradeoffs

- **Professionalization vs. Accessibility**: Increasing certification requirements improve quality assurance but risk excluding individuals whose lived experience with incarceration, homelessness, or marginalization makes them most effective as peers. Higher bars to entry disproportionately affect people of color and those with limited formal education. States requiring 100+ training hours and extensive background checks report more demographically homogeneous peer workforces than states with lower barriers.

- **Clinical Integration vs. Autonomy**: Embedding peer workers in clinical teams improves coordination and sustainability but risks co-opting peer roles into quasi-clinical functions, undermining the non-hierarchical, mutual relationship that defines effective peer support. Programs like Intentional Peer Support (IPS), developed by Shery Mead, explicitly reject clinical integration in favor of community-based, peer-controlled models, while organizations like the National Council for Mental Wellbeing advocate for integration as the path to scale.

- **Medicaid Reimbursement vs. Service Flexibility**: Billing through Medicaid provides sustainable funding but imposes documentation requirements, time-limited service models, and medical necessity criteria that conflict with the open-ended, relationship-based nature of peer support. Peer workers report spending 25% or more of their time on billing documentation rather than direct service delivery.

- **Evidence Standards vs. Innovation**: Demanding randomized controlled trial evidence before scaling peer services holds them to a higher standard than many established clinical practices, while the relational and contextual nature of peer support makes it inherently difficult to study with traditional methods. Only 18 RCTs of mental health peer support had been published as of 2023, compared to over 500 for cognitive behavioral therapy.

- **State Variation vs. National Standards**: Allowing state-level experimentation has produced innovation but also created a patchwork system where certification, scope of practice, reimbursement, and quality vary dramatically, hindering workforce mobility and program consistency. Peer specialists who relocate must often re-certify from scratch, a barrier that does not exist for most licensed clinical professions.

- **Lived Experience as Qualification vs. Lived Experience as Liability**: The peer model is built on the premise that personal experience with mental health conditions or substance use disorders is an asset. Yet the same system that values this experience may also penalize it -- through background check exclusions, employment discrimination, and workplace stigma that treats peers as less trustworthy than colleagues without disclosed psychiatric histories.

## Key Questions

1. How can peer support certification standards ensure quality without excluding the individuals whose lived experience makes them uniquely effective?
2. What reimbursement and financing models can sustain peer services without forcing them into the medical model frameworks they were designed to complement?
3. How should scope of practice be defined to protect both peer workers and the people they serve while preserving the distinctiveness of the peer role?
4. What evidence standards are appropriate for evaluating a service model rooted in shared experience, relationship, and mutuality rather than standardized clinical protocols?
5. How can the peer workforce achieve demographic parity with the populations it serves, particularly in communities of color and rural areas where behavioral health disparities are most severe?
6. What organizational and supervisory structures best support peer worker retention and professional development without imposing clinical hierarchies that undermine peer values?

## Vision of Success

A successful peer support system in the 21st century would feature:

- **Universal Coverage and Access**: Peer support services available and reimbursable in every state, through Medicaid, Medicare, private insurance, and Veterans Health Administration, with no geographic or demographic gaps in access. Every American in behavioral health crisis would have access to a peer specialist within 24 hours, whether through in-person, telehealth, or warmline services.
- **Living Wages and Career Pathways**: Peer specialists earning a median wage of at least $25 per hour, with clear career ladders into supervisory, training, and leadership roles, reducing annual turnover to below 15%. Advanced peer credentials in specialties such as crisis services, forensic support, and integrated care would carry commensurate salary increases.
- **Equitable Workforce**: A peer workforce that reflects the racial, ethnic, linguistic, and socioeconomic diversity of the populations it serves, with certification pathways accessible to people with criminal justice histories and limited formal education. Targeted recruitment programs in communities of color, rural areas, and tribal communities would address longstanding demographic gaps.
- **Integrated but Distinct**: Peer workers fully integrated into multidisciplinary behavioral health teams as valued members with a clearly defined, protected scope of practice that preserves the non-clinical, mutual nature of the peer relationship. Organizational cultures that actively affirm peer identity and protect against role drift into quasi-clinical functions.
- **Robust Evidence Base**: A sustained federal research investment producing rigorous evidence on peer support effectiveness across populations, settings, and service models, with findings driving continuous improvement. Peer-specific outcome measures capturing empowerment, hope, social connection, and self-determination alongside traditional clinical metrics.
- **Peer-Run Services at Scale**: A national network of at least 250 peer respite centers, warmlines in every state, and peer-run drop-in centers in every major metropolitan area, providing alternatives to institutional care and demonstrating the viability of consumer-controlled service models.
