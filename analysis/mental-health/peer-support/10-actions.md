# Peer Support Services: What You Can Do

Peer support services represent one of the most promising and cost-effective innovations in behavioral health, yet the peer workforce remains underpaid, undertrained, and underutilized because it lacks the organized political constituency that clinical professions enjoy. Citizens, whether they have personal experience with mental health conditions or not, can play a critical role in advocating for the policy changes needed to build a sustainable, evidence-based peer support system.

---

## Individual Actions

### Learn and Stay Informed

- Read the National Association of Peer Supporters (N.A.P.S.) Practice Guidelines to understand what peer support is and how it differs from clinical services: <https://www.peersupportworks.org/practice-guidelines>
- Follow SAMHSA's peer support resources page for updates on federal policy, training opportunities, and research: <https://www.samhsa.gov/brss-tacs/recovery-support-tools/peers>
- Subscribe to the National Council for Mental Wellbeing newsletter for updates on CCBHC expansion and community behavioral health policy: <https://www.thenationalcouncil.org/>
- Track your state's peer specialist certification requirements, Medicaid billing status, and reimbursement rates through NASHP's state policy tracker: <https://www.nashp.org/>
- Review your state's Medicaid fee schedule for peer support services (usually available on the state Medicaid agency website) to understand whether reimbursement rates in your state are adequate to support living wages for peer workers
- Familiarize yourself with the SAMHSA Core Competencies for Peer Workers (2015), the foundational document defining what peer specialists should know and be able to do: <https://www.samhsa.gov/brss-tacs/recovery-support-tools/peers>

### Make Personal Changes

- If you have lived experience with a mental health condition or substance use disorder, consider becoming a certified peer specialist. Training programs are available in 49 states and D.C., with most requiring 75 hours of coursework. Contact your state behavioral health authority for certification information. Many states offer training at low or no cost, and some provide stipends during training periods.
- If you are in recovery, share your story publicly when you are comfortable doing so. Personal narratives are the most powerful advocacy tool for peer support -- they demonstrate that recovery is real and that people with lived experience have expertise to offer. Organizations like Faces and Voices of Recovery and NAMI offer structured storytelling programs that provide training and support for sharing personal experiences.
- If you receive behavioral health services, ask your provider whether peer support services are available. Consumer demand drives service development. If your provider does not offer peer services, ask why, and request a referral to a program that does.
- Create or participate in a Wellness Recovery Action Plan (WRAP) group in your community. WRAP is a peer-led, evidence-based self-management tool available at no cost: <https://www.copelandcenter.com/>
- If you work in behavioral health, examine your own attitudes toward peer specialists. Do you treat them as equals on the care team? Do you include them in treatment planning meetings? Do you value their experiential expertise alongside your clinical training? Implicit bias against people with psychiatric histories affects even well-meaning clinicians.

### Use Your Voice

- **Contact Your Representatives**: Urge your U.S. senators and representatives to support federal legislation for peer workforce development, Medicaid rate adequacy, and Medicare expansion of peer services. Key congressional committees include Senate HELP and House Energy and Commerce. Use the Capitol switchboard (202-224-3121) to be connected to your representatives' offices.
- **Submit Public Comments**: When CMS, SAMHSA, or your state Medicaid agency proposes rules affecting peer services, submit public comments. Federal comment opportunities are posted at <https://www.regulations.gov/>. Even brief comments matter: agencies are required to read and respond to every comment, and high comment volumes signal public interest to policymakers.
- **Write Letters to the Editor**: When your local newspaper covers mental health topics, write letters highlighting peer support as a cost-effective, evidence-based service that deserves adequate funding. Cite the $200-$400 per day cost of peer respite versus $2,264 per day for psychiatric inpatient care (HCUP, 2022). Letters to the editor are among the most-read sections of local newspapers and are routinely monitored by elected officials' staffs.
- **Share Information**: Use social media to amplify peer support research findings, personal recovery stories, and policy developments. Tag organizations like NAMI, N.A.P.S., and Mental Health America to broaden reach. September is National Recovery Month, and peer support awareness events provide timely content hooks.
- **Attend Public Hearings**: State legislatures, Medicaid advisory committees, and behavioral health planning councils hold public hearings where citizen testimony can influence policy. Many states have Medicaid advisory committees that include consumer representation -- these bodies review Medicaid policy changes including peer service coverage and rates.

## Community Actions

### Organize Locally

- Form a peer support advocacy coalition in your community, bringing together people with lived experience, family members, behavioral health providers, and civic leaders to advocate for local peer service expansion. Successful local coalitions in states like Ohio, Pennsylvania, and Georgia have driven state-level policy changes by demonstrating community support for peer services.
- Host a community screening of documentaries about the mental health consumer/survivor movement, followed by discussion about peer support in your area. *Beyond the Medical Model* and other recovery-focused films are available through the National Empowerment Center. Community education events build public awareness and recruit potential advocates.
- Partner with your local community mental health center or CCBHC to establish or expand peer support services. More than 500 CCBHCs are required to provide peer services -- ask whether yours has adequate peer staffing and whether peer workers are meaningfully integrated into care teams or merely employed in token compliance.
- Organize a peer respite feasibility study in your community. Only ~50 peer respite programs currently operate nationally; new programs are needed in every region. The National Empowerment Center provides technical assistance for new programs. Start by identifying potential sites, assessing community need, and engaging local behavioral health leadership.
- Establish or join a warmline in your state. Thirteen states still lack any warmline service. Warmlines can be operated by small peer-run organizations with relatively modest budgets ($100,000-$300,000 annually), making them an achievable community-level project. The National Empowerment Center maintains a warmline development guide.
- Connect with your local Clubhouse International affiliate (approximately 350 clubhouses in the U.S.) to learn about the clubhouse model and identify opportunities for collaboration with the broader peer support network.

### Build Coalitions

- Connect with your local NAMI affiliate (600,000+ members nationally) to include peer workforce advocacy in their policy agenda. Many NAMI chapters already support peer services but may not prioritize workforce issues such as wages, certification portability, and career advancement.
- Partner with recovery community organizations (RCOs) in your area. Faces and Voices of Recovery maintains a directory of RCOs: <https://facesandvoicesofrecovery.org/>. RCOs are natural allies for peer support advocacy because they are led by people in recovery and often employ peer workers.
- Engage faith-based organizations in peer support advocacy. Many congregations operate informal peer support through pastoral care and support groups; connecting these efforts with the formal peer workforce can build unlikely alliances across political lines. The Interfaith Disability Advocacy Coalition has experience bridging faith communities and disability/mental health advocacy.
- Work with local law enforcement to advocate for peer specialists on mobile crisis response teams. Police departments that use peers report reduced use-of-force incidents and repeat 911 calls -- a compelling argument for law enforcement allies. The CAHOOTS model in Eugene, Oregon, and similar programs in Denver, Tucson, and other cities provide concrete examples to share.
- Partner with local hospitals and emergency departments. A growing number of hospitals (200+) are integrating peer specialists into ED behavioral health teams. Hospital social workers and emergency physicians who have seen peers de-escalate crises and facilitate discharge planning can be powerful advocates for expansion.
- Engage local workforce development boards and employment agencies. Peer specialist certification is a workforce credential that can be promoted through existing workforce development infrastructure, including American Job Centers (One-Stop Career Centers) and vocational rehabilitation programs.
- Connect with local veteran service organizations (VFW, DAV, American Legion). The VA's peer specialist program is widely respected among veterans; using it as a model for civilian expansion builds bipartisan coalition support.

## Political Actions

### Contact Elected Officials

**When to Contact**:

- During congressional budget season (February-October), when SAMHSA and HRSA appropriations are being set
- When your state legislature considers Medicaid budget bills or behavioral health workforce legislation
- When CMS issues proposed rules affecting Medicaid behavioral health coverage
- During State Plan Amendment or waiver renewal processes in your state
- When the Senate HELP Committee or House Energy and Commerce Committee holds hearings on mental health policy
- When your state behavioral health authority proposes changes to peer specialist certification requirements, scope of practice, or Medicaid billing codes
- During gubernatorial budget proposal season at the state level

**What to Say - Template Email**:

```text
Subject: Support Federal Investment in Peer Support Services

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

I am writing as a constituent from [City, State] to urge your support for
federal legislation to strengthen peer support services in our behavioral
health system. Peer specialists -- people who use their own recovery
experience to help others -- are among the most effective and cost-efficient
workers in mental health and addiction services, yet they earn a median wage
of just $17.65 per hour and face annual turnover rates of 30-50%.

I ask you to support legislation that would:
- Establish national minimum certification standards for peer specialists
  with interstate reciprocity
- Require Medicaid coverage of peer services in all states with adequate
  reimbursement rates
- Expand Medicare coverage of peer support to all behavioral health
  conditions, not just opioid treatment
- Fund peer workforce development through HRSA at $150 million annually

Peer respite programs cost $200-$400 per day compared to $2,264 per day for
psychiatric hospitalization, and studies show peer services reduce emergency
department visits by 9-23%. Investing in the peer workforce saves money
while improving outcomes for the 57.8 million Americans with mental illness.

[Optional personal paragraph: If you have personal experience with peer
support -- as a peer specialist, a family member, or a recipient of peer
services -- share it briefly here. Personal stories are the most persuasive
form of constituent communication.]

Thank you for your consideration of this critical behavioral health issue.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your City, State]
```

### Support Legislation

Current or proposed legislation to support:

- **Peer Support Workforce Sustainability Act** (proposed): Would authorize $150 million annually for peer workforce development through HRSA. Urge your representatives to introduce and co-sponsor this legislation.
- **Medicare peer support expansion** (building on SUPPORT Act, P.L. 115-271): The SUPPORT Act authorized Medicare coverage of peer services for opioid treatment only. Urge expansion to all behavioral health conditions.
- **CCBHC reauthorization and expansion**: Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinics are required to provide peer services. Support full CCBHC reauthorization with adequate peer workforce funding and quality standards for peer integration.
- **State-level Medicaid rate increases**: Contact your state legislators and Medicaid agency to advocate for peer service reimbursement rates at or above 75% of the substance abuse counselor rate in your state.
- **988 Implementation Act funding**: The 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline includes peer roles in crisis follow-up; support full funding for 988 implementation including peer specialist positions.
- **Criminal justice reform with peer provisions**: Support legislation that includes forensic peer specialist roles in diversion, reentry, and drug court programs.

### Electoral Engagement

**Questions to Ask Candidates**:

1. "Do you support federal funding for peer support workforce development, including training grants, career ladder programs, and loan forgiveness for peer specialists?"
2. "Will you advocate for Medicaid reimbursement rates that allow peer specialists to earn a living wage of at least $25 per hour?"
3. "Do you support expanding Medicare coverage of peer support services beyond opioid treatment to all behavioral health conditions?"
4. "What is your position on national minimum certification standards for peer specialists with interstate reciprocity?"
5. "How will you ensure that peer specialists are meaningfully integrated into crisis response teams, including 988 follow-up and mobile crisis units?"

**Voting Considerations**:

- Look for candidates who serve on or seek appointment to the Senate HELP Committee, House Energy and Commerce Committee, or state health and human services committees -- these are the committees where peer support legislation moves.
- Examine candidates' positions on behavioral health funding, Medicaid expansion, and community mental health investment. Candidates who support these broader priorities are more likely to support peer workforce investment.
- Review candidates' positions on criminal justice reform, as forensic peer support is a growing area that depends on diversion and reentry policies.
- At the state level, candidates who serve on budget, appropriations, or health committees have the most direct influence over Medicaid peer service rates and state workforce funding.

## Professional Actions

### For Behavioral Health Clinicians

- Advocate within your organization for equitable treatment of peer specialists. Support peer workers' inclusion in treatment team meetings, case conferences, and organizational decision-making. Peers who are excluded from care team discussions cannot fulfill their role effectively.
- Volunteer to provide supervision for peer specialists if your license qualifies. If you supervise peers, complete training in peer support competencies and values -- the N.A.P.S. Practice Guidelines for Supervisors (2019) provides a comprehensive framework. Do not supervise peer workers without understanding the peer role; clinical supervision skills alone are insufficient.
- Examine your organization's documentation requirements for peer services. Are peers forced to use clinical note formats (SOAP notes, DAP notes) that distort the nature of peer encounters? Advocate for peer-specific documentation templates that capture the relational and recovery-oriented dimensions of peer work.
- Challenge workplace stigma when you observe it. If colleagues make dismissive comments about peer workers ("they're not real clinicians," "I wouldn't trust a patient to do that job"), address these attitudes directly. Stigma from clinical colleagues is cited by 52% of peer specialists as a regular experience and is a significant driver of turnover.

### For Healthcare Administrators

- Review your organization's peer specialist wages and ensure they align with the goal of $25/hour. If your organization bills Medicaid for peer services, verify that reimbursement revenue flows to peer wages and benefits rather than being absorbed into general overhead.
- Create peer-specific career ladder structures with clear criteria for advancement from entry-level peer specialist to senior peer, peer supervisor, and peer program director. Publish the career ladder internally so peer workers can see a path forward.
- Conduct an organizational culture assessment focused on peer integration. Are peers included in staff meetings? Do they have access to electronic health records? Are they invited to organizational committees? Do they have a private space for peer-to-peer reflection and support? Organizational culture is as important as wages in determining peer retention.
- If your organization operates a CCBHC, ensure that peer service requirements are met through meaningful integration, not token compliance. A single part-time peer specialist who is marginalized within the clinical team does not fulfill the spirit of the CCBHC mandate.

### For Researchers

- Prioritize peer support research in your portfolio. Only 18 RCTs of mental health peer support have been published; the field needs large, well-powered trials. Include peer researchers and peer advisory boards in study design -- nothing about us without us.
- Develop and validate peer-specific outcome measures. Standard clinical instruments (PHQ-9, GAD-7) measure symptom reduction, but peer support's primary impacts are on empowerment, hope, social connection, and self-determination -- constructs that require different measures. The Recovery Assessment Scale and the Empowerment Scale exist but need further validation across diverse populations.
- Apply for NIH funding through NIMH's services research mechanisms. Peer support is an eligible topic under multiple NIH Funding Opportunity Announcements; the limiting factor is not eligibility but investigator interest and research infrastructure.
- Use community-based participatory research (CBPR) methods that include peer specialists and service recipients as co-investigators, not just subjects. Peer-led research produces insights that external researchers may miss and builds research capacity within the peer community.

### For Educators

- Integrate peer support content into social work, counseling, nursing, and medical school curricula. Future clinicians need to understand the peer role and how to collaborate effectively with peer specialists. Currently, most clinical training programs devote no curriculum time to peer support, leaving graduates unprepared to work alongside peers.
- Invite certified peer specialists as guest lecturers and co-instructors. Students benefit from hearing directly from people with lived experience about the value and distinctiveness of peer support -- and the experience challenges stereotypes about people with psychiatric histories.
- Develop continuing education courses on peer support competencies for licensed professionals who supervise peer workers. The gap between supervisors' clinical training and peer support values is a major source of role confusion, scope drift, and peer worker dissatisfaction.

### For Employers

- Offer peer specialist positions with full benefits, clear job descriptions, career advancement pathways, and qualified supervision. High turnover is expensive; investing in peer worker retention saves money and improves service quality. The fully loaded cost of replacing a peer specialist (recruitment, training, lost productivity, disrupted service relationships) is estimated at $15,000-$25,000 per departure.
- Review criminal background policies and eliminate blanket exclusions that disqualify individuals based on conviction type without individualized assessment. Many states require background checks but do not mandate specific exclusions; organizations have discretion to adopt more inclusive policies. An individualized review process that considers the nature of the offense, time since conviction, and relevance to the peer role can preserve public safety while expanding the talent pool.
- Ensure that peer specialists have organizational authority commensurate with their role. Peers should have electronic health record access, be listed as members of care teams, be included in team meetings, and be able to document their work under their own names rather than under a supervisor's credentials.

### For State Policymakers

- Review your state's peer specialist certification requirements for barriers that disproportionately exclude people of color, people with criminal justice histories, and people in early recovery. Conduct a rate adequacy analysis comparing peer reimbursement to the cost of delivering services.
- Consider adopting a peer supervisor credential if your state does not have one. The 15 states with peer supervisor credentials report higher peer worker satisfaction and lower turnover than states without them.
- Pursue reciprocity agreements with neighboring states to enable peer workforce portability. The shortage of peer specialists in rural border areas could be partially addressed by allowing certified peers from adjacent states to practice across state lines.
- Advocate for your state's Medicaid agency to participate in CMS rate adequacy reviews and to adjust rates when they fall below the cost of delivering services.

## Advocacy Toolkit

### Key Talking Points

When discussing peer support services with others, emphasize:

1. **Cost-effectiveness**: "Peer respite costs $200-$400 per day compared to $2,264 for psychiatric inpatient care, and studies show peer services reduce emergency department visits by 9-23%. Investing in peers saves money." (HCUP, 2022; meta-analysis, 2021)
2. **Workforce crisis**: "The United States has 30,000-35,000 peer specialists earning a median of $17.65 per hour -- less than many retail workers -- with annual turnover of 30-50%. We cannot build a sustainable service on poverty wages." (BLS, 2023; peer workforce surveys, 2022)
3. **Bipartisan appeal**: "Peer support is community-based mutual aid, not big government. It empowers individuals, reduces dependency on expensive institutions, and reflects the conservative value of personal responsibility alongside the liberal value of expanding access to care."
4. **Evidence base**: "SAMHSA has recognized peer support as an evidence-based practice since 2005. Meta-analyses show reductions in hospitalization (18-36%) and emergency department use (9-23%). The VA employs over 1,200 peer specialists with consistently positive outcomes."
5. **Workforce shortage solution**: "The U.S. faces a behavioral health workforce shortage projected at 10,000-35,000 providers. Peer specialists can fill critical gaps, especially in rural areas and communities of color where clinical provider shortages are most severe."
6. **Veteran validation**: "If peer support is good enough for our veterans -- and the VA employs over 1,200 peer specialists -- it should be good enough for all Americans."

### Common Misconceptions to Counter

| Misconception | Reality | Source |
|---------------|---------|--------|
| "Peer specialists are untrained volunteers" | Peer specialists must complete 40-100+ hours of competency-based training, pass a written exam (35 states), complete supervised experience, and maintain continuing education | SAMHSA certification tracker, 2024 |
| "People with mental illness cannot reliably help others" | Studies show peer-delivered services have safety profiles comparable to other paraprofessional services, with serious adverse events in less than 1% of encounters | Chinman et al., 2020 |
| "Peer support replaces real treatment" | Peer support complements clinical care; it is not a substitute. Peers help with recovery, navigation, and self-advocacy -- roles clinicians are not trained for | SAMHSA Core Competencies, 2015 |
| "There is no evidence peer support works" | 18 RCTs, multiple meta-analyses, and decades of program evaluation data demonstrate effectiveness in reducing hospitalization, improving recovery, and lowering costs | Lloyd-Evans et al., 2014; Johnson et al., 2018 |
| "Peer support is too expensive to expand" | Peer support generates net savings by diverting $10,000-$18,000 in inpatient costs per diverted hospitalization; the $29 million SAMHSA spends on peer grants is a fraction of the $6.5 billion SAMHSA budget | SAMHSA FY2024; economic analyses, 2022 |
| "Only people with mental illness benefit from peer support" | Family members, caregivers, veterans, justice-involved individuals, youth, and people with substance use disorders all benefit from peer support; specialized credentials serve each population | SAMHSA, 2024 |
