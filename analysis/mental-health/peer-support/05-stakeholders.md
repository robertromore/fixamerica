# Peer Support Services: Stakeholders

The peer support landscape is shaped by the interactions of several key groups, each with distinct interests, resources, and degrees of power over the direction of peer workforce policy.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Peer support workers | Directly affected by wages, certification, scope of practice, and workplace culture | 30,000-35,000 nationally | Living wages, career advancement, professional respect, clear scope of practice |
| People receiving peer services | Quality, availability, and continuity of peer support affects their recovery | ~1 million annually | Access to effective peer support, choice, cultural responsiveness |
| People with unmet behavioral health needs | Peer support could fill gaps in a system with severe clinician shortages | 57.8 million adults with mental illness (SAMHSA, 2022) | Access to any form of behavioral health support |
| People with substance use disorders | Peer recovery support is a core component of addiction treatment and long-term recovery | 46.3 million with SUD (SAMHSA, 2022) | Recovery support services, relapse prevention, community reintegration |
| Family members of people with mental illness | Family peer specialists provide support; peer services affect loved ones' recovery | 8.5 million family caregivers (NAMI, 2023) | Support for their family member, information, respite |

### Detailed Stakeholder Profiles

**Peer Support Workers**: The 30,000-35,000 certified peer specialists in the United States are the most directly affected stakeholder group. They face a unique occupational challenge: their primary qualification -- lived experience with mental health conditions or substance use disorders -- is simultaneously their greatest professional asset and a source of workplace vulnerability. Unlike other behavioral health workers, peer specialists must maintain their own recovery while supporting others through similar challenges, creating an emotional labor burden that is compounded by low wages, limited benefits, and institutional stigma. The 29% who report criminal justice histories face additional barriers, as background check policies in many states can disqualify them from employment despite their experiential expertise being the very reason the role exists.

**People Receiving Peer Services**: Approximately 1 million Americans receive peer-delivered services annually through Medicaid-funded programs, VA services, peer-run organizations, and community programs. These individuals report that peer support provides something clinical services cannot: the validation of shared experience, the modeling of recovery as possible, and a relationship built on mutuality rather than professional authority. Consumer satisfaction surveys consistently rate peer services higher than clinical services on dimensions of respect, cultural responsiveness, and empowerment (SAMHSA NOMS data, 2023). The relational continuity that peer support depends on is directly threatened by the 30-50% annual turnover rate; service recipients who lose their peer specialist through workforce attrition must rebuild a relationship from scratch, often with diminished trust.

**People with Unmet Behavioral Health Needs**: The 57.8 million American adults with mental illness include 14.1 million with serious mental illness (SMI), of whom only 65.4% received any mental health services in the prior year (SAMHSA, 2022). The behavioral health workforce shortage -- projected at 10,000 to 35,000 provider deficit depending on the methodology -- means that many people cannot access clinical services even when they seek them. Peer support represents one of the most scalable solutions to this gap, particularly in rural areas and communities of color where clinical provider shortages are most severe.

### Secondary Stakeholders

- **Veterans**: The VA employs over 1,200 peer specialists; peer support is a core component of VA mental health services. Veterans with mental health conditions and their families are affected by program quality and access. The VA's Whole Health initiative has expanded peer roles beyond traditional mental health into health coaching, pain management, and wellness planning, creating new career pathways within the VA system that do not yet exist in civilian settings.

- **Criminal justice-involved populations**: Forensic peer specialists support reentry and diversion programs in at least 15 states. People with co-occurring mental health and justice involvement benefit from peer workers who share similar backgrounds. Research from the GAINS Center shows that forensic peer support reduces recidivism by 20-30% among participants in mental health courts and diversion programs, making this population a significant beneficiary of peer service expansion.

- **Youth and transition-age young adults**: Young people with mental health conditions are increasingly served by youth peer specialists, particularly in school and community settings. The 18 states offering youth peer specialist credentials recognize that young people in distress are more likely to accept support from peers close to their own age and experience. The National Federation of Families for Children's Mental Health has advocated for expanded youth and family peer roles.

- **Rural communities**: Geographic access to any behavioral health services is limited in rural areas; peer support could help fill gaps but is disproportionately concentrated in urban areas. With only 2-5 peer specialists per 100,000 population in rural areas compared to 10-18 in urban areas, the peer workforce shortage mirrors and amplifies the broader provider shortage. Telehealth peer services offer a partial solution but are constrained by broadband access limitations.

- **Communities of color**: Racial disparities in mental health access make culturally responsive peer support especially valuable, but the peer workforce does not yet reflect the diversity of populations served. While 24% of peer specialists are Black/African American (exceeding the 13% population share), only 11% are Hispanic/Latino (well below the 19% population share). The Hogg Foundation for Mental Health, based in Texas, has funded initiatives specifically addressing the need for culturally responsive peer services in Latino and African American communities.

- **People experiencing homelessness**: An estimated 30-40% of people experiencing homelessness have a serious mental illness or substance use disorder (SAMHSA, 2023). Peer specialists with their own experience of homelessness are uniquely positioned to build trust and provide navigation support for this population. Programs like Pathways to Housing (New York) and DESC (Downtown Emergency Service Center, Seattle) have demonstrated the effectiveness of peer-delivered services within Housing First models.

- **LGBTQ+ individuals**: LGBTQ+ populations experience disproportionate rates of mental health conditions, substance use disorders, and suicidality. The 18% of peer specialists who identify as LGBTQ+ (National Peer Workforce Survey, 2022) provide culturally affirming peer support that may be unavailable from clinical providers in many communities. Organizations like the National Queer and Trans Therapists of Color Network have highlighted the importance of peer support models that center intersectional identities.

## Who Has Power

### Decision Makers

| Actor | Role | Authority | Resources | Influence Level |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) | Sets federal Medicaid/Medicare policy for peer services | Can mandate or authorize coverage, set conditions of participation | $1.5 trillion Medicaid/Medicare budget (FY2024) | High |
| SAMHSA | Federal agency for behavioral health policy, funding, and technical assistance | Administers $6.5 billion budget (FY2024); sets evidence-based practice standards | $29 million in peer support grants (FY2024) | High |
| State Medicaid agencies | Determine reimbursement rates, billing codes, covered services | Control state Medicaid budgets; file State Plan Amendments | Varies by state | High |
| State behavioral health authorities | Oversee certification programs, scope of practice, quality standards | Regulatory authority over peer workforce | Varies by state | High |

| Veterans Health Administration | Operates largest peer specialist employer program | Hires, trains, and supervises VA peer specialists | 1,200+ peer positions; VA health budget of $118 billion (FY2024) | Medium |

### Key Organizations and Institutions

- **National Association of Peer Supporters (N.A.P.S.)**: The primary national membership organization for peer workers, with approximately 5,000 individual members. Develops practice guidelines, advocates for policy changes, and promotes national certification standards. Published the National Practice Guidelines for Peer Specialists and Supervisors (2019), the most comprehensive practice framework in the field. Operates on an annual budget of approximately $2 million -- a fraction of what licensed professional associations command.

- **National Council for Mental Wellbeing**: Represents over 3,200 community behavioral health organizations; advocates for peer service expansion and CCBHC funding. Influential in congressional advocacy, with a dedicated government relations team and significant budget for policy engagement. The National Council's annual conference is the largest behavioral health provider gathering in the country.

- **National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI)**: The largest grassroots mental health advocacy organization (600,000+ members, over 600 local affiliates). Supports peer services as part of comprehensive care, though primary focus is on clinical treatment access and insurance parity. NAMI's "Peer-to-Peer" program, a structured peer education course, is one of the most widely disseminated peer interventions in the country, operating in all 50 states.

- **Copeland Center for Wellness and Recovery**: Maintains WRAP (Wellness Recovery Action Plan) training and certification; key developer of peer-led interventions. Founded by Mary Ellen Copeland. Has trained over 10,000 WRAP facilitators in more than 30 countries. The Copeland Center's model of peer-developed, peer-delivered interventions demonstrates that peer expertise can produce structured programs that meet evidence-based practice standards.

- **National Empowerment Center**: Consumer-run organization advocating for peer-controlled alternatives to traditional mental health services, directed by Daniel Fisher, MD, PhD (a psychiatrist with personal lived experience of psychosis and recovery). Provides training and technical assistance for peer respite and other peer-run programs. The center's emphasis on full recovery as the expected outcome -- rather than chronic illness management -- represents the most aspirational end of the peer support philosophical spectrum.

- **C4 Innovations**: Research and consulting organization specializing in peer workforce development; provides technical assistance on certification, Medicaid billing, and program design. C4's national peer workforce survey and Medicaid reimbursement analyses are the most comprehensive data sources on peer support financing and workforce conditions. Led by Harvey Rosenthal and Jonathan Edwards.

- **International Credentialing and Reciprocity Consortium (IC&RC)**: Offers national peer recovery support specialist credential used by some states; advocates for certification portability. IC&RC's credential is the closest existing approximation to a national certification standard, but its adoption is limited by state resistance to accepting external credentials.

- **Faces and Voices of Recovery**: The leading national recovery advocacy organization, representing the interests of people in recovery from substance use disorders and their families. Operates a network of Recovery Community Organizations (RCOs) across the country and advocates for peer recovery support services as distinct from clinical treatment.

- **Depression and Bipolar Support Alliance (DBSA)**: Operates over 700 peer-led support groups nationwide for people with mood disorders. DBSA's network demonstrates the scale that peer-led services can achieve through a standardized group model with volunteer facilitation.

### Influencers

- **Licensed professional associations (NASW, APA, ACA)**: Influence scope of practice regulations and reimbursement policy through lobbying. Have historically been cautious or resistant toward peer workforce expansion when it overlaps with licensed services. NASW's total annual budget exceeds $30 million; APA's exceeds $120 million. Their lobbying capacity at both state and federal levels dwarfs that of peer-specific organizations.

- **Managed care organizations (MCOs)**: Administer Medicaid benefits in most states. Their coverage decisions, prior authorization requirements, and reimbursement rates directly affect peer service availability. Major national MCOs -- including Centene, UnitedHealthcare Community and State, Molina Healthcare, and Anthem's Medicaid plans -- collectively manage over $400 billion in annual Medicaid spending. Their network adequacy decisions determine whether peer-run organizations can participate in Medicaid networks.

- **Academic researchers**: Shape the evidence base that payers and policymakers use to make coverage decisions. Peer support research is concentrated in a small number of academic centers, including Yale (Larry Davidson), Dartmouth (Matthew Chinman, in collaboration with VA), University of Pennsylvania, and the University of Illinois at Chicago. The concentration of research expertise means that a small number of investigators have outsized influence on the evidence narrative.

- **Foundations**: Philanthropic organizations have funded peer support innovation, research, and advocacy beyond what government funding supports. Key funders include the California Health Care Foundation, Robert Wood Johnson Foundation, Hogg Foundation for Mental Health (Texas), the Bristol-Myers Squibb Foundation (through its Together on Diabetes initiative, which included peer support), and the Open Society Foundations (through its International Disability Rights program).

- **Peer leaders and public intellectuals**: Individual peer advocates with national platforms shape the discourse. Patricia Deegan (originator of the concept of "personal medicine" and developer of the CommonGround decision support system), Shery Mead (founder of Intentional Peer Support), Daniel Fisher (National Empowerment Center), and Larry Fricks (pioneer of the Georgia model) have each influenced the field's direction through writing, speaking, and program development.

### Conflicting Interests

- **Peer workers vs. licensed professionals**: Peer workers seek expanded scope of practice and higher reimbursement; licensed professionals seek to maintain scope-of-practice boundaries and protect their market position. This conflict is structural, not personal: both groups are responding rationally to a system that treats behavioral health funding as a zero-sum competition.

- **Peer organizations vs. MCOs**: Peer organizations want higher reimbursement rates to fund living wages; MCOs benefit from low rates that reduce costs. MCOs may also impose administrative requirements (prior authorization, credentialing, documentation standards) that disproportionately burden small peer-run organizations lacking dedicated billing and compliance staff.

- **Peer autonomy advocates vs. clinical integration proponents**: Some peer leaders advocate for autonomous peer-run services outside the clinical system; others see integration into clinical teams as the path to sustainability and scale. This tension reflects a genuine philosophical divide within the peer community. Organizations like the National Empowerment Center and Intentional Peer Support emphasize autonomy, while the National Council for Mental Wellbeing and CCBHC framework emphasize integration.

- **State sovereignty proponents vs. national standards advocates**: States resist national certification standards that would override their regulatory authority; peer workers and employers want portability and consistency. This conflict mirrors broader federalism tensions and is complicated by the economic interests of state-specific certification vendors.

- **Quality assurance advocates vs. accessibility advocates**: Higher certification requirements improve quality but may exclude the most marginalized individuals from the workforce. This tension is particularly acute for criminal background exclusions, minimum education requirements, and lengthy unpaid training periods.

- **Hospital systems vs. peer respite advocates**: Hospitals lose revenue when peer respite programs successfully divert people from psychiatric inpatient admissions. While hospital leaders may publicly support community alternatives, their financial incentives favor maintaining inpatient utilization. This conflict is most visible in state-level debates about funding allocation between institutional and community-based services.

### The Behavioral Health Workforce Power Structure

```text
    +--------------------------------+
    |       Psychiatrists            |  Highest authority, highest pay
    |  (Prescribing + therapy)       |  ($247,350 median salary)
    +--------------------------------+
    |       Psychologists            |  Doctoral-level, testing authority
    |  ($85,330 median salary)       |
    +--------------------------------+
    |  Licensed Clinical Social      |  Master's-level, most common
    |  Workers ($62,940)             |  billable provider
    +--------------------------------+
    |  Licensed Professional         |  Master's-level counselors
    |  Counselors ($55,350)          |
    +--------------------------------+
    |  Substance Abuse Counselors    |  Often bachelor's or certificate
    |  ($49,710)                     |
    +--------------------------------+
    |  Peer Support Specialists      |  Certification based on lived
    |  ($36,712)                     |  experience; lowest authority
    +--------------------------------+
```

*Source: BLS Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics, May 2023*

This hierarchy determines who can bill, who supervises whom, who participates in treatment decisions, and whose expertise is valued. Peer specialists consistently occupy the bottom tier in authority, compensation, and organizational influence. The hierarchy is self-reinforcing: lower wages attract less political investment, less political investment produces less policy change, and less policy change preserves the wage gap.

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on peer support policy:

- **Lobbying**: Licensed professional associations (NASW, APA) spend significantly on state-level lobbying to protect scope-of-practice boundaries. NASW alone maintains registered lobbyists in most state capitals and spends several million dollars annually on government relations. The National Council for Mental Wellbeing lobbies Congress on CCBHC and community mental health funding, with a dedicated government affairs team. Peer-specific organizations have limited lobbying capacity due to budget constraints -- N.A.P.S.'s entire annual budget ($2 million) is less than what major professional associations spend on lobbying alone.

- **Campaign Contributions**: Mental health policy receives modest campaign contributions compared to healthcare broadly. Behavioral health PACs are small; peer organizations have essentially no PAC activity. The American Psychiatric Association's PAC contributed approximately $1.5 million to congressional candidates in the 2022 election cycle; no peer-specific PAC exists.

- **Regulatory Participation**: State behavioral health authorities and Medicaid agencies shape peer support through rulemaking. Peer organizations participate in public comment periods but often lack the staffing to provide detailed technical comments on complex regulations. In contrast, MCOs and licensed professional associations employ dedicated regulatory affairs teams.

- **Research and Evidence**: Academic researchers influence policy by producing (or not producing) evidence that payers demand. The peer support research community is small and underfunded, limiting its policy influence compared to researchers studying pharmaceutical or psychotherapy interventions. The pharmaceutical industry spends approximately $30 billion annually on research and development in the United States; peer support research receives approximately $12 million from NIH.

- **Public Advocacy**: NAMI, Mental Health America, and peer organizations use public awareness campaigns, storytelling, and media to build support for peer services. The personal narratives of peer specialists and the people they serve are among the most compelling advocacy tools in behavioral health. Events like World Mental Health Day, Recovery Month (September), and National Peer Support Celebration Day provide platforms for peer visibility.

- **Technical Assistance**: Organizations like C4 Innovations, SAMHSA's technical assistance centers, and the Copeland Center shape implementation through training, consultation, and model development, influencing how peer services are structured even when they do not directly control policy. Technical assistance providers occupy an influential intermediary role, translating policy mandates into operational reality.

- **Litigation**: Legal advocacy organizations, including the Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law and the National Disability Rights Network, have used litigation to advance peer support interests -- particularly through *Olmstead* enforcement, which supports community-based services (including peer support) as alternatives to institutionalization.
