# Peer Support Services: History

## Historical Development

The peer support movement in mental health did not emerge from the professional behavioral health establishment. It arose from the people the system was supposed to serve -- and often failed. The movement's roots lie in the psychiatric survivor and consumer movements of the 1970s, drawing on traditions of mutual aid that predate modern psychiatry by centuries. Its trajectory from radical grassroots activism to Medicaid-billable service reflects both remarkable progress and enduring tensions about who controls the narrative of mental health recovery.

---

### Mutual Aid Origins (Pre-1970): Peer Support Before the Movement

- **Alcoholics Anonymous (1935)**: The founding of AA by Bill Wilson and Dr. Bob Smith in Akron, Ohio, established the foundational model of peer-based recovery support -- one person with lived experience helping another. The Twelve Step model demonstrated that non-professional, experiential expertise could sustain long-term recovery, a concept that would later transfer to mental health. By the 1950s, AA had over 100,000 members and had inspired similar peer-based models for other conditions, including Narcotics Anonymous (1953) and Gamblers Anonymous (1957).
- **Fountain House (1948)**: Founded in New York City by former patients of Rockland State Hospital who called themselves WANA (We Are Not Alone), Fountain House pioneered the "clubhouse model" -- a community of mutual support where people with serious mental illness worked alongside staff as equals in a structured "work-ordered day." The model rejected the patient-provider hierarchy in favor of a community membership framework. By the 1970s, the clubhouse model had spread internationally, and Fountain House had become a training center for new clubhouse programs worldwide. John Beard, who became executive director in 1955, was instrumental in formalizing the model while preserving its peer-governed ethos.
- **Recovery Incorporated (1937)**: Founded by psychiatrist Abraham Low in Chicago, this self-help group for people with psychiatric conditions emphasized cognitive self-management techniques led by peers, predating cognitive behavioral therapy by decades. Recovery Inc. (now Recovery International) trained its peer leaders to facilitate structured group meetings using Low's method, establishing an early model of non-professional mental health facilitation that persists today with approximately 600 weekly meetings worldwide.
- **Grow International (1957)**: Founded in Sydney, Australia, by psychiatric patients who adapted the Twelve Step model specifically for mental health recovery. Grow established one of the earliest international networks of peer-led mental health support groups. Its emphasis on mutual help, personal responsibility, and community participation anticipated many of the values that would later define the peer support movement.
- **Historical context**: Mutual aid societies, settlement houses, and religious communities had long provided informal peer support for people in distress. The Quaker-run York Retreat (1796) in England introduced "moral treatment" that emphasized humane, community-based approaches to mental distress -- an early precursor to recovery-oriented care. In the United States, the National Committee for Mental Hygiene (1909) included former patient Clifford Beers, whose autobiography *A Mind That Found Itself* (1908) was an early example of lived experience informing advocacy. The formal peer support movement drew on these traditions while explicitly rejecting the medical model's authority over personal experience.

### The Consumer/Survivor Movement (1970-1990): Radical Roots

- **Deinstitutionalization backdrop**: As state psychiatric hospitals discharged hundreds of thousands of patients in the 1960s and 1970s -- often into homelessness and inadequate community care -- former patients organized to demand voice, choice, and alternatives to coercive treatment. The state hospital census dropped from approximately 560,000 in 1955 to under 100,000 by 1990, but community mental health funding never kept pace with deinstitutionalization, leaving millions without adequate support. This gap between institutional discharge and community resources created both the grievance and the organizing energy that fueled the consumer/survivor movement.

- **Insane Liberation Front (1970)**: Founded in Portland, Oregon, this was the first organized group of psychiatric survivors to adopt an explicitly political, civil rights-oriented framework. Members rejected the label "patient" and demanded the right to refuse treatment, access records, and participate in decisions about their care. The group's name -- deliberately provocative -- signaled a break from the medical model's authority over self-definition.

- **Mental Patients' Liberation Project (1971)**: Founded in New York City by former patients including Judi Chamberlin, this was among the most influential early organizations of psychiatric survivors demanding civil rights, self-determination, and an end to forced treatment. Similar groups formed in Boston (Mental Patients' Liberation Front), San Francisco (Network Against Psychiatric Assault), and other cities, creating a loosely connected national movement by the mid-1970s.

- **Judi Chamberlin and *On Our Own* (1978)**: Chamberlin's landmark book articulated a comprehensive vision of patient-controlled alternatives to the psychiatric system. She argued that people with psychiatric histories possessed unique expertise and that peer-run services were not merely supplements to professional care but fundamentally superior for some purposes. *On Our Own* became the intellectual foundation of the peer support movement and remained influential for decades. Chamberlin later co-founded the National Empowerment Center and served on the President's New Freedom Commission on Mental Health.

- **Howie the Harp (Howard Geld)**: A prominent psychiatric survivor and peer advocate who became one of the first former patients to establish a peer-run service organization. His Community Access programs in New York City demonstrated that people with serious mental illness could run their own housing and support services. The Howie the Harp Advocacy Center, named in his honor after his death in 1995, continues to train peer specialists in New York.

- **National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI, 1979)**: While primarily a family advocacy organization, NAMI's founding by Harriet Shetler and Beverly Young reflected the broader mobilization of people affected by mental illness, including consumers who would later advocate for peer roles within NAMI's structure. The relationship between NAMI (family-led) and the consumer/survivor movement (individual-led) was sometimes contentious, as the two groups held different views on involuntary treatment and the role of the medical model.

- **Alternatives Conference (1985)**: SAMHSA's predecessor agency, the National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH), began funding annual national conferences organized by and for mental health consumers, providing a crucial convening space for the movement. The Alternatives Conference ran from 1985 to 2012, bringing together hundreds of consumer/survivor activists each year and serving as an incubator for peer program innovation. The conference's cancellation in 2012, due to federal budget cuts, was mourned by the movement as a loss of irreplaceable organizing infrastructure.

- **Community Support Program**: NIMH's Community Support Program (established 1977) provided early federal recognition that consumer involvement in service planning and delivery was essential, though it stopped short of funding consumer-delivered services. The program's emphasis on "client-centered" care and consumer participation in governance laid groundwork for later federal support of peer roles.

- **Protection and Advocacy for Individuals with Mental Illness Act (PAMII, 1986)**: This legislation established Protection and Advocacy organizations in every state, empowered to investigate abuse and neglect in mental health facilities. While not directly a peer support law, PAMII provided legal infrastructure that consumer/survivor advocates used to challenge coercive practices and advocate for peer-controlled alternatives.

### Formalization and Federal Recognition (1990-2007): From Movement to Workforce

- **Americans with Disabilities Act (1990)**: The ADA prohibited discrimination against people with psychiatric disabilities in employment, laying groundwork for the argument that people with mental health histories should not be excluded from behavioral health jobs. The ADA's integration mandate -- later affirmed in *Olmstead v. L.C.* (1999) -- also supported the development of community-based peer services as alternatives to institutional care.

- **Wellness Recovery Action Plan (WRAP, 1997)**: Mary Ellen Copeland, a psychologist and person with lived experience of bipolar disorder, developed WRAP as a structured self-management and crisis planning tool created by and for people with mental health conditions. WRAP used a collaborative process in which people in recovery identified their own wellness tools, triggers, early warning signs, and crisis preferences. It became one of the first peer-developed interventions recognized by SAMHSA's National Registry of Evidence-based Programs and Practices (NREPP). The Copeland Center for Wellness and Recovery, founded to disseminate WRAP, has trained over 10,000 facilitators in more than 30 countries.

- **Consumer-Operated Service Programs (COSPs)**: In 1998, SAMHSA funded a multi-site study of consumer-operated service programs -- the first major federal research investment in peer-delivered services. The study examined eight COSP programs across five states, comparing outcomes for consumers who received COSP services plus usual care versus usual care alone. Published in 2006 (Campbell et al.), the study found that COSP participation produced significant improvements in empowerment, well-being, and social functioning compared to traditional services alone, while showing no adverse safety effects.

- **Georgia's Certified Peer Specialist Program (2001)**: Georgia became the first state to develop a comprehensive peer specialist certification program and to seek Medicaid reimbursement for peer-delivered services. The Georgia model -- developed by Larry Fricks, director of the Georgia Office of Consumer Relations, and colleagues at the Georgia Mental Health Consumer Network -- included 40 hours of training (later expanded to 75), a written certification exam, and a supervised practicum. The program became the template for state programs nationwide, and Fricks became one of the most influential figures in peer support workforce development.

- **Intentional Peer Support (IPS, 2001)**: Shery Mead, a psychiatric survivor and advocate, developed the Intentional Peer Support framework, which distinguished "intentional" peer support from both informal mutual aid and paraprofessional services. IPS emphasized mutual learning, co-creation of meaning, and the conscious use of relationship as a vehicle for change. The IPS framework influenced peer training curricula nationwide and provided a theoretical foundation for preserving the distinctiveness of peer support within clinical settings.

- **New Freedom Commission on Mental Health (2003)**: President George W. Bush's New Freedom Commission issued a transformative report, *Achieving the Promise: Transforming Mental Health Care in America*, calling for a recovery-oriented mental health system. The report explicitly recommended expanding "the role of individuals in recovery" as providers of services, marking the highest-level federal endorsement of peer support to date. Commissioner Judi Chamberlin ensured that the consumer/survivor perspective was represented in the commission's deliberations.

- **SAMHSA Recovery Support Strategic Initiative (2005)**: SAMHSA formally adopted a recovery-oriented framework that identified peer support as a core component, publishing the first working definition of recovery that centered lived experience. The 2005 SAMHSA Consensus Statement on Mental Health Recovery identified 10 fundamental components of recovery, including "peer support" as a distinct element. This framework legitimized peer support within the federal behavioral health infrastructure and provided a policy rationale for subsequent Medicaid coverage decisions.

### Medicaid Integration and Rapid Growth (2007-2018): Mainstreaming Peer Support

- **CMS State Medicaid Director Letter #07-011 (August 15, 2007)**: This landmark letter from the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services authorized states to bill Medicaid for peer support services, provided that states established certification programs and clinical supervision requirements. The letter was the single most consequential policy development in peer support history, transforming peer workers from volunteer or grant-funded roles into a sustainable, reimbursable workforce. The letter's four conditions -- lived experience, state certification, clinical supervision, and recovery orientation -- became the structural pillars of the peer support financing model.

- **Rapid state adoption (2007-2015)**: Following the CMS letter, states moved quickly to establish or formalize certification programs and file State Plan Amendments (SPAs) to bill Medicaid. Georgia, Arizona, and Pennsylvania were among the first to file SPAs. By 2010, 20 states had approved Medicaid billing; by 2015, the number reached 38. The speed of adoption reflected both the fiscal appeal of peer services (lower cost than clinical alternatives) and the growing political influence of the recovery advocacy movement.

- **Affordable Care Act (2010)**: The ACA expanded Medicaid eligibility to millions of Americans with incomes up to 138% of the federal poverty level, dramatically increasing the population eligible for peer services in the 36 states (plus D.C.) that adopted Medicaid expansion. Section 5313 authorized grants for community health workers, a category that included peer specialists. The ACA also created the Prevention and Public Health Fund, which supported community-based behavioral health initiatives including peer programs.

- **Veterans Affairs Peer Specialist Program (2007-2012)**: The VA launched its peer specialist program in 2007, hiring veterans with mental health or substance use recovery experience to support other veterans. The program was codified at 38 U.S.C. 7402, which authorized the VA to employ peer specialists as health technicians. By 2012, the program employed over 800 peer specialists across VA medical centers, establishing peers as a recognized workforce category in the largest integrated healthcare system in the nation. The VA program demonstrated that peers could be successfully integrated into complex institutional settings with appropriate training, supervision, and organizational support.

- **SAMHSA Bringing Recovery Supports to Scale Technical Assistance Center Strategy (BRSS TACS, 2011)**: This technical assistance center provided states and organizations with tools to implement peer support services, develop certification programs, and navigate Medicaid billing requirements. BRSS TACS published foundational documents including the 2015 Core Competencies for Peer Workers in Behavioral Health Services, which identified 12 competency domains spanning recovery orientation, person-centered practice, cultural responsiveness, communication, and advocacy. BRSS TACS operated until 2022.

- **International Credentialing and Reciprocity Consortium (IC&RC)**: IC&RC began offering a national peer recovery support specialist credential, providing a portable certification that some states recognized. The credential was based on IC&RC's existing framework for substance abuse counselor certification and included a competency-based examination. However, adoption remained limited due to state sovereignty over certification standards and resistance from state-specific credentialing bodies.

- **SAMHSA National Registry of Evidence-based Programs and Practices (NREPP)**: Between 2007 and 2018, several peer-delivered interventions were added to NREPP, including WRAP, the Certified Peer Specialist (Georgia model), and several peer-based addiction recovery programs. NREPP listing provided federal validation that peer services met evidence-based practice standards. However, NREPP was discontinued in 2018 as part of a broader SAMHSA reorganization, removing a key credentialing mechanism for peer interventions.

### Expansion and Contemporary Challenges (2018-Present): Scaling Under Pressure

- **SUPPORT Act (2018)**: The Substance Use-Disorder Prevention that Promotes Opioid Recovery and Treatment for Patients and Communities Act required Medicare coverage of opioid use disorder treatment services delivered by peer support specialists, marking the first time peer services were authorized under Medicare (for a specific condition). The law reflected the urgency of the opioid crisis and the recognition that peer recovery support was essential to overdose prevention and long-term recovery. However, by limiting authorization to OUD treatment, the SUPPORT Act established a condition-specific precedent rather than the universal peer support benefit advocates sought.

- **COVID-19 pandemic (2020-2022)**: The pandemic simultaneously expanded demand for peer services (as mental health crises surged -- SAMHSA reported a 25% increase in calls to the National Helpline in 2020) and created new delivery mechanisms (telehealth peer support). Thirty-two states authorized telephonic or video-based peer services during this period, many through emergency waivers that were subsequently made permanent. However, the pandemic also devastated the peer workforce through burnout, low wages, and lack of protective equipment in community settings. A 2021 survey found that 42% of peer specialists considered leaving the field during the pandemic, and actual turnover exceeded 40% in several states.

- **988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (2022)**: The launch of the 988 system created new roles for peer specialists in crisis follow-up, warm handoffs, and mobile response teams. SAMHSA guidance identified peers as essential members of crisis continuum staffing, including at 988 call centers, in mobile crisis teams, and in crisis stabilization units. The integration of peers into 988 infrastructure represented a significant policy endorsement of peer support in acute care settings, but also raised questions about training adequacy, scope of practice in crisis situations, and whether crisis peer roles were compatible with the non-hierarchical values of the peer model.

- **Bipartisan Safer Communities Act (2022)**: This legislation, passed in the wake of the Uvalde school shooting, provided $250 million in new funding for community mental health services, including peer support, and expanded Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinics (CCBHCs) that are required to provide peer services. The act represented the largest single federal investment in community mental health since the ACA.

- **Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinics (2017-present)**: The CCBHC model, initially authorized in the Excellence in Mental Health and Addiction Treatment Act of 2014 and expanded through demonstration programs beginning in 2017, requires peer support services as a condition of certification. As of 2024, over 500 CCBHCs operate in 46 states, creating guaranteed employment settings for peer specialists. The CCBHC model's Prospective Payment System (PPS) provides more sustainable funding than fee-for-service Medicaid, which has made CCBHCs among the more attractive employers for peer specialists.

- **Ongoing workforce crisis**: Despite expansion of roles and settings, peer specialist wages have not kept pace with inflation, and annual turnover rates of 30-50% threaten the sustainability of the entire model. The gap between the rhetoric of peer support as "essential" and the reality of poverty wages and limited career paths has become the central contradiction of the field.

## Lessons from History

### Pattern 1: Grassroots Innovation Precedes Institutional Adoption

Every major advance in peer support -- from AA's mutual aid model to Georgia's certification program to WRAP -- originated outside the formal behavioral health establishment. Peer innovations were developed by people with lived experience, often in opposition to professional orthodoxy, and were subsequently adopted by institutions only after proving their value. This pattern suggests that the most productive policy approach is to create conditions for grassroots experimentation rather than imposing top-down program designs. Federal and state policy has been most effective when it provides funding and infrastructure for innovations that emerged from the peer community itself.

### Pattern 2: Federal Policy Catalyzes State Action

The 2007 CMS letter triggered more state-level peer support infrastructure development than two decades of advocacy had achieved. Similarly, the SUPPORT Act (2018) and CCBHC expansion created structural incentives that accelerated peer workforce growth. This demonstrates that federal policy signals -- particularly those tied to funding -- are the most powerful levers for scaling peer support, even though implementation necessarily occurs at the state and local levels. The contrast between pre-2007 (slow, uneven growth) and post-2007 (rapid state adoption) is the clearest evidence of this pattern.

### Pattern 3: Professionalization Creates Both Opportunity and Risk

The trajectory from radical consumer movement to Medicaid-billable service has brought financial sustainability and institutional recognition but has also created tensions. Certification requirements, clinical supervision mandates, and documentation burdens risk transforming peer support from a mutual, non-hierarchical relationship into a quasi-clinical service that mirrors the professional system it was designed to complement. Each step toward professionalization requires vigilance to preserve the core values of mutuality, self-determination, and experiential expertise. The history of other paraprofessional health roles -- including community health workers and addiction counselors -- shows that professionalization tends to increase wages and institutional legitimacy while narrowing the demographic diversity and grassroots authenticity of the workforce.

### Pattern 4: Workforce Sustainability Lags Behind Role Expansion

The history of peer support shows a recurring pattern: new roles and settings for peer workers are created faster than the infrastructure to sustain them. The VA program, CCBHC requirements, 988 integration, and emergency department peer programs have all expanded demand for peer specialists without corresponding investments in wages, career pathways, and organizational support. This supply-demand imbalance threatens to undermine the quality and reputation of the peer model. The pattern is not new: similar dynamics affected community health workers in the 1960s and certified nursing assistants in the 1990s, both of which experienced rapid role expansion followed by chronic low wages and high turnover.

### Pattern 5: Crises Accelerate Adoption but Not Sustainability

The opioid crisis drove the SUPPORT Act's Medicare peer support provision. COVID-19 drove telehealth peer service expansion. The Uvalde shooting drove the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act's community mental health funding. In each case, a crisis created political conditions for expanding peer roles, but the resulting legislation addressed immediate needs without building long-term workforce infrastructure. Crisis-driven policy tends to create new peer positions without addressing the wages, career paths, and organizational supports needed to retain the workers who fill them.
