# Peer Support Services: Root Causes

## Systemic Analysis

The current challenges facing peer support services in the United States are not the result of a single failure but of several long-term, interlocking trends. Despite bipartisan support, growing evidence, and increasing demand, peer support remains marginalized within the behavioral health system. The root causes include the dominance of the medical model, inadequate financing structures, professional guild protectionism, stigma against people with psychiatric histories, and the failure to invest in workforce infrastructure. These factors reinforce one another, creating a system that simultaneously expands peer roles while denying peer workers the wages, respect, and structural support necessary to sustain those roles.

## Medical Model Dominance

### The Hierarchy of Expertise

The American behavioral health system is built on a medical model that privileges credentialed expertise over experiential knowledge. Psychiatrists sit at the top of the authority hierarchy, followed by psychologists, social workers, and licensed counselors. Peer specialists -- whose primary qualification is personal experience with the conditions they address -- occupy the bottom rung. This hierarchy is not merely symbolic; it determines reimbursement rates, scope of practice authority, hiring decisions, and organizational culture.

**Evidence**: Peer specialists earn a median wage of $17.65/hour compared to $28.63 for substance abuse counselors and $42.95 for clinical social workers (BLS, 2023). Reimbursement rates for peer services are typically 30-50% lower than rates for equivalent services delivered by licensed professionals, even when outcomes are comparable.

The hierarchy is institutionally reinforced through accreditation standards. The Joint Commission, the Commission on Accreditation of Rehabilitation Facilities (CARF), and state licensing bodies define quality in clinical terms -- treatment plans, progress notes, measurable outcomes tied to DSM diagnoses -- that systematically privilege clinical roles. Peer-specific quality indicators, such as mutuality, empowerment, or hope, are rarely included in accreditation standards, rendering the distinctive value of peer support invisible to institutional quality measurement systems.

### Diagnostic Gatekeeping

The medical model requires that services be tied to clinical diagnoses and medical necessity criteria. Peer support, which is fundamentally relational and recovery-oriented rather than symptom-focused, fits awkwardly within this framework. Medicaid billing for peer services often requires documentation of clinical symptoms, treatment plans signed by licensed clinicians, and measurable clinical outcomes -- all of which impose a clinical lens on a non-clinical intervention.

**Evidence**: A 2022 survey of peer specialists found that 67% reported spending more than 25% of their time on documentation required for billing purposes, time that could otherwise be spent providing direct support (National Association of Peer Supporters, 2022). In states requiring co-signature by a licensed clinician, peers report additional delays and bottlenecks: a peer specialist cannot submit documentation until a supervisor reviews and signs it, which may take days.

### The Recovery-Medical Model Tension

The recovery model -- which defines recovery as "a process of change through which individuals improve their health and wellness, live a self-directed life, and strive to reach their full potential" (SAMHSA, 2012) -- operates on fundamentally different assumptions than the medical model. The medical model defines success as symptom reduction or remission; the recovery model defines success as a meaningful, self-directed life that may or may not include symptom elimination. Peer support is grounded in the recovery model, but the financing, regulatory, and organizational infrastructure in which peers operate is designed for the medical model. This mismatch creates persistent friction: peer workers are asked to deliver recovery-oriented services using medical-model documentation, billing codes, and outcome measures.

## Financing and Reimbursement Failures

### The Medicaid Patchwork

While CMS authorized Medicaid billing for peer services in 2007, the letter left implementation entirely to states. The result is a patchwork of 46 different billing structures with wildly different rates ($8.50 to $72.00 per 15-minute unit), covered service types, documentation requirements, and supervision mandates. Four states still do not bill Medicaid for peer services at all.

**Evidence**: A 2023 analysis by C4 Innovations found that Medicaid reimbursement rates in 14 states were insufficient to cover the fully loaded cost of employing a peer specialist (wages, benefits, supervision, training, and administrative overhead), meaning that organizations providing peer services in these states operate at a financial loss. In these states, peer positions are subsidized by grant funding or cross-subsidized by revenue from clinical services -- arrangements that make peer positions the first to be cut when budgets tighten.

### Rate-Setting Methodology Failures

Most state Medicaid agencies set peer service reimbursement rates using cost-based or fee-schedule methods designed for clinical services. These methods typically base rates on direct service provider wages, but the baseline wage assumptions for peer specialists are circular: rates are set low because peer wages are low, and peer wages remain low because rates are set low. Few states have conducted independent rate adequacy analyses for peer services that account for the full cost of recruitment, training, supervision, benefits, and retention for a workforce with 30-50% annual turnover. The actuarial methods used for clinical services, which assume a stable professional workforce with predictable turnover, do not account for the structural instability of the peer workforce.

### Private Insurance Exclusion

Medicare covers peer services only for opioid use disorder treatment (under the SUPPORT Act, 2018), and most private insurers do not cover peer services at all. This means that working-age adults with private insurance -- and Medicare beneficiaries without opioid use disorders -- cannot access reimbursable peer support, limiting the market and the financial sustainability of peer service organizations.

**Evidence**: Fewer than 10% of commercial insurance plans include peer support services as a covered benefit (National Council for Mental Wellbeing, 2023). The mental health parity law (MHPAEA, 2008) requires parity between mental health and medical/surgical benefits, but because peer support is not classified as a standard behavioral health benefit, parity requirements have not been applied to its coverage. This exclusion creates a coverage paradox: peer support is recognized as an evidence-based practice by SAMHSA and the VA but is excluded from the benefit designs of most private health plans.

### Grant Dependence and Funding Instability

Peer-run organizations -- including peer respite programs, warmlines, and drop-in centers -- operate primarily on grants and state appropriations rather than insurance reimbursement. These funding sources are inherently unstable, subject to annual appropriations cycles and political priorities. The result is chronic organizational instability that undermines staff retention, program development, and long-term planning.

**Evidence**: SAMHSA peer support grants totaled $29 million in FY2024 -- a fraction of the $6.5 billion SAMHSA total budget. Peer-run organizations report an average operating budget of under $500,000, with 60% dependent on a single funding source (National Empowerment Center, 2023). The National Empowerment Center's 2023 survey of peer-run organizations found that 45% had experienced a funding disruption in the previous three years that required staff layoffs, and 22% reported periods of temporary closure.

### Fee-for-Service Misalignment

The dominant fee-for-service (FFS) payment model in Medicaid is structurally misaligned with peer support's relational, flexible, and often informal mode of delivery. FFS models require discrete, time-limited service encounters documented with billable codes. Peer support, by contrast, often involves informal check-ins, community activities, accompaniment to appointments, and extended conversations that do not fit neatly into 15-minute billing increments. The result is either that peer workers distort their practice to fit billing requirements -- becoming more clinical and less peer-like -- or that significant portions of their work go unbilled, reducing organizational revenue and peer wages.

## Professional Guild Protectionism

### Scope of Practice Turf Wars

Licensed professional organizations -- including the National Association of Social Workers (NASW), American Psychological Association (APA), and American Counseling Association (ACA) -- have at times opposed the expansion of peer support services on the grounds that peers may perform activities that constitute unlicensed practice of counseling, therapy, or case management. These concerns are not entirely without merit, but they also reflect economic interests: expanding the peer workforce creates competition for limited behavioral health funding.

**Evidence**: In at least 6 states between 2018 and 2024, proposals to expand peer scope of practice or create new peer billing codes were opposed by licensed professional associations during legislative or regulatory proceedings (C4 Innovations legislative tracker, 2024). In Arizona, a 2021 proposal to allow peer specialists to bill for crisis support services was opposed by the state chapter of NASW on grounds that crisis intervention constitutes a clinical activity. In Virginia, a 2022 proposal for a peer supervisor credential was modified after opposition from the Licensed Professional Counselors association, which argued that supervision of any behavioral health service required a clinical license.

### The Credentialism Escalator

Licensed professions have a structural incentive to raise entry requirements over time -- more training hours, higher degree requirements, more restrictive licensing exams. This "credentialism escalator" gradually increases the cost and exclusivity of professional practice, which protects existing practitioners but raises barriers to entry for new workers. When applied to peer support, this dynamic threatens to transform peer certification from an accessible pathway for people with lived experience into a credentialing regime that privileges educational attainment over experiential expertise.

**Evidence**: Between 2015 and 2024, the average training hours required for peer specialist certification across states increased from 55 to 75 hours, and 8 states added supervised experience requirements where none previously existed. While some of these increases reflect genuine quality improvements, they also create barriers for potential peer workers who cannot afford extended unpaid training or who lack access to supervised practicum sites.

### Supervision as Control

CMS requires that peer services be supervised by a "competent mental health professional," but it does not specify who qualifies or what supervision should look like. In practice, many states require supervision by a licensed clinician -- a social worker, psychologist, or counselor. This requirement, while intended to ensure quality, can function as a mechanism of professional control, placing peer workers under the authority of professionals who may not understand or value the peer role.

**Evidence**: In a 2022 survey, 41% of peer specialists reported that their supervisors had no training in peer support competencies or values, and 28% reported being asked by supervisors to perform tasks outside the peer role (e.g., clinical assessments or medication management) (Peer Workforce Survey, 2022). Only 15 states offer a dedicated peer supervisor credential, and fewer require it for individuals supervising peer workers. The absence of peer-competent supervision creates conditions for role drift, boundary confusion, and the gradual absorption of peer roles into clinical functions.

The alternative -- supervision by experienced peer specialists rather than licensed clinicians -- is practiced in some states (Pennsylvania, Connecticut, Georgia) and is supported by N.A.P.S. practice guidelines. However, it faces resistance from state licensing boards and MCOs that equate supervision quality with clinical licensure level.

## Political Economy

### Who Benefits from the Status Quo

| Actor | Benefit | Economic Value | Incentive to Block Reform |
|-------|---------|----------------|---------------------------|

| Managed care organizations (MCOs) | Lower reimbursement rates for peer services increase margins | Varies by state | Higher peer rates reduce profit; current low rates transfer savings to MCOs, not peer workers |

### Perverse Incentives

The current financing structure creates several perverse incentives that undermine peer support effectiveness:

1. **Low Medicaid rates incentivize low wages**: Organizations receiving Medicaid rates of $10-15 per unit cannot afford to pay peer specialists living wages while covering overhead. The low rates perpetuate the low-wage workforce. Organizations in low-rate states face a choice between paying poverty wages and operating at a financial loss.

2. **Fee-for-service billing incentivizes volume over relationship**: Peer support is inherently relational and not easily quantified in 15-minute billable increments. Fee-for-service models push peer workers toward brief, documentable encounters rather than the sustained, flexible relationships that drive outcomes. A peer specialist who spends two hours accompanying someone to a housing appointment and providing emotional support generates less revenue than one who conducts eight 15-minute "peer support sessions."

3. **Medical necessity requirements exclude prevention**: Medicaid's medical necessity standard means peer services are typically available only after someone has a diagnosed condition, excluding the preventive and early-intervention peer support that could reduce downstream costs. A person experiencing early signs of distress but without a DSM diagnosis cannot access Medicaid-funded peer support -- precisely the moment when peer intervention could prevent escalation to a costly crisis.

4. **Criminal background exclusions shrink the talent pool**: Many state certification programs exclude individuals with certain criminal convictions -- precisely the population whose lived experience with both behavioral health conditions and the justice system makes them most effective as forensic peer specialists. A 2022 N.A.P.S. analysis found that 12 states impose blanket exclusions for felony convictions regardless of the nature of the offense, time since conviction, or relevance to the peer role, eliminating an estimated 15-20% of otherwise qualified candidates.

5. **Turnover costs are externalized**: The costs of 30-50% annual peer workforce turnover -- recruitment, training, lost productivity, disrupted service relationships -- are borne by peer service organizations and by the consumers who lose their peer connections. The payers (Medicaid, MCOs) who set the low rates that drive turnover do not bear these costs directly, creating a misalignment between the entities that control rates and the entities that experience turnover consequences.

## Stigma and Institutional Culture

### The "Not Real Workers" Problem

Despite decades of progress, peer specialists continue to face workplace stigma. Clinical colleagues may view them as "not real" professionals, question their qualifications, or exclude them from clinical discussions and treatment planning. This stigma is compounded when peers disclose their own mental health or substance use histories in workplace settings, exposing them to judgment from colleagues who do not share the peer support value of mutual disclosure.

**Evidence**: A 2021 study in *Psychiatric Services* found that 52% of peer specialists reported experiencing stigma from clinical colleagues at least monthly, and 34% reported being excluded from team meetings where their service recipients' care was discussed (Vandewalle et al., 2021). A 2023 follow-up study found that stigma experiences were correlated with turnover intention: peer specialists who reported frequent stigma were 2.3 times more likely to leave their positions within 12 months.

### Organizational Culture Barriers

Even in organizations that formally employ peer specialists, institutional culture often fails to accommodate the distinctive nature of the peer role. Organizations built around clinical workflows, clinical documentation, and clinical authority structures may inadvertently marginalize peer workers by:

- Excluding peers from electronic health record access, preventing them from documenting their work
- Requiring peers to use clinical note formats (SOAP notes, DAP notes) designed for clinical encounters
- Scheduling peers into the same time-bounded appointment slots used for clinical services
- Housing peer workers in clinical spaces without creating peer-specific gathering areas
- Applying the same performance metrics (caseload size, productivity hours) to peers as to clinicians

### Internalized Stigma and Self-Doubt

Peer specialists may internalize the medical model hierarchy, doubting their own competence or deferring unnecessarily to clinical authority. Organizations that do not actively cultivate peer identity and values may inadvertently reinforce this internalized stigma. Research by Patricia Deegan and others has documented how institutional environments can undermine the "sense of agency and self-direction" that is central to recovery -- for peer workers as well as for the people they serve.

## Research and Evidence Gaps

### Underfunded Research

The evidence base for peer support, while growing, remains thinner than for most clinical interventions -- not because peer support is less effective, but because it receives far less research funding. NIH spending on peer support research totaled approximately $12 million in FY2023, compared to over $300 million for psychotherapy research (NIH RePORTER, 2024). This funding disparity makes it difficult to produce the large-scale randomized controlled trials that payers and policymakers demand before expanding coverage.

**Evidence**: A 2023 systematic review identified only 18 randomized controlled trials of mental health peer support services published in English, compared to over 500 RCTs for cognitive behavioral therapy (Cochrane Database, 2023). The existing trials are generally small (median sample size under 200), single-site, and conducted by research teams with limited funding, making it difficult to detect clinically meaningful effects or to generalize findings across diverse populations and settings.

### Methodological Challenges

Peer support is difficult to study using traditional clinical trial methods. The active ingredient is the relationship itself, which cannot be standardized or blinded. Outcomes that matter most to peer support recipients -- empowerment, hope, social connection, self-determination -- are difficult to measure with standard clinical instruments designed to detect symptom reduction. The field lacks validated, peer-specific outcome measures that capture the distinctive value of peer support, though instruments like the Recovery Assessment Scale (RAS) and the Empowerment Scale are used as proxies.

Additionally, the heterogeneity of peer support models makes comparison across studies challenging. A peer specialist embedded in a clinical team, a WRAP facilitator leading a community group, and a peer respite worker providing overnight support are all "peer support" but operate through different mechanisms in different settings. Aggregating their outcomes in meta-analyses risks obscuring important variation in what works, for whom, and under what conditions.

### The Evidence Catch-22

The research funding gap creates a self-reinforcing cycle: insufficient evidence leads payers to limit coverage, limited coverage restricts the settings where peer support can be studied, restricted settings limit the research base, and a limited research base reinforces payer skepticism. Breaking this cycle requires a deliberate, sustained federal research investment that does not wait for the evidence base to mature before funding the studies needed to build it.

## Why Reform Has Failed

Past efforts to strengthen peer support have achieved partial successes but have not overcome the fundamental structural barriers:

1. **CMS 2007 letter was permissive, not mandatory**: By authorizing rather than requiring Medicaid coverage of peer services, CMS left implementation to states, producing a patchwork system. States with the greatest need often had the least capacity to build peer infrastructure. The letter also deferred to states on rate-setting, supervision standards, and scope of practice, allowing the very disparities it was intended to address to persist.

2. **SUPPORT Act was condition-specific**: The 2018 law's Medicare peer support provision applied only to opioid use disorder, failing to establish the principle that peer support is valuable across all behavioral health conditions. This condition-specific approach fragments the peer workforce and reinforces the medical model's insistence on tying services to specific diagnoses.

3. **CCBHC mandates lack enforcement teeth**: While CCBHCs must provide peer services, the quality and integration of those services varies widely, and enforcement mechanisms focus on structural compliance rather than meaningful peer integration. A CCBHC can satisfy the peer service requirement by employing a single part-time peer specialist who is marginalized within the clinical team -- technically compliant but substantively inadequate.

4. **Certification standardization efforts have stalled**: Multiple attempts to establish national peer specialist certification standards -- including efforts by SAMHSA, IC&RC, and the National Association of Peer Supporters -- have been blocked by states protecting their regulatory autonomy and by certification vendors protecting their markets. The absence of a national standard perpetuates the portability problem, fragments the workforce, and undermines efforts to build a coherent professional identity.

5. **Advocacy organizations are under-resourced**: The peer support advocacy community -- organizations like the National Association of Peer Supporters, National Empowerment Center, and Copeland Center -- operates on budgets that are a fraction of those commanded by licensed professional guilds, limiting their capacity to influence policy at the state and federal levels. N.A.P.S. operates on an annual budget of approximately $2 million, compared to NASW's $30+ million. This resource asymmetry means that peer advocacy voices are systematically outspent in every legislative and regulatory arena where peer support policy is shaped.

6. **The workforce itself is politically marginalized**: People with mental health and substance use histories face stigma that reduces their political voice. Peer specialists earning $17.65/hour, often without job security, have limited capacity to engage in sustained political advocacy. The populations served by peer support -- people with serious mental illness, people experiencing homelessness, people in recovery from addiction -- are among the least politically organized constituencies in the country.
