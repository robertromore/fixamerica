# Peer Support Services: Current State

## The Peer Workforce

The peer support workforce has expanded significantly since CMS authorized Medicaid reimbursement in 2007, but precise national counts remain elusive because the Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) does not track peer specialists as a distinct occupational category. The closest BLS classification -- community health workers (SOC 21-1094) -- encompasses a broader range of roles including community health educators, patient navigators, and promotores de salud. Estimates from SAMHSA, workforce surveys, and state certification registries suggest the United States employs 30,000 to 35,000 individuals in designated peer support roles as of 2023, though the actual number may be higher when accounting for peers working under alternative job titles or in unregistered positions.

The absence of a dedicated Standard Occupational Classification code has consequences beyond data limitations. It makes the peer workforce invisible in labor market analyses, prevents targeted federal workforce development investments, and makes it difficult for policymakers to assess whether supply is keeping pace with demand. The National Association of Peer Supporters (N.A.P.S.) and SAMHSA have jointly petitioned the BLS to create a distinct peer specialist occupational category, but the BLS revision cycle is slow, and no change is expected before the 2028 SOC revision.

The demographic profile of the peer workforce reveals both strengths and gaps. The 24% Black/African American representation exceeds the 13% share of the general U.S. population, reflecting the important role peer services play in communities of color where distrust of traditional mental health systems is prevalent. However, the 11% Hispanic/Latino representation lags significantly behind the 19% share of the U.S. population, creating a gap in culturally and linguistically responsive peer services for the fastest-growing demographic group in the country. The 29% rate of criminal justice involvement highlights both the value of forensic peer expertise and the vulnerability of this workforce segment to background check exclusion policies.

The wage disparity is particularly consequential because many peer specialists were previously service recipients who became motivated to help others through their recovery experience. Their transition into the workforce often occurs after periods of disability, unemployment, or underemployment. Starting at $17.65 per hour -- often without comprehensive benefits -- means many peer workers remain near the poverty line, unable to achieve the financial stability that supports their own ongoing recovery. A 2022 C4 Innovations survey found that 34% of peer specialists reported difficulty meeting basic living expenses, and 19% reported that financial stress had negatively affected their own recovery or mental health.

### Turnover Drivers

The 30-50% annual turnover rate reflects multiple reinforcing factors:

| Driver | Prevalence | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Inadequate compensation | Primary driver (cited by 72% of departing peers) | Wages below living standard; inability to support family |
| Emotional exhaustion/burnout | Cited by 58% | Carrying dual burden of personal recovery and others' crises |
| Lack of career advancement | Cited by 51% | No clear path to supervisory or leadership roles |
| Role confusion and scope ambiguity | Cited by 44% | Being asked to perform clinical tasks outside peer scope |
| Workplace stigma from clinical colleagues | Cited by 38% | Feeling devalued, excluded from team decisions |
| Inadequate or inappropriate supervision | Cited by 33% | Supervisors untrained in peer support values and competencies |
| Administrative burden | Cited by 29% | Documentation demands reducing time for direct peer contact |

*Source: C4 Innovations National Peer Workforce Survey, 2022; state workforce retention studies, 2021-2023*

### Notable State Models

Several states have developed particularly innovative or comprehensive certification systems:

- **Georgia**: Pioneer state (2001). Developed by Larry Fricks and the Georgia Mental Health Consumer Network. The Georgia model -- 75 hours of training, written exam, supervised experience -- became the template replicated by the majority of states. Georgia's 2019 Medicaid rate increase from $12 to $22 per 15-minute unit was followed by a 23% reduction in peer turnover within two years, demonstrating the direct link between reimbursement adequacy and workforce stability.

- **Pennsylvania**: Operates one of the most comprehensive peer certification systems, including separate mental health and substance use credentials, a peer supervisor credential, and a forensic peer specialist track. The Pennsylvania Certification Board administers all credentials and requires 75 hours of training plus 25 hours of supervised practicum.

- **Texas**: The Via Hope Peer Specialist Training Program, designated by the Texas Health and Human Services Commission, provides a standardized statewide curriculum. Texas also operates a Mental Health Peer Specialist Medicaid benefit that covers both individual and group services, and was among the first states to authorize telehealth peer support billing.

- **Connecticut**: Notable for its high Medicaid reimbursement rate ($48 per 15-minute unit), which supports peer specialist wages averaging $22-$24/hour -- among the highest in the nation. Connecticut's Community Health Resources operates a model peer supervision program that pairs each peer specialist with both a peer supervisor and a clinical supervisor.

- **California**: In 2024, California overhauled its peer certification system through the Peer Support Specialist Certification Act (SB 803), creating a statewide certification administered by the Department of Health Care Access and Information. The new system replaces a patchwork of county-level certifications with a uniform standard, and includes provisions for culturally specific training tracks and criminal background waivers.

## Medicaid Billing and Reimbursement

### Federal Framework

CMS authorized Medicaid reimbursement for peer support services through State Medicaid Director Letter #07-011 (August 15, 2007). The letter established that peer support qualifies as a Medicaid-billable service when:

1. **Services are provided by peers**: Workers must have lived experience with mental health or substance use conditions
2. **Supervision is maintained**: A competent mental health professional must provide oversight
3. **State certification exists**: Providers must be certified through a state-recognized program
4. **Services are recovery-oriented**: Must be individualized and promote self-determination

The letter was permissive rather than mandatory -- it authorized states to cover peer services but did not require them to do so. This distinction has been consequential: as of 2024, four states still do not bill Medicaid for peer services, and among the 46 that do, the quality and scope of coverage varies dramatically.

### State Medicaid Coverage

| Coverage Status | Number of States | Trend |
|---|---|---|
| Medicaid billing approved | 46 | Increasing |
| State Plan Amendment filed | 43 | Increasing |
| 1915(i) Home and Community-Based waiver | 8 | Stable |
| 1115 Demonstration waiver | 6 | Increasing |
| No Medicaid billing for peer services | 4 | Decreasing |

*Source: National Academy for State Health Policy, 2024*

States use different Medicaid authorities to cover peer services, each with distinct implications:

- **State Plan Amendments (SPAs)**: The most common approach (43 states). Covers peer services as a Medicaid State Plan benefit, providing broad eligibility but subject to medical necessity requirements and state-determined rate structures.
- **1915(i) Waivers**: Used by 8 states to cover peer services under home and community-based services authority. Provides more flexibility in service design but limits eligibility to specific populations.
- **1115 Demonstration Waivers**: Used by 6 states to test innovative peer service models, including peer-delivered crisis services, peer respite, and peer support in criminal justice diversion programs. Time-limited and subject to federal evaluation requirements.

### Reimbursement Rates

Medicaid reimbursement rates for peer support services vary dramatically across states:

| Rate Category | States | Rate Range (per 15-minute unit) | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| Below $15/unit | 8 | $8.50-$14.99 | Mississippi ($9.25), Alabama ($11.00), Louisiana ($12.50) |
| $15-$25/unit | 18 | $15.00-$25.00 | Ohio ($18.75), Virginia ($20.50), Georgia ($22.00) |
| $25-$40/unit | 14 | $25.01-$40.00 | California ($32.00), New York ($35.50), Oregon ($28.75) |
| Above $40/unit | 6 | $40.01-$72.00 | Connecticut ($48.00), Massachusetts ($55.00), Vermont ($62.00) |

*Source: State Medicaid fee schedules compiled by C4 Innovations, 2024*

The variation in reimbursement rates is not merely a statistical curiosity; it directly determines whether peer specialists can earn a living wage. At $9.25 per 15-minute unit (Mississippi's rate), an organization billing for 5 hours of peer service per day generates $185 in revenue -- from which it must cover the peer specialist's wages, benefits, supervision, training, documentation time, administrative overhead, and facility costs. At these rates, paying a peer specialist more than $13-$14 per hour while maintaining organizational solvency is mathematically impossible.

### Billing Challenges

Common barriers to effective Medicaid billing for peer services:

| Challenge | Prevalence | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Medical necessity documentation burden | High (38 states) | Reduces time available for direct service delivery |
| Service duration limits (per day/month) | Moderate (22 states) | Caps the intensity of support during crises |
| Exclusion of telephonic/text-based services | Declining (14 states still exclude) | Limits reach, especially in rural areas |
| Group service billing restrictions | Moderate (19 states) | Cannot bill for peer-led group activities |
| Requirement for co-signature by licensed clinician | Moderate (16 states) | Creates bottleneck and reinforces hierarchy |
| Restriction to facility-based settings only | Low (9 states) | Prevents community-based peer outreach |
| Prohibition on billing during same visit as clinical service | Low (11 states) | Forces artificial scheduling separation |
| Prior authorization requirements | Low-Moderate (12 states) | Adds administrative delay before services begin |

Notable peer respite programs include:

- **Afiya House** (Springfield, MA): Operated by the Western Massachusetts Recovery Learning Community, Afiya is one of the longest-operating peer respite programs in the country. It serves as a training site for peer respite development nationally.
- **Rose House** (New York City): Operated by the New York Association for Psychiatric Rehabilitation Services, Rose House demonstrates peer respite viability in a high-cost urban setting.
- **Second Story** (Santa Cruz County, CA): One of the first peer respite programs on the West Coast, operating since 2013. Has diverted an estimated 70% of guests from psychiatric hospitalization.
- **Peer Support and Wellness Center** (Denver, CO): Integrates peer respite with a warmline and drop-in center, providing a continuum of peer-run crisis alternatives.

### Warmlines

Warmlines -- non-crisis, peer-staffed telephone support lines -- provide a critical bridge between no services and crisis services. Unlike the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, warmlines are staffed by peers and oriented toward emotional support, coping strategies, and connection rather than clinical crisis intervention.

## Recent Trends

### Growth and Expansion (2018-2024)

- **Rapid state adoption**: 8 additional states approved Medicaid billing for peer services between 2018 and 2024
- **COVID-19 acceleration**: The pandemic expanded telehealth peer services, with 32 states adding telephonic/video peer support billing (2020-2022)
- **988 Lifeline integration**: The 2022 launch of the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline created new roles for peers in crisis follow-up and mobile response teams
- **Veterans Affairs expansion**: VA peer specialist positions grew from 800 to over 1,200 between 2018 and 2024, including new roles in whole health, primary care-mental health integration, and women's health
- **CCBHC growth**: Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinics (CCBHCs), mandated to provide peer support, expanded from 10 demonstration states to over 500 clinics in 46 states by 2024
- **Emergency department integration**: A growing number of hospitals (estimated 200+) have added peer specialists to emergency department behavioral health teams, with early evidence showing reduced repeat ED visits and improved follow-up engagement
- **Criminal justice diversion**: Forensic peer specialist programs have expanded in at least 15 states, with peers providing support in drug courts, reentry programs, and jail-based services

## Emerging Challenges

### Workforce Sustainability Crisis

The behavioral health field faces a broader workforce shortage, and the peer support segment is particularly vulnerable. Low wages, limited benefits, and emotional toll create a revolving door that undermines service quality and continuity of care. The gap between peer wages and entry-level clinical positions continues to widen. Between 2019 and 2023, substance abuse counselor salaries grew by approximately 12% in nominal terms while peer specialist wages grew by only 5%, further depressing the relative value of peer work and incentivizing peers with educational qualifications to pursue licensed clinical roles instead.

The turnover problem is self-reinforcing: high turnover disrupts the relational continuity that is the core mechanism of peer support effectiveness, which weakens outcomes, which undermines the evidence base, which gives payers justification for maintaining low reimbursement rates.

### Scope of Practice Conflicts

As peer roles expand into crisis services (988 response teams), primary care integration, and emergency department settings, conflicts with licensed professional scope-of-practice boundaries are intensifying. Several state licensing boards have raised concerns about peers performing activities that resemble counseling or case management. In 2023, the National Association of Social Workers (NASW) issued a position statement supporting peer services but cautioning against "scope creep" that blurs the line between peer support and licensed clinical practice -- a statement interpreted by many peer advocates as protective of professional turf rather than genuinely concerned about safety.

### Technology and Telehealth Integration

While COVID-19 accelerated telehealth peer services, the digital divide creates access barriers for the populations most in need of peer support -- individuals experiencing homelessness, those in rural areas, and older adults with limited technology literacy. Additionally, telehealth delivery changes the nature of peer support: the casual, drop-in quality of peer interaction is difficult to replicate through scheduled video appointments, and the spontaneous community-building that occurs in peer-run spaces cannot be fully digitized.

### Certification Portability

Peer specialists who relocate face the burden of re-certification under different state requirements. The lack of reciprocity agreements or a national certification standard creates barriers to workforce mobility and limits the field's ability to address geographic shortages through interstate recruitment. As of 2024, fewer than 10 states have formal reciprocity agreements with any other state. The International Credentialing and Reciprocity Consortium (IC&RC) offers a national peer recovery support specialist credential, but adoption remains limited because most states do not accept it as equivalent to their own certification.

### Research Funding Gap

Despite growing adoption, federal research funding specifically for peer support services remains minimal. NIH spending on peer support research totaled approximately $12 million in FY2023, compared to over $300 million for psychotherapy research -- a ratio that does not reflect peer support's growing role in the behavioral health system. The research gap has practical consequences: payers and policymakers cite insufficient evidence as the primary justification for limited coverage, while researchers cite insufficient funding as the primary barrier to producing the evidence that payers demand.

### Managed Care Administration

As Medicaid managed care organizations (MCOs) administer behavioral health benefits in the majority of states, peer services face additional barriers. MCOs may apply prior authorization requirements, impose restrictive network adequacy standards that exclude smaller peer-run organizations, or set reimbursement rates below state fee schedule levels. A 2023 National Council for Mental Wellbeing survey found that peer service providers in managed care states reported 35% more administrative burden and 20% lower effective reimbursement rates compared to providers in fee-for-service Medicaid states.
