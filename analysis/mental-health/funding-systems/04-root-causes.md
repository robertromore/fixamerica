# Mental Health Funding Systems: Root Causes

## Systemic Analysis

The current challenges in mental health financing are not the result of a single failure but of several long-term, interlocking dynamics: the historical separation of mental health from general health care, the fragmentation of responsibility across levels of government, the political weakness of the primary beneficiary population, and economic incentives that reward cost-shifting over effective treatment. These structural forces explain why mental health remains chronically underfunded despite broad bipartisan agreement that the system is broken.

## Structural Fragmentation

### No Single Accountable Entity

Unlike physical health care, where CMS (for Medicare and Medicaid), state insurance departments (for commercial plans), and employer benefits departments each have defined oversight roles, mental health funding is administered by SAMHSA (block grants), CMS (Medicaid and Medicare), state mental health authorities, county behavioral health departments, the VA, and private insurers simultaneously. No single entity is responsible for ensuring that total funding is adequate or that services reach the people who need them. This stands in contrast to many peer nations: the United Kingdom's NHS has a single mental health commissioning framework; Australia's federal government administers the Better Access program through a single Medicare system; Canada's provinces each operate unified mental health budgets within their health systems.

**Evidence**: A 2019 GAO report (GAO-19-274) found that at least 13 federal agencies administered 112 programs related to mental health, with "limited coordination and potential overlap." A follow-up GAO report in 2023 (GAO-23-105375) found that fragmentation had not improved and recommended HHS establish a leadership structure to coordinate across agencies. State auditors in California, New York, and Texas have documented parallel findings at the state level, where mental health funding flows through multiple departments with separate reporting requirements and no unified outcome tracking.

### Categorical Funding Silos

Federal mental health funding is distributed through dozens of categorical programs, each with its own eligibility criteria, allowable uses, reporting requirements, and application processes. A community mental health center may simultaneously receive funding from Medicaid fee-for-service, a CCBHC demonstration grant, the Mental Health Block Grant, PATH (for homeless services), a SAMHSA discretionary grant, state general fund appropriations, and county levy dollars -- each requiring separate documentation, compliance activities, and audits.

**Evidence**: The National Council for Mental Wellbeing reported in 2022 that community behavioral health organizations spend an average of 17% of their revenue on billing and administrative compliance, compared to 8-12% for general medical practices. For organizations receiving five or more distinct funding streams, administrative costs can exceed 25% of revenue. A 2021 RAND study of California's behavioral health system found that county behavioral health departments spent approximately 22% of their budgets on administration, with the largest share devoted to compliance with multiple state and federal reporting requirements.

### The Fragmentation Tax

The administrative burden imposed by funding fragmentation represents a direct reduction in resources available for patient care. For every $1 billion in mental health funding, an estimated $170-$250 million is consumed by administrative costs associated with managing multiple funding streams. This "fragmentation tax" is borne disproportionately by safety-net providers that serve the most complex and vulnerable patients, because these providers are most likely to rely on multiple funding sources.

## The IMD Exclusion as a Structural Barrier

### Legal Architecture of Exclusion

The IMD exclusion, enacted in 1965, was a fiscal policy decision -- not a clinical one. By prohibiting federal Medicaid matching for care in psychiatric facilities with more than 16 beds, Congress ensured that states would continue to bear the full cost of institutional psychiatric care. The exclusion has no analog in general medicine; there is no restriction on Medicaid reimbursement for hospitals of any size providing medical or surgical care. A 500-bed cancer hospital, a 1,000-bed general hospital, and a 200-bed rehabilitation facility all receive full Medicaid reimbursement. Only psychiatric facilities are singled out for exclusion based on their bed count.

**Evidence**: The Congressional Budget Office estimated in 2019 that full repeal of the IMD exclusion would cost the federal government $3.4-$5.9 billion annually, because states would shift existing state-funded institutional costs to the federal Medicaid match. This confirms that the exclusion functions primarily as a cost-allocation mechanism, not a clinical standard. The American Hospital Association, the National Alliance on Mental Illness, and the Treatment Advocacy Center have all called for reform of the exclusion.

### Downstream Effects

The IMD exclusion contributes to:

- Emergency department boarding of psychiatric patients (average 34 hours vs. 4.3 hours for medical patients, per ACEP, 2022). In some states, psychiatric ED boarding times exceed 72 hours. The total cost of psychiatric ED boarding is estimated at $2.2 billion annually.
- Reliance on the criminal justice system as a de facto psychiatric inpatient system. An estimated 2 million people with serious mental illness cycle through U.S. jails annually, at a cost of approximately $15 billion per year (Treatment Advocacy Center, 2022). Los Angeles County Jail, Cook County Jail (Chicago), and Rikers Island (New York City) are the three largest de facto psychiatric facilities in the United States.
- Proliferation of "16-bed" facilities designed to comply with the exclusion threshold rather than to meet clinical need. An entire micro-industry of 16-bed psychiatric facilities has developed, with facility design optimized for regulatory compliance rather than patient care.
- State reluctance to invest in inpatient psychiatric capacity that cannot be partially offset by federal Medicaid matching. The number of state psychiatric hospital beds has declined from 559,000 (1955) to approximately 37,000 (2023) -- a rate of 11.7 beds per 100,000 population, the lowest since before the Civil War.
- Geographic deserts for inpatient psychiatric care. Approximately 77% of U.S. counties lack any inpatient psychiatric beds, forcing patients to travel long distances or board in emergency departments awaiting transfer.

## Political Economy

### Who Benefits from the Status Quo

| Actor | Benefit | Economic Value | Incentive to Block Reform |
|-------|---------|----------------|---------------------------|
| State governments | Avoid increased state Medicaid match costs from expanding coverage | Billions annually in avoided state costs | Resist new mandates or match requirements |
| Managed care organizations (MCOs) | Intermediation profits from administering Medicaid behavioral health | $18+ billion in annual Medicaid BH managed care revenue (Milliman, 2022) | Oppose models that bypass MCO intermediation |
| Commercial insurers | Lower costs from under-reimbursing mental health providers | Estimated $10-$15 billion annually in avoided costs from parity non-compliance (Kennedy Forum, 2023) | Oppose stronger parity enforcement and rate adequacy mandates |
| Pharmaceutical companies | Current system generates high drug spending relative to therapy | ~$37 billion in psychotropic drug sales (IQVIA, 2022) | Prefer medication-focused care over investment in psychosocial services |
| Congressional appropriators | Block grants allow fiscal control | Fixed annual outlays vs. open-ended entitlement risk | Prefer discretionary spending (controllable) over entitlement expansion |
| Private-pay providers and concierge practices | Two-tier system directs affluent patients to cash-pay | Growing cash-pay sector exceeding $5 billion annually | Limited incentive to reform public system |

### Perverse Incentives

The current funding architecture creates multiple incentives that work against effective care:

- **Medicaid fee-for-service** rewards brief medication checks over longer therapy sessions, because 15-minute medication management visits generate higher hourly revenue than 45-minute therapy sessions at current reimbursement rates. A psychiatrist billing Medicaid can generate approximately $140/hour through 15-minute medication management visits versus $90/hour through 45-minute therapy sessions.
- **The IMD exclusion** incentivizes states to keep psychiatric facilities at exactly 16 beds or fewer, regardless of community need, and to route psychiatric patients through general hospital emergency departments (where Medicaid does pay) rather than dedicated psychiatric facilities. The per-day cost of ED boarding ($2,000-$3,500) typically exceeds the per-day cost of inpatient psychiatric care ($1,000-$1,800).
- **Block grant funding** creates a ceiling effect: because block grant amounts are fixed regardless of need, states with growing populations or rising mental health prevalence receive no additional federal support. The MHBG's real purchasing power has declined by approximately 23% since 1981, even as the population has grown by 40% and mental illness prevalence has increased.
- **Parity law structure** requires non-quantitative treatment limitation (NQTL) comparability testing but imposes minimal penalties for non-compliance, making it economically rational for insurers to under-comply and pay occasional penalties rather than achieve full parity. Prior to the 2024 final rule, DOL had no authority to impose civil monetary penalties for MHPAEA violations.
- **County-level funding** creates a race to the bottom in some regions, where counties that invest more in mental health services become "magnets" for neighboring counties' populations, discouraging investment.
- **Medicaid managed care capitation** incentivizes MCOs to minimize behavioral health spending, because any savings from under-serving behavioral health populations accrue to the MCO as profit, while the downstream costs (ED use, incarceration, homelessness) are borne by other public systems.

## Stigma and Political Weakness

### The Beneficiary Population Lacks Political Power

People with serious mental illness -- the primary beneficiaries of mental health funding reform -- face barriers to political engagement that are inherent to their conditions. Cognitive impairment, social isolation, homelessness, and incarceration reduce the capacity for sustained advocacy. Unlike organized disease-specific lobbies (e.g., cancer, diabetes, cardiovascular disease), mental health advocacy is fragmented among dozens of organizations with overlapping but non-identical agendas. The National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI), the National Council for Mental Wellbeing, the Treatment Advocacy Center, Mental Health America, and the American Psychiatric Association all advocate for mental health funding reform, but they do not always agree on priorities (e.g., community vs. institutional care, coerced treatment, scope of practice).

**Evidence**: In the 2022 election cycle, mental health-related political action committees contributed less than $5 million to federal candidates, compared to $40 million from hospital and nursing home PACs and over $80 million from pharmaceutical industry PACs (OpenSecrets, 2023). The health insurance industry spent $163 million on federal lobbying in 2022; all mental health advocacy organizations combined spent approximately $12 million -- a 14:1 ratio favoring the status quo.

### Structural Stigma in Fiscal Policy

The IMD exclusion itself is a codification of structural stigma: it treats psychiatric facilities differently from all other health care facilities solely because they serve people with mental illness. No comparable restriction exists for cancer hospitals, rehabilitation facilities, or long-term care nursing homes. The Medicare 190-day lifetime limit on psychiatric inpatient care has no parallel for any other medical condition. These policies embed the assumption that mental illness is fundamentally different from physical illness in ways that justify differential treatment in financing law -- an assumption contradicted by decades of neuroscience, epidemiology, and clinical evidence.

### The "Deserving" vs. "Undeserving" Frame

Mental health funding is politically vulnerable to the implicit distinction between "deserving" populations (children, veterans, trauma survivors) and "undeserving" populations (adults with chronic SMI, people with co-occurring substance use disorders, people experiencing homelessness). Funding initiatives that focus on youth mental health, veteran PTSD, or school-based services garner bipartisan support far more readily than proposals to increase reimbursement for adult outpatient therapy or expand inpatient psychiatric capacity.

## Workforce-Funding Disconnect

### Funding That Cannot Be Spent

Even when funding is available, the mental health workforce shortage prevents its effective deployment. Approximately 160 million Americans live in designated Mental Health Professional Shortage Areas (HRSA, 2024). Community mental health centers report that unfilled positions are a more binding constraint than funding levels, because existing reimbursement rates are too low to attract qualified clinicians.

**Evidence**: A 2023 survey by the National Council for Mental Wellbeing found that 93% of community behavioral health organizations reported difficulty recruiting clinical staff, and 73% had turned away clients due to insufficient staff capacity. Average community mental health center salaries for licensed clinicians were $55,000-$65,000, compared to $85,000-$120,000 in private practice or hospital settings (NCCBH, 2023). A 2022 Merritt Hawkins survey found that the average wait time for a new patient psychiatry appointment was 67 days in non-metropolitan areas, compared to 25 days in major metropolitan areas.

### The Wage Gap Drives the Access Gap

The single most important driver of mental health workforce shortages is the gap between public-system reimbursement rates and private-practice or hospital compensation. A licensed clinical social worker working at a community mental health center earning $58,000/year can increase their income by 50-100% by moving to private practice, where they can charge $150-$250 per session out-of-pocket or negotiate higher commercial insurance rates. This wage differential creates a one-way flow of clinicians from public to private settings, concentrating workforce capacity among populations with the least clinical need.

## Why Reform Has Failed

### Incremental Approaches Cannot Overcome Structural Problems

Past reforms have targeted individual symptoms rather than the structural architecture. Parity legislation addressed insurance benefit design but not provider payment adequacy. The ACA expanded coverage but not the workforce to deliver care. CCBHC demonstrations proved the prospective payment model works but did not make it universal. The 988 system created a crisis number but not crisis infrastructure. Each reform addressed one piece of the fragmentation puzzle while leaving the others intact, and each was subsequently undermined by the structural forces it left unaddressed.

### Fiscal Federalism Creates a Collective Action Problem

Because mental health funding is shared between federal and state governments, neither level has sufficient incentive to solve the problem unilaterally. The federal government could increase block grant funding, but states might substitute federal dollars for state dollars rather than expanding services (the "supplantation" problem that has plagued every federal-state mental health funding arrangement since 1963). States could increase reimbursement rates, but doing so increases both state and federal spending, and states have limited control over federal matching formulas. Counties face the same dynamic with states.

### Crisis-Driven Budgeting Prevents Sustained Investment

Mental health funding increases tend to follow high-profile tragedies (mass shootings, celebrity suicides, pandemic-era mental health crises) and fade as public attention shifts. The pattern of crisis-driven supplemental appropriations followed by reversion to baseline prevents the multi-year, predictable investment needed to build sustainable infrastructure and workforce. The $5.5 billion in pandemic-era supplements (2020-2023) is now expiring, with 34 states anticipating shortfalls -- the latest iteration of a pattern that has repeated after every funding surge since the 1963 Community Mental Health Act.

### The Authorization-Appropriation Gap

Congress regularly authorizes mental health programs at funding levels that it then fails to appropriate. This gap between authorization and appropriation creates a persistent structural shortfall. The MHBG has never been appropriated at its authorized level. SAMHSA's total budget represents a fraction of its authorized programs. The 988 system was authorized without a dedicated funding mechanism. This pattern allows legislators to claim credit for authorizing programs while avoiding the fiscal and political costs of funding them.
