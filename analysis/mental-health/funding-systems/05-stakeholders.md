# Mental Health Funding Systems: Stakeholders

The financing of mental health services in the United States is shaped by the interactions of federal agencies, state and county governments, providers, insurers, advocacy organizations, and the tens of millions of Americans who live with mental illness. Each group has distinct interests, resources, and levels of power over how funding decisions are made.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Adults with any mental illness (AMI) | Directly affected by funding levels, insurance coverage, provider availability | 57.8 million (2022, SAMHSA NSDUH) | Affordable, accessible, comprehensive treatment |
| Adults with serious mental illness (SMI) | Most severely affected; most dependent on public funding | 14.1 million (2022, SAMHSA NSDUH) | Inpatient access, crisis services, long-term support, housing |
| Children/adolescents with SED | Dependent on Medicaid, CHIP, and school-based services | 7.7 million (2022, SAMHSA NSDUH) | Early intervention, school-based services, family support |
| Families and caregivers | Bear financial, emotional, and time costs of caregiving | ~8.4 million family caregivers for adults with SMI (NAMI, 2023) | Respite services, navigation support, caregiver health |
| Uninsured adults with mental illness | No coverage for treatment; rely on safety net | ~6.3 million uninsured with AMI (2022, SAMHSA NSDUH) | Any coverage pathway, safety-net funding |
| People with mental illness in jails/prisons | Receive inadequate treatment; system of last resort | ~2 million with SMI cycle through jails annually (Treatment Advocacy Center, 2022) | Diversion programs, reentry coverage, pre-release Medicaid |
| People with co-occurring MH/SUD conditions | Navigate two parallel funding systems with different rules | ~9.2 million adults (2022, SAMHSA NSDUH) | Integrated treatment, single-point-of-entry funding |

### Secondary Stakeholders

- **Employers**: Mental illness costs employers an estimated $210 billion annually in lost productivity, absenteeism, and disability (National Alliance on Mental Illness, 2023). The National Business Group on Health and the Business Group on Health represent large employers with direct financial interest in effective treatment, but employers generally resist mandate-driven cost increases through insurance premiums.
- **Emergency departments**: EDs serve as the default entry point for psychiatric crises. In 2022, mental health-related ED visits accounted for approximately 6.4 million visits, with an average boarding time of 34 hours for patients awaiting psychiatric placement (ACEP, 2022). The American College of Emergency Physicians has been a vocal advocate for IMD exclusion reform and crisis system funding.
- **Law enforcement**: Police respond to an estimated 10% of all calls involving people with mental illness. Local law enforcement budgets absorb the cost of mental health crisis response in the absence of adequate community services (PERF, 2023). The Major Cities Chiefs Association and the International Association of Chiefs of Police have both endorsed mental health funding reform.
- **Taxpayers**: Total public spending on mental health exceeds $150 billion annually across all levels of government. Taxpayers bear costs through both direct appropriations and indirect costs (ED use, incarceration, homelessness services). Taxpayer advocacy groups are divided: fiscal conservatives seek to limit spending, while others recognize that upstream mental health investment reduces downstream costs.
- **People experiencing homelessness**: An estimated 30-40% of people experiencing homelessness have a serious mental illness. Inadequate mental health funding is a direct driver of homelessness, and homelessness service providers (including HUD grantees and CoC agencies) depend on mental health funding to serve their populations.
- **Schools and educators**: Approximately 6 million children receive school-based mental health services. School districts increasingly fund counselors and social workers from general education budgets when mental health system funding is inadequate, diverting resources from educational programming.

## Who Has Power

### Decision Makers

| Actor | Role | Authority | Resources | Influence Level |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Congress (Appropriations Committees) | Sets federal funding levels for SAMHSA, MHBG, and authorizes Medicaid reforms | Spending power, legislative authority | Controls federal discretionary and mandatory spending | High |
| Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) | Administers Medicaid/Medicare, approves state plan amendments and 1115 waivers | Regulatory authority over nation's largest payers | $1.5+ trillion annual Medicaid/Medicare budget | High |
| SAMHSA | Administers block grants, discretionary grants, and CCBHC programs | Grant administration, technical assistance, data collection | $9.5 billion budget (FY2024) | Medium |
| State legislatures | Set state mental health budgets, Medicaid eligibility, and provider licensing | State appropriations authority, Medicaid state plan control | Varies by state; $44.8 billion total SMHA spending (FY2021) | High |
| State Mental Health Authorities (SMHAs) | Administer state MH systems, allocate block grant funds, manage state hospitals | Direct service delivery and contracting authority | Varies by state | Medium |
| Governors | Propose state budgets, control executive branch agencies, apply for federal waivers | Executive authority, budget proposal | Agenda-setting power | High |
| County boards of supervisors | In many states, set local mental health levies and contracts | Local taxing authority, service contracting | Varies widely by county | Medium (in county-administered states) |
| DOL Employee Benefits Security Administration | Enforces MHPAEA for employer-sponsored health plans | Investigative and enforcement authority under ERISA | $250+ million EBSA budget | Medium |

### Key Organizations and Institutions

- **National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI)**: The largest grassroots mental health advocacy organization, with 600+ local affiliates and over 500,000 members. NAMI advocates for increased funding, parity enforcement, and CCBHC expansion. Annual revenue approximately $35 million (2022). NAMI's annual "NAMIWalks" events raise $15+ million and generate significant media coverage. NAMI has been effective in building bipartisan congressional support through constituent engagement but has less influence in appropriations negotiations.
- **National Council for Mental Wellbeing**: Represents over 3,300 community behavioral health organizations. The National Council has been the primary advocate for CCBHC expansion and serves as the technical assistance provider for CCBHC implementation. Its annual Hill Day brings hundreds of community mental health leaders to Washington for legislative advocacy. The National Council's 2023 CCBHC Impact Report has been influential in building congressional support for permanent authorization.
- **American Psychiatric Association (APA)**: The professional association for psychiatrists (38,800 members). The APA advocates for Medicare/Medicaid reimbursement increases and opposes scope-of-practice expansions for non-physician providers. The APA's political action committee (APAPAC) is the largest mental health PAC, contributing approximately $1.8 million per election cycle.
- **American Psychological Association**: Represents 146,000+ psychologists. Advocates for parity enforcement, inclusion of psychologists in integrated care teams, and expanded reimbursement. Supports scope-of-practice expansion, including prescriptive authority for psychologists.
- **National Association of State Mental Health Program Directors (NASMHPD)**: Represents the 56 state and territory mental health commissioners. NASMHPD provides data through its NRI research arm, technical assistance, and a collective voice for state mental health systems. NASMHPD's annual State Mental Health Agency Profiling System is the authoritative source for state-level mental health spending data.
- **Medicaid and CHIP Payment and Access Commission (MACPAC)**: Independent federal body that advises Congress on Medicaid policy, including mental health coverage and payment adequacy. MACPAC's reports on Medicaid behavioral health reimbursement rates have been instrumental in documenting the gap between Medicaid and Medicare rates.
- **Treatment Advocacy Center**: Advocates for policies to improve access to treatment for people with the most serious mental illness, including IMD exclusion reform and expanded use of assisted outpatient treatment. Has been the leading voice on the psychiatric bed shortage and the criminalization of mental illness.
- **Mental Health America (MHA)**: Founded in 1909, MHA focuses on screening, prevention, and early intervention. Administers the most widely used online mental health screening tools (over 10 million screens annually). Advocates for community-based care and opposes coerced treatment.

### Influencers

- **Insurance industry (AHIP, BlueCross BlueShield Association)**: Lobby against stronger parity enforcement and rate adequacy mandates. The health insurance industry spent $163 million on federal lobbying in 2022 (OpenSecrets, 2023). AHIP has consistently argued that parity enforcement should focus on "outcomes" rather than process requirements, a position that critics view as an effort to avoid compliance with NQTL comparability testing.
- **Pharmaceutical industry (PhRMA)**: Support coverage expansion for psychotropic medications but oppose policies that would shift spending from medications to psychosocial services. Pharma PACs contributed over $80 million to federal candidates in the 2022 cycle (OpenSecrets, 2023). The psychotropic drug market generates approximately $37 billion annually in U.S. sales.
- **Managed care organizations (Centene, Molina, UnitedHealth, Elevance/Anthem)**: Administer Medicaid behavioral health in most states. MCOs support payment models that flow through their intermediation and resist direct-to-provider payment approaches such as CCBHC carve-outs. Centene alone manages Medicaid behavioral health for over 15 million enrollees across 29 states.
- **Kennedy Forum**: Founded by former Rep. Patrick Kennedy, advocates for mental health parity enforcement. Has been instrumental in pushing for CMS and DOL enforcement actions and was a key advocate for the 2024 MHPAEA final rule.
- **Inseparable**: A bipartisan mental health advocacy organization that has been effective in building Republican support for mental health funding reform. Founded in 2020, Inseparable has worked with over 50 Republican and Democratic members of Congress on mental health legislation.

## Stakeholder Interests and Alignment

### Aligned Interests (Potential Coalition Partners)

| Coalition | Shared Interest | Strength |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| NAMI + National Council + APA + NASMHPD | Increased federal mental health funding | Strong |
| Providers + patients + families | CCBHC expansion and higher reimbursement rates | Strong |
| Criminal justice reformers + mental health advocates | Diversion funding, pre-release Medicaid | Moderate |
| Employers + insurers (narrow) | Effective treatment that reduces disability costs | Moderate |
| State governments + CMS | Section 1115 waivers for flexibility (divergent on funding levels) | Moderate |
| Law enforcement + emergency physicians + mental health advocates | Crisis system funding, IMD reform | Strong |
| Housing advocates + mental health advocates | Permanent supportive housing funding | Moderate |

### Conflicting Interests

- **Providers vs. MCOs**: Community mental health providers want higher reimbursement and less administrative burden; MCOs profit from managing (and restricting) utilization. The National Council has directly opposed MCO intermediation in CCBHC payment, while MCOs argue they add value through care coordination and quality management.
- **States vs. Federal government**: States want maximum federal funding with minimum federal oversight; the federal government wants accountability for how funds are used. NASMHPD advocates for state flexibility while congressional appropriators seek performance accountability.
- **Psychiatrists vs. other mental health professionals**: Psychiatrists defend scope-of-practice restrictions and physician-level reimbursement; psychologists, social workers, and counselors seek expanded billing authority and higher rates. The APA has opposed Medicare's 75% payment rate for newly covered MFTs and MHCs, calling it discriminatory, while simultaneously opposing expanding prescriptive authority to psychologists.
- **Institutional vs. community care advocates**: Some advocates for people with SMI (e.g., Treatment Advocacy Center) support IMD exclusion repeal to expand inpatient access; community mental health advocates (e.g., Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law) fear repeal would divert funding back to institutional settings and undermine the *Olmstead* mandate for community integration.
- **Consumer/survivor movement vs. traditional advocacy**: Organizations led by people with lived experience of mental illness (e.g., National Empowerment Center, Hearing Voices Network) sometimes oppose policies supported by family-led organizations (e.g., NAMI) regarding coerced treatment, involuntary hospitalization, and the medical model of mental illness.

### The Iron Triangle of Mental Health Under-Funding

The persistent underfunding of mental health services is sustained by an interlocking set of relationships:

1. **Congressional appropriators** prefer discretionary block grants (which they control annually) over entitlement expansions (which grow automatically). The block grant structure gives appropriators leverage through annual funding decisions.
2. **State officials** prefer federal funding without federal mandates, making block grants politically attractive even though they produce inadequate total funding. The National Governors Association's consistent position against unfunded mandates reinforces this preference.
3. **Insurance industry lobbyists** resist parity enforcement and rate adequacy requirements that would increase private-sector mental health spending, leaving more of the burden on public systems. The 14:1 lobbying spending ratio between the insurance industry and mental health advocates ensures that industry preferences dominate regulatory proceedings.

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on mental health funding policy:

- **Lobbying**: The health insurance industry spent $163 million on federal lobbying in 2022. Mental health advocacy organizations spent approximately $12 million total -- a 14:1 ratio favoring the status quo (OpenSecrets, 2023). At the state level, MCOs such as Centene and UnitedHealth are among the top lobbying spenders in many state capitals.
- **Campaign Contributions**: Mental health-related PACs contributed less than $5 million to federal candidates in the 2022 cycle, compared to $40 million from hospital/nursing home PACs and $80 million from pharmaceutical PACs (OpenSecrets, 2023).
- **Revolving Door**: Former CMS and SAMHSA officials frequently move to managed care organizations and consulting firms that advise states on Medicaid behavioral health contracting. Former congressional health committee staff move to lobbying positions at AHIP, MCOs, and pharmaceutical companies.
- **Public Advocacy**: NAMI's annual "NAMIWalks" events raise $15+ million annually and generate significant media coverage, but this grassroots advocacy has not translated into proportional policy influence. Storytelling by patients and families is the most effective advocacy tool at the individual legislator level but does not counterbalance institutional lobbying.
- **Expertise/Information**: NASMHPD and MACPAC produce influential data and analysis that shapes congressional understanding of mental health funding needs, but their recommendations are often not implemented due to fiscal constraints. The National Council's CCBHC impact reports have been effective in building congressional support.
- **Litigation**: The Bazelon Center, Disability Rights Advocates, and state-level legal organizations use *Olmstead* litigation and ADA enforcement to compel states to fund community mental health services. While litigation has produced important court orders (e.g., Georgia's *Olmstead* settlement, Oregon's Lane v. Brown), enforcement of consent decrees is inconsistent.
