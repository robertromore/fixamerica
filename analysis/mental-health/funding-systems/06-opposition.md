# Mental Health Funding Systems: Opposition to Reform

Proposals to significantly expand or restructure mental health funding face resistance from fiscal conservatives concerned about government spending, insurers and managed care organizations protecting their financial interests, some state officials wary of federal mandates, and ideological opponents of expanded government involvement in health care. While virtually no one opposes "mental health" in the abstract, specific proposals to increase funding, enforce parity, repeal the IMD exclusion, or expand entitlement coverage face organized and effective opposition.

---

## Who Opposes Reform

### Organized Opposition

| Actor | Interest | Resources | Tactics |
|-------|----------|-----------|---------|
| America's Health Insurance Plans (AHIP) | Oppose stronger parity enforcement and rate adequacy mandates | $163 million in federal lobbying (2022, industry-wide) | Lobby against NQTL comparative analysis requirements; fund studies questioning parity compliance costs; challenge 2024 final rule provisions |
| Managed care organizations (Centene, Molina, UHC, Elevance) | Protect Medicaid BH managed care revenue ($18+ billion annually) | Significant state-level lobbying presence; among top lobbying spenders in multiple state capitals | Lobby state Medicaid agencies against direct-to-provider payment models; oppose CCBHC carve-outs from managed care; advocate for MCO-administered CCBHC payment |
| Congressional fiscal hawks (House Budget Committee, Senate deficit hawks) | Oppose new mandatory spending | Budget committee leadership positions; CBO scoring authority | Score legislation, invoke CBO cost estimates, enforce PAYGO and statutory spending caps |
| National Governors Association (conditional) | Oppose unfunded mandates on state Medicaid programs | Collective state government voice; bipartisan credibility | Lobby against federal requirements without federal funding; resist maintenance-of-effort provisions |
| Some pharmaceutical companies | Prefer medication-focused care over investment in psychosocial alternatives | $80+ million in PAC contributions (2022 cycle); extensive direct-to-consumer advertising | Fund research emphasizing pharmacological treatment; lobby against formulary restrictions; support coverage mandates for specific drugs |
| Telecommunications industry (CTIA, USTelecom) | Oppose 988 per-line fees | Significant lobbying presence | Lobby against new telecommunications surcharges; argue 988 should be funded through general appropriations |

### Ideological Opposition

- **Fiscal Conservative**: Objects to the scale of new spending required. CBO estimates for IMD exclusion repeal ($3.4-$5.9 billion/year) and CCBHC nationalization ($10-$15 billion/year) are cited as unaffordable given existing deficits and competing priorities. The Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget and Heritage Foundation have both criticized proposals for new mental health entitlement spending, arguing that they would worsen the long-term fiscal outlook without evidence that increased spending produces proportional improvements in outcomes.
- **Libertarian**: Opposes expanding government's role in health care financing on principle. Argues that government funding distorts the market, creates dependency, and crowds out private charity and innovation. The Cato Institute has published critiques of CCBHC expansion and block grant increases, arguing that deregulation (scope-of-practice liberalization, telehealth expansion, certificate-of-need repeal) would address access barriers more effectively than increased government spending.
- **States' Rights Conservative**: Opposes federal mandates on state Medicaid programs. Views block grants as the appropriate federal role -- providing funding with maximum state flexibility -- and resists entitlement expansions that constrain state discretion. The American Legislative Exchange Council (ALEC) has model legislation promoting block grant approaches and opposing new federal mental health mandates.
- **Free-Market Conservative**: Argues that low reimbursement rates and provider shortages are symptoms of over-regulation, not under-funding. Favors deregulation (expanded scope of practice, reduced licensing requirements, telehealth expansion, certificate-of-need repeal) over increased government spending. The American Enterprise Institute has published proposals for market-based mental health reform that emphasize regulatory relief over new funding.

### Specific Industry Opposition

**Managed Care Organizations and CCBHC Carve-Outs**

The most active current opposition front involves MCO resistance to CCBHC payment models that bypass managed care intermediation. In states where CCBHCs receive Medicaid payment directly from the state (carve-out model), MCOs lose both administrative revenue and control over behavioral health spending. MCO opposition has taken several forms:

- Lobbying state Medicaid agencies to route CCBHC payments through MCOs rather than directly from the state
- Proposing alternative "managed CCBHC" models that preserve MCO intermediation
- Arguing that CCBHCs lack the data infrastructure for cost reporting and that MCOs provide necessary oversight
- In at least three states (Kansas, Pennsylvania, Tennessee), MCO lobbying delayed or modified CCBHC implementation

**Insurance Industry and Parity Enforcement**

AHIP and its member companies have opposed multiple provisions of the 2024 MHPAEA final rule, including:

- Mandatory proactive NQTL comparative analyses (industry argues the requirement is administratively burdensome)
- Network adequacy standards tied to medical/surgical comparisons (industry argues mental health workforce shortages make equal network adequacy impossible)
- Documentation requirements for prior authorization disparities (industry argues clinical criteria are legitimately different for mental health)

## Opposition Arguments and Rebuttals

### Argument 1: The Federal Government Cannot Afford New Mental Health Spending

**Claim**: With federal deficits exceeding $1.5 trillion annually and national debt at $34 trillion (2024), the United States cannot afford to add billions in new mental health mandatory or discretionary spending. Expanding Medicaid entitlements for mental health will worsen the long-term fiscal outlook.

**Evidence Cited**: CBO projects that federal debt held by the public will reach 116% of GDP by 2034. IMD exclusion repeal alone would cost $3.4-$5.9 billion annually. Universal CCBHC expansion could cost $10-$15 billion annually. The Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget estimates that all pending mental health legislation would add $20-$30 billion in annual mandatory spending.

**Reality**: Mental illness already imposes enormous costs on public systems. Untreated mental illness costs the U.S. economy an estimated $282 billion annually in lost productivity, disability, homelessness services, criminal justice involvement, and emergency care (Lancet Commission on Global Mental Health, 2018). Studies consistently show that investment in community mental health services generates a return of $2-$7 for every dollar spent through reduced emergency department use, incarceration, and disability payments (RAND Corporation, 2022). The ASPE evaluation of the CCBHC demonstration found net savings of 18-25% on ED costs and 17-22% on hospitalization costs for enrolled populations.

**Rebuttal**: The question is not whether the nation can afford to invest in mental health but whether it can afford not to. Current spending on downstream consequences (ED boarding at $2.2 billion annually, incarceration of people with SMI at $15+ billion annually, homelessness services at $5+ billion annually) already exceeds the cost of upstream investment. Funding reform should be evaluated as a cost-effectiveness question, not solely as a spending increase.

### Argument 2: Repealing the IMD Exclusion Will Revive Institutionalization

**Claim**: Repealing the IMD exclusion will create a financial incentive for states to rebuild large psychiatric institutions, reversing decades of progress toward community-based care. Federal Medicaid dollars should not subsidize institutional care. The *Olmstead* mandate for community integration would be undermined.

**Evidence Cited**: Historical experience with state hospitals, where 559,000 patients were confined at the system's peak (1955). Civil liberties concerns about involuntary institutionalization. The Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law has argued that IMD repeal would "re-institutionalize" mental health care.

**Reality**: The modern mental health system faces the opposite problem: acute inpatient psychiatric beds have been reduced to approximately 37,000 nationwide -- a rate of 11.7 beds per 100,000 population, the lowest since before 1850 (Treatment Advocacy Center, 2023). The IMD exclusion does not prevent institutionalization; it prevents Medicaid from paying for it. People who need inpatient psychiatric care either receive it in general hospital settings (at higher cost), board in emergency departments for days, cycle through the criminal justice system, or go without treatment entirely. The era of 500-bed state hospitals cannot return because the physical infrastructure has been demolished, the workforce no longer exists, and modern civil commitment standards prevent prolonged involuntary hospitalization.

**Rebuttal**: Targeted IMD exclusion reform -- such as allowing Medicaid payment for short-term (30-60 day) acute stabilization stays with discharge planning requirements -- can expand needed inpatient capacity without creating incentives for long-term institutionalization. Section 1115 waivers in 37 states for SUD and 4 states for SMI have demonstrated this approach without evidence of institutional resurgence. Washington State's SMI waiver, active since 2018, produced a 38% reduction in psychiatric ED boarding with no increase in average length of institutional stay.

### Argument 3: Block Grants Provide Appropriate Flexibility

**Claim**: The Mental Health Block Grant structure appropriately gives states the flexibility to design mental health systems that meet local needs. Converting block grants to entitlements or adding federal mandates would impose a one-size-fits-all approach that ignores state variation. States are "laboratories of democracy" that innovate when given flexibility.

**Evidence Cited**: States have different population demographics, provider availability, and existing infrastructure. The MHBG allows states to allocate funding based on local priorities. Some states have used MHBG flexibility to innovate (e.g., California's first-episode psychosis programs, New York's crisis intervention initiatives).

**Reality**: The block grant structure has produced a 10:1 ratio in per capita state mental health spending (from $47 in Texas to $468 in the District of Columbia, FY2021). This variation reflects political choice rather than clinical need -- Texas has higher rates of mental illness and less insurance coverage than high-spending states. The block grant's fixed funding level means it has declined substantially in inflation-adjusted terms since 1981. When adjusted for population growth and inflation, the MHBG's purchasing power in 2024 is approximately 23% lower than the 1981 ADM block grant it replaced.

**Rebuttal**: Flexibility without adequacy is not a meaningful benefit. The block grant can preserve state flexibility within a reformed structure that establishes minimum per capita funding levels, requires states to meet performance benchmarks, and adjusts federal allocations based on need rather than solely on historical spending patterns. The Canadian model of conditional transfer payments demonstrates that accountability and flexibility are not mutually exclusive.

### Argument 4: Parity Enforcement Will Increase Premiums

**Claim**: Strengthening MHPAEA enforcement -- requiring comparative analyses of non-quantitative treatment limitations, mandating network adequacy standards, and imposing penalties for non-compliance -- will increase health insurance premiums and employer costs. Small businesses will be disproportionately affected.

**Evidence Cited**: Industry-funded studies estimating 1-3% premium increases from full parity compliance. AHIP comments on the 2024 proposed rule arguing that the administrative burden of NQTL comparative analyses alone would cost $1-$2 billion annually. Small business concerns about health care costs.

**Reality**: The original CBO estimate for MHPAEA projected less than a 0.5% premium increase, which has been confirmed by subsequent analysis. The actuarial firm Milliman, in its 2017, 2019, and 2021 parity reports, documented that out-of-network utilization for mental health is 5.4 times higher than for medical/surgical services, indicating that insurers are systematically failing to build adequate networks. The cost of parity non-compliance is borne by patients through higher out-of-pocket costs and by providers through lower reimbursement, not by insurers.

**Rebuttal**: Parity does not require insurers to spend more on mental health -- it requires them to cover mental health on the same terms as physical health. If parity increases premiums, it does so by ending a discriminatory subsidy in which mental health services have been systematically under-reimbursed relative to medical/surgical services. The 2024 MHPAEA final rule's Regulatory Impact Analysis estimated that the incremental compliance costs would be less than 0.1% of total health plan expenditures.

### Argument 5: Government Funding Crowds Out Private Innovation

**Claim**: Expanding government funding for mental health will crowd out private-sector innovation, including digital mental health tools, employer wellness programs, and philanthropic investment. The market is better positioned to develop new delivery models than government bureaucracies.

**Evidence Cited**: Growth in digital mental health startups ($5.5 billion in venture capital investment, 2021). Expansion of employer EAPs and mental health benefits. Telehealth companies (Talkspace, BetterHelp, Cerebral) serving millions of clients.

**Reality**: Digital mental health tools and employer programs serve primarily employed, commercially insured populations. They do not reach the uninsured, Medicaid populations, or people with serious mental illness who account for the majority of mental health costs and need. The populations most affected by funding inadequacy -- people with SMI, those experiencing homelessness, incarcerated individuals -- are precisely those the private market has no incentive to serve. Venture capital investment in mental health has declined from $5.5 billion (2021) to approximately $2.3 billion (2023), as profitability challenges become apparent in an underpriced market.

**Rebuttal**: Public and private investment are complementary, not competing. Public funding serves populations the market cannot serve; private innovation develops new tools that public systems can adopt. The CCBHC model, for example, is a government-funded framework that incorporates technological innovation and value-based payment within a publicly accountable structure. Many CCBHCs use telehealth platforms and digital tools developed by private companies, funded through their prospective payment rate.

## Historical Patterns of Opposition

Opposition to mental health funding reform follows a consistent pattern across decades:

1. **Agree in principle, oppose in practice**: Virtually all political actors express support for "mental health." Opposition emerges when specific funding mechanisms, regulatory requirements, or spending levels are proposed. This pattern was evident in the 2022 Bipartisan Safer Communities Act debate, where broad bipartisan support for "mental health funding" collapsed into narrow agreement on specific programs only after high-profile tragedy created irresistible political pressure.
2. **Invoke fiscal constraints selectively**: The same legislators who support tax cuts, defense spending increases, or other priorities cite fiscal constraints when opposing mental health investment. Mental health consistently loses the competition for scarce discretionary dollars. In FY2024, the entire SAMHSA budget ($9.5 billion) was less than the cost of a single aircraft carrier ($13.3 billion for the USS Gerald R. Ford).
3. **Cite state flexibility to block federal standards**: The preference for state flexibility has been used to oppose minimum funding levels, performance benchmarks, and parity enforcement -- the very mechanisms needed to ensure that flexibility produces adequate care rather than a race to the bottom. The 10:1 ratio in per capita state spending is the direct consequence of "flexibility" without accountability.
4. **Weaponize workforce shortages against funding increases**: Opponents argue that increasing funding is pointless because there are not enough providers to deliver care, ignoring that provider shortages are caused by the same low reimbursement rates that reform proposals would address. This circular argument -- "we cannot increase funding because there are not enough providers, and there are not enough providers because funding is inadequate" -- has been used to block every major mental health funding proposal since the 1980s.

## Counter-Strategies

### Political Strategies

- **Build the bipartisan coalition**: Mental health is one of the few health policy areas with genuine bipartisan support. Framing reforms as addressing the opioid crisis, veterans' mental health, and youth mental health can build broader coalitions than traditional health care reform. The CCBHC authorization bill has had 200+ bipartisan co-sponsors precisely because it is framed as a community health infrastructure investment rather than an entitlement expansion.
- **Leverage law enforcement and criminal justice allies**: Police chiefs, sheriffs, and county prosecutors who deal with the consequences of untreated mental illness are powerful messengers for funding reform. The Major Cities Chiefs Association, the National Sheriffs' Association, and the National District Attorneys Association have all endorsed mental health funding reform.
- **Use CBO scoring strategically**: Present mental health investment as a cost offset against existing spending on ED boarding, incarceration, and disability payments, requesting CBO to score the interaction effects. The CCBHC demonstration data showing 18-25% reductions in ED visits and 17-22% reductions in hospitalizations provide the evidence base for offset scoring.
- **Engage the business community**: Employer costs from untreated mental illness ($210 billion annually) provide a fiscal conservative argument for investment. The National Business Group on Health and the U.S. Chamber of Commerce Foundation have both published reports supporting mental health funding reform.

### Communication Strategies

- **Lead with fiscal responsibility**: Frame mental health funding as an investment that reduces downstream costs, not as a new spending program. The $282 billion annual cost of untreated mental illness (Lancet Commission, 2018) provides a powerful benchmark.
- **Personalize the impact**: Stories of individuals and families navigating the fragmented funding system are more persuasive than aggregate statistics. The most effective advocacy testimony comes from parents of children with SMI, veterans who cannot access VA mental health services, and law enforcement officers who respond to mental health crises.
- **Reframe the IMD exclusion**: Emphasize that no comparable restriction exists for any other medical condition. The exclusion is not a policy choice but an artifact of 1965 fiscal politics that codifies discrimination against people with mental illness.
- **Name the opposition**: Identify the specific financial interests that benefit from the status quo -- MCO profits, insurer parity non-compliance savings, pharmaceutical company revenue -- rather than debating abstract policy principles.

### Policy Design Strategies

- **Use demonstrations before mandates**: The CCBHC demonstration model -- prove effectiveness in a subset of states, then expand -- has been more politically successful than proposing nationwide mandates. Extend this approach to IMD reform (already demonstrated through 1115 waivers) and 988 funding (already demonstrated in 5 states with telecommunications fees).
- **Offer state flexibility within federal floors**: Establish minimum per capita funding levels and performance standards while allowing states discretion in program design. This addresses both conservative concerns about mandates and progressive concerns about adequacy.
- **Phase in costs gradually**: Proposals that phase in over 5-10 years, with escalating federal match rates, face less political resistance than proposals requiring immediate large appropriations. The CCBHC expansion from 67 clinics (2017) to 540+ (2024) demonstrates that phased implementation builds momentum.
- **Create dedicated revenue streams**: The 988 telecommunications fee model, now enacted in 5 states, provides a template for sustainable funding that bypasses the annual appropriations process and insulates mental health funding from discretionary spending competition.
