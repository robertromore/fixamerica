# Mental Health Funding Systems: Current State

## Total Mental Health Spending

The United States spent an estimated $280 billion on mental health and substance use disorder treatment services in 2020, representing approximately 5.5% of total national health expenditures. Mental health spending has grown in nominal terms but has not kept pace with the growing prevalence of mental illness, the expansion of evidence-based treatments, or the rising cost of care delivery. The gap between mental health's share of total health spending (5.5%) and its estimated share of the total disease burden (~13%, per the Lancet Commission on Global Mental Health, 2018) represents a structural underinvestment of approximately $100-$150 billion annually.

## Medicaid: The Largest Payer

Medicaid is the single largest source of funding for mental health services in the United States. In FY2021, Medicaid covered approximately 94 million enrollees, of whom an estimated 13.4 million received at least one mental health service. Medicaid's dominance in mental health financing is even more pronounced for specific populations: it covers approximately 50% of all spending on adults with serious mental illness (SMI) and 60% of spending on children with serious emotional disturbances (SED).

### IMD Exclusion

The Institutions for Mental Disease (IMD) exclusion, codified at 42 U.S.C. Section 1396d(a)(B), prohibits federal Medicaid matching payments for care provided to adults aged 21-64 in psychiatric facilities with more than 16 beds. This exclusion applies only to mental health and substance use disorder facilities, not to general medical hospitals of any size.

## Medicare Mental Health Coverage

Medicare provides mental health coverage for 66.7 million beneficiaries (2023), including those aged 65 and older and younger adults with disabilities. Medicare mental health coverage has improved significantly since the elimination of the discriminatory 50% coinsurance rate in 2014, but significant gaps remain, including the 190-day lifetime limit on psychiatric inpatient care and the low rates of psychiatrist participation.

## Federal Discretionary Programs

### Mental Health Block Grant (MHBG)

The Community Mental Health Services Block Grant, administered by SAMHSA, is the primary federal discretionary funding stream for community mental health services. It provides formula-based grants to states and territories. ## Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinics (CCBHCs)

The CCBHC model, first authorized by the Excellence in Mental Health and Addiction Treatment Act of 2014 (P.L. 113-93, Section 223), represents a shift from fee-for-service to prospective, cost-based payment for community behavioral health services. The model is modeled on the Federally Qualified Health Center (FQHC) framework and requires clinics to provide a comprehensive array of services, accept all patients regardless of ability to pay, and meet quality reporting requirements.

### CCBHC Key Metrics

| Metric | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Number of CCBHCs operating (2024) | 540+ | National Council for Mental Wellbeing, 2024 |
| States with CCBHCs | 46+ | National Council for Mental Wellbeing, 2024 |
| States in Section 2707 Medicaid demonstration | 10 (2024) | CMS, 2024 |
| Average increase in clients served (demo states) | 29% increase in first 2 years | ASPE, 2022 |
| Reduction in ED visits (demo clinics) | 18-25% reduction | ASPE, 2022 |
| Reduction in hospitalization (demo clinics) | 17-22% reduction | ASPE, 2022 |
| Required services | 9 core service categories | 42 U.S.C. 1396a(bbb) |
| Average CCBHC annual revenue | $8-$12 million | National Council, 2023 |
| Congressional co-sponsors for permanent authorization | 200+ (bipartisan) | Congress.gov, 2024 |

### Required CCBHC Services

1. **Crisis services** (24/7, including mobile crisis teams)
2. **Screening, assessment, and diagnosis** (including risk assessment)
3. **Patient-centered treatment planning**
4. **Outpatient mental health and substance use services**
5. **Primary care screening and monitoring**
6. **Targeted case management**
7. **Psychiatric rehabilitation services**
8. **Peer and family support services**
9. **Services for veterans and military families**

### CCBHC Payment Model

CCBHCs receive one of two payment methodologies under the Section 2707 Medicaid demonstration:

- **Prospective Payment System (PPS-1)**: A daily bundled rate based on anticipated per-patient costs, similar to the FQHC PPS. Used in 6 of 10 demonstration states.
- **Prospective Payment System (PPS-2)**: A monthly bundled rate with a quality incentive payment component (up to 10% of total payment tied to quality metrics). Used in 4 of 10 demonstration states.

Both models cover the full cost of delivering all nine required service categories, including non-billable services such as outreach, care coordination, and community engagement that fee-for-service models do not reimburse.

State Mental Health Authorities (SMHAs) collectively spent $44.8 billion in FY2021, of which approximately 64% came from state general funds and 29% from federal sources (primarily Medicaid matching). ### County-Level Funding

In many states, counties bear direct responsibility for funding and delivering mental health services. This creates additional fragmentation and inequality, as county revenue bases vary enormously based on local property values, economic conditions, and political priorities.

- **California**: Counties receive Mental Health Services Act (MHSA/Proposition 63) revenue from a 1% income tax surcharge on incomes over $1 million, generating approximately $3.7 billion annually (FY2023). County allocations vary widely based on population and millionaire density. Los Angeles County receives approximately $800 million; rural counties may receive under $5 million. In 2024, Governor Newsom signed Proposition 1, redirecting a portion of MHSA funds to housing for people with behavioral health conditions (California DHCS, 2023).
- **Ohio**: County Alcohol, Drug Addiction, and Mental Health (ADAMH) boards levy local property taxes to fund services. Per capita spending ranges from $28 to $198 across counties. Franklin County (Columbus) spends $145 per capita; rural Appalachian counties average $42 (Ohio ADAMH Board Association, 2023).
- **Colorado**: Regional behavioral health administrative service organizations coordinate services across counties, with state general fund appropriations supplemented by marijuana tax revenue. The 2022 Behavioral Health Administration Act (SB 22-181) created a unified state BHA, consolidating oversight that was previously split across three departments (Colorado BHA, 2023).
- **New York**: County mental health departments administer state-funded community mental health services through county local services plans. New York City's ThriveNYC initiative invested $250 million annually in mental health services (2015-2023), but evaluations found inconsistent outcome tracking (NYC Comptroller, 2023).

## Recent Trends

- **988 Implementation (2022-present)**: The launch of the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline in July 2022 created new demand for crisis services, with call volume increasing 40% in the first year. Federal funding of $502 million (FY2024) remains below the estimated $3.7 billion annual cost of a comprehensive national crisis system (Meadows Mental Health Policy Institute, 2023). Five states have enacted dedicated 988 telecommunications fees, but most states rely on federal grants and general fund appropriations.
- **Post-Pandemic Funding Cliff**: The American Rescue Plan Act (ARPA) and other pandemic-era legislation provided approximately $5.5 billion in supplemental mental health funding (2020-2023). As these funds expire, many states face significant shortfalls in behavioral health capacity. ARPA's enhanced 85% FMAP for Medicaid mobile crisis services (Section 9813) expires in 2025, threatening sustainability of newly established crisis teams (NASMHPD, 2023).
- **CCBHC Expansion**: The number of CCBHCs grew from 67 demonstration clinics in 8 states (2017) to over 540 in 46 states (2024), driven by SAMHSA expansion grants and growing state adoption of the model through Medicaid state plan amendments (National Council, 2024). Permanent authorization legislation has gained more than 200 bipartisan co-sponsors but has not yet been enacted.
- **Medicaid Unwinding**: The end of the continuous enrollment provision in April 2023 led to an estimated 1.5 million people with mental health conditions losing Medicaid coverage through administrative churning rather than ineligibility (KFF, 2024). States that implemented auto-renewal and ex parte processes retained more behavioral health enrollees than those relying on manual redetermination.
- **2024 MHPAEA Final Rule**: The Departments of Labor, HHS, and Treasury issued a final rule strengthening MHPAEA requirements, mandating that plans conduct and document comparative analyses of NQTLs and demonstrate that the factors and processes used to apply NQTLs to mental health benefits are comparable to those used for medical/surgical benefits. The rule takes effect in phases through 2026.

## Emerging Challenges

- **Workforce-Funding Mismatch**: Even when funding is available, the mental health workforce shortage prevents its effective deployment. Approximately 160 million Americans live in designated Mental Health Professional Shortage Areas (HRSA, 2024). Community mental health centers report that unfilled clinical positions -- not insufficient funding -- are the binding constraint on service capacity.
- **Managed Care Intermediation**: As states shift Medicaid behavioral health into managed care, the additional administrative layer of managed care organizations (MCOs) can reduce the share of funding that reaches direct service providers. MCO administrative overhead for behavioral health ranges from 12% to 18% of total behavioral health spending, compared to 5-7% for Medicaid fee-for-service administration (Milliman, 2022).
- **Measurement and Accountability**: No unified data system tracks mental health spending across all payers, making it impossible to determine whether total investment is adequate or whether funds are reaching the populations and services with the greatest need. SAMHSA's National Expenditure data is published on a 3-5 year lag.
- **Authorization-Appropriation Gap**: Many mental health programs are authorized at levels significantly higher than their actual appropriations. The gap between what Congress authorizes and what it appropriates creates a persistent structural shortfall that affects planning and service delivery.
- **Opioid Settlement Funds**: The $26 billion national opioid settlement (2022) is distributing funds to states and localities over 18 years, but monitoring of how these funds are used for behavioral health purposes is inconsistent, and some jurisdictions are directing settlement funds to general budgets rather than treatment services.
