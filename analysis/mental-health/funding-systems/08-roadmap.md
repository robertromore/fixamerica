# Mental Health Funding Systems: Implementation Roadmap

## Strategic Approach

Reforming mental health funding requires a sequenced strategy that builds political momentum through early bipartisan wins, uses demonstration evidence to justify larger structural changes, and phases in costs gradually to manage fiscal and political resistance. The roadmap is organized in three phases: Phase 1 consolidates existing bipartisan momentum (permanent CCBHC authorization, strengthened parity enforcement, expanded waivers); Phase 2 tackles structural reforms that require new legislation and higher spending (IMD exclusion reform, reimbursement rate adequacy, 988 sustainable funding); Phase 3 addresses the long-term transformation of the funding architecture itself (block grant reform, unified financing, spending parity with burden of disease).

Each phase builds on the preceding one. Phase 1 generates the evidence and political coalitions needed for Phase 2. Phase 2 creates the infrastructure and funding streams that make Phase 3 achievable. Attempting Phase 3 reforms without the groundwork of Phases 1 and 2 would repeat the historical pattern of ambitious structural reform without adequate implementation capacity.

### Sequencing Dependencies

```text
Phase 1: Foundation Building
   CCBHC permanent authorization (enables scaling)
   Parity enforcement (builds compliance data)
   1115 waiver expansion (generates IMD reform evidence)
   MHBG formula study (informs Phase 2 reauthorization)
   Rate adequacy data collection (informs Phase 2 rate floor)
       |
Phase 2: Structural Reform
   IMD exclusion reform (requires waiver evidence from Phase 1)
   Medicaid rate floor (requires rate data from Phase 1)
   988 sustainable funding (requires state fee models from Phase 1)
   Reentry coverage (requires waiver evidence from Phase 1)
   MHBG formula reform (requires study from Phase 1)
       |
Phase 3: System Transformation
   Unified financing architecture (requires Phase 2 infrastructure)
   Spending parity with burden of disease (requires Phase 2 funding streams)
   National data system (requires Phase 2 reporting)
   Geographic equity (requires Phase 2 rate and formula reforms)
```

## Phase 1: Foundation Building (Years 1-2)

### Objectives

- Permanently authorize the CCBHC model as a Medicaid state plan option available in every state
- Strengthen MHPAEA enforcement through administrative and legislative action
- Expand Section 1115 SMI/SED waivers to at least 15 additional states
- Establish the analytical infrastructure for Phase 2 reforms
- Secure 988 telecommunications fee legislation to create a sustainable crisis funding mechanism

### Key Actions

| Action | Lead Actor | Timeline | Resources Needed | Success Metric |
|--------|-----------|----------|------------------|----------------|
| Enact CCBHC permanent authorization legislation | Congress (bipartisan) | Months 1-12 | Existing CBO scoring; bipartisan co-sponsors (200+) | Bill signed into law |
| CMS issues final CCBHC certification criteria and state plan amendment guidance | CMS | Months 6-18 | CMS staff time; stakeholder engagement | Final rule published in Federal Register |
| DOL/HHS issue joint strengthened MHPAEA enforcement guidance requiring proactive NQTL comparative analysis | DOL, HHS | Months 3-12 | Existing regulatory authority; modest staffing increase ($15-$20 million) | Joint guidance published |
| Enact MHPAEA enforcement amendments (civil penalties, private right of action) | Congress | Months 6-18 | CBO scoring; bipartisan support | Bill signed into law |
| CMS expedites review and approval of Section 1115 SMI/SED waiver applications | CMS | Months 1-24 | CMS waiver review staff | 15+ states with approved SMI/SED waivers |
| Enact federal 988 telecommunications fee legislation | Congress, FCC | Months 6-18 | FCC rulemaking capacity; 5-state evidence base | Legislation enacted |
| SAMHSA commissions comprehensive study of MHBG allocation formula equity | SAMHSA, Congress | Months 6-18 | $2-$5 million study contract | Published report with reform recommendations |
| CMS initiates Medicaid mental health rate adequacy data collection | CMS, MACPAC | Months 12-24 | Data collection infrastructure; state participation | Baseline rate adequacy data for all 50 states |
| Federal grants to states for parity enforcement capacity building | DOL, HHS | Months 6-24 | $25-$40 million in enforcement grants | 35+ states with dedicated parity enforcement staff |

### Legislative Requirements

- **Community Behavioral Health Clinic Enhancement and Expansion Act**: Permanent CCBHC authorization with Medicaid state plan option. Bipartisan support exists; bill has had 200+ co-sponsors in recent Congresses. This is the single highest-priority legislative action.
- **MHPAEA Enforcement Act**: Legislation mandating proactive NQTL comparative analysis submission, establishing civil monetary penalties for violations ($10,000/violation/day), and creating a private right of action with fee-shifting.
- **988 Crisis System Sustainable Funding Act**: Authorize federal telecommunications surcharge ($0.50/line/month) dedicated to a 988 Trust Fund.
- **SAMHSA reauthorization**: Include MHBG formula study mandate and authorization for enhanced data collection on mental health spending.

### Expected Outcomes

By the end of Phase 1, the CCBHC model will have permanent statutory authorization, enabling states to adopt prospective payment for community behavioral health without relying on time-limited demonstrations. Parity enforcement will have measurably strengthened, with proactive insurer reporting creating a baseline of compliance data and civil penalties deterring non-compliance. At least 15 additional states will have SMI/SED waivers, generating evidence for Phase 2 statutory IMD reform. The 988 Trust Fund will have begun collecting revenue. The MHBG formula study and Medicaid rate adequacy data collection will establish the analytical foundation for Phase 2 reforms.

## Phase 2: Structural Reform (Years 3-5)

### Objectives

- Reform the IMD exclusion through federal legislation
- Establish a Medicaid mental health reimbursement rate floor with enhanced federal match
- Begin distributing 988 Trust Fund grants for crisis infrastructure
- Mandate Medicaid reentry coverage for behavioral health services
- Begin MHBG formula reform based on Phase 1 study findings

### Key Actions

| Action | Lead Actor | Timeline | Resources Needed | Success Metric |
|--------|-----------|----------|------------------|----------------|
| Enact IMD exclusion reform legislation allowing acute stabilization stays up to 60 days | Congress | Months 24-36 | CBO scoring; evidence from expanded 1115 waivers | Bill signed into law |
| CMS publishes IMD reform implementing regulations | CMS | Months 30-42 | CMS regulatory staff; quality standards development | Final rule published |
| Enact Medicaid MH reimbursement rate floor legislation (80% of Medicare) with 90% FMAP for incremental costs | Congress | Months 24-36 | $4-$6 billion annual federal cost; CBO scoring | Bill signed into law |
| States submit rate adjustment plans and implement rate increases (phased: 74% Yr 3, 77% Yr 4, 80% Yr 5) | States, CMS | Months 36-60 | State plan amendments; rate-setting analysis | 40+ states in compliance by Year 5 |
| 988 Trust Fund begins collecting revenue and distributing grants | SAMHSA, FCC | Months 24-36 | Trust fund administration; state grant applications | Revenue collection begins; first grants distributed |
| States build mobile crisis team and crisis stabilization capacity with 988 grants | States, SAMHSA | Months 30-60 | Workforce recruitment; facility development | 25+ states with comprehensive crisis continua |
| Enact Medicaid reentry coverage mandate for behavioral health | Congress | Months 30-42 | $1.2-$2.0 billion annual federal cost | Bill signed into law |
| Begin MHBG formula reform implementation with "hold harmless" provision | Congress, SAMHSA | Months 48-60 | $500 million-$1 billion additional annual MHBG funding | New formula allocations begin Year 5 |

### Legislative Requirements

- **Psychiatric Care Access Act**: Reform 42 U.S.C. Section 1396d(a)(B) to allow Medicaid payment for acute psychiatric stabilization stays (up to 60 days) in facilities of any size, with mandatory discharge planning, 7-day community follow-up, and quality reporting.
- **Mental Health Rate Adequacy Act**: Establish a federal floor for Medicaid mental health reimbursement at 80% of Medicare rates, with 90% FMAP for incremental rate increases above current state levels.
- **Medicaid Reentry Act**: Amend the Medicaid "inmate exclusion" to permit coverage for behavioral health services during the 30-90 day pre-release period.
- **MHBG Reauthorization**: Reauthorize the Mental Health Block Grant with a need-based allocation formula incorporating prevalence, poverty, uninsured rates, and provider shortage data.

### Expected Outcomes

By the end of Phase 2, the core structural barriers in mental health financing will have been addressed. The IMD exclusion will no longer block Medicaid payment for needed acute psychiatric care. Medicaid reimbursement rates will be adequate to recruit and retain providers. The 988 crisis system will have a sustainable, dedicated funding stream generating $3.0-$3.5 billion annually, and at least 25 states will have comprehensive crisis continua (call centers, mobile teams, stabilization units). Medicaid reentry coverage will improve care continuity for the approximately 2 million people with SMI who cycle through jails annually. The MHBG formula will begin to allocate funds based on need rather than historical spending patterns.

## Phase 3: System Transformation (Years 6-10)

### Objectives

- Consolidate fragmented mental health funding streams into a unified financing architecture
- Achieve mental health spending proportional to burden of disease
- Establish a comprehensive national mental health data system
- Eliminate geographic inequities in mental health access

### Key Actions

| Action | Lead Actor | Timeline | Resources Needed | Success Metric |
|--------|-----------|----------|------------------|----------------|
| Establish Federal Mental Health Financing Commission to design unified funding architecture | Congress, HHS | Months 60-72 | $10-$15 million commission budget; expert participation | Commission report with legislative recommendations |
| Enact legislation consolidating mental health funding streams with unified reporting | Congress | Months 72-96 | Comprehensive reform legislation | Bill signed into law |
| CMS implements unified mental health spending data system | CMS, SAMHSA | Months 72-108 | $50-$100 million IT investment; multi-payer data integration | Data system operational |
| Gradually increase total mental health spending from 5.5% to 8-10% of health expenditures | Congress, CMS, States | Months 60-120 | Incremental annual increases of $10-$15 billion | Measurable increase in spending share |
| States achieve minimum per capita mental health spending standards | States, CMS | Months 84-120 | Maintenance of effort requirements; continued enhanced FMAP | Reduction in interstate spending ratio from 10:1 to 3:1 |
| Implement mental health ring-fencing in Medicaid managed care contracts | CMS, States | Months 72-96 | Contract modification requirements; MCO compliance | MCOs spending minimum percentage on BH |

### Expected Outcomes

By the end of Phase 3, mental health funding will flow through a coherent, accountable architecture rather than eight or more separate streams with conflicting rules. A unified national data system will allow policymakers to track total spending, identify gaps, and measure outcomes across all payers. Per capita mental health spending variation across states will have narrowed from a 10:1 ratio to no more than 3:1. Total mental health spending will approach proportionality with the burden of disease. The comprehensive crisis infrastructure funded through the 988 Trust Fund will operate in every community.

## Governance and Oversight

Implementation requires a dedicated oversight structure:

- **Federal Interagency Mental Health Funding Coordination Council**: Chaired by the HHS Secretary, with representation from CMS, SAMHSA, DOL, VA, DOJ, and HUD. Meets quarterly to coordinate funding streams, resolve conflicts, and track implementation milestones. Established by executive order in Year 1. This council would address the GAO finding (GAO-23-105375) that 13 federal agencies administer 112 mental health programs with "limited coordination."
- **Congressional Mental Health Caucus Reporting**: Annual report to the bipartisan Congressional Mental Health Caucus on implementation progress, spending data, and outcome metrics. Public release of the report ensures transparency and creates political accountability.
- **GAO Biennial Audit**: GAO conducts biennial performance audits of mental health funding reform implementation, with specific focus on whether increased funding is translating into improved access and outcomes. Audit reports would be published and subject to congressional hearing.
- **State Accountability**: States receiving enhanced federal matching or reformed MHBG allocations must submit annual performance reports with standardized metrics on access, quality, and spending. Reports would be published by SAMHSA in a publicly accessible, state-by-state dashboard.
- **988 Trust Fund Oversight Board**: Independent board overseeing 988 Trust Fund revenue collection, grant distribution, and performance monitoring. Board membership includes representatives from crisis service providers, state mental health authorities, suicide prevention experts, and telecommunications industry.

## Risk Mitigation

### Risk 1: Political Will Dissipates After Phase 1

**Likelihood**: Medium. **Impact**: High.

**Mitigation Strategy**: Phase 1 is designed to produce visible, measurable results (more CCBHCs, stronger parity enforcement, reduced ED boarding in waiver states) that sustain political momentum. Building a broad bipartisan coalition early -- including law enforcement, families, providers, employers, and veterans -- creates durable political support that survives election cycles. The CCBHC expansion from 67 to 540+ clinics demonstrates that bipartisan momentum can be sustained.

**Contingency Plan**: If congressional action stalls, maximize administrative actions (CMS waivers, DOL/HHS enforcement guidance, SAMHSA grant priorities) that do not require new legislation. The CCBHC expansion grant program, 1115 waiver authority, and parity enforcement rulemaking all provide administrative pathways.

### Risk 2: States Substitute Federal Dollars for State Spending

**Likelihood**: High. **Impact**: Medium.

**Mitigation Strategy**: Include maintenance-of-effort (MOE) requirements in all federal legislation, requiring states to maintain or increase state mental health spending as a condition of receiving enhanced federal funds. Model on ACA Medicaid MOE provisions. Require SAMHSA to publish state-by-state spending data annually.

**Contingency Plan**: If some states reduce state spending despite MOE provisions, SAMHSA withholds enhanced federal funding from non-compliant states and publishes state-by-state compliance data publicly.

### Risk 3: Workforce Shortages Prevent Effective Deployment of New Funding

**Likelihood**: High. **Impact**: High.

**Mitigation Strategy**: Coordinate funding reform with parallel workforce investments: loan repayment programs ($250,000 for 4-year commitments at community mental health centers), training pipeline expansion (doubling psychiatry residency slots, expanding LCSW and LPC training programs), and competitive salary standards. The Medicaid rate floor (Phase 2) directly addresses the economic driver of workforce shortages by making community mental health salaries competitive.

**Contingency Plan**: Accelerate telehealth expansion, scope-of-practice modernization (particularly for prescriptive authority for psychologists and advanced practice nurses), and international recruitment through J-1 visa waivers for mental health professionals in shortage areas.

### Risk 4: Insurance Industry Litigation Delays Parity Enforcement

**Likelihood**: Medium. **Impact**: Medium.

**Mitigation Strategy**: Draft parity enforcement legislation with specificity that minimizes regulatory discretion subject to challenge. Include explicit statutory standards for NQTL comparability, network adequacy measurement, and penalty structures. The 2024 final rule's detailed implementation guidance provides a strong regulatory foundation.

**Contingency Plan**: Pursue parallel state-level parity enforcement in states with strong insurance regulatory authority while federal litigation proceeds. States such as California, New York, Massachusetts, and Illinois have state parity laws that exceed federal requirements and state enforcement capacity to implement them.

### Risk 5: Fiscal Crisis Triggers Across-the-Board Spending Cuts

**Likelihood**: Medium. **Impact**: High.

**Mitigation Strategy**: Establish mental health funding in mandatory (entitlement) accounts where possible, insulating it from discretionary spending cuts. The 988 telecommunications fee creates dedicated revenue outside appropriations. CCBHC Medicaid state plan payments are mandatory spending. The Medicaid rate floor would be a mandatory requirement.

**Contingency Plan**: Prioritize sustaining the CCBHC model and Medicaid rate floor (which have the strongest evidence base) if overall funding is reduced. Maintain the 988 Trust Fund (which is outside the appropriations process) even if discretionary SAMHSA programs are cut.

## Resource Requirements

### Funding

| Phase | Estimated Annual Cost | Cumulative Investment | Primary Funding Source | Notes |

| Phase 2 (Yrs 3-5) | $12-$18 billion | $36-$54 billion | New mandatory spending (Medicaid rate floor, IMD reform); 988 Trust Fund revenue; enhanced MHBG appropriations | Offset by $5-$10 billion annually in reduced ED, incarceration, and homelessness costs |

### Personnel

- **Phase 1**: 150-200 additional FTEs across CMS (waiver review, CCBHC certification), DOL (parity enforcement), and SAMHSA (formula study, data collection). 50-75 FTEs for state parity enforcement grants.
- **Phase 2**: 300-500 additional FTEs for CMS rate monitoring, 988 Trust Fund administration, state compliance review, and IMD reform implementation.
- **Phase 3**: Federal Mental Health Financing Commission (25-30 commissioners and staff); unified data system development team (50-75 FTEs).

### Infrastructure

- **Data Systems**: Unified mental health spending data platform integrating Medicaid, Medicare, commercial claims, block grant reporting, and state budget data. Estimated cost: $50-$100 million over 5 years.
- **988 Crisis Infrastructure**: Call centers, mobile crisis teams, crisis stabilization units in every community. Funded through 988 Trust Fund ($3.0-$3.5 billion annually at full build-out). Includes an estimated 3,000-5,000 mobile crisis teams and 2,000-3,000 crisis stabilization unit beds nationwide.
- **CCBHC Expansion**: Construction, renovation, and staffing for 2,000-3,000+ CCBHCs nationwide. Primary funding through Medicaid prospective payment (federal-state cost sharing). Estimated capital investment of $5-$10 billion over 10 years for facility development.
