# Mental Health Funding Systems: History

## Historical Development

The financing of mental health care in the United States has been shaped by two centuries of shifting responsibility between levels of government, evolving conceptions of mental illness, and recurring cycles of reform followed by fiscal retrenchment. The story is fundamentally one of cost-shifting -- from states to the federal government and back again -- with people with serious mental illness caught in the gaps between systems designed primarily to manage governmental liability rather than to deliver effective care.

---

### State Asylum Era (1840s-1963): State Governments Bear the Full Cost

- **Dorothea Dix's Crusade**: In the 1840s-1850s, reformer Dorothea Dix campaigned for the establishment of state-funded asylums. Her advocacy led to the creation of state psychiatric hospitals across the country, establishing the principle that state governments were responsible for the care of people with mental illness. Dix personally lobbied over 20 state legislatures and is credited with the founding or expansion of more than 30 state psychiatric institutions.
- **Federal Abdication (1854)**: President Franklin Pierce vetoed the "Bill for the Benefit of the Indigent Insane" in 1854, which would have used federal land sale revenues to fund state asylums. Pierce's veto message argued that the federal government had no constitutional authority to provide charity, establishing a precedent of federal non-involvement in mental health funding that persisted for over a century. The Pierce veto is arguably the most consequential single decision in the history of American mental health financing.
- **State Hospital System Growth**: By 1955, state psychiatric hospitals housed approximately 559,000 patients at their peak, representing a massive state-funded infrastructure. State legislatures funded these institutions through general appropriations, typically spending $3-$5 per patient per day (equivalent to roughly $30-$50 in 2024 dollars). The system employed approximately 500,000 staff across 300+ state hospitals.
- **Chronic Underfunding**: Despite the scale of the system, state hospitals were chronically underfunded, overcrowded, and understaffed. Patient-to-staff ratios routinely exceeded 100:1 in custodial units. Exposees such as Albert Deutsch's *The Shame of the States* (1948), the Life magazine photo essay "Bedlam 1946," and subsequent investigations by journalists and the Joint Commission on Mental Illness and Health created political pressure for alternatives.
- **National Mental Health Act (1946)**: The National Mental Health Act (P.L. 79-487) created the National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH) and authorized federal grants for research, training, and state mental health program development. This represented the first sustained federal involvement in mental health, though at a small scale compared to state spending.
- **Joint Commission on Mental Illness and Health (1955-1961)**: Congress established the Joint Commission to study national mental health needs. Its 1961 report, *Action for Mental Health*, recommended replacing large state hospitals with community-based care centers, laying the intellectual groundwork for deinstitutionalization.

---

### Federal Entry and Deinstitutionalization (1963-1981): The Federal Government Creates New Funding Streams

- **Community Mental Health Act (1963)**: President Kennedy signed the Community Mental Health Centers Construction Act (P.L. 88-164), authorizing $150 million in federal grants for the construction of community mental health centers (CMHCs). This represented the first major federal investment in mental health infrastructure. The original vision called for 2,000 CMHCs serving catchment areas of 75,000-200,000 people each; only approximately 789 were ever built. Kennedy's speech to Congress on February 5, 1963, calling for a "bold new approach" to mental illness, remains the only presidential special message to Congress devoted entirely to mental health.
- **Medicaid and the IMD Exclusion (1965)**: The Social Security Amendments of 1965 (P.L. 89-97) created Medicaid but deliberately excluded federal matching for care in "Institutions for Mental Disease" (IMDs) -- facilities with more than 16 beds primarily engaged in mental health treatment. Congress included the IMD exclusion to prevent states from shifting their existing state hospital costs to the new federal Medicaid program. The 16-bed threshold was drawn from the prevailing definition of a "small facility" in the Public Health Service Act. The exclusion has remained in effect for nearly 60 years and is the longest-standing discriminatory provision in federal health care law.
- **Medicare Discriminatory Copay (1965)**: The original Medicare statute imposed a 50% coinsurance rate for outpatient mental health services, compared to 20% for other medical services. This discriminatory rate was not fully eliminated until 2014, requiring 49 years to achieve outpatient parity within Medicare alone.
- **Supplemental Security Income (1972)**: The creation of SSI (P.L. 92-603) provided a federal income floor for people with disabilities, including mental illness. SSI eligibility also conferred automatic Medicaid eligibility in most states, creating a critical link between disability income and health care coverage. This linkage accelerated deinstitutionalization by making it financially advantageous for states to move patients from state-funded hospitals to community settings where they could access federal SSI and Medicaid dollars.
- **Deinstitutionalization Acceleration**: State hospital populations fell from 559,000 (1955) to approximately 138,000 (1980). The combination of new psychotropic medications (beginning with chlorpromazine in 1954), civil liberties litigation (culminating in *O'Connor v. Donaldson*, 422 U.S. 563, 1975), and the financial incentive to move patients from state-funded hospitals to federally subsidized community settings (Medicaid, SSI) drove rapid depopulation. Many states closed hospitals faster than community alternatives could be built.
- **Unfunded Community Care**: The community mental health infrastructure was never funded at levels sufficient to serve the population discharged from state hospitals. Federal CMHC grants declined in real terms throughout the 1970s. President Nixon impounded CMHC construction funds in 1973. By 1980, NIMH estimated that community services reached only 40% of the population that had been discharged from state hospitals.

---

### Block Grants and Retrenchment (1981-1996): Federal Retreat, State Variation

- **Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1981 (OBRA)**: The Reagan administration converted categorical federal mental health grants into the Alcohol, Drug Abuse, and Mental Health (ADM) Block Grant, cutting funding by 25% and giving states broad discretion over how to spend the reduced allocation. This marked a fundamental shift from federal program design to state flexibility, and from community mental health center construction to general block grant support. The 1981 cuts permanently lowered the baseline for federal discretionary mental health spending.
- **Growth of Homelessness**: The combination of deinstitutionalization, inadequate community services, and the 1981 block grant cuts contributed to a visible increase in homelessness among people with serious mental illness. The Stewart B. McKinney Homeless Assistance Act (1987, P.L. 100-77) created the PATH program for homeless individuals with mental illness, but at funding levels far below the scale of the problem.
- **Mental Health Block Grant Creation (1992)**: The ADAMHA Reorganization Act (P.L. 102-321) separated the ADM Block Grant into two distinct programs: the Community Mental Health Services Block Grant (MHBG) and the Substance Abuse Prevention and Treatment Block Grant (SABG). SAMHSA was created as the administering agency, separating service delivery programs from NIMH's research mission.
- **Medicaid Managed Care Expansion**: Throughout the 1990s, states increasingly moved Medicaid populations into managed care. Behavioral health "carve-outs" became common, with states contracting with specialized managed behavioral health organizations (MBHOs) such as Magellan Health, ValueOptions, and Beacon Health. This introduced a new intermediary layer between public funding and service delivery. By 1999, over half of all Medicaid beneficiaries were in some form of managed care.
- **State Spending Cuts**: The recession of the early 1990s triggered state budget cuts to mental health services. Between 1993 and 1996, at least 31 states cut community mental health funding in real terms (NASMHPD, 1997). Mental health was disproportionately targeted because, unlike K-12 education or Medicaid, it lacked constitutional or federal mandates protecting its funding level.
- **Welfare Reform (1996)**: The Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act (P.L. 104-193) severed the automatic link between welfare receipt and Medicaid eligibility for some populations, creating coverage gaps for people with mental illness who lost welfare benefits.
- **Olmstead v. L.C. (1999)**: The Supreme Court's decision in *Olmstead v. L.C.*, 527 U.S. 581 (1999), held that unnecessary institutionalization of people with disabilities constitutes discrimination under the ADA. This created a legal mandate for community integration but did not provide funding to implement it, setting up a persistent tension between the right to community-based care and the resources needed to deliver it.

---

### Parity and the ACA Era (1996-2020): Coverage Expansion with Persistent Gaps

- **Mental Health Parity Act (1996)**: P.L. 104-204 required group health plans that offered mental health benefits to apply the same annual and lifetime dollar limits as for medical/surgical benefits. The law was narrow: it did not require plans to offer mental health benefits, did not address treatment limits, and did not apply to individual or small-group markets. Nevertheless, it established the principle that discriminatory coverage of mental health was a policy problem requiring legislative remedy.
- **Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act (2008)**: MHPAEA (P.L. 110-343) substantially expanded parity requirements, prohibiting insurers from applying more restrictive financial requirements (copays, deductibles) or treatment limitations (visit limits, prior authorization) to mental health services than to medical/surgical services. The law was enacted as part of the Emergency Economic Stabilization Act of 2008 (the TARP legislation), reflecting the difficulty of passing mental health legislation on its own merits. Enforcement, however, remained weak, with DOL conducting fewer than 200 investigations per year against millions of covered plans.
- **Affordable Care Act (2010)**: The ACA (P.L. 111-148) was the most significant expansion of mental health coverage since Medicaid's creation. It required all individual and small-group plans to include mental health as an "essential health benefit," extended MHPAEA to individual and small-group markets, and expanded Medicaid to 138% of the federal poverty level in participating states. An estimated 2.8 million people with mental illness gained coverage through Medicaid expansion (SAMHSA, 2016). The ACA also authorized health home services for people with chronic conditions including mental illness.
- **Excellence in Mental Health and Addiction Treatment Act (2014)**: P.L. 113-93, Section 223, authorized a demonstration program to establish Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinics (CCBHCs) with prospective, cost-based reimbursement. Eight states were selected for the initial Section 2707 Medicaid demonstration, beginning services in 2017. The CCBHC model was modeled on the FQHC framework that had successfully expanded primary care access.
- **21st Century Cures Act (2016)**: P.L. 114-255 authorized $1 billion in new mental health and substance use funding over two years, including grants for early intervention, crisis services, and criminal justice diversion. It also established the 10% set-aside for first-episode psychosis programs within the MHBG and created the Interdepartmental Serious Mental Illness Coordinating Committee (ISMICC).

---

### Pandemic Era and Post-Pandemic (2020-Present): Surge Funding and Structural Questions

- **CARES Act and American Rescue Plan (2020-2021)**: Pandemic-era legislation provided approximately $5.5 billion in supplemental mental health and substance use funding, including enhanced FMAP for Medicaid community-based mobile crisis services (additional 85% match for 3 years under ARPA Section 9813). The ARPA also provided $3 billion for the Block Grant program (both MHBG and SABG combined) as supplemental funding.
- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (2022)**: The FCC designated 988 as the national suicide and crisis number (effective July 2022), but funding for the underlying crisis response infrastructure remained fragmented and insufficient. The National Suicide Hotline Designation Act of 2020 (P.L. 116-172) authorized the 988 number but did not provide a dedicated funding mechanism for the crisis response system beyond the call centers.
- **Bipartisan Safer Communities Act (2022)**: P.L. 117-159 provided $240 million for CCBHC expansion grants, extended telehealth flexibilities, and added marriage and family therapists and mental health counselors as Medicare-eligible provider types (effective January 2024). It was enacted in response to the Uvalde, Texas school shooting, continuing the pattern of crisis-driven mental health funding.
- **CCBHC Expansion**: The number of CCBHCs grew from 67 demonstration clinics in 8 states (2017) to over 540 in 46 states (2024), representing the most rapid expansion of community behavioral health infrastructure in decades. Two additional states (Michigan and Missouri) were added to the Section 2707 Medicaid demonstration in 2024.
- **Post-Pandemic Funding Cliff**: As supplemental pandemic funding expired, states confronted the challenge of sustaining expanded capacity with pre-pandemic funding levels. A 2023 NASMHPD survey found that 34 states anticipated behavioral health funding shortfalls as ARPA and other supplemental funds expired.
- **2024 MHPAEA Final Rule**: The Departments of Labor, HHS, and Treasury issued a final rule in September 2024 strengthening parity requirements, particularly for NQTLs. The rule requires plans to demonstrate that the factors and processes used to apply NQTLs to mental health benefits are comparable to those used for medical/surgical benefits, and mandates proactive documentation.

## Lessons from History

### Pattern 1: Cost-Shifting Drives Policy More Than Clinical Need

The central dynamic of mental health funding history is the effort by each level of government to shift costs to another. States created asylums to manage populations that communities could not handle. The federal government designed the IMD exclusion to prevent states from offloading institutional costs to Medicaid. States accelerated deinstitutionalization to move patients onto federal SSI and Medicaid rolls. Counties push costs to state systems; states push costs to federal programs. Each maneuver succeeded in moving costs but failed to ensure that adequate care followed the patient. The result is a funding architecture optimized for cost allocation rather than clinical effectiveness.

### Pattern 2: Reform Without Adequate Funding Produces Worse Outcomes

Every major structural reform -- deinstitutionalization, community mental health centers, block grants, parity legislation -- was initially underfunded relative to its ambitions. The Community Mental Health Act promised 2,000 centers; only 789 were built. Parity laws required equal coverage but not adequate coverage. The ACA expanded insurance but not the workforce to deliver care. The 988 system created a crisis number but not a crisis response infrastructure. The lesson is clear: structural reform without commensurate investment creates new categories of unmet need.

### Pattern 3: Crisis Creates Funding Surges That Fade

The pattern of crisis-driven appropriations (post-Sandy Hook, opioid epidemic, COVID-19 pandemic, Uvalde) followed by reversion to baseline funding levels has prevented the development of sustainable mental health infrastructure. Each funding surge expands services temporarily, but the lack of permanent baseline increases means that providers must repeatedly build and dismantle capacity. The 2020-2023 pandemic supplements of $5.5 billion are now expiring, with 34 states anticipating funding shortfalls.

### Pattern 4: Demonstrations Prove Models That Are Never Universalized

The CCBHC demonstration has proven the effectiveness of prospective, cost-based payment for community behavioral health, yet after a decade, permanent authorization has not been enacted. This mirrors the pattern of the original CMHC demonstration (proven effective in demonstration sites but never scaled) and multiple Medicaid waiver programs that generated positive evidence but remained time-limited. The gap between demonstration success and national implementation is a persistent failure of political will.
