# Mental Health Funding Systems: Solutions

Reforming mental health financing requires a multi-faceted approach that aims to increase total investment, rationalize fragmented funding streams, align payment incentives with outcomes, and ensure that funding reaches the populations and services with the greatest need.

---

## Reform Framework

### Guiding Principles

- Public mental health funding should be proportional to the burden of disease, not constrained by historical spending patterns or arbitrary statutory exclusions
- Payment models should reward comprehensive, coordinated, evidence-based care rather than volume of discrete services
- Federal funding should establish minimum adequacy standards while preserving state flexibility in program design
- Financing reform must be coupled with workforce investment to ensure that increased funding can be effectively deployed
- Dedicated, sustainable funding mechanisms are preferable to annual discretionary appropriations that are vulnerable to political cycles
- Accountability systems should measure outcomes (access, quality, cost-effectiveness) rather than merely inputs (dollars spent, services delivered)

### Theory of Change

Fragmented, inadequate funding produces access gaps, workforce shortages, and downstream costs (emergency department use, incarceration, homelessness) that exceed the cost of adequate upstream investment. By consolidating funding streams, raising reimbursement rates to parity with medical care, expanding proven models like CCBHCs, and reforming the IMD exclusion, the system can redirect spending from costly crisis response to effective prevention and treatment. Demonstrated cost savings from pilot programs and international models provide the evidence base for scaling these reforms.

The theory of change relies on three interconnected mechanisms:

1. **Payment reform** (CCBHC prospective payment, Medicaid rate floor) increases provider capacity by making community mental health financially sustainable
2. **Coverage reform** (IMD reform, reentry coverage, parity enforcement) closes gaps that currently leave millions without access
3. **System reform** (block grant restructuring, unified data systems, dedicated funding streams) creates the institutional infrastructure for sustained investment

## Proposed Solutions

### Solution 1: National CCBHC Expansion and Permanent Authorization

**Description**: Make the Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinic (CCBHC) model a permanent Medicaid option available in every state, rather than a time-limited demonstration program. CCBHCs receive prospective, cost-based payment that covers the full cost of delivering nine required service categories, including crisis services, outpatient treatment, primary care screening, and peer support. This model replaces the fee-for-service system that under-reimburses community mental health providers and discourages the provision of non-billable services.

**Evidence Base**: The ASPE evaluation of the initial Section 2707 demonstration (2022) found that CCBHC demonstration clinics increased the number of clients served by 29%, reduced emergency department visits by 18-25%, and reduced inpatient hospitalizations by 17-22% compared to pre-demonstration baselines. A 2023 analysis by the National Council for Mental Wellbeing found that CCBHCs outperformed non-CCBHC community mental health centers on 14 of 17 quality metrics, including engagement in treatment, follow-up after hospitalization, and depression symptom reduction.

**Implementation**: Congress would amend Title XIX of the Social Security Act to create a permanent CCBHC Medicaid state plan option under Section 1905(a). CMS would issue final certification criteria based on the existing demonstration standards, and states would submit state plan amendments. States would choose between PPS-1 (daily rate) and PPS-2 (monthly rate with quality bonus) payment methodologies. Implementation would begin with current demonstration states and expand over 5 years. SAMHSA would continue to provide technical assistance for clinic certification and cost reporting.

**Cost/Resources**: Estimated $10-$15 billion annually when fully implemented nationwide, offset by $3-$5 billion in reduced ED, inpatient, and criminal justice costs (CBO methodology applied to ASPE demonstration findings). The net cost to the federal government would be approximately $7-$10 billion annually.

**Timeline**: Legislation in Year 1; CMS rulemaking in Year 2; state plan amendments in Years 2-3; full implementation by Year 5.

**Challenges**: Managed care organizations may resist CCBHC carve-outs from Medicaid managed care. States must develop cost-reporting infrastructure. Workforce recruitment is needed to staff expanded CCBHCs. Some existing community mental health centers may not meet CCBHC certification criteria and could face competitive disadvantage.

**Impact Metrics**: Number of operational CCBHCs, clients served, ED visit rates among CCBHC clients, inpatient admission rates, 30-day readmission rates, quality metric performance.

### Solution 2: IMD Exclusion Reform

**Description**: Reform the IMD exclusion to allow Medicaid reimbursement for acute psychiatric stabilization stays of up to 60 days in psychiatric facilities of any size, with mandatory discharge planning and community follow-up requirements. This is a targeted reform, not a full repeal: it enables access to needed inpatient care while preserving incentives for community-based treatment.

**Evidence Base**: The four states with Section 1115 SMI/SED waivers (Washington, Idaho, Vermont, Indiana) have demonstrated that allowing short-term IMD stays reduces ED boarding times, improves care transitions, and does not lead to increased long-term institutionalization. Washington State reported a 38% reduction in psychiatric ED boarding in the first year of its waiver (Washington Health Care Authority, 2022). Idaho reported a 27% increase in timely follow-up after psychiatric hospitalization. The 37 states with SUD IMD waivers provide additional evidence of successful short-term institutional care under Medicaid.

**Implementation**: Congress would amend 42 U.S.C. Section 1396d(a)(B) to create an exception for acute psychiatric stabilization stays of up to 60 days. CMS would issue implementing regulations specifying quality standards (including minimum staffing ratios), discharge planning requirements (community follow-up within 7 days), and reporting obligations. States would implement through Medicaid state plan amendments.

**Cost/Resources**: Estimated $3.4-$5.9 billion annually in new federal Medicaid spending (CBO, 2019), offset by reduced ED boarding costs ($2.2 billion annually, ACEP, 2022) and reduced state-only funding for psychiatric inpatient care. Net federal cost estimated at $1.5-$3.5 billion annually.

**Timeline**: Legislation in Year 1; CMS rulemaking in Year 2; state implementation begins Year 3.

**Challenges**: Some community mental health advocates fear diversion of funding to institutional settings. States must ensure adequate discharge planning and community follow-up infrastructure. The Treatment Advocacy Center and the Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law disagree on the appropriate scope of reform.

**Impact Metrics**: Average ED boarding time for psychiatric patients, inpatient psychiatric bed availability, 30-day readmission rates, state spending on uncompensated institutional care, 7-day follow-up rates post-discharge.

### Solution 3: Medicaid Mental Health Reimbursement Rate Adequacy

**Description**: Establish a federal floor for Medicaid mental health reimbursement rates at 80% of Medicare rates, with an enhanced federal match (90% FMAP) for the incremental cost of rate increases above current levels. This addresses the fundamental driver of workforce shortages in public mental health: reimbursement rates that are too low to attract and retain clinicians. The 80% floor would be phased in over 3 years, with annual rate increase requirements of at least 3 percentage points per year.

**Evidence Base**: MACPAC (2023) documented that average Medicaid mental health reimbursement is 72% of Medicare rates, which are themselves below private-sector rates. Research by the Medicaid and CHIP Payment and Access Commission demonstrates a direct relationship between Medicaid reimbursement rates and provider participation: states with higher rates have shorter wait times and more available providers. A 2021 Health Affairs study found that a 10% increase in Medicaid outpatient mental health rates was associated with a 4.7% increase in provider participation and a 12% reduction in average wait times.

**Implementation**: Congress would establish minimum rate requirements in Title XIX. CMS would develop rate adequacy monitoring methodology, building on its existing payment rate transparency requirements. States would submit rate adjustment plans as part of their state plan amendments.

**Cost/Resources**: Estimated $4-$6 billion annually in additional federal Medicaid spending (based on MACPAC rate adequacy modeling). The enhanced 90% FMAP minimizes the state share of the cost increase (states would pay approximately $400-$600 million of the total increase).

**Timeline**: Legislation in Year 1; CMS rate methodology development in Year 2; state compliance in Years 3-5 with a phased implementation schedule.

**Challenges**: States may resist federal rate mandates as unfunded mandates despite the enhanced match. Setting rates too high could increase costs without proportional access improvements if workforce supply is inelastic in the short term. The enhanced FMAP will eventually expire, requiring states to maintain rate increases from state funds.

**Impact Metrics**: Medicaid-to-Medicare rate ratio, number of mental health providers accepting Medicaid, average wait time for mental health appointments, provider turnover rates at community mental health centers.

### Solution 4: Mental Health Block Grant Reform

**Description**: Reform the Mental Health Block Grant to include need-based allocation formulas, minimum per capita spending requirements, and performance accountability standards. The current formula primarily reflects historical spending patterns and population size, not mental health need or treatment gap.

**Evidence Base**: The 10:1 ratio in per capita state mental health spending ($47 in Texas to $468 in the District of Columbia, FY2021) demonstrates that the current allocation formula does not adjust for need. States with the lowest per capita spending tend to have the highest unmet need -- Texas has a higher SMI prevalence rate and a larger treatment gap than the District of Columbia (NRI/NASMHPD, 2023). A reformed formula incorporating prevalence, poverty, uninsured rates, and provider shortage designations would direct more funding to states with greater need.

**Implementation**: Congress would reauthorize the MHBG with a revised formula incorporating mental illness prevalence (NSDUH data), uninsured rates (ACS data), poverty rates (Census data), and provider shortage metrics (HRSA HPSA data). A "hold harmless" provision would ensure no state loses funding in the transition during the first 5 years. Performance requirements would include minimum treatment penetration rates, crisis system capacity standards, and data reporting obligations.

**Cost/Resources**: Estimated $500 million-$1 billion in additional annual MHBG appropriations to fund the formula adjustment without reducing any state's allocation.

**Timeline**: Reauthorization legislation in Year 1; SAMHSA formula revision in Year 2; new allocations beginning Year 3.

**Challenges**: States currently receiving disproportionately high allocations will resist formula changes. Congressional delegations protect existing allocations. The "hold harmless" provision increases the total cost but is politically necessary.

**Impact Metrics**: Variance in per capita MHBG allocations, correlation between MHBG funding and state mental health need indicators, treatment penetration rates in low-spending states.

### Solution 5: Strengthened Parity Enforcement

**Description**: Strengthen Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act enforcement by requiring insurers to submit comparative analyses of non-quantitative treatment limitations (NQTLs) to federal and state regulators proactively (not just upon request), establishing civil monetary penalties for parity violations, and creating a private right of action for individuals to enforce parity in federal court. Build on the 2024 MHPAEA final rule by adding statutory enforcement mechanisms.

**Evidence Base**: The DOL reported in 2023 that of 158 MHPAEA investigations in FY2022, 32 resulted in corrective actions -- indicating widespread non-compliance. The Milliman reports (2017, 2019, 2021) consistently documented that out-of-network utilization for mental health services is 5.4 times higher than for medical/surgical services, indicating that insurer networks are systematically inadequate. The *Wit v. United Behavioral Health* case (N.D. Cal. 2019) found that UBH applied internal coverage criteria more restrictive than generally accepted standards of care.

**Implementation**: Congress would amend ERISA and the Public Health Service Act to mandate proactive NQTL comparative analysis submission (annually, not upon request), establish civil penalties ($10,000 per violation per day), and create a private right of action with fee-shifting to prevailing plaintiffs. DOL and HHS would issue joint enforcement guidance. State insurance departments would receive federal grants to build parity enforcement capacity.

**Cost/Resources**: Minimal direct federal cost (enforcement within existing DOL/HHS budgets with modest staffing increases of 50-75 FTEs and $25-$40 million in state enforcement grants). Industry compliance costs estimated at $500 million-$1 billion annually (which represents the transfer of costs currently borne by patients through higher out-of-pocket spending).

**Timeline**: Legislation in Year 1; joint DOL/HHS rulemaking in Year 2; compliance deadline Year 3.

**Challenges**: Insurance industry will lobby vigorously against mandatory NQTL reporting and civil penalties. ERISA preemption issues may limit state enforcement capacity for employer-sponsored plans. The private right of action may generate litigation that takes years to produce definitive precedent.

**Impact Metrics**: Ratio of out-of-network MH utilization to medical/surgical, prior authorization denial rates for MH vs. medical, number of enforcement actions, average out-of-pocket spending differential.

### Solution 6: 988 Crisis System Sustainable Funding

**Description**: Establish a dedicated, sustainable funding mechanism for the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline and associated crisis response infrastructure through a per-line telecommunications fee (modeled on the existing 911 fee structure), replacing reliance on annual discretionary appropriations.

**Evidence Base**: The Meadows Mental Health Policy Institute (2023) estimated that a comprehensive national crisis system (call centers, mobile crisis teams, crisis stabilization units) would cost approximately $3.7 billion annually. Current federal funding of $502 million (FY2024) covers only call center operations, not the full crisis continuum. The 911 fee model, which generates approximately $3.5 billion annually across all states, demonstrates the viability of telecommunications-based funding. Five states (Washington, Virginia, Colorado, Nevada, California) have enacted 988 telecommunications fees, generating initial evidence of feasibility and revenue.

**Implementation**: Congress would authorize a federal 988 surcharge of $0.50/month per telephone line (wireless and wireline), with revenues dedicated to a 988 Trust Fund administered by SAMHSA. States would be eligible for grants from the Trust Fund to build crisis response infrastructure, including mobile crisis teams, crisis stabilization units, and crisis call center capacity. States with existing 988 fees would receive credit against the federal fee.

**Cost/Resources**: An estimated $3.0-$3.5 billion annually in telecommunications fee revenue, sufficient to fund the full crisis continuum. The fee would cost approximately $6/year per telephone line.

**Timeline**: Legislation in Year 1; FCC rulemaking in Year 2; fee collection begins Year 3.

**Challenges**: Telecommunications industry will oppose new fees. States with existing 988 fees may resist federal preemption. Building mobile crisis team capacity requires workforce development in parallel with funding. Rural areas face particular challenges in staffing 24/7 mobile crisis response.

**Impact Metrics**: 988 call answer rate, average response time for mobile crisis teams, psychiatric ED boarding rates, involuntary commitment rates, crisis stabilization unit bed availability.

### Solution 7: Medicaid Reentry Coverage

**Description**: Require all state Medicaid programs to provide coverage for behavioral health services during the 30-90 days prior to release from incarceration, ensuring continuity of care for people with mental illness transitioning from jails and prisons to community settings.

**Evidence Base**: CMS approved Section 1115 reentry waivers for 14 states as of 2024, with early evidence showing reduced recidivism and improved treatment engagement. A 2022 RAND study found that pre-release Medicaid enrollment reduced 12-month re-arrest rates by 16% for people with SMI. California's reentry waiver, among the most comprehensive, covers medication management, care planning, and community provider connections for 90 days pre-release.

**Implementation**: Congress would amend Title XIX to exclude the "inmate exclusion" for behavioral health services during the pre-release period (30-90 days). CMS would issue implementing guidance. State corrections departments and Medicaid agencies would develop coordination protocols.

**Cost/Resources**: Estimated $1.2-$2.0 billion annually in new federal Medicaid spending, with potential offsets from reduced recidivism (estimated at $1.5-$3.0 billion annually in avoided incarceration costs, per RAND, 2022).

**Timeline**: Legislation in Year 1; CMS guidance in Year 2; state implementation in Years 2-4.

**Challenges**: Some states may resist the mandate without additional federal funding. Coordination between corrections departments and Medicaid agencies is complex. Data sharing between corrections and Medicaid systems requires new infrastructure.

**Impact Metrics**: Pre-release enrollment rates, 30-day post-release treatment engagement, 12-month recidivism rates for enrollees.

## International Models

### United Kingdom: NHS Ring-Fenced Mental Health Funding

**Description**: The UK's National Health Service established a "mental health investment standard" (MHIS) in 2016, requiring each Clinical Commissioning Group (CCG) to increase mental health spending at least as fast as its overall budget increase. Mental health funding is "ring-fenced" -- protected from cuts even when overall health budgets are constrained.

**Results**: Mental health spending in the NHS increased from 11.8% to 14.1% of total health spending between 2016 and 2023 (NHS England, 2023). Access to psychological therapies (IAPT/Talking Therapies) expanded from 900,000 to 1.7 million referrals annually. Average wait times for IAPT treatment decreased from 28 days to 16 days.

**Applicability to U.S.**: The ring-fencing principle could be applied to the Mental Health Block Grant (requiring states to maintain or increase mental health spending as a condition of federal funding) or to Medicaid managed care contracts (requiring MCOs to spend a minimum percentage on behavioral health). The single-payer NHS structure is not directly replicable in the U.S. multi-payer system, but the accountability mechanism is transferable.

### Australia: Better Access Program

**Description**: Australia's Better Access program (2006-present) provides Medicare-funded rebates for up to 20 psychological therapy sessions per year, delivered by psychologists, social workers, or occupational therapists with a GP referral. The program is funded through the national Medicare system with no block grant or state allocation structure.

**Results**: In 2022, 3.5 million Australians (13.5% of the population) accessed Better Access services. Treatment access rates for common mental disorders increased from 37% to 54% between 2007 and 2022 (Australian Institute of Health and Welfare, 2023). The program costs approximately AUD $2.5 billion annually.

**Applicability to U.S.**: The Better Access model demonstrates the effectiveness of entitlement-based (rather than block-grant-based) mental health funding. The U.S. could adapt this approach through Medicare or Medicaid by establishing a defined benefit for a specified number of therapy sessions without prior authorization. The recent addition of MFTs and MHCs to Medicare (effective January 2024) creates a foundation for such expansion.

### Canada: Transfer Payment Accountability

**Description**: Canada's federal government provides mental health funding to provinces through the Canada Health Transfer, with conditions requiring provinces to report on how mental health funds are used. The 2017 federal budget included $5 billion over 10 years dedicated to mental health and addiction, with bilateral agreements requiring each province to report on access, quality, and spending metrics.

**Results**: Provincial mental health spending increased from 5.1% to 7.2% of total health spending between 2017 and 2023 (CIHI, 2023). Wait times for community mental health services decreased by an average of 23% across provinces. Nine of ten provinces met their bilateral agreement reporting requirements.

**Applicability to U.S.**: The Canadian model of conditional transfer payments with accountability metrics mirrors what a reformed MHBG could look like: federal funding with performance requirements and public reporting, but provincial/state flexibility in program design. The bilateral agreement structure provides a template for individualized federal-state mental health funding compacts.

## Pilot Programs and Demonstrations

- **CCBHC Section 2707 Demonstration (2017-present)**: 10 states participating in Medicaid demonstrations showing improved access, reduced ED use, and sustainable cost-based payment. Most successful U.S. pilot in mental health financing.
- **Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinic Expansion Grants (2018-present)**: SAMHSA grants have supported 540+ CCBHCs in 46 states, demonstrating scalability beyond the initial demonstration.
- **California MHSA (Proposition 63)**: Since 2004, a 1% income tax surcharge on incomes over $1 million has generated $3.7 billion annually (FY2023) for county mental health services. The dedicated funding stream has enabled California to expand prevention, early intervention, and community services independent of annual appropriations cycles, though outcome tracking has been inconsistent.
- **Section 1115 SMI/SED Waivers**: Four states (WA, ID, VT, IN) demonstrating IMD exclusion reform through Medicaid waivers, with early evidence of reduced ED boarding and improved care transitions. Washington State's results (38% reduction in ED boarding) are the most robust.
- **State 988 Telecommunications Fees**: Five states (WA, VA, CO, NV, CA) demonstrating dedicated crisis system funding through per-line telecommunications surcharges, providing the revenue evidence for a federal model.
- **ARPA Mobile Crisis Enhanced FMAP**: The 85% federal match for Medicaid mobile crisis services (ARPA Section 9813) demonstrated that enhanced federal matching can rapidly expand crisis infrastructure, with participating states establishing or expanding mobile crisis teams in 2022-2024.

## Sequencing and Prioritization

### Quick Wins (0-2 years)

- Permanent CCBHC authorization legislation (bipartisan support exists; 200+ co-sponsors)
- Strengthened parity enforcement rulemaking (can build on 2024 final rule using existing statutory authority)
- Expansion of Section 1115 SMI/SED waivers to additional states (administrative action, no legislation required)
- Federal 988 telecommunications fee legislation (5-state model provides evidence base)

### Medium-term Reforms (2-5 years)

- IMD exclusion statutory reform (building on expanded 1115 waiver evidence)
- Medicaid reimbursement rate adequacy requirements with enhanced FMAP
- Medicaid reentry coverage mandate (building on 14-state waiver evidence)
- MHPAEA private right of action and civil penalties legislation

### Long-term Transformation (5-10 years)

- Mental Health Block Grant formula reform
- Unified mental health funding architecture (consolidating overlapping streams)
- Mental health spending parity with burden of disease (5.5% of health spending rising to 10%+)
- Federal Mental Health Financing Commission to design comprehensive reform

## Complementary Policies

- **Workforce investment**: Loan repayment, training pipeline expansion, and competitive salaries (see [Workforce Shortage](../workforce-shortage/07-solutions.md))
- **Housing**: Permanent supportive housing for people with SMI (see [Homelessness](../homelessness/07-solutions.md))
- **Criminal justice diversion**: Mental health courts, crisis intervention teams, and pre-arrest diversion programs (see [Criminal Justice](../criminal-justice/07-solutions.md))
- **Insurance parity**: Full implementation and enforcement of MHPAEA (see [Insurance Parity](../insurance-parity/07-solutions.md))
- **Telehealth expansion**: Permanent Medicare and Medicaid telehealth flexibilities for mental health services
- **School-based mental health**: Federal funding for school counselors and integration with community mental health systems
