# Primary Care and Mental Health Integration: Current State

## Integration Models in Practice

The integration of behavioral health into primary care exists along a spectrum, from minimal coordination (referral to external specialists) to full integration (embedded team-based care with shared treatment plans). The SAMHSA-HRSA Center for Integrated Health Solutions defines six levels of integration across three broad categories: coordinated, co-located, and integrated.

### Levels of Integration

| Level | Category | Description | Prevalence (Estimated) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Level 1 | Coordinated | Minimal collaboration; separate systems | ~40% of primary care practices |
| Level 2 | Coordinated | Basic collaboration at a distance | ~25% of primary care practices |
| Level 3 | Co-located | Basic collaboration on-site | ~15% of primary care practices |
| Level 4 | Co-located | Close collaboration on-site, some system integration | ~10% of primary care practices |
| Level 5 | Integrated | Close collaboration approaching integration | ~7% of primary care practices |
| Level 6 | Integrated | Full collaboration in a transformed/merged practice | Fewer than 3% of primary care practices |

Source: SAMHSA-HRSA Framework; prevalence estimates from Psychiatry and Behavioral Health Learning Network, 2023

The distinction between levels matters because clinical outcomes and cost savings are concentrated at the higher end of the spectrum. Level 1-2 practices may screen for behavioral health conditions but lack the infrastructure to act systematically on positive results, often defaulting to referral. Level 3-4 practices co-locate behavioral health providers but may not share treatment plans, use registries, or employ measurement-based care. Only Level 5-6 practices fully implement the team-based, population-health approach that produces the outcomes documented in CoCM trials.

### The Collaborative Care Model (CoCM)

CoCM is the most extensively studied integration model. It requires four core components:

1. **Patient-Centered Team Care**: A primary care provider (PCP), behavioral health care manager (BHCM), and psychiatric consultant work as a team
2. **Population-Based Care**: A patient registry tracks all patients receiving behavioral health services, enabling proactive follow-up
3. **Measurement-Based Treatment-to-Target**: Validated tools (PHQ-9 for depression, GAD-7 for anxiety) are administered at every contact; treatment is adjusted until targets are met
4. **Evidence-Based Treatments**: Both pharmacotherapy and brief psychotherapy (e.g., behavioral activation, problem-solving therapy) are delivered within the primary care setting

| CoCM Metric | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| RCTs demonstrating efficacy | Over 90 | AIMS Center, University of Washington, 2024 |
| Depression remission rate (CoCM) | 45-55% at 12 months | Unutzer et al., *JAMA*, 2002; Archer et al., *Cochrane*, 2012 |
| Depression remission rate (usual care) | 19-25% at 12 months | Unutzer et al., *JAMA*, 2002 |
| Number needed to treat (NNT) for depression improvement | 5.4 | Gilbody et al., *Archives of Internal Medicine*, 2006 |
| Cost-effectiveness | $594 per QALY gained | Simon et al., *Archives of General Psychiatry*, 2001 |
| Practices billing CoCM codes nationally | Fewer than 3% | Psychiatry and Behavioral Health Learning Network, 2023 |
| Average caseload per BHCM for financial sustainability | 150+ active patients | AIMS Center, 2023 |
| Average time to treatment response (CoCM) | 8-10 weeks | Unutzer et al., *JAMA*, 2002 |

### Primary Care Behavioral Health (PCBH) Model

The PCBH model takes a different approach from CoCM. Rather than using a registry and psychiatric consultant for chronic condition management, PCBH embeds a behavioral health consultant (BHC) directly into the primary care workflow for same-day, brief (15-30 minute) functional assessments and interventions. The BHC sees patients through "warm handoffs" from the PCP during the visit, addressing a broad range of behavioral health concerns including adjustment disorders, health behavior change, chronic pain management, and mild-to-moderate depression and anxiety.

| PCBH Metric | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Estimated PCBH practices nationally | 3,000-5,000 | CFHA estimate, 2023 |
| Average BHC visit length | 15-30 minutes | Robinson & Reiter, *Behavioral Consultation and Primary Care*, 2016 |
| Same-day access rate (PCBH model) | 70-85% | Hunter et al., *Integrated Behavioral Health in Primary Care*, 2017 |
| VA primary care clinics with PCBH | Over 900 | Veterans Health Administration, 2023 |
| DoD primary care sites with embedded BHCs | Over 200 | Military Health System, 2023 |

The PCBH model is particularly prevalent in the Veterans Health Administration and Department of Defense settings. The VA has embedded behavioral health consultants in over 900 Patient-Aligned Care Teams (PACTs), making it the largest single implementation of PCBH in the country. However, PCBH has a less extensive randomized trial evidence base than CoCM, particularly for chronic depression and anxiety management. Some experts advocate combining PCBH (for acute needs and warm handoffs) with CoCM (for chronic condition management), creating a hybrid model that addresses both immediate and long-term behavioral health demands.

## Screening Prevalence

### USPSTF Recommendations

The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force has issued screening recommendations directly relevant to primary care integration:

| Screening | Recommendation | Grade | Target Population | Instrument |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Depression | Screen all adults | B | Adults 19+ | PHQ-9 or PHQ-2 |
| Anxiety | Screen all adults under 65 | B | Adults 19-64 | GAD-7 |
| Depression in adolescents | Screen all adolescents 12-18 | B | Ages 12-18 | PHQ-A |
| Substance use (alcohol) | Screen all adults | B | Adults 18+ | AUDIT-C |
| Suicide risk | Insufficient evidence for universal screening | I | General adult population | Columbia Protocol (C-SSRS) |
| Depression in older adults | Included in adult recommendation | B | Adults 65+ | PHQ-9 or Geriatric Depression Scale (GDS) |
| Perinatal depression | Screen during and after pregnancy | B | Pregnant and postpartum persons | Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) or PHQ-9 |

Source: USPSTF, 2016 (depression, reaffirmed 2023); 2023 (anxiety); 2016 (adolescent depression); 2018 (alcohol); 2019 (perinatal depression)

### Screening Instruments in Detail

The PHQ-9 (Patient Health Questionnaire-9) is a 9-item self-report instrument that maps directly to the nine DSM-5 criteria for major depressive disorder. Each item is scored 0-3 (not at all, several days, more than half the days, nearly every day), yielding a total score of 0-27. A score of 10 or above has 88% sensitivity and 88% specificity for major depression in primary care populations (Kroenke et al., *Journal of General Internal Medicine*, 2001). In CoCM, the PHQ-9 is administered at every contact and serves as the primary outcome measure; the treatment target is a score below 5 (remission) or a 50% reduction from baseline (response).

The GAD-7 (Generalized Anxiety Disorder-7) is a 7-item instrument with a similar 0-3 scoring structure, yielding a total of 0-21. A score of 10 or above has 89% sensitivity and 82% specificity for generalized anxiety disorder (Spitzer et al., *Archives of Internal Medicine*, 2006). It also screens effectively for panic disorder, social anxiety disorder, and PTSD, making it a useful transdiagnostic tool.

The AUDIT-C (Alcohol Use Disorders Identification Test-Consumption) is a 3-item screener for unhealthy alcohol use, widely deployed in VA and FQHC settings. Combined screening for depression, anxiety, and alcohol use in a single workflow can be completed in under 5 minutes and captures the most common behavioral health conditions presenting in primary care.

### Screening Implementation Rates

| Setting | Depression Screening Rate | Anxiety Screening Rate | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Large health systems (EHR-integrated) | 50-80% | 20-40% | CommonSpirit Health Report, 2023 |
| FQHCs | 65% (any screening) | 30% (estimated) | HRSA UDS Data, 2023 |
| Private primary care practices | 25-40% | 10-20% | Akincigil & Matthews, *Psychiatric Services*, 2017 (updated estimates) |
| Pediatric primary care | 45% for adolescent depression | N/A | Zuckerbrot et al., *Pediatrics*, 2018 |
| Rural primary care | 15-25% | Under 10% | Rural Health Information Hub, 2023 |
| Veterans Health Administration | Over 90% (annual PHQ-2/PHQ-9) | Over 80% (GAD-2 or equivalent) | VA Office of Mental Health, 2023 |
| Kaiser Permanente systems | 80-90% (depression) | 50-65% (anxiety) | Kaiser Permanente data, 2023 |

The gap between screening rates in large, integrated health systems (such as Kaiser Permanente and the VA) and private primary care practices underscores the role of organizational infrastructure. Systems with EHR-integrated screening prompts, quality measurement accountability, and embedded behavioral health staff achieve screening rates three to four times higher than practices without these supports.

A critical problem is the "screen and abandon" pattern: many practices screen for depression but lack protocols for acting on positive results. A 2022 study found that only 38% of patients screening positive for depression on the PHQ-9 in primary care received any documented follow-up within 30 days (Samples et al., *Psychiatric Services*, 2022). Screening without follow-up is clinically inert at best and potentially harmful if it raises expectations that are not met.

### SAMHSA-HRSA Center for Integrated Health Solutions (CIHS)

CIHS provides technical assistance, training, and resources to support integration in safety-net settings. Key programs include:

- **Primary and Behavioral Health Care Integration (PBHCI) Grants**: Funded 200+ community behavioral health organizations to integrate primary care (2009-2020); demonstrated that "reverse integration" (bringing primary care into behavioral health settings) improved physical health outcomes for people with serious mental illness
- **Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinics (CCBHCs)**: 540 clinics across 46 states (2024), required to provide integrated care with prospective payment. CCBHCs must offer nine types of services including crisis mental health services, outpatient mental health and substance use treatment, primary care screening and monitoring, and peer and family support. The prospective payment system (PPS) pays clinics based on anticipated costs rather than per-service billing, eliminating the financial penalty for providing comprehensive care
- **Behavioral Health Integration Capacity Expansion**: $52 million in new HRSA funding (FY2023) for FQHC behavioral health workforce, supporting hiring of licensed clinical social workers, psychologists, psychiatric nurse practitioners, and care managers

### Workforce Demographics and Pipeline

The behavioral health workforce faces not only a supply shortage but also significant demographic imbalances that affect its capacity to serve diverse populations. Over 80% of psychologists are White, 5% are Hispanic/Latino, and 4% are Black/African American (APA Workforce Survey, 2022). The psychiatrist workforce is older on average than most medical specialties, with approximately 25% over age 65 (AAMC data, 2023). Approximately 1,740 PGY-1 psychiatry residency positions are offered annually through the National Resident Matching Program (NRMP, 2024), which represents a modest increase from a decade ago but falls far short of replacement needs.

The behavioral health care manager role, which is central to CoCM, remains one of the most poorly defined roles in healthcare. There is no standardized credential, no national certification body, and no uniform training pathway. BHCMs are typically drawn from social work, nursing, and counseling backgrounds, with CoCM-specific training provided by the AIMS Center, individual health systems, or SAMHSA-funded programs. This lack of standardization contributes to inconsistent quality and impedes workforce planning.

## Payment and Billing

### CoCM Billing Codes (Medicare)

CMS established three CPT codes for CoCM effective January 1, 2017:

| CPT Code | Description | Time Requirement | 2024 Medicare Payment (Non-Facility) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 99492 | Initial psychiatric collaborative care management, first 70 minutes | First month, 70+ minutes | $162.36 |
| 99493 | Subsequent psychiatric collaborative care management, first 60 minutes | Subsequent months, 60+ minutes | $129.52 |
| 99494 | Initial or subsequent, each additional 30 minutes | Add-on, each additional 30 min | $67.32 |

Source: CMS Physician Fee Schedule, 2024

Key billing mechanics: CoCM codes are billed by the billing practitioner (typically the PCP) for services delivered by the care team. The time threshold includes all care manager activities (patient contact, registry management, caseload review, psychiatric consultation) during the calendar month. The psychiatric consultant's time is included in the billing practitioner's code; the consultant does not bill separately for caseload review. This structure means that the PCP practice bears the cost of employing the care manager and contracting with the psychiatric consultant, then recoups through CoCM billing, creating a cash flow challenge during the ramp-up period when caseloads are building.

## Technology and Innovation

### Telepsychiatry and Teleconsultation

Telehealth has become a critical enabler of integration, particularly for rural and underserved areas:

| Technology Model | Description | Scale | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Project ECHO (Extension for Community Healthcare Outcomes) | Hub-and-spoke telementoring connecting specialists to PCPs | 800+ ECHO programs in 50 states, 45+ countries | Arora et al., *NEJM*, 2011; ECHO Institute, 2024 |
| Massachusetts Child Psychiatry Access Program (MCPAP) | Statewide teleconsultation for PCPs treating children | All MA primary care providers; replicated in 40+ states | Sarvet et al., *Pediatrics*, 2010 |
| Collaborative Care via telehealth | BHCM and psychiatric consultant operate remotely | Adopted by ~30% of CoCM programs post-COVID | Fortney et al., *JAMA Psychiatry*, 2013 |
| Direct-to-patient telepsychiatry | Specialist care delivered via video from primary care site | Widespread post-COVID; coverage parity in 40 states | ATA, 2024 |
| Michigan Child Collaborative Care (MC3) | Statewide child/adolescent psychiatric teleconsultation and CoCM | Serves all MI primary care providers | MC3 program data, 2023 |

### Psychiatric Access Programs (Child and Adult)

The statewide psychiatric access program model, pioneered by MCPAP in Massachusetts in 2004, has become one of the most successful integration strategies for extending psychiatric expertise to primary care. These programs typically operate as call-in consultation services where any PCP in the state can reach a consulting psychiatrist by phone within 30 minutes for real-time clinical guidance on diagnosis, medication, and referral decisions.

As of 2024, over 40 states have child psychiatry access programs, but fewer than 15 states have equivalent programs for adults. The Pediatric Mental Health Care Access Program (PMHCA), funded through HRSA since 2018, has accelerated state-level program development for children and adolescents. However, adult access programs remain far less developed, creating a gap for the largest segment of the primary care population.

| EHR Challenge | Current State | Impact on Integration |
|---|---|---|

| Interoperability | Limited data sharing between BH and primary care systems | Fragments care for co-managed patients |
| Screening workflow integration | Varies widely; some EHRs have built-in PHQ-9/GAD-7, others require manual entry | Affects screening rates and data capture accuracy |

Notable EHR vendor developments: Epic Systems has introduced a behavioral health module with PHQ-9 and GAD-7 screening integration and a basic patient registry function. Oracle Health (formerly Cerner) has partnered with several CoCM implementation sites to develop care manager dashboards. However, both major vendors treat behavioral health integration features as optional add-ons rather than standard functionality, and smaller EHR vendors used by many independent practices have even less integration capability.

### State-Level Leaders

Several states have emerged as integration leaders through deliberate policy and payment action:

- **Washington State**: The first state to mandate Medicaid CoCM coverage (2017). The Washington State Health Care Authority reports a 3.5x increase in CoCM adoption compared to non-coverage states. The state also funds the University of Washington AIMS Center's training and implementation support programs.
- **Massachusetts**: Home to MCPAP, the pioneering child psychiatric access program, and the Massachusetts Behavioral Health Partnership. The state has achieved among the highest screening rates nationally (over 70% in primary care).
- **Minnesota**: The Diamond Initiative (Depression Improvement Across Minnesota, Offering a New Direction) unified six major health plans behind CoCM payment, demonstrating multi-payer feasibility. Results showed 40% depression response rates across participating clinics.
- **Oregon**: The Coordinated Care Organization (CCO) model integrates physical, behavioral, and dental health under a single managed care entity with global budget. Oregon's CCOs have specific behavioral health integration metrics tied to quality incentive payments.
- **New York**: Collaborative Care Medicaid Redesign Team (MRT) initiative has expanded CoCM coverage and training for adult and pediatric primary care practices statewide.

## Recent Trends

### Positive Developments (2019-2024)

- **USPSTF anxiety screening recommendation (2023)**: First-ever universal adult anxiety screening recommendation, creating new demand for primary care-based treatment
- **42 CFR Part 2 alignment (2024)**: SAMHSA final rule aligning substance use disorder records with HIPAA, reducing a major barrier to integrated care
- **CCBHC expansion (2024)**: Bipartisan Excellence in Mental Health and Addiction Treatment Act expanded CCBHCs to 540 clinics in 46 states with dedicated prospective payment
- **Telehealth flexibilities**: Post-COVID telehealth waivers made permanent or extended in most states, enabling remote CoCM delivery
- **Making Care Primary (MCP) model (2024)**: CMS Innovation Center launched 10-year primary care transformation model with explicit behavioral health integration track
- **Bipartisan Safer Communities Act (2022)**: Included $1 billion for CCBHC expansion and school-based mental health services, reflecting bipartisan recognition of the youth mental health crisis
- **CMS physician fee schedule updates**: Incremental increases in CoCM reimbursement rates (3-5% annually since 2020) and clarification of billing requirements have marginally improved the business case

### Persistent Challenges

- **CoCM adoption plateau**: Billing code utilization has grown slowly since 2017 launch, with fewer than 3% of practices billing
- **Workforce pipeline stagnation**: Graduate medical education funding for psychiatry and psychology internships has not kept pace with integration demand
- **Medicaid reimbursement gaps**: Only 22 states reimburse CoCM codes under Medicaid, and rates often fall below cost
- **Measurement infrastructure**: Most practices lack the registries and outcome tracking systems required for measurement-based care
- **Referral completion failure**: Over 50% of patients referred from primary care to specialty behavioral health never complete the referral (Cunningham, *Health Affairs*, 2009), yet the system continues to rely on referral as the default pathway
- **Insurance network fragmentation**: Behavioral health provider networks remain thinner than medical/surgical networks, with 2023 data showing patients are 5x more likely to go out-of-network for behavioral health than for primary care (Milliman, 2023)

## Emerging Challenges

- **Demand surge post-pandemic**: Depression and anxiety prevalence increased 25-30% during COVID-19 (WHO, 2022), straining already limited integration capacity
- **Digital behavioral health proliferation**: The rapid growth of mental health apps and digital therapeutics raises questions about integration with clinical care and evidence standards. Over 10,000 mental health apps are currently available, but fewer than 5% have been evaluated in peer-reviewed research (APA App Evaluation Model, 2023)
- **Consolidation of primary care**: Private equity acquisition of primary care practices may deprioritize behavioral health integration if it reduces short-term revenue. Private equity-backed firms now own or manage approximately 8% of U.S. physician practices (Physicians Advocacy Institute, 2023)
- **AI and clinical decision support**: Emerging AI tools for screening triage and treatment recommendations require governance frameworks within integrated care settings
- **Youth mental health crisis**: The Surgeon General's advisory on youth mental health (2021) has increased pressure on pediatric primary care to deliver behavioral health services, but pediatric CoCM infrastructure is underdeveloped. Pediatric CoCM billing under Medicare is limited because Medicare does not cover most children, and Medicaid pediatric CoCM coverage is even more fragmented than adult coverage
- **Substance use disorder integration**: The opioid and stimulant crises have intensified the need for primary care-based substance use treatment, including buprenorphine prescribing. The 2023 elimination of the DEA X-waiver requirement expanded PCP authority to prescribe buprenorphine, but most practices still lack the behavioral health infrastructure to provide comprehensive SUD treatment
