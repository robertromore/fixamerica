# Primary Care and Mental Health Integration: Stakeholders

The integration of behavioral health into primary care is shaped by the interactions of diverse groups, each with distinct interests, resources, and levels of influence over policy and practice.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Adults with untreated mental illness | 57.8% of adults with mental illness receive no treatment; integration would provide access in the settings where they already seek care | 28.3 million (SAMHSA, NSDUH, 2022) | Accessible, non-stigmatizing treatment in familiar settings |
| Primary care patients with comorbid conditions | Physical-behavioral comorbidity worsens outcomes and increases costs; integrated care addresses both simultaneously | ~60% of primary care patients have behavioral health component (Kathol et al., 2010) | Coordinated care that treats the whole person |
| People in Mental Health Professional Shortage Areas | 160 million Americans live in MHPSAs where specialty behavioral health is unavailable; integration via telehealth is their primary pathway to care | 160 million (HRSA, 2024) | Any access to behavioral health services |
| FQHC patients | 30.5 million patients at safety-net clinics, many low-income and uninsured, who depend on FQHCs as their medical home | 30.5 million (HRSA UDS, 2023) | Integrated services without additional appointments, transportation, or cost |
| Children and adolescents with behavioral health needs | Youth mental health crisis intensifying; pediatric primary care is the most accessible intervention point | 7.7 million youth with treatable mental health conditions (NAMI, 2023) | Developmentally appropriate screening and treatment in pediatric settings |
| Rural residents | Severe workforce shortages make specialty referral impractical; integration and telehealth are the primary viable models | 60 million rural Americans (Census, 2020) | Teleconsultation-enabled access to psychiatric expertise |
| People with serious mental illness (SMI) | Die 10-25 years earlier than general population, primarily from preventable physical conditions; integration addresses the mortality gap | 14.1 million adults with SMI (SAMHSA, 2022) | Coordinated physical and behavioral health to reduce premature mortality |
| Medicaid beneficiaries with mental health conditions | 26% of adult Medicaid beneficiaries have a diagnosed mental health condition; Medicaid coverage policy directly determines their access to integrated care | ~20 million (MACPAC, 2023) | Medicaid CoCM coverage in all 50 states; elimination of carve-out barriers |

### Secondary Stakeholders

- **Family members and caregivers**: An estimated 8.4 million family caregivers support adults with mental illness (NAMI, 2023). Integration reduces their burden by embedding support within routine medical visits. Family caregivers often serve as the primary advocates for treatment engagement and coordination, and they bear significant emotional, financial, and health costs when the system fails to provide integrated care.
- **Employers**: Mental health conditions cost employers an estimated $47.6 billion annually in lost productivity (American Heart Association CEO Roundtable, 2018). The National Business Group on Health reports that depression alone accounts for 400 million lost workdays per year. Integration through employer-sponsored health plans could reduce absenteeism and disability, and large self-insured employers have increasingly expressed interest in CoCM as a benefit design strategy.
- **Criminal justice system**: People with untreated mental illness are disproportionately represented in jails and prisons. Approximately 44% of people in jail and 37% of people in prison have a diagnosed mental health condition (Bureau of Justice Statistics, 2021). Effective primary care-based treatment could reduce justice system involvement by providing upstream intervention before crises occur.
- **Emergency departments**: Behavioral health visits to EDs increased 44% between 2006 and 2014 (HCUP, 2017), and the COVID-19 pandemic accelerated this trend. ED visits for behavioral health conditions cost an average of $2,264 per visit (HCUP, 2020), and many result in discharge without connection to outpatient follow-up. Integration can divert demand upstream into primary care settings where ongoing treatment is available.
- **Schools and school-based health centers**: Schools are increasingly the first point of identification for youth behavioral health needs. School-based health centers provide an integration site for behavioral health services, and school counselors and nurses benefit from primary care integration that offers a pathway for students identified with behavioral health concerns.
- **Tribal communities**: American Indian and Alaska Native populations experience mental illness and substance use disorders at disproportionately high rates, with limited access to specialty behavioral health care. The Indian Health Service (IHS) has begun integrating behavioral health into some primary care sites, but chronic underfunding and sovereignty considerations create unique challenges.

### Disproportionately Affected Populations

Integration gaps create disparities that compound existing health inequities:

| Population | Disparity | Impact | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Black adults | 50% less likely to receive mental health treatment than White adults | Depression underdiagnosis; cultural mistrust of healthcare system | SAMHSA NSDUH, 2022 |
| Hispanic/Latino adults | 33% less likely to receive treatment; language barriers in BH services | Untreated conditions; workforce does not reflect population demographics | SAMHSA NSDUH, 2022 |
| Rural residents | 5.4 BH providers per 100,000 vs. 43.8 urban | Near-total absence of specialty BH; reliance on PCPs without integration support | Rural Health Research Center, 2023 |
| LGBTQ+ youth | 2-3x higher rates of depression and anxiety; 4x higher suicide attempt rate | Many PCPs lack cultural competency; integration provides a non-stigmatizing entry point | Trevor Project, 2023 |
| Older adults (65+) | Depression screening rates lower than working-age adults; higher suicide rates among older men | Medicare CoCM billing available but underutilized in geriatric settings | CDC WISQARS, 2023 |
| Uninsured adults | No access to CoCM billing benefits; FQHC sliding scale is primary pathway | Complete dependence on FQHC integration capacity | HRSA, 2023 |

## Who Has Power

### Decision Makers

| Actor | Role | Authority | Resources | Influence Level |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) | Sets Medicare payment policy; approves state Medicaid plans | CoCM billing codes; Medicaid waivers; Innovation Center models | $1.5 trillion in annual spending (FY2024) | High |
| State Medicaid agencies | Decide whether to cover CoCM codes; set rates; manage carve-outs | State Plan Amendments; managed care contracts; rate-setting | $800+ billion combined state/federal Medicaid (FY2024) | High |
| Health Resources and Services Administration (HRSA) | Funds and oversees FQHCs; administers workforce programs | FQHC grants; NHSC; behavioral health workforce programs | $17.2 billion (FY2024) | High |
| SAMHSA | Administers block grants; certifies CCBHCs; operates CIHS | Community Mental Health Block Grant; CCBHC certification | $7.5 billion (FY2024) | High |
| Congress | Authorizes programs; appropriates funding; sets legislative mandates | Legislation; oversight; appropriations | Legislative authority | High |
| State legislatures | Medicaid policy; professional licensing; insurance regulation | State budgets; scope-of-practice laws; parity enforcement | Varies by state | Medium-High |
| CMS Innovation Center (CMMI) | Tests new payment and delivery models | Waiver authority; alternative payment model testing | $10 billion statutory funding | Medium-High |
| Office of the National Coordinator for Health IT (ONC) | Sets EHR certification standards | Health IT certification criteria; interoperability rules | Regulatory authority over $36 billion HITECH investment legacy | Medium |
| U.S. Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF) | Issues evidence-based screening recommendations | Grade B recommendations trigger ACA first-dollar coverage | Clinical authority; no direct funding | Medium |

### Key Organizations and Institutions

- **American Psychiatric Association (APA)**: The professional organization for psychiatrists, with approximately 38,800 members. APA has been a leading advocate for CoCM and integration, though some members express concern about shifts from direct patient care to consultative roles. APA publishes the Practice Guidelines that inform integration standards and led the multi-year CPT Editorial Panel process that created CoCM billing codes. The APA's budget is approximately $80 million annually, with $4.1 million spent on federal lobbying in 2023 (OpenSecrets).
- **American Academy of Family Physicians (AAFP)**: Represents over 129,000 family physicians and is a primary voice for primary care integration. AAFP has advocated for expanded CoCM payment and workforce training, and has incorporated behavioral health integration into its practice transformation resources. AAFP's lobbying expenditure was $3.2 million in 2023 (OpenSecrets).
- **American College of Physicians (ACP)**: Represents over 161,000 internal medicine physicians and subspecialists. ACP has endorsed behavioral health integration as a core component of primary care practice and has published clinical guidance on depression screening and treatment in internal medicine settings.
- **American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP)**: Represents over 67,000 pediatricians. AAP has been a leading advocate for pediatric behavioral health integration, including support for MCPAP-style psychiatric access programs, universal adolescent depression screening, and school-based integration models. AAP's Mental Health Task Force has published guidelines for pediatric primary care behavioral health.
- **National Association of Community Health Centers (NACHC)**: Represents FQHCs and advocates for expanded funding and integration support in safety-net settings. NACHC is a critical voice on Capitol Hill for health center behavioral health capacity. NACHC's annual Policy & Issues Forum brings hundreds of health center leaders to Washington for direct Congressional advocacy.
- **National Council for Mental Wellbeing**: Represents over 3,400 behavioral health organizations and has been a leading advocate for CCBHCs and integrated care. The National Council provides training, certification, and policy advocacy for integration. It also operates Mental Health First Aid, which has trained over 2.6 million people in the U.S. to recognize and respond to mental health crises.
- **University of Washington AIMS Center**: The research and training hub for CoCM, providing implementation support, clinical tools, and a national consultation network. Not a policy actor per se, but the primary source of CoCM evidence and training infrastructure. The AIMS Center has trained over 10,000 clinicians in CoCM and maintains the most comprehensive repository of CoCM research, tools, and implementation guides.
- **SAMHSA-HRSA Center for Integrated Health Solutions (CIHS)**: The federal technical assistance center for integration, providing resources, frameworks, and consultation to health centers and community behavioral health organizations. CIHS developed the widely used Standard Framework for Levels of Integrated Healthcare and provides free consultation to organizations pursuing integration.
- **Collaborative Family Healthcare Association (CFHA)**: The primary interdisciplinary professional organization dedicated to integrated healthcare. CFHA brings together physicians, psychologists, social workers, nurses, and other professionals working in integrated settings and provides annual conferences, training, and advocacy.

### Influencers

- **Insurance industry**: Managed care organizations (MCOs) and commercial payers control access to coverage for CoCM codes and integration services. Their decisions on coverage, rates, and prior authorization requirements directly shape practice economics. National payers such as UnitedHealthcare (UHG), Anthem (Elevance Health), and Aetna (CVS Health) collectively cover over 100 million enrollees. America's Health Insurance Plans (AHIP), the industry trade association, spent $8.6 million on federal lobbying in 2023 (OpenSecrets). Elevance Health (parent of Anthem and Carelon Behavioral Health) has a particularly complex position as it both sells medical insurance and operates one of the largest managed behavioral health organizations, giving it interests on both sides of the carve-in/carve-out debate.
- **Electronic health record vendors**: Epic, Cerner (now Oracle Health), athenahealth, and other EHR companies control the digital infrastructure of primary care. Their decisions about whether to build integrated behavioral health workflows, registries, and screening tools into standard products significantly affect integration feasibility. Epic holds approximately 35% of the hospital EHR market and an increasing share of the ambulatory market; its product decisions set industry standards.
- **Health system executives**: CEOs and chief medical officers of integrated delivery systems (e.g., Kaiser Permanente, Intermountain Health, Geisinger, CommonSpirit Health) make strategic decisions about investing in integration. Their choices influence adoption in their systems and serve as models for others. Kaiser Permanente, which serves over 12.5 million members in a fully integrated model, is frequently cited as the gold standard for behavioral health integration.
- **Academic medical centers**: Training institutions shape the pipeline of clinicians prepared for integrated practice. Decisions about residency and fellowship curriculum have long-term effects on workforce readiness. Medical schools such as the University of Washington, University of Massachusetts, and University of Colorado have developed model integrated care training programs that other institutions study and replicate.
- **Patient advocacy organizations**: NAMI (National Alliance on Mental Illness, 600+ local affiliates), Mental Health America (200+ affiliates), and the Kennedy Forum advocate for expanded access and integration policy. They bring consumer voice and political pressure. NAMI's annual Congressional testimony and state advocacy networks make it the most influential patient organization in mental health policy.
- **Private equity firms**: Firms such as Optum (UnitedHealth Group), Oak Street Health (CVS Health), and Agilon Health are acquiring or affiliating with primary care practices at an accelerating rate. Their investment decisions, which typically prioritize 3-5 year returns, can either accelerate or impede integration depending on whether they view it as financially attractive.

## Stakeholder Interests and Alignment

### Aligned Interests (Potential Coalition Partners)

| Coalition | Shared Interest | Strength |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| Primary care organizations (AAFP, ACP, AAP) + Patient advocacy (NAMI, MHA) | Expanding behavioral health access through primary care settings | Strong |
| FQHC networks (NACHC) + SAMHSA + HRSA | Safety-net integration with dedicated funding | Strong |
| Behavioral health organizations (National Council) + Primary care | CCBHC expansion with prospective payment for integrated services | Strong |
| Employers + commercial payers | Reducing total cost of care through integration-driven early intervention | Moderate |
| Telehealth advocates + rural health organizations | Technology-enabled integration to overcome geographic barriers | Moderate |
| Academic medical centers + workforce development advocates | Training pipeline reform to produce integration-ready clinicians | Moderate |
| Veterans organizations + VA Health System | Expanding proven VA PCBH model to civilian settings | Moderate |
| State Medicaid directors + CMS Innovation Center | Testing and scaling integrated payment models | Moderate |

### Conflicting Interests

- **Specialty behavioral health vs. primary care integration**: Some specialty behavioral health providers and organizations view integration as redirecting patients and revenue away from specialty settings, even though evidence suggests integration increases rather than decreases appropriate specialty referrals for severe conditions. The American Psychological Association has at times expressed concern about non-psychologist professionals delivering brief behavioral health interventions in primary care, framing it as a scope-of-practice issue.
- **Managed behavioral health organizations vs. carve-in advocates**: MBHOs have a direct financial interest in maintaining separate behavioral health benefit administration. Carving behavioral health into general medical management would reduce or eliminate their role. Carelon Behavioral Health (Elevance), Optum Behavioral Health, and other MBHOs represent billions in annual revenue at stake.
- **Fee-for-service providers vs. value-based payment proponents**: Practices and providers that benefit from high-volume FFS billing may resist population-based payment models that reward outcomes over encounters. High-volume psychiatry practices generating $300,000+ annually in specialty FFS billing face significant revenue disruption from a shift to consultative models.
- **Privacy advocates vs. information-sharing proponents**: Some patient advocacy groups resist the sharing of behavioral health records in integrated settings, citing stigma and potential discrimination, even though information barriers impede coordinated care. The 42 CFR Part 2 reform process exposed this tension, with some substance use disorder advocacy organizations opposing alignment with HIPAA on privacy grounds.
- **Large health systems vs. small/solo practices**: Large health systems have the infrastructure and capital to invest in CoCM, creating competitive advantages. Small and solo practices, which make up approximately 50% of primary care (AMA, 2022), lack these resources and may view integration mandates as disadvantaging them relative to larger competitors.

### The Integration Iron Triangle

The persistence of fragmented behavioral health care is reinforced by an interconnected system of interests:

1. **Insurance structures**: Behavioral health carve-outs channel administrative fees to MBHOs and create separate provider networks, making integration administratively complex. The carve-out industry's $8-12 billion annual revenue creates a powerful constituency for the status quo.
2. **Training institutions**: Medical schools and psychology programs perpetuate separate training tracks that produce clinicians unprepared for team-based integrated practice. These separate training structures have their own accreditation bodies (LCME for medical schools, APA for psychology programs, CSWE for social work), each with entrenched curricula that change slowly.
3. **Payment policy**: FFS payment rewards volume and discourages the population-health management activities central to CoCM. The CMS physician fee schedule, which drives payment across the system, was designed for individual clinician encounters, not team-based care.

Each element reinforces the others: separate training produces clinicians who are comfortable with separate practice; separate payment systems make it economically rational to practice separately; and separate insurance structures make it administratively difficult to integrate even when clinicians and payment models support it.

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on integration policy:

- **Lobbying**: The APA, AAFP, NACHC, and insurance industry maintain active lobbying operations in Washington. The APA spent $4.1 million on federal lobbying in 2023 (OpenSecrets, 2023). AHIP (insurance trade association) spent $8.6 million. The Pharmaceutical Research and Manufacturers of America (PhRMA) spent $29.3 million. Health center advocacy is coordinated through NACHC's annual policy and issues forum, which brings hundreds of health center leaders to Capitol Hill.
- **Campaign Contributions**: Healthcare industry political action committees are among the largest contributors to congressional campaigns. Insurance and pharmaceutical PACs contributed over $80 million in the 2022 election cycle (OpenSecrets, 2023). Behavioral health integration is rarely a direct campaign issue but is influenced by broader health policy positions. Members of the Senate HELP Committee and House Energy and Commerce Committee, which hold jurisdiction over HRSA and SAMHSA authorization, are among the top recipients.
- **Rulemaking Participation**: Federal regulatory processes, including CMS fee schedule rulemaking and SAMHSA regulatory updates, are shaped by public comments from stakeholder organizations. The 2024 42 CFR Part 2 final rule received over 1,000 public comments, heavily influenced by integration advocates. The annual CMS Physician Fee Schedule proposed rule regularly receives comments from APA and AAFP advocating for CoCM rate increases.
- **Public Advocacy**: Patient organizations (NAMI, Mental Health America) conduct awareness campaigns, Congressional education, and grassroots mobilization. The Kennedy Forum's Parity Implementation Coalition monitors parity enforcement and advocates for behavioral health coverage equity. NAMI's annual Congressional briefings on mental health access reach virtually every Congressional office.
- **Expertise and Evidence**: Academic researchers, particularly the AIMS Center at the University of Washington, provide the evidence base that shapes policy. CMS's decision to create CoCM billing codes was directly influenced by the accumulation of over 80 RCTs (at the time) demonstrating CoCM efficacy and cost-effectiveness. The USPSTF's screening recommendations are based on systematic evidence reviews that carry significant clinical and policy weight.
- **Model Programs and Demonstrations**: Organizations like Cherokee Health Systems, Intermountain Healthcare, and Kaiser Permanente serve as proof-of-concept models that policy advocates cite when making the case for integration. Site visits to these organizations by Congressional staff and state Medicaid officials have influenced multiple legislative and regulatory actions.
