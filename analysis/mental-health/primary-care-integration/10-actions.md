# Primary Care and Mental Health Integration: What You Can Do

Closing the gap between evidence-based integrated behavioral health care and what most Americans actually receive requires action at every level, from individuals choosing to engage with screening to clinicians transforming their practice models to advocates demanding policy change. The Collaborative Care Model has been validated in over 90 randomized controlled trials, yet fewer than 3% of primary care practices use it. Bridging this gap is not a technical problem; it is a political, organizational, and cultural challenge that citizen engagement can directly influence.

---

## Individual Actions

### Learn and Stay Informed

- Read the AIMS Center's overview of the Collaborative Care Model at <https://aims.uw.edu/collaborative-care> to understand how integration works and why it matters
- Subscribe to STAT News mental health coverage (<https://www.statnews.com/category/mental-health/>) for reporting on integration policy and implementation
- Follow SAMHSA's behavioral health data releases (<https://www.samhsa.gov/data/>) to track national treatment gap statistics, including the 57.8% of adults with mental illness receiving no treatment (NSDUH, 2022)
- Read the Commonwealth Fund's integration reports (<https://www.commonwealthfund.org>) for state-by-state analysis of screening rates and CoCM adoption
- Review HRSA's Health Professional Shortage Area data (<https://data.hrsa.gov/topics/health-workforce/shortage-areas>) to understand whether your community is in a Mental Health Professional Shortage Area
- Read the Congressional Research Service report on behavioral health integration (CRS Report R47521, 2023) for an authoritative overview of federal policy options

### Make Personal Changes

- **Ask your primary care provider about behavioral health screening**: If your practice does not routinely screen for depression (PHQ-9) and anxiety (GAD-7), ask why. The USPSTF recommends screening all adults for depression and all adults under 65 for anxiety (USPSTF, 2016; 2023). Patients asking about screening signals demand to practices. You can download the PHQ-9 and GAD-7 for free from the AIMS Center website to see what the screening tools look like.
- **Complete screenings when offered**: When your primary care practice offers a PHQ-9 or GAD-7 questionnaire, complete it honestly. Screening is the first step in measurement-based care and can only work if patients participate. If you screen positive, ask about follow-up options.
- **Request integrated care if available**: Ask your insurer or practice whether Collaborative Care Model services are available. If they are, ask to be enrolled. If they are not, this creates a documented demand signal. Specifically ask whether your practice has a behavioral health care manager or behavioral health consultant on staff.
- **Share your experience**: If you have benefited from (or been unable to access) integrated behavioral health in primary care, share your story with your provider, insurer, and elected officials. Personal narratives drive policy change more effectively than statistics alone.
- **Check your insurance coverage**: Contact your health plan and ask specifically whether CPT codes 99492, 99493, and 99494 (CoCM billing codes) are covered benefits. If they are not, file a complaint with your state insurance commissioner citing the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act, which requires parity between behavioral health and medical/surgical benefits.
- **Use available online screening tools**: Mental Health America offers free, confidential online screening at <https://screening.mhanational.org>. If results suggest a behavioral health concern, bring the results to your next primary care appointment and ask about integrated treatment options.

### Use Your Voice

- **Contact your representatives**: Urge your U.S. Senators and Representative to support legislation mandating Medicaid CoCM coverage in all 50 states (currently only 22 states reimburse CoCM codes). Emphasize that you support the Behavioral Health Care Manager Corps to expand the integration workforce.
- **Submit public comments**: When CMS, ONC, or HRSA publish proposed rules affecting behavioral health integration (e.g., EHR certification standards, Medicaid managed care requirements, Medicare physician fee schedule), submit public comments supporting integration mandates. Federal Register notices are posted at <https://www.federalregister.gov>. Comments from individual patients carry significant weight in rulemaking because they demonstrate public interest beyond professional stakeholders.
- **Write letters to the editor**: When local or national media covers the mental health crisis, write letters emphasizing that evidence-based solutions (CoCM, integrated care) already exist but are blocked by payment and policy barriers, not lack of knowledge. Local newspapers and their online platforms remain influential with state legislators.
- **Share information on social media**: Highlight the gap between evidence (over 90 RCTs for CoCM) and practice (fewer than 3% adoption) to build public awareness that the mental health treatment gap is a policy failure, not an inevitability.
- **Attend public hearings**: When your state legislature holds hearings on Medicaid policy, mental health funding, or insurance regulation, attend and testify. State-level action (Medicaid CoCM coverage, scope-of-practice reform, carve-in transitions) is as important as federal legislation.

## Community Actions

### Organize Locally

- **Form a local behavioral health integration advocacy group**: Connect patients, family members, primary care providers, and behavioral health professionals who support integration. Focus on local implementation barriers (Does your FQHC offer integrated behavioral health? Does your state Medicaid program cover CoCM codes?). Meet monthly and develop a specific advocacy agenda tied to your state's policy landscape.
- **Host community forums on mental health access**: Invite primary care providers, behavioral health professionals, and local health system leaders to discuss integration. Feature patient stories alongside data. Partner with NAMI affiliates, which operate in all 50 states and have experience organizing community mental health events.
- **Advocate to your local FQHC board**: FQHCs are governed by community boards that must include patient members (a federal requirement under Section 330). If your FQHC has not achieved Level 5-6 integration, advocate for it at board meetings. Ask about the center's behavioral health staffing levels, screening rates, and integration plans. Request that the center publish its UDS behavioral health data publicly.
- **Partner with faith communities**: Churches, mosques, synagogues, and other faith communities are often the first place people seek help for mental health concerns. Partner with faith leaders to educate congregations about integrated care and reduce stigma around seeking behavioral health treatment in medical settings. The National Alliance on Mental Illness has developed faith-community mental health resources that can be adapted for local use.
- **Engage local media**: Pitch stories to local reporters about the mental health treatment gap in your community. Provide concrete data: How many people in your county live in a Mental Health Professional Shortage Area? How long are wait times for psychiatric care? Does your local FQHC offer integrated behavioral health? Personal stories paired with local data are compelling to journalists and readers.

### Build Coalitions

- **Connect patient advocacy and provider organizations**: NAMI local affiliates, primary care professional societies (local AAFP chapters), and behavioral health provider groups often work in silos. Convene joint meetings to identify shared priorities around integration. A unified voice from patients and providers carries more weight with legislators than either group alone.
- **Engage employer groups**: Employers bear significant costs from untreated mental illness (estimated $47.6 billion annually in lost productivity, AHA CEO Roundtable, 2018). Local chambers of commerce and business coalitions can be powerful allies for integration, particularly for advocating commercial payer coverage of CoCM codes. Present the business case: integrated care reduces absenteeism, disability claims, and turnover.
- **Work with school-based health centers**: Schools are increasingly providing on-site health services. Advocate for integrated behavioral health in school-based health centers, connecting youth mental health crisis response to the primary care system. The school-based health center model is particularly important in communities where children may not have regular primary care access.
- **Engage tribal health organizations**: If your community includes tribal populations, connect with tribal health boards and Indian Health Service facilities. Tribal communities face some of the most severe behavioral health workforce shortages, and telehealth-enabled integration may be the most feasible path to access. Respect tribal sovereignty and self-determination in advocacy approaches.
- **Connect with university training programs**: If your community has a medical school, nursing school, or social work program, advocate for integrated care training rotations at local primary care sites. Trainees who experience integrated care during their education are more likely to practice in integrated settings after graduation.

## Political Actions

### Contact Elected Officials

**When to Contact**:

- During annual federal and state budget processes, when mental health and healthcare funding is determined
- When behavioral health integration legislation is introduced or pending committee action
- When CMS, HRSA, or SAMHSA announce new rules, programs, or grant competitions related to primary care integration
- When your state legislature considers Medicaid reforms, scope-of-practice changes, or behavioral health parity enforcement
- When the Medicare physician fee schedule proposed rule is published (typically July-August each year), which determines CoCM reimbursement rates

**What to Say - Template Email**:

```text
Subject: Support Behavioral Health Integration in Primary Care

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

I am writing as a constituent from [City, State] to urge your support for integrating behavioral health services into primary care. The Collaborative Care Model has been validated in over 90 randomized controlled trials and doubles depression remission rates, yet fewer than 3% of primary care practices use it because of payment, workforce, and policy barriers.

I specifically ask you to support:
1. Federal legislation mandating Medicaid CoCM coverage in all 50 states (only 22 states currently reimburse these services)
2. Funding for a Behavioral Health Care Manager Corps to train and deploy 10,000 new integrated care workers
3. Enhanced funding for FQHCs to achieve full behavioral health integration
4. EHR standards requiring behavioral health integration functionality
5. Expansion of statewide psychiatric access programs to all 50 states for adult and child populations

More than 57% of adults with mental illness receive no treatment (SAMHSA, 2022). Primary care integration is the most evidence-based strategy for closing this gap, but it requires legislative action to overcome structural barriers.

[Optional: personal story about your experience with mental health care access]

Thank you for your service and attention to this issue.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your City, State]
```

**What to Say - Phone Call Script**:

```text
"Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [City]. I'm calling to ask [Representative/Senator] [Name] to support behavioral health integration in primary care. Specifically, I'd like them to co-sponsor legislation mandating Medicaid coverage of the Collaborative Care Model in all 50 states and funding for a Behavioral Health Care Manager workforce. Fewer than 3% of primary care practices use evidence-based integration despite over 90 clinical trials proving it works. Can you please note my position? Thank you."
```

**What to Say - State Legislator Script** (for Medicaid CoCM coverage in non-coverage states):

```text
"Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [District]. I'm calling to ask [Representative/Senator] [Name] to support Medicaid coverage of Collaborative Care Model billing codes in [State]. Currently, [State] is one of 28 states that does not reimburse CoCM under Medicaid, meaning that the most evidence-based model for treating depression and anxiety in primary care is unavailable to our Medicaid population. States like Washington have seen a 3.5x increase in integration adoption after adding CoCM coverage. I urge [State] to follow their lead. Thank you."
```

### Support Legislation

Current or proposed legislation and programs to support:

- **Bipartisan Excellence in Mental Health and Addiction Treatment Act (Expansion)**: Expands CCBHCs nationally with prospective payment. Advocate for reauthorization and full national rollout to all states.
- **Primary Care Behavioral Health Integration Act** (proposed): Would mandate Medicaid CoCM coverage in all states and fund the BHCM Corps. Contact your member of Congress to request introduction or co-sponsorship.
- **Making Care Primary (MCP) Model**: CMS Innovation Center 10-year primary care transformation model includes behavioral health integration track. Support through public comments when CMS solicits feedback.
- **Mental Health Parity enforcement legislation**: Strengthened parity enforcement ensures that integrated services are covered without discriminatory limitations. Support bills adding transparency and reporting requirements to MHPAEA.
- **Pediatric Mental Health Care Access Program reauthorization**: HRSA program funding child and adolescent psychiatric access programs in 40+ states. Advocate for increased funding and expansion to include adult programs.
- **State-level CoCM Medicaid coverage**: If your state is among the 28 that do not reimburse CoCM codes under Medicaid, contact your state legislators and Medicaid director to advocate for coverage. Provide the evidence from Washington State (3.5x adoption increase) and the Milliman analysis of CoCM cost-effectiveness.

### Electoral Engagement

**Questions to Ask Candidates**:

1. "Do you support requiring all state Medicaid programs to cover the Collaborative Care Model, which has been proven in over 90 clinical trials to improve mental health outcomes in primary care?"
2. "What is your plan to address the behavioral health workforce shortage? Do you support federal funding to train and deploy behavioral health care managers in underserved communities?"
3. "Would you support requiring health insurance companies to cover integrated behavioral health in primary care without prior authorization barriers?"
4. "How would you ensure that FQHCs, which serve 30.5 million patients, have the resources to provide fully integrated behavioral health care?"
5. "Do you support expanding statewide psychiatric access programs so that every primary care provider in every state has access to same-day psychiatric consultation for their patients?"

**Voting Considerations**:

- Look for candidates who distinguish between mental health rhetoric (acknowledging the crisis) and mental health policy (specific proposals for integration, workforce, and payment reform). Many candidates express concern about mental health without committing to the structural reforms needed for integration.
- Key committee assignments that matter: Senate HELP Committee, Senate Finance Committee, House Energy and Commerce Committee, House Ways and Means Committee, and state health committee equivalents. Members on these committees have the most direct influence on integration legislation.
- Evaluate candidates on their record with the National Council for Mental Wellbeing and NAMI legislative scorecards
- Check whether candidates have supported or opposed Medicaid expansion in your state, which directly affects the population eligible for integrated behavioral health coverage

## Professional Actions

### For Primary Care Providers

- Explore CoCM implementation through the AIMS Center (<https://aims.uw.edu>) training programs, which offer both in-person and online formats
- Advocate within your practice or health system for hiring a behavioral health care manager. The AIMS Center provides business case tools to demonstrate financial viability at <https://aims.uw.edu/collaborative-care/business-case>
- Bill CoCM codes (CPT 99492-99494) if you deliver integrated services; increased billing creates the utilization data that drives payer adoption. Even if initial volumes are small, billing demonstrates demand.
- Implement universal PHQ-9 and GAD-7 screening using EHR-integrated templates. If your EHR does not support automated screening, advocate to your vendor and use paper forms in the interim.
- Join a Project ECHO behavioral health hub for ongoing telementoring in psychiatric decision-making. Find hubs at <https://hsc.unm.edu/echo/>
- If you prescribe buprenorphine (no longer requiring X-waiver as of 2023), integrate SUD treatment into your behavioral health workflow

### For Behavioral Health Professionals

- Seek training in primary care integration settings through CFHA, AIMS Center, or SAMHSA-funded programs
- Consider transitioning to or adding a behavioral health care manager role. The AIMS Center's BHCM training provides the most widely recognized preparation for this role.
- Join the Collaborative Family Healthcare Association (CFHA) at <https://www.cfha.net> for professional development in integrated care, networking with integration leaders, and annual conference attendance
- If you are a psychiatrist, explore the CoCM consulting psychiatrist role, which allows you to support 150-200 patients at 0.1-0.2 FTE while maintaining your existing practice. Contact the AIMS Center for consultative role training.
- If you are a psychologist or licensed clinical social worker, explore the behavioral health consultant (BHC) role in the PCBH model, which provides same-day, brief interventions in primary care settings
- Advocate within your professional associations (APA, NASW, NBCC) for integration-focused training standards and certification pathways

### For Health System Administrators

- Implement CoCM in at least one pilot site; use Intermountain Healthcare (46% depression remission, $2,800/patient savings) and Cherokee Health Systems (50%+ remission, 20% cost reduction) as implementation models
- Calculate the ROI of integration using the AIMS Center's business case tools. Present the business case to your CFO using practice-specific patient mix and payer data.
- Advocate to your EHR vendor for behavioral health integration features, including registry functionality, screening templates, and CoCM billing automation. Vendor responsiveness increases when multiple health systems make the same request.
- Explore CCBHC certification for behavioral health facilities within your system. The prospective payment model eliminates the per-visit financial penalties of integration.
- Track and report integration outcomes (PHQ-9 remission rates, screening rates, follow-up rates) to demonstrate value to payers and board members

### For Educators and Training Program Directors

- Embed integrated care training in medical school, residency, and behavioral health graduate curricula. The University of Washington's family medicine residency integration curriculum is available as a model.
- Apply for HRSA Title VII and Graduate Psychology Education grants for integration-focused training programs
- Develop clinical practicum sites in integrated primary care settings, including FQHCs, health system primary care clinics, and VA facilities
- Create interprofessional training experiences where medical, social work, psychology, and nursing students learn together in integrated care settings
- Include the AIMS Center's CoCM training modules in residency curriculum and track graduate practice patterns to evaluate whether training affects career integration practice

### For Insurance and Managed Care Executives

- Add CoCM codes (99492, 99493, 99494) to your covered benefits without prior authorization. Prior authorization for CoCM is counterproductive because the model depends on population-based enrollment, not per-patient approval.
- Develop value-based contracts that reward behavioral health integration outcomes (depression remission rates, screening rates) rather than visit volume
- Engage with the Diamond Initiative model (Minnesota) for multi-payer CoCM collaboration. Multi-payer alignment reduces practice administrative burden and accelerates adoption.
- Evaluate your behavioral health network adequacy in primary care settings specifically, not just specialty settings. Integration requires behavioral health providers who work in or with primary care practices.
- If your organization operates a behavioral health carve-out, begin planning for carve-in transition. States offering enhanced FMAP for carve-in transitions create a financial incentive for managed care organizations to propose carved-in models.

### For Employers and HR Professionals

- Ask your health plan whether it covers CoCM services (CPT 99492-99494). If not, request that these codes be added to covered benefits during your next plan renewal.
- Advocate for behavioral health integration as part of your employee health benefits. Use the $47.6 billion annual employer cost figure (AHA CEO Roundtable, 2018) to make the business case to your leadership.
- Track the impact of mental health coverage on absenteeism, disability claims, and turnover. This data supports continued investment in integrated behavioral health coverage.
- Consider Employee Assistance Program (EAP) models that connect to primary care integration rather than operating as standalone referral services
- Join the National Business Group on Health's mental health initiative to learn from other employers investing in integrated behavioral health

## Advocacy Toolkit

### Key Talking Points

When discussing primary care and mental health integration with others, emphasize:

1. **The evidence is overwhelming**: The Collaborative Care Model has been proven in over 90 randomized controlled trials to double depression remission rates compared to usual care, yet fewer than 3% of primary care practices use it (AIMS Center, 2024; Psychiatry and Behavioral Health Learning Network, 2023).
2. **The treatment gap is a policy failure, not an inevitability**: 57.8% of adults with mental illness (28.3 million people) receive no treatment (SAMHSA, 2022). The solutions exist; what is missing is the payment, workforce, and policy infrastructure to deliver them.
3. **Integration saves money**: CoCM has been shown to save $3,363 per patient over four years in reduced total medical costs (Unutzer et al., 2008). Untreated mental illness costs the U.S. economy an estimated $280 billion annually (Insel, 2022). CCBHC evaluation data shows Medicaid savings of $200-$600 per beneficiary annually.
4. **Rural and underserved communities need this most**: 160 million Americans live in Mental Health Professional Shortage Areas (HRSA, 2024). Telehealth-enabled integration through psychiatric access programs and Project ECHO is the most viable pathway to behavioral health access for rural communities.
5. **Payment reform is the key**: Only 22 states reimburse CoCM under Medicaid, and fewer than 40% of commercial payers cover CoCM codes. Without payment, practices cannot sustain integration regardless of the evidence.
6. **The referral model fails most patients**: Over 50% of patients referred from primary care to specialty behavioral health never complete the referral (Cunningham, *Health Affairs*, 2009). Integration eliminates this handoff failure by providing treatment where patients already receive care.

### Common Misconceptions to Counter

| Misconception | Reality | Source |
|---------------|---------|--------|
| "Integrating mental health into primary care will lower quality of care" | CoCM delivers care through structured teams with psychiatric oversight and measurement-based protocols, producing outcomes superior to traditional specialty referral for common conditions | Archer et al., *Cochrane Review*, 2012 (79 RCTs) |
| "Primary care providers don't have time for mental health" | CoCM uses embedded care managers to handle the time-intensive work; the PCP's role is to prescribe and adjust treatment based on team recommendations | AIMS Center CoCM implementation guide |
| "Integration costs too much" | CoCM saves $3,363 per patient over 4 years in total medical costs; untreated mental illness costs $280 billion/year in lost productivity and excess spending | Unutzer et al., 2008; Insel, 2022 |
| "People with mental health needs should see specialists" | Over 50% of patients referred to specialty behavioral health never complete the referral; integration eliminates this handoff failure | Cunningham, *Health Affairs*, 2009 |
| "The workforce doesn't exist to do this" | The workforce shortage is real but addressable through the BHCM Corps, telehealth-enabled consultation, and Project ECHO capacity building; the current shortage is perpetuated by the lack of infrastructure, not inherent limits | HRSA workforce data, 2024 |
| "My insurance covers mental health already" | Coverage on paper often does not mean access in practice; patients are 5x more likely to go out-of-network for behavioral health than primary care, and many behavioral health providers do not accept insurance | Milliman, 2023 |
