# Primary Care and Mental Health Integration: Root Causes

## Systemic Analysis

The current challenges in primary care and mental health integration are not the result of a single failure but of several long-term, interlocking trends. Despite over 90 randomized controlled trials demonstrating that the Collaborative Care Model (CoCM) improves outcomes and saves money, fewer than 3% of primary care practices use it. This gap between evidence and implementation is driven by fragmented payment systems, workforce shortages, siloed professional training, inadequate technology infrastructure, and a medical culture that has historically treated mental health as separate from and subordinate to physical health.

Understanding these root causes is essential because they are mutually reinforcing. Addressing any single cause in isolation produces limited results, as the remaining barriers compensate for and neutralize the reform. This explains why 20 years of accumulated RCT evidence, the creation of CoCM billing codes, and multiple federal demonstration programs have collectively failed to move adoption beyond 3%. Effective reform must address the system of causes, not individual causes.

## Payment System Fragmentation

### Fee-for-Service Misalignment

The dominant fee-for-service (FFS) payment model in American healthcare is structurally incompatible with collaborative care. CoCM depends on population-based caseload management, proactive registry-based follow-up, and team-based care coordination, none of which are well-compensated under FFS. While Medicare established CoCM billing codes (CPT 99492-99494) in 2017, these codes remain underutilized because they require practices to invest in infrastructure (registries, care managers, consulting relationships) before they can generate revenue.

The FFS misalignment operates at multiple levels. First, FFS rewards face-to-face encounters, but much of CoCM's value is delivered through non-face-to-face activities: care manager phone calls, registry reviews, caseload discussions with psychiatric consultants, and treatment plan adjustments. Second, FFS incentivizes volume rather than outcomes; a practice that sees 30 patients per day for brief medication checks generates more revenue than one that manages a caseload of 150 patients with proactive follow-up and measurement-based treatment adjustment. Third, FFS creates no mechanism for shared savings: when CoCM reduces emergency department visits and hospitalizations, those savings accrue to insurers and hospitals, not to the primary care practice that invested in the integration infrastructure.

**Evidence**: A 2023 analysis by the American Psychiatric Association found that practices billing CoCM codes required an average 6-12 month ramp-up period before reaching financial sustainability, during which start-up costs averaged $150,000-$250,000 per practice (APA, 2023). Fewer than 3% of primary care practices bill CoCM codes nationally (Psychiatry and Behavioral Health Learning Network, 2023). A 2022 analysis by the AIMS Center found that practices in fee-for-service environments took 40% longer to reach financial sustainability than practices operating under value-based contracts (AIMS Center, 2022).

### Behavioral Health Carve-Outs

Many Medicaid and commercial insurance plans contract with separate managed behavioral health organizations (MBHOs) to administer mental health benefits. These carve-outs create administrative barriers to integration by routing mental health claims through separate systems with different credentialing requirements, prior authorization processes, and provider networks. When a primary care practice attempts to deliver integrated behavioral health, it often must bill two separate entities for services delivered by the same team, in the same visit.

Carve-outs also create perverse data isolation: the MBHO has behavioral health utilization data but not medical claims data, and the medical plan has the reverse. Neither entity has the complete picture needed to evaluate whether integrated care is reducing total cost. This data fragmentation prevents the analytic work that would demonstrate integration's value and justify investment.

The carve-out industry is substantial. Major MBHOs including Magellan Health (acquired by Centene in 2022), Beacon Health Options (now part of Carelon Behavioral Health/Elevance Health), and Optum Behavioral Health collectively manage behavioral health benefits for tens of millions of Americans. Their business model depends on the administrative separation of behavioral health from medical care; full integration would eliminate their role.

**Evidence**: As of 2023, approximately 30% of Medicaid beneficiaries were enrolled in plans with behavioral health carve-outs (Medicaid and CHIP Payment and Access Commission, 2023). States that have eliminated carve-outs, such as Kansas and Iowa, have generally seen improved integration metrics, though other factors also contributed. A 2019 MACPAC analysis found that behavioral health carve-out plans had 23% lower access rates for mental health services compared to carved-in plans.

### Medicaid Reimbursement Gaps

Only 22 states reimburse CoCM codes under Medicaid as of 2023, and rates in many of those states fall below the cost of delivering the service. Medicaid serves a disproportionate share of people with mental illness (approximately 26% of adult Medicaid beneficiaries have a diagnosed mental health condition), making Medicaid payment policy the most critical determinant of integration access for the most vulnerable populations.

The state-by-state nature of Medicaid creates a patchwork where integration is financially viable in some states and impossible in others. Practices in states without Medicaid CoCM coverage face a choice: provide CoCM to Medicaid patients at a loss (cross-subsidized by Medicare and commercial CoCM billing), limit CoCM to non-Medicaid patients, or forgo CoCM entirely. For FQHCs, which serve predominantly Medicaid and uninsured populations, the absence of Medicaid CoCM coverage is particularly devastating.

**Evidence**: A 2023 Milliman analysis found that average Medicaid reimbursement for CoCM was 65-75% of Medicare rates in states that covered the codes, and that several states imposed additional requirements (e.g., prior authorization, limits on qualifying diagnoses) not present in Medicare policy (Milliman, 2023). Washington State, which was the first to implement Medicaid CoCM coverage, saw a 3.5x increase in CoCM adoption compared to neighboring states without coverage (Washington State Health Care Authority, 2022).

### Commercial Payer Fragmentation

Even among commercial payers who cover CoCM codes, coverage policies vary widely. Some payers require prior authorization for CoCM services, effectively negating the population-based model by requiring per-patient approval. Others limit CoCM coverage to specific diagnoses (e.g., major depressive disorder only, excluding anxiety and substance use disorders) or restrict the duration of CoCM billing (e.g., 6 months maximum). These variations mean that a single primary care practice may need to navigate different CoCM billing rules for each commercial payer, Medicare, and Medicaid, creating administrative complexity that deters adoption.

**Evidence**: The APA's 2023 Practice Survey found that among commercial payers covering CoCM, 35% required prior authorization, 28% limited qualifying diagnoses, and 15% imposed time limits on coverage. Only approximately 40% of commercial payers cover CoCM codes nationally. Practices report that commercial payer CoCM rates average 80-90% of Medicare rates (APA, 2023).

## Workforce Shortages and Maldistribution

### Insufficient Supply of Behavioral Health Professionals

The U.S. faces a severe shortage of behavioral health professionals across all disciplines. There are 6,585 designated Mental Health Professional Shortage Areas (MHPSAs) affecting 160 million Americans (HRSA, 2024). The shortage is most acute in the specific roles required for CoCM: behavioral health care managers (BHCMs) and consulting psychiatrists.

The BHCM shortage is unique because the role itself is poorly defined and lacks a standardized training pathway. Unlike nurse practitioners, physicians, or licensed clinical social workers, there is no national certification for behavioral health care management in the CoCM context. BHCMs are typically drawn from social work (MSW), nursing (RN/BSN), or counseling (MA/MS) backgrounds, with CoCM-specific training provided on the job or through AIMS Center workshops. This lack of standardization means that practices seeking to hire BHCMs must recruit from adjacent professions and invest in training, competing against established career paths that offer higher compensation and clearer professional identity.

**Evidence**: HRSA estimates that only 27.8% of the need for mental health professionals is being met in designated shortage areas (HRSA, 2024). The behavioral health care manager role, which is central to CoCM, has no standardized training pathway or certification, and fewer than 10,000 professionals currently serve in this capacity nationally (AIMS Center estimate, 2023). The median BHCM salary of $52,000-$68,000 (BLS/AIMS Center, 2023) is below the median for licensed clinical social workers ($55,000-$72,000) and significantly below psychiatric nurse practitioners ($120,000+), creating a wage competition that disadvantages BHCM recruitment.

### Geographic Maldistribution

Even where behavioral health professionals exist, they are concentrated in urban areas and academic medical centers. Rural communities, which are often served by the small primary care practices most in need of integration support, face the most severe shortages. This maldistribution is self-reinforcing: rural areas lack training programs, which reduces the pipeline of professionals familiar with and willing to practice in rural settings.

**Evidence**: Rural counties have an average of 5.4 behavioral health providers per 100,000 residents, compared to 43.8 per 100,000 in urban counties (Rural Health Research Center, 2023). Over 60% of rural counties have no practicing psychiatrist (HRSA, 2024). Over 80% of counties designated as MHPSAs are non-metropolitan (HRSA, 2024). The National Health Service Corps currently deploys over 6,500 behavioral health clinicians to underserved areas, but this represents a small fraction of the total need.

### Training Pipeline Disconnection

Medical schools, residency programs, and graduate training in psychology and social work remain largely organized around separate behavioral health specialty tracks. Primary care residents receive limited training in behavioral health assessment and treatment, and behavioral health trainees receive limited exposure to team-based primary care integration.

This separation begins at the earliest stages of training. Medical students receive an average of 6 weeks of psychiatry clerkship during their four years of education, during which they rotate through inpatient and outpatient psychiatry settings that bear little resemblance to primary care-based behavioral health. Psychology doctoral students complete clinical internships overwhelmingly in specialty behavioral health settings (hospitals, community mental health centers, VA outpatient clinics) rather than in primary care practices. Social work graduate programs focus on clinical therapy skills, case management, and community practice, with minimal exposure to medical team-based collaboration.

**Evidence**: A 2022 survey of family medicine residency programs found that only 34% offered structured training in CoCM, and the median number of hours devoted to integrated behavioral health training was 40 over the three-year residency (AAFP, 2022). Only 12% of clinical psychology internship programs included rotations in primary care integration settings (APA, 2023). The Accreditation Council for Graduate Medical Education (ACGME) does not currently require CoCM training in family medicine or internal medicine residency programs, though some programs voluntarily include it.

### Psychiatrist Workforce Constraints

The consulting psychiatrist role is a bottleneck for CoCM scaling. CoCM requires a psychiatric consultant who reviews a caseload of patients (typically 100-200 per consultant at 0.1-0.2 FTE), provides treatment recommendations to the care manager and PCP, and conducts occasional direct patient evaluation for complex cases. This consultative model is efficient in its use of psychiatrist time, but scaling nationally would require thousands of psychiatrists willing and trained to work in this capacity.

**Evidence**: Approximately 1,740 PGY-1 psychiatry residency positions are offered annually (NRMP, 2024), but many graduates enter private practice, forensic psychiatry, addiction psychiatry, or academic roles rather than consultative primary care support. A 2020 APA survey found that 38% of psychiatrists expressed reservations about consultative-only roles, citing concerns about liability, clinical authority, and the adequacy of indirect care (APA, 2020). The average psychiatrist in the U.S. is 53 years old, and approximately 25% are over 65 (AAMC, 2023), indicating significant retirement-driven attrition in the coming decade.

## Technology and Infrastructure Deficits

### Electronic Health Record Fragmentation

Most electronic health record (EHR) systems were designed for physical health documentation and do not natively support the workflows required for integrated behavioral health. Key deficits include the absence of patient registries for population-based management, lack of behavioral health screening templates integrated into primary care workflows, and limited capacity for shared care plans between PCPs and behavioral health team members.

The registry function is particularly critical. CoCM's population-based approach requires that a care manager maintain an active registry of all patients in behavioral health treatment, with standardized outcome measures (PHQ-9, GAD-7) recorded at every contact. The care manager uses the registry to identify patients who are not improving and to prompt treatment adjustments. Without an integrated registry, the care manager must maintain a parallel tracking system (often a spreadsheet), which is error-prone, not interoperable with the EHR, and invisible to the PCP.

**Evidence**: Fewer than 20% of EHR systems used in primary care have built-in registry functionality suitable for CoCM (ONC Health IT Dashboard, 2023). Practices adopting CoCM frequently must purchase third-party registry software (e.g., AIMS Center's Care Manager Tracking System, or CMTS) at additional cost of $5,000-$15,000 per year. Epic Systems has introduced some registry functionality, but it is not standard in base configurations and requires additional implementation support.

### 42 CFR Part 2 Regulatory Barriers (Partially Resolved)

Until 2024, federal regulations under 42 CFR Part 2 imposed confidentiality requirements on substance use disorder (SUD) records that were far more restrictive than HIPAA, effectively preventing the sharing of SUD treatment information within integrated care teams. While SAMHSA's 2024 final rule aligned Part 2 with HIPAA, implementation is ongoing and decades of practice based on the old rule have created entrenched information-sharing barriers.

The legacy of Part 2 extends beyond the regulation itself. Many EHR systems were configured to segregate SUD records from general medical records, and many clinicians developed practice habits of not documenting SUD-related information in shared records. Reversing these technical configurations and practice patterns will take years, even though the regulatory barrier has been substantially reduced.

**Evidence**: A 2021 survey found that 67% of primary care providers cited concerns about sharing SUD information as a barrier to integrated treatment of co-occurring disorders (National Council for Mental Wellbeing, 2021). A 2023 analysis estimated that EHR reconfiguration to fully implement the Part 2/HIPAA alignment would cost $50,000-$200,000 per health system (ONC estimate, 2023).

### Limited Broadband for Telehealth

Telehealth-enabled integration models, including Project ECHO and remote CoCM, depend on reliable broadband access. Many of the rural and underserved communities most in need of integration support lack adequate internet infrastructure.

**Evidence**: Approximately 21 million Americans lack access to broadband internet, disproportionately in rural areas (FCC Broadband Deployment Report, 2023). The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (2021) allocated $65 billion for broadband expansion, but deployment will take several years. In the interim, rural practices may lack the connectivity needed for video-based psychiatric consultation, telehealth-delivered behavioral health services, or cloud-based registry management.

## Professional Culture and Identity

### Mind-Body Dualism in Medical Training

Despite decades of biopsychosocial rhetoric, American medical education continues to treat physical and mental health as fundamentally different domains. Primary care training emphasizes biomedical diagnosis and pharmacotherapy; behavioral health training emphasizes psychotherapy and psychological assessment. The result is mutual unfamiliarity and, in some cases, mutual skepticism.

This dualism is embedded in the structure of medical education itself. The Flexner Report (1910), which established the biomedical foundation of modern medical education, did not address mental health as an integrated component of general medical training. Over a century later, the legacy persists: psychiatric education occurs in separate departments, separate clinical settings, and often separate buildings. Primary care residents may complete their entire training without substantive exposure to team-based behavioral health care.

**Evidence**: A 2021 study found that only 21% of primary care physicians felt "very confident" in managing depression, and only 11% felt "very confident" in managing anxiety, despite these being among the most common conditions in their patient panels (Anxiety and Depression Association of America, 2021). A 2022 study found that 45% of primary care residents reported receiving "inadequate" training in behavioral health assessment and treatment (AAFP Residency Survey, 2022).

### Specialist Identity and Referral Culture

Many psychiatrists and psychologists define their professional identity around specialty referral practice. Integration asks these professionals to shift to consultative and supervisory roles, which some view as a diminishment of their expertise and autonomy. The referral model reinforces professional status hierarchies in which the specialist is the definitive authority, whereas the consultative model positions the specialist as a support to the primary care team.

**Evidence**: A 2020 American Psychiatric Association survey found that 38% of psychiatrists expressed reservations about consultative-only roles in CoCM, citing concerns about liability, clinical authority, and the adequacy of indirect care (APA, 2020). Among the concerns, 42% of respondents expressed worry about being held clinically responsible for patients they had not personally examined, and 35% felt that indirect consultation underutilized their clinical training.

### Stigma and Patient Segmentation

The separation of behavioral health from primary care both reflects and reinforces stigma. When mental health treatment requires a separate appointment, a separate provider, and often a separate location, patients internalize the message that mental health is categorically different from and less important than physical health. This structural stigma reduces help-seeking and treatment engagement.

**Evidence**: A 2019 study found that 82% of primary care patients with depression preferred receiving mental health treatment in their primary care setting, citing convenience, reduced stigma, and trust in their PCP (Davis et al., *Primary Care Companion for CNS Disorders*, 2019). Over 50% of patients referred from primary care to specialty behavioral health never complete the referral (Cunningham, *Health Affairs*, 2009), and stigma is consistently cited as a leading reason for non-completion.

## Political Economy

### Who Benefits from the Status Quo

| Actor | Benefit | Economic Value | Incentive to Block Reform |
|-------|---------|----------------|---------------------------|
| Managed behavioral health organizations (MBHOs) | Profitable administration of carved-out behavioral health benefits | $8-12 billion annually in administrative fees | Carve-in integration eliminates their business model |
| Specialty behavioral health practices | Higher per-visit reimbursement in specialty settings | Average $150-250/visit specialty rates vs. $60-80 in integrated settings | Integration redirects patient volume away from specialty referral |
| Pharmaceutical companies | Current prescribing patterns favor specialty-initiated medication | $15.6 billion in antidepressant sales (IQVIA, 2023) | Measurement-based care may reduce unnecessary prescribing |
| EHR vendors | Separate behavioral health modules as add-on products | $2-5 billion in behavioral health IT market | Integration requires expensive system redesign |
| Fee-for-service beneficiaries | Higher volume billing in fragmented system | Varies by practice | Population-based models reduce visit volume |
| Private equity-backed primary care | Short-term profit maximization from high-volume FFS | Growing share of primary care market (~8% of practices) | Integration requires upfront investment that reduces near-term margins |

### Perverse Incentives

The current system creates multiple perverse incentives that work against integration:

- **Primary care providers** are incentivized to refer rather than treat behavioral health conditions, because referral generates no additional workload while integrated treatment does. Under FFS, the PCP receives no additional compensation for managing a patient's depression, but does receive standard visit reimbursement for the medical issues that prompted the visit.
- **Payers** benefit from behavioral health carve-outs that allow them to limit mental health spending through separate administrative structures. Carve-out MBHOs are incentivized to deny claims and restrict utilization; in carved-out arrangements, the medical plan does not bear the cost of untreated mental illness manifesting as physical health problems.
- **Health systems** receive higher reimbursement for specialist outpatient behavioral health visits than for primary care-embedded services, creating a financial disincentive to shift care settings. A psychiatrist seeing patients directly at $200-$300 per visit generates more revenue than the same psychiatrist serving as a CoCM consultant for a panel of 150 patients.
- **Patients** face copay structures that may charge differently for a behavioral health visit than a primary care visit, even when the service is delivered in the same location. Some plans require higher copayments for behavioral health services, effectively penalizing patients who seek mental health treatment.
- **Graduate medical education funding** flows through hospital-based residency programs that train specialists, not through primary care practices that would train integrated care teams. This creates a structural pipeline bias toward specialty practice.

```text
Separate training of PCPs and BH professionals --> Low comfort with integrated practice
    |
    v

    |
    v

    |
    v

    |
    v

    |

    |
    v

    |
    v

```

## Why Reform Has Failed

Past reform efforts have failed for several identifiable reasons:

1. **Piecemeal approaches**: Most integration initiatives have addressed one barrier at a time (e.g., payment codes without workforce investment, screening mandates without treatment capacity), rather than tackling the interconnected system of barriers simultaneously. The CoCM billing codes (2017) addressed payment without addressing workforce; the USPSTF screening recommendations (2016, 2023) addressed identification without addressing treatment capacity; FQHC behavioral health grants addressed workforce without addressing EHR infrastructure.
2. **Voluntary adoption**: Nearly all integration programs rely on voluntary practice transformation, which selects for already-motivated practices and leaves the majority untouched. The practices that adopt CoCM voluntarily tend to be larger, better-resourced, and already oriented toward quality improvement, meaning that voluntary adoption widens rather than narrows the integration gap.
3. **Insufficient duration**: Many federal demonstration programs (e.g., PBHCI grants, SIM grants) operated on 3-5 year timelines, too short to achieve sustainable practice transformation and demonstrate long-term cost savings. The CoCM evidence base shows that cost savings accumulate over 3-5 years (Unutzer et al., 2008), but demonstration programs are often evaluated and defunded before savings materialize.
4. **Lack of consumer demand**: Unlike many healthcare reforms driven by patient advocacy, integration reform has been primarily championed by clinicians and researchers. Patient awareness of integration benefits remains low, limiting political pressure for change. Most patients do not know what CoCM is or that it exists, and cannot demand a service they have never heard of.
5. **Resistance from behavioral health stakeholders**: Some behavioral health professionals and organizations view integration as a threat to their specialty identity, clinical autonomy, and reimbursement, creating unexpected opposition from within the mental health field. This intra-field division weakens the advocacy coalition and provides opponents with cover ("even mental health professionals disagree about integration").
6. **Fragmented federal authority**: Responsibility for integration policy is spread across multiple federal agencies (CMS, HRSA, SAMHSA, ONC, AHRQ) with no single coordinating authority. Each agency pursues its own integration initiatives within its own statutory authority, resulting in overlapping but uncoordinated programs that do not add up to a coherent national strategy.
7. **State-level variation**: Because Medicaid, professional licensing, insurance regulation, and scope of practice are primarily state functions, integration policy varies enormously across 50 states. A practice model that is financially viable in Washington State may be impossible in a state without Medicaid CoCM coverage, full-practice-authority for nurse practitioners, or a psychiatric access program. This variation makes national scaling inherently difficult.
