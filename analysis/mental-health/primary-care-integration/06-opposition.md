# Primary Care and Mental Health Integration: Opposition to Reform

Proposals to expand the integration of behavioral health into primary care face resistance from those who believe current specialty-based mental health delivery is adequate, that integration mandates represent government overreach, that integration dilutes the quality of behavioral health care, or that the costs of transformation are unjustified. Understanding these opposition forces is essential for designing reform strategies that can either address legitimate concerns or overcome structural resistance.

---

## Who Opposes Reform

### Organized Opposition

| Actor | Interest | Resources | Tactics |
|-------|----------|-----------|---------|
| Managed behavioral health organizations (MBHOs) | Behavioral health carve-out contracts generate $8-12 billion annually in administrative fees; integration eliminates their business model | Lobbying budgets; relationships with state Medicaid agencies; data on utilization management savings | Argue carve-outs provide superior cost control and specialized expertise; lobby against carve-in policies |
| Some specialty behavioral health providers | Referral-based practice models generate higher per-visit reimbursement ($150-250/visit) than integrated settings ($60-80) | Professional association advocacy; clinical authority; patient relationships | Frame integration as diluting quality of care; argue primary care providers are undertrained |
| Private equity-backed primary care groups | Integration requires upfront investment that reduces short-term profitability; 3-5 year investment horizons conflict with 6-12 month CoCM ramp-up | Financial resources; health system consolidation leverage; growing market share (~8% of practices) | Prioritize revenue-generating services; delay integration infrastructure investment |
| EHR vendors (some) | Separate behavioral health modules as add-on revenue ($2-5 billion BH IT market) | Market position; installed base lock-in; vendor advisory committees | Slow to integrate BH workflows into core products; charge premium for integration features |
| Fiscal conservatives (some) | Integration mandates require new federal and state spending; oppose expansion of government healthcare programs | Legislative influence; think tank support (Heritage Foundation, AEI health policy) | Argue market forces will drive adoption without mandates; cite federal deficit concerns |
| Pharmaceutical benefit managers (PBMs) | Current prescribing patterns generate revenue through specialty pharmacy channels | Market influence; relationships with payers | Measurement-based care may reduce unnecessary prescribing, affecting PBM revenue streams |

### Ideological Opposition

- **Free-market advocates**: Oppose government mandates for integration, arguing that market demand should drive adoption. Prefer voluntary adoption incentivized by value-based payment rather than regulatory requirements. Cite concerns about government defining clinical practice models. Organizations including the Heritage Foundation and Cato Institute have published analyses questioning the appropriateness of federal mandates for specific clinical delivery models, arguing that CMS should set outcome standards rather than prescribing how care is delivered.
- **Privacy absolutists**: Oppose the sharing of behavioral health records in integrated settings, arguing that reducing confidentiality protections for mental health and substance use information will deter people from seeking treatment. This position was particularly strong during the 42 CFR Part 2 reform process. The Legal Action Center and some substance use disorder advocacy organizations filed formal opposition to the 2024 Part 2/HIPAA alignment rule, arguing that it would discourage treatment-seeking among people with SUD.
- **Professional autonomy advocates**: Some psychiatrists and psychologists view integration as subordinating their clinical expertise to primary care workflows, reducing their professional autonomy, and shifting them to indirect consultative roles that they consider less clinically meaningful. Within the APA, a vocal minority has argued that the consultative psychiatrist role in CoCM reduces the profession to a "phone-a-friend" function that underutilizes years of specialized training.
- **Small-government conservatives**: View integration mandates as a federal expansion into healthcare delivery that should be left to states and private markets. Argue that FQHC expansion and CCBHC programs represent growing government dependency. Some congressional members have opposed CCBHC expansion on the grounds that it creates a new entitlement-like payment structure in behavioral health.
- **Anti-screening advocates**: A smaller but vocal constituency argues that universal screening for depression and anxiety leads to over-medicalization of normal human experience, excessive psychotropic prescribing, and pathologizing of poverty and social adversity. This critique, drawing on the work of scholars such as Allen Frances (*Saving Normal*, 2013) and Joanna Moncrieff (*The Myth of the Chemical Cure*, 2008), raises legitimate questions about screening thresholds while sometimes opposing the broader integration agenda.

## Opposition Arguments and Rebuttals

### Argument 1: Primary Care Providers Are Not Qualified to Treat Mental Illness

**Claim**: Mental health conditions require specialist training that primary care providers do not possess. Integrating behavioral health into primary care will result in inferior treatment, missed diagnoses, and harm to patients.

**Evidence Cited**: Primary care providers receive limited psychiatric training in medical school (median 6 weeks of psychiatry clerkship) and residency (median 40 hours of integrated behavioral health training across 3 years). Studies show variable quality of depression management in primary care, including overreliance on medication and underuse of therapy. A 2021 survey found only 21% of PCPs felt "very confident" in managing depression (ADAA, 2021).

**Reality**: The Collaborative Care Model does not ask primary care providers to become psychiatrists. Instead, CoCM provides systematic support through embedded behavioral health care managers and consulting psychiatrists who guide treatment using measurement-based protocols. Over 90 RCTs demonstrate that CoCM produces outcomes superior to specialty referral for common conditions like depression and anxiety. The IMPACT trial found CoCM patients were more than twice as likely to achieve depression remission compared to usual care (45% vs. 19%) (Unutzer et al., *JAMA*, 2002). Crucially, the PRISM-E trial found that patients were 2.5 times more likely to attend even one behavioral health visit when services were co-located in primary care (Bartels et al., *American Journal of Geriatric Psychiatry*, 2004), meaning that the theoretical superiority of specialty care is irrelevant for the majority of patients who never reach the specialist.

**Rebuttal**: Integration does not replace specialty care; it builds a system around the primary care provider that delivers structured, evidence-based treatment. Patients with severe or treatment-resistant conditions continue to receive specialty referral. The evidence overwhelmingly shows that structured primary care-based treatment outperforms unstructured specialist referral for mild to moderate conditions, largely because the majority of referred patients never complete the referral. Over 50% of patients referred from primary care to specialty behavioral health never attend a single appointment (Cunningham, *Health Affairs*, 2009).

### Argument 2: Integration Will Increase Healthcare Costs

**Claim**: Building integration infrastructure (care managers, registries, consulting relationships) is expensive, and expanding screening will identify more patients who need treatment, increasing overall healthcare expenditure.

**Evidence Cited**: Start-up costs for CoCM range from $150,000 to $250,000 per practice (APA, 2023). Universal depression and anxiety screening would identify millions of new patients needing treatment. Federal integration programs (CCBHCs, FQHC expansion) require new appropriations. The Congressional Budget Office has scored some integration proposals as increasing federal spending in the short term.

**Reality**: Multiple studies demonstrate that CoCM reduces total healthcare costs by decreasing emergency department use, hospitalization, and disability. The IMPACT trial found CoCM was cost-effective at $594 per quality-adjusted life year (QALY) gained (Simon et al., *Archives of General Psychiatry*, 2001), making it among the most cost-effective interventions in all of medicine (by comparison, statin therapy for primary prevention costs $3,000-$10,000 per QALY). A 2016 study found that CoCM for depression saved an average of $3,363 per patient over four years in reduced medical costs (Unutzer et al., *American Journal of Managed Care*, 2016). Intermountain Healthcare documented $2,800 per patient savings (Reiss-Brennan et al., 2016). Untreated mental illness costs the U.S. economy an estimated $280 billion annually in lost productivity, disability, and excess medical spending (Insel, *JAMA Psychiatry*, 2022).

**Rebuttal**: The upfront costs of integration are real but are dwarfed by the costs of the status quo. The question is not whether we can afford to integrate but whether we can afford not to. Current spending on untreated mental illness through emergency departments, disability, and criminal justice involvement far exceeds the cost of preventive, primary care-based treatment. CCBHC evaluation data shows Medicaid savings of $200-$600 per beneficiary annually (Mathematica, 2022), and the savings grow larger as integration infrastructure matures.

### Argument 3: Behavioral Health Carve-Outs Provide Better Cost Control

**Claim**: Managed behavioral health organizations provide specialized utilization management that keeps mental health spending in check. Eliminating carve-outs and integrating behavioral health into general medical management will remove this cost-control expertise and increase spending.

**Evidence Cited**: MBHOs point to lower per-capita behavioral health spending in carved-out plans compared to some carved-in arrangements. They cite their specialized clinical expertise in managing behavioral health utilization. Magellan Health, Optum Behavioral Health, and Carelon Behavioral Health collectively manage behavioral health benefits for tens of millions of Americans and publish data showing utilization management efficiency.

**Reality**: Lower spending is not the same as better outcomes. Carve-outs often achieve cost "savings" by creating barriers to access (prior authorization, narrow networks, high denial rates) that reduce utilization regardless of clinical need. A 2019 analysis found that behavioral health carve-out plans had 23% lower access rates for mental health services compared to carved-in plans (Medicaid and CHIP Payment and Access Commission, 2019). The cost "savings" from carve-outs are often shifted to emergency departments, criminal justice, and disability systems. The *Wit v. United Behavioral Health* ruling (N.D. Cal. 2019) found that United Behavioral Health had developed internal coverage criteria that were more restrictive than generally accepted standards of care, demonstrating how utilization management can function as a barrier rather than a quality tool.

**Rebuttal**: Integration does not abandon cost management; it replaces fragmented utilization management with population-based clinical management. CoCM's measurement-based approach identifies patients who are not improving and adjusts treatment, which is more clinically effective and ultimately less costly than administrative barriers to care. States that have transitioned from carve-out to carve-in models (e.g., Washington, Oregon, Arizona) have generally demonstrated improved access without the cost increases predicted by MBHO opponents.

### Argument 4: Market Forces Will Drive Integration Without Government Mandates

**Claim**: If integration is truly cost-effective, market forces will drive adoption without government intervention. Mandates distort the market and impose one-size-fits-all solutions on diverse practice settings.

**Evidence Cited**: Value-based payment models are growing; health systems are voluntarily adopting integration; innovation should come from the private sector, not government. Kaiser Permanente, Intermountain, and other systems integrated without mandates. The CMS Innovation Center is already testing voluntary integration models (Making Care Primary).

**Reality**: CoCM has been available since 2002, and Medicare has paid for it since 2017, yet fewer than 3% of practices use it (Psychiatry and Behavioral Health Learning Network, 2023). Voluntary adoption has failed over two decades despite overwhelming evidence. The market failure is driven by misaligned payment incentives (FFS rewards volume, not outcomes), information asymmetry (most practices are unaware of CoCM or how to implement it), public goods characteristics (integration benefits population health but individual practices bear the cost of transformation), and externalities (the savings from integration accrue to payers and hospitals, not to the practice that invests). The systems that integrated voluntarily (Kaiser, Intermountain, VA) operate under fundamentally different financial structures (capitation, integrated delivery, salaried models) that do not represent the majority of U.S. primary care.

**Rebuttal**: Healthcare is not a typical market. The combination of insurance-mediated payment, information asymmetry, public goods characteristics, and the vulnerability of the affected population (people with mental illness) makes pure market-based adoption insufficient. Strategic government action, including payment reform, workforce investment, and practice transformation support, is needed to correct the market failure, just as it was needed to establish primary care infrastructure, vaccination programs, and hospital quality standards.

### Argument 5: Integration Threatens Patient Privacy

**Claim**: Sharing mental health and substance use disorder records within integrated care teams increases the risk of stigma, discrimination, and breaches of confidentiality. Patients may avoid seeking treatment if they know their behavioral health information will be visible to their primary care team.

**Evidence Cited**: Mental health stigma remains pervasive; employer access to health records (through self-insured plans) could disadvantage employees; the 42 CFR Part 2 protections were specifically designed to encourage people to seek substance use treatment without fear of disclosure. The Legal Action Center has documented cases where disclosure of SUD records has led to employment discrimination, child custody challenges, and insurance denials.

**Reality**: Research consistently shows that patients prefer integrated care and value having their primary care provider aware of their mental health treatment. A 2019 study found that 82% of primary care patients with depression preferred receiving mental health treatment in their primary care setting, and 76% wanted their PCP to have access to their behavioral health records (Davis et al., *Primary Care Companion for CNS Disorders*, 2019). The 2024 alignment of 42 CFR Part 2 with HIPAA maintains consent requirements while enabling clinical coordination. Importantly, the fragmented system also creates privacy risks: when patients must repeat their history to multiple providers, they face more exposure points than in a coordinated system with controlled information sharing.

**Rebuttal**: Privacy protections are essential and must be maintained in integrated settings. The solution is not to prevent information sharing for clinical purposes but to strengthen protections against non-clinical access, enforce anti-discrimination laws (ADA, Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act), and design consent processes that give patients meaningful control. The greater threat to patients is the fragmented care that results from information barriers, including medication interactions, conflicting treatment plans, and missed diagnoses.

### Argument 6: One Model Does Not Fit All Practice Settings

**Claim**: CoCM was developed in large academic health systems and cannot be applied to solo practices, rural clinics, or settings with limited resources. Mandating a single integration model is impractical.

**Evidence Cited**: Solo and small-group practices make up approximately 50% of primary care (AMA Physician Practice Benchmark Survey, 2022). These practices lack the infrastructure to hire care managers or establish consulting relationships. Rural areas lack the broadband for telehealth models. The IMPACT trial enrolled patients at 18 clinics in 5 states, all within organized healthcare systems.

**Reality**: This is the strongest opposition argument and contains a legitimate concern. CoCM as originally designed requires infrastructure that many small practices lack. However, adaptation models exist and have been validated: Project ECHO provides teleconsultation to rural PCPs without requiring embedded staff; community-based organizations can serve as external care management resources (providing "virtual BHCMs" to multiple practices); and statewide consultation programs (e.g., MCPAP, Michigan MC3) provide psychiatric access to all primary care providers regardless of practice size. The Fortney et al. (2013) RCT demonstrated that telehealth-delivered CoCM in rural FQHCs achieved outcomes equivalent to in-person CoCM. The Primary Care Behavioral Health (PCBH) model, which embeds a single behavioral health consultant, requires less infrastructure than full CoCM and may be more appropriate for smaller settings.

**Rebuttal**: Integration mandates should allow flexibility in model selection while requiring core components (screening, measurement-based care, access to behavioral health consultation). The goal is universal access to integrated behavioral health, not universal adoption of a single delivery model. Federal and state programs should provide technical assistance and financial support for small and rural practices to adopt adapted integration models. A tiered approach, requiring screening and consultation access for all practices while reserving full CoCM implementation for practices above a certain size, could address this concern while maintaining progress.

### Argument 7: Integration Will Worsen Behavioral Health Workforce Shortages

**Claim**: Redirecting behavioral health professionals into primary care integration roles will drain an already depleted specialty workforce, reducing access to intensive treatment for people with serious mental illness.

**Evidence Cited**: There are 6,585 designated MHPSAs (HRSA, 2024). Psychiatrists are already in severe shortage, with over 55% of U.S. counties lacking any psychiatrist. Diverting psychiatrists to consultative CoCM roles reduces their direct patient care capacity.

**Reality**: CoCM actually represents the most efficient use of scarce psychiatric expertise. In a specialty referral model, a full-time psychiatrist sees approximately 1,000-1,500 patients per year in direct encounters. As a CoCM consultant at 0.1-0.2 FTE, the same psychiatrist oversees 150-200 patients while maintaining 80-90% of their time for direct specialty care. The efficiency gain comes from the "stepped care" approach: the BHCM handles routine monitoring and brief interventions, the psychiatrist reviews only the cases that require psychiatric-level decision-making. This means CoCM extends psychiatric reach without reducing specialty availability.

**Rebuttal**: Integration is the solution to workforce shortages, not their cause. By using psychiatrists consultatively rather than requiring direct patient encounters for every patient, CoCM multiplies the effective reach of each psychiatrist. The BHCM Corps proposal creates a new workforce category rather than redirecting existing specialty providers. Statewide psychiatric access programs similarly extend psychiatric reach without reducing specialty capacity.

## Historical Patterns of Opposition

Opposition to integration has evolved over time, reflecting changes in the evidence base, payment landscape, and political environment:

- **1990s-2000s (Conceptual opposition)**: Resistance was primarily conceptual: many clinicians and policymakers simply did not believe that mental health could be effectively treated in primary care. The accumulation of RCT evidence, particularly the IMPACT trial (2002), largely settled this debate. Few credible voices today argue that integration is clinically ineffective.
- **2000s-2010s (Economic and structural arguments)**: With clinical effectiveness established, opposition shifted to economic and structural arguments: integration was acknowledged as effective but deemed too expensive or complex to implement widely. The creation of CoCM billing codes (2017) and CCBHC prospective payment partially addressed these concerns.
- **2017-Present (Scope, privacy, and mandate concerns)**: With payment pathways available, current opposition increasingly centers on scope of practice, privacy, workforce impact, and government mandate concerns. MBHOs have shifted from arguing that carve-outs are clinically superior to arguing that carve-in transitions are administratively complex and risky.
- **Emerging (AI and technology concerns)**: As AI-powered clinical decision support and digital therapeutics enter the integration space, new opposition is emerging around the appropriate role of technology in mental health screening and treatment, the accuracy and bias of AI screening algorithms, and the potential for digital tools to substitute for rather than supplement human clinical relationships.

## Counter-Strategies

### Political Strategies

- **Build bipartisan coalitions**: Integration has rare bipartisan potential. Conservative supporters emphasize cost savings, reduced emergency department use, reduced criminal justice involvement, and local control. Liberal supporters emphasize access and equity. The CCBHC expansion has already demonstrated bipartisan support (Bipartisan Safer Communities Act, 2022). The Excellence in Mental Health and Addiction Treatment Act was co-sponsored by members of both parties.
- **Leverage employer support**: Major employers including Walmart, Amazon, JPMorgan Chase, and Starbucks have expressed interest in integrated behavioral health to reduce disability and absenteeism costs. The National Business Group on Health has endorsed integration as a cost-reduction strategy. Employer advocacy can bridge political divides because it frames integration as a productivity and economic competitiveness issue, not a government expansion.
- **Engage veterans organizations**: The VA has been a leader in integrated behavioral health (the PCBH model is standard across VA Patient-Aligned Care Teams) and can serve as a model that appeals to veteran-focused legislators across the political spectrum. VA outcome data provides compelling evidence from a system that both parties support.
- **Neutralize MBHO opposition through transition support**: Offer MBHOs a role in the transition to carved-in models (e.g., as network management subcontractors or quality oversight entities) rather than eliminating them entirely. This creates a glide path that reduces active opposition while moving toward integration.

### Communication Strategies

- **Lead with patient stories**: Personal narratives of patients whose lives were improved by integrated care are more persuasive than RCT data. Partner with patient advocacy organizations (NAMI, MHA) to collect and amplify these stories. Congressional testimony from patients who experienced CoCM has been effective in committee hearings.
- **Frame as primary care modernization**: Position integration as bringing primary care into the 21st century, not as a new government program. Emphasize that integration is a delivery model improvement, not an expansion of government healthcare. Use language of "modernization," "team-based care," and "whole-person health" rather than "mandate" or "requirement."
- **Quantify the cost of inaction**: Make visible the costs currently borne by emergency departments ($2,264 per behavioral health ED visit), criminal justice (44% of jail inmates have mental illness), disability (depression is the leading cause of disability worldwide, WHO), and lost productivity ($47.6 billion annually for employers). These costs are dispersed and invisible; integration advocacy must make them concrete and attributable.
- **Address privacy concerns directly**: Rather than dismissing privacy concerns, lead with patient preference data (82% prefer integrated care) and design explicit privacy protections into integration proposals. Acknowledge the historical harm documented by privacy advocates and demonstrate how integration models incorporate consent-based information sharing.

### Policy Design Strategies

- **Build in practice flexibility**: Mandate universal access to integrated behavioral health but allow practices to choose from multiple approved models (CoCM, PCBH, teleconsultation, community care manager models). This addresses the "one size fits all" critique while maintaining accountability for outcomes.
- **Phase implementation**: Require integration in large health systems and FQHCs first, then extend to smaller practices with financial and technical support. This reduces the burden on small practices and builds evidence for adaptation.
- **Protect privacy while enabling coordination**: Design information-sharing frameworks with robust consent processes, audit trails, and penalties for non-clinical disclosure. Address privacy concerns directly rather than dismissing them. Include provisions strengthening anti-discrimination protections for people whose behavioral health information is shared in integrated settings.
- **Create transition pathways for displaced stakeholders**: For MBHOs facing business model disruption, offer roles in quality oversight, care coordination administration, or network management within carved-in systems. For specialty providers concerned about volume loss, emphasize that integration increases appropriate specialty referrals for complex cases while redirecting mild-to-moderate cases to primary care.
