# Primary Care and Mental Health Integration: Solutions

Transforming the integration of behavioral health into primary care requires a multi-faceted approach that simultaneously addresses payment misalignment, workforce shortages, technology gaps, and organizational culture to move from fewer than 3% of practices using evidence-based models to universal access within a decade.

---

## Reform Framework

### Guiding Principles

- Evidence-based models (CoCM and validated adaptations) should be the foundation, while allowing flexibility for diverse practice settings
- Payment must align with the population-based, team-based nature of integrated care rather than fee-for-service encounter billing
- Workforce development must create new training pathways and expand existing pipelines to meet integration demand
- Technology must enable rather than obstruct integration, with shared records, registries, and telehealth as standard infrastructure
- Equity must guide implementation, prioritizing underserved populations and settings where specialty behavioral health is unavailable
- Integration models must address the full age spectrum, including pediatric, adult, and geriatric populations, with age-appropriate adaptations
- Substance use disorder treatment must be included within the integration framework, leveraging the 2024 42 CFR Part 2 alignment and the 2023 X-waiver elimination

### Theory of Change

The current integration gap is driven by the interaction of misaligned payment, insufficient workforce, inadequate technology, and siloed professional culture. No single reform will produce transformation because these barriers reinforce each other. The proposed strategy addresses all four barriers simultaneously through a coordinated federal-state initiative that provides financial incentives, workforce capacity, technical assistance, and regulatory framework reform. By creating the conditions for integration to be economically viable, professionally supported, technically feasible, and culturally normalized, the strategy breaks the self-reinforcing cycle of fragmentation.

The theory of change recognizes that voluntary adoption has failed over two decades. While the evidence base is sufficient, the structural incentives are not. The strategy therefore combines mandates (universal payer coverage, FQHC requirements, EHR standards) with incentives (enhanced FMAP, workforce corps, technical assistance) to produce the simultaneous, multi-domain action required for system transformation.

## Proposed Solutions

### Solution 1: Universal CoCM Payment Mandate

**Description**: Require all public and private payers to cover Collaborative Care Model services at Medicare-equivalent rates or above. Extend mandatory CoCM coverage to all 50 state Medicaid programs (currently 22 states), require commercial payers to cover CoCM codes without prior authorization, and establish minimum reimbursement floors indexed to the Medicare physician fee schedule. This addresses the fundamental economic barrier to integration: practices cannot sustainably deliver CoCM without adequate, predictable reimbursement.

**Evidence Base**: The University of Washington AIMS Center has documented that practices reaching 150+ patient caseloads with CoCM billing at Medicare rates achieve financial sustainability within 12-18 months (AIMS Center, 2023). In Washington State, Medicaid CoCM coverage has been associated with a 3.5x increase in CoCM adoption compared to non-coverage states (Washington State Health Care Authority, 2022). Medicare CoCM billing has grown 30% year-over-year since 2017 but remains limited by practice awareness and infrastructure. The Minnesota Diamond Initiative demonstrated that multi-payer CoCM alignment (six health plans agreeing on unified payment) accelerated adoption more than any single-payer initiative (Rossom et al., *Psychiatric Services*, 2017).

**Implementation**: Federal legislation mandating Medicaid CoCM coverage (modeled on the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act framework); CMS rulemaking to update Medicare CoCM rates annually and indexed to the Medicare Economic Index; state insurance commissioner enforcement of commercial coverage mandates; prohibition on prior authorization for CoCM codes (paralleling prior authorization reforms in several states for other services).

**Cost/Resources**: Estimated $2.4 billion annually in new Medicaid CoCM spending (based on 26% of adult Medicaid beneficiaries with mental health conditions, 30% engagement rate, average CoCM cost of $1,800/patient/year). Partially offset by reductions in ED use and hospitalization estimated at $1.2-1.8 billion (Congressional Budget Office methodology). Net cost declines over time as integration infrastructure matures and downstream savings accumulate.

**Timeline**: Legislation in Year 1; Medicaid implementation over Years 2-3; commercial payer compliance by Year 3.

**Challenges**: State Medicaid budget concerns; commercial payer resistance to new mandates; need for practices to build infrastructure before billing; risk that mandated coverage without workforce investment produces billing without clinical transformation.

**Impact Metrics**: Percentage of primary care practices billing CoCM codes (target: 25% by Year 5, 60% by Year 10); Medicaid CoCM encounter volume; average CoCM reimbursement rate relative to Medicare benchmark; patient-level PHQ-9/GAD-7 outcomes in CoCM-enrolled populations.

### Solution 2: Behavioral Health Care Manager Workforce Pipeline

**Description**: Establish a new federal Behavioral Health Care Manager Corps, modeled on the National Health Service Corps (NHSC), that trains, certifies, and deploys BHCMs to primary care practices in underserved areas. Create a standardized BHCM certification through collaboration between SAMHSA, HRSA, and professional associations (NASW, ANA, NBCC). Fund 10,000 new BHCM training positions over five years through competitive grants to schools of social work, nursing, and counseling. Include loan repayment incentives for BHCMs who serve in Mental Health Professional Shortage Areas for at least three years.

**Evidence Base**: The BHCM role is central to CoCM but has no standardized training pathway. Fewer than 10,000 professionals currently serve in this capacity nationally (AIMS Center estimate, 2023). The NHSC model has demonstrated success in deploying health professionals to shortage areas through loan repayment and scholarship incentives, with over 20,000 clinicians currently serving in NHSC-supported positions (HRSA, 2024). The UK's Improving Access to Psychological Therapies (IAPT) program provides a successful international model: IAPT has trained and deployed tens of thousands of psychological wellbeing practitioners since 2008, now treating over 1.2 million people annually with a 50% recovery rate (NHS Digital, 2022).

**Implementation**: HRSA administers the new corps; certification standards developed by an interagency working group including SAMHSA, HRSA, AIMS Center, NASW, ANA, and NBCC; training programs hosted at accredited institutions with clinical practica in integrated primary care settings; accelerated 12-18 month certification pathway for experienced social workers, nurses, and counselors who already hold relevant graduate credentials.

**Cost/Resources**: $1.5 billion over five years ($300 million/year) for training stipends, loan repayment, and program administration. Estimated cost per BHCM trained: $75,000 (2-year certification program including clinical placement). Loan repayment of up to $50,000 per BHCM serving 3+ years in MHPSA, modeled on NHSC loan repayment program.

**Timeline**: Program design and accreditation standards in Year 1; first cohort enrollment in Year 2; first 2,000 certified BHCMs deployed by Year 3; 10,000 by Year 7.

**Challenges**: Developing standardized certification acceptable to all states; recruiting candidates to underserved areas; ensuring training program quality; competing with higher-paying positions in private behavioral health practice; maintaining retention beyond the service commitment period.

**Impact Metrics**: Number of certified BHCMs; geographic distribution relative to MHPSAs; practice-level patient registry caseload sizes; BHCM retention rates at 3 and 5 years; patient outcomes in practices with BHCM Corps members.

### Solution 3: Statewide Psychiatric Access Programs in All 50 States

**Description**: Expand statewide psychiatric access programs (modeled on the Massachusetts Child Psychiatry Access Program, MCPAP) to all 50 states for both adult and child populations. These programs provide real-time teleconsultation to any primary care provider in the state, enabling PCPs to receive same-day psychiatric guidance without patient referral. Fund through a combination of federal grants and state Medicaid administrative matching. Require all programs to include adult psychiatric consultation (currently available in fewer than 15 states) in addition to child/adolescent services.

**Evidence Base**: MCPAP, launched in 2004, has been replicated in over 40 states for child psychiatry through the HRSA Pediatric Mental Health Care Access (PMHCA) program. Studies show MCPAP reduced child psychiatry wait times from 6-8 months to same-day consultation and increased PCP comfort with prescribing psychotropic medications by 60% (Sarvet et al., *Pediatrics*, 2010). Michigan's MC3 program has demonstrated similar results for both child and adolescent psychiatric consultation. Adult psychiatric access programs exist in fewer than 15 states and cover fewer populations, despite evidence that the model is equally applicable to adult primary care.

**Implementation**: HRSA competitive grants to states for program establishment or expansion; model program specifications based on MCPAP, MC3, and Project ECHO frameworks; mandatory participation of all Medicaid-enrolled primary care providers; 30-minute response time standard for telephone consultation.

**Cost/Resources**: $500 million over five years ($100 million/year in federal grants plus state match). Average state program operating budget: $3-8 million/year depending on state size and population density.

**Timeline**: Grant competition in Year 1; first new state programs operational in Year 2; all 50 states with adult and child programs by Year 5.

**Challenges**: Recruiting sufficient consulting psychiatrists (addressed partly by the consultative efficiency of the model); ensuring program sustainability after federal grant funding ends; adapting models for states with very low psychiatrist density (addressed through interstate teleconsultation agreements).

**Impact Metrics**: Number of states with operational adult psychiatric access programs; consultation volume per state; PCP utilization rates; time to consultation; patient outcomes (PHQ-9/GAD-7 score improvement) for patients whose care was guided by consultation.

### Solution 4: Integration-Ready EHR Standards

**Description**: Require all EHR systems certified under the ONC Health IT Certification Program to include core behavioral health integration functionality: patient registries with population-based tracking, behavioral health screening templates (PHQ-9, GAD-7, AUDIT-C, C-SSRS) integrated into primary care workflows, shared care plan modules for PCP-BHCM-psychiatrist collaboration, CoCM billing automation (monthly time calculation and code generation), and data exchange between behavioral health and primary care records using FHIR-based interoperability standards. Establish these requirements as part of the existing ONC certification criteria under 21st Century Cures Act authority.

**Evidence Base**: Fewer than 20% of primary care EHR systems currently support CoCM workflows (ONC Health IT Dashboard, 2023). Practices adopting CoCM frequently cite EHR limitations as a top-3 barrier (AIMS Center implementation survey, 2023). EHR-integrated screening has been shown to increase depression screening rates from 25-40% to 80-95% in health systems that implemented it (Commonwealth Fund, 2023). Kaiser Permanente and the VA, both of which have built custom EHR behavioral health functionality, achieve screening rates above 80%, while practices using standard commercial EHR products average 25-40%.

**Implementation**: ONC rulemaking under existing 21st Century Cures Act authority; EHR vendor compliance timeline of 24 months from final rule; certification testing for behavioral health functionality; ONC technical assistance grants for smaller vendors.

**Cost/Resources**: Minimal federal cost (regulatory action); vendor compliance costs estimated at $50-200 million across major EHR companies (absorbed in product development cycles). Practice-level cost: included in standard EHR licensing. ONC grants of $25 million over 3 years for small vendor compliance support.

**Timeline**: Proposed rule in Year 1; final rule in Year 2; vendor compliance deadline in Year 4.

**Challenges**: EHR vendor resistance (particularly from smaller vendors); potential burden on small vendors that may be addressed through extended compliance timelines; defining minimum functional requirements without stifling innovation; ensuring that registry functionality is usable and not merely checkbox-compliant.

**Impact Metrics**: Percentage of certified EHRs meeting behavioral health integration standards; practice-level registry utilization; screening rate changes in practices using upgraded EHRs; care manager satisfaction with EHR integration tools.

### Solution 5: FQHC Integration Mandate with Enhanced Funding

**Description**: Require all FQHCs to achieve Level 5-6 integration (fully integrated behavioral health) within five years as a condition of Section 330 grant funding. Provide $3 billion in targeted integration capacity funding over five years to support workforce hiring, training, EHR upgrades, and telehealth infrastructure. Establish integration performance metrics as part of the Uniform Data System (UDS) reporting requirements, including behavioral health screening rates, depression remission rates, follow-up after positive screen, and care manager caseload ratios.

**Evidence Base**: FQHCs serve 30.5 million patients, disproportionately low-income and minority (63.5% racial/ethnic minorities, 91% below 200% FPL), and 78% already offer some behavioral health services (HRSA UDS, 2023). However, fewer than 15% have achieved full integration (NACHC estimate, 2023). FQHCs that have implemented CoCM report 30-40% higher depression remission rates than those without integration (Reiss-Brennan et al., *Journal of General Internal Medicine*, 2016). The FQHC infrastructure, including community boards, sliding-fee scales, and Medicaid/Medicare enhanced reimbursement, makes FQHCs the ideal setting for integration scaling.

**Implementation**: HRSA rulemaking updating Section 330 grant requirements; phased compliance timeline (Year 1-2 for large FQHCs with 500+ patients per site, Year 3-5 for smaller sites); dedicated supplemental grants for integration infrastructure; SAMHSA-HRSA CIHS technical assistance for all transitioning FQHCs.

**Cost/Resources**: $3 billion over five years ($600 million/year) for integration grants; offset by expected reductions in ED use by FQHC patient populations (estimated $800 million over five years). FQHC integration grants would cover BHCM and psychiatric consultant hiring, EHR upgrades, telehealth equipment, training, and quality measurement infrastructure.

**Timeline**: Rulemaking in Year 1; first-wave compliance for large FQHCs by Year 3; universal compliance by Year 5.

**Challenges**: Small and rural FQHCs may struggle to recruit behavioral health staff (addressed through BHCM Corps deployment and telehealth-enabled CoCM); telehealth may be necessary where in-person co-location is infeasible (addressed through broadband investment and rural telehealth hub models); need to avoid unfunded mandates (addressed through dedicated $3 billion funding stream).

**Impact Metrics**: Percentage of FQHCs at Level 5-6 integration; behavioral health screening rates in UDS data; depression remission rates; patient satisfaction with integrated services; care manager-to-patient ratios; ED utilization for behavioral health among FQHC patients.

### Solution 6: Project ECHO Expansion for Behavioral Health

**Description**: Fund a national expansion of Project ECHO (Extension for Community Healthcare Outcomes) hubs focused on behavioral health integration. Establish 100 new ECHO hubs at academic medical centers, with each hub serving 200-500 primary care practices through telementoring in depression, anxiety, substance use disorder, PTSD, and child/adolescent behavioral health. Provide ongoing CME/CE credit for participating providers. Create specialty ECHO tracks for pediatric behavioral health, SUD/MAT management, and geriatric depression.

**Evidence Base**: Project ECHO was developed at the University of New Mexico by Sanjeev Arora and has been replicated in over 800 programs across 50 states and 45 countries (ECHO Institute, 2024). The model uses a hub-and-spoke framework where specialist teams at academic centers provide case-based telementoring to community providers, building capacity over time. Studies show ECHO participants achieve outcomes equivalent to specialist-treated patients for hepatitis C (Arora et al., *NEJM*, 2011) and behavioral health conditions. ECHO's didactic-plus-case-consultation format builds provider knowledge and confidence more effectively than traditional continuing education because it provides ongoing mentoring relationships rather than one-time instruction.

**Implementation**: HRSA competitive grants to academic medical centers for ECHO hub establishment; standardized behavioral health integration curriculum developed with AIMS Center, CFHA, and National Council; connection to BHCM training pipeline; partnership with existing ECHO infrastructure.

**Cost/Resources**: $250 million over five years ($50 million/year). Average ECHO hub cost: $500,000-$1.5 million/year. Cost per participating practice: approximately $2,000-$5,000/year. CME/CE credit administration adds minimal cost through existing accreditation bodies.

**Timeline**: Grant competition in Year 1; first 30 hubs operational in Year 2; 100 hubs serving 20,000+ practices by Year 5.

**Challenges**: Maintaining provider engagement over time (addressed through CME credit and practice-based learning community design); measuring clinical impact at practice level; ensuring sustainability beyond federal funding (addressed by embedding ECHO into academic medical center continuing education infrastructure).

**Impact Metrics**: Number of ECHO hubs and participating practices; provider knowledge and confidence scores (pre/post); patient-level clinical outcomes in ECHO-participating practices; prescribing pattern changes; proportion of practices with sustained ECHO participation at 2+ years.

### Solution 7: Medicaid Behavioral Health Carve-In Incentive

**Description**: Provide enhanced federal Medicaid matching (10 percentage point FMAP increase for 5 years) to states that eliminate behavioral health carve-outs and integrate behavioral health benefits into general managed care contracts. Carve-in plans must include CoCM coverage, integration quality metrics in managed care contracts, and network adequacy requirements for behavioral health providers in primary care settings. Establish a transition support program through SAMHSA-HRSA CIHS to assist states with the administrative, contractual, and clinical dimensions of carve-in implementation.

**Evidence Base**: States that have carved behavioral health into general Medicaid managed care have generally seen improved care coordination, reduced administrative complexity, and better integration outcomes. A 2019 MACPAC analysis found that carve-out plans had 23% lower access rates for mental health services (MACPAC, 2019). Washington, Oregon, and Arizona have demonstrated successful carve-in transitions with improved integration metrics. Oregon's Coordinated Care Organizations, which integrate physical, behavioral, and dental health under global budgets, have shown particular promise in reducing ED utilization for behavioral health conditions.

**Implementation**: CMS State Plan Amendment approval process; enhanced FMAP authorized by Congress; state implementation support from SAMHSA-HRSA CIHS; 3-year transition period for states to restructure managed care contracts.

**Cost/Resources**: Estimated $1.5-2.5 billion annually in enhanced federal match (depending on state uptake). Cost-neutral or cost-saving over 10 years as integration reduces total cost of care. Transition support grants of $5-15 million per state.

**Timeline**: Legislation in Year 1; first states transitioning in Year 2-3; target 35+ states carved in by Year 7.

**Challenges**: Strong MBHO lobbying opposition (Carelon Behavioral Health, Optum Behavioral Health, and others have significant financial interest in maintaining carve-outs); state administrative transition complexity; ensuring network adequacy during transition period; managing disruption for patients currently served through carve-out networks.

**Impact Metrics**: Number of states with carved-in behavioral health; Medicaid behavioral health access rates; emergency department utilization for behavioral health conditions; total cost of care per beneficiary; proportion of Medicaid primary care practices offering integrated behavioral health.

### Solution 8: Training Transformation Grants

**Description**: Fund curriculum reform in medical schools, family medicine residencies, internal medicine residencies, pediatric residencies, and behavioral health graduate programs to embed integrated care training as a core competency. Require ACGME-accredited primary care residencies to include structured CoCM training (minimum 80 hours over 3 years, including registry management and caseload review experience). Fund 200 integrated care training grants to schools of medicine, social work, psychology, and nursing over five years. Create an interprofessional education requirement in which medical, social work, psychology, and nursing students train together in integrated care settings.

**Evidence Base**: Only 34% of family medicine residency programs offer structured CoCM training (AAFP, 2022). Only 12% of clinical psychology internship programs include primary care integration rotations (APA, 2023). The training pipeline produces clinicians who are unprepared for integrated practice, perpetuating the culture of separation. Programs that have reformed their curricula, such as the University of Washington's family medicine residency (which requires CoCM rotations) and the University of Massachusetts' interprofessional primary care behavioral health training program, have demonstrated that graduates are significantly more likely to practice in integrated settings.

**Implementation**: HRSA Graduate Psychology Education grants and Title VII primary care training grants expanded to include integration requirements; ACGME residency review committee standards updated to include integrated care competencies; CSWE social work accreditation standards updated to include primary care integration field placement options; APA Commission on Accreditation updated to encourage primary care psychology training.

**Cost/Resources**: $400 million over five years ($80 million/year). Average grant: $500,000-$1 million per institution over 3 years. ACGME standards update is regulatory (no direct cost) but requires curriculum development investment at programs.

**Timeline**: Grant competition in Year 1; first cohort of reformed programs in Year 2; all primary care residencies meeting integration training standards by Year 7.

**Challenges**: Academic inertia; competing curriculum priorities (primary care residencies already face curriculum compression); accreditation body timelines; resistance from specialty-oriented faculty.

**Impact Metrics**: Percentage of primary care residencies with integrated care training; percentage of behavioral health graduates with primary care integration experience; trainee competency assessments (validated scales); post-training practice integration rates at 5 years.

## International Models

### United Kingdom: Improving Access to Psychological Therapies (IAPT)

**Description**: The NHS launched IAPT in 2008 as a national program to provide evidence-based psychological therapies for depression and anxiety, primarily through general practice (primary care) settings. IAPT trains thousands of psychological wellbeing practitioners who deliver stepped care, including guided self-help and cognitive behavioral therapy, in GP surgeries. The program was championed by economist Lord Richard Layard and psychologist David Clark, who made the economic case that treating depression would pay for itself through reduced disability benefits and increased employment.

**Results**: By 2022, IAPT was treating over 1.2 million people annually with a 50% recovery rate for those completing treatment (NHS Digital, 2022). Wait times for initial assessment were reduced to an average of 6 days (vs. months for traditional specialty referral). The program has been credited with reducing disability benefit claims and increasing employment among participants. Notably, IAPT publishes session-by-session outcome data publicly, creating transparency and accountability unprecedented in mental health services.

**Applicability to U.S.**: IAPT demonstrates that a national workforce training and deployment program can dramatically increase access to primary care-based psychological treatment. The U.S. would need to adapt for its multi-payer system, but the core model of training a new cadre of workers for stepped care in primary care settings directly parallels the proposed BHCM Corps. IAPT's emphasis on measurement-based care, treatment-to-target, and public outcomes reporting aligns directly with CoCM principles.

### Canada: Quebec Collaborative Care Networks

**Description**: Quebec has implemented province-wide collaborative care networks that embed mental health professionals in family medicine groups (Groupes de medecine de famille, GMFs). Every GMF has access to a psychologist or social worker for integrated behavioral health, funded through the provincial health insurance system.

**Results**: A 2019 evaluation found that GMFs with embedded behavioral health achieved 35% higher rates of follow-up care for depression and 28% lower emergency department use for mental health conditions compared to GMFs without embedded services (Fleury et al., *Canadian Journal of Psychiatry*, 2019).

**Applicability to U.S.**: The Quebec model demonstrates integration at scale within a single-payer system. While the U.S. cannot replicate single-payer financing, the model's use of standardized embedding protocols and province-wide quality metrics provides a template for state-level integration mandates. Oregon's CCO model shares structural similarities with Quebec's approach.

### Australia: Primary Health Networks and Access to Allied Psychological Services (ATAPS/AMHSP)

**Description**: Australia funds integrated behavioral health in primary care through Primary Health Networks (PHNs), which commission mental health services for GP practices, including psychological services, psychiatric consultation, and care coordination. The Australian Medicare Benefits Schedule reimburses GPs for mental health treatment plans and up to 10 individual allied health sessions per year.

**Results**: Australia has achieved 70%+ GP screening rates for depression using routine screening mandates and incentive payments (Australian Institute of Health and Welfare, 2022). The system provides universal access to primary care-based mental health treatment, though regional access disparities persist.

**Applicability to U.S.**: The Australian model demonstrates how payment incentives for GPs (similar to quality bonus payments) combined with commissioned community-based services can drive high screening and treatment rates. The PHN commissioning model could inform how U.S. Accountable Care Organizations or state Medicaid programs contract for integrated services.

## Pilot Programs and Demonstrations

- **Cherokee Health Systems (Tennessee)**: One of the oldest and most studied integrated care models in the U.S., Cherokee has provided fully integrated primary and behavioral health care since the 1980s under the leadership of Dennis Freeman and Parinda Khatri. Depression remission rates exceed 50%, and total cost of care is 20% below comparable non-integrated systems (Cherokee Health Systems, 2022). Cherokee's PCBH model has been studied as a complementary approach to CoCM for practices seeking rapid integration.
- **Intermountain Healthcare Mental Health Integration**: Intermountain implemented CoCM across its primary care network in Utah, demonstrating that large health system adoption is feasible. Results showed 46% depression remission at 12 months and $2,800 per patient savings in total medical costs (Reiss-Brennan et al., *Journal of General Internal Medicine*, 2016). Intermountain's work provided key evidence that CoCM could be implemented outside of research settings.
- **Diamond Initiative (Minnesota)**: A statewide CoCM implementation supported by six major health plans, demonstrating multi-payer collaboration. Participating clinics achieved 40% depression response rates, and six health plans agreed on unified CoCM payment (Rossom et al., *Psychiatric Services*, 2017). The Diamond Initiative remains the primary example of multi-payer alignment behind a single integration model.
- **AIMS Center Mental Health Integration Program (MHIP)**: The University of Washington AIMS Center has supported CoCM implementation in over 100 clinical sites through its structured implementation program, providing training, consultation, registry support, and quality monitoring. MHIP outcomes data shows average depression remission rates of 45-50% across diverse settings.
- **Veterans Health Administration PCBH**: The VA has embedded behavioral health consultants in over 900 Patient-Aligned Care Teams, creating the largest single PCBH implementation. VA data shows improved depression screening rates (over 90%), increased same-day behavioral health access, and high patient and provider satisfaction with the embedded model.

## Sequencing and Prioritization

### Quick Wins (0-2 years)

- Mandate Medicaid CoCM coverage in all 50 states through federal legislation
- Launch BHCM certification standards development through SAMHSA-HRSA interagency process
- Issue ONC proposed rule for EHR behavioral health integration standards
- Expand existing Project ECHO and psychiatric access program funding through supplemental appropriation
- Establish enhanced FMAP for Medicaid carve-in states

### Medium-term Reforms (2-5 years)

- Deploy first 5,000 certified BHCMs through the BHCM Corps
- Achieve all-state coverage of adult and child psychiatric access programs
- Complete FQHC integration mandate for large health centers
- Implement Medicaid carve-in incentive; target 20 states transitioned
- Update ACGME residency requirements for integrated care training
- Finalize EHR certification standards; begin vendor compliance period

### Long-term Transformation (5-10 years)

- Achieve 60%+ primary care practices delivering integrated behavioral health (Level 4-6)
- Complete FQHC universal integration (all sites at Level 5-6)
- Deploy 10,000 BHCMs; achieve self-sustaining training pipeline
- Behavioral health integration established as a core competency for all primary care residency graduates
- Measurement-based care with registry tracking standard of practice in majority of primary care settings
- National depression treatment gap narrowed from ~57% untreated to under 35%

## Complementary Policies

- **Mental Health Parity enforcement**: Strengthened enforcement of the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act ensures that integrated services are covered without discriminatory limitations. Recent CMS proposed rules on parity compliance reporting create an enforcement mechanism that directly supports integration.
- **Broadband expansion**: Rural broadband investment (Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act, 2021, $65 billion allocation) is essential for telehealth-enabled integration models. Integration advocacy should coordinate with broadband deployment to ensure that newly connected rural areas receive telehealth integration support.
- **988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline**: Robust crisis services reduce the burden on primary care while providing a safety net for patients identified through screening. Integration of 988 follow-up with primary care behavioral health teams can create a continuous care pathway from crisis to ongoing treatment.
- **Scope of practice reform**: Expanding nurse practitioner and psychologist prescribing authority in all states reduces dependence on psychiatrist supply for medication management in integrated settings. Currently, 12 states restrict NP independent practice, and psychologist prescribing is authorized in only 5 states plus the DoD, IHS, and VA.
- **Graduate medical education reform**: Increasing psychiatry residency positions (currently 1,740 PGY-1 positions nationally, NRMP 2024) is essential for long-term consultation capacity. A 15% increase in psychiatry positions (approximately 260 additional PGY-1 slots) would produce 1,000+ additional psychiatrists over a decade.
- **SUD treatment integration**: The X-waiver elimination (2023) and 42 CFR Part 2 alignment (2024) create the conditions for integrating medication-assisted treatment for opioid use disorder into primary care behavioral health. Practices with CoCM infrastructure can extend the model to include SUD treatment, using the same registry-based, measurement-based approach.
