# Workplace Mental Health: History

## Historical Development

The relationship between work and mental health has been recognized for centuries, but systematic policy responses are remarkably recent. The evolution of workplace mental health policy in the United States reflects broader shifts in understanding of mental illness, labor rights, occupational safety, and the role of employers in worker well-being.

---

### Early Occupational Medicine and Industrial Welfare (1800s-1940s): Work as a Source of Harm

- **Industrial Revolution impacts**: The emergence of factory labor in the 19th century brought attention to the physical hazards of work but largely ignored psychological effects. "Industrial fatigue" and "nervous exhaustion" were noted by physicians but attributed to individual weakness rather than working conditions. The concept of "neurasthenia," popularized by neurologist George Beard in 1869, described a constellation of fatigue, anxiety, and depression attributed to the pace of modern life and industrial work. While the diagnosis was applied primarily to middle-class professionals, it represented an early recognition that the conditions of work could produce psychological harm.

- **Progressive Era reforms**: Labor activists and reformers in the early 1900s focused primarily on physical safety (child labor, factory fires, mine collapses), but some voices connected working conditions to psychological harm. Florence Kelley and the National Consumers' League advocated for shorter work hours in part because of the mental toll of excessive labor. The Triangle Shirtwaist Fire (1911), while primarily a physical safety disaster that killed 146 workers, generated lasting psychological trauma among survivors and witnesses that drew attention to the human cost of unregulated industry.

- **Early welfare capitalism**: Beginning in the 1910s, some large employers (Ford Motor Company, National Cash Register) introduced "welfare secretaries" and social workers to address personal problems affecting worker productivity. These programs were paternalistic and focused on moral conduct, alcohol use, and family stability. Ford's $5-a-day wage (1914) was conditional on workers passing inspections of their home life by the company's "Sociological Department," an early example of employers linking personal well-being to employment while asserting control over workers' lives.

- **World War I and shell shock**: The psychological casualties of World War I forced recognition that environmental stressors could cause mental breakdown in otherwise healthy individuals. An estimated 80,000 British soldiers were treated for "shell shock," and similar numbers of American troops displayed psychological symptoms. This insight eventually influenced occupational thinking, though slowly. The war also accelerated psychological testing in employment, with the Army Alpha and Beta tests establishing precedent for using psychological assessment in workforce management.

- **Hawthorne Studies (1924-1932)**: Western Electric's landmark research at its Hawthorne Works plant in Cicero, Illinois, demonstrated that social and psychological factors profoundly influenced worker productivity and satisfaction, establishing the human relations school of management. The studies, conducted by Elton Mayo and colleagues from Harvard Business School, found that worker productivity increased when management paid attention to worker concerns and facilitated social interaction, regardless of changes to physical conditions. While the studies' methodology has been critiqued, their impact on management theory was transformative and laid groundwork for recognizing psychological factors in workplace performance.

- **World War II industrial psychology**: The demands of wartime production created unprecedented interest in worker motivation, fatigue, and morale. The U.S. government employed psychologists to study factory worker productivity and develop interventions to reduce absenteeism and turnover. After the war, these industrial psychologists entered the private sector, founding the field of organizational psychology.

- **Workers' compensation emergence**: By the 1940s, all states had workers' compensation systems, but coverage was limited almost exclusively to physical injuries. Mental health claims were virtually unrecognized. The few states that considered psychological injuries required a physical injury nexus, a requirement that persists in approximately 20 states to this day.

### The Birth of EAPs and Occupational Mental Health (1940s-1970s): Alcohol, Productivity, and the Troubled Employee

- **Occupational Alcoholism Programs (OAPs)**: In the 1940s-1950s, companies including DuPont, Eastman Kodak, and Consolidated Edison established formal programs to address alcoholism among employees. These programs focused on identifying "troubled employees" whose job performance had deteriorated. The key innovation was "constructive confrontation," in which supervisors would confront employees about declining performance and offer referral to counseling as an alternative to disciplinary action. By 1959, an estimated 50 major U.S. corporations had formal occupational alcoholism programs.

- **Kemper Insurance model (1962)**: Kemper Insurance developed one of the first comprehensive occupational alcoholism programs with a "broad brush" approach, recognizing that many employee problems beyond alcohol, including marital difficulties, financial stress, and emotional problems, affected performance. This "broad brush" model became the conceptual foundation for the transition from narrow alcoholism programs to comprehensive Employee Assistance Programs.

- **Community Mental Health Act (1963)**: President Kennedy signed the Community Mental Health Centers Act, shifting mental health care from state institutions to community-based settings. While not directly targeting workplace mental health, this law expanded the availability of outpatient mental health services that EAPs would eventually refer workers to, and it established the principle that mental health treatment should be accessible in the community rather than confined to institutions.

- **Hughes Act (1970)**: The Comprehensive Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism Prevention, Treatment, and Rehabilitation Act (P.L. 91-616) established the National Institute on Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism (NIAAA) and authorized the first federal funding for occupational alcoholism programs, providing the financial foundation for what would become EAPs. NIAAA's Occupational Programs Branch funded two "Thunderbird" consultants in each state to promote workplace alcoholism programs, creating the infrastructure for EAP development nationwide.

- **OSHA creation (1970)**: The Occupational Safety and Health Act created OSHA and established the general duty clause requiring employers to maintain workplaces "free from recognized hazards." The Act focused primarily on physical hazards, and psychosocial risks were not considered in the original framework. Congressional debate over the Act focused exclusively on injuries, illnesses, and deaths from physical workplace conditions. The omission of psychosocial hazards from OSHA's original mandate would shape workplace mental health policy for the next half-century.

- **Transition from OAPs to EAPs (1970s)**: Occupational Alcoholism Programs gradually broadened their scope to address all personal problems affecting job performance, rebranding as Employee Assistance Programs. The Employee Assistance Professionals Association (EAPA) was founded in 1971. The shift was driven partly by the recognition that employee performance problems had multiple causes, and partly by the desire to reduce the stigma associated with programs labeled as "alcoholism" services. By the late 1970s, the term "Employee Assistance Program" had become standard industry language.

- **Drug-Free Workplace Act (1988)**: This federal law required all organizations receiving federal contracts or grants to maintain drug-free workplace programs, further expanding EAP adoption as a compliance mechanism. The Act accelerated EAP growth among federal contractors, reaching an estimated 40% of the U.S. workforce by 1990.

### Legal Foundations and Parity Movements (1980s-2000s): Disability Rights and Insurance Reform

- **First EAP standards (1980s)**: EAPA published its first professional standards and program guidelines, establishing the "core technology" of EAPs: identification of employees with performance problems, constructive confrontation, referral, and follow-up. By 1985, an estimated 31% of the U.S. workforce had access to EAPs (Roman and Blum, 1988). During the 1980s, the EAP industry also underwent a fundamental structural transformation from internal, employer-staffed programs to external vendor contracts. This shift reduced costs but also reduced the depth of service, as external vendors faced pressure to minimize per-case costs and session counts.

- **Americans with Disabilities Act (1990)**: The ADA represented a watershed moment for workplace mental health by legally defining mental impairments as disabilities warranting reasonable accommodation. Title I prohibited employment discrimination against qualified individuals with disabilities, including those with mental health conditions. However, implementation was hampered by narrow judicial interpretations of who qualified as "disabled."

- **Family and Medical Leave Act (1993)**: FMLA provided up to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave for serious health conditions, including mental health conditions. While a significant advance, the exclusion of employers with fewer than 50 employees, the requirement for 12 months of employment, and the lack of paid leave limited its reach.

- **Mental Health Parity Act (1996)**: The first federal mental health parity law prohibited group health plans from imposing lower annual or lifetime dollar limits on mental health benefits compared to medical/surgical benefits. However, it did not require employers to offer mental health coverage and did not address treatment limitations or cost-sharing differences.

- **ADA judicial narrowing (1999-2002)**: A series of Supreme Court decisions, including *Sutton v. United Air Lines* (1999) and *Toyota Motor Manufacturing v. Williams* (2002), significantly narrowed the definition of disability, making it harder for individuals with mental health conditions to qualify for ADA protections.

- **Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act (2008)**: MHPAEA strengthened the 1996 law by requiring parity in financial requirements and treatment limitations for mental health and substance use disorder benefits. It addressed copayments, deductibles, visit limits, and prior authorization requirements. Final rules were issued in 2013. However, enforcement remained challenging, particularly regarding non-quantitative treatment limitations (NQTLs) such as prior authorization requirements, step therapy protocols, and network adequacy standards that insurers used to effectively limit mental health access while complying with the letter of the law. The Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2021 required health plans to perform comparative analyses of NQTLs and submit them to federal regulators, but compliance has been uneven.

- **ADA Amendments Act (2008)**: The ADAAA reversed the Supreme Court's narrow interpretations by broadening the definition of disability, explicitly including episodic conditions and conditions in remission, and shifting focus from whether someone is disabled to whether discrimination occurred. This significantly strengthened protections for individuals with mental health conditions. Under the ADAAA, conditions such as major depressive disorder, bipolar disorder, PTSD, generalized anxiety disorder, and OCD are more readily recognized as disabilities warranting reasonable accommodation. The EEOC issued updated guidance in 2011 applying the broadened definition to mental health conditions, establishing that conditions that substantially limit major life activities (including concentrating, thinking, communicating, and interacting with others) qualify for protection.

- **Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act (GINA, 2008)**: While primarily addressing genetic information, GINA's prohibitions on using health-related information in employment decisions indirectly supported workplace mental health by reinforcing the principle that health conditions should not affect employment status.

### The Burnout Era and Pandemic Transformation (2010s-Present): From Individual Wellness to Systemic Change

- **Rise of corporate wellness (2010s)**: The Affordable Care Act (2010) incentivized employer wellness programs by allowing employers to offer premium discounts of up to 30% for participation (increased to 50% for tobacco cessation). This led to rapid expansion of wellness programs, with mental health components added alongside existing physical health screenings and fitness incentives. However, most mental health offerings focused on individual resilience, mindfulness, and stress management rather than organizational change. The corporate wellness industry grew to over $20 billion by 2023 (Grand View Research, 2023). A parallel digital health boom emerged in the mid-2010s, with venture-funded startups (Calm, Headspace, Talkspace, BetterHelp, Lyra Health, Spring Health, Ginger) offering employer-packaged mental health apps and virtual therapy platforms.

- **OSHA ergonomics standard controversy (2000-2001)**: The defeat of OSHA's ergonomic hazard standard provides a critical historical precedent for psychosocial hazard regulation. OSHA finalized an ergonomics standard in November 2000, after a decade of rulemaking, but Congress used the Congressional Review Act to overturn it in March 2001. Business groups argued that the standard was overly broad, costly, and based on insufficient science, arguments identical to those now made against psychosocial hazard standards. The ergonomics defeat demonstrated the political difficulty of expanding OSHA's scope and shaped agency caution about pursuing new types of hazard regulation.

- **WHO burnout classification (2019)**: The World Health Organization included burnout in the ICD-11 as an "occupational phenomenon" resulting from "chronic workplace stress that has not been successfully managed." This classification explicitly linked burnout to workplace conditions rather than individual pathology, a significant conceptual shift.

- **COVID-19 pandemic (2020-2022)**: The pandemic fundamentally transformed workplace mental health discourse. Healthcare workers experienced PTSD rates comparable to combat veterans, with studies finding PTSD prevalence of 21-24% among ICU nurses (Shechter et al., *JAMA Network Open*, 2020). Remote work blurred boundaries between work and personal life, with 70% of remote workers reporting working on weekends and 45% reporting working longer hours than before the pandemic (Owl Labs, 2021). Essential workers, including grocery, transit, and warehouse workers, faced heightened anxiety with inadequate support, PPE shortages, and public hostility. The Great Resignation of 2021-2022, in which 47.4 million Americans voluntarily left their jobs (BLS, 2022), was widely attributed in part to burnout and poor working conditions. The pandemic also revealed stark disparities: workers able to work remotely reported better mental health outcomes than those required to be physically present, reinforcing the connection between working conditions and psychological well-being.

- **Dr. Lorna Breen Health Care Provider Protection Act (2022)**: Named for Dr. Lorna Breen, an emergency physician who died by suicide during the pandemic's first wave, this bipartisan federal law authorized $135 million in grants for healthcare worker mental health programs, training, and research. It represented the first federal legislation specifically addressing workplace mental health in a high-risk occupation and established a precedent for industry-specific intervention.

- **Surgeon General Framework (2022)**: U.S. Surgeon General Vivek Murthy released the "Framework for Workplace Mental Health and Well-Being" in October 2022, identifying five essentials: protection from harm, connection and community, work-life harmony, mattering at work, and opportunity for growth. While groundbreaking in scope, the framework was entirely voluntary.

- **WHO Workplace Mental Health Guidelines (2022)**: In September 2022, WHO released its first-ever guidelines on mental health at work, recommending organizational interventions to prevent psychosocial risks, training for managers, and accommodations for workers with mental health conditions. The guidelines explicitly called for regulatory action beyond voluntary employer initiatives. Key recommendations included: (a) organizational interventions to modify psychosocial risk factors; (b) training managers in mental health literacy; (c) individual-level interventions for workers with mental health conditions; (d) return-to-work programs after mental health leave; and (e) establishment of national policies on mental health at work. The guidelines were informed by a systematic review of evidence from 15,000 studies across 45 countries.

- **Australia psychosocial hazard regulations (2022-2023)**: Australia became the first major economy to adopt enforceable psychosocial hazard codes of practice across all states and territories, requiring employers to identify, assess, and control psychosocial hazards using the same risk management framework applied to physical hazards. Safe Work Australia developed a Model Code of Practice that identified 14 specific psychosocial hazards: job demands, low job control, poor support, lack of role clarity, poor organizational change management, low recognition and reward, poor organizational justice, traumatic events or material, remote or isolated work, poor physical environment, violence and aggression, bullying, harassment, and conflict or poor workplace relationships. The Australian approach is notable because it treats psychosocial hazards identically to physical hazards under existing work health and safety legislation, requiring no new statute. Violations carry penalties of up to AUD $3 million for corporations.

- **Canada's National Standard on Psychological Health and Safety (2013, updated 2023)**: Canada's CAN/CSA-Z1003 standard, while voluntary, became the world's first national standard for psychological health and safety in the workplace. Developed by the Mental Health Commission of Canada, it provides a framework for organizational assessment, action planning, and evaluation. Research on early adopters showed that organizations implementing the standard reported 27% reduction in short-term disability claims for mental health and 33% improvement in employee-reported psychological safety (Samra et al., *Journal of Occupational and Environmental Medicine*, 2020). Canada's experience demonstrates that voluntary national standards can drive meaningful adoption when supported by government promotion and employer champions.

- **State-level action (2023-2024)**: California enacted SB 553, the nation's first general industry workplace violence prevention standard, requiring all employers to develop written prevention plans that include psychological safety elements (effective July 2024). Oregon expanded workplace fairness protections for mental health leave. Multiple states enacted or expanded first responder PTSD presumption laws. New York launched a workplace mental health tax credit pilot for small businesses. These state-level actions represent a growing pattern of states filling the vacuum left by federal inaction, similar to the pattern that preceded the original OSHA Act in 1970.

- **Corporate mental health disclosure movement (2023-2024)**: Investor groups and ESG-focused stakeholders began pressing publicly traded companies to disclose workforce mental health metrics, including turnover rates, burnout survey results, and mental health benefit utilization. The Securities and Exchange Commission's evolving human capital disclosure requirements, while not yet mandating mental health-specific data, created an opening for standardized reporting that parallels the evolution of environmental and safety disclosure.

- **Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo (2024)**: The Supreme Court's decision overturning Chevron deference to agency interpretations of ambiguous statutes introduced significant new uncertainty about OSHA's authority to issue psychosocial hazard standards. Without Chevron deference, courts will independently interpret whether the OSH Act authorizes OSHA to regulate psychosocial risks, making robust congressional authorization potentially necessary for durable regulation.

## Evolution of Occupational Stress Research

The scientific understanding of workplace psychosocial hazards has evolved through several major theoretical frameworks:

### Job Demand-Control Model (Karasek, 1979)

Robert Karasek's landmark 1979 paper in *Administrative Science Quarterly* proposed that the combination of high job demands and low decision latitude (control) produces the highest levels of occupational stress and adverse health outcomes. The "high strain" quadrant (high demand, low control) has been consistently associated with elevated risk of cardiovascular disease, depression, anxiety, and musculoskeletal disorders across hundreds of studies over four decades. This model fundamentally shifted the focus from individual stress responses to organizational conditions as the primary drivers of work-related psychological harm.

### Effort-Reward Imbalance Model (Siegrist, 1996)

Johannes Siegrist's model proposed that the combination of high effort and low reward (encompassing financial compensation, esteem, and career security) creates health-damaging imbalances. This model complemented Karasek's by identifying additional organizational factors, particularly compensation, recognition, and job security, as independent predictors of occupational stress and disease.

### Psychological Safety (Edmondson, 1999)

Amy Edmondson's research at Harvard Business School introduced the concept of "psychological safety" as a team-level attribute: the shared belief that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking. Edmondson demonstrated that teams with higher psychological safety showed better learning behaviors, innovation, and error reporting. Google's Project Aristotle (2012) subsequently identified psychological safety as the strongest predictor of high-performing teams, popularizing the concept in corporate management.

### Copenhagen Psychosocial Questionnaire (COPSOQ, 2005)

Developed by the Danish National Research Centre for the Working Environment, COPSOQ provided the first comprehensive, validated instrument for measuring workplace psychosocial conditions. Now in its third iteration (COPSOQ III, 2018), the instrument measures 41 dimensions of the psychosocial work environment and has been validated in over 30 countries. COPSOQ became the primary assessment tool used in European and Australian psychosocial hazard regulations.

### WHO Classification of Burnout (ICD-11, 2019)

The World Health Organization's inclusion of burnout in the ICD-11 as an "occupational phenomenon" marked a pivotal conceptual shift. By defining burnout as resulting from "chronic workplace stress that has not been successfully managed" and characterizing it by exhaustion, cynicism, and reduced efficacy, the WHO explicitly located the cause in the workplace rather than the individual. This classification strengthened the case for organizational and regulatory responses.

## Lessons from History

### Pattern 1: The Arc from Individual Pathology to Systemic Recognition

Workplace mental health policy has followed a consistent pattern: problems are first attributed to individual weakness or character flaws (the "troubled employee"), then reframed as medical conditions requiring treatment (EAPs, ADA accommodations), and only much later recognized as systemic issues requiring organizational and regulatory responses. This pattern, visible in the evolution from occupational alcoholism programs to the Surgeon General's framework, mirrors the broader history of occupational safety, where physical hazards were also first attributed to "careless workers" before being recognized as systemic problems requiring employer responsibility. The shift from individual to systemic framing has accelerated since the COVID-19 pandemic, but translation into enforceable policy has not yet followed.

### Pattern 2: Voluntary Approaches Precede Regulation

In every area of occupational health, voluntary employer action has preceded regulatory mandates. OSHA standards for physical hazards followed decades of voluntary safety programs. The same pattern appears to be unfolding for psychosocial hazards: the Surgeon General's voluntary framework, WHO voluntary guidelines, and widespread corporate wellness adoption may be precursors to eventual enforceable standards.

### Pattern 3: Crisis as Catalyst

Major advances in workplace mental health policy have consistently followed crises. The Hughes Act followed growing alarm about workplace alcoholism's productivity costs. The ADA followed decades of disability rights activism. The MHPAEA followed insurance industry revelations about discriminatory coverage. The Surgeon General's framework followed the COVID-19 pandemic. The Dr. Lorna Breen Act followed the devastating mental health toll on healthcare workers during COVID-19. This pattern suggests that the current window of heightened attention may be the best opportunity for structural reform, but it also warns that crisis-driven policy windows are temporary and close when public attention shifts.

### Pattern 4: Legal Rights Without Enforcement Infrastructure

Each major legal advance for workplace mental health, including the ADA, FMLA, and MHPAEA, has been undermined by inadequate enforcement infrastructure. The EEOC is chronically underfunded relative to its caseload. FMLA excludes nearly half the workforce. MHPAEA compliance remains inconsistent. This pattern suggests that future reforms must include robust enforcement mechanisms to be effective.

### Pattern 5: Industry-Specific Crises Drive Targeted Legislation

The most successful legislative advances have often been targeted at specific high-sympathy occupations experiencing acute crises. The Dr. Lorna Breen Act (2022) passed with broad bipartisan support because it focused on healthcare workers during a pandemic. First responder PTSD presumption laws have passed in conservative and liberal states alike because of the recognized severity of occupational trauma in policing and firefighting. This pattern suggests that broad workplace mental health reform may advance most effectively through a series of occupation-specific measures that cumulatively establish precedent for universal standards.

### Pattern 6: The Pendulum Between Corporate Responsibility and Individual Blame

Throughout the history of workplace mental health, the dominant narrative has oscillated between recognizing corporate responsibility for working conditions and blaming individual workers for failing to cope. The welfare capitalism of the 1910s placed some responsibility on employers; the "troubled employee" framing of the 1960s-1970s individualized the problem; the ADA (1990) assigned employer accommodation duties; the corporate wellness boom of the 2010s again individualized solutions through resilience training and mindfulness apps; and the Surgeon General's Framework (2022) once again pointed toward organizational responsibility. The current evidence base overwhelmingly supports organizational-level intervention, but the political and economic incentives for individual-focused approaches remain powerful.

### Pattern 7: International Leadership Precedes U.S. Action

In every area of workplace mental health policy, international models have been developed and tested years or decades before the U.S. adopted similar measures. The EU Framework Directive on workplace health and safety (1989) covered psychosocial risks decades before any U.S. framework. The UK's HSE Management Standards (2004) preceded the Surgeon General's Framework (2022) by 18 years. Japan's mandatory Stress Check Program (2015) preceded any U.S. assessment mandate. Australia's enforceable psychosocial hazard codes (2022) remain unmatched in the U.S. This pattern suggests that international evidence and models are available to inform U.S. policy design, and that the barrier to adoption is political rather than evidentiary.

## EAP Industry Evolution

The transformation of Employee Assistance Programs from internal employer services to a multi-billion-dollar vendor industry has profoundly shaped workplace mental health:

| Era | Model | Typical Access | Session Limits | Provider |
|-----|-------|---------------|----------------|----------|
| 1940s-1960s | Occupational Alcoholism Programs | Supervisor referral only | Unlimited | On-site counselors |
| 1970s | Broad-brush internal EAPs | Supervisor and self-referral | Flexible | On-site professionals |
| 1980s-1990s | External vendor EAPs | Self-referral (telephonic) | 3-8 sessions | National vendor networks |
| 2000s | Managed care EAPs | Self-referral (telephonic/web) | 3-6 sessions | Integrated with health plans |
| 2010s | Digital-first EAPs | App-based, 24/7 | 3-8 sessions + digital tools | Tech-enabled vendors |
| 2020s | Hybrid models | Multi-channel (app, video, in-person) | Expanding (6-12 in premium models) | Mix of traditional and digital |

The shift to external vendors in the 1980s-1990s was driven by cost pressures, but it introduced a perverse incentive: vendors competing on per-employee-per-year pricing were rewarded for minimizing utilization and session counts. The average cost of an external EAP contract dropped from approximately $50 per employee per year in the 1980s to $12-$40 by the 2020s (EASNA, 2023), a decline that reflected both economies of scale and reduced service depth.

## The Arc of Federal Engagement

The history of federal engagement with workplace mental health can be characterized as a slow, halting progression from complete neglect to limited, voluntary guidance, with no enforceable standards yet achieved:

**Era 1: Neglect (1970-1990)** -- OSHA's creation ignored psychosocial hazards entirely. The EAP industry grew as a private-sector response to workplace distress, with no federal regulation of program quality, access, or outcomes. The federal role was limited to funding occupational alcoholism programs through NIAAA.

**Era 2: Accommodation (1990-2008)** -- The ADA and FMLA established the principle that workers with mental health conditions have legal rights to accommodation and leave. However, the framework was entirely reactive: it required employers to respond to individual workers who disclosed conditions and requested help, rather than preventing the conditions that cause harm.

**Era 3: Parity (2008-2019)** -- The MHPAEA and ADAAA strengthened the legal framework for insurance coverage and disability protection. The focus remained on ensuring access to treatment for individuals with diagnosed conditions, not on preventing workplace conditions from causing those conditions in the first place.

**Era 4: Awareness (2019-2022)** -- The WHO burnout classification, the COVID-19 pandemic, and the Surgeon General's Framework elevated workplace mental health to a mainstream policy issue. For the first time, the federal government acknowledged that workplace conditions cause psychological harm and that organizational-level change is necessary. However, all federal action remained voluntary.

**Era 5: Transition (2022-Present)** -- The current era is characterized by the tension between the growing evidence and international precedent for enforceable psychosocial hazard standards and the political, legal, and economic barriers to achieving them in the U.S. context. State-level action, industry-specific federal legislation, and the prospect of OSHA rulemaking represent potential pathways, but none has yet produced universal, enforceable workplace mental health protections.
