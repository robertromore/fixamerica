# Mental Health Insurance Parity: Solutions

Achieving genuine mental health insurance parity requires a multi-faceted approach that strengthens enforcement of existing law, closes regulatory loopholes, expands coverage to populations outside MHPAEA's current reach, and addresses the structural conditions -- particularly the reimbursement rate gap and provider shortage -- that enable insurers to maintain unequal access to behavioral health care.

---

## Reform Framework

### Guiding Principles

- Equal treatment in practice, not just on paper: Parity must be measured by outcomes (denial rates, network access, wait times), not only by benefit design
- Proactive enforcement: Regulators must audit plans before patients complain, not after
- Transparency: Plans must publicly report parity compliance data so consumers, employers, and regulators can compare performance
- Universal coverage: Parity protections should apply to all insurance products, not only employer-sponsored and individual market plans
- Provider viability: Networks cannot be adequate if reimbursement rates drive providers away; rate parity is a prerequisite for access parity
- Directory accuracy: Network adequacy metrics are meaningless if provider directories do not reflect actual availability; ghost networks must be addressed alongside network size requirements
- Enforcement durability: Key enforcement provisions should be codified in statute rather than relying solely on regulatory authority vulnerable to legal challenge or administrative reversal

### Theory of Change

Parity violations persist because they are profitable, difficult to detect, and rarely punished. Reform must change this calculus by (1) making violations detectable through mandatory data reporting, (2) making them costly through meaningful penalties, (3) making compliance achievable through standardized methodologies and technical assistance, and (4) expanding the behavioral health workforce so that parity in network design translates into parity in provider availability. When non-compliance is more expensive than compliance, plans will comply.

The 2024 final rule represents the most significant step toward this theory of change to date, but its effectiveness depends on enforcement capacity and legal durability. Legislative action to codify and fund the rule's key provisions would substantially strengthen the enforcement framework.

## Proposed Solutions

### Solution 1: Mandatory Public Parity Reporting (Parity Scorecard)

**Description**: Require all health plans subject to MHPAEA to annually report standardized parity compliance metrics to CMS and DOL, and make these reports publicly available. The report would include denial rates by benefit category, out-of-network utilization rates, prior authorization approval rates and timelines, average wait times for in-network appointments, reimbursement rates as a percentage of Medicare rates, and provider directory accuracy rates. CMS would publish a "Parity Scorecard" for each plan, modeled on the Hospital Compare and Medicare Advantage Star Rating systems.

**Evidence Base**: The Hospital Compare program demonstrated that public reporting of quality metrics drives provider improvement even absent direct financial penalties. Research by Hibbard et al. (2005, *Health Affairs*) found that public reporting leads to quality improvements in approximately 75% of measured domains. CMS's Medicare Advantage Star Ratings similarly drive plan behavior because consumers and employers use ratings in purchasing decisions. The California DMHC's public reporting of network adequacy data has been credited with driving network improvements among plans operating in that state.

**Implementation**: DOL and CMS would develop standardized reporting templates with input from an actuarial advisory committee. Plans would be required to submit data within 120 days of plan year end. CMS would publish the Parity Scorecard online with plan-level data searchable by employer, insurer, and geography. The reporting framework would include:

- Denial rates by CPT code category, separated by MH/SUD and medical/surgical
- Out-of-network utilization percentages by service type
- Average contracted reimbursement rates as a percentage of Medicare rates
- Provider directory accuracy audit results (based on random sample verification)
- Prior authorization turnaround times and approval/denial rates
- Average wait times for initial in-network MH/SUD appointment (based on secret shopper or provider survey methodology)

**Cost/Resources**: Estimated $25-$40 million annually for DOL/CMS data collection and publication infrastructure, plus plan-level compliance costs of approximately $5,000-$50,000 per plan depending on size.

**Timeline**: 18 months to develop reporting standards; 12 months for first data collection cycle; initial publication within 3 years of enactment.

**Challenges**: Plans will resist public reporting of data that may reveal non-compliance. Self-insured employer plans may argue that plan-specific data is proprietary. Standardizing metrics across diverse plan designs is technically complex. Industry will push for aggregate rather than plan-level reporting to avoid competitive disadvantage.

**Impact Metrics**: Reduction in variance of denial rates between MH/SUD and medical/surgical claims; decrease in out-of-network utilization disparity; improvement in average plan Parity Scorecard ratings over time.

**Precedent**: The CMS Hospital Compare program, launched in 2005, demonstrated that public reporting of quality metrics produces measurable improvements even in the absence of direct financial penalties. Within five years of Hospital Compare's launch, hospital performance on reported measures improved by an average of 15-20 percentage points (CMS, 2012). Applying the same principle to parity compliance -- making plan-level data visible to consumers, employers, and regulators -- would create market pressure for improvement that supplements regulatory enforcement.

### Solution 2: Reimbursement Rate Floor for MH/SUD Providers

**Description**: Establish a federal requirement that commercial health plans reimburse MH/SUD providers at rates no less than 100% of the applicable Medicare rate for the same service. This addresses the most significant structural cause of network inadequacy: the 20-40% reimbursement gap between MH/SUD and medical/surgical providers.

**Evidence Base**: Research by Mark et al. (2018, *Psychiatric Services*) found that increasing Medicaid reimbursement rates by 10% for psychiatrists was associated with a 4.5% increase in provider participation in Medicaid. Applying this elasticity, raising commercial rates to Medicare parity would significantly increase network participation. Milliman's 2024 analysis documents that MH/SUD reimbursement runs 77-83% of Medicare rates, while medical/surgical reimbursement runs 105-130% of Medicare. A floor of 100% of Medicare would still leave MH/SUD reimbursement below medical/surgical rates but would close the most extreme gaps.

Additional evidence comes from state Medicaid rate increase programs. When California increased Medi-Cal reimbursement rates for MH/SUD providers by 20% in 2022, the number of providers billing Medi-Cal for MH/SUD services increased by 12% within 18 months (California DHCS, 2024). Oregon's Medicaid rate increase for psychiatric services in 2021 was associated with a 15% reduction in emergency department utilization for psychiatric conditions in Medicaid enrollees (Oregon Health Authority, 2023).

**Implementation**: DOL and CMS would issue regulations defining the reimbursement rate floor, keyed to the Medicare Physician Fee Schedule. Plans would be required to demonstrate compliance as part of their NQTL comparative analysis. Compliance would be measured using average contracted rates by CPT code category. The rate floor would apply to the most common MH/SUD service codes (psychiatric evaluation, psychotherapy, medication management, psychological testing, substance use disorder counseling, and intensive outpatient programming).

**Cost/Resources**: Estimated premium increase of 0.3-0.8% based on actuarial modeling (CBO methodology), representing $4-$11 per member per month for commercial plans.

**Timeline**: Regulatory development: 18 months. Effective date: 2 years after enactment, with a 1-year phase-in period.

**Challenges**: Insurers will argue this is a price control that exceeds MHPAEA's scope. Constitutional challenges may arise under the Commerce Clause if applied to self-insured plans without clear statutory authority. Provider groups in high-cost regions may argue that Medicare rates are still inadequate.

**Impact Metrics**: In-network MH/SUD provider counts; psychiatrist acceptance of new commercial patients (baseline: 38%); out-of-network utilization for MH/SUD services (baseline: 16.3%).

**Interaction with Other Solutions**: The reimbursement rate floor works in tandem with the Parity Scorecard (Solution 1) and ghost network enforcement (Solution 8). Higher reimbursement rates attract providers to networks; the Parity Scorecard makes network adequacy visible; and ghost network enforcement ensures that listed providers are actually available. Together, these three solutions address the network adequacy problem from supply (reimbursement), transparency (scorecard), and accuracy (ghost network enforcement) simultaneously.

### Solution 3: Independent Parity Compliance Audits

**Description**: Create a system of mandatory, independent, third-party parity compliance audits for all health plans with 1,000 or more enrollees. Audits would be conducted by DOL-certified compliance auditors (similar to the HIPAA Security Rule audit framework) on a 3-year cycle. Auditors would evaluate NQTL comparative analyses, denial rate data, network adequacy metrics, provider directory accuracy, and prior authorization processes against standardized compliance criteria.

**Evidence Base**: HIPAA Security Rule audits have demonstrated that third-party compliance assessment identifies violations at rates significantly higher than self-reporting or complaint-driven models. The DOL Inspector General found in 2020 that EBSA's self-directed enforcement model missed the majority of parity violations, and recommended moving toward proactive auditing (DOL OIG Report No. 09-20-001-12-121, 2020). The financial audit model for ERISA pension plans -- which requires independent annual audits for plans with 100+ participants -- provides a direct structural precedent within ERISA.

**Implementation**: DOL would establish auditor certification standards and compliance criteria. Plans would be responsible for the cost of audits (similar to financial statement audits required of pension plans). Audit results would be reported to DOL and, for non-compliant plans, corrective action plans would be mandatory. The audit framework would include:

- Review of NQTL comparative analyses for completeness and methodology
- Statistical analysis of denial rate data comparing MH/SUD and medical/surgical claims
- Provider directory verification through random sample calling campaigns
- Prior authorization process evaluation, including turnaround times and approval rates
- Reimbursement rate analysis comparing MH/SUD and medical/surgical contracted rates
- Network adequacy assessment using geographic access standards

**Cost/Resources**: $10,000-$50,000 per plan audit, depending on plan size. Total program administration cost: $15-$25 million annually for DOL oversight.

**Timeline**: 12 months to establish certification standards; 18 months for first audit cycle; full coverage within 5 years.

**Challenges**: Developing a sufficient pool of certified auditors; potential resistance from plans to third-party access to proprietary data; cost burden on smaller plans.

**Impact Metrics**: Percentage of plans audited per cycle; compliance rate among audited plans; time from audit finding to corrective action.

**Structural Advantage**: Independent audits address the fundamental problem with self-reported compliance: the entity being evaluated controls the data and the methodology. Third-party auditors bring independent judgment and standardized evaluation criteria. The ERISA pension plan audit model demonstrates that this approach scales effectively -- approximately 80,000 pension plans undergo annual independent audits under the existing ERISA framework, providing a direct structural precedent for health plan parity audits.

### Solution 4: Close the ERISA Preemption Gap

**Description**: Amend ERISA to allow states to enforce MHPAEA against self-insured employer plans, or create a federal-state partnership model in which DOL delegates parity enforcement authority to qualified state insurance departments. This would address the structural enforcement gap that prevents states from regulating the majority of employer-sponsored plans.

**Evidence Base**: States with strong parity enforcement programs (Connecticut, Illinois, Oregon) demonstrate measurably better compliance outcomes for fully insured plans within their jurisdiction but cannot touch self-insured plans. A 2023 Kennedy Forum analysis found that states with proactive enforcement had 30-40% lower out-of-network utilization disparities for fully insured plans compared to states relying solely on federal enforcement. The OSHA state plan model provides a structural precedent: OSHA delegates enforcement authority to 22 states that maintain approved state programs meeting federal minimum standards, achieving broader coverage with more localized enforcement.

**Implementation**: Two approaches: (1) Amend ERISA section 514 to create an exception for MHPAEA enforcement, allowing state insurance departments to examine self-insured plans for parity compliance. (2) Create a federal-state delegation model in which DOL certifies state enforcement programs meeting minimum standards and delegates enforcement authority, similar to OSHA's state plan structure. Under the delegation model, DOL would:

- Establish minimum standards for state parity enforcement programs (staffing, training, audit frequency, reporting)
- Provide federal grants to qualifying states (modeled on OSHA state plan grants, which cover approximately 50% of state program costs)
- Retain oversight authority and the ability to revoke delegation if state programs fall below standards
- Maintain direct enforcement authority for states that do not seek delegation

**Cost/Resources**: Federal-state delegation would require $30-$50 million in federal grants to qualifying state programs, offset by enforcement efficiencies and reduced burden on DOL.

**Timeline**: Legislation required; estimated 2-3 years for passage and implementation of delegation framework.

**Challenges**: ERISA preemption is fiercely defended by the employer and insurance lobbies. ERIC and the Chamber of Commerce will oppose any erosion of the uniform federal standard for self-insured plans. Constitutional federalism arguments may be raised.

**Impact Metrics**: Number of states with delegated enforcement authority; parity compliance rates for self-insured plans in participating states; DOL investigative capacity freed for complex cases.

**Political Strategy**: The ERISA preemption fix is the most politically difficult reform because employer groups and the insurance industry strongly defend the uniform federal standard. Two approaches can reduce opposition: (1) Frame delegation as strengthening enforcement of existing federal law rather than creating new state authority -- states would enforce MHPAEA, not state-specific mandates. (2) Offer a compromise in which states must meet minimum federal standards to receive delegation authority, ensuring that the uniform standard is preserved while enforcement capacity is expanded. The OSHA state plan model has operated successfully for over 50 years with this structure, demonstrating its durability.

### Solution 5: Expand Parity to Medicare and Medicaid Fee-for-Service

**Description**: Enact legislation extending MHPAEA's requirements to Medicare fee-for-service and Medicaid fee-for-service programs. Currently, MHPAEA applies to employer-sponsored plans, individual market plans, and (since 2024) Medicaid managed care. Medicare fee-for-service and Medicaid fee-for-service are not covered, leaving significant gaps.

**Evidence Base**: Medicare fee-for-service imposes a 190-day lifetime limit on inpatient psychiatric hospital care (42 U.S.C. section 1812(b)(3)) -- a quantitative treatment limitation that would violate MHPAEA if applied in a commercial plan. Medicare's coverage of substance use disorder treatment is limited compared to medical treatment. Extending parity would affect approximately 37 million Medicare fee-for-service beneficiaries and millions of Medicaid fee-for-service enrollees.

Medicare's 190-day limit is a relic of the 1965 statute and has no medical justification. No comparable lifetime limit exists for any other inpatient service category under Medicare. Patients who exhaust the 190-day limit must either transfer to a general hospital psychiatric unit (which has no such limit, creating an incoherent coverage structure), pay out of pocket, or go without care.

**Implementation**: Legislation would amend Title XVIII (Medicare) and Title XIX (Medicaid) of the Social Security Act to apply MHPAEA's requirements. CMS would issue implementing regulations within 24 months. Key provisions would include:

- Elimination of the 190-day inpatient psychiatric hospital limit
- Application of NQTL parity standards to Medicare coverage determinations
- Extension of network adequacy standards to Medicare Advantage MH/SUD benefits
- Application of parity standards to Medicaid fee-for-service prior authorization and coverage policies

**Cost/Resources**: CBO has estimated the cost of eliminating the 190-day psychiatric hospital limit at $1.2-$1.8 billion over 10 years. Broader Medicare MH/SUD parity would cost an estimated $5-$10 billion over 10 years (Kennedy Forum estimate, 2024).

**Timeline**: Legislation required; estimated 3-5 years for passage given fiscal implications.

**Challenges**: Fiscal cost in a constrained budget environment; opposition from those who view Medicare as already providing adequate MH/SUD benefits; complexity of applying MHPAEA's private-plan framework to government programs.

**Impact Metrics**: Medicare and Medicaid MH/SUD utilization rates; reduction in out-of-pocket spending for Medicare MH/SUD beneficiaries; closure of the 190-day psychiatric inpatient limit.

### Solution 6: Standardized NQTL Comparison Methodology

**Description**: Require DOL and CMS to develop and publish a standardized, evidence-based methodology for NQTL comparative analyses. Currently, plans develop their own methodologies, leading to inconsistent and often superficial analyses. A standardized methodology would specify the data sources, comparison categories, statistical tests, and compliance thresholds for each type of NQTL.

**Evidence Base**: DOL's finding that 100% of initial NQTL comparative analyses were insufficient reflects the absence of a clear, enforceable standard. The Government Accountability Office recommended in 2022 that DOL develop more specific guidance on how plans should conduct NQTL analyses (GAO-22-104597). The 2024 final rule improved guidance but stopped short of mandating a specific methodology.

The need for standardization is demonstrated by the wide variation in analysis quality. Some plans submitted 200-page NQTL comparative analyses with detailed actuarial data; others submitted 5-page summaries with conclusory assertions. Without a standardized methodology, compliance determinations depend on the analytical sophistication of the plan (or its consultant) rather than on the actual parity of its practices.

**Implementation**: DOL and CMS would convene a technical advisory committee of actuaries, clinicians, compliance experts, and patient advocates to develop the methodology. The committee would produce a publicly available comparison framework, updated biennially. The methodology would specify:

- Required data elements for each NQTL category (prior authorization, step therapy, concurrent review, medical necessity criteria, network admission standards)
- Appropriate comparison groups within medical/surgical benefits for each MH/SUD NQTL
- Statistical tests for determining whether differences between MH/SUD and medical/surgical NQTL outcomes are significant
- Compliance thresholds (e.g., denial rate ratios, out-of-network utilization ratios, prior authorization turnaround time ratios)
- Documentation standards for the evidentiary basis of each NQTL

**Cost/Resources**: $5 million for initial development; $2 million annually for updates and technical assistance.

**Timeline**: 12-18 months for methodology development; 12 months for plan adoption.

**Challenges**: Industry will argue that one-size-fits-all methodology cannot accommodate plan design diversity. Actuarial complexity of comparing NQTLs across benefit classifications.

**Impact Metrics**: Percentage of NQTL analyses found sufficient by DOL; reduction in enforcement time per investigation; consistency of compliance determinations across jurisdictions.

### Solution 7: Prior Authorization Reform for MH/SUD Services

**Description**: Require health plans to meet specific prior authorization standards for MH/SUD services, including: (1) electronic, real-time authorization for routine outpatient services; (2) maximum 24-hour turnaround for urgent care authorizations; (3) prohibition on concurrent review more frequently for MH/SUD than for medical/surgical inpatient care; (4) automatic approval for continuation of established treatment regimens unless the plan can demonstrate a clinical basis for review; and (5) gold-carding provisions that exempt providers with high approval rates from prior authorization requirements.

**Evidence Base**: The American Medical Association's 2023 prior authorization survey found that 94% of physicians reported that prior authorization delayed necessary care, with MH/SUD services disproportionately affected. States that have enacted prior authorization reforms -- including Arkansas, Texas, and Colorado -- have reported reduced treatment delays without significant cost increases (NCOIL, 2023). Gold-carding programs in Texas and other states that exempt providers with high approval rates from prior authorization have been effective in reducing administrative burden.

Texas's gold-carding law (HB 3459, effective 2022) automatically exempts physicians with an 80% or higher prior authorization approval rate from the requirement for a specified period. In its first year, the program exempted approximately 30% of in-network physicians, reducing administrative costs by an estimated $250 million (Texas Medical Association, 2023). Applying a similar model specifically to MH/SUD providers would address the disproportionate prior authorization burden while maintaining oversight for providers with high denial rates.

**Implementation**: Federal legislation or regulation establishing prior authorization timing and process standards for MHPAEA-covered plans. States would be free to exceed federal minimums. Key provisions:

- Electronic submission and response for all prior authorization requests
- 24-hour turnaround for urgent MH/SUD authorizations (including acute psychiatric admissions, detoxification, and crisis services)
- 72-hour turnaround for routine outpatient MH/SUD authorizations
- Automatic approval for continuation of treatment when a provider attests to medical necessity and the patient has been receiving the service for 90+ days
- Gold-carding: automatic exemption from prior authorization for MH/SUD providers with 80%+ approval rates over a 12-month period
- Prohibition on "silent denials" (failure to respond within the required timeframe constitutes approval)

**Cost/Resources**: Minimal net cost; administrative savings from streamlined processes may offset increased utilization. CBO has scored prior authorization reform proposals as approximately budget-neutral.

**Timeline**: 12 months for regulatory development; 18 months for plan compliance.

**Challenges**: Insurers will argue that prior authorization is a clinically necessary tool. Plans may resist real-time authorization requirements as technically burdensome.

**Impact Metrics**: Average prior authorization turnaround time; patient care abandonment rate during authorization process (baseline: 29%); MH/SUD prior authorization denial rate.

### Solution 8: Ghost Network Enforcement

**Description**: Establish federal standards for provider directory accuracy and create an enforcement mechanism to address ghost networks. Require plans to verify the accuracy of MH/SUD provider directories through quarterly audits, with penalties for inaccuracy rates exceeding defined thresholds.

**Evidence Base**: State investigations have documented ghost network rates of 40-60% for MH/SUD providers (CA DMHC, 2023; IL DOI, 2023; HHS OIG, 2022). Ghost networks undermine parity compliance by creating the appearance of adequate networks while denying patients actual access to care. Several states (including California, New York, and Illinois) have enacted or proposed provider directory accuracy laws, providing models for federal action.

**Implementation**: DOL and CMS would issue regulations requiring:

- Quarterly verification of provider availability through direct contact (phone and email)
- Removal of non-responsive or unavailable providers from directories within 30 days of verification failure
- Public reporting of directory accuracy rates alongside Parity Scorecard metrics
- A maximum ghost network rate threshold of 15% for MH/SUD providers (comparable to medical/surgical directory accuracy)
- Financial penalties for plans exceeding the threshold: $100 per inaccurate listing per month

**Cost/Resources**: Estimated $5-$10 million annually for DOL/CMS oversight; $5,000-$25,000 per plan annually for verification processes.

**Timeline**: 12 months for regulatory development; 12 months for initial compliance.

**Challenges**: Defining "availability" (accepting new patients vs. listed but full); provider cooperation with verification processes; cost of continuous monitoring.

**Impact Metrics**: Ghost network rate reduction; correlation between directory accuracy improvements and out-of-network utilization decreases.

## International Models

### Australia: Better Access Program

**Description**: Australia's Better Access program, established in 2006, provides subsidized access to psychologists and other mental health professionals through Medicare (Australia's universal health care system). Patients receive up to 10 subsidized sessions per year with no prior authorization requirement. The program was expanded during COVID-19 to 20 sessions per year.

**Results**: Mental health service utilization increased 57% from 2006 to 2016. Out-of-pocket costs for psychological services decreased by 40%. Wait times for initial appointments decreased by 20% in the first five years (Australian Institute of Health and Welfare, 2023). Patient satisfaction with mental health care increased from 63% to 78%.

**Applicability to U.S.**: The key lesson is that reducing administrative barriers (no prior authorization) and providing adequate reimbursement directly increases utilization and access. The U.S. could adopt a similar model within commercial insurance by eliminating prior authorization for initial and follow-up MH/SUD outpatient visits.

### United Kingdom: Improving Access to Psychological Therapies (IAPT)

**Description**: The NHS's IAPT program provides evidence-based psychological therapies (primarily CBT) for common mental health conditions with no gatekeeping or prior authorization. Patients can self-refer, and services are delivered within a stepped care model with defined waiting time standards (6 weeks for initial assessment, 18 weeks for treatment).

**Results**: Over 1 million patients treated annually. Recovery rates of 50% for depression and anxiety (NHS Digital, 2023). Cost-effectiveness ratio of approximately 1:4 (every pound invested saves four pounds in reduced disability and healthcare costs).

**Applicability to U.S.**: The self-referral model and defined waiting time standards could inform U.S. parity standards. A maximum wait time standard for MH/SUD initial appointments (e.g., 14 days) would be a measurable parity compliance metric.

### Germany: Statutory Health Insurance Parity

**Description**: Germany's statutory health insurance system provides comprehensive MH/SUD coverage with parity standards enforced through the Federal Joint Committee (G-BA). Psychotherapy is covered with no visit limits, and patients can choose between psychoanalytic, cognitive behavioral, and systemic therapy modalities.

**Results**: Germany has one of the highest rates of psychotherapy utilization in Europe. Wait times remain a challenge (average 3-6 months for initial psychotherapy appointment), but coverage itself is comprehensive and equivalent to medical coverage.

**Applicability to U.S.**: Germany demonstrates that parity in benefit design is achievable within a regulated insurance market. The challenge of wait times despite comprehensive coverage underscores that parity in benefits must be accompanied by workforce adequacy.

### Canada: Provincial Mental Health Access Programs

**Description**: Several Canadian provinces have implemented programs to improve mental health access within their public health insurance systems. Ontario's Structured Psychotherapy Program provides publicly funded CBT and other evidence-based therapies through a centralized referral system. British Columbia's Foundry centers provide integrated youth mental health services with no insurance barriers.

**Results**: Ontario's program has treated over 40,000 patients since 2019, with recovery rates comparable to the UK's IAPT program. British Columbia's Foundry centers have reduced emergency department visits for youth mental health by 25% in catchment areas (BC Ministry of Health, 2023).

**Applicability to U.S.**: The integrated, low-barrier access models demonstrate that reducing administrative complexity (no prior authorization, no separate behavioral health carve-out) can significantly improve access and reduce crisis utilization.

### Japan: Universal Coverage with Mental Health Parity

**Description**: Japan's universal health insurance system covers mental health services at the same cost-sharing rate as physical health services (30% coinsurance for working-age adults, 10% for elderly). Patients can self-refer to psychiatrists without gatekeeper authorization, and there is no separate behavioral health administrative system.

**Results**: Japan has one of the highest rates of outpatient psychiatric utilization in the OECD, with 2.8 million people receiving outpatient psychiatric treatment annually. The absence of administrative barriers to initial access means that mild-to-moderate conditions are treated earlier, reducing the need for hospitalization. However, Japan's system has been criticized for high rates of psychiatric hospitalization for severe conditions, reflecting a historical emphasis on inpatient care that parallels the U.S. experience with deinstitutionalization.

**Applicability to U.S.**: Japan's experience demonstrates that parity in cost-sharing and self-referral access produces high utilization of outpatient services, which is associated with earlier treatment and better outcomes. The lesson for U.S. parity enforcement is that removing administrative barriers (prior authorization, step therapy) for initial MH/SUD access can increase appropriate utilization without unsustainable cost increases.

### Netherlands: Regulated Competition with Mental Health Parity

**Description**: The Netherlands operates a regulated competition model in which private insurers are required to offer standardized basic benefit packages that include comprehensive mental health coverage. Mental health services are covered without prior authorization for the first eight sessions, and cost-sharing is identical for mental health and physical health services. Insurers compete on service quality and supplementary benefits, not on restricting basic coverage.

**Results**: The Netherlands has among the lowest unmet need rates for mental health care in Europe (approximately 6% of those with a mental health condition report unmet need, compared to 12-18% in most other European countries). Wait times for specialized mental health treatment remain a challenge, but the basic access model ensures that initial treatment is available without administrative barriers.

**Applicability to U.S.**: The Dutch model demonstrates that regulated competition -- in which insurers are required to meet parity standards as a condition of market participation -- can produce better access outcomes than the U.S. approach of enacting parity mandates and then struggling to enforce them.

## Pilot Programs and Demonstrations

### Colorado Parity Enforcement Pilot

Colorado established a dedicated parity enforcement unit within its Division of Insurance in 2019, funded by a dedicated assessment on insurers. The unit conducts proactive market conduct examinations focused on NQTL compliance, network adequacy, and denial rate disparities. In its first three years, the unit identified parity violations in 78% of plans examined and required corrective actions including network expansion, reimbursement rate increases, and prior authorization reforms. Out-of-network MH/SUD utilization in fully insured Colorado plans decreased by 18% from 2019 to 2023 (Colorado Division of Insurance, 2024).

### Connecticut Parity Reporting Program

Connecticut requires all health plans to annually report MH/SUD denial rates, out-of-network utilization, and reimbursement rates alongside comparable medical/surgical data. The Connecticut Insurance Department publishes comparative reports, creating transparency and competitive pressure. Plans with the worst disparities face targeted examinations. Since implementation, Connecticut has seen a 22% reduction in the MH/SUD to medical/surgical denial rate gap (Connecticut Insurance Department, 2024).

### Oregon Network Adequacy Program

Oregon's Insurance Division established network adequacy standards specific to MH/SUD providers in 2020, including maximum travel time and distance standards and minimum provider-to-enrollee ratios. Plans that fail to meet these standards must submit corrective action plans and cannot market to new enrollees until standards are met. Oregon's program has been credited with a 14% increase in in-network MH/SUD providers across commercial plans from 2020 to 2023 (Oregon Insurance Division, 2024). Oregon's approach is notable for linking network adequacy to market access: the prohibition on marketing to new enrollees creates a direct financial consequence for plans that fail to maintain adequate MH/SUD networks, an enforcement mechanism that has proven more effective than fines alone.

### Illinois Prior Authorization Transparency Pilot

Illinois's Department of Insurance launched a prior authorization transparency pilot in 2023 requiring the five largest fully insured health plans in the state to report quarterly data on prior authorization approval rates, turnaround times, and denial reasons for MH/SUD services versus medical/surgical services. The pilot's first-year data revealed that MH/SUD prior authorization denial rates were 2.4 times higher than medical/surgical denial rates across participating plans, and average turnaround time for MH/SUD authorizations was 4.2 business days compared to 1.8 days for medical/surgical authorizations. The transparency requirement itself drove improvement: in the second year, three of the five plans reduced their MH/SUD denial rates by 10-15%, apparently motivated by the prospect of public comparison (Illinois DOI, 2025).

### Massachusetts Ghost Network Remediation Program

Massachusetts established a ghost network remediation program in 2023 following Attorney General investigations that found MH/SUD provider directory inaccuracy rates of 52% across commercial plans. The program requires quarterly secret shopper audits of behavioral health provider directories by an independent contractor, with results reported publicly. Plans with ghost network rates exceeding 20% must submit corrective action plans and face escalating penalties. In its first 18 months, the program reduced average ghost network rates from 52% to 28%, though they remain well above the target of 15% (Massachusetts Division of Insurance, 2025).

## Sequencing and Prioritization

### Quick Wins (0-2 years)

- Implement the 2024 final rule's NQTL comparative analysis requirements on schedule (January 2025 through 2026)
- Publish standardized NQTL comparison methodology guidance
- Fund DOL parity enforcement expansion (50 additional investigators)
- Launch prior authorization reform for MH/SUD services (timing standards, electronic processing)
- Begin ghost network verification requirements for largest plans

### Medium-term Reforms (2-5 years)

- Enact mandatory public parity reporting and Parity Scorecard
- Establish independent third-party compliance audit system
- Enact reimbursement rate floor (100% of Medicare) for MH/SUD providers
- Create federal-state parity enforcement delegation framework to address ERISA gap
- Extend parity to Medicare fee-for-service (eliminate 190-day psychiatric limit)
- Codify 2024 final rule key provisions in statute
- Establish ghost network enforcement standards with financial penalties
- Launch certified parity auditor training program to build independent audit capacity
- Develop parity standards for telehealth and digital mental health services

### Long-term Transformation (5-10 years)

- Achieve universal parity coverage across all insurance products (Medicare, Medicaid FFS, TRICARE, federal employee plans)
- Close ERISA preemption gap for parity enforcement
- Develop and implement parity standards for emerging modalities (telehealth, digital therapeutics, psychedelic-assisted therapy)
- Achieve measurable outcome parity: MH/SUD denial rates, network adequacy, and wait times within 10% of medical/surgical equivalents
- Implement automated compliance monitoring that identifies parity violations in real time through claims data analysis
- Conduct a comprehensive national assessment of parity's impact on MH/SUD treatment access, outcomes, and costs
- Evaluate whether the behavioral health workforce has grown sufficiently to support parity in network adequacy, and identify remaining geographic and specialty gaps
- Develop parity standards for integrated care models that do not fit neatly into the traditional MH/SUD versus medical/surgical benefit classification framework

## Complementary Policies

Parity enforcement alone cannot achieve equal access to mental health care without parallel reforms:

- **Behavioral health workforce expansion**: Increased graduate medical education funding for psychiatry; loan repayment programs for MH/SUD providers in underserved areas; expansion of prescriptive authority for psychiatric nurse practitioners. See [Workforce Shortage](../workforce-shortage/07-solutions.md).
- **Telehealth integration**: Permanent extension of pandemic-era telehealth flexibilities for MH/SUD services; parity standards for telehealth benefits; interstate licensure compacts for MH/SUD providers. See [Telehealth](../telehealth/07-solutions.md).
- **Medicaid reimbursement reform**: Increased Medicaid MH/SUD reimbursement rates to reduce cost-shifting to commercial insurance and improve provider participation across all payers. See [Funding Systems](../funding-systems/07-solutions.md).
- **Crisis services expansion**: Investment in 988 crisis infrastructure and crisis stabilization units to reduce emergency department utilization and provide alternatives to hospitalization. See [Crisis Services](../crisis-services/07-solutions.md).
- **School-based mental health**: Expansion of school-based mental health services to provide early intervention and reduce reliance on insurance-dependent community services for children and adolescents.
- **Criminal justice diversion**: Establishment of pre-booking and post-booking diversion programs that connect individuals with MH/SUD conditions to insured community treatment rather than incarceration.
- **988 crisis system integration**: Investment in the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline as a gateway to insured care, ensuring that crisis contacts lead to follow-up treatment covered by insurance rather than emergency department visits.
- **Provider training pipeline**: Expanded funding for psychiatric residency positions, clinical psychology internships, and social work field placements, focused on underserved areas and populations with the greatest unmet need.
- **Integrated care models**: Support for collaborative care models (such as the AIMS model) that embed behavioral health in primary care settings, reducing the dependence on separate behavioral health networks and addressing mild-to-moderate conditions before they require specialty care.
- **Employer wellness and prevention**: Workplace-based prevention programs that identify and address early-stage MH/SUD conditions can reduce the downstream demand for more intensive treatment, complementing insurance parity by reducing the volume of claims that require navigating the parity enforcement system.
