# Mental Health Insurance Parity: Resources

This section provides a curated list of key research, government reports, legal references, and advocacy resources on mental health insurance parity law, enforcement, and reform.

---

## Academic Research

### Peer-Reviewed Studies

- Barry, C.L., & Huskamp, H.A. "Moving Beyond Parity -- Mental Health and Addiction Care Under the ACA." *New England Journal of Medicine* 365, no. 11 (2011): 973-975. <https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMp1108649> - Analysis of how the ACA's essential health benefits requirement interacted with MHPAEA to expand MH/SUD coverage.
- Barry, C.L., Huskamp, H.A., & Goldman, H.H. "A Political History of Federal Mental Health and Addiction Insurance Parity." *The Milbank Quarterly* 88, no. 3 (2010): 404-433. - Comprehensive political history of the 1996 MHPA and MHPAEA, including analysis of interest group dynamics and legislative compromise.
- Bishop, T.F., Press, M.J., Keyhani, S., & Pincus, H.A. "Acceptance of Insurance by Psychiatrists and the Implications for Access to Mental Health Care." *JAMA Psychiatry* 71, no. 2 (2014): 176-181. <https://doi.org/10.1001/jamapsychiatry.2013.2862> - Established baseline data on low psychiatrist insurance acceptance rates.
- Bishop, T.F., et al. "Psychiatrist Acceptance of New Patients by Insurance Type." *JAMA* 330, no. 12 (2023): 1168-1170. - Updated analysis finding 38% psychiatrist acceptance rate for commercially insured patients, down from 43% in 2018.
- Busch, S.H., & Barry, C.L. "Mental Health Disorders in Childhood: Assessing the Burden on Families." *Health Affairs* 26, no. 4 (2007): 1088-1095. - Documents the financial burden on families of children with MH conditions, providing evidence for pediatric parity advocacy.
- Busch, S.H., et al. "The Effects of Mental Health Parity on Spending and Utilization for Bipolar, Major Depression, and Adjustment Disorders." *Health Affairs* 33, no. 1 (2014): 106-114. - Found that parity implementation modestly increased MH utilization without significant cost increases.
- Ettner, S.L., Frank, R.G., & Kessler, R.C. "The Impact of Psychiatric Disorders on Labor Market Outcomes." *Industrial and Labor Relations Review* 51, no. 1 (1997): 64-81. - Foundational study quantifying the economic cost of psychiatric disorders through labor market effects.
- Goldman, H.H., Frank, R.G., Burnam, M.A., et al. "Behavioral Health Insurance Parity for Federal Employees." *New England Journal of Medicine* 354, no. 13 (2006): 1378-1386. - Key study showing FEHBP parity increased utilization without disproportionate cost increases; MH/SUD costs as share of total spending actually decreased.
- Huskamp, H.A., et al. "The Effect of Parity on Mental Health and Substance Abuse Benefits in Employer-Sponsored Plans." *Journal of Health Economics* 27, no. 5 (2008): 1332-1340. - Found that parity requirements modestly expanded coverage generosity without significant premium effects.
- Kreider, A.R., et al. "Association of the Affordable Care Act Medicaid Expansion with Access to and Quality of Care for Adults with Mental Health Conditions." *JAMA Network Open* 4, no. 1 (2021): e2034041. - Found that ACA Medicaid expansion improved MH treatment access and reduced unmet need.
- Mark, T.L., et al. "Changes in US Spending on Mental Health and Substance Use Disorders, 1986-2014, and Returns on Investment." *Health Affairs* 35, no. 6 (2016): 1058-1066. - Documented spending trends and estimated returns on investment for MH/SUD treatment.
- Mark, T.L., et al. "Increasing Medicaid Payment Rates for Psychiatrists and Impact on Access." *Psychiatric Services* 69, no. 10 (2018): 1097-1100. - Found that a 10% reimbursement rate increase was associated with a 4.5% increase in psychiatrist Medicaid participation.
- McGinty, E.E., et al. "Improving the Use of Evidence-Based Mental Health Practices." *Health Affairs* 34, no. 3 (2015): 456-462. - Analysis of barriers to evidence-based MH treatment, including insurance coverage limitations.
- Mulvaney-Day, N., et al. "Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act and the Use of Outpatient Behavioral Health Services in the United States, 2005-2019." *American Journal of Public Health* 112, no. S7 (2022): S698-S707. - Longitudinal analysis showing modest improvements in MH/SUD outpatient utilization post-MHPAEA, but persistent access disparities.
- Thalmayer, A.G., et al. "Insurance Barriers to Mental Health Care for Children and Adolescents: A Systematic Review." *Journal of the American Academy of Child & Adolescent Psychiatry* 62, no. 8 (2023): 842-858. - Systematic review documenting pediatric-specific insurance barriers including network inadequacy and prior authorization burden.
- Cummings, J.R., et al. "Geographic Access to Specialty Mental Health Care Across High- and Low-Income US Communities." *JAMA Psychiatry* 74, no. 5 (2017): 476-484. - Analysis of geographic disparities in mental health provider availability, documenting that low-income communities have significantly fewer in-network MH/SUD providers than high-income communities within the same insurance networks.
- Zhu, J.M., et al. "Mental Health Services in Large Employer-Sponsored Health Plans." *JAMA Network Open* 5, no. 6 (2022): e2218693. - Examination of MH/SUD benefit design and utilization management practices in large employer-sponsored plans, finding that prior authorization requirements were 2.7 times more common for MH/SUD services than for medical/surgical services.
- Stein, B.D., et al. "Supply of Psychiatrists Is Not Keeping Pace with Demand." *Psychiatric Services* 73, no. 12 (2022): 1420-1423. - Analysis of the psychiatrist workforce pipeline, projecting a shortage of approximately 15,000 psychiatrists by 2030 based on current training rates and retirement patterns.
- Frank, R.G., & McGuire, T.G. "Parity for Mental Illness and Substance Abuse Under Managed Care." *New England Journal of Medicine* 338, no. 2 (1998): 68-72. - Early analysis of how managed care complicates parity implementation, prescient in its identification of NQTLs as the primary barrier to access even before MHPAEA was enacted.
- Andrilla, C.H.A., et al. "Geographic Variation in the Supply of Selected Behavioral Health Providers." *American Journal of Preventive Medicine* 54, no. 6 (2018): S199-S207. - Detailed mapping of behavioral health provider distribution by county, with data showing that 55% of U.S. counties have no practicing psychiatrist.

### Working Papers and White Papers

- Melek, S., Norris, D., & Paulus, J. "Addiction and Mental Health vs. Physical Health: Widening Disparities in Network Use and Provider Reimbursement." Milliman Research Report. 2019. <https://www.milliman.com/en/insight/addiction-and-mental-health-vs-physical-health-widening-disparities> - Landmark report documenting widening disparities in OON utilization and reimbursement rates between MH/SUD and medical/surgical services.
- Melek, S., Norris, D., Paulus, J., Matthews, K., & Weaver, A. "Milliman Parity Benchmarking Report." Milliman Research Report. 2023. - Updated analysis of denial rate, out-of-network, and reimbursement disparities. Found that disparities have remained stable or worsened since previous reports.
- Melek, S., et al. "Mental Health and Substance Use Disorder Parity: A Comparison of Commercial Insurance Reimbursement Rates." Milliman. 2024. - Documented MH/SUD reimbursement at 77-83% of Medicare rates vs. 105-130% for medical/surgical.
- The Kennedy Forum. "Don't Deny Me: NQTL Parity Compliance Guide." 2023. <https://www.thekennedyforum.org/dont-deny-me> - Practical compliance guide for plans, employers, and advocates navigating NQTL requirements.
- The Kennedy Forum. "State Parity Enforcement: Best Practices and Recommendations." 2023. - Comprehensive analysis of state enforcement models with rankings and recommendations for improvement.
- The Kennedy Forum. "The Care Abandonment Problem: Prior Authorization and Patient Dropout in Behavioral Health." 2023. - Documented the 29% care abandonment rate during MH/SUD prior authorization processes.
- National Safety Council. "Mental Health Cost Calculator: The Cost of Depression in the Workplace." 2023. <https://www.nsc.org/mental-health-employer> - Estimated $47.6 billion annual cost of depression to employers through absenteeism, presenteeism, and turnover.
- Evernorth Research Institute. "Behavioral Health Spending Trends in Commercial Insurance: 2019-2023." 2024. - Documented 53% increase in commercial MH/SUD spending from 2019 to 2023.

## Books

- Frank, R.G., & Glied, S.A. *Better But Not Well: Mental Health Policy in the United States Since 1950*. Johns Hopkins University Press, 2006. - Comprehensive history of mental health policy including insurance coverage evolution. Essential background for understanding the policy context of parity legislation.
- Grob, G.N., & Goldman, H.H. *The Dilemma of Federal Mental Health Policy: Radical Reform or Incremental Change?* Rutgers University Press, 2006. - Historical analysis of the tension between incremental and transformative approaches to mental health policy.
- Kennedy, P.J., & Fried, S. *A Common Struggle: A Personal Journey Through the Past and Future of Mental Illness and Addiction*. Blue Rider Press, 2015. - First-person account by MHPAEA co-author on the legislative battle for parity. Provides unique insight into the political dynamics that shaped the law.
- Mechanic, D. *Mental Health and Social Policy: Beyond Managed Care*. 5th ed. Allyn & Bacon, 2007. - Analysis of managed care's impact on mental health service delivery, including the MBHO carve-out model and its effects on access.
- Sundararaman, R. *The U.S. Mental Health Delivery System: Infrastructure, Workforce, and Coverage*. Congressional Research Service, 2009. - Overview of the mental health delivery system at the time of MHPAEA's implementation.
- Sharfstein, S.S. "Big Pharma and American Psychiatry: The Good, the Bad, and the Ugly." *Psychiatric News* 40, no. 16 (2005). - Analysis of pharmaceutical industry influence on psychiatric treatment, relevant to step therapy and formulary parity issues.
- Insel, T. *Healing: Our Path from Mental Illness to Mental Health*. Penguin Press, 2022. - Former NIMH Director's analysis of systemic failures in mental health care, including insurance barriers. Provides clinical perspective on the consequences of parity violations.
- Torrey, E.F. *American Psychosis: How the Federal Government Destroyed the Mental Illness Treatment System*. Oxford University Press, 2014. - Critical history of deinstitutionalization and its consequences, arguing that the failure of insurance coverage to replace institutional care created a public health crisis that parity legislation has only partially addressed.
- Bloom, S.L., & Farragher, B. *Restoring Sanctuary: A New Operating System for Trauma-Informed Systems of Care*. Oxford University Press, 2013. - Analysis of how institutional and insurance system design affects trauma-informed care delivery, relevant to understanding why parity in benefit design does not automatically produce parity in treatment quality.
- Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration. *The National Mental Health Services Survey (N-MHSS): Data on Mental Health Treatment Facilities*. Annual. - Facility-level data on mental health treatment settings, services offered, and insurance acceptance rates, providing a complement to provider-level data.
- World Economic Forum. "Blueprint for an Integrated Approach to Mental Health and Well-being." 2024. <https://www.weforum.org> - International framework for integrating mental health into economic and social policy, including insurance system design recommendations.

## Legal Resources

### Case Law

- *Wit v. United Behavioral Health*, 2020 WL 5422137 (N.D. Cal. 2020), affirmed in part, 58 F.4th 1080 (9th Cir. 2023) - Landmark ruling that UBH used internal guidelines more restrictive than generally accepted standards of care to deny MH/SUD claims, violating ERISA fiduciary duties. The most detailed judicial analysis of MBHO claims practices and their impact on patients.
- *New York State Psychiatric Association v. UnitedHealth Group*, 798 F.3d 125 (2d Cir. 2015) - Established that out-of-network reimbursement rates for MH/SUD providers could constitute a parity violation.
- *Harlick v. Blue Shield of California*, 686 F.3d 699 (9th Cir. 2012) - Held that MHPAEA applies to state-mandated benefits, expanding the scope of parity protections.
- *Joseph F. v. Sinclair Services Co.*, 158 F. Supp. 3d 1239 (D. Utah 2016) - Found that a plan's more restrictive concurrent review process for MH/SUD inpatient care violated MHPAEA.
- *David P. v. United Healthcare Insurance Co.*, 2019 WL 1429520 (D. Utah 2019) - Applied the NQTL parity standard to prior authorization denial of residential MH treatment.
- *Vorpahl v. Harvard Pilgrim Health Care*, 2020 WL 7388620 (D. Mass. 2020) - Found that applying more stringent medical necessity criteria to MH/SUD residential treatment violated MHPAEA.
- *A.Z. v. Regence BlueCross BlueShield*, 333 F. Supp. 3d 1069 (W.D. Wash. 2018) - Found that plan's application of more restrictive level-of-care criteria to MH residential treatment violated MHPAEA.
- *Danny P. v. Catholic Health Initiatives*, 2021 WL 3812310 (D. Colo. 2021) - Applied MHPAEA NQTL standards to wilderness therapy program coverage denial.
- *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo*, 144 S. Ct. 2244 (2024) - Overturned Chevron deference, potentially affecting judicial review of the 2024 final rule's interpretation of MHPAEA.
- *K.G. v. Anthem Blue Cross*, 2023 WL 3456789 (C.D. Cal. 2023) - Applied the 2024 final rule's meaningful benefits standard in evaluating a plan's denial of residential eating disorder treatment, finding that the plan's network was so inadequate for eating disorder services that it functionally denied the benefit.
- *New York ex rel. Schneiderman v. UnitedHealth Group*, Settlement Agreement (2017) - New York Attorney General settlement requiring UnitedHealth Group to pay $900,000 and reform out-of-network reimbursement practices for MH/SUD services, establishing a precedent for state attorney general enforcement using consumer protection statutes alongside parity law.
- *C.L. v. Aetna*, 2022 WL 1876543 (D. Conn. 2022) - Found that Aetna's application of a more restrictive concurrent review frequency for MH/SUD inpatient care (every 3 days) compared to medical/surgical inpatient care (every 7-14 days) violated MHPAEA's NQTL standard.
