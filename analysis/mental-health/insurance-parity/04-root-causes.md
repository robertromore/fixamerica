# Mental Health Insurance Parity: Root Causes

## Systemic Analysis

The persistent gap between the legal promise of mental health parity and the reality experienced by patients is not the result of a single failure but of several interlocking structural, economic, and institutional factors. These include the financial incentives of insurers and employers to manage behavioral health costs, a regulatory architecture that depends on patient-initiated enforcement, the complexity of comparing nonquantitative treatment limitations across benefit categories, the ERISA preemption framework that limits state enforcement, and a behavioral health provider labor market that insurers exploit to justify narrow networks. Together, these forces create a system in which parity violations are rational, profitable, and difficult to detect or punish.

## Financial Incentives to Restrict Behavioral Health

### Insurer Cost Management Strategies

Health insurers and the employers that fund self-insured plans have strong financial incentives to limit MH/SUD utilization.
Behavioral health care is labor-intensive, difficult to standardize, and -- unlike many medical procedures -- does not have clear clinical endpoints.
Insurers therefore apply utilization management tools (prior authorization, concurrent review, step therapy) more aggressively to MH/SUD services, knowing that some patients will abandon their care attempts during the administrative process.

**Evidence**: A 2023 Kennedy Forum study found that 29% of patients abandoned MH/SUD care during the prior authorization process, compared to 11% for medical/surgical services.
This "administrative attrition" reduces costs for the plan without requiring an explicit coverage denial.

The financial calculus is straightforward.
If a plan denies or delays authorization for 1,000 MH/SUD claims and 29% of patients abandon care, the plan avoids payment on 290 claims.
At an average MH/SUD outpatient claim value of $150-$300, this represents $43,500-$87,000 in avoided costs per 1,000 claims -- and a large national insurer processes millions of MH/SUD claims annually.
The economic incentive to maintain burdensome authorization processes is significant, and the likelihood of any individual patient filing a parity complaint is negligible.

### The Cost of MH/SUD Care Relative to Total Plan Spending

Behavioral health spending represents 7-10% of total employer health plan costs (Evernorth, 2024), making it a natural target for cost containment.
However, this percentage has increased from approximately 5% in 2018 to 9-10% in 2023, driven by increased utilization following the pandemic.
The rapid growth in MH/SUD spending as a share of total health costs creates intensifying pressure on plans and employers to deploy cost management tools -- pressure that translates into more aggressive prior authorization, narrower networks, and stricter medical necessity criteria for MH/SUD services.

### Behavioral Health as a Cost Center

Unlike many medical specialties where insurers can negotiate volume discounts with hospital systems, behavioral health care is delivered predominantly by solo practitioners and small group practices with limited bargaining power. Insurers respond by keeping reimbursement rates low -- 20-40% below comparable medical rates (Milliman, 2024) -- which reduces the cost per claim but also reduces the number of providers willing to join networks, pushing patients out-of-network or out of care entirely.

**Evidence**: Only 38% of psychiatrists accept new patients with commercial insurance, compared to 86% of primary care physicians (Bishop et al., JAMA, 2023). The reimbursement rate differential is the primary reason cited by providers for declining network participation.

### The Carve-Out Incentive Structure

The behavioral health carve-out model creates a specific financial incentive for MBHOs to restrict access. MBHOs are typically paid a per-member-per-month (PMPM) capitation rate by the insurer or employer, and their profit is the difference between that capitation and the claims they pay. This creates a direct financial incentive for the MBHO to deny claims, impose prior authorization barriers, and limit the network -- every dollar not spent on care is profit. The three largest MBHOs -- Optum Behavioral Health (UnitedHealth Group), Carelon Behavioral Health (Elevance Health, formerly Anthem/Beacon), and Magellan Health (acquired by Molina Healthcare) -- administer behavioral health benefits for over 100 million Americans. Their capitated incentive structure is fundamentally misaligned with the goal of parity.

**Evidence**: The *Wit v. United Behavioral Health* litigation documented internal UBH communications showing that claims review criteria were revised to reduce authorization rates, with the explicit goal of managing costs within capitation targets. The court found that UBH's Level of Care Guidelines were "infected" by financial considerations (Wit v. UBH, N.D. Cal. 2020).

## Regulatory Architecture Failures

### Complaint-Driven Enforcement Model

The primary enforcement mechanism for MHPAEA is patient complaints.
A patient must recognize that their plan has violated parity, understand the legal framework, navigate the appeals process, and -- if the plan does not voluntarily correct the violation -- file a complaint with DOL (for ERISA plans) or their state insurance department (for fully insured plans).
This model places the burden of enforcement on the most vulnerable population: patients actively seeking mental health or substance use treatment.

**Evidence**: Fewer than 0.5% of MH/SUD claims denials are appealed by patients (DOL EBSA, 2023), yet 50-75% of those that reach external review are overturned (NAIC, 2023).
The gap between the low appeal rate and the high overturn rate demonstrates that the complaint-driven model fails to capture the vast majority of violations.

The complaint-driven model fails at every step of the process:

1. **Detection failure**: 89% of patients are not "very familiar" with parity law (NAMI, 2023). Most do not know that the denial they received may be illegal.
2. **Attribution failure**: Even patients who know about parity may not recognize that a specific denial reflects a parity violation rather than a legitimate clinical determination. The distinction requires comparing MH/SUD and medical/surgical plan provisions -- information that is not readily accessible.
3. **Capacity failure**: Patients in mental health or substance use crises are, by definition, impaired in their ability to navigate complex administrative processes. Requiring the sickest patients to be the most effective advocates is a fundamental design flaw.
4. **Consequence failure**: Even when complaints are filed and violations confirmed, the consequences are typically limited to requiring the plan to correct the specific violation. There are no meaningful financial penalties in most cases, and no compensation for the harm caused to the patient during the period of non-compliance.

### ERISA Preemption

The Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA) preempts state regulation of self-insured employer health plans.
Approximately 65% of workers with employer-sponsored coverage are in self-insured plans (Kaiser Family Foundation, 2023).
These plans are subject only to federal enforcement by DOL's EBSA, which has approximately 500 investigators overseeing approximately 2.5 million plans.
State insurance departments -- which often have more staff, expertise, and proactive enforcement authority -- cannot regulate these plans.

**Evidence**: States with strong parity enforcement programs (such as Connecticut and Illinois) report compliance improvement in fully insured plans but cannot address violations in self-insured plans headquartered in their jurisdictions.
The ERISA preemption creates a structural enforcement gap that no amount of state legislation can close.

The ERISA preemption problem is amplified by the trend toward self-insurance.
The share of covered workers in self-insured plans has grown from approximately 44% in 1999 to 65% in 2023 (Kaiser Family Foundation Employer Health Benefits Survey).
As more employers self-insure -- attracted by the flexibility and cost savings of avoiding state mandates -- a growing majority of the insured population falls outside state enforcement authority.
The trend is strongest among large employers (86% of firms with 200+ employees self-insure) but is expanding to mid-size employers through "level-funded" arrangements that are functionally self-insured.

The growth of level-funded plans is particularly concerning.
Level-funded arrangements allow mid-size employers (50-500 employees) to technically self-insure while purchasing stop-loss insurance to limit their financial risk.
These plans are treated as self-insured for ERISA purposes, exempting them from state regulation, but they are administered by insurers in ways that closely resemble fully insured plans.
The number of workers in level-funded plans has grown approximately 40% since 2019 (Kaiser Family Foundation, 2023), representing an expanding gap in state enforcement coverage.

### Regulatory Complexity of NQTL Comparisons

Comparing nonquantitative treatment limitations across benefit classifications is inherently complex.
How does one compare the prior authorization process for inpatient psychiatric care to the process for cardiac surgery?
The 2013 final rule provided guidance but not a standardized methodology.
Plans developed their own comparison methods, many of which were superficial or self-serving.
The 2024 final rule strengthened requirements by mandating outcomes data, but the analytical challenge remains.

**Evidence**: DOL found that 100% of initial NQTL comparative analyses it reviewed were insufficient (DOL EBSA, 2023).
Plans lacked the data systems, actuarial expertise, or institutional commitment to conduct genuine comparative analyses.
Common deficiencies included: reliance on conclusory assertions ("our processes are comparable") without supporting data; failure to identify the specific factors and evidentiary standards used in applying each NQTL; and comparison of NQTLs across incorrect benefit classifications.

The technical difficulty is genuine but also strategically exploited.
Plans routinely argue that MH/SUD services are "not comparable" to medical/surgical services -- that prior authorization for psychiatric hospitalization cannot meaningfully be compared to prior authorization for a knee replacement because the clinical characteristics are fundamentally different.
This argument has a kernel of truth (the conditions are different) but misses the point of parity law, which requires that the *processes and standards* used to make coverage decisions be comparable, not that the underlying conditions be identical.

### Fragmented Regulatory Authority

The enforcement of MHPAEA is split among three federal agencies (DOL, CMS, and Treasury) and 50 state insurance departments, creating jurisdictional complexity that plans can exploit.
A single national insurer may administer plans that are subject to DOL jurisdiction (self-insured ERISA plans), CMS jurisdiction (individual market plans), and state insurance department jurisdiction (fully insured plans) -- each with different enforcement priorities, timelines, and remedies.
This fragmentation means that no single regulator has a comprehensive view of an insurer's parity compliance practices.
The 2024 final rule attempted to harmonize standards across the three federal agencies, but the fundamental jurisdictional split remains.

## Political Economy

### Who Benefits from the Status Quo

| Actor | Benefit | Economic Value | Incentive to Block Reform |
|-------|---------|----------------|---------------------------|
| Health insurers | Lower MH/SUD utilization through aggressive management | Estimated $15-25 billion/year in avoided MH/SUD claims (Milliman estimate extrapolation, 2023) | Stronger enforcement increases claims costs and administrative burden |
| Self-insured employers | Lower health plan costs through MH/SUD restrictions | Cost savings vary; average employer MH/SUD spending is 7-10% of total health spending | Parity compliance increases premiums and administrative complexity |
| Managed behavioral health organizations (MBHOs) | Revenue from managing restrictive behavioral health carve-outs | $15 billion annual industry revenue (IBISWorld, 2023) | Full parity could reduce demand for carve-out management |
| Pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs) | Step therapy and formulary restrictions on psychiatric medications | Revenue from manufacturer rebates on preferred drugs | Parity requirements may limit step therapy flexibility |
| Third-party administrators (TPAs) | Fee revenue from administering plans with complex MH/SUD restrictions | Part of $25 billion TPA industry revenue | Simpler parity-compliant designs may reduce TPA value |

### Perverse Incentives

The current system creates multiple perverse incentives that perpetuate parity violations:

1. **Denial and attrition profit**: Plans that deny claims or impose burdensome prior authorization know that most patients will not appeal, reducing costs. The 0.5% appeal rate means 99.5% of denied claims are never challenged.
2. **Network narrowing as cost control**: By keeping MH/SUD reimbursement rates low and credentialing requirements high, plans shrink their behavioral health networks. Patients who cannot find in-network providers may forgo care or pay out-of-pocket, reducing plan costs.
3. **Penalty asymmetry**: The financial consequences of parity violations are minimal compared to the savings from non-compliance. DOL recovered only $4.8 million in FY2023 across all parity enforcement actions -- a negligible amount compared to industry savings from restrictive practices.
4. **Compliance as cost**: Plans that invest in genuine parity compliance (adequate networks, comparable prior authorization processes, lower denial rates) incur higher costs than competitors who do not, creating a race to the bottom in the absence of uniform enforcement.
5. **Ghost network tolerance**: Plans face minimal penalties for inaccurate provider directories. Listing unavailable providers inflates apparent network adequacy without incurring the cost of actually contracting with available clinicians.
6. **Short enrollment cycles**: In employer-sponsored and marketplace plans, annual enrollment cycles mean that a patient harmed by a parity violation may switch plans before any enforcement action is completed. This reduces the deterrent effect of enforcement on any individual plan.

## Information Asymmetry

### Patients Cannot Detect Violations

Most patients do not know what "parity" means, do not have access to their plan's benefit design documents, and cannot compare the prior authorization process for their MH/SUD claim to the process for a medical claim. The violation is invisible to the person most affected by it.

**Evidence**: A 2023 NAMI survey found that only 11% of respondents were "very familiar" with parity law, and only 7% had ever filed a parity-related complaint with a regulator. Even among respondents who had experienced a MH/SUD claim denial, only 23% believed the denial might be a parity violation, and only 4% had successfully filed a complaint.

### Plans Control the Data

Until the 2024 final rule, plans were not required to collect or report data comparing MH/SUD and medical/surgical treatment limitations. Plans could assert parity compliance without providing evidence, and regulators had limited tools to verify claims. The 2024 rule's data requirements are designed to address this asymmetry, but implementation and enforcement remain ahead.

### Provider Information Gaps

Behavioral health providers are often unaware of parity law or their patients' rights under it. A 2023 APA survey found that only 34% of psychiatrists and 22% of psychologists reported being "very familiar" with MHPAEA's NQTL requirements. Providers who do not know parity law cannot effectively advocate for their patients when prior authorization is improperly denied, and they cannot identify patterns of non-compliance in their interactions with insurers.

## Structural Workforce Shortage

### Low Reimbursement Drives Provider Exodus

The behavioral health provider shortage is both a cause and a consequence of parity violations.
Low reimbursement rates discourage providers from accepting insurance, shrinking networks.
Plans then cite the provider shortage as justification for narrow networks, creating a self-fulfilling prophecy.

**Evidence**: The average commercial reimbursement rate for a 60-minute psychiatric evaluation is $130-$160, compared to a self-pay rate of $250-$400 (APA, 2023).
Providers who can fill their practice with self-pay patients have little financial incentive to join insurance networks.
A 2023 APA survey found that the primary reason psychiatrists gave for declining insurance network participation was "inadequate reimbursement" (cited by 72% of non-participating psychiatrists), followed by "administrative burden of prior authorization" (cited by 58%).

The reimbursement gap has specific structural causes.
MH/SUD providers -- predominantly solo practitioners and small groups -- lack the bargaining power of hospital systems and large medical group practices.
When a health system with 500 employed physicians negotiates with an insurer, it can demand rates of 120-150% of Medicare because the insurer cannot afford to lose access to the system's patient volume.
When a solo psychiatrist negotiates, the insurer has no comparable leverage, and the provider accepts the offered rate or declines the network entirely.
This structural bargaining power imbalance explains why MH/SUD reimbursement has stagnated at 77-83% of Medicare while medical/surgical reimbursement has grown to 105-130% of Medicare (Milliman, 2024).

### Geographic Maldistribution

Mental health provider shortages are most acute in rural and underserved areas.
Over 160 million Americans live in designated Mental Health Professional Shortage Areas (SAMHSA, 2023).
Plans operating in these areas may have genuine difficulty building adequate networks, but the low reimbursement rates they offer compound the problem.

The geographic problem intersects with the reimbursement problem.
Rural areas with few providers have the weakest bargaining position; a solo psychiatrist in a rural county has even less leverage than one in an urban area.
The result is that the areas with the greatest need have the fewest providers, the lowest reimbursement rates, and the worst network adequacy -- a compounding disadvantage that parity law alone cannot solve without complementary workforce and reimbursement reform.

The HRSA designation data reveals the scale of the geographic problem:

- 55% of U.S. counties have no practicing psychiatrist (HRSA, 2023)
- 70% of U.S. counties have no child or adolescent psychiatrist (AACAP, 2023)
- Rural residents are 65% more likely to have unmet MH/SUD treatment needs than urban residents (SAMHSA NSDUH, 2023)
- The average drive time to the nearest psychiatrist in rural Mental Health Professional Shortage Areas is 75 minutes (HRSA, 2023)

### Training Pipeline Constraints

The behavioral health workforce pipeline has structural bottlenecks that limit the supply of new providers:

- **Psychiatry residency slots**: Approximately 1,800 psychiatry residency positions are available annually, a number that has grown only modestly over two decades. The GME cap established by the Balanced Budget Act of 1997 limits the growth of Medicare-funded residency slots across all specialties.
- **Child and adolescent psychiatry**: Only approximately 300 child and adolescent psychiatry fellowship positions are available annually, producing far fewer specialists than needed. The AACAP estimates a current shortage of approximately 21,000 child and adolescent psychiatrists.
- **Psychologist training**: Doctoral programs in clinical psychology produce approximately 3,500 graduates annually, but many enter private practice rather than insurance-dependent settings.
- **Social work and counseling**: These are the largest MH/SUD workforce categories, but reimbursement rates for licensed clinical social workers and licensed professional counselors are even lower than for psychiatrists and psychologists, exacerbating retention challenges.

## Causal Chain

```text
Low MH/SUD reimbursement rates --> Providers decline network participation
    |
Narrow MH/SUD provider networks --> Patients cannot find in-network providers
    |
High out-of-network utilization --> Plans cite "provider shortage" as justification
    |
Aggressive utilization management (prior auth, denials) --> Patients abandon care
    |
Low MH/SUD utilization --> Plans save money --> Low reimbursement rates maintained
    |
Complaint-driven enforcement + ERISA preemption --> Minimal consequences for violations
    |
Rational non-compliance continues --> Parity gap persists
```

**Reinforcing feedback loop**: The causal chain is self-reinforcing. Each element strengthens the others, creating a stable equilibrium of non-compliance. Low reimbursement leads to narrow networks, which leads to high out-of-network utilization, which the plan uses to justify continued low investment in behavioral health networks. The only external forces capable of disrupting this equilibrium are (1) enforcement action that makes non-compliance more expensive than compliance, (2) reimbursement reform that makes network participation financially viable for providers, or (3) market competition that rewards plans with better behavioral health access.

## Why Reform Has Failed

### Incremental Legislation Without Enforcement Teeth

Each generation of parity legislation expanded the scope of protections without adequately addressing enforcement. The 1996 MHPA lacked coverage of treatment limitations. MHPAEA in 2008 addressed treatment limitations but lacked documentation requirements. The CAA of 2021 required documentation but set no standard for adequacy. The 2024 final rule finally addresses adequacy standards, but its effectiveness depends on DOL's capacity to review and enforce compliance -- a capacity that remains undersized relative to the scale of the problem.

### Industry Adaptation

The insurance industry has consistently found new mechanisms to restrict MH/SUD access within each generation of parity law. When quantitative limits were prohibited, plans shifted to NQTLs. When NQTLs were regulated, plans focused on network narrowing and reimbursement rate suppression. Reform must anticipate and address the next generation of circumvention, which is likely to involve data manipulation in compliance reporting, selective application of medical necessity criteria, and ghost network maintenance that creates the appearance of compliance.

### Political Will Without Funding

Congress has repeatedly passed parity legislation with strong bipartisan support but has not proportionally increased enforcement funding. DOL's EBSA budget has remained relatively flat while its MHPAEA enforcement responsibilities have expanded substantially. Without additional investigators, data analysts, and compliance review capacity, the 2024 final rule's potential may go unrealized.

### The Concentrated Benefits, Diffuse Costs Dynamic

The insurance industry's resistance to parity enforcement benefits a concentrated group of actors (insurers, MBHOs, PBMs) with substantial lobbying resources, while the costs of non-compliance are borne by a diffuse population of millions of patients, each experiencing individual harm that is difficult to organize around politically. This classic concentrated-benefits-diffuse-costs dynamic means that the political economy favors the status quo unless advocates can mobilize patient voices, provider organizations, and employer allies into a countervailing coalition.
