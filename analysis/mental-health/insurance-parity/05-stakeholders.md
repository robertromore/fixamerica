# Mental Health Insurance Parity: Stakeholders

The mental health insurance parity landscape is shaped by the interactions of patients seeking care, insurers managing costs, regulators enforcing compliance, providers navigating reimbursement, employers designing plans, and advocates pushing for reform. Each group has distinct interests, resources, and levels of influence over whether parity law translates into parity practice.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Adults with mental health conditions | Higher denial rates, narrower networks, greater out-of-pocket costs than medical patients | 57.8 million (22.8% of U.S. adults, SAMHSA 2023) | Equal access to affordable MH treatment through insurance |
| People with substance use disorders | Barriers to treatment including prior authorization delays, step therapy requirements, and limited network access | 48.7 million (17.3% of U.S. population 12+, SAMHSA 2023) | Timely access to SUD treatment without administrative barriers |
| Children and adolescents with MH conditions | Growing demand for services with inadequate pediatric behavioral health networks | 17.1% of children aged 6-17 (CDC, 2023) | Access to age-appropriate MH services with adequate in-network providers |
| Family members and caregivers | Bear the burden of navigating denials, appeals, and out-of-network costs | Estimated 8.4 million family caregivers (NAMI, 2023) | Reduced administrative burden, affordable care for their family members |
| Uninsured and underinsured individuals | Parity law does not help those without insurance coverage | ~26 million uninsured (Census Bureau, 2023) | Expansion of coverage, not just parity within existing coverage |
| People with co-occurring MH/SUD conditions | Face compounded barriers when seeking integrated treatment for both conditions simultaneously | 21.5 million adults (SAMHSA, 2023) | Integrated treatment access without separate authorization for each condition |

### Disproportionately Affected Populations

Parity violations do not affect all populations equally.
Several groups face compounded barriers due to intersecting vulnerabilities:

- **Rural residents**: Over 160 million Americans live in Mental Health Professional Shortage Areas (SAMHSA, 2023).
Rural residents face both provider supply shortages and the network adequacy failures that parity law is designed to address, but with fewer alternatives when in-network care is unavailable.
In rural areas, 55% of counties have no practicing psychiatrist, making the gap between in-network listings and actual availability even more consequential.
- **Racial and ethnic minorities**: Black and Hispanic Americans are less likely to have health insurance, less likely to receive MH/SUD treatment, and more likely to receive care through safety-net providers with limited insurance participation.
Cultural and linguistic barriers compound insurance access problems.
A 2023 study in *JAMA Psychiatry* found that Black patients were 30% less likely than white patients to have an MH/SUD claim approved on initial submission.
Hispanic patients face additional barriers, with only 30% of behavioral health providers offering services in Spanish (SAMHSA, 2023).
- **LGBTQ+ individuals**: Higher rates of MH/SUD conditions combined with discrimination in insurance coverage for gender-affirming care and limited availability of culturally competent in-network providers.
LGBTQ+ individuals are 2.5 times more likely to experience depression and anxiety than heterosexual individuals (National Alliance on Mental Illness, 2023), but insurer directories often lack information on provider cultural competency, making it difficult to find appropriate in-network care.
- **Adolescents and young adults**: The 2020-2023 youth mental health crisis produced a 42% increase in emergency department visits for MH conditions among adolescents (CDC, 2023).
Pediatric behavioral health networks are thinner than adult networks, with child/adolescent psychiatrists having the lowest insurance acceptance rate of any specialty (28%, AACAP, 2023).
The average wait time for a new patient appointment with a child psychiatrist is 7-11 months in many metropolitan areas (AACAP, 2023).
- **People in the criminal justice system**: Approximately 44% of people in jails and 37% of people in state prisons have a mental health condition (BJS, 2023).
Upon reentry, these individuals face insurance enrollment barriers and parity violations simultaneously.
The gap between release and insurance enrollment can be weeks to months, during which continuity of psychiatric medication and treatment is interrupted.
- **Veterans transitioning to civilian insurance**: Veterans leaving VA coverage for employer-sponsored or marketplace plans may experience a reduction in MH/SUD coverage quality due to parity violations in commercial insurance that do not exist in the VA system.
The VA provides integrated MH/SUD care without prior authorization for most services, making the transition to a commercial plan with prior authorization requirements and narrow networks particularly disruptive.
- **People with eating disorders**: Eating disorder treatment, particularly residential and intensive outpatient programs, is among the most frequently denied MH/SUD services.
A 2023 analysis by the Alliance for Eating Disorders Awareness found that insurers denied residential eating disorder treatment claims at approximately three times the rate of residential treatment for other MH conditions.
Eating disorders have the highest mortality rate of any mental health condition, making insurance barriers to treatment a life-or-death issue.
The average cost of residential eating disorder treatment ranges from $30,000 to $60,000 per month, creating enormous out-of-pocket exposure when claims are denied.
- **Frontline and essential workers**: Workers in healthcare, emergency services, retail, and food service experienced elevated rates of MH/SUD conditions during and after the COVID-19 pandemic.
Many of these workers are in small employer plans or part-time positions with limited behavioral health coverage, and they may lack the time and resources to navigate prior authorization and appeals processes.
A 2023 RAND study found that healthcare workers experienced depression rates 2.3 times higher than the general population, but were 40% less likely to access MH/SUD treatment through their insurance.
- **Older adults transitioning from employer coverage to Medicare**: Adults aging out of employer-sponsored plans into Medicare fee-for-service lose MHPAEA protections because Medicare FFS is not subject to the parity law.
This transition creates a coverage cliff for individuals with ongoing MH/SUD treatment needs.
The Medicare 190-day lifetime limit on psychiatric inpatient care has no equivalent in commercial insurance, making the transition particularly harmful for older adults with serious mental illness.

### Secondary Stakeholders

- **Employers**: Bear the cost of health plan premiums; affected by employee productivity losses from untreated MH/SUD conditions.
Estimated $47.6 billion in annual lost productivity from depression alone (National Safety Council, 2023).
Employers also face absenteeism costs ($3,600 per employee with untreated depression annually), presenteeism (reduced on-the-job productivity), and increased turnover among employees unable to access MH/SUD treatment.
A growing number of large employers -- including JPMorgan Chase, Starbucks, and Google -- have invested in enhanced MH/SUD benefits beyond minimum parity requirements, recognizing the business case for comprehensive coverage.
The National Business Group on Health (now the Business Group on Health) has published case studies demonstrating a 3:1 to 5:1 return on investment for comprehensive MH/SUD benefits, driven by reduced medical claims, disability costs, and presenteeism.
However, the majority of mid-size and small employers remain focused on cost containment and rely on their insurers or TPAs for parity compliance, often without independently evaluating whether their plans meet the standard.
- **State and local governments**: Fund Medicaid programs, safety-net mental health services, and criminal justice costs when MH/SUD goes untreated.
Untreated mental illness costs state and local governments an estimated $280 billion annually in emergency services, incarceration, and lost tax revenue (National Council for Mental Wellbeing, 2023).
State governments also bear the cost of operating community mental health centers and crisis services that serve as a safety net when insurance fails.
- **Criminal justice system**: Approximately 44% of people in jails and 37% of people in state prisons have a mental health condition (BJS, 2023).
Insurance barriers to community-based treatment contribute to criminal justice involvement.
County jails are now the largest providers of mental health care in many states -- a fact that underscores the failure of the insurance system to provide accessible community-based treatment.
The Los Angeles County Jail, Cook County Jail (Chicago), and Rikers Island (New York) each hold more people with serious mental illness than any psychiatric hospital in their respective states.
- **Emergency departments**: Serve as default MH/SUD treatment settings when outpatient care is unavailable.
Psychiatric-related ED visits increased 31% from 2017 to 2022 (CDC, 2023).
The average psychiatric ED visit costs $2,264, compared to $480-$800 for an outpatient MH/SUD session, making inadequate insurance coverage a driver of healthcare cost inflation.
Patients presenting to EDs with MH/SUD emergencies often face "psychiatric boarding" -- waiting hours or days in the ED for an inpatient psychiatric bed -- because insurer prior authorization requirements delay admission.
- **Public schools**: Schools are increasingly serving as de facto mental health providers for children who cannot access insured services.
The National Association of School Psychologists estimates that schools provide mental health services to over 10 million students annually, often because insurance barriers prevent access to community-based providers.
The average student-to-school-psychologist ratio is 1:1,211, far exceeding the recommended ratio of 1:500, reflecting the burden that insurance failures place on educational institutions.
- **Health plans and hospital systems**: Hospital systems face financial strain from uncompensated psychiatric ED care and the costs of psychiatric boarding.
These costs are ultimately passed to all health plan enrollees through higher negotiated rates.
Health systems that employ psychiatrists and behavioral health providers face the same reimbursement rate gap, making it difficult to sustain integrated behavioral health departments.
The American Hospital Association has estimated that psychiatric boarding costs hospitals approximately $2,200 per day per patient, with an average boarding duration of 2.5 days -- costs that are often uncompensated because the boarding occurs before formal admission (which requires insurer authorization).
- **Families navigating the appeals process**: The burden of filing appeals and navigating insurer denial processes falls disproportionately on family members who are already serving as caregivers.
NAMI's 2023 survey of family caregivers found that 68% had spent more than 10 hours managing insurance denials and appeals for a family member's MH/SUD treatment in the prior year.
The emotional toll of repeated denials compounds the clinical challenges of supporting a family member with a mental health condition, creating a secondary trauma effect that the system does not acknowledge or accommodate.
- **Telehealth and digital health providers**: The rapid expansion of telehealth for MH/SUD services during and after the COVID-19 pandemic created a new stakeholder group.
Telehealth platforms like Talkspace, BetterHelp, and Cerebral serve millions of patients, but their integration into insurer networks remains uneven.
Telehealth providers face the same reimbursement rate gaps and prior authorization barriers as in-person providers, and some insurers have imposed additional requirements (such as limiting the number of telehealth sessions per year) that do not apply to in-person medical services.

## Who Has Power

### Decision Makers

| Actor | Role | Authority | Resources | Influence Level |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| DOL Employee Benefits Security Administration (EBSA) | Enforces MHPAEA for ERISA-governed plans | Investigatory and corrective authority over ~2.5 million plans | ~500 investigators; FY2024 budget of $220 million (total EBSA) | High (for self-insured plans) |
| CMS Center for Consumer Information and Insurance Oversight (CCIIO) | Enforces parity for individual/small group and Medicaid managed care | Authority over ACA marketplace plans and Medicaid | Shared resources across multiple mandates | High (for individual market) |
| State insurance commissioners | Enforce parity for fully insured plans within their state | Examination, fine, and corrective action authority | Varies widely; 18 states have dedicated parity staff | Medium to High (state-dependent) |
| Congressional committees (HELP, Ways & Means, E&C) | Authorizing and appropriating legislation on parity enforcement | Legislative authority to expand, fund, or weaken parity requirements | Staff and hearing authority | High |
| Federal courts | Adjudicate private lawsuits and DOL enforcement actions under ERISA | Interpret MHPAEA requirements and remedies available | Case-by-case authority | Medium |
| State legislatures | Enact state parity laws that exceed federal minimum | Authority over fully insured plans | Varies by state | Medium |

### Key Organizations and Institutions

**Industry Organizations**:

- **America's Health Insurance Plans (AHIP)**: The national trade association representing health insurers.
AHIP lobbies against expansion of parity enforcement requirements, arguing that existing law is adequate and that further regulation increases costs.
Annual lobbying expenditure: approximately $11.4 million (OpenSecrets, 2023).
AHIP's member companies cover over 200 million Americans.
AHIP has submitted extensive regulatory comments opposing the 2024 final rule's outcomes data requirement and meaningful benefits standard.
- **ERISA Industry Committee (ERIC)**: Represents large self-insured employers.
ERIC opposes expansion of state authority over self-insured plans and argues that the 2024 final rule imposes excessive compliance costs.
Member companies include Fortune 500 employers that collectively cover tens of millions of workers.
ERIC's core position is that ERISA's uniform federal standard for self-insured plans should be preserved, even at the cost of reduced enforcement.
- **Blue Cross Blue Shield Association (BCBSA)**: Represents 34 independent BCBS companies covering over 115 million Americans.
BCBSA has taken a more moderate position than AHIP on some parity issues, reflecting its member companies' role as both insurers and community health organizations.
Several individual BCBS companies (notably Blue Cross Blue Shield of Massachusetts and CareFirst BlueCross BlueShield) have invested in enhanced behavioral health programs that exceed federal parity requirements.
- **National Association of Health Underwriters (NAHU)**: Represents health insurance brokers and agents who advise employers on plan selection.
NAHU's position on parity is mixed: its members benefit from plan complexity (which generates consulting fees) but also face employer demand for plans that genuinely address MH/SUD needs.

**Managed Behavioral Health Organizations (MBHOs)**: Companies that administer behavioral health benefits for insurers and employers. They implement the utilization management practices that most directly affect patient access.

| MBHO | Parent Company | Covered Lives (approx.) | Revenue Model |
|------|---------------|------------------------|---------------|
| Optum Behavioral Health | UnitedHealth Group | ~60 million | Capitation and fee-for-service |
| Carelon Behavioral Health | Elevance Health (formerly Anthem) | ~40 million | Capitation and ASO |
| Magellan Health | Molina Healthcare | ~20 million | Capitation |
| Evernorth Behavioral Health | Cigna Group | ~25 million | Integrated with pharmacy benefits |
| Lyra Health | Independent | ~10 million | Employer-sponsored EAP+ model |

**Advocacy Organizations**:

- **National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI)**: The largest grassroots mental health advocacy organization, with over 600 state and local affiliates.
NAMI has been a primary driver of parity enforcement legislation and public education.
Budget: approximately $44 million (2023).
NAMI's helpline receives over 200,000 calls annually, many related to insurance barriers.
NAMI's advocacy infrastructure includes a federal lobbying team, state advocacy network, and annual Congressional lobbying day that brings thousands of advocates to Washington.
- **The Kennedy Forum**: Founded by former Representative Patrick Kennedy, a co-author of MHPAEA.
The Kennedy Forum conducts parity compliance research, trains state regulators, and advocates for enforcement funding.
The Parity Track initiative monitors and publicly rates state enforcement efforts, creating accountability pressure on state insurance departments.
The Kennedy Forum has trained over 500 state regulators on parity enforcement methods since 2018.
- **American Psychiatric Association (APA)**: Professional association of approximately 37,000 psychiatrists.
APA advocates for higher reimbursement rates, reduced prior authorization burden, and stronger parity enforcement.
Budget: approximately $65 million (2023).
APA conducts the annual Practice Survey that provides key data on insurer participation rates and publishes the Practice Guidelines that serve as the clinical standard for psychiatric care.
- **American Psychological Association**: Represents approximately 122,000 psychologists.
Advocates for network inclusion, adequate reimbursement for psychological services, and practice authority expansion.
The APA (psychology) has been particularly active on prior authorization reform, documenting the time burden that authorization requirements place on psychologists and their patients.
- **National Association of Social Workers (NASW)**: Represents approximately 120,000 social workers, many of whom are licensed clinical social workers (LCSWs) providing MH/SUD services.
NASW advocates for reimbursement rate equity and reduced administrative burden.
LCSWs are the largest category of MH/SUD providers in the United States, making NASW's advocacy particularly significant for network adequacy.
- **National Council for Mental Wellbeing**: Represents over 3,000 community-based behavioral health organizations.
Advocates for expanded access and adequate Medicaid reimbursement.
Member organizations serve approximately 10 million people annually and are often the providers of last resort for patients who cannot access insured care.
- **American Society of Addiction Medicine (ASAM)**: Professional association for addiction medicine physicians.
ASAM publishes the ASAM Criteria for SUD treatment placement, which serves as the clinical standard against which insurer medical necessity determinations are measured.
The 2024 final rule's prohibition on the "independent professional judgment" defense was informed in part by ASAM's documentation of insurer departures from the ASAM Criteria.
- **American Academy of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry (AACAP)**: Professional association representing approximately 10,000 child and adolescent psychiatrists.
AACAP has been a leading voice on pediatric behavioral health network inadequacy, documenting the shortage of approximately 21,000 child and adolescent psychiatrists and the 28% insurance acceptance rate among practicing specialists.

**Legal Advocacy Organizations**:

- **Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law**: The leading national legal advocacy organization for people with mental health conditions. Provides legal support for parity enforcement litigation and produces compliance guides for advocates.
- **National Health Law Program (NHeLP)**: Provides legal analysis and litigation support on health law issues, including parity enforcement.
- **Legal Action Center**: Focuses on discrimination against people with addiction histories, including insurance parity enforcement for SUD treatment.

### Influencers

- **Milliman, Inc.**: Actuarial consulting firm that publishes the most widely cited research on parity compliance, including analyses of denial rates, out-of-network utilization, and reimbursement disparities. Milliman data is used by advocates, regulators, and courts as evidence of parity violations. The firm's Parity Benchmarking Report (published every 2-3 years) is the closest thing to a national parity compliance assessment.
- **Meadows Mental Health Policy Institute (MMHPI)**: Texas-based research organization that provides state-level mental health policy analysis. MMHPI's economic analyses of the cost of untreated mental illness have been influential in state legislative debates.
- **Patient advocacy coalitions**: Organizations including Mental Health America, Faces & Voices of Recovery, and the National Council on Alcoholism and Drug Dependence advocate for parity enforcement from the patient perspective.
- **State attorneys general**: Have filed enforcement actions and amicus briefs supporting stronger parity enforcement. The 2020 multistate attorney general letter urging DOL to strengthen NQTL enforcement contributed to the 2024 final rule. Attorneys general in California, Massachusetts, Illinois, and New York have been particularly active. California Attorney General Rob Bonta's 2023 investigation into ghost networks resulted in corrective action agreements with three major insurers, establishing a precedent for state-level network adequacy enforcement. Massachusetts Attorney General Andrea Campbell has used the state's consumer protection statute (Chapter 93A) alongside parity law to pursue insurers, a dual legal theory that has produced larger settlements than parity enforcement alone.
- **Actuarial firms and consultants**: Beyond Milliman, firms including Mercer, Aon, and Willis Towers Watson advise employers on health plan design and parity compliance. Their recommendations shape plan design for millions of workers. These firms occupy a gatekeeping role: their recommendations on utilization management design, network configuration, and cost containment strategy determine the practical parity compliance of the plans they advise. The 2024 final rule's NQTL comparative analysis requirement has created a new consulting market, with these firms offering parity compliance services that generate revenue from the same regulatory framework they advised clients to resist during the rulemaking process.
- **Third-party administrators (TPAs)**: Companies such as Meritain Health (Aetna subsidiary), Sedgwick, and UMR (UnitedHealth Group subsidiary) administer self-insured employer plans and are responsible for the day-to-day implementation of benefit design, claims adjudication, and utilization management. TPAs play a critical operational role in parity compliance because they execute the NQTLs that plans must now analyze and justify under the 2024 final rule. However, the ERISA framework holds the plan sponsor (employer), not the TPA, ultimately responsible for compliance, creating an accountability gap when TPAs apply discriminatory practices that the employer does not monitor.

## Stakeholder Interests and Alignment

### Aligned Interests (Potential Coalition Partners)

| Coalition | Shared Interest | Strength |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| NAMI, APA, Kennedy Forum, patient groups | Stronger NQTL enforcement, higher MH/SUD reimbursement, proactive audits | Strong |
| State insurance commissioners, state attorneys general | Greater state enforcement authority, federal-state coordination | Strong |
| Behavioral health providers (psychiatrists, psychologists, social workers) | Higher reimbursement rates, reduced prior auth burden, network adequacy | Strong |
| Employers concerned about productivity, bipartisan legislators | Evidence-based parity enforcement that improves workforce health | Moderate |
| Community mental health centers, safety-net providers, Medicaid advocates | Expanded parity coverage to Medicaid FFS and Medicare | Moderate |
| Emergency departments, hospital systems | Reduced psychiatric ED utilization through better outpatient insurance access | Moderate |
| School systems, pediatric advocates | Improved insurance coverage for child/adolescent MH services | Moderate |
| Telehealth platforms, digital health companies | Parity standards for telehealth that increase patient volume and reimbursement | Moderate |
| Criminal justice reform advocates, county governments | Reduced incarceration of people with MH/SUD through better insurance coverage | Moderate |

The strongest coalition potential exists between patient advocacy organizations, provider associations, and state regulators who share a common interest in stronger enforcement.
This coalition was instrumental in the 2024 final rule rulemaking, where NAMI, the APA, the Kennedy Forum, and the National Association of Insurance Commissioners submitted coordinated comments supporting the outcomes data requirement, the meaningful benefits standard, and the network composition provisions.
The coalition's effectiveness depends on its ability to maintain unity across organizations with different primary missions (patient advocacy, provider economics, regulatory authority) while presenting a coherent enforcement agenda to Congress and the regulatory agencies.

### Conflicting Interests

- **Insurers vs. patients/providers**: Insurers seek to manage MH/SUD costs through utilization controls; patients and providers want fewer barriers to care
- **Self-insured employers vs. state regulators**: Employers value ERISA preemption as protection from state mandates; states want authority to enforce parity in all plans within their borders
- **MBHOs vs. integrated care advocates**: MBHOs profit from behavioral health carve-out models; integrated care advocates argue carve-outs inherently create parity problems by separating MH/SUD management from medical benefits
- **Small employers vs. parity advocates**: Small employers fear compliance costs; advocates argue all employers should meet the same standards
- **TPAs vs. enforcement agencies**: Third-party administrators who administer self-insured plans may resist disclosure of plan-level data that reveals non-compliance, while enforcement agencies need this data to evaluate compliance
- **Pharmaceutical companies vs. PBMs on step therapy**: Pharmaceutical manufacturers want their brand-name psychiatric medications prescribed without step therapy barriers; PBMs want to maintain step therapy as a cost management and rebate negotiation tool
- **Telehealth platforms vs. traditional providers**: Telehealth companies benefit from parity enforcement that expands access and increases utilization, but some traditional providers view telehealth as competition that may lower reimbursement rates or reduce demand for in-person services
- **Plan sponsors (employers) vs. their own TPAs/MBHOs**: Employers bear fiduciary responsibility for parity compliance, but they delegate benefit administration to TPAs and MBHOs whose financial incentives may conflict with parity. This principal-agent problem means that employers may be held liable for violations they did not know about or approve

### The Insurance Industry Power Structure

The insurance industry's influence over parity enforcement operates through multiple channels:

1. **Lobbying**: AHIP and individual insurers spend over $150 million annually on federal and state lobbying (OpenSecrets, 2023). The five largest health insurers (UnitedHealth Group, Elevance Health, CVS/Aetna, Cigna, and Humana) collectively spent $52.3 million on federal lobbying in 2023.
2. **Plan design control**: Insurers and MBHOs design the NQTLs, medical necessity criteria, and network standards that determine whether parity is achieved in practice
3. **Data control**: Until the 2024 final rule, plans controlled the data needed to evaluate parity compliance and had no obligation to share it
4. **Revolving door**: Former CMS and DOL officials frequently move to positions in the insurance industry, and vice versa. A 2023 analysis identified 14 former senior EBSA officials who moved to health insurance companies or consulting firms within five years of leaving government.
5. **Regulatory comment dominance**: During the 2024 final rule comment period, the insurance industry submitted over 1,200 substantive comments, compared to approximately 400 from advocacy organizations and provider groups. While DOL ultimately adopted many advocate-supported provisions, the industry's comment volume and technical sophistication shape the regulatory process.

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on parity policy:

- **Lobbying**: AHIP spent $11.4 million on federal lobbying in 2023. UnitedHealth Group (parent of Optum Behavioral Health) spent $10.4 million. NAMI spent $1.2 million. The financial asymmetry is approximately 10:1 in favor of the insurance industry.
- **Campaign contributions**: Health insurance industry PACs contributed $43.7 million to federal candidates in the 2022 election cycle (OpenSecrets, 2023). Mental health advocacy organizations contributed less than $2 million. Insurance industry contributions are concentrated on members of the Senate HELP, Finance, and Banking committees, and the House Education & Workforce, Ways & Means, and Energy & Commerce committees -- the committees with jurisdiction over parity legislation.
- **Regulatory comments**: During the 2024 final rule comment period, AHIP, ERIC, and individual insurers submitted detailed comments opposing the outcomes data requirement and network composition standards. Advocacy organizations submitted comments supporting these requirements. The final rule adopted many advocate-supported provisions, but with extended compliance timelines sought by industry.
- **Litigation**: Insurers and employers have filed legal challenges to parity regulations, including challenges to the scope of NQTLs covered, the burden of proof standard, and the remedies available for violations. The legal landscape remains contested. Key pending litigation includes challenges to the 2024 final rule's outcomes data requirement and the meaningful benefits standard.
- **Public advocacy**: NAMI, the Kennedy Forum, and allied organizations conduct public education campaigns, organize lobby days, and maintain media attention on parity issues. The Kennedy Forum's Parity Track initiative publicly rates state parity enforcement efforts, creating accountability pressure.
- **Expertise and research**: Milliman's actuarial analyses provide the evidence base for parity enforcement. The Kennedy Forum and NAMI produce compliance guides and training materials used by state regulators. The APA's Practice Survey provides the most widely cited data on psychiatrist insurance participation rates.
- **Media investigations**: Investigative journalism by STAT News, ProPublica, and the New York Times has exposed specific insurer parity violations, generating public pressure that complements regulatory enforcement. The *Wit v. UBH* litigation was informed in part by investigative reporting on UBH's claims denial practices.
- **Whistleblowers and insider disclosures**: Former insurer employees and MBHO clinicians have provided critical evidence of internal practices that violate parity. In the *Wit v. UBH* case, former UBH employees testified that the company's internal utilization management guidelines were developed with explicit cost containment targets, contradicting UBH's public claim that its criteria reflected clinical best practices. Whistleblower protections for employees who report parity violations remain inadequate under current law.
- **Academic and think tank research**: Organizations including the Brookings Institution, the Urban Institute, and the Commonwealth Fund publish policy analyses that inform parity debates. The quality and independence of research matters because the insurance industry also funds research through industry-affiliated organizations. The distinction between independently funded and industry-funded research is not always clear to policymakers, creating information asymmetry in the policy process.

## Emerging Stakeholder Dynamics

### The 2024 Final Rule Realignment

The 2024 final rule has shifted stakeholder dynamics by creating new obligations and new opportunities:

- **Plan sponsors (employers) face new fiduciary exposure**: The rule's requirement that plans produce data-driven NQTL comparative analyses means that employers can no longer claim ignorance of their plan's parity compliance status. This creates fiduciary liability under ERISA for employers who fail to ensure compliance, even if they delegate benefit administration to TPAs.
- **TPAs and MBHOs face accountability pressure**: The rule's documentation and data requirements make it harder for TPAs and MBHOs to apply discriminatory NQTLs without detection. This has created a consulting market for parity compliance services, with firms like Mercer, Milliman, and Aon offering NQTL analysis packages.
- **State regulators gain leverage**: Although the 2024 rule applies directly only at the federal level, its detailed compliance standards provide a benchmark that state regulators can use to evaluate fully insured plans within their jurisdiction. Several states have already adopted the federal rule's framework as a minimum standard for state enforcement.
- **Patient advocates gain data**: For the first time, the rule creates a legal right to plan-level compliance data that advocates and researchers can use to evaluate parity performance. This data will enable more targeted advocacy and litigation.
- **The plaintiffs' bar is activated**: ERISA attorneys are developing litigation strategies based on the 2024 rule's outcomes data requirement, anticipating that plan-level data will provide evidence for class action claims that were previously impossible to substantiate.

### The Post-*Loper Bright* Legal Landscape

The Supreme Court's 2024 decision in *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* overturning Chevron deference has introduced a new stakeholder dynamic.
Industry groups (AHIP, ERIC, the Chamber of Commerce) now see the courts as a potentially more favorable venue for challenging parity regulation, because the Skidmore standard that replaced Chevron gives less deference to agency interpretations.
This has increased the strategic importance of Congressional action to codify the 2024 rule's key provisions, because statutory text is reviewed under a different standard than regulatory interpretation.
Advocacy organizations have responded by prioritizing legislative codification alongside their support for the 2024 rule's implementation.
