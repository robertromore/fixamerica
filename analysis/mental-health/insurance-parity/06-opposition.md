# Mental Health Insurance Parity: Opposition to Reform

Proposals to strengthen mental health insurance parity enforcement face resistance from those who believe that existing law is adequate, that stronger enforcement will increase costs and reduce plan flexibility, and that the behavioral health provider shortage -- rather than insurer practices -- is the primary barrier to care.

---

## Who Opposes Reform

### Organized Opposition

| Actor | Interest | Resources | Tactics |
|-------|----------|-----------|---------|
| America's Health Insurance Plans (AHIP) | Preserve insurer discretion in benefit design and utilization management | $11.4 million annual lobbying (OpenSecrets, 2023) | Lobbying, regulatory comments, legal challenges, industry research |
| ERISA Industry Committee (ERIC) | Protect self-insured employer plans from expanded regulation | $1.8 million annual lobbying (OpenSecrets, 2023) | Lobbying against state enforcement authority and expanded DOL mandates |
| UnitedHealth Group / Optum | Maintain behavioral health management model through Optum BH | $10.4 million annual lobbying (OpenSecrets, 2023) | Lobbying, regulatory comments, litigation |
| Elevance Health (formerly Anthem) / Carelon | Preserve behavioral health carve-out revenue model | $6.8 million annual lobbying (OpenSecrets, 2023) | Regulatory comments, industry coalitions |
| U.S. Chamber of Commerce | Oppose new employer mandates and compliance costs | $81 million annual lobbying (OpenSecrets, 2023) | Broad employer advocacy against regulatory expansion |
| National Federation of Independent Business (NFIB) | Protect small employers from compliance costs | $5.2 million annual lobbying (OpenSecrets, 2023) | Advocacy against mandate expansion |
| National Association of Manufacturers (NAM) | Protect large manufacturers' self-insured plan flexibility | $14.2 million annual lobbying (OpenSecrets, 2023) | Coalition lobbying with ERIC and Chamber |

### Ideological Opposition

- **Fiscal conservatives**: Object to regulations that increase health plan costs, arguing that higher premiums reduce coverage and that market competition, not government mandates, should drive benefit design. Organizations including the Heritage Foundation and the Cato Institute have published analyses arguing that parity mandates distort the insurance market.
- **Libertarians**: Oppose government mandates on benefit design as interference with private contracts between employers, insurers, and employees. Argue that individuals should be free to choose plans with or without MH/SUD coverage. The Reason Foundation has argued that parity mandates reduce consumer choice and increase premiums for those who do not value MH/SUD coverage.
- **Free-market advocates**: Contend that requiring parity distorts the insurance market, preventing plans from pricing MH/SUD benefits based on actuarial risk and clinical uncertainty. Argue that utilization management tools reflect legitimate cost control, not discrimination. The American Enterprise Institute has published research questioning whether regulatory parity can improve access without addressing underlying provider supply constraints.

### Professional Opposition

- **Some employer benefits consultants**: Benefits consulting firms that advise employers on plan design may resist parity enforcement because it constrains the cost management strategies they recommend. Consultants who specialize in utilization management optimization have a professional interest in maintaining prior authorization and step therapy flexibility. However, the 2024 final rule has created a countervailing incentive: some consultants now offer parity compliance services, generating revenue from the enforcement framework they previously resisted.
- **Some actuaries**: While Milliman's research supports parity enforcement, some actuaries in insurer employment argue that MH/SUD utilization management reflects legitimate actuarial principles -- that behavioral health treatment episodes are less predictable, harder to standardize, and more susceptible to overutilization than medical/surgical episodes. This view is contested by the APA and other clinical organizations but carries weight in regulatory proceedings where actuarial analysis is central.
- **Some primary care physician organizations**: A small segment of primary care advocacy has expressed concern that parity enforcement will redirect healthcare spending toward behavioral health at the expense of primary care. This concern is not widely shared but has appeared in regulatory comment letters from some physician groups who view parity as creating a relative advantage for behavioral health reimbursement.

### Institutional Inertia

Beyond organized and ideological opposition, parity enforcement faces resistance from institutional inertia within the insurance industry and the regulatory system:

- **Embedded operational systems**: Insurers and MBHOs have built their utilization management, claims processing, and network management systems around the assumption that MH/SUD benefits can be managed differently from medical/surgical benefits. Reconfiguring these systems for parity compliance requires significant technology investment, workflow redesign, and retraining of clinical reviewers. The compliance cost is not just financial but operational -- it requires changing how hundreds of thousands of claims are processed daily.
- **Regulatory capacity constraints**: Even regulators who support stronger enforcement face resource limitations. The DOL cannot audit more than a fraction of covered plans, and many state insurance departments lack dedicated parity staff. This capacity gap creates a de facto tolerance for non-compliance that the insurance industry has learned to exploit.
- **Knowledge asymmetry**: Plans possess the data needed to evaluate parity compliance, and they control the analytical methodology. Regulators and patients must rely on plan-generated information, creating an information asymmetry that favors the regulated entity. The 2024 final rule attempts to address this through mandatory data reporting, but the industry retains advantages in actuarial expertise and analytical sophistication.

## Opposition Arguments and Rebuttals

### Argument 1: Parity Enforcement Increases Premiums

**Claim**: "Stronger parity enforcement will force plans to cover more MH/SUD services, driving up premiums for all enrollees.
The Congressional Budget Office estimated that the 2024 final rule could increase premiums by 1-2% for affected plans."

**Evidence Cited**: CBO analysis of 2024 final rule premium impact; AHIP-commissioned studies on compliance costs; employer surveys indicating cost concerns.

**Reality**: CBO's own estimates place the premium impact at less than 1% for most plans, and the 2024 final rule's regulatory impact analysis estimated a per-enrollee annual cost increase of $1-$16 (89 Fed. Reg. 77586, 2024).
Independent analyses have consistently found that parity compliance costs are modest.
When MHPAEA was first enacted, industry predicted premium increases of 3-5%, but actual increases were negligible -- a 2012 DOL study found no measurable premium effect from MHPAEA implementation.
Moreover, untreated MH/SUD conditions impose far greater costs on employers through absenteeism, disability, and reduced productivity -- an estimated $47.6 billion annually from depression alone (National Safety Council, 2023).

The Federal Employee Health Benefits Program (FEHBP) experience provides direct evidence.
When FEHBP implemented full parity for MH/SUD benefits in 2001, premium increases were less than 1%, and total MH/SUD spending as a share of health plan costs actually decreased from 3.9% to 3.6% over the subsequent three years, as patients shifted from crisis and emergency care to less expensive outpatient treatment (Goldman et al., *New England Journal of Medicine*, 2006).

A 2024 Mercer analysis of large employer plans found that the average annual compliance cost for the 2024 final rule's NQTL requirements was $3,000-$8,000 per plan for employers with 500-2,000 employees.
For the average enrollee, this translates to less than $2 per year -- a cost that is dwarfed by the $3,600 annual productivity loss per employee with untreated depression.

**Rebuttal**: The cost of parity compliance is consistently overestimated by the industry and consistently small when measured.
The cost of non-compliance -- in terms of untreated illness, emergency care, criminal justice involvement, and lost productivity -- is far higher.
Parity enforcement is not a cost increase; it is a reallocation from hidden costs (emergency rooms, jails, lost productivity) to appropriate clinical care.

### Argument 2: Provider Shortages Are the Real Problem, Not Insurer Practices

**Claim**: "Plans cannot build adequate MH/SUD networks because there are not enough behavioral health providers.
The solution is to train more providers, not to punish plans for a workforce shortage they did not create."

**Evidence Cited**: SAMHSA Mental Health Professional Shortage Area designations (over 160 million Americans in shortage areas); APA workforce data showing aging psychiatrist population; AACAP data on child/adolescent psychiatrist shortages.

**Reality**: The provider shortage is real, but it is not the sole or even primary cause of network inadequacy.
Plans' own reimbursement practices drive providers out of networks.
Only 38% of psychiatrists accept new commercial insurance patients, compared to 86% of primary care physicians (Bishop et al., JAMA, 2023).
The gap is not because there are fewer psychiatrists but because insurance reimbursement for psychiatric services is 20-40% below comparable medical rates (Milliman, 2024).
Providers who can sustain their practices with self-pay patients rationally decline to accept insurance.
Network inadequacy is partly a supply problem and partly a demand problem created by insurer pricing.

The distinction is visible in the data: in metropolitan areas with high concentrations of mental health providers, out-of-network utilization for MH/SUD services remains far higher than for medical/surgical services.
If the problem were purely one of provider supply, out-of-network rates would be high in shortage areas and low in provider-rich areas.
Instead, out-of-network rates are elevated everywhere, indicating that network design and reimbursement -- not just supply -- are driving the disparity.
For example, in Manhattan -- which has one of the highest concentrations of psychiatrists per capita in the country -- the out-of-network rate for MH/SUD outpatient services is still approximately 14%, compared to 2.8% for medical/surgical outpatient services (Milliman, 2023).

**Rebuttal**: If plans paid competitive reimbursement rates, the number of in-network MH/SUD providers would increase substantially.
The provider shortage argument conflates two distinct problems: an absolute shortage of providers in some areas (which requires workforce investment) and an artificial shortage of in-network providers caused by low reimbursement (which parity enforcement can address).
Both problems need solutions, and one does not excuse the other.

### Argument 3: NQTLs Are Clinically Necessary, Not Discriminatory

**Claim**: "Prior authorization, step therapy, and medical necessity reviews are legitimate clinical tools that ensure patients receive appropriate care. They are not applied differently to MH/SUD; they reflect the clinical characteristics of behavioral health treatment, which is more variable and harder to standardize."

**Evidence Cited**: AHIP position papers on clinical appropriateness of utilization review; studies showing variation in MH/SUD treatment outcomes; actuarial arguments about clinical uncertainty.

**Reality**: The data directly contradicts this claim.
Prior authorization is imposed on 30-40% of MH/SUD services but only 10-15% of medical/surgical services (APA, 2023).
MH/SUD claims are denied at 22.4% compared to 11.7% for medical claims (Milliman, 2023).
When denials are appealed, 50-75% are overturned -- indicating that the initial denial was not clinically justified (NAIC, 2023).
If NQTLs were applied based on genuine clinical criteria rather than cost management, there would be no systematic disparity in denial rates, and appeals would not result in such high overturn rates.

The *Wit v. United Behavioral Health* case provided the most detailed evidence on this point.
The court found that UBH's medical necessity guidelines were not based on generally accepted standards of care but were instead developed by UBH's own staff with input from cost management considerations.
Expert testimony from multiple clinical witnesses established that UBH's criteria were significantly more restrictive than those published by the APA, ASAM, and AACAP for the same conditions and treatment settings.
Specifically, UBH's guidelines required that patients demonstrate imminent danger to themselves or others before authorizing inpatient treatment -- a standard far more restrictive than the APA's guideline, which authorizes inpatient care when a patient's condition cannot be safely managed at a lower level of care.

The concurrent review disparity is also telling.
For MH/SUD inpatient care, plans typically require re-authorization every 3-7 days.
For medical/surgical inpatient care (e.g., cardiac surgery, orthopedic procedures), concurrent review occurs every 7-14 days or not at all.
This disparity has no clinical justification; it reflects the insurer's greater motivation to limit MH/SUD inpatient stays.

**Rebuttal**: NQTLs are legitimate tools when applied comparably across benefit categories.
MHPAEA does not prohibit utilization review; it requires that the processes and standards applied to MH/SUD be no more restrictive than those applied to medical/surgical benefits.
The evidence shows a consistent, measurable disparity that cannot be explained by clinical differences alone.

### Argument 4: The 2024 Final Rule Exceeds Statutory Authority

**Claim**: "The 2024 final rule's requirement that plans evaluate outcomes data and demonstrate no disparate impact on MH/SUD access exceeds what MHPAEA authorizes. Congress required parity in benefit design, not parity in outcomes. The rule imposes an outcomes-based standard that has no basis in the statute."

**Evidence Cited**: Text of MHPAEA; legal analyses by employer and insurer groups; administrative law arguments about regulatory overreach; potential implications of *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* (2024) on agency deference.

**Reality**: The 2024 final rule is grounded in MHPAEA's text, which prohibits NQTLs that are applied more stringently to MH/SUD than to medical/surgical benefits. The rule interprets "applied more stringently" to encompass both design and effect -- a reasonable interpretation given that a facially neutral NQTL that produces severely disparate outcomes is, in practical terms, more restrictive. The CAA of 2021 explicitly authorized DOL to request and evaluate NQTL comparative analyses, and the legislative history of MHPAEA supports a broad reading of the NQTL prohibition. Courts will ultimately decide, but the regulatory agencies' interpretation is within the range of permissible readings of the statute.

The Supreme Court's 2024 decision in *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* overturned Chevron deference, which may affect courts' willingness to defer to the agencies' interpretation. However, the Skidmore standard that now applies still permits courts to give weight to agency interpretations based on their persuasiveness, and the agencies' interpretation is supported by the statutory text, legislative history, and the practical impossibility of enforcing parity without outcomes data.

**Rebuttal**: The argument that Congress intended parity in design but not outcomes would render parity law unenforceable.
A plan could design a facially neutral prior authorization system that, through undisclosed criteria, denies 50% of MH/SUD claims while approving 95% of medical claims -- and claim compliance because the "design" was the same.
Outcomes data is the only way to verify whether design-level parity translates into access-level parity.
The *Wit v. UBH* case illustrates this point precisely: UBH's medical necessity guidelines were facially neutral but produced dramatically different outcomes for MH/SUD patients compared to medical patients.
Without outcomes data, this pattern would have been invisible to regulators.
Congressional intent supports the agencies' interpretation: the legislative history of MHPAEA includes statements by its sponsors (Senators Domenici and Wellstone, Representatives Kennedy and Ramstad) emphasizing that the law was intended to achieve equal *access*, not merely equal benefit descriptions.
The CAA of 2021's requirement that plans submit comparative analyses for evaluation by DOL would be meaningless if those analyses could not consider whether the plans' NQTLs produced disparate results.

### Argument 5: Compliance Costs Are Disproportionate for Small Employers

**Claim**: "The 2024 final rule's documentation and data requirements impose compliance costs that small and mid-size self-insured employers cannot afford. These employers may respond by dropping MH/SUD coverage or moving to less comprehensive plans."

**Evidence Cited**: ERIC survey data on employer compliance cost estimates; NFIB concerns about small employer burden; administrative cost analyses.

**Reality**: MHPAEA already exempts the smallest employers (those with fewer than 50 employees and those who do not offer MH/SUD benefits). For mid-size self-insured employers, compliance burden is typically borne by third-party administrators (TPAs) and insurers who administer the plan, not by the employer directly. The 2024 final rule allows plans to rely on their TPAs and MBHOs for comparative analyses. Moreover, the ACA's essential health benefits requirement already mandates MH/SUD coverage in the individual and small group markets, and employers who self-insure make a voluntary decision to do so; they can purchase fully insured coverage if they prefer state-level regulation.

A 2024 analysis by Mercer found that the average compliance cost for the 2024 final rule's NQTL requirements was $3,000-$8,000 per plan for employers with 500-2,000 employees, with the bulk of the cost borne by the TPA. For large self-insured employers (5,000+ employees), the per-employee cost was less than $1.50 annually.

**Rebuttal**: The compliance cost argument is a legitimate concern that should be addressed through technical assistance and standardized templates, not through weakening enforcement. The solution is to make compliance easier, not to exempt large categories of plans from parity requirements.

### Argument 6: Ghost Network Enforcement Is Impractical

**Claim**: "Requiring real-time accuracy of provider directories is technically infeasible. Provider availability changes daily as clinicians adjust their caseloads, leave practices, or move. Holding plans accountable for directory accuracy imposes an impossible standard."

**Evidence Cited**: Technical challenges of maintaining real-time directories; provider reluctance to update availability status; turnover in behavioral health practices.

**Reality**: While perfect real-time accuracy may be unattainable, the current state -- where 40-60% of listed MH/SUD providers are unavailable (CA DMHC, 2023; HHS OIG, 2022) -- represents a systematic failure, not a technical limitation. Plans already maintain relatively accurate directories for medical/surgical providers, with ghost network rates estimated at 10-15% for primary care and specialty medical providers. The disparity in directory accuracy between behavioral health and medical providers is itself a parity issue.

Technology solutions exist: real-time eligibility verification systems, provider attestation portals, and third-party directory verification services (used by several state Medicaid programs) can significantly reduce ghost network rates. The CMS Interoperability and Patient Access final rule (2020) already requires some directory accuracy standards for Medicare Advantage plans.

**Rebuttal**: The question is not whether perfect accuracy is achievable but whether plans are making comparable efforts to maintain accurate directories for MH/SUD providers as they do for medical/surgical providers. Current evidence shows they are not, and the disparity contributes directly to the access gap that parity law is designed to prevent. Plans already maintain relatively accurate directories for high-volume medical specialties (primary care, cardiology, orthopedics) using automated verification systems. Applying the same technology and effort to behavioral health directories is not technically infeasible; it has simply not been prioritized because the consequences of inaccurate MH/SUD directories have historically been lower than for medical directories.

## Historical Patterns of Opposition

The insurance industry has opposed every significant expansion of parity law, predicting cost increases that failed to materialize.

| Year | Reform | Industry Prediction | Actual Outcome |
|------|--------|-------------------|----------------|
| 1996 | Mental Health Parity Act | Premium increases of 2-4% | No measurable effect (GAO, 2000) |
| 2001 | FEHBP parity implementation | Premium increases of 3-5% | <1% increase; MH/SUD share of costs decreased (Goldman et al., NEJM, 2006) |
| 2008 | MHPAEA | Premium increases of 3-5% | Negligible increase (DOL, 2012) |
| 2010 | ACA essential health benefits | Significant cost increase for individual market | Modest increase absorbed within overall ACA market changes |
| 2024 | 2024 Final Rule | Premium increases of 1-2% | TBD (estimated $1-$16/enrollee annually, 89 Fed. Reg. 77586) |

The pattern is consistent: each round of reform produces cost predictions that exceed actual costs.
This track record undermines the credibility of current cost objections.
The pattern also reveals a strategic purpose: cost predictions, even if inaccurate, create political uncertainty that slows or weakens reform.
By the time actual cost data shows the predictions were wrong, the legislative moment has passed and the weakened version of reform has been enacted.

The insurance industry's cost prediction methodology has been criticized by independent actuaries for systematic upward bias.
Milliman's analysis of industry cost projections found that projected parity compliance costs were overestimated by an average of 3-5x compared to actual costs in every case where post-implementation data was available (Milliman, 2023).
The overestimation reflects both strategic intent (higher projections create political opposition) and analytical bias (projections assume maximum utilization increases without accounting for the offsetting effects of earlier intervention and reduced crisis care).

## Legal Challenges

### Current and Anticipated Litigation

| Challenge | Challenger | Legal Theory | Status (as of 2025) |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|---------------------|
| 2024 final rule outcomes data requirement | AHIP, ERIC (anticipated) | Exceeds statutory authority; post-*Loper Bright* agency deference | Pre-litigation; industry signaling challenges |
| Meaningful benefits standard | Employer coalitions (anticipated) | No statutory basis for "effective access" standard | Pre-litigation |
| State parity enforcement of self-insured plans | Self-insured employers | ERISA preemption | Ongoing in multiple circuits |
| Reimbursement rate as parity metric | Individual insurers | MHPAEA addresses coverage design, not pricing | Pending in several district courts |

The post-*Loper Bright* environment creates additional uncertainty for the 2024 final rule.
Without Chevron deference, courts may scrutinize the agencies' interpretation more closely, potentially striking down provisions that the agencies would have prevailed on under the prior framework.
This legal risk underscores the importance of Congressional action to codify key provisions of the 2024 final rule into statute, reducing dependence on regulatory authority that may be judicially constrained.
Industry groups are aware of this legal landscape shift and may time their litigation strategy to coincide with favorable judicial appointments or circuit-specific precedents.
The prospect of legal challenges has also influenced industry behavior during the compliance phase: some plans are investing in minimal compliance (the least expensive interpretation of the rule's requirements) rather than robust compliance, calculating that the rule may be partially vacated before full enforcement begins.

## Counter-Strategies

### Political Strategies

- **Leverage bipartisan support**: Mental health parity has historically been a bipartisan issue. Frame enforcement strengthening as fulfilling the intent of Republican and Democratic lawmakers who passed MHPAEA.
- **Enlist employer allies**: Some large employers recognize that parity compliance improves workforce mental health and reduces long-term costs. Companies that have invested in comprehensive mental health benefits (e.g., Starbucks, Google, Unilever, JPMorgan Chase) can serve as models. The National Business Group on Health has published case studies showing positive ROI from comprehensive MH/SUD benefits.
- **Use state momentum**: The 39 states with parity laws exceeding federal requirements represent a political base for continued reform. State-level success stories provide evidence for federal action.
- **Engage veterans' organizations**: Veterans transitioning from VA to commercial insurance experience the parity gap directly. Veterans' organizations (VFW, American Legion, DAV) can provide powerful advocacy voices for enforcement. The VA provides MH/SUD care without prior authorization for most services, and veterans who transition to commercial plans experience the administrative burden of utilization management as a concrete reduction in their access to care.
- **Build bipartisan legislative coalitions**: The historical pattern of bipartisan support for parity (Domenici-Wellstone, Kennedy-Ramstad) should be replicated. Frame enforcement as fulfilling the promise of existing law rather than creating new mandates, which resonates with conservative legislators who value rule of law and government accountability.
- **Coordinate state attorney general actions**: Multi-state attorney general investigations create broader political pressure than individual state actions. The 2020 multistate letter to DOL demonstrated that coordinated state advocacy can influence federal regulatory action. Establishing a standing multistate parity enforcement working group would institutionalize this coordination.

### Communication Strategies

- **Lead with patient stories**: Personal accounts of care denied, treatment delayed, and families harmed are more persuasive than actuarial data. The Domenici-Wellstone legacy demonstrates that personal connection drives legislative action.
- **Reframe costs**: Shift the discussion from "the cost of parity compliance" to "the cost of parity non-compliance" -- emergency room visits, incarceration, lost productivity, and lives lost. The $280 billion annual cost of untreated mental illness dwarfs any compliance cost.
- **Use overturn data**: The fact that 50-75% of denied MH/SUD claims are overturned on appeal is a powerful proof point that initial denials are not clinically justified. This data point is difficult for the industry to rebut.
- **Highlight the 100% failure rate**: DOL's finding that 100% of initial NQTL comparative analyses were insufficient is a compelling indicator that the industry has not taken parity compliance seriously.
- **Contrast insurance industry profits with patient access**: The five largest health insurers reported combined net income of over $30 billion in 2023 (SEC filings). Juxtaposing industry profitability with patient denial rates and care abandonment creates a compelling narrative about where the healthcare dollar is going.
- **Use the care abandonment statistic**: The 29% care abandonment rate during MH/SUD prior authorization processes (versus 11% for medical/surgical) represents patients who did not receive a formal denial but were effectively denied care through administrative barriers. This statistic captures a harm that is invisible in denial rate data alone.
- **Center the youth mental health crisis**: Pediatric MH/SUD network inadequacy resonates across political lines because parents of all political orientations are struggling to find mental health care for their children. The 28% insurance acceptance rate among child and adolescent psychiatrists (AACAP, 2023) is a particularly compelling data point.
- **Frame parity as law enforcement, not new regulation**: Emphasize that MHPAEA has been law since 2008 and that stronger enforcement simply ensures that existing law is followed. This framing appeals to conservative and moderate audiences who support rule of law but are skeptical of new regulatory mandates.

### Policy Design Strategies

- **Build in technical assistance**: Address small employer concerns by providing compliance templates, standardized NQTL comparison methodologies, and DOL-funded technical assistance.
- **Phase implementation**: The 2024 final rule's phased compliance timeline reduces disruption and gives plans time to develop systems. Future legislation should continue this approach.
- **Create safe harbors**: Offer regulatory safe harbors for plans that meet specified standards (e.g., denial rate parity within 2 percentage points, out-of-network rate parity within 2x), reducing compliance uncertainty and giving plans a clear target.
- **Codify regulatory provisions**: Enact legislation that codifies the 2024 final rule's key provisions -- outcomes data requirement, meaningful benefits standard, network composition standards -- reducing vulnerability to legal challenge and ensuring durability regardless of administrative changes.
- **Fund enforcement separately**: Seek dedicated enforcement funding through user fees on plans rather than relying on annual appropriations, which are subject to political uncertainty and budget cuts.
- **Engage the business community**: Identify and amplify employers who have seen positive returns from comprehensive MH/SUD benefits. The Business Group on Health, which represents large employers, has published case studies demonstrating ROI from enhanced behavioral health coverage. When the business community itself advocates for parity enforcement, it undercuts the Chamber of Commerce and ERIC's arguments that enforcement is anti-business.
- **Document the substitution effect**: When industry adapts to new rules by shifting restrictions from regulated to unregulated categories, document and publicize the substitution. This creates political pressure for closing each new loophole and demonstrates that the industry's pattern of compliance gaming requires ongoing legislative attention.

### Anticipating the Next Wave of Opposition

Based on historical patterns, the next phase of opposition is likely to focus on:

- **Data manipulation in compliance reporting**: As plans are required to submit outcomes data under the 2024 final rule, some may engage in selective data presentation, coding manipulation, or methodological choices designed to minimize the appearance of disparities. Regulators should develop cross-validation techniques using external data sources (state claims databases, Milliman benchmarks) to detect these practices.
- **Reclassification of MH/SUD services**: Plans may attempt to reclassify certain MH/SUD services into benefit categories where comparison to medical/surgical services is more favorable, or create hybrid benefit classifications that obscure MH/SUD-specific restrictions.
- **Increased use of "value-based" coverage exceptions**: Plans may argue that differential coverage of MH/SUD services reflects "value-based" insurance design rather than parity violations, attempting to use the value-based care framework as a shield against enforcement.
- **Consolidation of behavioral health management**: As parity enforcement increases, the MBHO market may consolidate further, with smaller behavioral health managers acquired by larger entities that can absorb compliance costs. Greater consolidation could reduce competition and give the remaining MBHOs increased leverage over provider reimbursement rates, potentially creating new access barriers even as existing barriers are addressed.
