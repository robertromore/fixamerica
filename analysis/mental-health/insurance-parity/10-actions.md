# Mental Health Insurance Parity: What You Can Do

Mental health insurance parity is one of the few policy areas where individual action -- filing an appeal, submitting a complaint, or contacting a regulator -- directly advances systemic reform. Every appeal that overturns a wrongful denial generates enforcement data. Every complaint filed with DOL or a state insurance department adds to the evidentiary base that regulators use to identify and correct parity violations. The gap between parity law and parity practice persists in large part because fewer than 0.5% of denied MH/SUD claims are ever appealed (DOL EBSA, 2023). Closing that gap requires informed, persistent citizen engagement.

The actions in this section are organized by the audience best positioned to take them: individuals navigating their own insurance, community organizers building local coalitions, political advocates influencing legislation, and professionals working within the healthcare system. Each category includes specific, actionable steps with the evidence base for why they matter.

## Individual Actions

### Learn and Stay Informed

- **Know your rights**: Read NAMI's "Understanding the Mental Health Parity Act" guide (<https://www.nami.org/Your-Rights/Mental-Health-Parity>) to understand what parity law requires and what questions to ask your insurer. The guide covers MHPAEA's protections, the 2024 final rule changes, and step-by-step instructions for identifying potential violations.
- **Review your plan documents**: Request your plan's Summary Plan Description (SPD) and Evidence of Coverage (EOC). Look for prior authorization requirements, visit limits, and medical necessity criteria that apply differently to MH/SUD vs. medical services. Under ERISA, plans must provide the SPD within 30 days of your written request (29 U.S.C. section 1024(b)(4)).
- **Follow the 2024 final rule rollout**: The new NQTL comparative analysis requirements take effect in 2025-2026. Track implementation through the Kennedy Forum (<https://www.thekennedyforum.org>) and DOL EBSA updates. Key compliance dates to monitor: January 1, 2025 (comparative analysis documentation), January 1, 2026 (outcomes data reporting).
- **Read Milliman's parity research**: Milliman publishes the most comprehensive data on denial rate, out-of-network, and reimbursement rate disparities. Their reports are available at <https://www.milliman.com>. These reports provide the statistical evidence that supports parity enforcement advocacy.
- **Subscribe to NAMI Advocacy Updates**: Monthly email updates on federal and state parity legislation and enforcement. Sign up at <https://www.nami.org/Advocacy>
- **Understand your state's parity law**: Check Parity Track (<https://www.paritytrack.org>) to learn your state's parity enforcement tier, whether your state has laws exceeding the federal minimum, and which agency handles parity complaints. State enforcement varies significantly; knowing your state's approach helps target your advocacy effectively.
- **Learn the difference between fully insured and self-insured plans**: This distinction determines which regulator oversees your plan. If you are in a fully insured plan, your state insurance department has enforcement authority. If you are in a self-insured ERISA plan, DOL is the primary enforcer. Your HR department or plan administrator can tell you which type your plan is.

### Exercise Your Insurance Rights

- **Appeal every wrongful MH/SUD denial**: If your MH/SUD claim is denied, file an internal appeal within the plan's deadline (typically 180 days). Cite MHPAEA if the denial involves prior authorization, medical necessity, or visit limitations that appear more restrictive than for medical services. The overturn rate on appeal is 40-60% for internal appeals and 50-75% for external reviews (NAIC, 2023).
- **Request your plan's NQTL comparative analysis**: Under the CAA of 2021 (section 203), you have the right to request the comparative analysis your plan has conducted on each nonquantitative treatment limitation. If the plan fails to provide it within 30 days, file a complaint with DOL. The request itself sends a signal that plan members are monitoring compliance.
- **File external review requests**: If your internal appeal is denied, request external review through your state insurance department (for fully insured plans) or through the plan's external review process. External reviews are decided by independent reviewers, not the insurer. Under federal law, plans must comply with an external reviewer's decision.
- **Request a network adequacy exception**: If you cannot find an in-network MH/SUD provider within a reasonable distance or wait time, request a network adequacy exception from your plan. Most plans have a process (often unpublicized) for authorizing out-of-network providers at in-network rates when the network is inadequate. Document your search efforts: call at least 5-10 in-network providers and record the date, time, and result of each call.
- **Report ghost networks**: If providers listed in your plan's directory are unavailable (wrong phone number, not accepting new patients, not at the listed address), document each instance and report it to your state insurance department and to DOL. Ghost network reports are increasingly used by regulators to identify network adequacy failures.
- **Document everything**: Keep records of all communications, denials, authorizations, wait times, and appointment availability. This documentation is essential for complaints and appeals. Create a written log with dates, names of representatives you spoke to, reference numbers, and the substance of each conversation.
- **File DOL or state insurance department complaints**: When you experience a parity violation -- whether a denial, an excessive prior authorization delay, or an inability to find in-network providers -- file a formal complaint with the appropriate regulator. For self-insured (ERISA) plans, file with DOL EBSA at <https://www.dol.gov/agencies/ebsa/about-ebsa/ask-a-question/ask-ebsa>. For fully insured plans, file with your state insurance department (find yours at <https://content.naic.org/state-insurance-departments>). Every complaint builds the enforcement record that regulators use to identify patterns of non-compliance.
- **Request a peer-to-peer review**: When your MH/SUD claim is denied, request that your treating provider conduct a peer-to-peer review with the insurer's medical director. ERISA regulations require plans to provide access to their medical director or a clinical reviewer of the same specialty when a claim involves a clinical judgment. Many denials are reversed during peer-to-peer review because the treating provider can present clinical nuances that do not appear in the written clinical record.

### Use Your Voice

- **Contact your representatives**: Call or write your U.S. Senators and Representative to support increased funding for DOL parity enforcement (currently underfunded relative to the 2024 final rule's requirements)
- **Submit public comments**: When DOL, CMS, or state regulators issue proposed rules related to parity, submit public comments during the comment period. Instructions are posted at <https://www.regulations.gov>. Even brief comments matter: regulatory agencies are required to consider and respond to public comments, and comment volume signals public interest.
- **Write letters to the editor**: When your local newspaper covers mental health, insurance, or healthcare policy, write a letter emphasizing parity enforcement. Use specific data: "MH/SUD claims are denied at nearly twice the rate of medical claims (22.4% vs. 11.7%, Milliman 2023)."
- **Share your story**: NAMI, the Kennedy Forum, and Mental Health America collect patient stories about parity violations. These stories are used in Congressional testimony, regulatory comments, and media coverage. Submit your story at <https://www.nami.org/Get-Involved/Share-Your-Story>. Personal narratives are the most effective advocacy tool -- they humanize statistics and create political pressure.
- **Testify at public hearings**: State insurance departments and legislative committees regularly hold public hearings on health insurance issues. Testifying about your experience with a parity violation puts a face on the enforcement gap. Contact your state insurance department or NAMI affiliate to learn about upcoming hearing opportunities.
- **Use social media to share your experience**: When you encounter a parity violation -- a denied claim, a ghost network listing, or an excessive prior authorization delay -- share your experience on social media using the hashtags #ParityNow, #MentalHealthParity, and #EndInsuranceBarriers. Tag your insurer, your state insurance department, and NAMI. Public social media pressure has prompted several insurers to reverse specific denials and address systemic practices.
- **Request your Explanation of Benefits (EOB) and compare**: Review your EOBs carefully to compare how your MH/SUD claims are processed versus medical claims. Look for differences in coding, allowed amounts, copayment levels, and denial reasons. EOBs are often the first place where parity violations become visible to patients.
- **Know the emergency exception**: Under federal and state emergency parity rules, plans cannot impose prior authorization requirements on emergency MH/SUD care. If your insurer denies coverage for emergency psychiatric treatment because prior authorization was not obtained, the denial may itself be a parity violation. The ACA's prudent layperson standard requires plans to cover emergency services based on the patient's symptoms, not the ultimate diagnosis.
- **Consider filing a complaint with your state attorney general**: In addition to insurance department complaints, many state attorneys general have consumer protection divisions that investigate patterns of deceptive or unfair insurance practices. State AG offices often have more investigative resources and legal tools than insurance departments, and their enforcement actions can result in larger penalties and broader corrective action requirements.

## Community Actions

### Organize Locally

- **Form a parity enforcement working group**: Partner with local NAMI affiliates, mental health providers, and patient advocates to monitor insurer compliance in your community. Track appointment wait times, denial experiences, and network availability. Compile this data into a community parity report that can be shared with your state insurance department and elected officials.
- **Host "Know Your Rights" workshops**: Organize community events explaining parity law, how to read plan documents, and how to file appeals and complaints. Partner with legal aid organizations to provide free assistance. The Kennedy Forum provides training materials and speaker resources for community events.
- **Conduct a community ghost network audit**: Organize volunteers to call providers listed in major insurers' behavioral health directories and document availability. Record whether each provider is reachable, accepting new patients, and located at the listed address. Submit the results to your state insurance department. This grassroots audit methodology has been used effectively in California, Illinois, and Massachusetts.
- **Engage local employers**: Approach HR departments at local businesses to discuss parity compliance in their health plans. Many employers are unaware that their plans may be out of compliance. Provide them with DOL's compliance checklist and the Kennedy Forum's employer guide. Frame the conversation around business impact: untreated mental illness costs employers $47.6 billion annually in lost productivity.
- **Connect with your state insurance department**: Many state insurance departments have dedicated consumer assistance divisions. Establish a relationship so that community members can easily report suspected violations. Ask the department about its parity enforcement priorities and how community data can support its work.
- **Partner with schools**: School counselors and psychologists see the impact of insurance barriers on children's access to care daily. Partner with local school districts to document insurance-related barriers to child MH treatment and advocate for stronger pediatric network adequacy standards.

### Build Coalitions

- **Join or support your local NAMI affiliate**: NAMI has over 600 state and local affiliates across the country. These organizations provide the grassroots infrastructure for parity advocacy. Find your local affiliate at <https://www.nami.org/Find-Your-Local-NAMI>
- **Partner with provider organizations**: Psychiatrists, psychologists, and clinical social workers experience parity violations daily through prior authorization denials and low reimbursement rates. They are natural allies for community advocacy. Local chapters of the APA, APA (psychology), and NASW can provide professional voices for parity enforcement.
- **Engage recovery community organizations**: Substance use disorder parity violations are often the most severe, particularly for residential treatment and medication-assisted treatment (MAT). Recovery community organizations like Faces & Voices of Recovery provide advocacy infrastructure for SUD parity.
- **Coordinate with legal aid**: Organizations like the Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law and the National Health Law Program can provide legal support for parity complaints and litigation. Local legal aid organizations may also have health law attorneys who can assist with individual appeals.
- **Build interfaith coalitions**: Faith communities are often the first point of contact for people experiencing mental health crises. Engage faith leaders in parity advocacy by connecting insurance barriers to the moral imperative of caring for community members.
- **Engage business leaders**: The National Business Group on Health and local chambers of commerce can be allies when the conversation is framed around employee productivity, absenteeism, and healthcare cost efficiency. Identify local business leaders who have publicly discussed mental health and invite them to join parity advocacy efforts.
- **Create a community parity report card**: Compile data from community ghost network audits, patient surveys, and provider interviews into a published community parity report card that grades local insurers on MH/SUD access. Present the report card to your state insurance department and local media. Community-generated data supplements regulatory data and creates public accountability.
- **Connect with academic institutions**: Partner with local university public health, social work, or psychology programs. Graduate students can conduct research on parity compliance as thesis or capstone projects, providing free analytical capacity while training the next generation of parity advocates and researchers.
- **Organize "Parity Watch" volunteer teams**: Recruit volunteers to systematically monitor insurer compliance by tracking prior authorization turnaround times, documenting denial patterns, and calling provider directories. A structured volunteer network can generate data at a scale that individual patients cannot, while building community awareness of parity issues.

## Political Actions

### Contact Elected Officials

**When to Contact**:

- When DOL or CMS budget appropriations are under consideration (typically February-September of each fiscal year)
- When mental health or insurance legislation is pending in Congress or your state legislature
- When your state's insurance commissioner race is on the ballot (in states where the position is elected)
- When you or a family member experiences a parity violation -- specific constituent stories are powerful
- When new parity enforcement data is released (such as DOL's annual Report to Congress) -- reference the data in your communication

**What to Say -- Template Email**:

```text
Subject: Support Strong Mental Health Parity Enforcement

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

I am a constituent from [City, State] writing to urge your support for
stronger enforcement of the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act
(MHPAEA). Mental health claims are denied at nearly twice the rate of
medical claims (22.4% vs. 11.7%), and out-of-network utilization for
behavioral health is 5.4 times higher than for medical services -- clear
evidence that parity law is not being enforced.

I ask that you:

1. Support increased appropriations for DOL's Employee Benefits Security
   Administration to hire the investigators needed to enforce the 2024
   final rule on mental health parity.
2. Co-sponsor legislation to close the ERISA preemption gap that prevents
   states from enforcing parity in self-insured employer plans.
3. Support the extension of parity protections to Medicare fee-for-service,
   including elimination of the 190-day psychiatric inpatient limit.
4. Co-sponsor the Mental Health Parity Enforcement Act to codify the 2024
   final rule's key provisions and establish a public Parity Scorecard.

[Optional: Personal story of how a parity violation affected you or your
family -- this is the most persuasive element of any constituent letter.]

Mental health parity is the law of the land, but millions of Americans are
denied the care they are legally entitled to. Please act to close the
enforcement gap.

Thank you for your service and consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your City, State]
```

**What to Say -- Phone Call Script**:

```text
"Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [City]. I'm calling
to ask [Representative/Senator] [Last Name] to support stronger enforcement
of mental health parity law. Right now, mental health claims are denied at
twice the rate of medical claims, and the Department of Labor doesn't have
enough investigators to enforce the law. I'd like [Representative/Senator]
to support increased funding for DOL parity enforcement, legislation to
close the ERISA preemption gap, and the Mental Health Parity Enforcement
Act. Can you please note my position? Thank you."
```

### Support Legislation

Current or proposed legislation to support:

- **Mental Health Parity Enforcement Act**: Would authorize the Parity Scorecard, fund independent compliance audits, codify the 2024 final rule's outcomes data requirement and meaningful benefits standard, and increase civil penalties for violations -- Contact members of the Senate HELP Committee and House Education & Workforce Committee
- **ERISA Parity Enforcement Amendment**: Would create federal-state delegation authority for parity enforcement of self-insured plans -- Contact Senate Finance Committee and House Ways & Means Committee members
- **Medicare Mental Health Parity Act**: Would extend MHPAEA to Medicare fee-for-service and eliminate the 190-day psychiatric inpatient limit -- Contact Senate Finance Committee members
- **Provider Directory Accuracy Act**: Would establish federal ghost network standards and penalties -- Contact Senate HELP Committee and House Energy & Commerce Committee members
- **Prior Authorization Reform Act**: Would establish federal timing standards for prior authorization and create gold-carding provisions -- Contact House Energy & Commerce Committee members
- **State-level parity bills**: Check Parity Track (<https://www.paritytrack.org>) for pending legislation in your state. Many states are considering bills to require proactive parity reporting by insurers, dedicate enforcement staff, increase penalties for violations, or address ghost networks.

### Electoral Engagement

**Questions to Ask Candidates**:

1. "Do you support increased funding for DOL to enforce the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act? If so, how much additional funding would you support?"
2. "Do you support closing the ERISA preemption gap so that state insurance departments can enforce parity for self-insured employer plans?"
3. "Do you support extending parity protections to Medicare fee-for-service, including eliminating the 190-day psychiatric inpatient limit?"
4. "What specific actions would you take to ensure that mental health insurance parity is enforced, not just written into law?"
5. "Do you support requiring health plans to publicly report parity compliance data, including denial rates and network adequacy metrics?"

**Voting Considerations**:

- Look for candidates who support specific enforcement mechanisms, not just general statements of support for mental health. Most politicians support "mental health" in the abstract; the differentiator is willingness to fund enforcement and hold insurers accountable.
- Insurance commissioner races (in the 11 states where the position is elected: California, Delaware, Georgia, Kansas, Louisiana, Mississippi, Montana, North Carolina, North Dakota, Oklahoma, and Washington) are particularly important for parity enforcement. Ask candidates about their enforcement priorities and staffing plans.
- State legislative races matter for fully insured plan parity enforcement. Check whether your state representatives have supported parity bills.
- Evaluate candidates' campaign finance records: candidates who receive significant contributions from health insurance PACs may be less inclined to support aggressive parity enforcement. OpenSecrets (<https://www.opensecrets.org>) provides campaign contribution data.
- Organize candidate forums on mental health policy. In partnership with local NAMI affiliates and provider organizations, host public events where candidates must address specific parity enforcement questions. These forums create accountability and generate media coverage.
- Track how your elected officials vote on DOL and CMS appropriations bills that fund parity enforcement. Budget votes are the most concrete indicator of whether a legislator supports parity enforcement in practice, not just in rhetoric.
- Support ballot initiatives in states that allow them. Several states are considering ballot measures to strengthen insurance regulation, including measures that would affect parity enforcement for fully insured plans.

## Professional Actions

### For Healthcare Providers

- **Document every parity violation you encounter**: Prior authorization delays, claim denials, reimbursement rate disparities, and network exclusion. Maintain a running log that can be shared with your professional association and regulators.
- **Report violations systematically**: File complaints with DOL (for ERISA plans) or your state insurance department (for fully insured plans) when you identify a pattern of inappropriate denials or excessive prior authorization requirements. Include specific data: denial rates for your patients by insurer, authorization turnaround times, and comparison to medical/surgical referrals.
- **Join your state's parity enforcement advisory board**: If your state has one, participate. If it does not, advocate for its creation through your professional association.
- **Advocate through professional organizations**: The APA, APA (psychology), NASW, and ASAM all have advocacy infrastructure for parity enforcement. Participate in their lobby days, submit testimony for their regulatory comments, and support their policy positions with clinical evidence from your practice.
- **Help patients appeal**: When a patient's claim is denied, assist them with the appeal by providing clinical documentation that supports medical necessity. Cite published clinical guidelines (APA Practice Guidelines, ASAM Criteria, AACAP Practice Parameters) to counter insurer proprietary criteria.
- **Participate in network adequacy audits**: When regulators or advocacy organizations conduct network adequacy surveys, participate. Your response helps build the evidence base for enforcement action.
- **Track your prior authorization data**: Maintain a spreadsheet of every prior authorization request you submit, including the service type, insurer, submission date, decision date, and outcome. This data can reveal patterns of delay or denial that, when aggregated across providers, provides powerful evidence for regulatory action. The APA's prior authorization survey relies on provider-reported data of this kind.
- **Negotiate collectively**: When possible, work with other providers in your area to collectively advocate for higher reimbursement rates from insurers. While antitrust law limits certain forms of collective bargaining by independent providers, providers can advocate jointly through professional associations (APA, APA psychology, NASW) and can share information about insurer practices without violating antitrust restrictions.
- **Refuse to sign restrictive contracts**: Review insurer contracts carefully before signing. Some contracts include gag clauses that prohibit providers from disclosing insurer practices to patients or regulators, or "all products" clauses that require participation in all of an insurer's plans. Refusing these terms protects your ability to advocate for patients and report parity violations.

### For Employer Benefits Managers

- **Request your plan's NQTL comparative analysis and review it**: Compare MH/SUD denial rates, prior authorization requirements, and network adequacy metrics to medical/surgical equivalents. Under the CAA of 2021, plan sponsors have the right to receive this analysis from their insurer or TPA.
- **Require parity compliance as a contract term**: When negotiating with insurers or TPAs, include specific parity compliance requirements: denial rate parity within defined thresholds, network adequacy standards (e.g., MH/SUD OON rates within 2x of medical/surgical), and prior authorization turnaround time standards.
- **Benchmark your plan**: Use Milliman's parity benchmarking data and the Kennedy Forum's employer parity compliance guide to evaluate your plan's performance relative to industry standards.
- **Conduct ghost network audits**: Hire a third-party verification service or assign internal staff to conduct quarterly audits of your plan's MH/SUD provider directory. Require your insurer or TPA to remove inaccurate listings within 30 days.
- **Evaluate behavioral health carve-outs**: If your plan uses a behavioral health carve-out (MBHO), evaluate whether the carve-out model creates inherent parity challenges. Some employers have moved to integrated behavioral health models that reduce the structural separation between MH/SUD and medical/surgical benefit management.
- **Report results to employees**: Share parity compliance information with employees through benefits communications, creating internal transparency and encouraging employees to exercise their rights.
- **Include parity compliance in vendor selection**: When conducting RFPs for health insurance or TPA services, include specific parity compliance metrics as evaluation criteria. Require bidders to report their MH/SUD denial rates, out-of-network utilization, prior authorization turnaround times, and provider directory accuracy rates as part of their proposals. Making parity a selection criterion sends a market signal that compliance matters.
- **Consider employee parity ombudsman**: Large employers (5,000+ employees) should consider designating an internal benefits staff member as a parity ombudsman who can field employee complaints about MH/SUD coverage, track patterns, and escalate issues to the TPA or insurer. This creates an internal feedback loop that identifies violations before they become regulatory complaints.

### For Attorneys

- **Familiarize yourself with MHPAEA case law**: Particularly *Wit v. United Behavioral Health*, *New York State Psychiatric Association v. UnitedHealth Group*, *Harlick v. Blue Shield of California*, and the post-*Loper Bright* implications for agency deference.
- **Consider pro bono parity representation**: Many patients cannot afford attorneys for parity appeals and litigation. Contact the Bazelon Center or National Health Law Program for referrals.
- **File class action claims where patterns emerge**: When an insurer's practices create systematic parity violations affecting a class of enrollees, class action litigation under ERISA can create systemic change. The *Wit v. UBH* case demonstrated that ERISA litigation can produce detailed findings of fact that inform regulatory enforcement.
- **Advise employers on parity compliance**: Employer plan sponsors have fiduciary obligations under ERISA that include ensuring parity compliance. Counsel clients on their obligations under the 2024 final rule, including the NQTL comparative analysis requirement.
- **Monitor state enforcement opportunities**: In states with strong parity laws, state court litigation may provide remedies that are not available under ERISA's limited remedial framework.
- **Track emerging parity case law**: The post-*Loper Bright* legal landscape is evolving rapidly. Court decisions on the 2024 final rule will shape the scope of enforceable parity standards for years to come. Follow legal developments through the American Bar Association Health Law Section and Bloomberg Law Health.

### For Journalists

- **Request plan-level data**: The 2024 final rule creates new transparency requirements that will generate enforceable compliance data beginning in 2025-2026. Request plan-level data from DOL and state insurance departments under FOIA.
- **Compare MH/SUD denial rates across major insurers**: The insurer-specific data that is emerging from state regulatory filings and litigation discovery can be compiled into comparative analyses that reveal which companies are worst at parity compliance.
- **Investigate ghost networks**: Use secret shopper methodology to test provider directory accuracy for MH/SUD versus medical/surgical providers. Call providers listed in major insurer directories and document availability.
- **Contact the Kennedy Forum's media team**: The Kennedy Forum provides expert sources and data support for journalists covering parity issues.
- **Cover the enforcement gap**: The story of parity is not just that the law exists but that it is not enforced. The gap between the law's promise and its delivery -- measured by the 100% initial NQTL failure rate, the 0.5% appeal rate, and the 5.4x out-of-network disparity -- is a data-rich, human-impact story.
- **Track 2024 final rule implementation**: The phased compliance deadlines (2025-2026) will produce the first wave of plan-level NQTL comparative analyses with outcomes data. Requesting these analyses from plans and reporting on their quality would be a significant public service. Compare the analyses to DOL's published guidance on what constitutes a "sufficient" analysis.

### For Researchers

- **Access SAMHSA NSDUH data, Milliman reports, and DOL enforcement data**: These are the primary data sources for studying parity compliance trends.
- **Study the 2024 final rule's implementation**: The rule's outcomes data requirement will create new research opportunities beginning in 2026. Design studies to evaluate whether the rule produces measurable changes in denial rates, network adequacy, and patient access.
- **Apply for NIMH or AHRQ grants**: Both agencies fund research on insurance barriers to MH/SUD care. The 2024 final rule creates a natural experiment that should be studied rigorously.
- **Publish in both clinical and policy journals**: Parity research that reaches only clinical audiences misses the policy audience that drives enforcement. Target publications such as *Health Affairs*, *The Milbank Quarterly*, and *Psychiatric Services* that reach both clinical and policy readers.
- **Study the ERISA preemption gap**: Rigorous comparative analysis of parity compliance between fully insured and self-insured plans within the same state can provide evidence for the ERISA enforcement gap that policymakers need to justify legislative action.
- **Evaluate the 2024 final rule as a natural experiment**: The rule creates a natural experiment with measurable before-and-after data on NQTL compliance, denial rates, and network adequacy. Design studies that track these metrics across plan types, geographies, and enforcement jurisdictions to evaluate the rule's effectiveness.
- **Research the care abandonment phenomenon**: The 29% care abandonment rate during MH/SUD prior authorization is a critical but understudied metric. Research that identifies the patient characteristics, insurer practices, and system design features associated with care abandonment would inform both enforcement strategy and clinical intervention.

## Advocacy Toolkit

### Key Talking Points

When discussing mental health insurance parity with others, emphasize:

1. **The denial gap is measurable and severe**: "Mental health claims are denied at 22.4% compared to 11.7% for medical claims -- nearly double the rate. When these denials are appealed, 50-75% are overturned, proving the initial denials were not clinically justified." (Milliman 2023, NAIC 2023)
2. **The network gap is real**: "Patients must go out-of-network for behavioral health services at 5.4 times the rate of medical services, because only 38% of psychiatrists accept commercial insurance vs. 86% of primary care doctors." (Milliman 2023, Bishop et al., JAMA 2023)
3. **The law exists but enforcement is failing**: "Federal parity law has been on the books since 2008, but the Department of Labor found that 100% of initial plan compliance analyses were insufficient. Parity is the law; enforcement is the problem." (DOL EBSA, 2023)
4. **Non-compliance costs more than compliance**: "Untreated mental health conditions cost employers $47.6 billion annually in lost productivity, and cost the broader economy $280 billion. Enforcing parity costs a fraction of that." (National Safety Council, 2023; National Council for Mental Wellbeing, 2023)
5. **This affects everyone**: "57.8 million American adults have a mental health condition, and 48.7 million have a substance use disorder. Insurance parity affects nearly every family in the country." (SAMHSA, 2023)
6. **Ghost networks create an illusion of access**: "State investigations found that 40-60% of behavioral health providers listed in insurer directories are not actually available. Plans claim they have adequate networks, but the listings are phantoms." (CA DMHC, 2023; HHS OIG, 2022)
7. **The 2024 rule is a turning point, but only if enforced**: "The 2024 final rule requires plans to prove they treat mental health claims fairly for the first time. But DOL has only 500 investigators for 2.5 million plans. Without funding, the rule is just another unfulfilled promise." (DOL EBSA, 2024)

### Common Misconceptions to Counter

| Misconception | Reality | Source |
|---------------|---------|--------|
| "Mental health parity is already the law, so the problem is solved." | Parity is the law, but enforcement is failing: denial rates remain 2x higher for MH/SUD, and 100% of initial plan compliance analyses were found insufficient by DOL. | DOL EBSA, 2023; Milliman, 2023 |
| "The provider shortage is the real problem, not insurers." | Low reimbursement rates (20-40% below medical rates) drive providers out of networks. Only 38% of psychiatrists accept commercial insurance. | Milliman, 2024; Bishop et al., JAMA, 2023 |
| "Parity enforcement will increase premiums significantly." | CBO estimates parity compliance costs at less than 1% of premiums for most plans. Industry predictions of 3-5% increases have never materialized. | CBO, 2024; DOL, 2012 |
| "Prior authorization is just good clinical practice." | PA is applied to 30-40% of MH/SUD services vs. 10-15% of medical services, and 29% of patients abandon care during the process. | APA, 2023; Kennedy Forum, 2023 |
| "My self-insured employer plan is already regulated." | Self-insured plans are overseen by DOL, not state insurance departments, and DOL has only ~500 investigators for ~2.5 million plans. | DOL EBSA, FY2024 |
| "My plan has plenty of therapists in the directory." | State investigations found 40-60% of listed MH/SUD providers are unavailable -- wrong numbers, not accepting patients, or no longer in practice. | CA DMHC, 2023; HHS OIG, 2022 |
| "Filing an appeal won't make a difference." | 50-75% of MH/SUD denials are overturned on external review. Every overturned denial creates evidence that regulators use to identify compliance failures. | NAIC, 2023 |
