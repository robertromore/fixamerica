# Suicide Prevention: Implementation Roadmap

## Strategic Approach

The roadmap follows a three-phase strategy that builds crisis infrastructure and quick-win programs first (Years 1-2), expands systemic reforms and means safety initiatives in the medium term (Years 3-5), and achieves transformational outcomes in the long term (Years 6-10). Each phase is designed to generate measurable results that build political support for subsequent phases. The theory of change is that demonstrating early success with broadly supported crisis services creates momentum for more politically complex interventions such as federal means restriction and upstream social investment.

The sequencing is deliberately front-loaded with bipartisan measures (988 funding, veteran suicide prevention, healthcare integration) to build a coalition foundation before pursuing more contested reforms (federal ERPOs, waiting periods, social media regulation). This approach reflects the political reality documented in the opposition analysis: crisis services command near-universal support, while means restriction remains polarized.

## Phase 1: Foundation and Quick Wins (Years 1-2)

### Objectives

- Achieve sustainable 988 Lifeline funding through a federal telecom fee
- Launch rapid-deployment workforce initiatives (Crisis Counselor Corps, NHSC expansion)
- Implement healthcare system reforms through CMS billing code changes
- Expand voluntary lethal means safety programs nationwide
- Initiate federal ERPO and waiting period legislation

### Key Actions

| Action | Lead Actor | Timeline | Resources Needed | Success Metric |
|--------|-----------|----------|------------------|----------------|
| Enact 988 Sustainability Act (federal telecom fee) | Congress | Months 1-12 | Legislative effort; $0 net cost (fee-funded) | Bill signed into law |
| Issue CMS billing codes for safety planning, lethal means counseling, caring contacts | CMS | Months 6-12 | Rulemaking resources; revenue-neutral | Codes published in Federal Register |
| Launch Crisis Counselor Corps (1,000 positions Year 1) | SAMHSA | Months 6-18 | $200 million Year 1 appropriation | 1,000 counselors deployed |
| Expand NHSC mental health loan repayment (2,500 new slots) | HRSA | Months 1-12 | $250 million additional annual appropriation | 2,500 new commitments signed |
| Scale Gun Shop Project to all 50 states | SAMHSA/AFSP/NSSF | Months 1-18 | $25 million in grants | 10,000 retailers participating |
| Expand VA mental health eligibility to all veterans | Congress/VA | Months 1-18 | $1.2 billion annual appropriation | Eligibility regulation published |
| Introduce federal ERPO minimum standards legislation | Congress | Months 1-12 | Legislative effort | Bill introduced with bipartisan sponsors |
| Fund school-based prevention programs in high-need districts | Dept. of Education | Months 6-18 | $100 million Year 1 | 2,000 schools enrolled |

### Legislative Requirements

- **988 Sustainability Act**: Federal telecom fee ($0.50/line/month) dedicated to 988 operations, mobile crisis teams, and crisis stabilization units
- **VA CARE Act (expanded eligibility)**: Authorization for VA to provide mental health services to all veterans regardless of service-connected disability rating
- **National Suicide Prevention Improvement Act**: CMS mandate for Zero Suicide implementation, new billing codes, and 7-day follow-up requirements

### Expected Outcomes

By the end of Year 2: sustainable 988 funding mechanism enacted; in-state 988 answer rate improved from ~70% to ~80%; 1,000+ new crisis counselors deployed; safety planning and lethal means counseling reimbursable in all federally funded healthcare settings; 10,000+ gun retailers participating in voluntary means safety; VA mental health services available to all veterans.

## Phase 2: Systemic Reform and Expansion (Years 3-5)

### Objectives

- Deploy mobile crisis teams and crisis stabilization units in every state
- Implement Zero Suicide framework across all federally funded healthcare systems
- Enact and implement federal ERPO and waiting period legislation
- Launch social connectedness and upstream prevention programs at scale
- Regulate social media platforms for youth safety

### Key Actions

| Action | Lead Actor | Timeline | Resources Needed | Success Metric |
|--------|-----------|----------|------------------|----------------|
| Achieve statewide mobile crisis coverage in 40+ states | SAMHSA/States | Years 3-5 | $1.5 billion annually (from 988 fee) | 40 states with statewide coverage |
| Open crisis stabilization units (500 nationally) | SAMHSA/States | Years 3-5 | $500 million in capital grants | 500 units operational |
| Mandate Zero Suicide in Medicare/Medicaid-participating systems | CMS | Year 3 (rulemaking); Year 5 (compliance) | $250 million TA grants | 100% of federally funded systems enrolled |
| Enact federal ERPO minimum standards | Congress | Year 3 | State implementation grants: $200 million | ERPO laws in 40+ states |
| Enact universal 72-hour waiting period for firearm purchases | Congress | Year 3-4 | Enforcement costs: $50 million annually | Federal law enacted |
| Launch Surgeon General's Social Isolation Initiative | HHS | Year 3 | $10 million annually | Public awareness campaign active; research agenda published |
| Fund Sources of Strength in 50% of U.S. middle/high schools | Dept. of Education | Years 3-5 | $150 million annually | 15,000 schools participating |
| Implement FTC social media youth safety regulations | FTC | Years 3-4 (rulemaking) | FTC enforcement budget: $30 million | Regulations effective |
| Expand psychiatry residency slots (1,000 new positions) | CMS (GME funding) | Years 3-5 | $200 million annually | 1,000 new residencies established |

### Legislative Requirements

- **Federal ERPO Act**: Minimum standards for state ERPO laws including judicial due process, 14-day initial orders with full hearing requirement, false petition penalties, and prompt return of firearms after order expiration
- **Firearm Safety Waiting Period Act**: Universal 72-hour waiting period for all licensed dealer sales
- **Kids Online Safety Act** (or equivalent): FTC rulemaking authority for social media platforms regarding minors' mental health
- **Sources of Strength Expansion Act**: Dedicated Department of Education appropriation for school-based upstream prevention

### Expected Outcomes

By the end of Year 5: 988 in-state answer rate at ~90%; mobile crisis teams available in 40+ states; 500 crisis stabilization units operational; Zero Suicide framework implemented in all federally funded healthcare systems; 40+ states with ERPO laws meeting federal minimum standards; 72-hour waiting period effective; 15,000+ schools with prevention programs; social media platforms compliant with youth safety regulations.

## Phase 3: Transformation and Sustainability (Years 6-10)

### Objectives

- Achieve 95% in-state 988 answer rate and universal mobile crisis access
- Reduce the age-adjusted suicide rate to below 12 per 100,000
- Close mental health workforce shortage areas by 50%
- Establish sustainable upstream prevention infrastructure
- Achieve means safety culture change (80% secure storage)

### Key Actions

| Action | Lead Actor | Timeline | Resources Needed | Success Metric |
|--------|-----------|----------|------------------|----------------|
| Achieve 95% 988 answer rate and 60-minute mobile crisis response nationally | SAMHSA/States | Year 6-8 | Sustained 988 fee revenue | 95% answer rate; 60-min response in urban areas |
| Complete bridge barriers at all federally identified high-lethality sites | DOT/States | Years 6-10 | $500 million federal matching program | Barriers at 100% of identified sites |
| Expand social connectedness programs to all high-risk communities | SAMHSA/HHS | Years 6-10 | $300 million annually | Programs in every county with suicide rate > 20/100,000 |
| Achieve 50% reduction in Mental Health Professional Shortage Areas | HRSA | Year 10 | Cumulative workforce investments | MHPSA designations reduced by 50% |
| Evaluate and refine all programs based on 5-year outcome data | CDC/SAMHSA | Year 6-7 | $50 million for evaluation | Comprehensive evaluation report published |
| Establish permanent interagency Office of Suicide Prevention | White House/HHS | Year 6 | $20 million annually | Office operational with cross-agency authority |

### Expected Outcomes

By Year 10: age-adjusted suicide rate below 12 per 100,000 (from 14.3 in 2022); 95% 988 answer rate; universal mobile crisis access; 50% reduction in shortage areas; 80% secure firearm storage rate; veteran suicide rate reduced by 25%; youth suicide rate reduced by 25%; measurable improvements in social connectedness indicators.

## Governance and Oversight

**Interagency Coordination**: Establish a permanent Office of Suicide Prevention within HHS with cross-agency authority over SAMHSA, CDC, VA, DoD, IHS, and HRSA suicide prevention activities. The Office would report directly to the HHS Secretary and coordinate with the White House Domestic Policy Council.

**Congressional Oversight**: Require annual reporting to the Senate HELP Committee and House Energy and Commerce Committee, including progress on all success metrics, funding expenditures, and program evaluations.

**Independent Evaluation**: Commission the National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine to conduct a comprehensive 5-year evaluation of all federal suicide prevention programs and publish findings with recommendations for refinement.

**State Accountability**: Condition 988 federal funding on state compliance with minimum standards for answer rates, mobile crisis deployment, and crisis stabilization capacity. Publish annual state-by-state scorecards.

**Public Dashboard**: Maintain a publicly accessible dashboard (modeled on the current SAMHSA 988 Performance Dashboard) tracking all success metrics in real time or near-real time.

## Risk Mitigation

### Risk 1: Political Opposition Blocks Federal ERPO and Waiting Period Legislation

**Likelihood**: High

**Impact**: High -- firearm suicide accounts for 54.7% of all suicide deaths

**Mitigation Strategy**: Pursue federal legislation while simultaneously supporting state-level ERPO adoption through grants and technical assistance. Continue expanding voluntary programs (Gun Shop Project, VA gun lock distribution) to build cultural support for means safety. Frame all legislation as firearm safety, not gun control.

**Contingency Plan**: If federal legislation fails, accelerate state-level strategy with a goal of 40+ ERPO states by Year 5. Support litigation defending existing ERPOs against Second Amendment challenges.

### Risk 2: 988 Funding Mechanism Fails or Is Insufficient

**Likelihood**: Medium

**Impact**: High -- entire crisis continuum depends on sustainable funding

**Mitigation Strategy**: Pursue federal telecom fee as primary mechanism but build support for Medicaid crisis services reimbursement as a complementary funding stream. Encourage additional states to enact state-level telecom fees.

**Contingency Plan**: If federal fee fails, develop a state-by-state approach with federal matching grants. Expand Medicaid crisis services coverage under Section 1115 waivers.

### Risk 3: Workforce Shortages Prevent Scale-Up

**Likelihood**: High

**Impact**: Medium -- delays implementation but does not prevent it

**Mitigation Strategy**: Front-load workforce investments (Crisis Counselor Corps, NHSC expansion, residency slots). Expand scope of practice for peer support specialists and licensed counselors. Invest heavily in telehealth infrastructure to leverage existing providers across geographic boundaries.

**Contingency Plan**: Extend implementation timelines for rural and shortage areas. Prioritize telehealth-delivered services where in-person providers are unavailable.

### Risk 4: Cultural Resistance to LGBTQ+-Affirming Youth Programs

**Likelihood**: Medium

**Impact**: Medium -- blocks programs for highest-risk youth population

**Mitigation Strategy**: Frame youth suicide prevention as a universal parental concern. Present evidence on the 45% reduction in attempt rates associated with affirming environments. Build coalitions with pediatric professional organizations (AAP, APA).

**Contingency Plan**: In states with hostile political environments, fund community-based rather than school-based programs. Support LGBTQ+ youth crisis services (Trevor Project) with federal dollars that bypass state distribution.

### Risk 5: Social Media Platforms Resist Regulation

**Likelihood**: High

**Impact**: Medium -- affects youth populations specifically

**Mitigation Strategy**: Build bipartisan consensus around child safety (distinct from broader content moderation debates). Leverage existing momentum from the Kids Online Safety Act. Support state-level legislation as pressure for federal action.

**Contingency Plan**: Pursue voluntary industry agreements (similar to safe messaging guidelines for traditional media) while continuing to build support for regulatory authority.

### Funding

| Phase | Estimated Annual Cost | Funding Source | Notes |
|-------|----------------------|----------------|-------|
| Phase 1 (Yrs 1-2) | $2.8 billion | Federal telecom fee ($1.5B); appropriations ($1.3B) | Front-loaded workforce and infrastructure investment |
| Phase 2 (Yrs 3-5) | $4.2 billion | Federal telecom fee ($2.0B); appropriations ($2.2B) | Peak investment in systemic reform |
| Phase 3 (Yrs 6-10) | $3.5 billion | Federal telecom fee ($2.2B); appropriations ($1.3B) | Sustained operations with reduced start-up costs |
| **Total (10 years)** | **$36.5 billion** | | Against $510 billion annual economic cost of suicide |

**Cost-Benefit Context**: The total 10-year investment of $36.5 billion represents approximately 0.7% of the annual economic cost of suicide ($510 billion, CDC 2022). A 20% reduction in suicide deaths would yield estimated savings of $102 billion annually in reduced economic burden, producing a return exceeding 25:1 over the program's lifetime.
