# Suicide Prevention: Current State

## National Suicide Rates and Trends

The United States experienced 49,449 suicide deaths in 2022, an age-adjusted rate of 14.3 per 100,000 population (CDC WISQARS, 2023). This represents a 36% increase in the age-adjusted rate since 2000, when it stood at 10.5 per 100,000. After a brief decline in 2019-2020 (partly attributable to pandemic-era social factors and measurement challenges), rates rebounded sharply in 2021 and 2022.

Rural counties experience suicide rates approximately 1.8 times higher than urban counties (CDC MMWR, 2022). ### Method Substitution Evidence

A persistent misconception holds that reducing access to one method simply causes substitution to another. The evidence strongly contradicts this claim. When the UK reformulated paracetamol (acetaminophen) packaging in 1998 to limit blister pack sizes, suicides and liver transplants from paracetamol overdose declined by 43% over the following decade, with no increase in other poisoning methods (Hawton et al., *BMJ*, 2013). Similarly, when coal gas was detoxified in the UK during the 1960s, overall suicide rates declined by approximately one-third, despite coal gas suicide having accounted for roughly half of all suicide deaths at the time (Kreitman, *British Journal of Preventive and Social Medicine*, 1976). These natural experiments confirm that most individuals do not simply switch to another means when a preferred method becomes less accessible.

## 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline

The 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline launched on July 16, 2022, replacing the ten-digit National Suicide Prevention Lifeline (1-800-273-8255). It is administered by the Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA) and operated by Vibrant Emotional Health, which coordinates a network of over 200 local crisis centers.

### Implementation Challenges

The 988 Lifeline faces several structural challenges beyond funding:

- **Workforce**: A 2023 survey by the National Council for Mental Wellbeing found that 67% of crisis centers reported staffing vacancies above 20%. Starting salaries for crisis counselors average $35,000-$42,000 annually, contributing to high turnover.
- **Equity**: Spanish-language capacity remains inconsistent, with some states routing Spanish-language calls to a national backup center rather than local bilingual counselors. ASL video relay and TTY services are available but underutilized due to limited awareness.
- **Follow-up**: The 988 system was designed primarily for acute crisis response, not ongoing care coordination. Most centers lack the infrastructure to track whether callers access follow-up services, creating a gap between crisis intervention and sustained treatment.
- **Dispatch Integration**: Coordination between 988 and 911 dispatch systems remains inconsistent across jurisdictions. Some communities have co-response models where 988 can dispatch mobile crisis teams instead of law enforcement, but most lack this capability.
- **Data Standardization**: Call centers use varying definitions for metrics such as "resolved" and "referred," making cross-center performance comparison difficult.

### Older Adults

Older adults, particularly men over 75, represent a frequently overlooked high-risk population. The suicide rate among men aged 75 and older (40.8 per 100,000 in 2022) is the highest of any demographic group. Contributing factors include social isolation, chronic pain, loss of independence, bereavement, and access to firearms.

| Metric | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Suicide rate, men 75+ | 40.8 per 100,000 | CDC WISQARS, 2022 |
| Suicide rate, men 85+ | 49.3 per 100,000 | CDC WISQARS, 2022 |
| Firearm use in suicides among adults 65+ | 72.8% | CDC WISQARS, 2022 |
| Older adults living alone | 28% of adults 65+ (14.7 million) | Census Bureau, 2023 |
| Ratio of attempts to deaths (older adults) | ~4:1 (vs. 25:1 overall) | AFSP, 2023 |

The lower attempt-to-death ratio among older adults reflects both greater intent and greater access to highly lethal means (firearms). Prevention efforts for this population require integration with primary care (where older adults present frequently), home health services, and senior centers.

Rural populations face a compounding of risk factors: higher firearm ownership rates, fewer mental health providers, greater social isolation, economic volatility tied to agriculture and extractive industries, and cultural norms that stigmatize help-seeking. The closure of rural hospitals has further reduced access to emergency psychiatric care.

### Justice-Involved Individuals

Incarcerated individuals die by suicide at rates 3-5 times the general population. Jail suicides are particularly concentrated in the first 72 hours of confinement and disproportionately involve individuals held on minor charges who have not been convicted. The Bureau of Justice Statistics reported 343 jail suicides in 2019, the most recent year with complete data.

## Healthcare System Integration

### Screening Practices

Despite clinical guidelines recommending universal suicide risk screening, implementation varies widely.

| Setting | Recommended Screening | Implementation Rate | Tool Used |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary care | All adult visits (USPSTF review pending) | ~10-20% of practices | PHQ-9 Item 9 |
| Emergency departments | All behavioral health presentations | ~40-60% | Columbia Protocol (C-SSRS) |
| Inpatient psychiatric | Universal (Joint Commission requirement) | ~95% | C-SSRS or equivalent |
| Pediatric primary care | Adolescents 12+ (AAP recommendation) | ~15-25% | ASQ, PHQ-A |
| Substance use treatment | All intake and periodic reviews | ~50-70% | C-SSRS |

*Source: Joint Commission, 2023; USPSTF, 2023; AAP Guidelines, 2022*

### Zero Suicide Framework Adoption

The Zero Suicide framework, developed by the Suicide Prevention Resource Center, provides a healthcare systems approach to suicide prevention. It includes leadership commitment, workforce training, identification, engagement, treatment, transition, and improvement.

| Metric | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Health systems implementing Zero Suicide | ~300 nationwide | SPRC, 2024 |
| Reduction in suicide deaths (Henry Ford Health) | 75% reduction over 10 years | Coffey et al., 2015 |
| Reduction at Centerstone | 64% reduction in attempts | SPRC Case Studies, 2022 |
| Federal support (GLS Act + Zero Suicide grants) | ~$120 million/year (FY2024) | SAMHSA Budget, FY2024 |

### Care Transitions

The period immediately following discharge from psychiatric inpatient or emergency department care is among the highest-risk periods for suicide.

| Metric | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Suicide risk in first week post-discharge | 100-200x general population rate | Chung et al., *JAMA Psychiatry*, 2019 |
| Patients who receive follow-up within 7 days of psychiatric discharge | 54% (Medicaid), 60% (commercial) | NCQA HEDIS, 2023 |
| ED patients with suicidal ideation who receive follow-up | < 50% | Betz et al., *Annals of Emergency Medicine*, 2016 |
| Safety planning completion in ED before discharge | ~30-40% | Stanley & Brown study replications, 2022 |

### Evidence-Based Treatments

Several therapeutic interventions have demonstrated efficacy in reducing suicide attempts and deaths in randomized controlled trials:

| Treatment | Evidence | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Suicide Prevention (CBT-SP) | RCT by Brown et al., *JAMA*, 2005 | 50% reduction in repeat attempts over 18 months |
| Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) | Multiple RCTs since Linehan et al., 1991 | 50% reduction in suicide attempts; reduced self-harm |
| Safety Planning Intervention (SPI) | Stanley et al., *JAMA Psychiatry*, 2018 | 50% reduction in suicidal behavior post-ED discharge |
| Collaborative Assessment and Management of Suicidality (CAMS) | Jobes et al., *Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology*, 2017 | Faster resolution of suicidal ideation; reduced distress |
| Caring Contacts (letters, texts, calls) | Comtois et al., *JAMA Psychiatry*, 2019 | 30-40% reduction in suicide deaths in military populations |
| Brief Intervention and Contact (BIC) | Fleischmann et al., *Bulletin of the WHO*, 2008 | 83% reduction in deaths in 5-country WHO trial |

Despite this evidence, fewer than 10% of suicidal patients in community settings receive a suicide-specific evidence-based treatment (Jobes, 2012). The gap between available evidence and clinical practice remains one of the most consequential failures in the suicide prevention landscape.

## Emerging Challenges

### Social Media and Digital Environment

Algorithmic amplification of self-harm content, cyberbullying, and the spread of detailed method information online represent growing concerns, particularly for youth. Research published in *BMJ* (2019) found associations between social media use exceeding three hours daily and increased suicide risk among adolescents.

### Post-Pandemic Mental Health Crisis

The COVID-19 pandemic intensified social isolation, economic disruption, and substance use, all upstream drivers of suicide. Emergency department visits for suspected suicide attempts among adolescent girls rose 51% in early 2021 compared to the same period in 2019 (CDC MMWR, 2021).

### Workforce Shortages

The crisis counselor workforce faces burnout, low pay, and high turnover. A 2023 survey by the National Council for Mental Wellbeing found that 67% of crisis centers reported staffing vacancies above 20%, threatening 988 Lifeline answer rates and quality.

### Firearm Proliferation

An estimated 20 million firearms were sold in the United States in 2022 (NSSF-adjusted NICS data), adding to the approximately 400 million firearms in civilian circulation. First-time gun buyers -- estimated at 5.4 million in 2021 (NSSF, 2022) -- may be at elevated risk due to unfamiliarity with safe storage practices.

### Artificial Intelligence and Predictive Risk

Healthcare systems are increasingly experimenting with machine learning models to predict suicide risk using electronic health record data. The VA's REACH VET (Recovery Engagement and Coordination for Health - Veterans Enhanced Treatment) program uses a predictive algorithm to identify veterans at highest risk and trigger outreach. Early evaluations suggest the program reaches veterans earlier than clinical referral alone, but concerns about false positives, algorithmic bias, and patient privacy remain active areas of debate (McCarthy et al., *American Journal of Psychiatry*, 2021). Several large health systems, including Kaiser Permanente and Intermountain Healthcare, have deployed similar predictive models, though independent validation and transparency in algorithm design remain limited.

### Funding Landscape

Total federal spending on suicide prevention across all agencies remains modest relative to the burden:

|---|---|---|
| SAMHSA | ~$1.1 billion (including 988) | 988 operations, GLS grants, Zero Suicide grants |
| VA | ~$598 million | Veterans Crisis Line, clinical programs, research |
| DoD | ~$100 million | Active-duty prevention, transition programs |
| CDC | ~$70 million | Surveillance, research, technical assistance |
| IHS | ~$15 million (est.) | Tribal prevention programs |

For comparison, the annual economic cost of suicide is estimated at $510 billion (CDC, 2022), meaning total federal prevention spending represents approximately 0.4% of the economic burden. Private philanthropy adds approximately $50-75 million annually (AFSP alone provides ~$6 million in research grants), but this is insufficient to close the gap.
