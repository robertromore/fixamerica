# Child and Adolescent Mental Health: Stakeholders

The child and adolescent mental health system is shaped by the interactions of a diverse array of groups, each with distinct interests, resources, and degrees of influence. Unlike many policy areas, the primary affected population--children and adolescents--lacks direct political power, making the dynamics of advocacy, representation, and coalition-building especially important.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Children and adolescents with mental health conditions | Direct beneficiaries or victims of system failures; experience untreated illness, delayed care, stigma | 17.4 million (ages 3-17 with any diagnosed condition, CDC NSCH, 2022) | Timely access to effective, age-appropriate, non-stigmatizing treatment |
| Parents and caregivers | Bear emotional, financial, and logistical burden of navigating fragmented system; advocates for their children | ~35 million families with children affected by mental health needs | Accessible, affordable care; reduced wait times; school-based support; parental involvement in treatment |
| Youth in foster care | 50-80% have mental health diagnoses; disproportionately prescribed psychotropic medications; frequent placement disruptions | 368,530 in care (FY2022, ACF AFCARS) | Trauma-informed care; medication monitoring; continuity of treatment across placements |
| LGBTQ+ youth | 69% report persistent sadness/hopelessness; 4x rate of suicidal ideation compared to heterosexual peers | Estimated 3.5-5 million under 18 | Affirming care; confidential access; protection from conversion practices; safe school environments |
| Youth of color | Disproportionate exposure to adversity; lowest treatment rates; highest unmet need | 26.2 million children of color (Census, 2022) | Culturally competent providers; reduction of systemic barriers; equitable access regardless of race |
| Youth in rural areas | 65% of rural counties have no psychiatrist; extreme geographic barriers to care | ~9 million children in rural communities | Telehealth access; school-based services; transportation support; recruitment of rural providers |
| Youth in juvenile justice | 65-70% have at least one mental health disorder; many had no prior treatment | ~424,300 juvenile arrests annually (OJJDP, 2023) | Diversion from justice system to treatment; trauma-informed approaches; community-based alternatives |
| Homeless youth | 2-4x higher rates of mental health conditions than housed peers | ~1.3 million students experienced homelessness (2021-22, Dept. of Education) | Stable housing; accessible mental health services; coordination between shelters and schools |
| Youth with co-occurring intellectual/developmental disabilities | Higher rates of unrecognized mental health conditions; limited access to providers trained in dual diagnosis | ~7.4 million children with disabilities (Census, 2022) | Providers trained in dual diagnosis; integrated services; accessible treatment settings |
| Transition-age youth (18-25) | Fall between child and adult systems; highest prevalence but lowest treatment rate | ~33 million young adults | Seamless transition services; extended EPSDT-like coverage; college mental health funding |
| Immigrant and refugee children | High trauma exposure; cultural and linguistic barriers; fear of system engagement | ~5.1 million children with undocumented parent (MPI, 2023) | Culturally competent care; language access; immigration-status-neutral services |

### Secondary Stakeholders

- **Educators and school staff**: Serve as first-line identifiers of student mental health needs; absorb the classroom impact of untreated conditions; 77% of teachers report that student mental health issues have increased in their classrooms (EdWeek Research Center, 2023). Teachers report spending an average of 2.5 hours per week managing student mental health crises, diverting time from instruction (RAND Corporation, 2022)
- **Pediatricians and primary care providers**: Increasingly expected to screen for and manage mental health conditions due to specialist shortages; an estimated 50% of pediatric visits now involve behavioral health concerns (AAP, 2022). Only 21% of pediatricians report feeling "very comfortable" managing mental health conditions (AAP Periodic Survey, 2022)
- **Siblings and extended family**: Experience indirect effects of a child's untreated mental illness, including family stress, financial strain, and emotional burden. Research indicates that siblings of children with serious mental health conditions have elevated rates of anxiety and depression themselves (Feinberg et al., 2012)
- **Employers and the economy**: Youth mental health affects future workforce productivity; untreated childhood mental illness costs the U.S. an estimated $247 billion annually in healthcare, education, and lost productivity (National Research Council/IOM, 2009; adjusted for inflation). Employers increasingly recognize children's mental health as a workforce issue, as parental caregiving for children with mental health conditions contributes to absenteeism and reduced productivity
- **Child welfare workers**: Caseworkers in child protective services and foster care manage children with extremely high rates of mental health conditions (50-80% of foster children) while facing their own severe workforce shortages and burnout. Turnover rates among child welfare workers average 30-40% annually (Child Welfare League of America, 2023)
- **Juvenile justice professionals**: Judges, probation officers, and detention staff encounter youth with mental health conditions at rates of 65-70%, yet most juvenile justice facilities lack adequate mental health staffing and programming (OJJDP, 2022)
- **Early childhood educators and childcare providers**: Childcare workers are often the first adults outside the family to observe behavioral and developmental concerns in young children. However, they typically receive minimal mental health training, and preschool expulsion rates (6.67 per 1,000, three times the K-12 rate) reflect the system's inability to support children with challenging behaviors (Gilliam, 2005)
- **Transition-age youth (18-25)**: Young adults aging out of pediatric systems face unique challenges navigating adult services. Former foster youth, in particular, experience a "cliff" at ages 18-21 where mental health supports dramatically decrease. This population is both a stakeholder and an affected group with distinct advocacy needs

## Who Has Power

### Decision Makers

| Actor | Role | Authority | Resources | Influence Level |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| U.S. Congress | Authorizes and appropriates federal mental health funding; regulates technology platforms | Legislative authority over Medicaid, SAMHSA, FDA, FTC | Federal budget ($125 million SAMHSA children's MH, FY2024) | High |
| Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) | Administers Medicaid/CHIP including EPSDT; sets coverage and network adequacy standards | Regulatory authority over state Medicaid programs | Oversees $39.7 million children's Medicaid enrollment | High |
| SAMHSA | Administers federal block grants, CCBHC grants, and children's system of care programs | Grant-making; technical assistance; data collection | $7.5 billion total budget (FY2024) | High |
| State legislatures and governors | Determine state mental health budgets, Medicaid implementation, school mandates, social media laws | State legislative and executive authority | State mental health budgets ($10-$500+ million per state) | High |
| State Medicaid agencies | Implement EPSDT; set provider rates; manage behavioral health carve-outs | Administrative authority over state Medicaid programs | Control Medicaid mental health spending | High |
| School districts | Employ school psychologists, counselors, social workers; implement SEL programs | Local authority over school-based services | $700 billion total K-12 spending; mental health is a small fraction | Medium |
| U.S. Surgeon General | Issues advisories; convenes national attention; no regulatory authority | Moral authority and public health platform | No direct budget; depends on HHS and public influence | Medium |

### Key Organizations and Institutions

- **American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP)**: 67,000-member professional organization; declared children's mental health a national emergency (2021); advocates for universal screening, school-based services, and social media regulation. AAP's clinical guidelines influence pediatric practice nationwide, and its political advocacy through the AAP Federal Affairs office carries significant weight with Congress
- **American Academy of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry (AACAP)**: Primary professional organization for child psychiatrists (~10,000 members); produces workforce data, practice guidelines, and policy positions; advocates for training pipeline expansion and reimbursement parity. AACAP's annual workforce mapping project is the authoritative source for data on child psychiatrist distribution
- **National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI)**: Largest grassroots mental health advocacy organization in the U.S. (600+ local affiliates); mobilizes family advocates; lobbies for parity enforcement, funding increases, and anti-stigma campaigns. NAMI's family-to-family education programs reach tens of thousands of caregivers annually
- **Children's Hospital Association**: Represents 200+ children's hospitals; advocates for Medicaid funding, workforce development, and pediatric-specific mental health infrastructure. Children's hospitals provide a disproportionate share of intensive pediatric mental health services and are among the most politically credible advocates for federal investment
- **National Association of School Psychologists (NASP)**: Advocates for improved school-to-psychologist ratios; publishes workforce data; promotes comprehensive school mental health systems. NASP's recommended ratio of 500:1 has become the benchmark against which school mental health staffing is measured
- **American Psychological Association (APA)**: Publishes treatment guidelines, training standards, and workforce data for psychology. APA's Division 53 (Society of Clinical Child and Adolescent Psychology) maintains a database of evidence-based treatments for children
- **The Trevor Project**: Serves as both an advocacy organization and a direct service provider for LGBTQ+ youth mental health and suicide prevention. Operates the only national crisis intervention and suicide prevention lifeline specifically for LGBTQ+ young people, handling over 300,000 crisis contacts annually (Trevor Project, 2023)
- **Common Sense Media**: Research and advocacy organization focused on children's digital safety. Produces widely cited research on youth screen time, social media use, and digital wellbeing. Played a central role in building public and legislative support for the Kids Online Safety Act
- **Zero to Three**: National nonprofit focused on infant and early childhood development. Advocates for infant and early childhood mental health services, home visiting programs, and childcare quality. Produces training materials for early childhood mental health consultation
- **National Child Traumatic Stress Network (NCTSN)**: Federally funded network of 275+ centers that develops, disseminates, and implements evidence-based treatments for childhood trauma. Represents the largest organized effort to bridge the research-practice gap in children's mental health

### Influencers

- **Surgeon General Vivek Murthy**: Through advisories on youth mental health (2021) and social media (2023), elevated the issue to national prominence; proposed warning labels on social media platforms for youth
- **Technology companies (Meta, TikTok, Snap, Google/YouTube)**: Control the platforms that are central to the youth mental health debate; deploy massive lobbying resources ($70+ million combined federal lobbying in 2023, OpenSecrets); resist regulation while promoting voluntary "safety" features
- **Research institutions**: NIMH, university centers, and foundations (e.g., Robert Wood Johnson Foundation, Annie E. Casey Foundation) generate the evidence base that informs policy debates
- **Media and public opinion**: Investigative reporting on social media harms (Wall Street Journal "Facebook Files," 2021), high-profile youth advocates (e.g., Surgeon General's youth advisory council), and parental advocacy movements shape public demand for reform
- **Insurance industry trade groups (AHIP)**: Lobby against stronger parity enforcement and network adequacy mandates; argue that workforce shortages, not insurance policies, drive access problems

| Rural health advocates + telehealth companies + states | Telehealth expansion for pediatric behavioral health | Strong | Pandemic telehealth expansion now permanent in most states |
| Civil rights organizations + LGBTQ+ advocacy groups + AAP | Equitable access; affirming care; culturally competent services | Moderate | Advocacy against conversion therapy bans; culturally competent care standards |

| Employers + workforce development organizations + mental health advocates | Children's mental health as workforce pipeline investment | Emerging | Business Roundtable engagement; economic case for investment |

### Conflicting Interests

- **Parents vs. adolescents**: Parental consent requirements can create barriers for adolescents seeking confidential care, particularly LGBTQ+ youth and those experiencing family conflict. Some parents support consent mandates; adolescent advocates support expanded minor consent for mental health services. As of 2024, 29 states allow minors to consent to outpatient mental health treatment, while 21 require parental consent for all non-emergency services (Guttmacher Institute, 2024). This issue has become increasingly politicized, with some states restricting minor consent for gender-affirming mental health care while others expand it
- **Technology companies vs. child safety advocates**: Tech companies argue that age verification threatens privacy and that their platforms provide social connection benefits; child safety advocates prioritize design restrictions and duty-of-care requirements. This conflict mirrors the historical dynamics between the tobacco industry and public health advocates, with platforms deploying similar strategies: funding sympathetic research, promoting voluntary self-regulation, and litigating against regulatory efforts
- **Insurance industry vs. providers and families**: Insurers resist expanded network adequacy mandates and higher reimbursement rates; providers and families demand adequate networks and reimbursement that supports a viable practice. AHIP (the insurance trade group) argues that workforce shortages, not insurance barriers, cause access problems--an argument that deflects attention from reimbursement rates as a driver of provider avoidance
- **Federal mandates vs. state autonomy**: Federal EPSDT enforcement and minimum school staffing standards are opposed by governors and legislators who view them as unfunded mandates or federal overreach. This tension is particularly acute in states with limited fiscal capacity, where meeting federal standards would require significant new state investment even with enhanced federal matching
- **Pharmaceutical industry vs. therapy advocates**: Pharmaceutical companies have incentives to promote medication-first approaches; therapy advocates argue for increased access to evidence-based psychotherapy as first-line treatment for most youth conditions. The pharmaceutical industry's $29.3 million in federal lobbying (2023, OpenSecrets) dwarfs advocacy spending by therapy-focused organizations
- **For-profit behavioral health vs. public/nonprofit providers**: Private equity firms invested $24 billion in behavioral health companies between 2020 and 2023, acquiring treatment centers and therapy practices. For-profit providers tend to focus on higher-acuity, higher-reimbursement services and privately insured patients, while public and nonprofit providers serve Medicaid populations. The consolidation of behavioral health by for-profit entities may prioritize shareholder returns over access and equity
- **General education vs. mental health services in schools**: School administrators face pressure to allocate limited resources to academic instruction and test preparation rather than mental health services. The tension between using school time for SEL and mental health versus core academics reflects competing mandates and accountability systems
- **Evidence-based treatment advocates vs. eclectic practitioners**: Proponents of evidence-based practices (EBPs) argue for mandating the use of treatments with proven efficacy. Many community-based practitioners resist EBP mandates, arguing they constrain clinical judgment, fail to account for cultural context, and impose burdensome training and fidelity requirements. This tension affects how training dollars are spent and what treatments Medicaid reimburses

### The Children's Mental Health Iron Triangle

The entrenched power relationships in children's mental health involve:

1. **Congressional committees** (Senate HELP, House Energy and Commerce) that authorize but often underfund children's mental health programs. These committees hold jurisdiction over SAMHSA, CMS, and FTC--the agencies most central to children's mental health reform--and are heavily lobbied by insurance, pharmaceutical, and technology industry interests
2. **Federal agencies** (CMS, SAMHSA, HRSA) that administer programs but lack enforcement resources or political will. CMS has never imposed financial penalties on a state for failing to meet EPSDT mental health obligations. SAMHSA's total children's mental health budget ($125 million) is smaller than the annual lobbying budgets of the industries it is expected to regulate
3. **Industry interest groups** (insurance companies, pharmaceutical manufacturers, for-profit behavioral health chains, and increasingly technology companies) that lobby for favorable reimbursement structures and against burdensome regulation. These groups have well-funded, permanent lobbying presences in Washington and state capitals

This triangle operates to maintain a system that generates revenue for industry participants while underserving children--particularly low-income children on Medicaid, who represent the largest share of the affected population but the weakest political constituency.

### Breaking the Iron Triangle

Historically, iron triangles in other policy areas have been disrupted by external forces:

- **Media exposure**: The Wall Street Journal's "Facebook Files" and investigative reporting on foster care medication practices have created public pressure that the iron triangle's normal operation cannot absorb
- **Litigation**: Class action lawsuits enforcing EPSDT (e.g., *Rosie D.*, *Katie A.*) and the multi-state attorney general suits against social media companies create accountability mechanisms outside the legislative-regulatory-industry cycle
- **Bipartisan outrage**: When an issue generates sufficient bipartisan public anger--as social media and youth mental health has--the iron triangle's ability to block reform is weakened. The KOSA vote (91-3) demonstrates this dynamic
- **State-level action**: States can bypass federal gridlock and create models that build pressure for federal reform. California's $4.7 billion investment created a standard against which federal inaction is measured

## State-Level Stakeholder Dynamics

Stakeholder dynamics vary significantly across states, creating different political environments for reform:

| State Type | Key Dynamic | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| High-capacity progressive states | Strong advocacy coalitions; willing state investment; receptive legislatures | California, Massachusetts, Connecticut, Colorado |
| Moderate states with bipartisan interest | Social media concerns create bipartisan opening; workforce shortages affect both parties' constituents | Georgia, Texas (crisis services), Virginia, Ohio |
| Low-capacity states with limited infrastructure | Fewer advocacy organizations; limited state revenue; resistance to federal mandates | Mississippi, Arkansas, Alabama, West Virginia |
| States with active parental rights conflicts | School-based mental health services become entangled with culture war issues (gender identity, parental consent) | Florida, Texas, Missouri, Utah |

In many states, the most effective advocacy comes from children's hospitals and pediatric medical societies, which carry credibility across the political spectrum and can frame children's mental health as a healthcare infrastructure issue rather than a social policy issue.

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on children's mental health policy:

- **Lobbying**: Insurance industry (AHIP) and pharmaceutical companies spend tens of millions annually on federal lobbying. Technology companies spent $70+ million combined in 2023 (OpenSecrets). Children's mental health advocacy organizations have a fraction of these resources--NAMI's total annual budget is approximately $25 million
- **Campaign contributions**: Health sector PACs contributed $129 million to federal candidates in 2022 (OpenSecrets). Mental health-specific contributions are a small subset. Technology industry PACs contributed $35 million in 2022
- **Revolving door**: Former CMS, SAMHSA, and congressional staff frequently move to insurance companies, pharmaceutical firms, and for-profit behavioral health companies, carrying institutional knowledge and relationships
- **Public advocacy**: Parent-led movements, social media campaigns (#ProtectKidsOnline), and the Surgeon General's high-profile advisories have been effective at generating public attention. The 2021 "Facebook Files" reporting by the Wall Street Journal demonstrated investigative journalism's power to shift the debate
- **Expertise and data**: AACAP workforce data, CDC surveillance, and SAMHSA's National Survey on Drug Use and Health provide the evidentiary foundation for policy arguments. Control of this data gives federal agencies and professional organizations significant framing power
- **Litigation**: Lawsuits enforcing EPSDT, mental health parity, and platform liability have been used by legal advocacy organizations (National Health Law Program, state attorneys general) to compel systemic change when legislative and regulatory channels are insufficient
- **Data and framing**: Organizations that control key data sets (CDC, SAMHSA, AACAP) and those that frame the public narrative (Surgeon General, AAP, Common Sense Media) exercise significant influence over how the crisis is understood and what solutions are considered. The Surgeon General's decision to frame social media as a youth mental health threat--using the public health advisory format historically reserved for issues like tobacco and HIV--was itself a significant exercise of framing power
- **State-level innovation**: Governors and state legislatures in states like California, Colorado, Massachusetts, and Connecticut have demonstrated that state-level leadership can drive reform even in the absence of comprehensive federal action. These state innovations function as demonstrations that can influence federal policy and create competitive pressure on other states
- **Academic research influence**: Researchers at major universities (e.g., Jonathan Haidt at NYU, Jean Twenge at San Diego State, the Center for Youth Wellness in California) shape the public narrative through books, op-eds, and media appearances. The ongoing debate between researchers who emphasize social media's causal role and those who argue effects are small illustrates how academic framing shapes policy discourse
- **Grassroots parental mobilization**: Parent-led movements organized through social media (ironically) and community networks have become increasingly influential at both state and federal levels. Groups like ParentsTogether and Fairplay combine personal stories with policy sophistication. School board meetings have become a key venue for parent advocacy on children's mental health
- **Youth self-advocacy**: Young people sharing their own mental health experiences through social media, congressional testimony, and advocacy campaigns have added an authenticity and urgency to the debate that adult advocates alone cannot provide. The destigmatization of mental health challenges among Gen Z has created a generation of potential advocates

## Stakeholder Capacity and Limitations

### Why Children's Mental Health Advocacy Is Structurally Weak

Despite broad public concern, the advocacy infrastructure for children's mental health remains significantly weaker than for other major health and policy issues:

| Factor | Children's Mental Health | Comparison |
|---|---|---|
| NAMI total annual budget | ~$25 million | AHIP (insurance lobbying): $8.5 million federal lobbying alone |
| AACAP membership | ~10,000 | AMA membership: 250,000+ |
| Combined federal lobbying by child MH organizations | ~$5 million (estimated) | Tech industry federal lobbying: $70+ million (2023) |
| Children's vote | Zero (cannot vote) | AARP membership: 38 million voters |
| PAC contributions from child MH sector | Negligible | Health sector PACs: $129 million (2022) |

The structural weakness of children's mental health advocacy is compounded by the diffuse nature of the affected constituency. Unlike disease-specific advocacy (e.g., cancer, diabetes, HIV) that can organize around a single condition, children's mental health spans dozens of diagnoses, multiple age groups, and several distinct systems (schools, Medicaid, child welfare, juvenile justice), fragmenting advocacy efforts and diluting political impact.

### Emerging Stakeholder Dynamics

Several emerging dynamics are shifting the stakeholder landscape:

- **Youth-led advocacy**: Organizations like the JED Foundation, Active Minds, and student-led mental health clubs on college campuses have brought young people's own voices into the policy debate. The Surgeon General's youth advisory council represents an institutional effort to center youth perspectives in federal policy
- **Parent mobilization around social media**: Parent advocacy groups (ParentsTogether, Fairplay) have organized specifically around social media harms, creating a focused, passionate constituency with personal stories that resonate with legislators
- **Business community engagement**: Organizations including the Business Roundtable and the U.S. Chamber of Commerce have begun to recognize children's mental health as a workforce pipeline issue, potentially adding corporate advocacy to the coalition
- **State attorney general coordination**: The multi-state attorney general lawsuit against Meta represents a new enforcement mechanism. Attorneys general in both red and blue states have joined, demonstrating bipartisan state-level commitment to holding platforms accountable
- **Philanthropic investment**: Major foundations (Robert Wood Johnson Foundation, Annie E. Casey Foundation, Bloomberg Philanthropies) have increased investment in children's mental health research and advocacy, partially compensating for the limited resources of advocacy organizations

## Power Mapping: Stakeholder Influence vs. Stake in Outcomes

The following analysis maps stakeholders by their degree of influence over children's mental health policy and the intensity of their stake in outcomes. The fundamental structural problem is that those with the greatest stake (children and families) have the least power, while those with the most power (industry actors and federal agencies) often have competing priorities.

### High Influence, High Stake

- **CMS**: Controls Medicaid spending that covers 39.7 million children; EPSDT enforcement decisions directly affect millions of children's access to care
- **State Medicaid agencies**: Implement EPSDT; set provider rates; manage behavioral health carve-outs. Their decisions determine whether the federal entitlement translates into actual services
- **Congress (Appropriations Committees)**: Annual funding decisions for SAMHSA, HRSA, Title IV-A, and Medicaid directly determine the resources available for children's mental health

### High Influence, Moderate Stake

- **Insurance industry (AHIP)**: High influence through lobbying and political contributions; moderate stake because pediatric behavioral health represents a fraction of total insurance expenditures but parity enforcement could significantly increase costs
- **Technology companies**: Massive influence through lobbying ($70+ million combined, 2023) and First Amendment litigation; moderate-high stake because youth users are a significant but not dominant segment of their user base
- **Pharmaceutical industry**: High influence through lobbying ($29.3 million, 2023) and research funding; moderate stake because the pediatric psychotropic market (~$8-10 billion) is a fraction of total pharmaceutical revenue

### Low Influence, High Stake

- **Children and adolescents with mental health conditions**: 17.4 million children with diagnosed conditions; zero political power (cannot vote, donate, or lobby)
- **Parents and families**: 35+ million families affected; moderate political power but limited organizational infrastructure and resources compared to industry opponents
- **Youth in foster care**: Among the most severely affected populations (50-80% with diagnoses); essentially no independent political voice
- **LGBTQ+ youth**: Extremely high rates of mental health distress; limited political power; face active political opposition in some states
- **Rural children**: Severe access barriers; dispersed geographic distribution limits political organizing

### Low Influence, Low Stake

- **General public (without children)**: Low direct stake but potentially mobilizable through concerns about school safety, social media, and community wellbeing
- **International organizations**: WHO, UNICEF, and OECD provide data and models but have no direct influence on U.S. policy

## Key Stakeholder Positions on Major Reform Proposals

| Reform Proposal | Supportive Stakeholders | Opposing/Skeptical Stakeholders | Key Tension |
|---|---|---|---|
| EPSDT enforcement with financial penalties | NAMI, AACAP, child advocacy organizations, legal aid | State governors (NGA), state Medicaid directors, fiscal conservatives | Federal mandates vs. state capacity |
| Medicaid rate increases for behavioral health | Providers (AACAP, APA), families, children's hospitals | State budget offices, AHIP, managed care organizations | Cost vs. access |
| Kids Online Safety Act (KOSA) | AAP, Common Sense Media, bipartisan lawmakers, parent groups | ACLU, EFF, some tech companies, libertarian organizations | Safety vs. free expression and privacy |
| Universal school-based mental health staffing | NASP, ASCA, NAMI, teachers' unions | Fiscal conservatives, some school boards, parental rights groups | Funding and scope of school role |
| ACE screening in pediatric care | AAP, CDC, Zero to Three, SAMHSA | Some pediatricians (implementation concerns), CPS (capacity concerns) | Screening without adequate follow-up services |
| Social-emotional learning mandates | CASEL, education advocates, mental health organizations | Some parent groups, religious conservatives, education traditionalists | Cultural values and instructional time |
| Psychotropic medication oversight in foster care | Foster care advocates, GAO, state auditors | Pharmaceutical companies, some psychiatrists (clinical autonomy concerns) | Safety monitoring vs. access to treatment |
| Conversion therapy bans for minors | LGBTQ+ organizations, AAP, APA, Trevor Project | Some religious organizations, parental rights groups | Youth safety vs. parental and religious liberty |

## Stakeholder Engagement Recommendations

For reform advocates, stakeholder analysis suggests the following strategic priorities:

1. **Strengthen the weakest voices**: Invest in youth and family advocacy infrastructure, including funded positions for parent advocates, youth advisory councils, and grassroots organizing support
2. **Leverage bipartisan coalitions**: The social media and school safety dimensions of children's mental health create rare opportunities for cross-party collaboration. Build legislative coalitions around these entry points before expanding to more contested reforms
3. **Use litigation strategically**: Where legislative and regulatory channels are blocked by industry influence, EPSDT enforcement litigation and platform accountability lawsuits can create alternative pathways to reform
4. **Engage new allies**: Employers, business groups, and workforce development organizations can be engaged as allies by framing children's mental health as an economic competitiveness issue
5. **Elevate credible messengers**: Pediatricians, children's hospital leaders, and school principals carry bipartisan credibility that advocacy organizations alone may lack. Center these voices in legislative testimony and media advocacy
6. **Exploit industry divisions**: Technology companies, insurance companies, and pharmaceutical companies do not have identical interests. Targeted regulation of social media (which insurers and pharmaceutical companies may not oppose) can be pursued more easily than reforms that unite all industry opposition
7. **Document state innovation**: States that have invested in children's mental health (California, Massachusetts, Connecticut) provide evidence of feasibility that can counter claims of impracticality. Systematically documenting and publicizing state-level outcomes strengthens the case for federal action
