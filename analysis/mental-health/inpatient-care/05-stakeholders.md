# Inpatient Psychiatric Care: Stakeholders

The inpatient psychiatric care system is shaped by the interactions of patients, families, healthcare providers, payers, government agencies, and advocacy organizations, each with distinct and sometimes conflicting interests, resources, and levels of power.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Adults with serious mental illness (SMI) | Directly affected by bed availability, quality of care, length of stay, and discharge planning | ~14.2 million adults (5.6% of U.S. adults) with SMI (SAMHSA, 2023) | Access to timely inpatient care when needed, humane treatment conditions, effective discharge planning, continuity of care |
| Psychiatric emergency patients | Subjected to ED boarding when beds are unavailable; face restraint, sedation, and prolonged waits | ~4.3 million psychiatric ED visits annually (HCUP, 2023) | Rapid assessment and placement, safety during boarding, connection to appropriate care |
| Children and adolescents with acute psychiatric needs | Board in EDs at higher rates than adults; limited pediatric psychiatric beds | ~2.6 million youth with severe emotional disturbance (SAMHSA, 2023) | Age-appropriate care, educational continuity during hospitalization, family involvement |
| Families and caregivers of people with SMI | Bear emotional, financial, and caregiving burden; navigate fragmented system | ~8.4 million family caregivers of adults with SMI (NAMI, 2023) | Accessible crisis care, communication from providers, discharge planning that includes family, respite |
| Involuntarily committed patients | Loss of liberty; subjected to forced treatment; legal rights at stake | ~2.0 million involuntary evaluations annually (SAMHSA, 2023) | Due process protections, access to legal representation, least restrictive treatment, restoration of rights |

### Secondary Stakeholders

- **People experiencing homelessness with mental illness**: An estimated 250,000+ people who are homeless have a serious mental illness (HUD, 2023). Lack of inpatient beds contributes to chronic homelessness and cycling between shelters, streets, and emergency departments. The National Alliance to End Homelessness estimates that psychiatric hospitalizations for homeless individuals cost 3-5 times more than comparable admissions for housed individuals due to longer stays, higher acuity, and more complex discharge planning needs.
- **Incarcerated individuals with mental illness**: 44% of jail inmates and 37% of state prisoners have a diagnosed mental health condition (Bureau of Justice Statistics, 2023). Many would be better served by psychiatric hospitalization but are diverted to the criminal justice system due to bed shortages. The National Commission on Correctional Health Care has documented that jail-based psychiatric care is consistently inferior to hospital-based care, with lower staffing ratios, limited medication formularies, and inadequate crisis intervention capacity.
- **Emergency department patients with non-psychiatric conditions**: ED boarding of psychiatric patients reduces capacity for all patients, extending wait times and contributing to ambulance diversion. ACEP estimates that psychiatric boarding reduces effective ED capacity by 12-18% at hospitals with high boarding rates, directly impacting cardiac, stroke, and trauma patients.
- **Law enforcement officers**: Police are often the first responders to mental health crises. They spend an estimated 21% of their time responding to mental health calls (Police Executive Research Forum, 2022), frequently transporting individuals to EDs that cannot place them. Officers describe this role as frustrating, time-consuming, and outside their training. Crisis Intervention Team (CIT) programs, which train officers in mental health response, operate in approximately 2,700 communities (CIT International, 2023), but many departments still lack specialized training.
- **Emergency medical services (EMS) personnel**: Paramedics and EMTs transport psychiatric patients to emergency departments, often waiting with patients for hours in already-crowded EDs. Ambulance crews tied up in ED waiting areas cannot respond to other calls, creating cascading delays in emergency response across communities.
- **Community mental health center staff**: Outpatient providers serve as the primary treatment system for most people with serious mental illness but often cannot access inpatient beds when their patients decompensate. Community mental health centers, many of which are federally qualified health centers, report that the inability to hospitalize acutely ill patients is the single most frustrating barrier in their work (National Council for Mental Wellbeing, 2023).

## Who Has Power

### Decision Makers

| Actor | Role | Authority | Resources | Influence Level |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| U.S. Congress | Writes legislation governing Medicaid, Medicare, and mental health programs | Can repeal or modify IMD exclusion; authorizes funding | Controls federal appropriations | High |
| Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) | Administers Medicaid/Medicare; approves Section 1115 waivers | Regulates IMD exclusion enforcement; approves waiver applications | $1.5 trillion in annual spending authority | High |
| State legislatures | Appropriate state mental health budgets; set civil commitment laws | Control state psychiatric hospital funding; write involuntary hold statutes | State general fund budgets | High |
| State mental health authorities (SMHAs) | Operate state psychiatric hospitals; administer community mental health systems | Direct authority over state-funded psychiatric beds and programs | $44.5 billion nationally (FY2023) | Medium-High |
| State Medicaid directors | Administer state Medicaid programs; negotiate managed care contracts | Determine reimbursement rates; apply for IMD waivers | State Medicaid budgets (federal/state) | Medium-High |
| Hospital CEOs/boards | Decide whether to operate psychiatric units; set bed counts | Control capital allocation and service line decisions | Individual hospital budgets | Medium |
| Managed care organizations (MCOs) | Authorize or deny inpatient psychiatric admissions; set provider networks | Utilization review authority; network determination | Commercial and Medicaid managed care contracts covering 70%+ of insured population | Medium-High |

### Key Organizations and Institutions

- **SAMHSA (Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration)**: Federal agency that funds mental health programs, collects data on bed capacity through the National Mental Health Services Survey, and administers block grants. Annual budget of approximately $7.5 billion (FY2024). Influence: medium-high on policy direction; limited direct authority over bed capacity. Under the 21st Century Cures Act, SAMHSA was reorganized under an Assistant Secretary for Mental Health and Substance Use, elevating its position within HHS.
- **National Association of State Mental Health Program Directors (NASMHPD)**: Represents state mental health commissioners. Coordinates state positions on federal policy, publishes research on state psychiatric hospital systems, and advocates for increased funding. Produces the annual State Psychiatric Hospital Infrastructure Assessment and the NRI Spending Reports that provide the most detailed data on state-level mental health expenditures. Influence: high on state-level decisions and federal advocacy.
- **American Hospital Association (AHA)**: Represents approximately 5,000 hospitals. Advocates on reimbursement rates, regulatory burden, and EMTALA enforcement. Has mixed record on psychiatric bed advocacy, as many member hospitals have closed psychiatric units. The AHA has supported IMD exclusion reform but has not made it a top legislative priority. Influence: high on general hospital policy; moderate on psychiatric-specific issues.
- **Joint Commission**: Accredits hospitals including psychiatric facilities. Sets safety and quality standards, including requirements for suicide-resistant environments, restraint and seclusion protocols, and staffing adequacy. The Joint Commission's Sentinel Event Alert on psychiatric boarding (2020) drew national attention to the issue. Influence: high on facility operations and quality metrics.
- **National Quality Forum (NQF)**: Endorses quality measures used by CMS and commercial payers, including psychiatric readmission rates, 7-day follow-up after hospitalization, and use of seclusion and restraint. NQF-endorsed measures shape what gets measured and therefore what gets managed. Influence: medium-high on quality measurement and incentives.

### Influencers

- **Treatment Advocacy Center (TAC)**: Leading advocacy organization focused on psychiatric bed capacity and involuntary treatment laws. Produces widely cited bed-count data and state-level rankings. Primary advocacy position: expand psychiatric beds and strengthen civil commitment laws. Founded in 1998 by E. Fuller Torrey, M.D., TAC has been the most persistent voice arguing that bed expansion is necessary and that the current bed count is dangerously inadequate. Influence: high on public narrative around bed shortage.
- **National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI)**: Largest grassroots mental health organization with 600+ affiliates and an estimated 500,000 members. Represents families and individuals affected by mental illness. Advocates for increased funding, parity enforcement, and system reform. NAMI operates the NAMI HelpLine (1-800-950-NAMI), peer support programs, and family education courses (Family-to-Family) that reach tens of thousands annually. Influence: high on grassroots advocacy and congressional engagement.
- **Mental Health America (MHA)**: National advocacy organization that publishes annual state-level mental health rankings (the "State of Mental Health in America" report). Emphasizes prevention and community-based services alongside inpatient capacity. MHA's screening tools (mhascreening.org) have been used by over 7 million people since the pandemic began. Influence: medium on policy; high on public awareness.
- **Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law**: Leading legal advocacy organization focused on disability rights and community integration. Emphasizes alternatives to hospitalization and opposes expansion of involuntary commitment. Named for Judge David L. Bazelon, a pioneering jurist in mental health law. Has litigated or consulted on virtually every major *Olmstead* enforcement case. Influence: high on legal and civil rights dimensions.
- **American Psychiatric Association (APA)**: Professional organization for approximately 38,000 psychiatrists. Publishes the *Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders* (DSM-5-TR), clinical practice guidelines, and the journal *Psychiatric Services*. Advocates for workforce development, reimbursement parity, and scope of practice protections. Influence: high on clinical standards; medium on policy.
- **American College of Emergency Physicians (ACEP)**: Represents approximately 40,000 emergency physicians. Has been a leading voice documenting and opposing psychiatric boarding since the mid-2000s. ACEP's annual Psychiatric Boarding Survey and its policy statements on boarding have been influential in framing the issue as an emergency medicine problem, not just a psychiatry problem. Influence: high on ED boarding policy and EMTALA enforcement.
- **National Disability Rights Network (NDRN)**: Coordinates the federally funded Protection and Advocacy (P&A) systems in every state that monitor conditions in psychiatric facilities and represent patients' rights. P&A systems have legal authority to investigate abuse and neglect in state psychiatric hospitals and are the primary external oversight mechanism for institutional care. Influence: medium on policy; high on institutional oversight.

## Stakeholder Interests and Alignment

### Aligned Interests (Potential Coalition Partners)

| Coalition | Shared Interest | Strength |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| NAMI, TAC, APA, ACEP | Increasing psychiatric bed capacity and reducing ED boarding | Strong |
| State Medicaid directors, NASMHPD, AHA | Repealing or reforming the IMD exclusion to draw federal funding | Strong |
| Law enforcement organizations, NAMI, TAC | Reducing criminalization of mental illness through better acute care access | Moderate |
| Pediatric groups (AAP, AACAP), children's hospitals, NAMI | Expanding pediatric psychiatric bed capacity specifically | Strong |
| NASMHPD, state legislatures, SAMHSA | Modernizing state psychiatric hospital infrastructure | Moderate |
| Emergency medicine (ACEP, ENA), hospital associations (AHA) | Eliminating ED boarding through capacity expansion and diversion programs | Strong |
| Housing advocates (National Alliance to End Homelessness), NAMI, NASMHPD | Linking discharge planning to housing resources to reduce readmissions | Moderate |

### Conflicting Interests

- **Bed expansion vs. community investment**: TAC and some family groups prioritize expanding inpatient beds, while the Bazelon Center and disability rights organizations argue resources should go to community-based alternatives that prevent hospitalization. This is the most persistent stakeholder conflict in the field. The divide is philosophical as well as practical: advocates on one side see adequate inpatient capacity as a human right; advocates on the other see institutional expansion as a threat to community integration principles established by *Olmstead*.
- **Involuntary treatment vs. patient autonomy**: TAC and some family advocates support broadening involuntary commitment criteria, while the Bazelon Center, ACLU, and many consumer/survivor organizations oppose any expansion of forced treatment. The consumer/survivor movement -- individuals with lived experience of psychiatric hospitalization -- is particularly vocal in opposing coercive interventions, arguing that involuntary treatment is inherently traumatic and that alternatives (peer support, open dialogue, advance directives) should be prioritized.
- **Federal fiscal responsibility vs. state needs**: Congress and CMS resist repealing the IMD exclusion due to cost concerns ($10-15 billion/year in new federal Medicaid spending), while states argue the exclusion is unjust and forces them to bear costs that Medicaid covers for every other medical condition.
- **Hospital financial interests vs. patient needs**: Hospitals facing financial pressure from psychiatric care may prefer to close psychiatric units rather than operate them at a loss, even though communities need the beds.
- **For-profit vs. public interest**: For-profit psychiatric chains (Acadia Healthcare, UHS) benefit from constrained supply (higher occupancy, pricing power) even as they publicly advocate for bed expansion. Their selective admission practices and documented quality concerns create tension with public-interest advocates who seek universal access regardless of insurance status.

### The Iron Triangle of Inpatient Psychiatric Care

A self-reinforcing power structure maintains the status quo:

1. **State governments** avoid investing in state hospitals because the IMD exclusion means they get no federal match
2. **The federal government** maintains the IMD exclusion because repealing it would create massive new spending obligations
3. **Private insurers and managed care organizations** limit inpatient authorizations because shorter stays reduce costs
4. **Result**: All three powerful actors have financial incentives to limit psychiatric bed capacity, while the stakeholders most affected (patients and families) have the least political power

Breaking this iron triangle requires simultaneously changing the financial incentives for all three actors, which is why incremental reforms that address only one leg of the triangle (e.g., expanding waivers without changing insurer behavior, or enforcing parity without addressing the IMD exclusion) have failed to produce systemic change.

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on inpatient psychiatric care policy:

- **Lobbying**: The American Hospital Association spends approximately $25 million annually on federal lobbying (OpenSecrets, 2023). Insurance industry lobbying exceeds $150 million annually across all health issues. Mental health advocacy organizations (NAMI, TAC, MHA combined) spend less than $5 million annually, creating a significant asymmetry. The pharmaceutical industry, which spends over $350 million annually on federal lobbying, has not prioritized inpatient care reform, preferring to focus on medication access and pricing.
- **Campaign Contributions**: Hospital and insurance industry PACs contribute tens of millions per election cycle to congressional campaigns. Mental health advocacy PACs contribute comparatively minimal amounts. Members of the Senate Finance Committee and the House Energy and Commerce Committee, which have jurisdiction over Medicaid and the IMD exclusion, are among the largest recipients of healthcare industry contributions.
- **Litigation**: The Bazelon Center and ACLU use federal court litigation to enforce community integration requirements under *Olmstead v. L.C.* and to challenge conditions in state psychiatric hospitals. The National Disability Rights Network coordinates P&A system lawsuits against state hospitals with inadequate conditions. This legal strategy has been effective at driving state compliance but has sometimes contributed to bed closures without community alternatives.
- **Public Advocacy**: NAMI and TAC leverage family stories, media coverage of ED boarding and psychiatric homelessness, and grassroots campaigns (e.g., NAMI Walks, which raised $100+ million cumulatively, and NAMI's "StigmaFree" campaign) to build public support. The 988 launch amplified public awareness of crisis care gaps. TAC's media strategy of comparing county jail populations with mental illness to state hospital census figures has been particularly effective at framing the crisis.
- **Data and Expertise**: TAC's bed-count database, SAMHSA's National Mental Health Services Survey, CMS Hospital Compare psychiatric quality data, and academic research from institutions like the National Bureau of Economic Research provide the evidence base that shapes policy debates. The Treatment Advocacy Center's annual reports on psychiatric bed capacity are the single most cited source in congressional testimony and media coverage of the bed shortage.
- **Patient and Family Testimony**: Congressional hearings and state legislative sessions regularly feature testimony from family members who have experienced the failure of the system firsthand. These narratives -- parents who could not get beds for suicidal children, spouses who watched loved ones cycle through emergency departments, siblings who saw family members jailed for illness-related behavior -- are among the most powerful tools for shifting political will.
