# Inpatient Psychiatric Care: What You Can Do

The crisis in psychiatric inpatient care -- a 95% per-capita reduction in psychiatric beds since 1955, emergency department boarding averaging 8 hours, and jails functioning as the largest psychiatric facilities in the country -- is sustained in part by public silence. Mental illness remains heavily stigmatized, and individuals experiencing psychiatric crises and their families are often too overwhelmed to advocate for systemic change. Citizen engagement is essential to building the political will for reforms that will cost billions of dollars and require overcoming entrenched opposition from fiscal conservatives, insurers, and some disability rights advocates who view bed expansion as a return to institutionalization.

The actions described here are organized by level of engagement, from individual learning to professional advocacy. Each level builds on the preceding ones: effective political action requires being informed, and effective coalition-building requires personal connections.

---

## Individual Actions

### Learn and Stay Informed

- Read the Treatment Advocacy Center's state-by-state data on psychiatric bed capacity to understand your state's shortage: <https://www.treatmentadvocacycenter.org/evidence-and-research/learn-more-about/3696>
- Review your state's per-capita psychiatric bed rate (national average: 11.7 per 100,000 in 2024) and compare it to the recommended minimum of 40-60 per 100,000
- Subscribe to NAMI's policy newsletter for monthly federal and state legislative updates: <https://www.nami.org/advocacy>
- Follow SAMHSA's Behavioral Health Barometer for annual data on mental health treatment access in your state: <https://www.samhsa.gov/data/behavioral-health-barometer>
- Read Pete Earley's *Crazy: A Father's Search Through America's Mental Health Madness* for a powerful first-person account of navigating the inpatient system
- Read E. Fuller Torrey's *American Psychosis* for the definitive history of how federal policy created the current crisis
- Review Mental Health America's annual "State of Mental Health in America" report to see how your state ranks on access, prevalence, and system capacity: <https://www.mhanational.org/issues/state-mental-health-america>
- Understand the IMD exclusion: Read the Congressional Research Service report R46646 on the Medicaid IMD exclusion, available through your congressional representative's office or at <https://crsreports.congress.gov>
- Know your state's civil commitment laws: The Treatment Advocacy Center maintains a state-by-state guide to civil commitment procedures, hold durations, and who can initiate an evaluation: <https://www.treatmentadvocacycenter.org/browse-by-state>

### Make Personal Changes

- If you or a family member have experienced the psychiatric care system, document your experience (wait times, boarding incidents, discharge without follow-up, readmissions). Personal stories are among the most powerful advocacy tools. Write down dates, facility names, hours spent waiting, and outcomes.
- Learn your state's civil commitment process and the rights of patients and families during psychiatric emergencies. Most states have a 72-hour hold process; know the specific procedures in your state, including who can initiate a hold and what judicial review is available.
- If you have private insurance, request your insurer's medical necessity criteria for inpatient psychiatric care and compare them to criteria for medical/surgical inpatient care. Report discrepancies to your state insurance commissioner as potential parity violations. The Department of Labor's Employee Benefits Security Administration (EBSA) also accepts parity complaints for employer-sponsored plans: <https://www.dol.gov/agencies/ebsa/laws-and-regulations/laws/mental-health-and-substance-use-disorder-parity>
- Consider becoming a trained Crisis Intervention Team (CIT) volunteer or supporting CIT training for local law enforcement. CIT International provides information on local programs: <https://www.citinternational.org>
- Complete a Mental Health First Aid course to be prepared to help someone experiencing a psychiatric crisis: <https://www.mentalhealthfirstaid.org>
- If you are a family member of someone with serious mental illness, join a NAMI Family-to-Family course, a free 8-week education program that covers diagnosis, treatment, communication strategies, and advocacy skills: <https://www.nami.org/Support-Education/Mental-Health-Education/NAMI-Family-to-Family>

### Use Your Voice

- **Contact Your Representatives**: Call your U.S. senators and representative to advocate for IMD exclusion repeal. This is the single most impactful federal legislative action (see phone script below). Contact your state legislators about state psychiatric bed investments and crisis stabilization funding.
- **Submit Public Comments**: When CMS proposes rules related to Medicaid psychiatric coverage or MHPAEA enforcement, submit comments during the public comment period. Federal Register notices are published at <https://www.federalregister.gov>. Comments from individuals with personal experience carry particular weight with regulators.
- **Write Letters to the Editor**: When local news covers emergency department closures, homelessness, or criminal justice issues involving mental illness, write letters connecting these problems to the psychiatric bed shortage. Local newspapers remain influential with state legislators.
- **Share Information**: Share Treatment Advocacy Center and NAMI data on social media, particularly your state's specific psychiatric bed rate and how it compares to both the national average and international benchmarks.
- **Testify Before Local Government**: Many counties and municipalities have behavioral health boards or advisory committees that accept public testimony. Attend meetings and share data on local psychiatric bed shortages and boarding times. These local bodies often make funding recommendations that influence state and federal advocacy.
- **File Insurance Complaints**: If you or a family member has been denied inpatient psychiatric care by an insurer, file a complaint with your state insurance commissioner and with the federal EBSA (for employer-sponsored plans). Each complaint creates a record that regulators use to identify patterns of parity violations.

## Community Actions

### Organize Locally

- Form a local mental health advocacy chapter through NAMI's affiliate network. If your community does not have a NAMI affiliate, consider starting one: <https://www.nami.org/Get-Involved/NAMI-Affiliates>. NAMI affiliates operate in over 600 communities and provide the organizational infrastructure for sustained advocacy.
- Organize a community screening of a documentary on the mental health system (such as *Out of the Shadows*, *Bedlam*, or *The Definition of Insanity*) and host a post-screening discussion with local mental health professionals and elected officials
- Partner with your local hospital's emergency department to understand the extent of psychiatric boarding in your community. Ask hospital administrators for data on average psychiatric wait times and boarding hours. Many hospitals will share aggregate data with community partners.
- Attend your local mental health board meetings or county behavioral health authority meetings. These bodies often make funding decisions about crisis services and inpatient capacity. Consistent attendance and informed testimony over multiple meetings is more effective than a single appearance.
- Organize a Mental Health First Aid training course for community members, teachers, clergy, and first responders: <https://www.mentalhealthfirstaid.org>. Mental Health First Aid has trained over 3 million Americans since its introduction; each trained individual becomes a potential advocate.
- Host town hall meetings focused specifically on psychiatric care access, inviting local hospital administrators, law enforcement, community mental health center directors, and elected officials. Structure the events around specific data (local boarding times, local bed counts, local incarceration rates for mental illness) to ground the discussion.

### Build Coalitions

- Connect mental health advocates with emergency medicine professionals. The American College of Emergency Physicians is a powerful ally on psychiatric boarding reform and can provide data and testimony for legislative efforts. Local ACEP chapters and emergency medicine residency programs are often eager to partner.
- Build relationships with local law enforcement who regularly encounter individuals in psychiatric crisis. Officers are often among the most vocal advocates for expanded crisis services and psychiatric beds. Police chiefs and sheriffs can be particularly influential with elected officials.
- Engage faith communities. Churches, synagogues, mosques, and other religious institutions often serve as informal support systems for families affected by mental illness and can be powerful advocacy voices. Many faith traditions have specific ministries or committees focused on mental health.
- Partner with homeless service providers and supportive housing organizations that regularly see the consequences of inadequate psychiatric care. Organizations like the National Alliance to End Homelessness and local Continuum of Care (CoC) agencies can provide data linking homelessness to psychiatric bed shortages.
- Connect with local chapters of the National Association of Social Workers (NASW), which represents the professionals who most often coordinate psychiatric discharge planning and witness the consequences of inadequate bed capacity.
- Engage local bar associations, particularly attorneys who practice in mental health courts, guardianship proceedings, or disability rights. Legal professionals can advocate for due process protections while supporting capacity expansion, bridging the tension between civil liberties and treatment access.

### Support Local Crisis Infrastructure

- Advocate for your community to establish a crisis stabilization unit (CSU) if one does not already exist. CSUs divert 60-70% of psychiatric ED presentations from hospitalization and provide a more appropriate environment for crisis intervention. Present the Tucson Crisis Response Center and San Antonio Restoration Center models to local officials as evidence-based examples.
- Support local implementation of the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline by advocating for crisis receiving capacity (physical locations where 988 callers in acute distress can be connected to in-person care). Without crisis receiving centers, 988 identifies crises it cannot resolve.
- Advocate for co-responder programs that pair mental health professionals with law enforcement officers responding to psychiatric crisis calls. Research from communities with co-responder programs shows 20-40% reductions in psychiatric ED transport and 30-50% reductions in use of force during mental health encounters.

## Political Actions

### Contact Elected Officials

**When to Contact**:

- During federal budget and appropriations season (typically February through September), when SAMHSA, CMS, and HRSA budgets are being set
- When mental health legislation is pending in committee (check NAMI's legislative tracker at <https://www.nami.org/advocacy/policy-priorities>)
- After high-profile incidents involving untreated mental illness (mass violence, police encounters, homelessness crises), when political attention is momentarily focused on mental health
- During state legislative sessions when behavioral health budgets, crisis service funding, and civil commitment laws are considered
- When your congressional representative serves on or is assigned to the Energy and Commerce Committee, Finance Committee, or Appropriations Subcommittee on Labor-HHS-Education, which have jurisdiction over mental health funding and the IMD exclusion

**What to Say -- Template Email**:

```text
Subject: Support IMD Exclusion Repeal and Psychiatric Bed Expansion

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

I am writing as a constituent from [City] to urge your support for
repealing the Medicaid Institutions for Mental Diseases (IMD)
exclusion, which bars Medicaid reimbursement for adults ages 21-64
in psychiatric facilities with more than 16 beds.

The United States has only 11.7 psychiatric beds per 100,000
population, a more than 95% decline from 1955 levels. In [State],
psychiatric patients wait an average of [X] hours in emergency
departments for inpatient placement. The IMD exclusion is the single
largest financial barrier to expanding psychiatric bed capacity.

[Optional personal paragraph: If you have a personal or family
experience with the psychiatric care system, share it briefly here.
Personal stories are the most persuasive element of constituent
communications.]

I ask you to co-sponsor legislation to repeal the IMD exclusion and
to support full funding for SAMHSA's crisis stabilization and
psychiatric workforce programs. Every major mental health
organization, the American College of Emergency Physicians, and the
National Association of State Mental Health Program Directors
support this reform.

Thank you for your service and consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your City, State]
```

**What to Say -- Phone Call Script**:

```text
"Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [City].
I'm calling about the psychiatric bed shortage and the Medicaid IMD
exclusion. Our state has only [X] psychiatric beds per 100,000
people, well below the recommended minimum. I'd like the
[Representative/Senator] to support repealing the IMD exclusion,
which blocks Medicaid from paying for psychiatric hospital care.
Can you please note my position? Thank you."
```

**State-Level Advocacy Script**:

```text
"Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [City/County].
I'm calling about psychiatric bed capacity in our state. [State]
has only [X] beds per 100,000 people, and patients in my community
wait an average of [X] hours in emergency departments for
psychiatric placement. I'd like [Representative] to support
increased state funding for crisis stabilization units and
psychiatric bed expansion. Thank you."
```

### Support Legislation

Current or proposed legislation to support:

- **IMD Exclusion Repeal Bills**: Multiple bills have been introduced in recent Congresses to repeal or modify the IMD exclusion. Check the current Congress for active bills through NAMI's legislative tracker or Congress.gov. The Helping Families in Mental Health Crisis Act and the Mental Health Reform Act have included IMD exclusion provisions in past sessions.
- **Mental Health Parity Enforcement**: Support legislation strengthening MHPAEA enforcement, including creating a private right of action for patients denied medically necessary psychiatric care. The Parity Enforcement Act and similar bills have been introduced in recent Congresses.
- **988 Implementation Funding**: The 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline needs a crisis care delivery system behind it. Support full appropriations for SAMHSA's crisis care programs, including crisis stabilization grants and mobile crisis team funding.
- **Psychiatric Workforce Bills**: Support Medicare GME expansion for psychiatric residency positions and loan repayment programs for psychiatric providers in underserved areas. The Resident Physician Shortage Reduction Act has included psychiatry-specific provisions.
- **State-Level Crisis Service Legislation**: Many states are considering legislation to fund crisis stabilization units, mobile crisis teams, and bed registries. Contact your state legislators to support these initiatives. NAMI state organizations track state-level bills and provide advocacy guidance.
- **Certificate of Need Reform**: In states with CON laws that restrict psychiatric bed construction, support legislative efforts to exempt psychiatric facilities from CON requirements or to streamline the approval process.

### Electoral Engagement

**Questions to Ask Candidates**:

1. "Do you support repealing the Medicaid IMD exclusion, which prevents Medicaid from paying for psychiatric hospital care for adults in facilities with more than 16 beds?"
2. "What is your plan to address emergency department psychiatric boarding, where patients in mental health crises wait an average of 8 hours -- and sometimes days -- for an inpatient bed?"
3. "Will you support federal investment in expanding psychiatric bed capacity, which has declined by more than 95% per capita since 1955?"
4. "How do you plan to address the psychiatric workforce shortage, which leaves many new psychiatric units unable to find staff?"
5. "Do you support full funding for 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline infrastructure, including crisis stabilization units and mobile crisis teams?"
6. "What is your position on mental health parity enforcement? Do you support creating a private right of action for patients denied medically necessary psychiatric care?"

**Voting Considerations**:

- Look for candidates who serve on health, appropriations, or judiciary committees where mental health legislation is considered
- Check candidates' positions on Medicaid expansion and mental health parity -- support for these programs is a strong indicator of willingness to support psychiatric care reform
- Evaluate whether candidates distinguish between inpatient and outpatient mental health needs; effective reform requires both
- Review candidates' voting records on health appropriations bills, particularly SAMHSA and HRSA line items
- Assess whether candidates have personal connections to mental health issues (family experience, professional background) that might make them effective champions

## Professional Actions

- **For Emergency Physicians and Nurses**: Document psychiatric boarding incidents systematically. Share aggregate data with hospital administration and state medical societies. Support ACEP's advocacy for ED boarding solutions. Testify before state legislatures on the impact of boarding on patient safety and ED operations. Consider co-authoring case studies or research articles documenting local boarding patterns.
- **For Psychiatrists and Psychiatric Nurses**: Advocate through the American Psychiatric Association and American Psychiatric Nurses Association for IMD exclusion repeal, parity enforcement, and workforce investment. Consider practicing in underserved areas through NHSC loan repayment. Mentor medical students and nursing students interested in psychiatric careers to help build the workforce pipeline.
- **For Hospital Administrators**: Invest in psychiatric emergency services and crisis stabilization partnerships. Share data on the financial and operational costs of psychiatric boarding with state and federal policymakers. Support state and federal advocacy efforts through your hospital association. Consider reopening closed psychiatric units if IMD waiver or repeal creates a viable financial model.
- **For Attorneys and Legal Professionals**: Pursue parity enforcement cases on behalf of patients denied medically necessary inpatient psychiatric care. Support legal aid organizations that represent patients in civil commitment proceedings. File amicus briefs in key parity and commitment cases.
- **For Educators and Researchers**: Study and publish on the effects of bed shortages, boarding, and IMD exclusion. Provide data and expert testimony to legislative committees considering reform. Include inpatient care system analysis in medical school, nursing school, and social work curricula.
- **For Social Workers**: Document discharge planning failures and gaps in the care continuum. Advocate for supported housing and outpatient services that complement inpatient capacity. Report parity violations when insurers deny medically necessary inpatient care for your clients.
- **For Law Enforcement**: Support CIT training in your department. Document the time officers spend on mental health crisis calls and the outcomes of those calls (ED transport, arrest, on-scene resolution). Share data with local elected officials and advocate for crisis receiving centers that allow officers to return to patrol quickly.
- **For Clergy and Faith Leaders**: Offer mental health education in your congregation. Partner with NAMI for faith-based mental health awareness programs. Provide testimony to local and state officials about the impact of the mental health system's failures on families in your community. Consider hosting Mental Health First Aid training at your place of worship.

## Advocacy Toolkit

### Key Talking Points

When discussing inpatient psychiatric care reform with others, emphasize:

1. **The numbers are stark**: The U.S. has only 11.7 psychiatric beds per 100,000 population, a 95%+ per-capita decline since 1955. Comparable nations average 30-70 beds per 100,000. We have fewer psychiatric beds per capita than at any point since the 1850s.
2. **Jails are the new asylums**: 44% of jail inmates and 37% of state prisoners have a diagnosed mental health condition. The three largest psychiatric facilities in the country are the Los Angeles County Jail, Cook County Jail, and Rikers Island.
3. **A single policy is at the center**: The Medicaid IMD exclusion, enacted in 1965, bars federal Medicaid reimbursement for psychiatric inpatient care in facilities with more than 16 beds. No other medical condition has a comparable exclusion. Repealing it is the single most impactful reform.
4. **Emergency departments are overwhelmed**: Psychiatric patients wait an average of 8 hours for inpatient placement -- far longer than any other patient population. This harms psychiatric patients and delays care for all other ED patients.
5. **It costs more to do nothing**: Untreated serious mental illness costs the U.S. economy over $280 billion annually through emergency department visits, incarceration, homelessness services, and lost productivity (NAMI, 2023).
6. **Children are in crisis**: Pediatric psychiatric ED visits increased 45% from 2019 to 2022. Children routinely board in emergency departments for days. The U.S. Surgeon General declared youth mental health a national crisis.
7. **The workforce is the bottleneck**: 60% of U.S. counties have no psychiatrist. Even if we built beds tomorrow, we cannot staff them without investing in the psychiatric workforce pipeline.

### Common Misconceptions to Counter

| Misconception | Reality | Source |
|---------------|---------|--------|
| "We closed the asylums because they were inhumane; we should not go back." | Modern inpatient care bears no resemblance to mid-century state hospitals. The goal is modern, accredited, short-stay acute care facilities -- not long-term warehousing. | Treatment Advocacy Center, 2020 |
| "Community services can replace inpatient beds." | Community services are essential but cannot substitute for inpatient care when a patient is acutely psychotic, suicidal, or gravely disabled. Both are needed simultaneously. | SAMHSA National Guidelines, 2020 |
| "Expanding beds will lead to mass involuntary commitment." | The vast majority of psychiatric admissions (approximately 70%) are voluntary. Expanding beds primarily reduces wait times for patients who want treatment. | HCUP/AHRQ, 2023 |
| "The IMD exclusion protects against institutionalization." | The exclusion has not prevented institutionalization; it has redirected it to jails, prisons, and emergency departments -- settings far less therapeutic than psychiatric hospitals. | Bureau of Justice Statistics, 2023 |
| "We cannot afford to expand psychiatric beds." | The U.S. spends over $280 billion annually on the downstream consequences of untreated serious mental illness. Investing in psychiatric beds reduces these costs. | NAMI, 2023 |
| "People with mental illness are dangerous." | People with mental illness are 10 times more likely to be victims of violence than perpetrators. The vast majority of violence is committed by people without mental illness. | APA, 2022 |
| "The private market will solve the bed shortage." | For-profit chains concentrate in affluent areas and selectively admit commercially insured patients. Without Medicaid reimbursement (blocked by the IMD exclusion), the market cannot serve the populations most in need. | TAC, 2023 |
