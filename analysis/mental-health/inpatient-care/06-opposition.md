# Inpatient Psychiatric Care: Opposition to Reform

Proposals to expand inpatient psychiatric capacity and reform the IMD exclusion face resistance from several directions: fiscal conservatives concerned about federal spending, disability rights advocates who view institutional expansion as a step backward, libertarians who oppose involuntary treatment, and entrenched financial interests that benefit from the current system's constraints.

---

## Who Opposes Reform

### Organized Opposition

| Actor | Interest | Resources | Tactics |
|-------|----------|-----------|---------|
| Federal budget hawks (OMB, CBO, fiscal conservatives in Congress) | Preventing $10-15 billion/year in new federal Medicaid spending from IMD exclusion repeal | Control of budget scoring and appropriations process | Classify IMD repeal as "budget-busting"; insist on offsets; delay legislation in committee |
| Disability rights organizations (Bazelon Center, ADAPT, DREDF, NDRN) | Preventing a return to institutional warehousing; protecting community integration under *Olmstead* | Legal expertise; federal court litigation capacity; alliance with broader disability community | Litigation to enforce community integration; advocacy against bed expansion without community investment; congressional testimony |
| Managed care organizations and commercial insurers (UnitedHealth, Anthem, Cigna, Centene) | Maintaining low utilization and authorization rates for inpatient psychiatric care | $150+ billion lobbying apparatus across health insurance industry; 70%+ of insured Americans enrolled in managed care | Lobby against mandates to extend length of stay; resist reimbursement rate increases; support utilization review restrictions; fund research questioning cost-effectiveness of inpatient care |
| Psychiatric survivor/consumer movement (National Coalition for Mental Health Recovery, MindFreedom International) | Opposing expansion of involuntary treatment; protecting patient autonomy | Grassroots networks; lived experience authority; alliance with disability rights and civil liberties organizations | Public testimony; coalition with ACLU and Bazelon Center; challenge commitment law expansions in court; media advocacy emphasizing coercion narratives |
| State budget offices (paradoxically) | Avoiding increased state Medicaid match obligations that could accompany IMD repeal | Control of state budget processes; influence over governor's legislative agenda | Resist reforms that require new state spending even if federal match is available; argue that community alternatives are more cost-effective |
| Pharmaceutical industry (indirect opposition) | Maintaining medication-first treatment paradigm that minimizes inpatient stays | $350+ million annual lobbying; dominant presence in psychiatric research funding | Fund research emphasizing outpatient medication management; lobby for expanded formulary access rather than facility expansion; not actively opposing reform but not supporting it either |

### Ideological Opposition

- **Libertarian**: Opposes involuntary commitment as a fundamental violation of individual liberty. Views forced treatment as government overreach, regardless of clinical justification. Favors voluntary treatment and private market solutions. The Cato Institute has published analyses questioning whether government-funded bed expansion would be efficient and arguing that market-based approaches -- removing CON laws, deregulating facility licensing -- would produce better results. The libertarian critique extends to the 16-bed threshold itself, arguing that the IMD exclusion created a regulatory distortion that would not exist in a free market.
- **Disability rights**: Views institutional expansion as inconsistent with the Americans with Disabilities Act and the Supreme Court's *Olmstead* decision requiring community integration. Argues that resources should go to supported housing, assertive community treatment, and peer support rather than inpatient beds. The disability rights critique draws on decades of documented institutional abuse and frames any expansion of institutional capacity as a threat to hard-won civil rights. Organizations like ADAPT (formerly the American Disabled for Attendant Programs Today) have staged protests against institutional expansion, linking it to broader struggles against segregation of people with disabilities.
- **Fiscal conservative**: Opposes the estimated $10-15 billion annual cost of repealing the IMD exclusion without identified offsets. Questions whether bed expansion is cost-effective compared to community alternatives. The Heritage Foundation has published analyses suggesting that block-granting Medicaid mental health funding to states -- rather than expanding the federal entitlement through IMD repeal -- would provide more flexibility and accountability.
- **Anti-coercion/civil liberties**: Views expansion of involuntary commitment criteria as a threat to civil liberties, particularly for marginalized populations. Notes racial disparities in involuntary commitment (Black individuals are committed at 2.4 times the rate of white individuals, according to Psychiatric Services, 2022). The ACLU has not taken a formal position against all inpatient expansion but has actively opposed legislation that would broaden involuntary commitment criteria or weaken due process protections, publishing state-level legislative scorecards that count commitment law expansion as a negative indicator.
- **Progressive anti-carceral**: Some progressive advocates view psychiatric institutionalization as analogous to incarceration and oppose both. This perspective, drawing on the work of scholars like Michel Foucault and Erving Goffman, frames involuntary hospitalization as a form of social control rather than medical treatment. While this view is not mainstream in policy circles, it influences grassroots activism and can complicate coalition-building for reform.

## Opposition Arguments and Rebuttals

### Argument 1: IMD Exclusion Repeal Would Be Fiscally Irresponsible

**Claim**: Repealing the IMD exclusion would cost $10-15 billion annually in new federal Medicaid spending with no guaranteed improvement in outcomes, creating an open-ended fiscal liability at a time of record federal deficits.

**Evidence Cited**: CBO scoring estimates of IMD exclusion repeal; historical federal spending growth in Medicaid programs that lacked adequate cost controls; the observation that IMD repeal would represent one of the largest single Medicaid expansions since the ACA.

**Reality**: While the direct cost of IMD repeal is significant, it must be weighed against the costs the current system already imposes. Avoidable psychiatric ED visits, incarceration of people with mental illness, homelessness services, and lost productivity associated with untreated serious mental illness cost an estimated $280 billion annually (NAMI, 2023). A portion of IMD repeal costs would be offset by reduced ED boarding costs (estimated at $2,264 per boarding episode, with hundreds of thousands of episodes annually), reduced jail/prison costs, and reduced Medicaid spending on repeat ED visits. The Medicaid and CHIP Payment and Access Commission (MACPAC) has noted that the current system generates "hidden costs" distributed across emergency, criminal justice, and social service budgets that do not appear in CBO's scoring of IMD repeal.

**Rebuttal**: The federal government already pays for psychiatric crises through the most expensive and least effective channels (emergency departments under EMTALA, Medicaid-funded general hospital admissions, federal disability programs). Investing upstream in adequate inpatient capacity is likely to reduce total system costs, not increase them. Phased implementation with spending caps could address legitimate fiscal concerns. Congress has precedent for phased Medicaid expansions (the ACA's Medicaid expansion was implemented incrementally with enhanced federal match rates).

### Argument 2: Expanding Beds Would Reverse Deinstitutionalization Progress

**Claim**: Building more psychiatric beds risks recreating the abusive institutional system that the disability rights movement spent decades dismantling. Any expansion of institutional capacity violates the *Olmstead* principle that people with disabilities should receive services in the most integrated setting appropriate.

**Evidence Cited**: Historical abuses in state psychiatric hospitals documented by Dorothea Dix, Albert Deutsch, and subsequent investigations; *Olmstead v. L.C.*, 527 U.S. 581 (1999); Department of Justice investigations finding inadequate conditions in state psychiatric facilities in Mississippi, Georgia, Virginia, and other states; the eugenics-era forced sterilization of institutionalized patients.

**Reality**: The current system produces its own set of abuses: patients boarding for days in emergency departments without treatment, people with severe mental illness incarcerated in jails and prisons, and individuals dying on the streets from untreated illness. The choice is not between institutional care and community care; both are needed. *Olmstead* itself recognizes that institutional placement is appropriate when "the individual's treatment professionals determine that such placement is appropriate" and "the affected persons do not oppose such treatment." The 2024 system lacks adequate capacity at every level, inpatient and community alike.

**Rebuttal**: Modern inpatient psychiatric care, with accreditation standards, patient rights protections, trauma-informed care models, and quality reporting, is fundamentally different from the asylum system of the 1950s. Advocates for expansion can and do support robust community services simultaneously. The false dichotomy between beds and community services has paralyzed reform for decades; both must be expanded. The DOJ's own *Olmstead* enforcement guidance recognizes that some individuals require institutional care and that the standard is "most integrated setting appropriate," not "no institutional setting ever."

### Argument 3: Community Alternatives Are More Effective and Humane

**Claim**: Evidence-based community programs like Assertive Community Treatment (ACT), crisis stabilization units, supported housing, and peer support services produce better outcomes at lower cost than inpatient hospitalization. Resources should be directed to these alternatives rather than beds.

**Evidence Cited**: Research on ACT teams showing reduced hospitalization (Cochrane Review, 2010); crisis stabilization units diverting 60-70% of ED presentations from hospitalization (SAMHSA, 2023); supported housing reducing homelessness among people with SMI (HUD studies); Trieste, Italy's community-based model as an alternative to institutional care.

**Reality**: Community alternatives are effective for many individuals and should be expanded. However, they cannot replace inpatient care for patients who are acutely psychotic, actively suicidal, or gravely disabled. Crisis stabilization units typically have maximum stays of 23 hours and cannot manage patients requiring longer stabilization. ACT teams reduce but do not eliminate hospitalization. The evidence consistently shows that a functioning mental health system requires both robust community services and adequate inpatient capacity. The Trieste model, often cited by community-care advocates, operates with approximately 10 beds per 100,000 in dedicated psychiatric units plus extensive 24/7 community infrastructure -- still nearly matching the U.S. bed rate while providing vastly more community support.

**Rebuttal**: The community vs. institutional framing is a false choice. International models (Australia, United Kingdom, Canada, Germany) demonstrate that adequate inpatient capacity and strong community services are complementary, not competing priorities. The U.S. has the worst of both worlds: insufficient community services and insufficient beds. Every nation with a functioning mental health system maintains substantially more inpatient capacity than the United States while also investing heavily in community services.

### Argument 4: Expanding Involuntary Commitment Would Violate Civil Rights

**Claim**: Proposals to broaden involuntary commitment criteria or extend hold durations threaten fundamental constitutional rights, particularly for racial minorities and low-income individuals who are disproportionately subject to involuntary commitment.

**Evidence Cited**: Racial disparities in involuntary commitment rates (Black individuals committed at 2.4 times the rate of white individuals); historical misuse of psychiatry to control dissent (Soviet psychiatric abuse, Drapetomania diagnosis); constitutional protections under the 14th Amendment and *O'Connor v. Donaldson*; documented disparities in quality of care at facilities serving predominantly Medicaid populations.

**Reality**: Racial and socioeconomic disparities in involuntary commitment are real and must be addressed. However, the alternative to involuntary commitment for individuals who are gravely disabled or dangerous is often not freedom but rather homelessness, incarceration, or death. An estimated 10% of all law enforcement-related fatalities involve people with untreated mental illness (Treatment Advocacy Center, 2022). The civil rights of people with serious mental illness must include the right to receive treatment when illness impairs their capacity to seek it voluntarily. The current system's failure to provide adequate voluntary treatment options is itself a civil rights failure.

**Rebuttal**: Reform proposals can and should include strong due process protections, independent oversight, racial disparity monitoring, and regular judicial review. The goal is not to expand coercive power but to ensure that people in psychiatric crisis receive appropriate care rather than being jailed, left to deteriorate, or killed in encounters with law enforcement. Specific safeguards should include mandatory data collection on commitment by race, ethnicity, and socioeconomic status; independent patient advocates; time-limited holds with mandatory judicial review; and automatic legal representation.

### Argument 5: The Private Market Will Solve the Shortage

**Claim**: If government gets out of the way by removing CON requirements and reducing regulation, private psychiatric hospital companies will build beds where demand exists. Market forces are more efficient than government planning at allocating resources.

**Evidence Cited**: Growth of for-profit psychiatric hospital chains (Acadia Healthcare, Universal Health Services); responsiveness of private sector to market signals; evidence that CON laws constrain bed supply.

**Reality**: For-profit psychiatric hospitals have expanded in some areas, but they tend to concentrate in affluent suburban markets, avoid Medicaid-heavy populations, and select patients with better insurance and less acute conditions. The most underserved populations, Medicaid recipients, uninsured individuals, and patients with the most severe illness, are not profitable to serve. Market forces alone cannot solve a shortage driven primarily by a federal reimbursement policy (the IMD exclusion) that removes the largest payer from the market. Furthermore, investigative reporting has documented quality and billing concerns at for-profit psychiatric facilities, including the DOJ's $122 million False Claims Act settlement with UHS in 2020.

**Rebuttal**: Removing regulatory barriers like CON laws is a useful complement to reform, but it is not sufficient. The core problem is financial, not regulatory. Without Medicaid reimbursement for psychiatric facilities (currently blocked by the IMD exclusion), no amount of market deregulation will produce adequate bed capacity for the populations most in need. The private market has had decades to solve this problem within the existing regulatory framework and has not done so, because the fundamental economics of serving Medicaid and uninsured psychiatric patients are unfavorable without federal subsidy.

## Historical Patterns of Opposition

The opposition to inpatient psychiatric care expansion has followed a consistent pattern since the 1960s:

1. **1960s-1970s**: Opposition to institutional care focused on documented abuses and the civil rights of patients. This was legitimate and necessary, producing important legal protections including *O'Connor v. Donaldson* and *Addington v. Texas*.
2. **1980s-1990s**: Fiscal opposition became dominant as states sought to close hospitals to save money, framing the closures as "community integration" even when community alternatives were not available. The Reagan administration's conversion of community mental health funding to block grants provided the fiscal framework for this retrenchment.
3. **2000s-present**: The disability rights and fiscal arguments have merged, with advocacy organizations citing *Olmstead* while budget offices cite cost. The result is a coalition of convenience that effectively blocks both inpatient expansion and adequate community investment. The managed care industry has become a third force, using utilization review to limit inpatient stays regardless of bed availability.

### The Opposition Coalition's Internal Contradictions

The current opposition coalition contains significant internal contradictions that reformers can exploit:

- **Disability rights organizations** and **fiscal conservatives** have opposite views on community service funding -- disability rights groups want more community investment, while fiscal conservatives want less government spending overall. They agree only on opposing bed expansion.
- **Managed care organizations** and **consumer advocates** have opposite views on patient autonomy in treatment decisions -- MCOs restrict access through utilization review, while consumer advocates want patients to have more treatment choices.
- **State budget offices** and **the federal government** are engaged in a cost-shifting game where each wants the other to pay for psychiatric care. This conflict could be leveraged by proposing a federal-state cost-sharing arrangement that neither side loves but both can accept.

## Counter-Strategies

### Political Strategies

- **Build a bipartisan coalition**: Mental health has unusual bipartisan appeal. Frame bed expansion as public safety (conservative appeal), social justice (liberal appeal), and cost-effectiveness (centrist appeal). The 988 launch and post-COVID mental health awareness create a political window. Senators Chris Murphy (D-CT) and Bill Cassidy (R-LA), who co-authored the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act, represent the type of cross-party partnership needed.
- **Link to criminal justice reform**: Connect psychiatric bed expansion to reducing jail populations, which appeals to both progressive reformers and fiscal conservatives concerned about incarceration costs. Data showing that Los Angeles County Jail, Cook County Jail, and Rikers Island are the nation's three largest psychiatric facilities is a powerful bipartisan message.
- **Leverage the pediatric crisis**: The children's mental health emergency has generated broad public sympathy. Using pediatric boarding as the leading narrative can build support that extends to the broader system. Stories of 8-year-olds boarding in emergency departments for days are difficult for any political faction to dismiss.
- **Engage veterans' organizations**: The VA operates approximately 2,500 psychiatric beds and faces its own capacity challenges. Veterans' service organizations (VFW, American Legion, DAV) are powerful lobbying forces that can support psychiatric bed expansion as part of a broader veterans' care agenda.

### Communication Strategies

- **Replace "institutional expansion" with "crisis care access"**: Framing matters. "Building more psychiatric institutions" evokes the asylum era; "ensuring crisis care access for people in psychiatric emergencies" focuses on patient needs.
- **Lead with stories**: Individual stories of patients boarding in emergency departments for days, families unable to get care for loved ones, and law enforcement officers describing mental health calls are more persuasive than statistics.
- **Acknowledge the history**: Proactively acknowledge the abuses of the asylum era and explain how modern reforms include safeguards (accreditation, patient rights, community integration requirements).
- **Use the cost comparison**: Frame the $10-15 billion annual cost of IMD repeal against the $280 billion annual cost of the current system's failures. "We are already paying for this crisis -- we are just paying through emergency rooms, jails, and homeless shelters instead of hospitals."
- **Highlight international comparisons**: The fact that the U.S. has fewer psychiatric beds per capita than virtually every developed nation (and many developing nations) is a powerful counter to arguments that the current bed count is adequate.

### Policy Design Strategies

- **Pair bed expansion with community investment**: Design legislation that funds both inpatient capacity and community services (ACT teams, supported housing, crisis stabilization), making it harder for opponents to argue that one comes at the expense of the other. The Comprehensive Mental Health and Crisis Care Act model, which allocates funds to both, provides a template.
- **Include strong civil rights protections**: Build in racial disparity monitoring, independent oversight, maximum hold durations with judicial review, and patient advocate positions to address disability rights and civil liberties concerns. Every new psychiatric facility funded with federal dollars should be required to have an independent patient advocate.
- **Phase IMD exclusion repeal**: Start with expanded waivers (increasing from 30-day to 60-day stays) before moving to full repeal, allowing CBO to score manageable fiscal impacts and building evidence of cost-effectiveness. Use the phased approach to demonstrate that expanded coverage reduces total system costs, generating data that supports full repeal.
- **Require quality as a condition of funding**: Address opposition concerns about institutional abuse by conditioning all federal funding on Joint Commission accreditation (or equivalent), public quality reporting, independent oversight, and compliance with evidence-based standards for trauma-informed care, restraint reduction, and discharge planning.
