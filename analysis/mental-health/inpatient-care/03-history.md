# Inpatient Psychiatric Care: History

## Historical Development

The history of inpatient psychiatric care in the United States is a story of pendulum swings between institutional and community-based approaches, shaped by evolving understandings of mental illness, changing political ideologies, the development of psychiatric medications, and recurring failures of implementation. Understanding this history is essential because many of today's problems, including the bed shortage, the IMD exclusion, and the criminalization of mental illness, are the direct consequences of policy decisions made decades ago.

---

### The Colonial and Early Republic Period (1600s-1840s): Neglect and Local Custody

- **Colonial treatment**: In colonial America, individuals with mental illness were generally kept at home by families or, when families could not cope, placed in almshouses (poorhouses) or jails. There was no systematic public provision for psychiatric care. Some communities auctioned off the "care" of individuals with mental illness to the lowest bidder, a practice that invited neglect and abuse.
- **Pennsylvania Hospital (1752)**: The Pennsylvania Hospital in Philadelphia, established by Benjamin Franklin and Dr. Thomas Bond, was the first general hospital in America to admit psychiatric patients. It maintained a dedicated basement ward for "lunatics" -- a term then used without pejorative intent. Conditions were rudimentary, with treatment consisting primarily of confinement and occasionally bleeding, purging, and cold-water immersion.
- **The first state hospital**: The Public Hospital of Williamsburg, Virginia, established in 1773, was the first purpose-built psychiatric hospital in America. It operated initially on a custodial model, housing patients in cells with minimal treatment. Under Dr. John Minson Galt II, who served as superintendent from 1841 to 1862, the hospital became one of the first in the nation to adopt the "moral treatment" philosophy.
- **Moral treatment origins**: The moral treatment movement, influenced by the work of Philippe Pinel in France and the Tuke family at the York Retreat in England, held that mental illness could be treated through a structured environment emphasizing kindness, routine, occupation, and respect for the patient's dignity. This philosophy provided the intellectual foundation for the construction of state psychiatric hospitals throughout the 19th century.

---

### The Asylum Era (1841-1945): Rise of Institutional Care

- **Dorothea Dix's crusade**: Beginning in 1841, Dorothea Dix launched a national campaign to improve treatment of the mentally ill, successfully lobbying more than 30 states and the federal government to establish or expand state psychiatric hospitals. Dix documented deplorable conditions in almshouses and jails across the country, presenting detailed memorials to state legislatures that catalogued individual cases of abuse and neglect. By 1880, the U.S. had 140 public psychiatric hospitals, most built with state funding in response to Dix's advocacy.
- **The Kirkbride plan**: Dr. Thomas Story Kirkbride, superintendent of the Pennsylvania Hospital for the Insane, developed an influential architectural model for state hospitals. The "Kirkbride plan" specified a central administrative building with radiating wings, natural light, ventilation, and pastoral grounds. Between 1845 and 1900, approximately 75 Kirkbride-plan hospitals were constructed across the country. Many of these buildings, now crumbling or repurposed, remain landmarks of the era.
- **Overcrowding and decline**: By the late 1800s, the moral treatment model had given way to custodial warehousing. State hospitals became severely overcrowded, understaffed, and underfunded. Average institutional populations grew from hundreds to thousands per facility. Conditions deteriorated as states treated hospitals as repositories for the unwanted rather than therapeutic institutions. Immigrants, the elderly, individuals with epilepsy and intellectual disabilities, and "social undesirables" were committed alongside those with genuine psychiatric illness, swelling institutional populations beyond any hope of individualized treatment.
- **Eugenics and forced sterilization**: In the early 20th century, the eugenics movement influenced psychiatric practice. State hospitals became sites of forced sterilization programs targeting institutionalized patients. The Supreme Court's decision in *Buck v. Bell*, 274 U.S. 200 (1927), upheld compulsory sterilization of the "mentally defective," with Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes writing that "three generations of imbeciles are enough." An estimated 60,000-70,000 Americans were forcibly sterilized in state institutions, a practice that continued in some states into the 1970s.
- **Peak institutionalization**: State psychiatric hospital populations peaked at approximately 559,000 patients in 1955, representing roughly 340 per 100,000 population. The average length of stay was measured in years or decades, and many patients spent their entire adult lives institutionalized. By this point, state hospitals consumed the majority of state mental health budgets and functioned less as treatment centers than as warehouses for individuals whom society wished to remove from view.

---

### The Deinstitutionalization Era (1945-1980): Emptying the Hospitals

- **Post-war awakening**: World War II exposed millions of Americans to psychiatric illness through the experiences of combat veterans, and approximately 3,000 conscientious objectors who served as attendants in state hospitals during the war documented and publicized the horrific conditions they witnessed. These experiences laid the groundwork for post-war reform.
- **Expose journalism**: A series of journalistic exposes in the 1940s and 1950s, including Albert Deutsch's *The Shame of the States* (1948) and the Life magazine photo essay "Bedlam 1946" by photographer Jerry Cooke, revealed horrific conditions in state hospitals, generating public outrage and political pressure for reform. Deutsch compared conditions at Philadelphia State Hospital to Nazi concentration camps, a comparison that, while provocative, reflected the genuine severity of institutional neglect.
- **National Institute of Mental Health (1949)**: The creation of NIMH by Congress in 1949 established a federal research and policy apparatus for mental health for the first time. NIMH would become a leading advocate for community-based care and a driver of deinstitutionalization policy.
- **Chlorpromazine revolution**: The introduction of chlorpromazine (Thorazine) in 1954 provided the first effective pharmacological treatment for psychosis, making it conceivable to manage severe mental illness outside of hospital settings. This pharmaceutical breakthrough provided the clinical rationale for deinstitutionalization. Within a decade of chlorpromazine's introduction, state hospital populations had begun to decline, even before the major policy changes that would accelerate the process.
- **Joint Commission on Mental Illness and Health (1961)**: Congress established this commission in 1955; its 1961 report, *Action for Mental Health*, recommended a shift from large state hospitals to community-based care, setting the intellectual framework for Kennedy-era reforms.
- **Community Mental Health Act (1963)**: President John F. Kennedy signed the Community Mental Health Centers Construction Act, which authorized federal funding for community mental health centers intended to replace state hospitals. Kennedy envisioned a nationwide network of 2,000 centers; fewer than 800 were ever built, and most never provided the full range of services originally intended. Kennedy's assassination in 1963 and the escalation of the Vietnam War shifted federal attention and resources away from mental health reform.
- **Medicaid and the IMD exclusion (1965)**: When Congress created Medicaid in 1965 as part of the Social Security Amendments, it included the Institutions for Mental Diseases (IMD) exclusion, barring federal Medicaid payments for care of adults ages 21-64 in facilities with more than 16 beds that primarily treat mental illness. The exclusion was intended to prevent states from simply shifting the cost of their existing state hospital populations to the new federal program. However, it created a powerful financial incentive for states to close state hospital beds, since they received no federal matching funds for those patients. The 16-bed threshold was adopted without clinical justification; it was a fiscal policy decision that has had enduring clinical consequences.
- **Rapid depopulation**: State hospital populations plummeted from 559,000 in 1955 to approximately 215,000 by 1974 and continued falling. Between 1955 and 1980, the state hospital census declined by more than 60%. Thousands of patients were discharged to communities that had no services to receive them.
- **O'Connor v. Donaldson (1975)**: The Supreme Court ruled that a state cannot constitutionally confine a non-dangerous individual who is capable of surviving safely in freedom, either alone or with the help of willing and responsible family members or friends. This decision, along with other court rulings expanding patients' rights, further accelerated deinstitutionalization.
- **Addington v. Texas (1979)**: The Supreme Court established that civil commitment proceedings require proof by "clear and convincing evidence," a higher standard than the preponderance standard used in most civil cases, providing an additional procedural protection against inappropriate involuntary hospitalization.

---

### The Failed Transition (1980-2000): Community Care That Never Arrived

- **Reagan-era retrenchment**: In 1981, President Reagan signed the Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act, which converted federal community mental health center funding into block grants with reduced funding levels. This effectively ended the federal commitment to building community mental health centers that Kennedy had envisioned. The Mental Health Systems Act, signed by President Carter in 1980 as an ambitious expansion of community mental health services, was repealed in all but name.
- **Homelessness crisis**: The combined effect of deinstitutionalization without adequate community alternatives and the loss of federal mental health funding produced a visible homelessness crisis in the 1980s. Estimates suggested that one-third or more of the homeless population had a serious mental illness. Cities from New York to Los Angeles struggled with large populations of visibly mentally ill individuals living on the streets, a phenomenon that became a defining social issue of the decade.
- **Trans-institutionalization to jails**: As hospital beds disappeared, jails and prisons became the de facto mental health system. By 1999, the Bureau of Justice Statistics reported that 16% of state prison inmates and 16% of jail inmates had a mental health condition, and advocacy groups noted that the Los Angeles County Jail, Cook County Jail, and Rikers Island had become the three largest psychiatric facilities in the nation. This pattern of "trans-institutionalization" -- the transfer of individuals from psychiatric hospitals to correctional facilities -- represented one of the most consequential and least intended outcomes of deinstitutionalization.
- **Olmstead v. L.C. (1999)**: The Supreme Court held that unjustified institutionalization of people with mental disabilities is a form of discrimination under the Americans with Disabilities Act. The decision required states to provide community-based services for individuals who could be appropriately served in community settings. While *Olmstead* was a landmark for disability rights, it was sometimes invoked to justify further closure of institutional beds without commensurate investment in community alternatives.
- **Continued bed closures**: State hospital beds continued to decline throughout this period, falling to approximately 57,000 by 2000 (SAMHSA). Community services grew, but never at a pace or scale sufficient to replace institutional capacity. The gap between the promise of community care and its reality widened steadily.

---

### The Modern Crisis (2000-Present): Capacity Collapse and Emergency System Strain

- **Accelerated bed losses**: Between 2000 and 2024, the nation lost an additional 20,000+ psychiatric beds. Many general hospitals closed or downsized psychiatric units due to low Medicaid reimbursement, workforce shortages, and the IMD exclusion's financial disincentives. The closure of state psychiatric hospitals accelerated: states including Illinois, Minnesota, Connecticut, and Oregon closed major facilities or dramatically reduced census during this period.
- **Emergency department boarding emerges**: By the mid-2000s, psychiatric boarding in emergency departments had become a widely recognized crisis. The American College of Emergency Physicians began tracking and publicizing the problem, with average boarding times steadily increasing. ACEP's 2008 Psychiatric Emergency Survey found that 79% of emergency physicians reported boarding psychiatric patients for extended periods as a regular occurrence.
- **Parity legislation**: The Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act of 2008 required insurance plans to cover mental health services on par with medical/surgical services. While an important step, it did not address the supply-side shortage of beds and providers. Enforcement of parity requirements for inpatient psychiatric care has been uneven, with many insurers continuing to apply more restrictive utilization review criteria to psychiatric admissions than to medical/surgical admissions.
- **21st Century Cures Act (2016)**: This legislation included provisions allowing states to apply for Medicaid Section 1115 waivers to receive Medicaid reimbursement for short-term stays (up to 30 days) in IMDs, initially for substance use disorders and later expanded to serious mental illness in 2018. While an incremental step, the waivers are limited and require matching community service investments.
- **COVID-19 pandemic (2020-2024)**: The pandemic produced a surge in psychiatric emergencies, particularly among adolescents, while simultaneously reducing available inpatient capacity due to infection control measures, staff illness, and burnout. Psychiatric ED visits increased 31% among adults and 45% among adolescents from 2019 to 2022. The Surgeon General issued an advisory on youth mental health in 2021, and three major pediatric organizations declared a national emergency in children's mental health.
- **988 launch (2022)**: The implementation of the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline created an accessible entry point to crisis care, but exposed the lack of crisis receiving and inpatient capacity to serve callers in acute distress. The 988 system generated over 5.5 million contacts in its first full year, with call volume continuing to grow.

## Lessons from History

### Pattern 1: Deinstitutionalization Without Community Investment

The central lesson of the past 70 years is that closing institutional beds without building adequate community alternatives produces catastrophic results. The community mental health center system envisioned by Kennedy was never fully funded or built, and the 1981 block grant conversion further weakened it. The pattern has repeated at the state level: states close hospital beds to reduce costs, promise to redirect savings to community services, and then redirect the savings elsewhere. The result is a system with neither sufficient institutional nor community capacity. This pattern has been documented in state after state, from the closure of Illinois's Elgin Mental Health Center wards to Oregon's closure of the Dammasch State Hospital without adequate replacement services.

### Pattern 2: Financial Incentives Drive System Design

The IMD exclusion demonstrates how a single financial policy can reshape an entire system of care. By making it financially advantageous for states to close large psychiatric facilities (which received no federal Medicaid match) and shift patients to general hospital EDs (which do receive Medicaid reimbursement under EMTALA), the exclusion created the very boarding crisis that plagues the system today. Financial incentive structures, not clinical evidence or patient needs, have been the dominant force shaping inpatient psychiatric capacity. The lesson is that reforming the care system requires reforming the financing system first.

### Pattern 3: Pendulum Swings Between Extremes

The history of psychiatric care oscillates between institutional overreliance and institutional avoidance. Neither extreme has served patients well. The asylum era produced warehousing and abuse; the deinstitutionalization era produced homelessness and incarceration. A sustainable system requires a middle path that includes adequate inpatient capacity alongside robust community services, but achieving this balance has proven politically and financially difficult. The challenge is compounded by the fact that each era's excesses provide rhetorical ammunition for the opposing camp: advocates for community care point to asylum abuses; advocates for institutional care point to homelessness and incarceration.

### Pattern 4: Crisis Drives Reform, But Reform Is Incomplete

Major policy changes, from the Community Mental Health Act to the 21st Century Cures Act, have consistently been prompted by visible crises (institutional abuse, homelessness, ED boarding). However, each reform has addressed only part of the problem, often creating new unintended consequences. Comprehensive reform that addresses the entire continuum of care has never been enacted. The current crisis -- the combination of extreme bed shortages, epidemic boarding, workforce collapse, and the post-pandemic mental health surge -- may present the most compelling case for comprehensive reform since the 1960s, but history counsels caution about whether political will can be sustained long enough to complete the work.

### Pattern 5: Vulnerable Populations Bear the Consequences

At every stage of this history, the consequences of policy failures have fallen most heavily on those least able to advocate for themselves: people with severe and persistent mental illness, individuals experiencing homelessness, incarcerated populations, children, and communities of color. The political economy of psychiatric care ensures that the populations most in need of inpatient services are also the populations with the least political power to demand them.
