# Inpatient Psychiatric Care: Current State

## Psychiatric Bed Capacity

The United States has experienced a dramatic and sustained decline in psychiatric inpatient bed capacity over the past seven decades. As of 2024, the nation has approximately 37,679 total psychiatric beds across all facility types, yielding a rate of roughly 11.7 beds per 100,000 population (Treatment Advocacy Center, 2024). This figure represents a more than 95% per-capita decline from the 1955 peak of approximately 340 beds per 100,000. To put this in perspective, the current per-capita bed rate is lower than at any point since the 1850s, before Dorothea Dix's advocacy campaign led to the construction of state psychiatric hospitals across the country.

The bed shortage is not merely a statistical abstraction. It means that on any given day, thousands of Americans experiencing acute psychiatric emergencies -- psychotic breaks, suicidal crises, manic episodes -- have no psychiatric bed available to them within a reasonable distance or timeframe. It means emergency departments have become de facto psychiatric holding facilities. And it means that jails and prisons have become, by default, the largest providers of psychiatric care in the country.

State and county psychiatric hospitals remain the largest single category of psychiatric beds, but their share has been declining for decades as states close facilities or reduce census. Private psychiatric hospitals, operated primarily by for-profit chains such as Acadia Healthcare and Universal Health Services (UHS), represent the fastest-growing segment. Acadia Healthcare operates approximately 250 behavioral health facilities across 39 states and the United Kingdom, while UHS operates approximately 350 facilities (company annual reports, 2023). However, private facilities tend to concentrate in higher-income metropolitan areas and selectively admit patients with commercial insurance, leaving Medicaid and uninsured patients disproportionately dependent on shrinking state systems.

General hospital psychiatric units -- discrete units within general medical hospitals -- have experienced the steepest relative decline. Between 2010 and 2023, more than 170 general hospital psychiatric units closed nationally (American Hospital Association, 2023). Hospital administrators frequently cite operating losses as the primary reason: psychiatric units generate per-diem revenues 20-40% below medical/surgical units while requiring specialized staffing and security infrastructure.

The United States has fewer psychiatric beds per capita than any other OECD nation except Italy and Turkey. Even the United Kingdom and Canada, which have undertaken their own deinstitutionalization programs, maintain 2.5 to 3 times the U.S. per-capita rate. Japan's exceptionally high rate reflects a different historical trajectory in which long-stay hospitalization remains common, a model that carries its own concerns about patient autonomy. Germany's rate of 128 beds per 100,000, combined with well-funded community services, represents the integrated approach that many experts consider closest to optimal.

### Private Psychiatric Hospital Practices

The growth of for-profit psychiatric hospital chains has introduced distinct dynamics into the inpatient care landscape. Investigative reporting by *The New York Times* (2022) and *BuzzFeed News* (2021) documented practices at UHS and Acadia Healthcare facilities that raised serious concerns:

- **Selective admission**: Private facilities disproportionately admit commercially insured patients and patients with less acute presentations, while referring Medicaid and uninsured patients to public hospitals or emergency departments
- **Length-of-stay manipulation**: Some facilities have been documented holding patients longer than medically necessary when insurance would pay, while discharging other patients prematurely when coverage expired, regardless of clinical status
- **Staffing concerns**: Reports of staffing levels below state minimum requirements, particularly on overnight and weekend shifts
- **Revenue maximization**: Practices including billing for group therapy sessions that consisted of watching television and documenting individual therapy sessions that did not occur

The Department of Justice has pursued False Claims Act cases against several for-profit psychiatric chains, including a $122 million settlement with UHS in 2020 related to allegations of medically unnecessary admissions and inflated billing at multiple facilities.

## Emergency Department Boarding

### The Boarding Crisis

Emergency department (ED) boarding occurs when a patient requiring psychiatric inpatient admission remains in the ED because no inpatient bed is available. Psychiatric patients board at far higher rates and for far longer durations than medical/surgical patients.

| Metric | Value | Trend (2019-2024) | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Average ED wait for psychiatric bed placement | 8 hours | Increasing (up from 6.8 hours in 2019) | ACEP, 2023 |
| ED visits with psychiatric boarding >6 hours | 1 in 5 psychiatric ED visits | Increasing | ACEP, 2023 |
| ED visits with psychiatric boarding >24 hours | 1 in 10 psychiatric ED visits | Increasing | Psychiatric Services, 2023 |
| Longest reported ED boarding episodes | >7 days in multiple states | Stable (persistent) | Investigative media reports, 2023 |
| Pediatric psychiatric boarding rate | 37% higher than adult rate | Increasing sharply since 2020 | AAP, 2023 |
| Psychiatric ED visits as share of all ED visits | 4.4% of visits but 12% of ED boarding hours | Increasing | HCUP/AHRQ, 2023 |

### Impact of Boarding on Patients and Hospitals

Psychiatric boarding produces measurable harm:

- **Patient outcomes**: Patients boarding >24 hours show increased agitation, self-harm attempts, and need for physical restraint (Nicks & Manthey, 2012; Psychiatric Services, 2023). A study in *Annals of Emergency Medicine* (2023) found that psychiatric patients who boarded for more than 24 hours were 2.3 times more likely to require physical restraint and 1.8 times more likely to attempt self-harm during the boarding episode than those placed within 6 hours.
- **ED operations**: Psychiatric boarding reduces ED capacity for other patients, contributing to ambulance diversion and longer wait times for all patients. A 2023 ACEP survey found that 72% of emergency physicians reported that psychiatric boarding "significantly" or "severely" impacted their ability to care for other patients.
- **Hospital finances**: Boarded psychiatric patients generate minimal revenue while occupying ED beds, costing hospitals an estimated $2,264 per boarding episode (ACEP, 2023). For a medium-sized hospital with 200 psychiatric boarding episodes annually, this represents nearly $450,000 in annual losses from boarding alone.
- **Staff safety**: Psychiatric boarding is associated with increased rates of staff assault and burnout (Emergency Medicine Journal, 2022). ED nurses report psychiatric boarding as the leading contributor to workplace violence, with approximately 80% of ED nurses experiencing at least one physical assault per year in facilities with high boarding rates (Emergency Nurses Association, 2023).
- **EMTALA liability**: Hospitals face federal EMTALA enforcement risk when boarded patients deteriorate. CMS has increased enforcement actions against hospitals that fail to stabilize psychiatric patients, with several hospitals receiving conditions of participation-level citations for boarding-related patient harm events.

## Involuntary Commitment

### 72-Hour Hold Procedures

All 50 states and the District of Columbia have civil commitment statutes that allow involuntary psychiatric evaluation and short-term hold, typically for 72 hours (excluding weekends and holidays in most jurisdictions). The criteria and processes vary significantly by state.

| Element | Most Common Standard | Variation Range | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Initial hold duration | 72 hours | 24 hours (some states) to 120 hours | TAC State Survey, 2023 |
| Who can initiate a hold | Physicians, law enforcement, designated mental health professionals | Some states allow family petition; some require only law enforcement | State statutes |
| Standard for hold | "Danger to self or others" or "gravely disabled" | Some states require "imminent" danger; others allow "substantial" risk | State statutes |
| Judicial review timeline | Within 72 hours of hold initiation | 48 hours to 14 days | State statutes |
| Right to counsel | Required in all states | Quality and availability vary | State statutes |
| Extended commitment (after initial hold) | Requires court hearing, clear and convincing evidence | Some states allow 90-day renewals; others require 6-month review | State statutes |

### Racial and Socioeconomic Disparities in Commitment

Involuntary commitment is not applied equally across populations. A 2022 study in *Psychiatric Services* found that Black individuals are committed involuntarily at 2.4 times the rate of white individuals, after controlling for diagnosis and severity. Hispanic individuals are committed at 1.6 times the white rate. Individuals experiencing homelessness are committed at approximately 5 times the rate of housed individuals with comparable diagnoses.

These disparities reflect broader systemic inequities: communities of color have less access to voluntary outpatient treatment (due to provider shortages, insurance gaps, and cultural barriers), meaning that psychiatric crises are more likely to escalate to the point where involuntary commitment becomes the only available intervention. The disparities also raise civil rights concerns about the disproportionate application of coercive state power to marginalized populations.

### Assisted Outpatient Treatment (AOT)

Forty-seven states have enacted Assisted Outpatient Treatment laws (sometimes called "Kendra's Law" after the New York statute enacted in 1999 following the death of Kendra Webdale). AOT allows courts to order individuals with serious mental illness who meet specific criteria (typically a history of repeated hospitalizations or dangerous behavior linked to treatment non-adherence) to comply with outpatient treatment plans. Non-compliance can result in involuntary transport to a hospital for evaluation, though not automatic inpatient commitment.

Research on AOT programs, particularly the randomized controlled trial of New York's program, has shown reductions in hospitalization (77% fewer days), incarceration (83% fewer days), and homelessness (74% fewer days) among participants (*Psychiatric Services*, 2010). However, AOT remains controversial among disability rights advocates and consumer organizations who view it as coercive and note its disproportionate application to people of color.

## Length of Stay and Readmission

### Average Length of Stay (ALOS)

The average length of stay for inpatient psychiatric admissions has declined dramatically over the past three decades, driven by insurance pressures, utilization review, and the shift toward community-based care.

| Setting | Average LOS (2023) | Change from 2010 | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| General hospital psychiatric units | 7.2 days | Down from 8.4 days | HCUP/AHRQ, 2023 |
| State psychiatric hospitals | 45-60 days (median ~30 days) | Stable | SAMHSA NMHSS, 2023 |
| Private psychiatric hospitals | 10.3 days | Down from 12.1 days | AHA, 2023 |
| VA psychiatric units | 14.2 days | Down from 17.8 days | VA, 2023 |
| Pediatric psychiatric admissions | 8.5 days | Down from 10.1 days | HCUP, 2023 |

The 7.2-day average length of stay in general hospital psychiatric units is clinically significant. Most evidence-based treatment protocols for acute psychiatric stabilization recommend a minimum of 10-14 days for effective medication titration, symptom stabilization, and discharge planning. A 2022 study in the *Journal of Clinical Psychiatry* found that patients discharged before Day 10 had a 30-day readmission rate of 24.7%, compared to 12.1% for patients discharged on Day 10 or later, suggesting that the compressed length of stay directly contributes to the readmission crisis.

The compression of length of stay is largely driven by insurance utilization review. Commercial insurers typically authorize 3-7 days for acute psychiatric admission and require concurrent review (re-authorization every 1-3 days) to continue coverage. Managed care organizations employ nurse reviewers who apply proprietary medical necessity criteria that clinicians frequently describe as overly restrictive and clinically inappropriate.

### Readmission Rates

High readmission rates are a key indicator of system failure, suggesting patients are being discharged before they are stable or without adequate community supports.

| Metric | Value | Benchmark/Trend | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| 30-day all-cause psychiatric readmission rate | 19.3% | Increasing (was 17.5% in 2018) | CMS Hospital Compare, 2023 |
| 30-day readmission for schizophrenia | 22.8% | Highest among diagnostic groups | CMS, 2023 |
| 30-day readmission for bipolar disorder | 18.7% | Above average | CMS, 2023 |
| 30-day readmission for major depression | 14.2% | Below average | CMS, 2023 |
| 90-day readmission rate (all psychiatric) | 31.4% | Increasing | HCUP/AHRQ, 2023 |
| Patients discharged without follow-up within 7 days | ~38% | Improving slowly (was 42% in 2018) | HEDIS/NCQA, 2023 |
| "Revolving door" patients (3+ admissions/year) | ~8% of psychiatric inpatients | Stable | Psychiatric Services, 2022 |

The 7-day follow-up rate is a critical quality metric tracked by the National Committee for Quality Assurance (NCQA) through its HEDIS measures. The fact that 38% of discharged psychiatric patients have no outpatient follow-up within 7 days reflects a fundamental failure of discharge planning and care coordination. Patients discharged without a follow-up appointment within 7 days are 2.5 times more likely to be readmitted within 30 days (NCQA, 2023).

### Discharge Planning Failures

The discharge planning process -- the bridge between inpatient stabilization and community-based care -- is widely regarded as the weakest link in the psychiatric care continuum. Common failures include:

- **Discharge to homelessness**: An estimated 15-20% of patients discharged from psychiatric inpatient units have no stable housing to return to (NASMHPD, 2023). These patients are at extremely high risk of readmission, ED re-presentation, and involvement with the criminal justice system.
- **Medication gaps**: Patients discharged with new psychiatric medications frequently experience gaps in medication access due to insurance authorization delays, pharmacy access issues, or inability to afford copayments. A 2023 study in *Psychiatric Services* found that 22% of discharged psychiatric patients did not fill their prescribed medications within 7 days of discharge.
- **No outpatient appointment**: Despite clinical guidelines recommending follow-up within 7 days of discharge, many patients are discharged with appointment dates weeks or months in the future, or with instructions to "call for an appointment" that they may not have the functional capacity to execute.
- **Loss of Medicaid coverage**: Patients who were enrolled in Medicaid during hospitalization may lose coverage upon discharge if they fail to complete redetermination paperwork, particularly after the post-pandemic Medicaid unwinding.

## Regulatory and Legal Framework

### Key Laws and Regulations

| Law/Regulation | Year | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **IMD Exclusion** (42 U.S.C. 1396d(a)(B)) | 1965 | Bars Medicaid payment for adults 21-64 in psychiatric facilities with >16 beds |
| **EMTALA** (Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act) | 1986 | Requires EDs to stabilize psychiatric emergencies regardless of ability to pay |
| **Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act** | 2008 | Requires insurance plans to cover mental health on par with medical/surgical |
| **21st Century Cures Act** (Section 12003) | 2016 | Created Medicaid IMD waiver demonstration for SUD; expanded to SMI in 2018 |
| **CMS IMD Waiver for SMI** (Section 1115) | 2018 | Allows states to seek Medicaid reimbursement for short-term (up to 30-day) stays in IMDs for SMI |
| **988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline** | 2022 | Established national crisis number; created demand for crisis receiving capacity |
| **Bipartisan Safer Communities Act** | 2022 | Provided $8.6 billion for mental health programs including crisis services |
| **EMTALA psychiatric boarding cases** | Ongoing | CMS enforcement actions against hospitals that fail to stabilize psychiatric patients |
| **CMS Final Rule on Parity Compliance** | 2024 | Strengthened MHPAEA enforcement requirements for commercial and Medicaid managed care plans |

### IMD Exclusion Waiver Status

As of 2024, 12 states have approved Section 1115 IMD waivers for serious mental illness, and an additional 6 states have applications pending (CMS, 2024). However, the waivers are limited to stays of 30 days or fewer and require states to provide matching community-based services.

| Waiver Category | Count | Key Limitation | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| States with approved SMI IMD waivers | 12 | 30-day maximum stay; community service requirements | CMS, 2024 |
| States with pending SMI IMD waiver applications | 6 | Same limitations as approved waivers | CMS, 2024 |
| States with SUD IMD waivers (broader program) | 37 | SUD-specific; does not cover psychiatric-only | CMS, 2024 |
| States with no IMD waiver activity | 32 (for SMI) | Full exclusion still applies | CMS, 2024 |

States with approved SMI waivers include Oregon, Vermont, Idaho, Alaska, and several others. Early evaluation data from CMS waiver reports shows that participating states have experienced an average 23% reduction in psychiatric ED boarding times within the first two years of waiver implementation, along with improved discharge planning outcomes and modest increases in bed availability (CMS Waiver Evaluation Reports, 2023).

### State Hospital Conditions and Oversight

State psychiatric hospitals operate under a patchwork of state licensing requirements, Joint Commission accreditation (voluntary), and CMS Conditions of Participation (for Medicare/Medicaid-certified facilities). The Department of Justice (DOJ) Civil Rights Division has investigated conditions at state psychiatric hospitals in multiple states under the Civil Rights of Institutionalized Persons Act (CRIPA):

- **Mississippi**: DOJ investigation (2016-2023) found inadequate suicide prevention, excessive use of seclusion and restraint, and insufficient treatment planning at the Mississippi State Hospital
- **Georgia**: Consent decree following findings of inadequate community services leading to unnecessary institutionalization (*Olmstead* enforcement)
- **Virginia**: DOJ settlement (2012) requiring expansion of community services to reduce reliance on state hospitals; compliance monitoring ongoing
- **New Hampshire**: DOJ settlement (2014) addressing community integration requirements for individuals with serious mental illness

## Geographic Variation

### Regional Disparities

The psychiatric bed shortage is not uniform. Rural areas and certain regions face particularly acute shortages.

- **Rural areas**: 65% of rural counties have no psychiatrist, and many have no inpatient psychiatric beds within a 60-mile radius (HRSA, 2023). The Health Resources and Services Administration designates 5,833 Mental Health Professional Shortage Areas (HPSAs), covering approximately 158 million Americans.
- **Southern states**: Despite higher per-capita bed counts in some states (Mississippi, West Virginia), overall bed quality and community follow-up services are often weaker. Southern states also have lower Medicaid income thresholds and higher uninsured rates, reducing the payer base for psychiatric care.
- **Western states**: Many Western states (Nevada, Arizona, Colorado) have among the lowest per-capita bed counts and highest ED boarding rates. Vast geographic distances compound the shortage, with some areas requiring transport of 100+ miles to the nearest inpatient facility.
- **Northeastern states**: Generally higher capacity but also higher cost and older facility stock. States like Massachusetts, Connecticut, and New York have relatively robust community service systems but still face significant bed shortages in major metropolitan areas.
- **Tribal and frontier areas**: Extremely limited access; Indian Health Service (IHS) facilities have minimal psychiatric inpatient capacity, with only a small number of beds across the entire IHS system. Native Americans and Alaska Natives experience psychiatric hospitalization rates that are disproportionately low relative to their rates of mental illness, reflecting access barriers rather than lower need.

This variation reflects differences in state fiscal capacity, political priorities, Medicaid expansion decisions, and historical investment in mental health infrastructure.

## Recent Trends

### Post-COVID Surge (2020-2024)

The COVID-19 pandemic dramatically worsened the psychiatric bed crisis:

- Psychiatric ED visits increased 31% from 2019 to 2022 among adults and 45% among adolescents (CDC, 2023)
- ED boarding times for psychiatric patients increased approximately 18% from pre-pandemic levels (ACEP, 2023)
- Staff shortages in psychiatric facilities intensified, with vacancy rates for psychiatric nurses exceeding 20% in many states (ANA, 2023)
- Several states temporarily relaxed IMD exclusion rules during the public health emergency, but most reversion occurred after emergency declarations ended
- Telehealth expanded dramatically for outpatient psychiatric care but was of limited applicability to the inpatient setting, where patients require physical supervision and hands-on crisis intervention
- Substance use-related psychiatric emergencies surged, with opioid-involved psychiatric presentations increasing 28% and methamphetamine-involved presentations increasing 34% from 2019 to 2023 (SAMHSA, 2024)

### 988 Implementation

The launch of the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline in July 2022 created significant new demand for crisis receiving and stabilization capacity. In its first full year of operation, 988 received over 5.5 million contacts (calls, texts, and chats), a 50% increase from the prior 10-digit number (SAMHSA, 2024). By mid-2024, monthly contact volume exceeded 500,000, demonstrating sustained growth beyond the initial launch period. This surge highlighted the gap between crisis identification and crisis care delivery: many callers in acute distress have nowhere to go when inpatient beds are unavailable.

SAMHSA has identified the need for approximately 1,000 crisis stabilization units and crisis receiving centers nationwide to serve as the "care delivery arm" of the 988 system, but as of 2024, fewer than 250 such facilities exist (SAMHSA, 2024). The result is that 988 can identify and counsel individuals in crisis but often cannot connect them to immediate, in-person care.

## Emerging Challenges

### Pediatric Psychiatric Emergency

The mental health crisis among children and adolescents has created unprecedented demand for pediatric psychiatric beds. Pediatric psychiatric ED visits increased 45% from 2019 to 2022 (CDC, 2023), but pediatric psychiatric bed capacity did not expand to match. Children routinely board in EDs for 2-3 days or longer, with some boarding episodes exceeding a week. The U.S. Surgeon General declared youth mental health a national crisis in 2021.

The American Academy of Pediatrics, the American Academy of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry, and the Children's Hospital Association jointly declared a national emergency in children's mental health in October 2021. Specific drivers of the pediatric crisis include:

- **Social media and technology**: Association between heavy social media use and increased rates of depression, anxiety, and suicidal ideation among adolescents (Surgeon General's Advisory, 2023)
- **Academic disruption**: COVID-era school closures produced measurable increases in anxiety, depression, and behavioral disorders among school-age children
- **Child and adolescent psychiatrist shortage**: Only approximately 9,500 child and adolescent psychiatrists practice in the United States, compared to an estimated need of 30,000+ (AACAP, 2023)
- **Pediatric bed scarcity**: Fewer than 5,000 dedicated pediatric and adolescent psychiatric beds exist nationally, with many states having no dedicated pediatric psychiatric unit at all

### Workforce Shortage

The psychiatric workforce shortage is a binding constraint on bed expansion. Even if physical beds were available, staffing them is increasingly difficult:

- 60% of all U.S. counties lack a single psychiatrist (HRSA, 2023)
- The average age of practicing psychiatrists is 55 years, with large cohorts nearing retirement (AAMC, 2023)
- Psychiatric nursing vacancy rates exceed 20% nationally (ANA, 2023)
- Average annual turnover for psychiatric technicians exceeds 30% (BLS, 2023)
- Child and adolescent psychiatry fellowship positions fill at only 70-80% capacity despite enormous unmet demand (AAMC, 2023)
- Psychiatrist compensation averages $275,000 annually, well below cardiology ($450,000), orthopedic surgery ($550,000), and other procedure-based specialties, reducing the pipeline of medical students choosing psychiatry (Medscape Physician Compensation Report, 2023)

### Medicaid Redetermination

The unwinding of continuous Medicaid enrollment (post-pandemic) resulted in over 19 million people losing Medicaid coverage between April 2023 and early 2024 (KFF, 2024). This disproportionately affected individuals with serious mental illness, potentially increasing the number of uninsured patients seeking psychiatric care through emergency departments. States with the highest rates of coverage loss -- including Texas, Florida, and Georgia -- were already among the most underserved for psychiatric bed capacity.

### Substance Use and Co-Occurring Disorders

The intersection of the overdose crisis and the psychiatric bed shortage creates a compounding emergency. An estimated 50-60% of individuals with serious mental illness also have a co-occurring substance use disorder (SAMHSA, 2023). Patients presenting to EDs with combined psychiatric and substance use emergencies often face the longest boarding times, as many psychiatric facilities will not accept patients who are actively intoxicated and substance use treatment facilities are not equipped for acute psychiatric stabilization. The gap between mental health and substance use treatment systems -- sometimes called the "dual-diagnosis gap" -- leaves these patients in a no-man's-land between two inadequate systems.
