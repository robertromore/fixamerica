# Inpatient Psychiatric Care: Root Causes

## Systemic Analysis

The current challenges in inpatient psychiatric care are not the result of a single failure but of several long-term, interlocking structural problems. These include a federal reimbursement policy that actively discourages investment in psychiatric beds, a fragmented financing system that separates responsibility from authority, a workforce pipeline that cannot supply enough clinicians, and a political dynamic that has consistently favored cutting institutional capacity without building adequate alternatives. Together, these factors create a self-reinforcing crisis in which inadequate capacity produces poor outcomes, poor outcomes discourage further investment, and the cycle deepens.

## Financial and Regulatory Barriers

### The IMD Exclusion: The Central Distortion

The Medicaid Institutions for Mental Diseases (IMD) exclusion (42 U.S.C. 1396d(a)(B)) is the single most consequential policy driving the inpatient psychiatric bed shortage. By barring federal Medicaid reimbursement for adults ages 21-64 in psychiatric facilities with more than 16 beds, it removes the largest payer of mental health services from the inpatient psychiatric market.

**Evidence**: Medicaid covers approximately 21% of all adults with mental illness and a substantially higher share of those with serious mental illness (Kaiser Family Foundation, 2023). In states that have not obtained IMD waivers, state psychiatric hospitals must be funded entirely through state general funds, with no federal match. This creates an enormous fiscal disadvantage compared to medical/surgical care, where states receive a 50-76% federal match for Medicaid patients (depending on state FMAP rates). The result is predictable: states invest in services that draw federal matching funds and disinvest from services that do not.

**Mechanism**: The IMD exclusion operates through the definition of "institution for mental diseases" in 42 U.S.C. 1396d(i), which includes any hospital, nursing facility, or other institution of more than 16 beds that is "primarily engaged in providing diagnosis, treatment, or care of persons with mental diseases." The 16-bed threshold was adopted without clinical justification; it was a fiscal policy decision. CMS has interpreted the exclusion to apply when more than 50% of a facility's patients have a primary psychiatric diagnosis, creating additional complexity and gaming incentives for facilities seeking to avoid the designation.

**Scope of impact**: The Congressional Research Service estimates that repealing the IMD exclusion would cost the federal government $10-15 billion annually in new Medicaid spending (CRS, 2023). This estimate itself illustrates the magnitude of the distortion: $10-15 billion annually represents the amount of federal psychiatric inpatient spending that the IMD exclusion currently prevents, spending that the federal government provides without restriction for every other category of medical inpatient care.

### Insurance Reimbursement Disparities

Even outside the IMD exclusion, psychiatric inpatient care is systematically underpaid relative to medical/surgical inpatient care.

**Evidence**: A 2023 Milliman study found that behavioral health reimbursement rates averaged 20-40% lower than comparable medical/surgical rates across commercial, Medicare, and Medicaid payers. Psychiatric per-diem rates in many states fall below the cost of care, resulting in operating margins of -5% to -15% for psychiatric units (Milliman, 2023). General hospitals cite financial losses as the primary reason for closing psychiatric units: between 2010 and 2023, more than 170 general hospital psychiatric units closed nationally (AHA, 2023).

**Parity enforcement failures**: Despite the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act (MHPAEA) of 2008, which requires equal coverage for mental health and medical/surgical services, enforcement has been uneven. A 2023 Department of Labor report found that more than 50% of health plans subjected behavioral health inpatient claims to more restrictive prior authorization than comparable medical/surgical claims. Common violations include:

- Requiring concurrent review (re-authorization every 1-3 days) for psychiatric inpatient stays while allowing medical/surgical stays of similar length without concurrent review
- Applying stricter "fail first" or step-therapy requirements for psychiatric admissions
- Using narrower network adequacy standards for psychiatric providers, resulting in fewer in-network options
- Setting lower reimbursement rates for psychiatric per-diem services than for medical/surgical per-diem services of comparable acuity

### Fragmented Financing

The financing of inpatient psychiatric care is split across multiple payers and levels of government with misaligned incentives:

- **State governments** fund state psychiatric hospitals with state general funds (no federal Medicaid match due to IMD exclusion)
- **Medicaid** pays for psychiatric care in general hospital units (under 16 beds) and for non-IMD facilities, but not in dedicated psychiatric hospitals for adults 21-64
- **Medicare** pays for inpatient psychiatric care for beneficiaries over 65 with a 190-day lifetime limit -- a restriction that does not exist for any other category of inpatient care
- **Commercial insurers** cover acute inpatient stays but typically authorize only 3-7 days through utilization review processes that clinicians describe as adversarial
- **Uncompensated care** accounts for a disproportionate share of psychiatric admissions, as many patients with serious mental illness are uninsured or underinsured
- **Criminal justice systems** spend billions annually on individuals with mental illness who cycle through jails and prisons in the absence of adequate psychiatric care, but these expenditures are not counted as "mental health spending" and are not available to fund clinical alternatives

**Evidence**: This fragmentation means no single entity has both the financial incentive and the authority to ensure adequate bed capacity. States want to shift costs to Medicaid; the federal government refuses due to the IMD exclusion; private insurers minimize authorization; and patients fall through the gaps. The total system cost of this fragmentation -- including ED boarding, incarceration, homelessness services, and lost productivity -- far exceeds the cost of adequately funded psychiatric care, but the costs are distributed across so many budgets that no single decision-maker faces the full picture.

### Medicare's 190-Day Lifetime Limit

Medicare imposes a 190-day lifetime limit on inpatient psychiatric hospital care, a restriction that applies to no other category of inpatient medical care. A Medicare beneficiary can receive unlimited days of inpatient care for cancer, heart disease, or any other medical condition, but only 190 days in a psychiatric hospital over their entire lifetime. This limit was established in 1965 alongside the original Medicare legislation and reflects the same era's assumption that psychiatric care was fundamentally different from -- and less deserving of coverage than -- medical care. While most psychiatric admissions are far shorter than 190 days, the lifetime limit disproportionately affects individuals with chronic, severe mental illness who may require multiple extended hospitalizations over a lifetime.

## Structural and Institutional Factors

### Workforce Shortage

The psychiatric workforce is the binding constraint on bed expansion. Even if physical beds were built and funded, staffing them is increasingly difficult.

**Evidence**:

- 60% of all U.S. counties have no psychiatrist (HRSA, 2023)
- The average practicing psychiatrist is 55 years old, with large cohorts approaching retirement (AAMC, 2023)
- Psychiatric nursing vacancy rates exceed 20% nationally (ANA, 2023)
- Psychiatric technician turnover exceeds 30% annually (BLS, 2023)
- Psychiatric residency positions have grown only modestly (approximately 7% since 2015) while demand has surged (AAMC, 2023)
- Only approximately 9,500 child and adolescent psychiatrists practice in the United States, compared to an estimated need of 30,000+ (AACAP, 2023)

The workforce shortage is driven by multiple factors:

- **Lower compensation**: Psychiatrist compensation averages $275,000 annually, well below cardiology ($450,000), orthopedic surgery ($550,000), and other procedure-based specialties (Medscape, 2023). Medical students carrying $200,000+ in educational debt face strong financial incentives to choose higher-paying specialties.
- **High burnout and workplace violence**: Psychiatric inpatient settings have among the highest rates of workplace violence in healthcare. A 2022 survey by the American Psychiatric Nurses Association found that 55% of psychiatric nurses had been physically assaulted at work in the prior year. Burnout rates among inpatient psychiatric staff exceed 50% (Psychiatric Services, 2022).
- **Stigma within medicine**: Psychiatry continues to carry lower prestige within the medical profession than surgical or procedural specialties, reducing the pipeline of top students who select it.
- **Insufficient training slots**: The number of psychiatry residency positions is constrained by Medicare Graduate Medical Education (GME) funding caps established in 1997. Despite growing demand, the total number of GME-funded residency positions has been essentially frozen for over 25 years.
- **Immigration barriers**: International medical graduates fill approximately 30% of psychiatry residency positions (AAMC, 2023), demonstrating both the dependence on and the potential of immigration-based workforce strategies. However, visa processing delays, J-1 waiver requirements, and H-1B caps create barriers to retaining these physicians in practice.

### Certificate of Need and Regulatory Barriers

In 35 states that maintain Certificate of Need (CON) laws, new psychiatric facilities or bed expansions require state approval, a process that can take 12-24 months and is subject to challenge by competing facilities. CON laws were originally intended to control healthcare costs but often function as barriers to entry.

**Evidence**: States with CON requirements for psychiatric beds have, on average, 15% fewer psychiatric beds per capita than states without such requirements, after controlling for population and income (Mercatus Center, 2022). Incumbent facilities use the CON process to block new competitors, reducing supply even when demand is clearly unmet. For example, in North Carolina, a proposed 50-bed psychiatric hospital was delayed for over two years by legal challenges from existing facilities, despite documented ED boarding times exceeding 12 hours in the region.

### Physical Infrastructure Deficit

Many existing psychiatric facilities are decades old and do not meet modern standards. State psychiatric hospitals, in particular, were largely built in the mid-20th century and require substantial renovation or replacement.

**Evidence**: A 2023 NASMHPD survey found that the average age of state psychiatric hospital buildings was 47 years, with many predating modern fire codes, accessibility standards, and trauma-informed design principles. The estimated capital investment needed to bring existing state psychiatric infrastructure to modern standards exceeds $10 billion nationally (NASMHPD, 2023). Outdated facility design -- long corridors, shared rooms, poor sight lines, inadequate de-escalation spaces -- contributes to higher rates of patient and staff violence, use of seclusion and restraint, and overall poorer treatment environments.

### Liability and Risk Aversion

Hospitals and health systems face significant liability exposure from operating psychiatric units:

- **Elopement and suicide risk**: Psychiatric units must maintain physical environments that prevent patient self-harm and elopement. The cost of "ligature-resistant" design (removing all potential hanging points) and continuous observation staffing adds substantially to operating costs.
- **EMTALA obligations**: Under EMTALA, hospitals with psychiatric capabilities cannot turn away psychiatric emergencies. Some hospitals have responded by eliminating their psychiatric units entirely, thereby removing the EMTALA obligation for psychiatric emergencies at their facilities.
- **Malpractice exposure**: Psychiatric malpractice claims related to inadequate suicide precautions, premature discharge, and medication errors create insurance cost pressures that further discourage hospitals from operating psychiatric units.

## Political Economy

### Who Benefits from the Status Quo

| Actor | Benefit | Economic Value | Incentive to Block Reform |
|-------|---------|----------------|---------------------------|
| State budget offices | Avoiding cost of funding adequate state hospital capacity | Billions in avoided state spending annually | Repealing IMD exclusion could increase state Medicaid matching obligations; status quo keeps costs off budget |
| CMS/federal government | Avoiding open-ended Medicaid liability for psychiatric inpatient care | Estimated $10-15 billion/year in avoided federal spending if IMD exclusion were repealed | Fear that full repeal would create fiscal exposure without quality safeguards |
| General hospitals without psych units | No obligation to treat psychiatric patients they refer elsewhere | Avoided operating losses from psych units | Would face competitive pressure if psychiatric capacity expanded |
| Private insurers | Low inpatient authorization rates reduce claims costs | Billions in reduced behavioral health claims annually | Expanded capacity could increase utilization and costs |
| For-profit psychiatric hospital chains | Limited competition due to CON laws and workforce scarcity | Higher occupancy rates and revenue per bed | May publicly support expansion while privately preferring constrained supply |
| Pharmaceutical companies | Medication-first approach reduces demand for inpatient services they do not provide | Market positioning around outpatient medication management | Reduced attention to structural care delivery issues |

### Perverse Incentives

The current system creates several destructive incentive misalignments:

1. **States benefit from closing beds**: The IMD exclusion means states save money by closing state hospital beds and forcing patients into emergency departments (where EMTALA guarantees treatment and Medicaid/Medicare pays).
2. **Hospitals benefit from short stays**: Low reimbursement rates incentivize hospitals to discharge patients as quickly as possible, contributing to high readmission rates (19.3% within 30 days).
3. **Insurers benefit from denied authorizations**: Managed care organizations profit by limiting inpatient authorizations, often to 3-7 days even when clinical need exceeds that duration.
4. **No one is accountable for outcomes**: The fragmented system means no single entity is responsible for ensuring patients receive adequate care across the continuum.
5. **Emergency departments absorb the externalities**: EDs bear the cost of psychiatric boarding -- lost capacity, staff injuries, patient deterioration -- but have no control over the upstream factors (bed supply, insurance authorization, community services) that drive boarding.
6. **Criminal justice absorbs the overflow**: Jails and prisons function as the safety net of last resort, but correctional budgets are rarely counted in discussions of mental health spending, obscuring the true cost of the current system.

## Cultural and Behavioral Factors

### Stigma Against Psychiatric Facilities

Public resistance to psychiatric facilities in local communities (a "Not In My Backyard" or NIMBY dynamic) creates siting challenges for new or expanded facilities.

**Evidence**: Studies of psychiatric facility proposals show that community opposition based on safety fears occurs in approximately 40% of proposed projects, despite evidence that psychiatric facilities do not increase local crime rates (Psychiatric Services, 2021). Zoning challenges can add 6-18 months and significant legal costs to facility development. The NIMBY dynamic is particularly acute for facilities that serve involuntary patients, substance use patients, or forensic (court-ordered) patients. Community opposition frequently invokes property value concerns, despite research showing no measurable property value impact from psychiatric facility proximity (Urban Institute, 2020).

### Pervasive Mental Health Stigma

Broader societal stigma against mental illness contributes to political willingness to underfund psychiatric care.

**Evidence**: Public opinion surveys consistently find that while approximately 90% of Americans say mental health is a priority, willingness to fund inpatient psychiatric care through taxation ranks lower than willingness to fund cancer treatment, emergency medical care, and substance abuse services (Pew Research, 2023). This creates a "say-do" gap in which elected officials can express concern about mental health without facing political consequences for underfunding inpatient care. The stigma is compounded by media portrayals that disproportionately associate mental illness with violence, despite the fact that people with mental illness are far more likely to be victims of violence than perpetrators.

### Provider Stigma

Stigma operates within the medical profession itself. A 2022 survey of medical students found that psychiatry ranked among the lowest-prestige specialties and that students who expressed interest in psychiatry reported facing discouragement from faculty and peers (Academic Medicine, 2022). This intra-professional stigma constrains the workforce pipeline and contributes to the specialty's difficulty attracting top graduates.

## Why Reform Has Failed

Reform efforts have been incremental, partial, and repeatedly undermined by the same structural dynamics they seek to address:

1. **The 1963 Community Mental Health Act** envisioned community centers replacing state hospitals, but the centers were never fully funded, leaving a gap that has persisted for 60 years.
2. **The 2008 Parity Act** required equal insurance coverage for mental health but did not address the supply-side shortage of beds and providers.
3. **The 21st Century Cures Act (2016)** created IMD waivers but limited them to 30-day stays and imposed community service matching requirements that many states find burdensome.
4. **State-level reforms** have been piecemeal, with individual states adding beds or crisis services without addressing the underlying federal financing structure.
5. **Political dynamics** consistently favor visible, low-cost interventions (crisis hotlines, anti-stigma campaigns) over the expensive, long-term infrastructure investment needed to expand inpatient capacity.
6. **Coalition fragmentation**: Potential reform advocates are divided between those who prioritize bed expansion (TAC, some family groups) and those who prioritize community services (Bazelon Center, disability rights organizations), allowing opponents to exploit the disagreement and block comprehensive legislation.

The fundamental reason reform has failed is that the actors with the most power to change the system (Congress, CMS, state legislatures) also have the strongest financial incentives to maintain the status quo. Repealing the IMD exclusion would cost the federal government an estimated $10-15 billion annually in new Medicaid spending, while rebuilding state hospital systems would cost states billions in capital and operating expenditures. Without political pressure sufficient to overcome these fiscal barriers, incremental half-measures remain the norm.

The COVID-19 pandemic and the resulting surge in psychiatric emergencies, combined with the high-profile launch of the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline and the Surgeon General's youth mental health advisory, have created a political window that may be more favorable for comprehensive reform than any moment since the 1960s. Whether that window will be used effectively or will close with another round of incomplete reforms remains the central question.
