# Civil Commitment and Involuntary Treatment: Resources

This section provides a curated list of key research, reports, legal materials, and advocacy resources on civil commitment, involuntary treatment, and assisted outpatient treatment in the United States.

---

## Academic Research

### Peer-Reviewed Studies

- Swartz, M.S., et al. "New York State Assisted Outpatient Treatment Program Evaluation." *Duke University School of Medicine / NYS Office of Mental Health*, 2009. - Landmark evaluation of Kendra's Law showing 77% reduction in hospitalization, 83% reduction in arrests, 87% reduction in incarceration, and 74% reduction in homelessness among AOT participants backed by intensive services.
- Swartz, M.S., et al. "Can Involuntary Outpatient Commitment Reduce Hospital Recidivism?: Findings from a Randomized Trial with Severely Mentally Ill Individuals." *American Journal of Psychiatry* 156.12 (1999): 1968-1975. - North Carolina randomized trial finding that extended AOT (>180 days) with intensive services significantly reduced hospitalization, while short-term AOT did not.
- Kisely, S.R., Campbell, L.A., & O'Reilly, R. "Compulsory Community and Involuntary Outpatient Treatment for People with Severe Mental Disorders." *Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews* 3 (2017): CD004408. - Meta-analysis finding no significant reduction in hospitalization from compulsory community treatment absent adequate services. Widely cited by both proponents and opponents of AOT; the finding underscores that court orders without funded services are insufficient.
- Burns, T., et al. "Community Treatment Orders for Patients with Psychosis (OCTET): A Randomised Controlled Trial." *Lancet* 381.9878 (2013): 1627-1633. - UK randomized trial of community treatment orders finding no significant effect on readmission. The largest RCT on outpatient commitment to date.
- Snowden, L.R., Hastings, J.F., & Alvidrez, J. "Overrepresentation of Black Americans in Psychiatric Inpatient Care." *Psychiatric Services* 60.6 (2009): 779-785. - Documented racial disparities in involuntary psychiatric admission, finding Black Americans 2.4 times more likely to be involuntarily admitted than white Americans.
- Schwartz, R.C., & Blankenship, D.M. "Racial Disparities in Psychotic Disorder Diagnosis: A Review of Empirical Literature." *World Journal of Psychiatry* 4.4 (2014): 133-140. - Review documenting that Black individuals are significantly more likely to receive psychotic-spectrum diagnoses than white individuals presenting with identical symptoms.
- Monahan, J., et al. "Rethinking Risk Assessment: The MacArthur Study of Mental Disorder and Violence." *Oxford University Press*, 2001. - Foundational study on violence risk assessment accuracy, finding ~70-75% accuracy with best clinical tools. Developed the Classification of Violence Risk (COVR) instrument.
- Compton, M.T., et al. "The Police-Based Crisis Intervention Team (CIT) Model." *Psychiatric Services* 70.1 (2019): 44-54. - Found CIT training reduced arrests of people with mental illness by 18% but did not reduce use of force.
- Tsemberis, S., Gulcur, L., & Nakae, M. "Housing First, Consumer Choice, and Harm Reduction for Homeless Individuals with a Dual Diagnosis." *American Journal of Public Health* 94.4 (2004): 651-656. - Demonstrated 80% reduction in homelessness with Housing First model for individuals with SMI and co-occurring substance use disorders.
- Bond, G.R., et al. "Assertive Community Treatment for People with Severe Mental Illness: Critical Ingredients and Impact on Patients." *Disease Management and Health Outcomes* 9.3 (2001): 141-159. - Evidence for ACT reducing hospitalization by 20-40%.
- Honegger, L.N. "Does the Evidence Support the Case for Mental Health Courts?" *Law and Human Behavior* 39.5 (2015): 478-488. - Evidence review of mental health court effectiveness, finding improved due process outcomes.
- Katsakou, C., & Priebe, S. "Outcomes of Involuntary Hospital Admission: A Review." *Acta Psychiatrica Scandinavica* 114.4 (2006): 232-241. - Review of patient experiences and outcomes of involuntary treatment, documenting both therapeutic benefits and traumatic effects.
- Wynn, R. "Coercion in Psychiatric Care: Clinical, Legal, and Ethical Controversies." *International Journal of Law and Psychiatry* 78 (2021): 101726. - Analysis of Norway's 2017 capacity-based commitment reform, finding approximately 10% reduction in involuntary admissions.
- Gerretsen, P., et al. "Impaired Illness Awareness in Schizophrenia and Its Relationship to Structural Brain Measures." *Schizophrenia Research* 156.1 (2014): 92-97. - Neuroimaging study localizing anosognosia to frontal and parietal lobe dysfunction.
- Chinman, M., et al. "Peer Support Services for Individuals with Serious Mental Illnesses: Assessing the Evidence." *Psychiatric Services* 65.4 (2014): 429-441. - Review finding that peer support services improve treatment engagement and reduce hospitalization.
- Gowensmith, W.N., et al. "Lookin' for Beds in All the Wrong Places: Outpatient Competency Restoration as a Promising Approach to Modern Challenges." *Psychology, Public Policy, and Law* 22.3 (2016): 293-305. - Demonstrated that community-based competency restoration programs achieve comparable restoration rates to inpatient programs at one-third the cost for nonviolent defendants.
- Swanson, J.W., et al. "Racial Disparities in Involuntary Outpatient Commitment: Are They Real?" *Health Affairs* 28.3 (2009): 816-826. - Analysis of racial disparities in AOT in the context of upstream access inequities.
- Perlin, M.L. "Competence, Deinstitutionalization, and Homelessness: A Story of Marginalization." *Houston Law Review* 28 (1991): 63-142. - Foundational legal scholarship on the intersection of commitment, homelessness, and competency.

### Working Papers and Reports

- RAND Corporation. "Assisted Outpatient Treatment: Evaluating the Evidence." 2020. - Comprehensive evidence review concluding AOT is effective when coupled with intensive services; coercion alone insufficient. Also estimates cost-effectiveness of community treatment vs. incarceration.
- Medicaid and CHIP Payment and Access Commission (MACPAC). "Behavioral Health in the Medicaid Program -- People, Use, and Expenditures." 2023. - Analysis of Medicaid spending on behavioral health, including IMD exclusion impact and Section 1115 waiver outcomes.
- Amador, X. "I Am Not Sick, I Don't Need Help!" Vida Press, 2000; updated 2012. - Foundational work on anosognosia in schizophrenia; estimates 40-50% prevalence; provides clinical strategies for engaging individuals who lack illness awareness.
- New York Lawyers for the Public Interest. "Implementation of Kendra's Law Is Severely Biased." 2005. - Analysis documenting that Black New Yorkers were approximately 5 times more likely to be subject to AOT orders than white New Yorkers.
- Kennedy Forum. "Fixing America's Mental Health Crisis: The Case for Ending the IMD Exclusion." 2022. - Policy brief estimating that IMD exclusion repeal could enable 50,000-75,000 additional psychiatric beds over a decade.

## Government Reports and Data

### Federal Agencies

- Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA). "National Survey on Drug Use and Health (NSDUH)." Annual. <https://www.samhsa.gov/data/data-we-collect/nsduh-national-survey-drug-use-and-health> - Primary federal data source on mental illness prevalence and treatment rates. Provides estimates of SMI prevalence, treatment receipt, and unmet need.
- SAMHSA. "Behavioral Health Services Information System (BHSIS)." Annual. <https://www.samhsa.gov/data/data-we-collect/bhsis-behavioral-health-services-information-system> - Data on mental health facility capacity and utilization, including bed counts by state and facility type.
- SAMHSA. "988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline Performance Dashboard." Updated monthly. <https://www.samhsa.gov/find-help/988/performance-metrics> - Real-time data on 988 call volume, response rates, mobile crisis team dispatch rates, and outcomes.
- SAMHSA. "National Guidelines for Behavioral Health Crisis Care: Best Practice Toolkit." 2020. <https://www.samhsa.gov/sites/default/files/national-guidelines-for-behavioral-health-crisis-care-02242020.pdf> - Federal guidelines establishing the Crisis Now model as best practice for crisis care.
- Health Resources and Services Administration (HRSA). "Health Professional Shortage Areas (HPSA) Find." Updated quarterly. <https://data.hrsa.gov/tools/shortage-area> - Interactive tool for identifying mental health professional shortage areas by county.
- Bureau of Justice Statistics. "Mental Health Problems of Prison and Jail Inmates." 2021. <https://bjs.ojp.gov/library/publications/mental-health-problems-prison-and-jail-inmates> - Data on mental illness prevalence in incarcerated populations, including diagnosis-specific estimates.
- Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC). "Emergency Department Visit Rates for Mental Health Conditions." 2023. <https://www.cdc.gov/nchs/products/databriefs.htm> - Trend data on psychiatric ED presentations, including post-pandemic increases.
- National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH). "Mental Illness Statistics." Updated annually. <https://www.nimh.nih.gov/health/statistics/mental-illness> - National prevalence estimates for serious mental illness, including demographic breakdowns.
- Department of Justice, Civil Rights Division. "Olmstead: Community Integration for Everyone." <https://www.ada.gov/olmstead/> - DOJ enforcement actions and guidance on Olmstead community integration requirements.
- Association of American Medical Colleges (AAMC). "The Complexities of Physician Supply and Demand: Projections from 2019 to 2034." 2023. - Workforce projections including psychiatric specialty shortage estimates.

### Congressional Reports

- Congressional Research Service. "The Medicaid IMD Exclusion and Mental Health Care." Report R47350 (2023). - Comprehensive analysis of the IMD exclusion's history, impact, and reform options, including cost estimates for repeal.
- Congressional Research Service. "Assisted Outpatient Treatment: Background and Federal Role." Report R47129 (2022). - Overview of AOT programs and federal policy options, including block grant conditions.
- Government Accountability Office. "Mental Health: HHS Could Improve Efforts to Implement and Assess Behavioral Health Parity Protections." GAO-23-105282 (2023). - Assessment of mental health parity enforcement, finding ongoing insurer noncompliance.
- Government Accountability Office. "Behavioral Health: Research on Health Care Costs of Untreated Conditions is Limited." GAO-19-274 (2019). - Analysis of economic costs of untreated mental illness and the limitations of existing cost data.
- Congressional Budget Office. "Costs of Repealing the Medicaid IMD Exclusion." (Various estimates, 2016-2024). - CBO scoring of IMD exclusion repeal proposals, estimating $5-10 billion in annual federal cost increases.

### State Data Sources

- New York Office of Mental Health. "Assisted Outpatient Treatment Program Report." Annual. <https://omh.ny.gov/omhweb/statistics/> - Data on Kendra's Law implementation and outcomes, including enrollment, demographics, and clinical measures.
- Florida Department of Children and Families. "Baker Act Annual Report." Annual. <https://www.myflfamilies.com/services/mental-health/baker-act> - Data on Florida's involuntary examination statute, including the over 215,000 annual initiations.
- California Department of Health Care Services. "Laura's Law (AB 1421) Implementation Data." Annual. - County-level AOT implementation data, including enrollment and service delivery.
- California Department of Health Care Services. "CARE Act Implementation Reports." 2023-present. - Data on the CARE Court rollout, including petition volumes and treatment plan outcomes.
- Texas Health and Human Services Commission. "State Hospital System Data." Annual. - Data on Texas state hospital bed capacity, wait times, and forensic patient proportions.
- Oregon Health Authority. "Community Competency Restoration Pilot Evaluation." 2023. - Outcome data on Oregon's community-based competency restoration program.

## Books

- Appelbaum, P.S. *Almost a Revolution: Mental Health Law and the Limits of Change*. Oxford University Press, 1994. - Foundational text on the due process revolution in mental health law and its consequences, arguing that legal reforms were necessary but insufficient without treatment resources.
- Torrey, E.F. *American Psychosis: How the Federal Government Destroyed the Mental Illness Treatment System*. Oxford University Press, 2014. - Critical account of deinstitutionalization and its failures, from the Treatment Advocacy Center founder.
- Torrey, E.F. *The Insanity Offense: How America's Failure to Treat the Seriously Mentally Ill Endangers Its Citizens*. W.W. Norton, 2008. - Argument for expanded treatment authority, including case studies of individuals whose untreated illness led to violence.
- Grob, G.N. *The Mad Among Us: A History of the Care of America's Mentally Ill*. Harvard University Press, 1994. - Comprehensive history of mental health care in America from colonial era to modern period.
- Stefan, S. *Rational Suicide, Irrational Laws: Examining Current Approaches to Suicide in Policy and Law*. Oxford University Press, 2016. - Analysis of legal frameworks governing self-harm and suicide risk in civil commitment.
- Bloom, J.D., & Buckley, M.C. *Oregon's Psychiatric Security Review Board*. Springer, 2020. - Case study of Oregon's forensic mental health oversight system, one of the most effective in the nation.
- Deutsch, A. *The Shame of the States*. Harcourt, Brace, 1948. - Foundational investigative work documenting conditions in state mental hospitals that catalyzed the reform movement.
- Goffman, E. *Asylums: Essays on the Social Situation of Mental Patients and Other Inmates*. Anchor Books, 1961. - Influential sociological analysis of total institutions and their effects on residents.
- Saks, E.R. *The Center Cannot Hold: My Journey Through Madness*. Hyperion, 2007. - First-person account of living with schizophrenia while navigating the commitment system, by a USC law professor.
- Metzl, J.M. *The Protest Psychosis: How Schizophrenia Became a Black Disease*. Beacon Press, 2009. - Historical analysis of how the diagnosis of schizophrenia was used as a tool of racial control, particularly against Black men during the civil rights era.
- Beers, C.W. *A Mind That Found Itself*. Longmans, Green, 1908; reprinted University of Pittsburgh Press, 1981. - Autobiography of a former psychiatric patient that launched the mental hygiene movement and led to the founding of what became Mental Health America.
- Perlin, M.L. *The Hidden Prejudice: Mental Disability on Trial*. American Psychological Association, 2000. - Analysis of "sanism" -- prejudice against people with mental disabilities -- in the legal system, including commitment proceedings.
- Whitaker, R. *Mad in America: Bad Science, Bad Medicine, and the Enduring Mistreatment of the Mentally Ill*. Basic Books, 2002. - Critical history of psychiatric treatment in America, including the use of involuntary treatment.

## Legal Resources

### Case Law

- *O'Connor v. Donaldson*, 422 U.S. 563 (1975) - State cannot confine nondangerous individual capable of surviving safely in freedom.
- *Addington v. Texas*, 441 U.S. 418 (1979) - Minimum standard of proof for civil commitment is "clear and convincing evidence."
- *Youngberg v. Romeo*, 457 U.S. 307 (1982) - Involuntarily committed persons have liberty interests in safety, freedom from restraint, and minimally adequate training.
- *Olmstead v. L.C.*, 527 U.S. 581 (1999) - Unjustified institutionalization is discrimination under the ADA; requires community-based treatment when appropriate.
- *Washington v. Harper*, 494 U.S. 210 (1990) - State may administer medication to a prisoner against their will if they are dangerous and the treatment is medically appropriate.
- *Sell v. United States*, 539 U.S. 166 (2003) - Forced medication for competence restoration requires case-by-case judicial review.
- *Jackson v. Indiana*, 406 U.S. 715 (1972) - Indefinite commitment solely for competency restoration violates due process.
- *Vitek v. Jones*, 445 U.S. 480 (1980) - Transfer of a prisoner to a mental hospital requires due process protections including notice and a hearing.
- *Lessard v. Schmidt*, 349 F. Supp. 1078 (E.D. Wis. 1972) - First federal court ruling requiring criminal-procedure-level due process for civil commitment.
- *Rouse v. Cameron*, 373 F.2d 451 (D.C. Cir. 1966) - First federal appellate recognition of a right to treatment for committed persons.
- *Wyatt v. Stickney*, 325 F. Supp. 781 (M.D. Ala. 1971) - First comprehensive judicial order establishing minimum standards for institutional treatment.
- *Buck v. Bell*, 274 U.S. 200 (1927) - Supreme Court upheld involuntary sterilization of institutionalized individuals; never explicitly overruled.
- *Zinermon v. Burch*, 494 U.S. 113 (1990) - State may be liable for admitting a mentally ill person as a "voluntary" patient when the person was incompetent to give informed consent.

### Statutory References

- 42 U.S.C. 1396d(a)(B) (Medicaid IMD Exclusion) - Prohibits federal Medicaid reimbursement for care in institutions for mental diseases with more than 16 beds for patients aged 21-64.
- 42 U.S.C. 10801 et seq. (Protection and Advocacy for Individuals with Mental Illness Act) - Establishes the P&A system in every state.
- 42 U.S.C. 12101 et seq. (Americans with Disabilities Act) - Basis for *Olmstead* community integration mandate.
- N.Y. Mental Hygiene Law 9.60 (Kendra's Law) - New York's assisted outpatient treatment statute; national model for AOT.
- Cal. Welf. & Inst. Code 5345-5349.5 (Laura's Law) - California's AOT statute.
- Cal. Welf. & Inst. Code 5150 (Lanterman-Petris-Short Act) - California's involuntary hold statute.
- Fla. Stat. 394.451-394.47892 (Baker Act) - Florida's involuntary examination statute.
- 42 U.S.C. 300x et seq. (Community Mental Health Services Block Grant) - Authorizes federal block grants to states for community mental health services.
- Pub. L. 113-93, Section 223 (2014) - Authorized the Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinic (CCBHC) demonstration.
- 29 U.S.C. 1185a (Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act) - Requires health insurers to cover mental health services on terms no less favorable than medical/surgical services.
