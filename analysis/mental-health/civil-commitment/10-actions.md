# Civil Commitment and Involuntary Treatment: What You Can Do

Civil commitment policy affects millions of Americans -- people with serious mental illness, their families, emergency responders, and entire communities -- yet it receives far less public attention and citizen engagement than its impact warrants. The treatment gap that drives the civil commitment crisis is fundamentally a political choice: the community mental health infrastructure that was promised during deinstitutionalization was never funded because there was insufficient public demand. Changing this requires sustained, informed citizen engagement at the federal, state, and local levels.

The actions below are organized by level of effort and type of engagement. Some require only minutes; others require sustained commitment. All are more effective when done in coordination with others.

---

## Individual Actions

### Learn and Stay Informed

- Read the Treatment Advocacy Center's "Grading the States" report to understand your state's commitment laws, bed capacity, and AOT implementation: <https://www.treatmentadvocacycenter.org/grading-the-states>
- Subscribe to NAMI's policy newsletter for updates on federal and state mental health legislation: <https://www.nami.org/advocacy>
- Follow the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline performance data to track crisis system development in your area: <https://www.samhsa.gov/find-help/988/performance-metrics>
- Read "I Am Not Sick, I Don't Need Help!" by Xavier Amador for an accessible introduction to anosognosia and its implications for treatment and civil commitment
- Review your state's specific commitment statute and AOT law through your state legislature's website or the Treatment Advocacy Center's state-by-state database
- Read the Bazelon Center's publications for the disability rights perspective on civil commitment: <https://www.bazelon.org>
- Follow HRSA's Health Professional Shortage Area data to understand mental health workforce gaps in your community: <https://data.hrsa.gov/tools/shortage-area>
- Read "The Center Cannot Hold" by Elyn Saks for a first-person perspective on living with schizophrenia and experiencing the commitment system
- Review your county's or city's mental health budget to understand how local funds are allocated between crisis services, community treatment, and institutional care

### Make Personal Changes

- Save the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline number in your phone and share it with family and friends as the first call for a mental health emergency, rather than 911. Explain that 988 can connect callers with trained counselors and, in some areas, mobile crisis teams.
- If you have a family member with serious mental illness, develop a crisis plan that identifies preferred treatment providers, medication information, emergency contacts, and advance directive preferences before a crisis occurs. The NAMI Family-to-Family program provides guidance on crisis planning.
- Complete a Mental Health First Aid training course to learn how to recognize and respond to mental health crises in your community: <https://www.mentalhealthfirstaid.org>. Over 2.5 million Americans have been trained since the program's U.S. launch in 2008.
- If you or a family member has experienced the civil commitment system, consider sharing your story with advocacy organizations or legislative committees -- lived experience is the most powerful tool for changing policy. Both NAMI and the Treatment Advocacy Center maintain story-sharing platforms.
- Create or update a psychiatric advance directive (PAD), which allows you to document your treatment preferences while you have decision-making capacity, for use during a future crisis when you may not. PADs are legally recognized in 27 states (NDRN, 2024).
- Learn your state's specific involuntary hold process -- who can initiate a hold, what the timeframes are, and what rights the person has -- so that you are prepared if a family member or neighbor experiences a psychiatric crisis.

### Use Your Voice

- **Contact Your Representatives**: Call your state legislators about specific mental health funding bills, commitment law reform, and AOT implementation. State-level action is where most civil commitment reform occurs. Contact your federal representatives when Medicaid policy (IMD exclusion), SAMHSA funding, or 988 appropriations are under consideration. The most effective contacts are brief, specific, and personal.
- **Submit Public Comments**: When your state mental health agency proposes regulatory changes to commitment procedures, AOT implementation rules, or crisis system standards, submit public comments. These comment periods are often poorly publicized and receive minimal input, meaning that a small number of informed comments can have outsized influence.
- **Write Letters to the Editor and Op-Eds**: After media coverage of incidents involving untreated mental illness, write letters that reframe the issue from individual tragedy to systemic treatment failure. Emphasize funding and infrastructure, not just legal standards. Local newspapers remain influential with state legislators.
- **Share Information**: Use social media to share data on the treatment gap, the psychiatric bed shortage, and the 988 crisis system. Use the hashtags #MentalHealthReform, #TreatmentNotTrauma, and #FixTheTreatmentGap to connect with the broader advocacy community.
- **Testify at Public Hearings**: State legislative committees regularly hold hearings on mental health budgets and commitment law reform. Public testimony from constituents -- particularly family members and individuals with lived experience -- can influence committee votes. Contact your state NAMI affiliate for information on upcoming hearings.

## Community Actions

### Organize Locally

- **Attend county mental health board meetings**. These boards often control local funding for crisis services, AOT implementation, and community treatment programs. Most meet monthly, and public attendance is typically sparse. Your presence and testimony can influence funding priorities. Ask about specific allocation decisions: how much goes to crisis services, how much to community treatment, how much to institutional care.
- **Form a local mental health advocacy group** that brings together family members, people with lived experience, providers, and community members to advocate for specific improvements in your local crisis response and treatment system. Set concrete goals: a mobile crisis team, a crisis stabilization center, funded AOT services.
- **Partner with your local NAMI affiliate** to organize community forums on civil commitment, AOT, and the treatment gap. Invite state legislators, local mental health officials, law enforcement, and people with lived experience to participate. Forums that include all perspectives are more credible and more useful than those that present only one side.
- **Work with your local police department** to advocate for Crisis Intervention Team (CIT) training for all officers and the development of co-responder models that pair mental health clinicians with law enforcement on mental health calls. CIT International provides training resources and implementation guidance: <https://www.citinternational.org>
- **Engage local hospitals** in advocacy for psychiatric emergency service capacity. Ask hospital administrators about their psychiatric boarding rates, their policies for accepting patients on involuntary holds, and their relationship with the 988 crisis system. Hospital-based clinicians can be powerful advocates for system reform.
- **Support local peer-run organizations** that provide crisis alternatives, including peer respite centers, warm lines, and recovery support. These organizations often operate on shoestring budgets and benefit from volunteer time, small donations, and community visibility.

### Build Coalitions

- **Connect family advocacy organizations (NAMI affiliates) with disability rights organizations** to identify shared priorities. Both constituencies support increased funding for community mental health services, even when they disagree on commitment policy. Starting with shared goals (more funding, better crisis response, workforce development) builds trust for more difficult conversations about commitment standards.
- **Engage homeless service providers, emergency department staff, and jail officials** in advocacy for community mental health investment. These groups experience the consequences of the treatment gap daily and can be powerful voices for reform. Emergency department nurses, in particular, can provide compelling testimony about the human cost of psychiatric boarding.
- **Reach out to faith communities**, which are often the first point of contact for families dealing with mental illness. Many congregations are open to mental health education and advocacy partnerships. Organizations like Pathways to Promise (<https://www.pathways2promise.org>) and Mental Health Ministries provide training resources.
- **Connect with local chapters of the American Psychiatric Association, National Association of Social Workers, and American Nurses Association** to build clinician-citizen advocacy partnerships. Mental health professionals lend clinical credibility to advocacy efforts, while citizen advocates provide the political constituency that clinicians alone cannot generate.
- **Engage business leaders and chambers of commerce** by framing mental health investment as an economic development issue. The productivity costs of untreated mental illness ($193 billion annually; NIMH, 2023) and the costs of emergency service utilization affect local economies directly.

## Political Actions

### Contact Elected Officials

**When to Contact**:

- During your state's budget process, when mental health funding levels are set (typically January-June in most states)
- When commitment law reform bills are introduced in your state legislature
- When AOT implementation bills are pending
- When Congress considers Medicaid policy changes, including the IMD exclusion
- When SAMHSA reauthorization and block grant funding levels are debated
- After high-profile incidents involving untreated mental illness, to advocate for systemic reform rather than reactive legislation
- When your state is considering competency restoration reform or forensic system expansion
- When the 988 crisis system receives funding appropriations

**What to Say - Template Email**:

```text
Subject: Invest in Community Mental Health to Fix Our Civil Commitment System

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

I am writing as a constituent from [City/District] about the crisis in our mental health system. Our state has only [X] psychiatric beds per 100,000 residents -- far below the expert-recommended minimum of 50 per 100,000. Over [X] of our counties have no practicing psychiatrist. As a result, people with serious mental illness cycle through emergency departments, jails, and homelessness because they cannot access the community treatment they need.

I urge you to support [specific bill/funding request]. Research consistently shows that investing in community mental health services -- including crisis stabilization centers, assisted outpatient treatment backed by real services, and supported housing -- reduces emergency department use, incarceration, and homelessness while respecting individual rights and improving outcomes.

The cost of inaction is far greater than the cost of investment. Each day of incarceration costs approximately $100, while community mental health treatment costs approximately $35-50 per day. Treatment is both more effective and more cost-efficient than the status quo.

[OPTIONAL PERSONAL PARAGRAPH: As a family member of someone with serious mental illness / as a mental health professional / as a community member who has witnessed this crisis, I have seen firsthand how the lack of treatment infrastructure harms people and communities.]

Thank you for your attention to this critical issue.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your City, State]
```

**What to Say - Phone Call Script**:

```text
"Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [City]. I'm calling about
mental health funding and civil commitment reform. I'd like
[Representative/Senator] to support [specific bill or funding increase] to
expand community mental health services and ensure that people with serious
mental illness can access treatment before they reach crisis. Our state has
only [X] psychiatric beds per 100,000 people and [X] counties with no
psychiatrist. The treatment gap is costing our community in emergency
department boarding, incarceration, and homelessness. Can you please note my
position? Thank you."
```

### Support Legislation

Current and proposed legislation to support:

- **CCBHC Permanent Authorization**: Bills to make Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinics a permanent Medicaid benefit would expand access to comprehensive community treatment. Contact your members of Congress to support full CCBHC authorization with enhanced federal matching.
- **988 Funding**: The 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline needs sustained federal funding for mobile crisis teams and crisis stabilization centers. Support appropriations for the 988 crisis system and the Crisis Now model of comprehensive crisis response.
- **IMD Exclusion Repeal**: Bipartisan bills to repeal or modify the Medicaid IMD exclusion have been introduced in multiple Congresses. Contact your senators and representative to support repeal paired with community investment and Olmstead protections.
- **State AOT Implementation**: If your state has an AOT statute but has not adequately implemented it, advocate for state appropriations to fund the services needed to fulfill AOT orders. If your state is among the three without AOT (CT, MD, MA), advocate for adoption with strong due process protections and service delivery requirements.
- **Mental Health Workforce Bills**: Support legislation expanding the National Health Service Corps, funding new psychiatry residency positions, establishing peer specialist certification, and creating interstate telehealth licensure compacts.
- **Competency Restoration Reform**: Support legislation establishing maximum wait times for competency evaluation and funding community-based restoration programs as alternatives to inpatient restoration for nonviolent defendants.
- **Mental Health Parity Enforcement**: Support legislation strengthening enforcement of the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act, including requirements for insurer compliance reporting and penalties for violations.

### Electoral Engagement

**Questions to Ask Candidates**:

1. "What specific steps will you take to expand community mental health services so that people with serious mental illness can access treatment before they reach crisis?"
2. "Do you support expanding assisted outpatient treatment, and if so, will you also fund the community services needed to make AOT orders meaningful?"
3. "What is your plan to address the psychiatric bed shortage in our state? Will you support repealing or modifying the Medicaid IMD exclusion?"
4. "How will you ensure that civil commitment reforms include protections against racial disparities and due process violations?"
5. "What is your position on the 988 crisis system, and will you support full funding for mobile crisis teams and crisis stabilization centers?"
6. "How will you address the competency restoration backlog, where individuals wait months in jail for forensic evaluation?"

**Voting Considerations**:

- Look for candidates who address mental health as a systems issue, not just through the lens of individual tragedies or law enforcement response
- Key state committees: Health and Human Services, Judiciary, Appropriations
- Key Congressional committees: Energy and Commerce, Finance, Judiciary, Appropriations, Health subcommittees
- Check whether candidates have specific mental health policy platforms or merely generic statements of concern
- Look for candidates who acknowledge both the treatment access crisis and the civil rights dimensions of commitment reform, rather than framing the issue as a one-sided debate
- Examine candidates' voting records on mental health appropriations, not just their rhetoric

## Professional Actions

- **For Mental Health Clinicians**: Advocate within your professional organizations (APA, NASW, ANA) for stronger policy positions on community treatment funding and workforce development. Serve on state mental health planning councils, which are required to include clinicians and individuals with lived experience. Volunteer for Crisis Intervention Team training for law enforcement. Participate in AOT clinical teams. Provide pro bono evaluations for individuals in commitment proceedings. Join the American Academy of Psychiatry and the Law (AAPL) if you have forensic interests.
- **For Attorneys**: Volunteer as court-appointed counsel in commitment cases. Many jurisdictions lack specialized mental health law attorneys, and pro bono representation can improve due process outcomes. Join the Mental Health Law Section of the American Bar Association. Advocate for specialized mental health court divisions in your jurisdiction. Review your state's commitment statute for due process gaps and propose model reforms.
- **For Law Enforcement Officers**: Advocate for CIT training in your department (40-hour training available through CIT International). Support co-responder models that pair clinicians with officers on mental health calls. Share data on mental health-related calls to support the case for dedicated crisis response resources. Advocate for department partnerships with the 988 crisis system.
- **For Emergency Department Staff**: Document and report psychiatric boarding data -- hours of boarding, number of patients, and impact on ED operations. Advocate within your hospital system for psychiatric emergency service capacity, including dedicated psychiatric evaluation areas. Support 988 and crisis stabilization alternatives to ED-based crisis care. Join the American College of Emergency Physicians (ACEP) psychiatric boarding task force.
- **For Educators**: Incorporate mental health literacy into school curricula, using programs like Mental Health First Aid Youth and the Signs of Suicide (SOS) program. Train teachers and school counselors to recognize early signs of serious mental illness. Support school-based mental health services that connect students to treatment before crises develop. Advocate for school-based mental health funding.
- **For Journalists**: Cover civil commitment as a systemic policy issue, not just individual tragedies. Use data from SAMHSA, Treatment Advocacy Center, and BJS to contextualize incidents. Interview people with lived experience and family members alongside officials and advocates. Avoid framing mental illness as synonymous with violence. Investigate your state's commitment system: bed capacity, wait times, AOT implementation, forensic backlogs, and ED boarding rates.
- **For Researchers**: Conduct rigorous evaluations of commitment and AOT programs with attention to racial disparities and civil rights outcomes. Publish accessible summaries of research findings for policymakers and advocates. Serve as expert witnesses in commitment proceedings and legislative hearings. Develop standardized capacity assessment tools for commitment evaluations.
- **For Social Workers and Case Managers**: Advocate for adequate caseload limits in AOT and community treatment programs. Document service delivery gaps and unmet needs. Participate in state mental health planning processes. Support individuals in understanding their rights during commitment proceedings.
- **For Peer Support Specialists**: Share your lived experience with legislators, journalists, and community forums. Advocate for peer specialist certification and Medicaid reimbursement in all states. Provide peer support to individuals experiencing or recovering from involuntary treatment. Help design trauma-informed approaches to commitment proceedings.

## Advocacy Toolkit

### Key Talking Points

When discussing civil commitment reform with others, emphasize:

1. **The treatment gap is the root problem**: 3.9 million Americans with serious mental illness are not receiving treatment (SAMHSA, 2023). The civil commitment crisis is fundamentally a treatment access crisis, not a legal standards problem.
2. **Community investment saves money**: Treatment costs a fraction of incarceration. The average daily cost of community mental health treatment is approximately $35-50, compared to $100 for jail and $500-1,000 for state psychiatric hospitalization (Vera Institute, 2023; NASMHPD, 2023). Every dollar invested in community treatment saves $2-4 in emergency and criminal justice costs (RAND, 2020).
3. **AOT works when backed by services**: New York's Kendra's Law reduced hospitalization by 77% and incarceration by 87% -- but only because it was paired with intensive community services (Duke University/NYS OMH, 2009). Court orders without treatment infrastructure are meaningless.
4. **Both rights and treatment matter**: The debate should not be "more commitment" vs. "more rights." The answer is both: strengthen due process protections while simultaneously building the treatment infrastructure that makes involuntary intervention a genuine last resort.
5. **The 988 system needs full funding**: The 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline has demonstrated the demand for non-law-enforcement crisis response (7.5 million contacts in its first 18 months), but fewer than 10% of calls currently result in mobile crisis team dispatch because teams are not available in most areas.
6. **The IMD exclusion must be repealed**: This 1965 Medicaid provision creates a financial incentive for states to close psychiatric hospitals. It is the single most important policy obstacle to adequate inpatient capacity.

### Common Misconceptions to Counter

| Misconception | Reality | Source |
|---------------|---------|--------|
| "People with mental illness are violent" | Only 3-5% of violence is attributable to serious mental illness; people with SMI are far more likely to be victims of violence than perpetrators | American Psychiatric Association, 2023 |
| "Commitment is the only way to get treatment to people who need it" | The vast majority of individuals with untreated SMI cannot access voluntary treatment because it is unavailable, unaffordable, or inaccessible -- not because they refuse it | SAMHSA, 2023 |
| "Deinstitutionalization caused homelessness" | Deinstitutionalization without community investment caused the problem. Countries that built community infrastructure during deinstitutionalization (Italy, UK) did not experience the same crisis. | Grob, 1994; OECD, 2023 |
| "AOT doesn't work" | The Cochrane Review found that AOT without services doesn't work. Kendra's Law, with services, showed dramatic reductions in hospitalization, arrest, and incarceration. The court order activates services; without services, it is merely paper. | Cochrane, 2017; Duke/NYS OMH, 2009 |
| "Civil commitment is needed more now because mental illness is increasing" | The prevalence of serious mental illness has remained relatively stable at approximately 5.5% of adults. What has increased is the gap between need and available treatment. | NIMH, 2023 |
| "People who refuse treatment are making a free choice" | An estimated 40-50% of individuals with schizophrenia have anosognosia -- a neurological inability to recognize their own illness. For these individuals, treatment refusal is a symptom, not a choice. | Amador, 2000 |
| "We need more beds, not community services" | Beds and community services are both necessary. Without community services, hospital beds become a revolving door. The optimal system invests primarily in community treatment (reducing the need for beds) while maintaining adequate bed capacity for those who need it. | Treatment Advocacy Center, 2023 |
| "The commitment system is colorblind" | Black Americans are 2.4 times more likely to be involuntarily committed and up to 5 times more likely to be subject to AOT orders than white Americans. Racial disparities must be monitored and addressed. | Snowden et al., 2009; NYLPI, 2005 |
