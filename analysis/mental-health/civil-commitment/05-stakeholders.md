# Civil Commitment and Involuntary Treatment: Stakeholders

The civil commitment system is shaped by the interactions of several key groups, each with distinct interests, authority, and resources. The fundamental challenge is that those most directly affected -- individuals with serious mental illness -- often have the least political power, while those with the most influence over the system -- legislators, insurers, and organized interest groups -- face incentives that do not align with optimal patient outcomes.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Individuals with serious mental illness (SMI) | Directly subject to commitment proceedings; may lose liberty, be forced to take medication, experience lasting stigma | 14.1 million adults with SMI (SAMHSA, 2023) | Autonomy, access to voluntary treatment, dignity, recovery, housing, employment |
| Individuals subject to involuntary holds | Experience emergency detention, potential long-term commitment, loss of rights | ~1 million annual involuntary holds (SAMHSA, 2022) | Due process, humane conditions, least restrictive treatment, right to counsel |
| Family members and caregivers | Bear emotional, financial, and practical burden of caring for relatives with SMI; often unable to obtain treatment for loved ones | ~8.4 million family caregivers of adults with SMI (NAMI, 2023) | Access to treatment for relatives, information sharing, respite, safety |
| People experiencing homelessness with SMI | Disproportionately subject to commitment and law enforcement contact; lack stable housing needed for community treatment | ~250,000 (30-40% of chronically homeless; SAMHSA, 2023) | Housing, voluntary treatment access, protection from arbitrary detention |
| Incarcerated individuals with mental illness | Receive inadequate treatment in custody; face longer sentences and harsher conditions | ~550,000 in jails (44% of jail population; BJS, 2021) | Treatment, diversion from incarceration, reentry support |
| Forensic patients awaiting competency restoration | Detained in jails for months or years while waiting for forensic hospital beds | ~50,000 (NASMHPD, 2023) | Timely evaluation, appropriate treatment, due process, release from prolonged detention |

### Demographic Profile of Individuals Subject to Commitment

Individuals subject to civil commitment and AOT are not a random cross-section of the SMI population. They are disproportionately:

- **Male**: Men constitute approximately 55-60% of individuals subject to involuntary commitment (state reporting data, 2022-2023)
- **Racial and ethnic minorities**: Black Americans are 2.4 times more likely to be involuntarily admitted than white Americans, controlling for diagnosis (Snowden et al., *Psychiatric Services*, 2009). In New York, Black individuals are approximately 5 times more likely to be subject to AOT orders (New York Lawyers for the Public Interest, 2005)
- **Unhoused or unstably housed**: An estimated 30-40% of individuals subject to repeated involuntary holds are experiencing homelessness (SAMHSA, 2023)
- **Diagnosed with schizophrenia spectrum disorders**: Approximately 60-70% of committed individuals have a primary diagnosis of schizophrenia or schizoaffective disorder (state hospital data, 2023)
- **With co-occurring substance use disorders**: An estimated 50-60% of committed individuals have a co-occurring substance use disorder (NASMHPD, 2023)
- **Low-income or uninsured**: The majority of individuals in state hospitals are Medicaid-eligible or uninsured (NASMHPD, 2023)

### Secondary Stakeholders

- **Victims of violence by untreated individuals with SMI**: Although rare (3-5% of all violence; APA, 2023), incidents involving individuals with untreated SMI receive outsized media coverage and drive legislative change. Victims and their families advocate for broader commitment authority. Notable advocacy organizations include the Kendra Webdale Foundation and families involved in post-tragedy legislative campaigns.
- **Emergency department staff**: Psychiatric boarding consumes ED resources and staff time. Nurses and physicians face burnout from managing psychiatric crises in environments not designed for mental health care. The Emergency Nurses Association (2023) reported that 73% of ED nurses identified psychiatric boarding as their greatest safety concern. The American College of Emergency Physicians (ACEP) has called psychiatric boarding a public health crisis.
- **General public in communities with high homelessness**: Residents in cities with large visible homeless populations experience both compassion fatigue and safety concerns, driving political pressure for expanded commitment. This constituency has become increasingly vocal in cities like San Francisco, Portland, Seattle, and New York, where homelessness associated with untreated mental illness is a dominant political issue.
- **Taxpayers**: Bear the cost of emergency services, incarceration, and acute care that results from the failure to provide less expensive community-based treatment. The RAND Corporation (2020) estimated that the annual cost of the treatment gap -- including emergency, criminal justice, and social service costs -- exceeds $100 billion nationally.
- **Public defenders and legal aid attorneys**: Represent individuals in commitment proceedings, often with overwhelming caseloads. A 2021 NARPA study found that attorneys in commitment cases spend an average of 15 minutes per case in many jurisdictions.
- **Peer support specialists and consumer advocates**: Individuals with lived experience of mental illness and the commitment system who provide direct services, advocacy, and an essential perspective on the impact of involuntary treatment on recovery.

## Who Has Power

### Decision Makers

| Actor | Role | Authority | Resources | Influence Level |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| State legislatures | Set commitment standards, funding levels, and AOT statutes | Plenary authority over state mental health law | Vary by state; control state hospital funding | High |
| State mental health agencies | Administer state hospitals, community programs, and AOT implementation | Regulatory authority; allocate state mental health funds | SAMHSA block grants + state appropriations | High |
| Judges and hearing officers | Preside over commitment hearings; issue commitment and AOT orders | Judicial authority to deprive liberty; interpret statutes | Limited; depend on clinical testimony and available services | High |
| Governors | Appoint mental health agency heads; propose budgets; sign legislation | Executive authority; bully pulpit | State budget proposals | High |
| U.S. Congress | Sets Medicaid policy (including IMD exclusion), SAMHSA funding, ADA enforcement | Federal legislation and appropriations | Control of federal mental health spending | High |
| CMS (Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services) | Administers Medicaid, including IMD exclusion waivers | Regulatory authority over federal healthcare spending | Medicaid budget ($800+ billion total, 2024) | High |
| SAMHSA | Administers federal mental health programs and block grants | Program authority; data collection | $7.5 billion budget (FY2024) | Medium |
| Law enforcement | Initiates majority of involuntary holds; first responder for mental health crises | Authority to detain for emergency evaluation | Police budgets vary widely | Medium |
| Psychiatrists and clinical staff | Conduct evaluations, provide testimony, administer treatment | Clinical authority; expert testimony | Limited independent resources | Medium |
| County mental health boards | Allocate local mental health funding; oversee community programs | County-level authority over local services | County appropriations | Medium |
| Hospital administrators | Determine admission policies, bed allocation, discharge practices | Institutional authority over patient flow | Hospital operating budgets | Medium |

### Key Organizations and Institutions

- **Treatment Advocacy Center (TAC)**: The leading national advocacy organization for expanding access to treatment for individuals with serious mental illness, founded in 1998 by psychiatrist E. Fuller Torrey, author of *American Psychosis* and *The Insanity Offense*. TAC supports broader commitment standards, robust AOT implementation, and increased psychiatric bed capacity. Publishes influential reports including the "Grading the States" series and model legislation. Annual budget approximately $5 million.
- **National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI)**: The largest grassroots mental health advocacy organization in the U.S., founded in 1979 by Harriet Shetler and Beverly Young. Over 600 state and local affiliates. NAMI supports AOT when implemented with adequate services and due process. Represents primarily family members and individuals with lived experience. Annual budget approximately $50 million (national office and affiliates combined). NAMIWalks fundraising events raise approximately $25 million annually.
- **Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law**: The leading legal advocacy organization for the rights of people with mental disabilities, founded in 1972. Named for Judge David Bazelon, a pioneer in mental health law who authored the *Rouse v. Cameron* decision. Opposes expanded commitment authority and advocates for voluntary, community-based services. Files amicus briefs in key cases and publishes policy analysis. Annual budget approximately $3 million.
- **American Psychiatric Association (APA)**: The professional association of psychiatrists, with over 37,000 members. Publishes the *Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders* (DSM) and practice guidelines. Supports evidence-based commitment standards and adequate resources for treatment. Generally cautious about both overly broad and overly narrow commitment criteria. Annual budget approximately $60 million.
- **American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU)**: Opposes expansion of involuntary commitment on civil liberties grounds. Files litigation challenging commitment conditions, forced medication, and inadequate due process. The ACLU's Disability Rights Project has been active in challenging expanded commitment in multiple states. Annual revenue over $300 million.
- **National Disability Rights Network (NDRN)**: Coordinates the federally funded protection and advocacy (P&A) system in each state, established under the Protection and Advocacy for Individuals with Mental Illness (PAIMI) Act. P&A agencies represent individuals in commitment proceedings, monitor conditions in institutions, and investigate abuse and neglect. Federal funding approximately $40 million annually.
- **National Association of State Mental Health Program Directors (NASMHPD)**: Represents state mental health agency directors. Develops policy recommendations and technical assistance. Developed the Crisis Now model for comprehensive crisis response systems. Annual budget approximately $20 million.
- **National Council for Mental Wellbeing**: The membership organization for community mental health providers. Leads the CCBHC demonstration and advocates for community-based treatment expansion. Annual budget approximately $50 million.
- **Mental Health America (MHA)**: National advocacy organization founded in 1909 by Clifford Beers, a former psychiatric patient whose autobiography *A Mind That Found Itself* (1908) launched the mental hygiene movement. MHA provides screening tools, advocacy, and public education. Annual budget approximately $15 million.

### Influencers

- **Media organizations**: Coverage of individual tragedies involving untreated mental illness shapes public opinion and legislative action. High-profile cases (subway pushing incidents, mass shootings, street-level psychiatric crises in major cities) generate pressure for expanded commitment. The *New York Times*, *San Francisco Chronicle*, and *ProPublica* have published influential investigative series on the civil commitment crisis.
- **Academic researchers**: Mental health law scholars and clinical researchers produce the evidence base that informs policy. Key centers include the MacArthur Foundation Research Network on Mental Health and the Law (University of Virginia), the Program in Psychiatry and Law at Harvard Medical School, the Bazelon Center's research division, and university-based psychiatric research programs at Columbia, Duke, and UCLA. Key scholars include John Monahan (violence risk assessment), Paul Appelbaum (mental health law), Marvin Swartz (AOT evaluation), and Michael Perlin (commitment rights).
- **Legal aid organizations**: Represent individuals in commitment proceedings. In states where public defenders handle commitment cases, caseload pressures often result in minimal representation. Organizations like Disability Rights New York, Protection and Advocacy, Inc. (California), and Equip for Equality (Illinois) provide more specialized representation.
- **Faith-based organizations**: Churches, synagogues, mosques, and other religious institutions are often the first point of contact for families dealing with mental illness. The National Alliance on Mental Illness has developed faith-based mental health partnerships, and organizations like Pathways to Promise and the Mental Health Ministries Network provide training and resources.

## Stakeholder Interests and Alignment

### Aligned Interests (Potential Coalition Partners)

| Coalition | Shared Interest | Strength |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| NAMI + TAC + APA | Increased funding for community mental health services | Strong |
| NAMI + TAC + law enforcement associations | Expansion of crisis intervention teams and 988 crisis system | Strong |
| Bazelon Center + ACLU + NDRN + disability rights organizations | Strong due process protections in commitment proceedings | Strong |
| All stakeholders (rare consensus) | Ending psychiatric boarding in emergency departments | Moderate |
| NAMI + Bazelon Center (limited overlap) | Increased funding for voluntary treatment and supported housing | Moderate |
| TAC + some law enforcement + prosecutors | Expansion of AOT programs | Moderate |
| NAMI + APA + National Council | CCBHC expansion and permanent authorization | Strong |
| All mental health organizations | Mental health workforce development | Strong |
| NAMI + NDRN + MHA | Medicaid expansion and mental health parity enforcement | Moderate |

### Conflicting Interests

- **Autonomy vs. treatment access**: The Bazelon Center and ACLU prioritize individual autonomy and oppose expanded commitment, while NAMI, TAC, and many families argue that severe mental illness can impair the very capacity needed to make treatment decisions, and that refusing to treat is not respecting autonomy but abandoning people to suffering. This is the most fundamental and persistent conflict in mental health policy, and it has prevented the formation of a unified advocacy coalition for decades.
- **Legal protections vs. clinical judgment**: Defense attorneys and disability rights organizations advocate for robust adversarial proceedings with strict evidentiary standards, while psychiatrists and family advocates argue that legalistic processes delay treatment and produce outcomes driven by procedural technicalities rather than clinical need. The APA has taken intermediate positions, supporting both adequate due process and clinical authority.
- **State budgets vs. treatment adequacy**: State budget offices seek to contain mental health spending, while providers and advocates push for increased investment. The IMD exclusion amplifies this tension by making inpatient psychiatric care disproportionately expensive for states. The National Governors Association has generally supported IMD exclusion reform but has not made it a top legislative priority.
- **Local vs. state responsibility**: Counties often bear the immediate costs of psychiatric crisis response (ED boarding, jail housing) while states control commitment law and hospital funding, creating cost-shifting dynamics. In California, the state-county dynamic is particularly contentious, with counties arguing that state mandates for expanded commitment are unfunded mandates.
- **Research community divisions**: Academic researchers are divided on the effectiveness and appropriateness of AOT. Studies from Duke University (Marvin Swartz) and the Treatment Advocacy Center support AOT effectiveness, while research from Columbia (Lisa Dixon) and the Cochrane Collaboration emphasizes the limited evidence for coercion-specific effects. These academic divisions give both sides of the policy debate credible expert support.

### The Iron Triangle of Inaction

A stable pattern of interests perpetuates the status quo:

1. **State legislatures** avoid funding community mental health at adequate levels because the cost is high and the constituency is politically weak. NAMI's grassroots network is significant but cannot match the lobbying power of industries that would bear increased costs (insurers, hospitals, pharmaceutical companies).
2. **Insurance companies** resist full mental health parity enforcement because it increases costs. The insurance industry spends over $150 million annually on federal lobbying (OpenSecrets, 2023), dwarfing the combined lobbying expenditure of all mental health advocacy organizations.
3. **The federal government** maintains the IMD exclusion because repealing it would increase federal Medicaid spending by an estimated $5-10 billion annually (CBO estimate). The Congressional Budget Office scoring process treats this as a cost increase without crediting the offsetting savings in emergency, criminal justice, and social service spending.

These three actors share an interest in limiting spending, and the result is a system where commitment authority exists on paper but treatment resources do not exist in practice. Breaking the iron triangle requires either a major shift in public opinion (potentially catalyzed by tragedy) or a bipartisan policy entrepreneur willing to bear the political cost of increased spending.

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on civil commitment policy:

- **Lobbying**: TAC, NAMI, APA, and the insurance industry all maintain active lobbying operations at state and federal levels. The insurance industry spends over $150 million annually on federal lobbying (OpenSecrets, 2023), dwarfing the combined spending of mental health advocacy organizations (estimated at $10-15 million total). At the state level, NAMI affiliates and TAC are the most active mental health lobbyists, often appearing before health and human services committees during budget deliberations and commitment law reform hearings.
- **Litigation**: The ACLU, Bazelon Center, and NDRN/P&A agencies use strategic litigation to challenge commitment conditions, forced medication, and inadequate due process. Landmark cases (*O'Connor*, *Olmstead*, *Addington*) were driven by litigation advocacy. More recently, *Olmstead* enforcement actions by the Department of Justice have pushed states to expand community services, and P&A agencies have filed suit in multiple states challenging competency restoration backlogs.
- **Campaign Contributions**: Mental health advocacy organizations contribute minimal campaign funds compared to the healthcare industry. Mental health policy changes are more often driven by public opinion in the wake of tragedies than by campaign contributions.
- **Public Advocacy**: NAMI's grassroots network of over 600 affiliates provides a powerful voice in state legislatures. Media campaigns by TAC and NAMI have shifted public opinion toward supporting expanded treatment access. The Bazelon Center and ACLU use media to highlight civil liberties concerns. Social media has amplified the voices of consumer-survivor advocates and the Mad Pride movement.
- **Expertise and Data**: Academic researchers and organizations like TAC produce data (bed counts, commitment rates, outcomes research) that frame policy debates. The Cochrane Review and RAND studies on AOT effectiveness are frequently cited by both sides. TAC's "Grading the States" report card has become the most widely referenced source for state-by-state commitment policy comparison.
- **Tragedy-driven advocacy**: Families of individuals harmed by untreated mental illness (Kendra Webdale's family, families of mass shooting victims) become powerful advocates for specific legislative changes. This mechanism is politically effective but produces reactive rather than systemic reform. The "named law" phenomenon -- Kendra's Law, Laura's Law, Baker Act, Marchman Act -- reflects the political power of tragedy but also the limitation of reforms designed around individual cases rather than systemic analysis.
- **Judicial influence**: Federal and state judges shape the system through individual commitment decisions and through precedent-setting rulings on constitutional standards. Judges who preside over specialized mental health courts or commitment dockets develop expertise that general-jurisdiction judges lack, and their rulings can establish local practices that diverge significantly from statutory intent.
