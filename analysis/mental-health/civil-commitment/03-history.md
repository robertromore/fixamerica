# Civil Commitment and Involuntary Treatment: History

## Historical Development

The history of civil commitment in America is a story of dramatic swings between two failures: the abuse of unrestrained institutional power and the neglect that follows when that power is curtailed without building alternatives. Understanding this history is essential to designing a system that avoids both extremes. The recurring pattern -- coercion without treatment, followed by rights without resources -- has produced human suffering at every turn and left the fundamental questions of when, how, and under what conditions the state should intervene unresolved after more than two centuries of effort.

---

### The Asylum Era (1750s-1945): Institutional Confinement with Minimal Oversight

- **Colonial and Early Republic period**: Mental illness was primarily a family and community responsibility. Those deemed "lunatics" were confined in almshouses, jails, or private homes. No formal commitment process existed; local officials or family members could arrange confinement with little legal process. Virginia established the first public institution for people with mental illness in Williamsburg in 1773 -- the Eastern State Hospital -- but it served primarily as a custodial facility rather than a treatment center.
- **Dorothea Dix and the Asylum Movement (1840s-1870s)**: Reformer Dorothea Dix campaigned for the construction of state-funded mental hospitals after witnessing the confinement of mentally ill individuals in jails, almshouses, and cages. Between 1843 and 1880, over 30 state hospitals were established. The theory was "moral treatment" -- humane institutional care would cure mental illness through structured routines, productive labor, and a therapeutic environment. Thomas Story Kirkbride, a Philadelphia physician, designed the "Kirkbride Plan" for hospital architecture that became the standard template: linear buildings with staggered wings providing fresh air, natural light, and separate wards for different patient populations. Commitment during this era required only a physician's certificate or a family petition in most states.
- **Growth of State Hospital System (1880-1945)**: State hospitals expanded dramatically, but the original therapeutic optimism gave way to custodial warehousing as patient populations grew far beyond capacity. By 1900, over 150,000 patients were confined in state institutions. By 1940, the number exceeded 400,000. The transformation from therapeutic asylums to overcrowded warehouses had multiple causes: chronic underfunding, the admission of patients with chronic conditions (dementia, intellectual disability, epilepsy) for whom no treatment existed, and the use of commitment as a mechanism for removing socially undesirable individuals from public life, including immigrants, the poor, and women who defied social norms. Abuse, neglect, and involuntary sterilization became widespread. The eugenics movement provided pseudo-scientific justification for the involuntary sterilization of institutionalized individuals, a practice upheld by the Supreme Court in *Buck v. Bell*, 274 U.S. 200 (1927). Between 1907 and 1963, over 60,000 individuals in state institutions were forcibly sterilized (Reilly, 1991).
- **Legal Framework**: Most states permitted commitment based on a physician's certification that a person was "insane" or "in need of care and treatment." No meaningful adversarial process existed. Patients had no right to counsel, no right to hearing, and no mechanism for judicial review. The commitment process was closer to an administrative act than a legal proceeding. Elizabeth Packard, confined to an Illinois asylum by her husband in 1860 for disagreeing with his religious views, became an early advocate for patients' rights after her release, successfully lobbying for laws requiring jury trials before commitment in several states.

### Peak Institutionalization and Early Reform (1945-1965): The Seeds of Change

- **Post-World War II exposes**: Journalists and conscientious objectors who had served as attendants during the war published devastating accounts of conditions in state hospitals. Albert Deutsch's *The Shame of the States* (1948) documented filth, overcrowding, and brutality across multiple institutions. Life magazine published a photo essay titled "Bedlam 1946" showing patients at Byberry State Hospital in Pennsylvania living in conditions comparable to concentration camps. Mike Gorman's *Every Other Bed* (1956) documented that half of all hospital beds in the United States were occupied by psychiatric patients. These exposes shifted public opinion from acceptance of institutional confinement to horror at its conditions.
- **The National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH)**: Established in 1949, NIMH became the federal government's primary research and policy agency for mental health. Under the leadership of Robert Felix, NIMH promoted community-based treatment as an alternative to institutional confinement, laying the intellectual groundwork for deinstitutionalization. The Joint Commission on Mental Illness and Health, established by Congress in 1955, produced *Action for Mental Health* (1961), recommending a shift from large state hospitals to community mental health centers.
- **Peak institutionalization (1955)**: The state hospital census reached its all-time peak of 558,922 patients (National Institute of Mental Health, 1965). This represented approximately 340 patients per 100,000 population. The largest individual institutions -- Pilgrim State Hospital in New York (over 13,000 patients at peak), Byberry State Hospital in Pennsylvania, and Central State Hospital in Georgia -- were small cities unto themselves, with their own farms, power plants, and cemeteries.
- **Introduction of antipsychotic medication (1954)**: The FDA approved chlorpromazine (Thorazine), the first antipsychotic medication, fundamentally changing the treatment of schizophrenia and other psychotic disorders. Developed by French surgeons Henri Laborit and Pierre Deniker, chlorpromazine dramatically reduced hallucinations, delusions, and agitation in many patients, making community living possible for the first time. Within a decade, the drug was being administered to hundreds of thousands of institutionalized patients. State hospital populations began declining almost immediately after chlorpromazine's introduction, though the causal relationship between medication availability and deinstitutionalization is debated -- fiscal pressures, legal reforms, and ideological shifts all contributed.
- **Community Mental Health Act (1963)**: President John F. Kennedy signed the Community Mental Health Centers Construction Act on October 31, 1963, envisioning a network of 2,000 community mental health centers to replace state hospitals. Kennedy, whose sister Rosemary had been institutionalized and lobotomized in 1941, brought personal commitment to the issue. Congress authorized funding for construction but never fully funded the services. Only 789 centers were built (GAO, 1977), and many provided services primarily to the "worried well" rather than to individuals with serious mental illness discharged from state hospitals.

### Deinstitutionalization and the Due Process Revolution (1965-1985): Rights Without Resources

- **Rapid depopulation of state hospitals**: State hospital populations fell from 558,922 in 1955 to 132,164 by 1980 -- a 76% decline (NIMH). The pace was staggering: California reduced its state hospital population from 37,000 in 1959 to 7,000 by 1973. New York went from 93,000 patients in 1955 to 24,000 by 1978. Patients were discharged to communities that lacked the promised mental health infrastructure. Many ended up in nursing homes, board-and-care facilities, single-room-occupancy hotels, and on the streets.
- **Medicaid and the "IMD Exclusion" (1965)**: The creation of Medicaid included the "Institutions for Mental Diseases" (IMD) exclusion, which prohibited federal Medicaid payment for care in psychiatric facilities with more than 16 beds for patients aged 21-64. This single provision -- Section 1905(a)(B) of the Social Security Act -- created a powerful financial incentive for states to close large hospitals and shift costs to the federal government by moving patients to nursing homes, boarding houses, and community settings where federal Medicaid matching funds were available. The IMD exclusion was not designed to promote deinstitutionalization; it was intended to prevent the federal government from assuming the costs of state hospital systems. But its effect was to make every state hospital bed far more expensive for states than any alternative placement.
- **Landmark legal decisions**:
    - *Rouse v. Cameron*, 373 F.2d 451 (D.C. Cir. 1966): Judge David Bazelon held that a person committed to a psychiatric hospital has a right to treatment. If treatment is not provided, confinement becomes mere punishment without trial. This was the first federal appellate recognition of a right to treatment for committed persons.
    - *Wyatt v. Stickney*, 325 F. Supp. 781 (M.D. Ala. 1971): Federal Judge Frank Johnson found conditions at Alabama's Bryce Hospital unconstitutional and issued the first comprehensive judicial order prescribing minimum standards for institutional treatment, including staffing ratios, physical conditions, and individualized treatment plans. The case established the principle that commitment without adequate treatment violates constitutional rights.
    - *Lessard v. Schmidt*, 349 F. Supp. 1078 (E.D. Wis. 1972): Federal court struck down Wisconsin's commitment law, holding that civil commitment required due process protections analogous to criminal proceedings, including right to counsel, right to remain silent, and a "beyond reasonable doubt" standard of proof. The case was the first to apply rigorous due process analysis to civil commitment.
    - *O'Connor v. Donaldson*, 422 U.S. 563 (1975): The Supreme Court held that a state cannot constitutionally confine a nondangerous individual who is capable of surviving safely in freedom by himself or with the help of willing and responsible family members or friends. Kenneth Donaldson had been confined in the Florida State Hospital at Chattahoochee for nearly 15 years despite posing no danger, after his father committed him based on paranoid delusions. The Court's opinion, written by Justice Potter Stewart, established the principle that a mental illness diagnosis alone is insufficient to justify involuntary confinement.
    - *Addington v. Texas*, 441 U.S. 418 (1979): The Supreme Court established that the standard of proof for civil commitment must be at least "clear and convincing evidence," higher than the civil "preponderance" standard but lower than the criminal "beyond reasonable doubt" standard. Chief Justice Burger's opinion balanced the individual's liberty interest against the difficulty of proving psychiatric predictions.
    - *Youngberg v. Romeo*, 457 U.S. 307 (1982): The Court held that involuntarily committed persons have constitutionally protected liberty interests in safety, freedom from undue restraint, and minimally adequate training. The standard for determining whether these rights are met is whether professional judgment was exercised.
- **State law reforms**: Following these decisions, virtually every state rewrote its commitment statute between 1970 and 1985, narrowing criteria from "need for treatment" to "dangerousness" and adding procedural protections. The Uniform Law Commissioners drafted the Model Commitment Law in 1972, providing a template for state reform. By 1980, dangerousness had become the dominant criterion in most states, replacing the broader "need for treatment" standard that had prevailed for a century.

### The Consequences of Deinstitutionalization (1985-1999): Crisis Without Solutions

- **Emergence of mass homelessness (1980s)**: The intersection of deinstitutionalization, tightened commitment laws, loss of affordable housing (including the destruction of SRO hotels through urban renewal), cuts to federal housing programs under the Reagan administration, and inadequate community services contributed to a dramatic increase in visible homelessness. Studies in the late 1980s found that 25-50% of homeless individuals had serious mental illness (Torrey, 1988). The McKinney-Vento Homeless Assistance Act of 1987 was the first major federal response but provided emergency shelter rather than the supportive housing and treatment needed by individuals with SMI.
- **Transinstitutionalization to jails and prisons**: As state hospital beds disappeared, jails and prisons became the largest providers of mental health care. By 1992, the Los Angeles County Jail, Cook County Jail (Chicago), and Rikers Island (New York) were the three largest psychiatric facilities in the country (Torrey et al., 1992). This "transinstitutionalization" -- the movement of people with mental illness from psychiatric hospitals to correctional facilities -- was not a deliberate policy but the inevitable result of closing hospitals without building alternatives. A 1999 Bureau of Justice Statistics survey found that 16% of state prisoners and 16% of jail inmates reported a mental health condition.
- **"Dying with their rights on"**: Critics of the due process revolution argued that tightened commitment standards left severely ill individuals to deteriorate on the streets, cycle through emergency rooms, or die of exposure and self-neglect. The phrase "dying with their rights on," attributed to psychiatrist Darold Treffert in a 1973 article in *Prism* magazine, captured the argument that legal protections had become obstacles to treatment. Treffert argued that the pendulum had swung from "thank you, doctor" (automatic deference to clinical authority) to "your honor, my client" (legalization of treatment decisions) without stopping at a reasonable middle ground.
- **Family advocacy movement**: Organizations like the National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI, founded in 1979 by Harriet Shetler and Beverly Young in Madison, Wisconsin) gave voice to families frustrated by their inability to obtain treatment for severely ill relatives under the new legal regime. NAMI grew rapidly from a handful of families to over 600 state and local affiliates. Families became powerful advocates for broader commitment standards and outpatient commitment, arguing from direct experience that watching a loved one deteriorate while legal standards prevented intervention was its own form of cruelty.
- **Early AOT statutes**: North Carolina enacted the first outpatient commitment statute in 1983, allowing courts to order individuals to comply with treatment plans in the community. By the mid-1990s, approximately 40 states had some form of outpatient commitment authority, though most were rarely used due to lack of funded services and unclear enforcement mechanisms. The early statutes demonstrated that legal authority without treatment infrastructure was meaningless.

### The AOT Era and Olmstead (1999-2015): New Tools, Old Debates

- **Kendra's Law (New York, 1999)**: On January 3, 1999, Andrew Goldstein, a man with untreated schizophrenia who had been repeatedly hospitalized and released, pushed Kendra Webdale in front of a subway train in Manhattan, killing her. The incident galvanized New York to enact Kendra's Law (N.Y. Mental Hygiene Law 9.60), creating the most comprehensive AOT program in the nation. The law required a petition-based court process with specific eligibility criteria tied to treatment history and risk of deterioration. Unlike earlier AOT statutes, Kendra's Law was backed by a dedicated appropriation for enhanced services, including ACT teams and case management. The law was initially enacted as a temporary measure but was made permanent in 2017 after repeated evaluations demonstrated positive outcomes.
- **Olmstead v. L.C., 527 U.S. 581 (1999)**: The Supreme Court held that the unjustified institutionalization of persons with disabilities, including mental illness, constitutes discrimination under Title II of the Americans with Disabilities Act. Lois Curtis and Elaine Wilson, two women with mental illness and intellectual disabilities confined in a Georgia state hospital despite their treatment professionals' determination that they could be appropriately served in community settings, brought the case. The Court required states to provide community-based services when treatment professionals determine that community placement is appropriate, the affected person does not oppose such placement, and the placement can be reasonably accommodated. Justice Ginsburg's majority opinion established the principle that "institutional placement of persons who can handle and benefit from community settings perpetuates unwarranted assumptions that persons so isolated are incapable of or unworthy of participating in community life." *Olmstead* became the foundation for community integration litigation nationwide, with the Department of Justice filing enforcement actions in multiple states.
- **Laura's Law (California, 2002)**: California enacted its own AOT statute, but made implementation optional for counties. Los Angeles County became the first major county to implement the law in 2015 -- thirteen years after enactment. Uptake was extremely slow; fewer than 10 counties implemented the law in its first decade. The California experience demonstrated that optional AOT implementation produces minimal results when counties lack the political will or resources to participate.
- **Virginia Tech shooting (2007)**: On April 16, 2007, Seung-Hui Cho killed 32 people and wounded 17 others at Virginia Polytechnic Institute. Cho had previously been found to be a danger to himself by a special justice in a commitment hearing but was released to outpatient treatment rather than hospitalized, and the outpatient order was never enforced. The incident exposed multiple systemic failures: the lack of follow-through on commitment orders, gaps in reporting mental health adjudications to the National Instant Criminal Background Check System (NICS), and the absence of mechanisms to ensure individuals diverted to outpatient treatment actually received it. Virginia reformed its commitment laws, and Congress enacted the NICS Improvement Amendments Act of 2007, requiring all states to report mental health adjudications to the federal background check system.
- **Sandy Hook and the national conversation (2012)**: The shooting at Sandy Hook Elementary School in Newtown, Connecticut, by Adam Lanza, who had significant untreated mental health issues, renewed national debate about the intersection of mental illness, gun violence, and civil commitment. President Obama signed executive actions on gun violence and mental health and proposed $235 million in new mental health funding. The 21st Century Cures Act (2016) included mental health provisions, including the requirement for the establishment of an Assistant Secretary for Mental Health and Substance Use at HHS.
- **Continued growth of forensic populations**: The number of individuals found incompetent to stand trial and referred for restoration grew steadily throughout this period, creating massive backlogs in forensic state hospitals. By 2015, forensic patients occupied an estimated 40-60% of state hospital beds in many states, effectively converting civil psychiatric hospitals into extensions of the criminal justice system.

### Current Era (2015-Present): Renewed Urgency and Unresolved Tensions

- **COVID-19 pandemic**: The pandemic intensified mental health crises, increased emergency department psychiatric presentations, and further strained an already inadequate system. ED psychiatric visits rose 24% between 2019 and 2022 (CDC, 2023). Pediatric psychiatric emergencies increased 31% (AAP, 2023). The pandemic also disrupted community mental health services, with many programs shifting to telehealth and reducing in-person outreach, contributing to treatment disengagement among individuals with SMI.
- **988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (2022)**: The launch of the 988 number on July 16, 2022, created an alternative to 911 for mental health crises, processing over 7.5 million contacts in its first 18 months (SAMHSA, 2024). However, the system's connection to mobile crisis teams and treatment resources remains uneven. In many areas, 988 calls that require in-person response still result in 911 dispatch and law enforcement involvement because mobile crisis teams do not yet exist.
- **California SB 43 (2023)**: California expanded its definition of "gravely disabled" to include individuals with severe substance use disorders who cannot provide for personal safety, the most significant broadening of commitment criteria in a major state in decades. The law also expanded the definition to include the inability to provide for "personal safety" in addition to "food, clothing, and shelter."
- **California CARE Act (2022)**: Governor Newsom signed the Community Assistance, Recovery, and Empowerment (CARE) Act, creating a new civil court process -- CARE Court -- specifically for individuals with schizophrenia spectrum and other psychotic disorders. CARE Court allows family members, first responders, and others to petition for court-ordered treatment plans. The program began rolling out in October 2023 and represents one of the most significant innovations in civil commitment architecture in decades.
- **New York City CARE initiative (2022)**: Mayor Eric Adams directed first responders to involuntarily transport individuals who appeared to pose a danger to themselves, even if they were not imminently suicidal, expanding street-level commitment practice and sparking national debate about the boundary between protection and coercion.
- **Federal action**: The Bipartisan Safer Communities Act (2022) included $250 million for community mental health services and the 988 crisis system. The CCBHC demonstration continued expanding, with over 500 clinics in 46 states by 2024.
- **Ongoing debate**: The fundamental questions of the 1970s -- when should the state intervene, what process is due, and who decides -- remain unresolved, even as the human cost of the treatment gap becomes increasingly difficult to ignore. The current era is characterized by simultaneous pressure to broaden commitment authority (from families, law enforcement, and urban politicians) and to protect civil liberties (from disability rights organizations, the ACLU, and consumer-survivor advocates).

## Lessons from History

### Pattern 1: Pendulum Swings Between Coercion and Neglect

The history of civil commitment reveals a recurring pattern: periods of excessive institutionalization provoke reform movements that restrict commitment authority, which then leads to periods of neglect that eventually provoke calls for expanded commitment. Neither extreme has served people with serious mental illness well. The asylum era produced warehousing and abuse; the due process revolution produced homelessness and incarceration. The challenge is finding a stable middle ground with adequate resources to support it -- something no generation has yet achieved.

### Pattern 2: Legal Reform Without Resource Investment Produces Worse Outcomes

Every major legal reform in civil commitment history -- deinstitutionalization, the due process revolution, *Olmstead* -- has been undermined by the failure to fund the community services needed to make the new legal framework work. The Community Mental Health Centers Act promised 2,000 centers; 789 were built. *Olmstead* required community integration; twenty-five years later, many states still lack the community infrastructure to comply. AOT statutes authorize outpatient treatment in 47 states; only about 30 actively implement them. Changing legal standards without changing resource allocation simply shifts where people with mental illness suffer: from overcrowded hospitals to overcrowded jails, emergency departments, and streets.

### Pattern 3: Tragedy as Catalyst

Major policy changes in civil commitment have almost always been triggered by high-profile tragedies -- the death of Kendra Webdale, the Virginia Tech shooting, the Sandy Hook massacre, individual cases of neglect or abuse. This reactive pattern produces legislation shaped by emotional response rather than systematic analysis, and the resulting laws often address the specific failure mode of the precipitating event rather than the systemic problems underlying it. Kendra's Law addressed the specific scenario of a person with multiple hospitalizations who falls through the cracks of the outpatient system, but did not address the broader failure to fund community mental health. The Virginia Tech reforms focused on background check reporting but did not address the competency restoration crisis.

### Pattern 4: The Gap Between Law on the Books and Law in Action

Throughout the history of civil commitment, formal legal standards have diverged significantly from actual practice. Before the 1970s, broad commitment authority was exercised without regard for statutory procedures. After the due process revolution, narrowed standards were sometimes circumvented by clinical and law enforcement practices that did not match the law's intent. AOT statutes exist in 47 states but are actively implemented in only about 30. The gap between authorization and implementation is a persistent feature of this policy area and suggests that statutory reform alone -- whether broadening or narrowing authority -- is insufficient without corresponding changes in funding, training, and institutional practice.

### Pattern 5: The Federal Government as Absent Architect

The federal government has shaped the civil commitment landscape through Medicaid policy (the IMD exclusion), constitutional law (the due process revolution), disability rights law (*Olmstead*), and block grant funding (SAMHSA), but it has never established a coherent national mental health system or taken direct responsibility for ensuring adequate treatment capacity. The result is a system shaped by federal incentives and constraints but operated by states with widely varying resources and political will. This federal absence is both a cause of the current crisis and the single greatest obstacle to comprehensive reform.
