# Civil Commitment and Involuntary Treatment: Current State

## Legal Standards for Civil Commitment

All 50 states and the District of Columbia authorize some form of civil commitment, but the legal standards, procedural requirements, and available resources vary substantially. The basic framework requires the state to demonstrate that an individual meets specific criteria -- typically involving mental illness combined with dangerousness or grave disability -- through a judicial or quasi-judicial process. No federal statute establishes a uniform civil commitment code; instead, the constitutional floor set by Supreme Court decisions (*O'Connor v. Donaldson*, *Addington v. Texas*, *Youngberg v. Romeo*) constrains state discretion while leaving wide latitude for variation.

### Commitment Criteria by State

States use three primary standards for civil commitment, often in combination:

| Standard | Description | States Using (2024) |
|---|---|---|
| **Danger to self** | Imminent risk of self-harm or suicide due to mental illness | 50 states + D.C. |
| **Danger to others** | Imminent risk of harm to another person due to mental illness | 50 states + D.C. |
| **Gravely disabled** | Unable to provide for basic needs (food, shelter, safety) due to mental illness | 45 states + D.C. |
| **Need for treatment** | Deterioration likely without treatment; broader than dangerousness | 13 states |
| **Unable to make rational treatment decisions** | Impaired capacity to understand or weigh treatment options | 7 states |

Source: Treatment Advocacy Center, State Standards for Civil Commitment, 2024

The "need for treatment" and "unable to make rational treatment decisions" standards represent a broadening trend. States using these criteria -- including Iowa, Kansas, North Carolina, and Alaska -- can intervene before a full-blown crisis occurs, while states limited to dangerousness and grave disability must wait until the individual is in acute distress. Advocates for broader standards argue they align more closely with clinical realities, particularly for individuals with schizophrenia and severe bipolar disorder whose conditions deteriorate gradually. Opponents contend that these broader standards grant excessive state discretion and increase the risk of unnecessary confinement.

### Imminence Requirements

| Requirement Level | Description | Number of States |
|---|---|---|
| Imminent danger required | Harm must be likely in the near future (hours to days) | 26 states |
| Substantial likelihood | Danger need not be imminent but must be probable | 15 states |
| No explicit imminence requirement | Broader discretion for commitment | 9 states + D.C. |

Source: Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA), 2023

The imminence requirement is one of the most contested elements of commitment law. States that require imminent danger, such as Colorado, Illinois, and Pennsylvania, effectively mandate that a person be in active crisis before involuntary intervention can occur. This creates what clinicians call the "do-nothing-until-it's-too-late" paradox: the individual must deteriorate to the point of immediate peril before the state can act, by which time the crisis may have caused irreversible harm to the individual's health, housing, relationships, and social functioning. States with broader criteria, such as Hawaii and North Carolina, allow intervention during the deterioration phase, potentially preventing the crisis entirely but at the cost of lower certainty that involuntary treatment is necessary.

### Burden of Proof

Following *Addington v. Texas*, 441 U.S. 418 (1979), the constitutional minimum standard of proof for civil commitment is "clear and convincing evidence." Some states impose a higher standard:

| Standard of Proof | Description | Number of States |
|---|---|---|
| Clear and convincing evidence | Constitutional minimum per *Addington* | 38 states + D.C. |
| Beyond a reasonable doubt | Criminal standard; highest protection | 4 states (OR, WA, HI, NH) |
| Preponderance of evidence (initial hold only) | Lower standard for emergency detention | 8 states (for emergency holds only) |

Source: National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI), 2023

The four states using the beyond-a-reasonable-doubt standard -- Oregon, Washington, Hawaii, and New Hampshire -- apply the most protective burden of proof in the nation. Oregon adopted this standard through legislation in 1973, making it one of the earliest states to respond to the due process revolution. Critics of this elevated standard argue that it makes commitment nearly impossible to obtain in ambiguous cases, leaving clinicians and families without recourse even when serious deterioration is evident. Proponents counter that the severity of the liberty deprivation justifies the criminal standard of proof.

### Definition of "Gravely Disabled"

The definition of "gravely disabled" varies significantly across states and has become a focal point for recent legislative action:

| Approach | Description | Example States |
|---|---|---|
| Narrow (survival-level) | Unable to provide for basic physical needs of food, clothing, or shelter | California (pre-SB 43), New York, Illinois |
| Moderate (safety-inclusive) | Unable to provide for personal safety or meet basic needs | Texas, Florida, Ohio |
| Broad (deterioration-inclusive) | Likely to suffer substantial deterioration without treatment | North Carolina, Iowa, Alaska |

California's SB 43 (2023) expanded the state's definition of "gravely disabled" from the narrow survival-level standard to include individuals with severe substance use disorders who cannot provide for personal safety -- the most significant broadening of commitment criteria in a major state in decades. The law also added "personal safety" alongside the traditional "food, clothing, and shelter" framework. Implementation began in January 2024 and is being closely watched as a potential model for other states.

## Psychiatric Bed Capacity

### National Bed Shortage

The United States faces a severe shortage of psychiatric inpatient beds, a crisis that directly constrains the civil commitment system. When beds are unavailable, individuals meeting commitment criteria may be held in emergency departments for days or released without treatment.

| Metric | Value | Trend | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Total state psychiatric hospital beds | 37,679 | Stable since 2016 | Treatment Advocacy Center, 2023 |
| State hospital beds per 100,000 population | 11.7 | Down from 340 per 100,000 in 1955 | Treatment Advocacy Center, 2023 |
| Private psychiatric hospital beds | ~37,000 | Slight increase | American Hospital Association, 2023 |
| Total psychiatric beds (all settings) | ~100,000 | Stable | AHA Annual Survey, 2023 |
| Recommended minimum (expert consensus) | 50 beds per 100,000 | -- | Treatment Advocacy Center |
| Current rate vs. recommended | 23.5% of recommended minimum | -- | Calculated |
| Veterans Affairs psychiatric beds | ~5,400 | Slight decrease | VA, 2023 |
| Community hospital psychiatric units | ~25,000 beds | Declining | AHA, 2023 |

The bed shortage is not simply a numerical problem but a structural one. State psychiatric hospitals serve the most severely ill patients -- those committed involuntarily, forensic patients, and individuals who cannot be safely managed in community hospital settings. Private psychiatric hospitals and community hospital psychiatric units tend to admit patients voluntarily, accept only those with insurance, and impose shorter lengths of stay. The result is a two-tier system in which the state hospitals are overcrowded with the most acute patients while private beds may sit empty due to insurance limitations.

The disparity between states with the highest and lowest bed capacity is more than tenfold (Mississippi at 41.4 vs. Nevada at 3.9 per 100,000). This means that a person experiencing a psychotic crisis in Mississippi has access to roughly ten times more inpatient capacity per capita than the same person in Nevada. The states with the fewest beds tend to be in the West and Southwest, regions that also have some of the highest rates of homelessness among individuals with mental illness.

### Emergency Department Boarding

When psychiatric beds are unavailable, patients in mental health crisis are held in emergency departments (EDs) -- a practice known as "psychiatric boarding."

| Metric | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Average ED wait time for psychiatric patients | 7.4 hours (vs. 3.6 for all patients) | ACEP, 2023 |
| Psychiatric patients boarded > 24 hours | 21% | Psychiatric Services, 2022 |
| Psychiatric patients boarded > 72 hours | 8% | Psychiatric Services, 2022 |
| Longest documented boarding times | 7-14 days in some facilities | State reports, 2023 |
| States with anti-boarding laws | 12 | National Conference of State Legislatures, 2024 |
| Estimated annual cost of psychiatric ED boarding | $2.2 billion | Milliman, 2023 |
| ED nurses reporting safety concerns from psychiatric boarding | 73% | Emergency Nurses Association, 2023 |

Psychiatric boarding is not merely a logistical inconvenience; it is a form of de facto confinement without treatment. Patients in psychiatric crisis are held in emergency departments that lack psychiatric staff, therapeutic programming, or appropriate physical environments. Many are placed in restraints or kept in hallways. The Emergency Nurses Association (2023) reported that 73% of ED nurses identified psychiatric boarding as their greatest safety concern, citing assaults on staff and other patients. The American College of Emergency Physicians (ACEP) has called psychiatric boarding a "public health crisis" and supports legislative mandates for maximum boarding times.

Washington State enacted a landmark anti-boarding law in 2014 (RCW 71.05.050) that requires hospitals to accept patients on involuntary holds within a specified timeframe. Virginia, Connecticut, and Massachusetts have adopted similar measures. However, anti-boarding laws are effective only to the extent that beds are available; without increased capacity, they simply shift the problem from one facility to another.

## Assisted Outpatient Treatment (AOT)

### Adoption and Implementation

Assisted outpatient treatment (AOT) allows courts to order individuals with serious mental illness to comply with outpatient treatment plans as a condition of remaining in the community. AOT is sometimes called "outpatient commitment" or, in New York, "Kendra's Law."

| Metric | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| States with AOT statutes | 47 + D.C. | Treatment Advocacy Center, 2024 |
| States without AOT statutes | 3 (CT, MD, MA) | Treatment Advocacy Center, 2024 |
| States actively implementing AOT | ~30 | SAMHSA, 2023 |
| New York AOT participants (cumulative since 1999) | >85,000 | New York Office of Mental Health, 2024 |
| Current active AOT orders in New York | ~3,500 | New York Office of Mental Health, 2024 |
| California counties implementing Laura's Law | ~25 of 58 | California DHCS, 2024 |
| North Carolina AOT orders (annual) | ~1,200 | NC DHHS, 2023 |

The gap between AOT statutory authorization and implementation is one of the most significant features of the current landscape. Seventeen states have AOT statutes on the books but rarely or never use them, typically because the services needed to fulfill court orders are not funded. California illustrates this problem: Laura's Law was enacted in 2002, but implementation was left optional for counties. Two decades later, fewer than half of California's 58 counties participate, and those that do report chronic underfunding of the services needed to support court orders.

### Typical AOT Eligibility Criteria (New York Model)

AOT eligibility under New York's Kendra's Law (N.Y. Mental Hygiene Law 9.60) requires all of the following:

1. **Age**: 18 years or older
2. **Diagnosis**: Suffering from a mental illness
3. **Treatment history**: At least one of the following:
    - Two hospitalizations for mental illness in the preceding 36 months
    - One or more acts, threats, or attempts of serious violent behavior toward self or others in the preceding 48 months
4. **Deterioration**: Unlikely to survive safely in the community without supervision
5. **Treatment availability**: Outpatient treatment is available and the person has refused to participate voluntarily
6. **Necessity**: AOT is the least restrictive appropriate form of treatment

Other states use varying criteria. Florida's AOT statute requires a history of noncompliance that has led to hospitalization or incarceration at least twice in the preceding 36 months. Nevada's statute, enacted in 2019, closely follows the New York model. Some states permit broader eligibility, allowing AOT for individuals who are likely to deteriorate without treatment even without a documented history of hospitalization or violence.

### AOT Outcomes Evidence

| Study/Program | Finding | Source |
|---|---|---|
| New York Kendra's Law evaluation | 77% reduction in psychiatric hospitalization | Duke University/NYS OMH, 2009 |
| New York Kendra's Law evaluation | 83% reduction in arrests | Duke University/NYS OMH, 2009 |
| New York Kendra's Law evaluation | 87% reduction in incarceration | Duke University/NYS OMH, 2009 |
| New York Kendra's Law evaluation | 74% reduction in homelessness | Duke University/NYS OMH, 2009 |
| Nevada AOT pilot (2020-2023) | 55% reduction in ED visits | Nevada Division of Public and Behavioral Health, 2023 |
| North Carolina AOT evaluation | 57% reduction in hospitalization with extended order (>180 days) | Swartz et al., *American Journal of Psychiatry*, 1999 |
| Cochrane Review (meta-analysis) | No significant reduction in hospitalization from compulsory community treatment alone (absent adequate services) | Cochrane Collaboration, 2017 |
| RAND Corporation review | AOT effective when coupled with intensive services; coercion alone insufficient | RAND, 2020 |
| OCTET trial (UK) | No significant effect of Community Treatment Orders on readmission | Burns et al., *Lancet*, 2013 |

The evidence on AOT reveals a consistent pattern: court-ordered community treatment works when it is backed by funded, intensive services (as in the New York model), but the court order alone, without adequate service delivery, has no measurable impact on outcomes. This finding has profound policy implications: states that enact AOT statutes without appropriating funds for service delivery are creating the illusion of reform without the substance.

## The Commitment Process

### Typical Civil Commitment Procedure

Civil commitment generally proceeds through three phases:

1. **Emergency Detention (72-Hour Hold)**
    - **Initiated by**: Law enforcement officer, licensed clinician, or (in some states) family member petition
    - **Standard**: Reasonable cause to believe the person is mentally ill and poses imminent danger or is gravely disabled
    - **Duration**: 48-72 hours in most states (some permit up to 120 hours)
    - **Location**: Psychiatric emergency room, designated facility, or crisis stabilization unit
    - **Rights**: Right to be informed of reason for hold; right to contact attorney; right to hearing within statutory timeframe
    - **Volume**: An estimated 1 million emergency holds are initiated annually nationwide (SAMHSA, 2022)

2. **Probable Cause Hearing / Certification**
    - **Timeline**: Within 72 hours of detention (varies by state)
    - **Presided by**: Judge, magistrate, or hearing officer
    - **Representation**: Right to counsel (appointed if indigent in most states)
    - **Evidence**: Clinical testimony, police reports, witness statements
    - **Decision**: Continue involuntary hold or release
    - **Outcome rate**: Approximately 60-70% of emergency holds result in continued detention at the probable cause stage (state reports, 2022-2023)

3. **Extended Commitment Hearing**
    - **Timeline**: Typically within 14-30 days of initial detention
    - **Standard of proof**: Clear and convincing evidence (or beyond reasonable doubt in 4 states)
    - **Duration of commitment**: 90 days to 1 year (renewable)
    - **Review**: Periodic judicial review required (every 90-180 days in most states)
    - **Conversion to AOT**: In many states, individuals may be transitioned from inpatient commitment to AOT at the extended commitment hearing

### Procedural Protections

| Protection | Federal Constitutional Requirement | State Implementation |
|---|---|---|
| Right to notice of hearing | Yes (*Vitek v. Jones*, 1980) | All states |
| Right to counsel | Yes (*implicit in due process*) | All states; 37 provide appointed counsel |
| Right to independent evaluation | Varies by circuit | 42 states |
| Right to be present at hearing | Yes | All states |
| Right to cross-examine witnesses | Yes | All states |
| Right to appeal | Yes | All states |
| Standard of proof: clear and convincing | Yes (*Addington v. Texas*, 1979) | 46 states (4 states use higher standard) |
| Right to jury trial | Not constitutionally required | 6 states (TX, CO, WI, GA, WY, OK) |
| Right to refuse medication (separate proceeding) | Yes (*Washington v. Harper*, 1990) | 42 states require separate adjudication |

### Quality of Legal Representation

Despite formal rights to counsel, the quality of legal representation in commitment proceedings is widely criticized. A 2021 study by the National Association for Rights Protection and Advocacy (NARPA) found that court-appointed attorneys in commitment cases spend an average of 15 minutes per case in many jurisdictions, rarely call witnesses, almost never request independent evaluations, and frequently advise clients to consent to treatment rather than contest the petition. The Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law has documented that hearings in some states last less than five minutes. This pattern has led scholars to describe commitment counsel as "meet-and-greet lawyers" whose presence satisfies the formal constitutional requirement without providing meaningful advocacy (Perlin, *International Journal of Law and Psychiatry*, 2013).

## Guardianship and Capacity

### Guardianship of Persons with Mental Illness

Civil commitment and guardianship are distinct legal processes, but they often overlap for individuals with severe, chronic mental illness. A civil commitment order authorizes involuntary treatment; a guardianship order transfers decision-making authority from the individual to a court-appointed guardian. Both involve state restriction of individual autonomy, but they use different standards and serve different purposes.

| Metric | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Active adult guardianships in the U.S. | ~1.3 million | National Center for State Courts, 2023 |
| Guardianships involving persons with mental illness | ~30% (estimated) | American Bar Association, 2022 |
| States with limited/supported guardianship options | 36 | National Guardianship Association, 2024 |
| States requiring least-restrictive alternatives analysis | 43 | Uniform Law Commission, 2023 |
| Average cost of contested guardianship proceeding | $10,000-$15,000 | ABA, 2022 |

The Uniform Guardianship, Conservatorship, and Other Protective Arrangements Act (UGCOPAA), promulgated by the Uniform Law Commission in 2017, has been adopted or introduced in 15 states. It emphasizes supported decision-making as a less restrictive alternative to full guardianship, requiring courts to consider whether the individual's needs can be met through powers of attorney, representative payees, or other arrangements before imposing plenary guardianship. This aligns with the *Olmstead* principle that interventions should be the least restrictive necessary.

### Treatment Over Objection

When a committed individual refuses medication, separate legal standards apply. The Supreme Court addressed this in *Washington v. Harper*, 494 U.S. 210 (1990), holding that the state may administer medication to a prisoner against their will if they are dangerous and the treatment is medically appropriate. For civilly committed individuals, the standard varies by state.

| Standard | Application | States |
|---|---|---|
| Judicial review required | Court order needed for forced medication | 23 states |
| Administrative review | Hospital panel or medical director decision | 19 states |
| Clinical discretion | Treating psychiatrist decides | 8 states + D.C. |
| *Sell* standard (competence to stand trial) | Forced medication for competence restoration requires case-by-case judicial review | Federal courts (*Sell v. United States*, 2003) |

The distinction between states requiring judicial review and those permitting administrative or clinical discretion is significant. In judicial-review states, a committed patient who refuses medication may wait weeks for a court hearing, during which time they remain confined but untreated -- a situation that clinicians describe as "rotting with their rights on." In clinical-discretion states, medication may be administered more quickly but with less protection against overreach. The Bazelon Center and ACLU argue that judicial review is essential to prevent the abuse of forced medication; the APA and Treatment Advocacy Center argue that delays in medication administration prolong suffering and increase the risk of violence.

### Notable State Approaches

- **New York**: Most established AOT program (Kendra's Law, 1999); made permanent in 2017; robust evaluation data; ~3,500 active orders. New York also has a strong mental health court system and dedicated AOT coordinators in every county. The state spends approximately $32 million annually on Kendra's Law implementation (NYS OMH, 2024).
- **California**: Laura's Law (AOT) enacted 2002 but implementation left to counties; only ~25 of 58 counties participated as of 2024. SB 43 (2023) expanded "gravely disabled" definition to include severe substance use disorder. Governor Newsom's CARE Act (2022) created CARE Court, a new civil court process allowing family members, first responders, and others to petition for court-ordered treatment plans for individuals with schizophrenia and related psychotic disorders.
- **Florida**: Baker Act (1971) is one of the most-used involuntary examination statutes; over 215,000 Baker Act initiations in FY2022 (Florida DCF, 2023). Florida's high utilization rate reflects both a relatively low threshold for emergency examination (any "reason to believe" standard) and limited community alternatives, resulting in large numbers of holds that do not lead to commitment.
- **Texas**: Broad commitment authority but severe bed shortages; average wait for state hospital bed exceeded 90 days in 2023 (Texas Tribune, 2023). The state has 10 state psychiatric hospitals with approximately 2,400 beds, but over half are occupied by forensic patients awaiting competency restoration, leaving few beds for civil commitment.
- **Oregon**: Uses the beyond-a-reasonable-doubt standard for commitment, the highest protection in the nation. Oregon's Psychiatric Security Review Board provides a model for forensic oversight. However, the state faces severe bed shortages and one of the longest competency restoration backlogs in the country, with some defendants waiting over a year in jail for evaluation.
- **Virginia**: Overhauled commitment laws after the Virginia Tech shooting (2007), including mandatory outpatient treatment orders and improved background check reporting to NICS. In 2022, the state invested $230 million in crisis system expansion, including new crisis stabilization units and mobile crisis teams.

## Recent Trends

### Legislative Trends (2019-2024)

- **Expanding "gravely disabled" definitions**: California (SB 43, 2023) and several other states broadened criteria to include those unable to provide for personal safety or whose condition will substantially deteriorate without treatment. Washington State introduced similar legislation in 2024.
- **AOT expansion**: New states adopting or strengthening AOT laws; increased funding for implementation. Nevada enacted AOT in 2019; Vermont strengthened its statute in 2022.
- **Competence restoration reform**: Growing recognition that long waits for competency evaluation and restoration in criminal cases constitute de facto indefinite detention. Oregon, Washington, and Alabama have faced federal court orders to reduce forensic backlogs.
- **Psychiatric bed investment**: At least 18 states increased psychiatric bed funding between 2020 and 2024 (NASMHPD, 2024), including Texas ($450 million for new forensic and civil beds), Virginia ($230 million for crisis services), and New York ($1 billion for psychiatric infrastructure).
- **988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline**: Launched July 2022, providing an alternative to 911 for mental health crises; over 7.5 million contacts in first 18 months (SAMHSA, 2024). The 988 system has demonstrated the demand for non-law-enforcement crisis response but faces ongoing funding challenges.
- **CARE Court (California, 2022)**: Created a new civil court process specifically for individuals with schizophrenia spectrum and other psychotic disorders, allowing petitions from families, first responders, and community members. Implementation began in October 2023.
- **Red flag laws and mental health**: Several states have expanded extreme risk protection orders (ERPOs), which allow courts to temporarily remove firearms from individuals in crisis. While distinct from civil commitment, ERPOs interact with the commitment system and have prompted debate about the appropriate scope of preventive state intervention.

## Emerging Challenges

### Post-Pandemic Mental Health Demand

The COVID-19 pandemic significantly increased demand for mental health services. Emergency department visits for mental health crises among adults rose 24% between 2019 and 2022 (CDC, 2023). Pediatric psychiatric emergency visits increased 31% over the same period (AAP, 2023). Suicide rates among individuals aged 10-24 increased 36% between 2019 and 2023 (CDC, 2024). This surge has intensified bed shortages and increased reliance on emergency holds. The pandemic also disrupted community mental health services, with telehealth adoption partially compensating but in-person services, including ACT team visits and crisis stabilization, experiencing significant reductions during 2020-2021 that have not fully recovered.

### Workforce Shortages

Over 160 million Americans live in mental health professional shortage areas (HRSA, 2024). The shortage of psychiatrists, psychiatric nurses, and social workers constrains every aspect of the commitment system: emergency evaluation, inpatient treatment, outpatient follow-up, and AOT service delivery. The Association of American Medical Colleges (2023) projects a shortage of 14,280 to 31,091 psychiatrists by 2030. State psychiatric hospitals report vacancy rates of 15-25% for psychiatrists, with some states, including Mississippi, Oklahoma, and West Virginia, relying heavily on temporary and locum tenens physicians to staff facilities (NASMHPD, 2023). The workforce shortage is most acute in rural areas, where over 80% of counties have no practicing psychiatrist and telehealth infrastructure remains inadequate.

### Competency Restoration Backlog

An estimated 50,000 or more individuals are awaiting competency evaluation or restoration in state forensic systems at any given time (NASMHPD, 2023). Some defendants wait months or years in jail for transfer to forensic treatment facilities, raising both due process and Eighth Amendment concerns. *Jackson v. Indiana*, 406 U.S. 715 (1972), held that indefinite commitment solely for competency restoration violates due process, yet backlogs persist.

Federal courts have intervened in several states:

- **Oregon**: A federal judge found the state in contempt for failing to provide timely competency evaluations, with some defendants waiting over 14 months in jail (2023).
- **Washington**: The state was held in contempt repeatedly between 2015 and 2023 for failing to meet court-ordered deadlines for competency evaluation.
- **Alabama**: Federal litigation (2022-2024) challenged waits exceeding 300 days for forensic hospital admission.

The competency restoration crisis is compounded by the fact that many individuals referred for restoration have relatively minor charges (misdemeanors, nonviolent felonies) but cannot be diverted from the criminal process because community treatment alternatives are unavailable.

### Intersection with Homelessness

An estimated 30-40% of chronically homeless individuals have a serious mental illness (SAMHSA, 2023). Several cities, including New York City and San Francisco, have implemented or expanded involuntary removal policies targeting homeless individuals with severe mental illness, provoking intense debate about the appropriate use of civil commitment authority.

- **New York City**: Mayor Eric Adams announced the CARE initiative in November 2022, directing first responders to involuntarily transport individuals who appeared to pose a danger to themselves, even if not imminently suicidal. The initiative lowered the practical threshold for invoking commitment authority on the streets, prompting legal challenges from civil liberties organizations.
- **San Francisco**: Mayor London Breed expanded conservatorship authority and street-level mental health interventions in 2023-2024, using California's broadened SB 43 standards.
- **Portland, Oregon**: Increased encampment clearances raised questions about whether displacement without treatment constitutes constructive commitment.

These urban initiatives highlight the tension between visible homelessness as a political crisis and the legal and ethical constraints on using civil commitment as a response to housing instability. Critics argue that commitment authority is being repurposed as a tool of urban management rather than a medical intervention. Proponents contend that individuals living on streets in psychotic states are in immediate danger and that failing to intervene constitutes abandonment.

### Racial and Demographic Disparities

Documented disparities in commitment and AOT rates remain a persistent concern. Black Americans are overrepresented in involuntary commitment and AOT relative to their share of the population and their share of the SMI population. A 2009 study found that Black Americans were 2.4 times more likely to be involuntarily admitted to psychiatric facilities than white Americans, controlling for diagnosis and symptom severity (Snowden et al., *Psychiatric Services*, 2009). In New York, Black individuals are approximately 5 times more likely to be subject to AOT orders than white individuals (New York Lawyers for the Public Interest, 2005). These disparities reflect upstream inequities in access to voluntary treatment, cultural differences in help-seeking behavior, clinician bias in diagnostic assessment, and structural factors including poverty, housing instability, and disproportionate law enforcement contact.
