# Mental Health Workforce Shortage: Stakeholders

The mental health workforce shortage is shaped by the interactions of providers, patients, professional organizations, insurers, government agencies, educational institutions, and advocacy groups, each with distinct interests, resources, and levels of power.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| People seeking mental health care | Cannot access timely treatment; long wait times (avg. 48 days for new psychiatric appointment); delayed care worsens outcomes | 57.8 million adults with mental illness (SAMHSA, 2023) | Timely access to affordable, culturally responsive care |
| Behavioral health providers | Overwork, burnout, inadequate pay, administrative burden | ~700,000 licensed professionals | Fair compensation, manageable caseloads, reduced paperwork |
| Children and adolescents | Youth mental health crisis with insufficient child/adolescent specialists; school-based services lacking | 7.7 million youth with treatable mental health conditions (NAMI, 2023) | Access to age-appropriate care, school-based services |
| Rural communities | Geographic isolation from providers; 55% of counties lack any psychiatrist | ~60 million rural Americans | Any local provider access; telehealth; traveling clinicians |
| People with serious mental illness (SMI) | Most severe shortage of specialized providers; difficulty accessing medication management | ~14.1 million adults with SMI (SAMHSA, 2023) | Consistent access to psychiatrists and intensive services |
| Communities of color | Workforce lacks diversity; cultural and linguistic barriers; historical mistrust | Disproportionately affected populations | Culturally responsive care, providers who share backgrounds |

### Secondary Stakeholders

- **Primary care physicians**: Absorb unmet behavioral health demand; prescribe 79% of antidepressants despite limited psychiatric training (AMA, 2023). Many primary care providers report feeling inadequately trained to manage psychiatric medication, particularly for complex cases involving multiple psychiatric diagnoses or treatment-resistant conditions. The American Academy of Family Physicians reports that 40% of family physicians identify mental health as their greatest unmet training need (AAFP, 2023).
- **Emergency departments**: Serve as default crisis care when community providers are unavailable; psychiatric boarding -- holding patients in the ED awaiting inpatient psychiatric beds -- is a nationwide problem. The American College of Emergency Physicians estimates that psychiatric patients spend an average of 24 hours in the ED before disposition, compared to 4.5 hours for medical patients (ACEP, 2023). In some states, psychiatric boarding times exceed 72 hours.
- **Employers**: Workforce mental health affects productivity; employee assistance programs face same provider shortages; estimated $47.6 billion annual cost of untreated mental illness in lost productivity (NAMI, 2023). Large employers are increasingly recognizing behavioral health as a human capital issue: a 2023 Business Group on Health survey found that 77% of large employers rated mental health as a "top priority," up from 44% in 2019.
- **Criminal justice system**: Jails and prisons house people with mental illness due to community treatment gaps; estimated 44% of jail inmates have a mental health condition (DOJ, 2023). The largest mental health facilities in many states are county jails, not hospitals -- a consequence of the failure to build adequate community-based treatment capacity. Police departments increasingly recognize the workforce shortage as a driver of crisis calls: the National Alliance on Mental Illness estimates that law enforcement officers spend 21% of their time responding to mental health-related calls (NAMI, 2023).
- **Family caregivers**: Approximately 8.4 million Americans serve as unpaid caregivers for adults with mental illness, absorbing costs the system fails to provide (NAMI, 2023). These caregivers experience elevated rates of depression, anxiety, and physical health problems themselves, creating a secondary demand for behavioral health services that the system also cannot meet.
- **School systems**: Schools are increasingly expected to provide mental health services, particularly after the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act of 2022, but face the same workforce shortages as the broader system. School counselors, psychologists, and social workers are in short supply nationally, with many districts unable to fill open positions.
- **Veterans Affairs**: The VA operates one of the largest behavioral health systems in the country and faces chronic difficulties recruiting psychiatrists and psychologists to VA facilities, particularly in rural areas. VA behavioral health vacancy rates average 11% nationally but exceed 25% in some rural Veterans Integrated Service Networks (VA OIG, 2023).

## Who Has Power

### Decision Makers

| Actor | Role | Authority | Resources | Influence Level |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Congress | Funds NHSC, GME, SAMHSA; sets Medicare policy | Legislative authority over federal spending, GME cap | Controls ~$280 billion annual HHS budget | High |
| Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) | Sets Medicare reimbursement; approves Medicaid waivers | Rate-setting, coverage determinations, GME payment | Administers $1.5 trillion in annual spending | High |
| Health Resources and Services Administration (HRSA) | Administers NHSC, BHWET, HPSA designations | HPSA designation, workforce grant programs | ~$966 million NHSC budget (FY2024) | High |
| SAMHSA | Administers block grants, CCBHC program, 988 | Grant-making, program standards, workforce training | ~$9.4 billion budget (FY2024) | High |
| State legislatures | Set scope-of-practice laws, Medicaid rates, licensing | Licensing authority, Medicaid administration | Varies by state | High (state level) |
| State licensing boards | Control entry to practice, discipline, reciprocity | Licensing, examination, continuing education | Varies | Medium |

### Key Organizations and Institutions

- **American Medical Association (AMA)**: The largest physician organization; opposes scope-of-practice expansion for non-physicians; influential in GME policy through lobbying and political contributions (~$25 million annual lobbying, OpenSecrets 2023)
- **American Psychiatric Association (APA - physician)**: Represents ~37,000 psychiatrists; advocates for GME expansion and higher reimbursement; opposes prescriptive authority for psychologists
- **American Psychological Association (APA - psychology)**: Represents ~122,000 psychologists; advocates for prescriptive authority, training funding, and Medicare reimbursement for psychologists
- **National Association of Social Workers (NASW)**: Represents ~120,000 social workers; advocates for higher pay, reduced administrative burden, and licensure portability
- **American Association of Nurse Practitioners (AANP)**: Represents ~355,000 NPs; advocates aggressively for full practice authority in all states
- **National Council for Mental Wellbeing (formerly National Council for Behavioral Health)**: Represents ~3,400 community behavioral health organizations; leads CCBHC advocacy
- **National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI)**: Largest grassroots mental health advocacy organization; ~600 state and local affiliates; focuses on consumer/family perspectives

### Influencers

- **Insurance industry (AHIP, BlueCross BlueShield Association)**: Resist reimbursement rate increases; lobby against network adequacy mandates; ~$170 million annual federal lobbying (OpenSecrets, 2023). Insurers have a structural interest in maintaining low behavioral health reimbursement, as behavioral health represents only approximately 8% of total medical spending -- increases in behavioral health rates have minimal impact on insurer revenue but significant impact on provider economics.
- **Private equity firms in behavioral health**: Own a growing share of therapy practices and psychiatric facilities; influence through market consolidation and pricing. Private equity investment in behavioral health has grown substantially since 2018, with firms acquiring therapy group practices, residential treatment facilities, and substance use treatment centers. This consolidation can improve operational efficiency but often results in pressure on clinician caseloads and reduced compensation for non-ownership staff (Offodile et al., 2021).
- **Academic medical centers and teaching hospitals**: Control GME training slots; lobby to protect existing GME funding allocation formulas. The current GME funding formula directs resources primarily to institutions that had training programs in 1997, creating a structural advantage for established urban academic centers and a disadvantage for community-based training sites that have developed since the cap was imposed.
- **Accreditation bodies (ACGME, APA-CoA, CSWE, CACREP)**: Set training standards that determine pipeline capacity and quality. These bodies face a genuine tension between maintaining quality standards and expanding pipeline capacity -- stricter accreditation requirements reduce the number of training programs and graduates, while looser standards risk producing inadequately trained professionals.
- **Telehealth companies (Talkspace, BetterHelp, Cerebral, Done Health)**: Growing influence on workforce practice patterns and public expectations; spend significantly on direct-to-consumer advertising. These platforms employ or contract with thousands of behavioral health providers and have introduced new practice models (asynchronous messaging, brief check-ins) that challenge traditional assumptions about what constitutes a therapeutic session. Regulatory actions against some platforms (Cerebral's controlled substance prescribing practices; Done Health's ADHD diagnosis practices) have raised quality concerns about the telehealth-first model.
- **Philanthropic foundations**: Organizations such as the Robert Wood Johnson Foundation, the Hogg Foundation for Mental Health, and the Well Being Trust fund workforce research, pilot programs, and advocacy that shapes the policy agenda. Philanthropy has been particularly important in funding guaranteed income pilots and workforce diversity initiatives that lack federal funding.
- **State mental health authorities**: The directors of state mental health and substance use agencies (organized through the National Association of State Mental Health Program Directors, NASMHPD) administer state-level behavioral health services and are directly affected by workforce shortages in state hospitals, community programs, and crisis systems. They represent an important but often underutilized voice in federal workforce policy.

## Stakeholder Interests and Alignment

### Aligned Interests (Potential Coalition Partners)

| Coalition | Shared Interest | Strength |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| NAMI + NASW + AANP + patient advocates | Expand access through scope-of-practice reform and workforce expansion | Strong |
| Rural health organizations + NHSC + HRSA | Direct more providers to underserved areas through loan repayment and incentives | Strong |
| CCBHC operators + SAMHSA + National Council | Scale the CCBHC model with cost-based reimbursement | Strong |
| Primary care organizations + behavioral health groups | Integrate behavioral health into primary care through Collaborative Care Model | Moderate |
| Medical schools + residency programs + AAMC | Lift GME cap to expand all physician training | Moderate (but self-interested in funding formula) |

### Conflicting Interests

- **Physician organizations vs. NP/PA organizations**: The AMA and state medical societies oppose full NP practice authority, while AANP and AAPA advocate for it. This is the most intense and politically consequential conflict in mental health workforce policy.
- **Insurers vs. providers**: Insurers benefit from low reimbursement rates and narrow networks; providers want higher pay and reduced administrative burden. The two sides have fundamentally opposed financial interests.
- **Academic institutions vs. training sites**: Graduate schools benefit from high enrollment and tuition revenue; community agencies bear the cost of clinical training supervision without compensation.
- **Existing providers vs. expanded workforce**: Some established providers benefit from scarcity through higher fees and may be ambivalent about measures that would increase supply and reduce their market power.

### The Iron Triangle of Behavioral Health Underfunding

The shortage is maintained by an interlocking relationship among three powerful actors:

1. **Congress** sets low funding levels for NHSC, BHWET, and GME, constrained by budget pressures and the absence of a powerful behavioral health lobby
2. **CMS** sets low Medicare reimbursement rates that serve as benchmarks for commercial and Medicaid rates
3. **Insurers** use the resulting narrow networks and long wait times as a de facto rationing mechanism that holds down behavioral health spending

This triangle is sustained by the political weakness of mental health consumers relative to the organized power of the insurance and medical lobbies. Unlike other health constituencies -- cancer patients, HIV/AIDS activists, diabetes patients -- people with mental illness face stigma, fragmentation, and the very symptoms of their conditions as barriers to organizing effective political advocacy. The most organized patient advocacy group, NAMI, has approximately 600 state and local affiliates and an annual budget of roughly $60 million -- a fraction of the resources available to industry opponents.

## State-Level Stakeholder Dynamics

### States as Laboratories

State-level stakeholder dynamics vary enormously and often determine whether workforce reforms succeed or fail. Key variations include:

- **Scope-of-practice coalitions**: In states where NP full practice authority has been enacted (27 states + DC), coalitions of NPs, rural hospitals, patient groups, and businesses typically overcame opposition from state medical societies. In states where full practice authority has failed (e.g., Texas, California, Georgia), physician organizations maintained sufficient legislative influence to block reform.
- **Medicaid rate-setting**: States with higher Medicaid behavioral health reimbursement rates (e.g., Washington, Minnesota, Vermont) generally have better provider participation and shorter wait times. States with the lowest rates (e.g., Mississippi, Louisiana, Arkansas) have the worst provider shortages, as the financial incentive to accept Medicaid patients is minimal.
- **Licensing compact adoption**: The speed of PSYPACT and Counseling Compact adoption varies by state, with the most rapid adoption in states where rural access is a politically salient issue and the slowest adoption in states with strong licensing board opposition to external regulatory frameworks.

### Tribal and Native Communities

Native American and Alaska Native communities face the most extreme behavioral health workforce shortages in the nation. The Indian Health Service (IHS) has a psychiatrist vacancy rate exceeding 30% and relies heavily on locum tenens (temporary) providers, creating discontinuity of care for some of the most vulnerable populations. Tribal sovereignty creates additional complexity: tribal nations are sovereign entities with their own health systems, and federal workforce programs must navigate government-to-government relationships. Tribal behavioral health programs consistently report that cultural match -- having providers who understand Native cultural values and historical trauma -- is as important as raw provider numbers (IHS, 2023).

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on policy:

- **Lobbying**: The AMA spent $25.3 million on federal lobbying in 2023; AHIP and major insurers spent a combined $170+ million; mental health advocacy organizations spent a combined ~$15 million -- a fraction of their opposition (OpenSecrets, 2023)
- **Campaign Contributions**: Health sector PACs contributed $321 million in the 2022 election cycle, with insurance and physician groups as the largest contributors. Mental health-specific contributions were minimal (OpenSecrets, 2023)
- **Revolving Door**: Former CMS and SAMHSA officials frequently move to insurance companies, pharmaceutical firms, or lobbying firms, creating alignment between regulators and the industries they oversee
- **Public Advocacy**: NAMI's grassroots network (~600 affiliates) generates constituent pressure on legislators; the 988 campaign demonstrated the power of public awareness to drive policy change
- **Expertise/Information**: Professional associations (APA, AMA, NASW) provide testimony, white papers, and data that shape legislative understanding of the workforce; their framing of the problem often reflects their institutional interests
- **Litigation**: Legal challenges to parity enforcement (*Wit v. United Behavioral Health*, 2019) and insurer reimbursement practices have produced court decisions that compel behavioral health coverage improvements, indirectly affecting workforce demand and payment
- **Media and public opinion**: High-profile tragedies (school shootings, celebrity suicides) periodically elevate mental health in public discourse, creating windows of opportunity for workforce legislation. The 988 campaign demonstrated that sustained public awareness can generate durable policy change.

## The Missing Stakeholder: Patients as a Political Force

The most significant stakeholder dynamic in mental health workforce policy is the absence of an organized patient constituency with political power commensurate to the scale of the problem. Unlike other health domains -- where patient organizations (American Cancer Society, American Heart Association, American Diabetes Association) wield multi-billion dollar budgets, employ professional lobbyists, and mobilize millions of voters -- the mental health patient community is fragmented, under-resourced, and stigmatized.

NAMI is the closest equivalent, but with an annual budget of approximately $60 million and 600 affiliates, it is outspent 3:1 by the AMA alone and 10:1 by the insurance industry. Mental Health America, the Kennedy Forum, and the American Foundation for Suicide Prevention provide additional advocacy capacity, but the collective resources of the entire mental health advocacy sector are a fraction of what the insurance or physician lobbies deploy. This power imbalance means that workforce reforms -- which would benefit millions of Americans who cannot access care -- consistently lose to the organized interests of a smaller number of physicians and insurers who benefit from the status quo.
