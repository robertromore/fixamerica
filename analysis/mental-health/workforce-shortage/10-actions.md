# Mental Health Workforce Shortage: What You Can Do

The mental health workforce shortage affects every community in America. More than 160 million Americans live in federally designated Mental Health Health Professional Shortage Areas, 55% of U.S. counties lack even a single psychiatrist, and the average wait for a new psychiatric appointment exceeds 48 days. Citizen engagement -- at the individual, community, and political levels -- is essential to building the political will for the investments and reforms needed to close this gap.

---

## Individual Actions

### Learn and Stay Informed

- Follow HRSA's Health Professional Shortage Area data at <https://data.hrsa.gov/topics/health-workforce/shortage-areas> to understand your community's designation status
- Read the National Council for Mental Wellbeing's annual CCBHC reports at <https://www.thenationalcouncil.org/> to track progress on the most promising workforce model
- Subscribe to NAMI's policy updates at <https://www.nami.org/advocacy> for legislative action alerts on workforce bills
- Review your state's mental health workforce data through the Kaiser Family Foundation's state fact sheets at <https://www.kff.org/statedata/mental-health-and-substance-use-state-fact-sheets/>

### Make Personal Choices That Support the Workforce

- If you have insurance, use in-network behavioral health providers when possible; in-network utilization signals to insurers that demand exists and networks must be adequate
- If you encounter ghost networks (listed providers who are not actually accepting patients), report them to your state insurance commissioner and to the insurer -- complaints create enforcement pressure. Document the experience: note the date, insurer, listed provider name, and the reason the provider was unavailable (not accepting patients, wrong phone number, no longer at that location, etc.). Multiple documented complaints can trigger regulatory action.
- If you are considering a career in behavioral health, explore the NHSC loan repayment program at <https://nhsc.hrsa.gov/> -- commitments to underserved areas earn up to $50,000 in loan repayment. Also investigate state-level loan repayment programs, which often stack with federal programs.
- If you are a parent, advocate for school-based mental health services in your district -- school-based providers reach children who would otherwise go unserved. Attend school board meetings and ask about the school's counselor-to-student and psychologist-to-student ratios (national recommendations are 1:250 and 1:500 respectively; most schools fall far short).
- If you are a mental health consumer, consider requesting that your provider accept your insurance if they currently practice out-of-network. The more providers participate in insurance networks, the stronger those networks become. If your provider declines, ask them to explain why -- the answer often illuminates the reimbursement problems driving the shortage.
- If you are a supervisor or manager, advocate for behavioral health benefits in your workplace that include adequate network access, not just nominal coverage. Ask your company's benefits team to audit the behavioral health network for ghost providers and report findings to the insurer.

### Use Your Voice

- **Contact Your Representatives**: Urge support for the Mental Health Access Improvement Act (grants Medicare billing to LPCs and LMFTs), GME cap expansion for behavioral health specialties, and increased NHSC funding
- **Submit Public Comments**: When CMS proposes changes to the Medicare Physician Fee Schedule (typically published each summer), submit comments supporting higher reimbursement for behavioral health services and expanded provider categories
- **Write Letters to the Editor**: When your local newspaper covers mental health issues, write to highlight the workforce shortage as a root cause and advocate for specific legislative solutions
- **Share Information**: Use the key facts from this analysis -- 160+ million in HPSAs, 55% of counties without a psychiatrist, 48-day average wait -- in social media posts, community discussions, and conversations with elected officials

## Community Actions

### Organize Locally

- Form a community mental health access coalition that includes local providers, patients, families, faith leaders, and educators to advocate for workforce investment at the state and local level. Effective coalitions include diverse voices: parents of children who cannot access care, adults who have experienced long wait times, providers who are burned out, employers losing productivity, and law enforcement officers responding to crisis calls.
- Host community forums inviting local behavioral health providers to discuss the shortage and its impact on your area; invite local legislators or their staff. Use specific local data: how many Mental Health HPSAs are in your county? How many psychiatrists practice in your area? What is the average wait time for a new appointment? This data is available at <https://data.hrsa.gov/topics/health-workforce/shortage-areas>.
- Partner with local community health centers to advocate for CCBHC designation, which brings cost-based reimbursement, higher staff salaries, and reduced turnover. If your community already has a CCBHC, publicize its outcomes -- the data on salary increases, turnover reduction, and access expansion are compelling advocacy tools.
- Work with school boards to fund school-based mental health positions -- social workers, counselors, and psychologists embedded in schools reach underserved youth. Present the data: the national school psychologist ratio is 1:1,127 vs. the recommended 1:500 (NASP, 2023), and the school counselor ratio is 1:385 vs. the recommended 1:250 (ASCA, 2023).
- Organize local "mental health access audits" in which community members systematically call behavioral health providers listed in insurance directories to document ghost networks. Present findings to your state insurance commissioner, state legislators, and local media. The Senate Finance Committee's 2023 investigation found ~40% ghost network prevalence nationally, and local data is often even more striking.

### Build Coalitions

- Connect mental health advocacy with rural health advocacy; the workforce shortage is most acute in rural areas, and rural health organizations are powerful allies. The National Rural Health Association and state-level rural health associations are natural partners who bring political credibility in conservative-leaning districts where the shortage is most severe.
- Engage local chambers of commerce; employers lose an estimated $47.6 billion annually to untreated mental illness in lost productivity (NAMI, 2023), making workforce investment a business issue. Frame the issue in economic terms: untreated mental illness costs $3,600 per employee per year in absenteeism, presenteeism, and turnover (Center for Workplace Mental Health, 2023).
- Partner with law enforcement reform advocates; 44% of jail inmates have a mental health condition (DOJ, 2023), and police departments increasingly support mental health workforce expansion to reduce crisis calls. Police chiefs and sheriffs can be powerful bipartisan voices for behavioral health investment.
- Build alliances with faith communities; churches, mosques, and synagogues are often the first point of contact for people experiencing mental health distress and can be powerful advocates for professional workforce investment. Faith leaders in many communities have firsthand experience with the consequences of the shortage -- congregants who cannot find treatment, families in crisis, and the strain on pastoral care resources.
- Connect with veterans' service organizations (VFW, American Legion, DAV); veterans face behavioral health workforce shortages in both VA and civilian systems, and veterans' organizations bring significant bipartisan political influence. VA behavioral health vacancy rates average 11% nationally and exceed 25% in some regions (VA OIG, 2023).
- Engage university and graduate program leadership; academic institutions that train behavioral health professionals have a direct interest in expanding the pipeline and improving post-graduation employment conditions for their graduates. Deans of schools of social work, psychology departments, and nursing programs can be effective advocates for training funding and clinical placement infrastructure.

## Political Actions

### Contact Elected Officials

**When to Contact**:

- During Congressional budget and appropriations season (February-September), when NHSC, BHWET, and SAMHSA funding levels are set
- When the Mental Health Access Improvement Act or GME expansion legislation is introduced or moves through committee
- When your state legislature considers scope-of-practice bills (NP full practice authority, psychologist prescribing, interstate compact adoption)
- When CMS proposes the annual Medicare Physician Fee Schedule update (typically July, with comments due in September)

**What to Say -- Template Email**:

```
Subject: Support Mental Health Workforce Investment -- [Your State] Needs More Providers

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

As your constituent in [City], I am writing to urge your support for
federal investments to address the mental health workforce shortage.
More than 160 million Americans live in federally designated Mental
Health Professional Shortage Areas, and [your state-specific data --
e.g., "55% of counties in our state lack any psychiatrist" or "our
state ranks in the bottom 10 for behavioral health provider supply"].

I specifically ask that you support:

1. The Mental Health Access Improvement Act, which would allow
licensed professional counselors and marriage and family therapists
to bill Medicare directly -- expanding the effective provider supply
at minimal federal cost.

2. Increased funding for the National Health Service Corps behavioral
health program, which places providers in underserved communities
like ours.

3. Lifting the Graduate Medical Education cap for psychiatry and
behavioral health specialties, which has been frozen since 1997.

Without enough providers, insurance coverage mandates are hollow
promises. Our community needs a workforce adequate to meet the
demand for mental health care.

Thank you for your service and consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your City, State]
```

**What to Say -- Phone Call Script**:

```
"Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [City].
I'm calling about the mental health workforce shortage.
[Your state] has [state-specific data], and I'd like the
[Representative/Senator] to co-sponsor the Mental Health
Access Improvement Act and support increased funding for the
National Health Service Corps behavioral health program.
Can you please note my position? Thank you."
```

### Support Legislation

Current and proposed legislation to support:

- **Mental Health Access Improvement Act (H.R. 828 / S. 828)**: Grants direct Medicare billing authority to LPCs and LMFTs -- the single highest-impact, lowest-cost workforce reform available. This bill has had 300+ cosponsors in recent Congresses and is scored as approximately budget-neutral by CBO. Contact your Senators and Representative to co-sponsor.
- **Resident Physician Shortage Reduction Act**: Expands Medicare GME slots, including for psychiatry. Urge inclusion of behavioral health-specific set-asides and requirements that new slots be based in community settings and HPSAs.
- **CCBHC Reauthorization and Expansion**: Support legislation making the CCBHC model permanent with enhanced federal Medicaid matching. Contact Senate Finance and House Energy and Commerce Committee members. Emphasize the bipartisan track record: CCBHCs have been supported by administrations of both parties.
- **NHSC Reauthorization and Expansion**: Support increased appropriations for the NHSC behavioral health program and higher loan repayment awards. Emphasize the program's 80% one-year retention rate and its role as the most effective federal tool for placing providers in shortage areas.
- **Conrad State 30 and Physician Access Reauthorization Act**: Support permanent authorization and expansion of the Conrad 30 J-1 visa waiver program, which is a critical source of psychiatrists for underserved areas.
- **Telehealth Modernization Act**: Support permanent authorization of behavioral health telehealth flexibilities, including interstate practice and controlled substance prescribing via telehealth with appropriate safeguards.
- **State-level scope-of-practice bills**: Track and support bills in your state legislature granting full practice authority to NPs, authorizing psychologist prescribing, or adopting the PSYPACT and Counseling Compact. The National Academy for State Health Policy tracks these bills at <https://www.nashp.org/>.

### Electoral Engagement

**Questions to Ask Candidates**:

1. "Do you support lifting the Medicare GME cap for behavioral health specialties, which has been frozen since 1997 while the psychiatrist shortage has deepened?"
2. "Will you co-sponsor the Mental Health Access Improvement Act to allow licensed counselors and therapists to bill Medicare directly?"
3. "What is your plan to address the mental health provider shortage in [rural/underserved areas of your district]?"
4. "Do you support expanding the National Health Service Corps behavioral health program and increasing loan repayment awards to attract providers to underserved communities?"

**Voting Considerations**:

- Look for candidates who specifically address workforce supply, not just insurance coverage -- coverage without providers is an empty promise
- Pay attention to candidates' positions on scope-of-practice reform; candidates aligned with physician lobbies may oppose expanding NP and psychologist roles
- Check whether candidates support sustained appropriations for NHSC and BHWET, not just one-time authorizations
- The Senate HELP Committee and House Energy and Commerce Committee are the key committees for mental health workforce legislation; members of these committees have outsized influence

## Professional Actions

- **For Behavioral Health Providers**: Join your professional association's advocacy arm (APA, NASW, AANP, ACA) and participate in lobby days, public comment campaigns, and candidate forums. Your expertise lends credibility to advocacy. Consider serving as a clinical supervisor for pre-licensed clinicians; supervision capacity is a critical bottleneck in the workforce pipeline. If you practice in a well-served area, consider providing telehealth services to underserved populations through your state's telehealth programs or through interstate compacts (PSYPACT, Counseling Compact). Submit public comments when CMS proposes the annual Medicare Physician Fee Schedule (typically published each July with comments due in September).
- **For Primary Care Physicians**: Advocate within your practice and health system for Collaborative Care Model (CoCM) implementation. Train to use psychiatric consultation codes (99492-99494) and advocate for embedded behavioral health in your practice. Consider enrolling in Project ECHO programs for psychiatric consultation, which provide specialist mentoring to support your management of common psychiatric conditions. Primary care physicians prescribe 79% of antidepressants (AMA, 2023) -- greater competence in behavioral health management can bridge the gap while the specialist workforce grows.
- **For Educators**: Partner with behavioral health graduate programs to offer clinical training placements. If you lead an HBCU, HSI, or Tribal College, explore HRSA pipeline partnerships for behavioral health diversity. Consider developing undergraduate behavioral health career pathway programs that expose students to the field before they commit to graduate school. Faculty who mentor underrepresented students play a disproportionately important role in diversifying the pipeline.
- **For Employers and HR Leaders**: Expand Employee Assistance Program (EAP) coverage and advocate for adequate behavioral health networks in your company's insurance plans. Report ghost networks to your insurer and state regulator. Consider adding direct-contract behavioral health services as an employee benefit, bypassing insurance network limitations. Calculate the ROI of behavioral health investment: untreated mental illness costs approximately $3,600 per employee per year in productivity losses (Center for Workplace Mental Health, 2023), and adequate treatment access substantially reduces this cost.
- **For Students**: Explore careers in behavioral health, particularly psychiatry, psychiatric nursing, and clinical social work. Investigate NHSC scholarships and loan repayment programs before taking on debt -- the NHSC scholarship covers tuition, fees, and a living stipend in exchange for a service obligation in an HPSA. Consider practicing in an underserved area -- the need is greatest and the federal support is strongest. If you are from an underrepresented background, seek out HRSA-funded pipeline programs at your institution and national minority fellowship programs through APA, NASW, and SAMHSA.
- **For Hospital and Health System Leaders**: Advocate for behavioral health service line investment within your organization. Support the development of psychiatry residency and PMHNP training programs at your institution, particularly if located in an underserved area. Explore CCBHC certification for your behavioral health services to access cost-based reimbursement. Implement measurement-based care and structured clinical supervision programs to reduce clinician burnout and improve retention.
- **For State Legislators and Staff**: Review your state's scope-of-practice laws relative to evidence from states that have expanded NP authority and psychologist prescribing. Evaluate your state's participation in PSYPACT and the Counseling Compact. Examine your state's Medicaid behavioral health reimbursement rates relative to Medicare; states with the lowest rates consistently have the worst shortages. Consider legislation to fund state-level loan repayment programs that stack with federal NHSC programs.

## Advocacy Toolkit

### Key Talking Points

When discussing the mental health workforce shortage with others, emphasize:

1. **The shortage is massive and measurable**: More than 160 million Americans live in federally designated Mental Health Professional Shortage Areas, and HRSA estimates the nation needs 8,220 additional practitioners just to remove existing shortage designations (HRSA, 2024).
2. **The shortage has real consequences**: 55% of U.S. counties have zero psychiatrists (NAMI, 2023). The average wait for a new psychiatric appointment is 48 days. Only 35.7% of psychiatrists accept new Medicaid patients (MACPAC, 2023).
3. **The training pipeline has been frozen for decades**: The Medicare GME cap has been virtually unchanged since 1997. Psychiatry residency programs fill at 99.7%, meaning demand for training far exceeds available slots (NRMP, 2024).
4. **Solutions exist and are proven**: The CCBHC model has demonstrated that cost-based reimbursement raises salaries 15-25%, reduces turnover to 18-22%, and expands access (NCCBH, 2023). The NHSC places providers in shortage areas with 80% one-year retention (HRSA, 2022). Scope-of-practice reform can add tens of thousands of providers to Medicare at minimal cost.
5. **Investment pays for itself**: Untreated mental illness costs the U.S. economy $280 billion annually in lost productivity, disability, and premature mortality (Lancet Commission, 2024). Workforce investment is infrastructure, not charity.

### Common Misconceptions to Counter

| Misconception | Reality | Source |
|---------------|---------|--------|
| "There are enough therapists -- people just don't want help" | 160+ million Americans live in areas with officially designated provider shortages; the problem is supply, not demand | HRSA, 2024 |
| "Telehealth has solved the access problem" | Telehealth extends reach but does not increase the total number of trained providers; only ~700,000 licensed behavioral health professionals serve 330+ million Americans | BLS/HRSA, 2023-2024 |
| "Expanding who can prescribe medications is dangerous" | States that allow psychologist prescribing and NP full practice authority show no increase in adverse events; the DoD has used prescribing psychologists safely since 1991 | Idaho Board of Psychologists, 2023; Cochrane Review, 2023 |
| "The market will fix the shortage if government gets out of the way" | The populations with the greatest need (rural, Medicaid, uninsured) have the least ability to pay; no market signal can make it profitable to practice in a rural county at 72% of already-low Medicare rates | MACPAC, 2023 |
| "We just need to train more psychiatrists" | The psychiatrist pipeline is important but takes 12+ years (college through residency). Expanding the full behavioral health workforce -- NPs, social workers, counselors, peer specialists -- can produce results in 2-5 years | AAMC/HRSA, 2023-2024 |
| "This is just a rural problem" | While rural areas face the most severe shortages, urban areas also have significant unmet need, particularly in low-income neighborhoods. Urban ghost networks and long wait times affect millions of city-dwellers | Senate Finance Committee, 2023 |
| "Higher pay will just attract people who are in it for the money" | Every profession requires adequate compensation to attract and retain qualified practitioners. Physicians, nurses, teachers, and engineers are all compensated at levels that reflect their training and responsibility. Behavioral health should be no different | BLS, 2023 |

## State-Level Action Guide

### Identifying Your State's Status

Before taking action at the state level, determine your state's current position on key workforce policies:

| Policy Area | Where to Check | Key Question |
|-------------|---------------|--------------|
| NP full practice authority | AANP at <https://www.aanp.org/advocacy/state> | Does your state grant full practice authority to PMHNPs? |
| Psychologist prescriptive authority | APA at <https://www.apa.org/advocacy/scope-of-practice> | Is your state among the 7 that authorize psychologist prescribing? |
| PSYPACT participation | PSYPACT at <https://psypact.org/> | Has your state enacted PSYPACT legislation? |
| Counseling Compact participation | <https://counselingcompact.org/> | Has your state enacted the Counseling Compact? |
| Medicaid behavioral health rates | KFF at <https://www.kff.org/statedata/> | What is your state's Medicaid behavioral health reimbursement as a percentage of Medicare? |
| CCBHC participation | SAMHSA at <https://www.samhsa.gov/certified-community-behavioral-health-clinics> | Does your state participate in the CCBHC model? |
| Mental Health HPSAs | HRSA at <https://data.hrsa.gov/topics/health-workforce/shortage-areas> | How many HPSAs are in your state, and what percentage of the state population lives in one? |

### State-Level Legislative Priorities

Based on your state's current status, prioritize the following actions:

1. **If your state lacks NP full practice authority**: Advocate for legislation granting PMHNPs independent practice authority. Connect with your state nurses association and identify rural legislators whose constituents face the most acute shortages.
2. **If your state has not adopted PSYPACT or the Counseling Compact**: Contact your state psychology board or counseling board to understand their position, and advocate for compact legislation with your state representative and senator.
3. **If your state's Medicaid rates are below 80% of Medicare for behavioral health**: Advocate for a Medicaid behavioral health rate increase in your state's budget process. Present evidence from states where rate increases have improved provider participation.
4. **If your state does not participate in the CCBHC model**: Advocate for your state to apply for CCBHC participation and provide the state match required for the enhanced federal funding.
