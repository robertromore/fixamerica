# Mental Health Workforce Shortage: History

## Historical Development

The mental health workforce in the United States has been shaped by cycles of institutional expansion and contraction, shifting federal priorities, changing models of care, and the evolving relationship between medicine, psychology, social work, and other professions. Understanding this history is essential to grasping why the current shortage exists and why past reform attempts have fallen short.

---

### The Institutional Era (Pre-1960): Asylum-Based Workforce

- **State Hospital System**: From the mid-19th century through the 1950s, the primary locus of mental health care was the state psychiatric hospital. At their peak in 1955, state hospitals held approximately 560,000 patients across 322 state-operated facilities. The workforce consisted primarily of psychiatrists (often in superintendent roles), nurses, and attendants, with minimal involvement of psychologists or social workers in direct care. Staffing ratios were chronically inadequate: a 1948 survey by the Group for the Advancement of Psychiatry found that the average state hospital had one psychiatrist per 443 patients, a ratio that would be considered grossly unsafe by any modern standard.
- **Psychiatrist Supply**: The number of psychiatrists was small -- fewer than 5,000 nationally in 1940 -- and heavily concentrated in institutional settings and a small number of urban private practices. Psychoanalytic training, which dominated the field from the 1920s through the 1960s, was limited to physicians and centered in a handful of urban institutes, further restricting the pipeline.
- **Clinical Psychology Emerges**: World War II was a turning point. The Veterans Administration (VA) needed thousands of professionals to treat returning servicemembers with "combat fatigue" (now PTSD) and other conditions. The VA funded graduate training programs in clinical psychology, creating the Boulder Model (scientist-practitioner) in 1949 and launching psychology as a clinical profession. By 1950, the VA had established 42 clinical psychology internship programs, creating the institutional infrastructure that would anchor psychology training for decades. The GI Bill simultaneously funded thousands of graduate students, many of whom entered psychology and social work programs.
- **Psychiatric Social Work**: The profession of psychiatric social work grew out of settlement houses and child guidance clinics in the early 20th century, but remained small and low-paid. The Smith College School for Social Work, founded in 1918, was the first specialized psychiatric social work program. The National Association of Social Workers (NASW) was founded in 1955, consolidating several smaller professional organizations and beginning the professionalization of social work as a distinct discipline.
- **Psychiatric Nursing**: Psychiatric nursing developed as a distinct specialty in the 1950s, with Hildegard Peplau's 1952 publication of *Interpersonal Relations in Nursing* establishing the theoretical foundations. The National League for Nursing began accrediting psychiatric nursing programs, but the specialty remained small relative to general nursing throughout this period.

### Deinstitutionalization and the Community Mental Health Era (1960-1980): Building a New Workforce

- **Community Mental Health Centers Act (1963)**: President Kennedy signed the Community Mental Health Centers (CMHC) Construction Act, envisioning a national network of 2,000 community centers to replace state hospitals. The Act created enormous new demand for outpatient-trained mental health professionals -- demand the existing workforce could not meet. The original legislation authorized construction funding but did not include dedicated workforce training provisions, an omission that would prove consequential. The Joint Commission on Mental Illness and Health, whose 1961 report *Action for Mental Health* informed the legislation, had recommended a massive investment in professional training, but this recommendation was not funded at the scale proposed.
- **Medicare and Medicaid (1965)**: The establishment of Medicare and Medicaid created new financing for mental health care but also introduced the payment structures that would come to shape workforce supply. Notably, the IMD exclusion (Institutions for Mental Diseases) barred federal Medicaid payment to psychiatric facilities with more than 16 beds, accelerating deinstitutionalization without building corresponding community capacity. Medicare initially covered psychiatric hospitalization but imposed a 190-day lifetime limit on inpatient psychiatric care, a restriction that did not apply to any other medical condition and was not fully removed until the ACA.
- **Graduate Medical Education Funding**: The Medicare program began financing residency training through GME payments, establishing the mechanism that still funds the majority of physician training. Psychiatry residency positions grew modestly during this period, but GME funding was allocated primarily to academic medical centers in urban areas, establishing a geographic concentration of training that persists to the present day.
- **National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH) Training Grants**: NIMH funded training in psychiatry, psychology, social work, and psychiatric nursing through direct grants to universities. At their peak in the 1970s, these grants supported an estimated 10,000 trainees annually across all behavioral health disciplines and represented the primary federal investment in the behavioral health workforce pipeline. NIMH training grants were instrumental in building the first generation of community mental health professionals and establishing clinical training standards that would shape the professions for decades.
- **The Counseling Profession Expands**: The 1960s and 1970s saw the emergence of professional counseling as a distinct behavioral health discipline. The Association for Counselor Education and Supervision (ACES) began accrediting counseling programs, and states began creating licensed professional counselor (LPC) credentials. By 1980, approximately 20 states had LPC licensure laws, though the profession would not achieve universal state licensing until decades later.
- **Workforce Gap Emerges**: Deinstitutionalization moved hundreds of thousands of patients out of state hospitals between 1955 and 1980 (the census fell from 560,000 to approximately 130,000), but the community mental health workforce was never built to scale. Only about 750 of the planned 2,000 CMHCs were ever constructed, and many of those that opened were understaffed from the beginning. The gap between need and supply that defines the current crisis began here. A 1978 President's Commission on Mental Health, chaired by Rosalynn Carter, documented the emerging workforce shortage and recommended major federal investment in training, but its recommendations were largely overtaken by the political changes of the early 1980s.

---

### Retrenchment and Managed Care (1980-2000): The Pipeline Contracts

- **Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act (1981)**: The Reagan administration replaced categorical CMHC funding with the Mental Health Block Grant, cutting federal mental health funding by 25% and giving states broad discretion. Many states used the flexibility to shift funds away from workforce development and toward direct services for the most acutely ill. The block grant structure also eliminated federal accountability for workforce targets, making it difficult to track or enforce training commitments.
- **NIMH Training Cuts**: Federal training grants through NIMH were dramatically reduced in the 1980s. Annual NIMH training support fell from approximately $120 million in the late 1970s (adjusted for inflation) to less than $30 million by the early 1990s. By 1990, federal support for behavioral health training was a fraction of its 1970s level, and the pipeline stagnated. The cut was especially damaging to minority recruitment programs that had been funded through NIMH minority fellowship programs.
- **Rise of Managed Care**: The expansion of managed behavioral health carve-outs in the 1990s compressed reimbursement rates, shortened inpatient stays, and imposed utilization review requirements. Companies such as Magellan Health, ValueOptions, and United Behavioral Health became gatekeepers for behavioral health services, implementing prior authorization requirements and session limits that reduced provider autonomy. For providers, this meant lower pay, more paperwork, and less clinical autonomy -- factors that reduced the attractiveness of behavioral health careers. Between 1990 and 2000, the share of psychiatrists accepting insurance declined significantly, and the trend toward private-pay practice accelerated (Olfson et al., 2015).
- **Balanced Budget Act of 1997**: Congress capped the number of Medicare-funded GME residency slots at roughly 1997 levels (~100,000 total across all specialties). This cap, which remains in effect, froze the primary federal mechanism for expanding physician training, including psychiatry. The cap was enacted as a cost-containment measure during a period of budget balancing, not as a deliberate workforce policy. It is widely regarded as the single most consequential structural barrier to expanding the psychiatrist supply.
- **Surgeon General's Report on Mental Health (1999)**: Surgeon General David Satcher issued the first-ever Surgeon General's report dedicated to mental health, which documented the gap between treatment need and provider availability and called for workforce expansion. The report brought mainstream visibility to the workforce shortage but did not produce corresponding legislative action.
- **Workforce Diversity Stalls**: Efforts to diversify the behavioral health workforce during this period were limited. The NIMH Minority Fellowship Program, which had supported training for underrepresented students, saw flat or declining funding. The pipeline of Black, Latino, and Native American psychologists and psychiatrists remained far below population proportions, a gap that narrowed only marginally over the following two decades.

### Mental Health Parity and the Access Crisis (2000-2019): Demand Rises, Supply Lags

- **Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act (MHPAEA, 2008)**: This landmark law required commercial insurers to cover behavioral health services on par with medical/surgical services. However, parity increased demand without correspondingly increasing supply, widening the gap between insured access and provider availability. The legislation was the culmination of a decade-long advocacy campaign led by former Representative Patrick Kennedy and Senator Paul Wellstone, who argued that differential coverage for mental health constituted discrimination. In practice, enforcement of the parity mandate was slow and inconsistent, with insurers finding creative ways to limit behavioral health access through narrow networks, prior authorization requirements, and low reimbursement rates that technically complied with parity while functionally restricting access.
- **Affordable Care Act (2010)**: The ACA expanded Medicaid to millions of new enrollees and required behavioral health coverage in Essential Health Benefits. This further increased the number of people with insurance coverage for mental health care, but the provider workforce was already strained. Medicaid expansion states saw particularly acute shortages as new patients entered a system without enough providers to serve them. Between 2010 and 2016, the number of Americans with behavioral health insurance coverage increased by an estimated 30 million, but the net increase in behavioral health providers was fewer than 50,000 -- a demand-supply mismatch that produced longer wait times and more out-of-network utilization (HHS, 2016).
- **National Health Service Corps Expansion**: The ACA significantly expanded NHSC funding, growing the behavioral health corps from approximately 2,000 participants in 2009 to over 5,000 by 2016. Behavioral health became the largest single category of NHSC placements, reflecting both the severity of the shortage and the attractiveness of loan repayment to debt-burdened graduates. The NHSC expansion was one of the ACA's most successful workforce provisions, demonstrating that financial incentives can direct providers to shortage areas.
- **Annapolis Coalition and the Action Plan**: The Annapolis Coalition on the Behavioral Health Workforce published its updated *Action Plan for Behavioral Health Workforce Development* in 2007 and revised it through the 2010s, providing the most comprehensive framework for federal workforce strategy. The Action Plan influenced HRSA's BHWET program design and SAMHSA's workforce grant priorities, though funding never approached the levels the Coalition recommended.
- **988 Planning Begins**: The Federal Communications Commission recommended designating 988 as the national suicide and crisis hotline number in 2019, setting the stage for a massive expansion of crisis services that would require thousands of additional counselors and crisis workers. The National Suicide Hotline Designation Act of 2020 codified the 988 number and required all phone carriers to route calls, creating an irreversible commitment to crisis infrastructure that would demand a corresponding workforce.
- **Psychiatric Nurse Practitioner Growth**: The number of Psychiatric-Mental Health Nurse Practitioner (PMHNP) programs expanded rapidly in the 2010s, driven by demand and the growing acceptance of NP practice. PMHNPs became the fastest-growing segment of the prescribing mental health workforce. Annual PMHNP program graduates approximately doubled between 2018 and 2024, from roughly 3,000 to 6,000 per year (AACN, 2024). This growth was accelerated by the Institute of Medicine's 2010 report *The Future of Nursing*, which recommended that NPs practice to the full extent of their training.
- **Scope-of-Practice Battles**: Multiple states debated and sometimes enacted expanded scope of practice for psychologists (prescriptive authority), nurse practitioners (full practice authority), and counselors (Medicare reimbursement eligibility). New Mexico (2002), Louisiana (2004), Illinois (2014), Iowa (2017), Idaho (2017), Utah (2022), and Colorado (2023) each enacted psychologist prescriptive authority after protracted legislative battles against organized physician opposition. These debates intensified as the shortage worsened but remained politically contentious, with the AMA consistently opposing expanded scope for non-physician providers.

### The Pandemic Era and Beyond (2020-Present): Crisis Meets Demand Surge

- **COVID-19 Impact on Demand**: The pandemic triggered a surge in anxiety, depression, substance use, and suicidality that persists to this day. Youth mental health reached crisis levels, with the Surgeon General issuing an unprecedented advisory on the youth mental health crisis in December 2021. Demand for behavioral health services increased 30-40% between 2019 and 2023 (APA, 2023), far outstripping supply growth. Emergency department visits for mental health crises among adolescents increased by 31% for girls and 24% for boys between 2019 and 2022 (CDC, 2023). The pandemic also exacerbated substance use: overdose deaths exceeded 100,000 annually for the first time in 2021 and have remained above that level, intensifying demand for SUD treatment providers.
- **Telehealth Revolution**: Emergency telehealth waivers during COVID-19 allowed providers to practice across state lines and prescribe controlled substances via telehealth. These flexibilities dramatically expanded access but remained temporary and dependent on annual extensions. The DEA's telehealth prescribing flexibilities for controlled substances, originally set to expire in 2023, have been extended multiple times but remain uncertain. The uncertainty itself has deterred investment in telehealth-based behavioral health delivery systems.
- **988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline Launch (2022)**: The 988 system went live in July 2022, immediately increasing demand for trained crisis counselors. SAMHSA provided over $1 billion in initial funding, but staffing crisis call centers remains an ongoing challenge. In its first full year, the 988 Lifeline received over 5 million contacts (calls, texts, and chats), a 40% increase over the previous year's National Suicide Prevention Lifeline volume (SAMHSA, 2023). Answer rates have improved but remain inconsistent across states.
- **Burnout Crisis**: The pandemic worsened already-high burnout rates among behavioral health professionals. Surveys in 2022-2023 showed 40-60% of clinicians reporting burnout and 30% considering leaving the field (APA, SAMHSA). The workforce was simultaneously being asked to do more and losing members at an accelerated rate. The National Council for Mental Wellbeing reported that 42% of community behavioral health organizations experienced a net decrease in clinical staff between 2020 and 2023, despite active recruitment (NCBH, 2023).
- **Consolidated Appropriations Act (2021)**: Congress authorized 1,000 new Medicare GME residency slots over five years (200 per year), the first increase in the GME cap since the Balanced Budget Act of 1997. Psychiatry was among the targeted specialties, but 200 slots per year across all specialties is widely considered insufficient -- a symbolic gesture against a structural problem.
- **CCBHC Expansion**: The Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinic model, which uses cost-based reimbursement rather than fee-for-service, expanded from 10 states in 2017 to 46 states by 2024. CCBHCs demonstrate that adequate reimbursement improves staff salaries, reduces turnover, and expands access. The CCBHC model has been supported by both Democratic and Republican administrations and represents the most significant bipartisan consensus on behavioral health financing reform in decades.
- **Bipartisan Safer Communities Act (2022)**: Included $1 billion for school-based mental health professionals and community mental health services, the largest single federal investment in behavioral health workforce in decades. The Act was enacted in the wake of the Uvalde, Texas school shooting and represented a rare bipartisan agreement on mental health investment, though critics noted that the funding was time-limited and insufficient to address the structural school-based workforce shortage.
- **PSYPACT and Licensing Compacts**: The Psychology Interjurisdictional Compact (PSYPACT) expanded from 15 states in 2020 to 41 states by 2024, enabling psychologists to practice telepsychology across state lines. The Counseling Compact similarly expanded to 36 states. These compacts represent the most significant progress on licensing portability in behavioral health history, though they do not cover all disciplines (notably excluding social work as of 2024) and do not increase the total supply of providers.

**Key Observations**: Total workforce numbers have grown substantially, but the rate of growth has consistently lagged behind the rate of demand growth. Psychiatrist supply has been nearly flat since 2000 due to the GME cap, retirement, and insufficient pipeline expansion. The fastest growth has been in counselors, PMHNPs, and peer specialists -- precisely the professions that face the most significant scope-of-practice, reimbursement, and credentialing barriers.

## Lessons from History

### Pattern 1: Demand Expansion Without Supply Investment

Every major expansion of mental health coverage or access -- community mental health centers, Medicaid, parity, the ACA, 988 -- has increased demand for providers without a corresponding investment in workforce supply. The pattern is consistent: legislation creates a right to care without creating the workforce to deliver it.

### Pattern 2: The Training Cap Trap

The 1997 GME cap demonstrates how a single fiscal constraint, imposed for short-term budget reasons, can create a structural shortage that persists for decades. The cap was never intended as a permanent workforce policy, but it has functioned as one.

### Pattern 3: Burnout Undermines Expansion

Efforts to expand access without addressing working conditions are self-defeating. The history of community mental health shows that underfunded, overworked settings generate high turnover, which erodes the very capacity the system is trying to build.

### Pattern 4: Incrementalism Versus the Scope of the Problem

The history of federal mental health workforce policy is a history of incremental measures -- modest NHSC expansions, small GME increases, pilot programs -- applied to a problem that is structural and systemic. Each intervention helps at the margin but does not alter the fundamental trajectory.

### Pattern 5: Professional Turf Protection Slows Adaptation

Throughout this history, established professional groups have resisted the expansion of newer professions into their scope of practice. Psychiatrists opposed psychologists providing therapy independently in the 1950s-1960s; psychologists and psychiatrists jointly opposed social workers and counselors seeking independent licensure in the 1970s-1980s; and the AMA has consistently opposed NP full practice authority from the 1990s to the present. Each expansion of scope was eventually accepted as safe and effective, but only after years or decades of political opposition that delayed access expansion.

### Pattern 6: The Diversity Pipeline Was Never Prioritized

At no point in the 75-year history of the modern behavioral health workforce has the federal government invested at scale in building a workforce that reflects the demographic composition of the population it serves. NIMH minority fellowship programs and HRSA diversity grants have consistently been small, time-limited, and underfunded relative to the scale of the disparity. The result is a workforce that in 2024 is nearly as demographically unrepresentative as it was in 1974, with particularly stark gaps in Black, Latino, and Native American provider supply.
