# Older Adult Mental Health: Solutions

Addressing the older adult mental health crisis requires a multi-faceted approach that simultaneously expands the workforce pipeline, reforms financing and coverage, builds community-level social infrastructure, improves institutional care quality, and leverages technology to extend access. No single intervention can overcome the interlocking failures documented in the preceding analyses; reform must be systemic. The solutions proposed here are grounded in clinical evidence, informed by international models, and designed to be politically feasible within the constraints of the current American healthcare system while achieving transformative outcomes over a 10-year horizon.

---

## Reform Framework

### Guiding Principles

- **Age equity**: Older adults deserve the same quality of and access to mental health care as younger populations; ageism in clinical practice, research, and policy must be confronted directly. Federal law should recognize older adult mental health as a civil rights issue under the Americans with Disabilities Act and the Older Americans Act.
- **Integration over fragmentation**: Mental health care for older adults must be embedded in primary care, aging services, and long-term care settings rather than siloed in specialty systems most older adults never reach. The IMPACT collaborative care model and the PACE program demonstrate that integration works.
- **Prevention and early intervention**: Investing in social connection, screening, and early treatment is more effective and less costly than responding to crises, hospitalizations, and institutional placement. The National Academies estimate that social isolation costs Medicare $6.7 billion annually; prevention at a fraction of that cost produces net savings.
- **Evidence-based but pragmatic**: Reforms should be grounded in clinical evidence (IMPACT model, PROSPECT trial, PACE outcomes) while recognizing that perfect evidence is not available for every policy question and waiting for it costs lives. Demonstration programs and phased rollouts allow evidence to accumulate while action begins.
- **Person-centered**: Older adults must be treated as autonomous agents with preferences, values, and goals, not as passive recipients of services determined by providers or systems. Cultural competence, language access, and sensitivity to diverse aging experiences (including LGBTQ+, racial/ethnic minority, and rural populations) must be embedded in all programs.
- **Sustainability**: Reforms must be designed to become self-sustaining through demonstrated cost savings, constituency building, and bipartisan political support, rather than dependent on continued discretionary appropriations that are vulnerable to political change.

### Theory of Change

The fundamental barrier to adequate older adult mental health care is a self-reinforcing cycle: low political priority leads to inadequate funding, which produces workforce shortages and coverage gaps, which result in poor outcomes, which are then attributed to "normal aging" rather than system failure, reinforcing low priority. Breaking this cycle requires simultaneous action on multiple fronts: increasing the supply of trained providers, ensuring financial access through Medicare and Medicaid reform, building the social infrastructure that prevents mental health crises, and shifting cultural attitudes about aging and mental illness. Early wins from targeted investments (collaborative care, telehealth, PACE expansion) can generate evidence of cost savings and improved outcomes that builds political support for sustained investment. The Alzheimer's Association's advocacy success, growing NIH research funding from $566 million to $3.7 billion in a decade through grassroots mobilization and bipartisan coalition building, demonstrates that transformative change is achievable when the political strategy matches the scale of the problem.

## Proposed Solutions

### Solution 1: Geriatric Mental Health Workforce Expansion Act

**Description**: Establish a comprehensive federal program to increase the supply of geriatric mental health professionals across all disciplines. The program would include: (a) full funding of all available geriatric psychiatry fellowship positions through HRSA direct graduate medical education support; (b) loan forgiveness of up to $200,000 for geriatric psychiatrists, psychologists, social workers, and psychiatric nurse practitioners who commit to 5 years of practice serving Medicare populations; (c) mandatory geriatric psychiatry rotations in all psychiatry residency programs receiving federal GME funding; (d) creation of 50 new geriatric psychiatry fellowship positions at institutions serving rural and underserved areas; (e) geriatric mental health training grants for social work, nursing, and psychology graduate programs; (f) a mid-career geriatric mental health credential allowing experienced mental health professionals to gain geriatric competency through a structured continuing education pathway without completing a traditional fellowship; and (g) geriatric mental health faculty development awards to expand the teaching capacity at academic medical centers.

**Evidence Base**: The National Health Service Corps (NHSC) loan repayment program has demonstrated that financial incentives successfully direct providers to underserved areas and specialties, with 82% of NHSC participants continuing to serve in shortage areas after their obligation ends (HRSA, 2023). The AAGP has documented that medical students exposed to geriatric psychiatry rotations are three times more likely to pursue the subspecialty (AAGP, 2021). The current 57% fellowship fill rate (NRMP, 2023) indicates that financial disincentives, not lack of positions, are the primary barrier. International models support targeted workforce incentives: Australia's Specialist Training Program, which funds additional training places in underserved specialties, has successfully expanded geriatric mental health capacity.

**Implementation**: Requires Congressional authorization and appropriation through the HRSA workforce programs. GME rotation requirements would be implemented through CMS conditions of participation for teaching hospitals or ACGME program requirements. Fellowship funding would flow through existing HRSA mechanisms. The mid-career credential would be developed through collaboration between AAGP, AGS, and relevant credentialing bodies.

**Cost/Resources**: Estimated $450 million annually: $150 million for loan forgiveness (750 providers at $200,000 each over 5-year commitments), $100 million for fellowship positions and GME supplements, $100 million for training grants across disciplines, $50 million for mid-career credential development and faculty awards, $50 million for program administration and evaluation.

**Timeline**: Legislation in Year 1; first loan forgiveness awards in Year 2; mandatory rotations phased in over Years 2-4; mid-career credential launched in Year 3; measurable workforce growth by Year 5.

**Challenges**: Opposition from medical education establishments resistant to new rotation mandates; competition with other specialty workforce needs for limited funding; 5-8 year lag between training investment and practicing providers; need for mid-career credential pathway to bridge the gap.

**Impact Metrics**: Fellowship fill rate (target: 90% within 5 years); number of board-certified geriatric psychiatrists (target: 3,000 within 10 years); geriatric mental health specialist-to-population ratio (target: 1:10,000 within 10 years); geographic distribution improvement (target: all states with 15+ geriatric psychiatrists within 10 years).

### Solution 2: Medicare Geriatric Mental Health Parity and Access Act

**Description**: Eliminate remaining structural barriers to mental health care within Medicare through: (a) repeal of the 190-day lifetime limit on inpatient care in freestanding psychiatric hospitals (42 U.S.C. 1395d); (b) increased Medicare reimbursement rates for geriatric psychiatric services to at least 95% of commercial insurance averages; (c) mandatory coverage of collaborative care management (CoCM) codes with enhanced reimbursement incentives for primary care practices that implement the IMPACT model; (d) elimination of the in-person visit requirement for established telemental health patients aged 65+; (e) coverage of community health worker and peer support specialist services for older adult mental health; (f) network adequacy standards for Medicare Advantage plans requiring demonstrated access to geriatric mental health specialists within defined time and distance standards; (g) limits on Medicare Advantage prior authorization for behavioral health services, including a "gold carding" provision that exempts providers with low denial rates; and (h) creation of a Medicare mental health navigator benefit, reimbursing designated staff who help older adults identify and access mental health services.

**Evidence Base**: The IMPACT collaborative care model has been shown in randomized controlled trials to double depression treatment response rates and save $3,363 per patient over four years through reduced medical costs (Unutzer et al., 2008). MedPAC has repeatedly recommended eliminating the 190-day limit (MedPAC, 2023). Currently, only 54% of psychiatrists accept Medicare (AMA, 2023) and fewer than 5% of primary care practices bill CoCM codes (CMS, 2024), indicating that the current benefit structure fails to support evidence-based care delivery. The HHS OIG found that 13% of Medicare Advantage mental health prior authorization denials should have been approved, suggesting systematic underauthorization (OIG, 2023).

**Implementation**: Requires Medicare statutory amendments through Congress. CMS rulemaking for reimbursement rate adjustments and network adequacy standards. CoCM implementation supported through practice transformation grants. MA prior authorization reforms through CMS regulatory authority.

**Cost/Resources**: Estimated net cost of $2-3 billion annually in the first 5 years, declining as collaborative care and prevention reduce downstream spending. The 190-day limit repeal affects a small population (estimated CBO score: $200-400 million over 10 years). Reimbursement increases are the largest cost component but are offset by increased provider participation and reduced emergency and inpatient utilization.

**Timeline**: Legislation in Year 1; CMS rulemaking in Years 1-2; full implementation by Year 3; cost savings measurable by Year 5.

**Challenges**: Scoring by the Congressional Budget Office, which may not fully credit downstream savings; opposition from Medicare Advantage insurers to network adequacy mandates; resistance from fiscal conservatives to any Medicare benefit expansion.

**Impact Metrics**: Psychiatrist Medicare participation rate (target: 70% within 5 years); CoCM billing prevalence (target: 25% of primary care practices serving Medicare patients within 5 years); Medicare beneficiary mental health service utilization rate (target: 15% from current 10.5%); MA prior authorization denial rate for behavioral health (target: below 5%).

### Solution 3: National Social Isolation Prevention Program

**Description**: Establish a federally funded, locally administered program to reduce social isolation among older adults through: (a) a new dedicated OAA Title (or Title III amendment) providing $500 million annually for community-based social isolation prevention; (b) social prescribing pilots in 100 Medicare primary care practices, where providers refer socially isolated patients to community activities with follow-up, modeled on the UK's NHS social prescribing program; (c) expansion of transportation services for older adults to reach social, medical, and community destinations; (d) intergenerational programming grants linking senior centers with schools, universities, and youth organizations; (e) technology-assisted social connection programs (digital literacy training, tablet lending, virtual social groups) for homebound older adults; (f) support for naturally occurring retirement community (NORC) supportive service programs that bring social programming to apartment buildings and neighborhoods where older adults have aged in place; and (g) expansion of the Senior Companion Program and Foster Grandparent Program through the AmeriCorps Seniors framework.

**Evidence Base**: The UK's national social prescribing program has demonstrated a 28% reduction in primary care visits and significant improvements in patient-reported wellbeing (NHS England, 2023). The National Academies estimated that social isolation adds approximately $6.7 billion annually to Medicare spending through increased healthcare utilization (National Academies, 2020). Evidence-based programs such as PEARLS (Program to Encourage Active, Rewarding Lives) have shown significant depression reduction among socially isolated older adults in randomized trials (Ciechanowski et al., *American Journal of Geriatric Psychiatry*, 2004). The Surgeon General's 2023 Advisory on Loneliness recommended a National Strategy to Advance Social Connection, providing policy cover for federal investment.

**Implementation**: Funding channeled through existing OAA infrastructure (ACL to State Units on Aging to Area Agencies on Aging). Social prescribing pilots administered through CMS Innovation Center (CMMI). Transportation expansion coordinated with DOT and FTA transit programs. NORC programs supported through HUD-HHS collaboration.

**Cost/Resources**: $500 million annually for community programs; $100 million for social prescribing pilots; $200 million for transportation expansion; $50 million for technology programs; $50 million for NORC and intergenerational programs. Total: approximately $900 million per year.

**Timeline**: OAA reauthorization in Year 1-2; program launch in Year 2-3; full implementation by Year 4; measurable population-level impact by Year 6.

**Challenges**: Measuring "social isolation reduction" is methodologically difficult; local program quality will vary; sustainability depends on continued appropriations; some philosophical opposition to government involvement in social connection.

**Impact Metrics**: Prevalence of social isolation among community-dwelling older adults (target: reduction from 24% to 15% within 8 years); senior center participation rates; transportation access metrics; Medicare utilization reductions in participating communities.

### Solution 4: Nursing Home Mental Health Quality Standards

**Description**: Strengthen mental health care requirements in nursing homes through: (a) mandating that every nursing home provide access to a licensed mental health professional (on-site or via telehealth) within 72 hours of a referral; (b) requiring mental health assessments for all new admissions and quarterly for all residents, using validated instruments (PHQ-9 for depression, GAD-7 for anxiety); (c) setting a national target of less than 10% antipsychotic prescribing rate with financial penalties for facilities consistently above 15%, and expanding the quality measure to include all sedating psychotropic medications to prevent substitution; (d) requiring dementia-specific behavioral health training for all direct care staff (minimum 16 hours annually), based on evidence-based curricula such as STAR-VA; (e) creating a Nursing Home Mental Health Quality Improvement Program with technical assistance for underperforming facilities; (f) strengthening PASARR implementation to ensure that individuals with serious mental illness receive appropriate alternatives to nursing home placement; and (g) requiring nursing homes to report and reduce the use of all chemical restraints, not merely antipsychotics.

**Evidence Base**: CMS's National Partnership to Improve Dementia Care reduced antipsychotic use from 23.9% to 14.2% through education and transparency (CMS, 2024), demonstrating that quality improvement is achievable. The OBRA 1987 requirements for psychiatric screening and medication review established the principle of federal mental health standards in nursing homes but enforcement has been inadequate. Research shows that staff trained in person-centered dementia care (e.g., STAR-VA program) achieve 30-40% reductions in behavioral symptoms without medication (Teri et al., *Journal of the American Geriatrics Society*, 2005). The Dutch Grip on Challenging Behaviour program has demonstrated similar results in European nursing homes (Zwijsen et al., *BMC Geriatrics*, 2014).

**Implementation**: CMS rulemaking under existing statutory authority (Nursing Home Reform Act). Phase in over 3 years, with faster timelines for large chains and PE-owned facilities. Technical assistance funded through CMS quality improvement organizations (QIOs). Pair with Medicaid rate increases to support compliance.

**Cost/Resources**: Estimated $2-4 billion annually in additional facility costs for staffing and training. CMS technical assistance: $200 million annually. Cost partially offset by reduced hospitalizations, falls, and regulatory penalties. Medicaid rate increase to support compliance: $1-2 billion annually.

**Timeline**: Proposed rule in Year 1; final rule in Year 2; phased implementation Years 2-5; full compliance by Year 5.

**Challenges**: Nursing home industry opposition to unfunded mandates; rural facility capacity constraints; workforce shortages limiting availability of mental health professionals; enforcement capacity of state survey agencies.

**Impact Metrics**: Antipsychotic prescribing rate (target: below 10% nationally within 5 years); all-psychotropic-medication quality measure; facility mental health access compliance rate; resident depression screening rates; staff training completion rates; PASARR diversion rates.

### Solution 5: PACE Expansion and Integrated Care Models

**Description**: Dramatically expand PACE (Programs of All-Inclusive Care for the Elderly) and similar integrated care models through: (a) setting a national goal of enrolling 500,000 individuals in PACE by 2035 (from current ~65,000); (b) reducing regulatory barriers to PACE startup, including simplified application processes and reduced capital requirements for new programs; (c) creating a PACE Rural Health Model allowing modified program structures for low-population-density areas, including virtual PACE day centers and mobile interdisciplinary teams; (d) establishing PACE-Like Innovation Grants for organizations developing new integrated care models that combine medical, mental health, and social services for older adults; (e) requiring all PACE programs to include a behavioral health specialist on their interdisciplinary team; (f) creating a PACE Medicare-Only option that extends the model to older adults who are not Medicaid-eligible, broadening the eligible population; and (g) establishing PACE workforce development grants to train interdisciplinary team members.

**Evidence Base**: PACE participants experience 50% fewer hospitalizations and 20% fewer emergency department visits compared with matched non-PACE populations, with higher satisfaction rates (National PACE Association, 2023). PACE programs include mental health as a core service, avoiding the fragmentation that characterizes fee-for-service Medicare. Currently, only ~65,000 of an estimated 3.5 million eligible individuals are enrolled (National PACE Association, 2024), representing massive unmet potential. International parallels, including the Dutch Buurtzorg neighborhood care model and the Australian Home Care Packages program, demonstrate that integrated home- and community-based care can achieve comparable outcomes.

**Implementation**: Requires Congressional action to reduce regulatory barriers and authorize expansion incentives. CMS Innovation Center for PACE Rural Health Model and Medicare-Only option. HHS grants for PACE-Like programs and workforce development.

**Cost/Resources**: PACE costs approximately $4,000-5,000 per member per month but replaces both Medicare and Medicaid spending. CBO scoring should reflect net savings from reduced hospitalizations and nursing home admissions. Start-up grants: $500 million over 5 years. PACE-Like Innovation Grants: $200 million over 5 years. Medicare-Only pilot: budget-neutral if designed with appropriate capitation rates.

**Timeline**: Legislative authorization in Year 1-2; regulatory streamlining in Year 2-3; new programs launching in Year 3-5; Medicare-Only pilot in Year 3; enrollment target by Year 10.

**Challenges**: PACE's per-capita cost makes CBO scoring difficult despite documented savings; slow organizational development timeline (3-5 years per new program); limited applicability in very rural areas; enrollment requires dual Medicare-Medicaid eligibility (until Medicare-Only option is created), limiting the population served.

**Impact Metrics**: PACE enrollment (target: 500,000 by 2035); number of PACE organizations (target: 500 from current 156); hospitalization rates among PACE participants; participant and caregiver satisfaction; behavioral health specialist staffing compliance.

### Solution 6: Geriatric Suicide Prevention Initiative

**Description**: Establish a targeted national initiative to reduce suicide among older adults through: (a) a dedicated older adult track within the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline with counselors trained in late-life depression, chronic illness, bereavement, and retirement-related loss, modeled on the Friendship Line operated by the Institute on Aging; (b) universal depression screening for all Medicare beneficiaries at annual wellness visits with mandated follow-up referral protocols and tracking of screening-to-treatment completion rates; (c) lethal means counseling programs integrated into primary care and home health settings, with voluntary firearm storage partnerships with law enforcement agencies and gun shops; (d) a national gatekeeper training program educating postal workers, utility workers, Meals on Wheels volunteers, clergy, bank tellers, pharmacists, and other community members who interact with isolated older adults to recognize warning signs and make referrals; (e) funding for research on late-life suicide prevention interventions through NIMH, including randomized trials of lethal means counseling, social prescribing, and crisis line effectiveness; and (f) establishment of a national postvention program providing bereavement support to families and communities after older adult suicide.

**Evidence Base**: The PROSPECT study demonstrated that collaborative care for suicidal older adults in primary care reduced suicidal ideation by 2.2 times compared with usual care (Bruce et al., *JAMA*, 2004). Gatekeeper training programs (e.g., QPR - Question, Persuade, Refer) have shown effectiveness in increasing referrals and reducing suicide attempts in older adult populations (Burnette et al., *Suicide and Life-Threatening Behavior*, 2015). The 72% firearm completion rate among older adult suicides (CDC WISQARS, 2022) indicates that means restriction is a high-impact intervention. The Friendship Line's demonstrated success in reaching over 100,000 older adult callers annually shows that crisis services can reach this population when they are designed for it.

**Implementation**: 988 older adult track through SAMHSA. Screening mandates through CMS regulation. Gatekeeper training through ACL and SAMHSA grants. Research funding through NIMH. Means counseling training for primary care through HRSA.

**Cost/Resources**: Estimated $300 million annually: $100 million for 988 expansion; $50 million for screening implementation and tracking; $75 million for gatekeeper training; $50 million for means counseling programs; $25 million for research and postvention.

**Timeline**: 988 older adult track in Year 1-2; screening mandate in Year 2; gatekeeper training rollout Years 2-4; research programs ongoing.

**Challenges**: Stigma among older adults may limit help-seeking even when services are available; firearm means counseling is politically sensitive; measuring suicide prevention is inherently difficult (proving a negative); workforce limitations may slow screening follow-up.

**Impact Metrics**: Older adult suicide rate (target: 25% reduction within 10 years); 988 utilization by adults aged 65+ (target: proportional to population share); depression screening rates at Medicare wellness visits (target: 90%); gatekeeper training completion (target: 500,000 trained individuals within 5 years); screening-to-treatment completion rate (target: 70%).

### Solution 7: Medicare Telemental Health Permanent Access Program

**Description**: Make permanent and expand telehealth mental health access for older adults through: (a) eliminating all geographic restrictions on Medicare telemental health for beneficiaries aged 65+; (b) permanently authorizing audio-only (telephone) mental health visits at full reimbursement for beneficiaries without broadband or video capability; (c) funding a Digital Bridge program providing tablets, broadband subsidies, and digital literacy training to low-income older adults, filling the gap left by the expiration of the FCC's Affordable Connectivity Program; (d) establishing telehealth hub-and-spoke networks connecting geriatric psychiatrists at academic medical centers with primary care practices in underserved areas, where a single geriatric psychiatrist can consult on 50-100 patients through weekly case review sessions; (e) developing and validating telepsychiatry protocols specifically designed for older adults with cognitive impairment, vision or hearing limitations, and limited technology experience; (f) creating community telehealth access points in libraries, senior centers, and AAA offices with privacy-compliant spaces and trained facilitators; and (g) establishing Medicare reimbursement for "facilitated telehealth," where a trained community health worker or volunteer assists an older adult with the technology and is present during the visit to support communication.

**Evidence Base**: Medicare telemental health visits surged from 0.8 million in 2019 to 10.1 million in 2020 (OIG, 2022), demonstrating both demand and feasibility. Studies show that telepsychiatry produces outcomes equivalent to in-person care for depression and anxiety treatment in older adults (Hubley et al., *American Journal of Geriatric Psychiatry*, 2016). Audio-only visits reach older adults who lack broadband, devices, or digital literacy, and their elimination would disproportionately affect rural and low-income populations. The hub-and-spoke model, adapted from Project ECHO (Extension for Community Healthcare Outcomes), has been successfully used for other specialties and can dramatically extend the reach of scarce geriatric psychiatric expertise.

**Implementation**: Congressional action to make telehealth flexibilities permanent. CMS rulemaking for reimbursement parity and facilitated telehealth billing. FCC coordination for broadband subsidies. HRSA grants for Digital Bridge and hub-and-spoke networks. Library and senior center partnerships through ACL and IMLS.

**Cost/Resources**: $400 million annually: $200 million for Digital Bridge (devices, broadband, training); $100 million for hub-and-spoke networks; $50 million for community access points and facilitated telehealth; $50 million for protocol development, validation, and program evaluation. Telehealth reimbursement costs offset by reduced no-show rates, reduced travel costs, and avoided emergency utilization.

**Timeline**: Permanent authorization in Year 1; Digital Bridge launch in Year 1-2; hub-and-spoke networks in Years 2-4; community access points in Years 2-3; full program maturity by Year 5.

**Challenges**: Digital divide persists among the oldest old (22% of adults 65+ do not use the internet); cognitive impairment limits technology use; not all conditions are appropriate for telehealth; broadband infrastructure gaps in rural areas; some providers prefer in-person care.

**Impact Metrics**: Telemental health utilization among Medicare beneficiaries 65+ (target: 20% of all mental health visits); Digital Bridge device distribution (target: 2 million tablets in 5 years); geographic access parity metrics; patient satisfaction with telehealth; facilitated telehealth utilization.

## International Models

### United Kingdom: National Social Prescribing Program

**Description**: Since 2019, NHS England has funded over 3,000 social prescribing link workers embedded in primary care networks. These workers connect patients, including a substantial proportion of older adults, to community activities, groups, and services that address social isolation, loneliness, and mental health needs. Social prescribing is now available to all NHS patients and is a core component of the NHS Long Term Plan. Each link worker typically manages a caseload of 200-250 patients, providing personalized needs assessment, community resource navigation, and follow-up.

**Results**: NHS England reports that 1.4 million people were referred to social prescribing in 2022-2023. Evaluations show a 28% reduction in GP consultations, significant improvements in patient-reported wellbeing, and reductions in anxiety and depression symptoms. Older adults are the largest beneficiary group, comprising approximately 40% of referrals (NHS England, 2023). A 2022 Health Foundation analysis estimated a return on investment of 1.20 pounds for every 1 pound spent on social prescribing.

**Applicability to U.S.**: The model could be adapted through Medicare billing codes for social prescribing link workers or community health workers, channeling referrals through the Medicare Annual Wellness Visit. The decentralized AAA network could serve as the community service backbone. Key adaptation challenges include the U.S. lack of a unified primary care system, the fragmentation of community services across multiple funding streams, and the need to develop a community health worker workforce with cultural competency for diverse aging populations.

### Australia: Better Access Initiative

**Description**: Australia's Better Access program, established in 2006, provides Medicare-rebated mental health services including up to 10 individual sessions per year with psychologists, social workers, and occupational therapists, accessed through GP referral. The program includes specific provisions for older adults in residential aged care facilities, where visiting psychologists and mental health nurses provide services funded by Medicare.

**Results**: Better Access has increased mental health service utilization among older Australians by approximately 40% since implementation. The aged care component has demonstrated improved depression outcomes in residential facilities through regular psychological services (Australian Government Department of Health, 2023). Access to Psychological Therapies (IAPT) in the UK has shown similar results with structured psychological interventions for older adults.

**Applicability to U.S.**: The GP-referral model parallels the collaborative care approach already supported by Medicare CoCM codes. Expanding the U.S. model to include more provider types (as the 2024 MFT/LMHC coverage begins to do) and setting a clear session entitlement would move toward the Australian approach. The residential aged care mental health component is directly relevant to U.S. nursing home mental health reform.

### Netherlands: Integrated Neighborhood Care Teams

**Description**: The Netherlands has implemented integrated neighborhood care teams that include primary care physicians, nurses, social workers, and mental health professionals serving defined geographic populations. For older adults, these teams coordinate physical health, mental health, social services, and home care, eliminating the fragmentation that characterizes U.S. care delivery. The Buurtzorg model, which uses self-managing nurse-led teams, has been particularly effective for homebound older adults with mental health needs.

**Results**: Evaluations show reduced hospitalizations, improved mental health outcomes, and higher patient satisfaction compared with fragmented care models. The approach is particularly effective for older adults with comorbid physical and mental health conditions (RIVM, 2022). Buurtzorg teams achieve 40% lower costs per client compared with traditional home care agencies, primarily through reduced hospital and emergency department use.

**Applicability to U.S.**: The PACE model is the closest U.S. analog but serves only dual-eligible individuals. Extending integrated team-based care to all Medicare beneficiaries through accountable care organization (ACO) enhancements or new CMS demonstration programs would move toward the Dutch model. The Buurtzorg approach could inform Medicare home health reform.

### Japan: Community-Based Integrated Care Systems

**Description**: Japan, which has the world's oldest population (29% aged 65+), has implemented Community-Based Integrated Care Systems that coordinate medical, nursing, long-term care, social services, and mental health within defined geographic zones. The system includes mandatory screening for cognitive and mental health conditions at age 75, neighborhood-level support teams, and a robust network of day service centers that provide social engagement and health monitoring.

**Results**: The system has contributed to Japan's relatively low older adult suicide rate (which has declined by 40% since 2003) and high rates of dementia detection. Day service centers serve approximately 2.7 million older adults and have been associated with reduced depression and delayed institutional placement (Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare, 2023).

**Applicability to U.S.**: The mandatory screening model could inform Medicare Annual Wellness Visit reform. The day service center model is comparable to PACE day centers but serves a much larger population. The geographic zone approach aligns with AAA service areas and could be adapted to U.S. community planning.

## Pilot Programs and Demonstrations

- **IMPACT Collaborative Care**: Originally demonstrated at the University of Washington, IMPACT has been replicated in over 300 settings across 12 countries. The model embeds a depression care manager in primary care, coordinating with a consulting psychiatrist. Doubling of treatment response rates and net savings of $3,363 per patient over four years have been consistently demonstrated (Unutzer et al., 2008). Adaptations include the AIMS Center's implementation toolkit and training programs.
- **PEARLS (Program to Encourage Active, Rewarding Lives)**: An evidence-based home-based depression treatment program for socially isolated older adults developed at the University of Washington. Randomized trials showed significant depression reduction and improved quality of life. Currently implemented in approximately 30 communities through the Evidence-Based Leadership Council (2023). Uses problem-solving treatment and behavioral activation delivered by trained counselors in the home.
- **STAR-VA (Staff Training in Assisted Living Residences)**: A behavioral intervention training program for nursing home and assisted living staff that reduces behavioral symptoms of dementia without medication. Studies show 30-40% reduction in agitation and aggression (Teri et al., 2005). Adapted for VA settings with strong results. The program trains direct care staff in behavioral techniques including antecedent manipulation, communication strategies, and activity programming.
- **Friendship Line (Institute on Aging)**: The only accredited crisis line in the U.S. dedicated to older adults. Handles over 100,000 calls annually from a base in San Francisco. Has demonstrated reductions in suicidal ideation and loneliness among callers (Institute on Aging, 2023). Staffed by trained older adult volunteers and professional counselors. Could serve as a model for the proposed 988 older adult track. Annual budget of approximately $2 million, suggesting that a national older adult crisis infrastructure could be built at modest cost.
- **Senior REACH (Resources for Enhancing Alzheimer's Caregiver Health)**: A multisite randomized trial demonstrating that structured caregiver interventions reduce caregiver depression by 30% and delay institutional placement of care recipients. Evidence supports integration into AAA service delivery (Belle et al., *JAMA*, 2006). The intervention includes education, stress management, problem-solving, and support group components tailored to the needs of diverse caregiver populations.
- **Healthy IDEAS (Identifying Depression, Empowering Activities for Seniors)**: An evidence-based depression self-management program delivered by aging service providers (case managers, home visitors) as part of existing aging services. Demonstrates the integration principle: mental health support delivered within programs older adults already use, without requiring separate mental health system entry. Implemented in approximately 100 communities.
- **CAPABLE (Community Aging in Place - Advancing Better Living for Elders)**: Developed at Johns Hopkins, CAPABLE deploys an occupational therapist, nurse, and handyman team to address functional barriers that contribute to depression and isolation in homebound older adults. Randomized trials show reduced depression, improved function, and $10,000 per participant savings in Medicare costs over 5 months (Szanton et al., *JAMA Internal Medicine*, 2019).

## Sequencing and Prioritization

### Quick Wins (0-2 years)

- Permanent authorization of telemental health with audio-only parity for Medicare beneficiaries aged 65+
- Launch 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline older adult track
- CMS issuance of proposed rule on nursing home mental health access standards
- First cohort of geriatric mental health loan forgiveness awards
- Begin social prescribing pilot in 100 Medicare primary care practices
- Digital Bridge pilot: 100,000 tablets and digital literacy training for low-income older adults
- Medicare reimbursement for facilitated telehealth visits

### Medium-term Reforms (2-5 years)

- Repeal of the 190-day psychiatric hospital lifetime limit
- Medicare reimbursement rate increases for geriatric psychiatric services
- Full implementation of nursing home mental health quality standards
- OAA reauthorization with dedicated social isolation prevention title
- PACE regulatory streamlining and launch of 50 new programs
- Hub-and-spoke telepsychiatry networks operational in all HHS regions
- Medicare Advantage network adequacy and prior authorization reforms
- Gatekeeper training program reaching 250,000 community members

### Long-term Transformation (5-10 years)

- Geriatric psychiatrist-to-patient ratio reaches 1:10,000
- PACE enrollment reaches 500,000
- Social isolation prevalence among community-dwelling older adults reduced from 24% to 15%
- Nursing home antipsychotic prescribing rate below 10% nationally
- Older adult suicide rate reduced by 25%
- Collaborative care model implemented in 25% of primary care practices serving Medicare patients
- PACE Medicare-Only option serving non-dual-eligible population
- Every AAA integrating mental health screening and referral into core services

## Complementary Policies

- **Housing**: Expansion of affordable senior housing with integrated social services and mental health support. Zoning reforms to allow accessory dwelling units and cohousing that reduce isolation. Support for NORC supportive service programs. HUD-HHS collaboration on service-enriched housing models.
- **Transportation**: Increased federal and state investment in paratransit and demand-response transportation for older adults, enabling access to mental health appointments and social activities. Expansion of FTA Section 5310 funding. Volunteer driver program support.
- **Caregiver support**: Paid family leave for dementia caregivers; expansion of respite care programs through OAA Title III-E; mental health services for caregivers themselves through Medicare coverage of caregiver counseling; tax credits for family caregivers.
- **Elder abuse prevention**: Strengthened Adult Protective Services with mandatory mental health screening for abuse victims; increased prosecution resources for financial exploitation; integration of elder abuse detection into mental health assessment protocols.
- **Substance use**: Expanded Medicare coverage of substance use treatment tailored to older adults, including medication-assisted treatment for opioid and alcohol use disorders with geriatric-appropriate protocols. Screening with geriatric-validated instruments (SMAST-G). Integration of substance use treatment into aging services.
- **Research**: Increased NIMH funding specifically for older adult mental health research, with a target of proportionality to disease burden. Mandatory inclusion of adults aged 65+ in mental health clinical trials, with elimination of age-based exclusion criteria. Expanded research on late-life suicide prevention, social prescribing effectiveness, and nursing home behavioral health interventions.
