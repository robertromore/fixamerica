# Older Adult Mental Health: What You Can Do

Older adult mental health is a policy area where individual, community, and political action can make a tangible difference. Approximately 11.6 million Americans aged 65 and older experience mental health conditions, yet fewer than half receive any treatment. The gap between need and care is driven by workforce shortages, insurance barriers, stigma, and social isolation, all of which are amenable to citizen engagement. Whether you are an older adult, a family caregiver, a healthcare professional, or a concerned citizen, there are concrete steps you can take. The reforms outlined in this analysis require political will, and political will is built from the bottom up through informed citizens demanding action.

---

## Individual Actions

### Learn and Stay Informed

- Read the National Academies report *Social Isolation and Loneliness in Older Adults: Opportunities for the Health Care System* (2020) for a comprehensive evidence base on the health impacts of isolation. Available free at <https://nap.nationalacademies.org/catalog/25663/social-isolation-and-loneliness-in-older-adults-opportunities-for-the>
- Read the U.S. Surgeon General's Advisory "Our Epidemic of Loneliness and Isolation" (2023) for the federal government's recognition of social disconnection as a public health crisis. Available at <https://www.hhs.gov/surgeongeneral/priorities/connection/index.html>
- Follow KFF Health News (<https://kffhealthnews.org/>) for ongoing Medicare mental health policy coverage, including nursing home quality, telehealth, and workforce issues
- Review the Alzheimer's Association annual *Facts and Figures* report (<https://www.alz.org/alzheimers-dementia/facts-figures>) for the latest data on dementia prevalence, behavioral symptoms, and caregiver burden
- Subscribe to the American Association for Geriatric Psychiatry newsletter (<https://www.aagponline.org/>) for workforce and policy updates
- Review the American Geriatrics Society Beers Criteria (<https://www.americangeriatrics.org/>) to understand which medications are potentially inappropriate for older adults
- Monitor the CMS Nursing Home Compare website (<https://data.cms.gov/provider-data/>) to check the quality ratings and antipsychotic prescribing rates of specific facilities

### Make Personal Changes

- **Check on older adults in your life**: Regular contact with older family members, neighbors, and community members reduces isolation. A weekly phone call or visit can be a meaningful intervention. The National Academies report documents that even modest increases in social contact reduce depression and mortality risk.
- **Talk openly about mental health**: Help reduce stigma by discussing mental health with older adults in your life as you would discuss any other health condition. Use language that normalizes help-seeking. Remember that 47% of adults aged 65+ still view seeking help for emotional problems as a sign of weakness (AARP, 2023), and your attitude can help shift that perception.
- **Learn the warning signs**: Depression in older adults often presents as fatigue, pain, appetite changes, or withdrawal rather than expressed sadness. Learn to recognize atypical presentations. The PHQ-2 screening tool (two questions: "Over the last two weeks, how often have you been bothered by (1) little interest or pleasure in doing things, and (2) feeling down, depressed, or hopeless?") can guide a conversation. A "yes" to either question warrants further assessment.
- **Know the suicide warning signs specific to older adults**: Watch for giving away prized possessions, making or changing a will suddenly, stockpiling medications, withdrawing from usual activities, expressing hopelessness or being a burden, and increased alcohol use. Older adults who die by suicide rarely give direct verbal warnings.
- **Ensure medication safety**: If you help manage medications for an older adult, review the American Geriatrics Society Beers Criteria list of potentially inappropriate medications and discuss concerns with their provider. Pay particular attention to benzodiazepines, anticholinergic drugs, and multiple psychotropic medications. Ask the prescribing provider whether a deprescribing review has been conducted.
- **Secure firearms safely**: If an older adult in your life owns firearms and is experiencing depression or suicidal thoughts, have a compassionate conversation about temporary safe storage. Contact the Means Matter program (<https://www.hsph.harvard.edu/means-matter/>) for guidance. Firearms account for 72% of older adult suicides. Voluntary storage programs, including partnerships with gun shops and law enforcement, can save lives without restricting rights.
- **Help with technology**: If an older adult in your life could benefit from telemental health but lacks technology skills, help them set up and learn to use a tablet or smartphone for video visits. Digital literacy is a mental health intervention.
- **Address transportation barriers**: Offer rides to medical appointments, social activities, and community events. For older adults who have stopped driving, transportation loss is a leading driver of isolation and depression. A consistent weekly ride to a senior center or faith community can be transformative.

### Use Your Voice

- **Contact Your Representatives**: Urge your members of Congress to support Medicare mental health parity, geriatric workforce funding, and OAA reauthorization with social isolation provisions. Use the specific talking points and templates below.
- **Submit Public Comments**: When CMS proposes rules on nursing home quality standards or Medicare telehealth policy, submit comments supporting strong mental health provisions. Federal Register notices are posted at <https://www.regulations.gov/>. Public comments directly influence final rule content.
- **Write Letters to the Editor and Op-Eds**: Local newspapers are read by elected officials and shape community attitudes. Write about the older adult mental health crisis in your community, using local data and personal stories. Letters to the editor of hometown papers are often more politically influential than letters to national outlets.
- **Share Information**: Share data on older adult mental health, social isolation, and suicide prevention in community settings. The National Academies report, CDC suicide data, and Surgeon General's Advisory are public and shareable. When sharing statistics, contextualize them: "Adults aged 85+ have the highest suicide rate of any age group in America" is a fact that surprises most people.
- **Testify at Public Hearings**: State legislatures, county boards, and city councils hold public hearings on aging services, Medicaid budgets, and mental health funding. Your testimony, especially if you are an older adult, a caregiver, or a professional, carries weight with local officials.

## Community Actions

### Organize Locally

- **Start or support a senior visitor program**: Many communities lack organized programs to visit isolated older adults. Work with your local Area Agency on Aging (find yours at <https://eldercare.acl.gov/>) to establish or expand visitation programs. The Eldercare Locator (1-800-677-1116) can connect you with local resources. Volunteer visitor programs have been shown to reduce loneliness and depression in randomized trials.
- **Connect senior centers with mental health services**: Advocate for your local senior center to offer mental health screening, support groups, and referrals. The Older Americans Act allows funding for these services, but only 23% of AAAs currently offer them. Contact your AAA director and request that mental health screening be integrated into intake processes.
- **Create intergenerational programs**: Partner with schools, colleges, and youth organizations to create programs that bring older adults and younger people together. Intergenerational contact reduces isolation and benefits both groups. Models include reading mentorship programs, technology tutoring by students, and shared community garden projects. Contact your local school district and senior center about establishing a partnership.
- **Organize transportation for older adults**: Transportation barriers are a leading cause of isolation and missed mental health appointments. Coordinate volunteer driver programs or advocate for expanded paratransit services. Contact your local transit authority about demand-response services for older adults. Organizations like ITN America (<https://itnamerica.org/>) provide models for volunteer driver programs.
- **Host community conversations on aging and mental health**: Use the PBS documentary *The Quiet Epidemic*, the Surgeon General's Advisory on Loneliness, or data from this analysis as conversation starters. Invite local geriatric providers and aging services professionals. Hold events at accessible locations (libraries, faith communities, senior centers) at times convenient for older adults.
- **Establish a community social prescribing initiative**: Even without federal policy, communities can build social prescribing networks. Work with local primary care practices, AAAs, and community organizations to create a referral directory of social activities, support groups, exercise classes, and volunteer opportunities for older adults. Train practice staff to make referrals.
- **Support Meals on Wheels and congregate meal programs**: These programs serve as critical social contact points for isolated older adults. Volunteer as a meal deliverer (your visit may be the only human contact a homebound person receives that day). Advocate for increased funding at the local and state level.

### Build Coalitions

- **Connect aging and mental health organizations**: In many communities, aging services (senior centers, AAAs, Meals on Wheels) and mental health services (community mental health centers, crisis services) operate in silos. Bring leaders from both sectors together to identify collaboration opportunities. A joint needs assessment can reveal gaps and duplication.
- **Engage faith communities**: Religious congregations are often the last social institution connecting isolated older adults. Work with clergy and lay leaders to train them as mental health gatekeepers and to integrate screening and referral into pastoral care. The QPR (Question, Persuade, Refer) gatekeeper training program can be delivered in faith settings in 90 minutes. Offer to organize a training session.
- **Partner with libraries**: Public libraries are trusted community institutions that serve older adults. Advocate for libraries to host mental health programming, telehealth kiosks, and digital literacy training. The American Library Association has resources for health programming partnerships. Libraries can serve as community telehealth access points with minimal capital investment.
- **Engage local businesses**: Pharmacists, bank tellers, postal workers, and grocery store employees interact regularly with older adults and may notice changes in behavior that indicate mental health decline. Partner with local businesses to offer brief awareness training on recognizing warning signs and making referrals.
- **Form a local geriatric mental health task force**: Bring together AAA staff, mental health providers, primary care physicians, faith leaders, law enforcement, and older adult representatives to assess community needs, coordinate services, and advocate for local and state funding.

## Political Actions

### Contact Elected Officials

**When to Contact**:

- During Medicare and Medicaid budget negotiations (typically annual appropriations cycle, October-December)
- When the Older Americans Act is up for reauthorization (next reauthorization expected 2025-2026)
- When CMS proposes rules affecting nursing home quality standards or Medicare telehealth (monitor the Federal Register)
- When geriatric workforce legislation is introduced or pending in committee
- When state legislatures consider Medicaid budgets, scope of practice legislation, or aging services funding
- After a high-profile older adult suicide or nursing home quality scandal in your community or state

**What to Say - Template Email**:

```text
Subject: Support Older Adult Mental Health Funding and Reform

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

I am a constituent from [City, State] writing to urge your support for federal action on older adult mental health. Approximately 11.6 million Americans aged 65 and older experience mental health conditions, yet fewer than half receive treatment. Adults aged 85 and older have the highest suicide rate of any age group (22.4 per 100,000), and we have fewer than 1,800 geriatric psychiatrists in the entire country.

I urge you to support:

1. Increased funding for geriatric mental health workforce development through HRSA, including loan forgiveness for geriatric psychiatrists, psychologists, and social workers.
2. Repeal of the 190-day lifetime limit on Medicare inpatient psychiatric hospital care, which discriminates against mental health.
3. Permanent authorization of Medicare telemental health services, including audio-only visits, for older adults.
4. Dedicated social isolation prevention funding in the Older Americans Act reauthorization.
5. Stronger mental health access requirements for Medicare Advantage plans, including limits on prior authorization for behavioral health services.

These investments save money by preventing the emergency visits, hospitalizations, and institutional placements that result from untreated mental illness. The IMPACT collaborative care model has been shown to save $3,363 per patient over four years. Social isolation costs Medicare an estimated $6.7 billion annually.

[Optional: personal story about an older adult in your life affected by mental health conditions or care access barriers.]

Thank you for your service and attention to this critical issue.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your City, State]
```

**What to Say - Phone Call Script**:

```text
"Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [City]. I'm calling about
older adult mental health. Adults aged 85 and older have the highest suicide
rate in America, and we have only 1,800 geriatric psychiatrists for 58 million
older adults. I'd like the [Representative/Senator] to support geriatric mental
health workforce funding, Medicare telehealth permanence, repeal of the 190-day
psychiatric hospital limit, and social isolation prevention programs in the
Older Americans Act reauthorization. Can you please note my position?"
```

**State-Level Contacts**: Many critical policy levers are at the state level. Contact your state legislators about:

- Medicaid reimbursement rates for mental health services in nursing homes
- Scope of practice laws allowing nurse practitioners and clinical social workers to practice independently in geriatric mental health
- State funding for Area Agencies on Aging mental health programming
- Nursing home licensing requirements for mental health access
- State investments in senior center infrastructure and transportation

### Support Legislation

Current or proposed legislation to support:

- **Older Americans Act Reauthorization**: Advocate for inclusion of dedicated social isolation prevention funding and explicit mental health screening authorization. Contact members of the Senate HELP Committee and House Education and Labor Committee. The next reauthorization provides a once-in-five-years opportunity to embed mental health into the aging services framework.
- **Medicare mental health parity bills**: Support legislation to repeal the 190-day inpatient psychiatric hospital limit. Contact Senate Finance Committee and House Ways & Means Committee members. MedPAC has recommended this repeal; Congressional action is overdue.
- **Geriatric workforce legislation**: Support bills expanding HRSA loan forgiveness for geriatric mental health professionals and funding for geriatric psychiatry fellowship positions. Ask specifically about the Geriatric Workforce Enhancement Program (GWEP) funding levels.
- **Telehealth permanence**: Support legislation making permanent the COVID-era telehealth flexibilities for Medicare mental health services, including audio-only visits. Emphasize that older adults who have been receiving telemental health since 2020 will lose access if these provisions expire.
- **Nursing home quality**: Support CMS rulemaking on minimum staffing standards and advocate for inclusion of mental health access requirements. Submit public comments during the comment period.
- **Medicare Advantage reform**: Support legislation requiring MA plans to meet mental health network adequacy standards and limiting prior authorization for behavioral health services.
- **PACE expansion**: Support legislation reducing regulatory barriers to new PACE programs and creating a Medicare-Only PACE option.

### Electoral Engagement

**Questions to Ask Candidates**:

1. "What is your plan to address the shortage of geriatric mental health professionals? We have fewer than 1,800 geriatric psychiatrists for 58 million older Americans."
2. "Do you support repealing the 190-day Medicare lifetime limit on inpatient psychiatric hospital care, which discriminates against people with mental illness?"
3. "How would you address social isolation among older adults, which affects approximately 14 million Americans and costs Medicare an estimated $6.7 billion per year?"
4. "Do you support permanent Medicare telehealth authorization, including audio-only visits for older adults without broadband access?"
5. "What would you do to ensure that nursing home residents receive adequate mental health care instead of being sedated with inappropriate medications?"
6. "Do you support expanding PACE programs, which provide integrated care to nursing-home-eligible older adults in the community?"

**Voting Considerations**:

- Look for candidates who specifically mention older adult mental health, not just mental health generally
- Check positions on Medicare expansion, OAA funding, and nursing home quality
- Review endorsements from AARP, AAGP, and Alzheimer's Association
- Ask about committee assignments: Senate Finance, HELP, Special Committee on Aging; House Ways & Means, Energy & Commerce, and Appropriations are the most relevant committees
- Check voting records on prior geriatric and mental health legislation using GovTrack.us or the League of Conservation Voters aging policy equivalents

## Professional Actions

### For Healthcare Providers

- Integrate depression screening (PHQ-9) into every older adult visit. The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force recommends universal screening, and the PHQ-9 takes less than 5 minutes. Make it as routine as blood pressure measurement.
- Learn to use the Collaborative Care Model (CoCM) billing codes (CPT 99492-99494). These codes allow reimbursement for care management services that improve depression outcomes. The AIMS Center at the University of Washington (<https://aims.uw.edu/>) provides free implementation resources and training.
- If you are a primary care provider, seek training in basic geropsychiatry through AAGP's continuing education programs. You do not need to be a specialist to screen, initiate treatment, and manage uncomplicated depression.
- Practice lethal means counseling with older patients who screen positive for depression or suicidal ideation. Ask about firearms in the home. Provide information about voluntary safe storage options. The Counseling on Access to Lethal Means (CALM) training is free and takes 2 hours (<https://www.sprc.org/resources-programs/calm-counseling-access-lethal-means>).
- Review your older patients' medications using the Beers Criteria and consider deprescribing inappropriate psychotropics. Use the deprescribing.org resources for evidence-based tapering protocols.
- Assess social isolation as a health risk factor. Ask patients about living arrangements, social contacts, transportation access, and loneliness during routine visits. Document social isolation as a health concern that warrants intervention.

### For Social Workers

- Advocate within your agency for mental health screening as part of all older adult intake assessments. Use the PHQ-2 as a minimum screen and the PHQ-9 for follow-up.
- Connect clients to community mental health resources, including the Friendship Line (1-800-971-0016) for crisis support and the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline.
- Pursue geriatric social work certification (CSW-G) through the National Association of Social Workers to build expertise and demonstrate commitment to this population.
- Learn about and refer clients to evidence-based programs such as PEARLS (home-based depression treatment), Healthy IDEAS (depression self-management through aging services), and CAPABLE (home environmental intervention).

### For Nursing Home Administrators

- Establish telemental health partnerships with academic geriatric psychiatry programs. University-based geriatric psychiatrists can provide consultation to your residents via video or telephone, often within 48-72 hours rather than the 14-21 day average for in-person consultation.
- Train all direct care staff in person-centered dementia care using evidence-based curricula such as STAR-VA or the CARES (Connect with the person, Assess behavior, Respond appropriately, Evaluate what works, Share with the team) program.
- Review antipsychotic prescribing rates against the CMS national benchmark of 14.2% and set a facility target below 10%. Audit all antipsychotic prescriptions to ensure documented clinical justification and periodic dose reduction attempts.
- Participate in the CMS National Partnership to Improve Dementia Care and implement the Quality Assurance and Performance Improvement (QAPI) framework for behavioral health.
- Create a resident wellness program that includes structured social activities, meaningful engagement, and access to mental health support groups.

### For Educators

- If you teach in a medical, nursing, social work, or psychology program, advocate for required geriatric mental health content. Expose students to geriatric clinical settings, which research shows triples interest in the field (AAGP, 2021).
- Develop or expand geriatric mental health electives and clinical rotations. Partner with local nursing homes, PACE programs, and AAAs to provide field placements.
- Include ageism awareness and bias training in mental health professional education. Explicit attention to therapeutic nihilism and clinical ageism can change practice patterns.
- Invite geriatric psychiatrists, psychologists, and social workers to speak to students about career opportunities and the clinical rewards of working with older adults.

### For Clergy and Faith Leaders

- Participate in gatekeeper training (QPR or equivalent) to recognize mental health warning signs in older congregants. Training takes 90 minutes and is available through local mental health agencies and the QPR Institute (<https://www.qprinstitute.com/>).
- Create pastoral care programs that include mental health screening and referral. Train lay visitors and deacons to ask simple screening questions during home visits.
- Partner with local mental health providers for consultation. Establish a referral relationship so that when you identify a congregant in distress, you can make a warm handoff to a professional.
- Host mental health education events at your congregation, addressing stigma and normalizing help-seeking. Many older adults are more willing to attend a church-based event than a clinical setting.
- Establish "caring congregation" programs that assign regular phone calls and visits to isolated older members. Create buddy systems that pair isolated individuals with active congregants.

### For Employers

- If your organization employs caregivers of older adults, provide employee assistance programs with geriatric-specific mental health resources.
- Support flexible work arrangements for employees managing elder care, recognizing that caregiver burnout has cascading effects on productivity, absenteeism, and healthcare costs.
- Train HR staff and managers to recognize caregiver distress and connect employees to resources including FMLA protections, caregiver support groups, and respite care referrals.

## Advocacy Toolkit

### Key Talking Points

When discussing older adult mental health with others, emphasize:

1. **The crisis is measurable**: 11.6 million older Americans experience mental health conditions, yet fewer than half receive treatment. Adults aged 85+ have the highest suicide rate of any age group at 22.4 per 100,000 (CDC, 2022). White men aged 85+ die by suicide at 55.2 per 100,000, nearly four times the national average.
2. **The workforce does not exist**: There are fewer than 1,800 board-certified geriatric psychiatrists for 58 million older adults, a ratio of approximately 1:27,000. Only 57% of fellowship positions are filled (NRMP, 2023). The workforce may actually be shrinking as retirements outpace new certifications.
3. **Treatment works and saves money**: The IMPACT collaborative care model doubles depression treatment response and saves $3,363 per patient over four years. Social isolation adds an estimated $6.7 billion annually to Medicare spending (National Academies, 2020). Investment in geriatric mental health is cost-saving, not cost-adding.
4. **Nursing homes are failing at mental health**: 65% of nursing home residents have a mental health diagnosis, but fewer than 30% of facilities have adequate mental health staffing. Antipsychotic medications are used as chemical restraints in place of proper care, despite FDA warnings about mortality risk.
5. **This affects everyone**: If you are fortunate enough to reach old age, this system will serve you. If you have parents or grandparents, it affects them now. Investing in older adult mental health is investing in our own futures. The baby boomers are turning 65 at a rate of 10,000 per day; by 2030 there will be 73 million older Americans.

### Common Misconceptions to Counter

| Misconception | Reality | Source |
|---------------|---------|--------|
| "Depression is a normal part of aging" | Depression is not a normal part of aging. It is a treatable medical condition. Only 1-5% of community-dwelling older adults have major depression, though rates are much higher in institutional settings. | NIMH, 2023 |
| "Older adults won't use mental health services" | When services are accessible and delivered in trusted settings (primary care, home-based), older adults engage. The IMPACT model achieved high engagement rates. Telehealth utilization surged to 10.1 million Medicare visits in 2020. Barriers are systemic, not individual. | Unutzer et al., JAMA, 2002; OIG, 2022 |
| "We can't afford to expand Medicare mental health benefits" | Untreated mental illness in older adults costs far more than treatment. Avoidable hospitalizations ($12,000-$25,000 each), unnecessary ED visits ($2,500-$5,000), and premature institutionalization ($90,000+/year) dwarf the cost of outpatient treatment. | MedPAC, 2023; Unutzer et al., 2008 |
| "Social isolation is a personal problem, not a policy problem" | Social isolation adds $6.7 billion annually to Medicare spending. The health impact is comparable to smoking 15 cigarettes per day. Government-funded programs (OAA, senior centers, transportation) reduce isolation. The Surgeon General declared it an epidemic. | National Academies, 2020; Surgeon General, 2023 |
| "Nursing homes provide adequate mental health care" | 65% of nursing home residents have a mental health diagnosis, yet fewer than 30% of facilities have adequate mental health staffing. Wait times for psychiatric consultation average 14-21 days. Antipsychotic medications are used as chemical restraints. | CMS, 2023; OIG, 2023 |
| "Older adult suicide is a choice we must respect" | An estimated 70-90% of older adults who die by suicide had diagnosable, treatable depression. Only 20% had mental health contact in the prior 30 days. The 72% firearm completion rate suggests means restriction could save thousands of lives. | CDC WISQARS, 2022; Luoma et al., 2002 |
| "Telehealth doesn't work for older adults" | Studies show telepsychiatry outcomes equivalent to in-person care. Medicare telemental health visits increased from 0.8 million to 10.1 million in one year. Many older adults adopted telehealth successfully during the pandemic. | Hubley et al., 2016; OIG, 2022 |
