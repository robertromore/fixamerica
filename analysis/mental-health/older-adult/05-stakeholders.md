# Older Adult Mental Health: Stakeholders

The landscape of older adult mental health is shaped by the interactions of several key groups, each with its own interests, resources, and capacity to influence policy. The challenge of reform lies in aligning the priorities of vulnerable populations with limited political voice, an overstretched workforce, and powerful institutional actors whose economic interests may conflict with optimal care. Unlike many policy domains where stakeholder interests are relatively clear-cut, older adult mental health involves a web of overlapping jurisdictions, competing budget claims, and cultural attitudes that shape how society values the mental wellbeing of its oldest members.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Older adults with mental health conditions | Direct recipients of (or denied access to) care; experience suffering, disability, and premature death from untreated conditions | ~11.6 million (20% of 58.4 million adults 65+) | Access to affordable, evidence-based treatment; dignity; autonomy; social connection |
| Older adults with dementia and behavioral symptoms | Require specialized behavioral health services often delivered through medication rather than psychosocial intervention | 6.7 million with Alzheimer's/related dementias | Person-centered care; reduced chemical restraint; caregiver support; safety |
| Family caregivers of older adults with mental health conditions | Bear physical, emotional, and financial costs of caregiving; high rates of depression (40%), anxiety, and burnout | ~11.5 million dementia caregivers; millions more caring for older adults with depression/other conditions | Respite care; education; mental health support for themselves; financial assistance; workplace accommodations |
| Nursing home residents with mental health diagnoses | Subject to institutional care quality; high rates of depression, overmedication, and social isolation | ~845,000 (65% of 1.3 million nursing home residents) | Adequate mental health staffing; psychosocial activities; protection from inappropriate medication; dignity |
| Socially isolated older adults | Experience loneliness and disconnection that drives depression, cognitive decline, and mortality | ~14 million community-dwelling adults 65+ | Transportation; social programming; community connection; affordable housing near services |
| Older adults at risk of suicide | Disproportionately high completion rates; least likely to seek help; most lethal means (firearms) | Highest rate: white men 85+ (55.2 per 100,000) | Crisis services adapted to older adults; lethal means counseling; connected healthcare; reduced stigma |
| Older veterans with mental health conditions | PTSD, depression, substance use, and TBI compounded by aging; dual VA/Medicare navigation | ~2.7 million veterans aged 65+; est. 25-30% with mental health needs | Coordinated VA-Medicare care; PTSD treatment adapted for aging; peer support; suicide prevention |
| LGBTQ+ older adults | Higher rates of isolation, depression, and anxiety; smaller social networks; fear of discrimination in care settings | Est. 2.7 million adults 65+ identifying as LGBTQ+ | Culturally affirming care; protection from discrimination; inclusive social programming |
| Older adults of color | Structural barriers to care including provider shortage in minority communities, language barriers, cultural stigma, and historical mistrust | ~13.4 million adults 65+ who are non-white or Hispanic | Culturally competent providers; language access; trust-building; community-based outreach |

### Secondary Stakeholders

- **Adult children of aging parents**: Approximately 53 million Americans serve as informal caregivers to aging family members (AARP/National Alliance for Caregiving, 2020). They navigate complex systems to secure mental health care for parents, often across state lines, and frequently sacrifice their own employment, financial security, and health in the process. The "sandwich generation," simultaneously caring for aging parents and raising children, is particularly strained. Caregiver burnout has been estimated to cost the U.S. economy $522 billion annually in lost productivity, healthcare costs, and other expenses (AARP, 2021).
- **Grandchildren raised by grandparents**: Approximately 2.6 million children are raised by grandparents (Census Bureau, 2023). Grandparent mental health directly affects child welfare and educational outcomes. Grandfamilies face unique stressors, including legal custody challenges, financial strain, and grief over the circumstances that led to the caregiving arrangement (typically parental substance use, incarceration, or death).
- **Community-dwelling older adults without diagnosed conditions**: The broader population of older adults benefits from community mental health infrastructure, social connection programs, and destigmatization, even if they do not currently have a diagnosis. Prevention-oriented programs such as senior centers, congregate meals, and social prescribing serve this population and reduce the risk of future mental health conditions.
- **Healthcare system at large**: Untreated mental health conditions in older adults drive up utilization of emergency departments, hospitalizations, and primary care visits, creating costs borne across the system. Depression in older adults is associated with a 50% increase in total healthcare costs, even controlling for medical comorbidities (Unutzer et al., *American Journal of Psychiatry*, 2009). The cost burden falls on Medicare, Medicaid, hospitals, and private insurers.
- **Rural communities**: Rural areas disproportionately affected by provider shortages, hospital closures, and the departure of younger residents face compounded challenges. Rural older adults have higher rates of isolation, fewer transportation options, and less access to both general and specialized mental health care. Rural communities' economic dependence on nursing homes (often a major local employer) creates political dynamics that complicate reform.

## Who Has Power

### Decision Makers

| Actor | Role | Authority | Resources | Influence Level |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Congress (Senate Finance, HELP; House Ways & Means, Energy & Commerce) | Legislates Medicare/Medicaid benefits, OAA reauthorization, workforce funding | Authorization and appropriation of federal mental health programs | Controls $12.3 billion+ Medicare mental health spending; $2.3 billion OAA | High |
| Senate Special Committee on Aging | Oversight and investigation of aging issues; no legislative authority | Convenes hearings, publishes reports, generates public attention | Limited direct authority but significant agenda-setting capacity | Medium |
| Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) | Administers Medicare/Medicaid; sets reimbursement rates and quality standards | Regulatory authority over coverage, payment, nursing home oversight | Approximately $1.5 trillion total Medicare/Medicaid budget; ~1,500 survey staff | High |
| CMS Innovation Center (CMMI) | Tests new payment and service delivery models | Authority to launch demonstrations without Congressional authorization | ~$10 billion in testing authority (Section 3021 of ACA) | Medium-High |
| Administration for Community Living (ACL) | Administers Older Americans Act programs | Distributes OAA funding to states and AAAs; oversees elder abuse programs | $2.3 billion (FY2024) budget; network of 56 state/territory agencies | Medium |
| State Medicaid agencies | Administer Medicaid, including nursing home coverage and home/community-based services | Set Medicaid eligibility, covered services, and provider rates at state level | Combined state/federal Medicaid spending ~$800 billion; varies by state | High |
| State mental health authorities | Administer state mental health systems, including community mental health centers | Allocate state mental health funding; license providers; set standards | Average state mental health agency budget ~$2.5 billion; varies widely | Medium |
| State licensing boards | Control scope of practice for mental health professionals | Determine which providers can diagnose, treat, prescribe independently | Regulatory authority over practice boundaries | Medium |
| Area Agencies on Aging (622 nationwide) | Plan and coordinate local aging services; administer OAA programs at community level | Allocate local OAA funds; contract with service providers; conduct needs assessment | Combined budgets vary; $4-$100+ million per AAA depending on region | Medium (locally) |

### Key Organizations and Institutions

- **American Association for Geriatric Psychiatry (AAGP)**: Primary professional organization for geriatric psychiatrists. Advocates for workforce development, reimbursement reform, and training programs. Approximately 2,000 members. Publishes *The American Journal of Geriatric Psychiatry*. Limited budget constrains advocacy capacity relative to larger organizations, but provides essential clinical expertise and policy recommendations.
- **American Geriatrics Society (AGS)**: Multidisciplinary organization of healthcare professionals dedicated to improving care for older adults. Publishes the Beers Criteria for potentially inappropriate medication use in older adults, one of the most widely cited clinical guidelines in geriatric medicine. Over 6,000 members. Advocates for geriatric workforce development and integrated care models.
- **Alzheimer's Association**: The largest voluntary health organization for Alzheimer's disease. Annual revenue exceeding $350 million. Funds research, provides caregiver support, and advocates for increased federal funding. Significant political influence through grassroots network of 80+ chapters and approximately 500,000 advocacy network members. Has demonstrated exceptional lobbying effectiveness, helping to increase NIH Alzheimer's research funding from $566 million in FY2015 to $3.7 billion in FY2024. Focuses primarily on research and caregiver support rather than broader mental health service delivery.
- **AARP**: The largest organization for Americans aged 50+, with approximately 38 million members. Extensive lobbying capacity and political influence, spending $8.6 million on federal lobbying in 2023 (OpenSecrets). Has historically focused on Social Security, Medicare drug costs, and caregiving rather than mental health specifically, but increasing engagement on social isolation and loneliness, including commissioning national surveys and publishing policy briefs.
- **National Council on Aging (NCOA)**: Advocacy organization focused on economic security and health for older adults. Administers evidence-based health programs through community organizations, including chronic disease self-management programs and falls prevention programs. Has championed mental health screening integration in aging services. Operates BenefitsCheckUp, used by millions of older adults annually.
- **LeadingAge**: Association of nonprofit aging services providers, including nursing homes, assisted living, continuing care retirement communities, and community-based organizations. Over 5,000 member organizations. Advocates for workforce investment, regulatory reform that supports quality, and expanded HCBS options. Generally more reform-oriented than the for-profit trade association (AHCA).
- **American Health Care Association (AHCA)**: Association of for-profit and other long-term care facilities. Represents approximately 14,000 nursing homes and assisted living facilities. Annual lobbying spending of approximately $8 million. Advocates for reimbursement increases and regulatory flexibility. May oppose mandated staffing ratios and mental health access requirements that increase costs.
- **National PACE Association**: Advocacy organization for Programs of All-Inclusive Care for the Elderly. Promotes PACE expansion as a model for integrated care that includes mental health. Represents 156 PACE organizations serving approximately 65,000 participants. Advocates for regulatory streamlining and startup funding for new PACE programs.
- **National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI)**: The largest grassroots mental health advocacy organization in the U.S. Approximately 600 state and local affiliates. Focuses primarily on severe mental illness and younger populations but has increasing programming for older adults and family members of older adults with mental health conditions.
- **Mental Health America (MHA)**: Mental health advocacy and screening organization. Operates free online screening tools used by millions annually. Has published reports on older adult mental health but focuses primarily on broader population-level mental health policy.

### Influencers

- **National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH)**: Sets research priorities and funds studies on older adult mental health. Research findings shape clinical practice and policy. Spent approximately $90 million on aging-related mental health research in FY2023, a small fraction of its $2.2 billion total budget.
- **Medicare Payment Advisory Commission (MedPAC)**: Independent Congressional advisory body on Medicare payment policy. Recommendations on reimbursement rates, benefit design, and access influence Congressional action. Has recommended eliminating the 190-day psychiatric hospital limit and strengthening Medicare Advantage mental health network adequacy. MedPAC recommendations carry significant weight because of the commission's reputation for technical rigor and political independence.
- **Pharmaceutical industry**: Drug manufacturers of antidepressants, antipsychotics, and anxiolytics have a significant economic interest in older adult prescribing patterns. Industry lobbying and direct-to-provider marketing influence treatment choices. The combined U.S. market for geriatric psychotropic medications exceeds $12 billion annually. The 2023 FDA approval of brexpiprazole (Rexulti) for Alzheimer's agitation, with a list price of approximately $1,400 per month, illustrates the industry's interest in expanding the market for dementia behavioral treatments.
- **Academic medical centers**: Major research universities with geriatric psychiatry programs produce the research evidence, train the workforce, and develop the clinical models that shape policy options. Key institutions include the University of Pittsburgh (late-life depression research), Duke University (aging and mental health services), UCLA (geriatric mental health services research), Johns Hopkins University (dementia behavioral management), University of Washington (IMPACT model development and dissemination), and Dartmouth (mental health services research). These institutions shape the evidence base but their findings reach policymakers through a slow and filtered process.
- **U.S. Surgeon General**: The 2023 Advisory on Loneliness and Isolation elevated social isolation to national policy prominence. The Surgeon General's convening authority and public platform can shape national conversation and create political momentum for action, even without legislative power.
- **State long-term care ombudsmen**: Advocates for nursing home and assisted living residents who investigate complaints and identify systemic quality problems. The ombudsman program, funded through the OAA, provides critical oversight of mental health care in institutional settings.

## Stakeholder Interests and Alignment

### Aligned Interests (Potential Coalition Partners)

| Coalition | Shared Interest | Strength |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| AAGP + AGS + Alzheimer's Association | Increased federal funding for geriatric mental health research, training, and services | Strong |
| AARP + NCOA + LeadingAge | Expanded OAA funding and community-based social isolation programs | Strong |
| Family caregiver organizations + Alzheimer's Association + PACE | Integrated care models that reduce caregiver burden and improve outcomes | Moderate |
| CMS + quality advocacy organizations + consumer groups | Reduced inappropriate antipsychotic use in nursing homes | Strong (demonstrated success) |
| Telehealth companies + rural health advocates + AAGP | Expanded Medicare telehealth for geriatric mental health | Strong |
| State Medicaid agencies + ACL + AAAs | Home and community-based services as alternatives to institutional care | Moderate (fiscal constraints limit) |
| VA + AAGP + veterans' organizations | Improved geriatric mental health services for aging veteran population | Moderate |
| LGBTQ+ aging organizations (SAGE) + NCOA + NAMI | Culturally affirming mental health services for marginalized older populations | Emerging |

### Conflicting Interests

- **Nursing home industry vs. quality advocates**: Nursing home operators (represented by AHCA) seek regulatory flexibility and resist mandated mental health staffing requirements, which increase costs. Quality advocates and consumer organizations push for enforceable standards that ensure residents receive adequate mental health care. The tension is most acute around the CMS minimum staffing proposal, where AHCA argues that mandates without Medicaid rate increases will force closures, while consumer advocates argue that current staffing levels are dangerously inadequate.
- **Pharmaceutical industry vs. deprescribing advocates**: Pharmaceutical companies benefit from high rates of psychotropic prescribing in older adults. Geriatricians, pharmacists, and patient safety advocates increasingly promote deprescribing initiatives that reduce inappropriate medication use, directly threatening revenue. The industry counterargument emphasizes that medications are evidence-based treatments and that deprescribing can lead to symptom recurrence, a legitimate clinical concern that is also conveniently aligned with commercial interests.
- **Medicare Advantage plans vs. mental health access advocates**: Medicare Advantage plans (serving over 51% of Medicare beneficiaries) control costs partly through narrow provider networks and prior authorization requirements that can restrict mental health access. Mental health parity advocates push for network adequacy requirements and limits on prior authorization for behavioral health services. The 2023 OIG finding that 13% of MA mental health prior authorization denials should have been approved suggests systematic underauthorization.
- **Fiscal conservatives vs. program expansionists**: Those focused on Medicare's long-term solvency resist benefit expansions, while advocates argue that investing in mental health reduces downstream costs. This tension is structural rather than partisan: both parties have members on each side, and the CBO scoring methodology, which often fails to credit prevention-generated savings, reinforces fiscal objections.
- **Private equity nursing home owners vs. virtually everyone else**: Private equity firms that have acquired nursing homes seek returns through cost reduction, creating incentives that directly conflict with mental health care quality, which is inherently labor-intensive and relationship-dependent. LeadingAge (nonprofit providers), consumer advocates, labor unions, and quality organizations align in opposing the PE model's effects on care.

### The Geriatric Care Iron Triangle

A structural dynamic analogous to the classic "iron triangle" operates in older adult mental health:

- **Congressional aging committees** (Senate Special Committee on Aging, relevant subcommittees of Finance, HELP, Ways & Means, and Energy & Commerce) respond to organized constituencies and control authorization and appropriation
- **CMS and ACL** administer programs and influence spending within statutory authority, but are constrained by appropriations levels and statutory limitations
- **Industry and provider groups** (AHCA, pharmaceutical companies, large health systems, Medicare Advantage insurers) lobby for favorable reimbursement and regulatory treatment, wielding combined annual lobbying expenditures of $50+ million

This triangle tends to produce incremental adjustments that serve organized interests rather than the transformative reform needed to address the older adult mental health crisis. Older adults with mental health conditions, the primary stakeholders, have the weakest voice within this structure. They lack a dedicated grassroots advocacy organization comparable to the Alzheimer's Association, AARP, or NAMI that focuses specifically on geriatric mental health service delivery.

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on policy:

- **Lobbying**: AHCA and pharmaceutical industry spend a combined $50+ million annually on federal lobbying (OpenSecrets, 2023). AARP spent $8.6 million on lobbying in 2023. The AAGP operates on a far smaller budget, limiting its advocacy capacity relative to industry. Medicare Advantage insurers (through AHIP and individual company lobbying) spent approximately $30 million in 2023.
- **Campaign contributions**: The healthcare sector is consistently the largest source of campaign contributions. Long-term care and pharmaceutical interests direct contributions to members of relevant committees, particularly Senate Finance and House Ways & Means. In the 2023-2024 election cycle, the healthcare sector contributed over $700 million to federal candidates and parties (OpenSecrets).
- **Revolving door**: Former CMS administrators and Congressional health staff frequently join healthcare industry lobbying firms or consulting practices, creating relationships that advantage industry perspectives in policy discussions. Former ACL administrators have similarly moved to aging services organizations, though the financial stakes are smaller.
- **Public advocacy**: The Alzheimer's Association's grassroots advocacy network, the most mobilized constituency in geriatric care, has been effective in securing increased NIH funding for Alzheimer's research (from $566 million in FY2015 to $3.7 billion in FY2024). This represents the most successful advocacy campaign in geriatric policy and provides a model for what organized advocacy could achieve for broader geriatric mental health. Mental health advocacy has not achieved comparable grassroots mobilization for older adult services.
- **Expertise and information**: Academic researchers and professional organizations like AAGP and AGS provide evidence that shapes policy options. The IMPACT collaborative care model, the PROSPECT suicide prevention trial, and the Beers Criteria are all examples of research that has influenced policy. However, the translation of evidence into policy is slow and often filtered through interest group priorities. The 15-year gap between the IMPACT trial (2002) and Medicare CoCM billing codes (2017) illustrates this lag.
- **Media and narrative**: Investigative journalism (the New York Times series on geriatric mental health, ProPublica's nursing home reporting, KFF Health News coverage) creates public attention that pressures policymakers. However, coverage is episodic rather than sustained, and the issue competes for attention with more dramatic health policy stories.

## Stakeholder Engagement Gaps

Several important stakeholder groups are underrepresented in the policy process:

- **Older adults themselves**: While older adults vote at high rates, their political engagement on mental health issues is minimal, reflecting internalized stigma, the perception that mental health is a personal rather than political issue, and the dominance of economic issues (Social Security, Medicare drug costs) in aging advocacy.
- **Direct care workers**: CNAs, home health aides, and personal care attendants who provide the majority of hands-on care to older adults with mental health conditions are rarely consulted in policy development, despite their critical role in detection, support, and care continuity. Their perspectives on what is needed at the point of care could improve policy design.
- **Faith communities**: Clergy and religious congregations provide informal mental health support to millions of isolated older adults but are not systematically engaged in policy discussions about geriatric mental health infrastructure.
- **Community-based organizations**: Small nonprofits, volunteer programs, and mutual aid networks that provide social connection to isolated older adults operate outside formal healthcare and aging service systems and are invisible to most policymakers.
