# Older Adult Mental Health: Implementation Roadmap

## Strategic Approach

Reform of the older adult mental health system requires a phased strategy that builds from achievable administrative and legislative wins toward fundamental structural transformation. The approach recognizes three realities: (1) the workforce shortage cannot be solved quickly, so interim solutions (telehealth, collaborative care, scope of practice reform) must bridge the gap; (2) Medicare reform requires Congressional action and must be sequenced to build political support through early evidence of cost savings; and (3) community-level social infrastructure takes years to build but yields the greatest long-term returns in prevention and population health.

Each phase builds on the preceding one. Phase 1 establishes the administrative foundations and quick wins that generate evidence and political momentum. Phase 2 enacts the major legislative reforms that require Congressional action and appropriations. Phase 3 scales proven models to the national level and achieves the measurable population health targets that define success. The phasing also reflects political reality: Phase 1 actions can largely be accomplished through executive branch authority and bipartisan legislation, while Phase 2 requires larger appropriations and more complex legislative action that benefits from the evidence generated in Phase 1.

The strategy draws on the Alzheimer's Association advocacy model, which achieved transformative funding growth ($566 million to $3.7 billion) through a combination of grassroots mobilization, bipartisan coalition building, evidence-based messaging, and strategic attachment of provisions to must-pass legislation. Adapting this model to geriatric mental health requires building a comparable advocacy infrastructure, which is itself a Phase 1 objective.

## Phase 1: Foundation and Quick Wins (Years 1-2)

### Objectives

- Secure permanent Medicare telemental health authorization for older adults
- Launch geriatric mental health workforce pipeline investments
- Initiate nursing home mental health quality improvement through CMS rulemaking
- Establish the 988 older adult crisis track
- Begin social prescribing pilot programs
- Build advocacy coalition for Phase 2 legislative action

### Key Actions

| Action | Lead Actor | Timeline | Resources Needed | Success Metric |
|--------|-----------|----------|------------------|----------------|
| Permanent telemental health authorization with audio-only parity | Congress; CMS rulemaking | Months 1-12 | Legislative staff time; CMS regulatory capacity | Bill signed; rule published |
| Launch 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline older adult track | SAMHSA | Months 3-15 | $100 million Year 1; 500+ trained counselors | Track operational; call volume targets met |
| First cohort of geriatric mental health loan forgiveness awards | HRSA | Months 6-18 | $150 million; application infrastructure | 250+ awards in first cohort |
| CMS proposed rule on nursing home mental health access | CMS | Months 6-12 | CMS regulatory staff; public comment process | NPRM published in Federal Register |
| Social prescribing pilot launch in 100 Medicare practices | CMMI (CMS Innovation Center) | Months 9-24 | $100 million; practice recruitment; evaluation design | 100 practices enrolled; referral protocols established |
| Digital Bridge pilot: 100,000 tablets to low-income older adults | ACL with FCC coordination | Months 6-18 | $50 million; device procurement; digital literacy trainers | Devices distributed; training completed |
| Geriatric psychiatry mandatory rotation NPRM | ACGME with CMS | Months 6-18 | Regulatory development; stakeholder engagement | Proposed standard published |
| Establish Interagency Coordinating Committee on Older Adult MH | HHS Secretary | Months 1-6 | Executive order or HHS directive; staff assignment | Committee formed; first meeting held |
| Launch National Geriatric Mental Health Advocacy Coalition | AAGP, AARP, Alzheimer's Assoc., NCOA (conveners) | Months 1-12 | Foundation seed funding; coalition coordinator | Coalition established with shared policy agenda |
| Medicare Advantage mental health network adequacy guidance | CMS | Months 3-12 | CMS regulatory staff | Guidance published; compliance expectations set |

### Legislative Requirements

- Consolidated Appropriations or standalone bill permanently authorizing Medicare telemental health flexibilities (based on expiring COVID-era waivers)
- Appropriations for HRSA geriatric mental health workforce programs ($450 million)
- SAMHSA reauthorization or supplemental appropriation for 988 older adult track
- CMMI authority for social prescribing pilot (existing Innovation Center authority may suffice)

### Political Strategy for Phase 1

Phase 1 actions are designed to be achievable through bipartisan action and executive authority. Telehealth permanence has broad Congressional support; workforce investment appeals to both parties; nursing home quality improvement is politically popular. The coalition-building objective is critical: the National Geriatric Mental Health Advocacy Coalition would unite AAGP, AARP, Alzheimer's Association, NCOA, NAMI, LeadingAge, and consumer organizations around a shared legislative agenda, creating the grassroots infrastructure needed for Phase 2. The coalition should be modeled on the Alzheimer's Association advocacy network, with state-level chapters, Congressional district captains, and coordinated lobby days.

### Expected Outcomes

By the end of Year 2, the following should be achieved: permanent telemental health access for all Medicare beneficiaries aged 65+; a functioning crisis line track for older adults; the first wave of geriatric mental health professionals entering loan forgiveness commitments; published proposed rules for nursing home mental health standards; and operational social prescribing pilots generating early data. These actions require relatively modest new appropriations and can largely be accomplished through executive branch action and bipartisan legislation. Critically, each action generates constituency-building effects: older adults using telehealth, crisis counselors serving callers, providers receiving loan forgiveness, and community organizations participating in social prescribing pilots all become advocates for continued and expanded investment.

## Phase 2: Legislative Reform and System Building (Years 3-5)

### Objectives

- Enact Medicare Geriatric Mental Health Parity and Access Act
- Reauthorize and expand the Older Americans Act with dedicated social isolation prevention funding
- Finalize and implement nursing home mental health quality standards
- Expand PACE through regulatory streamlining
- Build hub-and-spoke telepsychiatry networks in all 10 HHS regions
- Reform Medicare Advantage mental health access requirements

### Key Actions

| Action | Lead Actor | Timeline | Resources Needed | Success Metric |
|--------|-----------|----------|------------------|----------------|
| Repeal 190-day psychiatric hospital lifetime limit | Congress (Senate Finance; House Ways & Means) | Year 3 | CBO scoring; legislative negotiation | Statutory change enacted |
| Medicare reimbursement rate increase for geriatric psychiatric services | CMS via Congressional direction | Years 3-4 | $1.5-2 billion annually; Physician Fee Schedule adjustment | Reimbursement reaches 95% of commercial average |
| OAA reauthorization with $500 million social isolation prevention title | Congress (Senate HELP; House Education & Labor) | Years 3-4 | New appropriation authorization; AAA capacity building | OAA signed with new title; first grants awarded |
| Final rule: nursing home mental health access standards | CMS | Year 3 | Survey agency capacity; facility compliance support | Final rule published; phase-in begins |
| PACE regulatory streamlining and Rural Health Model | CMS; Congress | Years 3-5 | $500 million in start-up grants; regulatory reform | 50 new PACE programs approved; Rural Model launched |
| Hub-and-spoke telepsychiatry networks | HRSA; academic medical centers | Years 3-5 | $100 million annually; partner recruitment | Networks operational in all 10 HHS regions |
| Mandatory CoCM implementation incentives | CMS | Years 3-4 | Enhanced reimbursement; practice transformation grants | 15% of Medicare primary care practices billing CoCM codes |
| National gatekeeper training program rollout | ACL; SAMHSA | Years 3-5 | $75 million annually; training curriculum development | 250,000 community members trained |
| Medicare network adequacy standards for MA mental health | CMS rulemaking | Year 3-4 | Regulatory capacity; MA plan compliance monitoring | Standards published and enforced |
| MA prior authorization reform for behavioral health | CMS rulemaking | Year 3 | Regulatory capacity; plan compliance monitoring | Prior authorization denial rates for MH below 5% |
| PACE Medicare-Only pilot launch | CMS Innovation Center | Year 3-4 | Budget-neutral capitation design; evaluation protocol | 5,000 Medicare-Only enrollees by Year 5 |
| Mid-career geriatric MH credential program launch | AAGP; AGS; credentialing bodies | Years 3-4 | Curriculum development; continuing education infrastructure | 500 providers credentialed by Year 5 |

### Legislative Requirements

- Medicare Geriatric Mental Health Parity and Access Act (comprehensive bill including 190-day repeal, reimbursement reform, CoCM incentives, MA network adequacy, community health worker coverage)
- Older Americans Act Reauthorization with Social Connection title
- PACE Expansion Act (regulatory reform, startup grants, Medicare-Only authority)
- Appropriations for all new programs

### Evidence-to-Policy Pipeline

Phase 2 legislation should be informed by early results from Phase 1 pilots. Social prescribing pilot data from CMMI should demonstrate feasibility and early cost savings. 988 older adult track call data should document demand and effectiveness. Nursing home mental health access proposed rules should generate public comment that informs final rule design. The Phase 1-to-Phase 2 pipeline is critical: policymakers need evidence that proposed reforms work before committing to large-scale appropriations.

### Expected Outcomes

By the end of Year 5, the structural reforms necessary for a functional older adult mental health system should be enacted and in early implementation. Medicare should provide equitable mental health coverage without discriminatory limits. Nursing homes should be subject to enforceable mental health access standards. The OAA should include dedicated social isolation prevention funding reaching communities nationwide. PACE should be expanding into new geographic areas, including a Medicare-Only pilot. The geriatric mental health workforce pipeline should show measurable growth, with fellowship fill rates approaching 80% and the first cohorts of loan-forgiveness providers completing their commitments. The advocacy coalition should be a permanent fixture of Washington policy, with the capacity to monitor implementation and respond to rollback attempts.

## Phase 3: Scaling and Transformation (Years 6-10)

### Objectives

- Achieve national workforce targets for geriatric mental health professionals
- Scale PACE enrollment to 500,000
- Achieve population-level reductions in social isolation, nursing home antipsychotic use, and older adult suicide
- Establish sustainable financing that makes older adult mental health a permanent system feature
- Integrate mental health into every point of contact with older adults

### Key Actions

| Action | Lead Actor | Timeline | Resources Needed | Success Metric |
|--------|-----------|----------|------------------|----------------|
| Second-wave workforce pipeline graduation | HRSA; medical schools | Years 6-8 | Sustained funding; program maintenance | 3,000+ board-certified geriatric psychiatrists |
| PACE enrollment reaches 500,000 | CMS; National PACE Association | Years 6-10 | Continued start-up grants; state regulatory cooperation | 500 PACE organizations; 500,000 enrollees |
| Social isolation prevention at scale | ACL; AAAs; community organizations | Years 6-10 | Sustained OAA appropriations | Social isolation prevalence below 15% |
| Nursing home full compliance with mental health standards | CMS; state survey agencies | Years 5-7 | Enforcement capacity; technical assistance | 95% facility compliance; antipsychotic rate below 10% |
| CoCM at scale in primary care | CMS; primary care practices | Years 6-10 | Sustained reimbursement incentives | 25% of Medicare primary care practices billing CoCM |
| Evidence-based evaluation and course correction | HHS; independent evaluators | Years 8-10 | Evaluation contracts; data infrastructure | Published evaluations; policy adjustments based on evidence |
| Older adult suicide reduction target | CDC; SAMHSA; CMS | Year 10 | Sustained prevention investment | 25% reduction from baseline |
| AAA mental health integration at scale | ACL; 622 AAAs | Years 6-10 | Training; screening tools; referral network development | 90% of AAAs integrating MH screening into intake |
| PACE Medicare-Only expansion (if pilot successful) | CMS; Congress | Years 7-10 | Legislative authority based on pilot results | 50,000 Medicare-Only PACE enrollees |
| Geriatric MH research funding at parity with burden | NIMH; Congress | Years 6-10 | Appropriations increase | NIMH geriatric MH spending proportional to disease burden |

### Expected Outcomes

By Year 10, the older adult mental health system should show measurable transformation. The geriatric psychiatrist-to-patient ratio should approach 1:10,000. PACE enrollment should demonstrate that integrated care can serve a significant portion of the eligible population. Social isolation prevalence should have declined meaningfully. Nursing home residents should receive mental health care as a standard component of their care rather than through chemical restraint. The older adult suicide rate should be declining. Crucially, the system should be self-sustaining, with evidence of cost savings reinforcing political support for continued investment and constituency-building effects making program rollback politically costly.

## Governance and Oversight

Effective implementation requires clear governance structures with accountability mechanisms:

- **Interagency Coordinating Committee on Older Adult Mental Health**: Chaired by the HHS Assistant Secretary for Mental Health and Substance Use, with representatives from CMS, ACL, HRSA, SAMHSA, NIMH, VA, DOD, HUD, and DOT. Meets quarterly to coordinate cross-agency implementation and resolve conflicts. Publishes annual implementation report with progress against milestones. The committee should include non-federal members representing AAGP, AARP, Alzheimer's Association, NCOA, and consumer organizations to ensure stakeholder input.
- **Congressional oversight**: Senate Special Committee on Aging and relevant authorizing committees (Senate Finance, HELP; House Ways & Means, Energy & Commerce) hold annual hearings on implementation progress, using the success metrics table as a scorecard. GAO conducts biennial audits of program effectiveness.
- **Independent evaluation**: GAO and the HHS Office of Inspector General conduct biennial evaluations of program effectiveness, cost-effectiveness, and implementation fidelity. CMMI evaluates social prescribing and PACE expansion pilots with rigorous methodology. Results are published publicly and inform policy adjustments.
- **Stakeholder advisory council**: AAGP, AGS, AARP, Alzheimer's Association, NCOA, LeadingAge, NAMI, and consumer representatives provide ongoing input through a federally chartered advisory committee. The council should include older adults with lived experience of mental health conditions and family caregivers, not only professional and organizational representatives.
- **Public reporting**: HHS publishes an annual Older Adult Mental Health Report Card tracking all success metrics, with data available at the state and county level where applicable. Report card methodology should be modeled on the CMS Nursing Home Compare system, which has demonstrated that public reporting drives quality improvement. The Report Card should include state-by-state rankings to create competitive incentive for state-level action.
- **Enforcement mechanism**: For nursing home standards, CMS should establish an escalating enforcement framework: technical assistance for first-time non-compliance, financial penalties for repeated violations, and decertification for persistent failure. For Medicare Advantage network adequacy, CMS should use sanction authority including enrollment freezes for non-compliant plans.

## Risk Mitigation

### Risk 1: Congressional Failure to Appropriate Funds

**Likelihood**: Medium-High

**Impact**: High

**Mitigation Strategy**: Design programs with multiple funding pathways (mandatory vs. discretionary; CMMI authority vs. new legislation). Build bipartisan support by demonstrating cost savings from Phase 1 quick wins before requesting larger Phase 2 appropriations. Attach mental health provisions to must-pass legislation (OAA reauthorization, Medicare extenders). Develop CBO-scorable proposals that demonstrate net deficit reduction over 10-year windows.

**Contingency Plan**: If Congressional funding stalls, maximize executive branch action through CMS rulemaking, CMMI demonstration authority, and reallocation within existing SAMHSA and HRSA budgets. Pursue state-level reforms in willing states to build evidence base. Seek private foundation funding for demonstration programs that generate evidence for future federal action.

### Risk 2: Workforce Pipeline Lag

**Likelihood**: High

**Impact**: Medium

**Mitigation Strategy**: Workforce growth takes 5-8 years from training investment to practicing providers. Bridge the gap with telehealth (connecting existing geriatric psychiatrists to more patients), scope of practice expansion (allowing NPs and clinical social workers to practice independently), collaborative care models (leveraging psychiatrist expertise through consultation rather than direct care), task-shifting (training primary care providers in basic geropsychiatry), and the mid-career geriatric credential (allowing experienced mental health professionals to gain geriatric competency in 12-18 months rather than completing a full fellowship).

**Contingency Plan**: If fellowship fill rates do not improve despite loan forgiveness, consider creating a new mid-career geriatric mental health credential for experienced mental health professionals, bypassing the traditional fellowship pathway. Expand international medical graduate recruitment for geriatric psychiatry positions, potentially through J-1 visa waiver programs for underserved areas.

### Risk 3: Nursing Home Industry Opposition Blocks Standards

**Likelihood**: Medium

**Impact**: Medium

**Mitigation Strategy**: Pair mandates with Medicaid rate increases and technical assistance. Phase in requirements over 3-5 years. Create telehealth exceptions for rural facilities. Focus on access to consultation (which can be remote) rather than on-site FTEs. Engage LeadingAge (nonprofit providers) as an ally distinct from AHCA (for-profit providers). Use the CMS National Partnership model, which achieved antipsychotic reduction through education and transparency rather than mandates, as a complementary strategy.

**Contingency Plan**: If industry succeeds in blocking federal standards, pursue state-level nursing home mental health requirements in states with receptive legislatures, building a patchwork that creates competitive pressure for federal action. Use public reporting of facility-level mental health quality data to create market pressure from consumers.

### Risk 4: Technology Divide Limits Telehealth Impact

**Likelihood**: Medium

**Impact**: Medium

**Mitigation Strategy**: Digital Bridge program addresses device and broadband gaps. Audio-only visits preserve access for the least technologically connected. Library and senior center telehealth kiosks provide community access points. Digital literacy training included in all telehealth expansion programs. Facilitated telehealth (with trained assistants) addresses technology barriers for older adults with cognitive or sensory limitations.

**Contingency Plan**: If digital adoption remains low among the oldest old, invest more heavily in mobile mental health services (providers traveling to patients) and hub-and-spoke models that bring geriatric expertise to local primary care offices in person. Maintain audio-only visit authorization indefinitely as a permanent access channel.

### Risk 5: Political Administration Change Reverses Progress

**Likelihood**: Medium

**Impact**: High

**Mitigation Strategy**: Embed reforms in statute rather than executive action wherever possible. Build bipartisan support by framing older adult mental health as a seniors' and veterans' issue rather than a progressive health policy issue. Create constituency-building programs (PACE, social prescribing, 988 older adult track) that generate visible benefits in Congressional districts, making reversal politically costly. Ensure that early implementation creates beneficiaries who will advocate for continuation.

**Contingency Plan**: Protect administrative gains through notice-and-comment rulemaking (harder to reverse than guidance). Document cost savings to make reversal fiscally as well as politically costly. Maintain advocacy coalition readiness for rapid response to proposed rollbacks. State-level programs can serve as a backstop if federal programs are defunded.

### Risk 6: CBO Scoring Does Not Credit Prevention Savings

**Likelihood**: High

**Impact**: Medium-High

**Mitigation Strategy**: Work with CBO to ensure that scoring models incorporate the best available evidence on prevention ROI. Develop proposals that include mandatory evaluation provisions, allowing savings to be documented and used to justify sustained funding. Use the CMMI Innovation Center's authority to test models with budget-neutrality requirements, generating CBO-credible evidence. Cite international evidence (UK social prescribing ROI, PACE hospitalization reduction) as supplementary evidence.

**Contingency Plan**: If CBO scoring prevents large-scale legislation, pursue smaller-scale authorizations within existing program authorities (CMMI demonstrations, HRSA workforce programs, SAMHSA grants) and build the evidence base for future scoring-favorable proposals.

## Resource Requirements

### Funding

| Phase | Estimated Annual Cost | Funding Source | Notes |
|-------|----------------------|----------------|-------|
| Phase 1 (Yrs 1-2) | $1.5 billion/year | Discretionary appropriations; CMMI authority; existing HRSA/SAMHSA programs | Relatively modest; can be partially funded through reallocation |
| Phase 2 (Yrs 3-5) | $4-5 billion/year | New authorizations; Medicare statutory changes; OAA reauthorization | Largest investment phase; requires Congressional action |
| Phase 3 (Yrs 6-10) | $3-4 billion/year | Sustained appropriations; Medicare savings reinvestment | Cost stabilizes as prevention reduces downstream spending |
| **Total (10 years)** | **$35-45 billion** | | Offset by estimated $50-70 billion in reduced Medicare hospitalization, ED, and institutional costs |

### Context for Investment Scale

The $35-45 billion 10-year total should be understood in context. Medicare spends over $900 billion annually. The $6.7 billion annual Medicare cost of social isolation alone (National Academies, 2020) exceeds the annual Phase 1 investment. NIH spending on Alzheimer's research ($3.7 billion in FY2024) exceeds the annual investment in all phases for geriatric mental health services. The proposed investment represents less than 0.5% of annual Medicare spending and is projected to generate net savings over time.

### Personnel

- **Phase 1**: 200-300 new federal FTEs across CMS, SAMHSA, HRSA, ACL for program administration. 500+ 988 counselors. 1,000+ community health workers for social prescribing pilots. 50+ digital literacy trainers.
- **Phase 2**: Sustained federal staffing plus 2,000+ state survey agency staff for nursing home oversight. 10,000+ community-based workers for social isolation programs. Growing geriatric mental health provider pipeline. 3,000+ social prescribing link workers.
- **Phase 3**: Reduced federal program staff as programs mature. Continued growth in clinical workforce to achieve targets. Transition to sustainable community-based workforce models.

### Infrastructure

- **Technology**: 988 Lifeline systems upgrade for older adult track; Digital Bridge tablet procurement and distribution; telehealth platform development and interoperability standards; data collection and evaluation infrastructure; community telehealth kiosk deployment.
- **Physical**: Senior center renovation and expansion in underserved communities; PACE facility development; telehealth kiosks in libraries and community centers; hub-and-spoke network administrative infrastructure.
- **Institutional**: CMMI evaluation capacity; state survey agency capacity building; AAA organizational development for social prescribing and mental health integration; advocacy coalition infrastructure; mid-career credential program development.
