# Older Adult Mental Health: Opposition to Reform

Proposals to expand geriatric mental health services, increase Medicare coverage, mandate nursing home mental health staffing, or invest in social isolation programs face resistance from those who believe such expansions are fiscally unsustainable, exceed the appropriate role of government, risk regulatory overreach, or are simply lower priorities than other healthcare needs. Understanding the structure, resources, and arguments of opposition forces is essential for designing reforms that can overcome resistance and for anticipating the implementation challenges that arise even after legislative victories.

---

## Who Opposes Reform

### Organized Opposition

| Actor | Interest | Resources | Tactics |
|-------|----------|-----------|---------|
| Fiscal conservatives in Congress | Oppose expanding Medicare benefits during projected insolvency (Hospital Insurance Trust Fund depletion projected 2031) | Control appropriations process; budgetary scoring leverage | Block new authorizations; insist on pay-fors; frame expansion as fiscally irresponsible |
| American Health Care Association (AHCA) | Opposes mandated mental health staffing ratios in nursing homes; seeks regulatory flexibility | ~$8 million annual lobbying budget; represents ~14,000 facilities | Lobby against staffing mandates; emphasize cost burden; argue for flexibility over prescription |
| Pharmaceutical industry (PhRMA) | Opposes restrictions on psychotropic prescribing; resists deprescribing mandates | $357 million annual lobbying (2023, OpenSecrets); direct-to-provider marketing | Fund physician education favoring medication; lobby against prescribing restrictions; challenge formulary limitations |
| Medicare Advantage insurers (AHIP) | Resist network adequacy mandates and mental health parity enforcement that increase costs | Significant lobbying budget (~$30 million annually); represent plans covering 51% of Medicare beneficiaries | Argue for flexibility; challenge CMS network adequacy rules; use prior authorization to manage utilization |
| Small-government advocates (Heritage Foundation, Cato Institute) | Oppose federal expansion of healthcare programs on principled grounds | Substantial think tank resources; media presence; Congressional allies | Publish policy papers opposing expansion; frame as government overreach; propose market-based alternatives |
| Private equity nursing home owners | Oppose any regulation that increases labor costs or restricts cost-reduction strategies | Financial resources; representation through AHCA; legal capacity to challenge regulations | Oppose staffing mandates; lobby for regulatory flexibility; threaten facility closures |
| State budget directors (some) | Resist Medicaid expansions and state-share increases for nursing home mental health | Control state Medicaid budgets; influence governors and state legislators | Argue fiscal impossibility; prioritize other Medicaid needs; resist matching fund requirements |

### Ideological Opposition

- **Fiscal conservatism**: The primary ideological objection centers on cost. Medicare faces projected insolvency of its Hospital Insurance Trust Fund by 2031 (CMS Trustees Report, 2024). Expanding mental health benefits, increasing provider reimbursement, and funding workforce development programs would add costs at a time when fiscal restraint is argued to be essential. The Heritage Foundation's Medicare reform proposals, for example, emphasize premium support and benefit restructuring rather than coverage expansion, and would likely oppose new mental health mandates.
- **Limited government**: Some oppose expanding federal involvement in mental health care as exceeding the proper scope of government. They argue that family, community, and faith institutions should address older adult isolation and emotional wellbeing, not taxpayer-funded programs. This perspective, articulated by organizations like the Cato Institute and some religious conservative groups, views government social programs as crowding out private charity and religious ministry.
- **Market orientation**: Free-market advocates contend that provider shortages are best addressed through deregulation (expanding scope of practice, reducing licensing barriers, eliminating certificate-of-need requirements) rather than through new federal spending programs like loan forgiveness or fellowship funding. They point to successful scope-of-practice reforms in states like Arizona and Montana as evidence that market solutions can work without government spending.
- **Personal responsibility**: Some argue that older adults who do not seek available mental health services are exercising personal choice, and that government should not create programs to overcome individual reluctance to seek care. This view frames help-seeking as a matter of individual agency rather than recognizing the systemic barriers (workforce shortage, transportation, stigma, insurance gaps) that prevent access.
- **Anti-regulation sentiment**: A broader resistance to federal regulation, particularly among nursing home operators and their Congressional allies, frames mental health staffing mandates as part of a pattern of regulatory overreach that increases costs, reduces flexibility, and threatens the viability of facilities, particularly those in rural areas or serving predominantly Medicaid populations.

## Opposition Arguments and Rebuttals

### Argument 1: Expanding Medicare Mental Health Benefits Is Fiscally Irresponsible

**Claim**: "Medicare already faces insolvency by 2031. Adding new mental health benefits or increasing reimbursement rates would accelerate the program's fiscal crisis and burden future generations with unsustainable obligations."

**Evidence Cited**: CMS Trustees Report projecting Hospital Insurance Trust Fund depletion in 2031; rising Medicare spending as a share of GDP (from 3.1% in 2023 to projected 4.0% by 2040); federal deficit projections exceeding $2 trillion annually; CBO long-term budget outlook showing healthcare spending as the primary driver of federal debt growth.

**Reality**: The fiscal concerns are legitimate, but they mischaracterize the cost dynamics. Untreated mental health conditions in older adults generate substantial downstream costs: unnecessary emergency department visits ($2,500-$5,000 each), avoidable hospitalizations ($12,000-$25,000 per admission), accelerated cognitive decline requiring earlier institutional placement ($90,000+ per year in nursing home costs), and increased medical utilization for somatic complaints driven by unaddressed depression (Unutzer et al., *American Journal of Psychiatry*, 2022). The IMPACT collaborative care model has been shown to save $3,363 per patient over four years through reduced medical costs, generating a net return on investment (Unutzer et al., 2008). The National Academies estimated that social isolation alone adds $6.7 billion annually to Medicare spending through increased healthcare utilization (National Academies, 2020).

**Rebuttal**: Investing in geriatric mental health is not an additional cost but a reallocation toward more effective spending. Targeted investments in collaborative care, telehealth, and prevention can reduce overall Medicare expenditures. The question is not whether we can afford to invest, but whether we can afford not to. CBO-scorable proposals should be developed that demonstrate net savings over 10-year budget windows. The 190-day limit repeal, for example, has been scored by CBO at only $200-400 million over 10 years, a negligible amount relative to Medicare's $900+ billion annual budget. Framing targeted geriatric mental health investments as cost-avoidance measures, not benefit expansions, may overcome fiscal objections.

### Argument 2: Mandating Nursing Home Mental Health Staffing Would Increase Costs and Close Facilities

**Claim**: "Nursing homes already operate on thin margins, with Medicaid reimbursement below the cost of care in many states. Mandating dedicated mental health professionals would force facilities to close, reducing access for the very population we are trying to help."

**Evidence Cited**: AHCA estimates that 60% of nursing homes operate at a loss on Medicaid residents; 400+ nursing homes closed between 2020 and 2023; labor shortages make hiring difficult even at current wage levels; rural and small nursing homes are particularly vulnerable to cost-increasing regulations.

**Reality**: The closure concerns are partially valid. Rural and small nursing homes do face genuine financial pressure, and the pandemic accelerated closures. However, the claim that mandates would force widespread closures overstates the impact. CMS's proposed minimum staffing rule (2023) was estimated to affect approximately 75% of facilities, but the majority could achieve compliance through modest staffing increases. Moreover, the current model of using antipsychotic medication as a substitute for adequate staffing creates its own costs: falls (averaging $30,000 per fall-related hospitalization), hospitalizations, regulatory penalties, and litigation. The Congressional Budget Office estimated the cost of a minimum staffing standard at approximately $4 billion per year, a fraction of total nursing home spending of $196 billion (FY2022).

It should also be noted that the "thin margins" claim requires scrutiny. AHCA's figures represent operating margins on Medicaid residents, not total facility profitability. Many nursing homes generate revenue from short-stay Medicare rehabilitation patients at substantially higher margins, and total facility margins are often positive. Private-equity-owned facilities, which report the thinnest operating margins, frequently extract value through related-party real estate transactions, management fees, and interest payments to affiliated companies that do not appear in operating margin calculations (Gupta et al., *NBER Working Paper*, 2021).

**Rebuttal**: Phase in requirements over 3-5 years to allow facilities time to adjust. Pair mandates with Medicaid rate increases to ensure compliance does not require operating at a loss. Create telehealth exceptions for rural facilities. Focus requirements on access to mental health consultation (which can be provided remotely) rather than on-site full-time staff, reducing cost burden while ensuring residents can access care. Distinguish between the legitimate concerns of small rural facilities and the resistance of large chains and PE-owned facilities whose opposition is driven by profit preservation.

### Argument 3: Government Programs Cannot Solve Social Isolation

**Claim**: "Social isolation is a personal and community problem, not a government problem. Federal programs cannot replicate the organic social connections that families, churches, and neighborhoods provide. Government spending on isolation programs is wasteful and paternalistic."

**Evidence Cited**: Limited evidence base for government-funded social isolation interventions at scale; examples of ineffective or poorly attended programs; philosophical arguments about the proper role of government; concerns about "social engineering" and government intrusion into personal relationships.

**Reality**: The philosophical objection has merit in the sense that government cannot manufacture genuine relationships. However, government can remove barriers to social connection (transportation, affordability, accessibility) and create infrastructure that facilitates it (senior centers, congregate meals, intergenerational programs, public spaces). The evidence for specific interventions is growing. The UK's national social prescribing program, in which healthcare providers refer patients to community activities, has shown reductions in primary care utilization and improvements in wellbeing (NHS England, 2023). The OAA Congregate Nutrition Program reaches 2.4 million older adults and is valued by participants as much for social interaction as for nutrition (ACL, 2024). The PEARLS (Program to Encourage Active, Rewarding Lives) program has demonstrated significant depression reduction among isolated older adults in randomized controlled trials (Ciechanowski et al., *JAMA*, 2004).

The claim that families and churches should handle isolation ignores demographic realities. Approximately 28% of older adults live alone, many without nearby family. Religious congregation membership has declined from 70% to 47% of Americans since 1999 (Gallup, 2023). The community institutions that opposition arguments invoke are themselves in decline, and their decline is part of what drives the isolation epidemic.

**Rebuttal**: The argument creates a false dichotomy between government and community action. Effective programs leverage government funding to support community-based organizations, faith institutions, and volunteer networks. The Older Americans Act model does exactly this, channeling federal funds through local Area Agencies on Aging to community organizations. Government does not replace community; it enables community organizations to function and reach isolated individuals who have fallen through informal safety nets. The alternative, doing nothing, has documented costs: the National Academies estimated that social isolation adds approximately $6.7 billion annually to Medicare spending (National Academies, 2020).

### Argument 4: Older Adult Suicide Is a Cultural Problem, Not a Policy Problem

**Claim**: "Suicide among older adults, particularly older men, reflects existential suffering and personal choices that cannot be addressed by more mental health services. Many of these individuals have access to care but choose not to use it."

**Evidence Cited**: Low utilization of crisis services by older adults; high proportion of older adult suicides without prior mental health contact; philosophical arguments about rational suicide and end-of-life autonomy; higher suicide rates in affluent countries with universal healthcare.

**Reality**: While it is true that only 20% of older adults who die by suicide had recent mental health service contact (compared to 50% for younger adults), this statistic reflects access barriers and stigma, not informed choice. When older adults are contacted through outreach programs, they are generally receptive to help. The PROSPECT study demonstrated that collaborative care for suicidal older adults in primary care reduced suicidal ideation by 2.2 times compared to usual care (Bruce et al., *JAMA*, 2004). The Friendship Line (Institute on Aging) handles over 100,000 calls annually from older adults, demonstrating demand for crisis services when they are adapted to this population. Means restriction interventions (firearm storage counseling) reduce suicide risk without restricting autonomy. The characterization of older adult suicide as a "choice" ignores the treatable depression, social isolation, and pain that drive the vast majority of cases.

**Rebuttal**: Respect for autonomy does not require passive acceptance of preventable death. Clinical depression is a treatable illness, not a rational response to aging. An estimated 70-90% of older adults who die by suicide had diagnosable depression at the time of death. Policy should focus on ensuring that every older adult has access to treatment, means counseling, and social connection, and can make truly informed choices with these supports in place. The 72% firearm completion rate among older adult suicides suggests that means restriction alone could save thousands of lives annually without restricting any individual's legal rights.

### Argument 5: The Market Will Solve the Workforce Shortage

**Claim**: "If we reduce regulatory barriers, expand scope of practice for non-physician providers, and let market forces work, the geriatric mental health workforce will grow without new federal spending programs."

**Evidence Cited**: Successful scope-of-practice expansions in states like Arizona and Montana; growing number of psychiatric nurse practitioners; market responsiveness to unmet demand; historical examples of workforce growth in other specialties following deregulation.

**Reality**: Scope of practice reform is a useful component of a comprehensive strategy, but it is insufficient alone. The fundamental problem is not that qualified providers are prevented from practicing but that too few people choose to train in geriatric mental health. Low reimbursement, high student debt, and lack of prestige make the field unattractive compared to alternatives. Market forces have had decades to address the geriatric psychiatry shortage and have manifestly failed: fellowship fill rates have declined, not improved, during a period of rapidly growing demand. The geriatric psychiatry workforce is not just insufficient but may be shrinking as retirements outpace new certifications. Non-physician providers (NPs, social workers) are valuable but face their own pipeline challenges and are not substitutes for physician expertise in complex geriatric cases involving medication management, comorbidity assessment, and capacity evaluation.

**Rebuttal**: Market-based and government approaches are complementary, not mutually exclusive. Scope of practice reform should be pursued alongside targeted federal investments in loan forgiveness, fellowship funding, and reimbursement reform. The National Health Service Corps and similar programs have demonstrated that targeted financial incentives can direct providers to underserved specialties and areas, with 82% of NHSC participants continuing to serve in shortage areas after their obligation ends (HRSA, 2023). A purely market-based approach has been tried for 30+ years and has produced a worsening shortage.

### Argument 6: Telehealth Cannot Replace In-Person Care for Older Adults

**Claim**: "Older adults lack the technology skills, devices, and broadband access for effective telemental health. Permanent telehealth authorization will create a two-tier system where younger, tech-savvy patients get convenience while older adults get inferior screen-based care."

**Evidence Cited**: 22% of adults 65+ do not use the internet (Pew Research, 2023); higher rates of non-use among the oldest old, rural, and low-income populations; concerns about diagnostic accuracy without physical examination; studies showing reduced therapeutic alliance in telehealth versus in-person psychotherapy.

**Reality**: The concern about the digital divide is legitimate and must be addressed through programs that provide devices, broadband, and digital literacy training. Audio-only (telephone) visits are an essential component of telehealth policy, as they reach populations without internet access. However, the broader claim that telehealth is inappropriate for older adults is contradicted by evidence. Studies show that telepsychiatry produces outcomes equivalent to in-person care for depression and anxiety treatment in older adults (Hubley et al., *American Journal of Geriatric Psychiatry*, 2016). The COVID-era surge in telemental health use (from 0.8 million to 10.1 million Medicare visits in one year) demonstrated that many older adults could and would adopt telehealth when it was available. For homebound older adults, telehealth is not a lesser alternative to in-person care but the only realistic alternative to no care at all.

**Rebuttal**: The digital divide is a problem to be solved, not a reason to deny access to those who can benefit. Telehealth should be permanent and available as an option, with continued investment in digital infrastructure (the Digital Bridge program), audio-only authorization, and community telehealth access points in libraries and senior centers. The alternative, returning to pre-pandemic geographic restrictions, would cut off millions of older adults who have been successfully receiving telemental health services since 2020.

## Historical Patterns of Opposition

Opposition to older adult mental health reform has been remarkably consistent over time. In the 1960s, the American Medical Association opposed including mental health benefits in Medicare at all. In the 1980s, the nursing home industry opposed OBRA 1987 requirements for psychiatric screening and medication restrictions. In the 2000s, fiscal conservatives delayed Medicare mental health parity for years, stretching the implementation of a single policy change from 2008 to 2014. In each case, the opposition eventually lost on the specific policy question but succeeded in weakening implementation or delaying action long enough for demographic growth to absorb the gains.

The most common outcome of opposition is not outright legislative defeat but implementation failure. OBRA 1987 mental health requirements went unenforced for decades due to inadequate survey and enforcement resources. Medicare parity took from 2008 to 2014 to phase in. The 190-day psychiatric hospital limit remains in place despite bipartisan recommendations for its removal spanning over a decade. CoCM billing codes were created in 2017 but remain used by fewer than 5% of primary care practices. This pattern suggests that reform advocates should expect prolonged implementation timelines and prepare for enforcement challenges even after legislative victories. Winning the bill is the beginning, not the end, of the fight.

The opposition also adapts to reforms in ways that preserve underlying incentives. When the CMS National Partnership reduced antipsychotic prescribing, some nursing homes substituted alternative sedating medications or manipulated diagnoses to exclude residents from the quality measure. When Medicare parity reduced coinsurance, the shift to Medicare Advantage plans created new access barriers through narrow networks and prior authorization. Reform design must anticipate these adaptive responses.

## Counter-Strategies

### Political Strategies

- **Leverage the aging constituency**: Older adults are the most reliable voting bloc, with turnout rates exceeding 70% in presidential elections. Frame geriatric mental health as a Medicare and seniors' issue, connecting it to the broader political salience of aging policy. Seek AARP endorsement and mobilize their 38 million members. Engage the Alzheimer's Association's grassroots network, which has demonstrated the ability to drive bipartisan action.
- **Build bipartisan coalitions**: Older adult mental health is less politically polarized than many health issues. Find common ground between fiscal conservatives (invest to save) and progressives (expand access). Frame suicide prevention as a veterans' and gun safety issue that can attract support across the aisle. The Alzheimer's research funding success (bipartisan growth from $566 million to $3.7 billion) provides a model for what bipartisan advocacy can achieve.
- **State-level action**: When federal action stalls, pursue state-level reforms. States control Medicaid, scope of practice laws, nursing home licensing, and many social service programs. State-level victories can build evidence and momentum for federal action. States like California, Colorado, New York, and Massachusetts have been leaders in geriatric mental health policy and can serve as testing grounds for national reform.
- **Use veterans as a bridge**: Geriatric mental health for aging veterans is an issue with bipartisan appeal and the backing of powerful veterans' organizations. Legislation framed around veteran mental health may face less opposition and can establish models that are later extended to the broader Medicare population.

### Communication Strategies

- **Humanize the issue**: Use caregiver stories and individual cases to make the human cost visible. The isolation, suffering, and preventable death of older adults with untreated mental illness is a powerful narrative when personalized. Organize "shadow days" where Congressional staff accompany AAA caseworkers visiting isolated older adults.
- **Reframe the fiscal argument**: Position geriatric mental health investment as cost-saving, not cost-adding. Use IMPACT study data and other evidence showing net savings from depression treatment. Frame untreated mental illness as the expensive option. Develop CBO-scorable legislative proposals that demonstrate deficit reduction.
- **Counter ageism directly**: Challenge the assumption that mental suffering is a normal part of aging. Use language that emphasizes treatability and the right to quality of life regardless of age. Highlight success stories of older adults who have recovered from depression or found relief from isolation through evidence-based programs.
- **Use data, not just stories**: While narratives are important for engagement, opponents will challenge anecdotal evidence. Lead with data: the $6.7 billion Medicare cost of social isolation, the 55.2 per 100,000 suicide rate among white men 85+, the $3,363 per-patient savings from collaborative care. Data-driven arguments are harder to dismiss as sentimentality.

### Policy Design Strategies

- **Phase in requirements**: Reduce industry opposition by providing transition periods for staffing mandates and coverage expansions. Build in flexibility for rural and small facilities while maintaining standards for large chains and PE-owned operators.
- **Pair mandates with funding**: Every new requirement should come with funding to support compliance. Unfunded mandates invite opposition and noncompliance. The CMS minimum staffing proposal's lack of corresponding Medicaid rate increases weakened its political viability.
- **Use demonstration programs**: Pilot new models (PACE expansion, collaborative care integration, social prescribing) and collect outcomes data before proposing national rollout. Evidence-based proposals are harder to oppose on principled grounds, and demonstrations generate local constituencies that support scaling.
- **Bundle mental health with popular programs**: Attach geriatric mental health provisions to broader legislation with strong support (Older Americans Act reauthorization, Medicare drug pricing reform, nursing home safety legislation). Standalone geriatric mental health bills struggle to gain attention; provisions attached to must-pass legislation have a better path.
- **Anticipate substitution and gaming**: Design regulations with built-in protections against the adaptive responses that have undermined past reforms. Include all sedating medications (not just antipsychotics) in quality measures. Require diagnostic validation for nursing home residents coded with SMI diagnoses. Build evaluation requirements into legislation so that implementation failures are detected early.
- **Create irreversibility**: Design programs that build constituencies. PACE programs, once established, create local stakeholders (participants, families, employees, community organizations) whose advocacy makes program elimination politically costly. Social prescribing pilots, once successful, create the same dynamic. Programs that people use and value are difficult to cut.
