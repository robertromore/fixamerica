# Older Adult Mental Health: History

## Historical Development

The treatment of older adults with mental health conditions in the United States has evolved through distinct eras, shaped by institutional practices, scientific advances, legislative action, and shifting cultural attitudes toward aging and mental illness. For much of American history, older adults with mental health conditions were consigned to custodial institutions with little hope of treatment. The modern era has brought significant improvements in clinical knowledge but has failed to build the systems necessary to deliver that knowledge to the people who need it. Understanding this history is essential because many of the structural failures in today's system, from the 190-day Medicare psychiatric hospital limit to the chronic underfunding of community mental health for older adults, are direct products of historical decisions whose effects persist decades later.

---

### The Institutional Era (Pre-1950s): Warehousing and Neglect

- **Almshouses and poorhouses**: Before the 20th century, older adults with mental illness were typically housed in local almshouses alongside the indigent, disabled, and orphaned. No distinction was made between psychiatric and age-related conditions. Conditions were generally squalid, with no medical treatment, minimal supervision, and high mortality. The 1890 Census documented approximately 74,000 "insane" persons in institutions, a significant proportion of whom were elderly.
- **State mental hospitals**: Beginning in the mid-1800s with the advocacy of Dorothea Dix, state mental hospitals absorbed many older adults. By the early 20th century, approximately 30% of state hospital residents were aged 65 and older, many with what we now recognize as dementia rather than primary psychiatric illness (Grob, 1994). State hospitals became de facto nursing homes for the elderly, housing individuals with senile dementia, chronic depression, and the behavioral consequences of untreated medical conditions alongside younger patients with severe mental illness.
- **Social Security Act of 1935**: The establishment of Old-Age Assistance payments inadvertently reinforced institutional placement by providing payments to older adults in public facilities. Many states transferred elderly residents from state hospitals to newly created nursing homes to shift costs from state to federal budgets. This cost-shifting dynamic, rather than any clinical rationale, drove one of the largest population transfers in American healthcare history. By the 1940s, the movement of older adults out of state hospitals and into private boarding homes and nursing facilities was already underway, decades before formal deinstitutionalization policy.
- **No geriatric specialty**: Geriatric psychiatry did not exist as a recognized discipline. Older adults received the same treatments as younger patients, including insulin coma therapy, electroconvulsive therapy (often without modern safeguards such as anesthesia or muscle relaxants), hydrotherapy, and extended custodial confinement. There was virtually no research into the specific presentation, course, or treatment of mental illness in older adults. The prevailing medical assumption was that mental decline was an inevitable consequence of aging and that therapeutic intervention was futile.
- **World War II and early VA system**: The establishment of the Veterans Administration hospital system after World War II created a parallel infrastructure that would later become significant for older adult mental health. Returning veterans who developed psychiatric conditions aged into a VA system that, while flawed, maintained a relationship between physical and mental health care that the civilian system lacked.

### Deinstitutionalization and Medicare (1950s-1970s): Shifting Populations

- **Psychopharmacological revolution**: The introduction of chlorpromazine (Thorazine) in 1954 enabled the discharge of many psychiatric patients, including older adults, from state hospitals. Between 1955 and 1980, state mental hospital populations declined from 559,000 to 138,000 nationally. The promise of medication-based management in community settings was genuine for some patients but created a false confidence that community care could be achieved without building the community infrastructure to support it.
- **Medicare and Medicaid (1965)**: The passage of Medicare and Medicaid fundamentally restructured financing for older adult care. Medicare included psychiatric benefits but with significant limitations, including the 190-day lifetime limit for freestanding psychiatric hospitals and a 50% coinsurance rate for outpatient mental health services (compared to 20% for medical services). These discriminatory provisions reflected the assumption that mental health care was less medically necessary and the Congressional fear that open-ended psychiatric coverage would be prohibitively expensive. The 190-day limit was modeled on the existing Veterans Administration psychiatric benefit and was intended as a compromise between those who wanted full psychiatric coverage and those who opposed any mental health benefit in Medicare. It has persisted for over 60 years despite repeated calls for repeal.
- **Nursing home expansion**: Medicaid's coverage of nursing home care created a massive financial incentive to move older adults from state psychiatric hospitals to nursing homes. Between 1963 and 1975, the nursing home population grew from approximately 500,000 to 1.3 million. Many of these individuals had primary psychiatric diagnoses but received no mental health treatment in their new settings. States aggressively transferred patients because Medicaid (jointly funded by federal and state governments) replaced the full state cost of maintaining patients in state hospitals. This process has been called "transinstitutionalization" rather than deinstitutionalization, as individuals moved from one institution to another rather than to genuine community living.
- **Community Mental Health Act (1963)**: President Kennedy signed legislation funding community mental health centers (CMHCs), envisioning a nationwide network of 2,000 centers that would replace institutional care with community-based treatment. However, these centers focused almost exclusively on younger adults with acute conditions. Older adults were systematically excluded from CMHC services, a pattern that persisted for decades. A 1977 report by the President's Commission on Mental Health found that older adults constituted only 4% of CMHC clients despite comprising 10% of the population, and that many centers explicitly refused to serve patients over age 65.
- **1971 White House Conference on Aging**: The conference included the first significant federal attention to older adult mental health, with a section report calling for integration of mental health services into aging programs. However, the recommendations were not translated into legislation or funding. The conference's mental health recommendations were largely overshadowed by the more politically salient issues of Social Security benefits and Medicare expansion.

### The Regulatory Response (1980s-1990s): OBRA and Early Reform

- **Emergence of geriatric psychiatry**: The American Association for Geriatric Psychiatry (AAGP) was founded in 1978 by a group of psychiatrists who recognized that older adults required specialized clinical approaches distinct from general adult psychiatry. Founding members included Carl Eisdorfer and Gene Cohen, who were among the first to establish rigorous research programs in late-life depression and dementia behavioral management. Geriatric psychiatry was recognized as an official subspecialty by the American Board of Psychiatry and Neurology in 1989, establishing board certification requirements and a training infrastructure. The first board examination was offered in 1991.
- **OBRA 1987 (Nursing Home Reform Act)**: The Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1987 represented the most significant nursing home reform in American history. Prompted by a landmark Institute of Medicine report ("Improving the Quality of Care in Nursing Homes," 1986) documenting pervasive abuse and neglect, including graphic accounts of residents tied to beds, overmedicated into stupor, and left in their own waste, OBRA 1987 required:
    - Pre-admission screening for mental illness (PASARR - Pre-Admission Screening and Annual Resident Review), designed to prevent inappropriate nursing home placement of individuals whose primary needs were psychiatric rather than medical
    - Reduction of unnecessary psychotropic medication use, including requirements for documented clinical justification and periodic dose reduction attempts
    - Comprehensive care planning that addressed residents' psychological and social needs as well as medical conditions
    - Rights protections for residents, including the right to refuse psychotropic medications
    - State survey and certification standards with enforcement mechanisms
- **OBRA 1990 mental health parity attempt**: Congress reduced the Medicare outpatient mental health coinsurance from 50% to 37.5%, a partial step toward parity with medical services. However, the discriminatory differential persisted for another two decades. The compromise reflected the political difficulty of achieving full parity in a single legislative step.
- **Decade of the Brain (1990s)**: Federal investment in neuroscience research expanded understanding of late-life depression, dementia, and the neurobiological basis of mental illness in older adults. The NIMH launched studies demonstrating that older adults respond to evidence-based treatments, including psychotherapy and antidepressant medication, when they can access them. The selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs), introduced in the late 1980s, provided a class of antidepressants with fewer side effects and lower toxicity in overdose than tricyclic antidepressants, making pharmacological treatment safer for older adults.
- **PACE established as permanent Medicare benefit (1997)**: The Balanced Budget Act of 1997 made PACE a permanent part of Medicare, following a decade of successful demonstration projects. The On Lok Senior Health Services program in San Francisco's Chinatown, established in 1973, served as the original PACE model, demonstrating that integrated medical and social services could safely maintain nursing-home-eligible individuals in the community. The transition from demonstration to permanent benefit was a milestone, but PACE growth remained slow due to regulatory complexity and capital requirements.
- **Nursing home antipsychotic crisis**: Throughout the 1990s, the use of antipsychotic medications in nursing homes became a growing concern. The introduction of atypical antipsychotics (risperidone in 1993, olanzapine in 1996, quetiapine in 1997) led to a surge in prescribing to nursing home residents for behavioral symptoms of dementia, despite the absence of FDA approval for this indication. By the early 2000s, an estimated 25-30% of nursing home residents were receiving antipsychotic medications, many without documented psychiatric diagnoses.

### The Parity and Integration Era (2000s-2010s): Legislative Progress

- **IMPACT study published (2002)**: The Improving Mood-Promoting Access to Collaborative Treatment (IMPACT) trial, led by Jurgen Unutzer at the University of Washington, was published in *JAMA* and became the landmark study in older adult mental health services research. The randomized controlled trial demonstrated that collaborative care for late-life depression, integrating a depression care manager into primary care, doubled the effectiveness of treatment compared with usual care. Over 1,800 patients across 18 primary care clinics participated. The IMPACT model showed that 45% of intervention patients had a 50% or greater reduction in depressive symptoms at 12 months, compared with 19% in usual care. This evidence base spawned the development of Medicare billing codes (CoCM codes 99492-99494) that were finalized in 2017, enabling reimbursement for collaborative care models.
- **PROSPECT trial (2004)**: The Prevention of Suicide in Primary Care Elderly: Collaborative Trial (PROSPECT), published in *JAMA* by Martha Bruce and colleagues, demonstrated that collaborative care for suicidal older adults in primary care reduced suicidal ideation by 2.2 times compared with usual care. The study provided the first strong evidence that primary care-based intervention could reduce suicide risk in older adults.
- **FDA black-box warnings (2005, 2008)**: The FDA issued black-box warnings for atypical antipsychotics (2005) and conventional antipsychotics (2008) regarding increased mortality risk in elderly patients with dementia-related psychosis. The warnings documented a 1.6-1.7 times increased risk of death compared with placebo, primarily from cardiovascular events and infections. Despite these warnings, antipsychotic prescribing in nursing homes declined only modestly in the years immediately following, highlighting the gap between regulatory action and clinical practice change.
- **Medicare Improvements for Patients and Providers Act (MIPPA, 2008)**: Congress finally achieved Medicare mental health payment parity by phasing out the discriminatory 50% coinsurance for outpatient mental health services, reducing it to the standard 20% by 2014. This eliminated the most visible financial barrier to outpatient mental health care for Medicare beneficiaries. The phase-in schedule (50% to 45% in 2010, 40% in 2011, 35% in 2012, 25% in 2013, 20% in 2014) reflected both political compromise and the need to manage budget impact.
- **Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act (MHPAEA, 2008)**: While primarily affecting employer-sponsored insurance, MHPAEA established the principle that mental health benefits must be comparable to medical/surgical benefits. Its impact on older adults was indirect but shaped expectations about equitable coverage. The law did not apply directly to Medicare, but it created a normative framework that strengthened arguments for Medicare mental health parity.
- **Affordable Care Act (ACA, 2010)**: The ACA expanded mental health coverage requirements for younger populations and funded behavioral health integration programs, but its direct impact on Medicare beneficiaries aged 65+ was limited. The law did establish the Medicare Annual Wellness Visit, which includes depression screening as a covered preventive service. However, research has shown that fewer than half of eligible beneficiaries receive the Annual Wellness Visit, and among those who do, depression screening is often perfunctory or omitted (Jensen et al., *Health Affairs*, 2015).
- **CMS National Partnership to Improve Dementia Care (2012)**: CMS launched a multiyear initiative targeting antipsychotic medication reduction in nursing homes, achieving a reduction from 23.9% to approximately 15% by 2018. The initiative combined public reporting (adding antipsychotic rates to Nursing Home Compare), educational resources, and state-level coalitions. It represented the most successful federal initiative to improve mental health care quality in nursing homes and demonstrated that sustained quality improvement could change prescribing behavior without legislative mandates. However, as antipsychotic rates declined, concerns emerged about substitution with other sedating medications, including benzodiazepines and gabapentinoids.
- **21st Century Cures Act (2016)**: This bipartisan legislation included provisions relevant to older adult mental health, including funding for mental health workforce development, requirements for mental health parity compliance, and support for evidence-based practices. The Act also increased NIH funding, contributing to the growth of Alzheimer's disease research from $566 million in FY2015 to over $3 billion by FY2020.

### The COVID-19 Era and Beyond (2020-Present): Crisis and Partial Response

- **COVID-19 pandemic impact**: The pandemic devastated older adult mental health. Nursing home residents experienced prolonged lockdowns, isolation from families, and mass death; over 200,000 nursing home residents and staff died of COVID-19 in the first two years of the pandemic (CMS, 2022). Community-dwelling older adults faced social isolation at unprecedented levels. Depression and anxiety rates among older adults approximately doubled during 2020-2021 (CDC, 2022). The pandemic exposed the fragility of the social connections that sustain mental health in aging: when senior centers closed, congregate meals stopped, and in-person visits were prohibited, the systems that millions of older adults depended on for social contact simply disappeared.
- **Telehealth expansion**: The Public Health Emergency declaration waived Medicare telehealth restrictions, allowing older adults to receive mental health services via video and telephone from their homes. Telehealth mental health visits among Medicare beneficiaries surged from 0.8 million in 2019 to 10.1 million in 2020 (OIG, 2022). The expansion demonstrated that many older adults could and would use telehealth for mental health services, contradicting assumptions about technology resistance. However, utilization was concentrated among younger, more affluent, and urban older adults, widening rather than narrowing access disparities.
- **Bipartisan Safer Communities Act (2022)**: Authorized Medicare coverage for marriage and family therapists and licensed mental health counselors, effective January 2024, expanding the provider network accessible to older adults. An estimated 270,000 MFTs and LMHCs nationally became newly eligible to serve Medicare beneficiaries, potentially the largest expansion of the Medicare mental health provider pool in decades.
- **988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (2022)**: The national transition to 988 as the mental health crisis number included provisions for specialized services, though infrastructure specifically for older adults remains limited. The existing Friendship Line, operated by the Institute on Aging in San Francisco, handles over 100,000 calls annually from older adults and could serve as a model for a dedicated 988 older adult track, but operates on a budget of approximately $2 million per year.
- **Surgeon General's Advisory on Loneliness (2023)**: Vivek Murthy issued a Surgeon General's Advisory declaring loneliness and isolation an epidemic, with older adults identified as a high-risk population. The advisory called for a National Strategy to Advance Social Connection and recommended expanding research, public health surveillance, and community-level interventions. While the advisory carried no legislative force, it elevated social isolation to national policy prominence.
- **Ongoing workforce crisis**: Despite growing recognition of the problem, the geriatric psychiatry workforce has continued to stagnate. Several fellowship programs have closed, and the fill rate remains below 60%. The number of practicing geriatric psychiatrists may actually be declining as retirements outpace new certifications.

## Lessons from History

### Pattern 1: Cost-Shifting Over Care

Throughout American history, policy changes affecting older adult mental health have been driven more by fiscal considerations than by clinical evidence or patient welfare. The transfer of older adults from state hospitals to nursing homes in the 1960s-1970s was motivated by states seeking to shift costs to the federal Medicaid program, not by evidence that nursing homes would provide better psychiatric care. The 190-day psychiatric hospital limit in Medicare reflected Congressional anxiety about open-ended costs, not clinical judgment about appropriate treatment duration. The ACA's limited impact on Medicare beneficiaries reflected the political calculus that expanding coverage for those already insured was less compelling than insuring the uninsured. This pattern suggests that reform advocates must frame proposals in fiscal terms, demonstrating that investment in mental health care reduces overall healthcare costs, to succeed politically. The IMPACT model's documented savings of $3,363 per patient over four years provides the type of evidence needed to make this fiscal case.

### Pattern 2: Regulatory Reform Without Resource Investment

OBRA 1987 established important requirements for nursing home mental health care, but chronic underfunding of survey and enforcement mechanisms has limited its real-world impact. The PASARR screening requirement was never adequately funded at the state level, leading to perfunctory assessments that failed to divert individuals from inappropriate nursing home placement. Similarly, Medicare mental health parity was achieved on paper in 2008-2014, but without adequate provider supply, beneficiaries face de facto access barriers. The CMS National Partnership reduced antipsychotic prescribing but did not fund the behavioral health alternatives (staff training, psychosocial programming) needed to replace medication-based management. Legislation without corresponding workforce investment and enforcement funding produces rights without remedies.

### Pattern 3: Crisis as Catalyst

Major advances in older adult mental health policy have followed crises or scandals. OBRA 1987 followed a devastating IOM report on nursing home conditions. The COVID-19 pandemic catalyzed telehealth expansion that had been debated for a decade. The ongoing opioid crisis has increased attention to substance misuse among older adults. The FDA antipsychotic black-box warnings (prompted by mortality data from clinical trials) intensified scrutiny of nursing home prescribing practices. The Surgeon General's Advisory on Loneliness, while not crisis-driven in the traditional sense, responded to accumulated evidence that social isolation is a population-level health threat. This pattern suggests that advocacy efforts should be prepared to leverage crisis moments with pre-developed policy proposals, as windows of opportunity for reform tend to be narrow.

### Pattern 4: The Ageism Barrier

Across every era, older adult mental health has received lower priority than mental health services for younger populations. Community mental health centers excluded older adults. Suicide prevention initiatives have historically focused on youth. Research funding for geriatric psychiatry has lagged behind child and adolescent psychiatry by a ratio of more than 2.5 to 1. The 21st Century Cures Act devoted substantial resources to mental health but included no provisions specifically targeting the geriatric population. Even within aging advocacy, mental health has been secondary to economic security (Social Security) and physical health (Medicare drug costs, long-term care). This persistent pattern of ageism, the assumption that mental suffering is a natural part of aging, may be the single most important barrier to reform and the hardest to address through legislation alone. Changing this cultural attitude requires sustained public education, clinical training reform, and the visible success of treatment programs that demonstrate that older adults can recover, improve, and thrive when given appropriate care.

### Pattern 5: The Integration Imperative

The most successful interventions in older adult mental health, including the IMPACT collaborative care model, the PACE program, and the CMS National Partnership, share a common feature: they integrate mental health care into settings where older adults already receive services, rather than requiring older adults to navigate to separate mental health systems. This integration imperative reflects both the practical barriers that prevent older adults from accessing specialty mental health care (transportation, stigma, provider shortage) and the clinical reality that mental and physical health are inextricable in aging populations. Future policy should be designed around integration rather than standalone mental health programs.
