# Community Mental Health: Stakeholders

The community mental health system is shaped by the interactions of several key groups, each with its own interests, resources, and degree of influence over policy and funding decisions. Understanding these stakeholders is essential to building effective reform coalitions.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Adults with serious mental illness (SMI) | Most severely affected; dependence on community services for housing, treatment, employment | 14.1 million | Access to comprehensive treatment, housing stability, recovery supports, civil rights |
| Adults with any mental illness (AMI) | Rely on outpatient community services for therapy, medication, crisis intervention | 57.8 million | Timely access to care, insurance coverage, affordable copays, stigma reduction |
| People with co-occurring mental illness and substance use disorders | Need integrated treatment rarely available in fragmented system | 21.5 million | Integrated treatment programs, reduced barriers between MH and SUD systems |
| Children and adolescents with mental health conditions | Depend on school-linked and community-based services | ~7.7 million youth (ages 6-17) | Early intervention, school-based services, family support, developmental appropriateness |
| Family members and caregivers | Provide unpaid care, navigate fragmented system, bear emotional and financial burden | ~8.4 million family caregivers of adults with SMI | Respite services, family psychoeducation, navigation support, crisis intervention access |

Source: SAMHSA NSDUH, 2022; National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI), 2023

### Secondary Stakeholders

- **People experiencing homelessness**: An estimated 30-40% of individuals experiencing homelessness have a serious mental illness. Community mental health is a gateway to housing stability and recovery (HUD Annual Homeless Assessment Report, 2023).
- **Incarcerated individuals with mental illness**: Approximately 356,268 inmates in jails and prisons have severe mental illness -- 10 times the number in state psychiatric hospitals. Community mental health services are essential for diversion and reentry (Treatment Advocacy Center, 2014).
- **Law enforcement officers**: Police are the first responders to most mental health crises; they bear the burden of a system that lacks adequate community crisis services. An estimated 10-20% of police calls involve individuals with mental illness (CIT International, 2023).
- **Emergency department staff**: Psychiatric boarding -- holding patients in EDs for days awaiting placement -- strains emergency medicine capacity. The average psychiatric ED hold exceeded 24 hours in 2023 (American College of Emergency Physicians, 2023).
- **Employers and the general economy**: Untreated mental illness costs employers an estimated $225 billion annually in lost productivity, absenteeism, and turnover (National Alliance on Mental Illness, 2023).
- **Veterans with mental health conditions**: An estimated 1.7 million veterans used VA mental health services in FY2023, but many veterans -- particularly those in rural areas or without VA eligibility -- rely on community mental health centers. The VA operates 1,062 Community-Based Outpatient Clinics (CBOCs), but geographic gaps remain, and coordination between VA and civilian community mental health systems is limited (VA, 2024).
- **Individuals with co-occurring physical and mental health conditions**: People with serious mental illness die 15-25 years earlier than the general population, primarily from cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and other preventable conditions (Parks et al., NASMHPD, 2006; updated through 2022). Community mental health centers that do not provide or coordinate primary care screening and monitoring contribute to this mortality gap.
- **Rural communities**: Entire rural counties lack any community mental health provider. The 60 million Americans living in rural areas face the combined burdens of provider scarcity, long travel distances, limited public transportation, and cultural barriers to mental health treatment (SAMHSA, 2023).

### Disproportionately Affected Populations

Certain populations bear disproportionate consequences of community mental health system failures:

| Population | Key Vulnerability | Estimated Size | Primary Service Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| Transition-age youth (18-25) | Aging out of pediatric systems; highest unmet need rate | 8.6 million with AMI | Continuity of care between child and adult systems |
| Older adults (65+) | Isolation, grief, medical comorbidities; underdiagnosed depression | 6.8 million with AMI | Integrated geriatric-psychiatric services |
| Pregnant and postpartum women | Perinatal depression affects 1 in 8; limited screening and treatment | ~500,000 annually | Perinatal mood disorder screening and treatment |
| LGBTQ+ individuals | 2-3x higher rates of mental illness; limited culturally competent providers | ~5 million with AMI | Affirming community mental health services |
| People re-entering from incarceration | 64% of jail inmates have mental illness; minimal reentry mental health linkage | ~600,000 released annually | Jail-to-community mental health transition services |

Source: SAMHSA NSDUH, 2022; Williams Institute, 2023; BJS, 2023; CDC, 2023

## Who Has Power

### Decision Makers

| Actor | Role | Authority | Resources | Influence Level |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| U.S. Congress | Authorizes and funds SAMHSA, MHBG, CCBHC programs | Appropriations power; legislative authority over Medicaid | Federal budget (~$7 billion in behavioral health spending, FY2024) | High |
| Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) | Administers Medicaid behavioral health coverage; approves State Plan Amendments for CCBHCs | Regulatory authority over largest payer | $89 billion in Medicaid behavioral health spending (FY2022) | High |
| SAMHSA | Administers block grants, CCBHC grants, PRNS programs; sets national guidelines | Grant-making authority; technical assistance | ~$7.5 billion total budget (FY2024) | High |
| State Mental Health Authorities (SMHAs) | Plan and oversee state community mental health systems; distribute block grant funds | Licensing, certification, state funding allocation | Varies ($30-$250+ per capita by state) | High |
| State Medicaid Directors | Set behavioral health reimbursement rates; implement CCBHC State Plan Amendments | Rate-setting, benefit design, managed care contracting | State Medicaid budgets (behavioral health portion) | High |
| HRSA | Administers behavioral health workforce programs; designates Mental Health HPSAs | Workforce funding, shortage area designation | $315 million in behavioral health workforce funding (FY2024) | Medium |

### Key Organizations and Institutions

- **National Council for Mental Wellbeing**: The primary trade association for community behavioral health organizations. Represents over 3,000 member organizations, including most CMHCs and CCBHCs. Leads CCBHC advocacy, provides technical assistance, and publishes annual data on the CCBHC model. The most influential industry voice in community mental health policy.
- **National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI)**: The largest grassroots mental health advocacy organization in the United States, with 600+ affiliates across all 50 states. Focuses on access to services, parity enforcement, and anti-stigma campaigns. Operates HelpLine, family support groups, and peer education programs.
- **Mental Health America (MHA)**: National nonprofit focused on prevention, early intervention, and community-based services. Publishes annual *State of Mental Health in America* report with state-by-state rankings.
- **Treatment Advocacy Center (TAC)**: Advocates for policies to ensure treatment access for individuals with severe mental illness, including Assisted Outpatient Treatment (AOT) and expanded inpatient capacity. Often in tension with consumer/recovery-oriented organizations on issues of involuntary treatment.
- **SAMHSA Office of Recovery**: Within SAMHSA, promotes recovery-oriented systems of care, peer support integration, and consumer involvement in service planning and governance.

### Influencers

- **National Association of State Mental Health Program Directors (NASMHPD)**: The organization of state mental health commissioners. Provides technical assistance to states, conducts research, and advises Congress and SAMHSA on policy. Significant influence on how federal dollars are distributed and spent.
- **American Psychiatric Association (APA)**: The professional organization of psychiatrists. Advocates for adequate psychiatric workforce, reimbursement rates, and scope of practice protections. Influential in clinical guidelines and payment policy.
- **American Psychological Association**: Advocates for psychologist workforce, research funding, and scope of practice (including prescribing authority in some states).
- **National Association of Social Workers (NASW)**: Represents licensed clinical social workers, the largest provider group in community mental health. Advocates for workforce development, loan forgiveness, and reimbursement equity.
- **Peer support advocacy organizations**: Including Depression and Bipolar Support Alliance (DBSA), Copeland Center for Wellness and Recovery, and state-level peer specialist associations. Advocate for peer specialist certification, reimbursement, and integration into clinical teams.
- **Managed Behavioral Health Organizations (MBHOs)**: Companies like Optum Behavioral Health, Beacon Health Options (now Carelon), and Magellan Health manage behavioral health benefits for Medicaid and commercial plans. Their reimbursement decisions and network management directly affect community mental health center viability.
- **Pharmaceutical Industry**: Companies manufacturing antipsychotic medications (e.g., Janssen/Johnson & Johnson, Otsuka, Alkermes) have a financial interest in community mental health infrastructure that facilitates medication management and long-acting injectable prescribing. The long-acting injectable antipsychotic market exceeds $10 billion annually. Pharmaceutical companies fund some community mental health advocacy and provider education, creating potential conflicts of interest.
- **Labor Unions**: The American Federation of State, County and Municipal Employees (AFSCME) represents many community mental health center workers. The Service Employees International Union (SEIU) represents peer support specialists and case managers in some states. These unions advocate for higher wages, better working conditions, and staffing ratios -- issues that directly affect workforce stability and service quality.

The bipartisan nature of CCBHC support -- with champions spanning from progressive Democrats to conservative Republicans -- is one of the most significant political features of community mental health policy. This bipartisanship is rooted in the model's appeal to both public safety/law enforcement constituencies (favored by Republicans) and healthcare access/equity constituencies (favored by Democrats).

## Stakeholder Interests and Alignment

### Aligned Interests (Potential Coalition Partners)

| Coalition | Shared Interest | Strength |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| NAMI + National Council + MHA + NASMHPD | Increased federal community mental health funding | Strong |
| National Council + CCBHCs + CMS | CCBHC expansion and Prospective Payment System adequacy | Strong |
| Law enforcement + NAMI + National Council | Crisis services expansion; 988 + mobile crisis team funding | Strong |
| APA + APA + NASW + HRSA | Behavioral health workforce investment | Moderate |
| Consumer/recovery organizations + SAMHSA | Recovery-oriented systems, peer support integration | Moderate |
| Hospitals + CMHCs + insurers | Reduction in psychiatric boarding through community alternatives | Moderate |

### Conflicting Interests

- **Treatment Advocacy Center vs. Consumer/Recovery Organizations**: TAC advocates for expanded Assisted Outpatient Treatment (AOT) and involuntary interventions for individuals with untreated SMI. Consumer and recovery organizations oppose involuntary treatment as a violation of autonomy and argue for voluntary, person-centered services. This is the most significant intra-advocacy split in community mental health.
- **State Fiscal Officers vs. Mental Health Advocates**: State budget offices seek to minimize state spending on mental health services. Advocacy organizations push for expanded investment. When federal funding increases (e.g., MHBG appropriations), states sometimes reduce their own spending -- a "crowd-out" effect that undermines the federal investment.
- **Managed Care Organizations vs. Community Providers**: MCOs managing Medicaid behavioral health benefits may set reimbursement rates below the cost of care, restrict authorization for services, and impose administrative burdens. Community providers argue that managed care prioritizes cost containment over access and quality.
- **Psychiatrists vs. Non-Physician Providers**: Scope of practice disputes (e.g., whether psychologists or nurse practitioners can prescribe medications) affect workforce capacity in community settings. Psychiatrists support scope limitations; non-physician providers argue that expanding scope is essential to addressing shortages in underserved communities.

### The Federal-State-Provider Triangle

Community mental health operates through a triangular relationship:

1. **Federal government** (Congress, SAMHSA, CMS): Sets policy, provides funding, establishes standards
2. **State governments** (SMHAs, Medicaid agencies): Distribute funds, license providers, set rates, plan systems
3. **Community providers** (CMHCs, CCBHCs, FQHCs): Deliver services, hire staff, manage operations

Each vertex depends on the others but has different priorities. Federal agencies want national standards and accountability. States want flexibility and minimal unfunded mandates. Providers want adequate reimbursement and reduced administrative burden. Reform requires aligning these interests.

### The Consumer and Family Voice

Individuals with lived experience of mental illness and their family members play a distinctive role in community mental health policy. Unlike most healthcare advocacy, where patients are represented by professional organizations, mental health policy has a strong tradition of consumer-driven advocacy:

- **Consumer-run organizations**: Consumer-operated service programs (COSPs) -- mental health services operated by individuals with lived experience -- number approximately 1,500 nationwide. These include drop-in centers, peer-run crisis respites, and recovery learning communities. They operate outside the traditional clinical system and represent the most direct form of consumer power in community mental health.
- **Family advocacy**: NAMI's 600+ affiliates are largely led by family members of individuals with serious mental illness. Family advocates bring moral authority and electoral influence that professional organizations cannot replicate. NAMI's annual advocacy days consistently generate thousands of constituent contacts with congressional offices.
- **Peer workforce**: The approximately 18,000 peer support specialists working in community mental health settings represent a workforce constituency with unique perspectives on system design and service delivery. Peer specialist unions and associations are emerging as a distinct advocacy voice.
- **Consumer advisory councils**: Federal law requires state mental health authorities to maintain consumer advisory councils. The Quality Improvement Program (QIP) under the MHBG requires states to report on consumer satisfaction and consumer involvement in planning. However, the effectiveness of these councils varies widely; in some states they exercise genuine influence, while in others they are perfunctory.

### The Criminal Justice Intersection

The intersection of community mental health and criminal justice has created a distinct set of stakeholder relationships:

- **Sheriffs and jail administrators**: County sheriffs increasingly advocate for community mental health investment because jails have become de facto psychiatric facilities. The National Sheriffs' Association and Major Cities Chiefs Association have both issued statements supporting community mental health alternatives to incarceration.
- **Drug courts and mental health courts**: Over 3,000 specialized courts (drug courts, mental health courts, veterans courts) operate nationwide, diverting individuals with mental illness and substance use disorders from incarceration to community treatment. These courts depend on the availability of community mental health services to function. Judges who oversee these courts are often powerful local advocates for CMHC funding.
- **Sequential Intercept Model**: The SAMHSA-supported Sequential Intercept Model identifies five points where individuals with mental illness can be diverted from the criminal justice system to community treatment: (1) law enforcement/emergency services, (2) initial detention/court hearings, (3) jails/courts, (4) reentry, and (5) community corrections. Each intercept point requires community mental health capacity.

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on policy:

- **Lobbying**: The National Council for Mental Wellbeing spent $1.2 million on federal lobbying in 2023. The American Psychiatric Association spent $2.1 million. NAMI spent $860,000. Mental health lobbying is modest compared to pharmaceutical or hospital industries but has proven effective on targeted legislation (e.g., CCBHC provisions in the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act) (OpenSecrets, 2024).
- **Campaign Contributions**: Mental health-specific PAC contributions are small (under $5 million total for the 2022 cycle), but mental health organizations leverage broader healthcare and disability coalitions. Key congressional champions (e.g., members of the Energy & Commerce and Appropriations Committees) receive targeted support.
- **Grassroots Advocacy**: NAMI's 600+ affiliates and 500,000+ supporters conduct annual advocacy days, constituent meetings, and letter-writing campaigns. The personal stories of individuals with mental illness and their families are among the most powerful advocacy tools in this policy area.
- **Data and Evidence**: The National Council's annual CCBHC outcomes data, SAMHSA's NSDUH surveys, and academic research provide the evidence base that policymakers rely on. CCBHC expansion has been driven largely by demonstration data showing cost savings and outcome improvements.
- **Media and Public Awareness**: Mass casualty events, investigative journalism about psychiatric boarding or jail conditions, and celebrity disclosure of mental health conditions periodically elevate public attention and create windows for legislative action.
- **Legal Advocacy**: Organizations like the Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law, the ACLU's Disability Rights Project, and state-level protection and advocacy organizations use *Olmstead* enforcement, ADA compliance actions, and class-action litigation to compel states to expand community mental health services. Legal advocacy has been particularly effective in states where legislative advocacy has failed.
- **Bipartisan Coalition Building**: The CCBHC legislative campaign deliberately built bipartisan support by framing community mental health as a public safety issue (appealing to Republican constituencies) and a healthcare equity issue (appealing to Democratic constituencies). This dual framing, led by the Stabenow-Blunt Senate partnership, produced the bipartisan votes needed for both the PAMA Section 223 authorization and the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act.

### State-Level Advocacy Ecosystems

The effectiveness of community mental health advocacy varies significantly by state, creating a "advocacy infrastructure" gap that mirrors the service infrastructure gap:

| State Type | Advocacy Characteristics | Examples | Impact on Policy |
|---|---|---|---|
| Strong advocacy infrastructure | Active NAMI affiliate; state MH association; provider coalition; consumer voice | New York, California, Oregon, Minnesota | Higher state MH spending; early CCBHC adoption; stronger parity enforcement |
| Moderate advocacy infrastructure | Active NAMI affiliate; some provider advocacy; limited consumer voice | Ohio, Virginia, Colorado, Michigan | Moderate state MH spending; CCBHC adoption in progress |
| Weak advocacy infrastructure | Limited organized advocacy; minimal consumer voice; few dedicated MH organizations | Mississippi, Idaho, Wyoming, South Dakota | Low state MH spending; late or no CCBHC adoption; minimal parity enforcement |

States with weak advocacy infrastructure face a compounding disadvantage: the populations that most need community mental health services are also the populations least able to organize politically to demand them. Individuals with serious mental illness, homeless populations, and incarcerated individuals are among the least politically powerful constituencies in any state.

## Stakeholder Power Analysis

### Power vs. Interest Matrix

| Stakeholder | Power Level | Interest Level | Strategy |
|---|---|---|---|
| Congress (Appropriations) | Very High | Moderate (episodic) | Sustain attention between crises through data and stories |
| CMS (Medicaid) | Very High | High | Work within regulatory process for favorable CCBHC guidance |
| State Medicaid Directors | High | Variable by state | Target persuadable states for CCBHC SPA adoption |
| SAMHSA | High | Very High | Leverage as lead federal agency for technical assistance |
| Managed Behavioral Health Organizations | High | Defensive | Address cost concerns through data on PPS cost-effectiveness |
| National Council for Mental Wellbeing | Medium-High | Very High | Primary advocacy vehicle for CCBHC expansion |
| NAMI (grassroots) | Medium | Very High | Leverage 600+ affiliates for constituent pressure |
| Community mental health center directors | Low-Medium | Very High | Amplify provider voices in policy discussions |
| Individuals with mental illness | Low | Very High | Elevate lived experience through testimonials and advisory boards |
| General public | Low | Low-Moderate | Increase awareness through crisis continuum visibility (988) |

The fundamental power imbalance in community mental health policy is that the populations most affected by system failures -- individuals with serious mental illness, people experiencing homelessness, incarcerated individuals -- have the least political power. Conversely, the actors with the most power to change the system -- congressional appropriators, state budget directors, managed care organizations -- face competing priorities and financial incentives that work against adequate investment.

Effective advocacy coalitions bridge this gap by connecting lived experience (which generates moral authority and media attention) with institutional power (which controls budgets and regulations). The CCBHC legislative campaign has been among the most successful examples of this bridging strategy in recent health policy.
