# Community Mental Health: History

## Historical Development

Community mental health in the United States has evolved through a series of dramatic shifts -- from custodial institutional care, through deinstitutionalization, to the modern experiment with Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinics. Each era built on the failures and lessons of the previous one, yet the central challenge has remained constant: how to provide adequate care for people with mental illness outside of large institutions while ensuring sufficient funding, coordination, and accountability.

---

### The Asylum Era (1840s-1950s): Institutional Confinement

- **Dorothea Dix's Campaign**: Beginning in 1843, reformer Dorothea Dix documented the horrific conditions in which people with mental illness were kept -- in jails, poorhouses, and cages. Her advocacy led to the construction of 32 state psychiatric hospitals by 1880. The "moral treatment" philosophy held that humane institutional environments could heal mental illness.
- **Rapid Institutional Growth**: By 1900, every state operated at least one psychiatric hospital. By 1955, state psychiatric hospitals housed approximately 559,000 patients -- the peak of institutional census (Torrey, 2014).
- **Overcrowding and Deterioration**: As patient populations grew faster than budgets, conditions deteriorated. Hospitals designed for hundreds held thousands. Investigative journalism, particularly Albert Deutsch's *The Shame of the States* (1948) and photojournalist Albert Maisel's Life magazine expose (1946), revealed warehousing conditions, abuse, and neglect.
- **Medical Developments**: The introduction of chlorpromazine (Thorazine) in 1954 -- the first effective antipsychotic medication -- made it possible for many patients to manage symptoms outside institutional settings, laying the pharmacological groundwork for deinstitutionalization.
- **The Beers Legacy**: Clifford Beers, who published *A Mind That Found Itself* in 1908 after being institutionalized for three years, founded the National Committee for Mental Hygiene (later Mental Health America). His work catalyzed the mental hygiene movement, which argued for prevention and community-based approaches decades before the CMHC Act.
- **National Mental Health Act (1946)**: Signed by President Truman, this Act created the National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH) and established the first federal role in mental health research and service development. NIMH became the intellectual engine behind deinstitutionalization, funding research on community-based alternatives to institutional care.

### The Community Mental Health Movement (1955-1980): A Promise Made

- **Joint Commission on Mental Illness and Health (1955)**: Congress authorized a comprehensive study of the nation's mental health needs. The Commission's 1961 report, *Action for Mental Health*, recommended shifting from custodial institutional care to community-based treatment.
- **Community Mental Health Centers Act (1963)**: President John F. Kennedy signed P.L. 88-164 on October 31, 1963, authorizing federal construction grants for Community Mental Health Centers (CMHCs). The legislation envisioned 1,500 centers nationwide, each serving a catchment area of 75,000-200,000 people and providing five essential services: inpatient care, outpatient care, partial hospitalization, emergency services, and consultation/education.
- **Federal Funding Model**: The Act provided construction funds but only time-limited operational funding. Federal support was designed to decrease over 8 years as states and local revenues took over -- a transition that rarely materialized.
- **Early Results**: By 1971, only 497 CMHCs had been funded. By 1980, approximately 750 centers were operating -- roughly half the original target of 1,500. Many underserved areas, particularly in the rural South and West, never received a center.
- **Mission Drift**: Without mandated service requirements for individuals with serious mental illness, many CMHCs shifted toward serving the "worried well" -- people with mild to moderate conditions who were easier to treat and more likely to pay. The most severely ill individuals were increasingly left without care.
- **1975 Amendments**: Congress added requirements for follow-up care, transitional living, and services for children and elderly, but enforcement was weak.
- **Mental Health Systems Act (1980)**: President Carter's Mental Health Commission, chaired by Rosalynn Carter, produced recommendations for comprehensive community mental health reform. Congress passed the Mental Health Systems Act (P.L. 96-398), which would have established federal standards for community mental health, increased funding, and mandated services for underserved populations. The Act was effectively repealed before implementation when the Reagan administration consolidated its funding into the ADM block grant in 1981 -- one of the most consequential setbacks in community mental health history.
- **Population Shifts During the CMHC Era**: The centers that did operate served an increasingly different population than originally envisioned. By the late 1970s, only 10-15% of CMHC clients had serious mental illness such as schizophrenia or bipolar disorder; the remainder had adjustment disorders, anxiety, or marital problems (Goldman & Morrissey, 1985). This "mission drift" meant that the population most in need of community mental health services -- those being discharged from state hospitals -- was the least likely to receive them.

### Deinstitutionalization (1965-1990): A Promise Broken

- **Medicaid and the IMD Exclusion (1965)**: The creation of Medicaid in 1965 included the "Institution for Mental Diseases" (IMD) exclusion, prohibiting federal Medicaid payment for care in psychiatric facilities with more than 16 beds for individuals aged 21-64. This created a powerful financial incentive for states to discharge patients from state hospitals (where states bore the full cost) to community settings or nursing homes (where Medicaid would pay a share). The IMD exclusion was designed to prevent federal subsidization of state hospitals, but it became a driver of premature discharge.
- **State Hospital Closures**: State psychiatric hospital populations fell from 559,000 in 1955 to approximately 72,000 by 1994 -- an 87% reduction. Between 1970 and 2000, more than 40 state hospitals closed entirely (Torrey, 2014).
- **The "Revolving Door"**: Without adequate community services, many discharged patients cycled through emergency rooms, homeless shelters, and jails. By the late 1970s, the term "revolving door" described patients repeatedly admitted to hospitals, stabilized, discharged to inadequate community services, decompensated, and re-admitted.
- **Homelessness Crisis**: The visible homelessness crisis of the 1980s was closely linked to deinstitutionalization. Studies estimated that one-third of homeless individuals had serious mental illness (Torrey, 1988). The McKinney-Vento Homeless Assistance Act of 1987 was, in part, a response to this failure.
- **Transinstitutionalization to Jails and Prisons**: As community services failed to absorb the discharged population, jails and prisons became de facto psychiatric institutions. By 2012, there were approximately 356,268 inmates with severe mental illness in jails and prisons -- 10 times the number remaining in state psychiatric hospitals (Treatment Advocacy Center, 2014).
- **Nursing Home Transfers**: A significant but often overlooked dimension of deinstitutionalization was the transfer of elderly patients with mental illness from state hospitals to nursing homes. Medicaid covered nursing home care but not state hospital care, creating a financial incentive for states to reclassify elderly psychiatric patients as nursing home residents. By 1977, an estimated 750,000 individuals with mental illness resided in nursing homes, many receiving no psychiatric treatment (Grob, 1991).
- **Legal Landmarks**: Several court decisions accelerated deinstitutionalization. *Wyatt v. Stickney*, 325 F. Supp. 781 (M.D. Ala. 1971), established the constitutional right to minimally adequate treatment in state hospitals, raising the cost of institutional care. *O'Connor v. Donaldson*, 422 U.S. 563 (1975), held that states could not confine a non-dangerous individual with mental illness capable of surviving in the community, establishing a legal standard for community placement that endures.
- **The Willowbrook Scandal (1972)**: Geraldo Rivera's television expose of conditions at Willowbrook State School on Staten Island shocked the nation, showing overcrowded, filthy wards where individuals with developmental disabilities and mental illness lived in squalor. The subsequent consent decree (*New York State Association for Retarded Children v. Rockefeller*, 1975) became a template for institutional reform litigation nationwide and strengthened the political case for community-based care.

### The Block Grant Era (1981-2000): Devolution and Stagnation

- **Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act (1981)**: The Reagan administration consolidated federal CMHC categorical grants into the Alcohol, Drug Abuse, and Mental Health (ADM) block grant, reducing federal funding by 25% and eliminating federal standards for CMHCs. States gained flexibility but lost accountability; many reduced mental health spending.
- **State Innovation**: Some states pioneered community-based approaches despite federal retrenchment. Wisconsin developed the Assertive Community Treatment (ACT) model at Mendota Mental Health Institute (1972), demonstrating that intensive community teams could serve even the most severely ill individuals outside hospitals. ACT spread gradually through the 1980s and 1990s.
- **SAMHSA Created (1992)**: The Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration was established within HHS, separating behavioral health policy from NIH research functions. SAMHSA administered the Mental Health Block Grant (MHBG) and Substance Abuse Block Grant (SABG), which became the primary federal funding streams for community mental health.
- **Americans with Disabilities Act (1990)**: The ADA established mental illness as a protected disability, creating legal frameworks for community integration that would later be reinforced by the Supreme Court's *Olmstead* decision.
- **CMHC De-Designation**: In 1981, when the ADM block grant replaced categorical CMHC funding, the federal government ceased designating new CMHCs. Existing centers continued operating but with no federal standards, no federal oversight, and diminishing federal support. Some states maintained robust community mental health systems (New York, Massachusetts, Wisconsin); others allowed infrastructure to atrophy (Mississippi, Idaho, Texas). This divergence established the geographic disparities that persist today.
- **The Rise of Managed Behavioral Health Care (1990s)**: During the 1990s, states increasingly contracted with managed behavioral health organizations (MBHOs) to administer Medicaid behavioral health benefits. Companies like Magellan Health, Beacon Health Options, and ValueOptions managed care through prior authorization, utilization review, and network management. While proponents argued that managed care improved efficiency, critics documented reduced access, denial of necessary services, and reimbursement rates that drove community providers to the financial margin. By 2000, over 60% of Medicaid behavioral health spending was managed through some form of managed care arrangement (Frank & Glied, 2006).
- **Program for Assertive Community Treatment (PACT) Expansion**: ACT, originally developed at Mendota Mental Health Institute in Wisconsin by Leonard Stein and Mary Ann Test in the early 1970s, gained broader adoption during the 1990s as research evidence accumulated. By 2000, an estimated 300+ ACT teams operated across approximately 30 states. The PACT model demonstrated that even individuals with the most severe and persistent mental illness could live successfully in the community with intensive support, but adoption remained spotty and underfunded.

### The Olmstead Era and Recovery Movement (1999-2013): Rights and Principles

- ***Olmstead v. L.C.* (1999)**: The Supreme Court ruled in *Olmstead v. L.C.*, 527 U.S. 581 (1999), that unjustified institutional isolation of people with disabilities -- including mental illness -- constitutes discrimination under the ADA. States were required to provide community-based services when professionals determined community placement was appropriate, the individual did not oppose it, and placement could be reasonably accommodated. The decision became the legal cornerstone for community mental health expansion.
- **New Freedom Commission (2003)**: President George W. Bush's New Freedom Commission on Mental Health issued *Achieving the Promise: Transforming Mental Health Care in America*, calling for a recovery-oriented, consumer-driven system. The report identified fragmentation as the fundamental problem and recommended individualized care plans, early screening, and technology-driven coordination.
- **Recovery Movement**: The concept of "recovery" -- defined not as cure but as living a meaningful life despite illness -- became the organizing philosophy of community mental health. SAMHSA adopted recovery as a guiding principle, promoting peer support services, person-centered planning, and integration of lived experience into service delivery.
- **Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act (2008)**: MHPAEA required insurance plans that offered mental health benefits to provide them at parity with medical/surgical benefits. While primarily affecting private insurance, the law signaled growing recognition that mental health treatment should be funded equivalently to physical health treatment.
- **Affordable Care Act (2010)**: The ACA expanded Medicaid eligibility to 138% of the federal poverty level in participating states, bringing millions of low-income adults with mental illness into coverage for the first time. The ACA also required Essential Health Benefits to include mental health and substance use disorder services. By 2020, an estimated 3.7 million additional adults with mental illness had gained coverage through Medicaid expansion (ASPE, 2022).
- **Sandy Hook and Legislative Response (2012-2013)**: The Sandy Hook Elementary School shooting in December 2012 generated intense congressional attention to mental health. The resulting legislative activity included the Excellence in Mental Health Act -- originally introduced by Senators Stabenow (D-MI) and Blunt (R-MO) in 2013 -- which proposed the CCBHC model. While the standalone bill did not pass, its core provisions were eventually incorporated into the Protecting Access to Medicare Act of 2014 as Section 223.
- **Peer Support Certification Expansion**: During this era, states increasingly recognized peer support specialists -- individuals with lived experience of mental illness or addiction -- as a reimbursable Medicaid provider category. By 2015, 39 states had established peer specialist certification programs, expanding the community mental health workforce beyond traditional clinical providers and institutionalizing the recovery movement's emphasis on lived experience (SAMHSA, 2015).
- **Collaborative Care Model Development**: The IMPACT study (2002) and subsequent research established the Collaborative Care Model (CoCM) as an evidence-based approach to integrating behavioral health into primary care. By 2017, Medicare had adopted CoCM billing codes (99492-99494), and community mental health centers began developing "reverse integration" -- embedding primary care screening within behavioral health settings.
- **Olmstead Enforcement**: The DOJ's Civil Rights Division increased Olmstead enforcement actions during the Obama administration, reaching settlement agreements with multiple states (including Georgia, Delaware, and North Carolina) that required expansion of community-based mental health services as alternatives to institutional care. These settlements provided significant funding boosts for community mental health in affected states.

---

### The CCBHC Era (2014-Present): Structural Reform

- **Protecting Access to Medicare Act (2014)**: Section 223 of PAMA authorized the CCBHC demonstration program, establishing criteria for a new model of community behavioral health clinic requiring comprehensive services, 24/7 crisis capacity, and a Prospective Payment System. The Excellence in Mental Health Act language was incorporated into PAMA after standalone passage proved difficult.
- **CCBHC Demonstration Launch (2017)**: Eight states (Minnesota, Missouri, Nevada, New Jersey, New York, Oklahoma, Oregon, Pennsylvania) began the initial demonstration with 67 clinics. Early results showed significant improvements in access, quality, and crisis response.
- **Demonstration Expansion (2020-2021)**: Congress expanded the demonstration to additional states and extended existing state participation, reflecting bipartisan support for the model.
- **Bipartisan Safer Communities Act (2022)**: In response to the Uvalde school shooting, Congress passed P.L. 117-159, which included permanent authorization of a CCBHC Medicaid state plan option (allowing any state to adopt the CCBHC model through a Medicaid State Plan Amendment rather than relying on demonstration authority) and $250 million in CCBHC expansion grants. This was the most significant community mental health legislation since the original 1963 Act.
- **Rapid Expansion (2022-2024)**: CCBHC certification accelerated from fewer than 100 clinics in 2020 to more than 540 across 40+ states by 2024, with additional states in the planning phase (National Council for Mental Wellbeing, 2024).
- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (2022)**: On July 16, 2022, the national crisis line transitioned from the 10-digit 1-800-273-8255 to the three-digit 988 dialing code. The transition, authorized by the National Suicide Hotline Designation Act of 2020 (P.L. 116-172), represented a shift toward a 911-equivalent for behavioral health emergencies. Call volume increased approximately 40% in the first year, though infrastructure gaps persisted -- some local crisis centers could not answer all incoming calls, leading to calls being rolled over to national backup centers (SAMHSA, 2023).
- **COVID-19 Pandemic Impact (2020-2022)**: The pandemic dramatically worsened mental health outcomes while simultaneously accelerating telehealth adoption in community mental health. Between 2019 and 2021, the prevalence of anxiety symptoms increased from 8.2% to 25.5% of adults, and depression symptoms from 6.6% to 24.3% (CDC Household Pulse Survey, 2022). Community mental health centers that had minimal telehealth capacity before March 2020 rapidly deployed virtual services; by 2023, 78% of CMHCs offered telehealth compared to 38% pre-pandemic (SAMHSA N-MHSS). Federal flexibilities, including Medicare and Medicaid telehealth waivers, were essential to this transition.

Despite the nominal increase in federal spending, the inflation-adjusted per-capita federal investment in community mental health in 2024 remains below the 1975 peak, even as the population has grown by over 100 million and mental illness prevalence has increased. The gap between authorization and appropriation has been a defining feature of every era in community mental health history.

While community-based care is generally preferable to institutional care, the speed and scale of bed reductions far outpaced community infrastructure development, creating the "care gap" that continues to manifest as homelessness, incarceration, and emergency department boarding.

## Lessons from History

### Pattern 1: Deinstitutionalization Without Investment

The most consequential pattern in community mental health history is the closure of institutional capacity without proportional investment in community alternatives. Between 1955 and 1994, state psychiatric hospital beds declined by 87%, but community mental health funding never approached the levels needed to serve the discharged population. This pattern repeated at smaller scales: the 1981 block grant consolidation reduced federal standards and funding simultaneously; the ACA expanded coverage but did not proportionally expand provider capacity. The lesson is that policy changes that reduce one form of care must be coupled with enforceable commitments to build another.

The arithmetic of deinstitutionalization illustrates the scale of the failure. In 1955, the United States spent approximately $1 billion annually (nominal) on state psychiatric hospitals serving 559,000 patients. If even half of those resources had been redirected to community mental health infrastructure, the community system would have been funded at levels far exceeding what was actually appropriated. Instead, state governments pocketed the savings from hospital closures, and the federal government never replaced the institutional spending with community investment.

### Pattern 2: Unfunded Federal Mandates and State Variation

Federal legislation has repeatedly established ambitious goals for community mental health without providing the sustained funding or enforcement mechanisms needed to achieve them. The 1963 Act authorized 1,500 CMHCs but funded fewer than half. The *Olmstead* decision established a legal right to community services but relied on underfunded states to provide them. The result is an 8:1 variation in state mental health spending per capita, meaning that the quality and availability of community mental health services depend heavily on where a person lives.

### Pattern 3: Crisis as Catalyst

Major community mental health reforms have consistently followed visible crises -- the asylum exposes of the 1940s led to the CMHC Act; the homelessness crisis of the 1980s led to McKinney-Vento; the Sandy Hook and Uvalde shootings accelerated CCBHC legislation. While crisis-driven policy can produce significant results (as the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act demonstrates), it also means that reforms are reactive rather than systematic, and legislative attention fades when the crisis recedes from public consciousness.

### Pattern 4: The Payment Model Determines Outcomes

Every successful period of community mental health expansion has been associated with a payment model that covered the actual costs of services. The original CMHC construction grants (1963-1975) built physical infrastructure; the Medicaid expansion (2014-present) brought millions into coverage; and the CCBHC Prospective Payment System (2017-present) addressed the chronic underfunding that fee-for-service billing perpetuated. Conversely, every period of system deterioration has been associated with inadequate payment: the transition from federal grants to state responsibility (1975-1981), the block grant funding cuts (1981-1992), and the managed care reimbursement squeezes (1990s-2010s). The CCBHC PPS represents the strongest alignment yet between service requirements and payment adequacy.

### Pattern 5: Workforce Follows Funding

The availability of mental health professionals in community settings has tracked funding levels with a predictable lag of 3-5 years. When federal CMHC grants were robust in the late 1960s and early 1970s, psychiatrists, psychologists, and social workers entered community practice in significant numbers. When block grant funding was cut in 1981, community mental health centers could no longer offer competitive compensation, and providers migrated to private practice and hospital settings. The current workforce crisis -- with 55% of counties lacking a psychiatrist -- is a direct result of decades of inadequate community mental health reimbursement. Conversely, the CCBHC model's prospective payment rates are already demonstrating improved workforce recruitment and retention in early-adopting states (National Council, 2023).

## State-Level Historical Variation

The history of community mental health is not uniform across states. Several states have served as innovation leaders while others have lagged persistently:

| State | Historical Role | Key Innovation | Current Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| New York | Largest state community MH system | NY/NY Agreements (housing); Kendra's Law (AOT) | 80+ ACT teams; 70+ CCBHCs |
| Wisconsin | ACT birthplace | Developed ACT model (Stein & Test, 1972) | 45+ ACT teams; strong fidelity oversight |
| Massachusetts | Early community MH leader | DMH community services; Clubhouse model | Robust CCBHC participation |
| Missouri | CCBHC pioneer | Original CCBHC demonstration state; statewide certification | All 25 CMHCs certified as CCBHCs |
| Oregon | Progressive MH policy | Early Medicaid behavioral health integration | CCBHC demonstration state |
| Texas | Persistent underinvestment | 39 Local Mental Health Authorities for 30 million people | Low per-capita spending; limited CCBHC adoption |
| Mississippi | Among lowest capacity | Minimal community infrastructure; reliance on state hospital | Among lowest SMHA per-capita spending nationally |

Source: SAMHSA URS, 2022; state DMH reports; National Council, 2024

The divergence between high-investment and low-investment states has widened over time. States that invested in community mental health infrastructure in the 1970s-1990s (New York, Massachusetts, Wisconsin) entered the CCBHC era with a foundation to build upon. States that did not (Texas, Mississippi, Idaho) face the dual challenge of building basic infrastructure while simultaneously attempting to adopt the CCBHC model.
