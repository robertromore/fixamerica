# Telehealth and Digital Mental Health: Stakeholders

The mental health telehealth landscape is shaped by the interactions of patients seeking care, providers delivering it, regulators controlling it, payers financing it, and technology companies building the platforms and tools that make it possible. Each group has distinct interests that frequently conflict, and the outcome of telehealth policy debates depends on which coalitions form and which interests prevail.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Adults with mental health conditions | Telehealth is primary or sole access pathway for many; telehealth policy determines whether, how, and at what cost they receive care | 57.8 million adults with any mental illness (SAMHSA, 2023) | Reliable access, affordable care, privacy, continuity of provider relationships across moves |
| People in Mental Health Professional Shortage Areas | Telehealth is often the only viable path to a psychiatrist or psychologist | 160 million+ in HPSAs (HRSA, 2024) | Broadband access, audio-only availability, interstate provider access |
| People with substance use disorders | Telehealth is critical for buprenorphine access and counseling continuity | 46.3 million with SUD (SAMHSA, 2023) | Permanent Ryan Haight waiver for buprenorphine, telehealth counseling coverage |
| Children and adolescents with mental health needs | Pediatric mental health crisis has intensified demand; school-based telehealth is a key delivery mechanism | 7.7 million children with anxiety/depression (CDC, 2023) | School-based access, parental consent frameworks, age-appropriate digital tools |
| Veterans | VA telemental health is the largest system in the country; policy changes affect millions of veterans | 1.7 million veterans using VA telemental health (VA, 2024) | Continued VA telehealth expansion, community care integration |
| Medicare beneficiaries 65+ | Disproportionately affected by temporary coverage status; 22% use telehealth for behavioral health | 65 million+ Medicare enrollees (CMS, 2024) | Permanent Medicare coverage, audio-only availability, device access |
| Medicaid enrollees | Coverage varies dramatically by state; most underserved population for telehealth | 93 million Medicaid/CHIP enrollees (CMS, 2024) | Consistent state Medicaid coverage, broadband access, digital literacy support |
| People with disabilities | Both high mental health service needs and high barriers to telehealth access | 42.5 million Americans with disabilities (Census, 2023) | Platform accessibility, ADA compliance, ASL interpreter integration, assistive technology compatibility |

### Secondary Stakeholders

- **Family members and caregivers**: Family-based therapy, caregiver support groups, and psychoeducation are increasingly delivered via telehealth. Caregivers of people with severe mental illness depend on telehealth for their own mental health support. The National Alliance for Caregiving estimates that 53 million Americans serve as unpaid caregivers, many of whom have limited ability to attend in-person mental health appointments.
- **Employers**: Employer-sponsored mental health benefits increasingly include telehealth platforms (e.g., Lyra Health, Spring Health, Modern Health). Employers want effective, cost-efficient mental health services to reduce absenteeism, presenteeism, and turnover. A 2024 Mercer survey found that 78% of large employers offer a dedicated mental health telehealth benefit, up from 35% in 2019. Employers are significant purchasers of telehealth platforms and their benefit design decisions shape the telehealth market.
- **Criminal justice system**: Courts, jails, and probation departments increasingly use telehealth for mandated mental health and substance use treatment. Disruptions in telehealth policy affect diversion and reentry programs. An estimated 44% of jail inmates have a mental health condition (Bureau of Justice Statistics, 2024), and telehealth is often the only mechanism for providing psychiatric care in facilities without on-site psychiatrists.
- **Schools and universities**: K-12 schools and higher education institutions are major delivery sites for telehealth mental health services, particularly in rural areas. School-based telehealth programs serve approximately 2 million students annually (Rural Health Information Hub, 2024). The Bipartisan Safer Communities Act of 2022 provided $1 billion in funding for school-based mental health services, much of which has been deployed through telehealth. University counseling centers, facing unprecedented demand, have integrated telehealth into their service models, with the American College Health Association reporting that 65% of campus counseling centers offered telehealth in 2024.
- **Primary care providers**: Primary care is the de facto front line for mental health care, with an estimated 60% of depression and anxiety treatment managed in primary care settings (AHRQ, 2024). The Collaborative Care Model (CoCM), which integrates psychiatric consultation into primary care via telehealth, is a growing delivery mechanism. Primary care providers are stakeholders in telehealth policy because psychiatric consultation via telehealth expands their capacity to manage mental health conditions.
- **Emergency departments**: Emergency departments serve as the default mental health crisis system for patients without access to outpatient care. Telehealth expansion that increases outpatient mental health access reduces ED utilization for psychiatric crises. A 2023 study in Annals of Emergency Medicine found that counties with higher telehealth mental health utilization had 12% lower rates of psychiatric ED visits.

## Who Has Power

### Decision Makers

| Actor | Role | Authority | Resources | Influence Level |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Congress (Senate HELP Committee, House Energy and Commerce) | Legislates Medicare telehealth coverage, broadband policy, FDA authority | Statutory authority over Medicare, Medicaid, controlled substances, FDA | Full legislative power; CBO scoring constraints | High |
| CMS (Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services) | Sets Medicare telehealth coverage rules, reimbursement rates, and conditions of participation | Regulatory authority under Social Security Act | $1.5 trillion annual Medicare/Medicaid spending | High |
| DEA (Drug Enforcement Administration) | Regulates controlled substance prescribing via telehealth under Ryan Haight Act | Enforcement and rulemaking authority under Controlled Substances Act | Federal law enforcement resources | High |
| FDA (Food and Drug Administration) | Regulates digital therapeutics, mental health apps that make clinical claims, and AI-based tools | Regulatory authority under Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act | Digital Health Center of Excellence (est. 2020) | Medium-High |
| FCC (Federal Communications Commission) | Regulates broadband deployment, USF programs, Connected Care Pilot | Broadband infrastructure and subsidy authority | $8.25 billion annual Universal Service Fund | Medium |
| FTC (Federal Trade Commission) | Enforces consumer protection for mental health apps and platforms, including data privacy | Section 5 authority over unfair and deceptive trade practices | Enforcement staff and litigation resources | Medium |
| State licensing boards | Issue and regulate professional licenses for mental health providers | State licensure authority | Independent revenue from licensing fees | Medium-High (collectively) |
| State Medicaid agencies | Determine Medicaid telehealth coverage and reimbursement in each state | State regulatory authority | State Medicaid budgets | Medium |
| State attorneys general | Enforce state consumer protection laws affecting telehealth platforms and mental health apps | State enforcement authority | Litigation resources; multi-state coordinated actions | Medium |

### Key Organizations and Institutions

- **American Psychiatric Association (APA)**: Represents 38,000+ psychiatrists. Supports permanent telehealth expansion with appropriate safeguards. Develops telepsychiatry practice guidelines and the App Advisor evaluation framework, which has reviewed over 600 mental health apps. Influential in DEA and CMS rulemaking through comment letters, expert testimony, and direct engagement with agency leadership. Annual lobbying expenditure approximately $3 million (OpenSecrets, 2024).
- **American Psychological Association (APA)**: Represents 146,000+ psychologists. Strong supporter of PSYPACT expansion. Advocates for permanent telehealth coverage including audio-only services. The APA's Practice Directorate provides practice guidance to psychologists transitioning to telehealth and tracks state-level policy changes. Annual lobbying expenditure approximately $2.5 million (OpenSecrets, 2024).
- **National Association of Social Workers (NASW)**: Represents 120,000+ social workers. Advocates for a social work licensure compact and permanent Medicaid telehealth coverage. NASW's policy positions emphasize equity, noting that social workers serve disproportionately low-income and marginalized populations. Annual lobbying expenditure approximately $700,000 (OpenSecrets, 2024).
- **National Council for Mental Wellbeing**: Represents 3,300+ community mental health organizations that are the safety-net providers for uninsured and Medicaid populations. Advocates for Medicaid telehealth coverage, broadband access, and workforce support for telehealth adoption. The Council's members--Community Mental Health Centers and Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinics (CCBHCs)--are critical delivery points for telehealth in underserved areas.
- **American Telemedicine Association (ATA)**: The leading telehealth industry association, representing telehealth providers, technology companies, health systems, and payers. Lobbies for permanent federal telehealth legislation, payment parity, and reduced regulatory barriers. Annual lobbying expenditure approximately $1.2 million (OpenSecrets, 2024). The ATA's annual conference is the largest telehealth industry event.
- **PSYPACT Commission**: Administers the Psychology Interjurisdictional Compact. Facilitates interstate telepsychology practice for psychologists in 42 states plus DC. The Commission maintains a database of authorized practitioners, processes complaints across jurisdictions, and provides technical assistance to states considering PSYPACT legislation.
- **Digital Therapeutics Alliance (DTA)**: Industry association for prescription digital therapeutics companies. Advocates for FDA regulatory pathways, CPT codes, and payer reimbursement for digital therapeutics. The DTA has become less influential following the bankruptcy of Pear Therapeutics and the financial difficulties of other members, but continues to advocate for a regulatory and reimbursement framework that would sustain the DTx market.
- **Association of State and Provincial Psychology Boards (ASPPB)**: Administers the Examination for Professional Practice in Psychology (EPPP) and provides the infrastructure for PSYPACT. The ASPPB's governance of professional standards for psychologists across states makes it a key institutional player in interstate licensure.

### Influencers

- **Telehealth platform companies (BetterHelp/Teladoc, Talkspace, Cerebral)**: Large commercial telehealth companies that deliver millions of mental health visits annually. They have strong financial incentives to expand telehealth access and reduce regulatory barriers. Teladoc reported 18.5 million total visits in 2023, with behavioral health as its largest category (Teladoc, 2024 10-K). BetterHelp, Teladoc's subsidiary, is the largest online therapy platform with over 35,000 licensed therapists and over 4 million users served since inception. However, the Cerebral and Done controversies have damaged the credibility of telehealth-first platforms in regulatory debates.
- **Employer mental health platform companies (Lyra Health, Spring Health, Modern Health)**: These companies sell integrated mental health benefit packages to employers, typically combining telehealth therapy, digital self-guided programs, and care navigation. Lyra Health was valued at $4.6 billion in its 2022 funding round, and Spring Health at $2.5 billion. Their employer-facing model gives them influence through corporate benefits purchasing decisions.
- **Venture capital and health tech investors**: Have invested billions in mental health technology companies. The mental health tech market attracted $5.1 billion in venture investment in 2021, declining to $2.4 billion in 2023 as the market matured (Rock Health, 2024). Investor interests favor minimal regulation and maximum market expansion, though the post-2022 downturn has shifted investor focus from growth to profitability and clinical validation.
- **Patient advocacy organizations (NAMI, MHA)**: The National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI) and Mental Health America (MHA) represent millions of patients and families. They advocate for access expansion, including telehealth, and are key voices in the audio-only and digital divide debates. NAMI's 600+ state and local affiliates provide grassroots infrastructure for advocacy campaigns. MHA's annual "State of Mental Health in America" report provides data used by advocates and policymakers.
- **Rural health advocacy groups**: The National Rural Health Association (NRHA) and State Offices of Rural Health advocate for telehealth as the primary solution to rural mental health access. They are the strongest advocates for audio-only coverage and broadband investment. The NRHA's annual Rural Health Policy Institute convenes rural health stakeholders with congressional staff and agency officials.
- **Veterans service organizations (VFW, American Legion, DAV)**: These organizations advocate for continued VA telehealth expansion and for applying VA telehealth models to the civilian healthcare system. Their bipartisan political influence is significant, and they provide a powerful constituency for telehealth permanence.

| FDA + digital therapeutics industry + researchers | Clear regulatory pathway for digital therapeutics | Moderate | FDA DHCE, DTA, academic medical centers |

### Conflicting Interests

- **Access vs. enforcement**: Patient groups and telehealth companies want the broadest possible telehealth prescribing authority, while the DEA and some medical boards want in-person requirements to prevent diversion and ensure quality. This conflict is structural: the DEA's mandate is drug enforcement, not healthcare access, and its institutional incentives do not account for the access consequences of restrictive prescribing rules.
- **Innovation vs. consumer protection**: Technology companies and investors want minimal FDA regulation of mental health apps to preserve innovation speed, while patient advocates and the APA want stronger oversight to protect consumers from unvalidated products. The Pear Therapeutics bankruptcy has complicated this debate: stronger regulation may improve quality but could also kill companies that cannot sustain the cost of compliance.
- **State autonomy vs. national access**: State licensing boards want to maintain their authority and revenue, while providers and patients want seamless interstate access through national compacts or federal preemption. This tension reflects a fundamental federalism question: is professional licensure primarily a state consumer protection function or a barrier to national healthcare access?
- **Cost control vs. access expansion**: CMS and CBO are concerned that permanent telehealth expansion will increase Medicare spending through new utilization, while providers and patients argue that telehealth substitutes for (and is often cheaper than) in-person care. This conflict cannot be resolved empirically until permanent telehealth coverage is enacted and utilization data is collected over multiple years.
- **Provider quality vs. platform scale**: Individual providers and professional associations want clinical standards (minimum session length, caseload limits, supervision requirements) that may conflict with the business models of large telehealth platforms that depend on high-volume, rapid-access service delivery. The tension between clinical integrity and commercial scale is a defining challenge of the telehealth market.
- **Privacy vs. data utilization**: Mental health apps collect sensitive data that can improve care through analytics but also poses privacy risks. Patient advocates want HIPAA-equivalent protections for all mental health data, while technology companies argue that excessive privacy requirements will stifle innovation and prevent the development of data-driven mental health tools.

### The Telehealth Iron Triangle

The telehealth policy space is characterized by an iron triangle connecting three sets of actors:

1. **Congressional committees** (Senate HELP, House Energy and Commerce) that control telehealth legislation and appropriations
2. **Federal agencies** (CMS, DEA, FDA) that implement telehealth regulations
3. **Industry and professional groups** (ATA, APA, telehealth companies) that lobby for favorable rules and provide expert testimony

This triangle tends to produce incremental, temporary policy changes rather than comprehensive reform, because each actor has incentives to avoid the political risk of permanent action. Congress avoids the CBO score of permanence. Agencies avoid the institutional risk of finalizing complex rules. Industry accepts temporary extensions because they are politically easier to obtain than permanent reform. The result is a stable equilibrium of instability: annual extensions that preserve the status quo while avoiding the structural reforms needed for a permanent framework.

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on mental health telehealth policy:

- **Lobbying**: The American Telemedicine Association and its members spent approximately $3.5 million on federal lobbying in 2023 (OpenSecrets, 2024). Telehealth companies like Teladoc, Amazon, and large health systems also lobby individually. The American Hospital Association, which opposes some aspects of telehealth expansion (particularly home-based telehealth that reduces facility revenue), spent approximately $29 million on federal lobbying in 2023, making it one of the largest healthcare lobbying forces in Washington.
- **Public comment**: The DEA's 2023 proposed rule on telehealth prescribing received over 38,000 public comments, one of the largest comment volumes for a DEA rulemaking. Comments from the APA, NAMI, and other organizations were influential in the DEA's decision to revise its proposal (DEA, 2024). CMS rulemaking on telehealth coverage also receives substantial public comment, though comment volumes are typically lower than the DEA's prescribing rules.
- **Campaign contributions**: Health technology and telemedicine PACs contributed approximately $8 million to congressional campaigns in the 2024 election cycle, with contributions split between parties and concentrated on members of the Senate HELP and House Energy and Commerce Committees (OpenSecrets, 2024). While modest compared to pharmaceutical or hospital industry contributions, these funds target the specific committees with jurisdiction over telehealth legislation.
- **Research and expert testimony**: Professional associations (APA, APA) and think tanks (Brookings, KFF, RAND, Commonwealth Fund) produce research that shapes congressional hearings, CMS rulemaking, and public debate. The evidence base for telehealth mental health equivalence is a key tool for access advocates. MedPAC's annual reports and recommendations carry particular weight with Congress because of the commission's nonpartisan reputation.
- **Grassroots advocacy**: NAMI's 600+ state and local affiliates mobilize patient and family advocates to contact legislators, particularly during annual debates over Medicare telehealth extensions. The #TelehealthNow campaign generated over 50,000 congressional contacts in 2024. Mental Health America's online screening tools (used by over 1 million people annually) provide a platform for mobilizing users as advocates.
- **Litigation and enforcement**: The FTC's enforcement actions against BetterHelp ($7.8 million settlement, 2023) and other companies have shaped the telehealth privacy debate. State attorneys general have also brought actions against telehealth companies for deceptive practices, establishing precedents that influence platform behavior even absent federal legislation.
- **Media and public narrative**: Investigative journalism (particularly STAT News's Cerebral coverage and The Wall Street Journal's reporting on telehealth prescribing) shapes public perception and political urgency. Provider and patient testimonials in national media create pressure for legislative action.
