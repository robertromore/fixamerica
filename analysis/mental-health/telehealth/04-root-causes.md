# Telehealth and Digital Mental Health: Root Causes

## Systemic Analysis

The current challenges in mental health telehealth are not the result of a single failure but of several long-term, interlocking structural problems. These include a fragmented regulatory architecture designed for in-person care, an infrastructure deficit that excludes the most underserved populations, a reimbursement system that creates annual uncertainty, and a digital marketplace that has outpaced the government's capacity to evaluate and regulate it. Each of these root causes reinforces the others, creating a system in which temporary emergency measures persist because the structural reforms needed to replace them face political, institutional, and economic obstacles.

## Regulatory Fragmentation

### State-by-State Licensure Architecture

The fundamental structure of professional licensure in the United States--with each state maintaining independent licensing boards, scope-of-practice definitions, and supervision requirements--creates a patchwork system that is inherently hostile to telehealth. A psychologist licensed in California cannot treat a patient in Nevada via video without also holding a Nevada license, even if the patient lived in California until last month.

**Evidence**: There are over 200 independent state mental health licensing boards across the 50 states and territories (covering psychologists, psychiatrists, social workers, counselors, and marriage and family therapists). Interstate compacts like PSYPACT (42 states + DC, 2024) have made progress, but no compact covers all mental health professions, and 8 states remain outside PSYPACT entirely (PSYPACT Commission, 2024). Licensed clinical social workers--the largest category of mental health providers with approximately 290,000 practitioners (BLS, 2024)--have no functioning interstate compact at all.

**Structural origin**: The state-by-state licensure system was established in the early 20th century when healthcare was exclusively local. The system was designed to protect consumers from unqualified practitioners within state borders. It was never designed for--and is structurally incompatible with--a telecommunications-enabled healthcare system in which the provider and patient can be anywhere. The 10th Amendment's reservation of police powers to states, which forms the constitutional basis for state licensure authority, creates a structural federalism barrier to national licensure reform.

**Scale of the problem**: A mental health provider who wants to serve patients in all 50 states would need to navigate up to 50 separate licensing applications, pay $10,000-25,000 in aggregate licensing fees, complete state-specific continuing education requirements, and maintain compliance with 50 different sets of scope-of-practice rules. Even with interstate compacts, the process remains burdensome: PSYPACT requires psychologists to pay an annual E.Passport fee ($115 as of 2024) on top of their home state license fee and subjects them to the regulations of each state where their patients are located.

### The Ryan Haight Act's Legacy

The Ryan Haight Act of 2008 addressed a legitimate problem--illegal online pharmacies selling controlled substances without medical evaluation--but created a structural barrier to legitimate telepsychiatry. The law's requirement of at least one in-person evaluation before telehealth prescribing of controlled substances was designed for a world of pill mills and rogue pharmacies, not for board-certified psychiatrists conducting thorough video evaluations.

**Evidence**: The DEA was mandated by the Ryan Haight Act to create a "special registration" process for telemedicine prescribers. It took over 15 years--until 2023--for the DEA to issue even a proposed rule, and no final rule has been published as of January 2025 (DEA, 2024). This regulatory vacuum forced the field to operate under emergency waivers rather than a permanent framework.

**Structural origin**: The Ryan Haight Act was drafted in the context of the early-2000s online pharmacy crisis, when websites were selling opioids, benzodiazepines, and other controlled substances with minimal or no medical evaluation. The legislative response--requiring an in-person visit--made sense in that context, where "telehealth" meant little more than an online questionnaire. The law's drafters did not anticipate a world in which a psychiatrist could conduct a comprehensive psychiatric evaluation via real-time video, observe patient affect and behavior, review medical records electronically, and coordinate care with other providers, all without physical presence. The law effectively equated all forms of remote prescribing with the abuses it sought to prevent.

**Compounding effect**: The DEA's failure to create the telemedicine special registration process for over 15 years meant that there was no legal mechanism for legitimate telehealth prescribing outside of emergency waivers. This regulatory vacuum was not accidental: the DEA's institutional culture prioritizes enforcement and views any expansion of remote prescribing authority as a diversion risk. The agency's incentive structure rewards preventing drug diversion, not expanding access to psychiatric care.

### Medicare's Statutory Restrictions

Prior to COVID-19 waivers, Medicare telehealth was governed by Section 1834(m) of the Social Security Act, which limited coverage to beneficiaries in rural Health Professional Shortage Areas who were physically present at an approved originating site (a clinic, hospital, or community mental health center). Patients could not receive telehealth services from home. These restrictions were written into statute in 1997 and required congressional action to modify.

**Evidence**: Before the PHE waivers, only 0.1% of Medicare beneficiaries used telehealth for mental health services. After the waivers, usage surged to over 20% (MedPAC, 2024). The restrictions were not based on clinical evidence; they were fiscal controls to limit Medicare telehealth spending.

**Structural origin**: When Congress authorized Medicare telehealth coverage in the Balanced Budget Act of 1997, it imposed geographic and site restrictions as a cost-containment mechanism. The concern was that unrestricted telehealth coverage would generate new utilization (and new Medicare spending) by making it easier for beneficiaries to access services. This concern was particularly acute for mental health, where unmet need was known to be enormous. The restrictions were, in effect, a rationing mechanism: by limiting telehealth to rural HPSAs and facility-based originating sites, Congress ensured that telehealth would supplement existing in-person infrastructure rather than creating new access pathways. The restrictions persisted for over two decades despite growing evidence that they were counterproductive.

## Infrastructure Deficit

### Broadband as a Social Determinant of Health

The digital divide is not merely a technology problem; it is a structural determinant of access to mental health care in a telehealth-enabled system. The same populations that face the greatest barriers to in-person mental health care--rural residents, low-income communities, older adults, tribal nations--also lack the broadband infrastructure required for video-based telehealth.

**Evidence**: 21% of rural Americans lack fixed broadband at 25/3 Mbps, compared to 3% of urban Americans (FCC Broadband Data Collection, 2023). On tribal lands, the figure rises to 35%. At the FCC's updated benchmark of 100/20 Mbps (adopted 2024), the rural gap is even larger: 42% of rural residents lack broadband sufficient for reliable video telehealth. The Bipartisan Infrastructure Law of 2021 authorized $65 billion for broadband expansion, but deployment timelines extend to 2028 and beyond, meaning the gap will persist for years.

**Structural origin**: Broadband infrastructure development has been driven primarily by market incentives, which favor deployment in high-density, high-income areas where return on investment is greatest. Rural and tribal areas, where the population density is lowest and the construction costs are highest, have been systematically underserved by market-based broadband deployment. Federal broadband subsidies--the Universal Service Fund, the Rural Health Care Program, and now the BEAD Program--have partially addressed this gap but have been insufficient in scale and speed. The result is that broadband access is inversely correlated with mental health need: the places with the fewest mental health providers are also the places with the worst broadband infrastructure.

**The Affordable Connectivity Program gap**: The FCC's Affordable Connectivity Program, which provided $30/month broadband subsidies to 23 million low-income households, expired in June 2024 after Congress failed to reauthorize it. This removed a critical affordability bridge for households that had broadband infrastructure available but could not afford monthly service costs. The program's expiration illustrates the fragility of subsidy-based access: infrastructure investment alone is insufficient if ongoing affordability is not addressed.

### Device and Digital Literacy Barriers

Even where broadband exists, many potential telehealth patients lack suitable devices or the digital literacy to use them for healthcare. Older adults, people with disabilities, and non-English speakers face compounding barriers.

**Evidence**: 34% of adults 65+ lack a smartphone, and 25% lack any broadband internet connection (Pew Research Center, 2024). Among adults with household income below $30,000, 27% lack home broadband and 15% rely exclusively on smartphone internet access, which is inadequate for sustained video therapy sessions (FCC, 2023). Non-English speakers face the additional barrier that most telehealth platforms and nearly all mental health apps are English-only.

**Structural origin**: Device access and digital literacy are downstream consequences of income inequality, educational disparities, and age-related technology adoption patterns. These are not problems that telehealth policy alone can solve, but they define the outer boundary of telehealth's reach. Without complementary investments in device access (tablet lending programs, subsidized smartphones) and digital literacy training, telehealth expansion risks creating a two-tier mental health system: video-based care for the digitally connected and audio-only or no care for everyone else.

### Accessibility for People with Disabilities

Many telehealth platforms are not fully accessible to people with disabilities, creating barriers for a population with disproportionately high rates of mental health conditions. The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) and Section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act require accessible digital services, but enforcement in the telehealth context has been minimal.

**Evidence**: A 2024 study by the National Disability Institute found that 63% of the 20 most popular telehealth platforms did not meet WCAG 2.1 Level AA accessibility standards. Common failures included insufficient screen reader compatibility, absence of captioning for video sessions, and lack of ASL interpreter integration. The Department of Justice has issued guidance stating that telehealth platforms must comply with the ADA, but no enforcement actions specific to telehealth accessibility have been brought.

## Reimbursement Instability

### The Annual Cliff Problem

The most corrosive structural problem facing mental health telehealth is the annual cycle of temporary extensions and expiration threats. Congress has extended Medicare telehealth flexibilities through annual appropriations bills four times since the PHE ended (2022, 2023, 2024, 2025), each time creating a period of uncertainty during which providers do not know whether their telehealth services will be reimbursed in the coming year.

**Evidence**: The American Telemedicine Association reported that 62% of telehealth providers surveyed in 2024 said regulatory uncertainty was their top business concern, and 38% said they had delayed investments in telehealth infrastructure due to the possibility that flexibilities would expire (ATA, 2024). Community mental health centers reported that they could not commit to multi-year contracts with telehealth technology vendors because of annual funding uncertainty.

**Structural origin**: The annual cliff is a product of CBO scoring methodology. The CBO scores permanent telehealth expansion as a cost increase because it assumes that expanded access will generate new utilization. Congress, unwilling to accept the 10-year budget impact of permanent expansion, opts for one-year extensions that carry lower scores. This creates a perverse cycle: the uncertainty caused by annual extensions discourages the investments that would make telehealth more efficient and cost-effective, which in turn makes it harder to demonstrate the cost savings that would justify permanence.

### Payment Parity Inconsistency

While 40 states plus DC require some form of telehealth payment parity for commercial insurance, these laws vary widely in scope, and no federal law mandates payment parity for telehealth services. Medicare payment parity is temporary. Without permanent parity, providers face the prospect of reduced reimbursement for telehealth visits, which would undermine the economic model that sustains telehealth access.

**Evidence**: A 2023 RAND study found that in states without payment parity mandates, telehealth reimbursement rates were on average 20-30% lower than in-person rates for the same behavioral health services (RAND, 2023). This creates a geographic lottery: a therapist in a parity state is reimbursed at the full rate for a telehealth session, while a therapist in a non-parity state receives 70-80 cents on the dollar for the same service.

**ERISA preemption**: Self-insured employer health plans (covering approximately 65% of employer-insured workers) are exempt from state insurance mandates under ERISA. This means that even in states with robust telehealth parity laws, a significant portion of the commercially insured population may not benefit. Only federal legislation can address the ERISA gap.

### Digital Therapeutics Reimbursement Failure

FDA clearance for digital therapeutics does not automatically translate to payer coverage or reimbursement. The current reimbursement infrastructure was designed for drugs and devices, not software. There are no established CPT codes for most digital therapeutics, and CMS has no coverage pathway for prescription digital therapeutics under Medicare Part B or Part D.

**Evidence**: Pear Therapeutics, which held FDA clearance for three digital therapeutics products (reSET, reSET-O, Somryst), filed for bankruptcy in April 2023, citing inability to achieve sustainable reimbursement from payers. Only 40% of commercial insurers covered any prescription digital therapeutic as of 2023 (Digital Therapeutics Alliance, 2023). The lack of standardized CPT codes meant that providers had to use miscellaneous or unlisted service codes when billing for digital therapeutics, resulting in inconsistent reimbursement.

**Structural origin**: The reimbursement system was built around tangible goods (drugs, devices) and defined services (visits, procedures). Software-based treatments do not fit neatly into existing benefit categories. A prescription digital therapeutic for substance use disorder--which delivers cognitive behavioral therapy through a smartphone app--is neither a drug nor a device nor a provider visit in the traditional sense. The lack of a benefit category means that even when payers are willing to cover digital therapeutics, they struggle with how to adjudicate claims, set appropriate prices, and verify utilization.

## Market and Regulatory Gaps

### The Consumer App Regulatory Vacuum

Over 10,000 mental health apps are available to consumers, but the FDA's regulatory framework covers only a tiny fraction of them. The FDA's 2019 Digital Health Innovation Action Plan and the 21st Century Cures Act's exemption for clinical decision support software created a broad space in which apps can make wellness claims, offer therapy-like exercises, and collect sensitive mental health data without any regulatory review.

**Evidence**: An analysis by the American Psychiatric Association's App Advisor program found that of 150 popular mental health apps reviewed, fewer than 5% had published peer-reviewed evidence supporting their clinical claims, 64% did not have a privacy policy that met minimum standards, and 29% shared user data with third parties (APA App Advisor, 2023). A separate analysis by the Organisation for the Advancement of Structured Information Standards (OASIS) found that 78% of mental health apps tested transmitted user data to third-party analytics services, often without clear user consent.

**Structural origin**: The regulatory gap exists because the FDA's device classification system was designed for hardware, not software. The 21st Century Cures Act's exemption for "clinical decision support" software was intended to reduce regulatory burden on low-risk tools, but it created an ambiguity that many mental health app developers exploit. An app that guides a user through a CBT exercise for depression may be functionally identical to an FDA-cleared digital therapeutic, but if it is marketed as a "wellness" rather than "clinical" tool, it falls outside FDA jurisdiction.

## Political Economy

### Who Benefits from the Status Quo

| Actor | Benefit | Economic Value | Incentive to Block Reform |
|-------|---------|----------------|---------------------------|
| State licensing boards | Maintain regulatory authority, fee revenue, and professional control | ~$500 million annually in licensing fees across all mental health professions | Interstate compacts reduce their authority; resist national licensure |
| In-person-only providers | Reduced competition from out-of-state telehealth providers | N/A (indirect competitive protection) | Telehealth expansion increases supply, potentially reducing demand for in-person care |
| Large health systems with physical infrastructure | Facility fees for in-person visits; capital investment in physical plants | Billions in annual facility fee revenue | Telehealth shifts care outside their facilities; lose facility fee revenue |
| Pharmaceutical benefit managers (PBMs) | Current formulary structures favor traditional drug distribution | N/A | Digital therapeutics disrupt their drug-centric business model |
| DEA | Maintains enforcement authority over controlled substance prescribing | Enforcement budget and regulatory authority | Telehealth prescribing is harder to monitor; prefer in-person requirements |
| Commercial telehealth platforms | Current regulatory vacuum allows rapid market growth without compliance costs | $5+ billion market (2024) | Stronger FDA/FTC regulation increases compliance costs and limits growth |

### Perverse Incentives

The current system creates several misaligned incentives:

- **Providers are incentivized to avoid controlled substances via telehealth** even when clinically appropriate, because the legal uncertainty around the Ryan Haight Act creates liability risk. This leads to undertreatment. A psychiatrist who prescribes stimulants via telehealth faces ambiguous legal exposure if the temporary waiver expires; the same prescription written after an in-person visit carries no such risk.
- **App developers are incentivized to avoid FDA regulation** by making "wellness" rather than "clinical" claims, even when their products function as de facto therapeutic interventions. This lets them reach market faster but with no quality assurance. The financial calculus is clear: FDA clearance requires clinical trials costing $5-20 million and taking 2-4 years, while a wellness app can launch in months with minimal regulatory cost.
- **Health systems are incentivized to require facility-based originating sites** for telehealth, because this allows them to bill a facility fee. This undermines the home-based access that makes telehealth most valuable. In 2023, facility fees for Medicare telehealth visits originating at health system sites averaged $50-100 per visit, generating substantial revenue for systems that require patients to come to a facility for their telehealth appointment.
- **Congress is incentivized to extend telehealth flexibilities temporarily** rather than permanently, because temporary extensions avoid the long-term CBO scoring implications of permanent telehealth expansion. A one-year extension costs $2-4 billion; a permanent extension costs $20-40 billion over 10 years. The political path of least resistance is the annual extension.
- **Telehealth platforms are incentivized to maximize visit volume** over visit quality, because their revenue models depend on patient throughput. Venture-backed platforms operating at losses are under particular pressure to demonstrate growth metrics to investors, which can conflict with clinical best practices regarding session length and caseload limits.

## Cultural and Institutional Factors

### Provider Resistance to Practice Change

Despite strong evidence supporting telehealth for mental health, significant segments of the clinical workforce remain skeptical. Some clinicians believe that in-person therapeutic relationships are inherently superior and that telehealth is a compromise, not an alternative. Training programs have been slow to incorporate telehealth competencies.

**Evidence**: A 2024 APA survey found that while 85% of psychiatrists offered telehealth, only 45% reported receiving any formal training in telehealth best practices during residency or continuing education (APA, 2024). Among psychoanalytically-oriented practitioners, resistance is particularly pronounced: a 2023 survey by the American Psychoanalytic Association found that 61% of respondents believed that psychoanalytic treatment could not be effectively delivered via telehealth, despite published case series suggesting otherwise.

### Patient Stigma and Preference

Some patients prefer the anonymity of a telehealth visit (reduced stigma of entering a mental health facility), while others distrust the technology or prefer the ritual of an in-person visit. Cultural factors affect adoption patterns, with lower telehealth uptake among older adults, Hispanic/Latino communities, and non-English speakers.

**Evidence**: A 2023 KFF survey found that 72% of adults 18-44 were comfortable receiving mental health care via video, compared to only 38% of adults 65+ (KFF, 2023). Among Hispanic/Latino adults, comfort with video-based mental health care was 15 percentage points lower than among non-Hispanic white adults, even after controlling for broadband access and income. Language barriers, cultural attitudes toward technology in healthcare, and distrust of data privacy practices all contribute to this gap.

### Institutional Inertia in Federal Agencies

Beyond the DEA's specific institutional caution around prescribing, a broader pattern of institutional inertia affects telehealth policy across federal agencies. CMS's rulemaking process for telehealth coverage changes typically takes 18-24 months from proposed rule to final implementation. The FDA's digital health regulatory framework has been "under development" since 2013. The FCC's broadband deployment programs operate on multi-year timelines that do not align with the urgent need for telehealth infrastructure. This multi-agency inertia means that even when there is political will for reform, the implementation timeline extends years beyond the legislative action.

## Why Reform Has Failed

Reform has stalled for three interlocking reasons:

1. **Congressional inaction on permanent legislation**: Multiple bills to permanently extend Medicare telehealth flexibilities (the CONNECT for Health Act, the Telehealth Modernization Act) have been introduced in every Congress since 2020 but have failed to advance past committee, largely because of CBO scoring concerns about increased Medicare spending and disagreements over audio-only coverage. The bills have bipartisan co-sponsors but lack the legislative urgency to overcome the scoring obstacle. The annual extension cycle, paradoxically, reduces urgency by ensuring that flexibilities continue for at least another year.

2. **DEA institutional caution**: The DEA's organizational culture prioritizes enforcement and diversion prevention. The agency has resisted creating a permanent telehealth prescribing framework because it views any relaxation of the in-person requirement as increasing diversion risk. The 38,000+ public comments on its 2023 proposed rule revealed intense stakeholder disagreement that the DEA has been unable to resolve. The Done Health indictment in 2024 further strengthened the DEA's institutional position that telehealth prescribing requires strict controls.

3. **No single powerful champion**: Unlike other healthcare reform areas where a single agency or committee has clear jurisdiction, telehealth policy spans CMS, the DEA, the FDA, HHS, FCC, and state governments. No single entity has the authority or political incentive to drive comprehensive reform. The HHS Secretary could theoretically coordinate interagency action, but telehealth has not been elevated to a Secretary-level priority.

4. **CBO scoring methodology**: The CBO's assumption that permanent telehealth expansion will generate net new Medicare spending (rather than substitute for more expensive in-person care or reduce downstream emergency utilization) creates a fiscal barrier to permanence that is difficult to overcome without changes to CBO's analytical framework.
