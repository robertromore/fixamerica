# Telehealth and Digital Mental Health: What You Can Do

The future of mental health telehealth depends on whether Congress makes temporary flexibilities permanent, the DEA establishes a workable prescribing framework, and federal agencies invest in closing the digital divide. Citizen engagement--particularly from patients who rely on telehealth, providers who deliver it, and communities that lack access--is critical to moving these reforms from policy proposals to enacted law. The annual cycle of temporary extensions creates recurring moments of political urgency that advocates can use to press for permanent reform.

---

## Individual Actions

### Learn and Stay Informed

- Follow the Center for Connected Health Policy's telehealth policy tracker at <https://www.cchpca.org/> for state and federal telehealth regulatory updates. The tracker is updated continuously and provides the most comprehensive overview of telehealth policy changes across all 50 states.
- Monitor the DEA's telehealth prescribing rulemaking process through the Federal Register at <https://www.federalregister.gov/> using the search term "telemedicine prescribing." Sign up for email alerts when new documents are published.
- Read NAMI's quarterly policy updates on telehealth legislation at <https://www.nami.org/Advocacy/> and subscribe to their email list for action alerts.
- Track your state's PSYPACT, Counseling Compact, and IMLC membership status at <https://psypact.org/> and <https://counselingcompact.org/>. If your state has not joined these compacts, this is a specific advocacy target for state-level action.
- Review the APA App Advisor at <https://www.psychiatry.org/psychiatrists/practice/mental-health-apps> before using or recommending any mental health app. Understanding which apps have evidence and which do not helps you make informed decisions and share credible information.
- Follow the FCC's broadband deployment progress through the Broadband Data Collection at <https://broadbandmap.fcc.gov/> to understand broadband availability in your area and identify gaps.

### Make Personal Changes

- If you use a mental health app, check whether it has published clinical evidence of effectiveness and review its privacy policy before sharing personal mental health data. The Mozilla Foundation's "*Privacy Not Included" project at <https://foundation.mozilla.org/en/privacynotincluded/> provides independent privacy evaluations of popular mental health apps.
- If you receive mental health care via telehealth, document your experience--positive and negative--so you can share it with policymakers and advocacy organizations when asked for patient perspectives. Specific details matter: how far you would have to travel for in-person care, whether telehealth made it possible to continue treatment during a move, whether audio-only was the only option available to you.
- If you live in a rural area or know someone who does, explore whether your community mental health center offers telehealth services, and inquire about device lending and broadband assistance programs. FQHCs and community mental health centers often have technology assistance programs that are underutilized because patients do not know they exist.
- If you are a Medicare beneficiary using telehealth for mental health, be aware that your access depends on annual congressional extensions. Your experience as a beneficiary makes your voice particularly relevant in advocacy to your representatives.
- If you are a veteran, explore the VA's telehealth options through VA Video Connect at <https://mobile.va.gov/app/va-video-connect>. The VA offers tablets to veterans who lack devices. Your use of VA telehealth strengthens the case for permanent civilian telehealth expansion.

### Use Your Voice

- **Contact Your Representatives**: Call your U.S. Senator and House Representative to urge support for permanent Medicare telehealth coverage for behavioral health. The CONNECT for Health Act and the Telehealth Modernization Act are bipartisan bills that need congressional support. Calls are most effective when timed to the annual extension debate (typically October-December).
- **Submit Public Comments**: When the DEA publishes its final proposed rule on telehealth prescribing of controlled substances, submit a public comment through regulations.gov sharing your experience with telehealth prescribing access. Public comments are read by agency staff and influence rulemaking. The DEA received over 38,000 comments on its 2023 proposed rule, demonstrating that public engagement matters.
- **Write Letters to the Editor**: Write to your local newspaper about the importance of telehealth for mental health access, particularly in rural and underserved areas. Local stories carry weight with state and federal legislators. Focus on specific, personal impacts--how far you or someone you know would have to travel without telehealth, how telehealth made treatment possible during a crisis.
- **Share Information**: Share credible data about telehealth mental health access on social media, emphasizing that over 160 million Americans live in Mental Health Professional Shortage Areas and telehealth is often the only viable path to care. Use data from HRSA, KFF, and NAMI to ensure accuracy.
- **Testify at Hearings**: State legislatures regularly hold hearings on PSYPACT, telehealth parity, and Medicaid telehealth coverage. If your state is considering any of these measures, your testimony as a patient, provider, or community member is directly influential. Contact your state representative's office to request an opportunity to testify.

## Community Actions

### Organize Locally

- Form a community coalition for telehealth access by bringing together patients, mental health providers, community health centers, and local elected officials to identify telehealth barriers in your area. Coalitions can map local broadband gaps, identify populations without device access, and develop community-specific advocacy agendas.
- Partner with your local library or community center to offer digital literacy workshops focused on using telehealth platforms, especially for seniors and non-English speakers. Libraries are increasingly serving as digital equity hubs, and many have existing technology training programs that can be expanded to include telehealth.
- Work with your school district to advocate for school-based telehealth mental health programs that connect students with providers via video when in-person clinicians are not available. The Bipartisan Safer Communities Act provides federal funding for school-based mental health services, and school boards make decisions about how that funding is deployed.
- Contact your local Federally Qualified Health Center or Community Mental Health Center to ask about their telehealth capabilities and what barriers they face. These organizations serve the most underserved populations and often have telehealth infrastructure needs (devices for patients, broadband upgrades, staff training) that community advocacy can help address.
- Organize a community telehealth access assessment: survey community members about their broadband access, device availability, digital literacy, and experience with telehealth. Share the results with local, state, and federal officials as evidence of need.

### Build Coalitions

- Connect with local NAMI affiliates (over 600 nationwide) to join existing advocacy campaigns for telehealth permanence. Find your local affiliate at <https://www.nami.org/Find-Your-Local-NAMI>. NAMI affiliates provide training on effective advocacy techniques and coordinate with the national organization's policy agenda.
- Bring together rural health advocates, broadband access advocates, and mental health advocates in your area to present a unified message to state and federal legislators about the connection between broadband infrastructure and mental health access. The intersection of these issues is powerful: broadband is not just a technology issue, it is a healthcare access issue.
- Engage faith communities, veterans' organizations, and senior centers as partners in advocating for telehealth access, as these groups represent populations that benefit significantly from remote mental health care. Veterans' organizations (VFW, American Legion, DAV) are particularly effective advocates because of their bipartisan credibility.
- Partner with local disability advocacy organizations to ensure that telehealth platforms and mental health apps in your community are accessible to people with disabilities. Accessibility testing of telehealth platforms used by local providers can identify specific barriers (lack of screen reader compatibility, absence of captioning, inaccessible user interfaces) that can be addressed through advocacy.
- Connect with your state's Area Health Education Center (AHEC) to support telehealth workforce training programs. AHECs operate in all 50 states and territories and focus on healthcare workforce development in underserved areas.

### Community-Level Broadband and Digital Equity

- Advocate for your community to participate in the NTIA BEAD Program broadband deployment. Contact your state broadband office to learn about deployment plans for your area and advocate for prioritization of areas with mental health professional shortages.
- Support local efforts to establish community broadband networks, particularly in areas where commercial providers have not deployed adequate infrastructure. Municipal broadband and cooperative broadband models have been successful in some rural areas.
- If the Affordable Connectivity Program successor is enacted, help community members enroll. If no successor exists, identify other broadband affordability programs (Lifeline, ISP low-income programs) and help connect community members with these resources.
- Organize device drives to collect and refurbish tablets and smartphones for distribution to community members who need devices for telehealth access. Partner with local community health centers to identify patients who could benefit.

## Political Actions

### Contact Elected Officials

**When to Contact**:

- During the annual congressional debate over extending Medicare telehealth flexibilities (typically October-December before expiration deadlines). This is the highest-leverage moment for advocacy because members of Congress are making active decisions about telehealth policy.
- When the DEA publishes proposed or final rules on telehealth prescribing of controlled substances. DEA rulemaking is open to public comment, and agency staff track the volume and content of comments.
- When your state legislature is considering PSYPACT, Counseling Compact, or other interstate licensure compact legislation. State-level compact adoption is often decided by a small number of committee members, making individual constituent contacts particularly influential.
- When federal broadband funding decisions are being made through NTIA and FCC programs. BEAD Program deployment plans are developed at the state level, and state broadband offices are responsive to community input.
- When the FTC or FDA publish proposed rules or guidance on mental health apps or digital therapeutics. Comment periods for proposed rules are typically 60-90 days.

**What to Say - Template Email**:

```text
Subject: Support Permanent Medicare Telehealth Coverage for Mental Health

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

I am a constituent from [City, State] writing to urge your support for
making Medicare telehealth coverage for mental health services permanent.
[I am a patient who relies on telehealth / I am a provider who delivers
telehealth services / I am a community member who has seen the impact
of telehealth access].

Since the COVID-19 pandemic, telehealth has accounted for over 36% of
all outpatient behavioral health visits, connecting millions of Americans
to care they could not otherwise access. Over 160 million Americans live
in Mental Health Professional Shortage Areas where telehealth is often the
only viable path to a psychiatrist or therapist.

The current system of annual temporary extensions creates uncertainty for
providers and patients. Each year, the possibility that flexibilities will
expire threatens access for millions of Americans. I urge you to
co-sponsor and advance the CONNECT for Health Act (or equivalent permanent
telehealth legislation) to end this cycle and provide stable, permanent
telehealth coverage.

[Optional: Personal story about how telehealth has affected you, your
family, or your community. Be specific: How far would you travel for
in-person care? Did telehealth make treatment possible during a crisis?
Is audio-only the only option available to you?]

Thank you for your consideration and service.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your City, State]
```

**What to Say - Phone Call Script**:

```text
"Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [City]. I'm calling
to ask [Representative/Senator] [Last Name] to support permanent Medicare
telehealth coverage for mental health services. Over 160 million Americans
live in areas without enough mental health providers, and telehealth is
often the only way they can access care. The annual cycle of temporary
extensions creates uncertainty that threatens patient access and provider
investment. I urge the [Representative/Senator] to co-sponsor the CONNECT
for Health Act and support making telehealth flexibilities permanent.
Can you please note my position? Thank you."
```

### Support Legislation

Current or proposed legislation to support:

- **CONNECT for Health Act**: Permanently removes Medicare's geographic and originating-site restrictions for telehealth. Contact Senate HELP Committee and House Energy and Commerce Committee members. This bill has been introduced with bipartisan co-sponsors in every Congress since 2016 and represents the most comprehensive telehealth permanence legislation.
- **Telehealth Modernization Act**: Establishes permanent Medicare telehealth coverage and payment parity. Bipartisan bill with broad provider support.
- **State PSYPACT Legislation**: If your state has not yet enacted PSYPACT, contact your state legislators to urge its adoption. Eight states plus territories still need to join. The PSYPACT Commission at <https://psypact.org/> provides advocacy resources and state-specific legislative tracking.
- **Counseling Compact**: If your state has not yet enacted the Counseling Compact (17 states remain), contact your state legislators to urge adoption. The Counseling Compact Commission at <https://counselingcompact.org/> provides state-level advocacy tools.
- **State Audio-Only Coverage**: If your state Medicaid program does not cover audio-only telehealth for behavioral health (13 states + DC do not), advocate for state legislation mandating coverage. Audio-only coverage is essential for populations without broadband.
- **Broadband Affordability**: Support legislation to fund a successor to the Affordable Connectivity Program. Contact your U.S. Senator and Representative to urge reauthorization of broadband affordability subsidies.

### Electoral Engagement

**Questions to Ask Candidates**:

1. "Do you support making Medicare telehealth coverage for mental health permanent, ending the annual cycle of temporary extensions?"
2. "What is your position on the DEA's proposed telehealth prescribing rules? Do you believe patients should be able to receive prescriptions for psychiatric medications via video visits without a prior in-person visit?"
3. "How do you plan to address the digital divide that prevents rural and low-income Americans from accessing telehealth mental health services?"
4. "Do you support federal incentives for states to join interstate licensure compacts like PSYPACT, so that patients can keep their therapist when they move across state lines?"
5. "Will you support reauthorization of the Affordable Connectivity Program or a successor broadband affordability program?"
6. "What is your position on FDA regulation of mental health apps and AI-powered mental health tools? Do you believe consumers should have access to information about whether the apps they use have clinical evidence?"

**Voting Considerations**:

- Look for candidates who have co-sponsored telehealth legislation or have a record of supporting mental health funding
- Members of the Senate HELP Committee and House Energy and Commerce Committee have the most direct influence on telehealth policy
- State legislative candidates' positions on PSYPACT and licensure compacts are particularly relevant
- Candidates' positions on broadband policy (infrastructure investment, affordability subsidies) directly affect telehealth access
- Candidates who have served in the military or represent communities with large veteran populations may be particularly receptive to telehealth arguments framed around extending the VA model

## Professional Actions

- **For Mental Health Clinicians**: Join your professional association's advocacy efforts for telehealth permanence. The APA, APA, NASW, and ACA all maintain advocacy portals for contacting Congress. If you deliver telehealth, share your clinical experience--outcomes, patient access stories, and challenges--with your association's government relations staff. Consider joining the APA's Telepsychiatry Committee or the APA's Telepsychology Taskforce to contribute to practice guideline development. If you work on a telehealth platform, document and report to your professional association any concerns about clinical pressure, volume expectations, or prescribing pressure.
- **For Primary Care Providers**: Advocate for permanent coverage of collaborative care model (CoCM) telehealth codes that allow psychiatric consultation via telehealth to support primary care mental health treatment. The CoCM model (CPT 99492, 99493, 99494) enables primary care practices to integrate psychiatric expertise via telehealth without requiring direct patient-specialist contact. Project ECHO provides a model for specialist consultation that can be adopted in your practice.
- **For Technology Professionals**: Contribute to open-source telehealth platform development or accessibility improvements. Volunteer technical skills with community mental health organizations to help them implement telehealth infrastructure. If you work in AI or machine learning, consider the ethical implications of AI-powered mental health tools and advocate for responsible development practices. Participate in the development of interoperability standards for telehealth platforms and electronic health records.
- **For Educators**: Advocate for telehealth competency requirements in mental health training program accreditation. If you teach in a clinical program, incorporate telehealth skills training into your curriculum. The VA's telehealth training model and the APA's telepsychiatry practice guidelines provide frameworks for curriculum development. Engage students in telehealth research to build the evidence base.
- **For Researchers**: Conduct studies on telehealth outcomes across diverse populations, modalities (video vs. audio-only), and clinical conditions. The evidence base for telehealth mental health care is strong but has gaps in specific populations (children, older adults, non-English speakers) and modalities (audio-only, asynchronous). Publish findings in peer-reviewed journals to inform policy. Apply for AHRQ and NIMH funding for telehealth outcomes research.
- **For Employers**: Evaluate your employee assistance program (EAP) and mental health benefits for telehealth access. Advocate for including telehealth mental health services in your company's health plan if not already covered. Support broadband access programs in your community. If you are a benefits decision-maker, consider offering dedicated mental health telehealth benefits through platforms like Lyra Health, Spring Health, or Headspace Health in addition to your health plan's general telehealth coverage. Advocate through the Business Group on Health or your industry association for federal telehealth permanence.
- **For Pharmacists**: Familiarize yourself with telehealth prescribing regulations in your state and the evolving DEA rules on telehealth prescribing of controlled substances. Engage with your state pharmacy board on issues related to filling prescriptions written via telehealth, particularly cross-state prescriptions. Advocate through the American Pharmacists Association for clear guidelines on pharmacist responsibilities in the telehealth prescribing chain.

## Advocacy Toolkit

### Key Talking Points

When discussing mental health telehealth with others, emphasize:

1. **Access gap**: Over 160 million Americans live in Mental Health Professional Shortage Areas (HRSA, 2024). Telehealth is often the only way they can reach a psychiatrist or therapist. The national median wait time for a first mental health appointment is 48 days (NAMI, 2023).
2. **Proven effectiveness**: Meta-analyses show that telepsychiatry and video-based psychotherapy produce outcomes comparable to in-person care for depression, anxiety, PTSD, and substance use disorders. This evidence spans over 100 randomized controlled trials.
3. **Expiration threat**: Most Medicare telehealth flexibilities are temporary and must be reauthorized annually by Congress. Without permanent legislation, millions could lose access. The annual cliff creates uncertainty that discourages provider investment.
4. **Rural and equity imperative**: 21% of rural Americans lack broadband access. Audio-only telephone visits serve the populations most in need, and eliminating their coverage would worsen mental health disparities. On tribal lands, 35% lack broadband.
5. **Bipartisan support**: Telehealth permanence has bipartisan appeal because it addresses rural access (a Republican priority) and health equity (a Democratic priority). The CONNECT for Health Act has bipartisan co-sponsors.
6. **Veterans**: The VA operates the nation's largest telemental health program with over 5 million encounters per year and excellent outcomes. Permanent civilian telehealth expansion extends the VA model to all Americans.
7. **Cost of inaction**: Untreated mental illness costs the U.S. an estimated $280 billion annually in lost productivity, disability, healthcare costs, and criminal justice involvement (Milliman, 2024). Telehealth that increases access to treatment can reduce these costs.

### Common Misconceptions to Counter

| Misconception | Reality | Source |
|---------------|---------|--------|
| "Telehealth is a lower standard of care" | Meta-analyses show comparable clinical outcomes for telepsychiatry vs. in-person care for depression, anxiety, PTSD, and substance use | Hubley et al., 2016; Langarizadeh et al., 2017 |
| "Telehealth prescribing leads to more drug diversion" | No statistically significant difference in inappropriate prescribing rates between telehealth and in-person psychiatry when controlling for provider type | Marcus et al., JAMA Psychiatry, 2024 |
| "Telehealth mainly benefits people who already have access" | Rural telehealth utilization is growing faster than urban utilization, and audio-only visits serve populations without broadband | MedPAC, 2024; RUPRI, 2024 |
| "Mental health apps are all regulated by the FDA" | Fewer than 30 mental health digital therapeutics have FDA clearance; over 10,000 apps operate without regulatory review | FDA, 2024; IQVIA, 2023 |
| "Making telehealth permanent will cost too much" | Increased mental health utilization addresses massive unmet need; early evidence shows telehealth reduces downstream costs (ED visits, hospitalizations) | Huskamp et al., JAMA, 2023; Milliman, 2024 |
| "Telehealth is just a pandemic thing" | Telehealth mental health existed before COVID-19 (the VA has used it since the 1990s) and has stabilized at 36% of behavioral health visits post-pandemic | VA, 2024; McKinsey, 2024 |
| "PSYPACT lets unqualified providers practice" | PSYPACT requires doctoral degree, EPPP examination, active license, and no disciplinary actions--often more stringent than individual state requirements | PSYPACT Commission, 2024 |
