# Telehealth and Digital Mental Health: Opposition to Reform

Proposals to permanently expand mental health telehealth face resistance from those who believe that remote care, loosened prescribing rules, and unregulated digital tools compromise patient safety, enable fraud, and undermine the clinical relationship that is the foundation of mental health treatment.

---

## Who Opposes Reform

### Organized Opposition

| Actor | Interest | Resources | Tactics |
|-------|----------|-----------|---------|
| Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) | Prevent controlled substance diversion; maintain enforcement authority over prescribing | Federal law enforcement authority; rulemaking power under Controlled Substances Act | Delayed rulemaking, proposed restrictive prescribing rules, public warnings about telehealth-facilitated diversion |
| Some state medical/licensing boards | Preserve state-level regulatory authority, licensing fee revenue, and consumer protection jurisdiction | State regulatory authority; influence over professional standards | Oppose PSYPACT expansion in holdout states, resist national licensure, require in-state supervision |
| Hospital and health system associations | Protect facility-based care revenue, facility fee billing, and physical infrastructure investments | Large lobbying budgets (AHA: ~$29 million in federal lobbying, 2023) | Advocate for originating-site requirements that keep telehealth within facilities, oppose home-based telehealth expansion |
| MedPAC (Medicare Payment Advisory Commission) | Control Medicare spending growth; ensure value for taxpayer money | Advisory influence on Congress; produces annual recommendations | Recommended against permanent audio-only coverage; raised concerns about telehealth overutilization |
| Some professional societies (selective opposition) | Protect in-person practice models; ensure clinical quality standards | Professional influence, testimony, and position statements | Advocate for in-person visit requirements, express concerns about "lower standard" of telehealth care |
| Pharmaceutical benefit managers (PBMs) | Protect formulary-based drug distribution model from digital therapeutics disruption | Lobbying budgets (PCMA: ~$7 million in federal lobbying, 2023); contractual relationships with insurers | Resist reimbursement pathways for digital therapeutics that could substitute for pharmaceutical interventions |

### Specific Opposition Groups and Their Positions

**DEA Leadership and Career Staff**: The DEA has been the most consistent institutional opponent of unrestricted telehealth prescribing. DEA Administrator Anne Milgram has publicly cited the Cerebral and Done investigations as evidence that telehealth prescribing safeguards are inadequate. Career DEA diversion investigators have testified before Congress that the in-person examination requirement is a critical tool for preventing controlled substance misuse. The DEA's 2024 revised proposed rule, while more flexible than its 2023 predecessor, still requires an in-person visit within 6 months for Schedule II prescribing--a requirement that many telehealth advocates consider unnecessarily burdensome.

**American Hospital Association (AHA)**: The AHA supports telehealth in principle but opposes specific provisions that shift care away from hospital facilities. The AHA has advocated for maintaining originating-site requirements that keep telehealth visits within hospital and health system facilities, where facility fees ($50-100 per visit) can be billed. The AHA has also expressed concerns about "unbounded telehealth expansion" that could reduce hospital outpatient department revenue. With a $29 million annual lobbying budget, the AHA is one of the most powerful healthcare lobbying organizations in Washington.

**State Psychology Licensing Boards (Holdout States)**: The eight states that have not enacted PSYPACT (as of 2024) include several states where the psychology licensing board has actively opposed the compact. Opposition arguments focus on preserving state-specific training requirements, maintaining disciplinary authority, and protecting in-state psychologists from out-of-state competition. In California, which enacted PSYPACT in 2023, the licensing board initially opposed the compact before being overridden by the legislature.

**American Psychoanalytic Association (APsaA)**: While a small organization (approximately 3,500 members), the APsaA has been vocal in questioning the adequacy of telehealth for intensive psychotherapy. A 2023 APsaA position paper argued that psychoanalytic treatment requires the "embodied presence" of the analyst and patient in a shared physical space, and that video-based treatment represents a "diluted" therapeutic relationship. This position, while not representative of the broader psychiatric community, provides intellectual cover for opponents of telehealth permanence.

### Ideological Opposition

- **Law enforcement and drug policy conservatives**: View telehealth prescribing flexibilities as creating new pathways for controlled substance diversion. Point to the Cerebral and Done investigations as evidence that telehealth platforms incentivize overprescribing. Favor strict in-person requirements for Schedule II substances. This perspective is shared by organizations like the National Sheriffs' Association and some district attorneys' associations, which have submitted comments supporting DEA restrictions on telehealth prescribing.
- **Fiscal conservatives**: Worry that permanent Medicare telehealth expansion will increase utilization and spending, as telehealth makes it easier for more people to access care. CBO has scored permanent telehealth expansion as increasing Medicare spending by $2-4 billion per year (CBO, 2024). Groups like the Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget have cited telehealth permanence as an example of unfunded healthcare expansion. The Heritage Foundation has published analyses arguing that telehealth-generated new utilization represents "induced demand" rather than legitimate unmet need.
- **Patient safety purists**: Believe that meaningful psychiatric assessment requires in-person contact, physical examination, and observation of nonverbal cues that are degraded or lost in video and especially audio-only encounters. Argue that telehealth is a compromise accepted during an emergency, not a permanent standard. Some psychiatrists in this camp note that certain diagnoses (e.g., conversion disorder, some psychotic presentations) require neurological examination and physical observation that cannot be conducted remotely.
- **Privacy advocates (selective opposition)**: Support telehealth in principle but oppose the current regulatory framework because it allows mental health apps and platforms to collect and share sensitive mental health data without adequate HIPAA-equivalent protections. Advocate for stronger regulation before expansion. Organizations like the Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) and the Electronic Privacy Information Center (EPIC) have submitted comments to Congress and the FTC arguing that telehealth expansion without privacy reform creates new avenues for data exploitation. The FTC's $7.8 million BetterHelp settlement for sharing therapy data with advertisers validated these concerns.
- **Anti-tech skeptics**: A smaller but vocal group opposes the increasing role of technology in healthcare generally, arguing that telehealth and especially AI-powered mental health tools depersonalize care and substitute corporate algorithms for human clinical judgment. This perspective appears in some patient advocacy spaces and among providers who view the corporatization of mental health care through telehealth platforms as fundamentally inconsistent with the therapeutic relationship.

## Opposition Arguments and Rebuttals

### Argument 1: Telehealth Facilitates Controlled Substance Diversion

**Claim**: Removing the in-person examination requirement for controlled substance prescribing creates new opportunities for drug diversion. Telehealth-only evaluations are too brief and superficial to properly diagnose conditions requiring controlled substances, and online platforms incentivize overprescribing to maximize revenue.

**Evidence Cited**: The DOJ investigation of Cerebral found that the company's clinicians prescribed controlled substances to over 100,000 patients, with some evaluations lasting as little as 10 minutes. The DEA reported a 30% increase in suspicious online prescribing activity for Schedule II stimulants between 2020 and 2023 (DEA, 2024). Done Health's founder was indicted in 2024 on fraud and drug distribution charges. The National Institute on Drug Abuse (NIDA) has noted that stimulant prescriptions increased by 45% between 2020 and 2023, with telehealth prescribing accounting for a growing share.

**Reality**: The Cerebral and Done cases involved specific business practices (pressuring clinicians to prescribe, inadequate clinical oversight, compensation structures tied to prescription volume) that are enforcement problems, not inherent telehealth problems. The same practices would be problematic in an in-person setting. The vast majority of telehealth prescribing is conducted by established psychiatric practices and health systems following standard clinical protocols. A 2024 JAMA study found no statistically significant difference in the rate of inappropriate prescribing between telehealth and in-person psychiatry visits when controlling for provider type and practice setting (Marcus et al., JAMA Psychiatry, 2024). A separate 2024 analysis in the American Journal of Psychiatry found that the increase in stimulant prescriptions correlated with increased ADHD diagnoses across all modalities (including in-person), not specifically with telehealth prescribing.

**Rebuttal**: The appropriate response to bad actors is targeted enforcement against fraudulent practices, not blanket restrictions that prevent millions of patients from accessing legitimate care. The DEA already has tools to identify and shut down problematic prescribers through prescription drug monitoring programs (PDMPs) and suspicious order monitoring. A permanent telehealth prescribing framework should include robust monitoring requirements--mandatory PDMP checks, suspicious activity reporting thresholds, and audit trails--rather than in-person visit mandates that restrict access for all patients to address the behavior of a small number of bad actors.

### Argument 2: Audio-Only Visits Are Clinically Inferior

**Claim**: Audio-only telephone visits for mental health lack the visual cues necessary for proper psychiatric assessment. Clinicians cannot observe affect, psychomotor changes, hygiene, or signs of substance intoxication over the phone. Covering audio-only visits at the same rate as video or in-person visits is paying for a lower standard of care.

**Evidence Cited**: MedPAC's March 2024 report recommended that Congress not permanently extend Medicare coverage of audio-only telehealth for behavioral health, noting that "audio-only visits provide less clinical information than video or in-person encounters." Some psychiatric assessments, particularly initial evaluations and medication management for psychotic disorders, rely heavily on visual observation. A 2023 study in the Journal of Clinical Psychiatry found that psychiatrists reported lower diagnostic confidence for initial evaluations conducted via audio-only compared to video or in-person modalities.

**Reality**: The evidence base for audio-only mental health care is thinner than for video, but emerging research shows positive outcomes for several conditions. A 2023 study in Psychiatric Services found that audio-only follow-up visits for depression management produced comparable PHQ-9 score improvements to video visits over 12 weeks (Connolly et al., Psychiatric Services, 2023). A 2024 VA study found that audio-only cognitive processing therapy for PTSD produced clinically significant symptom reduction in 68% of veterans treated, comparable to the 72% rate for video-delivered treatment. More importantly, for many underserved populations--the elderly, rural residents without broadband, people on tribal lands--audio-only is the only telehealth option. Eliminating audio-only coverage would disproportionately harm the populations with the greatest mental health access needs.

**Rebuttal**: The question is not whether audio-only is equivalent to video in all circumstances--it is not. The question is whether audio-only is better than no care at all, which is the realistic alternative for millions of Americans. A tiered approach that covers audio-only for established patients, follow-up visits, and crisis services while requiring video or in-person for initial evaluations and complex medication management addresses both quality and access concerns. The 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, which received over 5.5 million contacts in its first full year, operates primarily via audio-only telephone--demonstrating that the nation already relies on audio-only modalities for its most critical mental health crisis infrastructure.

### Argument 3: Telehealth Will Increase Medicare Spending

**Claim**: Making telehealth flexibilities permanent will increase total Medicare spending because telehealth makes it easier to access care, generating new utilization rather than substituting for in-person visits. Payment parity means Medicare pays the same amount for a visit that may be shorter, less complex, and conducted at lower overhead.

**Evidence Cited**: CBO has scored permanent Medicare telehealth extension at an estimated additional cost of $2-4 billion per year over 10 years. A 2023 Health Affairs study found that in the first year after telehealth expansion, total behavioral health utilization (telehealth + in-person) increased by 15% among Medicare beneficiaries, suggesting that some telehealth visits were additive rather than substitutive (Mehrotra et al., Health Affairs, 2023). MedPAC has expressed concern that telehealth's reduced barriers (no travel, no time off work) may encourage overutilization.

**Reality**: Increased utilization in a population with massive unmet mental health needs is not waste--it is the system working as intended. The 57.8 million Americans with mental illness include millions who were not receiving any care before telehealth expanded access (SAMHSA, 2023). The NIMH estimates that only 47.2% of adults with any mental illness receive treatment in a given year. Whether the additional spending represents good value depends on whether these new visits improve outcomes and reduce downstream costs (emergency department visits, hospitalizations, disability, criminal justice involvement). Early evidence suggests that telehealth-facilitated buprenorphine treatment reduces opioid overdose deaths and emergency department utilization (Huskamp et al., JAMA, 2023). A 2024 Milliman actuarial analysis found that comprehensive telehealth mental health coverage reduced total cost of care for commercially insured populations by 3-5% over two years, primarily through reduced ED visits and inpatient psychiatric admissions.

**Rebuttal**: The appropriate policy response to cost concerns is not to restrict access but to implement evidence-based utilization management, require outcome measurement, and study whether telehealth-generated utilization is clinically appropriate. Medicare already employs extensive utilization review mechanisms that can be applied to telehealth services. Moreover, the CBO's scoring methodology does not account for downstream cost offsets from improved mental health--reduced ED visits, fewer psychiatric hospitalizations, reduced disability claims, reduced criminal justice costs--because these savings accrue in different budget categories than Medicare Part B.

### Argument 4: Interstate Licensure Compacts Undermine State Consumer Protection

**Claim**: Licensure compacts like PSYPACT allow out-of-state providers to practice in a state without meeting that state's specific training, examination, or supervision requirements. State licensing boards exist to protect consumers, and bypassing them through interstate compacts weakens oversight and accountability.

**Evidence Cited**: Holdout states point to differences in training requirements, scope-of-practice boundaries, and disciplinary procedures across states. Some states require additional supervised hours, specific examinations, or cultural competency training that compact participants may not have completed. In 2023, the California Board of Psychology initially opposed PSYPACT legislation, arguing that California's consumer protection standards exceeded the compact's minimum requirements.

**Reality**: PSYPACT does not eliminate licensure requirements; it creates a mutual recognition framework. Participating psychologists must hold an active license in their home state, have a doctoral degree, pass the EPPP examination, and have no disciplinary actions. The compact includes reciprocal discipline provisions, so a complaint filed in any participating state triggers investigation. The PSYPACT Commission maintains a shared disciplinary database. The compact's standards are, in practice, more stringent than many individual state requirements: the doctoral degree and EPPP requirements exceed the licensure thresholds of some states.

**Rebuttal**: The current system, in which a psychologist must obtain and maintain separate licenses in every state where they have patients, is not a consumer protection measure--it is an administrative barrier that restricts access. The consumer protection rationale is undermined by the reality that state-by-state licensure primarily serves to protect incumbents from competition and generate fee revenue for licensing boards. Compact membership should be expanded, not restricted, and states should work to harmonize training standards rather than using state-specific requirements as barriers to interstate practice. The Nurse Licensure Compact, which has operated since 2000 with 41 member states and no documented decline in nursing care quality, demonstrates that interstate compacts can function safely at scale.

### Argument 5: Mental Health Apps Are Unregulated and Potentially Harmful

**Claim**: The mental health app market is a "Wild West" where thousands of apps make wellness or therapeutic claims without any evidence, clinical oversight, or regulatory review. Some apps may provide harmful advice, fail to recognize crisis situations, or collect and sell sensitive mental health data.

**Evidence Cited**: A 2023 Mozilla Foundation study rated 32 of 37 popular mental health apps as failing to meet minimum privacy standards. The APA App Advisor review found that fewer than 5% of popular mental health apps had published peer-reviewed evidence. The FTC's $7.8 million settlement with BetterHelp (2023) for sharing user therapy data with advertisers illustrated the privacy risks. The National Eating Disorders Association's AI chatbot "Tessa" was shut down in 2023 after providing harmful dietary advice to users with eating disorders. A 2024 BMJ study found that 28% of popular mental health chatbots tested failed to recognize or appropriately respond to simulated suicidal ideation.

**Reality**: These concerns are legitimate. The mental health app market does have significant quality and privacy problems. However, the solution is not to restrict telehealth expansion broadly but to establish a regulatory framework specifically for digital mental health tools. The FDA, FTC, and HHS can collaborate to create tiered oversight that distinguishes between general wellness apps, clinical decision support tools, and prescription digital therapeutics. The UK's NICE Evidence Standards Framework for Digital Health Technologies provides a model for risk-proportionate regulation that does not stifle innovation.

**Rebuttal**: The current regulatory gap is real and should be addressed through FDA guidance, FTC enforcement, and potentially new legislation requiring minimum evidence and privacy standards for apps that claim to treat mental health conditions. This is a separate issue from whether telehealth visits with licensed providers should be permanently covered. Conflating the two--using legitimate app concerns to oppose permanent telehealth coverage for licensed provider visits--is a category error that would restrict access to care to address a regulatory problem that can be solved through targeted oversight.

### Argument 6: Telehealth Corporatizes Mental Health Care

**Claim**: Large telehealth platforms operated by venture capital-backed companies reduce mental health care to a transactional service, prioritizing volume over quality. The therapeutic relationship--which requires time, continuity, and clinical autonomy--is incompatible with the efficiency metrics and growth imperatives of commercial platforms.

**Evidence Cited**: Therapists working for BetterHelp and Talkspace have reported being paid per-message or per-session rates that are significantly below their in-person equivalents, with high patient volumes required to earn a viable income. STAT News reporting on Cerebral documented internal dashboards tracking "patients per hour" as a key performance metric. A 2023 study in the Journal of General Internal Medicine found that telehealth visits were, on average, 4-6 minutes shorter than in-person visits for similar diagnoses.

**Reality**: The corporatization concern has legitimate dimensions. Commercial pressures on telehealth platforms can conflict with clinical best practices, and the business model of some platforms (particularly those with venture-backed growth imperatives) may incentivize quantity over quality. However, this is not unique to telehealth--the same tension exists in group practice, hospital-employed practice, and any setting where providers work within institutional structures. The solution is clinical practice standards (minimum session lengths, caseload limits, supervision requirements) enforced through licensure and accreditation, not restrictions on the telehealth modality itself.

**Rebuttal**: The appropriate response is to establish and enforce clinical quality standards for telehealth delivery through professional licensing boards and accreditation organizations, regardless of the platform or business model. Professional associations (APA, APA, NASW) should publish and enforce practice guidelines for telehealth that include minimum session durations, maximum caseloads, and supervision requirements. These standards would apply to all telehealth practice, whether in a large platform, a small group practice, or a solo practitioner's home office.

## Historical Patterns of Opposition

Opposition to mental health telehealth expansion follows patterns that are common across healthcare reform:

- **Incumbency protection disguised as quality concern**: Health systems and some provider groups oppose telehealth expansion in ways that happen to protect their revenue and competitive position, framing these financial interests as patient safety concerns. The AHA's opposition to home-based telehealth is consistent with its institutional interest in preserving facility-based care revenue.
- **Enforcement orientation over access orientation**: The DEA has consistently approached telehealth prescribing through a diversion-prevention lens rather than an access-expansion lens. Every proposed rule has started from the premise of "how do we prevent misuse" rather than "how do we maximize appropriate access." This framing ensures that restrictions are the default and access expansion requires affirmative justification.
- **Perfect-as-enemy-of-good**: Opponents of audio-only coverage and permanent telehealth expansion implicitly compare telehealth to a fully-resourced in-person visit, when the realistic alternative for many patients is no visit at all. The MedPAC recommendation against permanent audio-only coverage is based on comparing audio-only to video visits, not on comparing audio-only to the absence of any mental health care.
- **Crisis narratives**: Opponents amplify high-profile negative events (the Cerebral investigation, the Done indictment, the NEDA chatbot failure) to build a narrative of telehealth-as-danger, even when these events are outliers that do not represent the quality of the broader telehealth system. This mirrors historical opposition to other healthcare innovations, where single adverse events are used to justify broad restrictions.

## Counter-Strategies

### Political Strategies

- **Build the rural bipartisan coalition**: Mental health telehealth has natural bipartisan appeal because it addresses rural access, a priority for Republican legislators representing rural districts, while also advancing equity, a priority for Democratic legislators. Every rural-state senator is a potential ally. The CONNECT for Health Act's bipartisan co-sponsorship (senators from both parties in every Congress since 2020) demonstrates this potential.
- **Frame telehealth permanence as pro-military**: The VA's telemental health program is the nation's largest and most successful. Framing permanent telehealth expansion as extending the VA model to all Americans leverages bipartisan support for veterans. Veterans service organizations (VFW, American Legion, DAV) are natural coalition partners whose political influence is substantial and nonpartisan.
- **Use the opioid crisis**: Telehealth buprenorphine prescribing was already made permanent in 2023, demonstrating congressional willingness to act when the access need is urgent and visible. Frame other telehealth flexibilities as extending the same logic. The argument is straightforward: if Congress determined that buprenorphine telehealth prescribing is safe enough to be permanent, the same logic applies to other psychiatric medications.
- **Engage the employer community**: Large employers--who pay for much of the nation's mental health care through employee benefits--are natural advocates for telehealth permanence. The Business Group on Health, the National Business Group on Health, and the HR Policy Association can be mobilized to advocate for federal legislation that ensures their employees have stable access to telehealth mental health benefits.

### Communication Strategies

- **Lead with patient stories**: The most effective advocacy for telehealth permanence is the testimony of patients who would lose access to care if flexibilities expire. NAMI and MHA should organize patient advocacy campaigns timed to each congressional extension deadline. A rural veteran who accesses psychiatric care via VA telehealth, a parent whose child receives therapy through a school-based telehealth program, a person in recovery who receives buprenorphine via telehealth--these stories are more persuasive than policy papers.
- **Reframe "new utilization" as "unmet need being met"**: Counter the CBO scoring concern by reframing increased telehealth utilization as millions of untreated Americans finally receiving care, not as wasteful spending. The statistic that only 47.2% of Americans with mental illness receive treatment is the most powerful counter to the "cost" argument.
- **Distinguish between platform problems and telehealth problems**: When opponents cite the Cerebral investigation, respond by distinguishing between a business model problem (pressured prescribing) and a modality problem (telehealth). The solution is better business regulation, not less telehealth. The analogy: when a restaurant chain has a food safety scandal, the solution is better enforcement of health codes, not banning restaurants.

### Policy Design Strategies

- **Tiered audio-only framework**: Design a policy that covers audio-only for established patients, follow-up visits, and crisis services, while requiring video or in-person for initial diagnostic evaluations and complex medication management. This splits the opposition by addressing quality concerns while preserving access.
- **Robust monitoring in exchange for flexibility**: Offer enhanced prescription drug monitoring program (PDMP) requirements and mandatory outcome reporting in exchange for permanent telehealth prescribing authority. This gives the DEA the monitoring tools it wants while removing the in-person barrier. The trade is explicit: more data and oversight in exchange for fewer access restrictions.
- **Separate the app regulation debate from the telehealth coverage debate**: Do not allow legitimate concerns about unregulated apps to delay action on permanent coverage of telehealth visits with licensed providers. Address them through separate legislation or FDA guidance. The political strategy is to prevent opponents from bundling disparate issues to create an unresolvable legislative package.
- **Sunset review rather than sunset expiration**: Propose that permanent telehealth legislation include a mandatory review at year 5 (with data collection and evaluation) rather than an expiration date. This addresses the "we need more data" objection without creating the annual cliff problem.
