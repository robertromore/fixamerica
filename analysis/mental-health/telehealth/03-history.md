# Telehealth and Digital Mental Health: History

## Historical Development

Mental health telehealth has evolved from experimental closed-circuit television demonstrations in the 1950s to a mainstream delivery modality used by millions of Americans. This trajectory has been shaped by technological innovation, federal policy decisions, workforce shortages, and a pandemic that compressed decades of adoption into months.

---

### Early Experimentation (1956-1989): Television Psychiatry and Military Applications

- **First telepsychiatry demonstration (1956)**: The Nebraska Psychiatric Institute conducted the first documented use of closed-circuit television for psychiatric consultations, linking clinicians in Omaha with patients at Norfolk State Hospital 112 miles away. The system used a two-way interactive television link for group therapy, individual consultations, and training. The project was led by Cecil Wittson, M.D., and ran for several years, demonstrating that patients and clinicians could establish a working therapeutic relationship through a television screen.
- **Massachusetts General Hospital experiment (1959-1967)**: MGH established a telepsychiatry link between the hospital and a medical station at Boston's Logan International Airport, providing psychiatric consultations to travelers and airport employees via closed-circuit television. This represented one of the first applications of telepsychiatry in a non-institutional setting.
- **Dartmouth-New Hampshire experiment (1968)**: The Dartmouth Medical School established a microwave-linked telepsychiatry program connecting the medical school with the New Hampshire Hospital, demonstrating that remote psychiatric consultations could be clinically viable. The program operated for over a decade and produced some of the earliest published research on telepsychiatry outcomes.
- **STARPAHC project (1972-1975)**: NASA's Space Technology Applied to Rural Papago Advanced Health Care project deployed satellite-linked telemedicine to the Tohono O'odham (Papago) reservation in Arizona, including mental health consultations. The project demonstrated the potential of telecommunications to serve geographically isolated and underserved populations, prefiguring later applications on tribal lands.
- **Military and VA early adoption**: The Department of Defense and Veterans Affairs were early adopters, using satellite-based telemedicine to provide psychiatric support to personnel in remote locations. The VA's telemental health program, formalized in the 1990s, would become the largest telehealth mental health system in the world. Military interest was driven by the need to support personnel stationed at bases without on-site psychiatric services, particularly during the Cold War.
- **Technology limitations**: Analog television systems were expensive, required dedicated infrastructure, and produced poor-quality images. A single telepsychiatry installation in the 1960s could cost the equivalent of several hundred thousand dollars in today's currency. Adoption remained confined to academic demonstrations and government-funded pilots. The lack of affordable, scalable telecommunications infrastructure meant that telepsychiatry remained a curiosity rather than a viable service delivery model.
- **Early outcome research**: Despite technological constraints, early studies produced encouraging results. Wittson and Benschoter (1972) published findings showing that telepsychiatry consultations produced diagnostic agreement rates comparable to in-person evaluations. However, the small scale and methodological limitations of these studies meant they had little impact on mainstream psychiatric practice.

### Infrastructure Building (1990-2007): Internet, Medicare Rules, and the Ryan Haight Act

- **Telecommunications Act of 1996**: Federal legislation promoted broadband infrastructure development, laying the groundwork for internet-based telehealth. The Universal Service Fund's Rural Health Care Program began subsidizing telecommunications for rural healthcare providers. The act's requirement that telecommunications carriers provide service to rural areas at rates comparable to urban areas created a foundation for telehealth infrastructure, though actual broadband deployment lagged for years.
- **Internet-based videoconferencing emerges (late 1990s)**: The development of affordable internet-based videoconferencing systems (including early platforms like Polycom and later Skype, launched in 2003) made telepsychiatry technically accessible to a broader range of providers. Video quality improved dramatically while costs fell, removing the requirement for dedicated television infrastructure.
- **Medicare telehealth coverage (1999)**: The Balanced Budget Act of 1997 authorized limited Medicare reimbursement for telehealth services, but imposed strict geographic restrictions (rural Health Professional Shortage Areas only) and originating-site requirements (patient must be at an approved facility, not at home). Mental health was among the first covered specialties. The Medicare, Medicaid, and SCHIP Benefits Improvement and Protection Act of 2000 (BIPA) modestly expanded the list of eligible telehealth services but maintained the geographic and site restrictions.
- **VA telemental health expansion (2003-2007)**: The VA dramatically expanded its telemental health program, particularly for PTSD treatment of Iraq and Afghanistan veterans. By 2007, the VA was conducting over 100,000 telemental health encounters per year, making it the world's largest telepsychiatry system (VA Office of Telehealth Services, 2008). The program leveraged the VA's unique position as a single integrated health system not subject to state licensure barriers or Medicare's geographic restrictions--advantages that the civilian healthcare system did not share.
- **State licensure barriers**: As internet-based videoconferencing became feasible, the requirement that practitioners hold a license in the state where the patient is physically located emerged as a major barrier. Each state maintained independent licensure requirements with no reciprocity. A psychologist who wanted to treat patients in 10 states needed 10 separate licenses, each requiring its own application, fees (typically $200-500 per state), background checks, and continuing education documentation.
- **Telephone-based counseling services (2000s)**: Even before video telehealth became widespread, telephone-based crisis lines and counseling services expanded. The National Suicide Prevention Lifeline (now the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline) launched in 2005, and employer assistance programs increasingly offered telephone counseling. These services established a precedent for non-face-to-face mental health care but operated outside the formal telehealth regulatory framework.
- **Ryan Haight Online Pharmacy Consumer Protection Act (2008)**: Following the death of Ryan Haight, a teenager who obtained prescription drugs through an online pharmacy without a legitimate medical evaluation, Congress passed legislation requiring at least one in-person medical evaluation before a practitioner could prescribe controlled substances via the internet. While intended to combat online pharmacy fraud, the law created a major barrier to legitimate telepsychiatry prescribing of medications like stimulants, benzodiazepines, and buprenorphine. The DEA was tasked with creating a "special registration" process for telemedicine prescribers but did not finalize regulations for over 15 years. The Ryan Haight Act was passed with bipartisan support and little opposition from the psychiatric community, which at the time did not foresee the scale of telehealth prescribing that would emerge a decade later.

### Slow Growth and Evidence Building (2008-2019): Research, Parity, and Compacts

- **Evidence base development (2010-2019)**: A growing body of peer-reviewed research demonstrated the clinical equivalence of telepsychiatry to in-person care. Key meta-analyses by Hilty et al. (2013), Hubley et al. (2016), and Langarizadeh et al. (2017) showed comparable outcomes for depression, anxiety, PTSD, and substance use disorders. The American Psychiatric Association published telepsychiatry practice guidelines in 2018. By the end of the decade, the evidence base included over 100 randomized controlled trials and systematic reviews supporting the effectiveness of video-based telepsychiatry.
- **VA as the national leader (2010-2019)**: The VA continued to expand its telemental health program, reaching over 2 million encounters per year by 2019. The VA MISSION Act of 2018 authorized veterans to receive VA telehealth services regardless of their state of residence, effectively creating a national telehealth framework within the VA system. The VA also invested in tablet distribution programs (the VA Video Connect program), providing iPads to veterans without suitable devices, a model later replicated in civilian programs.
- **State telehealth parity laws (2012-2019)**: States began passing laws requiring commercial insurers to cover telehealth services and, in some cases, pay the same rate as in-person visits. By 2019, 42 states had some form of telehealth coverage mandate, but only 10 required full payment parity (Center for Connected Health Policy, 2019). The variation among state parity laws created a complex compliance environment for multi-state telehealth providers.
- **Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act enforcement (2008-2019)**: While not directly about telehealth, the 2008 parity law's requirement that insurers cover mental health on par with physical health created pressure for coverage of telemental health as a modality for delivering those covered services. Enforcement remained inconsistent, but the parity principle established the expectation that barriers to mental health service delivery--including modality restrictions--should not be more restrictive than those for medical/surgical services.
- **PSYPACT established (2015)**: The Psychology Interjurisdictional Compact was adopted by the Association of State and Provincial Psychology Boards (ASPPB) in 2015, creating a framework for psychologists to practice telepsychology and conduct temporary in-person practice across state lines. Georgia became the first state to enact PSYPACT in 2016. By late 2019, only 14 states had joined. The compact required psychologists to hold an active EPPP-qualified license, have a doctoral degree, and maintain no disciplinary history--standards designed to address state consumer protection concerns.
- **Interstate Medical Licensure Compact (2015)**: Parallel to PSYPACT, the Interstate Medical Licensure Compact was established to create an expedited licensure pathway for physicians, including psychiatrists, to practice across state lines. By 2019, 29 states had joined. While the IMLC did not create a single national license, it significantly streamlined the multi-state licensure process for psychiatrists.
- **Digital therapeutics emergence (2017-2019)**: The FDA cleared its first prescription digital therapeutic for a substance use disorder (reSET by Pear Therapeutics) in 2017, establishing a regulatory pathway for software-based mental health treatments. The Digital Therapeutics Alliance was founded in 2017 to establish industry standards. Pear Therapeutics received subsequent clearances for reSET-O (opioid use disorder, 2018) and Somryst (insomnia, 2020), positioning itself as the market leader in mental health PDTs.
- **BetterHelp and Talkspace launch and grow (2012-2019)**: Consumer telehealth platforms BetterHelp (founded 2013) and Talkspace (founded 2012) grew rapidly, offering text-based, phone, and video therapy sessions through subscription models. By 2019, BetterHelp had facilitated over 65 million therapy sessions. These platforms demonstrated consumer demand for remote mental health care but also raised concerns about clinical quality, therapist working conditions, and the commodification of therapy.
- **Pre-pandemic telehealth mental health utilization**: Despite a decade of evidence and policy development, telehealth accounted for only approximately 1% of outpatient mental health visits as of early 2020 (FAIR Health, 2021). Medicare's geographic restrictions, the Ryan Haight Act's in-person requirement, inconsistent state policies, and provider and patient inertia all constrained growth. The decade of slow growth demonstrated that favorable evidence alone cannot overcome structural regulatory barriers.

### The COVID-19 Transformation (2020-2021): Emergency Expansion

- **Public Health Emergency (PHE) declaration (March 2020)**: The declaration of the COVID-19 PHE triggered a cascade of telehealth regulatory changes that transformed mental health care delivery virtually overnight. The speed and scope of these changes were unprecedented in healthcare regulation.
- **CMS waivers (March 6, 2020)**: Under Section 1135 waiver authority, CMS waived Medicare's geographic and originating-site restrictions, allowing beneficiaries to receive telehealth services--including mental health--from their homes anywhere in the country. CMS also added over 135 services to the Medicare telehealth-eligible list and permitted audio-only telephone visits for behavioral health. The waivers were initially expected to last weeks or months; they have now been extended for over five years.
- **DEA waiver (March 16, 2020)**: The DEA suspended the Ryan Haight Act's in-person examination requirement, allowing practitioners to prescribe controlled substances, including stimulants and buprenorphine, via telehealth without a prior in-person visit for the first time. This waiver was issued just 10 days after the CMS waivers, reflecting the urgency of ensuring continuity of psychiatric care during lockdowns.
- **HIPAA enforcement discretion (March 17, 2020)**: HHS Office for Civil Rights announced it would not enforce HIPAA penalties for the use of non-compliant communication platforms (FaceTime, Skype, Zoom consumer version) for telehealth during the PHE, removing a major technical barrier. This allowed providers to begin telehealth immediately rather than waiting months to procure and implement HIPAA-compliant platforms.
- **Utilization explosion**: Telehealth mental health claims increased by 3,060% between February and April 2020 (FAIR Health, 2021). By mid-2020, telehealth accounted for over 50% of all outpatient behavioral health visits, a rate it had never previously exceeded 1%. The speed of adoption exceeded all projections--what telehealth advocates had predicted would take 10-15 years of gradual adoption occurred in 10 weeks.
- **State emergency actions (2020-2021)**: All 50 states issued executive orders or emergency regulations expanding telehealth during the pandemic. Common provisions included waiving in-state licensure requirements, mandating telehealth insurance coverage, and permitting audio-only visits. Over 400 state-level telehealth policy changes were enacted in 2020 alone (Center for Connected Health Policy, 2021). The variations among state emergency orders created a complex, ever-shifting regulatory environment for multi-state providers.
- **Buprenorphine prescribing transformation**: The waiver of the Ryan Haight Act, combined with the elimination of the "X-waiver" requirement for buprenorphine prescribing (finalized permanently in the Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2023), dramatically expanded access to medication-assisted treatment for opioid use disorder. Telehealth buprenorphine prescribing increased by over 1,500% (SAMHSA, 2022). Research published in JAMA Internal Medicine (2022) found that telehealth-initiated buprenorphine treatment had retention rates comparable to in-person initiation at 6 months.
- **Disparities in pandemic telehealth adoption**: Despite the overall explosion in utilization, adoption was uneven. A 2021 Health Affairs study found that Black and Hispanic Medicare beneficiaries were significantly less likely to use video telehealth and more likely to rely on audio-only visits than white beneficiaries. Rural Medicare beneficiaries used telehealth at approximately half the rate of urban beneficiaries in the first months of the pandemic. These disparities highlighted the digital divide's impact on the telehealth transition.

### Post-Pandemic Stabilization and Debate (2022-Present): Permanence, Regulation, and Risk

- **Congressional extensions (2022-2024)**: As the COVID-19 PHE ended in May 2023, Congress repeatedly extended Medicare telehealth flexibilities through annual appropriations legislation, most recently through December 31, 2025 (Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2024). However, these extensions are temporary, creating annual uncertainty. The repeated one-year extensions reflect congressional ambivalence: strong bipartisan support for telehealth access combined with CBO scoring concerns about the long-term cost of permanent expansion.
- **DEA rulemaking process (2023-2024)**: The DEA issued a proposed rule in February 2023 that would have required an in-person evaluation for controlled substance telehealth prescribing within 30 days of the initial telehealth prescription. After intense public comment (over 38,000 submissions), the DEA withdrew the proposal and issued a revised proposed rule in 2024 proposing a more flexible 6-month window for Schedule II substances and no in-person requirement for Schedules III-V. The protracted rulemaking process illustrates the difficulty of balancing the DEA's enforcement mission with the public health imperative of telehealth access.
- **Telehealth startup controversies (2022-2024)**: Companies like Cerebral and Done faced federal investigations for alleged overprescribing of stimulants via telehealth. The DOJ and DEA alleged that some telehealth platforms pressured clinicians to prescribe controlled substances quickly to meet business targets. Cerebral halted most controlled substance prescribing in 2022. Done Health's founder was indicted in 2024 on federal charges. These cases became central to the debate over telehealth prescribing safeguards and were cited by the DEA in its rulemaking as evidence of the need for in-person requirements.
- **PSYPACT rapid expansion (2021-2024)**: PSYPACT grew from 18 member states in 2020 to 42 states plus DC by 2024, driven by pandemic momentum and bipartisan state legislative support. It became the fastest-growing professional licensure compact in U.S. history. The Counseling Compact, launched in 2022, achieved 33 states by 2024, demonstrating that the compact model could be replicated across mental health professions.
- **Pear Therapeutics bankruptcy (April 2023)**: The leading prescription digital therapeutics company ceased operations, unable to sustain its business model despite FDA-cleared products. Low payer adoption and reimbursement challenges were cited as primary causes, casting doubt on the commercial viability of the FDA-regulated digital therapeutics model. The bankruptcy sent a chilling signal through the digital therapeutics industry: Akili Interactive went private in late 2023 after its stock collapsed, and several other DTx companies pivoted away from the FDA-cleared PDT model toward employer-direct or consumer models.
- **Audio-only policy debate (2023-2024)**: MedPAC recommended that Congress not permanently extend Medicare coverage of audio-only telehealth for mental health, citing concerns about clinical quality and fraud risk. Patient advocates and rural health groups strongly opposed this recommendation, arguing that audio-only is the only telehealth modality available to many underserved populations. The debate pits MedPAC's mandate to control Medicare spending against the access imperative for populations without broadband.
- **988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (2022-present)**: The launch of the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline in July 2022 created a national mental health crisis response infrastructure that relies heavily on telephone (audio-only) and text-based communication. The 988 system received over 5.5 million contacts in its first full year of operation (SAMHSA, 2024), underscoring the importance of non-video modalities in crisis mental health care.

## Lessons from History

### Pattern 1: Crises Drive Telehealth Adoption; Inertia Follows

Every major telehealth expansion has been crisis-driven--the need to serve remote VA patients, workforce shortages in rural areas, and above all the COVID-19 pandemic. Between crises, regulatory barriers, institutional inertia, and reimbursement limitations slow adoption to a crawl. The pre-pandemic decade of positive evidence produced only 1% telehealth utilization. This pattern suggests that without permanent legislative action, post-pandemic telehealth gains will erode as temporary flexibilities expire and urgency fades. The history of telemedicine is littered with promising pilot programs that were discontinued when grant funding ended or emergency authority expired.

### Pattern 2: Regulation Lags Technology by a Decade or More

The Ryan Haight Act was enacted in 2008, but the DEA did not issue its first proposed rule for the telemedicine special registration process until 2020--during a pandemic that forced emergency suspension of the law's requirements. The FDA's digital health regulatory framework has been under development since 2013, yet the vast majority of mental health apps still operate in a regulatory vacuum. This persistent regulatory lag creates a cycle of emergency action followed by extended uncertainty. The pattern is structural: regulatory agencies are designed for deliberative rulemaking, but technology markets move at commercial speed.

### Pattern 3: The VA as a Policy Laboratory

The VA has consistently been the earliest adopter and largest operator of telehealth mental health services. Its experience demonstrates what federal commitment can achieve: the VA now conducts over 5 million telemental health encounters annually. The VA's tablet distribution program, its use of asynchronous care modalities, and its integration of telehealth into PTSD treatment protocols have all served as models for civilian healthcare. However, the VA operates outside the constraints of state licensure, private insurance, and Medicare's geographic restrictions, which limits the transferability of its model to the broader healthcare system. The VA MISSION Act's authorization of national VA telehealth effectively created a parallel federal system that demonstrates what could exist for all Americans if similar structural barriers were removed.

### Pattern 4: Market Enthusiasm Outpaces Sustainability

The digital therapeutics market illustrates a recurring pattern in health technology: significant investor enthusiasm and FDA clearance do not guarantee commercial viability. Pear Therapeutics raised over $400 million in funding and received multiple FDA clearances, but went bankrupt because payers would not reimburse its products at a sustainable rate. Akili Interactive followed a similar trajectory. This suggests that FDA regulation alone is insufficient without a parallel reimbursement pathway. The pattern applies beyond digital therapeutics: venture capital-funded telehealth startups that achieved rapid growth during the pandemic (Cerebral, Done, Brightside) subsequently faced financial difficulties, investigations, or both when the growth-at-all-costs model collided with clinical and regulatory realities.

### Pattern 5: Access Gains Are Fragile Without Structural Change

The pandemic demonstrated that removing regulatory barriers produces dramatic access improvements. But those improvements are contingent on the continuation of temporary waivers. The history of telehealth is one of access expanding during emergencies and contracting when emergencies end. The post-pandemic period represents a critical window: either the temporary gains are locked in through permanent legislation, or they erode through a combination of waiver expiration, regulatory retrenchment, and loss of political urgency. The buprenorphine prescribing permanence enacted in 2023 provides a model, but it remains the only telehealth flexibility to have been made permanent through legislation.
