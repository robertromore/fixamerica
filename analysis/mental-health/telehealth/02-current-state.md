# Telehealth and Digital Mental Health: Current State

## Telehealth Utilization

Mental health is the dominant clinical use case for telehealth in the United States. While telehealth utilization across all medical specialties has stabilized well below pandemic peaks, behavioral health has retained the highest sustained adoption rate of any clinical category. The permanence of behavioral health telehealth adoption--even as other specialties reverted closer to pre-pandemic patterns--reflects the unique suitability of talk-based therapies for remote delivery and the severity of the mental health workforce shortage that makes telehealth a necessity rather than a convenience.

### Telehealth Utilization by Clinical Category (2024)

| Category | Telehealth Share of Visits | Change from Pre-Pandemic | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Behavioral health (all) | 36% of outpatient visits | +3,400% from 2019 baseline (~1%) | McKinsey, 2024 |
| Psychiatry | 50% of outpatient visits | +4,900% from 2019 baseline | APA Practice Survey, 2024 |
| Substance use disorder treatment | 30% of outpatient visits | +2,800% from 2019 baseline | SAMHSA, 2024 |
| Primary care | 10% of outpatient visits | +900% from 2019 baseline | McKinsey, 2024 |
| All specialties (weighted average) | 17% of outpatient visits | +1,600% from 2019 baseline | FAIR Health, 2024 |

### Telehealth Modality Breakdown for Behavioral Health (2024)

| Modality | Share of Telehealth Behavioral Health Visits | Clinical Applications |
|---|---|---|
| Live video (synchronous) | 60-65% | Psychotherapy, psychiatric medication management, group therapy |
| Audio-only telephone | 30-35% | Crisis intervention, medication check-ins, therapy for patients without broadband |
| Asynchronous (store-and-forward) | 3-5% | Psychiatric consultations, screening questionnaire review, provider-to-provider consultation |
| Remote patient monitoring | 1-2% | Mood tracking, medication adherence monitoring, wearable-based behavioral data |

*Source: CMS Office of the Actuary, 2023; APA Practice Survey, 2024*

The dominance of live video reflects both clinical preference and reimbursement incentives--video visits are reimbursed at full parity with in-person visits in most payer systems, while audio-only reimbursement remains inconsistent. However, the 30-35% audio-only share is significant and represents a population that disproportionately includes older adults, rural residents, and people without broadband access. Asynchronous modalities remain underutilized relative to their potential, partly because reimbursement pathways for store-and-forward behavioral health consultations are underdeveloped outside the VA system.

### Geographic Variation

Telehealth mental health utilization varies substantially by region, reflecting both broadband infrastructure and state regulatory environments:

- **Highest telehealth utilization**: States with permanent telehealth parity laws and strong broadband infrastructure (Massachusetts, Colorado, Washington, Oregon) report telehealth shares of behavioral health visits exceeding 40%.
- **Lowest telehealth utilization**: States with restrictive Medicaid telehealth coverage and limited broadband (Mississippi, West Virginia, Alabama) report telehealth shares below 20%.
- **Frontier counties**: In counties with fewer than 6 people per square mile, telehealth often represents the only available mental health service. However, broadband limitations mean many of these visits are audio-only, and some frontier areas lack any telehealth infrastructure.

## Regulatory Framework

### Federal Telehealth Flexibilities (Status as of January 2025)

The COVID-19 pandemic prompted a cascade of federal regulatory changes. Most remain temporary.

| Flexibility | Authority | Current Status | Expiration |
|---|---|---|---|
| Medicare geographic restriction waiver (patient can be at home) | CMS via 1135 waiver, extended by Congress | Active (temporary) | December 31, 2025 |
| Medicare originating site restriction waiver | CMS via 1135 waiver, extended by Congress | Active (temporary) | December 31, 2025 |
| Audio-only allowed for mental health (Medicare) | CMS regulation | Active (temporary) | December 31, 2025 |
| Federally Qualified Health Center / Rural Health Clinic as distant site | Congressional extension | Active (temporary) | December 31, 2025 |
| Ryan Haight Act in-person requirement waiver | DEA temporary rule | Active (temporary) | December 31, 2025 |
| HIPAA enforcement discretion for telehealth platforms | HHS OCR | Expired (returned to standard enforcement) | May 2023 |
| Prescribing buprenorphine without in-person visit | DEA + SAMHSA | Permanent (Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2023) | N/A (permanent) |
| Medicare payment parity for telehealth | Congressional action | Active (temporary) | December 31, 2025 |
| Medicare telehealth for initial psychiatric evaluations | CMS regulation | Active (temporary) | December 31, 2025 |

*Source: Congressional Research Service, "Telehealth and Medicare," R46239, updated 2024; DEA Temporary Rule 88 FR 12875, 2023*

The temporary nature of nearly all flexibilities except buprenorphine prescribing is the defining structural feature of the current regulatory landscape. Congress has extended these provisions four times since the PHE ended (in appropriations acts for FY2022, FY2023, FY2024, and FY2025), each time for approximately one year. This annual cycle creates a predictable period of uncertainty each fall, as providers, patients, and health systems wait to learn whether their telehealth services will continue to be reimbursed.

### Ryan Haight Act and DEA Prescribing Rules

The Ryan Haight Online Pharmacy Consumer Protection Act of 2008 (21 U.S.C. 829(e)) requires a practitioner to conduct at least one in-person medical evaluation before prescribing a controlled substance via the internet. The DEA waived this requirement during the COVID-19 public health emergency and has since issued a series of temporary extensions.

**DEA Proposed Permanent Rule (2024)**:

| Element | Proposed Requirement | Stakeholder Reaction |
|---|---|---|
| Schedule III-V substances (e.g., benzodiazepines, sleep aids) | May prescribe via telehealth without in-person visit for initial 6-month supply; re-evaluation required annually | Generally supported |
| Schedule II substances (e.g., stimulants for ADHD) | Requires at least one in-person visit within 6 months of initial telehealth prescription | Opposed by telehealth providers; supported by DEA, some medical groups |
| Buprenorphine for opioid use disorder | No in-person requirement (codified permanently in 2023) | Broadly supported |
| Telemedicine Special Registration | New DEA registration category for telehealth-only prescribers | Mixed; concerns about administrative burden |
| PDMP check mandate | Mandatory check of state Prescription Drug Monitoring Program before each telehealth controlled substance prescription | Broadly supported; implementation concerns in states with fragmented PDMP systems |

*Source: DEA Notice of Proposed Rulemaking, 89 FR 2024; American Psychiatric Association comment letter, 2024*

The DEA's 2023 initial proposed rule would have required an in-person visit within 30 days of the first telehealth prescription of any controlled substance. The proposal generated over 38,000 public comments--one of the largest volumes in DEA rulemaking history--with the overwhelming majority opposing the 30-day requirement as too restrictive. The 2024 revised proposal's 6-month window for Schedule II substances and no in-person requirement for Schedules III-V represents a significant concession, but the rule remains proposed, not final, as of early 2025.

### State Telehealth Regulatory Landscape

States retain significant authority over telehealth regulation through professional licensure, scope-of-practice laws, Medicaid coverage decisions, and commercial insurance mandates.

| Regulatory Area | Number of States | Trend | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Require commercial insurers to cover telehealth | 50 states + DC | Universal (post-pandemic) | Center for Connected Health Policy, 2024 |
| Require telehealth payment parity (same rate as in-person) | 40 states + DC | Growing | Center for Connected Health Policy, 2024 |
| Cover audio-only telehealth in Medicaid | 37 states + DC | Growing | Kaiser Family Foundation, 2024 |
| Enacted PSYPACT (Psychology Interjurisdictional Compact) | 42 states + DC | Growing rapidly | PSYPACT Commission, 2024 |
| Enacted Interstate Medical Licensure Compact | 41 states + DC | Growing | IMLC, 2024 |
| Enacted Counseling Compact | 33 states + DC | Growing | Counseling Compact Commission, 2024 |
| Require informed consent specific to telehealth | 28 states | Growing | CCHP, 2024 |
| Require established provider-patient relationship for telehealth prescribing | 15 states | Stable | CCHP, 2024 |

**Key state-level developments (2023-2024)**:

- **California**: Enacted AB 32 (2023), permanently codifying Medi-Cal telehealth coverage including audio-only for behavioral health, and requiring commercial insurers to maintain telehealth coverage at in-person parity rates.
- **Texas**: Joined PSYPACT in 2023 after years of resistance from the state psychology licensing board, removing a major gap in the compact's coverage of the nation's second-most-populous state.
- **Florida**: Enacted the Telehealth Prescribing Act (2024), creating state-specific requirements for telehealth prescribing of controlled substances that exceed federal requirements, including mandatory video (not audio-only) for initial prescriptions.
- **New York**: Enacted telehealth parity legislation in 2023 but excluded audio-only from the parity requirement, creating a coverage gap for Medicaid enrollees who rely on telephone visits.

### The Regulatory Gap

| Category | Approximate Number | FDA Oversight | Evidence Requirements | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Consumer mental health apps (wellness) | 10,000+ | None (general wellness exemption) | None | IQVIA, 2023 |
| Clinical decision support tools | ~500 | Limited (exempted under 21st Century Cures Act) | Varies | FDA, 2024 |
| Software as a Medical Device (SaMD) | ~200 mental health-related | Full FDA review | Clinical trials required | FDA, 2024 |
| Prescription Digital Therapeutics (PDTs) | Fewer than 30 cleared | Full FDA review | Randomized controlled trials | Digital Therapeutics Alliance, 2024 |

The gap between the roughly 10,000 consumer apps and the fewer than 30 FDA-cleared products represents a vast unregulated space where products may make implicit or explicit therapeutic claims without any evidence requirement, quality control, or safety oversight. The FDA's 2023 draft guidance on Clinical Decision Support Software attempted to clarify boundaries but left significant ambiguity about when a "wellness" app crosses the line into making medical claims that would trigger regulatory review.

### AI-Powered Mental Health Tools

The emergence of generative AI has added a new dimension to the digital mental health landscape:

- **AI therapy chatbots**: Companies like Woebot (CBT-based), Wysa (CBT and dialectical behavior therapy), and Youper (emotion-tracking with CBT elements) use natural language processing to deliver structured therapeutic interventions. These tools operate in a regulatory gray area--they interact with users in ways that resemble therapy but are marketed as "wellness" tools to avoid FDA oversight.
- **Large language model applications**: Following the release of GPT-4 and other large language models in 2023, dozens of startups launched AI-powered "therapist" products using general-purpose language models fine-tuned on therapeutic dialogue. The FDA issued a safety communication in late 2023 warning that AI chatbots providing diagnostic or therapeutic advice may be subject to medical device regulation.
- **Clinical AI tools**: AI-assisted diagnostic tools that analyze speech patterns, facial expressions, or text inputs to screen for depression, anxiety, or suicide risk are under development at major academic medical centers and technology companies. These tools are more clearly within FDA jurisdiction but raise significant concerns about algorithmic bias and the reliability of AI-based mental health assessment across diverse populations.

## The Digital Divide

### Broadband Access and Telehealth Readiness

| Metric | Urban | Rural | Tribal Lands | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Fixed broadband access (25/3 Mbps+) | 97% | 79% | 65% | FCC Broadband Data Collection, 2023 |
| Fixed broadband access (100/20 Mbps+) | 92% | 58% | 42% | FCC Broadband Data Collection, 2023 |
| Smartphone ownership | 90% | 80% | 69% | Pew Research Center, 2024 |
| Home computer/tablet ownership | 82% | 70% | 58% | Census Bureau American Community Survey, 2023 |
| Digital literacy (self-reported "confident" using video calls) | 78% | 62% | 51% | Pew Research Center, 2024 |

The FCC's updated broadband benchmark of 100/20 Mbps (adopted in 2024, replacing the decade-old 25/3 Mbps standard) reveals a more severe digital divide than older measures suggested. At the new benchmark, 42% of tribal land residents and 42% of rural Americans lack sufficient broadband for reliable video telehealth. The Bipartisan Infrastructure Law's $42.45 billion Broadband Equity, Access, and Deployment (BEAD) Program is deploying funding to states, but construction timelines extend to 2028-2030, meaning the gap will persist for the near term.

### Affordable Connectivity Program Expiration

The FCC's Affordable Connectivity Program (ACP), which provided $30/month broadband subsidies to low-income households, expired in June 2024 after Congress failed to reauthorize funding. At its peak, 23 million households were enrolled, including an estimated 4-5 million households using the subsidy to support telehealth access. The program's expiration removed a key bridge between broadband infrastructure investment (which will take years to deploy) and immediate telehealth access needs. No replacement program has been enacted as of early 2025.

### Medicaid Telehealth Coverage Variation

The six states with restrictive Medicaid telehealth coverage (as of 2024) tend to be states that did not expand Medicaid under the ACA, have smaller behavioral health budgets, and have not enacted state-level telehealth parity laws. This creates a compounding disadvantage: residents of these states are more likely to be uninsured or underinsured, more likely to live in Mental Health HPSAs, and less likely to have telehealth coverage through Medicaid.

### Commercial Insurance Landscape

Commercial payers have generally maintained telehealth coverage post-pandemic but with increasing variation:

- **Large national insurers** (UnitedHealthcare, Anthem/Elevance, Cigna, Aetna): All cover telehealth behavioral health at in-person parity rates, consistent with state mandates. Some have launched proprietary telehealth platforms or partnerships (e.g., UnitedHealthcare's Optum telehealth network).
- **Employer-sponsored plans**: Employer plans increasingly include dedicated mental health telehealth benefits through platforms like Lyra Health, Spring Health, and Headspace Health (formerly Ginger). A 2024 Mercer survey found that 78% of large employers (500+ employees) offer a dedicated mental health telehealth benefit in addition to their health plan's general telehealth coverage.
- **Self-insured plans**: Self-insured employer plans (covering approximately 65% of employer-insured workers) are exempt from state telehealth mandates under ERISA preemption, creating a coverage gap that only federal legislation can address.

### Digital Therapeutics Reimbursement

The reimbursement landscape for prescription digital therapeutics remains underdeveloped:

- **Medicare**: No established coverage pathway for PDTs under Medicare Part B or Part D. CMS has not issued a National Coverage Determination for any digital therapeutic.
- **Medicaid**: A small number of state Medicaid programs (notably North Carolina and Connecticut) have piloted coverage of specific PDTs, but no standardized approach exists.
- **Commercial insurance**: Approximately 40% of commercial insurers covered at least one PDT as of 2023 (Digital Therapeutics Alliance, 2023), but coverage is typically limited to specific products and negotiated on a case-by-case basis.
- **CPT codes**: The AMA has assigned temporary CPT codes for some digital therapeutics services, but permanent codes have not been established for most product categories.

### Provider Burnout and Telehealth

While telehealth has expanded access and reduced no-show rates, it has also introduced new sources of provider strain:

- **"Zoom fatigue"**: A 2024 APA survey found that 54% of psychiatrists and psychologists offering telehealth reported increased fatigue from sustained video sessions compared to in-person care. The cognitive load of compensating for reduced nonverbal cues in video calls is a contributing factor.
- **Caseload inflation**: Telehealth's efficiency (no travel time, fewer no-shows) has in some settings led to larger caseloads without corresponding increases in staffing or support.
- **Boundary erosion**: The ability to provide care from home has blurred work-life boundaries for many providers, particularly those in solo or small-group practice.
- **Platform pressure**: Providers working for large telehealth platforms (BetterHelp, Talkspace, Cerebral) report pressure to maintain high patient volumes, shorter sessions, and rapid medication management appointments. A 2024 investigative report by STAT News documented internal metrics at several telehealth companies tracking "patients per hour" as a key performance indicator.

Without an interstate compact, LCSWs cannot provide telehealth services across state lines without obtaining individual state licenses, a process that can take months and cost hundreds to thousands of dollars per state.

## Recent Trends

- **Telehealth-first startup scrutiny (2022-2024)**: Companies like Cerebral and Done faced DEA investigations and DOJ subpoenas over allegations of overprescribing stimulants via telehealth-only evaluations. Cerebral stopped prescribing most controlled substances in 2022 after a DOJ investigation. Done Health's founder and CEO was indicted in October 2024 on federal charges of conspiracy to commit healthcare fraud and unlawful distribution of controlled substances. These cases intensified the debate over telehealth prescribing safeguards (DOJ, 2023; DOJ, 2024).
- **Pear Therapeutics bankruptcy (April 2023)**: The leading prescription digital therapeutics company filed for Chapter 7 bankruptcy, raising questions about the commercial viability of FDA-cleared digital therapeutics for mental health when payer reimbursement is inconsistent (Pear Therapeutics SEC filing, 2023). The company's assets were acquired by a private equity firm, but its products have not returned to market as of early 2025.
- **Audio-only permanence debate (2023-2024)**: CMS and Congress have debated whether to permanently cover audio-only telehealth for mental health. MedPAC expressed concern that audio-only visits may not provide equivalent quality, while patient advocates argue they are essential for populations without broadband (MedPAC, March 2024 report). The debate remains unresolved.
- **PSYPACT rapid expansion (2021-2024)**: PSYPACT grew from 18 states in 2020 to 42 states plus DC by 2024, becoming the fastest-growing professional licensure compact in U.S. history (PSYPACT Commission, 2024). Texas's entry in 2023 was a particularly significant milestone.
- **AI mental health tools emergence (2023-2024)**: Generative AI chatbots marketed for mental health support proliferated, prompting FDA to issue guidance clarifying that AI-based tools making therapeutic claims would require regulatory review (FDA Draft Guidance on Clinical Decision Support, 2023). The National Eating Disorders Association (NEDA) shut down its AI chatbot "Tessa" in 2023 after it provided harmful diet advice to users with eating disorders, illustrating the risks.
- **School-based telehealth expansion (2023-2024)**: Federal funding through the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act (2022) expanded school-based mental health services, with many districts implementing telehealth as the delivery mechanism. An estimated 2 million students accessed mental health services through school-based telehealth programs in the 2023-2024 school year (Rural Health Information Hub, 2024).

## Emerging Challenges

- **Controlled substance diversion via telehealth**: The DEA reported a 30% increase in suspicious online prescribing activity for Schedule II stimulants between 2020 and 2023 (DEA, 2024). Establishing permanent telehealth prescribing rules that balance access and diversion prevention remains unresolved. The Done Health indictment has further elevated political sensitivity around this issue.
- **Platform consolidation**: Large telehealth companies (Teladoc, Amazon Clinic) are acquiring or outcompeting smaller mental health telehealth providers, raising concerns about market concentration and the corporatization of mental health care. Teladoc's 2020 acquisition of Livongo ($18.5 billion) and its operation of BetterHelp (over 4 million users served) make it the dominant commercial telepsychology platform.
- **Data privacy for mental health apps**: Mental health apps collect highly sensitive data but are not covered by HIPAA unless they are provided by a HIPAA-covered entity. The FTC has brought enforcement actions against BetterHelp (2023, $7.8 million settlement) and GoodRx (2023, $1.5 million settlement) for sharing user health data with advertisers. The absence of comprehensive federal health data privacy legislation leaves a structural gap.
- **Workforce sustainability**: While telehealth increases access, it also increases demand on an already strained workforce. Provider burnout from "Zoom fatigue" and high-volume telehealth caseloads is a growing concern (APA, 2024). The American Medical Association has called for research into the long-term effects of sustained telehealth delivery on provider well-being.
- **Measurement and quality**: There is no standardized federal framework for measuring the quality of telehealth mental health services. CMS quality programs (MIPS, APMs) do not include telehealth-specific quality measures, making it difficult to evaluate whether the expansion in telehealth utilization is producing equivalent outcomes.
- **Cross-border prescribing complexity**: When a provider in one state prescribes a controlled substance via telehealth to a patient in another state, questions arise about which state's prescribing laws apply, which PDMP the prescription should be reported to, and which state's pharmacy regulations govern dispensing. This multi-jurisdictional complexity is unresolved.
