# Trauma-Informed Care: Stakeholders

The landscape of trauma-informed care is shaped by the interactions of multiple groups -- from the millions of Americans directly affected by trauma to the professionals who serve them, the policymakers who allocate resources, and the organizations that advocate for reform. Understanding these stakeholders, their interests, and their power dynamics is essential for building effective coalitions and overcoming barriers to implementation.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Adults with trauma histories | Reduced health outcomes, mental illness, substance use, shortened lifespan | ~155 million (61% of adults with 1+ ACE) | Access to evidence-based treatment, destigmatization, systemic understanding |
| Children currently experiencing adversity | Disrupted brain development, academic failure, behavioral problems, removal from homes | ~35 million children (46% of U.S. children with 1+ ACE, NSCH 2022) | Safe environments, stable caregivers, access to therapeutic services |
| Parents and caregivers with unresolved trauma | Difficulty providing stable caregiving, intergenerational trauma transmission | ~30 million parents with 4+ ACEs (est.) | Parenting support, treatment access, family preservation |
| Foster children and youth in state care | Placement instability, compounded trauma, poor adult outcomes | 391,098 in foster care (FY2022) | Trauma-specific treatment, placement stability, permanency |
| Incarcerated individuals with trauma histories | Untreated trauma driving behavior, re-traumatization in custody | ~1.9 million (85-95% have significant trauma) | Trauma treatment in custody, alternatives to incarceration |
| Veterans with combat and military sexual trauma | PTSD, TBI, substance use, suicide risk | ~7 million with service-connected conditions | VA treatment access, evidence-based therapies, peer support |
| Communities of color disproportionately exposed to trauma | Compounded effects of racism, poverty, violence, and historical trauma | Varies; AI/AN adults 1.68x national ACE rate | Culturally responsive care, community-level investment, equity |
| LGBTQ+ youth and adults | Higher ACE exposure, rejection-based trauma, discrimination | ~20 million adults; 34.5% of LGBTQ+ youth report 3+ ACEs | Affirming care, protection from conversion therapy, peer support |
| Immigrants and refugees | War, persecution, displacement, detention, family separation | ~44.9 million foreign-born residents; ~3.6 million refugees resettled since 1980 | Culturally and linguistically appropriate trauma services, protection from re-traumatization in immigration system |
| Survivors of sexual assault and intimate partner violence | PTSD, depression, physical injury, economic disruption | ~43.6 million women and 24.8 million men (lifetime sexual violence, CDC 2022) | Trauma-specific services, safety planning, legal advocacy |

### Secondary Stakeholders

- **Employers and the workforce**: Adults with unaddressed trauma histories experience higher rates of absenteeism, disability, and reduced productivity. The annual lost productivity attributable to ACEs is estimated at $212 billion (CDC, 2022). The National Safety Council estimates that workplace-related stress, anxiety, and depression cost employers $8,000-$15,000 per affected employee annually in lost productivity and healthcare costs.
- **Healthcare payers (Medicaid, Medicare, private insurers)**: Trauma-related health conditions drive disproportionate healthcare utilization. Individuals with 4+ ACEs have 1.6x higher healthcare costs than those with 0 ACEs (Miller et al., 2020). Medicaid enrollees with trauma histories are among the highest-cost beneficiaries, with per-member costs 2-3x the Medicaid average in states that have studied the relationship (California Department of Health Care Services, 2022).
- **Taxpayers**: The $748 billion annual economic burden of ACEs (CDC, 2022) is ultimately borne by taxpayers through public health systems, criminal justice, child welfare, and safety net programs. Investment in prevention and treatment produces measurable returns: every $1 invested in the Nurse-Family Partnership returns $6.20 over 20 years (RAND, 2022).
- **Faith communities**: Religious institutions serve as de facto support systems for many trauma-affected individuals, particularly in rural and minority communities. An estimated 40% of Americans who seek help for mental health issues first approach a clergy member (LifeWay Research, 2014). Faith communities are stakeholders in how trauma is understood and addressed, and many have begun incorporating trauma awareness into pastoral care and congregational programming.
- **First responders**: Police officers, firefighters, EMTs, and emergency dispatchers experience high rates of occupational trauma exposure. An estimated 30% of first responders develop behavioral health conditions including PTSD, depression, and substance use disorders, compared to 20% in the general population (SAMHSA, 2018). First responder suicide rates exceed line-of-duty deaths in many departments.
- **Military families**: The approximately 2 million children of active-duty military personnel face unique stressors including frequent relocation, parental deployment, and the impact of parental combat trauma on family functioning. Military children report higher rates of anxiety, depression, and behavioral problems during deployment periods (American Academy of Pediatrics, 2019).

### Key Organizations and Institutions

#### Federal Agencies

- **Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA)**: The primary federal agency for behavioral health, administers the NCTSI ($82 million, FY2024) and the Community Mental Health Block Grant ($1.86 billion, FY2024). Sets national TIC framework and disseminates guidance. SAMHSA's 2014 framework remains the most widely adopted definition of TIC.
- **Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC)**: Leads ACE surveillance through the BRFSS, publishes research on ACE prevention, and maintains the ACE data portal. The Division of Violence Prevention houses the primary ACE research program. CDC's Essentials for Childhood framework provides guidance for state-level ACE prevention.
- **National Child Traumatic Stress Network (NCTSN)**: Federally funded network of 150+ centers developing and disseminating evidence-based child trauma treatments. Has trained over 1 million professionals since 2001. Operates as a national resource for child trauma treatment protocols, cultural adaptation guides, and implementation science.
- **Health Resources and Services Administration (HRSA)**: Funds community health centers (1,400+ sites serving 30 million patients), workforce development through the National Health Service Corps, and the Pediatric Mental Health Care Access program ($36 million, FY2024). Also administers the Maternal, Infant, and Early Childhood Home Visiting (MIECHV) program.
- **Department of Veterans Affairs (VA)**: Operates the largest integrated healthcare system with extensive trauma treatment infrastructure. Has trained over 10,000 clinicians in CPT and PE through national rollout programs since 2007. The National Center for PTSD (White River Junction, VT, and Palo Alto, CA) is the world's leading PTSD research and training institution.
- **Administration for Children and Families (ACF)**: Administers child welfare programs including Family First Prevention Services Act implementation, which expanded Title IV-E funding for trauma treatment. Oversees AFCARS (Adoption and Foster Care Analysis and Reporting System) and the National Child Abuse and Neglect Data System (NCANDS).
- **Department of Justice (DOJ)**: The Bureau of Justice Assistance (BJA) funds criminal justice reform initiatives, including trauma-informed justice programs. The Office for Victims of Crime (OVC) administers VOCA funds ($1.5 billion) that support trauma services for crime victims. The Office of Juvenile Justice and Delinquency Prevention (OJJDP) sets standards for juvenile justice trauma screening.

#### Professional Associations

- **American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP)**: Has issued policy statements supporting ACE screening and trauma-informed pediatric care. Represents 67,000 pediatricians. Published the influential 2012 technical report on toxic stress (Shonkoff & Garner, 2012).
- **American Psychological Association (APA)**: Publishes clinical practice guidelines for PTSD treatment, sets doctoral training standards, and conducts workforce surveys. Represents 122,000 psychologists. Has advocated for trauma training in accreditation standards.
- **National Association of Social Workers (NASW)**: Represents 120,000 social workers. Has issued practice standards for social workers in trauma-informed settings and advocates for manageable caseloads and workforce investment.
- **American Medical Association (AMA)**: Largest physician organization (250,000+ members). Has adopted resolutions supporting ACE screening in primary care and trauma-informed medical education.
- **National Association of School Psychologists (NASP)**: Advocates for school mental health staffing ratios (1:500 recommended) and trauma-informed school frameworks. Represents 25,000 school psychologists.

### Influencers and Advocacy Organizations

- **National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI)**: Largest grassroots mental health organization, with 600+ affiliates and 500,000+ supporters. Advocates for mental health parity, funding, and access. Conducts public education campaigns that reduce stigma.
- **ACEs Connection Network**: Online platform connecting 350,000+ ACE-aware practitioners, policymakers, and advocates in 50 states. Facilitates cross-sector community organizing. Maintains the most comprehensive database of community-level ACE initiatives.
- **Campaign for Trauma-Informed Policy and Practice (CTIPP)**: National coalition advocating for federal and state TIC legislation. Led the development of the RISE From Trauma Act. Coordinates annual Hill Day bringing trauma advocates to meet with congressional offices.
- **National Council for Mental Wellbeing**: Represents 3,300 behavioral health organizations serving 10 million adults and children. Provides Mental Health First Aid training (3 million+ trained). Administers technical assistance for Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinics (CCBHCs).
- **Trauma-informed professional associations**: EMDR International Association (11,000+ members), International Society for Traumatic Stress Studies (ISTSS, 2,800+ members from 70+ countries), Association for Behavioral and Cognitive Therapies (ABCT), Center for Deployment Psychology (military-focused training).
- **Casey Family Programs**: The nation's largest operating foundation focused on child welfare ($2 billion endowment). Funds research on trauma-informed child welfare, provides technical assistance to state child welfare agencies, and advocates for family-strengthening policies.
- **Nurse-Family Partnership**: Operates evidence-based home visiting programs in 41 states, serving 50,000+ families annually. Advocates for MIECHV expansion and universal eligibility for first-time mothers.
- **Mental Health America (MHA)**: National advocacy organization providing free mental health screenings (5 million+ completed), policy advocacy, and public education. Publishes annual state-by-state ranking of mental health access and quality.

### Key Individuals

Several individuals have played outsized roles in shaping trauma-informed care policy:

- **Dr. Nadine Burke Harris**: Former California Surgeon General (2019-2022) and founder of the Center for Youth Wellness in San Francisco. Championed the ACEs Aware Initiative, making California the first state to reimburse Medicaid providers for ACE screening. Her 2018 book *The Deepest Well* brought ACE science to a broad public audience.
- **Dr. Vincent Felitti and Dr. Robert Anda**: Co-investigators of the original ACE Study (1995-1998). Felitti, an internist at Kaiser Permanente, and Anda, a CDC epidemiologist, produced the foundational research linking childhood adversity to adult health outcomes.
- **Dr. Bessel van der Kolk**: Author of *The Body Keeps the Score* (2014), the most widely read book on trauma science with over 7 million copies sold. Professor of Psychiatry at Boston University and founder of the Trauma Center at JRI. Advocated for the Developmental Trauma Disorder diagnosis and body-based approaches to trauma treatment.
- **Dr. Bruce Perry**: Child psychiatrist and neuroscientist; co-author with Oprah Winfrey of *What Happened to You?* (2021). Founder of the ChildTrauma Academy and developer of the Neurosequential Model of Therapeutics. His work on brain development and trauma has been particularly influential in child welfare and education.
- **Dr. Jack Shonkoff**: Director of the Harvard Center on the Developing Child. Developed the "toxic stress" framework that bridges neuroscience, developmental psychology, and public policy. His work has shaped AAP policy statements and informed federal early childhood programs.

| Medical schools + licensing boards + professional associations | Updating professional training standards to include trauma competency | Moderate |

| Rural health advocates + telehealth companies + workforce development organizations | Expanding treatment access in underserved areas | Moderate |

### Conflicting Interests

- **Treatment access vs. fiscal restraint**: Mental health advocates push for expanded coverage and workforce investment; budget hawks resist new spending, particularly unfunded mandates to states. The tension is partially bridgeable through cost-of-inaction framing and return-on-investment data.
- **Universal screening vs. privacy and autonomy**: Public health advocates favor universal ACE screening; civil libertarians and some clinicians worry about data collection, surveillance, and retraumatization. Privacy-protective screening designs and robust data governance can partially address these concerns.
- **Professional autonomy vs. standardized protocols**: Many clinicians resist mandated training or standardized screening protocols as infringements on professional judgment; quality advocates argue that evidence-based standards are necessary to address the 63.7% of PTSD patients who receive no evidence-based treatment.
- **Family preservation vs. child safety**: TIC advocates emphasize keeping families together and addressing parental trauma; child protection advocates prioritize immediate safety, sometimes through removal. The Family First Prevention Services Act attempts to bridge this tension but implementation remains contested.
- **Pharmaceutical vs. psychotherapeutic approaches**: The pharmaceutical industry profits from medication-first approaches; trauma therapy advocates argue for psychotherapy as first-line treatment per VA/DoD clinical guidelines. The $28 billion antidepressant market creates significant financial interest in maintaining medication-dominant treatment patterns.
- **Western clinical models vs. culturally grounded healing**: Evidence-based treatments validated through randomized controlled trials may not adequately serve Indigenous, immigrant, and other communities that prefer culturally grounded healing practices. Tension exists between the rigor of controlled research and the relevance of cultural adaptation.
- **Mandatory reporting vs. therapeutic trust**: Mandatory reporting requirements for suspected child abuse, while essential for child safety, can deter trauma-affected parents from disclosing adversity in clinical settings for fear of child welfare involvement. This tension is particularly acute in communities with historical mistrust of government agencies.

### The Implementation Iron Triangle

The slow pace of TIC adoption is reinforced by three interconnected structures:

1. **Professional education institutions** that do not teach trauma science maintain a workforce unequipped for TIC
2. **Licensing and credentialing bodies** that do not require trauma competency perpetuate the training gap
3. **Healthcare payment systems** that do not reimburse TIC activities provide no financial incentive for adoption

These three structures reinforce each other: education programs do not teach what licensing does not require, licensing does not require what payment does not support, and payment does not support what professionals are not trained to deliver. Breaking this iron triangle requires simultaneous action on all three fronts -- accreditation changes, licensing updates, and payment reform -- because action on any single front alone will be absorbed by the inertia of the other two.

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on trauma-informed care policy:

- **Lobbying**: Pharmaceutical companies spent $374 million on federal lobbying in 2022 (OpenSecrets, 2023), far exceeding the combined advocacy budgets of trauma-informed care organizations. Mental health advocacy organizations, while politically active, have far fewer resources. NAMI's total advocacy budget is approximately $15 million; CTIPP operates with a staff of fewer than 10. The asymmetry in lobbying resources means that the financial interests opposing therapy-first approaches vastly outweigh the advocacy resources supporting them.
- **Campaign Contributions**: Health sector political contributions totaled $740 million in the 2022 cycle (OpenSecrets), with pharmaceutical and insurance industries dominating. Behavioral health contributions are a small fraction. This financial imbalance shapes legislative priorities and the composition of health policy staffs on key congressional committees.
- **Professional Associations**: The AAP, APA, NASW, AMA, and other professional organizations influence policy through position statements, practice guidelines, continuing education requirements, and testimony before legislative bodies. Professional association endorsement of TIC practices provides scientific credibility and political legitimacy.
- **Public Advocacy**: Organizations like NAMI, CTIPP, and ACEs Connection mobilize grassroots support through community education, media campaigns, and legislative advocacy days. CTIPP's annual Hill Day brings trauma advocates to Capitol Hill to meet with congressional offices. The ACEs Connection Network's community organizing model has built local TIC coalitions in over 350 communities.
- **Research and Expertise**: Academic trauma researchers and federally funded centers (NCTSN, CDC, National Center for PTSD) shape policy through published evidence, technical assistance, and expert testimony. The research base for TIC is extensive and growing, with over 2,000 peer-reviewed publications on ACEs and trauma-informed approaches since the original 1998 study.
- **Lived Experience Advocacy**: Trauma survivors increasingly participate in policy development, program design, and public education through peer support organizations, survivor-led advocacy groups, and advisory councils. The "Nothing About Us Without Us" principle -- originally articulated in the disability rights movement -- has been adopted by trauma-informed care advocates who insist that people with lived experience of trauma must be at the center of policy and program design.
- **Media and Public Narratives**: Books like *The Body Keeps the Score* (7 million copies sold) and *The Deepest Well*, combined with documentaries like *Resilience: The Biology of Stress and the Science of Hope* (2016), have brought trauma science to a broad public audience. Dr. Nadine Burke Harris's TED talk on ACEs has been viewed over 10 million times. These cultural products create public awareness that generates political pressure for policy change.
- **Litigation**: Legal advocacy, including class action lawsuits challenging inadequate mental health care in prisons (*Brown v. Plata*, 563 U.S. 493, 2011) and educational rights for traumatized students (*Endrew F. v. Douglas County School District*, 580 U.S. 386, 2017), establishes legal precedent that mandates trauma-responsive practices through court orders.
