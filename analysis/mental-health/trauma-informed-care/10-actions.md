# Trauma-Informed Care: What You Can Do

Advancing trauma-informed care in the United States requires action at every level -- from individual awareness to systemic advocacy. The science is clear: 61% of U.S. adults have experienced at least one adverse childhood experience, and the annual economic burden of ACEs is $748 billion (CDC, 2022). Yet the gap between what we know about trauma and what our public systems do about it remains vast. Closing that gap requires informed, persistent citizen engagement across individual, community, professional, and political domains.

---

## Individual Actions

### Learn and Stay Informed

- Read *The Deepest Well* by Dr. Nadine Burke Harris for an accessible introduction to ACE science and its policy implications; or *The Body Keeps the Score* by Dr. Bessel van der Kolk for a deeper clinical and neuroscience perspective
- Complete the free ACEs Aware training module (originally developed for California providers but available to all) at <https://www.acesaware.org/learn-about-screening/> -- this 2-hour module provides a solid foundation in ACE science, screening, and response
- Take the ACE questionnaire yourself to understand your own ACE score and its potential health implications: <https://www.cdc.gov/violenceprevention/aces/> (Note: ACE scores are population-level indicators, not individual diagnoses; a high score does not predict individual outcomes)
- Complete the PCE (Positive Childhood Experiences) questionnaire to understand your protective factors alongside your ACEs -- resilience research shows that protective factors can buffer the effects of adversity
- Follow the National Child Traumatic Stress Network (NCTSN) for evidence-based resources on child trauma: <https://www.nctsn.org>
- Subscribe to the ACEs Connection newsletter for monthly updates on state and local TIC initiatives: <https://www.acesconnection.com>
- Watch Dr. Nadine Burke Harris's TED talk "How Childhood Trauma Affects Health Across a Lifetime" (10+ million views) for a concise introduction to ACE science
- View the documentary *Resilience: The Biology of Stress and the Science of Hope* (2016) for a comprehensive overview of ACE science and community responses

### Make Personal Changes

- If you are a parent or caregiver, learn about the science of toxic stress and how to build protective factors (stable routines, responsive relationships, emotional regulation) in your family. The CDC's Positive Parenting Tips (<https://www.cdc.gov/ncbddd/childdevelopment/positiveparenting/>) and Triple P (Positive Parenting Program) provide evidence-based guidance.
- If you have experienced trauma, know that evidence-based treatments (CPT, PE, EMDR) have recovery rates of 53-80% for PTSD (VA/DoD CPG, 2023). Ask your provider specifically about these treatments by name -- many providers default to medication or non-specific talk therapy when evidence-based trauma treatments would be more effective. The Psychology Today therapist directory (<https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/therapists>) allows filtering by trauma specialization; the EMDR International Association directory (<https://www.emdria.org/find-an-emdr-therapist/>) lists EMDR-trained clinicians; and the National Center for PTSD provider locator (<https://www.ptsd.va.gov>) provides additional resources.
- If you are experiencing a mental health crisis, contact the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline by calling or texting 988; the Crisis Text Line by texting HOME to 741741; or the Veterans Crisis Line at 988 (press 1).
- Practice and model emotional regulation and conflict resolution skills in your relationships and community -- these are protective factors that buffer the effects of adversity for both adults and children
- Learn to recognize signs of trauma in others -- withdrawal, hypervigilance, difficulty trusting, emotional dysregulation, unexplained physical symptoms -- and respond with patience and compassion rather than judgment
- If you are a foster or kinship caregiver, seek trauma-specific training through your state's foster parent training program or the NCTSN's resources for caregivers (<https://www.nctsn.org/audiences/caregivers>)

### Use Your Voice

- **Contact Your Representatives**: Urge your members of Congress to support the Trauma-Informed Care Act and expanded funding for SAMHSA, NCTSN, and community mental health programs. The most impactful times are during budget season (February-September) and when behavioral health legislation is pending.
- **Submit Public Comments**: When CMS proposes Medicaid or Medicare rule changes affecting behavioral health, submit public comments supporting trauma screening reimbursement and mental health parity enforcement. Federal Register comment periods are tracked at <https://www.regulations.gov>. A single public comment takes 10-15 minutes and is read by agency staff.
- **Write Letters to the Editor**: Local newspapers influence state and local policymakers. Write about the need for trauma-informed schools, adequately funded school mental health services, and community-based prevention programs. Focus on local data and specific policy requests.
- **Share Information**: Share ACE science and trauma-informed care information with your personal and professional networks, using plain language rather than clinical jargon. The FrameWorks Institute research suggests framing messages around "building healthy foundations for children" rather than "preventing ACEs" for maximum public resonance.
- **Attend School Board Meetings**: School boards make decisions about discipline policies, mental health staffing, and social-emotional learning programs that directly affect trauma-informed practice. Public comment periods are open to all community members.

## Community Actions

### Organize Locally

- Start or join a local ACEs initiative. Over 350 communities across the U.S. have formed ACE-aware coalitions. The ACEs Connection Network (<https://www.acesconnection.com>) provides toolkits for launching a community initiative, including a step-by-step community organizing guide, meeting templates, and outreach materials.
- Organize a community screening of the documentary *Resilience: The Biology of Stress and the Science of Hope* (2016), followed by a discussion with local mental health professionals, educators, and policymakers. KPJR Films (<https://kpjrfilms.co/resilience/>) provides screening licenses and discussion guides.
- Partner with your local school district to advocate for trauma-informed discipline policies, expanded school counseling, and social-emotional learning programs. Request that the school board review suspension and expulsion data disaggregated by race, disability status, and gender to identify disparities.
- Work with faith communities to create trauma-aware congregations. Many religious organizations already provide de facto support for trauma-affected individuals; adding trauma awareness training makes these efforts more effective. The ACE Interface Academy provides training specifically for faith communities.
- Advocate with local law enforcement for Crisis Intervention Team (CIT) training. Contact CIT International (<https://www.citinternational.org>) for resources on how to approach your local police department. CIT training reduces use of force by 28% and increases treatment diversion by 52%.
- Support your local domestic violence shelter, rape crisis center, and victim services organization. These organizations provide frontline trauma services and are often underfunded. The National Domestic Violence Hotline (<https://www.thehotline.org>, 1-800-799-7233) can connect you to local resources.
- Advocate for local government investment in evidence-based community violence intervention programs (Cure Violence, READI Chicago, Advance Peace) if your community experiences high rates of gun violence.

### Build Coalitions

- Connect pediatricians, teachers, social workers, law enforcement, faith leaders, and parents in a cross-sector trauma-informed coalition. The most effective TIC initiatives operate across systems because trauma does not respect institutional boundaries. The Philadelphia ACE Task Force provides a model for cross-sector coalition building.
- Invite local elected officials to visit trauma-informed schools, courts, or healthcare settings to see the approach in action. Experiential learning is more persuasive than policy briefs. Arrange visits to schools using PBIS or restorative practices, trauma-informed courts, or community health centers with integrated behavioral health.
- Partner with local business leaders to articulate the economic case for TIC: workforce productivity losses from untreated trauma cost employers approximately $212 billion annually (CDC, 2022). The Society for Human Resource Management (SHRM) has published resources on trauma-informed workplace practices.
- Engage local media in covering ACE science and TIC implementation. Offer reporters data, personal stories, and expert contacts. Local coverage of trauma-informed schools, courts, and healthcare settings builds public support for investment.
- Connect with your state's ACE task force or commission (22 states have established these bodies) to align local efforts with statewide strategy. The NCSL maintains a directory of state ACE initiatives.

## Political Actions

### Contact Elected Officials

**When to Contact**:

- During federal budget deliberations (February-September), to advocate for SAMHSA, HRSA, NCTSN, and CDC behavioral health funding
- When mental health legislation is introduced or pending in committee (track at <https://www.congress.gov>)
- When your state legislature is considering TIC-related bills (26 states have passed some form of TIC legislation; check NCSL's tracker at <https://www.ncsl.org/health/adverse-childhood-experiences>)
- After mass casualty events or community crises, when political attention to trauma and mental health is heightened
- During school board elections and meetings, to advocate for trauma-informed school policies
- When CMS proposes rules affecting Medicaid behavioral health coverage (comment periods tracked at <https://www.regulations.gov>)
- When your state insurance commissioner conducts hearings on mental health parity compliance

**What to Say -- Template Email**:

```text
Subject: Support Trauma-Informed Care Legislation and Funding

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

I am writing as a constituent from [City, State] to urge your support for trauma-informed care (TIC) policies and funding. Research from the CDC shows that 61% of American adults have experienced at least one adverse childhood experience (ACE), and the annual economic burden of untreated childhood trauma is $748 billion.

I urge you to:
1. Support the Trauma-Informed Care Act to establish national TIC standards and screening integration
2. Increase funding for SAMHSA ($9.5 billion in FY2024) and the National Child Traumatic Stress Initiative ($82 million in FY2024), which are insufficient to meet demand
3. Support mental health parity enforcement to ensure insurance coverage of evidence-based trauma treatments
4. Support MIECHV (home visiting) reauthorization and expansion to prevent childhood adversity before it occurs

Evidence-based trauma treatments like Cognitive Processing Therapy and Prolonged Exposure have recovery rates of 53-80% for PTSD, yet only 36.3% of adults with PTSD currently receive evidence-based treatment. This is a solvable problem that saves both lives and taxpayer money.

Thank you for your service and consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your City, State]
```

### Support Legislation

Current or proposed legislation to support:

- **Trauma-Informed Care Act (proposed)**: Establishes a national framework for TIC integration across public systems, authorizes trauma screening reimbursement, and creates a National Trauma Training Center. Contact your representative to co-sponsor this legislation.
- **RISE From Trauma Act (proposed)**: Comprehensive federal TIC legislation championed by CTIPP. Contact the Senate HELP Committee and House Energy and Commerce Committee. Includes provisions for cross-system coordination, workforce development, and community resilience.
- **Mental Health Parity enforcement**: Urge your state insurance commissioner and federal representatives to enforce the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act for trauma treatment coverage. Report parity violations to the Department of Labor at <https://www.dol.gov/agencies/ebsa>.
- **Family First Prevention Services Act implementation**: Encourage your state child welfare agency to fully implement Title IV-E prevention services, including evidence-based trauma treatments for at-risk families. Only 38 states have approved implementation plans as of 2023.
- **MIECHV (home visiting) reauthorization and expansion**: Support expansion of the Maternal, Infant, and Early Childhood Home Visiting program to serve all eligible families (currently serving approximately 140,000 of 3 million eligible).
- **988 Implementation Act**: Support funding for 988 crisis infrastructure, including follow-up care and mobile crisis teams, to ensure that crisis callers receive not just immediate response but connection to ongoing trauma treatment.
- **School mental health staffing legislation**: Support federal and state proposals to fund school counselors, psychologists, and social workers at recommended ratios (1:250, 1:500, and 1:250 respectively).

### Electoral Engagement

**Questions to Ask Candidates**:

1. "What is your position on funding trauma-informed care and evidence-based mental health treatment in our schools, healthcare system, and justice system?"
2. "Do you support expanding SAMHSA funding and the National Child Traumatic Stress Initiative to address the $748 billion annual cost of childhood adversity?"
3. "How would you address the mental health workforce shortage, given that nearly half of U.S. counties lack a single psychologist?"
4. "Do you support enforcing mental health parity laws to ensure insurance coverage of evidence-based trauma treatments?"
5. "What is your position on expanding home visiting programs to prevent child abuse and neglect?"
6. "Would you support restoring the expanded child tax credit, which reduced child poverty by 30% when it was in effect?"

**Voting Considerations**:

- Look for candidates who support behavioral health funding, mental health parity enforcement, and school mental health investment
- Key congressional committees: Senate HELP Committee, House Energy and Commerce Committee, and the Appropriations subcommittees for Labor-HHS-Education
- At the state level, education committees and health committees are the primary venues for TIC legislation
- At the local level, school board members and county commissioners make decisions that directly affect trauma-informed practice in schools, courts, and public health departments

## Professional Actions

### For Healthcare Providers

- Integrate validated trauma screening (ACE questionnaire, PC-PTSD-5, Pediatric Symptom Checklist) into your practice. The ACEs Aware training module (<https://www.acesaware.org>) provides free training in clinical ACE screening protocols.
- Pursue training in evidence-based trauma treatments through NCTSN, the VA's training partnerships, or professional associations. Specific training resources:
    - CPT: Center for Deployment Psychology (<https://deploymentpsych.org>) and Medical University of South Carolina
    - PE: Center for the Treatment and Study of Anxiety at the University of Pennsylvania (<https://www.med.upenn.edu/ctsa/>)
    - EMDR: EMDR International Association (<https://www.emdria.org>)
    - TF-CBT: TF-CBT Web (<https://tfcbt2.musc.edu>) -- free web-based learning course
- Advocate within your professional association (AAP, APA, AMA, NASW) for trauma competency requirements in accreditation and continuing education standards
- If you are a prescriber, review the VA/DoD Clinical Practice Guideline for PTSD, which recommends psychotherapy as first-line treatment; ensure patients are offered evidence-based psychotherapy before or alongside medication

### For Educators

- Advocate within your school or district for trauma-informed discipline policies, social-emotional learning integration, and expanded school mental health staffing. Request that your school board review discipline data disaggregated by race, disability, and gender.
- Complete trauma awareness training through your state's trauma-informed schools initiative, NCTSN resources, or the free online modules from Starr Commonwealth (<https://www.starr.org>)
- Learn to implement restorative practices as alternatives to suspension and expulsion. The International Institute for Restorative Practices (<https://www.iirp.edu>) provides training and resources.
- Monitor your own wellbeing using the ProQOL scale (<https://proqol.org>) and seek support if you are experiencing secondary traumatic stress. Teaching in trauma-affected communities is inherently emotionally demanding; self-care is a professional responsibility, not a luxury.
- Advocate for your school to implement PBIS (Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports) if it has not already done so. Over 27,000 U.S. schools use PBIS; resources are available at <https://www.pbis.org>.

### For Law Enforcement

- Advocate for Crisis Intervention Team (CIT) training in your department. Contact CIT International (<https://www.citinternational.org>) for implementation resources. CIT training typically involves 40 hours of instruction on mental health, de-escalation, and diversion.
- Support co-responder models that pair officers with mental health professionals for crisis calls. The CAHOOTS model (Eugene, OR) diverts approximately 17% of police calls to mental health responders and has been replicated in multiple cities.
- Advocate for peer support programs within your department to address occupational trauma exposure. First responder suicide rates exceed line-of-duty deaths in many departments; peer support programs reduce stigma and connect officers to care.

### For Social Workers and Counselors

- Seek training in evidence-based trauma treatments and advocate for manageable caseloads that allow therapeutic relationship-building. The Child Welfare League of America recommends maximum caseloads of 15 active cases per caseworker.
- Use the Professional Quality of Life (ProQOL) scale to monitor your own secondary traumatic stress. Organizations have a responsibility to support worker wellbeing, but individuals can also take proactive steps.
- Advocate within your agency for reflective supervision, which research shows reduces secondary traumatic stress and improves clinical outcomes.

### For Attorneys and Judges

- Advocate for trauma-informed court practices, including trauma screening at intake, therapeutic jurisprudence approaches, and trauma-specific programming in correctional settings. The National Drug Court Institute (<https://www.ndci.org>) and the National Center for State Courts (<https://www.ncsc.org>) provide training resources.
- Support pre-sentencing trauma assessments for felony cases to inform appropriate treatment and supervision conditions
- Advocate for diversion programs that route individuals with trauma-related behavioral health conditions to treatment rather than incarceration

### For Researchers

- Prioritize research on TIC implementation fidelity measurement, cultural adaptation of evidence-based treatments, expanded ACE frameworks, and cost-effectiveness of trauma-informed system transformation
- Include diverse populations in clinical trials for trauma treatments; the evidence base has been disproportionately developed with white, English-speaking populations
- Conduct implementation science research that bridges the gap between efficacy studies and real-world practice
- Develop and validate organizational assessment tools that can distinguish genuine TIC implementation from superficial adoption

## Advocacy Toolkit

### Key Talking Points

When discussing trauma-informed care with others, emphasize:

1. **The scale of the problem**: 61% of U.S. adults report at least one ACE, and 16% report four or more. This is not a niche issue -- it is a foundational public health challenge affecting the majority of Americans (CDC BRFSS, 2021).
2. **The cost of inaction**: Untreated childhood trauma costs the U.S. economy $748 billion annually in healthcare, criminal justice, child welfare, and lost productivity (CDC, 2022). Investing in prevention and treatment is fiscal responsibility.
3. **Treatment works**: Evidence-based trauma therapies have recovery rates of 53-80% for PTSD (VA/DoD CPG, 2023). The problem is not that we lack effective treatments -- it is that only 36.3% of those who need them receive them (SAMHSA NSDUH, 2023).
4. **Prevention is possible**: Programs like the Nurse-Family Partnership reduce child abuse and neglect by 48% and return $6.20 for every $1 invested (RAND, 2022). We can break cycles of adversity before they start.
5. **This is bipartisan**: Protecting children, strengthening families, reducing incarceration costs, and improving public safety are values shared across the political spectrum. Twenty-six states have already passed TIC-related legislation with bipartisan support (NCSL, 2023).
6. **The workforce crisis**: Nearly half of U.S. counties lack a single psychologist. Even when people seek help, the providers are not there. Workforce expansion is as urgent as treatment expansion.

### Common Misconceptions to Counter

| Misconception | Reality | Source |
|---------------|---------|--------|
| "Trauma-informed care means making excuses for bad behavior" | TIC contextualizes behavior to enable more effective responses; it does not eliminate accountability. Trauma-informed discipline in schools reduces suspensions 30-50% while improving academic outcomes. | Maynard et al., 2019 |
| "ACE screening labels people and does more harm than good" | California's ACEs Aware Initiative screened 2.5 million patients with no documented increase in adverse events; 93% of patients reported the screening was acceptable. | ACEs Aware Annual Report, 2023 |
| "We can't afford trauma-informed care" | We cannot afford *not* to. The annual cost of ACEs ($748 billion) dwarfs the investment in prevention and treatment. Every $1 invested in child trauma treatment produces an estimated $7-11 in avoided downstream costs. | CDC, 2022; NCTSN, 2022 |
| "Trauma is a personal issue, not a government concern" | Trauma drives utilization in every public system -- healthcare, education, criminal justice, child welfare. Failing to address it costs taxpayers hundreds of billions and undermines the effectiveness of public institutions. | CDC, 2022 |
| "Only certain populations experience trauma" | 61% of all U.S. adults have at least one ACE, spanning every demographic, geographic, and socioeconomic group. Disparities exist in exposure rates and access to care, but trauma is a universal American experience. | CDC BRFSS, 2021 |
| "Kids are resilient -- they bounce back on their own" | Some children show remarkable resilience, but resilience is not automatic -- it depends on protective factors like stable caregivers, safe environments, and access to support. Without these, trauma produces lasting biological and psychological effects. | Shonkoff & Garner, 2012 |
| "Trauma treatment takes years and is impractical at scale" | The most effective trauma treatments are brief: CPT requires 12 sessions, PE requires 8-15 sessions, EMDR requires 6-12 sessions. These are among the most efficient treatments in all of medicine. | VA/DoD CPG, 2023 |
