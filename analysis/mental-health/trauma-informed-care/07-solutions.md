# Trauma-Informed Care: Solutions

Transforming American public systems to be trauma-informed requires a multi-faceted approach that integrates screening and assessment, evidence-based treatment, workforce development, system redesign, and upstream prevention. The solutions proposed here are grounded in existing evidence, draw on successful domestic and international models, and are designed for phased implementation.

---

## Reform Framework

### Guiding Principles

- Evidence-based approaches should be the foundation of all TIC policies, prioritizing interventions with demonstrated efficacy in randomized controlled trials or rigorous quasi-experimental designs
- Screening must always be paired with adequate treatment capacity and referral pathways; identifying trauma without the ability to respond does more harm than good
- Workforce development must precede or accompany mandates to ensure implementation fidelity; mandates without capacity produce performative compliance
- Cultural responsiveness and equity must be embedded in every aspect of design and delivery, not treated as add-on components
- Lived experience must inform policy development, program design, and quality improvement; people affected by trauma must have meaningful roles in shaping the systems that serve them
- Prevention and resilience-building are as important as treatment of existing trauma; a comprehensive approach requires both upstream and downstream investments
- Data governance and privacy protections must be built into screening and assessment systems from the outset, not retrofitted after implementation
- Sustainability requires integration into standard financing mechanisms (Medicaid, Medicare, commercial insurance) rather than dependence on time-limited grant funding

### Theory of Change

Universal trauma awareness among public-serving professionals, combined with validated screening at key system entry points, will identify trauma-affected individuals early and connect them to evidence-based treatment. Simultaneously, upstream investments in family support, early childhood, and community resilience will reduce the incidence of new trauma. Over time, this dual approach -- treatment of existing trauma and prevention of future adversity -- will reduce the downstream costs of untreated trauma (currently $748 billion annually) while improving health, educational, economic, and justice outcomes. The theory is supported by evidence from both domestic pilots (California ACEs Aware, San Francisco HEARTS, Tarrant County trauma consortium) and international models (Scotland's National Trauma Training Programme, Australia's recovery-oriented framework, British Columbia's provincial ACEs response).

## Proposed Solutions

### Solution 1: National Trauma Screening Integration Program

**Description**: Establish a federal framework for integrating validated trauma screening into routine encounters across healthcare, education, child welfare, and justice systems. This program would develop age-appropriate, culturally validated screening protocols; create standardized referral pathways; and build the data infrastructure needed to track screening rates and outcomes. Screening would be implemented through a tiered model: universal brief screening (e.g., PC-PTSD-5 or ACE questionnaire) followed by more detailed assessment for those who screen positive (e.g., CAPS-5, CANS, UCLA PTSD Reaction Index).

**Evidence Base**: California's ACEs Aware Initiative demonstrated that statewide Medicaid-reimbursed ACE screening is feasible, with 2.5 million screens conducted in three years and no documented increase in adverse events (ACEs Aware Annual Report, 2023). The VA's universal PTSD screening program, using the PC-PTSD-5, successfully identifies 90% of veterans with PTSD in primary care settings (Prins et al., 2016). The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF) recommends depression screening for all adults and intimate partner violence screening for women of reproductive age; trauma screening uses similar methodology and has comparable evidence support. Montefiore Medical Center's pediatric ACE screening program (25,000+ patients) found 90% parental acceptability and successful linkage to coordinated referrals (Conn et al., 2018).

**Implementation**: SAMHSA would develop screening guidelines in collaboration with professional associations (AAP, APA, AMA, NASW, AAFP). CMS would issue Medicaid and Medicare reimbursement codes for trauma screening, following the California model. States would submit implementation plans within 18 months. HRSA community health centers (1,400+ sites serving 30 million patients) would serve as early implementation sites, leveraging existing behavioral health integration infrastructure. Screening instruments would be culturally validated for major language groups and adapted for populations including children (Pediatric Symptom Checklist, SCARED), adults (PC-PTSD-5, ACE questionnaire), and older adults (modified screening with geriatric considerations).

**Cost/Resources**: $250 million annually for federal coordination, technical assistance, and data infrastructure; $1.2 billion annually in Medicaid reimbursement for screening encounters; $400 million annually for referral pathway development and community-based treatment expansion.

**Timeline**: Guidelines published within 12 months; state implementation plans within 24 months; full operational status within 48 months.

**Challenges**: Workforce capacity to respond to positive screens; privacy and data security; potential for screening to become perfunctory rather than therapeutic; cultural adaptation of instruments; ensuring that screening data is not misused in employment, insurance, custody, or law enforcement contexts.

**Impact Metrics**: Screening rates by system and population; positive screen rates; referral completion rates; time from positive screen to treatment initiation; patient-reported experience; downstream utilization changes.

### Solution 2: Evidence-Based Trauma Treatment Expansion

**Description**: Dramatically expand access to evidence-based trauma treatments (CPT, PE, EMDR, TF-CBT, CPP) through training subsidies, loan repayment for trauma-specialized providers, telehealth expansion, and insurance mandate enforcement. Currently, only 36.3% of adults with PTSD receive evidence-based treatment (SAMHSA NSDUH, 2023). This solution targets treatment access from multiple angles: training more providers, removing financial barriers, eliminating geographic obstacles through telehealth, and addressing treatment completion through culturally adapted delivery models.

**Evidence Base**: VA/DoD Clinical Practice Guidelines rate CPT, PE, and EMDR as Level A (strong evidence) treatments for PTSD, with remission rates of 53-80% (VA/DoD CPG, 2023). The VA's national CPT and PE rollout, which trained over 10,000 clinicians between 2007 and 2020, demonstrated that large-scale training is feasible and produces clinically significant improvements in patient outcomes (Karlin et al., 2010). Telehealth delivery of CPT and PE has demonstrated comparable efficacy to in-person delivery (Morland et al., 2020), with the VA reporting that telehealth trauma therapy visits increased 1,500% between 2019 and 2022 with no decline in outcomes. For children, TF-CBT produces 60-80% remission rates and is deliverable by master's-level clinicians, enabling task-shifting that expands the provider pool.

**Implementation**: Expand NHSC loan repayment to include trauma treatment specialization ($50,000-$100,000 loan repayment per provider committing to 3+ years in underserved areas). Fund training partnerships between VA training infrastructure and civilian provider networks, leveraging the VA's proven competency-based training model. Require commercial insurers and Medicaid/Medicare to cover evidence-based trauma treatments without preauthorization, consistent with mental health parity requirements. Extend telehealth flexibilities permanently for behavioral health. Fund cultural adaptation and validation of evidence-based treatments for underserved populations (Indigenous, immigrant, Black, Latino, AAPI communities). Support training of peer support specialists and community health workers to deliver structured trauma interventions (e.g., Skills Training in Affective and Interpersonal Regulation) under clinical supervision.

**Cost/Resources**: $500 million annually for provider training and loan repayment; $300 million for telehealth infrastructure and broadband expansion; $150 million for cultural adaptation research and dissemination; minimal net cost for insurance mandate enforcement (reduced downstream utilization offsets treatment costs).

**Timeline**: Training program expansion within 12 months; insurance mandate implementation within 24 months; target of 200,000 trained trauma therapists within 5 years (doubling current capacity).

**Challenges**: Training capacity constraints; clinician resistance to manualized treatments; supervision requirements for fidelity maintenance; rural broadband limitations for telehealth; treatment dropout rates (30-40%) requiring retention-focused strategies.

**Impact Metrics**: Number of providers trained in evidence-based trauma treatments; average wait time for trauma therapy; PTSD remission rates at population level; treatment completion rates; disparities in treatment access by race, geography, and insurance status.

### Solution 3: Trauma-Informed Schools Initiative

**Description**: Create a comprehensive federal-state partnership to make every public school trauma-informed within 10 years. Components include mandatory trauma training for all school personnel, expanded school-based mental health services, revision of discipline policies to eliminate zero-tolerance approaches, and implementation of Multi-Tiered Systems of Support (MTSS) that integrate social-emotional learning and trauma-responsive practices. The initiative would bring school counselor, psychologist, and social worker ratios to recommended professional association levels.

**Evidence Base**: Schools implementing trauma-informed practices have documented 30-50% reductions in suspensions and expulsions (Maynard et al., 2019), 15-25% improvements in academic performance (Dorado et al., 2016), and significant reductions in teacher burnout (Trauma-Informed Schools Initiative, Washington State, 2023). San Francisco Unified School District's HEARTS program achieved an 87% reduction in suspensions over 10 years (2008-2018) with simultaneous improvement in academic outcomes. The Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports (PBIS) framework, used in approximately 27,000 U.S. schools, provides a scalable structure for TIC integration. Massachusetts' Safe and Supportive Schools Initiative documented 25% reduction in chronic absenteeism in participating schools.

**Implementation**: Department of Education would issue TIC guidance and standards, including recommended staffing ratios and discipline policy frameworks. Title I funding would be augmented with a trauma-informed supplement requiring demonstration of TIC implementation as a condition of receiving additional funds. States would develop implementation plans with technical assistance from ED and SAMHSA. School counselor, psychologist, and social worker ratios would be brought to recommended levels (1:250, 1:500, and 1:250 respectively) through targeted funding and pipeline development. Teacher and administrator training would be delivered through a tiered model: all staff receive trauma awareness training (4-8 hours), frontline staff receive trauma-skilled training (16-24 hours), and specialized staff receive intensive clinical training.

**Cost/Resources**: $3 billion annually (including $1.5 billion for school mental health staffing, $800 million for training, $700 million for program implementation). Represents approximately 4% of federal education spending.

**Timeline**: Standards issued within 12 months; state implementation plans within 24 months; 50% of schools meeting standards within 5 years; 100% within 10 years.

**Challenges**: Teacher and administrator resistance; competing education priorities; rural school resource constraints; sustainability after grant funding ends; ensuring that TIC does not become another unfunded mandate layered on top of existing accountability requirements.

**Impact Metrics**: Suspension and expulsion rates (overall and by race/disability); school-based mental health service utilization; student well-being surveys; teacher retention rates; academic outcomes for trauma-affected students; staffing ratios relative to recommended levels.

### Solution 4: Trauma-Informed Justice System Transformation

**Description**: Embed trauma-informed practices across the criminal and juvenile justice continuum, from policing through reentry. Components include crisis intervention team (CIT) training for all law enforcement, trauma screening at booking and intake, trauma-specific programming in correctional facilities, trauma-informed courts, and reentry services that address trauma alongside housing, employment, and substance use. Special attention to incarcerated women, who report sexual/physical abuse histories at rates of 77-90% (Bureau of Justice Statistics, 2016).

**Evidence Base**: Trauma-informed programming in correctional settings reduces recidivism by 15-25% (Kubiak et al., 2017; Miller & Najavits, 2012). CIT-trained officers are 28% less likely to use force and 52% more likely to divert to treatment (Watson et al., 2021). Trauma-informed courts have demonstrated lower recidivism and higher treatment completion than conventional courts (National Drug Court Institute, 2023). Tarrant County, Texas's cross-system trauma initiative reduced juvenile detention admissions by 44% over 5 years (2015-2020). The Seeking Safety program, designed for individuals with co-occurring PTSD and substance use disorders, has demonstrated effectiveness in correctional settings and can be delivered by trained correctional staff.

**Implementation**: DOJ would establish a Trauma-Informed Justice Initiative with grants, technical assistance, and model standards. Bureau of Justice Assistance (BJA) grants would incentivize adoption by conditioning existing funding on TIC implementation milestones. The Federal Bureau of Prisons would implement system-wide TIC policies, including trauma screening at intake, staff training, and evidence-based programming. Juvenile justice systems would be required to screen for trauma at intake and provide evidence-based treatment as a condition of OJJDP funding. DOJ would develop model legislation for trauma-informed sentencing that includes mandatory pre-sentencing trauma assessment for felony cases.

**Cost/Resources**: $1.2 billion annually (including $500 million for law enforcement training, $400 million for correctional programming, $300 million for courts and reentry). Offset by estimated $2-3 billion in reduced incarceration costs based on 15-25% recidivism reductions.

**Timeline**: Federal standards within 12 months; state implementation incentivized within 24 months; 50% adoption within 5 years.

**Challenges**: Law enforcement culture; correctional officer training and turnover; political "soft on crime" attacks; measuring implementation fidelity in coercive settings; tension between security imperatives and therapeutic principles in correctional environments.

**Impact Metrics**: Use-of-force incidents; diversion rates; in-custody trauma treatment access; recidivism rates; reentry success metrics; juvenile detention rates; use of solitary confinement and restraint.

### Solution 5: National Trauma-Informed Workforce Development Program

**Description**: Transform professional education and continuing education to ensure that every public-serving professional has foundational trauma competency. This solution addresses the root cause of most implementation failures -- an unprepared workforce -- through requirements at the educational, licensing, and continuing education levels. The program would break the "implementation iron triangle" by simultaneously changing accreditation standards, licensing requirements, and payment incentives.

**Evidence Base**: The VA's national CPT/PE rollout (2007-2020) trained 10,000+ clinicians and produced measurable improvements in treatment outcomes, demonstrating that large-scale workforce transformation is possible (Karlin et al., 2010). Washington State's mandatory trauma training for school staff produced measurable improvements in school climate and reduced suspensions within two years (Washington OSPI, 2023). Scotland's National Trauma Training Programme trained 500,000 public workers (in a country of 5.4 million) within 5 years, achieving documented improvements in school exclusions, staff-client relationships, and use of restraint (NHS Education for Scotland, 2023).

**Implementation**: Work with accreditation bodies (LCME for medical schools, CSWE for social work, CAEP for education, APA-CoA for psychology) to require trauma content in curricula. Establish national trauma competency standards in collaboration with professional associations, using a tiered model: Level 1 (trauma-aware, all professionals), Level 2 (trauma-skilled, frontline workers), Level 3 (trauma-enhanced, specialized staff), Level 4 (trauma-specialist, clinical experts). Fund Train-the-Trainer programs to cascade expertise efficiently. Create a National Trauma Training Center modeled on the VA's training infrastructure, with both physical and virtual delivery capacity. Include continuing education requirements for license renewal in all states. Establish clinical supervision infrastructure to ensure fidelity maintenance after initial training.

**Cost/Resources**: $600 million annually (including $200 million for a National Training Center, $250 million for educational institution grants, $150 million for continuing education subsidies and supervision infrastructure).

**Timeline**: Competency standards developed within 12 months; accreditation requirements adopted within 36 months; first cohort of fully trauma-trained graduates within 5-7 years.

**Challenges**: Academic institutional autonomy; crowded curricula; resistance from faculty without trauma expertise; variation across professions and states; ensuring that training produces competency rather than mere exposure.

**Impact Metrics**: Trauma content hours in professional education; licensing exam coverage; continuing education completion; provider self-assessed competency; patient/client-reported provider sensitivity; treatment fidelity measures.

### Solution 6: Community Resilience and Prevention Infrastructure

**Description**: Invest in upstream protective factors that prevent trauma and build resilience at the community level. Components include evidence-based home visiting programs (Nurse-Family Partnership, Parents as Teachers, Healthy Families America), quality early childhood education, family economic support (child tax credits, earned income tax credits), safe and affordable housing, community violence intervention programs, and community resilience hubs that provide wraparound prevention services in high-ACE neighborhoods.

**Evidence Base**: The Nurse-Family Partnership has demonstrated 48% reduction in child abuse and neglect, 56% fewer emergency department visits, and $6.20 return per $1 invested over 20 years (RAND Corporation, 2022). Expanded child tax credits reduced child poverty by 30% in 2021 (Center on Budget and Policy Priorities, 2022). Community violence intervention programs reduce shootings by 30-60% in targeted areas (Cure Violence, 2023). The Perry Preschool Project demonstrated $7.16 return per $1 invested in quality early childhood education over 40 years, with participants showing lower rates of criminal justice involvement, higher earnings, and better health outcomes (Heckman et al., 2010). Bethell's research demonstrates that positive childhood experiences can buffer the effects of ACEs, with high-PCE adults showing 72% lower odds of depression even with high ACE scores.

**Implementation**: Expand MIECHV (Maternal, Infant, and Early Childhood Home Visiting) funding to serve all eligible families (currently serves approximately 140,000 of 3 million eligible). Restore and make permanent the expanded child tax credit. Fund community resilience hubs in high-ACE communities through SAMHSA block grants, providing coordinated access to home visiting, parenting support, economic assistance, legal services, and peer support. Scale community violence intervention programs (Cure Violence, READI Chicago, Advance Peace) to all high-violence communities. Invest in affordable housing as a trauma prevention strategy, given evidence that housing instability is both a consequence and a cause of ACE exposure.

**Cost/Resources**: $5 billion annually for home visiting expansion; $100 billion annually for expanded child tax credit (partially offset by reduced poverty-related costs); $1 billion annually for community resilience infrastructure; $500 million for community violence intervention scaling.

**Timeline**: Home visiting expansion within 24 months; child tax credit restoration immediately; community resilience infrastructure built over 5 years.

**Challenges**: Political opposition to income support programs; sustainability of community-level interventions; measurement of prevention outcomes; federal-state coordination; ensuring that prevention investments reach communities with highest need.

**Impact Metrics**: ACE prevalence rates (10-year trend); child maltreatment rates; child poverty rates; community violence rates; foster care entry rates; positive childhood experience measures.

### Solution 7: Secondary Traumatic Stress Prevention for the Workforce

**Description**: Require organizations that serve trauma-affected populations to implement evidence-based secondary traumatic stress (STS) prevention programs for their workforce. High turnover in trauma-proximate professions (30-40% annually in child welfare) undermines service quality, institutional memory, and therapeutic relationships. This solution addresses workforce sustainability as a prerequisite for system transformation, recognizing that no organization can be truly trauma-informed if it does not attend to the wellbeing of its own staff.

**Evidence Base**: Organizations implementing structured STS prevention programs -- including reflective supervision, manageable caseloads, wellness supports, and organizational resilience practices -- have demonstrated 25-40% reductions in turnover (Salloum et al., 2019). The ProQOL (Professional Quality of Life) scale provides a validated measurement tool for STS. The National Child Welfare Workforce Institute's Trauma-Informed Organizational Self-Assessment has been used by hundreds of child welfare agencies. The Sanctuary Model (Sandra Bloom, Drexel University) provides a comprehensive organizational change framework for trauma-proximate settings, with documented improvements in staff wellbeing and client outcomes across over 300 implementing organizations.

**Implementation**: Include STS prevention requirements in federal grant conditions for SAMHSA, HRSA, DOJ, and ED programs. Develop organizational self-assessment tools and technical assistance. Fund research on STS prevention effectiveness across professions. Establish manageable caseload standards for federally funded programs (e.g., maximum 15 active cases per child welfare caseworker, recommended by CWLA). Require reflective supervision for all staff delivering trauma-related services. Fund peer support programs for first responders, healthcare workers, and educators. Establish employee assistance programs specifically designed for trauma-proximate professionals.

**Cost/Resources**: $200 million annually for organizational supports, technical assistance, and research. Cost savings from reduced turnover estimated at $2.3 billion annually across child welfare alone (Casey Family Programs, 2022), with additional savings in healthcare, education, and justice settings.

**Timeline**: Grant requirements within 12 months; organizational assessment tools within 18 months; caseload standards within 36 months.

**Challenges**: Organizational resistance to transparency about workforce conditions; difficulty standardizing caseloads across diverse settings; balancing staff support with service demand; union negotiations around workload standards.

**Impact Metrics**: Workforce turnover rates by sector; STS prevalence (ProQOL scores); worker-reported organizational support; caseload ratios; client/patient continuity of care; sick leave utilization; workplace injury rates.

## International Models

### Australia: National Framework for Recovery-Oriented Mental Health Services

**Description**: Australia adopted a national framework in 2013 that integrates trauma-informed principles into all public mental health services. The framework establishes seven capabilities (uniqueness, real lives, genuinely participatory, social inclusion, evidence-based, collaborative relationships, and organizational commitment) and requires implementation across all publicly funded mental health services. The framework was developed collaboratively with consumers and carers, centering lived experience in policy design.

**Results**: Australia has documented improvements in consumer-reported experience (22% improvement from 2013 to 2022), reduced seclusion and restraint in psychiatric facilities (45% reduction 2013-2022), and increased workforce satisfaction (National Mental Health Commission, 2022). Victoria's Royal Commission into Mental Health (2021) recommended expanded trauma-informed services with $3.8 billion AUD in new funding.

**Applicability to U.S.**: The national framework approach provides a model for federal coordination without top-down mandates. However, Australia's single-payer health system simplifies implementation; the U.S. would need to coordinate across Medicaid, Medicare, private insurance, and uninsured populations. The consumer participation model is directly applicable.

### Scotland: National Trauma Training Programme

**Description**: Scotland launched a National Trauma Training Programme in 2018, developed by NHS Education for Scotland, that provides free, tiered trauma training for the entire public workforce. The program uses four levels: trauma-informed (all staff), trauma-skilled (frontline workers), trauma-enhanced (specialized staff), and trauma-specialist (clinical experts). Training materials are freely available online and have been adopted by local authorities across Scotland.

**Results**: By 2023, over 500,000 Scottish public workers had completed trauma-informed training -- in a country of 5.4 million people (equivalent to training 30 million Americans). Schools reported 32% reductions in exclusions; justice settings documented improved staff-client relationships and reduced use of restraint (NHS Education for Scotland, 2023). The programme's tiered model has been adopted or adapted by Wales, Northern Ireland, and several Australian states.

**Applicability to U.S.**: The tiered training model is highly applicable and could be adapted for state-level implementation. Scotland's centralized government makes universal adoption easier; the U.S. would need to incentivize adoption across 50 states and thousands of local jurisdictions. The free, online delivery model could be replicated through SAMHSA's National Trauma Training Center.

### Canada: British Columbia's Provincial ACEs Response

**Description**: British Columbia integrated ACE science into provincial health planning, embedding trauma-informed practice requirements in healthcare, education, and child welfare systems. The province funds community-level resilience initiatives through Regional Health Authorities and provides free trauma training through online platforms. BC's approach emphasizes Indigenous-specific trauma responses, recognizing the distinct impacts of colonization and residential schools on First Nations communities.

**Results**: BC documented 18% reductions in foster care entries in communities with comprehensive TIC implementation, 25% improvements in school attendance for identified at-risk children, and 15% reductions in emergency department visits for mental health among youth (BC Ministry of Health, 2022). The province's Indigenous-specific approaches have been recognized internationally as models for culturally grounded trauma response.

**Applicability to U.S.**: BC's integration model demonstrates that cross-system coordination is achievable at the sub-national level. U.S. states could adopt similar approaches, with federal incentives and technical assistance. BC's Indigenous-specific models are particularly relevant for U.S. tribal and urban Indian communities.

## Pilot Programs and Demonstrations

- **San Francisco Unified School District (HEARTS Program)**: Implemented comprehensive TIC across 115 schools. Documented 87% reduction in suspensions over 10 years (2008-2018) with simultaneous improvement in academic outcomes (Dorado et al., 2016). The HEARTS model integrates whole-school TIC with tiered mental health services and has been replicated in multiple districts.
- **Tarrant County, Texas -- Trauma-Informed Care Consortium**: Cross-system initiative spanning child welfare, juvenile justice, healthcare, and schools. Reduced juvenile detention admissions by 44% over 5 years (2015-2020). Trained 10,000+ professionals across systems. Demonstrated that cross-system coordination produces greater impact than single-system implementation.
- **Philadelphia ACE Task Force**: City-wide initiative integrating ACE awareness into public health, education, and social services. Trained 30,000+ professionals and documented improved system coordination (Philadelphia ACE Task Force, 2022). Developed the expanded Philadelphia ACE Survey capturing community-level adversity.
- **Montefiore Medical Center (Bronx, NY)**: Integrated ACE screening into pediatric primary care for 25,000+ patients. 90% of families found screening acceptable; positive screens linked to coordinated referrals (Conn et al., 2018). Model has been replicated at other Federally Qualified Health Centers.
- **Cherokee Nation (Oklahoma)**: Implemented culturally grounded trauma-informed practices integrating Indigenous healing traditions with evidence-based clinical treatments. Documented improvements in treatment engagement and retention among Native American youth. Demonstrates feasibility of cultural adaptation.
- **READI Chicago**: Community violence intervention program combining cognitive behavioral therapy, transitional employment, and supportive services for men at highest risk of gun violence involvement. Documented 63% reduction in violent crime arrests among participants. Demonstrates integration of trauma treatment into violence prevention.

## Sequencing and Prioritization

### Quick Wins (0-2 years)

- Issue SAMHSA national TIC guidance and competency standards
- Establish CMS reimbursement for ACE/trauma screening in Medicaid and Medicare
- Expand telehealth flexibilities for evidence-based trauma treatment
- Include STS prevention requirements in federal grant conditions
- Fund NCTSN expansion and community resilience pilot programs
- Launch National Trauma Training Center (virtual capacity first)
- Enforce existing mental health parity requirements for trauma treatment

### Medium-term Reforms (2-5 years)

- Implement Trauma-Informed Schools Initiative with Title I supplement
- Scale National Trauma Training Center to all 50 states
- Expand NHSC loan repayment for trauma-specialized providers
- Establish DOJ Trauma-Informed Justice Initiative
- Bring school mental health staffing to recommended ratios in 50% of schools
- Expand MIECHV to serve 50% of eligible families
- Adopt accreditation requirements for trauma competency in health, education, and social work programs

### Long-term Transformation (5-10 years)

- Achieve universal trauma screening across healthcare, education, child welfare, and justice
- Train 200,000-300,000 evidence-based trauma therapists (doubling to tripling current capacity)
- Reduce ACE prevalence through upstream prevention investment
- Achieve recommended staffing ratios in 100% of public schools
- Document measurable reductions in incarceration, foster care entries, and healthcare utilization attributable to TIC adoption
- Transition to sustainable financing models integrated into standard benefit design

## Complementary Policies

- **Mental health parity enforcement**: Rigorous enforcement of the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act to ensure insurance coverage of trauma treatment; see [Mental Health](../01-overview.md)
- **Affordable housing**: Stable housing is a prerequisite for healing; housing instability is both a consequence and a cause of trauma; see [Housing](../../housing/01-overview.md)
- **Poverty reduction**: Economic security reduces ACE exposure; child tax credits, EITC expansion, and minimum wage increases are complementary investments that address the structural determinants of childhood adversity
- **Criminal justice reform**: Trauma-informed justice requires broader reforms in sentencing, policing, and reentry; see [Justice](../../justice/01-overview.md)
- **Substance use treatment**: Trauma and substance use are deeply intertwined; integrated treatment models that address both simultaneously are essential; see [Drug Policy](../../drugs/01-overview.md)
- **Child welfare reform**: Family First Prevention Services Act implementation, kinship care expansion, and placement stability improvements are essential complements to TIC in child welfare
- **Broadband expansion**: Telehealth delivery of trauma treatment depends on reliable internet access, particularly in rural and tribal communities; infrastructure investment is a precondition for equitable treatment access
