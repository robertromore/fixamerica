# Trauma-Informed Care: Current State

## Prevalence of Trauma and Adverse Childhood Experiences

The epidemiological burden of trauma in the United States is extensive and well-documented. The CDC's Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System (BRFSS), which added an ACE module adopted by 48 states as of 2023, provides the most comprehensive population-level data on childhood adversity. In parallel, the National Survey of Children's Health (NSCH), administered by the Health Resources and Services Administration (HRSA), provides real-time data on current childhood adversity, confirming that 46% of U.S. children ages 0-17 have experienced at least one ACE (NSCH, 2022). Together, these surveillance systems reveal that trauma is not a niche concern but a majority experience that shapes population health across the lifespan.

The original 10-item ACE questionnaire does not capture community-level adversity such as neighborhood violence, racism, poverty, or bullying -- a significant limitation that expanded frameworks are beginning to address (see "Expanded ACE Frameworks" below).

### Disparities in ACE Exposure

ACE exposure is not evenly distributed across the population. Structural inequities produce significant disparities in who experiences childhood adversity and who has access to healing resources. These disparities are not attributable to individual or cultural factors but to the cumulative effects of historical and ongoing systemic disadvantage -- residential segregation, discriminatory policing, wealth gaps, educational inequity, and unequal access to healthcare.

These disparities compound: individuals who belong to multiple marginalized groups (e.g., low-income, rural, racial minority) face intersecting disadvantages in both trauma exposure and treatment access. American Indian/Alaska Native communities face particularly concentrated adversity, reflecting centuries of colonization, forced removal, boarding school policies, and ongoing socioeconomic marginalization.

### Expanded ACE Frameworks

Researchers have increasingly recognized that the original 10-item ACE questionnaire, developed from a predominantly white, insured, middle-class sample at Kaiser Permanente in San Diego, does not capture the full range of adversity that shapes health and development. Several expanded frameworks have emerged:

- **Philadelphia ACE Survey (2013)**: Added community-level ACEs including witnessing neighborhood violence, experiencing racism, living in unsafe neighborhoods, experiencing bullying, and living in foster care. Studies found that 40% of Philadelphia adults reported community ACEs not captured by the original questionnaire (Public Health Management Corporation, 2013).
- **Positive Childhood Experiences (PCEs)**: Christina Bethell and colleagues (2019) developed a complementary framework measuring protective factors -- feeling safe, having supportive adults, belonging to a community, and having positive school experiences. Adults with high PCEs had 72% lower odds of depression and 48% lower odds of poor mental health even with high ACE scores (Bethell et al., JAMA Pediatrics, 2019).
- **Benevolent Childhood Experiences (BCEs)**: Narayan et al. (2018) developed a 10-item measure of positive childhood experiences that specifically predicts resilience in adults with high ACE scores.
- **Racial trauma**: Research by Robert Carter, Monnica Williams, and others has documented the traumatic effects of racial discrimination, microaggressions, and race-based violence on mental and physical health, effects not captured by the original ACE framework but increasingly recognized in clinical practice and research.

## Health Consequences of Trauma

### ACE-Associated Health Outcomes

The dose-response relationship between ACEs and poor health outcomes is among the most replicated findings in public health research. Each additional ACE incrementally increases risk across multiple disease categories. The biological mechanisms are well-characterized: chronic activation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis produces sustained cortisol elevation, chronic inflammation, and immune dysregulation, damaging cardiovascular, metabolic, and neurological systems over time.

| Health Outcome | Risk Increase (4+ ACEs vs. 0 ACEs) | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Depression | 4.6x | Felitti et al., 1998; Hughes et al., 2017 |
| Suicide attempt | 12.2x | Felitti et al., 1998 |
| Alcoholism | 7.4x | Felitti et al., 1998 |
| Illicit drug use | 4.7x | Felitti et al., 1998 |
| Ischemic heart disease | 2.2x | Felitti et al., 1998 |
| Cancer | 1.9x | Felitti et al., 1998; Brown et al., 2013 |
| Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease | 3.9x | Felitti et al., 1998 |
| Smoking (current) | 2.2x | CDC, 2021 |
| Obesity | 1.6x | CDC, 2021 |
| PTSD (lifetime) | 7.3x | Koenen et al., 2017 |
| Autoimmune disease | 2.7x | Dube et al., 2009 |
| Diabetes (Type 2) | 1.6x | Huang et al., 2015 |
| Life expectancy reduction | 19.6 years (6+ ACEs) | Brown et al., 2009 |

### Mental Health Burden

Beyond the ACE-specific dose-response data, the overall burden of trauma-related mental health conditions is substantial:

- **PTSD**: 6.1% lifetime prevalence among U.S. adults (~15.5 million people). Among specific populations, prevalence is much higher: 11-20% of Iraq/Afghanistan veterans, 23% of sexual assault survivors, and 28% of survivors of mass violence events (National Center for PTSD, 2023).
- **Complex PTSD**: Recognized by the ICD-11 in 2022, C-PTSD is estimated to affect 1-8% of the general population and up to 50% of individuals in residential treatment settings. C-PTSD includes disturbances in self-organization, affect regulation, and relational capacity beyond the core PTSD symptoms of re-experiencing, avoidance, and hyperarousal (Cloitre et al., 2019).
- **Developmental trauma**: Children exposed to chronic interpersonal trauma (abuse, neglect, domestic violence) often present with symptoms that span multiple diagnostic categories -- ADHD, oppositional defiant disorder, conduct disorder, anxiety, depression -- leading to misdiagnosis and inappropriate treatment. Bessel van der Kolk's proposed "Developmental Trauma Disorder" diagnosis, while not yet included in the DSM, has gained clinical recognition.

ACEs contribute to both of these conditions, meaning the $748 billion figure includes overlapping costs while also capturing unique costs in child welfare, criminal justice, and education not attributable to any single disease.

## Trauma-Informed Care Implementation by System

### Healthcare System

Healthcare represents the most advanced but still incomplete sector for TIC adoption. Integration varies widely by setting and specialty. The Veterans Health Administration (VA) leads in systematic implementation, having rolled out evidence-based trauma treatments (CPT and PE) to over 10,000 clinicians since 2007 and achieving universal PTSD screening in primary care through the PC-PTSD-5. Civilian healthcare systems lag significantly, with most implementation occurring through piecemeal local initiatives rather than coordinated system transformation.

### Education System

Schools represent a critical setting for trauma identification and intervention, given that children spend approximately 1,000 hours per year in school. Schools also play a de facto mental health system role, particularly in communities where clinical services are scarce -- an estimated 70-80% of children who receive mental health services access them through their school (SAMHSA, 2014). Yet schools' capacity to fulfill this role is severely constrained by staffing shortages and the absence of mandated trauma-informed frameworks in most states.

The ESSER (Elementary and Secondary School Emergency Relief) funds allocated during the COVID-19 pandemic provided an unprecedented but time-limited injection of resources for school mental health. As these funds expire through 2024-2025, many districts face "fiscal cliffs" that threaten positions and programs created with pandemic funding. The sustainability challenge underscores the need for permanent federal investment in school mental health staffing and trauma-informed frameworks.

### Justice System

The justice system both encounters and sometimes inflicts trauma. Trauma-informed justice practices remain in early stages of adoption. The fundamental tension is that correctional environments -- characterized by confinement, loss of autonomy, potential for violence, and separation from supportive relationships -- are inherently antithetical to trauma recovery principles. Implementing TIC in justice settings requires not just adding therapeutic programming but reconceptualizing the relationship between system authority and individual healing.

### Child Welfare System

The child welfare system is perhaps the most trauma-proximate public institution, yet its own practices can compound the adversity children experience. The system's paradox is acute: children are removed from harmful environments only to enter a system where placement instability, severed attachments, congregate care settings, and inadequate therapeutic services can inflict additional harm. The Family First Prevention Services Act (2018) represented a significant policy shift toward prevention and evidence-based treatment, but implementation has been uneven across states.

## SAMHSA Framework and Federal Activity

### SAMHSA Six Principles of Trauma-Informed Care

SAMHSA's 2014 framework provides the most widely adopted definition of TIC. The six principles are:

1. **Safety**: Physical and psychological safety for staff and clients
2. **Trustworthiness and Transparency**: Operations conducted with transparency to build trust
3. **Peer Support**: Recognition of the healing value of shared experience
4. **Collaboration and Mutuality**: Leveling power differences between staff and clients
5. **Empowerment, Voice, and Choice**: Prioritizing individual agency and self-determination
6. **Cultural, Historical, and Gender Issues**: Incorporating cultural responsiveness and addressing historical trauma

SAMHSA also articulates the "Four R's" of a trauma-informed approach: **Realize** the widespread impact of trauma, **Recognize** the signs and symptoms of trauma in clients and staff, **Respond** by integrating knowledge about trauma into policies and practices, and **Resist re-traumatization** by actively avoiding practices that could retraumatize individuals. This framework is organizationally agnostic -- designed to be applied in any setting, from hospitals to courtrooms to classrooms -- which is both its strength (broad applicability) and its weakness (insufficient specificity for implementation in any particular sector).

Moreover, federal funding is fragmented across at least 12 agencies with separate funding streams, reporting requirements, and administrative cultures, producing coordination challenges that a 2021 GAO report characterized as involving "limited coordination, potential duplication, and unclear effectiveness" (GAO-21-437).

### Treatment Gaps and Barriers

Despite the strong evidence base for trauma treatments, significant gaps persist between what research demonstrates and what patients actually receive:

- **Treatment dropout**: Approximately 30-40% of individuals who begin evidence-based trauma therapy do not complete treatment, with higher dropout rates among racial minorities, individuals with complex trauma, and those with co-occurring substance use disorders (Najavits, 2015).
- **Provider fidelity**: Studies of community-based therapists delivering CPT and PE find that adherence to treatment protocols is often poor, with therapists modifying, abbreviating, or abandoning evidence-based protocols due to discomfort with trauma exposure components (Becker et al., 2004).
- **Complex trauma**: The evidence base is strongest for single-incident PTSD; individuals with chronic, early-onset, interpersonal trauma (the population most affected by ACEs) often present with complex symptom profiles that require phased treatment approaches not fully captured by existing clinical guidelines.
- **Medication-first defaults**: Despite clinical guidelines recommending psychotherapy as first-line treatment for PTSD, many patients receive psychotropic medications (particularly SSRIs and benzodiazepines) without being offered evidence-based psychotherapy, reflecting both workforce shortages and pharmaceutical industry influence on prescribing practices.

## Recent Trends

### Developments in the Last 5-10 Years

- **Post-COVID trauma surge**: The COVID-19 pandemic produced a documented increase in trauma exposure, with childhood abuse reports increasing 8.5% and youth mental health emergency department visits increasing 31% between 2019 and 2023 (CDC, 2023). The pandemic also exacerbated existing disparities, with low-income communities and communities of color experiencing disproportionate losses of life, employment, and social stability.
- **ACEs science expansion**: Researchers have expanded beyond the original 10 ACEs to include community-level adversity (racism, poverty, neighborhood violence), sometimes called "expanded ACEs" or "PCEs" (positive childhood experiences). The Philadelphia ACE Survey, Bethell's PCE framework, and racial trauma research represent significant expansions of the original construct.
- **Telehealth expansion**: Virtual delivery of CPT, PE, and EMDR demonstrated comparable efficacy to in-person delivery, expanding access in underserved areas (Morland et al., 2020). The VA reported that telehealth trauma therapy visits increased 1,500% between 2019 and 2022, with no decline in treatment outcomes.
- **International Classification of Diseases recognition**: ICD-11 (2022) introduced Complex PTSD as a formal diagnosis, recognizing trauma responses that extend beyond traditional PTSD, including disturbances in self-organization, affect regulation, and interpersonal functioning.
- **Bipartisan legislative interest**: TIC legislation has passed in both red and blue states, with 26 states enacting some form of TIC-related law by 2023. The framing of TIC as child protection, public safety, and fiscal responsibility has enabled cross-partisan support.
- **Psychedelic-assisted therapy research**: MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD completed Phase 3 clinical trials showing 67-71% remission rates in treatment-resistant PTSD (Mitchell et al., 2021). FDA regulatory decisions on MDMA and psilocybin-assisted therapy are pending and could significantly expand the treatment landscape.
- **988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline**: The July 2022 launch of 988 as a national crisis line, replacing the 10-digit National Suicide Prevention Lifeline number, increased crisis call volume by 50% in the first year, revealing both the demand for crisis services and the infrastructure challenges in meeting it.

## Emerging Challenges

- **Implementation fidelity**: Many organizations claim to be "trauma-informed" without evidence of meaningful practice change or outcome improvement, creating a risk of TIC becoming a diluted buzzword. No standardized measurement tool exists for assessing organizational TIC implementation, though several self-assessment instruments (SAMHSA TIC Assessment, ARTIC scale, TICAT) are in development.
- **Secondary traumatic stress**: Frontline workers in trauma-proximate fields (child welfare, emergency response, education) experience high rates of burnout and secondary trauma, with turnover rates of 30-40% in child welfare (Casey Family Programs, 2022). The COVID-19 pandemic intensified secondary traumatic stress across healthcare and education workforces, with healthcare worker burnout rates reaching 50-60% by 2023 (National Academy of Medicine).
- **Workforce pipeline**: The mental health workforce is not growing fast enough to meet demand, with projected shortages of 31,091 psychologists and 12,040 psychiatrists by 2030 (HRSA, 2022). The pipeline problem is compounded by the length of clinical training (7-12 years for psychologists and psychiatrists), limited training program capacity, and the cost of graduate education.
- **Cultural adaptation**: Evidence-based treatments were predominantly developed and validated with white, English-speaking populations, raising questions about cultural appropriateness for diverse communities. Cultural adaptations of CPT, PE, and TF-CBT exist but have limited dissemination, and Indigenous, Black, and Latino communities have expressed preference for culturally grounded healing practices alongside or instead of Western clinical models.
- **Measurement challenges**: No consensus exists on how to measure whether an organization has truly become "trauma-informed" versus merely adopting the language. The field lacks standardized outcome metrics that go beyond process measures (training hours completed) to capture genuine system transformation (improved client outcomes, reduced re-traumatization, workforce sustainability).
- **Fiscal cliff from pandemic funding**: As ESSER, ARPA, and other pandemic-era funding streams expire, programs and positions created with temporary funds face elimination, potentially reversing gains made during the 2020-2024 period in school mental health, crisis services, and community-based treatment.
- **AI and digital mental health**: The rapid expansion of AI-based mental health tools (chatbots, digital therapeutics, automated screening) raises questions about quality, safety, and equity in trauma care. While digital tools may expand access, they lack the relational foundation that trauma recovery requires and may produce harm if used without clinical oversight.
