# Mental Health Policy: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes U.S. mental health policy through nine distinct political perspectives, evaluating current state assessments, root cause analyses, proposed solutions, and legislation. Mental health policy is unusual in the American political landscape because it generates broad rhetorical consensus -- virtually every political perspective agrees that mental health is important -- while producing deep practical disagreements about funding levels, government authority, the role of insurance mandates, the scope of involuntary treatment, and whether the system's failures are caused by too much or too little government intervention.

The analysis in the parent-level mental health policy files concludes that the U.S. mental health system is in crisis, that the treatment gap is driven by chronic underfunding, workforce shortages, insurance parity violations, and the unfinished legacy of deinstitutionalization, and that closing the gap requires rigorous parity enforcement, major workforce expansion, a national crisis system, youth-focused prevention, integrated behavioral health in primary care, serious mental illness reform, Medicaid modernization, and data-driven accountability. Each perspective is evaluated on the degree to which it agrees with these assessments and the proposed solutions, including the two major federal bills proposed in the legislation chapter: the Mental Health Parity Enforcement Act and the Mental Health Crisis System Act, as well as the two model state bills: the Model State Mental Health Workforce Act and the Model State 988 Crisis System Funding Act.

### Engagement Consistency (1-5)

This metric assesses how reliably a perspective's stated positions align with its underlying principles, and how stable those positions are when concerns are addressed. Higher scores indicate more predictable negotiating partners.

| Level | Label | Indicators |
|-------|-------|------------|
| 5 | Highly Consistent | Stated positions reflect underlying principles; positions stable when concerns addressed |
| 4 | Mostly Consistent | Principles generally align with positions; minor strategic flexibility |
| 3 | Mixed Consistency | Some positions principled, others reflect coalition pressures or unstated concerns |
| 2 | Low Consistency | Positions shift frequently; stated concerns differ from underlying priorities |
| 1 | Unpredictable | Positions appear driven primarily by opposition dynamics rather than principles |

**Coalition-Building Considerations:**

- Alignment gap: When stated concerns differ from underlying priorities, addressing stated concerns may not secure support
- Position stability: Perspectives with shifting positions require ongoing negotiation
- Principle consistency: Perspectives that apply principles uniformly are more predictable partners
- Internal diversity: Some perspectives contain factions with different priorities

## Perspective Analyses

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 3/5 - Mixed Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | States support for mental health treatment and parity, but consistently votes to underfund programs and opposes the regulatory mandates necessary to make parity effective |
| Principle consistency | Inconsistent application of limited government principles -- supports government-mandated involuntary treatment and criminal justice approaches but resists government-mandated insurance enforcement and workforce spending |
| Goalpost stability | Has shifted meaningfully since school shootings and the opioid crisis brought mental health to the center of public debate; increasingly willing to fund crisis services and treatment but still resists systemic reform |
| Zero-sum behavior | Moderate -- willing to fund discrete mental health programs (988, school safety) but often conditions support on avoiding broader insurance and Medicaid mandates |

**Justification:** The conservative position on mental health has evolved significantly since 2012, when Sandy Hook and subsequent mass shootings put mental health squarely in the gun policy debate. Conservatives have increasingly embraced mental health rhetoric -- "it's a mental health problem, not a gun problem" -- but this rhetoric has not consistently translated into the funding and regulatory enforcement needed to fix the system. The Bipartisan Safer Communities Act (2022) represented genuine conservative engagement, yet core tensions remain: conservatives resist the government spending levels and insurance mandates that the analysis identifies as necessary. The strongest conservative consistency is on involuntary treatment, where the law-and-order and family-protection impulses align with actual policy positions.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Republican support for the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act (2022, Pub. L. 117-159): 15 Republican senators joined to pass mental health and school safety funding after Uvalde
- Heritage Foundation, "Addressing the Mental Health Crisis" (2023): Emphasized faith-based approaches, family support, and state flexibility while opposing federal spending mandates
- Republican governors expanding crisis services (e.g., Texas 988 implementation, Ohio opioid response): Demonstrates genuine engagement at state level, though often at lower funding levels than experts recommend

#### Alternative Proposals

- **Market-based parity**: Allow insurers to compete across state lines with parity-compliant plans, using market competition rather than enforcement mandates to achieve parity
- **Faith-based mental health initiative**: Expand federal support for church-based counseling, recovery programs, and community support networks as a supplement to clinical services
- **Deregulate telehealth**: Remove all state-level restrictions on interstate telehealth practice for behavioral health, allowing providers to see patients across state lines without additional licensure
- **Family-centered youth policy**: Strengthen parental notification and consent requirements for youth mental health treatment; fund family counseling and parenting programs rather than school-based universal screening
- **State block grant flexibility**: Convert federal mental health funding into flexible block grants allowing states to allocate resources based on local priorities rather than federal mandates
- **Expand involuntary treatment**: Lower the threshold for civil commitment and AOT in all states, with judicial oversight, to reach people with serious mental illness before they become homeless or incarcerated

#### Coalition Potential

- **Natural allies:** Religious Right (shared emphasis on faith-based services, family values, involuntary treatment for SMI), Populist (shared concern about elite institutions, support for community-based approaches)
- **Potential bridges:** Centrist (crisis system investment, bipartisan mental health funding as demonstrated by Bipartisan Safer Communities Act), Libertarian (scope-of-practice deregulation, telehealth expansion)
- **Unlikely partners:** Progressive (fundamental disagreement on funding levels, insurance regulation, and structural analysis), Democratic Socialist (disagreement on government role and public system design)

### Liberal Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Genuine commitment to expanding access, enforcing parity, and addressing disparities; voting record generally aligns with stated positions |
| Principle consistency | Consistent application of government-as-problem-solver framework to mental health; some tension between desire for universal systems and willingness to work within the existing private insurance framework |
| Goalpost stability | Positions have moved steadily toward more comprehensive reform, from supporting the original MHPAEA to advocating for parity enforcement, 988 investment, and youth mental health programs |
| Zero-sum behavior | Low -- willing to incorporate conservative concerns (faith-based programs, involuntary treatment with safeguards) into broader reform packages |

**Justification:** The liberal perspective on mental health policy is well-developed and largely consistent. Liberals have championed parity legislation from the original 1996 Mental Health Parity Act through the 2008 MHPAEA and its 2023 strengthening. The main inconsistency is the gap between legislative ambition and follow-through on funding: Democratic-controlled Congresses have passed mental health legislation but often failed to appropriate the funding levels needed for implementation. Some liberals also struggle with the involuntary treatment question, caught between progressive civil liberties advocates and the practical reality that some people with serious mental illness need intervention they will not seek voluntarily.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Democratic Party platform (2024): Endorses mental health parity enforcement, 988 investment, school-based services, and insurance reform
- Sen. Chris Murphy and Rep. Mark DeSaulnier, mental health legislative records: Consistent sponsorship of parity, crisis services, and youth mental health bills

### Progressive Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Genuine commitment to treating mental health as a public good requiring universal public investment; positions consistently reflect structural critique |
| Principle consistency | Highly consistent application of structural analysis -- connects mental health system failures to economic inequality, racial injustice, and corporate power |
| Goalpost stability | Stable positions that have moved mainstream discourse over time; progressives advocated for parity enforcement and crisis system investment before these became bipartisan priorities |
| Zero-sum behavior | Moderate -- may resist incremental reforms as insufficient, potentially blocking achievable progress in pursuit of comprehensive transformation |

**Justification:** The progressive perspective provides the most comprehensive structural analysis of mental health system failure, connecting it to broader patterns of economic inequality, corporate power, racial disparities, and the commodification of healthcare. Progressives have been consistent advocates for mental health investment, parity enforcement, and reducing the criminalization of mental illness. The main weakness is a tendency to frame every mental health reform as inadequate unless it includes fundamental changes to the insurance system, which can delay achievable improvements. Progressives may also underweight the role of cultural factors, individual behavior, and the genuine complexity of serious mental illness in favor of structural explanations.

**Evidence for assessment:**

#### Alternative Proposals

- **Medicare for All as mental health reform**: Eliminate the parity enforcement problem entirely by making all healthcare coverage public, with mental health as a fully integrated benefit
- **Public mental health service system**: Create a publicly owned and operated mental health system modeled on the VA, providing free, universal mental health care outside the insurance framework
- **Social determinants-first approach**: Invest in housing, income support, education, and employment as the most effective mental health prevention strategy

### Libertarian Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Principled opposition to government mandates and spending in mental health, consistent with broader anti-government philosophy |
| Principle consistency | Mostly consistent -- opposes insurance mandates and government spending programs while supporting deregulation of provider scope of practice and removal of barriers to voluntary treatment |
| Goalpost stability | Stable positions on government overreach, though the severe consequences of untreated SMI (homelessness, violence, incarceration) create some tension with pure anti-government positions |
| Zero-sum behavior | Low -- willing to support any reform that reduces government authority, regardless of origin |

**Justification:** The libertarian perspective on mental health is more nuanced than on drug policy because mental health involves a genuine tension between individual liberty and the capacity-reducing nature of some conditions. Libertarians consistently oppose government insurance mandates, federal spending programs, and involuntary treatment. However, the most intellectually honest libertarian thinkers acknowledge that serious mental illness (particularly psychosis with anosognosia) presents a hard case: when a person cannot recognize their own illness, the libertarian framework of voluntary, informed consent faces a genuine philosophical challenge. The main weakness is that libertarian solutions -- deregulation, market competition, voluntary charity -- are insufficient to address a crisis driven by market failure (insurance companies profiting from denying coverage) and public goods problems (crisis services, workforce training).

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Cato Institute, "Health Care Beyond Government" (2024): Argues for deregulating the mental health marketplace, including scope-of-practice reform and interstate telehealth
- Reason Foundation, mental health coverage: Criticizes government programs for inefficiency while acknowledging the scale of the crisis
- Libertarian Party platform (2024): Opposes government mandates in healthcare, including insurance coverage mandates

#### Alternative Proposals

- **Full scope-of-practice deregulation**: Remove all state-level restrictions on who can provide mental health services, allowing market competition to expand supply and reduce costs
- **Interstate telehealth deregulation**: Eliminate all barriers to cross-state telehealth practice, creating a national marketplace for mental health providers
- **Health savings account expansion**: Allow tax-free savings for mental health services, making individuals direct purchasers of care rather than dependents of insurance bureaucracies
- **Eliminate Certificate of Need laws**: Remove state laws that restrict the construction of psychiatric facilities, allowing market forces to determine supply
- **Voluntary mutual aid networks**: Support private, community-based mental health support systems (including faith-based and peer-based models) rather than government programs
- **Repeal, don't enforce, insurance mandates**: Replace the MHPAEA with a transparent marketplace where plans compete on mental health coverage quality

### Constitutionalist Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Genuine focus on constitutional structure, federalism, and enumerated powers; positions flow logically from these principles |
| Principle consistency | Mostly consistent -- applies federalism principles to mental health by favoring state authority over federal mandates; occasional tension when federal enforcement aligns with preferred policy outcomes (e.g., involuntary treatment) |

## Compromise Proposals

### Compromise 1: Bipartisan Mental Health Crisis and Youth Protection Act

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Progressive + Populist + Centrist + Religious Right + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** Nearly all perspectives agree that the 988 crisis system needs full funding, that mobile crisis teams should be available to divert people from jails and emergency rooms, and that youth mental health demands urgent action -- including regulation of social media platforms harming children. These are the two highest-support reform categories across all perspectives.

**Policy Description:**

A two-part bill combining: (1) National crisis system completion -- mandatory Medicaid coverage of crisis services (call, mobile, stabilization), $5 billion in infrastructure grants over 5 years, state crisis system planning requirements, and 60/90-minute mobile crisis response time standards; and (2) Youth mental health and social media protection -- federal funding to achieve the 1:250 student-to-counselor ratio, universal mental health screening at pediatric well-child visits (with parental notification and consent), FTC authority to regulate manipulative design features targeting minors (infinite scroll, autoplay, push notifications for children under 16), age verification requirements, and a social media youth mental health trust fund financed by an assessment on platforms with more than 50 million users. Faith-based crisis response and youth counseling programs are eligible for all funding on equal terms with secular providers.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Conservative | Crisis services reduce law enforcement burden and incarceration costs; youth protection aligns with family values; no insurance mandates or parity enforcement |
| Liberal | Major investment in crisis infrastructure and school-based services; social media regulation protects children; Medicaid crisis benefit expands access |
| Progressive | Public infrastructure investment; addresses youth mental health structurally; social media accountability |
| Populist | Protects working-class communities and children; holds social media companies accountable; community-based crisis services |
| Centrist | Evidence-based, bipartisan approach with strong precedent (Bipartisan Safer Communities Act model); measurable outcomes |
| Religious Right | Protects children from social media harms; includes faith-based providers; parental notification in screening; youth-focused |
| Democratic Socialist | Public crisis infrastructure; school-based services as public goods; corporate accountability for social media companies |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Accepting mandatory Medicaid crisis benefit and federal spending program |
| Liberal | Deferring parity enforcement, Medicaid modernization, and integrated care to separate legislation |
| Progressive | Accepting incremental reform rather than comprehensive system transformation |
| Populist | Accepting federal program design rather than purely community-controlled services |
| Centrist | Moving faster on social media regulation than evidence may fully warrant |
| Religious Right | Accepting universal screening at well-child visits (with parental protections) |
| Democratic Socialist | Accepting crisis and youth reforms within existing insurance framework rather than single-payer |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 7/9 perspectives (Libertarian and Constitutionalist excluded due to opposition to federal mandates and spending)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 4/7 coalition members rated 4+ consistency (Liberal, Progressive, Centrist, Democratic Socialist)
- **Durability:** High -- crisis services and youth mental health have stable, broad-based support unlikely to erode; social media regulation has growing momentum
- **Implementation Path:** Bipartisan bill building on the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act (2022); introduced with both Republican and Democratic sponsors; crisis provisions build on American Rescue Plan Act Medicaid authority; youth provisions build on existing Title I education framework

### Compromise 2: Mental Health Workforce and Access Act

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Progressive + Centrist + Religious Right + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** All perspectives agree that there are too few mental health providers and that scope-of-practice restrictions and licensing barriers contribute to the shortage. Workforce expansion is the reform category with the broadest support at the lowest ideological cost, because it does not require choosing between government mandates and market solutions -- it includes both.

**Policy Description:**

A comprehensive workforce bill combining: (1) Scope-of-practice reform incentives -- federal grants to states that adopt full practice authority for psychiatric nurse practitioners and prescriptive authority for psychologists; (2) National Mental Health Service Corps -- full tuition and loan repayment for providers committing to 4 years in Mental Health Professional Shortage Areas; (3) GME expansion -- 1,000 new psychiatry residency positions funded over 5 years; (4) Peer support workforce -- 200 certified peer support specialist training programs with Medicaid reimbursement; (5) Telehealth expansion -- interstate practice compact for behavioral health providers; and (6) Faith-based and community counselor recognition -- federal standards for faith-integrated counselors to participate in Medicaid and grant-funded programs. All provisions include rural and underserved community targeting.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Conservative | Scope-of-practice deregulation aligns with market principles; state-level incentives respect federalism; faith-based counselors recognized |
| Liberal | Major workforce investment closing provider gap; Service Corps targets underserved communities; GME expansion addresses long-term pipeline |
| Progressive | Workforce investment addresses systemic shortage; peer support empowers lived experience; targets underserved communities |
| Centrist | Evidence-based, bipartisan workforce reform with proven models (NHSC); practical and measurable |
| Religious Right | Faith-based counselors formally recognized and funded; peer support includes faith-based recovery specialists |
| Democratic Socialist | Public investment in workforce; peer support as democratized mental health; targets geographic equity |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Accepting significant federal spending ($3 billion annually) on workforce programs |
| Liberal | Accepting faith-based counselor recognition without requiring identical clinical training standards |
| Progressive | Accepting market-oriented scope-of-practice reform alongside government programs; deferring worker protections |
| Centrist | Accepting GME expansion costs and faith-based counselor provisions |
| Religious Right | Accepting that faith-based counselors must meet federal competency standards |
| Democratic Socialist | Accepting workforce reform within existing market system rather than public employment model |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 6/9 perspectives (Libertarian supports only deregulatory elements; Constitutionalist prefers state-only approach; Populist supports but at lower intensity)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 4/6 coalition members rated 4+ consistency (Liberal, Progressive, Centrist, Democratic Socialist)
- **Durability:** High -- workforce shortage is worsening and reform has no organized opposition comparable to insurance industry lobbying against parity
- **Implementation Path:** Building on existing NHSC authority and bipartisan support for workforce investment; scope-of-practice reform proceeds at state level with federal incentives; GME expansion requires amending the Balanced Budget Act cap

### Compromise 3: Parity Enforcement and Medicaid Mental Health Modernization Act

**Bridges:** Liberal + Progressive + Centrist + Populist + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** A narrower but committed coalition agrees that insurance companies must be held accountable for parity violations and that Medicaid -- the single largest mental health payer -- must be modernized to cover psychiatric care adequately. The IMD exclusion reform has bipartisan appeal because it removes a federal restriction rather than imposing a new mandate.

**Policy Description:**

A two-part bill combining: (1) Parity enforcement -- tripling DOL enforcement staff, mandatory comparative analyses, civil monetary penalties (up to $10,000 per violation per day), private right of action with attorney's fees, and annual public reporting of behavioral health denial rates by all commercial plans; and (2) Medicaid modernization -- repeal of the IMD exclusion for stays up to 60 days with quality safeguards, increase of Medicaid behavioral health reimbursement rates to 80% of Medicare (with enhanced 80/20 FMAP for states meeting the target), and permanent authorization of Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinics (CCBHCs) with mandatory Medicaid coverage and prospective payment methodology.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Liberal | Achieves genuine parity enforcement; modernizes Medicaid as the mental health backbone; expands CCBHCs permanently |
| Progressive | Holds insurance industry accountable; strengthens public mental health infrastructure; expands Medicaid capacity |
| Centrist | Evidence-based Medicaid reforms (CCBHCs proven effective); phased parity enforcement with clear standards |
| Populist | Punishes insurance companies for denying mental health claims; improves care for working families on Medicaid |
| Democratic Socialist | Strengthens public insurance system; corporate accountability; expands community-based public care |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Liberal | Accepting phased enforcement rather than immediate full implementation |
| Progressive | Accepting parity enforcement within private insurance rather than single-payer replacement |
| Centrist | Accepting private right of action and aggressive penalties (prefers collaborative compliance first) |
| Populist | Accepting complex regulatory mechanisms rather than simple punitive measures |
| Democratic Socialist | Accepting reform of private insurance system rather than replacement; Medicaid reform rather than Medicare for All |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 5/9 perspectives (Conservative, Libertarian, Constitutionalist, and Religious Right excluded -- first three oppose insurance mandates and federal spending expansion; Religious Right cautious about mandates on religious employers)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 4/5 coalition members rated 4+ consistency (Liberal, Progressive, Centrist, Democratic Socialist)
- **Durability:** Moderate-high -- parity enforcement is politically defensible (enforcing existing law), and Medicaid reforms have strong evidence base. Insurance industry lobbying is the primary threat to durability
- **Implementation Path:** Parity enforcement can build on the 2023 MHPAEA proposed rules; IMD exclusion reform has bipartisan support (45 states already seeking waivers); CCBHC authorization can build on existing SAMHSA demonstration. May require reconciliation for Medicaid spending provisions

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The most productive coalition for mental health reform is the **Liberal-Centrist core**, supplemented by **Conservative and Religious Right** engagement on crisis services and youth mental health, and **Progressive and Democratic Socialist** support for stronger investment levels. This coalition has already produced results: the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act (2022) included significant mental health funding with 15 Republican senators supporting. The strategy should be to begin with the broadest-support reforms (crisis system, youth mental health, workforce) and build trust and momentum for the narrower-coalition reforms (parity enforcement, Medicaid modernization).

### Key Obstacles

1. **Insurance industry lobbying** is the most powerful obstacle to parity enforcement. AHIP and its members spend $170+ million annually on lobbying and have successfully blocked effective enforcement for nearly two decades. This is the reform with the deepest industry opposition and the most resources arrayed against it.

2. **Federal spending levels** required for comprehensive reform ($20+ billion annually for workforce, crisis, and Medicaid modernization) exceed what most conservatives and many centrists will support in a single bill. Sequencing reforms to demonstrate cost savings from early investments is essential.

3. **Involuntary treatment** generates genuine moral conflict between the civil liberties concerns of progressives and libertarians and the practical concerns of conservatives, families, and law enforcement. This is not a disagreement that can be resolved through compromise -- it requires careful policy design with robust safeguards.

4. **State variability** means that even federal reforms will be implemented differently across 50 states. The states with the worst mental health systems are often the most resistant to federal mandates, creating a geographic equity problem that block grants alone cannot solve.

### Low Consistency Partners

The **Populist perspective** (2/5 consistency) is the most challenging coalition partner in mental health reform. Populist positions oscillate between anti-insurance-company anger (which supports parity enforcement) and anti-government sentiment (which opposes the enforcement mechanisms). Strategies for managing populist unpredictability include:

- Frame reforms around protecting families and communities, not expanding government
- Lead with anti-insurance-company accountability, which is the most consistent populist position
- Use personal stories of insurance denial from working families to build populist support for enforcement
- Avoid technocratic language; frame reforms in plain terms about what they will change for ordinary people

The **Conservative and Religious Right perspectives** (both 3/5 consistency) are more reliable on crisis services and youth mental health than on parity enforcement and Medicaid expansion. The most effective approach is to build on the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act precedent and frame reforms in terms these perspectives embrace: protecting children, supporting families, reducing crime, and allowing faith-based organizations to participate.

### Recommended Approach

The recommended legislative strategy is a **sequenced, trust-building approach**:

1. **Year 1-2:** Bipartisan Mental Health Crisis and Youth Protection Act (Compromise 1) -- broadest coalition (7/9), highest urgency, builds trust. This is the "quick win" that demonstrates bipartisan commitment and delivers visible results.

2. **Year 2-3:** Mental Health Workforce and Access Act (Compromise 2) -- broad coalition (6/9), addresses the structural bottleneck that prevents all other reforms from working. Without providers, no expansion of services is possible.

3. **Year 3-5:** Parity Enforcement and Medicaid Modernization Act (Compromise 3) -- narrower but committed coalition (5/9), addresses the deepest structural failures in the insurance and Medicaid systems. By this point, crisis system and workforce reforms have built public confidence and political capital.

4. **Year 5+:** Comprehensive integrated behavioral health expansion and data accountability as political conditions permit, building on the infrastructure and workforce created by the first three reforms.

This sequencing moves from highest-consensus to most-contested reforms, building coalitions incrementally and demonstrating results at each stage. Early wins on crisis services and youth mental health create political capital for the harder fights on parity enforcement and Medicaid modernization.
