# Prison Labor Reform: Stakeholders

## Overview

Prison labor reform involves numerous stakeholders with varying levels of power, different interests, and distinct roles to play in achieving change. This chapter maps the stakeholder landscape and analyzes each group's position, influence, and potential role in reform.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Incarcerated Workers

The people most directly affected by prison labor policies.

**Population**: ~800,000 incarcerated workers

| Characteristic | Data |
|---------------|------|
| Percentage working | 65-80% of prison population |
| Racial composition | ~38% Black, ~21% Hispanic, ~35% White |
| Average age | 36 years |
| Education level | 70%+ lack high school diploma/GED |
| Pre-incarceration employment | Many chronically unemployed |

**Stakes**:

- Immediate working conditions and safety
- Wages and ability to support families
- Skills development and post-release employment
- Dignity and freedom from forced labor
- Good-time credits often tied to work

**Power**: Low formal power, but collective action (strikes) demonstrates potential

**Reform Position**: Strongly support reform, lead organizing efforts

### Families of Incarcerated People

Bear enormous financial and emotional costs of the current system.

**Population**: Estimated 2.7 million children with incarcerated parent; millions more family members

| Impact | Estimated Cost |
|--------|---------------|
| Lost family income | $15,000-30,000/year per incarcerated person |
| Communication costs | $500-2,000/year |
| Commissary support | $500-1,500/year |
| Visitation costs | $500-2,000/year |
| Total family financial burden | Billions annually |

**Stakes**:

- Financial strain from supporting incarcerated loved ones
- Emotional toll of exploitative conditions
- Futures of returning family members
- Children's wellbeing and stability

**Power**: Growing advocacy presence, compelling moral voice

**Reform Position**: Strongly support reform

### Formerly Incarcerated People

Unique perspective from lived experience, directly affected by skills gaps.

**Population**: ~600,000 released annually; 70+ million with criminal records total

**Stakes**:

- Post-release employment (skills from prison work often untransferable)
- Occupational licensing barriers
- Criminal record discrimination
- Debt accumulated during incarceration
- Advocating for those still inside

**Power**: Growing political voice, credibility from experience

**Reform Position**: Generally strongly support reform

### Correctional Officers and Staff

Direct involvement in implementing any reforms.

**Population**: ~420,000 correctional officers nationwide

| Characteristic | Data |
|---------------|------|
| Average salary | $45,000-65,000 |
| Union membership | High in many states |
| Turnover rate | 30-50% annually |
| Injury rate | Among highest of any profession |

**Stakes**:

- Job security
- Working conditions and safety
- Institutional stability
- Retirement benefits

**Power**: Significant through unions and political connections

**Reform Position**: Mixed; often resistant to changes perceived as reducing control

### Prison Administrators

Responsible for facility operations and implementing policy.

**Stakes**:

- Managing institutional operations
- Budget management
- Security and safety
- Political/career considerations

**Power**: Substantial discretion in implementation

**Reform Position**: Varies widely; some reform-minded, many resist disruption

## Government Stakeholders

### State Departments of Corrections

**Population**: 50 state DOCs + DC and territories

**Stakes**:

- Budget management (prison labor subsidizes operations)
- Institutional control
- Political accountability
- Recidivism rates and public safety

**Power**: High within state systems; control implementation

**Reform Position**: Generally resistant; budget concerns paramount

### Federal Bureau of Prisons

**Population**: ~130,000 federal prisoners

**Stakes**:

- UNICOR operations (~$500 million revenue)
- Federal facility management
- Policy implementation

**Power**: Significant federal influence

**Reform Position**: Historically resistant to wage/labor reforms

### State Legislators

Control prison labor policy in their states.

| Reform-Minded | Resistant |
|---------------|-----------|
| Criminal justice reform caucuses | "Tough on crime" members |
| Progressive Democrats | Many Republicans, some Democrats |
| Some libertarian Republicans | Those with prison industry constituents |

**Stakes**:

- Constituent concerns
- Budget implications
- Political positioning
- Re-election considerations

**Power**: Direct lawmaking authority

**Reform Position**: Varies widely by ideology and constituency

### Federal Legislators

Control federal prison policy and can influence states.

**Key Committees**:

- House/Senate Judiciary Committees
- House/Senate Appropriations Committees
- Congressional Progressive Caucus (reform-oriented)

**Stakes**:

- National policy direction
- Federal budget
- Constitutional amendments

**Power**: Can pass federal legislation, propose constitutional amendments

**Reform Position**: Small but growing reform caucus

### Governors

Executive authority over state prison systems.

**Stakes**:

- State budget
- Public safety narrative
- Political positioning

**Power**: Appointment of corrections officials, veto power, commutations

**Reform Position**: Varies; some have championed reform

## Economic Stakeholders

### Private Prison Companies

| Company | 2023 Revenue | Prisoners Held |
|---------|--------------|---------------|
| CoreCivic | ~$1.9 billion | ~45,000 |
| GEO Group | ~$2.3 billion | ~50,000 |
| MTC | ~$500 million | ~25,000 |

**Stakes**:

- Profit margins dependent on incarceration
- Stock price and shareholder value
- Government contracts

**Power**: Significant lobbying resources, political donations

**Reform Position**: Strongly oppose reforms that reduce incarceration or increase costs

### Prison Industry Programs

**Federal**: UNICOR (~$500 million annual revenue)

**State examples**:

- CALPIA (California): ~$200 million
- TCI (Texas): ~$90 million
- PRIDE (Florida): ~$75 million

**Stakes**:

- Revenue generation
- Government contracts
- Organizational survival

**Power**: Established relationships with government customers

**Reform Position**: Oppose wage increases; some support skill training reforms

### Corporations Using Prison Labor

Companies benefit through:

- Direct employment (PIECP programs)
- Supply chain integration
- Service contracts

**Stakes**:

- Cost savings from cheap labor
- Reputational risks from exposure
- Supply chain reliability

**Power**: Economic influence, but vulnerable to public pressure

**Reform Position**: Publicly may express concern; privately often resist change

### Companies Not Using Prison Labor

Compete with those who do use prison labor.

**Stakes**:

- Unfair competition from below-market labor
- Desire for level playing field
- Potential reputational benefit from ethical positioning

**Power**: Limited but growing as issue gains prominence

**Reform Position**: May support reform as competitive leveling

### Labor Unions

| Union Position | Unions |
|---------------|--------|
| Historically opposed prison labor competition | AFL-CIO, Building Trades |
| Aligned with abolition movement | SEIU, some locals |
| Correctional officer unions | Often oppose reform |

**Stakes**:

- Member wages and jobs
- Competition from prison labor
- Solidarity principles (some)

**Power**: Political influence, campaign contributions, member mobilization

**Reform Position**: Increasingly supportive; tension between free-world worker protection and solidarity with incarcerated workers

| Category | Likely Supporters | Likely Opponents | Persuadable |
|----------|-------------------|------------------|-------------|
| Directly Affected | Incarcerated workers, Families | Some correctional staff | Prison administrators |
| Government | Reform legislators | Most DOCs, Private prison allies | Moderate legislators |

| Civil Society | Reform orgs, Religious groups | Victims' rights (some) | General public |
| Labor | Some unions | Correctional officer unions | AFL-CIO leadership |

## Strategic Implications

### Coalition Building Priorities

1. **Core Coalition**: Incarcerated workers, families, formerly incarcerated, reform organizations
2. **Strategic Allies**: Labor unions (non-correctional), religious organizations, human rights groups
3. **Persuasion Targets**: Moderate legislators, corporations vulnerable to public pressure, general public
4. **Opposition to Neutralize**: Private prison companies, prison industry programs, resistant unions

### Leverage Points

| Stakeholder | Potential Leverage |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Corporations | Consumer pressure, shareholder activism, reputational risk |
| Legislators | Constituent pressure, electoral consequences, budget arguments |
| Prison administrators | Litigation, labor actions, media exposure |
| Unions | Solidarity appeals, economic analysis, member education |
| General public | Education campaigns, media coverage, moral framing |
