# Prison Labor Reform: Current State

## Overview of Prison Labor in America

Prison labor is pervasive across the American carceral system. An estimated 65-80% of incarcerated people participate in work programs, ranging from facility maintenance to manufacturing goods for government agencies and private corporations. This chapter examines the current landscape of prison labor programs, compensation structures, and working conditions.

## Federal Prison Labor: UNICOR/Federal Prison Industries

### Structure and Operations

UNICOR, operating as Federal Prison Industries (FPI), is a wholly-owned government corporation established in 1934. It operates as the primary employer of federal prisoners:

| UNICOR Statistics | Data |
|-------------------|------|
| Number of factories | 80+ |
| Federal prisoners employed | ~12,000 |
| Annual revenue | ~$500 million |
| Product categories | 7 major categories |
| Government customers | 70+ federal agencies |

### UNICOR Product Categories

1. **Office Furniture**: Desks, chairs, filing systems
2. **Clothing and Textiles**: Military uniforms, linens, mattresses
3. **Electronics**: Cable assemblies, electrical components
4. **Fleet Management**: Vehicle tags, license plates
5. **Industrial Products**: Signs, lockers, bins
6. **Office Supplies**: Binders, folders, envelopes
7. **Services**: Contact centers, data entry, recycling

### UNICOR Wage Structure

| Pay Grade | Hourly Wage | Typical Positions |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Grade 1 | $0.23 | Entry-level assembly |
| Grade 2 | $0.29 | Basic production |
| Grade 3 | $0.35 | Skilled production |
| Grade 4 | $0.52 | Quality control |
| Grade 5 | $1.15 | Supervisory/technical |

*Note: Up to 50% may be deducted for court-ordered obligations, victim restitution, and family support*

### Mandatory Purchasing Requirements

Federal agencies are required to purchase from UNICOR if:
- The product is available through UNICOR
- UNICOR can meet the agency's needs
- The price is comparable to market rate

## State Prison Labor Programs

### State Prison Industries Overview

All 50 states operate prison industries programs. These vary significantly in:
- Size and scope of operations
- Wage structures (or absence thereof)
- Types of goods and services produced
- Relationships with private sector

### States with No Compensation for Work

| State | Policy |
|-------|--------|
| Alabama | No wages for most prison work |
| Arkansas | No wages for most prison work |
| Georgia | No wages for most prison work |
| Mississippi | No wages for most prison work |
| Texas | No wages for most prison work |

### State Wage Ranges

| Category | Hourly Wage Range |
|----------|-------------------|
| Regular prison jobs | $0.00 - $0.63 |
| State-owned industries | $0.33 - $1.41 |
| Private industry on prison grounds | $0.00 - $5.15 |
| Work release (rare) | Minimum wage (with deductions) |

### Major State Prison Industry Programs

**California Prison Industry Authority (CALPIA)**
- Revenue: ~$200 million annually
- Employees: ~7,000 incarcerated workers
- Products: Furniture, clothing, food, printing, construction

**Texas Correctional Industries (TCI)**
- Revenue: ~$90 million annually
- Products: Furniture, signs, mattresses, meat processing
- Wages: None (one of five states)

**PRIDE Enterprises (Florida)**
- Semi-private corporation
- Revenue: ~$75 million annually
- Wages: $0.20 - $0.55/hour

## Private Sector Prison Labor

### Prison Industry Enhancement Certification Program (PIECP)

Established in 1979, PIECP allows private companies to employ incarcerated workers. Requirements include:

| Requirement | Standard |
|-------------|----------|
| Wages | "Prevailing wage" for similar work |
| Deductions | Up to 80% allowed for room/board, taxes, victims, family support |
| Union consultation | Required before establishing program |
| Displacement protection | Cannot displace free-world workers |

### Net Wages Under PIECP

Despite "prevailing wage" requirements, deductions often reduce net pay:

| Deduction Category | Typical Percentage |
|--------------------|-------------------|
| Room and board | 25% |
| Crime victims fund | 5-20% |
| Family support | 5-15% |
| Taxes | 15-25% |
| **Net to worker** | **20-40%** |

### Companies Known to Use Prison Labor (Historical and Current)

| Company | Type of Labor |
|---------|---------------|
| Victoria's Secret | Sewing (historical) |
| Whole Foods | Cheese, fish processing |
| McDonald's | Uniforms, plastic utensils |
| Walmart | Produce, goods |
| AT&T | Call center (historical) |
| BP | Oil spill cleanup |
| Starbucks | Packaging (through suppliers) |
| Microsoft | Packaging (historical) |

*Note: Many companies have ended or denied these relationships following public pressure*

## Types of Prison Work

### Institutional Maintenance (Most Common)

The majority of prison work involves maintaining the facility:

- Kitchen and food service
- Laundry services
- Janitorial and groundskeeping
- Building maintenance
- Administrative support

**Wages**: Typically $0.00 - $0.25/hour

### Manufacturing

Production of goods for government and private markets:

- Furniture assembly
- Clothing and textile production
- License plate manufacturing
- Signage and metalwork
- Electronics assembly

**Wages**: Typically $0.25 - $1.15/hour

### Agricultural Labor

Many prisons operate farms producing:

- Food for prison consumption
- Cash crops for sale
- Livestock and dairy

**Wages**: Often $0.00 - $0.20/hour

### Firefighting (California)

California's Conservation Camp program:

| Statistic | Data |
|-----------|------|
| Incarcerated firefighters | ~3,400 |
| Fire camps | 35+ |
| Daily base pay | $5.80 |
| Hazard pay (active fire) | $1.00/hour additional |
| Savings to California | ~$100 million annually |

### Call Centers and Services

Growing sector involving:

- Customer service for government agencies
- Data entry and processing
- Telemarketing
- Booking services (hotels, travel)

## Working Conditions

### Safety Concerns

| Issue | Impact |
|-------|--------|
| OSHA exclusion | No federal workplace safety enforcement |
| Workers' compensation | Excluded in most states |
| Injury rates | 2-3x higher than comparable free-world jobs |
| Protective equipment | Often inadequate or unavailable |
| Chemical exposure | Limited monitoring and protection |

### Documented Safety Issues

- **Agricultural work**: Pesticide exposure, heat illness, injuries
- **Manufacturing**: Repetitive stress injuries, machinery accidents
- **Firefighting**: Burns, smoke inhalation, fatigue-related injuries
- **Recycling/e-waste**: Toxic material exposure
- **Meat processing**: Cuts, infections, repetitive stress

### Work Hours and Conditions

| Aspect | Typical Conditions |
|--------|-------------------|
| Hours per week | 20-40+ |
| Overtime pay | Generally not provided |
| Breaks | At discretion of supervisors |
| Sick leave | None |
| Vacation | None |
| Healthcare for injuries | Limited prison medical care |

## Refusal to Work

### Consequences for Work Refusal

| Consequence | Frequency |
|-------------|-----------|
| Loss of good-time credits | Common |
| Disciplinary segregation (solitary) | Common |
| Loss of commissary access | Common |
| Loss of phone/visitation privileges | Common |
| Transfer to higher-security facility | Occasional |
| Negative impact on parole | Common |

### Legal Status of Forced Labor

Courts have generally upheld mandatory prison labor:

- **Vanskike v. Peters (1992)**: 7th Circuit held mandatory work is not unconstitutional
- **Mikeska v. Collins (1990)**: 5th Circuit upheld mandatory labor
- **Ali v. Johnson (2001)**: Mandatory work at $0.04/hour upheld
