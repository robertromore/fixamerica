# Surveillance Technology in Criminal Justice: History

## Introduction

The history of surveillance technology in American policing reflects broader patterns of technological adoption, racial targeting, and the expansion of state power. Understanding this history is essential for recognizing how current technologies fit into longer patterns of surveillance and control.

---

## Timeline of Surveillance Technology Development

### Pre-Digital Era (1900-1970)

| Year | Development | Significance |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| 1901 | Scotland Yard fingerprint bureau | First systematic biometric identification |
| 1924 | FBI Identification Division created | Centralized federal fingerprint database |
| 1930s | FBI begins political surveillance | Targeting of labor organizers, activists |
| 1950s-60s | COINTELPRO | FBI surveillance of civil rights, antiwar movements |
| 1963 | Wiretapping technology advances | Electronic eavesdropping becomes routine |
| 1967 | Katz v. United States | Fourth Amendment extended to electronic surveillance |
| 1968 | Omnibus Crime Control Act | First federal electronic surveillance statute |

### Early Digital Era (1970-1990)

| Year | Development | Significance |
|------|-------------|--------------|

| 1974 | Privacy Act | Limited protections for federal records |
| 1978 | FISA enacted | Framework for national security surveillance |
| 1980s | NCIC database expansion | Computerized criminal records sharing |
| 1984 | NSA surveillance expansion | Secret domestic surveillance programs |
| 1986 | Electronic Communications Privacy Act | Extended wiretap laws to electronic communications |
| 1988 | Computer Matching and Privacy Protection Act | Regulated government data matching |

### Post-Cold War Era (1990-2001)

| 1998 | Integrated Automated Fingerprint ID System | FBI fingerprint database modernization |
| 1999 | Carnivore/DCS1000 | FBI email monitoring system |

### Post-9/11 Era (2001-2010)

| 2008 | Social media monitoring begins | Police begin monitoring Facebook, etc. |

## Historical Patterns in Police Surveillance

### Pattern 1: Racial Targeting

Surveillance technology has consistently been deployed disproportionately against communities of color:

| Era | Example | Targets |
|-----|---------|---------|
| 1950s-70s | COINTELPRO | Civil rights leaders, Black organizations |
| 1980s-90s | Gang databases | Black and Latino youth |
| 2000s | Muslim surveillance | Post-9/11 NYPD mapping |
| 2010s | Predictive policing | Low-income minority neighborhoods |
| 2010s | Social media monitoring | BLM activists |
| 2020s | Facial recognition | Disproportionate misidentification |

### Pattern 2: Mission Creep

Surveillance technologies routinely expand beyond their stated purpose:

| Technology | Original Purpose | Expanded Use |
|------------|------------------|--------------|
| ALPR | Finding stolen cars | Universal location tracking |
| Body cameras | Police accountability | Facial recognition source |
| DNA databases | Violent crimes | Familial searching, minor offenses |
| Stingrays | Terrorism | Drug cases, minor crimes |
| Fusion centers | Counterterrorism | General law enforcement |
| ShotSpotter | Gun violence reduction | General patrol deployment |

### Pattern 3: Secrecy and Deception

Surveillance programs have repeatedly been implemented secretly:

| Program | Period Hidden | How Exposed |
|---------|---------------|-------------|
| COINTELPRO | 1956-1971 | Senate Church Committee |
| NSA warrantless wiretapping | 2001-2005 | Press investigation |
| Stingray NDA requirements | 2000s-present | ACLU litigation |
| Palantir New Orleans | 2012-2018 | Press investigation |
| Baltimore aerial surveillance | 2016 | Bloomberg investigation |
| Hemisphere program | 2007-2013 | Document leak |

### Pattern 4: Post-Crisis Expansion

Surveillance typically expands dramatically after crises:

| Crisis | Surveillance Expansion |
|--------|------------------------|
| World War II | FBI domestic surveillance institutionalized |
| Cold War | COINTELPRO, mass political surveillance |
| 9/11 attacks | PATRIOT Act, fusion centers, mass data collection |
| Boston Marathon bombing | Expanded camera networks, facial recognition |
| Ferguson uprising | Body cameras (with accountability loopholes) |
| George Floyd protests | Expanded social media monitoring |

## Key Historical Investigations

### Church Committee (1975-1976)

Investigated FBI, CIA, NSA surveillance abuses:
- Documented COINTELPRO operations
- Revealed NSA domestic surveillance
- Led to FISA and other reforms
- Established congressional oversight (later weakened)

### Pike Committee (1975-1976)

House investigation of intelligence agencies:
- Report suppressed by CIA pressure
- Documented surveillance overreach
- Demonstrated limits of congressional oversight

### Post-Snowden Investigations (2013-2014)

Examined NSA surveillance revealed by Edward Snowden:
- Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board findings
- Presidential review panel recommendations
- Limited reforms enacted (USA FREEDOM Act)

## Evolution of Legal Framework

### Fourth Amendment Interpretation

| Case | Year | Impact |
|------|------|--------|
| Olmstead v. United States | 1928 | No protection for wiretaps (overruled) |
| Katz v. United States | 1967 | Reasonable expectation of privacy standard |
| Smith v. Maryland | 1979 | Third-party doctrine (phone records) |
| United States v. Jones | 2012 | GPS tracking requires warrant |
| Riley v. California | 2014 | Cell phones require warrant to search |
| Carpenter v. United States | 2018 | Cell location data requires warrant |

### Statutory Evolution

| Statute | Year | Impact |
|---------|------|--------|
| Omnibus Crime Control Act | 1968 | First comprehensive wiretap law |
| Privacy Act | 1974 | Limited federal database protections |
| FISA | 1978 | Foreign intelligence surveillance framework |
| ECPA | 1986 | Extended protections to electronic communications |
| CALEA | 1994 | Required telecom surveillance capability |
| PATRIOT Act | 2001 | Massive expansion of surveillance authority |
| FISA Amendments Act | 2008 | Legalized warrantless surveillance |
| USA FREEDOM Act | 2015 | Limited NSA bulk collection (weak reform) |

## Lessons from History

### 1. Technology Outpaces Regulation

Legal frameworks consistently lag behind technological capability, creating periods of unregulated surveillance.

### 2. Secrecy Enables Abuse

Programs hidden from public scrutiny tend toward overreach and civil liberties violations.

### 3. Disparate Impact is Consistent

New surveillance technologies are consistently deployed first and most heavily against marginalized communities.

### 4. Reforms Are Fragile

Post-scandal reforms are often weakened or reversed over time.

### 5. Private Sector Role Grows

Increasing integration of private surveillance infrastructure with law enforcement.

### 6. Oversight Mechanisms Fail

Congressional and judicial oversight have repeatedly proven inadequate.
