# Surveillance Technology in Criminal Justice: Stakeholders

## Introduction

The surveillance technology ecosystem involves numerous stakeholders with varying interests, resources, and influence. Understanding these stakeholders is essential for developing effective reform strategies and building coalitions for change.

## Impacted Communities

### Primary Targets of Surveillance

| Community | Surveillance Experienced | Specific Concerns |
|-----------|-------------------------|-------------------|
| Black Americans | Facial recognition, predictive policing, gang databases | Highest misidentification rates, historical targeting |
| Latino/a Americans | ICE surveillance, ALPR near borders | Immigration enforcement, family separation |
| Muslim Americans | Social media monitoring, informants | Religious profiling, mosque surveillance |
| Immigrants | All technologies | Deportation, family separation |
| Low-income communities | ShotSpotter, predictive policing, heavy camera deployment | Over-policing, privacy violations |
| Activists/protesters | Social media monitoring, facial recognition | First Amendment chilling |
| Youth | Gang databases, school surveillance | Long-term consequences, criminalization |

### Secondary Impacts

| Group | Impact |
|-------|--------|
| Defense attorneys | Unable to challenge algorithmic evidence |
| Journalists | Chilled sources, surveillance of investigations |
| Domestic violence survivors | Location tracking concerns |
| LGBTQ+ individuals | Outing, targeted surveillance |
| Political dissidents | Monitoring of legal political activity |

## Law Enforcement Stakeholders

### Federal Agencies

| Agency | Surveillance Role | Interests |
|--------|-------------------|-----------|
| FBI | Facial recognition (NGI), fusion centers | Maximum capability, minimal restrictions |
| Department of Homeland Security | Border surveillance, grants | Technology deployment, information sharing |
| Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) | Location tracking, data purchases | Immigration enforcement capability |
| Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) | Hemisphere program, cell surveillance | Maintaining investigative tools |
| Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms (ATF) | eTrace, ballistic imaging | Firearm investigation capability |
| U.S. Marshals | Stingrays, facial recognition | Fugitive apprehension |

### State and Local Law Enforcement

| Stakeholder | Position | Key Interests |
|-------------|----------|---------------|
| Police chiefs/commissioners | Generally pro-surveillance | Efficiency, liability protection, modernization |
| Police unions | Strongly pro-surveillance | Officer tools, opposing accountability |
| Sheriffs | Varied | Often less regulated, significant autonomy |
| State police | Generally pro-surveillance | Highway enforcement, statewide databases |
| Prosecutors | Generally pro-surveillance | Evidence gathering, conviction rates |

### Law Enforcement Associations

| Association | Position | Activities |
|-------------|----------|------------|
| International Association of Chiefs of Police (IACP) | Pro-surveillance | Lobbying, policy development |
| Fraternal Order of Police (FOP) | Pro-surveillance | Lobbying, political endorsements |
| Major Cities Chiefs Association | Pro-surveillance with guidelines | Best practices, vendor relationships |
| National Sheriffs' Association | Pro-surveillance | Lobbying, training |
| Police Executive Research Forum (PERF) | Reform-minded | Research, guidelines |

## Technology Industry

### Major Surveillance Technology Vendors

| Company | Products | Market Position |
|---------|----------|-----------------|
| Axon (formerly TASER) | Body cameras, cloud storage, AI | 70%+ body camera market |
| Motorola Solutions | ALPR, body cameras, radios | Integrated communications leader |
| Palantir Technologies | Predictive analytics, data integration | Major federal and local contracts |
| Clearview AI | Facial recognition | Controversial but widely used |
| Amazon | Ring cameras, Rekognition | Consumer-police partnership |
| SoundThinking (ShotSpotter) | Gunshot detection | Near-monopoly in sector |
| NEC Corporation | Facial recognition | Major government contracts |
| L3Harris Technologies | Stingrays, surveillance | Defense contractor |
| Vigilant Solutions | ALPR, data analytics | Part of Motorola |

### Tech Industry Position

| Company/Sector | Position | Notable Actions |
|----------------|----------|-----------------|
| IBM | Reformed | Exited facial recognition market |
| Amazon | Mixed | Moratorium on Rekognition police sales |
| Microsoft | Reformed | Ended facial recognition sales to police |
| Google | Cautious | Avoided direct police facial recognition |

| Tech workers | Mixed | Some activism against police contracts |

## Stakeholder Power Analysis

### Influence Matrix

| Stakeholder | Resources | Access | Organization | Overall Power |
|-------------|-----------|--------|--------------|---------------|
| Surveillance vendors | High (money) | High | High | Very High |
| Police unions | High (money, votes) | High | High | Very High |
| Federal agencies | High | Very High | High | Very High |
| Tech civil society | Medium | Medium | High | Medium |
| Impacted communities | Low | Low | Varied | Low |
| Researchers | Low | Medium | Medium | Low-Medium |
| Local activists | Low | Low | Varied | Low |

### Coalition Potential

| Alliance | Shared Interest | Tension Points |
|----------|-----------------|----------------|
| Civil liberties left-right | Privacy, government limits | Racial justice framing |
| Tech workers + advocates | Ethical technology | Employment concerns |
| Communities + researchers | Evidence-based reform | Academic distance |
| Local + national groups | Policy change | Resource competition |
| Defense attorneys + advocates | Challenging algorithmic evidence | Different priorities |
