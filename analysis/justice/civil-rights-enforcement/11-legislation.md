# Civil Rights Enforcement Legislation

## Overview

This document provides comprehensive legislative frameworks for reforming civil rights enforcement in criminal justice. The legislation addresses the key barriers identified throughout this analysis: qualified immunity, limited DOJ resources, restrictive municipal liability, weak criminal enforcement standards, and inadequate state remedies.

The legislative approach operates on three levels:
1. **Federal legislation** to reform Section 1983, enhance DOJ authority, and strengthen criminal civil rights statutes
2. **State model legislation** to create independent state remedies
3. **Local ordinances** to establish effective civilian oversight

---

## Federal Legislation

### Bill 1: Civil Rights Enforcement Restoration Act

**Purpose:** Eliminate qualified immunity and restore meaningful civil rights enforcement under 42 U.S.C. § 1983.

**Draft Text:**

```
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Civil Rights Enforcement Restoration Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
    (1) The Constitution guarantees all persons equal protection of the 
        laws and protection against unreasonable seizures and the 
        deprivation of life, liberty, or property without due process.
    (2) In 1871, Congress enacted what is now codified as section 1983 
        of title 42, United States Code, to provide a remedy against 
        any person who, under color of law, deprives another of 
        constitutional rights.
    (3) The Supreme Court has created the doctrine of qualified immunity, 
        which shields government officials from civil liability unless 
        their conduct violated "clearly established" law.
    (4) The qualified immunity doctrine has no basis in the text of 
        section 1983 or in historical common law immunities.
    (5) The clearly established law standard has been applied so 
        restrictively that officers escape accountability even for 
        egregious constitutional violations simply because no prior 
        case involved identical facts.
    (6) This immunity doctrine undermines the congressional purpose 
        of section 1983 and leaves victims of constitutional violations 
        without adequate remedies.
    (7) Reform of qualified immunity is necessary to restore 
        accountability and deter future violations.

SEC. 3. ELIMINATION OF QUALIFIED IMMUNITY.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 1983 of title 42, United States Code, is 
    amended by adding at the end the following:
    
    "(b) QUALIFIED IMMUNITY NOT A DEFENSE.—It shall not be a defense 
    or immunity to any action brought under this section that—
        "(1) the defendant was acting in good faith, or that the 
            defendant believed, reasonably or otherwise, that his or 
            her conduct was lawful at the time when it was committed; or
        "(2) the rights, privileges, or immunities secured by the 
            Constitution or laws were not clearly established at the 
            time of their deprivation, or that the state of the law 
            was not sufficiently clear that every reasonable official 
            would have understood that what he or she was doing 
            violated such rights, privileges, or immunities.
    
    "(c) RULE OF CONSTRUCTION.—Nothing in this section shall—
        "(1) affect any defenses available under the Constitution; or
        "(2) prevent any State from providing additional liability 
            protections to any person.".

SEC. 4. MUNICIPAL LIABILITY.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 1983 of title 42, United States Code, as 
    amended by section 3, is further amended by adding at the end 
    the following:
    
    "(d) LIABILITY OF LOCAL GOVERNMENT ENTITIES.—
        "(1) VICARIOUS LIABILITY.—A local government entity shall be 
            liable for damages under this section caused by any act 
            or omission of an employee or agent of such entity acting 
            within the scope of the employee's or agent's official 
            duties or employment.
        "(2) NO QUALIFIED IMMUNITY.—A local government entity may not 
            assert qualified immunity as a defense to an action under 
            this section.
        "(3) DEFINITIONS.—In this subsection:
            "(A) LOCAL GOVERNMENT ENTITY.—The term 'local government 
                entity' includes any city, county, town, township, 
                village, school district, special district, joint 
                powers authority, or other political subdivision of 
                a State, and any agency or instrumentality thereof.".

SEC. 5. CONTRIBUTION REQUIREMENT.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 1983 of title 42, United States Code, as 
    amended by sections 3 and 4, is further amended by adding at 
    the end the following:
    
    "(e) OFFICER CONTRIBUTION TO JUDGMENTS.—
        "(1) REQUIREMENT.—If a court enters a judgment for a plaintiff 
            under this section and the judgment is against an individual 
            defendant, the individual defendant shall contribute to the 
            payment of such judgment an amount equal to not less than—
            "(A) 1 percent of the judgment, or $1,000, whichever is 
                greater, in cases where the judgment is less than 
                $100,000; and
            "(B) 5 percent of the judgment, or $5,000, whichever is 
                greater, in cases where the judgment is $100,000 or more.
        "(2) CAP.—In no case shall an individual defendant be required 
            to contribute more than $25,000 per judgment under this 
            subsection.
        "(3) WAIVER.—A court may waive or reduce the contribution 
            requirement under paragraph (1) upon a showing of 
            extraordinary circumstances.
        "(4) PROHIBITION ON INDEMNIFICATION.—No public entity may 
            indemnify, reimburse, or otherwise pay or assume any 
            portion of an individual defendant's contribution required 
            under this subsection.".

SEC. 6. STATUTE OF LIMITATIONS.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 1983 of title 42, United States Code, as 
    amended by sections 3, 4, and 5, is further amended by adding 
    at the end the following:
    
    "(f) STATUTE OF LIMITATIONS.—A civil action under this section 
        may be commenced not later than 4 years after the date on 
        which the cause of action accrued. Such period shall be 
        tolled during any period in which—
        "(1) the plaintiff was incarcerated;
        "(2) criminal proceedings related to the conduct at issue 
            were pending; or
        "(3) an internal investigation of the conduct at issue was 
            pending.".

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment and shall apply 
to any claim pending on or after such date.
```

**Explanation of Key Provisions:**

| Section | Purpose | Effect |
|---------|---------|--------|
| Section 3 | Eliminate qualified immunity | Officers liable if rights violated, regardless of prior cases |
| Section 4 | Establish municipal vicarious liability | Cities liable for officer misconduct within scope |
| Section 5 | Require officer contribution | Personal financial stake in judgments |
| Section 6 | Extend and toll statute of limitations | More time to bring claims |

**Constitutional Basis:**

- Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment (enforcement power)
- Article I, Section 8 (regulate courts, necessary and proper)
- Historical precedent: Section 1983 enacted 1871

### Bill 2: Department of Justice Civil Rights Enforcement Expansion Act

**Purpose:** Dramatically expand DOJ capacity for pattern-or-practice investigations and consent decree enforcement.

**Draft Text:**

```
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Department of Justice Civil Rights 
Enforcement Expansion Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
    (1) Section 12601 of title 34, United States Code, authorizes 
        the Attorney General to bring civil actions against law 
        enforcement agencies engaged in patterns or practices of 
        constitutional violations.
    (2) The Department of Justice Civil Rights Division has 
        insufficient resources to investigate more than a small 
        fraction of the approximately 18,000 law enforcement 
        agencies in the United States.
    (3) The Special Litigation Section has approximately 25 attorneys 
        to oversee all law enforcement agencies nationwide.
    (4) Investigations routinely take 3-5 years or longer due to 
        resource constraints.
    (5) Additional resources are necessary to ensure effective 
        federal oversight of law enforcement.

SEC. 3. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

(a) SPECIAL LITIGATION SECTION.—There is authorized to be 
    appropriated for the Special Litigation Section of the Civil 
    Rights Division of the Department of Justice—
    (1) $50,000,000 for fiscal year 2026;
    (2) $75,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
    (3) $100,000,000 for fiscal year 2028; and
    (4) such sums as may be necessary for each fiscal year thereafter.

(b) USE OF FUNDS.—Funds appropriated under subsection (a) shall 
    be used for—
    (1) hiring additional attorneys, investigators, and support staff;
    (2) conducting pattern-or-practice investigations;
    (3) monitoring compliance with consent decrees and settlement 
        agreements;
    (4) providing technical assistance to law enforcement agencies;
    (5) developing and maintaining data systems; and
    (6) training and professional development.

SEC. 4. STAFFING REQUIREMENTS.

(a) MINIMUM STAFFING.—The Attorney General shall ensure that the 
    Special Litigation Section maintains not fewer than—
    (1) 100 attorneys by the end of fiscal year 2027; and
    (2) 150 attorneys by the end of fiscal year 2028.

(b) MONITORING STAFF.—The Attorney General shall ensure that the 
    Department of Justice maintains not fewer than 50 full-time 
    employees dedicated to monitoring compliance with consent 
    decrees and settlement agreements by the end of fiscal year 2027.

SEC. 5. INVESTIGATION TIMELINES.

(a) PRELIMINARY INQUIRY.—The Department shall complete a preliminary 
    inquiry into any complaint or referral regarding potential 
    patterns or practices within 6 months of receipt.

(b) FULL INVESTIGATION.—The Department shall endeavor to complete 
    full pattern-or-practice investigations within 18 months of 
    commencement.

(c) REPORT.—At the conclusion of any investigation, the Department 
    shall issue a public report of findings within 90 days.

SEC. 6. MANDATORY REPORTING.

(a) ANNUAL REPORT.—Not later than March 1 of each year, the Attorney 
    General shall submit to Congress a report that includes—
    (1) the number of complaints received alleging patterns or 
        practices of civil rights violations;
    (2) the number of preliminary inquiries conducted;
    (3) the number of full investigations opened, pending, and closed;
    (4) the number of consent decrees and settlement agreements 
        in effect;
    (5) an assessment of compliance with each consent decree and 
        settlement agreement;
    (6) the number of declinations to investigate and the reasons 
        therefor;
    (7) the resources allocated to pattern-or-practice enforcement; and
    (8) any legislative recommendations.

SEC. 7. EXPEDITED INVESTIGATION AUTHORITY.

(a) EXPEDITED INVESTIGATION.—The Attorney General may designate 
    an investigation as expedited if—
    (1) there is evidence of ongoing, serious constitutional violations;
    (2) a death has occurred as a result of law enforcement conduct; or
    (3) there is evidence of systemic discrimination.

(b) TIMELINE.—An expedited investigation shall be completed within 
    12 months.

SEC. 8. TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE PROGRAM.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Attorney General shall establish a voluntary 
    technical assistance program to help law enforcement agencies 
    implement best practices and avoid patterns or practices of 
    constitutional violations.

(b) COMPONENTS.—The program shall include—
    (1) policy review and development;
    (2) training assistance;
    (3) data system development;
    (4) early intervention system implementation; and
    (5) community engagement support.
```

**Explanation of Key Provisions:**

| Section | Purpose | Effect |
|---------|---------|--------|
| Section 3 | Authorize major funding increase | Enable significant capacity expansion |
| Section 4 | Mandate minimum staffing | Ensure sufficient personnel |
| Section 5 | Set investigation timelines | Accelerate investigations |
| Section 6 | Require annual reporting | Congressional oversight |
| Section 7 | Create expedited track | Urgent cases get priority |
| Section 8 | Establish technical assistance | Prevent violations proactively |

### Bill 3: Law Enforcement Accountability in Criminal Prosecutions Act

**Purpose:** Reform 18 U.S.C. § 242 to enable more effective criminal prosecution of civil rights violations.

**Draft Text:**

```
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Law Enforcement Accountability in 
Criminal Prosecutions Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
    (1) Section 242 of title 18, United States Code, criminalizes 
        the willful deprivation of constitutional rights under 
        color of law.
    (2) The "willfulness" requirement has been interpreted to 
        require proof of specific intent to deprive a person of 
        their constitutional rights.
    (3) This specific intent standard makes prosecution of even 
        egregious civil rights violations extremely difficult.
    (4) Fewer than 100 officers are prosecuted federally for civil 
        rights violations each year, despite thousands of incidents.
    (5) The conviction rate for prosecuted cases is approximately 
        50 percent, far below other federal crimes.
    (6) Reform is necessary to ensure meaningful criminal 
        accountability for civil rights violations.

SEC. 3. AMENDMENTS TO 18 U.S.C. § 242.

(a) MENS REA STANDARD.—Section 242 of title 18, United States Code, 
    is amended by striking "willfully" and inserting "knowingly or 
    with reckless disregard".

(b) NEW OFFENSES.—Section 242 of title 18, United States Code, is 
    amended by adding at the end the following:
    
    "(b) DUTY TO INTERVENE.—Whoever, while acting under color of 
        any law, statute, ordinance, regulation, or custom, witnesses 
        another person acting under color of law depriving any person 
        of rights, privileges, or immunities secured by the 
        Constitution or laws of the United States, and—
        "(1) has an opportunity to intervene to prevent or stop such 
            deprivation; and
        "(2) fails to intervene;
        shall be fined under this title, imprisoned not more than 
        5 years, or both; and if bodily injury results from such 
        failure to intervene, shall be fined under this title, 
        imprisoned not more than 10 years, or both; and if death 
        results, shall be fined under this title, imprisoned for 
        any term of years or for life, or both, or may be sentenced 
        to death.
    
    "(c) FAILURE TO RENDER AID.—Whoever, while acting under color 
        of any law, statute, ordinance, regulation, or custom—
        "(1) uses force against any person;
        "(2) knows or should know that such person requires medical 
            attention; and
        "(3) fails to promptly summon medical assistance or render 
            first aid;
        shall be fined under this title, imprisoned not more than 
        10 years, or both; and if death results, shall be fined 
        under this title, imprisoned not more than 20 years, or both.
    
    "(d) OBSTRUCTION OF EVIDENCE.—Whoever, while acting under color 
        of any law, statute, ordinance, regulation, or custom—
        "(1) knowingly disables, obstructs, or interferes with the 
            operation of any body-worn camera, dashboard camera, 
            or other official recording device with intent to 
            prevent the recording of an official action; or
        "(2) knowingly destroys, alters, conceals, or falsifies any 
            record, document, or tangible object with intent to 
            obstruct a civil rights investigation;
        shall be fined under this title, imprisoned not more than 
        10 years, or both.".

SEC. 4. INDEPENDENT PROSECUTION UNIT.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established within the Civil Rights 
    Division of the Department of Justice an Independent Law 
    Enforcement Prosecution Unit.

(b) FUNCTION.—The Unit shall—
    (1) investigate and prosecute violations of section 242 of 
        title 18, United States Code;
    (2) provide training and assistance to United States Attorneys' 
        offices on civil rights prosecutions;
    (3) maintain a database of referrals, investigations, and 
        prosecution decisions; and
    (4) issue an annual public report on civil rights prosecutions.

(c) STAFFING.—The Unit shall be staffed by not fewer than 50 
    attorneys and such investigators and support staff as necessary.

(d) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated $30,000,000 
    annually for the Unit.

SEC. 5. MANDATORY DECLINATION REPORTING.

(a) REPORT.—The Attorney General shall submit to Congress an annual 
    report on all referrals for prosecution under section 242 of 
    title 18, United States Code, including—
    (1) the number of referrals received;
    (2) the source of each referral;
    (3) the disposition of each referral;
    (4) for each declination, the reasons therefor; and
    (5) demographic information regarding victims and subjects.

(b) PUBLIC SUMMARY.—A public summary of the report required under 
    subsection (a) shall be published, redacting identifying 
    information as appropriate.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment and shall apply 
to conduct occurring on or after such date.
```

**Explanation of Key Provisions:**

| Section | Purpose | Effect |
|---------|---------|--------|
| Section 3(a) | Lower mens rea requirement | Reckless disregard sufficient for prosecution |
| Section 3(b)(b) | Create duty to intervene offense | Officers criminally liable for failing to stop violations |
| Section 3(b)(c) | Create failure to render aid offense | Must provide medical attention after force |
| Section 3(b)(d) | Create evidence obstruction offense | Tampering with recordings criminalized |
| Section 4 | Establish independent unit | Overcome local prosecutor conflicts |
| Section 5 | Require declination reporting | Transparency and accountability |

### Bill 4: National Law Enforcement Misconduct Database Act

**Purpose:** Create a comprehensive national database of law enforcement misconduct to prevent problem officers from moving between agencies.

**Draft Text:**

```
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "National Law Enforcement Misconduct 
Database Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
    (1) Officers who engage in misconduct frequently resign or are 
        terminated from one agency only to be hired by another.
    (2) No comprehensive national system exists to track law 
        enforcement misconduct across jurisdictions.
    (3) State decertification databases are incomplete and not 
        universally accessible.
    (4) Communities deserve protection from officers with documented 
        histories of misconduct.
    (5) A national database is necessary to ensure that hiring 
        agencies have access to complete misconduct histories.

SEC. 3. ESTABLISHMENT OF DATABASE.

(a) IN GENERAL.—The Attorney General shall establish and maintain 
    a national database of law enforcement officer misconduct records.

(b) CONTENTS.—The database shall include, for each law enforcement 
    officer in the United States—
    (1) any termination, resignation in lieu of termination, or 
        other separation for misconduct;
    (2) any finding of misconduct by an internal affairs investigation 
        or civilian oversight body;
    (3) any civil judgment or settlement for civil rights violations;
    (4) any criminal conviction or pending prosecution;
    (5) any decertification action;
    (6) any sustained complaint alleging—
        (A) excessive force;
        (B) sexual misconduct;
        (C) dishonesty;
        (D) discrimination;
        (E) domestic violence; or
        (F) other serious misconduct;
    (7) any Brady/Giglio disclosure obligations; and
    (8) such other information as the Attorney General determines 
        appropriate.

SEC. 4. REPORTING REQUIREMENTS.

(a) FEDERAL AGENCIES.—Each federal law enforcement agency shall 
    report all information required under section 3(b) to the 
    database within 30 days of any reportable event.

(b) STATE AND LOCAL AGENCIES.—
    (1) REQUIREMENT.—Each State shall require all law enforcement 
        agencies within the State to report all information required 
        under section 3(b) to the database within 30 days of any 
        reportable event.
    (2) CERTIFICATION.—Not later than 2 years after the date of 
        enactment, each State shall certify compliance with 
        paragraph (1).

(c) PENALTY FOR NON-COMPLIANCE.—A State that fails to comply with 
    subsection (b) shall be ineligible for funds under the Edward 
    Byrne Memorial Justice Assistance Grant Program.

SEC. 5. PRE-HIRE QUERY REQUIREMENT.

(a) IN GENERAL.—No law enforcement agency that receives federal 
    funding may hire any individual as a law enforcement officer 
    unless the agency has—
    (1) queried the database established under section 3 regarding 
        the individual; and
    (2) received the results of such query.

(b) DOCUMENTATION.—Each hiring agency shall maintain documentation 
    of each query conducted under subsection (a) and the hiring 
    decision made.

(c) FACTORS.—Nothing in this section shall be construed to require 
    an agency to decline to hire any individual based solely on 
    information contained in the database, but agencies shall 
    consider such information in making hiring decisions.

SEC. 6. ACCESS TO DATABASE.

(a) LAW ENFORCEMENT HIRING.—Law enforcement agencies shall have 
    access to the database for purposes of hiring decisions.

(b) PROSECUTORS.—Prosecutors shall have access to the database 
    for purposes of Brady/Giglio disclosure obligations.

(c) DEFENDANTS.—Criminal defendants shall have access to information 
    in the database regarding any officer who is a witness in their 
    prosecution.

(d) RESEARCHERS.—The Attorney General shall make aggregate, 
    de-identified data available to qualified researchers.

(e) PUBLIC ACCESS.—The Attorney General shall publish annual 
    aggregate statistics from the database.

SEC. 7. DUE PROCESS PROTECTIONS.

(a) NOTICE.—An officer shall receive notice before any information 
    is entered into the database regarding that officer.

(b) OPPORTUNITY TO CONTEST.—An officer shall have 60 days to contest 
    the accuracy or completeness of any information before it is 
    entered into the database.

(c) APPEAL.—An officer may appeal any decision regarding database 
    entries to an administrative law judge.

(d) EXPUNGEMENT.—Information may be expunged from the database if—
    (1) a finding of misconduct is reversed on appeal;
    (2) criminal charges are dismissed or result in acquittal; or
    (3) other circumstances warrant expungement as determined by 
        the Attorney General.

SEC. 8. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There is authorized to be appropriated $50,000,000 for fiscal year 
2026 and such sums as may be necessary thereafter for the 
establishment and maintenance of the database.

SEC. 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

(a) DATABASE.—The database shall be operational not later than 
    18 months after the date of enactment.

(b) REPORTING.—Reporting requirements shall take effect 24 months 
    after the date of enactment.
```

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Civil Rights Act

**Purpose:** Create state-level civil rights enforcement independent of federal law.

**Draft Text:**

```
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Civil Rights Act".

SECTION 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.—The Legislature finds and declares that:
    (1) The people of [State] have fundamental rights guaranteed 
        by the [State] Constitution, including rights to be free 
        from unreasonable searches and seizures, to due process 
        of law, and to equal protection of the laws.
    (2) Federal civil rights enforcement has proven inadequate to 
        protect these rights, due in part to judicially created 
        doctrines such as qualified immunity.
    (3) A state civil rights cause of action is necessary to 
        provide meaningful remedies for violations of constitutional 
        rights by government officials.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to:
    (1) Create a state law remedy for violations of state 
        constitutional rights;
    (2) Ensure that victims of civil rights violations have 
        access to meaningful compensation;
    (3) Deter future civil rights violations; and
    (4) Promote constitutional policing and government conduct.

SECTION 3. DEFINITIONS.

As used in this Act:

(a) "Peace officer" means any person who by virtue of office or 
    public employment is vested by law with a duty to maintain 
    public order or to make arrests for offenses.

(b) "Political subdivision" means any city, county, town, school 
    district, special district, or other political subdivision 
    of the State.

(c) "State constitutional rights" means rights guaranteed by 
    Article [X] of the [State] Constitution, including but not 
    limited to:
    (1) The right to be free from unreasonable searches and seizures;
    (2) The right to due process of law;
    (3) The right to equal protection of the laws;
    (4) The right to be free from cruel or unusual punishment; and
    (5) Such other rights as the courts may determine.

SECTION 4. CAUSE OF ACTION.

(a) LIABILITY.—Any person who, while acting under color of law, 
    subjects any other person to the deprivation of any rights, 
    privileges, or immunities secured by the [State] Constitution 
    shall be liable to the injured party for legal and equitable 
    relief, including but not limited to:
    (1) Compensatory damages;
    (2) Punitive damages, where the conduct is found to be willful 
        or wanton;
    (3) Injunctive relief; and
    (4) Declaratory relief.

(b) NO QUALIFIED IMMUNITY.—Qualified immunity, or any substantially 
    similar affirmative defense, shall not be available to any 
    defendant in an action brought under this section.

(c) STANDARD OF LIABILITY.—A defendant shall be liable under this 
    section if the defendant deprived the plaintiff of a state 
    constitutional right, without regard to whether:
    (1) The right was clearly established at the time of the 
        deprivation; or
    (2) The defendant believed the conduct was lawful.

SECTION 5. GOVERNMENTAL LIABILITY.

(a) VICARIOUS LIABILITY.—A political subdivision shall be liable 
    for any damages awarded against a peace officer or employee 
    of such political subdivision for conduct occurring within 
    the scope of the officer's or employee's duties or employment.

(b) DIRECT LIABILITY.—A political subdivision may also be held 
    directly liable for:
    (1) Maintaining any policy or custom that causes a deprivation 
        of constitutional rights;
    (2) Failing to adequately train, supervise, or discipline 
        employees; or
    (3) Negligent hiring or retention.

SECTION 6. INDIVIDUAL LIABILITY AND CONTRIBUTION.

(a) INDIVIDUAL LIABILITY.—Nothing in this Act shall be construed 
    to limit the individual liability of any person acting under 
    color of law.

(b) CONTRIBUTION REQUIREMENT.—Where a judgment is entered against 
    a peace officer, the officer shall contribute to the payment 
    of such judgment an amount equal to:
    (1) For judgments under $100,000: 5% of the judgment or $1,000, 
        whichever is greater, not to exceed $5,000;
    (2) For judgments of $100,000 or more: 10% of the judgment or 
        $10,000, whichever is greater, not to exceed $25,000.

(c) PROHIBITION ON INDEMNIFICATION.—No political subdivision may 
    indemnify, reimburse, or otherwise pay any portion of an 
    individual defendant's contribution required under subsection (b).

(d) WAIVER.—A court may waive or reduce the contribution requirement 
    upon a finding of extraordinary circumstances.

SECTION 7. ATTORNEY FEES.

(a) PREVAILING PARTY.—In any action brought under this Act, the 
    court shall award reasonable attorney fees and costs to the 
    prevailing plaintiff.

(b) FEE CALCULATION.—Fees shall be calculated using the lodestar 
    method, and the court may apply a multiplier of up to 2.0 in 
    cases involving:
    (1) Significant public interest;
    (2) Exceptional results; or
    (3) Substantial risk undertaken by counsel.

(c) NO FEE WAIVERS.—In any settlement of an action under this Act, 
    no provision shall require the plaintiff to waive or reduce 
    attorney fees as a condition of settlement.

SECTION 8. STATUTE OF LIMITATIONS.

(a) LIMITATION PERIOD.—An action under this Act shall be commenced 
    within 4 years of the date the cause of action accrued.

(b) TOLLING.—The limitation period shall be tolled during any 
    period in which:
    (1) The plaintiff was incarcerated;
    (2) Criminal proceedings related to the conduct were pending;
    (3) An internal investigation of the conduct was pending; or
    (4) The defendant fraudulently concealed the cause of action.

SECTION 9. RELATIONSHIP TO OTHER REMEDIES.

(a) SUPPLEMENTAL REMEDY.—The remedies provided under this Act are 
    supplemental to any other remedies available under state or 
    federal law.

(b) NO EXHAUSTION.—A plaintiff need not exhaust administrative 
    remedies before bringing an action under this Act.

(c) NO ELECTION.—A plaintiff may pursue remedies under this Act 
    regardless of whether the plaintiff also pursues remedies 
    under federal law.

SECTION 10. SEVERABILITY.

If any provision of this Act or application thereof is held invalid, 
the invalidity shall not affect other provisions or applications 
that can be given effect without the invalid provision.

SECTION 11. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on [date] and shall apply to claims 
arising on or after such date.
```

### Model State Pattern-or-Practice Investigation Act

**Purpose:** Grant state attorneys general authority to investigate law enforcement agencies.

**Draft Text:**

```
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Law Enforcement 
Accountability Act".

SECTION 2. ATTORNEY GENERAL AUTHORITY.

(a) INVESTIGATION.—The Attorney General may conduct an investigation 
    of any law enforcement agency in this State if the Attorney 
    General has reasonable cause to believe that the agency has 
    engaged in a pattern or practice of conduct that:
    (1) Deprives persons of rights, privileges, or immunities 
        secured by the [State] Constitution or laws; or
    (2) Constitutes unlawful discrimination on the basis of race, 
        color, national origin, sex, religion, disability, or 
        sexual orientation.

(b) COOPERATION.—Upon notice from the Attorney General, a law 
    enforcement agency shall:
    (1) Cooperate fully with the investigation;
    (2) Provide access to all relevant records, documents, data, 
        and facilities; and
    (3) Make personnel available for interviews.

(c) SUBPOENA POWER.—The Attorney General may issue subpoenas for 
    documents and testimony in connection with any investigation 
    under this section.

SECTION 3. CIVIL ACTION.

(a) AUTHORITY.—If the Attorney General finds that a law enforcement 
    agency has engaged in a pattern or practice described in 
    Section 2(a), the Attorney General may bring a civil action 
    in [appropriate court] to obtain:
    (1) Declaratory relief;
    (2) Injunctive relief, including requirements for specific reforms;
    (3) Appointment of a monitor to oversee compliance; and
    (4) Such other relief as the court deems appropriate.

(b) CONSENT DECREES.—The Attorney General may negotiate and enter 
    into consent decrees to resolve investigations without litigation.

SECTION 4. COMMUNITY PARTICIPATION.

(a) COMPLAINT PROCESS.—The Attorney General shall establish a 
    process for receiving and reviewing complaints from community 
    members regarding potential patterns or practices.

(b) PUBLIC REPORT.—Upon conclusion of any investigation, the 
    Attorney General shall issue a public report of findings.

(c) COMMUNITY INPUT.—In any consent decree negotiation, the Attorney 
    General shall solicit and consider input from affected communities.

SECTION 5. MONITORING AND ENFORCEMENT.

(a) MONITORS.—The court may appoint independent monitors to assess 
    compliance with any injunction or consent decree.

(b) CONTEMPT.—Violations of any court order under this Act may be 
    punished as contempt.

(c) REPORTING.—Any law enforcement agency subject to a consent 
    decree shall submit regular compliance reports to the court 
    and the Attorney General.

SECTION 6. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There is appropriated from the General Fund to the Office of the 
Attorney General $[amount] annually to carry out this Act.
```

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Authority

| Reform | Constitutional Basis | Potential Challenges |
|--------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Qualified immunity elimination | 14th Amendment § 5, Art. I § 8 | 11th Amendment concerns (addressed by § 1983 history) |
| Municipal liability expansion | 14th Amendment § 5 | Federalism objections (addressable) |
| DOJ investigation authority | 14th Amendment § 5 | Already upheld in litigation |
| National database | Commerce Clause, Spending Power | Privacy concerns (addressed by due process provisions) |
| State civil rights acts | State constitutional authority | Officer due process (addressed) |

### Preemption Analysis

| Federal Provision | Preemption Effect |
|-------------------|------------------|
| Section 1983 reforms | Supplemental to state remedies |
| DOJ authority expansion | Does not preempt state investigation authority |
| National database | Preempts contrary state confidentiality laws |
| Grant conditions | Voluntary participation |

### Standing and Justiciability

| Claim Type | Standing Requirements | Notes |
|------------|----------------------|-------|
| Section 1983 damages | Personal injury from violation | Individual standing |
| DOJ pattern-or-practice | Attorney General parens patriae | No individual standing required |
| State civil rights | Personal injury from violation | Individual standing |
| Consent decree enforcement | Parties to agreement | Third parties may seek to intervene |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Constitutional defenses remain | Defendants may still raise constitutional defenses like self-defense | Medium |
| State indemnification | States could create new indemnification mechanisms | High |
| Arbitration override | Union contracts could require arbitration that limits effectiveness | High |
| Sovereign immunity workarounds | Creative pleading to invoke state sovereign immunity | Medium |
| Definition of "scope of employment" | Narrow interpretation could limit municipal liability | Medium |
| Database accuracy | Incomplete or erroneous reporting could undermine database utility | High |
| Declination discretion | Prosecutors retain discretion to decline cases | High |
| Monitor capture | Monitors may become too deferential to departments | Medium |
| Consent decree termination | Political pressure for premature termination | High |
| Funding cuts | Future administrations could reduce funding | High |
| State preemption | State legislatures could preempt local reforms | Medium |
| Officer resignation | Officers may resign before discipline, avoiding database entry | Medium |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|-----------|
| Does not address union contract barriers | Reforms can be undermined by collective bargaining | Labor law complexity |
| Relies on political will for enforcement | Effectiveness varies with administration | Political nature of enforcement |
| Does not create private right of action for DOJ failures | No remedy if DOJ fails to investigate | Sovereign immunity |
| May face Supreme Court hostility | Court could limit reforms judicially | Judicial ideology |
| Does not address implicit bias | Structural racism not directly addressed | Complexity of discrimination |
| Limited criminal accountability expansion | "Knowingly" standard still requires proof of knowledge | Political compromise |
| No civil rights court specialization | Cases handled by generalist judges | Judicial structure |
| Funding depends on appropriations | Not mandatory spending | Budget process |
| Database relies on accurate reporting | Garbage in, garbage out | Human factors |
| No mandatory decertification | States retain discretion | Federalism |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Address union contract barriers**: Include provisions that declare certain contract terms void as against public policy (e.g., 48-hour rule, record purging)

2. **Strengthen enforcement durability**: 
   - Make database funding mandatory rather than discretionary
   - Create independent enforcement body with fixed terms
   - Establish statutory triggers for investigation

3. **Create backup enforcement mechanisms**:
   - Allow private attorneys general to pursue pattern-or-practice claims after DOJ declination
   - Establish state AG concurrent jurisdiction
   - Create citizen petition process for DOJ investigation

4. **Enhance judicial review**:
   - Create civil rights court or specialized magistrates
   - Establish appellate review for qualified immunity grants
   - Mandate en banc review of circuit splits

5. **Strengthen database integrity**:
   - Establish verification procedures
   - Create penalties for false or incomplete reporting
   - Require periodic audits
   - Enable community reporting with verification

6. **Address resignation loophole**:
   - Require reporting of pending investigations at time of separation
   - Toll investigation for separation
   - Enter "separation during investigation" in database

7. **Prevent consent decree capture**:
   - Require community representatives on monitoring teams
   - Establish minimum monitoring periods
   - Create clear compliance metrics
   - Enable community challenges to termination

8. **Address state preemption**:
   - Condition federal funding on states not preempting local reforms
   - Create federal floor for local authority

### General Implementation Concerns

| Concern | Mitigation Strategy |
|---------|-------------------|
| Political opposition will block legislation | Build bipartisan coalition, use state-level strategy |
| Courts will narrow reforms | Draft carefully, anticipate challenges |
| Enforcement will be inconsistent | Create clear standards, mandatory reporting |
| Unions will resist | Distinguish disciplinary from liability reforms |
| Costs will be criticized | Document cost of status quo (settlements, distrust) |
| Officers will leave profession | No evidence supports this; address recruitment separately |
| Crime will increase | No evidence supports this; accountability improves public safety |
