# Solitary Confinement Reform: Stakeholders

## Overview

Solitary confinement reform involves a complex web of stakeholders with varying interests, power levels, and positions. Understanding these stakeholders is essential for building effective coalitions and anticipating opposition.

## Stakeholder Categories

### 1. Those Directly Affected

#### Currently Incarcerated People in Solitary

| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Estimated number** | 80,000-100,000 on any given day |
| **Primary interest** | End of isolation, humane treatment |
| **Power level** | Extremely limited (disenfranchised, limited communication) |
| **Voice** | Occasional hunger strikes, litigation, letters |
| **Key concerns** | Immediate suffering, mental health, family contact |

#### Formerly Incarcerated Survivors

| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Estimated number** | Hundreds of thousands have experienced solitary |
| **Primary interest** | Prevention for others, healing, accountability |
| **Power level** | Growing through advocacy organizations |
| **Voice** | Testimony, organizing, media |
| **Key concerns** | Long-term trauma effects, preventing future harm |

**Notable Survivor Advocates**:
- Five Omar Mualimm-Ak (New York)
- Dolores Canales (California)
- Johnny Perez (Formerly Rikers)
- Albert Woodfox (Louisiana, deceased)
- Curtis Howard (Colorado)

#### Families of Incarcerated People

| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Estimated number** | 2.7 million children with incarcerated parent |
| **Primary interest** | Contact with loved ones, humane treatment |
| **Power level** | Moderate, growing through organizing |
| **Voice** | Advocacy groups, testimony, voting |
| **Key concerns** | Communication restrictions, witnessing trauma |

### 2. Corrections System

#### Departments of Corrections (DOCs)

| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Number** | 50 state DOCs + Federal Bureau of Prisons |
| **Position** | Generally resistant to reform, some exceptions |
| **Power level** | High (control implementation) |
| **Primary interest** | Institutional order, staff safety, liability avoidance |
| **Key concerns** | Maintaining control, fear of violence, resources |

**DOC Variation**:

| Category | States/Systems | Position |
|----------|---------------|----------|
| Reform leaders | Colorado, North Dakota, Connecticut | Actively implementing alternatives |
| Moderate | California, New York (post-legislation) | Responding to mandates |
| Resistant | Texas, Florida, Arizona | Minimal voluntary change |

#### Correctional Officers and Unions

| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Number** | ~430,000 correctional officers nationwide |
| **Major unions** | AFSCME, CCPOA (California), NYSCOPBA (New York) |
| **Position** | Generally opposed to reducing solitary |
| **Power level** | High (political influence, implementation control) |
| **Primary interest** | Job safety, wages, working conditions |

**Key Claims by Officers' Unions**:
- Solitary is necessary for safety
- Alternatives put officers at risk
- Those in solitary are "worst of the worst"
- Reform advocates don't understand prisons

**Counter-Evidence**:
- States reducing solitary have not seen increased staff assaults
- Colorado: 85% reduction, no safety decline
- Relationship-based models improve officer safety

#### Prison Wardens and Administrators

| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Position** | Varies widely by individual and system |
| **Power level** | Moderate (facility level, within DOC constraints) |
| **Primary interest** | Running orderly facilities, career advancement |
| **Key concerns** | Liability, incident avoidance, staff relations |

### 3. Legal System

#### Judiciary

| Level | Role in Solitary |
|-------|-----------------|
| Federal courts | Constitutional claims (8th, 14th Amendment) |
| State courts | State constitutional claims, habeas |
| Supreme Court | Limited engagement, *Wilkinson v. Austin* key precedent |

**Key Cases**:
- *Wilkinson v. Austin* (2005): Some due process required for supermax placement
- *Madrid v. Gomez* (1995): Pelican Bay conditions violate 8th Amendment for mentally ill
- *Ashker v. Governor* (2015): Settlement reforming California SHU

#### Prosecutors and District Attorneys

| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Position** | Rarely engaged, generally deferential to corrections |
| **Power level** | High in charging, limited in corrections |
| **Potential role** | Reform-oriented DAs could advocate for change |

#### Defense Attorneys and Public Defenders

| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Position** | Generally supportive of reform |
| **Power level** | Limited (focus on trials, not prison conditions) |
| **Potential role** | Post-conviction advocacy, impact litigation |

### 4. Government

#### Federal Executive Branch

| Agency | Role |
|--------|------|
| Department of Justice | BOP oversight, civil rights enforcement |
| Bureau of Prisons | Operates federal facilities, ADX |
| Office of Civil Rights | Investigates conditions |

**Recent Positions**:
- Obama Administration: DOJ report on solitary, federal juvenile ban
- Trump Administration: Minimal engagement
- Biden Administration: Some reform interest, limited action

#### Congress

| Body | Recent Activity |
|------|-----------------|
| Senate Judiciary Committee | Hearings on solitary (2012, 2014, 2019) |
| House Judiciary Committee | Various bill introductions |

**Key Legislators**:
- Sen. Dick Durbin (D-IL): Led hearings
- Sen. Cory Booker (D-NJ): Reform advocate
- Rep. Cedric Richmond (D-LA): Introduced federal legislation
- Some Republican interest (libertarian wing)

#### State Legislators

| Position | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| Reform champions | NY Assembly (HALT Act sponsors) |
| Neutral/persuadable | Majority of legislators |
| Opposed | Those aligned with corrections unions |

#### Governors

| Type | Examples |
|------|----------|
| Reform governors | Colorado (Hickenlooper, Polis), New Jersey (Murphy) |
| Neutral | Most governors |
| Opposed | Those with strong "law and order" positioning |

### 6. Health Professionals

| Group | Stake |
|-------|-------|
| Prison mental health staff | Treating (or unable to treat) those in solitary |
| Outside mental health professionals | Advocacy, expert testimony |
| Medical associations | Policy positions, ethical guidance |

**Ethical Tensions**:
- Mental health staff asked to "clear" people for solitary
- Medical ethics vs. institutional demands
- Dual loyalty problems

## Stakeholder Power Analysis

| Stakeholder | Interest in Reform | Power Level | Strategy Implication |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------|---------------------|
| Incarcerated people | Very High | Very Low | Center voices, build platforms |
| Survivors | Very High | Growing | Leadership, testimony |
| Families | High | Moderate | Mobilize, humanize |
| Corrections unions | Low/Opposed | High | Counter narratives, find allies |
| DOCs | Low | High | Find reform leaders, mandate change |
| Legislators | Variable | High | Education, constituent pressure |
| National advocates | High | Moderate-High | Coordination, litigation |
| Researchers | High | Moderate | Evidence production |
| Media | Variable | High | Story placement, investigation |
| Faith communities | High | Moderate | Moral authority |

## Building Reform Coalitions

Successful reform requires:

1. **Centering those most affected** - Survivor leadership
2. **Building unusual alliances** - Some conservatives, fiscal hawks
3. **Engaging persuadable stakeholders** - Legislators, wardens open to evidence
4. **Neutralizing opposition** - Counter union claims with evidence
5. **Leveraging high-power allies** - Media, legal community
6. **Sustained pressure** - Multi-year campaigns
