# Parole and Probation Reform: Legislation

## Overview

This document provides draft legislation for comprehensive parole and probation reform at both federal and state levels. The legislation addresses supervision length, conditions, technical violations, fees, disparities, and parole board reform.

## Federal Legislation

### Community Supervision Reform Act

**Purpose:** To reform federal probation and supervised release, reduce technical violation incarceration, and establish standards for evidence-based supervision.

```
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Community Supervision Reform Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) Approximately 130,000 individuals are under federal supervision at any time.
(2) Technical violations of supervision conditions account for a significant portion of federal prison admissions.
(3) Research demonstrates that shorter supervision terms produce equivalent public safety outcomes.
(4) Excessive supervision conditions often set individuals up for failure rather than success.
(5) Supervision fees create financial barriers to successful reintegration.
(6) Racial disparities persist throughout the federal supervision system.
(7) Evidence-based supervision practices can improve outcomes while reducing costs.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
(1) TECHNICAL VIOLATION.—The term "technical violation" means a violation of a condition of supervised release or probation that does not constitute the commission of a new criminal offense.
(2) RISK ASSESSMENT.—The term "risk assessment" means a validated, evidence-based instrument used to determine an individual's likelihood of reoffending.
(3) GRADUATED SANCTIONS.—The term "graduated sanctions" means a system of responses to supervision violations that are proportionate to the severity of the violation and escalate with repeated violations.
(4) EARNED COMPLIANCE CREDIT.—The term "earned compliance credit" means a reduction in supervision term based on compliant behavior.

SEC. 4. SUPERVISION TERM LIMITS.

(a) MAXIMUM TERMS.—Section 3583 of title 18, United States Code, is amended—
(1) in paragraph (b), by striking "five years" and inserting "two years"; and
(2) by adding at the end the following:
"(k) PRESUMPTION OF TERMINATION.—
(1) The court shall review each case of supervised release after the individual has served one-half of the imposed term.
(2) At such review, the court shall terminate supervision unless the Government demonstrates by clear and convincing evidence that continued supervision is necessary to protect public safety.
(3) The court shall terminate supervision upon a finding that the individual has substantially complied with conditions for one year.".

(b) EARNED COMPLIANCE CREDITS.—Section 3583 of title 18, United States Code, is further amended by adding:
"(l) EARNED COMPLIANCE CREDITS.—
(1) For each month of substantial compliance with supervision conditions, an individual shall receive one month of credit toward the completion of the supervision term.
(2) Additional credits of up to 30 days per occurrence shall be awarded for:
(A) Completion of educational programs or degrees;
(B) Completion of vocational training programs;
(C) Obtaining and maintaining employment for 90 consecutive days;
(D) Completion of substance abuse treatment;
(E) Completion of mental health treatment; and
(F) Other significant milestones as determined by the court.".

SEC. 5. LIMITATION ON CONDITIONS.

(a) STANDARD CONDITIONS.—Section 3583(d) of title 18, United States Code, is amended by adding:
"(5) LIMITATION ON CONDITIONS.—
(A) The court shall impose only those conditions that are reasonably related to the nature and circumstances of the offense and the history and characteristics of the defendant.
(B) The court shall not impose more than eight conditions, including standard conditions, unless the court makes specific findings justifying additional conditions.
(C) Standard conditions shall be limited to:
(i) Reporting to a probation officer as directed;
(ii) Maintaining lawful employment, training, or education;
(iii) Providing accurate identifying and contact information;
(iv) Notifying the probation officer of any change in residence or employment;
(v) Refraining from committing new criminal offenses; and
(vi) Such other conditions as are specifically tailored to the individual's risks and needs.
(D) The following conditions shall not be imposed unless specifically justified in writing:
(i) Curfews;
(ii) Restrictions on internet use or social media;
(iii) Blanket prohibitions on association with categories of persons;
(iv) Travel restrictions beyond the district;
(v) Residence in a specific location;
(vi) Electronic monitoring.".

SEC. 6. GRADUATED RESPONSES TO VIOLATIONS.

(a) RESPONSE FRAMEWORK.—Section 3583 of title 18, United States Code, is amended by adding:
"(m) GRADUATED RESPONSES TO VIOLATIONS.—
(1) Upon learning of a violation of a condition of supervised release, the probation officer shall assess the violation using a graduated response matrix.
(2) For technical violations, the probation officer shall first employ administrative responses, which may include:
(A) Verbal warning;
(B) Written warning;
(C) Modification of conditions;
(D) Increased reporting requirements;
(E) Referral to treatment or services; and
(F) Community service.
(3) Court intervention for technical violations shall occur only after administrative responses have proven insufficient.
(4) LIMITATIONS ON INCARCERATION.—
(A) Incarceration for a first technical violation shall not exceed 5 days.
(B) Incarceration for a second technical violation shall not exceed 15 days.
(C) Incarceration for a third technical violation shall not exceed 30 days.
(D) Revocation of supervised release for technical violations shall occur only after at least three prior graduated responses.
(5) MAXIMUM INCARCERATION FOR TECHNICAL VIOLATIONS.—The total time an individual may be incarcerated for technical violations shall not exceed 90 days.".

SEC. 7. PROHIBITION ON SUPERVISION FEES.

(a) FEE PROHIBITION.—No person under federal supervision shall be required to pay:
(1) Supervision fees;
(2) Drug testing fees;
(3) Electronic monitoring fees;
(4) Program fees for court-mandated programs; or
(5) Collection fees.

(b) RESTITUTION SEPARATION.—
(1) Restitution obligations shall be converted to civil judgments upon termination of supervision.
(2) Failure to pay restitution shall not constitute a supervision violation.
(3) Payment of restitution shall not be a condition of early termination of supervision.

SEC. 8. DISPARITY REPORTING AND REDUCTION.

(a) DATA COLLECTION.—The Administrative Office of the United States Courts shall collect and publish data on:
(1) Supervision populations by race, ethnicity, age, and gender;
(2) Conditions imposed by race, ethnicity, age, and gender;
(3) Violation rates by race, ethnicity, age, and gender;
(4) Revocation rates by race, ethnicity, age, and gender;
(5) Incarceration for technical violations by race, ethnicity, age, and gender; and
(6) Average supervision length by race, ethnicity, age, and gender.

(b) ANNUAL REPORT.—The Administrative Office shall publish an annual report analyzing racial and ethnic disparities in federal supervision and recommending measures to reduce identified disparities.

(c) DISPARITY REDUCTION.—Each federal judicial district shall develop and implement a plan to reduce identified racial and ethnic disparities in supervision outcomes.

SEC. 9. PROBATION OFFICER TRAINING.

(a) REQUIRED TRAINING.—All federal probation officers shall receive training in:
(1) Evidence-based supervision practices;
(2) Motivational interviewing;
(3) Cognitive behavioral approaches;
(4) Trauma-informed practice;
(5) Implicit bias recognition and mitigation;
(6) De-escalation techniques;
(7) Connection to community resources; and
(8) Graduated response implementation.

(b) ONGOING EDUCATION.—Probation officers shall complete at least 40 hours of continuing education annually, with at least 16 hours focused on evidence-based practices.

SEC. 10. IMPLEMENTATION.

(a) EFFECTIVE DATE.—This Act shall take effect 180 days after the date of enactment.

(b) RETROACTIVE APPLICATION.—
(1) Within 180 days of enactment, the court shall review the conditions of all individuals currently on supervision and modify conditions as necessary to comply with this Act.
(2) Earned compliance credits shall be calculated retroactively for all individuals currently on supervision.

(c) REGULATIONS.—The Administrative Office of the United States Courts shall promulgate regulations to implement this Act within 120 days of enactment.

SEC. 11. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated such sums as may be necessary to carry out this Act.
```

**Explanation of Key Provisions:**

| Provision | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|
| Two-year maximum terms | Research shows diminishing returns after 12-24 months |
| Presumption of termination at midpoint | Automatic review ensures warranted termination |
| Day-for-day earned credits | Incentivizes compliance, reduces population |
| Eight-condition limit | Prevents condition proliferation |
| Graduated response matrix | Proportionate responses before revocation |
| Cap on technical violation incarceration | Prevents excessive punishment for non-criminal conduct |
| Fee prohibition | Eliminates financial barriers to success |
| Disparity reporting | Transparency drives accountability |

**Challenges to Passage:**
- Opposition from those who view supervision as insufficiently punitive
- Concerns about "soft on crime" political attacks
- Law enforcement opposition to limiting enforcement tools
- Budgetary concerns about fee revenue loss

**Refinements Based on Stakeholder Input:**
- Consider phase-in period for condition reduction
- Develop detailed graduated response matrices with input from probation officers
- Create judicial education programs on new requirements
- Establish pilot programs to demonstrate effectiveness

### Federal Parole Modernization Act

**Purpose:** To reform the United States Parole Commission, establish presumptive parole, and improve parole decision-making for those still under parole jurisdiction.

```
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Federal Parole Modernization Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) Approximately 5,000 individuals remain under the jurisdiction of the United States Parole Commission.
(2) Parole decision-making often lacks transparency and consistency.
(3) Parole denial rates have remained high despite decreasing crime rates.
(4) People who have served substantial prison terms and demonstrated rehabilitation should be returned to their communities.

SEC. 3. PRESUMPTIVE PAROLE.

(a) PRESUMPTION OF RELEASE.—The United States Parole Commission shall grant parole to any eligible prisoner unless the Commission finds, by a preponderance of the evidence, that:
(1) The prisoner poses a specific and articulable risk to public safety that cannot be adequately managed through supervision; and
(2) No conditions of parole exist that would adequately mitigate that risk.

(b) BURDEN.—The burden of demonstrating that parole should be denied rests with the Commission, not the prisoner.

(c) WRITTEN FINDINGS.—Any decision to deny parole shall include specific written findings explaining the basis for denial.

SEC. 4. PAROLE HEARING RIGHTS.

(a) COUNSEL.—Every prisoner appearing before the Parole Commission shall have the right to be represented by counsel. The Commission shall appoint counsel for any prisoner who cannot afford representation.

(b) EVIDENCE.—Prisoners shall have the right to present evidence, including witnesses, at parole hearings.

(c) VICTIM PARTICIPATION.—Victims may provide input to the Commission, but victim opposition alone shall not be sufficient basis for denial.

(d) TRANSPARENCY.—Parole hearings shall be open to the public unless the prisoner requests a closed hearing.

SEC. 5. DECISION-MAKING STANDARDS.

(a) FACTORS.—In making parole decisions, the Commission shall consider:
(1) Validated risk assessment results;
(2) Institutional conduct and programming;
(3) Reentry plan quality;
(4) Time served relative to offense;
(5) Age and health status;
(6) Family and community support; and
(7) Evidence of rehabilitation and personal growth.

(b) PROHIBITED FACTORS.—The Commission shall not deny parole based on:
(1) The nature of the offense alone;
(2) Prosecutor or law enforcement opposition alone;
(3) Victim opposition alone;
(4) Failure to complete unavailable programs;
(5) Maintaining innocence in a case where the prisoner has exhausted appeals; or
(6) Political considerations.

SEC. 6. PAROLE CONDITION LIMITS.

(a) CONDITIONS.—The Commission shall impose only those parole conditions that are:
(1) Reasonably related to the nature of the offense;
(2) Based on the individual's assessed risks and needs; and
(3) Achievable given the individual's circumstances.

(b) LIMITATION.—The Commission shall not impose more than eight conditions.

(c) REVIEW.—Conditions shall be reviewed every six months and reduced as warranted.

SEC. 7. IMPLEMENTATION.

This Act shall take effect 90 days after the date of enactment.
```

### Supervision Transparency and Accountability Act

**Purpose:** To require standardized data collection and reporting on community supervision outcomes nationwide.

```
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Supervision Transparency and Accountability Act".

SEC. 2. NATIONAL SUPERVISION DATA SYSTEM.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Bureau of Justice Statistics shall establish and maintain a national data system on probation and parole outcomes.

(b) DATA ELEMENTS.—The system shall collect:
(1) Supervision population counts by demographics;
(2) Entry and exit data;
(3) Conditions imposed;
(4) Violations by type;
(5) Responses to violations;
(6) Revocation rates and reasons;
(7) Recidivism outcomes;
(8) Supervision lengths;
(9) Fee collections; and
(10) Such other data as the Bureau determines necessary.

SEC. 3. STATE REPORTING REQUIREMENTS.

(a) CONDITION OF FUNDING.—As a condition of receiving funds under the Byrne Justice Assistance Grant program, states shall report supervision data to the Bureau of Justice Statistics according to standards established by the Bureau.

(b) COMPLIANCE TIMELINE.—
(1) States shall begin reporting within two years of enactment.
(2) States failing to report shall have grant funds reduced by 10% per year of non-compliance.

SEC. 4. PUBLIC REPORTING.

(a) ANNUAL REPORT.—The Bureau of Justice Statistics shall publish an annual report on national and state supervision outcomes.

(b) PUBLIC DATA.—De-identified data shall be made publicly available for research purposes.

(c) DISPARITY ANALYSIS.—Each annual report shall include analysis of racial and ethnic disparities in supervision outcomes.

SEC. 5. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There is authorized to be appropriated $10,000,000 annually to carry out this Act.
```

## State Model Legislation

### Model Community Supervision Reform Act

**Purpose:** To reform state probation and parole systems to reduce populations, limit conditions, implement graduated responses, eliminate fees, and reduce disparities.

```
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Community Supervision Reform Act".

SECTION 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.—The Legislature finds that:
(1) [State] currently supervises [number] individuals on probation and parole;
(2) Research demonstrates that shorter supervision terms produce equivalent public safety outcomes while reducing costs;
(3) Excessive supervision conditions set people up for failure rather than success;
(4) Technical violations account for [percentage] of prison admissions in [State];
(5) Supervision fees create financial barriers and disproportionately burden low-income individuals;
(6) Significant racial disparities exist in supervision populations and outcomes; and
(7) Evidence-based supervision practices can improve outcomes while saving taxpayer resources.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to:
(1) Reduce supervision terms to evidence-based lengths;
(2) Limit conditions to those directly related to offense and individual needs;
(3) Implement graduated responses to technical violations;
(4) Eliminate supervision fees;
(5) Reduce racial and ethnic disparities; and
(6) Promote evidence-based supervision practices.

SECTION 3. DEFINITIONS.

As used in this Act:
(a) "Community supervision" means probation, parole, supervised release, or any other form of conditional release to community supervision.
(b) "Technical violation" means a violation of a condition of supervision that does not constitute a new criminal offense.
(c) "Earned compliance credit" means a reduction in supervision term based on compliant behavior.
(d) "Graduated response" means a proportionate response to a supervision violation.
(e) "Risk assessment" means a validated instrument for assessing likelihood of reoffending.
(f) "Administrative sanction" means a response to a violation that does not require court or board intervention.

SECTION 4. SUPERVISION TERM LIMITS.

(a) MAXIMUM TERMS.—
(1) For misdemeanor offenses, probation shall not exceed 12 months.
(2) For nonviolent felony offenses, probation shall not exceed 18 months.
(3) For violent felony offenses, probation shall not exceed 24 months.
(4) The court may extend these limits only upon specific written finding that public safety requires additional supervision.

(b) PAROLE TERMS.—
(1) Parole terms shall not exceed 24 months for any offense.
(2) The parole board may extend this limit only upon specific written finding that public safety requires additional supervision.

(c) EARLY TERMINATION REVIEW.—
(1) The court or parole board shall review every case after the individual has served one-half of the imposed term.
(2) Supervision shall be terminated at such review unless specific articulable reasons justify continuation.

(d) EARNED COMPLIANCE CREDITS.—
(1) For each 30 days of substantial compliance, an individual shall receive 30 days credit toward completion.
(2) Additional credits shall be awarded for:
(A) Employment maintenance for 90 days: 30 days credit;
(B) Educational program completion: 30 days credit;
(C) Treatment program completion: 30 days credit;
(D) Obtaining stable housing: 15 days credit;
(E) Positive drug tests after period of use: 15 days credit per consecutive negative month.

SECTION 5. CONDITIONS OF SUPERVISION.

(a) LIMITATION.—
(1) Courts and the parole board shall impose only conditions that are:
(A) Directly related to the nature of the offense;
(B) Based on the individual's assessed risks and needs; and
(C) Achievable given the individual's circumstances.
(2) The total number of conditions shall not exceed eight.

(b) PROHIBITED CONDITIONS.—Unless specifically justified in writing, courts and the parole board shall not impose:
(1) Curfews for adults not convicted of offenses involving nighttime activity;
(2) Blanket prohibitions on association with categories of persons;
(3) Requirements to obtain permission before traveling within the state;
(4) Requirements to obtain permission before changing residence;
(5) Restrictions on social media or internet use;
(6) Warrantless search conditions; or
(7) Electronic monitoring as a condition of standard supervision.

(c) CONDITION MODIFICATION.—
(1) Individuals may petition for modification of conditions at any time.
(2) The court or board shall grant modification unless specific reasons justify continuation.

SECTION 6. GRADUATED RESPONSES TO VIOLATIONS.

(a) RESPONSE MATRIX.—The [Department of Corrections/Court Administrative Office] shall develop a graduated response matrix that:
(1) Categorizes violations by severity;
(2) Specifies appropriate responses for each severity level;
(3) Requires administrative responses before court or board intervention; and
(4) Limits incarceration for technical violations.

(b) ADMINISTRATIVE RESPONSES.—For technical violations, supervision officers shall first employ administrative responses including:
(1) Verbal warning;
(2) Written warning;
(3) Increased reporting;
(4) Modified conditions;
(5) Referral to services;
(6) Community service up to 40 hours; and
(7) Day reporting center.

(c) INCARCERATION LIMITS.—
(1) Incarceration for a first technical violation shall not exceed 3 days.
(2) Incarceration for a second technical violation shall not exceed 10 days.
(3) Incarceration for a third or subsequent technical violation shall not exceed 30 days.
(4) Total incarceration for technical violations shall not exceed 90 days.
(5) Revocation to prison for technical violations shall occur only after at least three graduated responses.

(d) NO INCARCERATION FOR CERTAIN VIOLATIONS.—Incarceration shall not be imposed for:
(1) Failure to pay financial obligations;
(2) Positive drug or alcohol test without other indicators;
(3) First missed appointment;
(4) Late curfew of less than one hour; or
(5) Technical violations of electronic monitoring.

SECTION 7. ELIMINATION OF SUPERVISION FEES.

(a) FEE PROHIBITION.—No person on community supervision shall be required to pay:
(1) Monthly supervision fees;
(2) Drug or alcohol testing fees;
(3) Electronic monitoring fees;
(4) Program fees for court-mandated treatment or classes;
(5) Collection fees; or
(6) Administrative fees.

(b) EXISTING FEES.—
(1) All outstanding supervision fees as of the effective date of this Act are waived.
(2) No person's supervision shall be extended or revoked due to unpaid fees.

(c) RESTITUTION.—
(1) Restitution obligations shall be separated from supervision.
(2) Failure to pay restitution shall not be a supervision violation.
(3) Upon termination of supervision, unpaid restitution shall be converted to a civil judgment.

(d) ABILITY TO PAY.—If any fee is permitted under other law, an ability-to-pay determination shall be made, with a presumption of inability for individuals earning less than 200% of the federal poverty level.

SECTION 8. PAROLE BOARD REFORM.

(a) PRESUMPTIVE PAROLE.—
(1) The parole board shall grant parole unless it finds, by a preponderance of evidence, that the individual poses a specific and articulable risk that cannot be managed through supervision.
(2) Denial shall include specific written findings.

(b) HEARING RIGHTS.—
(1) Individuals shall have the right to counsel at parole hearings.
(2) The state shall provide counsel for those who cannot afford representation.
(3) Individuals may present witnesses and evidence.

(c) TRANSPARENCY.—
(1) Parole hearings shall be open to the public unless the individual requests closure.
(2) All decisions shall include written rationale.
(3) The board shall publish annual data on grant and denial rates.

(d) BOARD COMPOSITION.—
(1) At least one board member shall have professional experience in rehabilitation, reentry, or social work.
(2) The board shall reflect the diversity of the state's population.

SECTION 9. RACIAL DISPARITY REDUCTION.

(a) DATA COLLECTION.—The [Department of Corrections] shall collect and publish data on:
(1) Supervision populations by race and ethnicity;
(2) Conditions imposed by race and ethnicity;
(3) Violation rates by race and ethnicity;
(4) Responses to violations by race and ethnicity;
(5) Revocation rates by race and ethnicity;
(6) Supervision length by race and ethnicity.

(b) ANNUAL REPORT.—The [Department] shall publish an annual report analyzing disparities and progress toward reduction.

(c) DISPARITY REDUCTION PLAN.—Each judicial district or parole region shall develop and implement a plan to reduce identified disparities.

SECTION 10. TRAINING.

(a) REQUIRED TRAINING.—All probation and parole officers shall receive training in:
(1) Evidence-based supervision practices;
(2) Motivational interviewing;
(3) Trauma-informed approaches;
(4) Implicit bias recognition;
(5) Graduated response implementation; and
(6) Connection to community resources.

(b) ANNUAL REQUIREMENT.—Officers shall complete at least 24 hours of continuing education annually.

SECTION 11. PRIVATE PROBATION PROHIBITION.

(a) PROHIBITION.—No court shall assign an individual to private probation supervision.

(b) EXISTING CONTRACTS.—
(1) All existing private probation contracts shall terminate within 180 days.
(2) The [Department] shall assume supervision of individuals under private supervision.

(c) TRANSITION.—The [Department] shall develop a transition plan within 90 days of enactment.

SECTION 12. IMPLEMENTATION.

(a) EFFECTIVE DATE.—This Act takes effect [date].

(b) RETROACTIVE APPLICATION.—
(1) Earned compliance credits shall apply retroactively to all individuals currently on supervision.
(2) Courts and the parole board shall review conditions for all individuals currently on supervision within 180 days.
(3) All outstanding fees shall be waived.

(c) REGULATIONS.—The [Department] shall promulgate regulations within 120 days.

(d) REPORTING.—The [Department] shall report to the Legislature annually on implementation progress.

SECTION 13. APPROPRIATIONS.

There is appropriated $[amount] to implement this Act, including:
(a) Additional probation and parole officers to maintain recommended caseloads;
(b) Training programs;
(c) Data system improvements; and
(d) Expanded treatment and service capacity.
```

### Model Electronic Monitoring Reform Act

**Purpose:** To regulate and limit the use of electronic monitoring in community supervision.

```
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Electronic Monitoring Reform Act".

SECTION 2. FINDINGS.

The Legislature finds that:
(a) Electronic monitoring imposes significant burdens including stigma, employment barriers, and liberty restrictions;
(b) Many individuals are charged substantial fees for monitoring;
(c) Technical problems frequently result in false violation allegations;
(d) Electronic monitoring has expanded beyond its original purpose as an alternative to incarceration; and
(e) Regulation is needed to protect supervised individuals' rights.

SECTION 3. LIMITATION ON USE.

(a) PRESUMPTION AGAINST EM.—Electronic monitoring shall not be imposed unless the court or parole board finds:
(1) The individual poses a specific flight or public safety risk that cannot be managed through other means; and
(2) Electronic monitoring is the least restrictive means to address that risk.

(b) ALTERNATIVES FIRST.—Before ordering electronic monitoring, the court or board shall consider:
(1) Standard supervision;
(2) Increased reporting;
(3) Day reporting; and
(4) Check-in technology such as phone apps.

(c) TIME LIMITS.—
(1) Electronic monitoring shall not exceed 90 days for nonviolent offenses.
(2) Electronic monitoring shall not exceed 180 days for violent offenses.
(3) Extensions require new findings justifying continued monitoring.

SECTION 4. COST PROHIBITION.

(a) STATE PAYMENT.—All costs of electronic monitoring shall be borne by the state.

(b) NO INDIVIDUAL FEES.—No individual shall be charged fees for:
(1) Equipment;
(2) Monitoring services;
(3) Installation or removal;
(4) Battery chargers or replacement; or
(5) Equipment damage.

SECTION 5. EQUIPMENT STANDARDS.

(a) ACCURACY.—All equipment shall meet accuracy standards established by the [Department].

(b) TESTING.—Equipment shall be tested quarterly for accuracy.

(c) DOCUMENTATION.—Any equipment malfunction shall be documented and shall not be basis for violation.

SECTION 6. RESPONSE TO ALERTS.

(a) GRACE PERIODS.—
(1) GPS alerts shall include a 15-minute grace period before triggering response.
(2) Low battery alerts shall allow 4 hours to charge before triggering response.
(3) Brief signal loss shall not trigger violation.

(b) INVESTIGATION.—Before any sanction for monitoring violation, officers shall investigate to determine whether violation was genuine or result of equipment malfunction.

(c) PROPORTIONATE RESPONSE.—First violations of monitoring conditions shall receive administrative responses, not incarceration.

SECTION 7. PRIVACY PROTECTIONS.

(a) DATA RETENTION.—Location data shall be retained for no more than 30 days unless needed for specific investigation.

(b) DATA USE.—Location data shall not be used for any purpose other than supervision compliance.

(c) DATA SHARING.—Location data shall not be shared with law enforcement except pursuant to warrant or in exigent circumstances.

SECTION 8. REMOVAL PROCESS.

(a) PETITION.—Individuals may petition for removal of monitoring at any time.

(b) AUTOMATIC REVIEW.—Monitoring shall be automatically reviewed every 30 days.

(c) PROMPT REMOVAL.—Upon termination of monitoring requirement, equipment shall be removed within 48 hours.

SECTION 9. IMPLEMENTATION.

This Act takes effect on [date].
```

## Regulatory Framework

### Administrative Policies for Supervision Reform

**Purpose:** To provide guidance for administrative implementation of supervision reform.

#### Caseload Standards

| Supervision Level | Maximum Caseload | Contact Frequency |
|-------------------|------------------|-------------------|
| Intensive | 25:1 | Weekly minimum |
| High | 50:1 | Bi-weekly minimum |
| Medium | 75:1 | Monthly minimum |
| Low | 150:1 | Quarterly minimum |
| Administrative | 500:1 | Annual verification |

#### Risk Assessment Use

| Practice | Standard |
|----------|----------|
| Tool selection | Use only validated, bias-tested instruments |
| Override policy | Allow professional override with documentation |
| Reassessment | Reassess at 6-month intervals minimum |
| Training | All officers trained in proper administration |
| Auditing | Regular audits for accuracy and consistency |

#### Graduated Response Matrix

| Violation Category | Level 1 Response | Level 2 Response | Level 3 Response |
|-------------------|------------------|------------------|------------------|
| Missed appointment | Verbal warning | Written warning | Increased reporting |
| Positive drug test | Warning, referral | Treatment requirement | Short jail sanction |
| Failed to report change | Warning | Written warning | Modified conditions |
| Curfew violation | Warning, curfew review | Stricter curfew | Short jail sanction |
| New arrest | Case review | Modified supervision | Potential revocation |

| Issue | Analysis |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Condition stacking | Imposing maximum conditions on every case | High |
| "Willfull" violation finding | Interpreting all violations as willfull to increase sanctions | High |
| Risk assessment override | Overriding low-risk scores to impose intensive supervision | Medium |
| Violation category manipulation | Categorizing violations at higher severity level | High |
| New charge circumvention | Charging new offense instead of treating as technical violation | High |
| Fee replacement | Shifting to "voluntary" payments or program costs | Medium |
| Private probation workarounds | Creating quasi-governmental entities | High |
| EM for "consent" | Claiming individual "agreed" to monitoring | Medium |
| Condition modification delay | Slow-walking condition reduction requests | Medium |
| Data reporting gaps | Failing to collect required disparity data | Medium |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Insufficient service capacity | Cannot provide treatment even when mandated | Funding limitations |
| Training limitations | Evidence-based practices not fully implemented | Budget, culture |
| Caseload persistence | Caseloads remain high despite standards | Funding, hiring |
| Judicial culture | Some judges continue excessive condition practices | Judicial independence |
| Parole board culture | Risk aversion persists despite presumptive parole | Appointment, training |
| Technology limitations | Data systems cannot track new requirements | Legacy systems |
| Implementation variation | Inconsistent application across jurisdictions | Decentralization |
| Enforcement gaps | No meaningful remedy for violations of reform law | Weak enforcement |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Strengthen Condition Limits**
   - Add explicit prohibition: "The eight-condition limit shall not be circumvented by combining multiple requirements into single conditions"
   - Require judicial training on condition limitations
   - Create appellate review for excessive conditions

2. **Close Violation Finding Loopholes**
   - Define "willful" violation to require affirmative finding of ability to comply
   - Require documented evidence of violation, not officer assertion alone
   - Create presumption of non-willfulness for first violations

3. **Strengthen Fee Prohibitions**
   - Define "fee" broadly to include any payment required as condition of supervision
   - Prohibit "voluntary" payment programs
   - Require annual audit of any payments from supervised individuals

4. **Close Private Probation Workarounds**
   - Define "private probation" to include any non-governmental supervision
   - Prohibit contracts that shift any supervision functions to private entities
   - Establish state takeover process for any discovered workarounds

5. **Enforce Disparity Requirements**
   - Create private right of action for failure to collect/report data
   - Establish independent monitoring body with enforcement authority
   - Tie funding to demonstrated disparity reduction

6. **Address Service Capacity**
   - Require service availability as condition of imposing treatment conditions
   - Prohibit violation findings for failure to complete unavailable programs
   - Mandate state funding for adequate service capacity

7. **Strengthen Implementation**
   - Establish implementation oversight body with diverse membership
   - Require quarterly implementation reports
   - Create expedited administrative review for non-compliance

### General Implementation Concerns

| Concern | Mitigation |
|---------|------------|
| Cultural resistance | Multi-year training program, leadership change, performance metrics reform |
| Resource constraints | Phased implementation, reinvestment of savings |
| Political backlash | Bipartisan coalition, crime victim support, data on outcomes |
| Judicial independence | Judicial education, peer leadership, appellate guidance |
| Data system limitations | Phased technology upgrade, interim manual tracking |
| Stakeholder fatigue | Quick wins, celebration of progress, sustained coalition |
