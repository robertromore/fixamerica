# Parole and Probation Reform: Stakeholder Analysis

## Overview of Key Stakeholders

Parole and probation reform involves a complex ecosystem of actors with varying interests, power, and influence. Understanding these stakeholders—their motivations, concerns, and potential roles in reform—is essential for building effective coalitions and anticipating resistance.

## 1. People Under Supervision

### Profile

| Characteristic | Details |
|----------------|---------|
| Population | 4.5 million (probation + parole) |
| Demographics | Disproportionately Black, low-income, male |
| Average age | 34 years |
| Prior incarceration | 60%+ have prior conviction |
| Employment rate | ~60% (below general population) |
| Housing stability | 30-40% experience instability |

### Stakes and Interests

| Interest | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Successful completion | End supervision, move on with life |
| Manageable conditions | Conditions they can actually meet |
| Affordable fees | Not bankrupted by supervision |
| Fair treatment | Consistent, unbiased enforcement |
| Access to services | Treatment, housing, employment help |
| Family stability | Maintain relationships |
| Privacy | Reduced surveillance and monitoring |
| Future opportunities | Avoid permanent stigma |

### Challenges to Participation

| Barrier | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Power imbalance | Fear of retaliation for speaking out |
| Stigma | Dismissed as "criminals" |
| Legal restrictions | Voting rights limited in many states |
| Economic constraints | No time/resources for advocacy |
| Fragmentation | Hard to organize dispersed population |
| Survival focus | Daily compliance consumes energy |

### Potential Role in Reform

| Contribution | Value |
|--------------|-------|
| Lived experience testimony | Humanizes issue, reveals reality |
| Policy feedback | Know what works and doesn't |
| Community organizing | Grassroots power |
| Voting (where possible) | Electoral pressure |
| Media stories | Public education |

## 3. Probation and Parole Officers

### Profile

| Characteristic | Details |
|----------------|---------|
| Number | ~100,000 nationally |
| Education | Most have bachelor's degree |
| Starting salary | $35,000-50,000 typical |
| Turnover | High (20-30% annually in many agencies) |
| Caseloads | Average 75-150, often higher |
| Demographics | Increasingly diverse |

### Stakes and Interests

| Interest | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Manageable caseloads | Ability to do meaningful work |
| Clear expectations | Know what success looks like |
| Safety | Protection from harm |
| Resources | Tools to help clients |
| Professional respect | Recognized for difficult work |
| Career advancement | Path to growth |
| Job security | Stable employment |
| Liability protection | Not blamed for client actions |

### Current Frustrations

| Frustration | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| Impossible caseloads | Can't provide real supervision |
| Paperwork burden | Documentation over relationship |
| Lack of resources | No services to connect clients to |
| Conflicting roles | Helper vs. enforcer |
| Blame culture | Blamed when anything goes wrong |
| Burnout | High stress, low support |

### Potential Role in Reform

| Contribution | Value |
|--------------|-------|
| Implementation expertise | Know what works practically |
| Credibility | Law enforcement perspective |
| Change agents | Can model new approaches |
| Policy feedback | Identify unworkable requirements |
| Union leverage | Collective bargaining power |

### Profile

| Characteristic | Details |

| Selection | Elected district attorneys lead offices |
| Power | Enormous discretion |

### Stakes and Interests

| Interest | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Convictions | Performance measured by "wins" |
| Public safety | Reduce crime in jurisdiction |
| Political viability | Win re-election |
| Efficiency | Move cases through system |

### Potential Role in Reform

| Charging decisions | Reduce supervision-eligible offenses |
| Violation response | Not seek revocation for technical |
| Reform leadership | Progressive prosecutors driving change |

## 8. Parole Boards

### Profile

| Characteristic | Details |
|----------------|---------|
| Composition | 5-15 members typically |
| Appointment | Usually gubernatorial |
| Qualifications | Few states require criminal justice experience |
| Decision-making | Often opaque, minimal documentation |

### Stakes and Interests

| Interest | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Risk avoidance | Don't release someone who reoffends |
| Political safety | Not be blamed for high-profile crime |
| Victim satisfaction | Respond to victim concerns |
| Job security | Maintain appointment |
| Efficiency | Process volume of cases |

### Potential Role in Reform

| Contribution | Value |
|--------------|-------|
| Release decisions | Grant parole more frequently |
| Condition setting | Impose fewer, reasonable conditions |
| Revocation restraint | Respond proportionately to violations |
| Transparency | Open decision-making |

## 9. Private Probation Companies

### Profile

| Characteristic | Details |
|----------------|---------|
| Major companies | Sentinel, Providence, Judicial Correction Services |
| Business model | Fees from supervised individuals |
| Revenue | $400+ million annually |
| States active | 12+ states |
| Population supervised | 500,000+ |

### Stakes and Interests

| Interest | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Revenue | Maximize fees collected |
| Contracts | Maintain and expand government contracts |
| Regulation avoidance | Minimize oversight |
| Liability protection | Avoid lawsuits |
| Market expansion | Enter new jurisdictions |

### Resistance to Reform

| Reform | Threat to Company |
|--------|-------------------|
| Fee elimination | Revenue loss |
| Supervision length limits | Shorter revenue period |
| Government takeover | Contract loss |
| Regulation | Compliance costs |
| Transparency | Exposure of practices |

## 10. Electronic Monitoring Companies

### Profile

| Characteristic | Details |
|----------------|---------|
| Major companies | BI Incorporated (GEO Group), SCRAM Systems, Attenti |
| Products | GPS ankle monitors, alcohol monitors, phone check-ins |
| Revenue | $1+ billion annually |
| Growth | 10-15% annually |

### Stakes and Interests

| Interest | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Market expansion | More people on EM |
| Product adoption | New technologies mandated |
| Revenue growth | Increase fees and contracts |
| Positive reputation | EM seen as humane alternative |

### Resistance to Reform

| Reform | Threat to Company |
|--------|-------------------|
| EM restrictions | Reduced market |
| Fee caps | Revenue limits |
| Accuracy requirements | Compliance costs |
| Time limits | Shorter monitoring periods |
