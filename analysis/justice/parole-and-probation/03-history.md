# Parole and Probation Reform: Historical Development

## Origins of Community Supervision

The American probation and parole systems emerged from 19th-century reform movements that sought alternatives to the harsh prison conditions of the era. Originally conceived as humanitarian interventions focused on rehabilitation, these systems have transformed dramatically over 170 years into instruments of surveillance and control that now supervise more Americans than the prison and jail systems combined.

## The Birth of Probation (1841-1900)

### John Augustus: The Father of Probation

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1841 | John Augustus, Boston shoemaker, posts bail for first "probationer" |
| 1841-1858 | Augustus personally supervises nearly 2,000 individuals |
| 1858 | Augustus dies after pioneering community supervision concept |
| 1878 | Massachusetts passes first probation law |
| 1880 | Massachusetts appoints first paid probation officer |
| 1898 | Vermont becomes second state to authorize probation |
| 1900 | 6 states have probation laws |

### Augustus's Original Vision

John Augustus's approach differed fundamentally from modern probation:

| Augustus's Approach | Modern Probation |
|--------------------|------------------|
| Personal relationship | Caseload management |
| Material assistance | Fee collection |
| Employment help | Employment mandates |
| Housing provision | Housing restrictions |
| Moral encouragement | Surveillance |
| Community integration | Community restrictions |
| Second chances | Zero tolerance |

### Early Probation Philosophy

The original probation concept was grounded in:

1. **Individualized treatment** - Each person's circumstances considered
2. **Social work orientation** - Officers as helpers, not enforcers
3. **Community support** - Maintaining family and work ties
4. **Rehabilitation focus** - Addressing underlying causes of crime
5. **Judicial discretion** - Judges tailoring responses to individuals

## Origins of Parole (1870-1920)

### Early Influences

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1840 | Alexander Maconochie implements "marks" system in Australia |
| 1854 | Sir Walter Crofton develops Irish system with "ticket of leave" |
| 1870 | American Prison Association endorses indeterminate sentencing |
| 1876 | Zebulon Brockway implements parole at Elmira Reformatory |
| 1877 | New York passes first parole law |
| 1898 | 20 states have parole |
| 1920 | 44 states have parole |

### The Elmira Model

Zebulon Brockway's Elmira Reformatory established:

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Indeterminate sentences | No fixed release date |
| Marks system | Earn early release through behavior |
| Education programs | Academic and vocational training |
| Parole supervision | Post-release monitoring |
| Parole officers | State agents to supervise |

### Expansion of Parole

| Period | Development |
|--------|-------------|
| 1880-1900 | Eastern states adopt parole |
| 1900-1920 | Southern and Western states follow |
| 1920-1940 | Parole becomes standard practice |
| 1942 | All states have parole systems |
| 1940s-1950s | Discretionary parole at peak |

## The Collapse of Rehabilitation (1970-1985)

### Critique from All Sides

| Source | Critique |
|--------|----------|
| Conservatives | Too lenient, ineffective |
| Liberals | Arbitrary, discriminatory |
| Academics | Lack of evidence for treatment |
| Prisoners' rights movement | Indeterminate sentences unjust |
| Victims' rights movement | Offenders released too early |

### The Martinson Report (1974)

Robert Martinson's "What Works?" article devastating impact:

| Finding | Implication |
|---------|-------------|
| "Nothing works" (mischaracterized) | Abandonment of rehabilitation |
| Treatment programs ineffective | Shift to punishment |
| Rehabilitation not measurable | Embrace of incapacitation |
| Individual treatment questioned | Standardized sentences |

## Lessons from History

### What Changed

| Original Vision | Current Reality |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| Help people succeed | Monitor for failure |
| Individualized attention | Mass processing |
| Social work approach | Law enforcement model |
| Community integration | Community restriction |
| Rehabilitation | Surveillance |
| Second chances | Extended punishment |
| Officer discretion | Rigid rules |
| Minimal conditions | Maximum conditions |

### Recurring Patterns

| Pattern | Historical Examples |
|---------|---------------------|
| Reform → Expansion | Probation alternative becomes net-widening |
| Alternatives become additional | Supervision plus jail sanctions |
| Good intentions → Unintended consequences | Intensive supervision increases revocation |
| Technology promised improvements | EM creates new violations |
| Fiscal crises drive policy | Fees introduced during budget cuts |
| Race shapes implementation | Disparities persist across eras |
