# Stakeholders in Fines and Fees Reform

## Overview

The fines and fees system involves a complex web of stakeholders with varying interests, power levels, and stakes in reform outcomes. Understanding this landscape is essential for building effective coalitions and anticipating opposition.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Those Directly Affected

#### People with Court Debt

| Characteristic | Detail |
|----------------|--------|
| Number affected | 10+ million Americans |
| Median income | $15,598 (pretrial detainees) |
| Demographics | Disproportionately Black, Latino, low-income |
| Primary concerns | Debt relief, license restoration, avoiding jail |
| Power level | Low individually; growing through organizing |

**Key organizations representing this group:**
- Formerly incarcerated advocacy organizations
- Public defender client organizations
- Community bail funds
- Reentry coalitions

#### Family Members

| Impact | Scope |
|--------|-------|
| Children of incarcerated parents | 5+ million |
| Family members paying fines/fees | Millions |
| Those supporting incarcerated relatives | Significant unpaid labor |
| Intergenerational wealth impact | Documented poverty transmission |

### Criminal Justice Professionals

#### Judges

| Role | Stake |
|------|-------|
| Impose fines/fees | Discretion in sentencing |
| Oversee compliance hearings | Workload concerns |
| Court administration | Funding concerns |
| Constitutional obligations | Legal exposure concerns |

**Positions on reform:**

| Position | Prevalence | Reasoning |
|----------|------------|-----------|
| Support reform | Growing | Reduce workload, constitutional concerns |
| Neutral | Common | Await legislative direction |
| Oppose reform | Some | Revenue concerns, judicial culture |

#### Public Defenders

| Role | Stake |
|------|-------|
| Represent indigent defendants | Client welfare |
| Challenge excessive fees | Constitutional advocacy |
| Navigate fee system | Practical knowledge |
| Workload management | Capacity constraints |

**Position:** Generally strong reform advocates due to direct client impact.

#### Prosecutors

| Role | Stake |
|------|-------|
| Recommend sentences | Include fines in plea deals |
| Seek restitution | Victim advocacy |
| Office funding | Some fee-dependent |
| Caseload management | Efficiency interests |

**Position:** Mixed; some progressive prosecutors support reform, traditional offices often neutral.

#### Probation/Parole Officers

| Role | Stake |
|------|-------|
| Collect supervision fees | Direct involvement |
| Report violations | Including non-payment |
| Monitor compliance | Workload affected |
| Employment security | Salary sometimes fee-dependent |

**Position:** Often conflicted between client success and institutional pressures.

#### Law Enforcement

| Role | Stake |
|------|-------|
| Issue citations | Generate fines |
| Execute warrants | For non-payment |
| Department funding | Some fee-dependent |
| Community relations | Affected by revenue policing |

**Position:** Varies significantly; some chiefs support reform for community relations, unions often oppose.

### Government Actors

#### State Legislators

| Consideration | Detail |
|---------------|--------|
| Budget pressures | Fees reduce general fund needs |
| Constituent concerns | Both impacted people and "taxpayers" |
| Political positioning | Crime policy implications |
| Committee jurisdictions | Judiciary, Finance, Corrections |

**Key legislative allies:**
- Criminal justice reform caucuses
- Progressive caucuses
- Some libertarian/conservative members (fiscal, liberty concerns)

#### Local Officials

| Role | Stake |
|------|-------|
| Mayors | Municipal budget balance |
| City councils | Fee-setting authority |
| County commissioners | Court funding responsibilities |
| City managers | Operational efficiency |

**Position:** Often defensive of revenue; some reformers emerge post-Ferguson.

#### Court Administrators

| Role | Stake |
|------|-------|
| Manage court operations | Funding adequacy |
| Implement collection | System complexity |
| Report to judiciary | Institutional interests |
| Staff management | Workload concerns |

## Stakeholder Mapping

### Power vs. Interest Matrix

| | High Interest in Reform | Low Interest in Reform |
|---|------------------------|------------------------|
| **High Power** | Reform-minded legislators, Foundations, Major advocacy groups | Municipal finance directors, Private industry, Some court administrators |
| **Low Power** | People with court debt, Family members, Public defenders | General public, Most media, Academic researchers |

### Potential Coalition Partners

| Coalition Type | Members | Common Ground |
|----------------|---------|---------------|
| Progressive | Civil rights groups, public defenders, formerly incarcerated | Racial and economic justice |
| Fiscal conservative | Taxpayer groups, libertarians, budget hawks | Cost-effectiveness, limited government |
| Law enforcement | Reform-minded chiefs, community policing advocates | Community trust, effective policing |
| Faith-based | Religious organizations, congregations | Redemption, second chances |
| Business | Employer groups, chambers of commerce | Workforce access, license restoration |

### Opposition Coalitions

| Opposition Type | Members | Motivation |
|-----------------|---------|------------|
| Revenue-dependent | Municipal associations, court administrators | Budget protection |
| Private industry | Bail bond, private probation, monitoring | Profit protection |
| Traditional justice | Some prosecutors, police unions | Tough-on-crime ideology |
| Victims' rights | Some victim advocates | Restitution concerns |

## Stakeholder Engagement Strategies

### For People with Court Debt

| Strategy | Approach |
|----------|----------|
| Direct organizing | Base-building in affected communities |
| Storytelling | Personal narratives for advocacy |
| Leadership development | Training affected people as advocates |
| Coalition building | Connecting to broader movements |

### For Policymakers

| Strategy | Approach |
|----------|----------|
| Data and research | Provide evidence for reform |
| Fiscal analysis | Show costs of current system |
| Model legislation | Provide ready solutions |
| Constituent pressure | Mobilize affected communities |

### For Courts

| Strategy | Approach |
|----------|----------|
| Bench cards | Practical guidance for judges |
| Training programs | Judicial education |
| Pilot programs | Demonstrate alternatives |
| Technical assistance | Implementation support |

### For the Public

| Strategy | Approach |
|----------|----------|
| Media campaigns | Raise awareness |
| Personal stories | Humanize the issue |
| Economic framing | Emphasize costs to all |
| Fairness messaging | Universal value appeal |
