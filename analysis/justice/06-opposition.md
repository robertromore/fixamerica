# Criminal Justice Reform: Opposition

## Primary Opponents

### Private Prison Industry

**Key Players**:

| Company | Revenue | Political Spending |
|---------|---------|-------------------|
| CoreCivic | $1.9 billion | $3+ million/cycle |
| GEO Group | $2.3 billion | $4+ million/cycle |

**Opposition Tactics**:

- Lobby against sentencing reform
- Promote "tough on crime" candidates
- Fund ALEC model legislation
- Diversify into "alternatives" (monitoring, reentry)
- Emphasize job creation in rural areas

### Law Enforcement Organizations

**Police Unions**:

- Fraternal Order of Police (FOP)
- Police Benevolent Associations (PBAs)
- International Association of Chiefs of Police (IACP)

**Opposition Areas**:

| Issue | Opposition Position |
|-------|---------------------|
| Qualified immunity | Defend absolutely |
| Use of force standards | Resist restrictions |
| Civilian oversight | Oppose or weaken |
| Body cameras | Accept with officer control |
| Discipline transparency | Block disclosure |
| Decertification | Resist national database |

**Sheriffs' Associations**:

- National Sheriffs' Association
- State-level associations
- Oppose jail reforms, bail reform
- Support immigration enforcement cooperation

### Prosecutors' Organizations

**National District Attorneys Association (NDAA)**:

- Opposes discovery reform
- Resists prosecutorial accountability
- Defends charging discretion
- Opposes mandatory minimums reform

**State Associations**:

- Lobby against bail reform
- Oppose expungement expansion
- Resist restorative justice programs
- Defend cash bail system

### Bail Industry

**American Bail Coalition**:

- $2+ billion industry
- Lobbies against bail reform
- Funds opposition campaigns (California Prop 25)
- ALEC partnership for model legislation

**Tactics**:

- Frame bail reform as danger to public safety
- Highlight rare failures of pretrial release
- Campaign against progressive prosecutors
- State-level lobbying

### Prison Guards Unions

**California Correctional Peace Officers Association (CCPOA)**:

- Major political force in California
- Opposed early release programs
- Funded Three Strikes campaign
- Model for other state unions

**General Pattern**:

- Resist prison population reduction
- Oppose programming that reduces recidivism
- Support longer sentences
- Fight prison closures

### Victim Advocacy Groups (Some)

**Traditional Orientation**:

- National Organization for Victim Assistance
- Parents of Murdered Children
- Some chapters of MADD

**Positions**:

- Prioritize punishment over rehabilitation
- Oppose early release
- Support mandatory minimums
- Emphasize victim notification over system reform

**Note**: Many victims and victim groups support reform (see Stakeholders).

## Their Incentives

### Economic Interests

**Private Prisons**:

- Per-diem payment per prisoner
- Occupancy guarantees (80-90% minimums)
- Revenue depends on incarceration levels
- Diversification into monitoring maintains dependence

**Prison Services**:

| Sector | Incentive |
|--------|-----------|
| Phone services | High incarceration = more customers |
| Healthcare | More prisoners = more contracts |
| Commissary | Captive market depends on prisoner count |
| Food services | Volume contracts tied to population |
| Construction | Prison building = construction contracts |

**Bail Industry**:

- 10% fee on bail bonds
- Cash bail elimination = business elimination
- No incentive to support pretrial services

### Career Interests

**Prosecutors**:

- Conviction rates as success metric
- Plea bargaining power from harsh sentences
- Political ambitions (tough-on-crime image)
- Resistance to second-guessing

**Police**:

- Arrest statistics as performance measure
- Union protection from accountability
- Qualified immunity protects personally
- Overtime in certain enforcement

**Corrections Officers**:

- Job security tied to prison population
- Rural communities dependent on prison employment
- Union power from membership numbers
- Oppose closures even if reform works

### Political Interests

**Tough-on-Crime Politicians**:

- Fear of being labeled "soft on crime"
- No political reward for reducing incarceration
- Victim advocacy provides political cover
- One-way ratchet on criminal penalties

**Rural Legislators**:

- Prison as economic development
- Census counts boost representation
- Constituent employment in corrections
- Federal funding tied to population

## Their Tactics

### Fear Mongering

**Crime Wave Narratives**:

- Exaggerate crime increases
- Highlight individual violent incidents
- Blame reform for any crime uptick
- Cherry-pick statistics

**Predator Framing**:

- Dehumanizing language
- Focus on worst cases
- "They" vs. "us" rhetoric
- Ignore redemption stories

### Political Campaigns

**Against Reform Prosecutors**:

| Location | Target | Tactic |
|----------|--------|--------|
| San Francisco | Chesa Boudin | Recall campaign |
| Los Angeles | George Gasc√≥n | Recall attempts |
| Philadelphia | Larry Krasner | Impeachment effort |

**Against Ballot Initiatives**:

- California Prop 25 (bail reform) defeated
- Fund opposition with industry money
- Misleading advertising
- Victim testimony

### Legislative Obstruction

**Federal Level**:

- Block sentencing reform bills
- Attach poison pills
- Delay hearings
- Demand "tough" amendments

**State Level**:

- Preempt local reforms
- Weaken reform bills in committee
- Add sunset clauses
- Reduce funding for alternatives

### Co-optation

**"Reform" Positioning**:

- Private prisons pivot to "reentry"
- Police support body cameras (under their control)
- Accept small reforms to block larger ones
- Define reform narrowly

**Bipartisan Branding**:

- Justice Action Network (industry-friendly)
- Emphasize cost savings over justice
- Focus on "nonviolent" offenders only
- Exclude police accountability

### Litigation

**Challenging Reforms**:

- Qualified immunity defense in every case
- Challenge consent decrees
- Sue over prosecutor policies
- Constitutional challenges to reform laws

## Their Messaging

### Public Safety Frame

**Core Arguments**:

- "Reform puts criminals on the street"
- "Crime is rising because of reform"
- "Victims are being ignored"
- "Law enforcement is being handcuffed"

**Response**:

| Claim | Counter |
|-------|---------|
| Reform causes crime | Crime rose nationally regardless of local policy |
| Need tough sentences | Long sentences don't deter; increase recidivism |
| Victims want punishment | Many victims prefer accountability + prevention |
| Police need immunity | Other professionals manage without absolute immunity |

### Jobs and Economy

**Arguments**:

- "Prison closures destroy rural communities"
- "Private prisons save taxpayer money"
- "Corrections jobs support families"

**Response**:

- Economic development shouldn't depend on incarceration
- Private prisons don't save money when quality controlled
- Transition support for affected workers

### Procedural Concerns

**Arguments**:

- "Reforms move too fast"
- "Evidence isn't clear"
- "Unintended consequences"
- "Local control"

**Response**:

- Status quo urgently harms millions
- Evidence strongly supports reform
- Current system's consequences are known
- Local control enables disparities

### Victim Centering

**Arguments**:

- "Speaking for victims"
- "Victims want justice"
- "Closure requires punishment"

**Response**:

- Victims have diverse views
- "Justice" can mean accountability, not just punishment
- Many victims support reform
- Center victim services, not victim rhetoric

## Weaknesses

### Economic Vulnerabilities

**Private Prison Dependence**:

- Concentrated in few companies
- Vulnerable to policy changes
- Dependent on contracts
- Divestment campaigns effective

**Bail Industry Decline**:

- Some states eliminating cash bail
- Pretrial services proving effective
- Constitutional challenges succeeding
- Public opinion shifting

### Political Vulnerabilities

**Changing Demographics**:

- Communities most affected gaining political power
- Younger voters favor reform
- Suburban shift on crime politics
- Progressive prosecutors winning elections

**Fiscal Pressure**:

- Prison costs straining budgets
- Conservative states leading reform for fiscal reasons
- COVID exposed system fragility
- Alternatives cheaper and more effective

### Moral Vulnerabilities

**Exposed Injustice**:

- Video of police violence
- Exonerations highlight system failure
- Racial disparities undeniable
- International comparisons embarrassing

**Victim Advocacy Evolution**:

- Crime Survivors for Safety and Justice
- Alliance for Safety and Justice
- Victims advocating for reform

### Empirical Vulnerabilities

**Evidence Against Their Claims**:

| Their Claim | Evidence |
|-------------|----------|
| Incarceration reduces crime | Crime fell as incarceration rose AND fell |
| Long sentences deter | Little evidence of marginal deterrence |
| Private prisons save money | Similar or higher costs with lower quality |
| Cash bail protects safety | Risk assessment equally effective |
| Qualified immunity is necessary | Other countries manage without it |

## Counter-Strategies

### Building Coalitions

**Bipartisan Alliances**:

- Fiscal conservatives + progressives
- Religious conservatives + civil rights
- Victim advocates + formerly incarcerated
- Business community + labor

**Unusual Allies**:

| Group | Interest |
|-------|----------|
| Koch network | Fiscal, liberty concerns |
| Faith communities | Redemption, second chances |
| Business | Workforce, costs |
| Conservatives | Limited government |

### Changing the Narrative

**Reframe the Debate**:

- Public safety includes incarceration harm
- Victim voices for reform
- Fiscal responsibility argument
- American values of redemption

**Humanize the Issue**:

- Personal stories
- Family impact
- Success stories
- Children of incarcerated

### Electoral Strategy

**Prosecutor Elections**:

- Support reform candidates
- Voter education
- Turnout in primaries
- Accountability campaigns

**Local Focus**:

- Sheriffs, judges, prosecutors elected locally
- Local reform builds momentum
- Demonstrate success at local level

### Economic Pressure

**Divestment**:

- University endowments
- Pension funds
- Shareholder activism
- Bond rating pressure

**Economic Alternative**:

- Reentry services as alternative industry
- Prevention as investment
- Community-based alternatives
- Transition support for affected workers

### Legal Strategy

**Litigation**:

- Civil rights enforcement
- Pattern-or-practice cases
- Consent decrees
- Conditions of confinement

**Model Legislation**:

- ALEC alternatives
- State-by-state strategy
- Pilot programs
- Demonstrate success
