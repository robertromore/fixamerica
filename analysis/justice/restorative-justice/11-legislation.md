# Restorative Justice: Legislation

## Overview

This document provides comprehensive model legislation for implementing and expanding restorative justice at federal, state, and local levels. The legislative framework addresses criminal justice diversion, school discipline, victim rights, practitioner standards, and funding mechanisms. All draft legislation includes analysis of potential weaknesses and mechanisms for addressing them.

## Federal Legislation

### The Restorative Justice Advancement Act

**Purpose**: To establish a federal framework supporting restorative justice programs, provide funding for state and local implementation, and ensure equitable access to restorative options.

**Draft Text**:

```
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Restorative Justice Advancement Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) Restorative justice approaches have demonstrated significant benefits for 
    victims, offenders, and communities, including higher victim satisfaction 
    rates, reduced recidivism, and lower costs compared to traditional 
    prosecution and incarceration.
(2) Despite evidence of effectiveness, restorative justice programs remain 
    underutilized and inconsistently available across jurisdictions.
(3) Victims of crime often report that the traditional criminal justice 
    system does not meet their needs for information, voice, validation, 
    restitution, or closure.
(4) The traditional criminal justice system has perpetuated racial 
    disparities that can be addressed through properly implemented 
    restorative approaches.
(5) Indigenous communities have practiced restorative justice principles 
    for millennia and their wisdom should be honored and supported.
(6) Federal investment in restorative justice can reduce costs, improve 
    outcomes, and advance justice for all Americans.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
(1) RESTORATIVE JUSTICE.—The term "restorative justice" means a response 
    to wrongdoing that—
    (A) focuses on repairing harm caused to victims, communities, and 
        relationships;
    (B) involves, to the extent possible, those affected by an incident 
        in determining an appropriate response;
    (C) holds individuals who caused harm accountable for their actions 
        through acceptance of responsibility and making amends; and
    (D) seeks to address underlying causes of harmful behavior and 
        prevent future harm.
(2) RESTORATIVE PRACTICE.—The term "restorative practice" means any 
    practice consistent with restorative justice principles, including—
    (A) victim-offender mediation or dialogue;
    (B) family group conferencing;
    (C) community conferencing;
    (D) restorative circles;
    (E) peer mediation; and
    (F) other practices that meet the definition of restorative justice.
(3) VICTIM.—The term "victim" means any individual who has suffered direct 
    physical, emotional, or economic harm as a result of a crime or harmful 
    act, including family members of homicide victims.

SEC. 4. OFFICE OF RESTORATIVE JUSTICE.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established within the Department of Justice 
    an Office of Restorative Justice.
(b) DIRECTOR.—The Office shall be headed by a Director, appointed by the 
    Attorney General, who shall—
    (1) have demonstrated experience in restorative justice practice and 
        policy;
    (2) serve a term of 5 years; and
    (3) be removable only for cause.
(c) DUTIES.—The Director shall—
    (1) administer the grant programs established under this Act;
    (2) provide technical assistance to states, localities, tribal nations, 
        and territories in developing restorative justice programs;
    (3) develop model standards for restorative justice programs and 
        practitioners;
    (4) coordinate research on restorative justice effectiveness;
    (5) develop guidance on equitable access to restorative justice;
    (6) submit an annual report to Congress on the state of restorative 
        justice in the United States; and
    (7) consult with the Advisory Council established under section 5.
(d) STAFF.—The Director may appoint such staff as necessary to carry out 
    the duties of the Office.

SEC. 5. NATIONAL RESTORATIVE JUSTICE ADVISORY COUNCIL.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established a National Restorative Justice 
    Advisory Council.
(b) MEMBERSHIP.—
    (1) IN GENERAL.—The Council shall consist of 17 members, appointed 
        by the Director, including—
        (A) 3 crime victims or victim advocates;
        (B) 2 individuals with lived experience of incarceration;
        (C) 2 restorative justice practitioners;
        (D) 2 researchers with expertise in restorative justice;
        (E) 2 representatives of tribal nations;
        (F) 1 prosecutor;
        (G) 1 defense attorney;
        (H) 1 judge;
        (I) 1 educator with experience in school-based restorative 
            practices;
        (J) 1 representative of community organizations; and
        (K) 1 juvenile justice professional.
    (2) DIVERSITY.—In making appointments, the Director shall ensure 
        that membership reflects the demographic, geographic, and 
        experiential diversity of the United States.
(c) DUTIES.—The Council shall—
    (1) advise the Director on grant priorities and administration;
    (2) provide input on model standards;
    (3) identify barriers to restorative justice implementation;
    (4) recommend strategies for addressing racial and economic 
        disparities; and
    (5) issue an annual report with recommendations.

SEC. 6. RESTORATIVE JUSTICE GRANT PROGRAM.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—The Director is authorized to make grants to States, 
    localities, tribal nations, territories, and nonprofit organizations 
    to develop, implement, and expand restorative justice programs.
(b) USE OF FUNDS.—Grant funds may be used for—
    (1) program development and implementation;
    (2) training of practitioners, justice system professionals, 
        and educators;
    (3) outreach to victims and communities;
    (4) evaluation and research;
    (5) development of standards and quality assurance mechanisms; and
    (6) coordination and technical assistance.
(c) PRIORITY.—In awarding grants, the Director shall give priority to 
    applicants that—
    (1) demonstrate commitment to addressing racial and economic 
        disparities in access to restorative justice;
    (2) include meaningful participation of victims and survivors 
        in program design;
    (3) collaborate with community-based organizations;
    (4) support tribal sovereignty and Indigenous justice traditions; and
    (5) commit to rigorous evaluation.
(d) APPLICATION.—An applicant for a grant under this section shall submit 
    an application at such time, in such manner, and containing such 
    information as the Director may require, including—
    (1) a description of the proposed program;
    (2) an equity plan for ensuring access across demographic groups;
    (3) a plan for victim engagement and support;
    (4) quality assurance mechanisms;
    (5) an evaluation plan; and
    (6) a sustainability plan.
(e) TRIBAL SET-ASIDE.—Of amounts appropriated for grants under this 
    section, not less than 10 percent shall be reserved for grants to 
    tribal nations or organizations serving tribal communities.
(f) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There are authorized to be 
    appropriated to carry out this section—
    (1) $75,000,000 for fiscal year 2025;
    (2) $100,000,000 for fiscal year 2026;
    (3) $125,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
    (4) $150,000,000 for fiscal year 2028; and
    (5) $175,000,000 for fiscal year 2029.

SEC. 7. SCHOOL-BASED RESTORATIVE PRACTICES GRANT PROGRAM.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—The Secretary of Education, in coordination with the 
    Director, is authorized to make grants to local educational agencies 
    and State educational agencies to implement restorative practices 
    as alternatives to exclusionary discipline.
(b) USE OF FUNDS.—Grant funds may be used for—
    (1) professional development for teachers, administrators, and 
        school staff;
    (2) employment of restorative practices coordinators;
    (3) student training in peer mediation and restorative practices;
    (4) family engagement activities;
    (5) development of restorative discipline policies; and
    (6) evaluation.
(c) REQUIREMENTS.—A grantee shall—
    (1) commit to reducing exclusionary discipline;
    (2) track and report discipline data disaggregated by race, 
        ethnicity, gender, disability status, and other relevant 
        categories;
    (3) involve students, families, and community members in 
        program implementation; and
    (4) participate in evaluation activities.
(d) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There are authorized to be 
    appropriated to carry out this section $50,000,000 for each of 
    fiscal years 2025 through 2029.

SEC. 8. FEDERAL CRIMINAL JUSTICE SYSTEM RESTORATIVE JUSTICE PILOT.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Attorney General shall establish pilot programs 
    in at least 5 federal judicial districts to offer restorative 
    justice options for eligible federal cases.
(b) ELIGIBILITY.—Cases eligible for the pilot program shall include—
    (1) cases in which the victim expresses interest in restorative 
        justice;
    (2) cases in which the defendant accepts responsibility; and
    (3) cases that meet other criteria established by the Attorney 
        General.
(c) EVALUATION.—The Director shall evaluate the pilot programs and 
    submit a report to Congress within 3 years of enactment.
(d) EXPANSION.—Based on evaluation results, the Attorney General may 
    expand restorative justice options to additional districts.

SEC. 9. RESEARCH AND EVALUATION.

(a) RESEARCH AGENDA.—The Director, in consultation with the National 
    Institute of Justice, shall develop a restorative justice research 
    agenda addressing—
    (1) effectiveness across offense types and populations;
    (2) implementation fidelity and quality;
    (3) cost-effectiveness;
    (4) racial and economic equity;
    (5) victim experiences and outcomes; and
    (6) long-term effects.
(b) EVALUATION REQUIREMENTS.—Programs receiving grants under this Act 
    shall participate in evaluation activities as required by the 
    Director.
(c) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There are authorized to be 
    appropriated $10,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2025 through 2029 
    for research and evaluation under this section.

SEC. 10. VICTIM RIGHTS IN RESTORATIVE JUSTICE.

(a) RIGHTS.—A victim participating in a restorative justice process 
    shall have the right—
    (1) to receive clear information about the restorative process, 
        potential risks and benefits, and available alternatives;
    (2) to participate voluntarily and to withdraw at any time 
        without consequence;
    (3) to be accompanied by a support person of their choice;
    (4) to have their safety concerns addressed;
    (5) to have their confidentiality protected to the extent 
        permitted by law;
    (6) to receive information about the outcome of any agreement; and
    (7) to access victim services regardless of participation decision.
(b) PROTECTIONS.—A victim's decision not to participate in restorative 
    justice shall not affect—
    (1) the victim's access to any services;
    (2) the prosecution of the case; or
    (3) the victim's standing as a crime victim.

SEC. 11. DUE PROCESS PROTECTIONS.

(a) PROTECTIONS FOR ACCUSED PERSONS.—An individual participating in 
    restorative justice as a person who caused harm shall—
    (1) participate voluntarily;
    (2) be informed of their rights, including the right to 
        consult with counsel;
    (3) have the opportunity to withdraw without the statements 
        made in the restorative process being used against them; and
    (4) not be required to waive any constitutional rights.
(b) CONFIDENTIALITY.—Statements made during restorative justice 
    processes shall be confidential and shall not be admissible 
    in any civil, criminal, or administrative proceeding, except—
    (1) with the consent of all parties;
    (2) to prove a threat of harm to persons or property; or
    (3) to the extent required by applicable law regarding 
        mandatory reporting of child abuse.

SEC. 12. REPORTING REQUIREMENTS.

(a) ANNUAL REPORT.—The Director shall submit an annual report to 
    Congress on—
    (1) grants awarded under this Act;
    (2) program outcomes disaggregated by relevant demographic 
        categories;
    (3) research findings;
    (4) recommendations for legislative action; and
    (5) other matters the Director considers appropriate.
(b) STATE REPORTING.—As a condition of receiving grants under this 
    Act, States shall report to the Director on restorative justice 
    programs operating within the State.

SEC. 13. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 180 days after the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This comprehensive federal bill establishes infrastructure for national restorative justice advancement while respecting state and local primacy in criminal justice. The Office of Restorative Justice provides coordination, the Advisory Council ensures diverse stakeholder input, and grant programs fund expansion.

**Legal Challenges**: The bill operates through spending power, avoiding federalism concerns by incentivizing rather than mandating state action.

**Refinements**: Congress may adjust appropriation levels, modify Advisory Council composition, or expand federal system application based on pilot results.

### The Victims' Restorative Justice Access Act

**Purpose**: To ensure victims of federal crimes have access to restorative justice options.

**Draft Text**:

```
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Victims' Restorative Justice Access Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) The Crime Victims' Rights Act establishes important protections but 
    does not ensure access to restorative justice.
(2) Many victims desire restorative options that the current system 
    does not provide.
(3) Restorative justice can provide victims with voice, validation, 
    answers, and closure that traditional prosecution often cannot.

SEC. 3. AMENDMENT TO CRIME VICTIMS' RIGHTS ACT.

Section 3771 of title 18, United States Code, is amended—
(1) in subsection (a), by adding at the end the following:
    "(10) The right to be informed of the availability of restorative 
    justice options and, where such options exist, to participate 
    voluntarily in restorative processes."; and
(2) in subsection (c), by adding at the end the following:
    "(3) RESTORATIVE JUSTICE.—A responsible official shall ensure that 
    victims are informed in a timely manner of any restorative justice 
    options available and shall facilitate victim participation in 
    restorative processes upon request, where such processes are 
    available and appropriate.".

SEC. 4. DEVELOPMENT OF RESTORATIVE JUSTICE OPTIONS.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Not later than 2 years after the date of enactment of 
    this Act, the Attorney General shall ensure that restorative justice 
    options are available in every federal judicial district for at 
    least some categories of cases.
(b) VICTIM SERVICES.—The Office for Victims of Crime shall provide 
    resources to support victim participation in restorative justice 
    processes, including—
    (1) preparation and support before, during, and after processes;
    (2) transportation and accommodation assistance; and
    (3) mental health support.

SEC. 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 1 year after the date of enactment.
```

## State Model Legislation

### Model Comprehensive Restorative Justice Act

**Purpose**: To provide a comprehensive statutory framework for restorative justice in criminal justice, juvenile justice, and schools.

**Draft Text**:

```
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Comprehensive Restorative Justice Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

As used in this Act:
(a) "Community member" means an individual residing in the community 
    affected by an offense or harm who participates in a restorative 
    process.
(b) "Facilitator" means an individual trained to conduct restorative 
    processes who meets the qualifications established by the Commission.
(c) "Participant" means any individual who participates in a restorative 
    process.
(d) "Restorative agreement" means a written agreement resulting from a 
    restorative process that may include restitution, community service, 
    apology, behavioral requirements, or other terms agreed upon by 
    participants.
(e) "Restorative justice" means a response to harm or conflict that—
    (1) focuses on repairing harm caused to victims and communities;
    (2) involves those affected by an incident in determining the 
        response;
    (3) holds those who caused harm accountable through acceptance of 
        responsibility and making amends; and
    (4) addresses underlying causes of harm.
(f) "Restorative process" means any process consistent with restorative 
    justice principles, including victim-offender mediation, conferencing, 
    circles, or other processes approved by the Commission.
(g) "Victim" means an individual who has suffered physical, emotional, 
    or economic harm as a result of an offense or harmful act.

SECTION 3. RESTORATIVE JUSTICE COMMISSION.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established a [State] Restorative Justice 
    Commission.
(b) MEMBERSHIP.—The Commission shall consist of 15 members, appointed 
    as follows:
    (1) 3 members appointed by the Governor, including at least 1 crime 
        victim and 1 individual with lived experience of incarceration;
    (2) 3 members appointed by the Chief Justice;
    (3) 2 members appointed by the Senate President;
    (4) 2 members appointed by the Speaker of the House;
    (5) 1 representative of the Department of Corrections;
    (6) 1 representative of the Department of Education;
    (7) 1 representative of the Attorney General's office;
    (8) 1 representative of the public defender's office; and
    (9) 1 representative of tribal nations within the State.
(c) TERMS.—Members shall serve 4-year terms and may be reappointed once.
(d) DUTIES.—The Commission shall—
    (1) develop standards for restorative justice programs and 
        facilitators;
    (2) approve programs for inclusion in the statewide network;
    (3) develop training curricula;
    (4) monitor program quality and outcomes;
    (5) provide technical assistance;
    (6) submit an annual report to the Legislature; and
    (7) advise the Governor and Legislature on restorative justice policy.

SECTION 4. CRIMINAL JUSTICE RESTORATIVE DIVERSION.

(a) ELIGIBILITY.—Any criminal case may be referred to restorative 
    justice if—
    (1) the victim consents or, if there is no identifiable victim, 
        the community would benefit from a restorative approach;
    (2) the accused person accepts responsibility for the conduct; and
    (3) the referring authority determines the case is appropriate.
(b) REFERRAL AUTHORITY.—Cases may be referred by—
    (1) a law enforcement officer, before arrest or citation;
    (2) a prosecuting attorney, before or after charges are filed;
    (3) a court, at any stage of proceedings; or
    (4) a probation or parole officer, for violations.
(c) PROHIBITED EXCLUSIONS.—No category of offense shall be categorically 
    excluded from restorative justice eligibility. Each case shall be 
    assessed individually based on the factors in subsection (a).
(d) EFFECT OF SUCCESSFUL COMPLETION.—Upon successful completion of a 
    restorative agreement—
    (1) if the referral was made before charges were filed, no charges 
        shall be filed;
    (2) if charges were filed, the charges shall be dismissed with 
        prejudice; and
    (3) no record of the arrest or charge shall be accessible to the 
        public.
(e) EFFECT OF UNSUCCESSFUL COMPLETION.—If a restorative agreement is 
    not successfully completed—
    (1) the case may be returned to the referring authority for 
        traditional processing;
    (2) statements made during the restorative process shall not be 
        admissible; and
    (3) the participant's willingness to participate shall not be 
        held against them.
(f) TIMELINE.—A restorative process shall be completed within 90 days 
    of referral unless the participants agree to an extension.

SECTION 5. JUVENILE JUSTICE RESTORATIVE PRACTICES.

(a) PREFERENCE.—Restorative approaches shall be the preferred response 
    for juvenile offenses except where public safety requires secure 
    detention.
(b) DIVERSION.—Law enforcement officers and intake officers shall 
    consider restorative diversion before filing a petition.
(c) COURT REFERRAL.—Juvenile courts shall consider restorative options 
    at all stages of proceedings.
(d) FAMILY INVOLVEMENT.—Restorative processes involving juveniles shall 
    include family members to the extent appropriate.
(e) SCHOOL COORDINATION.—Juvenile justice agencies shall coordinate with 
    schools to ensure consistent restorative approaches.

SECTION 6. SCHOOL-BASED RESTORATIVE PRACTICES.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Each public school shall implement restorative practices 
    as a component of its discipline system.
(b) MINIMUM REQUIREMENTS.—School restorative practices shall include—
    (1) training for all staff in restorative approaches;
    (2) classroom community-building activities;
    (3) restorative alternatives to suspension for all but the most 
        serious offenses; and
    (4) reentry support for students returning from suspension.
(c) DISCIPLINE CODE.—School discipline codes shall—
    (1) require consideration of restorative options before suspension;
    (2) limit suspension to cases where the student poses a genuine 
        threat to safety; and
    (3) require restorative reentry support after any suspension.
(d) REPORTING.—Schools shall report annually on—
    (1) restorative practices implemented;
    (2) discipline actions, disaggregated by race, ethnicity, gender, 
        disability status, and other relevant categories;
    (3) disparities in discipline; and
    (4) outcomes of restorative interventions.

SECTION 7. CORRECTIONS-BASED RESTORATIVE JUSTICE.

(a) PROGRAMMING.—The Department of Corrections shall offer restorative 
    justice programming in all correctional facilities, including—
    (1) victim impact education;
    (2) victim-offender dialogue programs for cases where victims 
        request such dialogue; and
    (3) restorative circles for community building within facilities.
(b) REENTRY.—The Department shall support restorative approaches to 
    reentry, including—
    (1) family conferencing before release;
    (2) community circles to support reintegration; and
    (3) connection to community-based restorative programs.
(c) VICTIM NOTIFICATION.—Victims shall be notified of opportunities for 
    restorative dialogue when an incarcerated individual requests such 
    dialogue.

SECTION 8. VICTIM RIGHTS AND PROTECTIONS.

(a) RIGHTS.—Victims participating in restorative processes shall have—
    (1) the right to be informed about the process;
    (2) the right to participate voluntarily and withdraw at any time;
    (3) the right to be accompanied by a support person;
    (4) the right to have safety concerns addressed;
    (5) the right to confidentiality to the extent permitted by law; and
    (6) the right to victim services regardless of participation.
(b) PROTECTIONS.—
    (1) No victim shall be pressured to participate.
    (2) A victim's decision not to participate shall not affect 
        prosecution or the victim's access to services.
    (3) Facilitators shall screen for safety concerns.

SECTION 9. DUE PROCESS PROTECTIONS.

(a) VOLUNTARINESS.—Participation in restorative justice shall be 
    voluntary for all parties.
(b) RIGHT TO COUNSEL.—Individuals referred to restorative justice shall 
    be advised of their right to consult with an attorney.
(c) NO WAIVER OF RIGHTS.—Participation in restorative justice shall not 
    require waiver of constitutional rights.
(d) CONFIDENTIALITY.—Statements made during restorative processes—
    (1) shall be confidential;
    (2) shall not be admissible in any proceeding except with consent 
        of all parties or to prove a threat of harm; and
    (3) shall not be disclosed in discovery.
(e) WITHDRAWAL.—A participant may withdraw from a restorative process 
    at any time without consequence.

SECTION 10. FACILITATOR QUALIFICATIONS.

(a) STANDARDS.—The Commission shall establish minimum qualifications 
    for facilitators, including—
    (1) completion of approved training;
    (2) cultural competence;
    (3) understanding of trauma;
    (4) demonstrated skill in facilitation; and
    (5) ongoing professional development.
(b) CERTIFICATION.—The Commission shall establish a certification 
    process for facilitators.
(c) SUPERVISION.—Programs shall provide supervision for facilitators.
(d) REMOVAL.—The Commission shall establish procedures for removing 
    certification from facilitators who violate standards.

SECTION 11. FUNDING.

(a) RESTORATIVE JUSTICE FUND.—There is established a Restorative Justice 
    Fund.
(b) SOURCES.—The Fund shall consist of—
    (1) appropriations;
    (2) federal grants;
    (3) a percentage of court fees as determined by the Legislature; and
    (4) private donations.
(c) USES.—Fund moneys shall be used for—
    (1) grants to programs approved by the Commission;
    (2) training;
    (3) Commission operations; and
    (4) research and evaluation.
(d) APPROPRIATION.—There is appropriated from the general fund 
    $[amount] for fiscal year [year] for the purposes of this Act.

SECTION 12. REPORTING AND EVALUATION.

(a) PROGRAM REPORTING.—Programs receiving state funds shall report to 
    the Commission on—
    (1) cases served;
    (2) participant demographics;
    (3) types of offenses addressed;
    (4) outcomes, including agreement completion and recidivism;
    (5) victim satisfaction; and
    (6) other data as required by the Commission.
(b) COMMISSION REPORTING.—The Commission shall submit an annual report 
    to the Legislature on—
    (1) the state of restorative justice in [State];
    (2) program outcomes;
    (3) disparities in access or outcomes;
    (4) recommendations for improvement; and
    (5) budget needs.

SECTION 13. SEVERABILITY.

If any provision of this Act is held invalid, the remainder shall 
remain in effect.

SECTION 14. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on [date].
```

### Model Restorative Justice Diversion Act

**Purpose**: A focused bill for jurisdictions wanting to establish prosecutorial diversion programs.

**Draft Text**:

```
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Restorative Justice Diversion Act".

SECTION 2. PROSECUTORIAL DIVERSION PROGRAM.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—Each prosecuting attorney's office shall establish 
    or contract with a restorative justice program to provide diversion 
    options for eligible cases.
(b) ELIGIBILITY.—A case is eligible for restorative diversion if—
    (1) the defendant accepts responsibility for the conduct;
    (2) the victim, if any, consents to restorative justice or, if the 
        victim declines, a community-based process may proceed without 
        the victim; and
    (3) the prosecuting attorney determines that restorative justice 
        is appropriate.
(c) NO CATEGORICAL EXCLUSIONS.—No offense category shall be 
    categorically excluded from eligibility. Each case shall be 
    assessed individually.
(d) VICTIM CONSULTATION.—Before making a diversion decision, the 
    prosecuting attorney shall consult with the victim regarding the 
    victim's preferences and concerns.

SECTION 3. RESTORATIVE PROCESS.

(a) REFERRAL.—Upon determining that a case is appropriate, the 
    prosecuting attorney shall refer the case to an approved restorative 
    justice program.
(b) PROCESS.—The restorative justice program shall conduct a process 
    consistent with restorative justice principles.
(c) AGREEMENT.—If the process results in an agreement, the agreement 
    shall be reduced to writing and signed by the participants.
(d) MONITORING.—The program shall monitor compliance with the agreement.
(e) TIMELINE.—The restorative process shall be completed within 90 days 
    unless participants agree to an extension.

SECTION 4. EFFECTS OF DIVERSION.

(a) HOLD ON PROSECUTION.—Upon referral to restorative justice, 
    prosecution shall be held in abeyance.
(b) SUCCESSFUL COMPLETION.—Upon successful completion—
    (1) charges shall be dismissed with prejudice;
    (2) the case shall be sealed; and
    (3) the defendant may answer that no arrest or charges occurred.
(c) UNSUCCESSFUL COMPLETION.—If the agreement is not completed—
    (1) the case may be returned for prosecution;
    (2) statements made during the process are not admissible; and
    (3) participation shall not be held against the defendant.

SECTION 5. VICTIM RIGHTS.

(a) INFORMATION.—Victims shall be informed of the restorative option 
    and what participation involves.
(b) VOLUNTARINESS.—Victim participation shall be voluntary.
(c) SUPPORT.—Victims shall be offered support before, during, and 
    after the process.
(d) NO PRESSURE.—Victims shall not be pressured to participate or to 
    forgive.

SECTION 6. DEFENDANT RIGHTS.

(a) VOLUNTARINESS.—Defendant participation shall be voluntary.
(b) COUNSEL.—Defendants shall be advised of the right to consult with 
    an attorney.
(c) NO WAIVER.—Participation shall not require waiver of constitutional 
    rights.
(d) WITHDRAWAL.—Defendants may withdraw at any time.

SECTION 7. REPORTING.

(a) ANNUAL REPORT.—Each prosecuting attorney's office shall report 
    annually on—
    (1) cases referred to restorative justice;
    (2) cases completed successfully;
    (3) participant demographics;
    (4) offense types; and
    (5) outcomes.
(b) DISAGGREGATION.—Reports shall disaggregate data by race, ethnicity, 
    gender, and other relevant categories.

SECTION 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect [date].
```

### Model School Restorative Practices Act

**Purpose**: Legislation focused specifically on K-12 education.

**Draft Text**:

```
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] School Restorative Practices Act".

SECTION 2. FINDINGS.

The Legislature finds that:
(a) Exclusionary discipline removes students from learning opportunities 
    and increases their risk of negative outcomes.
(b) Racial disparities in school discipline are well-documented and 
    harmful.
(c) Restorative practices have been shown to reduce suspensions, improve 
    school climate, and reduce disciplinary disparities.
(d) All students deserve access to restorative approaches that address 
    harm while keeping them in school.

SECTION 3. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Exclusionary discipline" means out-of-school suspension, expulsion, 
    or referral to law enforcement.
(b) "Restorative practices" means approaches that—
    (1) build community and positive relationships;
    (2) address harm through dialogue and repair; and
    (3) keep students in school when possible.

SECTION 4. RESTORATIVE PRACTICES REQUIREMENT.

(a) IMPLEMENTATION.—Each public school shall implement restorative 
    practices.
(b) COMPONENTS.—Implementation shall include—
    (1) community-building circles or similar practices in classrooms;
    (2) restorative alternatives to suspension;
    (3) restorative responses to conflicts between students, and 
        between students and staff; and
    (4) reentry support for students returning from any absence due 
        to discipline.
(c) TIMELINE.—Schools shall fully implement restorative practices 
    within 3 years of the effective date.

SECTION 5. LIMITATIONS ON EXCLUSIONARY DISCIPLINE.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Before imposing exclusionary discipline, schools shall—
    (1) consider restorative alternatives;
    (2) document why restorative approaches are not appropriate; and
    (3) minimize the length of any exclusion.
(b) PERMITTED EXCLUSION.—Exclusionary discipline shall be permitted only 
    when—
    (1) the student poses a genuine, imminent threat to safety; and
    (2) no restorative alternative can address the safety concern.
(c) REENTRY.—Following any exclusion, schools shall conduct a restorative 
    reentry process.

SECTION 6. PROFESSIONAL DEVELOPMENT.

(a) TRAINING.—All school staff shall receive training in restorative 
    practices.
(b) HOURS.—Training shall include at least—
    (1) 8 hours of initial training; and
    (2) 4 hours of annual refresher training.
(c) CONTENT.—Training shall address—
    (1) restorative philosophy and values;
    (2) community-building practices;
    (3) restorative responses to harm;
    (4) cultural responsiveness; and
    (5) trauma-informed approaches.

SECTION 7. STUDENT AND FAMILY ENGAGEMENT.

(a) STUDENT EDUCATION.—Schools shall educate students about restorative 
    practices.
(b) PEER PROGRAMS.—Schools shall develop peer mediation or peer support 
    programs.
(c) FAMILY ENGAGEMENT.—Schools shall engage families in restorative 
    practices.

SECTION 8. REPORTING.

(a) SCHOOL REPORTING.—Each school shall report annually on—
    (1) restorative practices implemented;
    (2) discipline incidents and responses;
    (3) exclusionary discipline imposed; and
    (4) reentry processes conducted.
(b) DISAGGREGATION.—Reports shall disaggregate data by race, ethnicity, 
    gender, disability status, English learner status, and other 
    relevant categories.
(c) STATE REPORTING.—The Department of Education shall compile school 
    reports and submit an annual report to the Legislature.

SECTION 9. FUNDING.

The Department of Education shall provide funding to support 
implementation, including—
(a) grants for restorative practices coordinators;
(b) training resources; and
(c) technical assistance.

SECTION 10. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect at the beginning of the school year following 
enactment.
```

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Prosecutor discretion | Prosecutors may decline to refer cases despite eligibility | High |
| "Appropriate" standard | Vague appropriateness standard allows inconsistent gatekeeping | High |
| Victim veto | Victim refusal may deny access to offenders who accept responsibility | Medium |
| Funding contingency | Without adequate funding, programs cannot operate | High |
| Training shortcuts | Minimum training hours may be met with inadequate quality | Medium |
| Selection bias | Programs may select "easy" cases to boost success metrics | Medium |
| Confidentiality exceptions | Broad exceptions may chill participation | Medium |
| Commission capture | Commission may be dominated by system insiders | Medium |
| Record sealing gaps | Sealed records may still be accessible to some entities | Low |
| Equity monitoring gaps | Demographic data may not capture all relevant disparities | Medium |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Categorical exclusions prohibited but individual exclusion easy | Serious offenses may rarely access RJ | System resistance to RJ for serious cases |
| Victim consent requirement | Cases without victim consent may not proceed | Victim-centered framing |
| Limited federal system application | Federal cases have fewer options | Pilot program scope |
| No private right of action | Individuals cannot enforce RJ access | Limiting litigation |
| Funding dependent on appropriations | Programs vulnerable to budget cuts | Political process |
| No mandate for prosecutors | DA cooperation voluntary | Separation of powers |
| School implementation unfunded | Schools may lack resources | Budget constraints |
| Tribal consultation limited | Indigenous perspectives may be marginalized | Structural issues |
| Facilitator shortage | Insufficient trained practitioners | Capacity building takes time |
| Research gaps | Evidence base incomplete | Research funding limited |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Prosecutor Discretion Abuse**
   - Require written justification for declining eligible referrals
   - Publish referral and declination data by demographics
   - Create community oversight of diversion decisions
   - Allow victims to request RJ directly to programs

2. **Vague Standards**
   - Commission develops detailed appropriateness criteria
   - Regular review and refinement of criteria
   - Training on consistent application
   - Appeals process for denied referrals

3. **Victim Consent Issues**
   - Allow community-based processes without direct victim
   - Develop surrogate victim participation options
   - Provide victim outreach and education
   - Track victim consent rates and reasons for refusal

4. **Funding Instability**
   - Establish dedicated funding streams (court fees, savings reinvestment)
   - Diversify funding sources
   - Build cost-effectiveness case
   - Mandate minimum appropriations

5. **Training Quality**
   - Accreditation for training providers
   - Competency-based (not just hours-based) certification
   - Ongoing supervision requirements
   - Peer review processes

6. **Selection Bias**
   - Require programs to track case severity and demographics
   - Audit case selection decisions
   - Incentivize handling of challenging cases
   - Separate performance metrics from case selection

7. **Confidentiality Protection**
   - Narrow exceptions explicitly
   - Require court order for disclosure
   - Train facilitators on confidentiality
   - Participant notification of limits

8. **Commission Composition**
   - Mandate lived experience representation
   - Term limits prevent entrenchment
   - Transparent appointment process
   - Conflict of interest policies

9. **Record Accessibility**
   - Comprehensive list of who can access sealed records
   - Regular audits of access
   - Penalties for improper disclosure
   - Automatic expungement after time period

10. **Equity Gaps**
    - Require collection of multiple demographic categories
    - Intersectional analysis
    - Community input on relevant categories
    - Regular equity audits with public reports

### General Implementation Concerns

| Concern | Mitigation Strategy |
|---------|---------------------|
| Net-widening | Limit RJ to cases that would otherwise be prosecuted |
| Co-optation | Maintain community-based program operation |
| Deprofessionalization | Balance volunteer engagement with quality standards |
| Cultural mismatch | Require culturally responsive programming |
| Power imbalances in processes | Train facilitators, screen cases, support participants |
| System resistance | Build coalitions, demonstrate outcomes |
| Sustainability | Diversify funding, institutionalize in policy |
| Quality erosion at scale | Maintain standards, provide supervision |
| Political vulnerability | Build bipartisan support, document success |
| Victim backlash | Center victim voice, ensure voluntariness |
