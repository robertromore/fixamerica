# Stakeholders: Who Is Involved and Their Interests

## Overview

Mental health crisis response reform involves a complex web of stakeholders with intersecting and sometimes conflicting interests. Understanding these stakeholders—their perspectives, incentives, and concerns—is essential for building coalitions and designing reforms that can gain broad support.

## Primary Stakeholders

### People with Mental Illness and Their Families

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Population | 52 million adults with any mental illness; 14 million with serious mental illness |
| Primary interests | Safe, effective crisis response; access to treatment; dignity and autonomy |
| Concerns | Police violence; criminalization; coerced treatment; loss of rights |
| Perspective variation | Split between those favoring more intervention and those prioritizing autonomy |

**What They Need:**

- Response that doesn't escalate crisis
- Connection to voluntary, quality treatment
- Alternatives to arrest and incarceration
- Housing and community supports
- Voice in system design

**Internal Tensions:**

- Some families want easier commitment laws; many patients oppose coerced treatment
- Some want more police involvement; others want police-free response
- Treatment Advocacy Center vs. disability rights perspectives

### Law Enforcement

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Population | ~700,000 sworn officers; 18,000 departments |
| Current role | Default first responders to mental health crises |
| Interests | Officer safety; manageable workload; clear protocols |
| Stated concerns | Lack of training; no place to take people; liability |

**What They Say:**

- "We're not trained for this"
- "We don't have anywhere to take people"
- "We spend hours on calls that aren't crimes"
- "We get blamed when things go wrong"

**Range of Positions:**

| Position | Held By |
|----------|---------|
| Support alternatives | Many officers privately; some chiefs publicly |
| Prefer co-responder models | Police unions often |
| Oppose being replaced | Some unions, rank-and-file |
| Mixed | Most departments |

### Police Unions

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Interests | Member protection; working conditions; influence |
| Typical position | Support for CIT and co-responders; skepticism of non-police response |
| Concerns | Safety; jurisdiction; liability; funding allocation |

### 911 Dispatchers and Call Centers

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Population | ~100,000 dispatchers nationally |
| Interests | Clear protocols; manageable call volume; reduced liability |
| Concerns | How to triage mental health calls; training needs; technology changes |

**Current Challenges:**

- Minimal mental health training
- No mental health responders to dispatch
- Liability concerns drive "send police" default
- System not designed for alternative response

### Emergency Medical Services (EMS)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Interests | Patient care; scope of practice clarity; manageable call volume |
| Current role | Often secondary to police; transport to ER |
| Potential role | Primary or co-responder for mental health calls |
| Concerns | Safety; training; protocol clarity |

### Hospital Emergency Departments

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Interests | Reduce mental health boarding; appropriate patient flow |
| Current burden | 12 million mental health ER visits annually |
| Concerns | Safety; costs; lack of psychiatric beds; long waits |

**ER Perspective:**

- Mental health patients wait longest (boarding)
- ERs not designed for psychiatric care
- Medical clearance requirements create bottlenecks
- Need alternatives to divert patients

### Mental Health Providers

#### Community Mental Health Centers

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Interests | Adequate funding; manageable caseloads; workforce |
| Current challenges | Underfunded; understaffed; waitlists; crisis not core mission |
| Potential role | Operate crisis services if funded |

#### Private Practitioners

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Interests | Sustainable practice; patient wellbeing; avoiding liability |
| Typical position | Support crisis services but not directly involved |
| Limitations | Not available 24/7; avoid high-acuity patients |

#### Crisis Service Providers

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Interests | Funding; integration with 911; workforce development |
| Current status | Limited coverage; grant-dependent; fragmented |
| Potential role | Central to alternative response models |

### Jails and Sheriffs

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Interests | Manageable population; reduced liability; cost control |
| Current burden | 15-20% of jail population seriously mentally ill |
| Stated concerns | Not equipped for mental health; costs; suicide liability |

**Sheriff Perspectives:**

| Position | Prevalence |
|----------|------------|
| "We shouldn't be the mental health system" | Common |
| Support diversion | Growing |
| Frustrated with lack of resources | Universal |
| Want better mental health care in jails | Variable |

### Courts

#### Criminal Court Judges

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Interests | Docket management; appropriate outcomes; reducing recidivism |
| Current challenges | Competency evaluation backlogs; lack of treatment options |
| Potential role | Mental health court expansion; diversion |

#### Mental Health Court Judges

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Interests | Participant success; adequate resources; treatment availability |
| Challenges | Limited slots; waiting lists for treatment; funding uncertainty |

### Public Defenders and Defense Attorneys

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Interests | Client wellbeing; avoiding criminalization; alternatives to prosecution |
| Position | Generally support diversion; concerned about coercion |
| Concerns | Competency delays; inadequate treatment; criminalization of illness |

### Prosecutors

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Interests | Public safety; case disposition; justice |
| Position | Variable—some support diversion, others prioritize prosecution |
| Concerns | Repeat offenders; public safety; accountability |

### Medicaid and Insurance

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Interests | Cost control; appropriate care; quality outcomes |
| Current role | Primary payer for mental health services |
| Key issue | IMD exclusion limits psychiatric bed funding |

### Federal Government

#### Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA)

| Role | Details |
|------|---------|
| Function | Federal mental health/substance abuse agency |
| Tools | Block grants; discretionary grants; technical assistance |
| Recent role | 988 implementation; crisis system funding |

#### Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS)

| Role | Details |
|------|---------|
| Function | Medicaid/Medicare policy and funding |
| Key issue | IMD exclusion policy |
| Recent action | Some waiver flexibility for crisis services |

#### Department of Justice

| Role | Details |
|------|---------|
| Function | Civil rights enforcement; police reform |
| Tools | Pattern-or-practice investigations; consent decrees |
| Recent role | Addressing police use of force against mentally ill |

### State Governments

| Role | Details |
|------|---------|
| Function | Primary responsibility for mental health systems |
| Levers | State hospitals; Medicaid administration; 988 funding; licensing |
| Variation | Enormous differences in state investment and approach |

### Local Governments

| Role | Details |
|------|---------|
| Function | Police departments; 911; jails; some mental health services |
| Challenges | Limited budgets; no mandate for mental health crisis services |
| Potential | Can implement alternative response models |

| Mental Health America | Advocacy for services and rights; policy reform |
| Treatment Advocacy Center | Advocates for easier commitment; more intervention |

| Organization | Perspective |
|--------------|-------------|
| Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law | Legal advocacy; community integration; rights |
| Autistic Self Advocacy Network | Opposes police response; supports alternatives |
| Disability Rights Advocates | Legal challenges; community services |

| Organization | Perspective |
|--------------|-------------|

## Stakeholder Alignment

| Reform Element | Strong Support | Mixed | Opposition |
|----------------|---------------|-------|------------|
| Non-police crisis response | Patients, disability rights, reform advocates | Police chiefs (some), unions | Some unions, "law and order" advocates |
| CIT expansion | NAMI, many police, most reformers | — | Those who see it as insufficient |
| Co-responder models | Many police, mental health providers | Reform advocates (mixed) | — |
| Mental health courts | Most stakeholders | Some defense attorneys (coercion concerns) | — |
| Diversion | Most stakeholders | Prosecutors (some) | Victims' groups (some) |
| 988 expansion | Nearly universal | Concern about police dispatch | — |
| More psychiatric beds | Treatment Advocacy Center, some families | Disability rights (community focus) | — |
| Easier commitment | Treatment Advocacy Center | Most stakeholders | Disability rights, many patients |

## Coalition-Building Opportunities

### Areas of Broad Agreement

| Reform | Supporting Coalition |
|--------|---------------------|
| More crisis services | Nearly everyone |
| Better 911 triage | 911 centers, police, reformers |
| Reduce jail mental health burden | Sheriffs, reformers, mental health advocates |
| Fund alternatives | Mental health providers, police, advocates |

### Strategic Alignments

| Unlikely Allies | Common Ground |
|-----------------|---------------|
| Police unions + reform advocates | Officers want fewer mental health calls |
| Sheriffs + decarceration advocates | Both want fewer mentally ill in jail |
| Fiscal conservatives + progressives | Cost savings from diversion |
| Families + patients | Both want better outcomes (may differ on means) |
