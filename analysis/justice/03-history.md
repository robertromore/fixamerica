# Criminal Justice Reform: History

## Early American Justice

### Colonial and Founding Era

**Characteristics**:

- Local, informal justice
- Corporal punishment (whipping, stocks, branding)
- Capital punishment for many offenses
- Fines and restitution
- Incarceration rare (jails for pretrial only)

**Emerging Ideas**:

- Enlightenment influence on punishment
- Proportionality concerns
- Rehabilitation concepts

### Early Republic

**Penitentiary Movement (1790s-1830s)**:

- Philadelphia: Solitary confinement and reflection
- Auburn, NY: Silent labor during day, solitary at night
- Belief in rehabilitation through isolation and discipline
- Quickly became overcrowded and brutal

**Key Developments**:

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 1790 | Walnut Street Jail (Philadelphia) - first penitentiary |
| 1816 | Auburn Prison opens |
| 1829 | Eastern State Penitentiary |
| 1841 | First probation (John Augustus, Boston) |

## 19th Century

### Antebellum Period

**Northern Prisons**:

- Penitentiary system expands
- Contract labor
- Discipline through silence, labor, religion

**Southern "Justice"**:

- Slavery as primary form of control
- Minimal formal criminal justice for whites
- Brutal slave codes for enslaved people
- Plantation punishment

### Post-Civil War

**Convict Leasing (1865-1920s)**:

- 13th Amendment exception: "except as punishment for crime"
- Black Codes criminalized Black life (vagrancy, loitering)
- Convicts leased to private companies
- Mortality rates higher than slavery
- Continuation of slavery by another name

**Key Developments**:

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 1865 | 13th Amendment (slavery exception) |
| 1865-1890s | Black Codes across South |
| 1870s-1920s | Convict leasing peak |
| 1876 | Reformatory movement (Elmira) |

### Progressive Era

**Reformatory Model**:

- Focus on youth and "reformable" offenders
- Education and vocational training
- Indeterminate sentences
- Parole expansion

**Juvenile Justice**:

- First juvenile court (Cook County, 1899)
- Separate system for youth
- Rehabilitation focus (in theory)

**Probation Expansion**:

- Formalized in law
- Alternative to incarceration
- Supervision in community

## Early 20th Century

### 1900-1960

**Characteristics**:

- Relatively stable incarceration rates (~100-150 per 100,000)
- Indeterminate sentencing dominant
- Rehabilitation as stated goal
- Parole boards with wide discretion
- Significant regional variation

**Key Developments**:

| Era | Development |
|-----|-------------|
| 1900s-1930s | Professional policing movement |
| 1920s-1933 | Prohibition and organized crime |
| 1930s | Federal Bureau of Prisons established |
| 1940s-1950s | Prison treatment programs |
| 1961 | Mapp v. Ohio (exclusionary rule) |
| 1963 | Gideon v. Wainwright (right to counsel) |
| 1966 | Miranda v. Arizona (warning requirement) |

### Civil Rights Era Courts

**Warren Court Revolution**:

- Extended Bill of Rights to states
- Exclusionary rule
- Right to counsel
- Miranda warnings
- Limitations on police

**Backlash**:

- "Law and order" politics emerge
- Nixon campaigns against Warren Court
- Crime becomes political issue

## The Punitive Turn (1970s-2000s)

### Origins

**Political Context**:

- Civil rights backlash
- Urban unrest
- Rising crime rates (1960s-1990s)
- Nixon's "law and order" campaign
- Reagan's war on drugs

**Intellectual Context**:

- "Nothing works" on rehabilitation (Martinson, 1974)
- Just deserts theory
- Incapacitation arguments
- Rational choice criminology

### Key Legislation

| Year | Law | Effect |
|------|-----|--------|
| 1973 | Rockefeller Drug Laws (NY) | First mandatory minimums for drugs |
| 1984 | Sentencing Reform Act | Federal guidelines, abolished parole |
| 1986 | Anti-Drug Abuse Act | Crack/powder disparity, mandatory minimums |
| 1988 | Anti-Drug Abuse Act | Death penalty for drug kingpins |
| 1994 | Violent Crime Control Act | Three strikes, truth in sentencing, prison funding |
| 1996 | PLRA | Limited prisoner litigation |
| 1996 | AEDPA | Limited habeas corpus |

### Mass Incarceration

**Growth**:

| Year | Incarceration Rate | Total Incarcerated |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|
| 1972 | 93 per 100,000 | ~200,000 |
| 1980 | 139 | ~320,000 |
| 1990 | 297 | ~740,000 |
| 2000 | 478 | ~1.4 million |
| 2008 | 760 (peak) | ~2.3 million |

**Drivers**:

- Mandatory minimums
- Truth in sentencing (serve 85%+)
- Three strikes laws
- War on drugs
- Increased prosecution rates
- Longer sentences
- Parole revocations

### Racial Disparities

**Drug War**:

- Similar usage rates across races
- Black arrest rates 4x white
- Crack vs. powder disparity (100:1)
- Targeting of urban communities

**Policing**:

- Stop and frisk
- Broken windows
- Racial profiling
- War on crime rhetoric

## Reform Era (2000s-Present)

### Early Signs

**2000s**:

- Crime decline continued
- Fiscal pressures on states
- Red state reform (Texas Right on Crime)
- Innocence movement

### Building Momentum

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 2010 | Fair Sentencing Act (reduced crack disparity) |
| 2011 | Brown v. Plata (California prison overcrowding) |
| 2013 | Smart Sentencing Act introduced |
| 2014 | Ferguson and Black Lives Matter |
| 2015 | DOJ Ferguson report |
| 2016 | Sentencing Commission retroactivity |
| 2018 | First Step Act |

### State-Level Reform

**Common Reforms**:

- Reducing mandatory minimums
- Bail reform
- Marijuana legalization/decriminalization
- Sentencing commission establishment
- Justice reinvestment initiatives

**Leaders**:

- Texas, Georgia (conservative states)
- New Jersey, California (liberal states)
- Bipartisan coalitions

### Current Tensions

**Reform Momentum vs. Backlash**:

- Crime increases (2020-2022) after historic lows
- Defund police debate
- Progressive prosecutor recalls
- State-level reform reversals in some jurisdictions
- Continued bipartisan support for some reforms
