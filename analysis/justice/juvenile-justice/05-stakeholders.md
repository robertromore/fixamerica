# Juvenile Justice Reform: Stakeholders

## Primary Stakeholders

### System-Involved Youth

**Current Situation**:

- ~37,000 in residential placement
- Millions with juvenile records
- Disproportionately youth of color
- Often from trauma backgrounds
- Limited voice in system decisions

**Stakes**:

- Freedom and development
- Education and future opportunity
- Family connections
- Mental health and well-being
- Life trajectory

**Advocacy Capacity**:

- Youth advocacy organizations emerging
- Limited political power (can't vote)
- Stigmatized and marginalized
- Growing survivor organizing
- Increasing media attention

### Families of System-Involved Youth

**Current Situation**:

- Often blamed by system
- Limited resources to navigate
- Separated from children
- Financial burden (fees, travel, calls)
- Marginalized in proceedings

**Stakes**:

- Family unity
- Child's well-being
- Economic survival
- Parental rights
- Emotional toll

**Advocacy Capacity**:

- Family advocacy organizations growing
- Coalition potential
- Personal stories powerful
- Often overlaps with other justice-involved communities

### Youth at Risk of System Involvement

**Current Situation**:

- Youth in under-resourced communities
- Youth in schools with heavy policing
- Youth with untreated mental health needs
- Youth in foster care
- Youth experiencing poverty

**Stakes**:

- Avoiding system involvement
- Access to support and opportunity
- Educational success
- Safety without criminalization
- Healthy development

## Justice System Actors

### Juvenile Court Judges

**Role and Power**:

- Make detention decisions
- Make transfer decisions
- Order dispositions
- Significant discretion
- Set local court culture

**Current Interests**:

- Manage caseloads
- Reduce liability
- Public safety (as they define it)
- Professional reputation
- Efficiency

**Reform Position**: Mixed—some are reform leaders, others resist

### Prosecutors

**Role and Power**:

- Decide whether to file charges
- Make transfer decisions in some states
- Recommend dispositions
- Control plea bargaining
- Shape case outcomes

**Current Interests**:

- Conviction rates
- Career advancement
- Victim satisfaction
- Office policies
- Public safety framing

**Reform Position**: Generally cautious; some progressive prosecutors reform-oriented

### Defense Attorneys

**Role and Power**:

- Represent youth in proceedings
- Advocate for clients
- Often overworked
- Quality varies significantly
- Critical to due process

**Stakes**:

- Client outcomes
- Workload manageable
- Resources adequate
- Professional satisfaction
- Systemic reform

**Reform Position**: Generally supportive of reducing incarceration, improving due process

### Probation Officers

**Role and Power**:

- Supervise youth on probation
- Make recommendations to court
- Can violate youth for non-compliance
- Often high caseloads
- Significant discretion

**Current Interests**:

- Manageable caseloads
- Job security
- Professional standards
- Risk management
- Compliance-focused culture

**Reform Position**: Mixed—depends on department culture and individual

### Detention and Facility Staff

**Role and Power**:

- Day-to-day control over youth
- Significant discretion
- Can recommend progress or problems
- Set facility culture
- Often underpaid, undertrained

**Stakes**:

- Job security
- Safety at work
- Wages and benefits
- Working conditions
- Professional identity

**Reform Position**: Often resistant (job threat); unions may oppose

## Schools and Education

### School Administrators

**Role**:

- Set discipline policies
- Decide on police presence
- Control referrals to court
- Shape school culture
- Balance competing pressures

**Stakes**:

- Test scores and accountability
- School safety
- Legal liability
- Parent satisfaction
- Orderly environment

**Reform Position**: Varies widely—some lead reform, others rely on exclusion

### Teachers

**Role**:

- Front-line with students
- Make referrals for discipline
- Can de-escalate or escalate
- May request police intervention
- Shape classroom environment

**Stakes**:

- Classroom management
- Professional support
- Safety
- Time and resources
- Student success

**Reform Position**: Mixed—need support to reduce reliance on removal

### School Resource Officers

**Role**:

- Police officers assigned to schools
- May handle discipline
- Can arrest students
- Visible authority presence
- Often inadequate training for youth

**Stakes**:

- Job continuation
- Define role (law enforcement vs. mentoring)
- Professional standing
- Safety (as they define it)

**Reform Position**: Generally defensive of role; police unions support presence

## Government Actors

### State Legislators

**Role**:

- Set age of jurisdiction
- Create transfer laws
- Appropriate funding
- Mandate or limit practices
- Significant power over system

**Stakes**:

- Reelection
- Public safety perception
- Fiscal responsibility
- Constituent concerns
- Party politics

**Reform Position**: Bipartisan support growing; some remain punitive

### Governors

**Role**:

- Appoint agency leaders
- Set priorities
- Veto or sign legislation
- High-profile cases
- State leadership

**Reform Position**: Varies by party and individual; fiscal argument persuasive

### Federal Government

**Role**:

- OJJDP funding and standards
- Research and data
- Grant conditions
- Civil rights enforcement
- Model development

**Reform Position**: Generally supportive of reform (Biden administration)

## Community and Services

### Community-Based Organizations

**Role**:

- Provide alternative programs
- Serve diverted youth
- Advocate for reform
- Employment and training
- Mentoring and support

**Stakes**:

- Funding for programs
- Youth outcomes
- Community investment
- Contract opportunities
- Mission fulfillment

**Reform Position**: Strong reform—alternatives depend on system change

### Mental Health and Treatment Providers

**Role**:

- Provide treatment to youth
- Assess and diagnose
- Work within facilities or community
- Expertise on development
- Therapeutic interventions

**Stakes**:

- Access to serve youth
- Funding for services
- Professional standards
- Collaboration with justice system
- Effective treatment

**Reform Position**: Generally reform-oriented; treatment preferred to incarceration

## Private Industry

### Private Facility Operators

**Role**:

- Run facilities under contract
- Profit from incarceration
- Lobby for contracts
- Variable quality

**Stakes**:

- Revenue
- Contracts maintained
- Growth potential
- Regulatory environment

**Reform Position**: Generally oppose reform that reduces incarceration

### Technology Companies

**Role**:

- Monitoring technology
- Risk assessment tools
- Case management systems
- Surveillance technology

**Stakes**:

- Government contracts
- Market growth
- Regulatory approval
- Public acceptance

**Reform Position**: Varies—some products support reform, others increase surveillance
