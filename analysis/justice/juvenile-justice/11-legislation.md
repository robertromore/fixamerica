# Juvenile Justice Reform: Legislation

## Overview

Juvenile justice reform requires legislation addressing incarceration, transfer to adult court, school discipline, and juvenile records. Federal legislation can provide funding and standards; state legislation is essential for system transformation.

## Constitutional Considerations

### Relevant Constitutional Provisions

**Eighth Amendment**: Prohibits cruel and unusual punishment; Supreme Court has applied to juveniles

**Fourteenth Amendment**: Due process and equal protection apply to juveniles

**Sixth Amendment**: Right to counsel applies in delinquency proceedings

### Key Supreme Court Precedents

| Case | Holding | Impact |
|------|---------|--------|
| *In re Gault* (1967) | Due process applies | Fundamental rights |
| *Roper v. Simmons* (2005) | No death penalty | "Children are different" |
| *Graham v. Florida* (2010) | No LWOP for non-homicide | Rehabilitation required |
| *Miller v. Alabama* (2012) | No mandatory LWOP | Individualized sentencing |

## Federal Legislation

### Youth Justice System Improvement Act

**Purpose**: Establish federal standards and funding for youth justice reform.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Youth Justice System
Improvement Act of 2024".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that:
(1) Adolescent brain development research establishes that
youth are more amenable to rehabilitation and less culpable
than adults.
(2) Youth incarceration has declined 60% from its peak with
no increase in youth crime.
(3) Community-based alternatives are more effective and less
costly than incarceration.
(4) Racial disparities in youth incarceration are profound
and unjustified.
(5) Transfer to adult court harms youth and public safety.

SEC. 3. GRANTS FOR JUVENILE JUSTICE REFORM.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—The Administrator of the Office of Juvenile
Justice and Delinquency Prevention shall award grants to States
for juvenile justice reform.

(b) PRIORITY USES.—Grant funds shall be used for:
(1) Community-based alternatives to incarceration;
(2) Diversion programs;
(3) Mental health and substance abuse treatment;
(4) Family engagement programs;
(5) Closing large facilities and transitioning to small,
therapeutic settings.

(c) CONDITIONS.—To receive grants, States must:
(1) Commit to reducing youth incarceration;
(2) Collect and report data on racial disparities;
(3) Limit transfer of youth to adult court;
(4) Prohibit solitary confinement exceeding 4 hours;
(5) Provide automatic sealing of juvenile records.

(d) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There is authorized
$500 million annually for this program.

SEC. 4. PROHIBITION ON YOUTH IN ADULT FACILITIES.

(a) No youth under age 18 shall be held in an adult jail or
prison, regardless of the court of jurisdiction.

(b) Youth ages 18-21 held in adult facilities shall be housed
separately from adults over 21.

(c) All youth shall have access to education, mental health
services, and rehabilitative programming.

SEC. 5. DATA COLLECTION.

(a) REQUIRED DATA.—States receiving funds under this Act
shall collect and report data on:
(1) Youth incarceration by race, ethnicity, and gender;
(2) Disparities at each decision point;
(3) Transfers to adult court;
(4) Conditions of confinement;
(5) Recidivism outcomes.

(b) PUBLIC REPORTING.—Data shall be publicly available and
reported to Congress annually.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect one year after enactment.
```

### School Safety Without Police Act

**Purpose**: End criminalization of school discipline.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "School Safety Without Police Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that:
(1) Police in schools contribute to the school-to-prison
pipeline.
(2) Black students are arrested in school at 3 times the
rate of white students.
(3) Students with disabilities are disproportionately
arrested and disciplined.
(4) Counselors and mental health professionals are more
effective than police for school safety.

SEC. 3. SCHOOL COUNSELOR GRANTS.

(a) The Secretary of Education shall award grants to local
educational agencies to hire school counselors, social workers,
and mental health professionals.

(b) GRANT AMOUNTS.—Grants shall be sufficient to achieve a
ratio of 1 counselor per 250 students.

(c) CONDITION.—Grant recipients shall not use Federal funds
for school-based law enforcement.

(d) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized $2 billion annually.

SEC. 4. LIMITATION ON FEDERAL FUNDS FOR SCHOOL POLICE.

(a) No Federal education funds may be used to hire, train,
or equip school-based law enforcement officers.

(b) This prohibition includes COPS in Schools funding.

SEC. 5. REPORTING.

(a) Local educational agencies receiving Federal funds shall
report annually on:
(1) School arrests by race, disability status, and gender;
(2) Referrals to law enforcement;
(3) School discipline by race, disability status, and gender.

(b) Reports shall be publicly available.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect at the beginning of the first
school year beginning after enactment.
```

## State Model Legislation

### Model Youth Justice Reform Act

**Purpose**: Comprehensive reform of state juvenile justice system.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Youth Justice Reform Act".

SECTION 2. PURPOSE.

The purpose of this Act is to:
(a) Reduce reliance on incarceration for youth;
(b) Expand evidence-based community alternatives;
(c) Address racial and ethnic disparities;
(d) Protect the rights and development of youth.

SECTION 3. RAISE THE AGE OF JUVENILE JURISDICTION.

(a) JUVENILE JURISDICTION.—The juvenile court shall have
exclusive original jurisdiction over:
(1) All persons under age 21 alleged to have committed
a delinquent act; and
(2) All persons under age 25 for offenses committed
before age 21.

(b) NO ADULT PROSECUTION.—No person shall be prosecuted as
an adult for any offense committed before age 21.

SECTION 4. PROHIBITION ON TRANSFER TO ADULT COURT.

(a) REPEAL.—All statutory provisions allowing transfer,
waiver, or certification of youth to adult court are repealed.

(b) JUVENILE COURT EXCLUSIVE.—The juvenile court shall have
exclusive jurisdiction over all persons for offenses committed
before age 21.

SECTION 5. DIVERSION.

(a) PRESUMPTION.—There shall be a presumption of diversion
for all first-time offenses and all misdemeanors.

(b) DIVERSION OPTIONS.—Diversion options shall include:
(1) Warning and release;
(2) Community service;
(3) Restitution;
(4) Restorative justice conference;
(5) Counseling or treatment;
(6) Community-based program.

(c) RECORD.—Diverted cases shall not create a public record.

SECTION 6. DETENTION LIMITS.

(a) PRESUMPTION OF RELEASE.—Youth shall be released pending
adjudication unless detention is necessary to ensure appearance
or protect public safety.

(b) RISK ASSESSMENT.—Detention decisions shall be based on
validated risk assessment.

(c) TIME LIMITS.—
(1) Initial detention hearing within 24 hours;
(2) Maximum detention of 21 days before adjudication;
(3) Extensions only with court finding and youth consent.

SECTION 7. DISPOSITION LIMITS.

(a) LEAST RESTRICTIVE.—The court shall order the least
restrictive disposition consistent with youth rehabilitation
and public safety.

(b) COMMITMENT LIMITS.—
(1) No commitment for misdemeanors;
(2) Maximum commitment of 1 year for most felonies;
(3) Maximum commitment of 3 years for violent felonies.

(c) REVIEW.—All committed youth shall have disposition
reviewed every 90 days.

SECTION 8. FACILITY CLOSURES.

(a) Within 3 years, the State shall close all secure
facilities with capacity exceeding 30 youth.

(b) Funds from closures shall be reinvested in community
alternatives.

SECTION 9. PROHIBITION ON HARMFUL PRACTICES.

(a) SOLITARY CONFINEMENT.—No youth shall be held in solitary
confinement or room confinement exceeding 4 hours.

(b) RESTRAINTS.—Mechanical restraints shall not be used
except when necessary to prevent imminent physical harm.

(c) NO SHACKLING.—Youth shall not be shackled in court
proceedings.

SECTION 10. JUVENILE RECORDS.

(a) AUTOMATIC SEALING.—Juvenile records shall be automatically
sealed:
(1) Upon diversion completion;
(2) Upon dismissal;
(3) One year after disposition for misdemeanors;
(4) Three years after disposition for felonies.

(b) AUTOMATIC EXPUNGEMENT.—Juvenile records shall be
automatically expunged at age 25.

(c) LIMITATIONS.—Sealed records shall not be disclosed to
employers, landlords, or educational institutions.

SECTION 11. RACIAL DISPARITY REDUCTION.

(a) DATA.—The Department shall collect and publish data on
racial and ethnic disparities at every decision point.

(b) DISPARITY REDUCTION PLAN.—If disparities exceed 1.5:1
at any decision point, the Department shall develop and
implement a disparity reduction plan.

(c) ACCOUNTABILITY.—Progress on disparity reduction shall
be reported annually.

SECTION 12. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect one year after enactment.
```

### Model School Discipline Reform Act

**Purpose**: End school-to-prison pipeline.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Student Support Act".

SECTION 2. LIMITATIONS ON SUSPENSIONS AND EXPULSIONS.

(a) PROHIBITION.—No student shall be suspended or expelled
for:
(1) Tardiness or truancy;
(2) Dress code violations;
(3) Disrespect or defiance;
(4) Cell phone use.

(b) GRADUATED RESPONSE.—Before out-of-school suspension,
schools shall first employ:
(1) Restorative conversation;
(2) Family conference;
(3) Behavioral intervention;
(4) In-school alternatives.

SECTION 3. LIMITATIONS ON SCHOOL-BASED ARRESTS.

(a) No student shall be arrested on school grounds for
conduct that does not constitute a felony involving physical
violence or weapons.

(b) Law enforcement shall not be used for routine discipline.

SECTION 4. REMOVAL OF SCHOOL RESOURCE OFFICERS.

(a) Within 3 years, no public school shall employ or contract
for school-based law enforcement officers.

(b) Funds previously used for school police shall be
redirected to counselors, social workers, and mental health
professionals.

SECTION 5. SCHOOL SUPPORT STAFF.

(a) Within 5 years, schools shall achieve:
(1) 1 counselor per 250 students;
(2) 1 social worker per 400 students;
(3) 1 psychologist per 500 students.

SECTION 6. DATA COLLECTION AND REPORTING.

(a) Schools shall collect and report data on:
(1) Suspensions by race, disability, and gender;
(2) Expulsions by race, disability, and gender;
(3) Arrests by race, disability, and gender;
(4) Referrals to law enforcement.

(b) Data shall be publicly available.

SECTION 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect at the beginning of the first
school year after enactment.
```

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Status offense conversion | Charging status offenses as delinquency | High |
| Probation violations | Using technical violations to incarcerate | High |
| Secure treatment facilities | Calling secure placement "treatment" | Moderate |
| Charter school exclusion | Excluding from reform | Moderate |
| Age-off provisions | Youth aging into adult system | Moderate |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Community program quality | Variable outcomes | Underfunding, no standards |
| Rural capacity | Limited alternatives | Geographic challenges |
| Workforce | Insufficient trained staff | Inadequate investment |
| Implementation variation | Uneven reform | Local discretion |
| Data systems | Incomplete information | Technology gaps |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Status offense protections**: Explicit prohibition on converting status offenses to delinquency charges; require evidence of delinquent conduct.

2. **Probation violation limits**: Cap probation terms; prohibit incarceration for technical violations; graduated responses required.

3. **Secure facility definitions**: Define "secure" by actual conditions, not labels; apply protections regardless of facility name.

4. **Charter school inclusion**: Explicitly apply discipline reforms to all publicly funded schools; require same data reporting.

5. **Age-off protections**: Require juvenile court retention even after age 21 for disposition completion; prohibit transfer to adult system for violations.

### General Implementation Concerns

- Adequate funding for community alternatives before facility closures
- Training for all system actors on new approaches
- Community capacity building, especially in rural areas
- Data systems to track implementation
- Political will to sustain reform through challenges
- Youth and family voice in implementation
