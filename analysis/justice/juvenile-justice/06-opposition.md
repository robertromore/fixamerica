# Juvenile Justice Reform: Opposition

## Organized Opposition

### Private Facility Operators

**Major Players**:

- Youth Services International
- Rite of Passage
- Sequel Youth and Family Services
- Regional contractors
- Residential treatment chains

**Tactics**:

| Tactic | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Lobbying | Fight facility closures |
| Political contributions | Influence legislators |
| Contract cultivation | Build relationships with counties |
| Quality claims | Argue private is better than public |
| Job creation | Emphasize local employment |

**Resources**:

- For-profit revenue funds lobbying
- Campaign contributions
- Trade associations
- Relationships with juvenile justice officials

### Law Enforcement Organizations

**Groups**:

- National Association of School Resource Officers
- Fraternal Order of Police
- State and local police unions
- School police associations

**Positions**:

- SROs essential for school safety
- Youth crime requires law enforcement response
- Alternatives are soft on crime
- Police protect students

**Tactics**:

- Emphasize school shootings
- Cite individual positive relationships
- Lobby against removing SROs
- Frame reform as endangering children

### Corrections Officers Unions

**Organizations**:

- AFSCME juvenile justice locals
- State correctional officer unions
- Facility staff associations

**Stakes**:

- Job preservation as facilities close
- Wages and benefits
- Working conditions
- Political influence

**Tactics**:

- Oppose facility closures
- Lobby legislators
- Emphasize public safety
- Cite rehabilitation programs they run

### Victims' Rights Organizations

**Position**:

- Youth who commit serious offenses should face serious consequences
- Victims deserve justice
- Families of victims want accountability
- Reform may re-traumatize victims

**Nuance**: Some victims' rights groups support reform; division in movement

## Political Opposition

### "Tough on Crime" Politicians

**Arguments**:

- Rising crime attributed to reform
- Youth crime becoming more violent
- Victims' families deserve justice
- Public safety paramount
- "Superpredator" framing may return

**Constituencies**:

- Suburban voters concerned about crime
- Victims and families
- Law enforcement
- Older voters
- Some communities of color experiencing violence

### Prosecutors' Associations

**Organizations**:

- National District Attorneys Association
- State prosecutors' associations
- Local district attorney associations

**Positions**:

- Prosecutorial discretion must be maintained
- Transfer to adult court necessary for serious crimes
- Detention needed for safety
- Reform goes too far

**Tactics**:

- Oppose raising age of jurisdiction
- Defend transfer authority
- Emphasize serious cases
- Lobby against reform legislation

## Arguments Against Reform

### Public Safety Arguments

**"Youth Crime Is Rising"**:

- Claims juvenile crime increasing
- Cites individual serious cases
- Links reform to crime increases
- Emphasizes violence

**Rebuttal**:

- Youth crime down significantly overall
- Individual cases don't reflect trends
- States with reform haven't seen crime spikes
- Incarceration increases recidivism

### "Accountability" Arguments

**"Youth Must Face Consequences"**:

- Rehabilitation isn't accountability
- Victims deserve justice
- Actions have consequences
- Deterrence requires punishment

**Rebuttal**:

- Alternatives provide accountability
- Victims often support restorative approaches
- Consequences can be rehabilitative
- Deterrence research doesn't support incarceration

### System Arguments

**"Current System Works"**:

- Professionals know best
- System protects public
- Reforms are untested
- Change is risky

**Rebuttal**:

- Current system has 70-80% recidivism
- Evidence-based alternatives proven
- Many reforms already successful
- Status quo is the risky choice

### Capacity Arguments

**"No Alternatives Available"**:

- Community can't handle all youth
- Treatment not available
- Staff not trained
- Infrastructure doesn't exist

**Rebuttal**:

- Alternatives can be built
- Money from closing facilities can fund alternatives
- Many successful models exist
- Phased implementation possible

## Overcoming Opposition

### Addressing Public Safety Concerns

**Evidence and Data**:

- Present research on recidivism
- Show success of reform states
- Document harms of incarceration
- Feature rehabilitative success stories

**Coalition Building**:

- Crime survivors for reform
- Law enforcement voices for reform
- Conservative reform supporters
- Bipartisan messengers

### Addressing Victim Concerns

**Restorative Justice**:

- Offer victim-centered alternatives
- Include victims in process
- Show victim satisfaction with alternatives
- Address trauma without incarceration

**Honest Engagement**:

- Acknowledge harm done
- Distinguish accountability from punishment
- Include victim advocates in reform
- Don't dismiss concerns

### Addressing Economic Concerns

**Reinvestment**:

- Commit to reinvest savings in communities
- Job training for displaced workers
- Alternative careers in community programs
- Economic development in affected areas

### Addressing Political Concerns

**Bipartisan Framing**:

- Fiscal responsibility (conservatives)
- Family values (conservatives)
- Racial justice (progressives)
- Youth development (universal)

**Winning Messengers**:

- Conservative voices
- Law enforcement reform supporters
- Formerly incarcerated youth
- Families affected
