# Policing Reform: Opposition

## Police Unions

### Major Organizations

| Organization | Membership | Influence |
|--------------|------------|-----------|
| Fraternal Order of Police | 360,000+ | National lobbying, endorsements |
| Police Benevolent Associations | Varies | Strong in major cities |
| State FOP lodges | Varies | State legislation |
| Local unions | Varies | Contract negotiations |

### Positions on Key Issues

| Issue | Union Position | Rationale Offered |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| Qualified immunity | Defend absolutely | Officers need protection from frivolous suits |
| Use of force standards | Resist restrictions | Split-second decisions can't be second-guessed |
| Civilian oversight | Oppose or weaken | Officers should be judged by peers |
| Body cameras | Accept with conditions | Officer control of footage, privacy |
| Discipline transparency | Block disclosure | Privacy, due process |
| Decertification | Resist | Career should be protected |
| Chokeholds | Mixed | Sometimes necessary |

### Tactics

**Contract Negotiations**:

- Waiting periods before investigation
- Access to evidence before statements
- Discipline appeal procedures
- Record expungement provisions

**Political Action**:

- Endorsements (leverage over politicians)
- Campaign contributions
- Mobilization of officers and families
- "We won't protect you" threats

**Public Relations**:

- Frame reform as anti-officer
- Highlight officer deaths and injuries
- "Who will you call?" messaging
- Victim advocacy partnerships

### Specific Campaigns

**Against Reform Legislation**:

- George Floyd Justice in Policing Act opposition
- State qualified immunity bills
- Use of force standard changes
- Civilian oversight authority

**Against Reform Prosecutors**:

- Recall campaigns
- Endorsement of opponents
- "No confidence" votes
- Public criticism

## Law Enforcement Executives

### International Association of Chiefs of Police

**Position**: More moderate than rank-and-file unions

**Selective Reform Support**:

- Accept some use of force changes
- Support training improvements
- Defend qualified immunity
- Protect institutional authority

### Police Executives Research Forum (PERF)

**Position**: Reform-oriented leadership organization

**But Still**:

- Defends profession
- Incremental change preferred
- Union relationships constrain

### National Sheriffs' Association

**Position**: Conservative

| Issue | Position |
|-------|----------|
| Jail reform | Resist |
| Immigration enforcement | Support 287(g) |
| Consent decrees | Oppose |
| Federal oversight | Oppose |

## Political Actors

### Tough-on-Crime Politicians

**Incentives**:

- Police endorsements valuable
- Fear of "soft on crime" attacks
- Victim advocacy political cover
- Crime increases exploited

**Tactics**:

- Block reform legislation
- Blame reform for crime increases
- "Law and order" rhetoric
- Attack reform prosecutors

### Conservative Media

**Messaging**:

| Claim | Reality |
|-------|---------|
| "War on cops" | Officer deaths at historic lows |
| Reform causes crime | Crime rose nationally regardless of local policy |
| Defund = chaos | Few cities actually reduced police budgets |
| Officers can't do their jobs | Officers never required to violate rights |

### ALEC and Policy Organizations

**Role**: Model legislation opposing reform

**Positions**:

- Defend qualified immunity
- Oppose civilian oversight
- Resist use of force standards
- Support police union protections

## Arguments Against Reform

### Officer Safety

**Claim**: Reforms endanger officers

**Response**:

| Claim | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| Reform increases officer deaths | No evidence; deaths at historic lows |
| Officers hesitate to act | Hesitation to use force not documented |
| De-escalation endangers officers | De-escalation reduces injuries to officers |

### Effective Policing

**Claim**: Reforms make policing impossible

**Response**:

| Claim | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| Can't police without immunity | Other professionals work without absolute immunity |
| Oversight chills enforcement | Officers can enforce law within constitutional limits |
| Use of force standards unrealistic | Other countries police with stricter standards |

### Crime Increases

**Claim**: Reform causes crime increases

**Response**:

| Claim | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| 2020-22 crime increase from reform | Increases were national, including non-reform areas |
| Ferguson effect | Limited evidence; other factors explain trends |
| Police "pulling back" | Discretionary work; core functions unchanged |

### Due Process

**Claim**: Officers deserve due process

**Response**:

| Claim | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| Officers need contract protections | Extraordinary protections exceed other employees |
| Discipline should be appealed | Arbitrators inappropriately overturn discipline |
| Officers presumed guilty | Standard is accountability, not presumption of guilt |

## Weaknesses in Opposition

### Declining Trust

**Public Opinion Shifts**:

- Majority now supports some reforms
- Younger Americans more pro-reform
- Video evidence changes minds
- International comparisons embarrassing

### Evidence Base

**Research Against Opposition Claims**:

| Claim | Research |
|-------|----------|
| Training doesn't matter | Countries with more training have fewer killings |
| Qualified immunity necessary | Other countries manage without it |
| Aggressive policing works | Limited evidence; community trust matters more |

### Internal Contradictions

**Problems**:

| Contradiction | Explanation |
|---------------|-------------|
| Defend bad officers | "Few bad apples" claim undermined by defense of all |
| Claim professionalism | Resist professional standards |
| Want respect | Resist accountability that builds respect |

### Costs

**Liability and Settlements**:

- Taxpayers pay millions for misconduct
- Insurance costs rising
- Some cities self-insuring at high risk

**Recruitment and Retention**:

- Difficulty recruiting in hostile environment
- Reform could improve working conditions
- Legitimacy crisis hurts officers

## Counter-Strategies

### Building Coalitions

**Unlikely Allies**:

| Group | Interest in Reform |
|-------|-------------------|
| Fiscal conservatives | Lawsuit costs, efficiency |
| Libertarians | Civil liberties |
| Some officers | Want to improve profession |
| Victim advocates | Safety requires trust |

### Reframing

**Messages That Work**:

| Frame | Approach |
|-------|----------|
| Officer safety | De-escalation protects officers too |
| Effective policing | Trust enables cooperation |
| Professionalism | Doctors, lawyers have accountability |
| Common ground | Everyone wants safe communities |

### Electoral Strategy

**Winnable Races**:

- Reform prosecutors
- Sheriffs in some jurisdictions
- City council candidates
- State legislators

### Legal Strategy

**Litigation**:

- Civil rights cases (despite immunity)
- Pattern-or-practice (federal and state)
- Consent decrees
- State constitutional claims

### Direct Action

**Pressure Tactics**:

- Protests and demonstrations
- Sustained organizing
- Media attention
- Political accountability
