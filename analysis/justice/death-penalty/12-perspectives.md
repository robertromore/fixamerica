# Death Penalty Reform and Abolition: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes death penalty reform and abolition through nine distinct political perspectives, evaluating current state assessments, root cause analyses, proposed solutions, and legislation. The death penalty is an unusually cross-cutting issue: unlike most criminal justice topics, it produces unexpected alignments, with some conservatives joining progressives in opposing capital punishment on fiscal, religious, or limited-government grounds. Each perspective receives engagement consistency ratings and position scores, culminating in compromise proposals that bridge multiple viewpoints.

## Perspective Definitions

| Perspective | Core Values | Policy Orientation |
|-------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Tradition, limited government, individual responsibility | Incremental change, fiscal restraint, market solutions |
| Liberal | Progress, equality, social responsibility | Government as problem-solver, regulation, safety nets |
| Progressive | Systemic change, economic justice, structural reform | Transformative reform, wealth redistribution, worker power |
| Libertarian | Individual liberty, minimal state, free markets | Deregulation, privatization, voluntary association |
| Constitutionalist | Original intent, enumerated powers, federalism | Strict constitutional limits, states' rights |
| Populist | Anti-elite, pro-worker, economic nationalism | Trade protection, anti-monopoly, immigration restriction |
| Centrist | Pragmatism, compromise, incremental progress | Bipartisan solutions, evidence-based policy |
| Religious Right | Faith-based values, traditional morality | Social conservatism, religious liberty |
| Democratic Socialist | Worker ownership, public goods, democratic control | Public investment, labor rights, universal programs |

- Internal diversity: Some perspectives contain factions with different priorities

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 3/5 - Mixed Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Genuinely divided; traditional "tough on crime" faction supports death penalty, but growing fiscal-conservative and faith-based factions oppose it |
| Principle consistency | "Limited government" and "fiscal restraint" principles argue against death penalty, but "law and order" principle argues for it; internal tension is real |
| Goalpost stability | Shifting; conservative opposition to death penalty has grown steadily since 2000 but remains a minority position |
| Zero-sum behavior | Moderate; some conservatives oppose reform because it is associated with liberal causes, regardless of merits |

**Justification:** The conservative position on the death penalty is genuinely divided in a way that distinguishes it from many other criminal justice issues. Organizations like Conservatives Concerned About the Death Penalty and the American Conservative Union's evolving position reflect real principled disagreement. The "limited government should not have the power to execute citizens" argument has gained traction alongside the fiscal argument that capital punishment costs 2-3 times more than life imprisonment. However, the dominant conservative position still supports capital punishment, driven by "tough on crime" politics and victim-oriented retribution narratives.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Conservatives Concerned About the Death Penalty has organized in 20+ states with Republican legislators as members
- New Hampshire abolished the death penalty in 2019 through a bipartisan vote including Republican legislators who overrode a governor's veto
- Republican governors in Ohio and Arizona have imposed moratoriums
- Heritage Foundation and National Review maintain pro-death-penalty editorial positions
- 2024 Republican platform reaffirms support for capital punishment

**Justification:** Progressives have the most consistent and comprehensive opposition to the death penalty. Their analysis integrates capital punishment into a broader framework of state violence, structural racism, and class inequality. The progressive position does not depend on individual cases or pragmatic calculations; it is rooted in the principle that the state should not have the power to execute people. This produces unwavering opposition that extends to all proposed reforms short of abolition, which they view as necessary steps but insufficient endpoints.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Bryan Stevenson's Equal Justice Initiative has framed death penalty within history of racial terror from slavery through lynching to modern capital punishment
- Progressive position is consistent regardless of the crime, defendant, or political context
- Support for comprehensive criminal justice transformation, not just death penalty reform

**Justification:** Libertarians have one of the most principled positions on the death penalty. ### Constitutionalist Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 3/5 - Mixed Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Genuine textualist commitment to constitutional interpretation; the Fifth Amendment's reference to "capital" crimes suggests the Framers contemplated the death penalty |
| Principle consistency | Textualism supports death penalty's constitutionality; but "limited government" and "individual rights" principles cut the other way; selective application |
| Goalpost stability | Moderate; constitutional arguments are consistent but application varies with political context |
| Zero-sum behavior | Moderate; more principled than some perspectives but influenced by conservative coalition alignment |

**Justification:** Constitutionalists face a genuine interpretive tension. The Fifth Amendment references "capital" crimes, which textualists read as evidence that the Framers accepted capital punishment. However, the Eighth Amendment's "cruel and unusual" clause has been interpreted as evolving, and originalists debate whether 18th-century practices should control modern application. Most Constitutionalists resolve this tension in favor of the death penalty's constitutionality but support state-level decision-making on whether to retain it. Their position is more principled than the Conservative perspective but still influenced by coalition dynamics.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Justice Scalia's originalist defense of capital punishment in *Glossip v. Gross* concurrence
- Federalist Society scholars generally support death penalty's constitutionality but acknowledge state authority to abolish
- Some originalist scholars (notably Evan Mandery) have argued the Eighth Amendment should be interpreted to evolve
- Consistent support for state authority over criminal punishment

### Populist Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 2/5 - Low Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Claims concern about public safety and justice for victims, but positions are strongly driven by emotional response to high-profile crimes and "tough on crime" identity politics |
| Principle consistency | "Trust the people" populist principle would suggest following majority opinion, which has shifted toward opposition to the death penalty; right-populists ignore this shift |
| Goalpost stability | Unstable; positions are reactive to high-profile crimes and media coverage rather than consistent principles |
| Zero-sum behavior | High; death penalty positions are used to signal cultural alignment rather than reflect policy analysis |

**Justification:** Populist positions on the death penalty are among the least principled. Right-populists use support for capital punishment as a cultural signifier, part of a broader "tough on crime" identity that resonates with their base. The actual policy merits -- cost, deterrence evidence, wrongful convictions -- are largely irrelevant to the position. Left-populists are somewhat more consistent, connecting opposition to capital punishment to class-based critiques of who gets executed (poor, minority defendants) versus who does not (wealthy defendants who can afford excellent counsel). The deep right-left populist split makes this perspective particularly incoherent as a unified category.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Trump administration resumed federal executions in 2020 after a 17-year pause, executing 13 people in 6 months
- Right-populist support for death penalty is closely tied to "law and order" identity politics
- Left-populist critiques focus on class disparities in who receives the death penalty
- Positions fluctuate with high-profile crimes; after mass shootings, support temporarily increases across populist base

## Compromise Proposals

### Compromise 1: State-Level Moratorium with Bipartisan Study Commission

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Libertarian + Centrist + Religious Right

**Core Agreement:** Before executing anyone, a state should ensure its system is fair, accurate, and cost-effective; a moratorium with a study commission allows evidence-based deliberation.

**Policy Description:**

State legislation imposing a 3-5 year moratorium on executions while a bipartisan commission studies the state's death penalty system. The commission examines costs compared to life without parole, wrongful conviction risk, racial and geographic disparities, defense quality, and victim family impact. Commission membership includes prosecutors, defense attorneys, victim family members, faith leaders, and fiscal analysts. The commission reports findings and recommendations to the legislature, which then votes on whether to retain, reform, or abolish capital punishment. Based on the State Death Penalty Moratorium Act model from the legislation file.

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The Liberal-Progressive-Centrist-Democratic Socialist-Libertarian coalition (all rated 4+ consistency) provides the strongest base for death penalty reform. This five-perspective coalition is notably stronger than on many other criminal justice issues because Libertarians are principled allies on death penalty abolition specifically (state power critique) in ways they are not on other government intervention questions. Centrists have been persuaded by accumulated evidence. The key strategic question is whether to pursue abolition directly or build toward it through procedural reforms and moratoriums that can attract Conservative and Religious Right factions.

### Key Obstacles

- **State-level variation:** With 27 states retaining the death penalty, abolition requires sustained state-by-state campaigns
- **Populist opposition:** Right-populist "tough on crime" identity politics makes the death penalty a cultural signifier resistant to evidence
- **Prosecutorial resistance:** Elected district attorneys in many jurisdictions use capital charging as a political tool and will resist reform
- **Supreme Court composition:** Current Court is unlikely to rule capital punishment unconstitutional; some Justices may restrict existing procedural protections
- **High-profile crimes:** Mass shootings and terrorism cases generate temporary spikes in death penalty support that can derail reform efforts

### Low Consistency Partners

Populist (2/5) and Religious Right (2/5) perspectives are the least reliable partners. However, the Religious Right is more persuadable than the Populist perspective because the "consistent ethic of life" argument creates genuine theological tension with pro-death-penalty positions. The growing evangelical anti-death-penalty movement (Shane Claiborne, National Association of Evangelicals resolution) provides a pathway for engagement. The Populist perspective's right-wing faction is largely unreachable through policy arguments because their position is identity-driven rather than evidence-driven; the left-populist faction is already aligned with the reform coalition.

### Recommended Approach

1. **Innocence protection first:** Pursue the Innocence Protection and Defense Quality Act as the broadest possible coalition (6/9 perspectives), establishing procedural reforms that make the death penalty rarer and costlier while building momentum
2. **State moratoriums with commissions:** Push moratorium-and-study-commission legislation in winnable states, using Republican governors' precedent (Ohio, Arizona) for bipartisan cover
3. **State legislative abolition:** Target states where moratoriums have created political space; prioritize states with small death rows and supportive governors
4. **Federal abolition:** Pursue federal death penalty abolition as a standalone bill without state restrictions, maximizing the coalition to include Constitutionalists
5. **Cost and competence messaging:** Lead with fiscal arguments and government competence critiques, which resonate across Conservative, Libertarian, and Centrist perspectives; reserve racial justice framing for coalitions where it strengthens rather than narrows support
6. **Faith engagement:** Invest in the growing evangelical and Catholic anti-death-penalty movement; the "consistent ethic of life" framework can move Religious Right opinion over time
7. **Prosecutorial reform:** Elect reform prosecutors who decline to seek death; each reform DA reduces death sentences and changes the political landscape
8. **Long-term:** Build toward either Supreme Court recognition of death penalty as cruel and unusual (through accumulating state abolitions that demonstrate "evolving standards of decency") or eventual national legislative abolition
