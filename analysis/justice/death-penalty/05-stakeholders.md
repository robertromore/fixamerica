# Death Penalty Reform: Stakeholder Analysis

## Overview

The death penalty debate involves a complex web of stakeholders with varying degrees of power, interest, and moral standing. Understanding these stakeholders—their motivations, interests, and potential roles in reform—is essential for developing effective abolition strategies.

## Primary Stakeholders

### 1. Death Row Inmates and Their Families

**Current Situation:**
- Approximately 2,300 people on death row
- Average 20+ years awaiting execution or resolution
- Severe isolation, limited contact with family
- Ongoing trauma of uncertain fate

**Interests and Needs:**

| Interest | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Survival | Avoiding execution |
| Humane conditions | Better death row treatment |
| Legal representation | Competent post-conviction counsel |
| Family contact | Visitation and communication |
| Hope | Possibility of sentence reduction |
| Vindication | Exoneration if innocent |

**Power and Influence:**
- Limited direct political power
- Cannot vote in most states
- Stigmatized and dehumanized
- Dependent on external advocates

**Potential Role in Reform:**
- Humanizing the issue through their stories
- Demonstrating rehabilitation potential
- Exposing system failures
- Connecting with victim families for reconciliation

### 2. Victim Families

**Diversity of Perspectives:**

| Group | Approximate % | Position |
|-------|--------------|----------|
| Support execution | 40-50% | Want death penalty carried out |
| Oppose execution | 15-25% | Against death penalty |
| Ambivalent/evolving | 30-40% | Complex feelings, may change |

**Organizations:**
- **Murder Victims' Families for Reconciliation (MVFR)**: Opposes death penalty
- **Journey of Hope**: Victims against executions
- **Parents of Murdered Children**: Mixed positions
- **Various state victim groups**: Often pro-death penalty

**Interests:**

| Interest | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Justice | Accountability for murder |
| Closure | Resolution of trauma |
| Voice | Being heard in the process |
| Support | Services regardless of position |
| End to re-traumatization | Decades of appeals painful |

**Power and Influence:**
- Significant moral authority
- Media access for compelling narratives
- Politically valuable endorsements
- Can shift public opinion

**Potential Role in Reform:**
- Victim families against execution most persuasive advocates
- Challenge "closure" narrative
- Demonstrate diverse victim perspectives
- Build bridges across political divides

### 3. Prosecutors and District Attorneys

**Current Role:**

| Function | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Death-seeking decisions | Unilateral authority to seek death |
| Case presentation | Shape jury perceptions |
| Plea negotiations | Use death as leverage |
| Post-conviction opposition | Fight appeals and clemency |
| Policy advocacy | Often oppose reform |

**Types of Prosecutors:**

| Type | Prevalence | Death Penalty Approach |
|------|------------|----------------------|
| Traditional "tough on crime" | Majority historically | Seek death frequently |
| Reform-minded DAs | Growing minority | Rarely or never seek death |
| Selective seekers | Common | Death for "worst" cases only |
| Reluctant seekers | Some | Follow office policy, personal doubts |

**Interests:**

| Interest | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Electoral success | Winning re-election |
| Conviction rates | Appearance of effectiveness |
| Victim family approval | Political and personal |
| Professional reputation | Peer recognition |
| Resource allocation | Budget and staffing |
| Personal morality | Varies widely |

**Power and Influence:**
- Enormous direct power over individual cases
- Political influence in criminal justice policy
- Professional associations (NDAA) lobby effectively
- Trusted by public on crime issues

**Potential Role in Reform:**
- Reform DAs can end death penalty in their jurisdictions
- Prosecutor organizations can change policy positions
- Individual prosecutors can share insider critiques
- Can model alternatives that work

### 4. Defense Attorneys

**Types and Roles:**

| Type | Role | Challenges |
|------|------|------------|
| Public defenders | Represent most capital defendants | Underfunded, overworked |
| Court-appointed | Assigned to capital cases | Often inexperienced |
| Death penalty specialists | Focus on capital cases | Few in number |
| Innocence project lawyers | Post-conviction | Limited capacity |
| Federal habeas attorneys | Final appeals | Strict deadlines |

**Interests:**

| Interest | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Client survival | Preventing execution |
| Adequate resources | Funding for investigation |
| Fair procedures | Meaningful judicial review |
| Professional fulfillment | Winning against odds |
| Systemic change | Abolition |

**Power and Influence:**
- Limited resources compared to prosecution
- Professional expertise
- Constitutional protection role
- Moral authority of defending rights

**Potential Role in Reform:**
- Expose system failures
- Develop legal challenges
- Train new defenders
- Advocate for reform

### 5. Judges

**Types and Roles:**

| Type | Death Penalty Role | Pressures |
|------|-------------------|-----------|
| Trial judges | Preside over capital trials | Local politics, election |
| State appellate | Review convictions | Finality concerns |
| State supreme court | Final state review | Political pressure |
| Federal district | Habeas corpus review | AEDPA constraints |
| Circuit court | Federal appeals | Precedent, politics |
| Supreme Court | Constitutional interpretation | Ideological composition |

**Interests:**

| Interest | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Correct outcomes | Getting it right legally |
| Efficiency | Managing dockets |
| Precedent adherence | Following higher courts |
| Reputation | Professional standing |
| Election/retention | Where applicable |

**Power and Influence:**
- Enormous in individual cases
- Limited in systemic change (except SCOTUS)
- Shape procedural protections
- Finality concerns limit review

### 6. Law Enforcement

**Organizations and Positions:**

| Organization | Typical Position | Influence |
|--------------|-----------------|-----------|
| Fraternal Order of Police | Pro-death penalty | Significant |
| Police unions | Generally pro | Political donations |
| Police chiefs organizations | More moderate | Some |
| Sheriffs associations | Generally pro | Local politics |

**Interests:**

| Interest | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Officer safety | Death penalty for cop-killers |
| Deterrence belief | Believe it protects officers |
| Symbolic support | Ultimate penalty shows value |
| Victim family relationships | Often close to murder victims |

**Power and Influence:**
- Significant political lobbying power
- Trusted by public on crime issues
- Can endorse or oppose candidates
- Symbol of public safety

**Potential Role in Reform:**
- Law enforcement against death penalty groups growing
- Can provide credible reform voice
- Challenge deterrence assumptions
- Support evidence-based alternatives

## Secondary Stakeholders

### 7. Corrections Officers and Execution Teams

**Roles and Experience:**

| Role | Experience |
|------|------------|
| Death row guards | Daily contact with condemned |
| Warden | Ultimate responsibility for execution |
| Execution team | Carry out procedure |
| Medical staff | Controversial involvement |
| Chaplains | Spiritual support |

**Interests:**

| Interest | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Personal wellbeing | Psychological impact of role |
| Job security | Employment concerns |
| Professional identity | Conflicted feelings |
| Humane treatment | Some advocate for condemned |

**Emerging Voices:**
- Former wardens speaking against death penalty
- Execution team members reporting trauma
- Guards humanizing death row inmates
- Corrections unions sometimes neutral

### 8. Medical and Mental Health Professionals

**Ethical Tensions:**

| Issue | Professional Position |
|-------|----------------------|
| Lethal injection participation | AMA opposes physician involvement |
| Competency evaluations | Ethical guidelines in flux |
| Treatment to restore competency | Controversial |
| Mental health treatment on death row | Underfunded |

**Interests:**

| Interest | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Professional ethics | "Do no harm" principle |
| Patient care | Treating inmates |
| Licensing | Avoiding discipline |
| Advocacy | Many oppose participation |

### 9. Faith Communities

**Positions by Tradition:**

| Faith Tradition | Official Position |
|-----------------|------------------|
| Catholic Church | Opposes (catechism amended 2018) |
| Mainline Protestant | Generally oppose |
| Evangelical Protestant | Divided |
| Jewish (Reform) | Opposes |
| Jewish (Orthodox) | Theoretical support, practical opposition |
| Muslim | Varied |
| Buddhist | Generally oppose |

**Interests:**

| Interest | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Redemption | Belief in rehabilitation |
| Sanctity of life | Consistent life ethic |
| Mercy | Core religious value |
| Justice | Varied interpretations |
| Political influence | Moral voice in policy |

### 10. Business Community

**Emerging Stakeholder:**

| Interest | Connection |
|----------|------------|
| Pharmaceutical companies | Refuse to supply execution drugs |
| Legal costs | Taxes fund expensive trials |
| Reputation | State image affects business |
| Employee values | Workforce preferences |

**Corporate Actions:**
- Drug companies restricting sales for executions
- Some business leaders speaking out
- Economic development concerns
- ESG considerations growing

### 11. International Community

**Positions and Actions:**

| Entity | Position | Actions |
|--------|----------|---------|
| European Union | Strongly opposes | Diplomatic pressure |
| United Nations | Calls for moratorium | Resolutions, reporting |
| Foreign governments | Many refuse extradition | Diplomatic cases |
| International human rights orgs | Oppose | Documentation, advocacy |

**Interests:**

| Interest | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Human rights norms | Universal standards |
| Diplomatic relations | Extradition, treaties |
| Moral leadership | Global example |
| Consistency | U.S. human rights credibility |

## Stakeholder Mapping

### Power vs. Interest Matrix

| Stakeholder | Power Level | Interest Level | Strategy |
|-------------|-------------|----------------|----------|
| Death row inmates | Low | High | Empower, tell stories |
| Victim families (all) | Medium | High | Engage, diversify |
| Prosecutors | High | High | Convert, pressure |
| Defense attorneys | Medium | High | Resource, coordinate |
| Judges | High | Medium | Educate, independence |
| Law enforcement | High | Medium | Convert leaders |
| Legislators | High | Variable | Lobby, educate |
| Media | Medium | Low | Shape coverage |
| General public | High (voting) | Low | Reframe debate |

### Coalition Opportunities

**Traditional Abolition Coalition:**
- Civil rights organizations
- Religious groups
- Defense bar
- International community
- Academic researchers

**Emerging/Unusual Allies:**
- Conservative fiscal hawks (cost argument)
- Evangelical Christians (redemption)
- Libertarians (government power)
- Business leaders (state image)
- Former law enforcement
- Exonerees (credibility)
- Victim families against execution

### Opposition Analysis

**Core Death Penalty Supporters:**

| Group | Primary Motivation |
|-------|-------------------|
| Victim advocacy groups | Retribution, closure |
| Law enforcement unions | Officer protection, symbolism |
| Traditional prosecutors | Tool, political benefit |
| Conservative politicians | Tough-on-crime branding |
| Religious conservatives (some) | Biblical interpretation |

**Persuadable Constituencies:**
- Fiscal conservatives (cost)
- Rural communities (cost, limited benefit)
- Victim families (diverse views)
- Moderate Republicans
- Faith communities

## Strategic Implications

### High-Priority Stakeholder Engagement

1. **Reform prosecutors**: Elect and support
2. **Victim families against execution**: Platform and amplify
3. **Conservative opponents**: Credibility with right
4. **Exonerees**: Humanize the issue
5. **Faith communities**: Moral authority

### Stakeholder-Specific Messaging

| Stakeholder | Effective Frame |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Fiscal conservatives | Cost and waste |
| Religious communities | Redemption and mercy |
| Victim families | True closure, peace |
| Law enforcement | System failure, resources |
| General public | Innocence and error |
| Prosecutors | Alternatives that work |
