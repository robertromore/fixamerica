# Prosecutorial Reform: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes prosecutorial reform -- specifically open-file discovery, Brady enforcement, prosecutorial accountability and immunity, conviction integrity units, charging standards, plea bargaining reform, external oversight commissions, and the reform prosecutor movement -- through nine distinct political perspectives. The core questions are: how should prosecutors be held accountable for their enormous discretionary power, what transparency and oversight mechanisms should constrain that power, and how should the system balance effective prosecution with defendants' rights? Each perspective receives engagement-consistency ratings and position scores, culminating in compromise proposals that bridge multiple viewpoints.

Prosecutorial reform presents a less uniformly cross-ideological alignment than some criminal justice issues (such as asset forfeiture). While virtually all perspectives agree that *some* accountability is needed, deep disagreements exist over the scope of reform, particularly regarding absolute immunity, the reform prosecutor movement, and whether the problem is systemic or limited to individual bad actors. The primary institutional opposition comes from district attorneys' associations, prosecutors' unions, and law enforcement allies who benefit from the current discretionary structure.

### Engagement Consistency (1-5)

| Level | Label | Indicators |
|-------|-------|------------|
| 5 | Highly Consistent | Stated positions reflect underlying principles; positions stable when concerns addressed |
| 4 | Mostly Consistent | Principles generally align with positions; minor strategic flexibility |
| 3 | Mixed Consistency | Some positions principled, others reflect coalition pressures or unstated concerns |
| 2 | Low Consistency | Positions shift frequently; stated concerns differ from underlying priorities |
| 1 | Unpredictable | Positions appear driven primarily by opposition dynamics rather than principles |

**Coalition-Building Considerations:**

- Alignment gap: When stated concerns differ from underlying priorities, addressing stated concerns may not secure support
- Position stability: Perspectives with shifting positions require ongoing negotiation
- Principle consistency: Perspectives that apply principles uniformly are more predictable partners
- Internal diversity: Some perspectives contain factions with different priorities

## Perspective Analyses

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 2/5 - Low Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Stated commitment to limited government and due process supports reform, but law enforcement coalition loyalty and tough-on-crime branding frequently override principle |
| Principle consistency | "Government accountability" and "due process" principles clearly support prosecutorial oversight, yet many conservatives exempt prosecutors from the same scrutiny they demand of other government actors |
| Goalpost stability | Low; conservative positions shift dramatically depending on whether reform is framed as holding government accountable (support) or being soft on crime (oppose) |
| Zero-sum behavior | Moderate to high; reform prosecutor elections have become a partisan wedge issue, causing conservatives to oppose reforms they might otherwise support |

**Justification:** Conservatives face a deep internal contradiction on prosecutorial reform. Their core commitments to limited government, due process, and individual liberty logically support robust accountability for the most powerful actors in the criminal justice system. Organizations like Right on Crime and the Texas Public Policy Foundation have recognized this and supported some reforms. However, the political salience of "tough on crime" messaging, coalition loyalty to prosecutors' associations and law enforcement, and the partisan backlash against reform prosecutors (particularly in cities like San Francisco, Philadelphia, and Chicago) have made conservative positions highly unstable. Many conservatives who champion government accountability in other contexts actively defend prosecutorial impunity.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Right on Crime (conservative criminal justice reform organization) has supported some transparency and accountability measures
- Texas Public Policy Foundation has published on criminal justice reform including prosecutorial issues
- Conservative legislators and governors have attacked reform prosecutors as "soft on crime" and sought to remove or override them (Florida, Georgia, Texas)
- Heritage Foundation has published favorably on wrongful conviction prevention but opposes most structural accountability reforms
- Sen. Chuck Grassley (R-IA) supported some federal discovery reform in bipartisan sentencing discussions but backed away from comprehensive prosecutorial accountability

- Some Democratic legislators have hesitated on immunity reform under pressure from prosecutors' associations

### Populist Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 2/5 - Low Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Genuine anger when prosecutors abuse power against ordinary people; but also genuine support for prosecutors who are "tough on crime" against perceived enemies |
| Principle consistency | "Government shouldn't abuse regular people" principle supports reform; "law and order" and "support the police" principles oppose it |
| Goalpost stability | Low; populist positions on prosecution are heavily dependent on who the prosecutor is targeting |
| Zero-sum behavior | High; populists tend to support prosecutorial power when wielded against their perceived opponents and oppose it when wielded against their allies |

**Justification:** Populist engagement on prosecutorial reform is deeply inconsistent because populism is oriented around group identity rather than procedural principles. Right-populists have cheered prosecutors who pursue political opponents while denouncing prosecutors who they perceive as targeting their allies. Left-populists focus on prosecutors who harm working-class communities. Neither faction applies a consistent procedural standard. This makes populist positions extremely framing-dependent and unstable as coalition partners, because their support can evaporate when the political dynamics change.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Right-populist media celebrated prosecution of January 6th defendants by some while decrying "political prosecution" of allies
- Left-populist organizations focused on prosecutors' role in criminalizing poverty and labor activism
- Polling shows populist voters respond strongly to framing: "government taking from regular people" (support reform) vs. "letting criminals go free" (oppose reform)
- Trump administration simultaneously attacked prosecutors investigating the president while supporting aggressive prosecution in other contexts

### Compromise 1: Brady Enforcement and Conviction Integrity Package

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Progressive + Libertarian + Constitutionalist + Populist + Centrist + Religious Right + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** Prosecutors who hide exculpatory evidence from defendants are violating a fundamental obligation, and innocent people should not remain in prison. Brady enforcement and conviction review are areas of near-universal agreement.

**Policy Description:**

State or federal legislation establishing three interconnected reforms: (1) Mandatory Brady checklists requiring prosecutors to document their disclosure decisions in every case, with supervisory review for felonies, and automatic referral to the state bar for court-identified violations. (3) Mandatory conviction integrity units in all offices serving populations over 100,000, with regional units for smaller jurisdictions, funded by state appropriation. CIUs must accept defense referrals, review plea convictions, and publish quarterly reports on cases reviewed and outcomes.

### Compromise 2: Open-File Discovery with Witness Safety Provisions

**Bridges:** Liberal + Progressive + Libertarian + Constitutionalist + Centrist + Democratic Socialist + Conservative (partial) + Populist (partial) + Religious Right (partial)

**Core Agreement:** Both sides in a criminal case should have access to the same evidence, and defendants should be able to make informed decisions about whether to plead guilty or go to trial. Witness safety is a legitimate concern that can be addressed through narrowly tailored protective orders.

**Policy Description:**

State legislation requiring prosecutors to disclose all evidence to the defense within 21 days of arraignment, including all police reports, witness statements, forensic evidence, impeachment material, informant agreements, and evidence favorable to the defendant. Disclosure is an ongoing obligation. No guilty plea may be accepted until at least 30 days after complete disclosure. Prosecutors must certify compliance in writing. Narrow witness safety exceptions are available through protective orders requiring specific, articulable threat documentation, reviewed every 30 days, and never preventing defense counsel access under protective order. Violations result in evidence exclusion, potential charge dismissal, and mandatory bar referral.

- **Implementation Path:** State legislation as the most achievable starting point; federal legislation conditioning Byrne JAG grants on state compliance (modeled on Prosecutorial Accountability Act Section 7); technical assistance funding to address capacity concerns in smaller offices

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The core reform coalition consists of six perspectives rated 4+ for engagement consistency: Liberal, Progressive, Libertarian, Constitutionalist, Centrist, and Democratic Socialist. This coalition is unified by shared commitment to due process and government accountability, though individual perspectives arrive at these commitments through different philosophical frameworks. The Libertarian-Progressive alliance on prosecutorial accountability is particularly noteworthy because it mirrors the left-libertarian coalition that has driven criminal justice reform in other areas (sentencing reform, asset forfeiture). The Centrist position provides a pragmatic bridge and the evidentiary framing needed to attract moderate support.

### Key Obstacles

- **Prosecutors' associations:** The National District Attorneys Association and state-level prosecutors' associations are the primary opponents of every significant reform. They have significant political influence because DAs are locally elected and often have close relationships with state legislators.
- **Law enforcement coalition:** Police unions and prosecutors' associations form a mutual defense alliance that mobilizes against accountability reforms. Conservative and populist politicians who depend on law enforcement endorsements are reluctant to break from this coalition.
- **Partisan framing of reform prosecutors:** The reform prosecutor movement has become a partisan wedge issue, with conservatives and populists framing reform prosecutors as "soft on crime." This poisons the well for broader prosecutorial accountability reforms that might otherwise command cross-ideological support.
- **Absolute immunity doctrine:** *Imbler v. Pachtman* remains good law, and the Supreme Court has shown no inclination to reconsider it. Legislative reform of immunity faces intense opposition from prosecutors and ambivalence from judges who are often former prosecutors.
- **Information asymmetry:** Because prosecutorial decisions are largely invisible, the public does not know the extent of the problem, reducing political pressure for reform.

### Low Consistency Partners

Conservative and Populist perspectives (rated 2/5) are the most challenging coalition partners. Their inconsistency is not merely about framing preferences -- it reflects genuine internal contradictions between their stated principles (government accountability, due process) and their political commitments (law enforcement support, tough-on-crime branding). Addressing stated concerns (e.g., witness safety provisions, transition support) may not secure their support if the underlying political dynamic remains unchanged. The most productive approach is to work with the subset of conservatives and populists who have demonstrated willingness to act on their principles (e.g., state-level Republicans who have supported discovery reform) rather than expecting uniform support from these perspectives.

The Religious Right (rated 3/5) is a somewhat more accessible partner because its inconsistency stems from disengagement rather than active opposition. Engaging evangelical criminal justice reform organizations (Prison Fellowship, ERLC) and framing reforms through biblical justice principles can activate a moral framework that exists within the tradition but is underutilized.

### Recommended Approach

1. **Data transparency first:** Begin with the Model Prosecutorial Transparency Act, which faces the least opposition and creates the evidentiary foundation for all subsequent reforms. Once disparities and misconduct patterns are documented publicly, political pressure for further reform increases.
2. **Brady enforcement and CIUs:** Move immediately to the Brady Enforcement and Conviction Integrity Package (Compromise 1), which commands the broadest consensus. Frame as protecting the innocent and enforcing existing constitutional obligations.
3. **Open-file discovery:** Expand adoption of open-file discovery state by state, building on the evidence from the approximately 10 states that have already adopted it. Evidence of effectiveness neutralizes the "harmful to prosecution" argument.
4. **Federal funding conditions:** Use Byrne JAG grant conditions to incentivize state compliance with transparency, discovery, and conviction integrity requirements. This leverages federal spending power without direct mandates.
5. **Immunity reform through litigation and legislation:** Pursue immunity reform on parallel tracks: litigation challenging absolute immunity through the courts (building on Justice Thomas's skepticism) and legislation defining Section 1983 immunity.
6. **Reframe the reform prosecutor debate:** Shift the conversation from "soft on crime vs. tough on crime" to "accountability and effectiveness." Emphasize data showing that reform prosecutors have reduced incarceration without increasing crime in most jurisdictions.
7. **Build bipartisan partnerships:** Identify and cultivate right-of-center champions (libertarian-leaning Republicans, conservative criminal justice reformers) who can provide political cover for reforms that might otherwise be dismissed as partisan.
