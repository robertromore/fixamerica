# Sentencing Reform: Stakeholders

## Those Directly Affected

### Incarcerated People

**Population Overview**:

| Category | Number | Percentage |
|----------|--------|------------|
| Total state/federal prison | ~1.2 million | 100% |
| Serving 10+ years | ~600,000 | 50% |
| Serving 20+ years | ~200,000 | 17% |
| Life sentences | ~200,000 | 17% |
| Life without parole | ~55,000 | 5% |
| Elderly (55+) | ~200,000 | 17% |
| Drug offenses (primary) | ~200,000 | 17% |

**Demographics**:

| Race/Ethnicity | Prison Population | U.S. Population |
|----------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Black | 38% | 13% |
| White | 30% | 60% |
| Hispanic | 25% | 19% |
| Other | 7% | 8% |

**Interests and Concerns**:

- Sentence length reduction
- Second look opportunities
- Earned time credits
- Access to programming
- Parole eligibility
- Aging in prison
- Maintaining family connections
- Reentry preparation

### Families of Incarcerated People

**Scope**:

| Impact | Number |
|--------|--------|
| Children with incarcerated parent | 2.7 million |
| Adults with immediate family member incarcerated | 20+ million |
| Annual family visits to prisons | Declining due to distance |
| Average distance to prison from family | 100+ miles |

**Burdens Carried**:

| Burden | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| Financial support | Phone calls ($), commissary ($), visits ($) |
| Emotional trauma | Separation, uncertainty, stigma |
| Child welfare | Single parenting, child trauma, CPS involvement |
| Housing instability | Lost income, eviction, displacement |
| Healthcare | Stress-related illness, mental health needs |
| Collateral consequences | Restricted housing, employment effects |

**Interests**:

- Reduced sentences
- Parole opportunities
- Closer placement
- Affordable communication
- Visiting access
- Reentry support
- Ending collateral consequences

### Crime Survivors

**Diverse Perspectives**:

| Perspective | Position |
|-------------|----------|
| Traditional victim advocacy | Support harsh sentences |
| Restorative justice-oriented | Support alternatives |
| Survivors seeking accountability | Want acknowledgment over years |
| Those harmed by incarceration | Oppose additional punishment |

**Organizations**:

| Organization | Position |
|--------------|----------|
| Crime Survivors for Safety and Justice | Reform-oriented |
| Alliance for Safety and Justice | Supports reform |
| National Organization of Victims of Juvenile Murderers | Opposes juvenile parole |
| Mothers of Murdered Children | Opposes sentence reduction |

**Interests (Reform-Oriented)**:

- Accountability without excessive punishment
- Restorative justice options
- Prevention investment
- Services for survivors
- Voice in process

## Judicial Stakeholders

### Judges

**Federal Judges**:

| Position | Number |
|----------|--------|
| District court judges | ~670 |
| Magistrate judges | ~550 |
| Senior judges | ~450 |
| Appeals court judges | ~180 |

**State Court Judges**:

- ~30,000 state court judges
- Elected in ~39 states
- Appointed in ~11 states

**Judicial Perspectives on Sentencing**:

| View | Prevalence |
|------|------------|
| Mandatory minimums too harsh | Majority (surveys) |
| Guidelines too rigid | Substantial minority |
| More discretion needed | Most |
| Current system adequate | Minority |

**Notable Judicial Criticism**:

- Justice Kennedy: Called mandatory minimums "unjust"
- Judge Gleeson: Wrote extensively against 924(c) stacking
- Judge Block: Publicly criticized crack disparity
- Multiple judges resigned over sentencing requirements

**Interests**:

- Restored discretion
- Ability to consider circumstances
- Proportionate sentencing
- Elimination of mandatory minimums
- Respect for judicial role

### U.S. Sentencing Commission

**Role**:

- Promulgate and amend sentencing guidelines
- Collect and analyze sentencing data
- Report to Congress
- Train judges and practitioners

**Composition**:

- 7 voting members (appointed by President, confirmed by Senate)
- At least 3 must be federal judges
- No more than 4 from same party

**Recent Positions**:

| Issue | Commission Position |
|-------|-------------------|
| Mandatory minimums | Recommended reform |
| Crack/powder disparity | Supported elimination |
| 924(c) stacking | Recommended change |
| Retroactivity | Applied to several amendments |
| Data transparency | Strongly supports |

## Prosecutors

### Federal Prosecutors

**U.S. Attorneys and AUSAs**:

- 93 U.S. Attorney offices
- ~6,000 Assistant U.S. Attorneys
- Appointed by President (U.S. Attorneys); hired (AUSAs)
- Answer to DOJ leadership

**Prosecutorial Power in Sentencing**:

| Decision | Effect |
|----------|--------|
| What to charge | Determines guideline range and mandatory minimums |
| How much drug quantity | Sets mandatory minimum trigger |
| Whether to file prior felony | Doubles/triples mandatory minimum |
| Substantial assistance motion | Only way below mandatory |
| Government variance motion | Major factor in below-range sentences |

**DOJ Positions**:

| Administration | Charging Policy |
|----------------|----------------|
| Obama (Holder Memo) | Avoid mandatory minimums for low-level |
| Trump (Sessions Memo) | Charge most serious provable offense |
| Biden (Garland Memo) | Discretion for just outcomes |

### State Prosecutors

**Elected District Attorneys**:

- ~2,400 chief prosecutors
- ~95% elected
- Average term: 4 years
- Most run unopposed

**Progressive Prosecutor Movement**:

| DA | Jurisdiction | Reform Position |
|----|--------------|-----------------|
| Larry Krasner | Philadelphia | Reduce sentences, end cash bail |
| George Gascon | Los Angeles | Eliminate sentencing enhancements |
| Kim Foxx | Cook County | Reduce prosecutions |
| Chesa Boudin | San Francisco | Reform (recalled 2022) |
| Alvin Bragg | Manhattan | Decline certain prosecutions |

**Traditional Prosecutor Associations**:

| Organization | Position |
|--------------|----------|
| National District Attorneys Association | Generally opposes reform |
| National Association of Attorneys General | Mixed |
| State prosecutor associations | Generally oppose reform |

## Defense Stakeholders

### Public Defenders

**Scope**:

- ~15,000 public defenders nationally
- Handle 80%+ of criminal cases
- Severely underfunded
- Excessive caseloads

**Caseload Crisis**:

| Recommended | Actual (typical) |
|-------------|------------------|
| 150 felonies/year | 400-500+ |
| 400 misdemeanors/year | 1,000+ |

**Interests**:

- Reduced sentences for clients
- Elimination of mandatory minimums
- Restored discretion
- Adequate funding
- Reasonable caseloads

## Corrections System

### Bureau of Prisons (Federal)

**Scope**:

- ~150,000 federal prisoners
- 122 institutions
- ~37,000 employees

**Budget**: ~$8 billion annually

**Interests**:

- Population management
- Security and safety
- Program administration
- Budget protection

### State Departments of Corrections

**Collective Scope**:

- ~1 million state prisoners
- 1,800+ state prisons
- ~450,000 correctional employees

**Interests**:

- Manageable populations
- Adequate funding
- Program resources
- Staff safety
- Reducing recidivism (some)

### Correctional Officer Unions

| Organization | Influence |
|--------------|-----------|
| CCPOA (California) | Major political force |
| AFSCME (multiple states) | Political engagement |
| State FOP chapters | Law-and-order politics |

**Interests**:

- Job security
- Wage and benefit protection
- Staff safety
- Adequate staffing ratios

## Legislative Stakeholders

### Congressional Committees

**Judiciary Committees**:

- Primary jurisdiction over sentencing
- Hold hearings on reform
- Mark up legislation
- Confirm judicial nominees

**Appropriations Committees**:

- Fund Bureau of Prisons
- Grant programs
- DOJ funding

**Key Congressional Members**:

| Position | Typical Role |
|----------|--------------|
| Judiciary Chair | Gatekeeping on reform bills |
| Ranking Member | Opposition or cooperation |
| Appropriators | Funding decisions |
| Subject matter experts | Drive specific reforms |

### State Legislatures

**Variation**:

- Some states leading reform
- Some states resisting
- Some states backsliding

**Recent Patterns**:

| State Category | Examples |
|----------------|----------|
| Reform leaders | California, New York, New Jersey |
| Bipartisan reform | Texas, Georgia, Louisiana |
| Reform resistance | Florida, Missouri, Oklahoma |
| Mixed picture | Most states |
