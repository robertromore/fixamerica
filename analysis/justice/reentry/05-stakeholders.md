# Reentry Reform: Stakeholders

## Overview

Reentry policy affects millions of individuals directly and touches virtually every aspect of American society. Understanding stakeholder interests, power dynamics, and potential alignments is essential for building effective reform coalitions.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Formerly Incarcerated Individuals

**Population**: 70+ million Americans have criminal records; 600,000+ released annually from prison

| Characteristic | Details |
|----------------|---------|
| Demographics | Disproportionately Black/Latino, male, low-income |
| Geographic concentration | Urban areas, specific neighborhoods |
| Political power | Limited (often disenfranchised) |
| Organizational representation | Growing but underfunded |
| Lived expertise | Essential for policy design |

**Key Interests**:

- Employment opportunities without discrimination
- Stable, affordable housing
- Restoration of voting rights
- Access to education and training
- Family reunification
- Record clearing
- Healthcare access (especially mental health, addiction)
- Fair supervision conditions

**Barriers to Influence**:

- Disenfranchisement in many states
- Stigma limits public voice
- Poverty limits resources
- Criminal record used against advocates
- Fragmented, not organized as voting bloc

### Families of Incarcerated/Formerly Incarcerated

**Population**: Millions of family members affected

| Group | Number |
|-------|--------|
| Children with incarcerated parent | 2.7 million |
| Parents of incarcerated persons | Millions |
| Partners/spouses | Millions |
| Extended family | Tens of millions |

**Key Interests**:

- Family reunification
- Affordable communication during incarceration
- Stable housing for returning family member
- Financial stability
- Children's wellbeing and stability
- End to guilt-by-association policies
- Support services for families

**Unique Perspective**:

- Bear financial burden of incarceration
- Experience secondary punishment
- Critical to successful reentry
- Often excluded from housing due to returning member
- Provide most reentry support without resources

### Crime Survivors and Victims' Advocates

**Composition**: Diverse community with varying perspectives

| Group | General Orientation |
|-------|---------------------|
| Punitive victims' rights groups | Often oppose reform |
| Restorative justice advocates | Support rehabilitation |
| Crime Survivors for Safety and Justice | Support reform |
| Family members of victims | Mixed |

**Key Interests**:

- Safety and security
- Accountability for harm
- Healing and closure
- Prevention of future victimization
- Restitution where appropriate
- Voice in the process

**Important Note**: Many victims and survivors support reentry reform, recognizing that successful reintegration prevents future victimization. The victims' rights movement is not monolithic.

## Employment Ecosystem

### Employers

| Employer Type | Orientation | Key Concerns |
|---------------|-------------|--------------|
| Large corporations | Increasingly open | Liability, reputation |
| Small businesses | Mixed | Lack of knowledge, risk |
| Fair chance employers | Supportive | Need liability protection |
| Government | Policy-dependent | Set example |
| Gig economy | Varies | Background check policies |

**Key Interests**:

- Liability protection
- Reliable workforce
- Tax incentives
- Clear guidance on hiring
- Insurance coverage
- Positive publicity for fair chance hiring

**Potential Allies**:

- Labor shortage industries
- Companies with DEI commitments
- Social enterprises
- Industries with high turnover

### Occupational Licensing Boards

| Characteristic | Details |
|----------------|---------|
| Number of boards | Thousands nationwide |
| Regulatory approach | Often restrictive |
| Composition | Industry practitioners |
| Accountability | Limited |

**Key Interests**:

- Public safety (stated)
- Professional standards
- Control over profession
- Reduced competition (unstated)

**Reform Opportunity**: Many licensing restrictions lack evidence of public safety benefit and could be reformed without risk.

### Workforce Development Organizations

| Type | Role in Reentry |
|------|-----------------|
| American Job Centers | Basic services |
| Community-based orgs | Specialized reentry programs |
| Trade unions | Some apprenticeship programs |
| Community colleges | Education and training |

**Key Interests**:

- Funding for programs
- Employer partnerships
- Outcome measurement
- Reducing recidivism

## Housing Stakeholders

### Housing Authorities

| Level | Role |
|-------|------|
| HUD (federal) | Policy, funding |
| State housing agencies | State policy, funding |
| Local PHAs | Direct administration |

**Current Approach**: Discretionary exclusion policies vary widely

**Reform Opportunity**: PHAs can change policies without federal action

### Private Landlords

| Size | Approach |
|------|----------|
| Large property companies | Standardized background checks |
| Small landlords | Individual discretion |
| Corporate landlords | Blanket policies common |

**Key Concerns**:

- Property damage
- Liability
- Other tenant concerns
- Insurance requirements
- Ease of tenant selection

### Homelessness Service Providers

| Interest | Connection to Reentry |
|----------|----------------------|
| Client population | Many formerly incarcerated |
| Resource limitations | Overwhelming demand |
| Housing First model | Aligns with reentry needs |

## Coalition-Building Opportunities

### Bipartisan Criminal Justice Reform

| Conservative Arguments | Progressive Arguments |
|-----------------------|----------------------|
| Fiscal responsibility | Racial justice |
| Limited government | Civil liberties |
| Family values | Economic opportunity |
| Redemption | Human dignity |
| Public safety through reintegration | Reducing recidivism |

### Unlikely Allies

| Stakeholder Pair | Common Interest |
|------------------|-----------------|
| Business + Reform advocates | Workforce development |
| Conservatives + Progressives | Reducing recidivism |
| Victims + Formerly incarcerated | Prevention |
| Libertarians + Civil rights groups | Due process |
