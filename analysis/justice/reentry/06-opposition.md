# Reentry Reform: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to reentry reform comes from diverse sources with varying motivations. Understanding these opposition forces—their interests, arguments, and strategies—is essential for anticipating resistance and developing effective counter-strategies.

## Economic Beneficiaries

### Background Check Industry

**Industry Size**: ~$4 billion annually

| Company Type | Business Model | Opposition Interest |
|--------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Consumer reporting agencies | Sell criminal record data | Clean slate threatens revenue |
| Employment screening firms | Employer-paid checks | Fewer checks = less revenue |
| Data aggregators | Compile and sell records | Expungement reduces product value |
| Technology platforms | Automated screening | Ban the box creates complexity |

**Opposition Activities**:

- Lobby against automatic expungement
- Oppose restrictions on record dissemination
- Fund research questioning reform effectiveness
- Advocate for "accuracy" over restriction

**Counter-Arguments**:

- Criminal records are often inaccurate (up to 50% error rate)
- Old records have limited predictive value
- Industry profits from perpetuating barriers
- Clean slate laws include exceptions for safety-sensitive positions

### Private Prison Industry

**Market Size**: ~$5 billion annually

| Company | Revenue Source | Conflict of Interest |
|---------|---------------|---------------------|
| CoreCivic | Incarceration | Higher recidivism = more inmates |
| GEO Group | Incarceration | Same |
| Reentry contractors | Service provision | Conflict: incarceration + reentry |

**Opposition Activities**:

- Political donations to tough-on-crime candidates
- Lobby against sentencing reform
- Oppose earned time credits
- Fund opposition research

**Counter-Arguments**:

- Profit motive conflicts with public safety
- Recidivism benefits their business model
- Reforms can redirect funding to community services
- Some companies diversifying into reentry (mixed motives)

### Electronic Monitoring Companies

| Company | Product | Interest |
|---------|---------|----------|
| BI Incorporated (GEO) | Ankle monitors | Supervision expansion |
| SCRAM Systems | Alcohol monitoring | More conditions = more revenue |
| Securus | Multiple technologies | Incarceration infrastructure |

**Concern**: These companies benefit from expanded supervision and more restrictive conditions.

## Political Opposition

### "Tough on Crime" Politicians

**Motivation**: Electoral incentive to appear strong on public safety

| Tactic | Example |
|--------|---------|
| Fearmongering | Ads featuring crimes by parolees |
| Victim exploitation | Highlighting crimes by people with records |
| Race-coded messaging | "Dangerous criminals" imagery |
| Opposition research | Attacking reformers as "soft" |

**Where This Is Changing**:

- Bipartisan reform momentum (First Step Act)
- Conservative criminal justice reform movement
- Cost-focused arguments gaining traction
- Some victims' groups support reform

### Prosecutors and District Attorneys

| Position | Typical Stance |
|----------|---------------|
| Traditional prosecutors | Oppose reduced penalties, expanded clearing |
| Reform prosecutors | Support reentry reform |
| Associations (NDAA) | Mixed, often conservative |

**Opposition Arguments**:

- Need records for future prosecutions
- Public safety concerns
- Victim notification requirements
- Plea bargaining leverage

**Counter-Arguments**:

- Prosecutorial records would remain accessible
- Evidence shows successful reentry reduces crime
- Reform prosecutors demonstrate alternative approaches

### Law Enforcement Unions

| Union | Typical Position |
|-------|-----------------|
| FOP | Generally oppose reform |
| Police unions (local) | Varies by jurisdiction |
| Sheriffs' associations | Often oppose reform |

**Interests**:

- Maintain access to records for investigations
- Preserve tough-on-crime political allies
- Resist supervision reform that might reduce positions

## NIMBY Opposition

### Neighborhood Opposition to Reentry Housing

**Manifestation**: Opposition to transitional housing, halfway houses, treatment facilities

| Concern (Stated) | Underlying Fear |
|------------------|-----------------|
| Property values | Economic self-interest |
| Safety | Fear of crime |
| "Character of neighborhood" | Class/race dynamics |
| Traffic/parking | Pretext |

**Opposition Tactics**:

- Zoning challenges
- Public comment mobilization
- Political pressure on officials
- Litigation
- Media campaigns

**Counter-Strategies**:

- Good neighbor agreements
- Community engagement before siting
- Evidence on property values (minimal impact)
- Integrate facilities into neighborhoods
- Highlight local residents who have records

### Business District Opposition

| Concern | Response |
|---------|----------|
| Panhandling/homelessness | Services reduce visible homelessness |
| Store theft | Employment reduces theft |
| Customer comfort | Education changes perceptions |

## Ideological Opposition

### Retributivist Philosophy

**Core Belief**: Punishment is deserved; offenders forfeit rights

| Argument | Counter-Argument |
|----------|------------------|
| "You do the crime, you do the time" | Sentence is the time; collateral consequences are extra |
| Felons are "bad people" | People can change; most committed crimes young |
| Rights are forfeited | Constitutional rights are not forfeited by conviction |
| Punishment should deter | Evidence of deterrence is weak |

### Victim-Centered Framing

Some victims' rights groups frame any reform as anti-victim:

| Claim | Reality |
|-------|---------|
| Reform ignores victims | Many victims support reform |
| Accountability requires barriers | Barriers increase recidivism and victimization |
| Offenders have too many rights | Constitutional rights are not privileges |

**Important Distinction**: Many crime survivors support reentry reform (e.g., Crime Survivors for Safety and Justice). The victims' movement is not uniformly opposed.

## Institutional Inertia

### Government Bureaucracies

| Agency | Resistance Source |
|--------|-------------------|
| Corrections departments | Budget tied to incarceration |
| Parole boards | Risk-averse culture |
| Licensing boards | Protectionism |
| Benefits agencies | Administrative complexity |

**Manifestation**:

- Slow implementation of reforms
- Narrow interpretation of new laws
- Inadequate staff training
- Data system limitations
- "That's not how we've done it"

### Court Systems

| Resistance | Source |
|------------|--------|
| Complex expungement processes | Judicial resources |
| High filing fees | Court funding |
| Limited capacity | Backlogs |
| Narrow eligibility interpretations | Judicial discretion |

## Special Interest Opposition

### Occupational Licensing Boards

| Board Type | Opposition Basis |
|------------|------------------|
| Healthcare licensing | Stated: patient safety; Unstated: competition |
| Legal licensing | Character and fitness concerns |
| Trade licensing | Protectionism |
| Financial services | Liability concerns |

**Reform Challenge**: Boards are often captured by incumbents who benefit from reduced competition

### Housing Industry

| Stakeholder | Opposition Interest |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Landlord associations | Tenant selection freedom |
| Insurance companies | Underwriting concerns |
| Property management | Administrative simplicity |
| HOAs | Property values, homogeneity |

### Financial Services

| Institution | Barrier | Rationale |
|-------------|---------|-----------|
| Banks | Account denial | Risk/compliance |
| Credit card companies | Credit denial | Credit risk |
| Insurance companies | Coverage denial | Actuarial claims |

## Opposition Strategies

### Messaging Tactics

| Tactic | Example |
|--------|---------|
| Cherry-picking | Highlight rare failures |
| Fearmongering | "Dangerous criminals" |
| Slippery slope | "Next they'll let murderers vote" |
| Victim exploitation | Parade sympathetic victims |
| "Soft on crime" labeling | Attack reformers |

### Political Tactics

| Tactic | Implementation |
|--------|----------------|
| Primary challenges | Threaten reform-supporting officials |
| Opposition ads | Crime-focused negative advertising |
| Voter initiatives | Anti-reform ballot measures |
| Legislative riders | Attach to unrelated bills |
| Implementation obstruction | Delay/undermine enacted reforms |

### Legal Tactics

| Tactic | Application |
|--------|-------------|
| Constitutional challenges | Challenge reforms in court |
| Regulatory challenges | Sue over implementation |
| Preemption | State preemption of local reform |

## Geographic and Demographic Patterns

### Where Opposition Is Strongest

| Factor | Higher Opposition |
|--------|-------------------|
| Rural vs. urban | Rural more opposed |
| Age | Older voters more opposed |
| Personal experience | Those without exposure more opposed |
| Media consumption | Heavy crime news consumers more opposed |
| Political identity | Some conservative constituencies |

### Where Opposition Is Weakening

| Trend | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| Bipartisan reform | First Step Act, state legislation |
| Conservative support | Right on Crime, Koch network |
| Business support | Fair chance hiring movement |
| Faith community | Prison Fellowship, Catholic support |
| Fiscal concerns | Cost of incarceration |

## Responding to Opposition

### Counter-Messaging

| Opposition Claim | Effective Response |
|------------------|-------------------|
| "Soft on crime" | "Smart on crime"; public safety focus |
| "What about victims?" | Victim voices supporting reform |
| "They chose to commit crime" | Sentence is the punishment |
| "Public safety" | Recidivism data; reform increases safety |
| "They can't change" | Success stories; redemption narrative |

### Coalition-Building

| Strategy | Application |
|----------|-------------|
| Business allies | Emphasize workforce needs |
| Conservative allies | Fiscal, limited government arguments |
| Faith community | Redemption, second chances |
| Law enforcement | Some chiefs support reform |
| Victim allies | Crime Survivors for Safety and Justice |

### Evidence-Based Advocacy

| Evidence Type | Application |
|---------------|-------------|
| Recidivism data | Show barriers increase recidivism |
| Cost analysis | Show fiscal waste |
| Comparative data | Show other countries' success |
| Local success stories | Demonstrate feasibility |
| Program evaluations | Show what works |
