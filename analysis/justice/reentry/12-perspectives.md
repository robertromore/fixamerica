# Reentry Reform: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes reentry reform -- specifically employment barriers (ban the box, occupational licensing), housing discrimination, voting rights restoration, record clearing (Clean Slate), supervision reform (probation and parole), and public benefits access -- through nine distinct political perspectives. The core questions are: what obligations does society owe to people who have completed their criminal sentences, how should collateral consequences be structured, and what balance between accountability and reintegration best serves public safety? Each perspective receives engagement-consistency ratings and position scores, culminating in compromise proposals that bridge multiple viewpoints.

Reentry reform occupies an unusual position in the political landscape. Unlike many criminal justice issues, it has attracted genuine bipartisan interest in recent years. The First Step Act (2018) passed with overwhelming bipartisan support. State-level Clean Slate laws have been enacted by both Republican and Democratic legislatures. The economic argument -- that 600,000 annual prison releases who cannot find work or housing cost taxpayers billions in recidivism -- resonates across ideological lines. Yet deep tensions remain around the scope of reform, the role of government mandates on private employers and landlords, and the degree to which structural racism should be centered in the analysis.

### Engagement Consistency (1-5)

This metric assesses how reliably a perspective's stated positions align with its underlying principles, and how stable those positions are when concerns are addressed. Higher scores indicate more predictable negotiating partners.

| Level | Label | Indicators |
|-------|-------|------------|
| 5 | Highly Consistent | Stated positions reflect underlying principles; positions stable when concerns addressed |
| 4 | Mostly Consistent | Principles generally align with positions; minor strategic flexibility |
| 3 | Mixed Consistency | Some positions principled, others reflect coalition pressures or unstated concerns |
| 2 | Low Consistency | Positions shift frequently; stated concerns differ from underlying priorities |
| 1 | Unpredictable | Positions appear driven primarily by opposition dynamics rather than principles |

**Coalition-Building Considerations:**

- Alignment gap: When stated concerns differ from underlying priorities, addressing stated concerns may not secure support
- Position stability: Perspectives with shifting positions require ongoing negotiation
- Principle consistency: Perspectives that apply principles uniformly are more predictable partners
- Internal diversity: Some perspectives contain factions with different priorities

## Perspective Analyses

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 3/5 - Mixed Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Genuine fiscal conservatism and redemption-narrative interest in reducing recidivism; but tough-on-crime politics and employer-freedom concerns create competing incentives |
| Principle consistency | "Individual responsibility" and "limited government" principles support removing government-created barriers to self-sufficiency; but "personal accountability" framing also justifies maintaining consequences for criminal behavior |
| Goalpost stability | Moderate; conservative support for reentry reform has grown since 2015 (Right on Crime, Koch network), but can retreat under "soft on crime" political pressure |
| Zero-sum behavior | Moderate; some conservatives view reentry reform as a left-wing criminal justice agenda rather than evaluating proposals on principle |

**Justification:** Conservatives have a real but internally conflicted relationship with reentry reform. The fiscal argument is compelling: $87 billion annually for incarceration, plus billions in lost productivity from 600,000+ annual releases who cannot find work. Right on Crime (a project of the Texas Public Policy Foundation) and the Koch network have championed reentry reform as consistent with conservative principles of limited government, personal responsibility, and fiscal discipline. The First Step Act passed with strong conservative support. However, the movement is uneven. When "ban the box" is framed as a government mandate on private employers, conservative support drops sharply. When voting rights restoration is framed as enfranchising "felons," tough-on-crime instincts prevail. The result is genuine support for some reforms but inconsistency across the full agenda.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Right on Crime has advocated for reentry reform since 2010, including record clearing and supervision reform
- Koch Industries implemented its own fair chance hiring practices before advocating for policy changes
- The First Step Act (2018) received overwhelming Republican support, including from the Trump administration
- Conservative opposition to comprehensive "ban the box" legislation has persisted in multiple state legislatures due to employer mandate concerns
- Some Republican legislators have reversed course on reentry reform under primary election pressure from tough-on-crime challengers

- Tort reform to reduce employer liability for hiring people with records
- Occupational licensing reform as part of broader deregulation agenda

**Justification:** Progressives have the most comprehensive and consistent critique of the reentry system. Their analysis situates collateral consequences within the broader framework of mass incarceration, structural racism, and the criminalization of poverty. They view the 44,000+ collateral consequences not as individual policy failures but as a system designed to maintain a permanent underclass, disproportionately composed of Black and brown Americans. Their positions are driven by principle rather than political calculation. They have advocated for the most expansive reforms -- complete elimination of collateral consequences, automatic record clearing, universal voting rights -- and have not moderated these positions under political pressure.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Drug Policy Alliance connects reentry barriers to War on Drugs critique
- Progressive organizations consistently advocate for automatic, not petition-based, record clearing
- Support for voting rights during incarceration (not just after release)
- Willing to advocate for reforms even in politically difficult environments

### Constitutionalist Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Genuine concern about due process, federalism, and the proper scope of government power in imposing collateral consequences |
| Principle consistency | Constitutional text supports limiting excessive punishment (Eighth Amendment), ensuring due process (Fifth/Fourteenth Amendments), and questioning disenfranchisement (Fourteenth Amendment Section 2); some tension with deference to state legislative prerogatives |
| Goalpost stability | Stable; constitutional analysis provides a fixed reference point |
| Zero-sum behavior | Low; constitutional analysis applies regardless of partisan dynamics |

**Justification:** Constitutionalists have a strong textual basis for supporting many reentry reforms. The Eighth Amendment's prohibition on cruel and unusual punishment can be read to encompass collateral consequences that effectively extend punishment indefinitely. The Fourteenth Amendment's Section 2 implicitly disapproves of disenfranchisement by reducing a state's representation for denying the vote. Due process protections apply to government-imposed barriers. However, constitutionalists also emphasize federalism and state legislative prerogatives, creating tension with federal reform mandates. The Commerce Clause authority for federal employment mandates on private employers is a genuine constitutional question.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Federalist Society panels have addressed collateral consequences and occupational licensing
- Justice Thomas has questioned the constitutional basis for expansive government licensing powers
- Constitutional scholars have debated the Eighth Amendment implications of permanent collateral consequences
- Heritage Foundation constitutional analysis supports limiting government power over individual livelihood

### Compromise 1: Occupational Licensing and Supervision Reform Package

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Libertarian + Centrist + Constitutionalist + Populist + Religious Right + Progressive + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** Government should not create barriers that prevent people who have served their sentences from earning a living, and supervision should reward compliance rather than set people up for failure.

**Policy Description:**

A combined state-level package that (1) enacts the Model Fair Licensing Act, prohibiting blanket criminal record disqualifications from occupational licenses and requiring individualized assessment with direct-nexus and time-limitation standards; and (2) enacts the Model Supervision Success Act, establishing earned discharge after 6-12 months of compliance, graduated sanctions for technical violations, caseload caps, and prohibition on incarceration for inability to pay supervision fees. The package includes pre-application determination procedures so individuals can assess their licensing eligibility before investing in education and training, and data collection on licensing decisions and supervision outcomes broken down by offense type and demographics.

### Compromise 2: State-Level Clean Slate with Judicial Safeguards

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Libertarian + Centrist + Religious Right + Progressive + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** People who have completed their sentences and remained crime-free for a reasonable period should be able to move on with their lives without a permanent record, but the process should include appropriate safeguards for public safety.

**Policy Description:**

A state-level Clean Slate Act that provides automatic sealing of arrest records (no conviction) after one year, misdemeanor convictions after three to five years, and non-violent felony convictions after seven years, provided no new convictions during the waiting period. Exclusions apply for sex offenses, murder, offenses against children, and domestic violence. For excluded offenses, a petition-based pathway allows judicial review with a presumption of sealing after ten years. The sealed record remains accessible to law enforcement, courts, and specified agencies. A data broker compliance provision requires removal of sealed records within 90 days with civil penalties for non-compliance. The state creates an automated identification system and notifies individuals when records are sealed.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Conservative | Waiting periods ensure demonstrated rehabilitation; judicial review for serious offenses; exclusions for most dangerous crimes |
| Liberal | Automatic process removes barriers for those who cannot afford lawyers or navigate bureaucracy; addresses racial disparities in record-based discrimination |
| Libertarian | Government records that impede liberty are sealed; individual freedom restored |
| Centrist | Evidence from 11+ states demonstrates effectiveness; automated process is efficient and scalable |
| Religious Right | Clean slate reflects redemption theology; waiting period represents demonstrated change; judicial review preserves accountability |
| Progressive | Automatic sealing is the most effective approach; data broker compliance addresses private-sector perpetuation of records |
| Democratic Socialist | Class barriers to record clearing eliminated through automation; data broker enforcement protects working people |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Accept automatic process rather than requiring petition and judicial review for all offenses |
| Liberal | Accept exclusions for serious offenses rather than universal eligibility |
| Libertarian | Accept that records remain accessible to law enforcement rather than being fully expunged |
| Centrist | Accept comprehensive reform rather than incremental approach |
| Religious Right | Accept automatic process rather than requiring demonstrated faith-based rehabilitation |
| Progressive | Accept exclusions and waiting periods rather than immediate universal expungement |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 7/9 perspectives (Constitutionalist and Populist are moderate supporters at state level)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 5/7 core coalition members rated 4+ consistency
- **Durability:** High; Clean Slate laws have already been adopted by 11+ states with bipartisan support; no state has repealed a Clean Slate law after adoption; the automated approach reduces ongoing political controversy
- **Implementation Path:** State legislation modeled on Pennsylvania (first Clean Slate, 2018) and Michigan (most comprehensive); bipartisan sponsorship; Clean Slate Initiative provides model language and advocacy support

### Compromise 3: Fair Chance Employment with Employer Protections

**Bridges:** Liberal + Centrist + Conservative + Religious Right + Populist + Progressive + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** Returning citizens deserve a fair chance at employment, and employers who give them that chance deserve protection from lawsuit risk.

**Policy Description:**

A combined approach that (1) establishes ban-the-box for public employers and federal contractors (delaying criminal history inquiry until after conditional offer), phased in by employer size for private employers above 15 employees; (2) creates robust employer safe harbor from negligent hiring liability for employers who follow fair chance hiring procedures (individualized assessment, consideration of time elapsed and rehabilitation evidence); (3) expands the Federal Bonding Program to provide free fidelity bonding for all employers hiring people with records in compliance with the Act; (4) enhances the Work Opportunity Tax Credit for hiring formerly incarcerated individuals; and (5) requires data collection on hiring decisions, disaggregated by race, to monitor for both direct and statistical discrimination. The law does not apply to positions requiring security clearances, law enforcement positions, or positions where federal law specifically requires background checks.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Conservative | Safe harbor and bonding address employer liability concerns; tax credits incentivize voluntary hiring; public sector and contractor focus reduces private mandate; phase-in by employer size protects small businesses |
| Liberal | Ban the box removes initial screening barrier; data collection enables monitoring for racial discrimination; broad coverage eventually includes most employers |
| Centrist | Evidence-based reform with employer supports; phased implementation is practical |
| Religious Right | Employers of faith who believe in second chances are protected; tax credits support redemption mission |
| Populist | Small businesses protected by phase-in; tax credits help regular businesses; safe harbor reduces lawsuit risk |
| Progressive | Comprehensive coverage for most employers; data collection addresses statistical discrimination concern |
| Democratic Socialist | Workers with records get fair access to employment; data collection enables accountability |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Accept eventual mandate on mid-to-large private employers (though with safe harbor and employer supports) |
| Liberal | Accept phase-in by employer size rather than immediate universal coverage; accept exceptions for security and law enforcement |
| Centrist | Accept comprehensive legislation rather than pilot programs |
| Religious Right | Accept government mandate on some private hiring decisions |
| Populist | Accept that employers cannot categorically exclude people with records |
| Progressive | Accept phase-in, exceptions, and employer safe harbor rather than immediate comprehensive coverage with full private right of action |
| Democratic Socialist | Accept employer protections that may reduce legal accountability for discrimination |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 7/9 perspectives (Libertarian and Constitutionalist remain opposed to private employer mandates but support the public sector and employer protection components)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 4/7 core coalition members rated 4+ consistency
- **Durability:** Moderate to high; the combination of mandates and protections creates a balanced package that gives both reformers and employers something they want; the phase-in reduces immediate opposition; however, employer mandate provisions remain politically vulnerable to small-business lobbying
- **Implementation Path:** Federal legislation for public employers and contractors (can be done by executive order as interim step); state legislation for private employer provisions; bipartisan sponsorship framing as "fair chance plus employer protection"

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The core coalition for reentry reform -- Liberal, Progressive, Centrist, and Democratic Socialist (all rated 4-5 consistency) -- provides a reliable foundation of four highly committed perspectives. Libertarian support (4/5 consistency) is strong on government-created barriers (licensing, supervision, record clearing) but drops sharply on private employer mandates. Conservative support (3/5 consistency) is strongest on fiscal and deregulation arguments and weakest on government mandates and voting rights. The most effective strategy is to sequence reforms from highest to lowest cross-ideological support: begin with occupational licensing reform and earned discharge (9-of-9 support), then Clean Slate (7-of-9 core, broader with state framing), then employer protections (broad support), and finally the more contested reforms (ban the box for private employers, voting rights restoration, public benefits reform).

### Key Obstacles

- **Employer mandate opposition:** Private employers and business associations resist ban-the-box mandates, and this opposition resonates with conservative, libertarian, and populist perspectives that prioritize employer freedom
- **"Soft on crime" politics:** Reentry reform is vulnerable to attack ads framing it as helping criminals rather than improving public safety, particularly in primary elections
- **Federalism objections:** Federal legislation on employment, housing, and voting faces principled constitutional objections from constitutionalists and conservative preference for state action
- **Low public salience:** Despite strong support when specific reforms are explained, reentry reform has low public visibility, reducing political pressure for action
- **Law enforcement institutional resistance:** Supervision agencies may resist earned discharge and graduated sanctions that reduce their caseloads (and budgets)
- **Data broker non-compliance:** Clean Slate laws are only as effective as data broker compliance, which has proven difficult to enforce

### Low Consistency Partners

Populists (2/5) and, to a lesser extent, Conservatives and the Religious Right (both 3/5) are the most unpredictable partners. For Populists, framing is everything: reforms presented as "helping working people get back on their feet" secure support; the same reforms presented as "giving felons special treatment" produce opposition. The strategic approach should ensure that populist-targeted messaging always leads with concrete stories of working people trapped by the system and emphasizes economic self-sufficiency rather than government benefits. For Conservatives, the most effective frame is fiscal responsibility and deregulation: reentry reform saves taxpayer money and removes government barriers. For the Religious Right, redemption theology provides a powerful and authentic frame that connects to their deepest values.

### Recommended Approach

1. **Lead with licensing and supervision reform:** These command 9-of-9 support and face the least institutional opposition; enact them at the state level in as many states as possible to build momentum
2. **State-level Clean Slate:** Build on the 11+ state track record; target states with bipartisan criminal justice reform traditions; the automated approach reduces ongoing political controversy
3. **Employer safe harbor first, mandate second:** Pass employer liability protections and bonding expansion before or alongside ban-the-box requirements; this neutralizes the strongest employer argument against fair chance hiring
4. **Multiple simultaneous frames:** Use fiscal responsibility framing for right-of-center audiences, racial justice framing for left-of-center audiences, redemption framing for religious audiences, and "regular people" framing for populist audiences -- all pointing to the same substantive reforms
5. **Federal action on federal matters:** Clean Slate for federal records, ban the box for federal contractors and agencies, and voting rights restoration in federal elections are constitutionally uncontroversial and should be pursued at the federal level
6. **Data as foundation:** Build comprehensive data collection into every reform; evidence of effectiveness builds the case for subsequent reforms and neutralizes "soft on crime" attacks
7. **Sequence contested reforms last:** Save private employer mandates, voting rights restoration, and public benefits reform for after the less contested reforms have built political capital and demonstrated results
