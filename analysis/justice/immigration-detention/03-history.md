# Immigration Detention and Enforcement: History

## Pre-Modern Era (Before 1980)

### Early Immigration Enforcement

**Ellis Island Era (1892-1954)**
- Ellis Island served as primary detention and processing center
- Detention was generally brief, focused on health screening
- Most arrivals processed within hours or days
- Excluded: those with diseases, likely public charges, anarchists
- Peak detention capacity: ~1,500

**Bracero Program and Operation Wetback (1942-1964)**
- Bracero Program brought Mexican workers legally
- Operation Wetback (1954) mass deportation campaign
- Estimated 1 million+ deportations
- Minimal formal detention infrastructure
- Local jails used on ad hoc basis

**Pre-1980 Detention**
- No dedicated immigration detention facilities
- INS used local jails and federal prisons
- Daily detention population: ~2,000
- Parole was default for most asylum seekers
- No mandatory detention requirements

## The Modern Detention Era Begins (1980-1995)

### Mariel Boatlift Crisis (1980)

**The Event**
- April-October 1980: 125,000 Cubans arrived by boat
- Castro emptied prisons, mental institutions
- Overwhelmed immigration system
- Military bases converted to detention camps

**Policy Response**
- "Excludable" Cubans detained indefinitely
- No constitutional protections for arriving aliens
- Supreme Court upheld indefinite detention (Shaughnessy v. Mezei precedent applied)
- Established detention as deterrent policy

### Haitian Refugee Crisis (1981-1994)

**1981-1991 Interdiction**
- Reagan Executive Order 12324: Haitian Interdiction Program
- Coast Guard intercepted boats at sea
- Asylum seekers returned without hearings
- Krome Detention Center opened (1980)
- Discriminatory treatment compared to Cubans

**Guantanamo Detention (1991-1994)**
- 40,000+ Haitians held at Guantanamo
- HIV-positive refugees segregated
- Legal challenges (Haitian Centers Council v. Sale)
- Camps closed 1994 after litigation

### Immigration Act of 1990

- Increased legal immigration quotas
- Created Diversity Visa lottery
- Strengthened employer sanctions
- Did not significantly expand detention

## The Great Expansion (1996-2001)

### Illegal Immigration Reform and Immigrant Responsibility Act (IIRIRA) of 1996

**Mandatory Detention Provisions**
- Section 236(c): Mandatory detention for criminal aliens
- "Aggravated felony" definition vastly expanded
- No individualized custody determinations
- Retroactive application to old convictions

**Expedited Removal**
- Created for arriving aliens
- 14-day window, 100-mile zone
- Minimal judicial review
- Credible fear standard for asylum

**287(g) Program Created**
- Authorized state/local police to enforce immigration law
- Voluntary agreements with INS (later ICE)
- Training for local officers
- Foundation for future expansion

### Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act (AEDPA) of 1996

- Expanded deportable offenses
- Limited judicial review
- Streamlined removal procedures
- Worked in conjunction with IIRIRA

### Growth of Private Detention

**Corrections Corporation of America (now CoreCivic)**
- Founded 1983
- First private immigration detention contract: 1984
- Rapid expansion in 1990s
- Lobbied for mandatory detention laws

**Wackenhut/GEO Group**
- Entered immigration detention market 1980s
- Aggressive expansion strategy
- Became dominant player by 2000s

## Post-9/11 Transformation (2001-2008)

### Creation of DHS and ICE (2002-2003)

**Homeland Security Act of 2002**
- INS abolished, functions transferred to DHS
- Three new agencies created:
  - ICE (Immigration and Customs Enforcement)
  - CBP (Customs and Border Protection)
  - USCIS (Citizenship and Immigration Services)
- Immigration framed as national security issue

### NSEERS and Special Registration

- National Security Entry-Exit Registration System
- Targeted men from 25 predominantly Muslim countries
- Required registration, interviews, fingerprinting
- Thousands detained for minor violations
- Program ended 2011, rule removed 2016

### Detention Bed Mandate Origins

**2004 Intelligence Reform Act**
- Mandated 8,000 additional detention beds by 2006
- "Catch and release" criticism
- Bed space drives detention decisions

### Operation Streamline (2005)

- Criminal prosecution for illegal entry
- Mass hearings, rapid guilty pleas
- Border Patrol sectors in Arizona, Texas
- Overwhelmed federal courts
- Increased criminal detention

### Secure Communities Launched (2008)

- Automatic fingerprint sharing with ICE
- All arrestees screened against immigration databases
- Detainers issued for suspected noncitizens
- Initially voluntary, later mandatory

## Continued Expansion (2008-2016)

### Detention Bed Quota (2009-2017)

**Congressional Mandate**
- 2009 Appropriations: 33,400 bed minimum
- Increased to 34,000 beds by 2012
- "Bed quota" or "detention bed mandate"
- Required ICE to fill beds regardless of need
- Pressure to maximize detention

### Family Detention Expansion (2014)

**2014 Border Crisis**
- Surge of Central American families and children
- Unaccompanied minors from Guatemala, Honduras, El Salvador
- Administration response: deterrence through detention

**Artesia and Dilley**
- Artesia, NM facility opened (closed after litigation)
- South Texas Family Residential Center (Dilley) opened 2014
- Karnes expanded for families
- Flores Settlement Agreement challenges

### Priority Enforcement Program (2014-2017)

- Replaced Secure Communities (nominally)
- Focused on serious criminals
- Reduced detainer issuance
- Still relied on fingerprint sharing

## Maximum Enforcement Era (2017-2020)

### Executive Orders (January 2017)

**Border Security and Immigration Enforcement Improvements**
- Immediate construction of border wall
- End "catch and release"
- Expand detention capacity
- Hire 5,000 additional Border Patrol

**Enhancing Public Safety in the Interior**
- Hire 10,000 additional ICE officers
- Expand 287(g) program
- Punish sanctuary jurisdictions
- Prioritize all removable aliens

### Zero Tolerance Policy (2018)

**Family Separation**
- April-June 2018 policy
- Criminal prosecution of all border crossers
- Children separated, reclassified as unaccompanied
- 2,800+ children separated
- International condemnation
- Executive Order ended policy June 20, 2018

**Continuing Effects**
- Reunification delayed for hundreds
- Psychological trauma documented
- Inspector General investigations
- Ongoing litigation

### Remain in Mexico / MPP (2019)

**Migrant Protection Protocols**
- Asylum seekers returned to Mexico to wait
- 70,000+ enrolled
- Dangerous conditions in Mexican border cities
- COVID-19 suspension, Biden termination, court revival

### Detention Capacity Expansion

## Pandemic and Recent Developments (2020-Present)

### COVID-19 Impact

**Title 42 Expulsions**
- March 2020: CDC order authorized rapid expulsion
- Public health rationale contested
- 2.8 million+ expulsions by 2023
- Bypassed asylum protections
- Ended May 2023

**Detention Population Changes**
- Initial reduction for health reasons
- Deaths from COVID-19 in detention
- Inadequate medical response documented
- Gradual return to pre-pandemic levels

### Biden Administration (2021-Present)

**Initial Policies**
- 100-day deportation moratorium (blocked by courts)
- Ended MPP (Remain in Mexico) - revived by courts, then terminated
- Ended Zero Tolerance family separation
- Narrowed enforcement priorities

**Continued Detention**
- Family detention maintained
- Private detention contracts continued
- Some facility closures (minor)
- ATD expansion significant

### Current Trajectory (2024-2026)

**Recent Developments**
- Title 42 ended, Title 8 processing resumed
- Asylum transit ban litigation
- Record border encounters
- Detention population 38,000-50,000
- Private prison contracts renewed
- State anti-sanctuary laws proliferating

## Lessons from History

### Patterns and Trends

1. **Crisis Response â†’ Permanent Expansion**: Each "crisis" leads to detention expansion that becomes permanent
2. **Bipartisan Growth**: Both parties have expanded detention (Clinton 1996, Bush post-9/11, Obama bed mandate, Trump maximum enforcement)
3. **Privatization Accelerates Growth**: Private prison lobbying correlates with expansion
4. **Deterrence Doesn't Work**: Despite expansion, migration continues
5. **Rights Erode Incrementally**: Each era sees further due process restrictions

### Historical Injustices

- Japanese internment parallels noted by scholars
- Racial disparities in enforcement throughout history
- Asylum seekers treated as criminals
- Economic migrants criminalized
- Family separation as recurring practice
