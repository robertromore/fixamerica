# Immigration Detention and Enforcement: Model Legislation

## Overview

This document provides model federal legislation and state model bills to reform immigration detention, protect immigrant rights, and establish humane alternatives to detention.

---

# PART I: FEDERAL LEGISLATION

---

## Bill 1: Dignity for Detained Immigrants Act

### Purpose
To establish enforceable standards for conditions in immigration detention facilities and ensure humane treatment of all detained individuals.

```
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Dignity for Detained Immigrants Act of 2025."

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:

(a) The United States detains approximately 40,000 immigrants on any given
day in over 200 facilities across the country.

(b) The Department of Homeland Security Office of Inspector General has
documented persistent and dangerous conditions in immigration detention
facilities, including inadequate medical care, spoiled food, improper use
of solitary confinement, and unsafe conditions.

(c) At least 300 individuals have died in immigration detention since 2004,
with many deaths attributable to inadequate medical care and negligence.

(d) Unlike criminal detention, immigration detention is civil in nature
and should not be punitive, yet conditions often mirror or exceed the
restrictiveness of criminal prisons.

(e) Current detention standards are not legally enforceable and lack
meaningful oversight and accountability mechanisms.

(f) All individuals in government custody, regardless of immigration
status, are entitled to humane treatment and basic dignity.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:

(1) COVERED FACILITY.—The term "covered facility" means any facility,
including Service Processing Centers, Contract Detention Facilities,
facilities operating under Intergovernmental Service Agreements, and
family residential centers, that is used to detain individuals pursuant
to the Immigration and Nationality Act.

(2) DETAINEE.—The term "detainee" means any individual held in a covered
facility pursuant to the Immigration and Nationality Act.

(3) SECRETARY.—The term "Secretary" means the Secretary of Homeland Security.

SEC. 4. ENFORCEABLE DETENTION STANDARDS.

(a) MINIMUM STANDARDS.—The Secretary shall promulgate regulations
establishing minimum detention standards that shall include:

(1) MEDICAL CARE.—
    (A) Medical screening by a licensed healthcare provider within 12 hours
    of admission;
    (B) 24-hour access to emergency medical care;
    (C) Chronic disease management protocols;
    (D) Mental health services provided by licensed mental health
    professionals;
    (E) Dental care beyond emergency extractions;
    (F) Full reproductive healthcare for women;
    (G) Medication continuity and management;
    (H) Independent medical review upon request.

(2) LIVING CONDITIONS.—
    (A) Minimum of 50 square feet of living space per detainee;
    (B) Maximum facility capacity of 80 percent;
    (C) Access to natural light and outdoor recreation for minimum of
    2 hours daily;
    (D) Temperature maintained between 68 and 76 degrees Fahrenheit;
    (E) Clean drinking water available at all times;
    (F) Nutritionally adequate and culturally appropriate food;
    (G) Clean bedding and personal hygiene supplies.

(3) COMMUNICATION.—
    (A) Free telephone calls to legal counsel;
    (B) Access to telephones for personal calls at reasonable rates not
    exceeding local market rates;
    (C) Video visitation options;
    (D) In-person visitation rights including contact visits;
    (E) Mail access without unreasonable censorship;
    (F) Access to electronic communication where available.

(4) LEGAL ACCESS.—
    (A) Confidential attorney-client meeting spaces;
    (B) Access to law library or legal research materials;
    (C) Know Your Rights presentations within 48 hours of admission;
    (D) Access to legal orientation programs;
    (E) Ability to receive and possess legal documents.

(5) SOLITARY CONFINEMENT.—
    (A) Prohibition on solitary confinement for more than 15 consecutive
    days or 20 days in any 60-day period;
    (B) Prohibition on solitary confinement for vulnerable populations
    including pregnant women, individuals with mental illness, and LGBTQ
    individuals seeking protective custody;
    (C) Mandatory documented review every 24 hours for any segregation;
    (D) Access to outdoor recreation and meaningful human contact for all
    segregated individuals.

(6) VULNERABLE POPULATIONS.—
    (A) Special protections for pregnant women, nursing mothers, and
    individuals with disabilities;
    (B) Trauma-informed care protocols;
    (C) LGBTQ-specific housing and protection protocols;
    (D) Accommodation of religious practices.

(b) ENFORCEABILITY.—The standards established under this section shall be
legally enforceable through:

(1) A private right of action in federal district court for any detainee
or organization acting on behalf of detainees;

(2) Injunctive relief and declaratory judgment;

(3) Attorney's fees and costs for prevailing plaintiffs.

SEC. 5. INDEPENDENT OVERSIGHT.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established within the Department of Homeland
Security an independent Office of Immigration Detention Oversight (OIDO).

(b) DUTIES.—The OIDO shall:

(1) Conduct unannounced inspections of all covered facilities at least
quarterly;

(2) Investigate complaints from detainees, family members, and advocates;

(3) Review all deaths and serious incidents in custody;

(4) Issue public reports on facility conditions;

(5) Make binding recommendations for facility closure or contract
termination when warranted.

(c) INDEPENDENCE.—The OIDO Director shall serve a 6-year term, may only
be removed for cause, and shall have independent hiring authority.

(d) ACCESS.—OIDO staff and designated community monitors shall have
unrestricted access to all covered facilities, detainees, and records.

SEC. 6. TRANSPARENCY AND REPORTING.

(a) PUBLIC REPORTING.—The Secretary shall publish quarterly reports including:

(1) Total detained population by facility;
(2) Average length of detention;
(3) Deaths, sexual assaults, and serious incidents;
(4) Use of solitary confinement;
(5) Medical staffing levels;
(6) Grievances filed and resolved;
(7) Inspection results.

(b) NOTIFICATION.—Facilities shall notify the Secretary and OIDO within
24 hours of any death, hospitalization, or serious incident.

SEC. 7. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There is authorized to be appropriated $50,000,000 annually to carry out
this Act.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 180 days after the date of enactment.
```

## Bill 2: Fairness to Freedom Act

### Purpose
To end mandatory immigration detention, establish a presumption of release, and ensure individualized custody determinations.

```
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Fairness to Freedom Act of 2025."

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:

(a) The Immigration and Nationality Act currently mandates detention for
broad categories of noncitizens without individualized assessment of flight
risk or danger to the community.

(b) Mandatory detention applies retroactively to individuals with decades-old
criminal convictions who have lived law-abiding lives in the United States
for years.

(c) Studies demonstrate that community-based alternatives to detention
achieve appearance rates of 99 percent or higher at a fraction of the cost
of detention.

(d) The average cost of detention exceeds $150 per person per day, while
effective alternatives cost $5-15 per person per day.

(e) Detention separates families, causes psychological trauma, and impedes
individuals' ability to prepare their immigration cases.

(f) The presumption in a free society should be liberty, not incarceration,
particularly for civil immigration proceedings.

SEC. 3. ELIMINATION OF MANDATORY DETENTION.

(a) REPEAL.—Section 236(c) of the Immigration and Nationality Act (8 U.S.C.
1226(c)) is repealed.

(b) PRESUMPTION OF RELEASE.—Section 236(a) of the Immigration and Nationality
Act (8 U.S.C. 1226(a)) is amended to read as follows:

"(a) CUSTODY PENDING REMOVAL PROCEEDINGS.—

"(1) PRESUMPTION OF RELEASE.—An alien who is in removal proceedings shall
be released from custody pending a final determination of the alien's case
unless the Secretary of Homeland Security demonstrates by clear and
convincing evidence that:

"(A) the alien poses a specific, identifiable flight risk that cannot be
mitigated through conditions of release; or

"(B) the alien poses a specific, identifiable danger to another person or
the community that cannot be mitigated through conditions of release.

"(2) INDIVIDUALIZED ASSESSMENT.—Any custody determination shall be based
on an individualized assessment of the alien that considers:

"(A) the alien's ties to the community, including family relationships,
employment history, and length of residence;

"(B) the alien's prior immigration and criminal history, including
compliance with prior immigration orders and court appearances;

"(C) the alien's manner of entry into the United States;

"(D) the alien's cooperation with immigration authorities;

"(E) the alien's mental and physical health;

"(F) the availability of community-based supervision or alternatives to
detention;

"(G) the alien's likelihood of success on the merits of their case.

"(3) PROHIBITED CONSIDERATIONS.—A custody determination shall not be based
solely on:

"(A) a prior criminal conviction, without consideration of the nature and
circumstances of the offense, time elapsed, and evidence of rehabilitation;

"(B) lack of documentation or immigration status;

"(C) national origin, race, ethnicity, or religion;

"(D) the capacity of detention facilities.

"(4) BOND.—If release on recognizance is not appropriate, bond shall be set
at an amount the alien can reasonably afford, considering:

"(A) the alien's financial resources and ability to pay;

"(B) the availability of family or community members to assist;

"(C) alternative forms of security.

"(5) CONDITIONS OF RELEASE.—Conditions of release may include:

"(A) periodic check-ins with immigration authorities;

"(B) electronic monitoring, with a preference for least restrictive methods;

"(C) community supervision programs;

"(D) geographic restrictions;

"(E) reporting requirements;

"(F) deposit of documents."

SEC. 4. BOND HEARINGS.

(a) AUTOMATIC HEARING.—Any alien detained pursuant to the Immigration and
Nationality Act shall receive a custody hearing before an immigration judge
within 48 hours of detention.

(b) BURDEN OF PROOF.—At any custody hearing, the Department of Homeland
Security shall bear the burden of establishing by clear and convincing
evidence that detention is necessary.

(c) PERIODIC REVIEW.—Any alien who remains detained shall receive a custody
redetermination hearing:

(1) Every 30 days for the first 90 days of detention; and

(2) Every 60 days thereafter.

(d) CHANGED CIRCUMSTANCES.—An alien may request a bond redetermination
hearing at any time based on changed circumstances.

SEC. 5. SPECIAL POPULATIONS.

(a) PRESUMPTIVE RELEASE.—The following categories of aliens shall be released
from detention absent extraordinary circumstances:

(1) Asylum seekers who have established a credible fear of persecution;

(2) Pregnant women and nursing mothers;

(3) Primary caregivers of minor children;

(4) Individuals with serious medical conditions or mental health needs;

(5) Survivors of torture or trafficking;

(6) Individuals over age 65;

(7) LGBTQ individuals at risk in detention;

(8) Individuals with disabilities.

(b) FAMILIES.—No child shall be detained for immigration purposes. Family
unity shall be preserved through community-based case management.

SEC. 6. ALTERNATIVES TO DETENTION.

(a) PREFERENCE FOR ALTERNATIVES.—When conditions of release are appropriate,
the Secretary shall utilize the least restrictive conditions necessary,
with a preference for community-based supervision over electronic monitoring.

(b) COMMUNITY PROGRAMS.—The Secretary shall expand community-based
alternatives including:

(1) Case management programs;

(2) Legal orientation and representation programs;

(3) Community sponsorship programs;

(4) Wrap-around social services.

(c) FUNDING.—Not less than 50 percent of funds appropriated for immigration
detention shall be used for alternatives to detention.

SEC. 7. LENGTH OF DETENTION.

(a) MAXIMUM DETENTION.—No alien shall be detained for more than 6 months
pending resolution of removal proceedings unless:

(1) The alien is subject to mandatory detention under section 236(e) of
the Immigration and Nationality Act for terrorism-related grounds; or

(2) A court finds by clear and convincing evidence that the alien poses
an imminent risk of serious harm to others.

(b) PERIODIC JUDICIAL REVIEW.—Any alien detained for more than 90 days shall
receive automatic judicial review of the detention.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 90 days after the date of enactment.
```

## Bill 3: End Private Immigration Detention Act

### Purpose
To phase out the use of private, for-profit immigration detention facilities.

```
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "End Private Immigration Detention Act of 2025."

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:

(a) Over 80 percent of individuals in immigration detention are held in
facilities operated by private, for-profit corporations, primarily GEO
Group and CoreCivic.

(b) Private prison corporations receive over $1.4 billion annually from
federal immigration detention contracts.

(c) Private detention facilities have documented higher rates of violations,
abuse, and inadequate conditions compared to government-operated facilities.

(d) Private prison corporations have spent millions of dollars lobbying
for expanded detention and enforcement-heavy immigration policies.

(e) The profit motive inherent in private detention creates incentives to
maximize detention populations and minimize expenditures on detainee welfare.

(f) Private corporations have used political contributions and lobbying to
influence immigration policy in ways that benefit their financial interests.

(g) The incarceration of human beings should not be a source of private profit.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:

(1) PRIVATE DETENTION FACILITY.—The term "private detention facility" means
any facility operated by a private entity pursuant to a contract with the
Federal Government to hold individuals detained under the Immigration and
Nationality Act.

(2) PRIVATE ENTITY.—The term "private entity" means any corporation,
company, partnership, firm, association, or organization that is not a
Federal, State, local, or Tribal government entity.

SEC. 4. PROHIBITION ON NEW PRIVATE DETENTION CONTRACTS.

(a) PROHIBITION.—Beginning on the date of enactment of this Act, the
Secretary of Homeland Security may not enter into any new contract or
agreement with a private entity for the detention of individuals pursuant
to the Immigration and Nationality Act.

(b) CONTRACT EXTENSIONS.—The prohibition in subsection (a) includes contract
extensions, renewals, expansions, and modifications that would increase
capacity or extend the duration of existing contracts.

SEC. 5. PHASE-OUT OF EXISTING CONTRACTS.

(a) NON-RENEWAL.—The Secretary shall not renew any contract with a private
entity for immigration detention upon expiration of such contract.

(b) PHASE-OUT SCHEDULE.—All existing contracts with private entities for
immigration detention shall be terminated not later than 5 years after the
date of enactment of this Act.

(c) EARLY TERMINATION.—The Secretary shall prioritize early termination of
contracts with facilities that have:

(1) Documented histories of violations of detention standards;

(2) Deaths in custody attributable to inadequate care;

(3) Sexual abuse or assault incidents;

(4) Persistent grievance patterns.

SEC. 6. TRANSITION PLANNING.

(a) TRANSITION PLAN.—Not later than 180 days after the date of enactment,
the Secretary shall submit to Congress a plan for transitioning away from
private immigration detention that includes:

(1) A timeline for ending each private detention contract;

(2) An assessment of alternative community-based programs;

(3) A workforce transition plan for affected employees;

(4) An economic impact assessment and mitigation strategy for affected
communities;

(5) Cost estimates for the transition.

(b) ALTERNATIVES INVESTMENT.—Funds previously allocated for private
detention contracts shall be redirected to:

(1) Community-based alternatives to detention;

(2) Legal representation programs;

(3) Immigration court capacity;

(4) Workforce transition assistance.

SEC. 7. WORKER PROTECTIONS.

(a) TRANSITION ASSISTANCE.—Workers employed at private detention facilities
shall be eligible for:

(1) Retraining programs;

(2) Job placement assistance;

(3) Relocation assistance;

(4) Extended unemployment benefits.

(b) HIRING PREFERENCE.—Federal agencies shall give hiring preference to
former employees of private detention facilities who meet job qualifications.

SEC. 8. LOBBYING AND POLITICAL ACTIVITY.

(a) DISCLOSURE.—Any private entity that has held a contract for immigration
detention within the previous 10 years shall disclose:

(1) All lobbying expenditures related to immigration policy;

(2) All political contributions to federal, state, and local candidates;

(3) All contributions to trade associations and advocacy organizations.

(b) BAN ON FUTURE CONTRACTS.—Any private entity that violates the disclosure
requirements of this section shall be permanently barred from receiving
federal contracts.

SEC. 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect immediately upon enactment.
```

## Bill 4: Access to Counsel in Immigration Proceedings Act

### Purpose
To establish a right to appointed counsel in immigration proceedings.

```
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Access to Counsel in Immigration Proceedings
Act of 2025."

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:

(a) Immigration proceedings can result in deportation, family separation,
and return to countries where individuals face persecution, torture, or
death.

(b) Despite these severe consequences, there is no right to government-
appointed counsel in immigration proceedings.

(c) Approximately 70 percent of detained individuals and 40 percent of
non-detained individuals in immigration proceedings are unrepresented.

(d) Represented individuals are 5 times more likely to obtain relief from
removal than unrepresented individuals.

(e) Represented individuals are significantly more likely to be released
on bond and to appear for court hearings.

(f) The complexity of immigration law requires legal expertise that most
individuals cannot navigate without assistance.

(g) The lack of counsel contributes to wrongful deportations, prolonged
detention, and separation of families.

(h) The provision of counsel improves court efficiency by reducing
continuances, frivolous filings, and appeals based on inadequate representation.

SEC. 3. RIGHT TO APPOINTED COUNSEL.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 240(b) of the Immigration and Nationality Act
(8 U.S.C. 1229a(b)) is amended by adding at the end the following:

"(8) RIGHT TO APPOINTED COUNSEL.—

"(A) IN GENERAL.—An alien in removal proceedings shall have the right to
be represented by counsel appointed by the Government if the alien cannot
afford counsel.

"(B) PRIORITY CATEGORIES.—The Attorney General shall prioritize the
provision of counsel to:

"(i) detained individuals;

"(ii) unaccompanied children;

"(iii) individuals with mental disabilities or serious mental illness;

"(iv) asylum seekers and individuals seeking withholding of removal or
protection under the Convention Against Torture;

"(v) victims of trafficking, domestic violence, or serious crimes;

"(vi) individuals who have been lawful permanent residents for 5 years
or more;

"(vii) individuals with U.S. citizen or lawful permanent resident family
members.

"(C) STANDARDS.—Counsel provided under this paragraph shall meet standards
established by the Attorney General, including:

"(i) training in immigration law;

"(ii) caseload limits to ensure quality representation;

"(iii) continuing legal education requirements;

"(iv) supervision for non-attorney representatives.

"(D) PROVIDER MODELS.—The Attorney General may provide counsel through:

"(i) a federal public defender model;

"(ii) contracts with nonprofit legal services organizations;

"(iii) contracts with private attorneys;

"(iv) law school clinics;

"(v) a combination of the above.

"(E) NOTIFICATION.—Immigration judges shall notify aliens of their right
to appointed counsel at the commencement of proceedings."

SEC. 4. LEGAL ORIENTATION PROGRAMS.

(a) EXPANSION.—The Attorney General shall ensure that all detained
individuals receive legal orientation presentations within 48 hours of
detention.

(b) CONTENT.—Legal orientation programs shall include:

(1) Information about immigration proceedings and potential forms of relief;

(2) Information about the right to appointed counsel;

(3) Information about the bond process;

(4) Self-help legal materials in appropriate languages.

SEC. 5. FUNDING.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated $1,500,000,000
annually to provide counsel under this Act.

(b) ALLOCATION.—Funds shall be allocated to ensure geographic coverage,
with priority to areas with high detention populations and immigration
court locations.

SEC. 6. IMPLEMENTATION.

(a) PHASE-IN.—The right to counsel shall be phased in as follows:

(1) YEAR 1.—All detained individuals;

(2) YEAR 2.—All individuals in priority categories;

(3) YEAR 3.—All individuals in removal proceedings.

(b) REGULATIONS.—Not later than 180 days after the date of enactment, the
Attorney General shall promulgate regulations implementing this Act.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 180 days after the date of enactment.
```

## Bill 5: Immigration Court Independence Act

### Purpose
To establish an independent immigration court system insulated from political influence.

```
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Immigration Court Independence Act of 2025."

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:

(a) Immigration courts are currently housed within the Department of Justice,
an executive agency, making immigration judges subject to political pressure
and influence.

(b) Immigration judges are not afforded the protections of judicial
independence, including life tenure and salary protection.

(c) The Attorney General has used case certification authority and other
mechanisms to influence immigration court decisions on substantive legal
issues.

(d) Immigration judges have been subject to performance quotas and case
completion deadlines that undermine due process.

(e) The immigration court backlog has grown to over 3 million cases,
resulting in multi-year waits for hearings.

(f) An independent court system would improve fairness, efficiency, and
public confidence in immigration adjudication.

SEC. 3. ESTABLISHMENT OF INDEPENDENT IMMIGRATION COURTS.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established in the judicial branch of the
United States Government an independent United States Immigration Court.

(b) TRANSFER.—All functions, personnel, assets, and liabilities of the
Executive Office for Immigration Review relating to immigration proceedings
are transferred to the United States Immigration Court.

SEC. 4. IMMIGRATION JUDGES.

(a) APPOINTMENT.—Immigration judges shall be appointed by the President,
by and with the advice and consent of the Senate.

(b) TERM.—Immigration judges shall serve for a term of 15 years and may
be reappointed.

(c) REMOVAL.—Immigration judges may be removed only for cause, including
misconduct, disability, or neglect of duty, through procedures established
by the Court.

(d) SALARY.—Immigration judges shall receive a salary equal to that of
magistrate judges in the federal district courts.

(e) QUALIFICATIONS.—Immigration judges shall:

(1) Be attorneys in good standing;

(2) Have at least 7 years of experience in immigration law;

(3) Demonstrate commitment to fair and impartial adjudication.

SEC. 5. STRUCTURE.

(a) TRIAL DIVISION.—The trial division shall conduct removal proceedings,
bond hearings, and other matters currently within the jurisdiction of
immigration courts.

(b) APPELLATE DIVISION.—The appellate division shall hear appeals from
decisions of trial division judges, replacing the current Board of
Immigration Appeals.

(c) CIRCUIT COURTS.—Immigration courts shall be organized into circuits
corresponding to the federal judicial circuits.

(d) CHIEF JUDGE.—The Chief Judge of the United States Immigration Court
shall be selected by the judges of the Court for a term of 5 years.

SEC. 6. ADMINISTRATION.

(a) ADMINISTRATIVE OFFICE.—There is established an Administrative Office
of the United States Immigration Courts, headed by a Director appointed
by the Chief Judge.

(b) FUNCTIONS.—The Administrative Office shall:

(1) Manage court operations and dockets;

(2) Hire and manage support staff;

(3) Develop and implement technology systems;

(4) Collect and publish statistics;

(5) Develop training programs;

(6) Establish rules of procedure.

(c) INDEPENDENCE.—The Attorney General and Secretary of Homeland Security
shall have no authority over court operations, case assignments, or
procedural rules.

SEC. 7. RULES OF PROCEDURE.

(a) PROMULGATION.—The United States Immigration Court shall promulgate rules
of procedure governing proceedings before the Court.

(b) DUE PROCESS.—Rules shall ensure:

(1) Adequate time for case preparation;

(2) Rights to present evidence and witnesses;

(3) Right to cross-examine government witnesses;

(4) Right to appeal adverse decisions;

(5) Translation and interpretation services.

SEC. 8. JUDICIAL REVIEW.

(a) APPELLATE REVIEW.—Decisions of the appellate division shall be subject
to review by the United States Courts of Appeals for the circuit in which
the proceedings were held.

(b) STANDARD OF REVIEW.—Review shall be conducted under the same standards
as review of district court decisions in civil cases.

SEC. 9. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There is authorized to be appropriated such sums as may be necessary to
carry out this Act, including:

(1) $300,000,000 for the appointment of additional immigration judges;

(2) $200,000,000 for support staff and court personnel;

(3) $100,000,000 for technology and facilities;

(4) $50,000,000 for training and education.

SEC. 10. TRANSITION.

(a) TIMELINE.—The transition to the independent court system shall be
completed within 3 years of the date of enactment.

(b) CURRENT JUDGES.—Immigration judges employed by the Executive Office for
Immigration Review on the date of enactment may apply for appointment to
the United States Immigration Court.

(c) PENDING CASES.—Cases pending before EOIR on the transition date shall
be transferred to the United States Immigration Court.

SEC. 11. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect immediately upon enactment, with full operation
of the independent court system within 3 years.
```

## Model State Bill 1: State Trust Act

### Purpose
To limit state and local cooperation with federal immigration enforcement and protect community trust.

```
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Trust Act."

SECTION 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.—The Legislature finds that:

(1) Community trust between residents and law enforcement is essential for
effective policing and public safety.

(2) Entanglement of state and local law enforcement with federal immigration
enforcement erodes community trust and discourages immigrants from reporting
crimes, serving as witnesses, and accessing public services.

(3) State and local resources are limited and should be focused on core
state functions rather than federal immigration enforcement.

(4) All residents, regardless of immigration status, are entitled to due
process and equal protection under the law.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to:

(1) Separate state and local law enforcement from federal immigration
enforcement activities;

(2) Ensure that state resources are not used to facilitate civil immigration
enforcement;

(3) Protect the due process rights of all state residents;

(4) Foster community trust in local government and law enforcement.

SECTION 3. DEFINITIONS.

As used in this Act:

(a) "Civil immigration enforcement" means any activity undertaken to
investigate, apprehend, arrest, detain, or remove individuals for
violations of civil immigration law.

(b) "Immigration detainer" means a request from a federal immigration
agency to a state or local law enforcement agency to:

(1) Notify the federal agency of an individual's release date; or

(2) Hold an individual beyond the time they would otherwise be released.

(c) "Law enforcement agency" means any state or local agency or department
with the authority to enforce criminal laws.

(d) "Sensitive location" means:

(1) Schools, including prekindergarten through higher education;

(2) Healthcare facilities;

(3) Places of worship;

(4) Courthouses;

(5) Public assistance offices;

(6) Domestic violence shelters;

(7) Any location at which children are being cared for.

SECTION 4. LIMITATIONS ON COOPERATION WITH IMMIGRATION ENFORCEMENT.

(a) PROHIBITION ON DETAINERS.—No law enforcement agency shall:

(1) Detain any individual solely on the basis of an immigration detainer
after the individual is otherwise eligible for release; or

(2) Arrest any individual solely based on a civil immigration warrant.

(b) PROHIBITION ON INQUIRIES.—No state or local agency or employee shall:

(1) Inquire about an individual's immigration status for the purpose of
immigration enforcement;

(2) Condition provision of services on immigration status except as required
by federal law;

(3) Disclose information regarding an individual's immigration status to
federal immigration authorities except as required by federal law.

(c) PROHIBITION ON ENFORCEMENT ACTIVITIES.—No law enforcement agency or
officer shall:

(1) Perform any function of a federal immigration officer, including
investigation, apprehension, detention, or removal;

(2) Participate in any joint task force for the purpose of civil immigration
enforcement;

(3) Enter into any 287(g) agreement or similar agreement;

(4) Allow federal immigration authorities access to individuals in state or
local custody for immigration enforcement purposes, except pursuant to a
judicial warrant.

(d) PROHIBITION ON USE OF STATE RESOURCES.—No state or local resources,
including funds, personnel, equipment, or facilities, shall be used for
civil immigration enforcement activities.

SECTION 5. SENSITIVE LOCATIONS.

(a) PROHIBITION.—No state or local law enforcement agency shall assist
federal immigration authorities in enforcement activities at sensitive
locations.

(b) NOTIFICATION.—State and local agencies shall not provide notification
to federal immigration authorities of an individual's presence at a
sensitive location.

SECTION 6. NOTIFICATION OF RIGHTS.

(a) REQUIRED NOTICE.—Any individual arrested or detained by a state or local
law enforcement agency shall be notified:

(1) That they have the right to remain silent regarding their immigration
status;

(2) That they have the right to speak with an attorney before answering
any questions;

(3) That they have the right to contact their consulate;

(4) Of any request for information about them from federal immigration
authorities.

(b) FORMAT.—Notice shall be provided in writing and verbally in a language
the individual understands.

SECTION 7. DATA COLLECTION AND REPORTING.

(a) DATA COLLECTION.—Each law enforcement agency shall collect and maintain
data on:

(1) Immigration detainer requests received;

(2) Responses to detainer requests;

(3) Transfers to federal immigration custody.

(b) REPORTING.—Each law enforcement agency shall report data collected under
this section to the [Attorney General/designated state agency] annually.

(c) PUBLIC REPORTING.—The [Attorney General/designated state agency] shall
publish an annual report compiling data from all law enforcement agencies.

SECTION 8. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—Any individual whose rights under this Act
are violated may bring a civil action against the responsible agency for:

(1) Declaratory and injunctive relief;

(2) Actual damages;

(3) Attorney's fees and costs.

(b) STATE ENFORCEMENT.—The Attorney General may bring an action to enforce
this Act.

SECTION 9. SEVERABILITY.

If any provision of this Act is held invalid, the remaining provisions
shall remain in full force and effect.

SECTION 10. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 90 days after enactment.
```

## Model State Bill 2: State Immigration Detention Standards Act

### Purpose
To establish state standards for any immigration detention within the state's jurisdiction.

```
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Immigration Detention Standards Act."

SECTION 2. FINDINGS.

The Legislature finds that:

(a) Individuals held in immigration detention within this state are entitled
to humane treatment and conditions that meet minimum standards of decency.

(b) Reports have documented inadequate medical care, unsanitary conditions,
and abuse at immigration detention facilities.

(c) The state has a legitimate interest in regulating conditions within
facilities located in this state, regardless of whether the facilities are
operated by federal authorities, private companies, or local governments.

(d) Current federal standards are not legally enforceable and are inadequate
to protect detained individuals.

SECTION 3. DEFINITIONS.

As used in this Act:

(a) "Immigration detention facility" means any facility within this state
that holds individuals pursuant to civil immigration proceedings, including
facilities operated by private contractors and local jails holding
individuals under intergovernmental service agreements.

(b) "Detainee" means any individual held in an immigration detention facility.

SECTION 4. MINIMUM DETENTION STANDARDS.

(a) APPLICABILITY.—The standards established by this section apply to all
immigration detention facilities within this state.

(b) MEDICAL CARE.—Every immigration detention facility shall:

(1) Provide medical screening by a licensed healthcare provider within 12
hours of admission;

(2) Ensure access to 24-hour emergency medical care;

(3) Provide chronic disease management by appropriate specialists;

(4) Provide mental health services by licensed mental health professionals;

(5) Maintain medication continuity for all detainees;

(6) Provide dental care beyond emergency services;

(7) Provide reproductive healthcare for women.

(c) LIVING CONDITIONS.—Every immigration detention facility shall:

(1) Maintain occupancy below 80 percent of capacity;

(2) Provide minimum of 50 square feet of living space per detainee;

(3) Provide access to outdoor recreation for minimum of 2 hours daily;

(4) Maintain temperature between 68 and 76 degrees;

(5) Provide clean drinking water at all times;

(6) Provide nutritionally adequate meals;

(7) Provide clean bedding and hygiene supplies.

(d) SOLITARY CONFINEMENT.—

(1) No detainee shall be held in solitary confinement for more than 15
consecutive days or 20 days in any 60-day period;

(2) Vulnerable populations shall not be placed in solitary confinement;

(3) All solitary confinement placements shall be documented and reviewed
every 24 hours.

(e) COMMUNICATION.—Every immigration detention facility shall:

(1) Provide free telephone calls to legal counsel;

(2) Provide telephone access at reasonable rates;

(3) Allow in-person visitation;

(4) Provide mail access.

(f) LEGAL ACCESS.—Every immigration detention facility shall:

(1) Provide confidential attorney-client meeting spaces;

(2) Provide access to legal materials;

(3) Provide Know Your Rights presentations.

SECTION 5. STATE LICENSING.

(a) LICENSE REQUIRED.—No immigration detention facility may operate within
this state without a license from the [Department of Corrections/appropriate
agency].

(b) LICENSE CONDITIONS.—Licenses shall be conditioned on compliance with
all standards established by this Act.

(c) INSPECTIONS.—The licensing agency shall conduct unannounced inspections
of all licensed facilities at least quarterly.

(d) REVOCATION.—The licensing agency may revoke a facility's license for:

(1) Failure to comply with standards;

(2) Pattern of violations;

(3) Death or serious harm resulting from negligence.

SECTION 6. PROHIBITION ON NEW PRIVATE FACILITIES.

No new immigration detention facility operated by a private, for-profit
entity shall be licensed or permitted to operate in this state after the
effective date of this Act.

SECTION 7. REPORTING.

(a) FACILITY REPORTING.—Each immigration detention facility shall report
quarterly to the licensing agency:

(1) Population data;

(2) Deaths, hospitalizations, and serious incidents;

(3) Use of solitary confinement;

(4) Grievances filed and resolved;

(5) Medical staffing levels.

(b) PUBLIC REPORTING.—The licensing agency shall publish annual reports
on conditions in immigration detention facilities.

SECTION 8. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) CIVIL PENALTIES.—Violations of this Act may result in civil penalties
of up to $10,000 per violation per day.

(b) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—Any detainee may bring a civil action for
violations of this Act.

SECTION 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 180 days after enactment.
```

## Model State Bill 3: State Immigration Legal Defense Fund Act

### Purpose
To establish state-funded legal representation for immigrants facing removal.

```
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Immigration Legal Defense Fund Act."

SECTION 2. FINDINGS.

The Legislature finds that:

(a) Residents of this state facing immigration proceedings have no right to
appointed counsel, regardless of the severity of consequences.

(b) Approximately [X] percent of immigrants in this state facing removal
are unrepresented.

(c) Represented individuals are significantly more likely to obtain relief
and to appear for court hearings.

(d) The complexity of immigration law makes effective self-representation
nearly impossible.

(e) Deportation of state residents causes significant harm to families,
employers, and communities.

(f) Investment in legal representation is cost-effective compared to the
economic and social costs of deportation.

SECTION 3. ESTABLISHMENT OF FUND.

(a) CREATION.—There is hereby established the [State] Immigration Legal
Defense Fund within the [Office of the Governor/appropriate agency].

(b) PURPOSE.—The Fund shall provide legal representation to residents of
this state facing immigration removal proceedings who cannot afford counsel.

SECTION 4. ELIGIBILITY.

(a) ELIGIBLE INDIVIDUALS.—An individual is eligible for representation
through the Fund if they:

(1) Are a resident of this state;

(2) Are in immigration removal proceedings;

(3) Cannot afford legal representation as determined by income guidelines;

(4) Do not have representation from another source.

(b) PRIORITY CATEGORIES.—The Fund shall prioritize representation for:

(1) Detained individuals;

(2) Unaccompanied minors;

(3) Individuals with mental disabilities;

(4) Asylum seekers and individuals seeking protection;

(5) Survivors of trafficking, domestic violence, or serious crimes;

(6) Long-term residents with family in this state;

(7) Individuals facing deportation to countries with documented human
rights crises.

SECTION 5. ADMINISTRATION.

(a) FUND ADMINISTRATOR.—The [Governor/appropriate official] shall appoint
a Fund Administrator to oversee the program.

(b) LEGAL SERVICES PROVIDERS.—The Fund shall provide representation through:

(1) Contracts with nonprofit legal services organizations;

(2) Contracts with qualified private attorneys;

(3) Law school clinical programs;

(4) Public defender programs.

(c) STANDARDS.—The Administrator shall establish:

(1) Attorney qualification requirements;

(2) Caseload limits;

(3) Training requirements;

(4) Quality assurance measures;

(5) Client intake and eligibility processes.

SECTION 6. FUNDING.

(a) APPROPRIATION.—There is hereby appropriated $[X] for the Fund for fiscal
year [X].

(b) ADDITIONAL SOURCES.—The Fund may accept:

(1) Federal grants;

(2) Private donations;

(3) Cy pres awards from class action litigation.

SECTION 7. REPORTING.

(a) ANNUAL REPORT.—The Fund Administrator shall submit an annual report
to the Legislature including:

(1) Number of individuals represented;

(2) Case outcomes;

(3) Demographics of clients served;

(4) Costs per case;

(5) Funds expended and remaining.

(b) PUBLIC AVAILABILITY.—Annual reports shall be made publicly available.

SECTION 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect upon appropriation of funds.
```

# PART III: LOOPHOLES, SHORTCOMINGS, AND RECTIFICATION

## Analysis of Federal Legislation

### Bill 1: Dignity for Detained Immigrants Act

| Loophole/Shortcoming | Description | Potential Exploitation | Rectification |
|---------------------|-------------|----------------------|---------------|
| Enforcement delay | 180-day effective date allows continued violations | Facilities may rush problematic practices before standards take effect | Add provision for immediate emergency standards; require facilities to demonstrate compliance plan within 30 days |
| Private right of action barriers | Detainees may lack resources to pursue litigation | Companies may continue violations knowing suits are unlikely | Establish pro bono legal fund; allow fee-shifting; permit class actions |
| OIDO independence vulnerable | Director can be removed "for cause" | Political pressure through creative "cause" findings | Require Senate confirmation for removal; define "cause" explicitly |
| Standards subject to rulemaking | Secretary has discretion in promulgating regulations | Weak regulations may be issued | Establish statutory minimums; require notice and comment with stakeholder participation |
| No automatic contract termination | Contracts continue despite violations | Profit continues during litigation | Add automatic suspension/termination for serious violations |
| $50 million authorization may be insufficient | Underfunded oversight is ineffective | Inadequate inspections and monitoring | Index to detention population; mandate minimum inspector ratios |
| No individual liability | Only facilities/contractors liable | Personnel may act with impunity | Add personal liability for gross violations; require reporting of individual misconduct |

### Bill 2: Fairness to Freedom Act

| Loophole/Shortcoming | Description | Potential Exploitation | Rectification |
|---------------------|-------------|----------------------|---------------|
| "Clear and convincing" standard may be weakened | Judges may interpret standard inconsistently | Continued high detention rates in some jurisdictions | Provide explicit guidance on evidence required; mandate training for judges |
| Risk assessment tools can embed bias | Tools may discriminate by race, national origin | Disparate impact on certain communities | Require bias audits; prohibit use of factors correlating with protected classes |
| 6-month maximum detention has exceptions | Terrorism exception may be broadly interpreted | "Terrorism" label applied broadly | Narrow terrorism exception; require specific evidence; provide for judicial review |
| ATD funding not mandated | "Not less than 50%" can be gamed | Accounting tricks reduce actual ATD spending | Require separate line item; annual reporting on ATD vs. detention spending |
| Electronic monitoring default | Preference for community supervision may not be enforced | Ankle monitors remain prevalent | Require documented justification for electronic monitoring; cap usage percentage |
| No damages for wrongful detention | Individuals detained in violation have no remedy | Government may violate standards without consequence | Add compensatory damages; fee-shifting for prevailing parties |
| Bond affordability assessment subjective | What is "affordable" may vary widely | High bonds continue for some | Establish objective standards; cap bonds at percentage of income; require unsecured options |

### Bill 3: End Private Immigration Detention Act

| Loophole/Shortcoming | Description | Potential Exploitation | Rectification |
|---------------------|-------------|----------------------|---------------|
| 5-year phase-out too long | Harm continues for years | Private companies maximize profit before shutdown | Accelerate timeline to 3 years; immediate closure of worst facilities |
| No prohibition on government subcontracting | Government facilities may subcontract services | Private profit continues through service contracts | Prohibit subcontracting of core functions; cap service contracts |
| Lobbying disclosure may not prevent influence | Disclosure doesn't equal prohibition | Companies continue lobbying while disclosing | Add cooling-off periods; ban contributions from companies with recent contracts |
| Transition funds may be diverted | No guarantee funds go to ATD | Funds may be spent on government-operated detention | Mandate specific percentages to community programs; prohibit new government detention construction |
| No prohibition on private ATD | GEO subsidiary operates ISAP | Private profit shifts to alternatives | Include ATD in private prohibition; require nonprofit/government ATD operation |
| Worker transition assistance unfunded | Authorization without appropriation | Workers left without support | Include mandatory appropriation; tie to contract savings |
| "Private entity" definition may have gaps | Complex corporate structures may evade | Shell companies, subsidiaries may continue | Expand definition to include affiliates, subsidiaries, joint ventures |

### Bill 4: Access to Counsel in Immigration Proceedings Act

| Loophole/Shortcoming | Description | Potential Exploitation | Rectification |
|---------------------|-------------|----------------------|---------------|
| 3-year phase-in delays universal access | Many remain unrepresented during phase-in | Expedited removal continues without counsel | Accelerate phase-in; immediate access for detained and asylum seekers |
| $1.5 billion may be insufficient | Underfunding leads to inadequate representation | High caseloads, poor quality counsel | Index funding to caseload; require caseload limits as condition |
| Provider standards set by Attorney General | Standards may be weak or politically influenced | Poor quality representation | Establish statutory minimum standards; independent oversight body |
| No requirement for continuity | Cases may be transferred between providers | Disrupted representation | Require single attorney/team per case unless client consents |
| Geographic disparities possible | Urban areas may have better access | Rural and detention facility clients underserved | Mandate geographic distribution; remote representation options |
| No explicit interpreter requirement | Language barriers persist | Ineffective representation for non-English speakers | Add explicit interpreter requirements; fund interpreter services |
| Detained individuals may not be informed | Notification may be inadequate | Detainees don't know they have rights | Require written and verbal notification in native language; verification of understanding |

### Bill 5: Immigration Court Independence Act

| Loophole/Shortcoming | Description | Potential Exploitation | Rectification |
|---------------------|-------------|----------------------|---------------|
| 15-year terms may not ensure independence | Reappointment possibility may influence decisions | Judges may rule to curry favor | Single non-renewable terms; lifetime appointment option |
| Senate confirmation may politicize appointments | Partisan gridlock may block appointments | Court understaffed due to delays | Allow recess appointments; establish supermajority requirement to reject |
| 3-year transition too long | EOIR dysfunction continues during transition | Backlog grows; political influence continues | Accelerate to 18-month transition; immediate transfer of new judges |
| No mandatory funding | Court dependent on annual appropriations | Underfunding undermines independence | Establish permanent appropriation; index to caseload |
| Current judges may continue | Poor performers grandfathered in | Problematic judges retained | Require performance review; qualification assessment for all applicants |
| No diversity requirements | Bench may not reflect communities served | Homogeneous perspectives | Require diversity in appointments; community input on nominations |
| Appeals to circuit courts may create inconsistency | Different circuits rule differently | Forum shopping; unequal outcomes | Consider specialized appellate court; promote uniformity through Supreme Court review |
