# Wrongful Convictions: Stakeholders

## Primary Stakeholders

### The Wrongfully Convicted

**Current Situation**:

- Hundreds in prison for crimes they didn't commit
- Thousands previously exonerated
- Ongoing trauma and struggle
- Inadequate support post-exoneration
- Powerful voices for reform

**Stakes**:

- Freedom
- Vindication
- Compensation
- Rebuilding life
- Preventing future cases

**Advocacy Capacity**:

- Growing exoneree advocacy
- Witness for Innocence
- Healing Justice Project
- Powerful personal testimony
- Increasing media attention

### Crime Victims

**Complexity**:

- Victims in original case often support reform after exoneration
- Some resist acknowledging error
- Real victims still seek justice
- Revictimization when case reopened

**Stakes**:

- True justice (right person held accountable)
- Closure
- Safety (real perpetrator found)
- Not being used to convict innocent

### Actual Perpetrators' Victims

**Often Overlooked**:

- While innocent person was convicted, real perpetrator was free
- Many committed additional crimes
- 150+ additional violent crimes by real perpetrators in DNA cases
- These victims also suffer from wrongful conviction

## Justice System Actors

### Prosecutors

**Role in Both Causing and Preventing**:

- Misconduct causes wrongful convictions
- Conviction integrity units can prevent and remedy
- Significant discretion at all stages
- Can resist or embrace reform

**Stakes**:

| Interest | Concern |
|----------|---------|
| Conviction integrity | Accuracy over winning |
| Office reputation | Credibility |
| Case finality | Don't reopen old cases |
| Accountability | Personal consequences |

### Defense Attorneys

**Role**:

- Quality defense prevents wrongful convictions
- Post-conviction representation essential
- Often resource-limited
- Innocence projects fill gap

**Stakes**:

- Client outcomes
- Resources to investigate
- Access to testing
- Ability to prove innocence

### Judges

**Role**:

- Rule on evidence at trial
- Decide post-conviction claims
- Can facilitate or obstruct review
- Set evidentiary standards

**Stakes**:

- Finality of judgments
- System credibility
- Justice
- Workload

### Police

**Role**:

- Investigation quality determines outcomes
- Procedures affect accuracy
- Training matters
- Culture affects behavior

**Stakes**:

- Case clearance
- Reputation
- Accountability
- Relationship with prosecutors

## Innocence Organizations

### Innocence Projects

**National Organizations**:

| Organization | Role |
|--------------|------|
| Innocence Project | Largest, pioneering |
| Innocence Project Network | 65+ affiliates |
| Centurion | First innocence organization |
| Last Resort Exoneration Project | Non-DNA focus |

**Local/State Projects**:

- Most law schools have projects
- Limited capacity
- Thousands of applications
- Rigorous case selection

### Advocacy Organizations

| Organization | Focus |
|--------------|-------|
| Innocence Project Policy | Federal/state policy |
| Witness to Innocence | Death row exonerees |
| National Registry of Exonerations | Data, research |
| Death Penalty Information Center | Capital cases |

## Government Actors

### State Legislators

**Role**:

- Create post-conviction review processes
- Establish compensation laws
- Fund innocence investigation
- Reform procedures

**Stakes**:

- Constituent concerns
- Fiscal impact
- Law enforcement support
- Justice

### Federal Government

**Role**:

- DOJ oversight of forensics
- Funding for DNA testing
- Civil rights enforcement
- Model legislation

**Current Position**: Varies by administration; generally supportive of reform

### Conviction Integrity Units

**Role**:

- Review questionable convictions
- Partner with innocence organizations
- Reform internal practices
- Independent review

**Stakes**:

- Office credibility
- Justice
- Resources
- Political support

**Role**:
