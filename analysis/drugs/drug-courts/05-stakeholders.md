# Drug Courts: Stakeholders

The drug court system is shaped by the interactions of several key groups, each with its own interests, power, and resources. Unlike traditional criminal courts, drug courts require collaboration among actors who are typically adversaries, creating a unique stakeholder dynamic that is both a strength and a source of tension.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Drug court participants | Direct recipients of court-supervised treatment; face sanctions for noncompliance and charge dismissal or reduction upon graduation | ~150,000 annually (NADCP, 2023) | Access to treatment, fair sanctions, charge dismissal, avoiding incarceration |
| Family members of participants | Indirectly affected by participant's recovery or failure; may bear financial and caregiving burdens | ~400,000-600,000 (estimated 3-4 family members per participant) | Participant recovery, family stability, reduced family disruption |
| Eligible defendants not served | Approximately 90% of those who could benefit lack access to drug courts | ~1.35 million annually (SAMHSA/NADCP estimates, 2023) | Access to treatment alternatives, avoiding incarceration for addiction |
| Communities affected by drug-related crime | Neighborhoods experiencing drug-related crime, disorder, and public health consequences | Tens of millions in high-impact communities | Reduced crime, public safety, community health |
| Victims of drug-related offenses | Individuals harmed by crimes committed by people with substance use disorders | Varies by jurisdiction | Restitution, safety, reduced recidivism |

### Secondary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Employers | Drug court participants who achieve recovery return to the workforce; SUD costs employers $81 billion annually in lost productivity (NIDA, 2023) | Affected employers nationally | Workforce productivity, reduced absenteeism, recovery-friendly hiring |
| Taxpayers | Drug courts cost $5,000-$11,000 per participant but generate $2.21-$27 per dollar invested (WSIPP, 2018; NPC Research, 2019) | All taxpayers in jurisdictions with drug courts | Cost-effective use of public funds, public safety |
| Children of participants | Family treatment courts address child welfare; 8.7 million children have a parent with SUD (SAMHSA, 2023); 36% of foster care entries involve parental SUD (AFCARS, 2022) | ~8.7 million children nationally | Family stability, reduced foster care, parental recovery |
| Treatment workforce | Substance abuse counselors, social workers, and clinical supervisors providing treatment; faces 250,000-300,000 professional shortage (SAMHSA, 2023) | ~400,000 SUD treatment professionals nationally | Professional standards, adequate compensation, manageable caseloads |
| Insurance companies and Medicaid | Payers for treatment services; Medicaid covers SUD treatment in all 50 states; drug court treatment reduces long-term healthcare costs | National health insurance market | Cost containment, treatment efficacy, appropriate utilization |
| Law enforcement agencies | Police departments that make drug arrests feeding drug court referrals; may also operate pre-arrest diversion programs | ~18,000 law enforcement agencies nationally | Maintaining arrest authority, managing drug-related calls, community safety |

### Specialized Court Population Stakeholders

- **Military veterans and veteran service organizations**: Veterans treatment courts serve approximately 8,000 participants annually. Organizations including the Veterans of Foreign Wars (VFW), American Legion, Iraq and Afghanistan Veterans of America (IAVA), and Disabled American Veterans (DAV) advocate for VTC expansion. The VA provides healthcare coordination and benefits navigation. Veterans with SUD frequently also have PTSD (55%) and traumatic brain injury (30%), requiring integrated treatment approaches (VA/SAMHSA, 2022).
- **Tribal nations and Native American communities**: Approximately 135 tribal healing-to-wellness courts serve Native American communities with the highest overdose death rates in the country (56.1 per 100,000, CDC WONDER, 2024). The Tribal Law and Policy Institute provides technical assistance. Sovereignty considerations, cultural integration, and the chronic underfunding of the Indian Health Service are central concerns.
- **Parents in child welfare proceedings**: Family dependency treatment courts serve approximately 15,000 parents annually. Child welfare agencies, foster care providers, and family courts are all affected by FDTC outcomes. Successful parental treatment can prevent foster care placement, which costs $25,000-$35,000 per child per year (Child Trends, 2022).
- **DWI/DUI offenders and road safety organizations**: DWI courts serve approximately 10,000 participants annually. Mothers Against Drunk Driving (MADD) and other road safety organizations have complicated relationships with DWI courts -- supporting treatment for addiction but wary of any approach perceived as lenient toward impaired driving.

### Key Roles within Drug Court Teams

Drug courts are built around multidisciplinary teams that include professionals from multiple agencies. The dynamics within these teams shape day-to-day court operations:

- **Judge**: Sets the tone and pace of the court; conducts status hearings; makes sanction and incentive decisions; interacts directly with participants. The MADCE found that judicial engagement quality is the single strongest predictor of participant outcomes. Drug court judges typically spend 3-5 minutes per participant per status hearing (Carey et al., 2012).
- **Drug court coordinator**: Manages day-to-day operations; coordinates between team members; manages data systems; handles administrative functions. Often the most stable member of the team, providing institutional continuity when judges or attorneys rotate.
- **Prosecutor (on team)**: Reviews compliance reports; recommends sanctions; advises on legal issues; may serve as gatekeeper for new referrals. The prosecutor's role is fundamentally different from traditional adversarial prosecution -- team-based rather than adversarial.
- **Defense attorney (on team)**: Advocates for participant interests within the team framework; advises participants on legal rights; may challenge proposed sanctions. Defense attorneys face ethical tension between zealous advocacy and the collaborative team model.
- **Treatment provider representative**: Reports on participant progress; recommends treatment modifications; provides clinical perspective on sanctions and incentives.
- **Probation/supervision officer**: Monitors compliance with court conditions; conducts home visits; coordinates with law enforcement.
- **Peer recovery specialist**: Increasingly integrated into drug court teams, providing lived-experience support, mentoring, and community navigation. The VA's peer specialist model in veterans treatment courts has been particularly effective.

### Influencers

- **National Association of Criminal Defense Lawyers (NACDL)**: Has expressed concerns about due process protections in drug courts and the waiver of rights required for participation. Advocates for defense counsel to play a more active advocacy role. NACDL published a report in 2009 ("America's Problem-Solving Courts") raising concerns about the erosion of adversarial protections in drug court settings.
- **Drug Policy Alliance (DPA)**: Advocates for decriminalization and harm reduction rather than court-based treatment mandates. While not opposed to drug courts per se, DPA argues they should be replaced by voluntary, community-based treatment and pre-arrest diversion programs. DPA's annual budget exceeds $20 million (2023), and its advocacy has influenced state-level decriminalization efforts that directly affect drug court referral volumes.
- **American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU)**: Monitors drug court operations for civil liberties concerns, particularly regarding coerced treatment, religious programming (12-step requirements), and disparate impact on minority defendants. ACLU litigation has challenged mandatory 12-step participation in government-ordered programs on First Amendment grounds (e.g., *Inouye v. Kemna*, 504 F.3d 705, 9th Cir. 2007).
- **Heritage Foundation / Manhattan Institute / Right on Crime**: Conservative think tanks that support drug courts as a cost-effective, accountability-focused alternative to incarceration. Right on Crime, a project of the Texas Public Policy Foundation, has been particularly influential in building conservative support for drug courts and other diversion programs. Their endorsement has been critical for bipartisan political support and helped pass drug court legislation in Republican-controlled states.
- **Addiction medicine professional organizations**: American Society of Addiction Medicine (ASAM) and the American Academy of Addiction Psychiatry (AAAP) advocate for MAT access and evidence-based treatment standards in drug courts. ASAM's position paper on drug courts (2021) called for mandatory MAT access and recommended that treatment decisions in drug courts be made by clinicians rather than judges.
- **Faces & Voices of Recovery**: A national advocacy organization representing people in recovery from substance use disorders. Supports drug court reform to improve participant experience, reduce punitive approaches, and incorporate peer recovery support services.
- **National Council of Juvenile and Family Court Judges (NCJFCJ)**: Provides training and technical assistance for family drug courts and juvenile drug courts. Has published guidelines on integrating substance use treatment with child welfare proceedings.

### Stakeholder Engagement Opportunities

Several emerging opportunities exist for strengthening stakeholder coalitions:

- **Peer recovery support**: The growing peer recovery workforce (people with lived experience of SUD who provide support services) offers a bridge between participant perspectives and professional stakeholder interests. Integrating peer specialists into drug court teams improves outcomes and ensures lived experience informs program design.
- **Business community engagement**: Employers affected by SUD-related workforce losses are potential allies for drug court funding advocacy. The National Safety Council estimates that employer costs from substance use disorders exceed $81 billion annually (2023). Recovery-friendly workplace initiatives can be linked to drug court employment services.
- **Health system partnerships**: As drug courts develop formal partnerships with federally qualified health centers (FQHCs) and community mental health centers, healthcare system stakeholders become invested in drug court success. These partnerships create shared interest in Medicaid reimbursement for court-ordered treatment and care transition protocols.
- **Academic partnerships**: University research partnerships provide drug courts with independent evaluation capacity while giving researchers access to data. The Treatment Research Institute, NPC Research, and university-based criminal justice research centers have established productive collaborations with drug courts nationwide.

### Conflicting Interests

- **Prosecutors vs. Defense Attorneys**: Prosecutors want to control who enters drug court (gatekeeping authority), while defense attorneys want broader eligibility and stronger due process protections. This tension shapes the fundamental design of drug court programs. In most jurisdictions, prosecutors retain veto power over individual referrals, allowing political and discretionary factors to override clinical screening. Defense attorneys argue this system gives prosecutors undue influence over who accesses treatment.

- **Drug Court Advocates vs. Decriminalization Advocates**: NADCP and the drug court establishment view drug courts as a proven model that should be expanded. Organizations like the Drug Policy Alliance view drug courts as an inadequate intermediate step that should eventually be replaced by voluntary community-based treatment accessible without criminal justice involvement. This conflict intensified following Oregon's Measure 110 (2020) and its partial reversal (2024), which tested whether a non-criminal-justice model could work at scale.

- **Abstinence-Based Treatment Providers vs. MAT Advocates**: Some treatment providers that contract with drug courts are philosophically committed to abstinence-only approaches and resist the shift toward medication-assisted treatment. Addiction medicine organizations view MAT restrictions as clinically negligent. This conflict has economic dimensions: abstinence-based providers may lose contracts and revenue if courts require MAT-capable providers.

- **Judges vs. Standardization Advocates**: Drug court judges value their discretion to tailor programs to local conditions and individual participants. Researchers and standard-setting bodies push for greater standardization based on evidence, which some judges view as infringing on judicial independence. The Conference of Chief Justices has been cautious about endorsing mandatory standards, preferring voluntary best practice guidelines.

- **Participants vs. Court Authority**: Participants want treatment and charge dismissal with minimal court intrusion. Courts impose extensive monitoring, testing, and compliance requirements. The balance between supervision and autonomy is a source of ongoing tension. Participants who receive jail sanctions for relapse may view the court as punitive rather than therapeutic, undermining the treatment relationship.

- **Federal Funders vs. Local Autonomy**: BJA and SAMHSA use grant conditions to promote evidence-based practices (e.g., MAT access, equity reporting), but local courts resist what they perceive as federal overreach. This tension between federal quality standards and local judicial independence is a recurring theme in drug court policy.

- **Child Welfare vs. Criminal Justice Orientation**: In family drug courts, the child welfare system's priority (child safety and permanency) may conflict with the drug court model's priority (participant recovery). Judges must balance the timeline for parental recovery with the child's developmental need for permanency, creating tension between Adoption and Safe Families Act timelines (15 of 22 months) and drug court program durations (12-24 months).

### The Drug Court Iron Triangle

Drug courts operate within a self-reinforcing power structure:

1. **NADCP** sets standards, provides training, and lobbies Congress for funding
2. **BJA** distributes federal grants based on NADCP-influenced criteria
3. **Local drug courts** apply for and receive grants, implement NADCP-endorsed practices, and generate outcome data that NADCP uses to justify continued funding

This triangle has been effective at sustaining and gradually improving drug courts but has also created resistance to structural reforms that would fundamentally alter the model -- such as replacing court-based treatment mandates with pre-arrest diversion or community-based voluntary treatment.

### The Treatment Provider Network

A secondary power structure exists around the treatment provider ecosystem:

1. **Treatment providers** contract with drug courts to deliver services, creating a revenue stream dependent on court referrals
2. **Drug testing companies** provide mandatory testing services, generating approximately $2 billion annually from court-mandated testing
3. **Private probation and monitoring companies** provide supervision services, generating approximately $1.2 billion annually
4. **Electronic monitoring vendors** supply GPS and smartphone-based tracking technology

These commercial interests benefit from the continuation and expansion of the drug court model in its current form. Reforms that would reduce testing frequency, shorten program duration, or shift to pre-arrest diversion models that bypass court supervision directly threaten these revenue streams.

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on drug court policy:

- **Lobbying**: NADCP maintains a Washington, D.C. office and conducts annual legislative advocacy. The organization's "National Drug Court Month" campaign (May) generates constituent contacts to Congress. NADCP spent approximately $320,000 on lobbying in 2023 (OpenSecrets, 2024). NADCP's annual advocacy conference brings hundreds of drug court professionals to Capitol Hill for meetings with congressional offices.
- **Campaign Contributions**: Drug court stakeholders do not make significant direct campaign contributions, but the broader criminal justice reform coalition -- including organizations like the Charles Koch Institute, ACLU, and Right on Crime -- has spent tens of millions supporting reform-minded candidates. Police unions, which may oppose diversion programs, spent $6.5 million in the 2022 federal cycle (OpenSecrets, 2023).
- **Revolving Door**: Former drug court judges, prosecutors, and coordinators frequently join NADCP, NDCI, or consulting firms that provide technical assistance to drug courts, creating a professional network that reinforces the current model. This revolving door provides institutional continuity but may also create resistance to fundamental structural reforms that would alter the existing professional ecosystem.
- **Public Advocacy**: Drug court graduation ceremonies are effective public relations events, often attended by local media and elected officials. Participant testimonials are among the most powerful advocacy tools for drug court funding. NADCP's "All Rise" campaign uses participant stories to build public and political support. Graduation ceremonies serve a dual function: therapeutic (affirming participants' achievement) and political (demonstrating the model's success to decision-makers).
- **Expertise/Information**: NADCP and NDCRC control the primary data systems, training curricula, and research dissemination channels for the drug court field. This information monopoly gives them significant influence over how drug courts are understood by policymakers and the public. Academic researchers (e.g., at the Urban Institute, RAND, NPC Research) provide independent evaluation but depend on access to court data that NADCP-affiliated organizations facilitate.
- **Legal Advocacy**: The ACLU, NACDL, and legal aid organizations use litigation and policy briefs to influence drug court operations. First Amendment challenges to mandatory 12-step participation, Eighth Amendment challenges to MAT denial, and equal protection challenges to racially disparate referral practices have all shaped drug court policy.
- **Media Coverage**: Investigative journalism by outlets including The Marshall Project, ProPublica, and Filter Magazine has brought critical scrutiny to drug court practices, particularly regarding racial disparities, MAT restrictions, and the use of jail sanctions. Positive media coverage, conversely, often focuses on individual success stories and graduation ceremonies.

## Power Dynamics and Access Disparities

The stakeholder landscape reflects fundamental power asymmetries:

- **Who has power vs. who is affected**: The populations most affected by drug court policy -- participants, their families, communities with high arrest rates -- have the least formal power over drug court design and operations. Judges, prosecutors, and NADCP have the most power but are structurally insulated from the consequences of their decisions.
- **Professional vs. lived experience**: Drug court policy is overwhelmingly shaped by credentialed professionals (judges, attorneys, researchers, clinical providers). The voice of people with lived experience of substance use disorders, criminal justice involvement, and drug court participation is largely absent from policy development, despite growing recognition that peer input improves program design.
- **Urban vs. rural**: Drug court advocacy organizations, research institutions, and funding bodies are concentrated in urban areas. Rural communities, which face some of the most severe substance use burdens, have limited representation in policy discussions and less access to the technical assistance and grant-writing capacity needed to establish and maintain drug courts.
- **Tribal sovereignty and self-determination**: Tribal healing-to-wellness courts operate under tribal law and reflect tribal values, yet federal funding criteria and NADCP standards are designed primarily for non-tribal courts. The Tribal Law and Policy Institute advocates for approaches that respect tribal sovereignty while enabling access to federal resources.
- **Gender-specific needs**: Women constitute approximately 35% of drug court participants (NADCP National Survey, 2022) and face distinct challenges including higher rates of trauma history, childcare responsibilities that conflict with court attendance, and different patterns of substance use initiation (often through intimate partner relationships). Gender-responsive programming, which addresses these specific needs, is available in fewer than 30% of drug courts (NPC Research, 2021).

## The Participant Perspective

Drug court participants are the most directly affected stakeholder group yet have the least formal influence over drug court policy and design. Understanding the participant experience is essential for assessing stakeholder dynamics:

### Participant Experience: What Research Shows

- **Perceived fairness matters**: Research by Tyler (2006) and others on procedural justice shows that participants' perception of fairness -- whether they feel heard, treated with respect, and subject to consistent rules -- is a stronger predictor of compliance than the severity of sanctions. Drug courts that prioritize procedural justice have better retention and graduation rates.
- **Judicial interaction is valued**: Participants consistently report that direct interaction with the judge -- being recognized, praised, held accountable by name -- is the most meaningful aspect of the drug court experience (Goldkamp et al., 2001). This personal recognition distinguishes drug courts from the impersonal processing of traditional courts.
- **Sanctions can be experienced as punishment**: Participants who receive jail sanctions for positive drug tests may perceive the court as punitive despite its therapeutic intent. This perception can undermine the treatment relationship and increase dropout risk. Best practice courts use jail sanctions sparingly and only after other graduated responses have been exhausted.
- **MAT access is a life-or-death concern**: For participants with opioid use disorder, access to medication is not a philosophical question but a survival issue. Participants denied MAT in drug courts face significantly higher overdose risk, particularly upon relapse after a period of enforced abstinence.
- **Collateral consequences persist**: Even successful drug court graduates may face collateral consequences from their arrest records, including employment barriers, housing discrimination, and professional licensing restrictions. Expungement provisions vary widely by jurisdiction.
