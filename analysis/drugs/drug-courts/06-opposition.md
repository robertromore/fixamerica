# Drug Courts: Opposition to Reform

Proposals to reform drug courts face resistance from two distinct directions: those who believe drug courts are already too lenient and those who believe drug courts do not go far enough in removing substance use from the criminal justice framework. This creates an unusual political dynamic in which the drug court model must defend itself against critics on both flanks.

### Ideological Opposition

- **Law-and-Order Conservative**: Drug courts are "soft on crime." Diversion programs reduce the deterrent effect of criminal sanctions. Drug offenders need punishment, not coddling. Expanded eligibility would let dangerous offenders avoid accountability. This perspective is strongest in jurisdictions where crime rates are high and public safety concerns dominate political discourse. It is amplified during election seasons, when candidates may attack drug court support as evidence of being "soft on crime."

- **Libertarian**: The government should not compel treatment. Drug courts violate individual autonomy by conditioning freedom on compliance with state-mandated programs. The better approach is full decriminalization and allowing individuals to make their own healthcare choices without government coercion. Some libertarian critiques extend to the drug testing and monitoring apparatus of drug courts as an expansion of state surveillance. The Cato Institute has published analyses questioning whether government-supervised treatment represents an appropriate use of judicial authority.

- **Progressive/Abolitionist**: Drug courts extend criminal justice control over people who should never have been arrested in the first place. They medicalize a social problem while maintaining a punitive framework. The correct response is decriminalization, voluntary treatment, and investment in social determinants of health (housing, employment, education). Some progressive critics argue that drug courts function as "net-widening" mechanisms, subjecting people to more intensive supervision than they would have received through traditional processing. The Drug Policy Alliance's position paper on drug courts (2021) argues that the model "perpetuates the myth that the criminal justice system is an appropriate vehicle for addressing public health problems."

- **Religious Right (selective)**: Some faith-based organizations oppose MAT requirements as conflicting with abstinence-based spiritual recovery programs (particularly those rooted in 12-step traditions). Others support drug courts as redemptive but resist secularization of treatment components. The National Association of Evangelicals has not taken a formal position on drug courts, but individual megachurches and faith-based treatment networks (e.g., Teen Challenge) have lobbied against MAT mandates at the state level, arguing that they infringe on religious freedom in program design.

- **Public Health Purists**: Some public health professionals argue that embedding treatment within the criminal justice system -- even through the relatively benign drug court model -- normalizes criminal justice involvement in healthcare delivery and diverts resources from community-based public health infrastructure. This perspective holds that every dollar spent on drug court operations would be better invested in prevention, community mental health centers, and voluntary treatment programs accessible without any criminal justice contact.

## Opposition Arguments and Rebuttals

### Argument 1: Drug Courts Are Soft on Crime

**Claim**: Drug courts let drug offenders avoid punishment. By diverting defendants into treatment instead of jail or prison, drug courts undermine deterrence and send the message that drug crimes have no consequences.

**Evidence Cited**: Individual anecdotes of drug court participants who reoffend; cases where drug court graduates commit subsequent crimes; comparisons of drug court sentences (treatment) to traditional sentences (incarceration).

**Reality**: Drug courts impose extensive requirements -- 12-24 months of supervision, regular drug testing, mandatory treatment attendance, community service, and the threat of incarceration for noncompliance. Participants who fail are returned to traditional prosecution and often receive longer sentences than they would have originally. The MADCE (2012) found that drug court participants spent an average of 11 more days in court-ordered activities than comparison defendants -- drug court is not an easy alternative.

**Rebuttal**: The question is not whether drug courts impose consequences -- they do -- but whether those consequences produce better outcomes. The evidence is clear: drug court participants have a 12 percentage point lower recidivism rate than traditionally processed defendants (40% vs. 52%, MADCE, 2012). Drug courts reduce future crime more effectively than incarceration, which means they make communities safer, not less safe.

### Argument 2: Expanding Eligibility Endangers Public Safety

**Claim**: Allowing individuals with violent offense histories, serious criminal records, or high-risk profiles into drug courts puts communities at risk. Drug courts were designed for low-level, nonviolent offenders and should stay that way.

**Evidence Cited**: Media coverage of crimes committed by defendants on diversion; general public concern about drug-related violence; prosecutor resistance to expanding eligibility.

**Reality**: The MADCE specifically found that drug courts were *most effective* for high-risk/high-need participants. Research published in *Drug Court Review* (Marlowe et al., 2016) demonstrated that restricting drug courts to low-risk defendants actually reduces their cost-effectiveness, because low-risk defendants are unlikely to reoffend regardless of intervention. Jurisdictions that have expanded eligibility to include moderate- and high-risk defendants (e.g., Tulsa County, OK; Hamilton County, OH) have achieved comparable or better outcomes without increased safety incidents.

**Rebuttal**: Evidence-based risk assessment, not categorical exclusion, should guide eligibility. The NADCP Best Practice Standards recommend that drug courts target high-risk/high-need individuals because that is where the model produces the greatest impact. Categorical exclusions based on offense type sacrifice effectiveness for political comfort.

### Argument 3: Drug Courts Extend Criminal Justice Control Over Healthcare

**Claim**: Drug courts perpetuate the criminalization of addiction by requiring arrest and prosecution as prerequisites for treatment. They expand the reach of the criminal justice system into healthcare, using coercion rather than clinical judgment to drive treatment decisions. The correct approach is decriminalization and voluntary, community-based treatment.

**Evidence Cited**: The requirement for criminal charges before drug court entry; coerced guilty pleas; the power imbalance between judges and participants; racial disparities in the criminal justice system that feed drug courts; Oregon Measure 110 as an alternative model.

**Reality**: This critique has substantial merit. Drug courts do require criminal justice contact as a prerequisite for treatment, and this requirement imposes collateral consequences (arrest records, stigma) that a pure public health model would avoid. However, the critique underestimates two practical realities: (1) in the current system, drug courts represent the best available option for defendants who are already in the criminal justice system, and (2) the supervised structure of drug courts produces better treatment retention and completion rates than purely voluntary programs. The average retention rate for voluntary outpatient treatment is approximately 40% at 90 days (SAMHSA TEDS, 2023), compared to 72% at 12 months for drug court participants (NPC Research, 2023).

**Rebuttal**: Drug courts and community-based voluntary treatment are not mutually exclusive. The optimal system includes robust voluntary treatment available before criminal justice contact AND high-quality drug courts for those already in the system. Eliminating drug courts without building adequate community-based alternatives would leave hundreds of thousands of defendants with no treatment pathway. Reform should expand voluntary treatment access while simultaneously improving drug courts for those who need them.

### Argument 4: Drug Courts Perpetuate Racial Disparities

**Claim**: Because drug courts depend on the criminal justice system for referrals, they inherit and reproduce the racial disparities embedded in arrest, prosecution, and plea bargaining. Black and Hispanic defendants are less likely to be referred, less likely to be accepted, and less likely to graduate. Expanding drug courts without addressing upstream racial bias amplifies these disparities.

**Evidence Cited**: Documented disparities in referral, acceptance, and graduation rates (e.g., 64% white graduation vs. 52% Black graduation, NADCP National Survey, 2022); four-to-one racial disparity in drug arrests (ACLU, 2020); prosecutor-controlled referral as a mechanism for racial filtering.

**Reality**: These disparities are real and well-documented. However, eliminating drug courts would not eliminate racial disparities in the criminal justice system -- it would simply return minority defendants to a traditional system that is equally or more biased and offers no treatment alternative. The solution is to reform drug courts to reduce disparities while simultaneously addressing upstream racial bias in arrest and prosecution.

**Rebuttal**: Racial equity should be a mandatory component of drug court design, not a reason to eliminate them. Specific reforms include: removing prosecutor-controlled referral in favor of clinical screening at booking, adopting culturally responsive treatment models, requiring courts to monitor and report outcomes by race, and implementing implicit bias training for all drug court team members. The NADCP Best Practice Standards address equity, but compliance is voluntary and uneven.

### Argument 5: Coerced Treatment Violates Individual Autonomy

**Claim**: People should not be forced to undergo medical treatment as a condition of avoiding incarceration. Coerced treatment undermines patient autonomy, violates the therapeutic relationship, and is ethically incompatible with informed consent. Treatment works best when it is voluntary.

**Evidence Cited**: Medical ethics principles of autonomy and informed consent; ABA Standards on Treatment Courts; studies showing equivalent outcomes for voluntary vs. coerced treatment in some settings.

**Reality**: The ethical concern is legitimate but must be balanced against practical realities. Research on coerced treatment shows mixed but generally positive results. A meta-analysis by Parhar et al. (2008) found that mandated treatment was associated with significant reductions in recidivism, though voluntary treatment may produce slightly larger effects per participant. The National Institute on Drug Abuse (NIDA) states that "treatment does not need to be voluntary to be effective" and notes that legal pressure can increase treatment retention.

**Rebuttal**: In the current system, the alternative to coerced treatment is not voluntary treatment -- it is no treatment at all, followed by incarceration. Until voluntary treatment is universally accessible and adequately funded, drug courts provide a pathway to treatment that would otherwise not exist for most participants. The ethical framework should focus on ensuring that coerced treatment is clinically appropriate, evidence-based, and delivered with maximum respect for participant dignity.

### Argument 6: MAT Mandates Violate Treatment Provider Autonomy

**Claim**: Requiring drug courts to allow medication-assisted treatment imposes a one-size-fits-all medical standard that overrides the clinical judgment of treatment providers and the philosophical preferences of faith-based programs. Not all participants need or benefit from MAT.

**Evidence Cited**: Treatment provider objections; 12-step tradition emphasizing abstinence from all substances; individual participant preferences for drug-free recovery.

**Reality**: While it is true that not all participants have opioid use disorder (for which MAT is most relevant), the claim that MAT mandates override clinical judgment inverts the actual problem. The evidence is overwhelming that MAT reduces overdose mortality by 50% or more (Larochelle et al., 2018, *Annals of Internal Medicine*), reduces opioid use, and improves treatment retention. Courts that prohibit MAT are overriding clinical evidence, not complying with it.

**Rebuttal**: MAT mandates do not require every participant to take medication -- they require that medication be *available* to those who need it. No participant should be denied access to the most effective treatment for their condition because a court or treatment provider has a philosophical objection to pharmacotherapy. This is a patient safety issue, not a matter of treatment philosophy. Every major medical organization -- including ASAM, AMA, APA, and WHO -- recognizes MAT as the standard of care for opioid use disorder. Courts that deny MAT are not exercising clinical judgment; they are overriding it.

### Argument 7: Drug Courts Are Too Expensive for the Number of People They Serve

**Claim**: Drug courts cost $5,000-$11,000 per participant for 12-24 months of intensive supervision, treatment, and court proceedings. Given that they serve only 150,000 people annually out of 1.5 million eligible defendants, the per-capita cost makes universal drug court access prohibitively expensive. Public health approaches could treat more people for less money.

**Evidence Cited**: NADCP estimates that universal drug court access would require approximately $3.8 billion annually. Community-based outpatient treatment costs $2,000-$4,000 per episode. Pre-arrest diversion programs like LEAD cost $800-$1,500 per participant.

**Reality**: Drug courts are more expensive per participant than some alternatives, but they produce larger effects. The WSIPP meta-analysis (2018) found that drug courts generate $3.97 in benefits per $1 invested, and cost-benefit analyses consistently show positive returns. The higher cost reflects the judicial supervision, drug testing, and intensive monitoring components that produce better treatment retention (72% at 12 months) than voluntary programs (~40% at 90 days). Community-based treatment is cheaper per participant but reaches fewer people and retains them less effectively.

**Rebuttal**: The correct approach is not drug courts OR community-based treatment but a continuum that includes both. Pre-arrest diversion for low-risk individuals, community-based voluntary treatment for those who will engage, and drug courts for those who need the structure of judicial supervision. Cost-effectiveness comparisons must account for the full range of benefits, including reduced incarceration ($35,000-$40,000 per year per inmate), reduced crime, and increased economic productivity.

## Historical Patterns of Opposition

Opposition to drug courts has evolved through distinct phases:

- **1989-2000 (Skepticism)**: Critics questioned whether courts should be involved in treatment at all. Prosecutors and judges initially resisted the nonadversarial model as incompatible with judicial function. The National District Attorneys Association initially expressed reservations about prosecutors serving in nonadversarial roles.
- **2000-2012 (Effectiveness Debate)**: As drug courts expanded, opposition focused on whether they actually worked. Early single-site evaluations with methodological limitations provided ammunition for skeptics. The accumulation of rigorous evidence -- particularly the MADCE (2012) and the Campbell meta-analysis (2012) -- largely resolved this debate, though some critics continued to question the quality of individual evaluations.
- **2012-2020 (Quality and Equity)**: Opposition shifted to the quality and equity of drug courts. Critics on the left focused on racial disparities and coerced treatment. The Drug Policy Alliance began arguing that drug courts extended criminal justice control unnecessarily. Critics on the right focused on eligibility expansion and MAT mandates, arguing that these changes weakened accountability. The debate over MAT became particularly intense during this period.
- **2020-Present (Structural Critique)**: The decriminalization movement and the fentanyl crisis have intensified the debate about whether drug courts represent the right model or an outdated one. Opposition is now structural: should substance use be processed through courts at all? Oregon's Measure 110 (2020) tested the alternative -- and its partial reversal in 2024 complicated the critique. The fentanyl crisis has also created new arguments for court-supervised treatment, as the lethality of the drug supply makes unsupervised relapse far more dangerous.

## Case Studies in Opposition

### Oregon: The Decriminalization Experiment

Oregon's Measure 110 (November 2020) represented the most direct challenge to the drug court model: decriminalizing personal drug possession and replacing criminal justice processing with a voluntary treatment system funded by cannabis tax revenue. Drug courts in Oregon saw reduced referral volumes as criminal charges were no longer filed for simple possession. However, voluntary treatment uptake was far below expectations (only about 1% of individuals receiving citations called the treatment hotline in the first year), public frustration with visible drug use grew, and the state legislature partially reversed the measure in 2024 (HB 4002). The Oregon experience is cited by both drug court advocates (proving that voluntary treatment alone is insufficient) and drug court critics (arguing that the failure was in implementation, not the concept).

### Philadelphia: Blocked Supervised Consumption and Drug Court Tensions

Philadelphia's Safehouse project proposed opening a supervised consumption site in the city, arguing that the criminal justice pathway to treatment was too slow for the fentanyl crisis. The DOJ sued under the federal "crack house" statute (21 U.S.C. 856), and the Third Circuit ultimately ruled against the site. This legal battle highlighted the tension between the drug court model (which works within the criminal justice framework) and harm reduction approaches (which seek to operate outside it). Philadelphia's drug courts, meanwhile, continued operating in a city where approximately 1,200 people died of overdose in 2023.

### Hennepin County, MN: Internal Opposition to Equity Reform

When Hennepin County (Minneapolis) implemented equity-focused drug court reforms -- including removing prosecutor-controlled referral, adopting culturally responsive treatment, and requiring equity-specific outcome monitoring -- the changes faced internal resistance from prosecutors who viewed the loss of referral authority as an erosion of their role, and from some drug court team members who perceived equity monitoring as an implicit accusation of bias. The reforms ultimately reduced the Black-white graduation rate gap by 6 percentage points, validating the approach but illustrating the resistance that equity reforms face even within courts committed to improvement.

## Counter-Strategies

### Political Strategies

- **Build bipartisan coalitions around cost savings**: Drug courts are one of the few criminal justice programs supported by both the Heritage Foundation and the Center for American Progress. Emphasizing the $2-$27 return on investment appeals across ideological lines.
- **Leverage the opioid crisis**: The fentanyl epidemic has created political urgency for treatment-based approaches. Drug court advocates can frame expansion as an emergency public health response, not a "soft on crime" policy.
- **Engage veterans**: Veterans treatment courts have the broadest bipartisan support. Positioning drug court expansion alongside veterans court funding increases political viability.

### Communication Strategies

- **Lead with public safety data**: Frame drug courts as a public safety tool that reduces crime more effectively than incarceration. The 12 percentage point recidivism reduction is a compelling safety argument. When addressing law-and-order audiences, emphasize that drug courts hold participants accountable through 12-24 months of continuous supervision -- more intensive than most probation sentences.
- **Use participant stories responsibly**: Graduation ceremony testimonials are powerful but should be paired with systemic data to avoid reliance on anecdote. Include stories that acknowledge difficulty and relapse, not just success narratives, to build credibility.
- **Acknowledge limitations honestly**: Drug court advocates strengthen their credibility by acknowledging equity concerns, MAT gaps, and scalability challenges rather than presenting the model as perfect. Honest acknowledgment of limitations disarms critics and demonstrates commitment to improvement.
- **Tailor messaging to audience**: Conservative audiences respond to cost-savings data ($2-$27 return per dollar), accountability language, and the Heritage Foundation/Right on Crime endorsement. Progressive audiences respond to treatment access data, racial equity concerns, and the public health framing. Libertarian audiences may respond to the argument that drug courts are better than incarceration even if decriminalization would be better still.
- **Use the fentanyl crisis as a catalyst**: The lethality of fentanyl creates a moral urgency that transcends ideological boundaries. Drug court advocates can frame MAT access, naloxone distribution, and treatment expansion as life-or-death measures, not ideological preferences.
- **Leverage veteran support**: Veterans treatment courts have the broadest bipartisan appeal of any drug court type. Leading with VTC success stories and funding requests can open doors for broader drug court advocacy. Congressional offices that are resistant to drug court funding may be receptive to VTC-specific requests.
- **Engage media proactively**: Rather than waiting for critical coverage (e.g., from The Marshall Project or ProPublica), drug court programs should proactively share outcome data, equity monitoring results, and reform initiatives with local media. Transparency builds public trust and preempts criticism.

### Policy Design Strategies

- **Decouple eligibility from prosecutor discretion**: Shift to clinical screening at booking to reduce the influence of prosecutorial gatekeeping and reduce racial bias in referral. A 2019 study in the *Journal of Criminal Justice* found that clinical screening-based referral increased Black defendant enrollment by 35% without safety incidents.
- **Mandate equity reporting**: Require all drug courts receiving federal funds to report outcomes disaggregated by race, ethnicity, gender, and income. Frame equity monitoring as a quality improvement tool, not an accusation.
- **Create a pre-arrest diversion track**: Offer drug courts as one option within a broader continuum that includes pre-arrest diversion, reducing the critique that courts require criminal processing. Jurisdictions that operate both drug courts and LEAD-model diversion programs can offer appropriate intervention intensity based on individual risk and need.
- **Grandfather abstinence-only providers**: Allow existing providers a transition period to incorporate MAT, reducing the abruptness of mandate-driven change. Provide technical assistance and training for providers transitioning to MAT-inclusive programming.
- **Use pilot programs to build evidence in contested areas**: When opposition is rooted in safety concerns (e.g., expanded eligibility for violent-history defendants), pilot programs in willing jurisdictions can generate evidence that addresses those concerns without requiring systemwide mandates.
- **Engage victims' rights organizations proactively**: Rather than allowing victims' rights concerns to be weaponized by opposition, include victim representatives in drug court design conversations and demonstrate that treatment-based approaches reduce future victimization more effectively than incarceration.

### Legal and Constitutional Strategies

- **First Amendment challenges to 12-step mandates**: Courts have ruled that mandatory participation in religiously oriented 12-step programs violates the Establishment Clause when ordered by government (*Inouye v. Kemna*, 504 F.3d 705, 9th Cir. 2007). This precedent can be used to challenge drug courts that require 12-step participation without secular alternatives.
- **Eighth Amendment challenges to MAT denial**: The precedent set in *Pesce v. Coppinger* (355 F. Supp. 3d 35, D. Mass. 2018), which held that denying MAT to an incarcerated individual violated the Eighth Amendment, may be applicable to drug court participants denied MAT.
- **Equal protection challenges to disparate referral**: Documented racial disparities in drug court referral and acceptance rates may support equal protection litigation if evidence demonstrates that discretionary decision-making produces disparate outcomes without adequate justification.

## Net-Widening Concerns

A significant critique from progressive and abolitionist perspectives is that drug courts may function as "net-widening" mechanisms -- subjecting people to more intensive supervision than they would have received through traditional processing. The argument has several dimensions:

- **Diversion from what?** In many jurisdictions, low-level drug possession charges would result in probation, a fine, or case dismissal through traditional processing. Drug court imposes 12-24 months of intensive supervision, regular drug testing, mandatory treatment, and the risk of jail sanctions for noncompliance. For participants who would have received a light sentence, drug court may represent a net increase in state control.
- **Failure consequences**: Participants who are terminated from drug court for noncompliance are returned to traditional prosecution, often facing harsher sentences than they would have originally received. The NADCP acknowledges that approximately 25% of participants are terminated for noncompliance, and some portion of those individuals receive sentences enhanced by their drug court failure.
- **Surveillance expansion**: Drug court participation involves extensive monitoring -- frequent drug testing, mandatory court appearances, possible electronic monitoring, home visits, and employment verification. Critics argue this level of surveillance is disproportionate for offenses that would otherwise result in minimal supervision.
- **Empirical evidence**: Research on net-widening in drug courts is limited but suggestive. A 2015 study by Rempel and DeStefano found that in some New York City drug courts, a significant percentage of participants would have had their charges dismissed or received non-incarceration sentences through traditional processing, raising questions about whether drug court was diverting from incarceration or from case dismissal.

**Counter-argument**: Drug courts are designed for individuals with diagnosed substance use disorders who are at risk of continued criminal behavior without treatment. Even if the immediate sentence might have been light, the revolving door of arrest, release, rearrest -- without treatment -- imposes its own costs on participants and communities. The relevant comparison is not the immediate sentence but the long-term trajectory of untreated addiction.

## The Decriminalization vs. Drug Court Debate

The most fundamental policy debate affecting drug courts is whether substance use should be processed through the criminal justice system at all. This debate frames drug courts and decriminalization as opposing approaches, though the reality is more nuanced:

| Dimension | Drug Court Model | Decriminalization Model |
|---|---|---|
| Entry mechanism | Arrest and criminal charges | No criminal charges for personal possession |
| Treatment pathway | Court-supervised, semi-coerced | Voluntary community-based |
| Accountability mechanism | Judicial supervision, sanctions | None (beyond personal motivation) |
| Treatment retention (12-month) | ~72% | ~40% (voluntary outpatient) |
| Collateral consequences | Arrest record (may be expunged upon graduation) | None |
| Population reached | ~10% of eligible (capacity-limited) | Potentially all drug users |
| Evidence base | Strong (150+ evaluations, MADCE, meta-analyses) | Limited (Oregon's implementation had significant problems) |
| Equity implications | Inherits racial disparities from criminal justice system | Removes criminal justice disparities but may not ensure treatment access |

The Oregon experience (2020-2024) suggests that decriminalization without adequate treatment infrastructure and active engagement mechanisms is insufficient. Drug courts, for all their limitations, provide a structure for treatment engagement that purely voluntary systems have struggled to replicate. The emerging consensus in the field is that both models have a role: pre-arrest diversion and decriminalization for low-risk individuals, and court-supervised treatment for higher-risk individuals who need more intensive structure.

## Key Vulnerabilities of the Drug Court Model

Understanding the genuine vulnerabilities of the drug court model -- as opposed to politically motivated critiques -- helps advocates prepare for legitimate challenges:

1. **The criminal justice prerequisite is a real limitation**: Requiring arrest before treatment access is indefensible from a pure public health standpoint. Drug court advocates should support pre-arrest diversion as a complement, not a competitor.
2. **Racial disparities are real and persistent**: The 12-percentage-point graduation rate gap between white and Black participants cannot be explained away by controlling for other variables. Courts must actively address this disparity or risk losing credibility.
3. **MAT restrictions endanger lives**: Courts that prohibit MAT are responsible for preventable overdose deaths. This is the strongest argument opponents have, and the drug court establishment must enforce its own standards.
4. **Scalability is genuinely limited**: Drug courts cannot serve all 1.5 million eligible defendants annually without a transformation in funding, treatment capacity, and workforce development that may take decades.
5. **Judicial dependence is a quality risk**: Any system whose quality depends on the personality of individual judges is inherently fragile. Drug courts must develop institutional quality assurance mechanisms that do not rely on individual judicial excellence.
6. **Net-widening is a legitimate concern**: If drug courts are diverting individuals from case dismissal rather than from incarceration, the model's justification is significantly weakened. Courts must be able to demonstrate that participants face genuine incarceration risk absent drug court intervention.
