# Drug Courts: What You Can Do

Drug courts affect hundreds of thousands of Americans directly and millions more through their impact on public safety, treatment access, and the criminal justice system. Citizen engagement is essential for expanding access, improving quality, and ensuring equity in drug court programs.

---

## Individual Actions

### Learn and Stay Informed

- Subscribe to the NADCP newsletter (<https://www.nadcp.org/>) for updates on drug court policy, research, and legislative developments
- Follow the National Drug Court Resource Center (<https://ndcrc.org/>) for the latest data on drug court operations and outcomes
- Read the Campbell Collaboration meta-analysis (Mitchell et al., 2012) and the MADCE report (Rossman et al., 2011) to understand the evidence base
- Use the NDCRC Drug Court Locator to find your local drug court and learn about its eligibility criteria, treatment services, and outcomes

### Make Personal Changes

- Educate yourself about substance use disorders as a medical condition, not a moral failing. Understanding addiction science reduces stigma and supports recovery.
- If you know someone who is justice-involved and struggling with substance use, share information about drug court eligibility and local treatment resources
- Challenge misconceptions about drug courts when you encounter them -- whether the misconception is that drug courts are "soft on crime" or that they extend criminal justice control inappropriately
- Support recovery-friendly workplaces by advocating for hiring policies that do not automatically exclude individuals with drug court or treatment histories

### Use Your Voice

- **Contact Your Representatives**: Urge federal legislators to increase drug court funding, expand MAT mandates, and require racial equity reporting. State legislators control drug court authorizing statutes and state funding levels.
- **Submit Public Comments**: When the Bureau of Justice Assistance issues solicitations for drug court grants, public comments can influence funding priorities. When state legislatures consider drug court bills, written and oral testimony is influential.
- **Write Letters to the Editor**: When local media covers drug-related crime, write letters that highlight drug court effectiveness data (12 percentage point recidivism reduction, $2-$27 return on investment per $1) as an alternative to incarceration-focused responses.
- **Share Information**: Share drug court evidence and success stories on social media platforms, particularly during National Drug Court Month (May).

## Community Actions

### Organize Locally

- Attend your local drug court graduation ceremony. Most drug courts hold public graduation events that are open to community members. Showing community support matters to participants and judges.
- Form a community advocacy group to monitor your local drug court's performance. Request annual outcome data including graduation rates, recidivism rates, and demographic breakdowns.
- Partner with local treatment providers and recovery organizations to identify gaps in drug court treatment services, particularly MAT access.
- Organize a community forum on drug court policy, inviting the drug court judge, defense attorneys, treatment providers, and former participants to share perspectives.

### Build Coalitions

- Connect with local criminal justice reform organizations, treatment advocacy groups, and faith-based recovery communities to build a broad coalition supporting drug court improvement
- Engage local business communities by highlighting the economic benefits of drug courts: reduced incarceration costs, increased workforce participation, and lower crime rates
- Partner with veteran service organizations to advocate for veterans treatment court expansion in your community
- Engage child welfare organizations and family advocacy groups to support family drug court expansion, emphasizing that parental treatment through family drug courts reduces foster care placements and improves child outcomes
- Connect with tribal organizations and Native American community groups to support healing-to-wellness court development and culturally responsive programming
- Build relationships with local public health departments to advocate for drug court-public health integration, including naloxone distribution and harm reduction practices

### Monitor and Advocate for Equity

- Request annual outcome data from your local drug court disaggregated by race, ethnicity, and gender. If the court does not collect this data, advocate for data collection as a quality improvement measure.
- Compare your local drug court's referral, acceptance, and graduation rates by race with national benchmarks (referral: 38% white vs. 25% Black; graduation: 64% white vs. 52% Black, NADCP National Survey, 2022). If local disparities exceed national averages, engage the drug court team to understand and address the causes.
- Advocate for removing prosecutor-controlled referral in favor of clinical screening at booking, which research shows reduces racial bias in drug court access (a 2019 *Journal of Criminal Justice* study found that clinical screening increased Black defendant enrollment by 35%).
- Support culturally responsive treatment programming by identifying and connecting local cultural organizations, faith communities, and community health workers with drug court treatment providers.
- Attend public meetings of your local criminal justice coordination council (or equivalent body) to raise equity concerns and advocate for evidence-based reforms.

### Support Participants and Families Directly

- Volunteer as a mentor in your local drug court program if mentoring opportunities are available (particularly in veterans treatment courts, which use volunteer veteran mentors extensively)
- Provide transportation assistance to drug court participants who face barriers to attending court hearings and treatment sessions -- transportation is cited as a top barrier to drug court completion in rural areas
- If you are an employer, consider offering recovery-friendly employment opportunities for drug court participants and graduates. Contact your local drug court coordinator to learn about job placement programs.
- If you are a landlord or housing provider, consider offering housing to drug court participants. Housing instability affects 20-30% of participants and is a leading predictor of program failure (Urban Institute, 2022).
- Support organizations that provide wraparound services (clothing, food, childcare, identification documents) to drug court participants, who often face material needs that treatment alone does not address.

## Political Actions

### Contact Elected Officials

**When to Contact**:

- During federal and state budget season (February-September), when drug court funding levels are set
- When drug court-related legislation is pending at the state or federal level
- During National Drug Court Month (May), when NADCP coordinates advocacy efforts
- When your local drug court faces funding cuts, capacity reductions, or closures

**What to Say - Template Email**:

```text
Subject: Support Drug Court Funding and Reform

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

I am writing as a constituent from [City, State] to urge your support for increased funding and evidence-based reforms for drug courts.

Drug courts are among the most effective programs in the criminal justice system. The National Institute of Justice's Multisite Adult Drug Court Evaluation found that drug court participants have a 12 percentage point lower recidivism rate than defendants processed through traditional courts. Cost-benefit analyses consistently show returns of $2 to $27 for every $1 invested. Yet drug courts currently serve only about 10% of eligible defendants due to limited funding and capacity.

I urge you to:
1. Support increased federal funding for the Drug Court Discretionary Grant Program
2. Require all drug courts receiving federal funds to permit medication-assisted treatment (MAT)
3. Mandate racial equity data reporting to address documented disparities in drug court access and outcomes

Drug courts save lives, reduce crime, and save taxpayer money. They deserve your support.

Thank you for your service and consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your City, State]
```

**What to Say - Phone Call Script**:

```text
"Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [City]. I'm calling about drug court funding and reform. Drug courts reduce recidivism by 12 percentage points and save $2 to $27 for every dollar invested, but they only serve about 10% of eligible defendants. I'd like the [Representative/Senator] to support increased drug court funding and require medication-assisted treatment access in all federally funded courts. Can you please note my position?"
```

### Support Legislation

Current or proposed legislation to support:

- **Drug Court Reauthorization and Improvement Act**: Reauthorizes the DCDGP with increased funding, expanded MAT requirements, and racial equity provisions -- Contact members of the Senate and House Judiciary Committees
- **Comprehensive Addiction and Recovery Act (CARA) reauthorization**: Continuation and expansion of federal drug court authority -- Support through congressional office contacts
- **State-level drug court expansion bills**: Many states consider drug court legislation annually -- Contact your state representative and senator during legislative sessions
- **Veterans Treatment Court Expansion Act**: Bipartisan legislation to expand veterans treatment courts to all VA-served regions -- Especially receptive to veteran and military family constituent contacts
- **Family Drug Court Expansion Act**: Legislation to create dedicated federal funding for family dependency treatment courts -- Contact members of both Judiciary and Health/Education committees
- **Tribal Healing-to-Wellness Court Development Act**: Legislation to fund and support tribal court development -- Contact members of the Senate Committee on Indian Affairs and the House Natural Resources Committee
- **Drug Court Equity Act**: Legislation requiring disaggregated racial equity data reporting as a condition of all federal drug court funding -- Frame as quality improvement, not punitive monitoring

### Electoral Engagement

**Questions to Ask Candidates**:

1. "Do you support increased federal and state funding for drug courts, which reduce recidivism by 12 percentage points and save $2-$27 per dollar invested?"
2. "Do you believe all drug courts should be required to allow medication-assisted treatment for opioid use disorder, which reduces overdose deaths by 50%?"
3. "What is your plan to address documented racial disparities in drug court access and outcomes?"
4. "Do you support pre-arrest diversion programs like LEAD as complements to drug courts?"

**Voting Considerations**:

- Look for candidates who support evidence-based criminal justice reform and treatment over incarceration
- Pay attention to candidates' positions on the broader opioid crisis response, as drug courts are one component of the solution
- Check whether candidates serve on the Judiciary, Appropriations, or Health committees, which have jurisdiction over drug court policy

## Professional Actions

### For Attorneys

- If you practice criminal law, familiarize yourself with your local drug court's eligibility criteria and refer eligible clients. Many defense attorneys are unaware of drug court availability or eligibility requirements.
- If your jurisdiction lacks a drug court, advocate for establishing one with the local bar association and judiciary. The BJA provides planning grants for new drug court development.
- If you serve on a drug court team, advocate for expanded eligibility criteria based on clinical assessment rather than categorical offense exclusions. Raise due process concerns when appropriate.
- Join the NACDL Treatment Court Committee or your state bar association's equivalent to contribute to policy development regarding defense counsel ethics in drug courts.
- Pro bono: Assist drug court graduates with expungement petitions, collateral consequence mitigation, and record sealing where state law permits. The arrest record from the underlying case can create barriers to employment and housing even after successful graduation.

### For Healthcare Providers

- Partner with your local drug court to provide treatment services, particularly MAT. If you hold a DEA registration (all physicians do), you can prescribe buprenorphine for opioid use disorder without any special waiver since the X-waiver requirement was eliminated in 2023.
- Offer to train drug court teams on addiction medicine and evidence-based treatment practices. Many drug court judges and coordinators have limited training in pharmacotherapy and benefit from clinical education.
- If you operate a federally qualified health center (FQHC), community health center, or behavioral health practice, explore formal partnership agreements with local drug courts for care transition at graduation. Many participants lose treatment access upon program completion.
- Advocate within your professional organizations (ASAM, AMA, APA) for stronger positions on MAT access in drug courts and against abstinence-only treatment mandates in court settings.
- Participate in drug court staffing meetings when invited. Clinical input into sanction decisions can prevent medically inappropriate responses to relapse (e.g., interrupting MAT during brief jail sanctions).

### For Educators and Researchers

- Include drug court policy in criminal justice, public health, and social work curricula. Drug courts represent one of the most extensively studied criminal justice innovations and offer rich case studies in therapeutic jurisprudence, evidence-based policy, and racial equity.
- Encourage students to observe drug court proceedings (most are open to the public) and intern with drug court programs. Experiential learning in drug court settings is valuable for future practitioners in criminal justice, social work, and public health.
- Conduct independent evaluations of local drug courts, particularly examining equity outcomes. Many courts lack evaluation capacity and welcome academic partnerships. Share results with drug court teams and policymakers.
- Contribute to the evidence base on underresearched areas: stimulant use disorders in drug courts, tribal healing-to-wellness court outcomes, family drug court effects on child welfare, and long-term (5+ year) post-graduation outcomes.
- Apply for NIJ or NIDA research grants focused on drug court policy questions. Priority areas include: identifying effective interventions for racial disparity reduction, evaluating MAT integration models, and comparing drug court outcomes to pre-arrest diversion programs.

### For Employers

- Adopt hiring policies that do not automatically exclude individuals with drug court histories. Participants who successfully complete drug court have demonstrated sustained recovery, accountability, and perseverance.
- Participate in drug court job placement programs. Contact your local drug court coordinator to learn about partnership opportunities. Recovery-friendly workplaces contribute to long-term success.
- Consider offering flexible scheduling for employees who are current drug court participants and need to attend court hearings and treatment sessions during work hours.
- Join the National Safety Council's "Employers for Recovery" initiative, which provides resources for creating recovery-friendly workplaces and connecting with drug court employment services.

### For Social Workers and Counselors

- Seek specialized training in drug court practices through the National Drug Court Institute (NDCI). NDCI offers training programs for treatment providers working in drug court settings.
- Advocate for evidence-based treatment and MAT access in your practice setting. If you work for a treatment provider that contracts with drug courts, advocate internally for MAT-inclusive programming.
- Pursue certification as a peer recovery specialist if you have lived experience of substance use disorder recovery. Peer specialists are increasingly integrated into drug court teams and provide a valued perspective.
- Address cultural competency in your practice. Culturally responsive treatment -- programming that reflects the cultural background, values, and experiences of participants -- improves engagement and outcomes for minority participants.

### For Journalists and Media Professionals

- Cover drug court proceedings and graduations, but go beyond success stories to examine systemic issues including racial disparities, MAT restrictions, and funding gaps.
- Request annual outcome data from local drug courts for data-driven reporting. Compare local results to national benchmarks.
- Interview drug court participants and graduates (with their consent) to provide perspective on the participant experience, including both positive aspects and criticisms.
- Investigate local drug court practices against NADCP Best Practice Standards, particularly regarding MAT access, eligibility criteria, and equity monitoring.

## Advocacy Toolkit

### Key Talking Points

When discussing drug courts with others, emphasize:

1. **Drug courts reduce crime**: Participants have a 12 percentage point lower recidivism rate than defendants processed through traditional courts (MADCE, 2012). That means fewer victims and safer communities.
2. **Drug courts save money**: Every $1 invested in drug courts generates $2 to $27 in savings from reduced incarceration, healthcare, and criminal justice costs (WSIPP, 2018; NPC Research, 2019).
3. **Drug courts need reform**: Only ~10% of eligible defendants can access drug courts, 21% still prohibit life-saving medication-assisted treatment, and persistent racial disparities undermine the model's promise.
4. **Treatment works better than incarceration**: RAND Corporation research shows treatment is seven times more cost-effective than domestic law enforcement at reducing drug use.

### Common Misconceptions to Counter

| Misconception | Reality | Source |
|---------------|---------|--------|
| "Drug courts let criminals off easy" | Drug courts require 12-24 months of supervision, regular drug testing, mandatory treatment, and community service. Participants who fail are returned to traditional prosecution. | NADCP Best Practice Standards, 2018 |
| "Drug courts don't work" | The evidence base includes 150+ evaluations, a 1,798-participant multisite study, and a Campbell Collaboration meta-analysis -- all showing significant recidivism reduction | Mitchell et al., 2012; MADCE, 2012 |
| "Drug courts are just for minor offenders" | Research shows drug courts are *most effective* for high-risk/high-need participants; restricting to low-risk defendants reduces impact | Marlowe et al., 2016 |
| "Medication-assisted treatment is just trading one addiction for another" | MAT reduces overdose mortality by 50%+, is endorsed by every major medical organization, and is considered the standard of care for opioid use disorder | Larochelle et al., 2018; WHO, 2009 |
| "Drug courts serve everyone equally" | Documented disparities exist: Black defendants are referred at lower rates (25% vs. 38%), accepted at lower rates (63% vs. 72%), and graduate at lower rates (52% vs. 64%) | Marlowe et al., 2018; NADCP Survey, 2022 |
| "Drug courts are too expensive" | Drug courts cost $5,000-$11,000 per participant but generate $2-$27 per dollar invested through reduced incarceration, crime, and healthcare costs; far cheaper than incarceration ($35,000-$40,000/year) | WSIPP, 2018; NPC Research, 2019 |
| "Decriminalization would solve the problem without courts" | Oregon's Measure 110 showed that decriminalization without treatment infrastructure leads to low voluntary treatment uptake (~1% of cited individuals called the treatment hotline); drug courts and decriminalization can complement each other | Oregon Health Authority, 2023 |

## State-Level Advocacy Guide

### How to Advocate in Your State

1. **Identify your state drug court coordinator**: Most states have a coordinating office or designated coordinator within the state court administrator's office. This person can provide information on state drug court operations, standards, and funding.

2. **Learn your state's drug court landscape**: Use the NDCRC Drug Court Locator (<https://ndcrc.org/>) to find all drug courts in your state. Note which counties lack drug courts and which court types are available.

3. **Understand your state's legislative process**: Drug court legislation typically moves through the judiciary or criminal justice committee. Identify the committee chair and members. Determine whether your state has a drug court caucus or equivalent.

4. **Identify state-level reform priorities**: Common state-level reforms include:
    - Establishing statewide drug court standards (38 states have some form of standards as of 2023)
    - Requiring MAT access in all state-funded drug courts
    - Creating dedicated state funding for drug courts (beyond federal grants)
    - Mandating equity data collection and reporting
    - Expanding drug court eligibility criteria
    - Authorizing new court types (family, veterans, tribal) where they do not exist

5. **Build a state advocacy coalition**: Connect local drug court professionals, treatment providers, recovery organizations, veterans' groups, and criminal justice reform organizations. A diverse coalition is more politically effective than any single organization.

6. **Use data strategically**: Present your state's specific drug court outcome data alongside national benchmarks. Highlight the gap between your state's eligible population and the number actually served. Quantify the cost of incarceration that drug court expansion could offset.

7. **Engage the media**: Local media coverage of drug court graduations, equity concerns, or funding challenges can shift public opinion and create political pressure for reform. Offer to connect reporters with drug court judges, participants, and advocates.

### State Reform Priority Matrix

| State Category | Top Priority | Second Priority | Third Priority |
|---|---|---|---|
| No statewide standards | Establish evidence-based state standards | Create state coordination office | Develop data collection system |
| Standards but no MAT mandate | Mandate MAT access in all state-funded courts | Require equity data reporting | Fund training on best practices |
| Standards and MAT but limited coverage | Expand to underserved counties | Create dedicated state funding stream | Develop specialized courts (VTC, family, tribal) |
| Comprehensive system | Pursue national certification readiness | Eliminate racial disparities | Integrate with community health systems |
