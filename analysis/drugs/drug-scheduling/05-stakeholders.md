# Drug Scheduling: Stakeholders

The drug scheduling system is shaped by the interactions of federal agencies, researchers, healthcare providers, patients, industry actors, advocacy organizations, and international bodies, each with distinct interests, resources, and levels of influence.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Patients with conditions treatable by Schedule I substances | Denied legal access to potentially effective therapies (cannabis for chronic pain, epilepsy; psilocybin for depression; MDMA for PTSD) | Tens of millions (e.g., ~3.4 million cannabis patients in state medical programs, NCSL 2024) | Legal access to therapeutic substances; reduced stigma; insurance coverage |
| Medical researchers | Schedule I barriers delay studies by 9-18 months; limited substance supply; institutional reluctance | ~780 DEA-registered Schedule I researchers (DEA, 2023); thousands more deterred by barriers | Streamlined research access; adequate supply; removal of regulatory duplication |
| Cannabis industry workers and businesses | Operate in conflict with federal law; denied banking, tax deductions, bankruptcy protection | ~440,000 full-time jobs in legal cannabis (Leafly, 2024); ~$33.6 billion market (MJBizDaily, 2024) | Federal legalization or rescheduling; banking access; end of 26 U.S.C. Section 280E |
| People arrested or convicted for Schedule I offenses | Criminal records, incarceration, collateral consequences (housing, employment, education, immigration) | ~1.5 million drug arrests annually; ~350,000 for cannabis (FBI UCR, 2023) | Decriminalization; expungement; reduced penalties |
| People with substance use disorders | Scheduling affects treatment access (e.g., methadone distribution restrictions under Schedule II) | 48.7 million Americans with SUD (SAMHSA NSDUH, 2024) | Treatment access; reduced stigma; evidence-based scheduling of treatment medications |

### Secondary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Healthcare providers | Prescribing authority constrained by scheduling; separate registrations for Schedule I research; electronic prescribing mandates for Schedule II | ~1.1 million DEA-registered practitioners (DEA, 2023) | Prescribing autonomy; evidence-based formulary access; reduced regulatory burden |
| State governments | Operate cannabis programs in conflict with federal law; face legal uncertainty, banking obstacles | 38 states with medical programs; 24 with recreational programs | Federal accommodation of state programs; regulatory clarity; tax revenue |
| Veterans | VA physicians cannot recommend cannabis; MDMA/psilocybin research for PTSD slowed by Schedule I barriers | ~1.5 million veterans seek VA treatment for chronic pain, PTSD (VA, 2023) | Access to cannabis and psychedelic therapies; expanded VA treatment options |
| Families and communities | Communities of color disproportionately affected by Schedule I enforcement; cascading effects through criminal records | Millions of families affected by ~20 million cannabis arrests since 1970 | Expungement; end of racial disparities; community reinvestment |
| Taxpayers | Federal drug enforcement spending and incarceration costs driven by scheduling | All U.S. taxpayers | Cost-effective drug policy; reduced incarceration spending |
| Employers | Workplace drug testing policies tied to scheduling; legal states create employment law conflicts | All employers with drug testing programs (~56% of U.S. employers, Quest Diagnostics 2023) | Clear federal guidance; liability protection; workforce access |
| Universities and research institutions | Institutional barriers to Schedule I research; security requirements; insurance complications | ~4,000 degree-granting institutions; hundreds with active research programs | Streamlined research access; reduced compliance burden; competitive research positioning |
| International partners | U.S. scheduling decisions influence international classification; treaty dynamics | 186 signatories to the 1961 Single Convention | Treaty modernization; regulatory harmonization; evidence-based international classification |

### Tertiary Stakeholders

- **Legal professionals**: Defense attorneys, prosecutors, and judges whose daily work is shaped by scheduling-based penalties. Public defenders handle hundreds of thousands of drug cases annually in which the schedule classification determines charging decisions, plea bargaining leverage, and sentencing outcomes.
- **Insurance companies**: Health insurers whose formulary decisions and coverage policies are influenced by scheduling. Schedule I substances are categorically excluded from insurance coverage, even when state-legal medical programs authorize their use. Rescheduling cannabis to Schedule III would potentially trigger insurance coverage obligations.
- **Agricultural communities**: Hemp farmers whose livelihoods depend on the 0.3% THC threshold established by the 2018 Farm Bill, and who would be affected by changes to cannabis scheduling that blur the hemp-marijuana distinction.
- **Financial institutions**: Banks and credit unions that face regulatory risk for serving cannabis businesses, and payment processors that cannot handle cannabis transactions due to Schedule I status.
- **Technology companies**: Drug testing technology firms, laboratory services, and compliance software companies whose business models depend on the current scheduling framework.

## Who Has Power

### Decision Makers

| Actor | Role | Authority | Resources | Influence Level |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| DEA Administrator | Final scheduling authority; administers CSA | Can schedule, reschedule, or deschedule substances; sets manufacturing quotas; registers researchers | $3.3 billion budget (FY2024); ~10,000 employees | High |
| HHS Secretary | Provides binding scientific/medical evaluations | HHS recommendations on scientific matters are binding on DEA under 21 U.S.C. Section 811(b) | FDA, NIDA, SAMHSA scientific resources | High (on scientific questions) |
| FDA Commissioner | Leads 8-factor analysis for scheduling decisions | Evaluates abuse potential, medical use, safety | FDA drug evaluation division; clinical trial expertise | High |
| NIDA Director | Funds and shapes drug abuse research | Controls largest federal investment in drug research; historically controlled cannabis supply | NIDA budget: ~$1.6 billion (FY2024) | Medium-High |
| Attorney General | DEA is within DOJ; AG can direct scheduling priorities | Legal authority over DEA; can direct rescheduling proceedings | DOJ institutional authority | High |
| Congressional committees | Can schedule/deschedule directly by legislation; oversee DEA/FDA | Senate Judiciary, House Judiciary, House Energy and Commerce | Appropriations power; legislative authority | High |
| President | Directs executive agencies; issues policy directives | Cannot unilaterally reschedule but can direct AG/HHS to initiate review | Executive authority; public platform | Medium-High |

### Key Organizations and Institutions

- **Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA)**: The primary gatekeeper of the scheduling system. The DEA administers the CSA, processes scheduling petitions, conducts enforcement, and registers researchers. Its institutional culture is oriented toward prohibition, and its institutional interests align with maintaining restrictive classifications.
- **Food and Drug Administration (FDA)**: Conducts the scientific analysis underlying scheduling recommendations. The FDA's evaluation is binding on the DEA for medical and scientific questions, but the DEA retains discretion on non-scientific factors. The FDA's clinical trial framework shapes what counts as "currently accepted medical use."
- **National Institute on Drug Abuse (NIDA)**: The largest federal funder of drug abuse research. NIDA has historically focused on the harmful effects of drug use rather than therapeutic potential, and its monopoly on research cannabis supply (until 2021) significantly shaped the research landscape.
- **Office of National Drug Control Policy (ONDCP)**: Coordinates federal drug policy and certifies the national drug control budget ($46.1 billion, FY2025). ONDCP does not have direct scheduling authority but shapes policy priorities and messaging.

### Academic and Scientific Influencers

Key researchers and institutions whose work has shaped the scheduling debate:

- **David Nutt (Imperial College London)**: Published landmark studies ranking drugs by evidence-based harm criteria (*The Lancet*, 2007, 2010) that demonstrated systematic misalignment between scheduling and actual risk. Fired as chair of the UK's ACMD for publicly criticizing the government's rejection of scientific advice.
- **Nora Volkow (NIDA Director, 2003-present)**: Has gradually shifted NIDA's messaging from a focus exclusively on drug abuse to acknowledging the need for more nuanced research, including therapeutic potential of cannabis and psychedelics.
- **National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine**: 2017 report *The Health Effects of Cannabis and Cannabinoids* established the scientific consensus that Schedule I barriers impede cannabis research and that cannabis has evidence-based therapeutic applications.
- **Roland Griffiths (Johns Hopkins, d. 2023)**: Pioneered modern psilocybin research; published landmark studies on psilocybin for depression, anxiety, and addiction. His work was instrumental in earning FDA breakthrough therapy designations for psilocybin.
- **Matthew Johnson (Johns Hopkins)**: Leading psychedelic researcher; published extensively on psilocybin for smoking cessation and depression; testified before Congress on Schedule I research barriers.

## Stakeholder Interests and Alignment

### Aligned Interests (Potential Coalition Partners)

| Coalition | Shared Interest | Strength |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| Researchers + patients + medical associations | Removing Schedule I research barriers and expanding therapeutic access | Strong |
| Cannabis industry + state governments + tax reform advocates | Federal cannabis legalization/rescheduling for banking, tax, and regulatory clarity | Strong |
| Civil liberties organizations + racial justice advocates + criminal justice reformers | Ending Schedule I cannabis enforcement; expungement; addressing racial disparities | Strong |
| Veterans organizations + psychedelic researchers + PTSD treatment advocates | Rescheduling psilocybin and MDMA to expand therapeutic options for PTSD | Moderate-Strong |
| Libertarians + progressives + some conservatives | Reducing federal scheduling authority over substances with state-level legalization | Moderate |

### Conflicting Interests

- **DEA vs. researchers**: The DEA's institutional interest in maintaining broad scheduling authority conflicts with researchers' need for streamlined access to controlled substances. The DEA's registration and quota systems create bottlenecks that serve enforcement objectives at the expense of scientific progress. This conflict is structural: as long as the DEA controls both enforcement and research gatekeeping, it will prioritize enforcement.
- **Pharmaceutical industry vs. plant-medicine advocates**: Pharmaceutical companies benefit from Schedule I classification of natural substances (cannabis, psilocybin) that could compete with patented medications. The pharmaceutical industry also resists scheduling reforms that could enable low-cost, unpatentable therapies. However, some pharmaceutical companies are beginning to position themselves to enter the cannabis and psychedelic therapy markets, creating divisions within the industry.
- **Federal enforcement agencies vs. state legalization advocates**: Federal agencies (DEA, DOJ) are legally bound to enforce the CSA regardless of state law. State governments and the cannabis industry want federal accommodation of state programs. This conflict is increasingly difficult to sustain as the number of legal states grows and the economic footprint of the cannabis industry expands.
- **Rescheduling advocates vs. descheduling advocates**: Within the cannabis reform movement, there is a significant strategic disagreement between those who support rescheduling (moving cannabis to Schedule III, within the existing CSA framework) and those who support descheduling (removing cannabis from the CSA entirely and regulating it like alcohol or tobacco). Rescheduling is more politically achievable but maintains federal restrictions; descheduling would fully resolve the federal-state conflict but is more politically difficult.
- **Equity advocates vs. industry advocates**: Social equity advocates insist that scheduling reform must include retroactive justice provisions -- expungement, community reinvestment, equity licensing -- while some industry-focused advocates want rapid reform without conditions that could slow the legislative process. This tension has contributed to legislative gridlock on comprehensive cannabis reform bills.
- **Public health cautionists vs. access advocates**: Some public health professionals, while supporting rescheduling for research purposes, express concern about the commercialization dynamics that follow reclassification. They point to the tobacco and alcohol industries as cautionary examples of how commercial interests can undermine public health even within a legal regulatory framework. Access advocates counter that prohibition causes more harm than regulated legalization and that public health safeguards can be built into regulatory frameworks.
- **Criminal justice reformers vs. harm reduction advocates**: Criminal justice reformers focus on reducing penalties and expunging records tied to scheduling. Harm reduction advocates focus on drug checking, naloxone access, and treatment -- concerns that overlap with but are distinct from the scheduling debate. Both groups support reform but prioritize different dimensions of it.

### The DEA Iron Triangle

The scheduling system is sustained by an "iron triangle" of mutually reinforcing interests:

1. **DEA** (agency): Maintains scheduling authority, enforcement mandate, and budget
2. **Congressional committees** (legislature): Judiciary committees receive campaign contributions from law enforcement groups and private prison industry; use "tough on drugs" messaging
3. **Law enforcement interest groups** (lobbying): National Association of Police Organizations, Fraternal Order of Police, and private prison companies lobby to maintain punitive drug policies and oppose rescheduling

This iron triangle is supplemented by pharmaceutical industry lobbying, which quietly opposes scheduling reforms that would enable competition from natural substances.

### The Counter-Coalition

Opposing the iron triangle is an emerging counter-coalition that brings together unusual political allies:

| Coalition Member | Primary Motivation | Political Orientation |
|---|---|---|
| Cannabis industry | Federal legalization for business viability | Mixed (increasingly bipartisan) |
| Libertarian advocacy groups (Cato, Reason) | Government overreach and individual liberty | Right-libertarian |
| Progressive organizations (DPA, ACLU) | Racial justice and civil liberties | Left-progressive |
| Veterans organizations (VCP, Heroic Hearts) | Treatment access for PTSD and pain | Bipartisan (conservative-leaning veterans) |
| Medical associations (AMA, AAP) | Research access and evidence-based medicine | Non-partisan |
| State governors and legislatures | Federalism and state regulatory autonomy | Bipartisan |
| Psychedelic research organizations (MAPS) | Therapeutic access and scientific freedom | Non-partisan |
| Criminal justice reform groups (FAMM, Sentencing Project) | Proportional penalties and mass incarceration | Bipartisan |
| Religious organizations (some evangelical, Catholic social justice) | Compassion, addiction treatment, mass incarceration | Mixed |

This counter-coalition's diversity is both its strength and its weakness: it brings broad political appeal but struggles to coalesce around a single reform strategy (rescheduling vs. descheduling vs. incremental research access reform).

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on scheduling policy:

- **Lobbying**: The pharmaceutical industry spent $374 million on federal lobbying in 2023 (OpenSecrets, 2024). Cannabis industry lobbying is growing but smaller -- the NCIA spent ~$2.1 million in 2023; the total cannabis industry lobbying spend was approximately $11 million (OpenSecrets, 2024). Law enforcement groups and private prison companies also lobby against scheduling reform. The disparity between pharmaceutical and cannabis lobbying resources -- roughly 34:1 -- reflects the structural disadvantage reformers face in the legislative arena.
- **Campaign Contributions**: Pharmaceutical companies are among the top contributors to members of the Senate Judiciary and Health committees. The private prison industry (CoreCivic and GEO Group combined) contributed approximately $2.5 million to federal candidates in the 2022 cycle, primarily to Republican members who oppose cannabis reform (OpenSecrets, 2024). Cannabis industry PAC contributions have grown from near zero in 2016 to approximately $6 million in the 2024 cycle, reflecting the industry's increasing political engagement.
- **Revolving Door**: Former DEA officials frequently move to pharmaceutical industry consulting, lobbying firms, or private sector drug testing companies. Former congressional staff move to industry lobbying. At least 12 former DEA officials have registered as federal lobbyists since 2015 (OpenSecrets, 2024). This revolving door reinforces institutional resistance to scheduling reform by ensuring that individuals with DEA enforcement perspectives occupy positions of influence in adjacent sectors.
- **Public Advocacy**: Organizations like NORML, DPA, and MAPS conduct public education campaigns, grassroots organizing, and ballot initiative efforts. The success of state-level cannabis legalization has been driven primarily by ballot initiatives rather than legislative action.
- **Expertise/Information**: The DEA, FDA, and NIDA control the scientific and enforcement data that inform scheduling decisions. Researchers and advocacy organizations challenge this information monopoly through independent studies, but face structural disadvantages in resources and institutional credibility.
- **Litigation**: Organizations like Americans for Safe Access have filed lawsuits challenging scheduling decisions and demanding DEA action on rescheduling petitions. While litigation has forced procedural improvements (e.g., requiring DEA to license additional cannabis cultivators), courts have generally deferred to DEA expertise on substantive scheduling questions.
- **Ballot Initiatives**: State-level ballot initiatives have been the most successful mechanism for cannabis scheduling reform, bypassing legislative processes that are more susceptible to lobbying and institutional resistance. Every state that has legalized recreational cannabis did so initially through ballot initiative, not legislative action.
- **Economic Pressure**: The cannabis industry's growing economic footprint -- $33.6 billion in annual revenue, 440,000 jobs, and billions in state tax revenue -- creates economic pressure for federal accommodation. State governments that depend on cannabis tax revenue become institutional advocates for scheduling reform.
- **International Precedent**: Advocates increasingly cite international precedents (Canada, Germany, Uruguay, Australia) to demonstrate that scheduling reform is feasible and does not produce the catastrophic outcomes opponents predict. This is particularly effective in neutralizing treaty compliance arguments.

## Power Dynamics and Trajectory

The balance of power in the scheduling debate has shifted significantly over the past decade:

| Factor | 2015 | 2020 | 2025 | Trend |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Public support for cannabis legalization | 58% (Gallup) | 68% (Gallup) | 70%+ (Gallup) | Strongly favorable |
| States with recreational cannabis | 4 | 15 | 24 + D.C. | Accelerating |
| Cannabis industry revenue | ~$5.4 billion | ~$17.5 billion | ~$33.6 billion | Rapidly growing |
| DEA institutional resistance | Very high | High | Medium-High | Gradually declining |
| Congressional support for reform | Limited (mostly Democratic) | Moderate (House passage of MORE Act) | Growing bipartisan | Gradually increasing |
| Psychedelic research legitimacy | Emerging (early trials) | Established (Phase II/III) | Mainstream (breakthrough therapy) | Rapidly growing |
| International reform momentum | Limited (Uruguay only) | Growing (Canada) | Strong (Canada, Germany, Australia) | Accelerating |

The overall trajectory favors reform, but the pace of change remains constrained by the structural factors identified in the root causes analysis: DEA authority, statutory design flaws, and political risk aversion.

### Stakeholder Engagement Recommendations

For advocates seeking to advance scheduling reform, the stakeholder analysis suggests several strategic priorities:

1. **Prioritize bipartisan stakeholders**: Veterans' organizations, medical associations, and state officials (both Republican and Democratic) carry bipartisan credibility that neutralizes partisan framing of scheduling reform as a "liberal" issue.
2. **Leverage economic interests**: State governments dependent on cannabis tax revenue, cannabis businesses employing hundreds of thousands of workers, and banking institutions seeking regulatory clarity all have material incentives to support reform that transcend ideology.
3. **Address equity stakeholder concerns directly**: Failing to pair scheduling reform with retroactive justice provisions risks fracturing the reform coalition. Including expungement, community reinvestment, and social equity licensing provisions maintains coalition cohesion.
4. **Separate research access from commercial legalization**: The research access reform argument faces the least organized opposition and has the broadest stakeholder support. Leading with research access reforms builds momentum and credibility for broader structural changes.
5. **Engage patients as the most compelling advocates**: Patient stories -- veterans with PTSD, children with epilepsy, cancer patients seeking pain relief -- are the most politically potent arguments for scheduling reform because they humanize abstract pharmacological debates and create pressure that is difficult for opponents to counter.

## Stakeholder Impact of Specific Reform Scenarios

Different reform approaches would produce different winners and losers among stakeholders:

### Scenario 1: Cannabis Rescheduled to Schedule III (Administrative)

| Stakeholder | Impact | Net Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Cannabis businesses | Section 280E eliminated (~$2B tax relief); banking access improved but not guaranteed; federal prescribing rules apply | Strongly positive |
| Patients | Medical access federally recognized; VA physicians may recommend; insurance coverage possible | Positive |
| Researchers | Standard DEA registration; multiple suppliers; streamlined access | Strongly positive |
| State recreational programs | Remain in legal gray area (Schedule III does not authorize recreational use) | Mixed |
| People with cannabis convictions | Reduced penalties prospectively; no automatic expungement | Marginally positive |
| DEA | Retains some cannabis enforcement authority; reduced scope | Moderately negative (for institutional interests) |
| Pharmaceutical industry | Increased competition from cannabis therapeutics; some firms position to enter market | Mixed |

### Scenario 2: Cannabis Descheduled with Federal Regulatory Framework (Legislative)

| Stakeholder | Impact | Net Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Cannabis businesses | Full federal legalization; banking, tax, and bankruptcy access; interstate commerce possible | Strongly positive |
| Patients | Full access; potential insurance coverage; quality standards | Strongly positive |
| Researchers | No scheduling barriers for cannabis research | Strongly positive |
| State programs | Protected and grandfathered; federal quality floor established | Positive |
| People with cannabis convictions | Automatic expungement (if included in legislation) | Strongly positive |
| DEA | Cannabis entirely removed from enforcement mandate | Negative (for institutional interests) |
| Alcohol/tobacco industries | New competitor in legal intoxicant market | Negative |
| Drug testing industry | Cannabis testing justification undermined | Negative |

### Scenario 3: Independent Drug Classification Commission Established (Legislative)

| Stakeholder | Impact | Net Effect |
|---|---|---|
| All patients | Evidence-based classification improves therapeutic access across substances | Positive |
| All researchers | Science-driven classification process; mandatory review ensures responsiveness | Strongly positive |
| DEA | Loses scheduling authority; retains enforcement of classifications set by Commission | Strongly negative (authority reduced) |
| Congress | Reduced direct control over scheduling; Commission makes binding decisions | Mixed (reduced political risk but also reduced power) |
| Pharmaceutical industry | Faces more responsive classification of natural competitors | Negative |
| Public health | Classification reflects actual risk profiles; improved credibility of government health messaging | Strongly positive |
