# Drug Scheduling: What You Can Do

The federal drug scheduling system affects millions of Americans -- patients denied access to therapeutic substances, researchers blocked from studying Schedule I compounds, people facing criminal penalties disproportionate to actual harm, and communities bearing the weight of racially disparate enforcement. Citizen engagement is essential because scheduling reform requires both Congressional action and sustained administrative pressure. Here is how you can make a difference.

---

## Individual Actions

### Learn and Stay Informed

- Subscribe to *Marijuana Moment* (<https://www.marijuanamoment.net/newsletter/>) for daily coverage of federal cannabis scheduling developments
- Follow the DEA's Federal Register notices on scheduling actions: <https://www.deadiversion.usdoj.gov/fed_regs/>
- Read the National Academies report *The Health Effects of Cannabis and Cannabinoids* (2017) for the scientific consensus on cannabis therapeutic potential
- Track the cannabis rescheduling process through CRS reports at <https://crsreports.congress.gov/> (search "marijuana scheduling")
- Follow MAPS (<https://maps.org/>) for updates on MDMA and psilocybin therapeutic research and scheduling implications

### Make Personal Changes

- Educate yourself about the actual harm profiles of common substances; David Nutt's *The Lancet* (2010) harm ranking is an accessible starting point
- Challenge misconceptions about drug scheduling in personal conversations -- most people are surprised to learn that cannabis is classified alongside heroin while fentanyl is Schedule II
- If you are a healthcare provider, stay current on clinical evidence for Schedule I substances and support institutional participation in clinical trials
- If you are a researcher, consider whether Schedule I substances are relevant to your research questions and investigate the registration process; the National Academies (2017) has documented the research gap
- If you are a veteran, learn about how scheduling affects your access to cannabis and psychedelic therapies through the VA
- If you are an employer, review your workplace drug testing policies to determine whether they are evidence-based or simply tracking federal scheduling; consider whether testing for cannabis use (as opposed to impairment) serves legitimate workplace safety objectives
- If you have been affected by cannabis arrest or conviction, explore expungement resources in your state (many legal states now offer automatic or streamlined expungement)

### Use Your Voice

- **Contact Your Representatives**: Urge your Senators and House member to support scheduling reform legislation (see specific bills below). Call during key moments: when scheduling bills are in committee, during DEA rulemaking comment periods, and during appropriations debates.
- **Submit Public Comments**: When the DEA publishes proposed scheduling rules (as it did in May 2024 for cannabis rescheduling), submit public comments through the Federal Register at <https://www.regulations.gov/>. Comments supporting evidence-based scheduling that cite specific scientific evidence are most impactful.
- **Write Letters to the Editor**: Local newspapers are read by Congressional staff. Write about how scheduling reform would benefit research, patients, or your community. Focus on specific, local impacts.
- **Share Information**: Share evidence-based scheduling reform content on social media. NORML, Drug Policy Alliance, and MAPS produce shareable fact sheets and graphics.

## Community Actions

### Organize Locally

- Form a local advocacy group focused on drug scheduling reform; connect with national organizations (NORML, DPA, MAPS) for resources and training
- Host community forums featuring researchers, patients, and medical professionals who can speak to the impact of scheduling on their work
- Partner with veterans' organizations to advocate for psychedelic therapy research access -- this is one of the most effective bipartisan entry points for scheduling reform
- Connect with local harm reduction organizations to understand how scheduling affects drug checking, naloxone access, and treatment availability

### Build Coalitions

- Build bipartisan coalitions by emphasizing shared interests: conservatives care about federalism and government overreach; libertarians care about individual liberty; progressives care about racial justice; veterans' organizations care about PTSD treatment access
- Engage medical professional associations at the local level -- county medical societies, nursing associations, and pharmacy boards can pass resolutions supporting scheduling reform
- Partner with university researchers who face Schedule I barriers; their stories are compelling and credible with both legislators and the public
- Connect with local business organizations -- chambers of commerce in legal cannabis states increasingly support federal reform for banking access, tax relief, and regulatory clarity
- Engage faith-based organizations that support criminal justice reform and addiction treatment; many evangelical and Catholic organizations have adopted positions supportive of alternatives to incarceration
- Work with local bar associations to promote awareness of scheduling reform among attorneys who handle drug cases; the legal profession's credibility adds weight to reform advocacy

### Participate in the Federal Rulemaking Process

Federal rulemaking provides direct opportunities for citizen engagement on scheduling decisions:

- **Monitor the Federal Register**: The DEA publishes proposed scheduling rules at <https://www.federalregister.gov/> -- search "controlled substances" or "scheduling" for relevant notices
- **Submit public comments**: When the DEA publishes a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (NPRM) for scheduling actions, citizens can submit comments at <https://www.regulations.gov/>. Comments that cite specific scientific evidence, personal experience, or policy analysis are more impactful than form letters
- **Request administrative hearings**: Under the CSA, any interested party may request an administrative hearing on a proposed scheduling action. While individual requests are unlikely to be granted, organized coalition requests carry more weight
- **Track comment periods**: Scheduling comment periods typically last 30-60 days. Organizations like NORML, DPA, and MAPS provide alerts when comment periods open and offer guidance on effective comment writing
- **Review DEA responses**: After comment periods close, the DEA must address substantive comments in its final rule. Reviewing how the DEA responds to public input illuminates the agency's reasoning and provides material for subsequent advocacy

## Political Actions

### Contact Elected Officials

**When to Contact**:

- During DEA rulemaking on cannabis rescheduling (comment periods and hearing schedules published in Federal Register)
- When scheduling reform bills are in committee markup (track at <https://www.congress.gov/>)
- During annual appropriations process (opportunity to defund enforcement of cannabis prohibition or fund scheduling reform studies)
- After major scheduling-related developments (new clinical trial results, state legalization votes, international reform actions)

**What to Say - Template Email**:

```text
Subject: Support Evidence-Based Drug Scheduling Reform

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

I am writing as a constituent from [City, State] to urge your support for reforming the federal drug scheduling system to align with scientific evidence.

Cannabis has been classified as Schedule I -- alongside heroin -- since 1970, despite legalization in 24 states and the District of Columbia, authorization for medical use in 38 states, and HHS's own 2023 recommendation to reschedule it to Schedule III. Meanwhile, fentanyl, which killed approximately 75,000 Americans in 2023, is classified as Schedule II. This system is not based on science.

I urge you to:
1. Support completing cannabis rescheduling to Schedule III
2. Co-sponsor legislation to remove Schedule I barriers to medical research (such as the SAFE Research Act)
3. Support creating mandatory periodic scientific review of all scheduled substances

Evidence-based scheduling reform is a matter of medical research, patient access, and basic government credibility. Thank you for your consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your City, State]
```

**What to Say - Phone Call Script**:

```text
"Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [City]. I'm calling to urge [Representative/Senator] [Name] to support drug scheduling reform. Cannabis has been Schedule I for over 50 years despite HHS recommending rescheduling. I'd like the [Representative/Senator] to support the cannabis rescheduling process and co-sponsor legislation to remove Schedule I research barriers. Can you please note my position? Thank you."
```

### Support Legislation

Current or proposed legislation to support:

- **Cannabis rescheduling (administrative)**: Support the DEA's proposed rule to reschedule cannabis to Schedule III by submitting public comments at <https://www.regulations.gov/>
- **MORE Act (Marijuana Opportunity Reinvestment and Expungement Act)**: Descheduling cannabis, establishing federal regulatory framework, and automatic expungement -- contact House Judiciary Committee members
- **Cannabis Administration and Opportunity Act (CAOA)**: Comprehensive Senate descheduling bill -- contact Senate Judiciary Committee members
- **SAFE Banking Act**: Allows cannabis businesses in legal states to access banking services -- contact Senate Banking Committee members
- **SAFE Research Act**: Removes Schedule I research barriers -- contact Senate HELP Committee members
- **Right to Try for Psychedelics Act**: Expands access to psilocybin and MDMA for terminal patients -- contact Senate sponsors

### Electoral Engagement

**Questions to Ask Candidates**:

1. "Do you support rescheduling or descheduling cannabis at the federal level? If so, what specific legislative action would you take?"
2. "Do you believe the DEA -- a law enforcement agency -- should have final authority over the scientific classification of drugs? If not, what alternative would you support?"
3. "Would you support legislation to create mandatory periodic scientific review of all scheduled substances?"
4. "What is your position on expanding research access for Schedule I substances like psilocybin and MDMA that have received FDA breakthrough therapy designations?"

**Voting Considerations**:

- Check candidates' positions on cannabis legalization and scheduling reform at Vote Smart (<https://votesmart.org/>) and through candidate questionnaires from NORML and DPA
- Review candidates' campaign contributors at OpenSecrets (<https://www.opensecrets.org/>) -- pharmaceutical industry and private prison donations may predict opposition to scheduling reform
- Key committees: Senate Judiciary, House Judiciary, House Energy and Commerce, Senate HELP Committee, Senate Banking (for SAFE Banking Act)
- Look for candidates who have specific policy positions on scheduling reform (rescheduling, descheduling, research access) rather than vague statements about "looking at the issue"
- Support candidates at all levels -- state and local officeholders who have implemented successful cannabis programs often advance to federal office with practical reform experience

**Running for Office**:

If you are considering running for local, state, or federal office, drug scheduling reform is an issue with broad public support that cuts across traditional partisan lines:

- 70% of Americans support cannabis legalization (Gallup, 2023), making this a mainstream position
- Scheduling reform can be framed to appeal to conservatives (federalism, government overreach, individual liberty), moderates (evidence-based policy, fiscal responsibility), and progressives (racial justice, criminal justice reform)
- Practical experience with state-level cannabis or psychedelic regulation provides credibility for federal reform advocacy
- Veterans' advocacy for therapeutic access (cannabis, psilocybin, MDMA) provides one of the most compelling bipartisan narratives for scheduling reform

**Campaign Finance and Advocacy**:

- Donate to candidates who explicitly support scheduling reform -- even small donations signal constituent priorities
- Contribute to PACs focused on cannabis reform: NORML PAC, Marijuana Policy Project PAC, Cannabis Trade Federation PAC
- Attend candidate town halls and ask scheduling reform questions -- questions about cannabis scheduling force candidates to take public positions
- Volunteer for campaigns of pro-reform candidates in competitive races where scheduling reform could be a differentiating issue

### State and Local Action

State and local action has been the most successful pathway for drug scheduling reform to date. Key opportunities include:

- **Support state-level cannabis legalization**: If your state has not yet legalized recreational or medical cannabis, support ballot initiatives or legislative efforts. State legalization creates facts on the ground that drive federal reform.
- **Support state psychedelic reform**: Oregon and Colorado have led on therapeutic psilocybin access. If similar measures are proposed in your state, support them through advocacy, volunteering, and voting.
- **Engage state legislators on scheduling conflicts**: State legislators who operate cannabis regulatory programs are natural allies for federal scheduling reform. Encourage them to formally communicate the federal-state conflict to Congressional delegations.
- **Participate in municipal decriminalization efforts**: Over 15 cities have passed resolutions decriminalizing psychedelics or deprioritizing enforcement. These local actions build momentum for state and federal reform.
- **Attend public meetings**: State cannabis regulatory boards, health department advisory committees, and legislative hearings on drug policy provide opportunities for public testimony.
- **Support social equity programs**: If your state has a cannabis equity program, support it through mentorship, investment, or advocacy. Demonstrating that cannabis reform can produce equitable outcomes strengthens the case for federal reform.

## Professional Actions

- **For Researchers**: Apply for Schedule I research licenses; publish findings on barriers and delays; sign open letters calling for research access reform (such as the 2020 *Science* editorial signed by 150+ scientists); testify before Congress; document the time and cost of Schedule I research compliance as data for reform advocacy
- **For Healthcare Providers**: Support medical association resolutions on scheduling reform; discuss scheduling barriers with patients; participate in clinical trials for Schedule I substances; write to professional journals about clinical implications of scheduling; educate peers about the evidence for cannabis and psychedelic therapies
- **For Attorneys**: File or support rescheduling petitions; challenge scheduling decisions through administrative and judicial review; provide pro bono representation to researchers and patients affected by scheduling; publish legal scholarship on scheduling reform; represent clients seeking cannabis conviction expungement
- **For Educators**: Incorporate evidence-based drug harm information into curricula; invite drug policy researchers to speak; support student research on scheduling topics; teach the history of drug scheduling as a case study in the interaction of science, politics, and race
- **For Veterans**: Share your story about how scheduling barriers affect access to cannabis or psychedelic therapy; engage your local VA facility in conversations about scheduling reform; connect with Veterans Cannabis Project or Heroic Hearts Project; testify at Congressional hearings on veteran healthcare
- **For Journalists**: Cover the scheduling system's inconsistencies and the federal-state conflict; profile researchers impeded by Schedule I barriers; report on the economic impact of scheduling on cannabis businesses and state tax revenue; investigate the lobbying dynamics behind scheduling opposition
- **For Business Leaders**: If you operate a cannabis business, engage with trade associations (NCIA, U.S. Cannabis Council) on federal lobbying; share economic data with elected officials; participate in banking reform advocacy. If you operate in any industry, review your drug testing policies for evidence-based justification.
- **For Financial Professionals**: Advocate for banking access reform (SAFE Banking Act); provide financial services to cannabis businesses where legally permissible; publish analysis of the economic costs of scheduling-based banking restrictions
- **For Public Health Professionals**: Contribute to evidence-based public health monitoring of scheduling changes; publish research on the public health outcomes of state legalization; serve on advisory committees for state cannabis regulatory programs; provide expert testimony on the public health dimensions of scheduling reform

## Measuring Your Impact

How to assess whether your advocacy is making a difference:

- **Track Congressional co-sponsorship**: Monitor the number of co-sponsors on key legislation (MORE Act, SAFE Research Act, SAFE Banking Act). Growing co-sponsorship lists indicate effective advocacy.
- **Monitor DEA rulemaking**: Track the progress of the cannabis rescheduling process and any new scheduling actions. Administrative action often follows sustained public pressure.
- **Follow state-level progress**: Track the number of states with legal cannabis programs and psychedelic reform measures. State-level momentum drives federal action.
- **Measure media coverage**: Increased media coverage of scheduling reform -- especially coverage that frames the issue in terms of science, patients, and research access -- indicates growing public awareness.
- **Assess elected official positions**: Track whether your elected officials have publicly stated positions on scheduling reform. A shift from silence to public support indicates effective constituent pressure.
- **Connect with national organizations**: NORML, DPA, and MAPS track the impact of their advocacy campaigns and can provide data on the effectiveness of citizen engagement.

## Advocacy Toolkit

### Key Talking Points

When discussing drug scheduling reform with others, emphasize:

1. **The Schedule I Absurdity**: "Cannabis is classified alongside heroin as Schedule I while fentanyl -- responsible for 75,000 deaths in 2023 -- is Schedule II. The scheduling system does not reflect science."
2. **Research Barriers**: "Schedule I classification blocks the medical research needed to determine whether substances have therapeutic value. Researchers face 9-12 months of additional licensing just to study Schedule I substances. This is a research barrier, not a safety measure."
3. **Patient Access**: "Over 3.4 million Americans use medical cannabis under physician supervision in state programs, but federal Schedule I status blocks VA doctors from recommending it to veterans and prevents FDA-standard clinical trials that would improve patient care."
4. **Bipartisan Issue**: "70% of Americans support cannabis legalization (Gallup, 2023). Support crosses party lines. The First Step Act showed bipartisan criminal justice reform is possible."

### Common Misconceptions to Counter

| Misconception | Reality | Source |
|---------------|---------|--------|
| "Schedule I means a drug is the most dangerous" | Schedule I means "no accepted medical use," not "most dangerous." Fentanyl (Schedule II) is far more lethal than cannabis (Schedule I). Alcohol and tobacco -- causing 95,000 and 480,000 deaths annually -- are not scheduled at all. | 21 U.S.C. Section 812; CDC WONDER, 2024 |
| "Cannabis was placed in Schedule I based on scientific evidence" | Cannabis was placed in Schedule I as a "temporary" measure in 1970. The Shafer Commission recommended decriminalization in 1972, but Nixon rejected the recommendation for political reasons. | National Commission on Marihuana and Drug Abuse, 1972 |
| "Rescheduling cannabis means legalizing it" | Rescheduling to Schedule III would allow medical prescriptions and research access but would not legalize recreational use. Cannabis would be regulated like ketamine or buprenorphine. | CRS Report R47931, 2024 |
| "The DEA scheduling process is purely scientific" | The DEA is a law enforcement agency that has repeatedly overridden scientific recommendations. The DEA rejected its own ALJ's recommendation on MDMA and ignored the Shafer Commission on cannabis. | Brookings Institution, 2022 |
| "Rescheduling would violate international treaties" | Canada, Uruguay, and Germany have legalized cannabis while remaining parties to the 1961 Convention. The UN removed cannabis from Schedule IV in 2020. | UN Commission on Narcotic Drugs, 2020 |
| "Cannabis legalization increases youth use" | A 2023 JAMA Pediatrics meta-analysis found no significant increase in adolescent cannabis use following state-level legalization. Canada's five-year data also shows stable or declining youth use. | Anderson et al., *JAMA Pediatrics*, 2023 |
| "We need to keep cannabis Schedule I because of the fentanyl crisis" | Cannabis scheduling has no relationship to fentanyl enforcement. Fentanyl is already Schedule II, and its lethality has not been prevented by scheduling. Rescheduling cannabis would free law enforcement resources for fentanyl response. | CDC WONDER, 2024; DEA, 2024 |
| "The scheduling system works for most substances" | The scheduling system has not prevented the worst drug crisis in U.S. history (fentanyl), has not kept pace with novel psychoactive substances (900+ identified since 2009), and has blocked research on therapeutic substances for decades. | DEA National Drug Threat Assessment, 2024 |

## Urgency and Context

Drug scheduling reform is not an abstract policy debate -- it has immediate consequences for millions of Americans:

- **Patients denied treatment**: Over 3.4 million Americans are enrolled in state medical cannabis programs, but their treatment exists in legal limbo due to federal Schedule I status. Veterans with PTSD cannot access cannabis through the VA. Patients with treatment-resistant depression cannot access psilocybin therapy despite breakthrough therapy designation.
- **Researchers blocked from discovery**: Only approximately 780 researchers hold DEA Schedule I registrations, and the average licensing process takes 9-12 months. The scientific knowledge lost to scheduling barriers is incalculable -- two decades of MDMA therapeutic research were lost to the 1985 Schedule I classification, and cannabis research has been impeded for over 50 years.
- **Lives destroyed by enforcement**: Approximately 350,000 Americans are arrested for cannabis offenses annually. An estimated 20 million Americans have been arrested for cannabis since 1970. Black Americans are nearly four times more likely to be arrested despite comparable usage rates.
- **The fentanyl crisis continues**: While scheduling debates focus on cannabis and psychedelics, the scheduling system's inability to keep pace with novel fentanyl analogs contributes to approximately 75,000 synthetic opioid deaths annually.

Every day that scheduling reform is delayed represents continued harm to patients, researchers, communities, and the credibility of evidence-based public health policy. Citizen action is not optional -- it is the mechanism through which the political will for reform is generated and sustained.
