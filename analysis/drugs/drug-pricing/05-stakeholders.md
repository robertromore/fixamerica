# Drug Pricing: Stakeholders

The drug pricing system is shaped by the interactions of several key groups, each with distinct financial interests, political power, and capacity to influence policy outcomes. The system's complexity -- spanning patents, regulation, insurance, and distribution -- means that no single actor controls prices, but the balance of power overwhelmingly favors those who benefit from high prices.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Patients requiring prescription drugs | Bear the direct cost of high prices through copays, coinsurance, and full-price purchases; 29% skip medications due to cost | ~131 million adults with chronic conditions; 66 million Medicare beneficiaries | Affordable medications; transparent pricing; access to new treatments |
| Uninsured and underinsured Americans | Pay full or near-full retail prices; face the most severe affordability barriers | ~27 million uninsured; ~42 million underinsured | Access to discount programs; price caps; generic availability |
| Medicare beneficiaries | Largest single group affected by drug pricing; IRA reforms directly benefit them | ~66 million (2024) | Part D coverage improvements; negotiated prices; out-of-pocket caps |
| Employers purchasing group health insurance | Bear rising drug costs through premiums; largest private payer group | ~157 million Americans covered by employer insurance | Controlling premium growth; PBM accountability; value-based purchasing |
| State Medicaid programs | Must cover most FDA-approved drugs; depend on manufacturer rebates for affordability | ~90 million enrollees across 50 states | Adequate federal rebates; 340B program integrity; supplemental rebate authority |
| Safety-net healthcare providers | Depend on 340B discounts to serve low-income patients; face financial pressure from drug costs | ~50,000 covered entities | 340B program preservation; drug cost predictability; adequate reimbursement |

### Secondary Stakeholders

- **Taxpayers**: Federal spending on prescription drugs through Medicare, Medicaid, VA, and other programs exceeds $200 billion annually, funded by general revenue and payroll taxes. Every dollar spent on inflated drug prices is a dollar unavailable for other federal priorities.
- **Pharmacies (independent and chain)**: Face declining reimbursement from PBMs; independent pharmacies are particularly squeezed by PBM-owned competitors. An estimated 1,500 independent pharmacies closed between 2019 and 2023 (NCPA, 2024), with PBM reimbursement practices cited as the primary financial pressure.
- **Healthcare providers**: Must navigate formulary restrictions, prior authorization requirements, and patient affordability concerns when prescribing. Physicians report spending an average of 14 hours per week on prior authorization paperwork (AMA, 2023).
- **Researchers and academic institutions**: Drug pricing affects research funding through NIH budgets and industry-sponsored clinical trials. Academic medical centers also participate in the 340B program, creating financial interests in drug pricing structures.
- **Generic and biosimilar manufacturers**: Dependent on timely patent expiration and regulatory pathways for market entry. Companies like Teva, Sandoz, Viatris, and Coherus face both patent barriers from brand-name companies and race-to-the-bottom pricing in the generic market.
- **Group purchasing organizations (GPOs)**: Negotiate drug prices for hospital systems and other institutional buyers. The five largest GPOs (Vizient, Premier, HealthTrust, Intalere, ROi) manage approximately $300 billion in annual purchasing, but their fee structures (funded by manufacturer "administrative fees" of 1-3% of sales) create potential conflicts of interest similar to PBM rebate arrangements.
- **State and local governments**: Beyond Medicaid, state and local governments purchase drugs for correctional facilities, public health programs, state employee health plans, and first responder agencies. State employee health plans alone cover approximately 18 million Americans.

### Disproportionately Affected Communities

| Community | Impact | Data |
|---|---|---|
| Uninsured adults | Pay full retail prices; highest non-adherence rates | 44% skip medications due to cost (CDC/NHIS, 2023) |
| Black and Hispanic Americans | Higher non-adherence rates; lower access to specialty drugs | 34% Black, 33% Hispanic non-adherence vs. 26% White (KFF, 2024) |
| Rural populations | Fewer pharmacies; limited generic availability; pharmacy deserts | 630+ rural pharmacy closures (2003-2023); 4.5 million rural Americans live in pharmacy deserts (RUPRI, 2024) |
| Seniors on fixed incomes | Highest per-capita drug spending; most dependent on Medicare Part D | Average senior spends $2,400/year on prescriptions (AARP, 2024) |
| People with chronic conditions | Multiple medication costs compound; highest absolute spending | 131 million adults with chronic conditions; 40% take 4+ medications (CDC, 2023) |
| People with rare diseases | Limited competition; highest per-drug prices; often no generic alternatives | Average orphan drug costs $150,000+/year; 90% have no generic alternative (NORD, 2024) |

This level of vertical integration means that the same corporate entity can influence drug selection (through PBM formulary decisions), drug pricing (through PBM rebate negotiations), drug dispensing (through owned pharmacies), and insurance coverage (through affiliated health plans).

## Who Has Power

### Decision Makers

| Actor | Role | Authority | Resources | Influence Level |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Congress | Legislates drug pricing policy, patent law, Medicare/Medicaid rules | Unlimited within constitutional bounds; budget/appropriations power | Full legislative authority | High |
| CMS (Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services) | Administers Medicare Part D, IRA negotiation program, Medicaid drug rebate program | Regulatory authority over $145 billion in Part D spending; IRA negotiation authority | 6,000+ staff; $1.5 trillion total program spending | High |
| FDA (Food and Drug Administration) | Approves drugs, generics, and biosimilars; administers REMS; manages exclusivity periods | Controls market entry for all drugs; patent listing in Orange Book | ~$7 billion budget (FY2024); 18,000+ employees | High |
| HHS Secretary | Leads IRA drug price negotiation; oversees CMS, FDA, and HRSA | Direct authority over Medicare negotiation; 340B program oversight | Cabinet-level authority | High |
| FTC (Federal Trade Commission) | Enforces antitrust law; investigates PBMs; challenges pay-for-delay | Section 5 authority; merger review; rulemaking | ~$430 million budget (FY2024); 1,100+ staff | Medium |
| U.S. Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) | Grants patents; inter partes review (IPR) proceedings | Determines patent validity; can invalidate patents through IPR | ~$4 billion budget (FY2024, fee-funded) | Medium |
| State legislatures | Enact state drug pricing laws, PBM regulations, insulin copay caps | Authority over state-regulated insurance, Medicaid programs | 50 state legislatures; 7,383 state legislators | Medium |

### Key Organizations and Institutions

- **PhRMA (Pharmaceutical Research and Manufacturers of America)**: The primary trade association for brand-name pharmaceutical manufacturers; 33 member companies; spent $374 million on lobbying in the 2022-2023 cycle; advocates against government price negotiation, international reference pricing, and importation
- **BIO (Biotechnology Innovation Organization)**: Represents biotechnology companies, including many smaller firms; advocates for patent protections and opposes compulsory licensing; 1,000+ member organizations
- **Pharmaceutical Care Management Association (PCMA)**: Trade association for PBMs; advocates against PBM transparency mandates and rebate pass-through requirements; represents CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, OptumRx, and others
- **Association for Accessible Medicines (AAM)**: Represents generic and biosimilar manufacturers; advocates for patent reform, faster FDA approvals, and policies that promote competition
- **National Community Pharmacists Association (NCPA)**: Represents independent pharmacies; advocates against PBM anticompetitive practices and for direct-and-indirect remuneration (DIR) fee reform
- **AARP**: The largest advocacy organization for seniors (38 million members); has campaigned aggressively for Medicare drug price negotiation, insulin copay caps, and out-of-pocket limits
- **Patients for Affordable Drugs**: Non-profit patient advocacy organization; campaigns for drug pricing reform; does not accept pharmaceutical industry funding; founded by David Mitchell, a multiple myeloma patient
- **Mark Cuban Cost Plus Drugs**: Transparent cost-plus pharmacy model demonstrating that generic drugs can be sold profitably at dramatically lower prices; 2,500+ medications; has generated legislative interest in cost-plus pricing mandates
- **340B Health**: Trade association representing 340B-covered hospitals and health systems; advocates for program preservation and expansion; opposes manufacturer restrictions on 340B contract pharmacy arrangements
- **Purchaser Business Group on Health (PBGH)**: Coalition of large employers advocating for healthcare value and cost control; 40+ employer members representing 15 million covered lives; has pushed for PBM transparency and cost-plus drug pricing

### Federal Government Purchasers

The federal government is the single largest purchaser of prescription drugs in the United States, but its purchasing power is fragmented across multiple programs with different pricing mechanisms:

| Program | Drug Spending | Pricing Mechanism | Average Discount |
|---|---|---|---|
| Medicare Part D | $145 billion (FY2023) | Plan negotiation + IRA negotiation (10 drugs) | 15-30% (plan); 38-79% (IRA negotiated) |
| Medicare Part B | $50+ billion | Average Sales Price + 6% | No discount; physician incentive for costlier drugs |
| Medicaid | $30 billion (net) | Mandatory rebate (23.1% + inflation penalty) | 50-70% effective discount |
| VA/DoD | $12 billion | Federal Supply Schedule negotiation | 40-60% discount |
| 340B Program | $53.7 billion (acquisition cost) | Statutory discount (linked to Medicaid rebate) | 25-50% discount |
| IHS (Indian Health Service) | $1.5 billion | FSS + tribal discounts | 40-60% discount |

Source: CMS, VA, HRSA, multiple years

The wide variation in discounts across federal programs highlights a central inefficiency: the VA negotiates prices 40-60% below commercial levels, while Medicare Part B reimburses at ASP+6% with no real negotiation. A unified federal purchasing framework could save tens of billions annually.

### Influencers

- **Academic health economists**: Researchers at institutions like Harvard, Johns Hopkins, and RAND produce the evidence base for drug pricing policy; their findings shape Congressional debate
- **Congressional Budget Office (CBO)**: CBO "scores" (cost estimates) of drug pricing legislation can determine whether a bill advances; CBO's methodology for estimating innovation effects of price regulation has been contentious
- **State attorneys general**: Have pursued pharmaceutical companies for pricing practices, deceptive marketing, and opioid-related harms; multistate settlements have generated billions in recoveries
- **Institutional investors**: Large shareholders (BlackRock, Vanguard, State Street) own significant stakes in pharmaceutical companies and PBMs; investor pressure can influence corporate pricing decisions
- **Patient advocacy groups**: Organizations like the American Diabetes Association, American Cancer Society Cancer Action Network, and disease-specific groups advocate for affordability; some accept pharmaceutical industry funding, which can influence their positions

## Public Opinion on Drug Pricing

Public support for drug pricing reform is among the strongest and most bipartisan of any policy issue in the United States:

| Reform Proposal | Public Support | Partisan Breakdown | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Allow Medicare to negotiate drug prices | 83% | D: 95%, R: 71%, I: 82% | KFF, 2023 |
| $35 insulin copay cap for all insurance | 87% | D: 93%, R: 82%, I: 86% | KFF, 2024 |
| Penalize manufacturers for price increases above inflation | 79% | D: 89%, R: 68%, I: 79% | KFF, 2023 |
| $2,000 annual OOP cap for all insurance | 84% | D: 91%, R: 76%, I: 84% | KFF, 2024 |
| Allow drug importation from Canada | 75% | D: 80%, R: 70%, I: 76% | KFF, 2024 |
| Require PBM transparency | 78% | D: 82%, R: 74%, I: 78% | KFF, 2023 |

This overwhelming public support creates a fundamental disconnect between public preferences and policy outcomes -- a gap explained by the pharmaceutical industry's lobbying power, the complexity of drug pricing policy, and the difficulty of sustaining public attention on the issue.

## Stakeholder Interests and Alignment

### Aligned Interests (Potential Coalition Partners)

| Coalition | Shared Interest | Strength |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| Patients, employers, state Medicaid programs | Lower drug prices; PBM transparency; generic competition | Strong -- combined constituency of 250+ million Americans |
| Generic/biosimilar manufacturers, FTC, patients | Patent reform; anti-pay-for-delay enforcement; faster generic entry | Strong -- aligned economic and policy interests |
| Independent pharmacies, patients, state regulators | PBM regulation; transparent pricing; anti-spread-pricing | Moderate -- aligned but pharmacies have limited political power |
| AARP, patient advocacy groups, labor unions | Medicare negotiation expansion; out-of-pocket caps; insulin affordability | Strong -- large membership bases and public support |
| Safety-net providers, public health advocates, Medicaid programs | 340B program preservation; drug affordability for low-income populations | Moderate -- complicated by 340B program controversies |

### Conflicting Interests

- **Manufacturers vs. Patients/Payers**: Brand-name manufacturers seek to maximize prices and extend monopoly periods; patients and payers seek affordable drugs and timely generic competition. This is the fundamental conflict in drug pricing.
- **PBMs vs. Independent Pharmacies**: PBMs use vertical integration to steer prescriptions to PBM-owned pharmacies and reimburse independent pharmacies below acquisition cost; independent pharmacies seek fair reimbursement and protection from PBM market power.
- **Brand Manufacturers vs. Generic/Biosimilar Manufacturers**: Brand companies use patent evergreening, pay-for-delay, and REMS abuse to block generic entry; generic companies seek timely market access and patent reform.
- **340B Covered Entities vs. Manufacturers**: Covered entities seek to maintain and expand 340B discounts; manufacturers argue the program has grown beyond its original intent and seek to restrict contract pharmacy arrangements.
- **Innovation Advocates vs. Affordability Advocates**: Some stakeholders (industry, some academic researchers, IP attorneys) argue that high prices are necessary to fund innovation; affordability advocates argue that the current system generates excess profits that go to shareholders rather than R&D.

### Power Asymmetry

The most striking feature of the drug pricing stakeholder landscape is the extreme asymmetry of power between those who benefit from high prices and those who bear their costs:

| Factor | Industry (Pro-Status Quo) | Patients/Payers (Pro-Reform) |
|---|---|---|
| Lobbying spending (2022-2023) | $374 million (PhRMA + companies) | ~$15 million (AARP + patient groups combined) |
| Registered lobbyists | 1,500+ | ~100 |
| Campaign contributions (2022) | $64 million | ~$5 million |
| Organizational resources | Concentrated among a few wealthy corporations | Diffuse across millions of individuals |
| Technical expertise | Deep patent, regulatory, and pricing expertise | Limited; depends on academic researchers |
| Time horizon | Long-term; consistent engagement across election cycles | Episodic; often driven by personal crises |
| Media access | Extensive paid advertising and PR | Dependent on earned media and social media |

This 25:1 spending advantage in lobbying, combined with the industry's ability to sustain pressure across decades, explains why drug pricing reform has been so difficult despite overwhelming public support (83% of Americans favor Medicare negotiation, KFF 2023).

### The Pharmaceutical Iron Triangle

The pharmaceutical industry maintains its influence through a classic iron triangle of mutually reinforcing relationships:

1. **Congress**: Pharmaceutical companies and their trade associations provide campaign contributions ($64 million in the 2022 cycle, OpenSecrets) and lobbying (1,500+ registered lobbyists). Key members of the Energy and Commerce, Finance, and HELP committees receive disproportionate contributions.
2. **Regulatory Agencies (FDA, CMS)**: The revolving door between industry and regulators ensures that agency officials often have industry backgrounds and may return to industry after government service. FDA user fees (PDUFA) make the agency partially dependent on industry funding.
3. **Industry**: Pharmaceutical manufacturers benefit from favorable legislation (patent extensions, non-interference clause, 12-year biologic exclusivity) and regulatory decisions (Orange Book patent listings, REMS approvals) that protect pricing power.

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on policy:

- **Lobbying**: PhRMA and individual pharmaceutical companies spent $374 million on lobbying in the 2022-2023 cycle (OpenSecrets, 2024). The pharmaceutical and health products sector deploys approximately 1,500 registered lobbyists, roughly 3 per member of Congress. Key firms include Akin Gump, Covington & Burling, and Sidley Austin.
- **Campaign Contributions**: The pharmaceutical and health products industry contributed $64 million to federal candidates and political committees in the 2022 election cycle (OpenSecrets, 2024). Contributions are bipartisan but heavily weighted toward members of committees with jurisdiction over drug policy.
- **Revolving Door**: Former members of Congress and senior staff frequently join pharmaceutical companies or lobbying firms. Notable examples include former Rep. Billy Tauzin (R-LA), who became PhRMA president after shepherding the Medicare Modernization Act; and numerous former FDA and CMS officials who join pharmaceutical company boards or consulting firms.
- **Astroturf and Patient Group Funding**: Pharmaceutical companies fund patient advocacy groups that then advocate against price controls. A 2020 study in the *Journal of General Internal Medicine* found that 12 of the 14 largest patient advocacy groups received pharmaceutical industry funding, and industry-funded groups were significantly less likely to support drug pricing reform.
- **Academic and Think Tank Funding**: Pharmaceutical companies fund academic research and think tank reports that support industry positions. Industry-funded studies are more likely to find that price regulation harms innovation (Lexchin et al., *BMJ*, 2003).
- **Legal Challenges**: The pharmaceutical industry uses litigation to delay or block reform implementation. Nine federal lawsuits challenging the IRA's constitutionality demonstrate the industry's willingness to use courts as an extension of its lobbying strategy.

## Stakeholder Power Dynamics Over Time

The balance of power in drug pricing has shifted over the past four decades. In the pre-Hatch-Waxman era, manufacturers had relatively unchallenged pricing power. The rise of managed care and PBMs in the 1990s-2000s introduced intermediary bargaining power, but PBMs' evolution from cost-controllers to profit-maximizers has meant that this intermediary power has not consistently benefited patients. Government purchasing power has been deliberately constrained (the Medicare non-interference clause) or fragmented (separate pricing mechanisms for Part D, Part B, Medicaid, VA, and 340B).

Recent developments have begun to shift this balance:

- **IRA negotiation**: For the first time, Medicare has direct bargaining power over drug prices, though limited in scope
- **FTC investigation**: Federal regulatory attention on PBMs threatens the intermediary profit model
- **State affordability boards**: New government actors with potential price-setting authority
- **Market disruptors**: Cost Plus Drugs and compounding pharmacies creating competitive pressure from outside the system
- **Public attention**: Sustained media coverage and patient advocacy raising the political cost of inaction

Whether these shifts produce lasting structural change or are absorbed by the industry's adaptive capacity (as past reforms have been) is the central question of the current era.

## Emerging Stakeholders

Several relatively new actors are reshaping the drug pricing landscape:

- **Mark Cuban Cost Plus Drugs**: As both a pharmacy and a transparency advocate, Cost Plus Drugs has become a de facto stakeholder in drug pricing policy by demonstrating that generic medications can be sold at dramatically lower prices than traditional pharmacy channels. The company's transparent pricing model has generated bipartisan legislative interest and served as evidence in policy debates about PBM markups and supply chain costs.
- **GLP-1 Manufacturers (Novo Nordisk, Eli Lilly)**: The emergence of GLP-1 receptor agonists for diabetes and obesity has made Novo Nordisk and Eli Lilly uniquely consequential stakeholders. The potential $100+ billion annual market for GLP-1 drugs, combined with the dramatic U.S.-international price gap ($900-$1,350/month in the U.S. vs. $79-$140/month abroad), has made GLP-1 pricing a flashpoint for broader drug pricing reform. Congressional hearings on GLP-1 pricing in 2024 drew significant public attention.
- **Compounding Pharmacies**: The emergence of compounding pharmacies producing semaglutide (Ozempic's active ingredient) at $100-$200 per month has created a new stakeholder category. Compounders argue they are filling a market gap during drug shortages; manufacturers argue compounders are infringing patents and endangering patient safety. The legal and regulatory framework for compounding in the drug pricing context remains contested.
- **State Prescription Drug Affordability Boards (PDABs)**: The 9 states plus D.C. with PDABs have created new institutional stakeholders -- government bodies with authority to review and potentially limit drug prices. As PDABs gain experience and demonstrate results (or face legal challenges), they will become increasingly important actors in the drug pricing ecosystem.
- **Institutional Investors and ESG Advocates**: Shareholder advocacy groups (ICCR, As You Sow) have filed resolutions demanding greater pharmaceutical pricing transparency and access. While these resolutions rarely pass, they generate media attention and pressure management to address pricing concerns. Some large institutional investors (CalPERS, New York State Common Retirement Fund) have publicly supported drug pricing reform.
- **Nonprofit Generic Manufacturers (Civica Rx)**: Founded in 2018 by health systems, philanthropies, and the VA, Civica Rx manufactures essential generic drugs at cost-plus prices and sells directly to member hospitals. Civica's 2024 partnership with Cost Plus Drugs to produce insulin at $30 per vial demonstrated that nonprofit manufacturing can provide an alternative to the for-profit generic market. If the Civica model scales, it could fundamentally alter the stakeholder landscape by introducing a manufacturer whose incentives are aligned with affordability rather than profit maximization.
