# Drug Pricing: Overview

## Overview

Americans pay far more for prescription drugs than citizens of any other developed nation. Total U.S. prescription drug spending reached approximately $405 billion in 2023, with per capita spending roughly 2.5 times the average of other OECD countries. A single vial of insulin that costs $10 to manufacture can retail for over $300 in the United States while selling for $30-$50 in Canada or Europe. The cancer drug Keytruda (pembrolizumab), the world's top-selling pharmaceutical product at $25 billion in global revenue in 2023, costs U.S. payers roughly $191,000 per year -- more than double the price in many European markets. These prices are not driven by manufacturing costs or market forces alone; they are the product of deliberate policy choices that shield the pharmaceutical industry from the price competition and government negotiation that exist in every other wealthy country.

The U.S. pharmaceutical market is shaped by several distinctive features that drive costs. Three pharmacy benefit managers (CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, and OptumRx) control approximately 80% of prescription drug claims, operating with minimal transparency while extracting billions in rebates, spread pricing margins, and fees. Brand-name manufacturers routinely extend their effective monopoly periods far beyond the 20-year patent term by filing dozens or hundreds of secondary patents on minor modifications (AbbVie accumulated 132 patents on Humira alone). The generic drug market, while successfully driving generic utilization to 90% of prescriptions, suffers from a structural crisis that has produced a record 323 active drug shortages (ASHP, 2024). New GLP-1 receptor agonists for diabetes and obesity (Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro) list at $900-$1,350 per month in the U.S. while costing $79-$140 per month in other countries, with manufacturing costs estimated at $5-$40 per month.

The structural foundations of high drug prices in the United States include the absence of comprehensive government price negotiation (only partially addressed by the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022), an expansive patent system that enables brand-name manufacturers to extend monopoly periods through "evergreening" and "pay-for-delay" settlements with generic competitors, a pharmacy benefit manager (PBM) system that operates with minimal transparency while extracting billions in rebates that often fail to reach patients, and the prohibition on commercial drug importation from countries with lower prices. The Medicare Modernization Act of 2003 explicitly prohibited Medicare from negotiating drug prices for nearly two decades, a provision that cost taxpayers an estimated $90 billion or more over that period.

The Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) of 2022 represented the most significant drug pricing reform in a generation, authorizing Medicare to negotiate prices on a limited set of high-expenditure drugs, capping insulin copays at $35 per month for Medicare beneficiaries, and penalizing manufacturers who raise prices faster than inflation. The first round of negotiated prices, announced in August 2024, achieved savings of 38-79% on 10 drugs, with CMS projecting $6 billion in first-year savings. However, the IRA's negotiation authority covers only 10 drugs in its first year (rising to 20 per year by 2029), applies only to Medicare (not commercial insurance), and excludes drugs in their first 9-13 years on the market. Comprehensive reform remains unfinished.

At the state level, 37 states have enacted some form of drug pricing legislation, and 9 states plus the District of Columbia have established prescription drug affordability boards with varying authority to set upper payment limits on expensive drugs. The Federal Trade Commission issued a landmark interim report on pharmacy benefit managers in July 2024, finding that the three largest PBMs engage in practices that may increase rather than decrease drug costs. These developments have created the most active period of drug pricing reform activity in U.S. history, but the fundamental structural drivers of high prices -- patent abuse, PBM opacity, and the absence of comprehensive price regulation -- remain largely intact.

High drug prices have direct, measurable consequences for patient health. An estimated 29% of U.S. adults report not taking medications as prescribed due to cost, including skipping doses, cutting pills, or abandoning prescriptions entirely. This medication non-adherence contributes to an estimated 125,000 preventable deaths and $290 billion in avoidable healthcare costs annually. The burden falls disproportionately on the uninsured (44% non-adherence), the elderly, low-income households (38% non-adherence for incomes below $25,000), and communities of color (34% non-adherence for Black adults, 33% for Hispanic adults).

The pharmaceutical industry spent $374 million on lobbying during the 2022-2023 election cycle -- more than any other sector -- and deployed approximately 1,500 registered lobbyists, roughly three for every member of Congress. This lobbying infrastructure has successfully blocked or weakened drug pricing legislation for decades. Between 2000 and 2022, at least 35 significant drug pricing bills were introduced in Congress; only two enacted meaningful price controls. The industry has also filed nine federal lawsuits challenging the IRA's constitutionality, though all courts that have ruled to date have upheld the law.

## Why This Matters

Drug pricing is not merely an economic or healthcare policy issue -- it is a matter of life and death. The downstream effects of unaffordable medications cascade through the healthcare system and American society:

- **Health Outcomes**: An estimated 125,000 Americans die annually from medication non-adherence driven primarily by cost (NCPA/IMS Health, 2023). These are preventable deaths that do not occur in countries with regulated drug prices.
- **Economic Burden**: Medication non-adherence generates an estimated $290 billion in avoidable healthcare costs annually through emergency room visits, hospitalizations, and disease progression that could have been prevented by consistent medication use.
- **Inequality**: The burden of high drug prices falls disproportionately on the uninsured (44% non-adherence rate), low-income households (38% for incomes below $25,000), and communities of color (34% for Black adults, 33% for Hispanic adults). Drug pricing is a health equity issue.
- **Fiscal Impact**: Federal spending on prescription drugs through Medicare, Medicaid, VA, and other programs exceeds $200 billion annually. Every dollar spent on inflated drug prices represents a dollar unavailable for other public priorities.
- **System Integrity**: The pharmaceutical industry's $374 million lobbying apparatus -- the largest of any sector -- represents a direct threat to democratic governance, as the gap between overwhelming public support for reform (83% favor Medicare negotiation) and legislative outcomes demonstrates.
- **Employer and Wage Effects**: Rising prescription drug costs are a primary driver of health insurance premium increases for the 157 million Americans with employer-sponsored coverage. Every dollar employers spend on inflated drug costs is a dollar not available for wages, creating a hidden drag on worker compensation that disproportionately affects middle- and lower-income workers.
- **Innovation Misdirection**: The current pricing system incentivizes pharmaceutical companies to invest in incremental modifications of existing drugs (which extend patent monopolies) rather than genuinely novel therapies. A 2023 study in *JAMA Internal Medicine* found that 57% of new cancer drugs approved between 2010 and 2020 showed no statistically significant overall survival benefit at the time of FDA approval, yet many were priced at $100,000-$300,000 per year.

## Scope

This analysis covers the key aspects of drug pricing policy:

- **Price Levels and International Comparisons**: How U.S. drug prices compare to other countries and what drives the differential
- **Patent and Exclusivity Practices**: Patent evergreening, pay-for-delay settlements, REMS abuse, patent thickets, and other tactics that delay generic and biosimilar competition
- **Medicare and Government Purchasing**: Medicare Part D, the IRA drug price negotiation program, Medicaid drug rebates, the 340B program, and VA/DoD purchasing
- **Pharmacy Benefit Managers and the Rebate System**: The role of PBMs in drug pricing, rebate flow, spread pricing, rebate walls, and transparency failures
- **Cost Transparency and Patient Burden**: Out-of-pocket costs, list price vs. net price dynamics, copay accumulator programs, and patient assistance
- **Generic and Biosimilar Competition**: Barriers to generic entry, biosimilar adoption rates, and policies to accelerate competition
- **Emerging Pricing Challenges**: GLP-1 receptor agonist affordability, cell and gene therapy financing, and the role of compounding pharmacies
- **State-Level Reform**: Prescription drug affordability boards, state transparency laws, insulin copay caps, PBM regulation, and drug importation programs
- **Alternative Models**: Cost-plus pharmacy models (Mark Cuban Cost Plus Drugs), international reference pricing, value-based pricing frameworks, and direct government manufacturing proposals
- **International Comparisons**: Comprehensive price evaluation systems in Germany (AMNOG), the UK (NICE), Japan, and Australia that achieve lower prices while maintaining access to new therapies
- **Drug Shortage Crisis**: Root causes of generic drug shortages, manufacturing quality problems, supply chain fragility, and policy responses including strategic reserves and minimum viable pricing
- **Pharmaceutical Industry Political Economy**: Industry lobbying, campaign contributions, revolving door dynamics, and the structural reasons why reform has been so difficult despite overwhelming public support

## Core Tensions and Tradeoffs

- **Innovation vs. Affordability**: The pharmaceutical industry argues that high U.S. prices fund the research and development that produces new treatments. However, the 15 largest pharmaceutical companies spent more on stock buybacks and dividends ($747 billion, 2012-2021) than on R&D ($552 billion) over the same period. The tension is real but often overstated by industry lobbyists to block any price regulation.
- **Patent Protection vs. Market Competition**: Patents are essential to incentivize drug development, but the current system enables manufacturers to extend effective monopoly periods well beyond the intended 20-year patent term through evergreening strategies, sometimes blocking generics for 30+ years. Balancing innovation incentives with timely generic entry is a central policy challenge.
- **List Price vs. Net Price Transparency**: The gap between a drug's "list price" (what is published) and "net price" (what is actually paid after rebates and discounts) can exceed 50%. This opacity benefits PBMs and manufacturers while obscuring true costs from patients and policymakers. The gross-to-net bubble has expanded from 18% in 2007 to 52% in 2023 (Drug Channels, 2024), with patients bearing the costs through copays and coinsurance calculated on inflated list prices.
- **Federal Authority vs. Market Freedom**: Government price negotiation is standard practice in every other developed country but has been fiercely opposed in the U.S. as an intrusion on free markets. The IRA's limited negotiation authority faces ongoing legal challenges from the pharmaceutical industry. The VA has negotiated drug prices since its founding, achieving prices 40-60% below Medicare Part D (CBO, 2005), demonstrating that government negotiation and pharmaceutical market participation can coexist.
- **Medicare Coverage vs. Commercial Market**: The IRA's negotiation authority applies only to Medicare, leaving the 177 million Americans with employer-sponsored or individual insurance subject to unconstrained prices. This creates a two-tier system where the same drug can cost dramatically different amounts depending on the patient's coverage.
- **Generic Affordability vs. Supply Security**: The generic drug market's success in driving prices down (90% of prescriptions filled with generics) has paradoxically created a supply crisis. Average generic drug prices fell from $0.48 to $0.24 per prescription between 2012 and 2022 (IQVIA, 2023), driving manufacturer exits and producing a record 323 active drug shortages (ASHP, 2024). Policies must balance affordability with sustainable manufacturing incentives.
- **Short-Term Savings vs. Long-Term Access**: One-time gene therapies costing $1-$3.5 million per patient (Hemgenix at $3.5M, Casgevy at $2.2M, Zolgensma at $2.1M) challenge existing insurance and payment frameworks. These potentially curative treatments may save money over a patient's lifetime but require financing mechanisms that current annual insurance models cannot easily accommodate.

## Key Questions

1. How should the United States balance pharmaceutical innovation incentives with the need for affordable medications, and does the current pricing system actually maximize innovation?
2. Should Medicare's drug price negotiation authority be expanded to cover more drugs more quickly, and should negotiated prices extend to commercial insurance markets?
3. What reforms to the patent system, FDA approval process, and PBM regulation would most effectively increase competition and lower prices?
4. How can international reference pricing or drug importation be implemented while maintaining drug safety and supply chain integrity?
5. Should pharmacy benefit managers be required to pass 100% of manufacturer rebates to patients at the point of sale, and should PBMs be required to divest from vertically integrated pharmacies?
6. How should the U.S. healthcare system finance one-time gene therapies costing $1-$3.5 million per patient, and what pricing frameworks are appropriate for curative treatments?
7. What role should transparency models like Mark Cuban Cost Plus Drugs play in demonstrating the gap between manufacturing costs and retail prices, and can cost-plus pricing be scaled to brand-name drugs?
8. How should the United States address the generic drug shortage crisis, and should the federal government establish a Strategic Pharmaceutical Reserve or invest in domestic generic manufacturing capacity?
9. What role should states play as laboratories for drug pricing reform, and do prescription drug affordability boards represent a viable model for state-level price regulation?
10. How can the 340B Drug Pricing Program be reformed to ensure that its $53.7 billion in drug purchases primarily benefits the low-income patients it was designed to serve?

## Emerging Disruptions

Several developments are reshaping the drug pricing landscape:

- **Mark Cuban Cost Plus Drugs**: Launched in 2022, Cost Plus Drugs (costplusdrugs.com) sells generic medications at a transparent markup of manufacturer cost plus 15% margin plus a $5 pharmacist fee plus $5 shipping. The model has demonstrated that many generic drugs can be sold profitably at 50-90% below typical pharmacy prices, exposing the magnitude of PBM and supply chain markups. By 2024, Cost Plus offered over 2,500 medications and had expanded to physical pharmacies. The model's transparency has pressured traditional pharmacy channels and generated bipartisan legislative interest in cost-plus pricing requirements.
- **GLP-1 Affordability Crisis**: The emergence of GLP-1 receptor agonists (Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro, Zepbound) for diabetes and obesity has created a potential fiscal crisis. These drugs list at $900-$1,350 per month in the U.S. but cost $79-$140 per month in other countries. With an estimated 42 million Americans clinically eligible for GLP-1 obesity treatment, unrestricted coverage at current prices would exceed $400 billion annually -- more than current total U.S. drug spending. Compounding pharmacies have begun producing semaglutide (Ozempic's active ingredient) at $100-$200 per month, provoking legal challenges from Novo Nordisk.
- **State Prescription Drug Affordability Boards**: Nine states and the District of Columbia have established affordability boards with varying authority to set upper payment limits on expensive drugs. Colorado's board, the most advanced, began reviewing drugs for potential price-setting in 2024. These state experiments may provide models for federal action.
- **Gene Therapy Pricing**: One-time gene therapies with list prices of $1-$3.5 million per patient (Hemgenix at $3.5 million, Casgevy at $2.2 million, Zolgensma at $2.1 million) challenge existing insurance and reimbursement frameworks. Insurers and Medicaid programs struggle to absorb multi-million-dollar one-time costs, and no consensus exists on how to finance these treatments sustainably. The FDA pipeline includes over 60 gene and cell therapies expected to reach the market by 2030, making this financing challenge increasingly urgent. Proposed solutions include outcomes-based annuity payments, reinsurance pools, and federal gene therapy financing facilities modeled on infrastructure financing authorities.

## Vision of Success

A successful drug pricing policy in the 21st century would feature:

- **Affordable Medications for All**: No American skips prescribed medications due to cost; out-of-pocket spending is capped at reasonable levels across all insurance types, and uninsured patients have access to fair prices
- **Robust Generic and Biosimilar Competition**: Generic drugs enter the market promptly after patent expiration, biosimilar adoption reaches 80%+ within 3 years of approval, and anti-competitive tactics like pay-for-delay are effectively prohibited
- **Transparent Pricing**: Drug prices reflect actual costs and value; the rebate system is reformed to pass savings directly to patients; PBM conflicts of interest are eliminated through structural reform
- **Sustainable Innovation**: Pharmaceutical R&D investment grows through targeted public funding and reformed incentive structures, while drug prices reflect therapeutic value rather than monopoly power
- **International Parity**: U.S. drug prices fall within a reasonable range of prices paid in other wealthy democracies, ending the era in which American patients bear a disproportionate share of global pharmaceutical costs
- **Supply Chain Integrity**: Generic drug shortages are addressed through strategic reserves, manufacturing incentives, and minimum viable pricing; the pharmaceutical supply chain is resilient against disruption
- **Accountable PBMs**: Pharmacy benefit managers operate as transparent fiduciaries for patients and plan sponsors, with conflicts of interest eliminated through structural reform, 100% rebate pass-through, and divestiture of vertically integrated pharmacy operations
- **Evidence-Based Policy**: Drug pricing policy is informed by rigorous evidence on costs, therapeutic value, and international comparisons, with an independent U.S. health technology assessment body comparable to the UK's NICE or Germany's IQWiG evaluating whether drug prices reflect clinical benefit
