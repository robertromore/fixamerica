# Drug Pricing: History

## Historical Development

The high cost of prescription drugs in the United States is not an accident of market forces but the cumulative result of deliberate policy choices made over four decades. The history of U.S. drug pricing reflects a recurring pattern: Congress creates frameworks intended to balance innovation with affordability, and the pharmaceutical industry exploits, circumvents, or lobbies to weaken those frameworks. Each era has produced new tools for controlling prices -- and new strategies by industry to maintain pricing power.

---

### The Pre-Modern Era (Before 1984): Unregulated Markets

- **Limited Patent Exploitation**: Before the mid-1980s, the pharmaceutical market was smaller, drug development was less capital-intensive, and the modern strategies of patent evergreening did not yet exist. Most drugs were small-molecule chemicals with relatively short development cycles.
- **Government as Price Setter**: The federal government set prices for drugs purchased through the VA and military health systems. Medicare did not cover outpatient prescription drugs, so the question of government negotiation for a large civilian population had not yet arisen.
- **Early Generic Market**: The generic drug market was underdeveloped. Without a clear regulatory pathway for generic approvals, brand-name manufacturers faced limited competition even after patents expired, and generic drugs accounted for a small fraction of prescriptions. Before 1984, a generic manufacturer essentially had to repeat the full New Drug Application (NDA) process, including expensive clinical trials, making generic development economically impractical for many drugs.
- **Growing Research Enterprise**: The National Institutes of Health (NIH) funded basic biomedical research that laid the foundation for many blockbuster drugs, but the translation from basic research to commercial products was slower and less systematic than it would later become.
- **Kefauver-Harris Amendment (1962)**: Following the thalidomide crisis, the Kefauver-Harris Amendment required manufacturers to demonstrate drug effectiveness (not just safety) before FDA approval. This increased development costs and timelines, which manufacturers later cited as justification for extended patent protection. The amendment also established the framework for Good Manufacturing Practices (GMP) that governs drug quality to this day.
- **Early Price Controversies**: Even before the modern era, drug pricing generated political controversy. Senator Estes Kefauver's 1959-1962 Senate hearings exposed pharmaceutical company profit margins exceeding those of any other industry and documented instances of identical drugs sold under brand names at markups of 1,000%+ over generic equivalents. Kefauver's proposed price regulation was stripped from the final 1962 legislation under industry pressure.

### Key Legislation: Pre-Modern Era

| Year | Legislation | Impact |
|------|-------------|--------|
| 1938 | Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act | Required drugs to be proven safe before marketing; established FDA approval authority |
| 1951 | Durham-Humphrey Amendment | Distinguished prescription from over-the-counter drugs; created the prescription drug category |
| 1962 | Kefauver-Harris Amendment | Required proof of drug effectiveness (not just safety); established Good Manufacturing Practices |

### The Hatch-Waxman Era (1984-2002): Balancing Innovation and Access

- **Drug Price Competition and Patent Term Restoration Act (1984)**: The Hatch-Waxman Act created the modern framework for U.S. drug pricing by establishing two key mechanisms: (1) the Abbreviated New Drug Application (ANDA) process, which allowed generic manufacturers to gain FDA approval by demonstrating bioequivalence to brand-name drugs rather than repeating full clinical trials, and (2) patent term restoration, which compensated brand-name manufacturers for time lost during FDA review by extending effective patent life by up to 5 years.
- **Generic Drug Boom**: Hatch-Waxman catalyzed the growth of the generic drug industry. Generic utilization rose from approximately 19% of prescriptions in 1984 to over 47% by 2000. Generic drugs saved consumers an estimated $734 billion between 1984 and 2000 (Generic Pharmaceutical Association, 2002).
- **Paragraph IV Challenges**: The Act created a pathway for generic manufacturers to challenge brand-name patents before expiration (Paragraph IV certification), with a 180-day exclusivity incentive for the first generic filer. This mechanism was intended to promote generic entry but also created the conditions for pay-for-delay settlements.
- **Emergence of Pay-for-Delay**: Beginning in the late 1990s, brand-name manufacturers discovered they could pay generic companies to abandon or delay Paragraph IV challenges. The FTC documented the first major pay-for-delay settlement in 1999 (Hytrin/Abbott Laboratories). By the early 2000s, pay-for-delay had become a widespread industry strategy.
- **Medicaid Drug Rebate Program (1990)**: The Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1990 (OBRA 90) required drug manufacturers to provide rebates to Medicaid in exchange for Medicaid coverage. The program established the "best price" requirement -- manufacturers must offer Medicaid the lowest price available to any purchaser -- and created an inflation-based additional rebate for price increases exceeding CPI.
- **340B Program Established (1992)**: The Veterans Health Care Act of 1992 created the 340B Drug Pricing Program, requiring manufacturers participating in Medicaid to offer discounted outpatient drugs to safety-net providers. Initially limited to a narrow set of entities, the program would expand dramatically in subsequent decades.
- **Rise of Specialty Drugs**: The 1990s saw the emergence of high-cost biologic drugs, beginning with treatments like erythropoietin (Epogen/Procrit) for anemia and interferons for multiple sclerosis. These drugs cost thousands of dollars per month and created new affordability challenges that the Hatch-Waxman framework was not designed to address.
- **PBM Emergence and Growth**: Pharmacy benefit managers emerged in the late 1980s and grew rapidly through the 1990s as employers sought to control rising prescription drug costs. Early PBMs functioned primarily as claims processors and formulary managers, but by the late 1990s they had begun negotiating manufacturer rebates and consolidating into larger entities. The seeds of the current PBM oligopoly were planted during this period, with Merck's acquisition of Medco (1993) representing the first major vertical integration between a manufacturer and a PBM.
- **Generic Drug Scandal (1989)**: An FDA investigation revealed widespread corruption in the generic drug approval process, with generic manufacturers bribing FDA reviewers to expedite approvals and submitting fraudulent bioequivalence data. The scandal led to criminal convictions and temporarily undermined public confidence in generic drugs, but ultimately strengthened the ANDA review process through reforms in the early 1990s.

### The Medicare Part D Era (2003-2009): The Non-Negotiation Clause

- **Medicare Modernization Act of 2003 (MMA)**: The MMA established Medicare Part D, the first outpatient prescription drug benefit for Medicare beneficiaries, effective January 2006. The program was structured as a subsidy to private insurance plans rather than a direct government benefit, and -- critically -- Section 1860D-11(i) of the Social Security Act explicitly prohibited the Secretary of Health and Human Services from "interfering" with drug price negotiations between plans and manufacturers. This "non-interference clause" was the pharmaceutical industry's primary legislative achievement in exchange for its support of the bill.
- **Industry Lobbying**: PhRMA and individual drug companies spent an estimated $116 million on lobbying in 2003 to shape the MMA. Former Rep. Billy Tauzin (R-LA), who shepherded the bill through the House, became president of PhRMA in 2004 at a reported salary of $2 million per year, one of the most prominent examples of the pharmaceutical revolving door.
- **Coverage Gap ("Donut Hole")**: The original Part D design included a notorious "donut hole" in which beneficiaries paid 100% of drug costs between $2,250 and $5,100 in annual spending, creating a cliff that forced many seniors to choose between medications and other necessities.
- **Cost Overruns**: Medicare Part D cost significantly more than originally projected. The CBO initially estimated $395 billion over 10 years (2004-2013); actual spending exceeded $550 billion over that period. The absence of price negotiation authority was a primary cost driver.
- **International Comparison**: The MMA's non-interference clause made the U.S. the only major developed country in which the government purchaser of drugs for elderly citizens could not negotiate prices. The VA, by contrast, negotiated drug prices averaging 40% below Medicare Part D prices for the same medications (CBO, 2005).
- **340B Program Expansion**: The MMA and subsequent legislation expanded 340B eligibility to include children's hospitals, critical access hospitals, sole community hospitals, and rural referral centers. This expansion, combined with the growth of contract pharmacy arrangements, transformed 340B from a modest safety-net program into a multi-billion-dollar system. By 2010, 340B purchase volume had reached $12 billion, up from approximately $3 billion in 2003.
- **PBM Consolidation**: The post-MMA period saw rapid PBM consolidation. Express Scripts merged with Medco in 2012, creating a PBM with over $100 billion in annual drug spending under management. CVS Caremark formed through CVS's 2007 acquisition of Caremark Rx. By the end of this era, three PBMs controlled over 70% of the market, up from a fragmented market in the early 2000s. This consolidation would later become a central concern of drug pricing reformers.
- **Early Failed Negotiation Attempts**: In 2007, the Democratic-controlled House passed H.R. 4, the Medicare Prescription Drug Price Negotiation Act, by a vote of 255-170. The bill would have required the HHS Secretary to negotiate drug prices for Part D. The bill died in the Senate, where the pharmaceutical industry's lobbying prevented the 60 votes needed to overcome a filibuster. This marked the first of many Congressional attempts to reverse the non-interference clause.

### The ACA and Biologic Competition Era (2010-2021): Incremental Progress

- **Affordable Care Act (2010)**: The ACA included several drug pricing provisions: (1) phased closure of the Medicare Part D donut hole by 2020 through manufacturer discounts and federal subsidies; (2) creation of the Biologics Price Competition and Innovation Act (BPCIA), establishing a regulatory pathway for biosimilar approval; (3) increased Medicaid drug rebates from 15.1% to 23.1% for brand-name drugs.
- **Biosimilar Pathway**: The BPCIA granted 12 years of market exclusivity to reference biologics before biosimilar competition could begin -- significantly longer than the 5 years of data exclusivity for small-molecule drugs. The first U.S. biosimilar (Zarxio, a biosimilar to Neupogen) was approved in 2015, nearly 6 years after the BPCIA was enacted. By contrast, Europe had approved its first biosimilar in 2006.
- **Price Increases as Business Strategy**: Between 2010 and 2020, average annual brand-name drug price increases exceeded inflation by 5-10 percentage points annually. Pharmaceutical manufacturers adopted a strategy of regular, above-inflation list price increases as a primary revenue growth mechanism, even for drugs with no new clinical evidence supporting higher prices.
- **High-Profile Price Shocks**: Public outrage over drug pricing surged following several highly publicized incidents:
    - **Sovaldi (2013)**: Gilead Sciences priced its hepatitis C cure at $84,000 for a 12-week course ($1,000 per pill), provoking a Senate investigation.
    - **Daraprim (2015)**: Martin Shkreli's Turing Pharmaceuticals acquired Daraprim and raised its price from $13.50 to $750 per tablet overnight (5,456% increase).
    - **EpiPen (2016)**: Mylan raised the price of the EpiPen auto-injector from $94 to $609 between 2007 and 2016, a 548% increase for a product containing approximately $1 of epinephrine.
    - **Insulin Pricing (2012-2019)**: The average annual cost of insulin tripled between 2012 and 2019, from approximately $3,000 to $9,600, despite the drug having been discovered in 1921 and production costs of approximately $2-$6 per vial.
- **Trump Administration Actions (2017-2020)**: President Trump signed several executive orders on drug pricing in 2020, including the Most Favored Nation (MFN) rule linking Medicare Part B drug prices to international prices. Most provisions were either blocked by courts or reversed by the Biden administration before taking effect. The administration's "Rebate Rule" -- which would have required PBM rebates to be passed through to patients at the point of sale -- was finalized but never implemented.
- **State-Level Innovation Begins**: As federal reform stalled, states began experimenting with drug pricing legislation. Maryland became the first state to enact an anti-price-gouging law for generic drugs in 2017, though it was initially struck down as unconstitutional before being upheld on appeal in revised form. California enacted the first state drug price transparency law (SB 17) in 2017, requiring manufacturers to provide advance notice of price increases exceeding 16%. By 2020, over 20 states had enacted some form of drug pricing transparency legislation.
- **Insulin Pricing Crisis Intensifies**: Between 2012 and 2019, the average annual cost of insulin tripled from approximately $3,000 to $9,600, despite manufacturing costs of approximately $2-$6 per vial. The three insulin manufacturers (Eli Lilly, Novo Nordisk, Sanofi) were accused of "shadow pricing" -- coordinating list price increases in lockstep. State attorneys general and private plaintiffs filed dozens of lawsuits alleging price-fixing, though proving explicit coordination proved legally difficult.
- **Congressional Stalemate**: Multiple bipartisan drug pricing bills advanced in Congress between 2019 and 2021, including the Elijah Cummings Lower Drug Costs Now Act (H.R. 3) and the bipartisan Senate Finance Committee bill (S. 2543, the Prescription Drug Pricing Reduction Act). None passed both chambers due to pharmaceutical industry lobbying and partisan disagreements over the scope of government intervention. The pharmaceutical industry spent $306 million on lobbying in the 2019-2020 cycle alone (OpenSecrets, 2021).
- **Orphan Drug Pricing**: The Orphan Drug Act of 1983, which provided tax credits and 7-year market exclusivity for drugs treating rare diseases affecting fewer than 200,000 Americans, was increasingly exploited during this period. Manufacturers priced orphan drugs at $100,000-$750,000 per year, with some reaching over $1 million annually. A 2019 JAMA study found that orphan drug designations increased from 58 per year (2000-2005) to 233 per year (2015-2019), and that many orphan drugs were later approved for common conditions, generating billions in revenue from a program designed for rare diseases.
- **Rise of Specialty Drug Spending**: Specialty drugs (typically biologics, gene therapies, and complex small molecules) grew from 28% of total drug spending in 2010 to 50% in 2020, despite representing only 2% of prescriptions dispensed (IQVIA, 2021). This concentration of spending in a small number of high-cost drugs fundamentally changed the drug pricing debate from a consumer issue (generic availability) to a fiscal issue (catastrophic costs for insurers, employers, and government programs).

### The PBM Consolidation Timeline

The consolidation of pharmacy benefit managers into vertically integrated conglomerates is one of the most consequential structural changes in the U.S. drug pricing system over the past two decades:

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1993 | Merck acquires Medco | First major manufacturer-PBM vertical integration |
| 2003 | FTC forces Merck to divest Medco | Vertical integration deemed anti-competitive; Medco spun off as independent company |
| 2007 | CVS acquires Caremark Rx | Created CVS Caremark (PBM + retail pharmacy) |
| 2012 | Express Scripts merges with Medco | Created largest PBM by claims volume; market concentration surges |
| 2015 | UnitedHealth Group acquires Catamaran (PBM) | Merged into OptumRx; established UnitedHealth as major PBM player |
| 2018 | CVS Health acquires Aetna (insurer) | Created first PBM + insurer + pharmacy + retail clinic conglomerate |
| 2018 | Cigna acquires Express Scripts | Combined insurer + PBM; created Evernorth |
| 2023 | Top 3 PBMs control ~80% of market | Highest concentration in PBM industry history |

This consolidation occurred with minimal antitrust scrutiny. The FTC approved the CVS-Aetna and Cigna-Express Scripts mergers with limited conditions, and the resulting oligopoly now controls virtually all prescription drug purchasing for American patients. The failure to prevent PBM consolidation is widely regarded by health economists as one of the most consequential antitrust enforcement failures of the 21st century. The effects -- higher drug costs, pharmacy closures, and reduced competition -- are now being documented by the FTC more than a decade after the mergers that caused them were approved.

### The IRA Era (2022-Present): Breakthrough with Limitations

- **Inflation Reduction Act of 2022**: After decades of failed efforts, the IRA authorized Medicare drug price negotiation for the first time, along with the $35 insulin copay cap, inflation-based rebate penalties, and the $2,000 out-of-pocket cap. The legislation passed with zero Republican votes and through the budget reconciliation process, which limited its scope.
- **First Negotiation Cycle (2023-2024)**: CMS selected 10 high-expenditure Medicare drugs in August 2023 and announced negotiated prices in August 2024, with savings ranging from 38% to 79% off list prices. The first negotiated prices take effect in January 2026.
- **Industry Legal Challenges**: Pharmaceutical companies and trade groups filed nine federal lawsuits challenging the IRA's constitutionality. As of early 2025, all judicial rulings have upheld the law, with courts finding that manufacturers voluntarily participate in Medicare and can exit if they choose. Cases remain in various stages of appeal.
- **Voluntary Price Reductions**: In anticipation of the IRA and public scrutiny, several manufacturers announced voluntary price cuts. Eli Lilly, Novo Nordisk, and Sanofi all capped insulin list prices at $35 per vial for all patients (not just Medicare). Amgen reduced the list price of its Humira biosimilar.
- **Continued Gaps**: The IRA does not cover commercial insurance, addresses only a small fraction of drugs, and excludes drugs in their first 9-13 years on the market (small-molecule drugs are eligible 9 years after approval; biologics after 13 years). GLP-1 receptor agonists like Ozempic and Wegovy are not yet eligible for negotiation under the IRA timeline.
- **Mark Cuban Cost Plus Drugs (2022)**: The launch of Mark Cuban Cost Plus Drugs in January 2022 demonstrated that generic medications could be sold profitably at dramatically lower prices than traditional pharmacy channels. The company's transparent cost-plus model -- manufacturer cost plus 15% margin plus fixed fees -- exposed the magnitude of PBM and supply chain markups. Imatinib (generic Gleevec), which retailed for over $2,500 at traditional pharmacies, was offered at $17.10 through Cost Plus. The model generated significant media attention and bipartisan political interest, with several states exploring cost-plus pricing requirements for Medicaid programs.
- **FTC PBM Investigation (2022-2024)**: The FTC launched a formal investigation of PBM practices in 2022, issuing compulsory orders (6(b) orders) to the six largest PBMs. The interim staff report, published in July 2024, documented practices including specialty drug markups of 30-60%, steering to PBM-owned pharmacies, and rebate retention that may increase net drug costs. The report represented the most comprehensive federal examination of PBM practices to date and generated momentum for bipartisan PBM reform legislation.
- **Second IRA Negotiation Cycle (2025)**: CMS selected 15 additional drugs for Medicare negotiation in January 2025, including treatments for Crohn's disease, multiple sclerosis, blood cancers, and blood clots. Negotiated prices for the second cycle are expected to be announced in late 2025, with prices taking effect in January 2027.
- **GLP-1 Revolution (2022-Present)**: The rapid adoption of GLP-1 receptor agonists (Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro, Zepbound) for diabetes and obesity emerged as the most significant new drug pricing challenge since the specialty drug revolution. With 42 million Americans clinically eligible for GLP-1 obesity treatment and U.S. list prices of $900-$1,350/month (vs. $79-$140/month internationally), unrestricted coverage at current prices could exceed $400 billion annually. The GLP-1 affordability crisis prompted compounding pharmacies to produce semaglutide at $100-$200/month, provoking litigation from Novo Nordisk and raising fundamental questions about the boundary between compounding and manufacturing.
- **State PDAB Maturation (2024-2025)**: Colorado's Prescription Drug Affordability Board initiated its first drug price reviews in 2024, examining medications with annual costs exceeding $30,000. The board's authority to set upper payment limits -- and the pharmaceutical industry's legal challenges to that authority -- established a new front in the drug pricing debate. Whether state PDABs can withstand legal challenge and produce meaningful price reductions will significantly influence the trajectory of federal reform.
- **Bipartisan PBM Reform Momentum (2024-2025)**: For the first time, comprehensive PBM reform legislation attracted significant bipartisan support in both chambers of Congress. The FTC's 2024 interim report provided an authoritative evidence base that made PBM practices politically indefensible for members of either party. Multiple PBM reform bills advanced through committee in the 118th Congress, though none was enacted before adjournment. This bipartisan momentum was historically unusual for a healthcare reform issue and reflected the unusual political dynamics of PBM practices, which lack any credible public interest defense.

## Lessons from History

The history of U.S. drug pricing policy reveals eight recurring patterns that should inform the design of future reforms. Understanding these patterns is essential to crafting legislation that anticipates and prevents the industry adaptation, legal challenge, and political erosion that have undermined past reform efforts.

### Pattern 1: Industry Adaptation to Regulation

Every major drug pricing reform has been followed by pharmaceutical industry adaptation that erodes the reform's effectiveness. Hatch-Waxman created generic competition, and industry responded with pay-for-delay settlements and patent evergreening. The Medicaid rebate program tied discounts to "best price," and manufacturers responded by structuring rebates and discounts to circumvent the best-price calculation. The ACA created a biosimilar pathway, and manufacturers responded with patent thickets (Humira amassed 132 patents). This pattern suggests that any drug pricing reform must be designed with anti-circumvention provisions and must be updated regularly to close emerging loopholes.

### Pattern 2: The Non-Negotiation Clause as Cautionary Tale

The 2003 decision to prohibit Medicare from negotiating drug prices stands as one of the most consequential and costly lobbying victories in American legislative history. For nearly 20 years, the world's largest drug purchaser was barred from negotiating with its suppliers -- a provision that no other developed country has adopted and that cost American taxpayers tens of billions of dollars. The lesson is that structural provisions embedded in foundational legislation can be extraordinarily difficult to reverse, even when their costs are well-documented.

### Pattern 3: Public Outrage as Catalyst

Major drug pricing reforms have consistently been catalyzed by high-profile pricing controversies that generate public anger. The Sovaldi ($84,000), Daraprim (5,456% increase), and EpiPen (548% increase) controversies each generated Congressional hearings and legislative proposals. The insulin pricing crisis produced the IRA's $35 copay cap. History shows that sustained public pressure is necessary to overcome pharmaceutical industry lobbying, but that public attention is episodic and difficult to maintain.

### Pattern 4: Incrementalism vs. Comprehensive Reform

U.S. drug pricing reform has been overwhelmingly incremental -- addressing one drug, one program, or one market segment at a time. The IRA covers only Medicare, only a small number of drugs, and only after extended exclusivity periods. This incrementalism leaves the majority of the drug market unaddressed and allows industry to shift pricing pressure to uncovered segments. Countries that have achieved lower drug prices (Germany, UK, Japan, Australia) have done so through comprehensive, system-wide frameworks -- not piecemeal fixes.

### Pattern 5: States as Laboratories

When federal reform stalls, states have repeatedly served as proving grounds for drug pricing innovation. State Medicaid programs pioneered supplemental rebate negotiations in the 2000s. State transparency laws (California, 2017) demonstrated that requiring advance notice of price increases does not cause manufacturers to leave the market. State insulin copay caps (Colorado, 2019) proved the model that the IRA later adopted for Medicare. State prescription drug affordability boards (Maryland, 2019) are testing whether states can set upper payment limits on expensive drugs. This pattern suggests that state-level reforms, while limited in scope, generate evidence and political precedent that enables eventual federal action.

### Pattern 6: Market Disruption from Outside the System

Some of the most effective challenges to pharmaceutical pricing have come from actors outside the traditional healthcare system. Mark Cuban Cost Plus Drugs demonstrated that generic medications could be sold at a fraction of traditional pharmacy prices. Compounding pharmacies challenged GLP-1 pricing by producing semaglutide at $100-$200 per month. International comparisons -- widely publicized through social media and investigative journalism -- have made the U.S. pricing premium a matter of public knowledge. These external disruptions have often been more effective at shifting public opinion than decades of policy advocacy, suggesting that transparency and alternative supply models can complement legislative reform.

### Pattern 7: Vertical Integration as Emergent Threat

The consolidation of PBMs, insurers, and pharmacies into three vertically integrated conglomerates (CVS Health/Aetna/Caremark, UnitedHealth/OptumRx, Cigna/Express Scripts) represents a structural transformation that occurred with minimal antitrust scrutiny. Each consolidation was approved individually, but the cumulative effect was to concentrate control over drug purchasing, pricing, dispensing, and insurance coverage in three corporate entities. This pattern demonstrates the danger of evaluating mergers in isolation rather than assessing the cumulative market impact of serial consolidation. The FTC's 2024 investigation represents the first serious federal examination of the consequences of PBM consolidation -- more than a decade after the critical mergers occurred.

### Pattern 8: The Persistence of the Gross-to-Net Bubble

The growing gap between list prices and net prices -- from 18% in 2007 to 52% in 2023 -- illustrates how the rebate system creates perverse incentives that worsen over time. Manufacturers raise list prices to fund larger rebates for PBMs, who demand ever-larger rebates to maintain margins, creating an upward spiral in list prices even when net prices are stable or declining. Patients, whose copays and coinsurance are typically based on list prices, bear the costs of this artificial inflation. The gross-to-net bubble is arguably the single most harmful structural feature of the modern drug pricing system, and it has proven remarkably resistant to reform because every intermediary in the supply chain benefits from the opacity it creates.
