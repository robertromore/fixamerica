# Prescription Drug Policy: History

## Historical Development

The regulation of prescription drugs in the United States has evolved from virtually no oversight to a complex, multi-layered system spanning federal agencies, state programs, and industry self-regulation. This history is punctuated by crises -- thalidomide, Vioxx, OxyContin -- each of which exposed regulatory failures and prompted reform, often after significant harm had already occurred. Understanding this trajectory is essential for evaluating current policy and anticipating future challenges.

---

### The Foundations of Drug Regulation (1906-1950): From Patent Medicines to Federal Authority

- **Pre-Regulation Era**: In the 19th and early 20th centuries, the American pharmaceutical market was essentially unregulated. Patent medicines containing opium, cocaine, and alcohol were sold openly and marketed with extravagant health claims. The term "prescription drug" did not exist as a legal category.
- **Pure Food and Drug Act (1906)**: Following Upton Sinclair's *The Jungle* and muckraking journalism exposing adulterated products, Congress passed the first federal law requiring accurate labeling of food and drugs. The Act prohibited misbranded and adulterated products but did not require proof of safety or efficacy before sale.
- **Harrison Narcotics Tax Act (1914)**: This landmark law imposed a tax on the production and distribution of opiates and coca products and required registration of physicians and pharmacists who dispensed them. It was the first federal attempt to control prescription narcotics, though enforcement shifted to criminal prosecution of physicians who prescribed to addicts.
- **Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (1938)**: After the sulfanilamide disaster of 1937 -- in which an untested elixir killed 107 people, including many children -- Congress passed the FDC Act requiring manufacturers to prove drug safety before marketing. This established the pre-market approval paradigm that remains the foundation of FDA authority.
- **Durham-Humphrey Amendment (1951)**: This critical amendment to the FDC Act created the legal distinction between prescription and over-the-counter drugs, establishing the modern two-tier system. Drugs that were habit-forming, potentially toxic, or required medical supervision could only be dispensed with a prescription. Before this amendment, there was no consistent legal standard for which drugs required a prescription, and pharmacists could dispense many drugs at their own discretion.

---

### The Modern Regulatory Era (1951-1980): Safety, Efficacy, and Controlled Substances

- **Thalidomide Crisis (1961-1962)**: The thalidomide tragedy -- in which a sedative caused severe birth defects in over 10,000 children worldwide -- catalyzed the most important pharmaceutical reform in American history. Although the drug was never approved in the U.S. (largely due to FDA reviewer Frances Oldham Kelsey's insistence on additional safety data), the near-miss galvanized Congressional action.
- **Kefauver-Harris Amendment (1962)**: Passed in direct response to thalidomide, this amendment required manufacturers to prove both safety *and* efficacy through "adequate and well-controlled" clinical trials before FDA approval. It also established requirements for informed consent in clinical trials, adverse event reporting, and good manufacturing practices. This law transformed the FDA from a reactive agency into a proactive gatekeeper.
- **Controlled Substances Act (1970)**: The CSA created the five-schedule classification system for drugs with abuse potential, established the Drug Enforcement Administration (later formalized in 1973), and imposed registration, recordkeeping, and quota requirements on manufacturers, distributors, prescribers, and dispensers of controlled substances. Schedule I drugs (including heroin, LSD, and cannabis) were deemed to have no accepted medical use and high abuse potential; Schedule II drugs (including opioids like morphine and oxycodone) required special prescribing procedures.
- **Drug Listing Act (1972)**: Required all commercial drug establishments to register with the FDA and list their products, creating the first comprehensive inventory of marketed drugs.
- **Benzodiazepine Introduction (1960s-1970s)**: Chlordiazepoxide (Librium, 1960) and diazepam (Valium, 1963) were introduced as safer alternatives to barbiturates, rapidly becoming the most prescribed drugs in America. By 1977, Valium was the most prescribed drug in the world. The "Mother's Little Helper" era -- in which benzodiazepines were marketed heavily to women for anxiety and insomnia -- established prescribing patterns and cultural norms around sedative use that persist today. Concerns about dependence emerged by the late 1970s, but prescribing continued at high levels for decades.
- **Early Prescription Monitoring**: New York became the first state to implement a prescription monitoring program in 1918, using triplicate prescription forms for controlled substances. This paper-based system -- which required prescribers to write controlled substance prescriptions in triplicate, with one copy sent to the state -- became a model for other states but was labor-intensive and generated data that was difficult to analyze. By 1980, only 10 states had any form of prescription monitoring, and all relied on paper-based systems with significant lag times between prescribing and data availability.
- **Stimulant Regulation History**: Amphetamines, first synthesized in the 1920s, were widely used in the mid-20th century for conditions ranging from depression to obesity. By the 1960s, an estimated 10 billion amphetamine tablets were produced annually in the United States (Rasmussen, *On Speed*, 2008). The Controlled Substances Act placed amphetamines in Schedule II, but prescribing remained largely unmonitored. Methylphenidate (Ritalin) was approved in 1955 and began its rise as the standard ADHD treatment in the 1960s-1970s.

### The Rise of Pharmaceutical Marketing (1980s-Present)

The history of prescription drug policy cannot be understood without tracing the evolution of pharmaceutical marketing, which has shaped prescribing behavior, regulatory policy, and public expectations about medication.

| Period | Key Development | Impact |
|--------|----------------|--------|
| 1962-1980 | Post-Kefauver marketing focused on physician detailing | Drug representatives became primary marketing channel; direct physician contact |
| 1981 | First direct-to-consumer (DTC) drug advertisement | Boots Pharmaceuticals ran the first DTC ad; minimal regulatory framework |
| 1985 | FDA imposes moratorium on DTC advertising | Concerns about adequacy of risk disclosure |
| 1997 | FDA relaxes DTC advertising rules | Allowed broadcast ads with "adequate provision" for risk information; spending surged |
| 2002 | PhRMA adopts voluntary DTC advertising guidelines | Self-regulatory code; widely criticized as insufficient |
| 2007 | FDA Amendments Act strengthens DTC ad review | Required pre-clearance for certain ads; established Office of Prescription Drug Promotion |
| 2010 | CMS Open Payments (Sunshine Act) enacted | Required disclosure of industry payments to physicians; first data published 2014 |
| 2023 | DTC advertising spending reaches $6.6 billion | U.S. and New Zealand remain the only countries permitting DTC prescription drug advertising |

### The Generic Drug Revolution and Market Dynamics (1980-1996): Access, Competition, and Industry Growth

- **Hatch-Waxman Act (1984)**: The Drug Price Competition and Patent Term Restoration Act fundamentally reshaped the pharmaceutical market. It created the Abbreviated New Drug Application (ANDA) pathway, allowing generic manufacturers to gain FDA approval by demonstrating bioequivalence to the brand-name drug rather than repeating full clinical trials. It also granted brand-name manufacturers patent term extensions to compensate for time lost during FDA review. The Hatch-Waxman Act is directly responsible for the growth of the generic drug industry from less than 20% of prescriptions in 1984 to 91% in 2023.
- **180-Day Exclusivity and Its Consequences**: Hatch-Waxman's Paragraph IV provision granted the first generic applicant 180 days of market exclusivity, creating a powerful financial incentive for generic companies to challenge brand patents. However, this provision also created opportunities for pay-for-delay settlements, in which brand-name manufacturers paid generic challengers to delay market entry.
- **Prescription Drug Marketing Act (1987)**: In response to the diversion of prescription drug samples and concerns about counterfeit drugs entering the supply chain, Congress imposed requirements for the distribution of drug samples and prohibited the sale or trading of drug samples.
- **Prescription Drug User Fee Act (PDUFA, 1992)**: This law authorized the FDA to collect fees from pharmaceutical manufacturers to fund the drug review process, substantially reducing average review times from over 30 months to approximately 12 months. PDUFA fundamentally changed the FDA's relationship with the industry it regulates, creating a revenue dependency that critics argue compromises regulatory independence.
- **Beginning of Modern PBMs**: Pharmacy benefit managers emerged in the 1980s as third-party administrators processing prescription drug claims. Initially, PBMs served a straightforward administrative function, but by the 1990s they had expanded into formulary management, rebate negotiation, and mail-order pharmacy services, becoming powerful intermediaries in the drug supply chain.
- **Stimulant Prescribing Growth (1990s)**: ADHD diagnosis rates surged in the 1990s following the publication of DSM-IV criteria (1994) and increased awareness campaigns. Stimulant prescriptions tripled between 1990 and 2000, with the introduction of extended-release formulations (Concerta, 2000; Adderall XR, 2001) further expanding the market. Concerns about diversion, particularly on college campuses, emerged by the late 1990s.
- **Medicare Prescription Drug Benefit Debate (1990s-2003)**: Throughout the 1990s, the absence of a prescription drug benefit in Medicare became a major political issue as drug costs rose and seniors increasingly struggled to afford medications. The debate culminated in the Medicare Modernization Act of 2003 (MMA), which created the Part D prescription drug benefit -- the largest expansion of Medicare since its creation. The MMA included a controversial "noninterference clause" that prohibited Medicare from directly negotiating drug prices, a provision secured by pharmaceutical industry lobbying that would stand for nearly two decades until the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022.

### The Opioid Prescribing Crisis (1996-2016): Corporate Malfeasance and Regulatory Failure

- **OxyContin Launch (1996)**: Purdue Pharma's launch of extended-release oxycodone, marketed under the brand name OxyContin, marked the beginning of the prescription opioid epidemic. Purdue aggressively promoted OxyContin as having a low risk of addiction -- a claim that was demonstrably false and that the company later admitted was misleading. Purdue's marketing budget for OxyContin reached $200 million annually by 2001.
- **"Pain as the Fifth Vital Sign" Campaign (1990s-2000s)**: The American Pain Society's 1996 initiative to establish pain as a vital sign equal to temperature, blood pressure, pulse, and respiration -- later adopted by the Joint Commission on Accreditation of Healthcare Organizations (JCAHO) in 2001 -- created institutional pressure to treat pain more aggressively. While well-intentioned, this movement was amplified by pharmaceutical industry funding and contributed to a prescribing culture that normalized opioid use for chronic non-cancer pain.
- **Prescribing Peak (2010-2012)**: Opioid prescribing in the United States peaked at 255 million prescriptions in 2012, equaling approximately 81.3 prescriptions per 100 persons. This rate was more than double the prescribing rate of any other developed nation.
- **Purdue Pharma Guilty Plea (2007)**: Purdue Pharma and three executives pleaded guilty to federal charges of misbranding OxyContin, paying $634 million in fines. Despite this criminal conviction, Purdue continued to promote opioids aggressively, and no executive served prison time -- a failure of accountability that became a lasting symbol of corporate impunity.
- **First Electronic PDMPs**: Oklahoma launched the first electronic PDMP in 2004, beginning the transition from paper-based triplicate prescription systems to real-time electronic databases. By 2010, 35 states had operational electronic PDMPs, though most lacked mandatory-use requirements.
- **CDC Prescribing Guidelines (2016)**: The CDC issued its first comprehensive guidelines for prescribing opioids for chronic pain, recommending non-opioid therapies as the preferred first-line treatment and urging caution with dosages exceeding 90 morphine milligram equivalents (MME) per day. The guidelines were influential but also controversial, with some pain management specialists arguing they were overly restrictive and contributed to patient access problems.
- **Drug Take-Back Programs**: The Secure and Responsible Drug Disposal Act of 2010 authorized pharmacies and other entities to maintain year-round drug collection receptacles, expanding beyond the DEA's semiannual National Prescription Drug Take Back Day events (first held in 2010). The legislative impetus came from recognition that unused prescription medications -- particularly opioids -- stored in household medicine cabinets were a primary source of diversion. By 2016, the DEA reported over 6,500 authorized collection sites and cumulative collections exceeding 4,000 tons of medications.
- **Pill Mill Crackdowns (2010-2015)**: Florida, which had become the epicenter of the pill mill crisis -- with Broward County alone hosting more pain clinics per capita than any other area in the country -- enacted comprehensive pain clinic regulation in 2010-2011. The state prohibited physician dispensing of Schedule II and III drugs from pain clinics, required clinic registration with the state health department, and activated its PDMP (E-FORCSE). These measures, combined with DEA enforcement actions, reduced the number of Florida physicians among the nation's top opioid prescribers from 90 in 2010 to fewer than 13 by 2015 (CDC, 2016). Similar crackdowns followed in West Virginia, Ohio, and Kentucky.

### Reform and Reckoning (2016-Present): Litigation, Legislation, and Structural Change

- **SUPPORT Act (2018)**: The Substance Use-Disorder Prevention that Promotes Opioid Recovery and Treatment for Patients and Communities Act was the most comprehensive federal opioid legislation in a generation. It expanded Medicaid coverage for substance use treatment, funded PDMP improvements, imposed electronic prescribing requirements for Medicare Part D controlled substances, and addressed workforce shortages. Authorized spending totaled approximately $8 billion over five years.
- **National Opioid Litigation**: Beginning in 2017, thousands of state, county, and city governments filed lawsuits against pharmaceutical manufacturers, distributors, and pharmacy chains for their roles in the opioid crisis. By 2024, total settlements exceeded $50 billion, with Purdue Pharma's bankruptcy alone yielding $6 billion (though the Sackler family immunity provisions were struck down and renegotiated by the Supreme Court).
- **Inflation Reduction Act (2022)**: The IRA authorized Medicare to negotiate prices on select high-cost drugs for the first time, imposed inflation penalties on manufacturers who raise prices faster than inflation, and capped Medicare Part D out-of-pocket costs at $2,000 annually. The first 10 drugs for negotiation were selected in 2023, with prices announced in August 2024.
- **FTC PBM Investigation (2022-2024)**: The Federal Trade Commission launched a comprehensive investigation into PBM business practices, issuing an interim report in July 2024 that documented spread pricing, rebate retention, specialty pharmacy steering, and anti-competitive consolidation.
- **X-Waiver Elimination (2023)**: The removal of the X-waiver requirement for buprenorphine prescribing -- a barrier that had limited the number of providers authorized to prescribe this critical opioid use disorder treatment -- expanded access to medication-assisted treatment significantly.
- **Missouri PDMP (2023)**: Missouri became the final state to establish a statewide PDMP, completing nationwide coverage. The long delay was primarily due to opposition from a single state senator who blocked legislation for nearly a decade on privacy grounds.
- **CDC Revised Prescribing Guidelines (2022)**: The CDC published updated Clinical Practice Guidelines for Prescribing Opioids in November 2022, significantly revising the 2016 guidelines. The updated guidelines emphasized individualized patient care, discouraged rigid dose ceilings, explicitly warned against involuntary tapers of patients on stable opioid therapy, and addressed the misapplication of the 2016 guidelines by insurers and pharmacies. The revision was informed by public comment receiving over 5,500 submissions, including substantial input from chronic pain patients and their advocates.
- **Stimulant Shortage Crisis (2022-Present)**: Beginning in late 2022, a national shortage of amphetamine mixed salts (Adderall) and methylphenidate exposed tensions between DEA production quotas -- which had been tightened in response to concerns about telehealth-based overprescribing -- and the growing demand from an expanding patient population. The FDA reported that shortages persisted for over 18 consecutive months, affecting millions of ADHD patients. The episode raised fundamental questions about how drug supply management systems should adapt to rapidly changing prescribing patterns.
- **Telehealth Prescribing of Controlled Substances**: The Ryan Haight Online Pharmacy Consumer Protection Act of 2008 had required an in-person medical evaluation before prescribing controlled substances via telemedicine. COVID-19 era Public Health Emergency flexibilities waived this requirement beginning in March 2020, creating a massive expansion in telehealth prescribing that persisted beyond the emergency. DEA proposed permanent rulemaking in 2023-2024 to establish a framework for telehealth prescribing of Schedule III-V substances (including buprenorphine) without in-person exams, while maintaining the in-person requirement for Schedule II stimulants and opioids. As of early 2025, the rulemaking remains under development.

## History of Prescription Drug Monitoring Programs

The development of PDMPs represents one of the most important -- and delayed -- infrastructure investments in prescription drug policy.

### Evolution of PDMP Technology

| Era | Technology | States | Capabilities | Limitations |
|-----|-----------|--------|-------------|-------------|
| 1918-1990 | Paper triplicate forms | 5-10 | Manual tracking of controlled substance prescriptions | Months of delay; data difficult to analyze; labor-intensive |
| 1990-2004 | Early electronic databases | 15-20 | Electronic submission of dispensing data; batch queries | Not real-time; limited prescriber access; no interstate sharing |
| 2004-2015 | Modern electronic PDMPs | 35-49 | Web-based portals; near-real-time data; delegate access | Standalone systems; inconsistent mandates; poor EHR integration |
| 2015-2023 | Interoperable PDMPs | 49-50 + D.C. | Interstate data sharing via PMPInterConnect; some EHR integration | Variable mandate strength; incomplete interoperability; limited analytics |
| 2024+ | Integrated clinical tools | Emerging | Real-time EHR integration; automated alerts; predictive analytics | Only 35% of prescribers use integrated workflows |

## History of Prescriber Education Requirements

### Evolution of Controlled Substance Training Mandates

| Period | Requirements | Gaps |
|--------|-------------|------|
| Pre-2000 | No specific controlled substance prescribing education required beyond medical school pharmacology | Medical schools averaged <10 hours of pain/addiction pharmacology |
| 2000-2010 | Some states begin requiring opioid-specific CME (pioneered by California, 2001) | Voluntary compliance; inconsistent content; industry-funded CME prevalent |
| 2010-2016 | Growing state mandates; 23 states require some opioid education by 2016 | Requirements vary from 1 to 8 hours; no federal standard |
| 2016-2018 | CDC guidelines increase awareness; more states adopt mandates | Guidelines voluntary; misapplied as rigid rules by third parties |
| 2018-2023 | SUPPORT Act mandates 8-hour training for all DEA registrants | First federal CME requirement; compliance tracking challenging |
| 2023+ | 37 states have additional state-level CME mandates | Overlap with federal requirements creates confusion; content quality varies |

### Industry-Funded vs. Independent CME

A critical subtheme in prescriber education history is the role of pharmaceutical industry-funded continuing medical education. Throughout the 2000s, pharmaceutical companies funded approximately 50% of all CME activities in the United States (ACCME, 2008). Industry-funded CME on pain management was instrumental in promoting aggressive opioid prescribing practices. The Accreditation Council for Continuing Medical Education (ACCME) tightened conflict-of-interest rules in 2004 and again in 2011, but concerns persist about indirect industry influence through unrestricted educational grants.

Key reforms in CME independence:

- **2004**: ACCME Standards for Commercial Support updated to require separation of education from promotion
- **2008**: Industry-funded CME peaks at $1.2 billion annually (ACCME data)
- **2011**: ACCME adopts new accreditation criteria emphasizing independence from commercial interests
- **2017**: Major medical societies begin prohibiting industry funding for opioid-related education
- **2023**: SUPPORT Act CME requirements specify that training content must be evidence-based and independent of industry influence

## Lessons from History

### Pattern 1: Crises Drive Reform, But Reform Lags Behind Harm

Every major advance in prescription drug regulation -- the 1938 FDC Act, the 1962 Kefauver-Harris Amendment, the CDC's 2016 opioid guidelines -- came only after a crisis had already caused significant harm. The sulfanilamide disaster killed 107 people before safety testing was required. Thalidomide harmed over 10,000 children before efficacy requirements were imposed. OxyContin contributed to hundreds of thousands of deaths before prescribing guidelines were issued. This pattern suggests that the United States lacks effective proactive surveillance mechanisms and that the political system responds to pharmaceutical industry harm only when public outrage reaches a critical threshold.

### Pattern 2: Industry Capture of Regulatory Processes

The pharmaceutical industry has repeatedly succeeded in shaping regulatory frameworks to serve its interests. PDUFA's user fee model created revenue dependency between the FDA and the industry it regulates. Hatch-Waxman's patent provisions were designed to facilitate generic competition but were systematically exploited through pay-for-delay and patent thicketing. The "pain as the fifth vital sign" campaign was amplified by industry funding. PBMs evolved from administrative processors into opaque profit centers. Each regulatory innovation created new mechanisms for industry capture, suggesting that structural safeguards -- not just new rules -- are needed to prevent regulatory capture.

### Pattern 3: Fragmented Federalism Delays National Solutions

The PDMP experience illustrates how American federalism can impede necessary national infrastructure. It took over two decades (2001-2023) to achieve universal electronic PDMP coverage, and full interoperability remains incomplete. Missouri's decade-long resistance to establishing a PDMP -- due to a single state senator's objections -- allowed doctor shopping to continue unchecked at a cost of lives. Similarly, state-by-state approaches to PBM regulation, prescribing mandates, and drug pricing have produced a patchwork of standards that sophisticated actors can exploit.

### Pattern 4: Unintended Consequences of Well-Intentioned Policies

Opioid prescribing restrictions successfully reduced prescription opioid use but contributed to transitions to heroin and fentanyl, which proved far more lethal. Hatch-Waxman successfully expanded generic access but also created the Paragraph IV litigation system that incentivized pay-for-delay. PDUFA accelerated drug approvals but may have compromised safety oversight. CDC opioid prescribing guidelines intended to prevent overprescribing were misapplied as hard limits, causing documented harm to chronic pain patients. DEA production quota reductions aimed at opioid supply control contributed to stimulant shortages affecting ADHD patients. Effective prescription drug reform must anticipate and mitigate these downstream effects through adaptive policy design with built-in evaluation and correction mechanisms.

### Pattern 5: The Take-Back Gap -- Supply-Side Interventions Without Disposal Infrastructure

The opioid crisis revealed that prescribing and manufacturing controls are incomplete without mechanisms to remove unused medications from circulation. For most of the 20th century, there was no systematic mechanism for patients to return unused medications. The DEA's drug take-back programs were not established until 2010, years after the prescribing crisis peaked. Even now, take-back infrastructure remains insufficient: the majority of unused medications still end up in household storage, trash, or waterways. This pattern suggests that future prescribing policy must be designed with full lifecycle management in mind, from prescription to disposal.

### Pattern 6: Technology Disrupting Regulatory Frameworks

The rise of telehealth prescribing during and after COVID-19 demonstrated how technological changes can outpace regulatory frameworks. The Ryan Haight Act's in-person requirement, designed to prevent internet pharmacy abuses, became an access barrier when telehealth emerged as a legitimate care delivery model. Similarly, electronic PDMPs replaced paper-based triplicate systems only after decades of lag, and EHR integration of PDMP data remains incomplete. The lesson is that prescription drug regulatory frameworks must be designed with technological adaptability built in, rather than encoding specific technologies into statute.

## Timeline of Major Opioid Litigation and Settlements

The legal reckoning for the opioid crisis represents one of the largest mass tort settlements in American history, exceeding the tobacco Master Settlement Agreement in scale.

| Year | Case/Settlement | Amount | Significance |
|------|----------------|--------|--------------|
| 2007 | Purdue Pharma guilty plea (federal misbranding) | $634 million | First major criminal conviction; no executive prison time |
| 2019 | Reckitt Benckiser (Indivior) settlement | $1.4 billion | Marketing fraud related to Suboxone formulation |
| 2020 | Generic opioid manufacturer settlements (Teva, Allergan, Endo) | ~$8 billion (combined) | First settlements from generic manufacturers |
| 2021 | Johnson & Johnson global settlement | $5 billion | Covered J&J's role as raw material supplier (opium poppy processing) |
| 2021 | "Big Three" distributors settlement (McKesson, Cardinal, AmerisourceBergen) | $21 billion | Largest single opioid settlement; funds distributed to states/localities |
| 2022 | Purdue Pharma/Sackler family revised settlement | $6 billion | After Supreme Court struck down original immunity provisions |
| 2022 | Walmart, CVS, Walgreens pharmacy settlements | ~$13 billion (combined) | Addressed pharmacy-level dispensing failures |
| 2023 | Teva Pharmaceuticals revised settlement | $4.35 billion | Global settlement with states and subdivisions |
| 2024 | Total cumulative settlements | >$50 billion | Ongoing litigation against remaining defendants |

### Settlement Fund Allocation Concerns

A persistent concern is whether opioid settlement funds will be directed to evidence-based treatment, prevention, and monitoring or diverted to general state revenue -- repeating the pattern of tobacco settlement funds, of which less than 3% was spent on tobacco cessation programs (Campaign for Tobacco-Free Kids, 2023). Many settlement agreements include provisions requiring funds be spent on opioid remediation, and oversight commissions have been established in most states, but enforcement mechanisms vary widely. By 2024, approximately $15 billion had been distributed, with reported spending concentrated on treatment services, naloxone distribution, and PDMP improvements, though comprehensive accounting remains incomplete.
