# Prescription Drug Policy: Stakeholders

The prescription drug system is shaped by the interactions of several key groups, each with its own interests, resources, and influence over the regulatory and market structures that determine how drugs are approved, priced, prescribed, and monitored.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Patients/Consumers | Bear the cost of high drug prices, face access barriers, risk of overprescribing or underprescribing | ~66% of U.S. adults take at least one prescription drug (~175 million) | Affordable access, safe prescribing, accurate information |
| Chronic pain patients | Subject to prescribing restrictions following opioid crisis; risk of undertreatment | ~50 million adults with chronic pain (CDC, 2023) | Adequate pain management, prescriber access, reduced stigma |
| People with substance use disorders | Affected by controlled substance monitoring, treatment access, criminal penalties | ~48.7 million with SUD (SAMHSA, 2024) | Treatment access, reduced stigma, rational monitoring that does not deter care |
| Uninsured/underinsured individuals | Pay highest out-of-pocket prices; limited access to manufacturer programs | ~27 million uninsured (Census, 2023); millions more underinsured | Affordable drug prices, generic availability, patient assistance |
| Medicare beneficiaries | Largest prescription drug consumer group; affected by Part D design, IRA reforms | ~67 million (CMS, 2024) | Lower out-of-pocket costs, expanded negotiation, formulary coverage |
| Prescribers (physicians, NPs, PAs) | Subject to monitoring, prescribing mandates, continuing education requirements | ~1.87 million DEA registrants | Clinical autonomy, adequate tools, reduced administrative burden |

### Secondary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size/Scope | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|-----------|---------------|
| Independent pharmacies | Face below-cost PBM reimbursement, DIR fee clawbacks, specialty pharmacy steering | ~19,400 (down from 23,100 in 2016, NCPA, 2024) | Fair reimbursement, PBM reform, specialty pharmacy access |
| Employers purchasing health insurance | Bear rising prescription drug costs as plan sponsors; limited PBM transparency | Cover ~155 million Americans | Drug cost containment, PBM accountability, transparent contracts |
| State Medicaid programs | Cover prescription drugs for low-income populations; manage formularies and fraud prevention | ~90 million enrollees; $74 billion Rx spending (FY2023) | Cost control, formulary management, rebate maximization |
| Taxpayers | Fund Medicare, Medicaid, VA, and other federal/state drug spending | Federal Rx spending >$200 billion (FY2023) | Fiscal responsibility, value for public investment |
| Law enforcement | DEA, state drug task forces, and local agencies enforce controlled substance laws | DEA: ~10,000 employees; state/local: varies | Diversion prevention, prosecution of pill mills, PDMP access |
| Addiction treatment providers | Provide MAT, counseling, and recovery services; affected by prescribing policy | ~16,000 treatment facilities (SAMHSA, 2024) | Treatment access, rational monitoring that supports care entry |
| Rural and tribal communities | Disproportionately affected by pharmacy deserts, prescribing restrictions, and limited monitoring infrastructure | ~60 million rural residents; 574 federally recognized tribes | Pharmacy access, prescriber availability, culturally appropriate services |

### Tertiary Stakeholders

- **Academic medical institutions**: Shape prescribing norms through training programs, clinical guidelines, and research. Medical school curricula determine the pharmacology knowledge base of future prescribers.
- **EHR vendors (Epic, Cerner/Oracle Health, Meditech)**: Control the technical infrastructure through which clinical decision support and PDMP integration are delivered. Vendor cooperation is essential for prescribing oversight tools.
- **Drug disposal and take-back operators**: Companies providing deactivation products (DisposeRx, Deterra) and organizations managing take-back logistics have a growing role in the prescription drug lifecycle.
- **International pharmaceutical regulators**: Health Canada, EMA, and other international agencies influence U.S. policy through drug importation frameworks, reference pricing comparisons, and safety surveillance data sharing.
- **Investigative journalists**: Reporters at outlets including *STAT News*, *The Washington Post*, *ProPublica*, and *Charleston Gazette-Mail* have driven public awareness and accountability through investigative reporting on opioid distribution, PBM practices, and regulatory failures.

## Who Has Power

### Decision Makers

| Actor | Role | Authority | Resources | Influence Level |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| U.S. Congress | Sets drug regulation law, FDA/DEA authority, Medicare/Medicaid rules | Legislative authority over drug pricing, monitoring, scheduling | Appropriations, oversight hearings | High |
| FDA | Approves drugs, oversees safety, regulates marketing | Pre-market approval, post-market surveillance, labeling requirements | ~$6.9 billion budget (FY2024); ~18,000 FTEs | High |
| DEA | Controls scheduling, production quotas, prescriber registration | Registration authority, quota-setting, enforcement actions | ~$3.4 billion budget (FY2024); ~10,000 employees | High |
| CMS | Administers Medicare and Medicaid drug benefits | Part D oversight, IRA drug negotiation, Medicaid rebate program | Administers >$200 billion in annual drug spending | High |
| FTC | Investigates anti-competitive practices, PBM conduct | Antitrust enforcement, unfair practices authority | ~$450 million budget (FY2024) | Medium |
| State medical boards | License and discipline prescribers | Can revoke, suspend, or restrict medical licenses | 70 boards across 50 states + D.C. | Medium |
| State pharmacy boards | Regulate pharmacies and dispensing | Licensing, inspection, disciplinary authority | Varies widely by state | Medium |

### Key Organizations and Institutions

- **Pharmaceutical Research and Manufacturers of America (PhRMA)**: The principal lobbying organization for brand-name drug manufacturers. Spent $29.2 million on lobbying in 2023 (OpenSecrets) and coordinates industry-wide advocacy against price regulation, importation, and patent reform. Members include Pfizer, Johnson & Johnson, Merck, AbbVie, and other major companies.
- **National Association of Boards of Pharmacy (NABP)**: Operates the PMPInterConnect system for interstate PDMP data sharing and establishes standards for pharmacy practice. NABP accredits internet pharmacies through its VIPPS program.
- **Pharmacy Benefit Managers (CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, OptumRx)**: Control formulary design, rebate negotiation, and pharmacy reimbursement for approximately 80% of the insured population. Their combined revenue exceeds $400 billion annually, and their market leverage gives them extraordinary power over which drugs are covered, at what cost, and through which pharmacies.
- **American Medical Association (AMA)**: Represents approximately 271,000 physician members. The AMA has advocated for PDMP improvements, prescriber education, and reduced administrative burdens but has also defended physician prescribing autonomy against regulatory mandates.
- **National Community Pharmacists Association (NCPA)**: Represents approximately 19,400 independent pharmacies. NCPA has been the most vocal critic of PBM practices, advocating for transparency mandates, fair reimbursement, and prohibitions on specialty pharmacy steering.

### Influencers

- **Patient advocacy organizations**: Groups such as the National Pain Advocacy Center, Patients for Affordable Drugs, and the American Cancer Society Cancer Action Network influence public discourse and lobby for patient-centered reforms. Patients for Affordable Drugs has become particularly effective, raising more than $50 million since 2017 to campaign for drug pricing reform.
- **Academic medical institutions**: Research institutions shape prescribing guidelines and evidence-based practice through clinical research. The CDC, AHRQ, and NIH fund studies that inform prescribing standards and PDMP evaluation.
- **Insurance industry**: Health insurers (UnitedHealthcare, Anthem/Elevance, Cigna, Aetna/CVS) negotiate drug coverage through their PBM subsidiaries and lobby on payment and coverage rules. Many major insurers are vertically integrated with PBMs.
- **Generic and biosimilar manufacturers**: Companies such as Teva, Viatris, Sandoz, and Amneal advocate for policies that reduce barriers to generic entry and compete against brand-name patent extension strategies.
- **Investigative journalists and researchers**: Reporting by outlets including *STAT News*, *The Washington Post*, *ProPublica*, and academic researchers has driven public awareness of pharmaceutical industry practices, PBM opacity, and regulatory failures.
- **State Attorneys General**: State AGs have played a critical role in prescription drug policy through opioid litigation (over $50 billion in cumulative settlements), enforcement actions against PBM practices, and investigation of pharmaceutical pricing. The bipartisan nature of AG enforcement actions has made them one of the most effective accountability mechanisms.
- **Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA)**: Beyond its formal regulatory role, the DEA exerts significant informal influence through production quota decisions, registration enforcement priorities, and its institutional culture regarding controlled substance oversight. DEA policy decisions -- such as the 65% reduction in opioid production quotas since 2017 -- directly shape the prescribing landscape.
- **PDMP Technical Assistance Centers**: Organizations such as the PDMP Training and Technical Assistance Center (TTAC) at Brandeis University provide research, training, and technical support to state PDMP programs, influencing operational standards and best practices nationwide.

### Lobbying and Political Spending by Sector

| Sector | Federal Lobbying (2023) | Campaign Contributions (2023-2024 cycle) | Lobbyists Deployed | Source |
|--------|------------------------|----------------------------------------|-------------------|--------|
| Pharmaceutical manufacturers (PhRMA + individual companies) | $374 million | ~$75 million | ~2.6 per member of Congress | OpenSecrets, 2024 |
| PBMs and pharmacy chains | ~$35 million | ~$18 million | ~150 | OpenSecrets, 2024 |
| Health insurance industry | ~$175 million | ~$45 million | ~800 | OpenSecrets, 2024 |
| Generic/biosimilar manufacturers | ~$15 million | ~$8 million | ~75 | OpenSecrets, 2024 |
| Patient advocacy groups (all) | ~$5 million | Minimal | ~30 | OpenSecrets, 2024 |
| Independent pharmacy associations | ~$3 million | ~$2 million | ~20 | OpenSecrets, 2024 |

The resource asymmetry is striking: the pharmaceutical industry outspends patient advocacy groups on lobbying by a ratio of approximately 75:1. This imbalance shapes the legislative process at every stage, from committee drafting through floor votes to implementation rulemaking.

### Industry Funding of Patient Advocacy Organizations

A significant complication in the stakeholder landscape is pharmaceutical industry funding of patient advocacy organizations. A 2020 study in *JAMA Internal Medicine* (McCoy et al.) found that:

- 83% of large U.S. patient advocacy organizations received pharmaceutical industry funding
- Industry-funded organizations were significantly less likely to support drug pricing reform
- The median annual industry funding was $1.5 million per organization
- Only 25% of organizations disclosed their industry funding prominently on their websites

This creates a "astroturf" dynamic in which industry-funded groups present patient perspectives that align with industry interests, blurring the line between genuine patient advocacy and industry lobbying.

## Stakeholder Interests and Alignment

### Aligned Interests (Potential Coalition Partners)

| Coalition | Shared Interest | Strength |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| Independent pharmacies + patients + employers | PBM transparency and fair pricing | Strong -- clear economic alignment |
| Generic manufacturers + patients + payers | Faster generic entry, reduced patent gaming | Strong -- broad cost reduction benefit |
| Pain patients + physicians | Balanced prescribing guidelines that do not restrict legitimate treatment | Moderate -- complicated by SUD concerns |
| State regulators + DEA + public health advocates | Improved PDMP interoperability and monitoring | Strong -- shared public safety mission |
| Consumer groups + employers + state AGs | Pharmaceutical price transparency and accountability | Strong -- bipartisan support base |
| Addiction treatment providers + public health advocates + law enforcement (progressive) | Treatment access expansion, harm reduction | Moderate -- law enforcement alignment varies |
| Rural health advocates + independent pharmacies + tribal health organizations | Pharmacy access, equitable drug disposal infrastructure, telehealth prescribing rules | Strong -- geographic overlap in underserved areas |
| Environmental groups + public health advocates | Safe drug disposal, reduction of pharmaceutical contamination in waterways | Moderate -- emerging coalition |

### Conflicting Interests

- **Patients needing affordable drugs** vs. **Brand-name manufacturers** seeking maximum revenue: This is the fundamental tension in drug pricing policy. Manufacturers argue that high prices fund innovation; patients argue that innovation is meaningless if they cannot afford the drugs.
- **Pain patients** vs. **Addiction prevention advocates**: Restrictions on opioid prescribing protect public health but can harm patients with legitimate pain needs. The two communities have sometimes been pitted against each other rather than working toward balanced solutions.
- **Independent pharmacies** vs. **PBMs/chain pharmacies**: PBM-owned pharmacies benefit from preferential reimbursement and patient steering that directly harm independent competitors. This conflict is intensifying as independent pharmacies close and pharmacy deserts expand in rural and underserved areas.
- **FDA** vs. **generic manufacturers**: The FDA's mission to ensure drug safety requires rigorous review processes that generic manufacturers argue create unnecessary delays. Industry critics argue that the FDA's brand-name user fee revenue creates a bias against generic prioritization.
- **DEA** vs. **patients needing controlled substances**: DEA production quotas and enforcement actions restrict aggregate supply and create a chilling effect on prescribers, but loosening controls risks enabling a return to overprescribing. The stimulant shortage crisis exemplifies this tension.
- **Telehealth platforms** vs. **traditional prescribers**: Online platforms increase access but create competition dynamics that can incentivize liberal prescribing; traditional providers argue telehealth lacks adequate safeguards for controlled substance evaluation.
- **Pharmaceutical industry** vs. **state AGs**: States using litigation to hold manufacturers accountable for opioid crisis costs face industry arguments about preemption, federal regulatory compliance, and the limits of state enforcement authority.
- **Drug disposal advocates** vs. **convenience**: Expanding take-back programs and restricting quantities prescribed requires behavioral changes that patients and prescribers may resist, particularly when alternatives (physical therapy, non-opioid pain management) are less accessible or not covered by insurance.

### The Pharmaceutical-PBM Iron Triangle

The relationship among brand-name pharmaceutical manufacturers, PBMs, and insurers creates a self-reinforcing system that resists price reduction:

1. **Manufacturers** set high list prices to generate large rebates that PBMs demand
2. **PBMs** prefer high-list-price drugs with large rebates because their compensation is tied to rebate volumes
3. **Insurers** accept this structure because they receive a share of rebates, offsetting premium costs
4. **Patients** bear the cost through high copays and coinsurance calculated on list prices

This iron triangle means that all three parties benefit from the existing high-price, high-rebate system, even though patients and the healthcare system as a whole would benefit from lower list prices with smaller rebates.

### The Pain Patient vs. Addiction Prevention Divide

One of the most consequential stakeholder dynamics is the tension between chronic pain patients and addiction prevention advocates. These groups should be natural allies -- both are harmed by a dysfunctional prescribing system -- but have often been pitted against each other:

| Perspective | Concern | Policy Priority |
|------------|---------|----------------|
| Chronic pain patients | Prescribing restrictions and PDMP mandates have caused involuntary tapers, pharmacy refusals, and loss of prescriber access | Balanced guidelines, clinical flexibility, protection against forced tapers |
| Addiction prevention advocates | Overprescribing created the opioid epidemic; monitoring and prescribing limits save lives | Mandatory PDMP checks, dose limits, prescriber education |
| Palliative care and hospice providers | Dying patients should not face barriers to pain relief; regulatory overreach is cruel | Exemptions for palliative/end-of-life care, streamlined prescribing |
| Pediatric prescribers | Children with pain, ADHD, or other conditions need appropriate access to controlled substances | Age-appropriate guidelines, stimulant supply adequacy |
| Patients with co-occurring conditions | SUD and chronic pain often co-exist; patients need integrated care, not exclusion from either system | Integrated treatment models, reduced stigma, provider training |

The 2022 CDC guideline revision represented the most significant institutional attempt to bridge this divide, explicitly acknowledging that the 2016 guidelines had been misapplied in ways that harmed pain patients while affirming the importance of monitoring and evidence-based prescribing.

### Emerging Stakeholder: Telehealth Prescribing Platforms

Digital health companies that prescribe controlled substances via telehealth (Cerebral, Done, Ahead, and others) have become significant stakeholders. Their business models -- typically subscription-based with monthly medication management visits -- create new stakeholder dynamics:

- They expand access to underserved populations and reduce barriers to care
- Their high-volume prescribing patterns have drawn DEA and state medical board scrutiny
- They have lobbied against permanent reinstatement of the Ryan Haight Act's in-person requirement
- DOJ fraud charges against Cerebral's former CEO (2024) highlighted the regulatory gap in telehealth prescribing oversight
- They employ substantial numbers of nurse practitioners and physician assistants, shifting prescribing from traditional medical settings

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on prescription drug policy:

- **Lobbying**: The pharmaceutical and health products industry spent $374 million on federal lobbying in 2023 (OpenSecrets), more than any other industry. The top spenders include PhRMA ($29.2 million), Pfizer ($13.0 million), and the Biotechnology Innovation Organization ($9.8 million). PBMs and pharmacy chains also lobby extensively -- CVS Health spent $10.6 million in 2023.
- **Campaign Contributions**: Pharmaceutical/health products PACs and individuals contributed approximately $75 million to federal candidates and party committees in the 2023-2024 election cycle, with spending roughly evenly split between parties (OpenSecrets, 2024).
- **Revolving Door**: Former FDA and DEA officials frequently move to pharmaceutical industry positions, and industry executives move into regulatory roles. A 2019 *Science* analysis (Piller) found that 11 of the FDA's 16 most recent commissioners went on to work for pharmaceutical companies or serve on their boards after leaving the agency.
- **Public Advocacy**: Patient advocacy groups, many of which receive pharmaceutical industry funding, advocate for policies that may align with industry interests. A 2020 *JAMA Internal Medicine* study (McCoy et al.) found that 83% of large patient advocacy organizations received industry funding, and funded organizations were less likely to support drug pricing reform.
- **Expertise/Information**: The pharmaceutical industry funds the vast majority of clinical trials, controls access to proprietary data, and employs the largest concentration of pharmacology expertise outside of academia. This information asymmetry gives industry outsized influence over clinical guidelines, formulary decisions, and regulatory standards.
- **Litigation**: State attorneys general, municipalities, and patient groups use litigation to hold industry accountable and force policy change. The $50+ billion in opioid litigation settlements has been the most consequential accountability mechanism for pharmaceutical misconduct, funding treatment, prevention, and monitoring programs.
- **State-Level Policy Innovation**: States serve as laboratories for prescription drug reform, with innovations in PBM regulation, importation programs, PDMP mandates, and prescribing limits that can demonstrate effectiveness and build momentum for federal action. Over 40 states have enacted some form of PBM reform since 2020.
- **Patient Storytelling**: Personal narratives from patients -- including those who have experienced drug cost hardship, involuntary opioid tapers, pharmacy desert impacts, and medication shortages -- are among the most effective advocacy tools. Organizations like Patients for Affordable Drugs and T1International have built their advocacy strategies around patient testimony.
- **Data and Research**: Academic institutions, think tanks, and government agencies produce research that shapes policy debates. The CDC's prescribing rate data, the FTC's PBM investigation findings, the RAND Corporation's international price comparisons, and CBO's cost estimates for reform proposals all exert significant influence on legislative and regulatory outcomes.
- **Media Coverage**: Sustained investigative reporting has been essential to building public awareness and political will for reform. The *Charleston Gazette-Mail*'s Pulitzer Prize-winning reporting on opioid distribution in West Virginia (2017), the *Washington Post*'s DEA database investigation (2019), and *STAT News*'s ongoing pharmaceutical industry coverage have all influenced policy outcomes.
- **Coalition Building**: Cross-sector coalitions that unite patient advocates, employers, pharmacies, and state officials have proven more effective than single-interest advocacy. The Alliance for Transparent and Affordable Prescriptions (ATAP) -- which unites employers, pharmacies, and patient groups against PBM opacity -- demonstrates the power of broad-based coalition advocacy.

## Geographic Stakeholder Variation

Prescription drug policy stakeholder dynamics vary significantly by region, reflecting differences in prescribing patterns, healthcare infrastructure, and economic conditions.

### High-Prescribing States (Appalachia, Southeast)

| Stakeholder Dynamic | Description |
|---------------------|-------------|
| Dominant concerns | Pain management access, prescriber shortages, pharmacy closures, opioid settlement fund allocation |
| Key stakeholders | Rural health clinics, community health workers, state PDMP administrators, opioid settlement oversight commissions |
| Political dynamics | Strong physician lobby resistance to prescribing mandates; community-level support for monitoring; tension between enforcement and access |
| Unique factors | Higher chronic pain prevalence, fewer alternative treatment options, greater pharmacy desert risk, legacy of pill mill era |

### Urban Centers (Northeast, West Coast)

| Stakeholder Dynamic | Description |
|---------------------|-------------|
| Dominant concerns | Drug pricing affordability, PBM practices, specialty drug access, GLP-1 costs, stimulant shortages |
| Key stakeholders | Hospital systems, academic medical centers, patient advocacy organizations, employer coalitions |
| Political dynamics | Generally supportive of government price negotiation and PBM regulation; divided on telehealth prescribing restrictions |
| Unique factors | Higher concentration of specialty prescribing, more extensive insurance coverage, greater PBM market penetration |

### Border and Interstate Regions

| Stakeholder Dynamic | Description |
|---------------------|-------------|
| Dominant concerns | Interstate PDMP interoperability, cross-border prescribing, Canadian drug importation |
| Key stakeholders | State pharmacy boards, border-region prescribers, NABP/PMPInterConnect administrators |
| Political dynamics | Strong support for federal PDMP standardization; importation support in northern border states |
| Unique factors | Doctor shopping across state lines more prevalent; importation from Canada more accessible in northern states |

### Tribal Nations

| Stakeholder Dynamic | Description |
|---------------------|-------------|
| Dominant concerns | Indian Health Service pharmacy access, tribal PDMP integration, culturally appropriate prescribing, opioid settlement allocations |
| Key stakeholders | Tribal health departments, IHS, tribal epidemiology centers, urban Indian health organizations |
| Political dynamics | Sovereignty concerns interact with federal drug policy; tribal consultation requirements for DEA rulemaking |
| Unique factors | IHS pharmacy formulary constraints, geographic isolation, historical trauma affecting prescribing relationships, limited take-back infrastructure on reservations |
