# International Drug Policy: Stakeholders

The international drug control system is shaped by the interactions of multilateral institutions, national governments, law enforcement agencies, advocacy organizations, and affected populations, each with distinct interests, resources, and influence.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Drug crop farmers (coca, opium, cannabis) | Livelihoods destroyed by eradication; criminalized for poverty-driven cultivation | ~6 million farmers globally (UNODC estimate) | Economic survival, land rights, legal alternatives |
| People who use drugs (globally) | Criminalized, incarcerated, denied treatment; overdose and disease risk | ~296 million (UNODC World Drug Report, 2024) | Access to treatment, harm reduction, decriminalization |
| Communities in producer/transit countries | Drug violence, corruption, displacement, institutional erosion | Tens of millions across Latin America, West Africa, Southeast Asia | Security, governance, economic opportunity |
| Indigenous populations | Traditional practices (coca, cannabis, peyote, ayahuasca) criminalized by treaties | Hundreds of millions globally; Bolivia alone has 4+ million coca-chewing population | Cultural rights, sovereignty, treaty reform |
| Victims of drug violence | Homicides, kidnapping, extortion, forced displacement | 400,000+ killed in Mexico since 2006; 260,000+ disappeared in Colombia | Justice, security, reparations |

### Secondary Stakeholders

- **U.S. taxpayers**: Fund $5.5 billion/year (FY2024) in international drug control with limited evidence of effectiveness
- **Health workers and researchers**: Constrained by scheduling barriers that limit drug research; health-based approaches underfunded
- **Legal cannabis industries**: Canadian and Uruguayan legal markets face international trade barriers due to treaty framework
- **Migrants and refugees**: Drug violence is a primary driver of migration from Central America, Mexico, and Colombia, creating humanitarian and political consequences in the United States. UNHCR estimates that drug-related violence was a contributing factor in the displacement of 2.5 million Central Americans between 2014-2022.
- **Environmental communities**: Aerial fumigation of coca with glyphosate has damaged ecosystems, contaminated water sources, and destroyed legal crops adjacent to coca fields. Deforestation for coca and poppy cultivation has cleared an estimated 300,000 hectares of forest in Colombia alone (UNODC/Colombia Environment Ministry, 2023). Environmental organizations including the Sierra Club, World Wildlife Fund, and Amazon Watch have documented the ecological costs of both drug cultivation and eradication programs.
- **Pharmaceutical companies**: Both affected by and benefiting from the scheduling system -- restricted from developing cannabis and psychedelic-based medicines but protected from competition by natural substances. The emerging psychedelic medicine sector (psilocybin, MDMA, ketamine) is creating new stakeholders with economic interests in scheduling reform, while traditional pharmaceutical companies often oppose rescheduling of competing substances.
- **Children and families**: Children in producer and transit countries are disproportionately affected by drug violence, recruitment into trafficking organizations, and the incarceration of parents for drug offenses. UNICEF estimates that 130,000 children in Mexico have lost one or both parents to drug violence (UNICEF Mexico, 2022).
- **Academic and research community**: Drug policy researchers at institutions including the London School of Economics, RAND Corporation, Johns Hopkins University, and the Instituto Colombiano de Estudios de Desarrollo face scheduling barriers that impede research on controlled substances. The current scheduling system requires researchers to obtain special licenses, adding months or years to study timelines and significantly limiting the scope of clinical research. Over 300 leading scientists signed a 2023 open letter calling for reform of scheduling barriers to research.
- **Financial institutions**: Banks and financial technology companies are increasingly affected by the gap between domestic cannabis legalization and federal/international prohibition. Cannabis businesses in legal U.S. states lack access to normal banking services, creating a cash-dependent industry that is more vulnerable to theft and money laundering. The American Bankers Association has called for clarity on cannabis banking, representing a potential reform ally.

## Who Has Power

### Decision Makers

| Actor | Role | Authority | Resources | Influence Level |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| U.S. President and National Security Council | Sets international drug policy priorities | Executive authority over diplomacy, military, intelligence | $5.5 billion/year international drug budget | High |
| U.S. Congress (Judiciary, Foreign Affairs, Appropriations Committees) | Authorizes and funds programs; conducts oversight | Legislative authority, appropriations power | Controls all U.S. drug spending | High |
| Commission on Narcotic Drugs (CND) | Primary UN drug policy body; schedules substances | Policy resolutions; scheduling decisions | Part of UNODC budget | High (formal) |
| International Narcotics Control Board (INCB) | Treaty compliance monitoring | Quasi-judicial review; public criticism of non-compliance | ~$7 million via UNODC | Medium (moral/reputational) |
| UNODC Executive Director | Leads UN drug control operations | Research, technical assistance, norm-setting | ~$410 million annual budget | Medium |
| WHO Expert Committee on Drug Dependence | Scientific scheduling recommendations | Recommends scheduling to CND based on health evidence | Limited (expert body) | Medium (scientific authority) |
| Producer-country governments (Colombia, Peru, Bolivia, Mexico, Myanmar) | Implement drug policy on the ground; negotiate with U.S. | Sovereignty; domestic law enforcement; diplomatic leverage | Varied | Medium-High (increasing) |

### Key Organizations and Institutions

#### U.S. Government Agencies

- **U.S. State Department Bureau of International Narcotics and Law Enforcement Affairs (INL)**:
    - Manages the largest bilateral drug control portfolio in the world ($1.56 billion FY2024)
    - Funds counter-narcotics police training, judicial reform, eradication programs, and alternative development in over 90 countries
    - INL's orientation has traditionally been enforcement-first, though it has gradually incorporated development elements
    - Employs approximately 4,000 staff globally, including foreign national contractors in partner countries
    - Key programs include the Aviation Division (helicopter fleet for counter-narcotics), vetted units training, and judicial reform

- **U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA)**:
    - Operates 93 foreign offices in 69 countries with approximately 1,000 overseas agents
    - Conducts investigations, intelligence gathering, and capacity building for foreign law enforcement
    - Has resisted reform efforts and maintains a strong institutional commitment to the prohibition paradigm
    - The DEA's foreign-deployed intelligence program (FAST teams) has conducted operations in Afghanistan, Central America, and West Africa
    - Sometimes operates in a quasi-military capacity that blurs the line between law enforcement and combat operations
    - Key controversies: Jose Irizarry corruption case (2020), Honduran drug war operations, intelligence sharing practices

- **U.S. Department of Defense**:
    - DOD counter-narcotics programs (~$1.2 billion FY2024) fund detection and monitoring, military training for partner nations, and equipment transfers
    - Counter-narcotics authority (Section 284) has been used to build military relationships in Latin America that serve broader strategic objectives
    - Southern Command (SOUTHCOM) treats counter-narcotics as a primary mission
    - Provides intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance (ISR) assets that are critical to interdiction operations

- **Joint Interagency Task Force South (JIATF-S)**:
    - The primary U.S. operational command for drug interdiction in the Western Hemisphere
    - Based in Key West, Florida
    - Coordinates assets from 10 U.S. agencies and 20+ partner nations
    - Tracks and intercepts drug shipments by air, sea, and land
    - In FY2023, operations resulted in the seizure of approximately 240 metric tons of cocaine
    - This represents an estimated 15-20% of the total flow (JIATF-S, 2024)

- **U.S. Agency for International Development (USAID)**:
    - Manages alternative development and governance programs in drug-producing regions (~$350 million FY2024)
    - Programs are generally more development-oriented than INL or DOD programs but receive a smaller share of funding
    - Most successful programs: Peru's DEVIDA partnership, the Colombia Cocoa Alliance, Bolivia cacao programs
    - These have demonstrated that market-linked crop substitution can reduce cultivation, though they remain underfunded relative to enforcement programs

#### International Bodies

- **European Monitoring Centre for Drugs and Drug Addiction (EMCDDA/EUDA)**:
    - The EU's drug policy research agency, providing data-driven analysis that supports evidence-based approaches
    - Renamed the European Union Drugs Agency (EUDA) in 2024 with an expanded mandate
    - Publishes the annual European Drug Report, the most comprehensive regional drug data source
    - Has become increasingly influential in shaping European drug policy toward harm reduction

- **Europol**:
    - Coordinates EU law enforcement on drug trafficking
    - Jointly publishes the EU Drug Markets Report with EUDA
    - Focuses on organized crime networks rather than personal use enforcement

- **Inter-American Drug Abuse Control Commission (CICAD/OAS)**:
    - The OAS body responsible for drug policy coordination in the Americas
    - Published the landmark 2013 report "The Drug Problem in the Americas"
    - Increasingly supports evidence-based and health-centered approaches
    - Provides technical assistance to member states on drug policy reform

### Influencers

- **Global Commission on Drug Policy**: Founded in 2011, includes former presidents of Brazil, Colombia, Mexico, Switzerland, and Portugal, plus Kofi Annan, Richard Branson, and other public figures. Has published influential reports calling for decriminalization, harm reduction, and regulated markets. Has no formal authority but significant media and political influence. The Commission's 2011 report, signed by former UN Secretary-General Kofi Annan and former heads of state from five continents, was the highest-profile challenge to the prohibition consensus in the history of international drug policy.

- **Drug Policy Alliance (DPA)**: The largest U.S.-based drug policy reform organization ($20+ million annual budget). Funds advocacy, litigation, and ballot initiatives. Has driven cannabis legalization and harm reduction expansion in U.S. states.

- **Transform Drug Policy Foundation**: UK-based organization producing detailed policy analyses and model regulatory frameworks. Published influential "After the War on Drugs" blueprint, which has been translated into 15 languages and cited in policy debates in over 30 countries.

- **International Drug Policy Consortium (IDPC)**: Network of 192 organizations in 70+ countries advocating for evidence-based drug policies. Coordinates civil society engagement with UN drug policy processes. IDPC's annual "Shadow Report" on CND proceedings provides an independent assessment that frequently contradicts official narratives.

- **Transnational Institute (TNI)**: Amsterdam-based research organization providing analysis on drug policy reform, particularly regarding treaty flexibility and Latin American perspectives. TNI's legal analyses of treaty flexibility have been cited by governments including Bolivia, Uruguay, and Canada in defending their reform policies.

- **Washington Office on Latin America (WOLA)**: Monitors U.S. policy in Latin America, including drug policy. Produces influential analyses of Plan Colombia, Merida Initiative, and human rights impacts. WOLA's research on human rights abuses in U.S.-funded counter-narcotics programs has led to Congressional holds on military assistance.

- **Beckley Foundation**: UK-based organization funding scientific research on drug policy and psychedelic substances. Has partnered with universities and government agencies in Brazil, Switzerland, and the UK to advance evidence-based scheduling reform.

- **InSight Crime**: Research organization tracking organized crime and drug trafficking in the Americas. Provides granular analysis of cartel dynamics, trafficking routes, and policy impacts that informs both academic research and policymaking.

- **Law Enforcement Action Partnership (LEAP)**: Organization of current and former law enforcement officials who support drug policy reform. LEAP's members -- including former police chiefs, prosecutors, judges, and DEA agents -- provide politically powerful testimony that undermines the narrative that law enforcement uniformly opposes reform. Their endorsement of decriminalization and regulation has been particularly influential with conservative audiences.

- **Harm Reduction International (HRI)**: London-based organization that monitors harm reduction policies globally, publishes the influential "Global State of Harm Reduction" report, and advocates against the death penalty for drug offenses. HRI's annual reporting on executions for drug offenses has become the primary data source for international advocacy against capital punishment in drug cases.

## Stakeholder Interests and Alignment

### Aligned Interests (Potential Coalition Partners)

| Coalition | Shared Interest | Strength |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| Latin American reformist governments + Global Commission + IDPC | Ending prohibition-driven violence; treaty flexibility | Strong (shared experience and values) |
| Public health community + harm reduction NGOs + WHO | Prioritizing health outcomes over enforcement metrics | Strong (evidence-based consensus) |
| Libertarian/civil liberties groups + progressive reformers | Individual autonomy; ending criminalization | Moderate (different underlying philosophy) |
| U.S. fiscal conservatives + reform advocates | Reducing wasteful international drug spending | Moderate (potential but underdeveloped) |
| Legal cannabis industries (Canada, Uruguay, U.S. states) + trade advocates | Treaty modernization to enable legal international cannabis trade | Growing (economic interest aligning with reform) |
| Indigenous rights organizations + cultural sovereignty advocates | Protecting traditional practices criminalized by treaties | Moderate (strong moral argument, limited political power) |
| Environmental organizations + farmer cooperatives | Ending aerial fumigation; sustainable alternative development | Moderate (growing alignment on environmental harms of drug war) |

### Conflicting Interests

- **U.S. enforcement agencies vs. reform advocates**: DEA, INL, and DOD counter-narcotics programs are institutionally committed to supply-side enforcement. Reform advocates seek to rebalance funding toward health and development. This is the central conflict in U.S. international drug policy.

- **INCB vs. legalizing nations**: The INCB views cannabis legalization as a treaty violation. Canada, Uruguay, and Germany assert that their reforms are consistent with treaty obligations or that health protection justifies departure. The INCB has no enforcement mechanism, making this a reputational rather than legal conflict.

- **Producer-country governments vs. U.S. pressure**: Colombia, Bolivia, and Mexico face U.S. pressure to maintain eradication and enforcement programs that their own populations increasingly oppose. These governments must balance sovereignty, domestic politics, and the economic leverage the U.S. holds through aid and trade. Colombia's President Petro has been the most vocal in rejecting U.S. enforcement demands, telling the UN General Assembly in 2022 that the drug war has "failed" and calling for a new approach.

- **Pharmaceutical industry vs. traditional use advocates**: Pharmaceutical companies benefit from strict scheduling that limits competition from natural substances (cannabis, psilocybin, coca). Indigenous communities seek protection for traditional practices that the treaty framework criminalizes.

- **Death penalty countries vs. human rights advocates**: Thirty-five countries retain the death penalty for drug offenses (Harm Reduction International, 2023). Iran executed an estimated 255 people for drug offenses in 2023 alone. China, Singapore, Malaysia, and Indonesia also regularly execute drug offenders. Human rights organizations and the UN High Commissioner for Human Rights have called for abolition of the death penalty for drug offenses, creating a persistent tension within the CND between punitive and rights-based approaches.

### The Supply-Side Iron Triangle

The most entrenched power structure in international drug policy is the triangle connecting:

1. **U.S. enforcement agencies** (DEA, INL, DOD counter-narcotics) that depend on supply-side programs for budgets and institutional identity
2. **Defense and security contractors** that supply helicopters, surveillance equipment, weapons, and training services for counter-narcotics programs. Key contractors include Lockheed Martin (Black Hawk helicopters for Colombia), DynCorp International (aerial eradication operations), and L3Harris Technologies (surveillance systems). DynCorp alone received over $1 billion in counter-narcotics contracts in Colombia between 2000 and 2016.
3. **Congressional committees** (Judiciary, Armed Services, Foreign Affairs) whose members receive campaign contributions from defense contractors and political support from law enforcement unions

This triangle ensures continued funding for supply-side programs even when evaluations show they are not achieving their stated objectives. Breaking this triangle requires building an alternative constituency around health outcomes, fiscal responsibility, and human rights.

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on international drug policy:

- **Lobbying**: Defense contractors (Lockheed Martin, Bell Helicopter/Textron, Raytheon) lobby Congress for counter-narcotics equipment sales. The pharmaceutical industry lobbies to maintain scheduling restrictions on competing substances. Law enforcement unions lobby against decriminalization. In the 2022 election cycle, defense contractors contributed $32.4 million to Congressional candidates (OpenSecrets), while drug policy reform organizations contributed approximately $5 million.

- **Diplomatic Pressure**: The United States uses aid conditions, trade negotiations, and the annual certification process to pressure partner nations on drug policy. The INCB uses public criticism to pressure reforming nations. The EU uses development aid conditions to promote harm reduction. Latin American governments have increasingly pushed back, as demonstrated at the 2012 Cartagena Summit and the 2016 UNGASS.

- **Public Advocacy**: The Global Commission and reform organizations use media, reports, and public campaigns to shift public opinion. Counter-narcotics agencies use seizure announcements and threat briefings to justify continued funding. The Global Commission's 2011 report, "War on Drugs," generated coverage in over 100 countries and prompted editorials in major newspapers calling for reform.

- **Expertise/Information**: UNODC and EMCDDA/EUDA shape the evidence base through research publications. The DEA and ONDCP produce competing analyses emphasizing enforcement successes. Think tanks (RAND, Brookings, WOLA, TNI) provide independent analysis. The UNODC World Drug Report, published annually, is the most cited data source in international drug policy debates, and its framing of data can significantly influence policy discussions.

- **Campaign Contributions**: In the U.S., defense contractors contributed $32.4 million to Congressional candidates in the 2022 cycle (OpenSecrets). Drug policy reform organizations contributed significantly less but have been effective through ballot initiatives in states where direct democracy bypasses legislative gatekeepers.

- **Multilateral Bloc Voting**: At the CND, nations organize into voting blocs that shape policy outcomes:
    - **Reformist bloc**: EU (minus Hungary), Canada, Colombia, Mexico, Uruguay, Australia, New Zealand
    - **Prohibitionist bloc**: Russia, China, Japan, Singapore, Iran, Saudi Arabia, most Middle Eastern states
    - **Swing states**: African nations, Caribbean states, India, Brazil, South Korea
    - The 2020 cannabis reclassification vote (27-25) demonstrated how narrow the margin between these blocs has become

- **Extradition as Leverage**: The United States uses extradition -- or the threat of extradition -- as a powerful tool of influence over partner-country drug policy. Colombian and Mexican officials have noted that the threat of extradition to the U.S. shapes domestic law enforcement priorities and sometimes overrides local judicial independence. Since 2000, the U.S. has extradited over 2,500 individuals from Latin America on drug charges, including former presidents, military commanders, and cartel leaders. Colombia's Otoniel (Dairo Antonio Usuga) was extradited in 2022 despite Colombia's own transitional justice proceedings, highlighting the tension between U.S. enforcement demands and partner-country judicial sovereignty.

## Emerging Stakeholders

Several groups are becoming increasingly important stakeholders in international drug policy as the landscape evolves:

- **Legal cannabis industry**: The global legal cannabis market ($43 billion in 2023) has created a corporate constituency for treaty reform. Companies including Tilray Brands, Canopy Growth, and Aurora Cannabis in Canada, along with multi-state operators in the United States, have economic interests in international trade liberalization. However, industry capture of reform debates raises concerns about creating a "Big Cannabis" that prioritizes profit over public health.
- **Psychedelic medicine sector**: Companies developing psilocybin, MDMA, and ketamine-based therapies (Compass Pathways, MAPS/Lykos Therapeutics, Atai Life Sciences) are pushing for scheduling reform that would facilitate clinical research and market access. The sector's growth creates new allies for treaty modernization, particularly around WHO scheduling recommendations.
- **Cryptocurrency and dark web**: The growth of cryptocurrency-facilitated drug markets on the dark web has created a new dimension of international drug trade that operates largely outside traditional enforcement frameworks. Law enforcement agencies, cryptocurrency exchanges, and blockchain analysis firms (Chainalysis, Elliptic) have become stakeholders in the evolving enforcement landscape.
- **Chinese and Indian chemical industry**: As the primary sources of fentanyl precursor chemicals, chemical manufacturers in China and India are stakeholders in any precursor control regime. Their interests in maintaining legitimate chemical commerce must be balanced against the need to prevent diversion to illicit drug manufacturing.

## Affected Communities: Voices Often Excluded

The following communities bear disproportionate costs of international drug policy but are rarely included in policy design:

### Coca, Poppy, and Cannabis Farmers

- **Population**: Approximately 6 million farmers globally (UNODC estimate)
- **Key countries**: Colombia (~200,000 coca-growing families), Peru (~60,000), Bolivia (~35,000), Myanmar (~100,000 poppy-growing households), Afghanistan (pre-ban: ~250,000 households)
- **Key concern**: Livelihoods destroyed by eradication without viable alternatives
- **Representation**: Farmer cooperatives and unions in Colombia (COCCAM), Peru, and Bolivia advocate for development-first approaches and against forced eradication

### Communities Displaced by Drug Violence

- **Mexico**: Over 340,000 people internally displaced by drug violence between 2006-2023 (Internal Displacement Monitoring Centre)
- **Colombia**: 8+ million people displaced during the armed conflict, with drug violence as a major driver (UNHCR)
- **Central America**: 2.5+ million people displaced, with drug trafficking violence as a contributing factor (UNHCR)
- **Key concern**: Security, justice, reparations, right of return

### Indigenous Peoples

- **Andean coca communities**: Bolivia's 4+ million traditional coca users; Peruvian and Colombian indigenous coca-using populations
- **Cannabis traditions**: Multiple African and Asian cultures with traditional cannabis use
- **Ayahuasca practitioners**: Amazonian indigenous communities whose ceremonial practices are complicated by international drug scheduling
- **Peyote users**: Native American Church members in the U.S. and Mexico
- **Khat users**: East African and Yemeni diaspora communities whose traditional stimulant is criminalized in many Western countries
- **Key concern**: Cultural rights, sovereignty, protection of traditional practices from criminalization

### People in Countries with Death Penalty for Drug Offenses

- **35 countries** retain the death penalty for drug offenses (Harm Reduction International, 2023)
- **Iran**: Estimated 255 executions for drug offenses in 2023; Iran amended its drug laws in 2017 to raise the threshold for mandatory death sentences, commuting approximately 5,000 sentences, but continued executing individuals for trafficking above the revised threshold
- **China**: Exact numbers classified but estimated at hundreds annually; mass sentencing rallies are often held around the International Day Against Drug Abuse and Illicit Trafficking (June 26)
- **Singapore**: 11 executions for drug offenses in 2023 (highest per capita rate); mandatory death penalty for trafficking above specified thresholds has drawn international condemnation, particularly for execution of foreign nationals
- **Indonesia**: Executed 14 people for drug offenses in 2015, including foreign nationals, provoking diplomatic crises with Australia, Brazil, and Nigeria
- **Saudi Arabia**: Regularly executes individuals for drug trafficking; drug-related executions increased significantly in 2022-2023
- **Key concern**: Right to life, proportionality, due process; disproportionate impact on foreign nationals, low-level couriers, and people from economically disadvantaged backgrounds
