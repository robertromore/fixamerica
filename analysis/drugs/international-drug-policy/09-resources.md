# International Drug Policy: Resources

This section provides a curated list of key research, reports, data sources, and organizations working on international drug policy and reform.

---

## Academic Research

### Peer-Reviewed Studies

- Csete, J., et al. "Public Health and International Drug Policy." *The Lancet* 387, no. 10026 (2016): 1427-1480. <https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(16)00619-X> - Comprehensive review by the Johns Hopkins-Lancet Commission on Drug Policy and Health; foundational evidence base for health-oriented reform.
- Greenwald, G. "Drug Decriminalization in Portugal: Lessons for Creating Fair and Successful Drug Policies." *Cato Institute White Paper* (2009). <https://www.cato.org/white-paper/drug-decriminalization-portugal> - Early influential analysis of Portugal's decriminalization outcomes.
- Hughes, C.E., & Stevens, A. "What Can We Learn from the Portuguese Decriminalization of Illicit Drugs?" *British Journal of Criminology* 50, no. 6 (2010): 999-1022. - Rigorous academic evaluation of Portugal's reform showing positive health outcomes without increased drug use.
- Werb, D., et al. "Effect of Drug Law Enforcement on Drug Market Violence: A Systematic Review." *International Journal of Drug Policy* 22, no. 2 (2011): 87-94. - Meta-analysis finding that drug law enforcement increases violence.
- Mejia, D., & Restrepo, P. "The Economics of the War on Illegal Drug Production and Trafficking." *Journal of Economic Behavior & Organization* 126 (2016): 255-275. - Economic analysis of the balloon effect and supply-side failure.
- Kilmer, B., et al. "What America's Users Spend on Illegal Drugs, 2006-2016." *RAND Corporation* (2019). <https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RR3140.html> - Most comprehensive estimate of the U.S. illicit drug market size.
- Caulkins, J.P., Reuter, P., & Coulson, C. "Basing Drug Scheduling Decisions on Scientific Ranking of Harmfulness." *Addiction* 106, no. 11 (2011): 1886-1890. - Analysis showing that international drug scheduling does not correlate with scientific evidence of harm.
- Degenhardt, L., et al. "Global Patterns of Opioid Use and Dependence: Harnessing the World Drug Report Data for Action." *Addiction* 114, no. 10 (2019): 1748-1762. - Global epidemiological analysis of opioid use patterns relevant to international policy.
- Caulkins, J.P., & Reuter, P. "How Drug Enforcement Affects Drug Prices." *Crime and Justice* 39, no. 1 (2010): 213-271. - Analysis of the relationship between enforcement and drug prices, demonstrating the price elasticity of demand.
- Babor, T., et al. *Drug Policy and the Public Good*. 2nd ed. Oxford University Press, 2018. - Comprehensive evidence review by leading drug policy researchers; updated edition covers international comparative evidence.
- Kilmer, B., & Pacula, R.L. "Understanding and Learning from the Diversification of Cannabis Supply Laws." *Addiction* 112, no. 7 (2017): 1128-1135. - Analysis of the various models for cannabis supply regulation and their outcomes.
- Camacho, A., & Mejia, D. "The Health Consequences of Aerial Spraying Illicit Crops: The Case of Colombia." *Journal of Health Economics* 54 (2017): 147-160. - Peer-reviewed evidence on health harms from aerial fumigation of coca with glyphosate.

### Working Papers and White Papers

- LSE Expert Group on the Economics of Drug Policy. "Ending the Drug Wars." London School of Economics. 2014. <https://www.lse.ac.uk/ideas/projects/drug-policy> - Expert analysis of the economic failures of the War on Drugs.
- Rydell, C.P., & Everingham, S.S. "Controlling Cocaine: Supply Versus Demand Programs." RAND Drug Policy Research Center. 1994. <https://www.rand.org/pubs/monograph_reports/MR331.html> - Foundational RAND study showing treatment is 7x more cost-effective than enforcement.
- Walsh, J.M. "Are We There Yet? Measuring Progress in the U.S. War on Drugs in Latin America." WOLA Briefing Paper. 2004. - Early analysis of the gap between drug war metrics and outcomes.
- Bewley-Taylor, D. "Towards Revision of the UN Drug Control Conventions: Harnessing Like-Mindedness." *International Journal of Drug Policy* 24, no. 1 (2013): 60-68. - Legal analysis of treaty reform pathways.
- Pardo, B., Taylor, J., Caulkins, J., et al. "The Future of Fentanyl and Other Synthetic Opioids." RAND Corporation. 2019. <https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RR3117.html> - Comprehensive analysis of the synthetic opioid crisis and its implications for international drug policy.
- Caulkins, J.P., et al. "Considering Marijuana Legalization: Insights for Vermont and Other Jurisdictions." RAND Corporation. 2015. - Analysis of legalization models and their potential outcomes, applicable to international comparisons.
- Reuter, P. "Why Has U.S. Drug Policy Changed So Little over 30 Years?" *Crime and Justice* 42, no. 1 (2013): 75-140. - Analysis of the political economy of drug policy inertia.

## Legal Resources

### Case Law

- *Gonzales v. Raich*, 545 U.S. 1 (2005) - Upheld federal authority to prohibit marijuana under Commerce Clause despite state legalization; key to federal-state drug policy conflict.
- *ICJ Advisory Opinion on the Legality of Nuclear Weapons* (1996) - Established principles of treaty interpretation relevant to drug convention flexibility debates.
- *Bolivia v. INCB* (2012-2013) - Bolivia's withdrawal and re-accession to the 1961 Convention with coca reservation; precedent for treaty modification.
- *US v. Safehouse*, Third Circuit (2021) - Addressed the legality of supervised consumption sites under the federal Controlled Substances Act; relevant to international harm reduction debates.
- *R v. Parker* (Ontario Court of Appeal, 2000) - Canadian ruling that struck down criminal prohibition of medical marijuana; part of the legal trajectory leading to Canada's 2018 legalization.
- *Colombia Constitutional Court Ruling C-221* (1994) - Decriminalized personal drug possession in Colombia on constitutional grounds; influential precedent in Latin America.
- *South Africa Constitutional Court: Minister of Justice v. Prince* (2018) - Ruled that personal cannabis use and cultivation are constitutionally protected; first African national court to do so.

### Statutory References

- 21 U.S.C. Ch. 13 (Controlled Substances Act) - Domestic scheduling system that parallels international convention schedules.
- 22 U.S.C. Section 2291-2291k (International Narcotics Control, Foreign Assistance Act) - Authorizes U.S. international drug programs.
- 21 U.S.C. Section 1901-1908 (Foreign Narcotics Kingpin Designation Act) - Sanctions framework for foreign drug traffickers.
- Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs, 1961 (as amended by 1972 Protocol) - Foundational international drug control treaty.
- Convention on Psychotropic Substances, 1971 - Controls synthetic and psychotropic drugs.
- Convention against Illicit Traffic in Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances, 1988 - Enforcement-oriented treaty.
- Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties, 1969 - Governs treaty interpretation and modification; Article 41 on inter se modification is key to reform pathways.

### Model Legislation and Regulatory Frameworks

- Transform Drug Policy Foundation. "After the War on Drugs: Blueprint for Regulation." 2009 (updated 2020). - Detailed model for regulated drug markets.
- IDPC. "Model Drug Policy." 2016. - Model national drug legislation based on health and human rights principles.
- Portugal. Law 30/2000. - Text of Portugal's decriminalization law; model for other nations.
- Canada. Cannabis Act (S.C. 2018, c. 16). - Full text of Canada's cannabis legalization framework.
- Uruguay. Law 19.172 (2013). - Text of Uruguay's cannabis regulation law.
- Germany. Cannabis Act (Cannabisgesetz, 2024). - Text of Germany's cannabis legalization framework.
- Bolivia. Law of General Coca (Law 906, 2017). - Legal framework for Bolivia's regulated coca cultivation system.

## Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Balloon effect** | The displacement of drug production from one area to another in response to enforcement pressure |
| **CND** | Commission on Narcotic Drugs, the primary UN drug policymaking body |
| **Decriminalization** | Removing criminal penalties for personal drug possession while maintaining prohibition on production and trafficking |
| **Gedoogbeleid** | Dutch "tolerance policy" permitting cannabis sales in licensed coffeeshops |
| **HAT** | Heroin-assisted treatment, providing pharmaceutical-grade heroin to treatment-resistant users |
| **INCB** | International Narcotics Control Board, the quasi-judicial treaty monitoring body |
| **INL** | Bureau of International Narcotics and Law Enforcement Affairs (State Department) |
| **Inter se modification** | Treaty modification agreed among a subset of parties |
| **MAT** | Medication-assisted treatment using FDA-approved medications for substance use disorders |
| **Precursor chemicals** | Legal chemicals used as raw materials in the synthesis of illicit drugs |
| **Risk premium** | The markup on drug prices attributable to the costs and risks of operating in illegal markets |
| **Scheduling** | The classification of substances under international conventions or domestic law based on perceived harm and medical utility |
| **UNGASS** | United Nations General Assembly Special Session |
| **UNODC** | United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime |
