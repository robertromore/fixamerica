# International Drug Policy: Opposition to Reform

Proposals to reform international drug policy -- including treaty modernization, decriminalization, harm reduction expansion, and rebalancing from enforcement to health-based approaches -- face resistance from actors who believe the current prohibitionist framework is either fundamentally sound, insufficiently enforced, or politically necessary.

---

## Who Opposes Reform

### Organized Opposition

| Actor | Interest | Resources | Tactics |
|-------|----------|-----------|---------|
| International Narcotics Control Board (INCB) | Institutional mandate to enforce treaty compliance; relevance depends on prohibition framework | ~$7 million budget; moral authority; annual reports | Public criticism of reforming nations; pressure on CND delegates; media statements |
| U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) | Institutional mission and budget depend on prohibition enforcement; international operations span 69 countries | ~$600 million international operations budget | Diplomatic pressure; intelligence sharing conditions; public threat assessments |
| Russia, China (at CND) | Geopolitical opposition to Western-led reform; domestic authoritarian drug policies | CND voting power; Security Council influence | Bloc voting at CND; procedural obstruction; framing reform as Western decadence |
| Nations with death penalty for drugs (Singapore, Indonesia, Iran, Saudi Arabia, China, Malaysia) | Domestic political systems built on punitive drug enforcement | Legal systems; UN voting blocs; cultural authority | Procedural blocking at CND; sovereignty arguments; death penalty as deterrent claims |
| U.S. Congressional drug enforcement caucuses | Political identity built on "tough on drugs" messaging; campaign contributions from defense/law enforcement | Appropriations authority; committee chairmanships | Blocking reform legislation; defunding progressive programs; public hearings |
| Defense/security contractors | Revenue from counter-narcotics equipment and training contracts | Lobbying budgets; campaign contributions ($32.4 million in 2022 cycle) | Congressional lobbying; revolving door staffing; threat inflation |
| Pharmaceutical industry | Scheduling restrictions limit competition from natural substances | Lobbying budgets exceeding $350 million annually (OpenSecrets, 2023) | Lobbying against rescheduling; funding opposition research; regulatory capture |

### Ideological Opposition

- **Social Conservative**: Drug use is immoral and should be punished; international treaties embody a moral consensus that must be upheld; harm reduction "enables" drug use; legalization signals societal approval. Organizations such as the Family Research Council, the Heritage Foundation, and Smart Approaches to Marijuana (SAM) advocate against reform from this perspective.
- **National Security Hawks**: Drug trafficking organizations are security threats; weakening enforcement empowers cartels; drug policy reform undermines U.S. leverage in bilateral relationships. Proponents include former DEA administrators, retired military officers, and think tanks such as the Center for Strategic and International Studies (CSIS) drug program.
- **Law Enforcement Traditionalists**: The enforcement model works when properly resourced; measurement problems mask real successes; reform advocates are naive about organized crime. Major Police Chiefs Association and National Narcotic Officers' Associations Coalition have lobbied against decriminalization and harm reduction initiatives.
- **Prohibitionist Public Health Advocates**: Some public health voices argue that legalization increases availability and thus consumption; the "commercialization" concern (following the tobacco and alcohol model). Smart Approaches to Marijuana (SAM), led by former Obama administration official Kevin Sabet, argues that legalization creates a "Big Cannabis" industry that prioritizes profit over public health.
- **Religious Opposition**: In several regions, opposition to drug policy reform is rooted in religious doctrine. Islamic law (sharia) in countries like Saudi Arabia and Iran treats drug trafficking as a capital offense. In the United States, some evangelical organizations frame drug use as a spiritual problem requiring moral rather than medical solutions. The Holy See, while not a CND voting member, has generally aligned with prohibitionist positions at UN forums, though Pope Francis has signaled openness to harm reduction by criticizing drug war violence and supporting treatment-centered approaches.
- **Bureaucratic Self-Preservation**: International drug control has created a substantial institutional ecosystem -- the INCB, UNODC, national drug control agencies, and associated NGOs and contractors -- whose continued relevance depends on maintaining the current prohibition framework. Career officials in these institutions may oppose reform not out of ideological conviction but because reform threatens their organizational mandates, budgets, and career trajectories. The INCB's own secretariat staff, funded through UNODC, have a structural interest in maintaining the monitoring regime that justifies their positions.

## Opposition Arguments and Rebuttals

### Argument 1: The Treaty Framework is Working and Must Be Preserved

**Claim**: The three UN drug conventions represent decades of international consensus and provide a necessary framework for controlling dangerous substances. Weakening them would lead to a global free-for-all.

**Evidence Cited**: Global drug use rates would be higher without the conventions; international cooperation on precursor chemicals and money laundering depends on the treaty framework; scheduling provides a shared classification system. The INCB's 2023 Annual Report argues that the conventions provide an "indispensable framework" for international cooperation that would be "irresponsible" to weaken.

**Reality**: Global drug use has steadily increased despite the conventions. The UNODC World Drug Report (2024) estimates 296 million people used drugs in 2022, up 20% from a decade earlier. Coca cultivation hit all-time records in 2022. The conventions have not achieved their stated goal of eliminating non-medical drug use. Meanwhile, the parts of the treaty framework that facilitate cooperation (precursor control, mutual legal assistance) could be preserved while reforming the prohibitionist requirements.

**Rebuttal**: No one proposes dismantling international drug cooperation entirely. Reform proposals target the mandatory prohibition elements that have demonstrably failed, while preserving and strengthening cooperation on trafficking, money laundering, and precursor control. The conventions can be reformed through flexible interpretation, inter se modification (agreements among willing parties), or formal amendment. The experience of cannabis legalization in five countries -- with no INCB sanctions and no collapse of international cooperation -- demonstrates that treaty flexibility is already a reality.

### Argument 2: Decriminalization and Legalization Increase Drug Use

**Claim**: Removing criminal penalties for drug use sends a permissive signal that increases consumption, particularly among youth. Portugal is an exception, not a rule.

**Evidence Cited**: Cannabis use rates increased in some U.S. states following legalization; Uruguay saw modest consumption increases; the availability of legal drugs encourages experimentation.

**Reality**: The evidence is mixed but does not support the claim that decriminalization produces large increases in drug use. Portugal's decriminalization did not produce meaningful increases in drug use over two decades (EMCDDA, 2023). Canada's post-legalization surveys show youth cannabis use remained stable through 2023 (Health Canada). U.S. states that legalized cannabis saw modest increases in adult use but generally stable or declining youth use (NSDUH, 2023). A 2020 systematic review in *The Lancet* found "no strong evidence that decriminalization leads to increased drug use." Importantly, decriminalization aims to reduce *harms* (overdose, disease, incarceration), not necessarily *use* -- and on harm reduction metrics, the evidence strongly favors reform.

**Additional evidence**: The Czech Republic decriminalized personal drug possession in 2010 and subsequently maintained drug use rates among the lowest in Central Europe (EMCDDA, 2023). The Netherlands, despite decades of cannabis tolerance, has lower cannabis use rates than the United States, which maintained strict prohibition until recently. A 2019 RAND Corporation review of cannabis legalization in U.S. states found that adult use increased modestly but youth use did not (Caulkins et al., 2019).

**Rebuttal**: The relevant question is not whether any increase in use occurs but whether overall harms decrease. Portugal's drug-related deaths fell dramatically; HIV infections among people who inject drugs plummeted 95%; drug-related incarceration dropped by nearly half. Even if some adult use increases, the public health benefits of decriminalization (reduced overdose, reduced disease transmission, reduced incarceration) vastly outweigh modest increases in prevalence.

### Argument 3: Reform Would Empower Drug Trafficking Organizations

**Claim**: Weakening enforcement and legalizing drugs would benefit cartels by reducing the risks they face and allowing them to enter legal markets.

**Evidence Cited**: Cartels already diversify into legal businesses; legalization of cannabis has not eliminated the black market; organized crime adapts to legal environments.

**Reality**: Prohibition is the source of cartel power, not the solution to it. Trafficking organizations exist because prohibition creates enormous profit margins -- estimated at 500-1,000% by the Global Commission on Drug Policy (2011). Every historical example of drug prohibition has produced the same result: violent criminal organizations dominating the market. Canada's legal cannabis market has captured approximately 70% of the market by 2023, demonstrating that regulation can erode black market share. Regulation does not empower cartels; it competes with them.

**Additional evidence**: Uruguay's legal cannabis market has not been captured by organized crime; instead, the regulated system has reduced the exposure of cannabis consumers to illicit market actors who also sell more dangerous substances (JND Uruguay, 2023). In the United States, the end of alcohol prohibition in 1933 did not empower the Mafia; it eliminated the primary revenue source that had funded organized crime's expansion during Prohibition.

**Rebuttal**: The status quo is the cartel empowerment policy. Prohibition guarantees trafficking organizations exclusive access to a $500+ billion global market. Regulated legal markets -- properly designed with competitive pricing and quality control -- undercut illicit operations by providing safer, more convenient alternatives. The transition period requires maintaining enforcement against trafficking organizations, but the long-term strategy should be to shrink the illicit market through regulation, not to perpetuate it through prohibition.

### Argument 4: The United States Should Not Retreat from International Drug Control Leadership

**Claim**: The U.S. built the international drug control system and maintains it as a tool of global influence. Retreating from prohibition would undermine American credibility and diplomatic leverage.

**Evidence Cited**: Drug certification gives the U.S. leverage over partner nations; counter-narcotics cooperation provides channels for broader security cooperation; U.S. withdrawal would create a vacuum filled by less responsible actors.

**Reality**: U.S. "leadership" in international drug control is increasingly resented rather than respected. Latin American nations view the certification process as paternalistic and hypocritical, given that the United States is the world's largest consumer of illicit drugs. The 2012 Cartagena Summit demonstrated that U.S. drug policy is a source of diplomatic friction, not leverage. The U.S. can maintain leadership by leading reform -- as it partially did with the 2016 UNGASS -- rather than defending a framework that its own allies are abandoning.

**Additional evidence**: A 2018 Pew Research Center survey found that 63% of Latin Americans view U.S. drug policy unfavorably. Mexico's AMLO government explicitly refused to cooperate with DEA operations on multiple occasions, citing sovereignty concerns -- demonstrating that the coercive approach generates resistance rather than cooperation. Colombia's President Petro, despite leading the largest recipient of U.S. drug assistance, has publicly called for ending the War on Drugs at the UN General Assembly.

**Rebuttal**: True leadership means acknowledging when a strategy has failed and pioneering better approaches. The United States could regain genuine international credibility by leading the transition to evidence-based drug policy, supporting treaty modernization, and investing in health-based programs that address the root causes of both drug supply and demand.

### Argument 5: Alternative Development Has Proven Insufficient -- Enforcement Remains Necessary

**Claim**: Programs that offer farmers alternative crops have failed to eliminate drug cultivation because no legal crop can match drug crop income. Only enforcement can deter production.

**Evidence Cited**: Colombian alternative development programs have not prevented record coca cultivation; Afghan alternative livelihood programs failed comprehensively; farmers return to drug crops when enforcement pressure eases.

**Reality**: The premise is partially correct -- alternative development has often failed -- but the conclusion is wrong. Alternative development fails when it is underfunded relative to enforcement, when it is conditioned on prior eradication (forcing farmers to destroy their only income before receiving uncertain assistance), and when it ignores market access, infrastructure, and land tenure. UNODC research (2015) shows that alternative development succeeds when it is sequenced before eradication, provides genuine market access for legal crops, invests in infrastructure (roads, processing facilities), and addresses governance gaps. Thailand's successful transition from opium production in the 1970s-1990s demonstrates that alternative development works when adequately resourced and sustained over decades.

**Additional evidence**: Peru's San Martin region reduced coca cultivation by 70% between 2003 and 2015 through a development-first approach that invested in coffee, cacao, and palm oil production alongside road construction and market access (DEVIDA/UNODC, 2016). Bolivia's "social control" model -- community-based enforcement combined with limited legal cultivation -- has maintained relatively stable coca levels without the violence associated with forced eradication. Colombia's own "Families in Action" program demonstrated that conditional cash transfers to coca-farming families reduced cultivation by 13-16% in targeted areas (Camacho & Mejia, 2017).

**Rebuttal**: Enforcement alone has demonstrably failed. The relevant comparison is not between enforcement and alternative development in isolation, but between their relative cost-effectiveness. When alternative development is properly designed and adequately funded, it addresses the root cause of drug cultivation -- poverty and lack of alternatives -- rather than merely suppressing a symptom.

### Argument 6: Harm Reduction Enables Drug Use and Delays Recovery

**Claim**: Programs like needle exchanges, supervised consumption sites, and drug checking services make drug use safer and therefore more attractive, delaying the point at which users "hit bottom" and seek treatment.

**Evidence Cited**: Some individuals continue using drugs while receiving harm reduction services; the concept of "rock bottom" as necessary motivation for recovery; moral objections to government-facilitated drug use.

**Reality**: The "rock bottom" theory has been rejected by addiction medicine. The American Society of Addiction Medicine, the World Health Organization, and virtually every major medical association recognize that harm reduction saves lives and increases -- rather than decreases -- treatment engagement. Switzerland's heroin-assisted treatment program led to a 60% reduction in crime and increased treatment retention rates. Syringe service program participants are 5 times more likely to enter treatment than non-participants (CDC, 2019). OnPoint NYC's supervised consumption sites in New York City have made over 5,200 treatment referrals since opening in 2021.

**Rebuttal**: Harm reduction does not enable drug use; it keeps people alive until they are ready for treatment. Dead people cannot recover. The evidence overwhelmingly shows that harm reduction programs serve as the most effective pathway to treatment engagement for people who would not otherwise access healthcare. Opposing harm reduction on moral grounds while accepting 107,000+ annual overdose deaths in the United States alone is a moral position that requires examination.

## Historical Patterns of Opposition

Opposition to international drug policy reform has followed consistent patterns since the 1960s:

| Period | Opposition Action | Outcome |
|--------|-------------------|---------|
| 1961-1970s | U.S. and European colonial powers shaped Single Convention to reflect prohibition norms | Traditional use (coca, cannabis, opium) targeted for elimination; India's cannabis compromise allowed 25-year phase-out |
| 1988 | 1988 Convention adopted at peak of U.S. crack cocaine panic | Most enforcement-oriented treaty passed by consensus; no country opposed |
| 2001 | INCB criticized Portugal's decriminalization | No enforcement action; Portugal proceeded and produced positive outcomes |
| 2009-2012 | U.S. and INCB dismissed Latin American reform declarations as "irresponsible" | Reform advocates characterized as "pro-drug"; INCB President warned of "chaos" |
| 2013 | INCB condemned Uruguay's cannabis legalization as treaty violation | No enforcement action; Uruguay proceeded |
| 2016 | Russia, China blocked consensus at UNGASS on harm reduction language | Term "harm reduction" removed from final text; "risk and harm minimization" retained |
| 2018 | INCB criticized Canada's Cannabis Act as treaty violation | No enforcement action; Canada became largest legal market |
| 2020 | Russia, China, Japan opposed cannabis reclassification at CND | Reclassification passed narrowly (27-25); first scheduling reform |
| 2024 | Germany legalization faced opposition from neighboring EU states and INCB | Implementation proceeded; pilot commercial program planned |

**Key pattern**: The INCB criticizes every reform but takes no enforcement action -- because it has no enforcement mechanism. This pattern demonstrates that treaty flexibility is already a practical reality, even as formal opposition language persists. The gap between rhetorical opposition and practical enforcement creates a strategic opportunity: nations can pursue reform with full expectation that criticism will be vocal but consequences will be negligible.

**Implications for reform strategy**: The historical record shows that opponents of reform can delay but not prevent change when multiple nations demonstrate political will. Each reform that proceeds without consequences reduces the deterrent effect of opposition rhetoric on other nations considering similar changes. The accumulation of reform precedents -- from Portugal (2001) through Germany (2024) -- has progressively narrowed the scope of credible opposition arguments.

### Evolving Opposition Tactics

Opposition has adapted over time:

- **1960s-1980s**: Direct treaty design -- U.S. and allies wrote the conventions to encode prohibition
- **1990s-2000s**: Diplomatic coercion -- certification, aid conditions, and bilateral pressure to enforce compliance
- **2010s**: Bloc voting at CND -- Russia, China, and Middle Eastern states organized to block reform resolutions
- **2020s**: Procedural obstruction and narrative control -- opposition shifts from blocking reform to slowing implementation and controlling public framing; focus on "concerns" language rather than outright prohibition
- **Emerging**: Information warfare -- some prohibitionist states fund media campaigns portraying reform nations as experiencing drug policy failures; selective citation of data showing increased drug use without context on reduced harms
- **Emerging**: Strategic concession -- some opponents adopt partial reform positions (supporting medical cannabis while opposing recreational, accepting harm reduction while opposing decriminalization) to appear reasonable while slowing transformative change

## Regional Opposition Profiles

### Russia

- **Position**: Strongest prohibitionist voice at CND; opposed to harm reduction, decriminalization, and any treaty flexibility
- **Motivation**: Domestic authoritarianism; framing drug policy reform as "Western decadence"; geopolitical opposition to U.S.-led reform
- **Tactics**: Bloc voting at CND; procedural obstruction; successfully removed "harm reduction" from 2016 UNGASS text
- **Domestic context**: Russia has one of the world's highest heroin use rates (1.6% of population) and the fastest-growing HIV epidemic among people who inject drugs, driven in part by hostility to harm reduction

### China

- **Position**: Prohibitionist at CND; limited engagement on reform debates
- **Motivation**: Domestic drug policy built on punitive enforcement; resistance to perceived interference in sovereignty; leverage on fentanyl precursor cooperation
- **Tactics**: Bloc voting with Russia; linking precursor cooperation to broader U.S.-China negotiations
- **Domestic context**: China executes more people for drug offenses than any other country (exact numbers classified); simultaneously is the primary source of fentanyl precursor chemicals

### Singapore

- **Position**: Most vocal defender of the death penalty for drug offenses
- **Motivation**: Political system built on strict social control; drug enforcement seen as essential to national identity
- **Tactics**: Sovereignty arguments at CND; public diplomacy defending the death penalty; funding for anti-reform research
- **Domestic context**: Singapore executed 11 people for drug offenses in 2023, the highest per capita execution rate for drug offenses in the world

### Japan

- **Position**: Strongly prohibitionist at CND; one of only two countries to vote against the 2020 cannabis reclassification alongside Russia
- **Motivation**: Strict domestic drug policy with severe penalties for cannabis possession; cultural stigma around drug use; limited experience with harm reduction
- **Tactics**: Votes with prohibitionist bloc at CND; opposes flexibility language in resolutions
- **Domestic context**: Japan has among the lowest drug use rates in the developed world but extremely punitive policies, including prosecution for positive drug tests and media shaming of public figures convicted of drug offenses

### United States (Internal Opposition)

- **DEA**: Institutional opposition to any reform that reduces enforcement mandate; uses intelligence-sharing leverage with partner countries; has 93 foreign offices providing intelligence and training that would be reduced under reform
- **Congressional drug caucuses**: Members whose political identity is built on enforcement; receive campaign contributions from defense contractors and law enforcement unions
- **Defense contractors**: Lockheed Martin, DynCorp, L3Harris Technologies derive significant revenue from counter-narcotics contracts; lobbying expenditures exceed $100 million annually
- **Pharmaceutical industry**: Opposes rescheduling of cannabis, psilocybin, and other natural substances that could compete with pharmaceutical products; lobbying expenditures exceed $350 million annually (OpenSecrets, 2023)
- **Law enforcement unions**: The Fraternal Order of Police (FOP) and similar organizations consistently oppose decriminalization and harm reduction measures, arguing they undermine officer safety and public order

## Counter-Strategies

### Political Strategies

- **Build bipartisan coalitions around fiscal waste**: Frame reform as eliminating wasteful government spending on programs that do not work ($5.5 billion/year with no reduction in drug supply). This appeals to fiscal conservatives. The Cato Institute, R Street Institute, and other libertarian-leaning organizations have supported drug policy reform on fiscal and liberty grounds.
- **Center families and communities affected by drug violence**: Latin American families who have lost members to drug violence are powerful advocates. U.S. families who have lost members to fentanyl overdoses can support demand-reduction investment. The Mothers of the Disappeared in Mexico and the Colombian Truth Commission provide compelling testimonies.
- **Leverage state-level reform momentum**: 24 U.S. states have legalized cannabis, creating a domestic constituency for international treaty reform. State attorneys general and governors can be enlisted as reform advocates. The emerging interstate cannabis commerce debate could generate pressure for federal reform and, subsequently, treaty modernization.
- **Work through willing coalitions**: Rather than seeking global consensus, build coalitions of reformist nations (Canada, Uruguay, Germany, Portugal, Colombia) to establish new norms through practice. The precedent of cannabis legalization by multiple nations -- without INCB sanctions -- demonstrates that norm change can precede formal treaty change.
- **Engage the private sector**: The global legal cannabis industry ($43 billion in 2023, growing 15-20% annually) has an economic interest in treaty reform that would enable international trade. Financial institutions seeking clarity on cannabis banking, and pharmaceutical companies exploring psychedelic medicine, represent potential private-sector allies.

### Communication Strategies

- **Reframe from "legalization" to "regulation"**: Public opinion research shows that "regulation" polls better than "legalization." Emphasize that reform means controlling drugs through regulated frameworks, not abandoning control. Transform Drug Policy Foundation's "After the War on Drugs: Blueprint for Regulation" demonstrates effective use of this framing.
- **Lead with health outcomes**: Present reform in terms of lives saved, overdoses prevented, and diseases reduced. Health framing is less politically polarizing than rights or liberty framing. Switzerland's heroin-assisted treatment program -- which reduced crime by 60% and new heroin initiations by 82% -- provides compelling health-centered evidence.
- **Use fiscal language**: "$5.5 billion per year in international drug spending with no reduction in drug availability" is a powerful message for voters across the political spectrum.
- **Cite conservative reform champions**: Former Secretary of State George Shultz, the Cato Institute, and libertarian Republicans have supported drug policy reform. Citing conservative champions reduces partisan resistance. Former DEA chief judge Francis Young's 1988 ruling that marijuana is "one of the safest therapeutically active substances known to man" can be cited to challenge DEA credibility.
- **Humanize the impact**: Stories of specific individuals and communities affected by failed drug policies -- the Mexican mother whose son was disappeared, the Colombian farmer whose legal crops were destroyed by fumigation, the American family who lost a child to fentanyl -- are more persuasive than abstract statistics.

### Policy Design Strategies

- **Propose incremental reforms**: Rather than demanding full treaty renegotiation, advocate for flexible treaty interpretation, inter se agreements among willing nations, and executive branch rebalancing of program funding
- **Include robust enforcement against trafficking**: Reform proposals should explicitly maintain and strengthen enforcement against large-scale trafficking organizations, distinguishing clearly between personal use and commercial trafficking
- **Build in evaluation mechanisms**: Require evidence-based evaluation of all programs, with funding tied to demonstrated effectiveness in reducing drug harms (not enforcement activity metrics)
- **Protect sovereignty**: Frame reform as enabling nations to choose evidence-based approaches that fit their circumstances, rather than imposing a single model
- **Address the fentanyl crisis directly**: Reform proposals must include credible fentanyl-response strategies (precursor diplomacy, demand reduction, harm reduction) to avoid being dismissed as irrelevant to the most urgent drug policy challenge
- **Sequence reforms strategically**: Begin with least controversial reforms (flexible treaty interpretation, alternative development expansion) and build toward more ambitious changes (treaty modification, regulated markets)
- **Anticipate and preempt opposition narratives**: For each reform proposal, prepare rebuttals to predictable opposition arguments before they gain traction. The "response to specific opposition scenarios" table below provides a template for this approach.
- **Build a coalition of unusual allies**: The most effective political coalitions for drug policy reform have combined progressive reformers, libertarian conservatives, faith-based organizations focused on mercy and rehabilitation, and fiscal hawks. Organizations like the Law Enforcement Action Partnership (LEAP) provide politically powerful conservative voices for reform.

## The Role of Media and Public Opinion

Public opinion on drug policy reform has shifted significantly in recent decades, though opposition forces retain significant media influence:

- **Shifting public opinion**: Global surveys show growing support for alternative approaches. A 2023 Gallup poll found that 70% of Americans support cannabis legalization, up from 25% in 1995. A 2022 Pew Research survey found that 65% of Americans believe the government should focus on providing treatment rather than prosecuting drug users.
- **Media framing**: Prohibition-aligned institutions (DEA, INCB) generate media coverage through seizure announcements, threat assessments, and "drug bust" reporting that reinforces enforcement narratives. Reform organizations struggle to generate equivalent coverage because public health successes (overdoses prevented, lives saved) are less dramatic than enforcement actions.
- **Opposition media strategy**: Some prohibitionist states fund media campaigns in reform-leaning countries. Russia-affiliated media outlets have published reports characterizing cannabis legalization as causing social decay. Chinese state media has emphasized U.S. drug problems as evidence of Western societal decline while minimizing China's own drug challenges.
- **Counter-narratives**: Effective reform communication centers the stories of affected individuals -- the Mexican mother whose son was disappeared, the Colombian farmer whose crops were destroyed, the American family that lost a member to fentanyl. Human stories are more persuasive than abstract policy arguments and harder for opposition to dismiss.
