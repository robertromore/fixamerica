# International Drug Policy: Implementation Roadmap

## Strategic Approach

Reform of U.S. international drug policy proceeds in three phases: foundation-building (executive action, diplomatic signaling, pilot programs), structural reform (Congressional reauthorization, new funding streams, treaty modernization), and systemic transformation (full spending rebalance, treaty reform, alternative development at scale). Each phase builds on the previous one, creating political space and evidentiary support for progressively more ambitious reforms. The strategy prioritizes executive branch actions that can proceed without Congressional approval in Phase 1, while building the political coalition needed for legislative action in Phase 2.

### Strategic Prerequisites

Before launching the phased implementation, several groundwork activities must be completed:

- **Interagency assessment**: ONDCP commissions a comprehensive review of all international drug programs, assessing cost-effectiveness against health outcome metrics (not enforcement activity metrics). The GAO and SIGAR Afghanistan counter-narcotics review provide models for this assessment.
- **Diplomatic sounding**: State Department consults with key reform-aligned nations (Canada, Germany, Portugal, Colombia, Uruguay, Mexico) to gauge willingness to co-lead reform efforts at the CND and in bilateral forums.
- **Congressional engagement**: Identify bipartisan champions in the Senate Foreign Relations, House Foreign Affairs, and Appropriations committees. The Western Hemisphere Drug Policy Commission's 2020 recommendations provide a bipartisan foundation.
- **Stakeholder mapping**: Identify and engage the key institutional actors who will resist reform (DEA, DOD counter-narcotics, defense contractors) and develop mitigation strategies for each.
- **Communication strategy**: Develop public messaging that frames reform as fiscally responsible, evidence-based, and tough on trafficking organizations while being smart on public health. Prepare talking points that counter anticipated opposition arguments and highlight bipartisan support for reform.
- **Baseline data collection**: Commission UNODC and EMCDDA/EUDA to establish baseline data on drug-related health outcomes, violence, and cultivation in all priority countries before reforms begin, ensuring that impact can be measured against a clear starting point.

### Phase 1 Diplomatic Strategy

The diplomatic strategy in Phase 1 focuses on building a coalition of reformist nations while avoiding direct confrontation with prohibitionist states. Key elements:

- **Reformist core**: Canada, Germany, Portugal, Uruguay, Colombia, Mexico form the initial nucleus. These nations have already demonstrated commitment to evidence-based reform and have institutional experience with alternative approaches.
- **European engagement**: The EU as a bloc generally supports evidence-based approaches. Engaging the EU through the EUDA (European Union Drugs Agency) and the EU-CELAC (Community of Latin American and Caribbean States) dialogue provides a multilateral channel.
- **CND strategy**: At the annual CND session in Vienna, the reform caucus focuses on process-oriented resolutions (calling for evidence review, national discretion language, health-centered metrics) rather than directly challenging the conventions, which would trigger prohibitionist bloc opposition.
- **Bilateral with China**: Embed fentanyl precursor cooperation in the broader U.S.-China diplomatic agenda, offering specific incentives (technology transfer for chemical industry monitoring, joint research) in exchange for enforcement commitments.

### Legislative Requirements

- No new legislation required for Phase 1 -- all actions within existing executive authority
- Congressional notification required for budget reprogramming above $1M
- Presidential Decision Directive formalizing the new international drug policy approach
- Begin informal Congressional engagement to prepare for Phase 2 legislation
- Identify bipartisan Congressional champions for Phase 2 legislative vehicles

### Expected Outcomes

Phase 1 establishes the policy foundation through executive action: a revised ONDCP strategy, a diplomatic coalition at the CND, pilot harm reduction programs generating evidence, and the beginning of a shift from certification to cooperation. These actions create political space and evidentiary support for the Congressional action required in Phase 2.

### Phase 1 Key Deliverables

- Published ONDCP strategy revision with health-outcome metrics replacing enforcement-activity metrics
- State Department Legal Adviser opinion supporting flexible treaty interpretation
- Coalition of 15+ reform-aligned nations at the CND
- Pilot harm reduction programs operational in 5 high-burden countries with baseline data collected
- 3-5 bilateral cooperative agreements signed (replacing certification with mutual accountability)
- USAID development-first pilot programs operational in 3 coca-growing regions
- Comprehensive independent evaluation of all international drug programs completed
- Interagency precursor control working group operational with action plan for Phase 2 negotiations

- Achieve Congressional reauthorization of international drug programs with new allocation mandates
- Establish the Global Drug Harm Reduction Initiative
- Triple alternative development funding

- Formalize cooperative bilateral agreements replacing certification

| Triple USAID alternative development funding | Congress/USAID | Years 3-5 | $1B/year (up from $350M) | Expanded programs in all major producer countries |

### Phase 2 Legislative Strategy

Congressional action in Phase 2 requires building a bipartisan coalition. Key legislative vehicles:

- **INL Reauthorization Act**: Reauthorizes the Bureau of International Narcotics and Law Enforcement Affairs with new statutory language mandating 60/40 demand/supply allocation. Includes provisions requiring evidence-based evaluation of all programs.
- **Global Drug Harm Reduction Initiative Act**: New legislation modeled on PEPFAR, authorizing $500M/year for international harm reduction. Key selling points: PEPFAR precedent, bipartisan global health support, lives saved metric.
- **Foreign Assistance Act Amendment**: Repeals the certification process (Section 490) and replaces it with cooperative framework language. Include sunset clause to ensure review.
- **USAID Alternative Development Authorization**: Expands USAID authority and funding for alternative development with explicit sequencing requirements (development before eradication).

### Legislative Requirements

- Reauthorization of INL programs with allocation mandates (requires new legislation)
- Global Drug Harm Reduction Initiative Act (new legislation, modeled on PEPFAR reauthorization)
- Appropriations increases for USAID alternative development
- Repeal of certification requirements in the Foreign Assistance Act

### Expected Outcomes

Phase 2 institutionalizes reform through legislation: Congressional mandates for spending rebalancing, a dedicated harm reduction initiative, expanded alternative development, and the replacement of certification with cooperative frameworks. The fentanyl precursor agreement addresses the most urgent synthetic drug challenge. A CND resolution affirms the legitimacy of diverse national approaches.

## Phase 3: Systemic Transformation (Years 6-10)

### Objectives

- Complete the transition to 60/40 demand/supply spending balance
- Achieve measurable reductions in drug violence and cultivation in partner countries
- Establish an international track record of evidence-based reform success
- Support formal treaty modernization or inter se modification

### Key Actions

| Action | Lead Actor | Timeline | Resources Needed | Success Metric |
|--------|-----------|----------|------------------|----------------|
| Complete spending rebalance to 60/40 demand/supply | ONDCP/OMB | Years 6-7 | Budget authority | Verified 60/40 allocation |
| Scale alternative development to full coverage in major producer regions | USAID | Years 6-10 | $1B+/year sustained | Measurable cultivation reduction |
| Operationalize fentanyl precursor control agreement | State/International body | Years 6-8 | $200-300M/year | Inspection regime operational |
| Support inter se treaty modification among willing nations | State Department | Years 7-10 | Diplomatic effort | Agreement among 30+ nations |
| Publish comprehensive 10-year evaluation of reform outcomes | ONDCP/GAO | Year 10 | $15-20M evaluation | Report published with data |
| Develop international regulatory framework for legal cannabis trade | State/USTR | Years 7-10 | Diplomatic/legal resources | Framework agreement among legalizing nations |
| Establish permanent international precursor monitoring body | State/Multilateral | Years 8-10 | $100-200M/year ongoing | Operational monitoring body with inspection capacity |
| Support partner-country institutional reforms to sustain gains | USAID/State | Years 6-10 | Sustained assistance | Institutional capacity assessments show improvement |

### Phase 3 Treaty Reform Strategy

By Phase 3, the accumulation of state practice, evidence, and diplomatic momentum should make formal treaty modernization feasible. Options include:

- **Inter se modification** (Article 41, Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties): A subset of parties agrees to modify the conventions among themselves, while other parties continue under the original text. This requires that the modification does not affect the rights of non-modifying parties and is not incompatible with the treaty's object and purpose. A coalition of 30+ reform-aligned nations pursuing inter se modification would represent a critical mass.
- **Protocol approach**: Adopt a new protocol to the 1961 Convention that creates an opt-out mechanism for cannabis and potentially other substances, while preserving the core framework for trafficking cooperation, precursor control, and mutual legal assistance.
- **Formal amendment**: The most ambitious but least likely path. Requires a diplomatic conference and ratification by a majority of parties. Prohibitionist states would almost certainly block this approach.
- **Normative evolution**: Continue the pattern of state practice diverging from strict treaty text, allowing the conventions to evolve through interpretation and practice rather than formal amendment. This is the path already being followed de facto with cannabis legalization.

### Expected Outcomes

Phase 3 completes the transformation: a fully rebalanced international drug budget, measurable declines in drug violence and cultivation in partner countries, an operational precursor control regime, and formal or informal treaty modernization. The 10-year evaluation provides the evidence base for sustained policy and identifies areas for further reform.

### Phase 3 Key Milestones

- **Year 6**: Spending rebalance reaches 55/45 demand/supply; precursor control agreement enters into force; first inspection cycle completed
- **Year 7**: Full 60/40 spending rebalance achieved; 50+ nations receive U.S. harm reduction assistance; alternative development programs serve 1.5 million farming families
- **Year 8**: Inter se treaty modification agreed among 30+ nations; international cannabis regulatory framework established among legalizing nations; permanent precursor monitoring body operational
- **Year 9**: Comprehensive mid-Phase 3 evaluation published; policy adjustments based on evidence; transition planning for partner-country ownership of programs
- **Year 10**: 10-year comprehensive evaluation published; next-generation reform agenda developed; partner countries assume primary ownership of health and development programs with continued U.S. technical support

## Governance and Oversight

- **ONDCP**: Primary coordination responsibility for interagency international drug policy; publishes annual progress reports against roadmap metrics
- **Congressional Oversight**: Foreign Affairs and Appropriations Committees conduct biannual hearings on reform implementation; GAO conducts independent evaluations
- **Interagency Review Board**: New body comprising representatives from State, USAID, DEA, DOD, HHS, and ONDCP meets quarterly to review program performance and resolve disputes. The Board has authority to recommend budget adjustments based on program performance.
- **Independent Evaluation**: GAO and contracted independent evaluators produce biennial assessments of all programs against health and development outcome metrics (replacing enforcement-activity metrics). Evaluations are published publicly to ensure transparency.
- **International Accountability**: Annual reports to CND on U.S. reform progress; peer review by partner nations. Joint evaluation mechanisms with partner countries ensure mutual accountability.
- **Civil Society Advisory Committee**: Establish a standing advisory committee including representatives from reform organizations (DPA, IDPC, WOLA, Transform), public health organizations, affected communities, and independent researchers to provide input on program design and evaluation.

## Risk Mitigation

### Risk 1: Congressional Resistance to Spending Rebalancing

**Likelihood**: High

**Impact**: High

**Mitigation Strategy**: Build bipartisan coalition around fiscal waste narrative ($5.5 billion/year with no supply reduction); enlist fiscal conservatives who oppose ineffective government spending; present independent evaluations showing enforcement program failures; engage state-level leaders from cannabis-legal states. Identify specific Congressional champions: historically, drug policy reform has attracted both libertarian-conservative (e.g., Rand Paul, Mike Lee) and progressive-liberal (e.g., Cory Booker, Earl Blumenauer) support.

**Contingency Plan**: If Congressional reauthorization stalls, maximize executive branch reprogramming authority; use pilot program results to build case; seek bipartisan champions. Executive branch can reprogram up to 10% of INL budget without Congressional notification, and up to 25% with notification.

### Risk 2: Diplomatic Backlash from Prohibitionist Nations

**Likelihood**: Medium

**Impact**: Medium

**Mitigation Strategy**: Frame reform as enabling national discretion, not imposing a model; maintain strong enforcement against transnational trafficking; engage Russia and China on shared fentanyl concerns; build reformist coalition to outnumber prohibitionist bloc at CND. Avoid framing reform as a Western agenda by including Latin American, African, and Asian reformist voices prominently.

**Contingency Plan**: If CND resolution fails, pursue reform through bilateral agreements and inter se modification without formal CND endorsement; cite precedent of cannabis-legalizing nations operating outside strict treaty compliance without sanction.

### Risk 3: Escalation of Drug Violence During Transition

**Likelihood**: Medium

**Impact**: High

**Mitigation Strategy**: Maintain enforcement against major trafficking organizations during transition; target financial networks rather than agricultural supply chains; support partner-country institutional strengthening; phase transition gradually to avoid destabilizing existing security arrangements. Ensure that alternative development programs include security components (community policing, protection for cooperative farmers).

**Contingency Plan**: If violence escalates in specific countries, provide emergency security assistance while maintaining long-term reform trajectory; adapt country-specific programs to local conditions; avoid reverting to failed enforcement-only approaches.

### Risk 4: China Refuses Meaningful Fentanyl Precursor Cooperation

**Likelihood**: Medium-High

**Impact**: High

**Mitigation Strategy**: Embed precursor cooperation in broader U.S.-China diplomatic framework; offer reciprocal concessions in areas China values; build multilateral pressure through allies; develop alternative supply chain monitoring capabilities using AI and financial intelligence. Engage India as an alternative partner for precursor monitoring given India's large chemical industry.

**Contingency Plan**: If bilateral negotiations fail, pursue multilateral precursor agreement without China; invest in border detection technology; focus on domestic demand reduction to limit the market for fentanyl; work with India and other alternative precursor sources. Develop unilateral detection and interdiction capabilities as a bridge strategy.

### Risk 5: Alternative Development Programs Fail to Reduce Cultivation

**Likelihood**: Medium

**Impact**: Medium

**Mitigation Strategy**: Apply lessons from Thailand and UNODC best practices; ensure programs address market access and infrastructure (not just crop substitution); provide multi-year guaranteed purchase agreements; involve communities in program design; sequence development before eradication. Build in rapid evaluation cycles (6-month check-ins) with authority to redesign underperforming programs.

**Contingency Plan**: If specific programs underperform, conduct rapid evaluations and redesign; accept that transition from drug crops takes 10-15 years (as it did in Thailand); maintain realistic expectations and communicate them to Congress.

### Risk 6: Domestic Political Backlash ("Soft on Drugs")

**Likelihood**: Medium-High

**Impact**: Medium

**Mitigation Strategy**: Proactively communicate that reform means *smarter* enforcement (targeting trafficking organization finances, not farmer livelihoods) combined with health investment. Emphasize that the current approach is "soft on results" -- $50+ billion spent with no reduction in drug supply. Lead with fentanyl crisis response credentials. Maintain visible enforcement actions against trafficking organizations throughout the transition.

**Contingency Plan**: If political backlash intensifies, slow the pace of public-facing reforms while continuing executive branch rebalancing. Focus messaging on fentanyl-specific responses and fiscal responsibility. Engage law enforcement voices who support reform (e.g., Law Enforcement Action Partnership).

### Personnel

- Phase 1: 50-100 new public health and development specialists across embassies and USAID missions
- Phase 2: 200-300 additional staff for Global Drug Harm Reduction Initiative; expanded USAID alternative development teams; 50+ precursor control specialists
- Phase 3: Sustained staffing for mature programs; precursor control inspectorate (100-200 international positions); evaluation and monitoring staff; transition planning for partner-country ownership of programs

### Infrastructure

- **Alternative development**: Roads, irrigation systems, processing facilities, cold storage in drug-producing regions (funded through USAID). Key investments include approximately 5,000 km of rural roads in Colombia, Peru, and Bolivia to connect farming communities to markets, plus 200+ processing facilities for value-added agricultural products.
- **Harm reduction**: Treatment facilities, naloxone distribution networks, syringe service programs, and supervised consumption sites in partner countries. Priority infrastructure includes 50+ opioid substitution therapy clinics in Mexico and Central America, 100+ syringe service access points across West Africa, and naloxone distribution through existing primary health care networks.
- **Precursor control**: Laboratory analysis equipment, monitoring technology, shared databases, and a network of regional precursor analysis laboratories. The precursor inspection regime requires facilities in at least 15 countries, with advanced mass spectrometry and AI-powered chemical identification capabilities.
- **Evaluation**: Data collection and monitoring systems in partner countries; secure communications for joint evaluation mechanisms. Requires investment in drug use and health surveillance systems in partner countries that currently lack reliable data.
- **Digital infrastructure**: Secure communication networks for multilateral cooperation, blockchain-based supply chain tracking for precursor chemicals, and integrated data platforms linking health, law enforcement, and development data across partner countries.
