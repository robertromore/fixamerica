# International Drug Policy: Solutions

Reforming international drug policy requires a multi-faceted approach that rebalances U.S. foreign drug spending from enforcement to health, modernizes the treaty framework, strengthens alternative development, and supports partner nations in adopting evidence-based approaches.

---

## Reform Framework

### Guiding Principles

- Evidence over ideology: Base international drug policy on scientific evidence and public health outcomes, not on moral prohibition or political expedience
- Health outcomes as the primary metric: Measure success by reductions in overdose deaths, HIV transmission, drug-related violence, and incarceration -- not by hectares eradicated or kilograms seized
- Respect sovereignty: Support partner nations in choosing approaches that fit their circumstances, rather than conditioning aid on compliance with U.S. enforcement priorities
- Address root causes: Invest in the poverty, governance failures, and lack of economic opportunity that drive drug crop cultivation and trafficking
- Maintain enforcement against trafficking organizations: Continue targeting large-scale trafficking networks and their financial infrastructure while decriminalizing personal use
- Proportionality: Ensure that drug policy responses are proportionate to the harms they address and do not themselves cause greater harm than the drug use they target

### Theory of Change

The current system fails because it targets the supply of drugs rather than the demand, economics, and governance failures that drive the drug trade. Reform works by:

1. **Shifting U.S. international spending from enforcement to health and development** -- redirecting the $5.5 billion annual budget from programs that suppress symptoms (eradication, interdiction) to programs that address causes (poverty, governance, demand)
2. **Supporting treaty flexibility that enables evidence-based national approaches** -- building a coalition of nations that interpret the conventions to permit decriminalization, harm reduction, and regulated markets
3. **Investing in alternative development that addresses why farmers grow drug crops** -- providing viable economic alternatives with market access, infrastructure, and land tenure
4. **Building partner-country health systems to reduce demand** -- applying the PEPFAR model to drug-related health challenges, including treatment, naloxone distribution, and syringe services
5. **Targeting trafficking organizations' finances rather than their agricultural supply chains** -- shifting enforcement focus from farmers and street-level dealers to the financial networks that sustain transnational criminal organizations
6. **Addressing the synthetic drug challenge through precursor diplomacy** -- establishing multilateral chemical monitoring regimes that control fentanyl production at the source without relying on agricultural interdiction models

The theory of change assumes that evidence from pilot programs and early reforms will build the political coalition needed for more ambitious structural changes. This is consistent with the historical pattern in which Portugal's decriminalization (2001) created the political space for Uruguay's legalization (2013), which supported Canada's legalization (2018), which influenced Germany's legalization (2024).

## Proposed Solutions

### Solution 1: Rebalance U.S. International Drug Spending from Enforcement to Health and Development

**Description**: Restructure the allocation of the approximately $5.5 billion U.S. international drug control budget to direct at least 60% toward demand reduction, alternative development, public health infrastructure, and governance strengthening, with no more than 40% to supply-side enforcement. Currently, approximately 80% goes to supply-side programs and 20% to demand-side programs. This rebalancing would shift approximately $2.2 billion annually from enforcement to health and development.

**Evidence Base**: RAND Corporation research consistently demonstrates that treatment and prevention are 7-10 times more cost-effective than supply-side interventions at reducing drug consumption (Rydell & Everingham, 1994; Caulkins et al., 2005). The London School of Economics IDPP report (2016) concluded that enforcement-heavy approaches have failed to reduce global drug supply. USAID's own evaluations show that development-oriented programs produce more sustainable outcomes than enforcement-conditioned assistance. The Western Hemisphere Drug Policy Commission (2020) recommended this rebalancing in its report to Congress.

**Implementation**: Executive branch rebalancing through ONDCP strategy revision and Presidential Decision Directive; Congressional reauthorization of INL and DOD counter-narcotics programs with new allocation mandates; phased transition over 5 years to avoid destabilizing existing programs. Specific reallocation priorities:

| From | To | Annual Shift |
|------|------|-------------|
| Aerial eradication programs | Alternative development with market access | ~$300 million |
| Military equipment transfers | Public health infrastructure in partner countries | ~$400 million |
| DEA capacity building (enforcement-only) | Integrated enforcement + health training | ~$200 million |
| General interdiction spending | Harm reduction and treatment programs abroad | ~$500 million |
| Military counter-narcotics training | Governance, anti-corruption, and institutional reform | ~$300 million |
| Detection and monitoring (excess capacity) | Precursor chemical intelligence and monitoring | ~$200 million |
| Surplus DOD counter-narcotics | USAID rural infrastructure in producer regions | ~$300 million |

**Cost/Resources**: Budget-neutral at the aggregate level -- same total spending, different allocation. May require new personnel with public health and development expertise at INL and in U.S. embassies. Estimated 200-300 additional health and development specialists needed across embassies and USAID missions.

**Timeline**: 2 years for executive branch rebalancing; 3-5 years for Congressional reauthorization; 5-7 years for full transition.

**Challenges**: Institutional resistance from DEA, DOD, and defense contractors; Congressional defense hawks; concern about abandoning partner-country security forces; workforce transition from enforcement to development professionals.

**Impact Metrics**: Share of budget allocated to demand reduction vs. supply reduction; partner-country treatment access rates; overdose rates in countries receiving U.S. assistance; independent program evaluations; drug-related violence trends in partner countries.

### Solution 2: Support UN Drug Treaty Modernization Through Flexible Interpretation

**Description**: Lead a coalition of willing nations to advance flexible interpretation of the existing drug conventions -- rather than formal treaty amendment -- to accommodate evidence-based approaches including decriminalization, harm reduction, and regulated markets. This approach uses the "constitutional principles" and "basic concepts of legal system" language in Article 3(2) of the 1988 Convention, plus the general welfare clauses in all three treaties, to argue that nations may adopt health-oriented approaches consistent with their treaty obligations.

**Evidence Base**: Legal scholarship by Bewley-Taylor (2012), Walsh (2014), and the Transnational Institute documents extensive treaty flexibility. The INCB has not taken enforcement action against any nation that has legalized cannabis, suggesting de facto acceptance. The Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties permits interpretation in light of subsequent practice, and the practice of 30+ nations that have decriminalized constitutes significant subsequent practice. The International Law Commission's work on treaty modification through practice provides additional legal support.

**Implementation**:

- Issue a formal legal opinion from the State Department Legal Adviser supporting flexible treaty interpretation
- Co-sponsor a CND resolution affirming national discretion in drug policy
- Establish an inter se agreement among reformist nations (Canada, Uruguay, Germany, Portugal, Colombia) creating a "drug policy reform caucus" at the CND
- Commission an independent legal analysis from the International Law Commission on the scope of treaty flexibility
- Engage the WHO Expert Committee on Drug Dependence to provide scientific assessments supporting reclassification of additional substances

**Cost/Resources**: Minimal direct cost -- diplomatic effort and legal analysis. Estimated $5-10 million for diplomatic engagement and legal work.

**Timeline**: Legal opinion within 1 year; CND resolution within 2 years; inter se agreement within 3 years.

**Challenges**: Opposition from Russia, China, and prohibitionist states at CND; INCB criticism; domestic political criticism of "weakening" treaties; complexity of multinational legal negotiations.

**Precedents and Legal Foundation**: Multiple legal analyses support the viability of flexible treaty interpretation:

- **The "general welfare" clauses**: Article 38 of the 1961 Convention requires parties to "give special attention to and take all practicable measures for the prevention of abuse of drugs and for the early identification, treatment, education, after-care, rehabilitation and social reintegration of the persons involved." Reformist legal scholars argue this language supports health-oriented approaches that may depart from strict prohibition requirements.
- **Article 3(2) of the 1988 Convention**: Conditions criminalization requirements on "constitutional principles and the basic concepts of its legal system," providing explicit flexibility for nations whose legal traditions conflict with mandatory criminalization.
- **Bolivia precedent (2012)**: Bolivia's successful withdrawal and re-accession with a coca reservation demonstrated that treaty flexibility is achievable through existing diplomatic mechanisms.
- **De facto acceptance of cannabis legalization**: The INCB has not initiated any enforcement process against Canada, Uruguay, or Germany, suggesting that the practical enforcement of strict prohibition has already eroded.
- **Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties, Article 31**: Subsequent practice that establishes agreement regarding interpretation may be considered in treaty interpretation. The practice of 30+ nations that have decriminalized constitutes significant subsequent practice.

**Impact Metrics**: Number of nations supporting flexible interpretation; CND voting outcomes; INCB annual report language; number of nations adopting evidence-based reforms.

### Solution 3: Scale Effective Alternative Development Programs

**Description**: Dramatically expand and improve alternative development programs in drug-producing regions, drawing on lessons from Thailand's successful transition from opium production and UNODC best practices. Key improvements include: sequencing alternative development *before* eradication (not after); investing in market access infrastructure (roads, processing facilities, cold chains); securing land tenure for farming communities; and providing multi-year guaranteed purchase agreements for legal crops.

**Evidence Base**: Thailand's Royal Project reduced opium cultivation from 17,920 hectares (1965) to under 300 hectares (2002) through sustained investment in alternative crops, infrastructure, and market access over 30+ years. UNODC Alternative Development Guidelines (2015) identify sequencing, market access, and participatory design as critical success factors. Colombia's own studies show that 78% of coca farmers would switch to legal crops given equivalent income and market access (Colombia National Survey, 2018). Peru's San Martin region reduced coca cultivation by 70% between 2003 and 2015 through a development-first model focused on coffee, cacao, and palm oil production with road construction and export market access (DEVIDA/UNODC, 2016).

**Key Design Principles** (derived from successful programs):

| Principle | What Works | What Fails |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Sequencing | Development assistance before eradication | Eradication conditioned on prior crop destruction |
| Market access | Roads, processing facilities, export agreements | Crop substitution without market linkages |
| Land tenure | Formal land titles for farming communities | Programs on contested or untitled land |
| Time horizon | 10-15 year commitments (as in Thailand) | 3-5 year project cycles |
| Community participation | Participatory design with farmer input | Top-down program design by external agencies |
| Income parity | Guaranteed purchase agreements matching drug crop income | Below-market prices for alternative crops |
| Security | Development in secured areas | Development in active conflict zones |

**Implementation**: Triple USAID alternative development funding from $350 million to $1 billion annually; establish multi-year guaranteed purchase programs for coca/poppy region farmers; invest in rural infrastructure; condition eradication on availability of viable alternatives; create public-private partnerships for legal crop marketing.

**Cost/Resources**: $1 billion/year (up from $350 million); offset by reducing eradication and military programs. 10-year total investment of approximately $10 billion, comparable to Plan Colombia's total but directed at root causes.

**Timeline**: Program design and partnerships in years 1-2; initial deployments in years 2-3; measurable cultivation shifts in years 5-7; sustained transition in years 7-15.

**Challenges**: Farmer skepticism based on past broken promises; narco-trafficking organizations threatening farmers who switch to legal crops; infrastructure deficits in remote growing regions; political difficulty of reducing eradication budgets.

**Impact Metrics**: Hectares transitioned from drug crops to legal agriculture; farmer income levels; road and infrastructure investment; legal crop export volumes; cultivation survey trends.

### Solution 4: Establish International Harm Reduction Funding

**Description**: Create a dedicated U.S. international harm reduction funding stream of $500 million annually to support naloxone distribution, opioid substitution therapy, syringe service programs, drug checking services, and supervised consumption facilities in partner countries. Model this on the PEPFAR (President's Emergency Plan for AIDS Relief) framework, which demonstrated that U.S. global health investments can achieve transformative results.

**Evidence Base**: PEPFAR invested $100+ billion since 2003 and is credited with saving 25+ million lives through HIV/AIDS treatment and prevention. Harm reduction programs have been shown to reduce HIV transmission by 50-70% (WHO, 2023), reduce overdose deaths by 25-50% where naloxone is widely available (CDC, 2023), and reduce drug-related crime by connecting users to services. Switzerland's heroin-assisted treatment program reduced crime among participants by 60% and new heroin initiations by 82%. The PEPFAR model demonstrates several transferable lessons:

- **Presidential leadership**: PEPFAR's success was driven by presidential-level commitment (George W. Bush), which insulated the program from bureaucratic resistance
- **Evidence-based metrics**: PEPFAR tracked specific health outcomes (lives saved, infections prevented), not enforcement outputs
- **Country ownership**: PEPFAR programs were designed in partnership with recipient countries, building local capacity rather than imposing external models
- **Bipartisan support**: PEPFAR maintained bipartisan Congressional support across four administrations, demonstrating that global health initiatives can transcend partisan divides

**Implementation**: Congressional authorization of a "Global Drug Harm Reduction Initiative" modeled on PEPFAR; housed at USAID with coordination by the Global Health Security Agenda; bilateral agreements with high-burden countries; partnerships with WHO and UNODC.

**Priority Countries**: Based on burden of drug-related disease and deaths:

| Priority Tier | Countries | Key Interventions |
|---------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Tier 1 (highest burden) | Mexico, Colombia, Brazil, Russia, Ukraine, South Africa, Kenya, Nigeria | Full harm reduction package: naloxone, OST, SSPs, SCS |
| Tier 2 | Peru, Central America, Philippines, Thailand, Myanmar, Iran, Pakistan | OST, naloxone, SSPs, drug checking |
| Tier 3 | West Africa, Central Asia, Southeast Asia, Caribbean | SSPs, naloxone, treatment referral networks |

**Cost/Resources**: $500 million/year; offset partially by reduced enforcement spending. This represents roughly 10% of U.S. international drug spending redirected to harm reduction.

**Timeline**: Congressional authorization in year 1-2; initial programs in year 2-3; scale-up in years 3-5; global coverage of high-burden countries by year 7.

**Challenges**: Congressional opposition to funding harm reduction internationally; stigma around supervised consumption; partner-country legal barriers to harm reduction; coordination with existing health programs.

**Impact Metrics**: Lives saved (overdose deaths averted); HIV infections prevented; treatment enrollment rates; naloxone kits distributed; syringe service program coverage.

### Solution 5: Restructure Fentanyl Response Around Precursor Diplomacy and Demand Reduction

**Description**: Shift the U.S. fentanyl response from interdiction-focused (seizing drugs at the border) to precursor-focused (controlling chemical supply chains) and demand-focused (expanding treatment at home and abroad). Establish a multilateral precursor chemical monitoring system modeled on the Chemical Weapons Convention's verification regime, with dedicated inspectors, declared facilities, and challenge inspections.

**Evidence Base**: CBP seized 27,023 kg of fentanyl in FY2023 -- representing an unknown but likely small fraction of total flow. The DEA estimates that one kilogram of fentanyl can produce approximately 500,000 lethal doses, meaning interdiction alone cannot prevent mass casualties. The November 2023 Biden-Xi agreement on fentanyl precursors produced limited enforcement results through 2024. A comprehensive precursor control regime, combined with demand reduction (expanded MAT and harm reduction), offers a more viable path.

**Implementation**:

- Negotiate a multilateral Fentanyl Precursor Control Agreement with China, India, and other precursor-producing nations
- Establish a dedicated inspection and monitoring body with technical staff capable of identifying novel precursors
- Create an early warning system for novel precursors, using AI-powered chemical intelligence to identify emerging synthesis routes before they are widely adopted
- Deploy advanced detection technologies (mass spectrometry, Raman spectroscopy) at key transit points
- Simultaneously expand domestic treatment capacity and international harm reduction
- Create a multilateral fund for precursor-producing countries to build chemical industry oversight capacity

**Cost/Resources**: $200-300 million annually for the multilateral precursor regime; additional demand-reduction costs addressed in domestic policy. Total new international spending of approximately $300 million/year.

**Timeline**: Diplomatic negotiations in years 1-3; treaty ratification in years 2-4; inspection regime operational in years 3-5; measurable precursor supply reduction in years 4-7.

**Challenges**: China's reluctance to enforce precursor controls; India's large chemical industry; detection of novel precursors; diplomatic complexity of a new multilateral regime.

**Lessons from the Chemical Weapons Convention**: The proposed multilateral precursor regime draws on the Chemical Weapons Convention (CWC) model, which has successfully monitored chemical production globally since 1997:

- The CWC's Organisation for the Prohibition of Chemical Weapons (OPCW) conducts approximately 250 inspections annually across 98 countries
- The CWC uses a tiered scheduling system (Schedules 1, 2, 3) that could be adapted for fentanyl precursors, with the most dangerous chemicals subject to the most rigorous controls
- The CWC's "challenge inspection" mechanism -- which allows states to request inspections of suspected violations -- provides a model for addressing suspected illicit precursor production
- Key differences: fentanyl precursors are dual-use chemicals with legitimate industrial applications, requiring a more nuanced approach than the CWC's near-total prohibition on chemical weapons
- The OPCW's experience demonstrates that international chemical monitoring is technically feasible and politically sustainable when nations perceive a shared security interest

**Impact Metrics**: Volume of scheduled precursors seized; number of clandestine labs identified; fentanyl purity/availability trends; overdose death trends.

### Solution 6: End the Drug Certification Process and Replace with Cooperative Frameworks

**Description**: Repeal the U.S. drug certification process -- the annual Presidential Determination that judges whether major drug-producing countries are cooperating sufficiently with U.S. anti-drug efforts -- and replace it with bilateral cooperation agreements that establish shared metrics, mutual accountability, and joint evaluation. The certification process is widely viewed as a paternalistic, unilateral tool that generates resentment and undermines genuine cooperation.

**Evidence Base**: The Western Hemisphere Drug Policy Commission (2020) recommended moving away from unilateral certification toward cooperative frameworks. Latin American governments and civil society organizations have consistently criticized the process as hypocritical -- the world's largest drug-consuming nation judging producer countries. The process has not demonstrably improved cooperation; nations deemed "fully cooperative" (like Mexico under the Merida Initiative) have often seen drug production and violence increase. No nation has been decertified without a waiver since 2008, rendering the process largely symbolic -- a diplomatic irritant that produces no tangible enforcement benefit.

**Implementation**: Congressional repeal of certification requirements in the Foreign Assistance Act; negotiation of bilateral cooperation agreements with major partner countries; establishment of joint monitoring and evaluation mechanisms with shared metrics. Model agreements should include:

- **Shared metrics**: Both nations agree on measurable outcomes (drug-related deaths, violence rates, treatment access)
- **Mutual accountability**: The U.S. reports on its own demand-reduction progress alongside partner-country supply data
- **Joint evaluation**: Independent evaluators assess both nations' contributions, replacing unilateral judgment
- **Sunset clauses**: Agreements reviewed and renewed every 5 years based on evidence of effectiveness

**Cost/Resources**: Minimal direct cost; savings from reduced bureaucratic overhead. Diplomatic investment in negotiating new agreements.

**Timeline**: Congressional action in years 1-2; bilateral agreements negotiated in years 2-4; new cooperative framework operational by year 5.

**Challenges**: Congressional reluctance to relinquish a perceived leverage tool; concern about losing pressure on non-cooperative nations; need for credible alternative accountability mechanisms.

**Impact Metrics**: Number of bilateral cooperation agreements signed; partner-country satisfaction surveys; joint evaluation outcomes; drug-related violence and health metrics in partner countries.

### Solution 7: Support Cannabis Treaty Reform and International Trade

**Description**: As cannabis legalization expands globally, lead the development of an international regulatory framework for legal cannabis that enables safe international trade while maintaining public health protections. The current situation -- in which five nations have legalized cannabis in apparent violation of the 1961 Convention -- is unsustainable and undermines the broader treaty framework.

**Evidence Base**: The global legal cannabis market was valued at approximately $43 billion in 2023 and is growing 15-20% annually (Grand View Research, 2024). Canada's legal market has captured approximately 70% of domestic sales. The absence of a legal international trade framework means that cannabis-legal nations cannot export or import, limiting market development and forcing continued reliance on domestic production that may not be the most efficient or sustainable.

**Implementation**:

- Support a CND resolution acknowledging national discretion in cannabis policy
- Negotiate an inter se agreement among cannabis-legalizing nations establishing minimum regulatory standards
- Develop model regulations for international cannabis trade (potency limits, testing requirements, packaging standards, age verification)
- Advocate for the formal amendment of the 1961 Convention to create an opt-out mechanism for cannabis, similar to the alcohol and tobacco carve-outs

**Cost/Resources**: Minimal direct cost; primarily diplomatic effort. Potential for significant economic benefits through international trade.

**Timeline**: CND resolution in years 1-2; inter se agreement in years 2-4; formal treaty amendment advocacy in years 5-10.

**Challenges**: Prohibitionist opposition at CND; U.S. federal cannabis prohibition complicates international leadership; risk of corporate capture of regulatory framework.

**Impact Metrics**: Number of nations joining the inter se agreement; volume of legal international cannabis trade; black market share in participating countries; public health outcomes in participating countries.

## International Models

### Portugal: Decriminalization with Health Investment

**Description**: Portugal decriminalized personal possession of all drugs in 2001 (Law 30/2000), replacing criminal penalties with administrative Dissuasion Commissions. Simultaneously invested heavily in treatment infrastructure, including opioid substitution therapy for approximately 40,000 people.

**Results**: Drug-related deaths fell from 80 (2001) to 16 (2012); HIV infections among people who inject drugs fell from 1,016 (2001) to 18 (2017); drug-related incarceration fell from 44% to ~24% of prison population. Drug use did not significantly increase (EMCDDA, 2023). Cost savings estimated at EUR 18 million annually in criminal justice costs.

**Key Design Features**:

- **Dissuasion Commissions**: Three-person panels (lawyer, social worker, psychologist) evaluate each case individually, offering treatment referrals, fines for refusal, or no sanction depending on circumstances
- **Threshold system**: Personal possession defined as a 10-day supply for each substance, providing clear guidance to police
- **Treatment investment**: Simultaneous expansion of opioid substitution therapy (serving approximately 40,000 people), therapeutic communities, and outpatient services
- **No criminal record**: Drug possession cases do not result in criminal records, preserving employment and housing access

**Applicability to U.S. International Policy**: Portugal demonstrates that decriminalization, when paired with health investment, produces measurable improvements. The U.S. should support partner nations in adopting similar approaches rather than pressuring them to maintain criminal penalties. USAID and INL should fund technical assistance to help countries design and implement Dissuasion Commission models. The Portuguese government has offered technical cooperation to interested nations, and the U.S. should facilitate these exchanges.

### Switzerland: Heroin-Assisted Treatment

**Description**: Beginning in 1994, Switzerland introduced heroin-assisted treatment (HAT) for treatment-resistant opioid users, providing pharmaceutical-grade heroin in supervised clinical settings.

**Results**: Crime among HAT participants fell 60%; new heroin initiations declined 82% over 10 years; employment among participants increased from 14% to 32%; HIV transmission declined sharply. The program costs approximately CHF 15,000 ($17,000) per patient per year, compared to CHF 75,000 ($85,000) for incarceration (Swiss Federal Office of Public Health, 2023). The program now operates in 23 clinics serving approximately 1,800 patients, and has been replicated in six additional countries.

**Political Context**: Switzerland adopted HAT after a national referendum in which 68% of voters supported the program (2008), demonstrating that evidence-based harm reduction can win democratic support even in a conservative society. The program survived two subsequent referendum challenges, establishing a durable political consensus. The Swiss experience shows that pilot programs, when rigorously evaluated and transparently reported, can build public support over time -- a lesson directly applicable to the proposed international harm reduction initiative.

**International Replication**: HAT programs modeled on the Swiss experience now operate in Germany, the Netherlands, Denmark, Canada, Spain, and the United Kingdom. Each adaptation has confirmed the core findings: reduced crime, improved health, increased treatment retention, and cost savings compared to incarceration.

**Applicability to U.S. International Policy**: The Swiss model demonstrates that providing alternatives to illicit markets reduces both crime and public health harms. The U.S. should support HAT and other medication-assisted treatment programs in partner countries through the proposed Global Drug Harm Reduction Initiative.

### Thailand: Alternative Development Success

**Description**: Thailand's Royal Project (1969-present) transitioned highland communities from opium cultivation to legal crops including coffee, strawberries, and cut flowers, with sustained investment in roads, irrigation, education, and market access.

**Results**: Opium cultivation fell from 17,920 hectares (1965) to under 300 hectares (2002). Former opium-growing communities now earn higher incomes from legal agriculture. Thailand is now a net exporter of highland agricultural products. The total investment of approximately $2.5 billion over 35 years (Thai government and international donors) produced a complete economic transformation of highland communities.

**Critical Success Factors**:

- **Long time horizon**: The Royal Project operated for 30+ years with sustained royal patronage and government commitment
- **Infrastructure investment**: Roads connecting highland communities to markets were as important as crop substitution
- **Research and development**: The Royal Project invested in identifying high-value crops suited to local climate and soil conditions
- **Market linkages**: Partnerships with processors, exporters, and retail chains ensured farmers had reliable buyers for legal crops
- **Community participation**: Highland communities were involved in program design and management from the outset

**Applicability to U.S. International Policy**: Thailand's success demonstrates that alternative development works when it is adequately funded, sustained over decades, and addresses market access and infrastructure -- not just crop substitution. The U.S. should model its alternative development programs in Colombia, Peru, and Bolivia on Thai principles.

### Canada: National Cannabis Regulation

**Description**: Canada's Cannabis Act (2018) legalized recreational cannabis with federal regulation and provincial implementation, including age restrictions, potency limits, packaging requirements, and public health campaigns.

**Results**: Legal market captured approximately 70% of sales by 2023; youth use remained stable; impaired driving did not significantly increase; cannabis-related criminal charges dropped 74% (Statistics Canada, 2023). Tax revenue from legal cannabis reached C$1.2 billion in fiscal year 2022-23.

**Key Design Features**:

- **Federal-provincial division**: Federal government sets minimum standards (age, potency, packaging); provinces manage retail licensing, distribution, and additional restrictions
- **Public health emphasis**: Plain packaging with health warnings; strict advertising limits; THC potency labeling requirements
- **Equity provisions**: Cannabis record suspension program to address prior convictions
- **Research framework**: Cannabis tracking system and public health surveillance to monitor impacts

**Challenges and Lessons**:

- **Illicit market persistence**: Despite capturing 70% of sales, the legal market has not fully displaced the black market, partly due to high taxation and limited retail access in some provinces
- **Industry consolidation**: Corporate consolidation of the legal cannabis industry has raised concerns about creating a "Big Cannabis" that prioritizes profit over public health
- **International friction**: Canada's legalization generated diplomatic tensions with countries that maintain strict prohibition, though no formal sanctions resulted

**Applicability to U.S. International Policy**: Canada's experience demonstrates that national cannabis regulation is viable in a G7 economy without the catastrophic consequences predicted by prohibition advocates. The U.S. should support treaty flexibility that enables similar regulatory approaches globally. Canada's regulatory framework also provides a template for international regulatory standards that could facilitate legal cannabis trade.

### Bolivia: Community-Based Coca Regulation

**Description**: Bolivia's "cato" system allows each registered farmer to cultivate 1,600 square meters of coca for traditional use and industrialization (coca tea, flour, medicines), with community-based enforcement ("social control") rather than forced eradication.

**Results**: Coca cultivation has remained relatively stable, community violence associated with eradication has been virtually eliminated, and traditional coca use has been preserved as a cultural practice. Bolivia's approach has maintained diplomatic friction with the United States but generated significant regional support.

**Applicability to U.S. International Policy**: Bolivia demonstrates that community-based regulation can manage coca cultivation without the violence and human rights abuses associated with forced eradication. The U.S. should study and potentially support community regulation models rather than insisting on eradication.

## Pilot Programs and Demonstrations

- **Colombia Total Peace Program (2022-present)**: President Petro's government is piloting a "total peace" approach that treats coca communities as development partners rather than enforcement targets, offering land titles, crop substitution support, and infrastructure investment in exchange for voluntary transition away from coca. Early results are mixed but the approach merits monitoring and potential U.S. support. Key elements include land titling for approximately 3 million hectares of peasant landholdings and integration of former FARC territories into legal economic networks.
- **USAID Development-First Model (Peru, 2019-present)**: USAID-Peru is testing an approach that sequences development assistance before eradication, providing market access and infrastructure to coca communities that voluntarily reduce cultivation. Initial evaluations show higher farmer participation rates than eradication-first models. The program focuses on coffee and cacao cooperatives in the VRAEM (Valley of the Apurimac, Ene and Mantaro Rivers) region, providing processing equipment, quality certification, and export linkages.
- **Bolivia Coca Regulation Model (2004-present)**: Bolivia allows legal coca cultivation within a defined area (the "cato" system of 1,600 square meters per farmer) for traditional use and industrialization (coca tea, flour, medicines). This model regulates rather than prohibits cultivation, with community-based enforcement. Community unions monitor compliance and can sanction excess cultivation, achieving compliance rates estimated at 70-80% without state violence. Bolivia has also developed legal coca-based products including beverages, flour, and cosmetics, creating a licit coca economy valued at approximately $400 million annually.
- **Netherlands Legal Supply Pilot (2024-present)**: Ten Dutch municipalities are piloting a legal cannabis supply chain to resolve the "back door problem" (legal retail sales but illegal wholesale supply). If successful, this could provide a model for addressing supply-chain regulation in other countries. The experiment includes licensed growers supplying participating coffeeshops under a closed regulatory chain, with quality testing and seed-to-sale tracking.
- **New Zealand Drug Checking Framework (2020-present)**: New Zealand has created a legal framework for community drug checking services, enabling organizations to test substances at festivals and community settings without criminal liability. The information gathered feeds into public health surveillance systems. In the 2023-24 festival season, over 12,000 drug samples were checked, identifying dangerous adulterants in approximately 15% of samples and potentially preventing multiple overdose events.
- **Thailand Cannabis Regulation Experiment (2022-present)**: Thailand delisted cannabis from its narcotics schedule in June 2022, becoming the first Asian country to effectively decriminalize cannabis. The rapid change -- without a comprehensive regulatory framework in place -- created a largely unregulated market. The experience provides valuable lessons about the risks of moving faster on legalization than on regulation, highlighting the importance of sequenced implementation.
- **Iceland Prevention Model (adapted internationally)**: Iceland's "Planet Youth" prevention program reduced teen substance use by over 60% through community-based interventions (increased supervised activities, parental engagement, curfew enforcement) rather than criminal justice approaches. The model has been adopted in 35 countries and demonstrates that prevention investment can dramatically reduce demand without criminalization.

## Sequencing and Prioritization

### Quick Wins (0-2 years)

- Issue executive branch guidance rebalancing ONDCP international strategy toward health outcomes
- Announce diplomatic support for flexible treaty interpretation at the CND
- Begin bilateral negotiations to replace the certification process with cooperative agreements
- Launch a pilot international harm reduction program in 3-5 high-burden countries
- Commission a comprehensive independent evaluation of all international drug programs

### Medium-term Reforms (2-5 years)

- Achieve Congressional reauthorization of INL and DOD counter-narcotics programs with new allocation mandates
- Establish the Global Drug Harm Reduction Initiative with Congressional authorization and $500 million annual funding
- Triple USAID alternative development funding to $1 billion annually
- Negotiate a multilateral fentanyl precursor control agreement
- Co-sponsor a CND resolution on cannabis policy discretion

### Long-term Transformation (5-10 years)

- Achieve a formal CND resolution affirming national discretion in drug policy
- Complete the transition to 60/40 demand/supply spending balance in international programs
- Establish a track record of alternative development success that enables broader eradication phase-down
- Support inter se treaty modification among willing nations to formalize reform approaches
- Develop an international regulatory framework for legal cannabis trade
- Operationalize the multilateral fentanyl precursor control agreement with inspection capacity
- Publish a comprehensive 10-year evaluation demonstrating reform outcomes versus baseline
- Build institutional capacity in partner countries to sustain health-based programs independently of U.S. assistance

## Complementary Policies

- **Domestic Drug Policy Reform**: International reform is difficult to advance while U.S. domestic policy remains anchored in prohibition. Federal cannabis legalization, expanded treatment access, and harm reduction funding domestically would strengthen the credibility of reform advocacy abroad. The U.S. cannot persuade partner nations to adopt evidence-based approaches while maintaining a federal prohibition that criminalizes cannabis use at home. See [Cannabis Legalization](../cannabis-legalization/07-solutions.md) and [Harm Reduction](../harm-reduction/07-solutions.md).
- **Anti-Money Laundering**: Strengthening financial intelligence and anti-money laundering enforcement targeting drug trafficking organizations' assets is essential to maintain enforcement credibility while shifting away from supply-side agricultural programs. The Financial Action Task Force (FATF) framework provides existing mechanisms for targeting drug money laundering that should be strengthened as enforcement resources are rebalanced. See [Drug Trafficking](../drug-trafficking/07-solutions.md).
- **Immigration Policy**: Drug violence is a primary driver of migration from Central America and Mexico. Effective international drug policy reform would reduce push factors for migration. A comprehensive approach to Central American migration must address drug violence as a root cause rather than treating migration as a separate problem. See [analysis/immigration/](../../immigration/01-overview.md).
- **Anti-Corruption**: Drug trafficking fuels corruption in producer and transit countries, undermining governance and rule of law. Reform should include strengthened anti-corruption programs in partner countries, including judicial independence, transparency, and civil society support. The United States should expand programs modeled on CICIG (the UN-backed anti-corruption commission in Guatemala) that build independent investigative capacity in partner countries.
- **Climate Adaptation**: As climate change shifts the geographic viability of drug crop cultivation, alternative development programs should be designed to promote climate-resilient agricultural practices that provide both economic and environmental benefits. UNODC research (2023) projects that changing precipitation patterns could shift coca cultivation into new areas, including currently protected forests, making proactive alternative development even more critical.
- **Trade Policy**: U.S. trade agreements can include provisions that support alternative development by reducing tariffs on agricultural products from drug-producing regions and establishing market access guarantees for crops grown by former drug crop farmers. The Andean Trade Preference Act, which provided tariff preferences for Andean nations as an alternative development incentive, provides a partial model but was allowed to lapse in 2013 and should be renewed and expanded.
- **Scientific Research Cooperation**: International drug policy reform should include expanded scientific cooperation on drug research, including clinical trials of cannabis-based and psychedelic-based medicines. The current treaty framework impedes international research collaboration by imposing onerous licensing requirements. Streamlined research authorization would accelerate the evidence base for medical applications while demonstrating the benefits of treaty flexibility.
