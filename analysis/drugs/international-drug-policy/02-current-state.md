# International Drug Policy: Current State

## The UN Drug Control Framework

The international drug control system rests on three treaties that together create a global prohibition framework, administered by specialized UN bodies.

### The Three Conventions

| Convention | Year | Parties | Core Requirement |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs** | 1961 (amended 1972) | 186 | Limits production and trade of plant-based drugs to medical and scientific purposes; mandates scheduling system |
| **Convention on Psychotropic Substances** | 1971 | 184 | Extends controls to synthetic and psychotropic substances; establishes four-schedule classification |
| **Convention against Illicit Traffic** | 1988 | 191 | Requires criminalization of drug trafficking; mandates precursor chemical controls; enables asset seizure and extradition |

The 1961 Single Convention is the cornerstone, establishing the scheduling system for narcotic drugs and requiring parties to limit production, manufacture, and trade exclusively to medical and scientific purposes. The 1988 Convention significantly expanded the enforcement framework by requiring criminal penalties for drug trafficking and possession for trafficking purposes. Article 3(2) of the 1988 Convention also calls on parties to criminalize personal possession, though it includes language ("subject to its constitutional principles and the basic concepts of its legal system") that reformers cite as permitting decriminalization.

### Treaty Tensions and Interpretation Debates

The conventions are under unprecedented strain. As of 2024, the following state practices directly conflict with strict treaty interpretation:

- **Cannabis legalization**: Uruguay (2013), Canada (2018), Germany (2024), Malta (2021), and Luxembourg (2023) have legalized recreational cannabis, while 24 U.S. states have done so under federal prohibition. These policies are inconsistent with the 1961 Single Convention's requirement to limit cannabis to medical and scientific purposes.
- **Coca leaf chewing**: Bolivia withdrew from the 1961 Convention in 2012 and re-acceded with a reservation protecting traditional coca use -- the only country to have navigated this process. Colombia's Constitutional Court has also protected traditional coca use as a cultural right.
- **Harm reduction services**: Supervised consumption sites operating in 14 countries and heroin-assisted treatment programs in Switzerland, the Netherlands, Germany, Canada, and the UK push against the 1988 Convention's criminalization requirements. The INCB has criticized these programs but taken no enforcement action.

Legal scholars including Bewley-Taylor (2012) and Walsh (2014) have identified multiple pathways for treaty flexibility: the "constitutional principles" language in Article 3(2), the general welfare clauses present in all three conventions, the Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties' provisions for interpretation in light of subsequent practice, and the possibility of inter se modification (agreements among subsets of treaty parties).

### UN Institutional Architecture

| Body | Role | Membership | Budget (2023) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **UN Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC)** | Research, technical assistance, norm-setting | Universal | ~$410 million |
| **International Narcotics Control Board (INCB)** | Treaty compliance monitoring, precursor control | 13 elected members | ~$7 million (via UNODC) |
| **Commission on Narcotic Drugs (CND)** | Policy-making body; schedules substances | 53 member states | Part of UNODC budget |
| **World Health Organization (WHO)** | Scientific assessment for scheduling recommendations | Universal | Expert Committee funded separately |

The INCB has historically been the most conservative institutional actor, publicly criticizing Portugal's decriminalization, supervised injection facilities, and cannabis legalization. However, the INCB's 2023 Annual Report adopted a notably less confrontational tone, acknowledging the need for "evidence-based" approaches and declining to formally condemn cannabis-legalizing nations. The CND, which includes representatives from major drug-producing and drug-consuming nations, has become a battleground between prohibitionist and reformist blocs.

The CND's 53 member states meet annually in Vienna. Voting patterns at the 2023 and 2024 sessions revealed a growing reformist bloc: the European Union (minus Hungary), Canada, Colombia, Mexico, Uruguay, and several other Latin American and Caribbean nations generally support language emphasizing health approaches and national discretion. The prohibitionist bloc -- led by Russia, China, Japan, Singapore, and most Middle Eastern states -- defends strict treaty compliance and criminal enforcement. The United States has historically occupied an awkward middle position, supporting some harm reduction language while defending the treaty framework it designed.

### DEA International Presence

The Drug Enforcement Administration maintains the most extensive overseas law enforcement presence of any U.S. agency:

- **93 offices** in **69 countries** (DEA, 2024)
- **Approximately 1,000 agents** stationed overseas
- **Key functions**: Intelligence gathering, joint investigations with host-country counterparts, capacity building, extradition coordination
- **High-profile operations**: DEA-coordinated extraditions include Joaquin "El Chapo" Guzman (Mexico, 2017), Dairo Antonio Usuga "Otoniel" (Colombia, 2022), and hundreds of lower-profile cases annually
- **Controversies**: DEA operations have been linked to "fast and furious"-style weapons trafficking (ATF, 2011), corruption within the agency (Jose Irizarry case, 2020), and allegations of entrapment in international cases

Despite two decades and $12+ billion of U.S. investment in Colombia, coca cultivation in 2022 reached an all-time high of 230,000 hectares, far exceeding the 136,200 hectares recorded when Plan Colombia began in 2000. Colombia alone accounts for approximately 65% of global coca cultivation. Cocaine production has also become more efficient: improved processing techniques mean that each hectare of coca now produces more cocaine than in previous decades, with yields estimated at 7.6 kg of cocaine per hectare in 2022 compared to 4.7 kg in 2005 (UNODC, 2023).

The cocaine market has also diversified geographically. Europe has overtaken the United States as the largest cocaine market by volume, with record seizures at European ports -- particularly Antwerp (110 metric tons seized in 2022), Rotterdam (52 metric tons), and Hamburg (35 metric tons). West African nations serve as major transit points, with cocaine moving through Guinea-Bissau, Nigeria, and Ghana en route to European markets.

The Taliban's April 2022 ban on opium poppy cultivation produced a dramatic 95% reduction in Afghan production in 2023, the most rapid supply disruption in the history of international drug control. However, this has not eliminated heroin from global markets; Myanmar has expanded production, and stockpiled Afghan opium continues to supply trafficking networks. The ban's sustainability remains uncertain given Afghanistan's economic crisis.

The Afghan opium collapse has had significant secondary effects. Farm-gate prices for opium in Afghanistan increased from $69/kg in 2022 to $350/kg in 2023, making remaining production enormously profitable. Myanmar's Shan State has expanded poppy cultivation in the context of political instability following the 2021 military coup, with armed ethnic groups controlling much of the production. Mexico's poppy cultivation has been declining since 2018, driven less by enforcement than by the shift of Mexican cartels toward synthetic fentanyl production, which is more profitable and less dependent on agricultural conditions.

Synthetic drugs represent a fundamental challenge to the traditional supply-side model. Fentanyl requires no agricultural inputs and can be manufactured from precursor chemicals in small clandestine laboratories anywhere in the world. Chinese chemical companies remain the primary source of fentanyl precursors, though production has shifted to Mexico, where cartels -- primarily the Sinaloa and Jalisco New Generation cartels -- manufacture the finished product.

The synthetic drug landscape extends well beyond fentanyl. Methamphetamine production has become a global phenomenon, with industrial-scale labs operating in Mexico, Myanmar, and Afghanistan (where the Taliban has not banned methamphetamine production). Captagon -- an amphetamine-type stimulant -- has become a multi-billion-dollar industry centered in Syria and Lebanon, flooding Middle Eastern markets. Novel synthetic opioids called nitazenes, which are even more potent than fentanyl, have been detected with increasing frequency in European and North American drug supplies since 2022.

### The Portugal Model in Detail

Portugal's 2001 decriminalization remains the most studied and cited alternative to prohibition. Key elements include:

- **Legal Framework**: Law 30/2000 eliminated criminal penalties for possession of up to a 10-day personal supply of any drug; possession remains an administrative offense
- **Threshold Quantities**: Specific limits per substance -- 25g of cannabis, 2g of cocaine, 1g of heroin, 1g of MDMA, 0.5g of amphetamine -- above which criminal prosecution applies
- **Dissuasion Commissions**: Three-person panels (typically a lawyer, social worker, and physician) assess individuals found with drugs and recommend treatment, education, or community service. Approximately 25,000-30,000 cases heard annually (SICAD, 2023).
- **Investment in Treatment**: Portugal simultaneously expanded treatment capacity, including opioid substitution therapy, to approximately 40,000 slots. Treatment coverage for opioid use disorder exceeds 60%, among the highest in Europe.
- **Outcomes (2001-2023)**: Drug-related deaths fell from 80 in 2001 to a low of 16 in 2012, though they have risen to approximately 74 in 2022 amid Europe-wide trends; HIV infections among people who inject drugs fell from 1,016 in 2001 to 18 in 2017; drug-related incarceration fell from 44% of prison population to approximately 24%
- **Cost savings**: Portugal estimates savings of approximately EUR 18 million annually in criminal justice costs, offset by increased treatment spending of approximately EUR 75 million annually -- a net investment that produces significant returns in reduced healthcare utilization, reduced incarceration, and improved productivity (EMCDDA, 2023)
- **Limitations**: Drug-related deaths have risen since 2012, partly due to an aging cohort of long-term users and the emergence of synthetic drugs in European markets. Critics note that Portugal's success may be difficult to replicate in countries without universal healthcare. Treatment waiting lists have grown in recent years as demand has increased and austerity-era budget cuts reduced capacity.

### The Swiss Heroin-Assisted Treatment Model

Switzerland's heroin-assisted treatment (HAT) program, launched as a trial in 1994 and made permanent in 1999, is among the most rigorously evaluated drug policy interventions in the world:

- **Eligibility**: Treatment-resistant individuals who have failed at least two other treatment modalities (typically methadone and/or therapeutic communities)
- **Operations**: 23 treatment centers across Switzerland dispense pharmaceutical-grade diacetylmorphine (heroin) under clinical supervision, typically two to three times daily
- **Participants**: Approximately 1,800 patients as of 2023 (Swiss Federal Office of Public Health)
- **Outcomes**: Crime among participants fell 60%; property offenses fell 80%; employment rose from 14% to 32%; new heroin initiations in Switzerland declined 82% from 1990 to 2005; retention in treatment exceeds 70% at 12 months
- **Cost-effectiveness**: CHF 15,000 ($17,000) per patient per year, compared to CHF 75,000 ($85,000) for incarceration. Net societal savings estimated at CHF 45,000 per patient per year when reduced crime, healthcare, and welfare costs are included (Swiss Federal Office of Public Health, 2023).
- **Replication**: Following Switzerland's success, heroin-assisted treatment programs have been established in the Netherlands (since 1998), Germany (since 2002), Canada (since 2005, SALOME trial and NAOMI trial), the United Kingdom (since 2019, expanded), Denmark (since 2010), and Belgium (since 2011).

### The Netherlands Coffeeshop Model

The Dutch tolerance policy (gedoogbeleid), in effect since 1976, represents a pragmatic middle ground:

- **Structure**: Cannabis sales are tolerated in approximately 565 licensed coffeeshops (down from 1,500 in 1995) under strict conditions: no advertising, no sales to minors, no hard drugs, maximum 5g per transaction, and no sales to non-residents in border provinces
- **The "Back Door Problem"**: While retail sales are tolerated, wholesale supply remains illegal, creating a paradox in which coffeeshops sell legally but must source from illegal growers. A 2024 pilot program in 10 municipalities is testing legal supply chains to resolve this contradiction.
- **Outcomes**: Dutch cannabis use rates are comparable to the European average and lower than many countries with stricter policies; Dutch hard drug use rates are below the EU average; the separation of cannabis and hard drug markets is considered a key success (EMCDDA, 2023).
- **Limitations**: The tolerance policy has created corruption associated with the illegal wholesale supply chain. The government has been gradually tightening regulations, and the coffeeshop count has been reduced by half since the 1990s.

### The Certification Process

The annual Presidential Determination process, established in 1986 and modified in 2002, requires the President to:

1. **Identify** major drug-producing and drug-transit countries (currently 22 nations listed)
2. **Certify** that each identified country has cooperated fully with U.S. anti-drug efforts or provide a national security waiver
3. **Sanction** decertified countries through aid cuts and opposition to multilateral lending (rarely invoked in practice)

This process has been widely criticized as a unilateral tool of coercive diplomacy that alienates partner nations and prioritizes U.S. enforcement priorities over evidence-based policy.

The 22 nations on the 2024 Majors List include: Afghanistan, The Bahamas, Belize, Bolivia, Burma (Myanmar), Colombia, Costa Rica, Dominican Republic, Ecuador, El Salvador, Guatemala, Haiti, Honduras, India, Jamaica, Laos, Mexico, Nicaragua, Pakistan, Panama, Peru, and Venezuela. Bolivia and Venezuela have been decertified but granted national security waivers in recent years. No nation has been decertified without a waiver since 2008, rendering the process largely symbolic.

### The Extradition Framework

Extradition for drug offenses is a major tool of U.S. international drug enforcement and a significant source of diplomatic tension:

- **Bilateral extradition treaties**: The U.S. has extradition treaties with over 100 nations, most of which cover drug trafficking offenses
- **Colombia**: Has extradited over 2,000 individuals to the U.S. since the 1997 reinstatement of extradition, making it by far the largest source of drug-related extraditions. Extradition is considered such a powerful deterrent that Colombian traffickers have historically used it as a bargaining chip in negotiations with the government.
- **Mexico**: The U.S.-Mexico extradition relationship is among the most contentious bilateral drug policy issues. Mexico extradited 87 individuals to the U.S. in 2023, down from a peak of 135 in 2016 (DOJ, 2024). The extradition of Joaquin "El Chapo" Guzman in 2017 was a high-water mark; the AMLO administration's reluctance to extradite cartel leaders created significant friction.
- **Controversies**: Extradition has been criticized for removing targets from their home country's justice system, violating sovereignty norms, and enabling plea bargains that result in lighter sentences than domestic prosecution might produce

## Geographic Variation

### Latin America and the Caribbean

Latin America bears the heaviest costs of the international drug war. Mexico has experienced over 400,000 drug-related killings since President Calderon deployed the military against cartels in 2006. Colombia, despite $12+ billion in U.S. assistance, recorded record coca cultivation in 2022. The Northern Triangle countries (Guatemala, Honduras, El Salvador) have some of the world's highest homicide rates, driven in part by drug trafficking routes. Former presidents of Mexico, Colombia, Brazil, and Guatemala have formed the Latin American Commission on Drugs and Democracy, calling the War on Drugs a failure and advocating for decriminalization.

Country-specific conditions vary significantly:

- **Colombia**: President Gustavo Petro (elected 2022) has explicitly called for ending the War on Drugs and pursuing a "total peace" approach. His government has suspended aerial fumigation of coca, prioritized voluntary substitution, and engaged coca-growing communities as development partners. Coca cultivation remains at record levels, and Colombia continues to face U.S. pressure to maintain eradication targets.
- **Mexico**: Under President Claudia Sheinbaum (inaugurated 2024), Mexico continues the AMLO-era approach of "hugs not bullets" (abrazos no balazos), resisting U.S. pressure for aggressive military counter-narcotics operations. The Sinaloa and Jalisco New Generation cartels dominate fentanyl and methamphetamine production. Drug violence remains above 30,000 homicides annually.
- **Peru**: The world's second-largest coca producer, Peru has pursued a combination of eradication and alternative development. The Peruvian CORAH (Special Project for the Control and Reduction of Illicit Crops) eradicates approximately 25,000-30,000 hectares annually, but total cultivation continues to rise (UNODC Peru, 2023).
- **Bolivia**: The only country to have formally protected traditional coca use under international law. Bolivia's "cato" system allows each registered farmer to cultivate 1,600 square meters of coca for traditional use and industrialization. Community-based enforcement ("social control") has kept cultivation relatively stable.

### Europe

The European Union represents the most advanced regional laboratory for drug policy reform. Portugal's decriminalization model has been influential. The Netherlands has maintained its tolerance policy for decades. Germany legalized cannabis in 2024. The European Monitoring Centre for Drugs and Drug Addiction (EMCDDA, now EUDA) serves as a data-driven hub for evidence-based policy. However, European nations remain divided, with Sweden and Hungary maintaining strict prohibitionist approaches.

Key European developments:

- **Germany's Cannabis Act (2024)**: Permits adults to possess up to 25g, grow up to three plants, and join cannabis social clubs (up to 500 members). The law does not create a commercial retail market -- a deliberate choice to stay closer to treaty compliance. A pilot program for licensed commercial sales is planned for select cities.
- **Luxembourg**: Legalized home growing of up to four plants per household in 2023, becoming the first EU country to do so. Commercial sales remain prohibited.
- **European cocaine crisis**: Europe has become the world's fastest-growing cocaine market, with record seizures at major ports. The EU Drug Markets Report (2024) describes "unprecedented" cocaine availability and purity levels in European cities.

### Asia

Asian drug policy remains predominantly punitive. Several nations -- including China, Singapore, Indonesia, and Iran -- impose the death penalty for drug offenses. The Philippines under President Duterte (2016-2022) conducted extrajudicial killings of thousands of suspected drug users and dealers. Japan maintains near-zero tolerance. However, Thailand legalized cannabis in 2022 (though is reconsidering restrictions), and India permits traditional cannabis preparations (bhang) in some states.

Notable developments:

- **Thailand**: Removed cannabis from the narcotics list in June 2022, creating an unregulated market that saw rapid commercialization (estimated 6,000+ cannabis shops opened within two years). The government is now drafting a Cannabis Act to impose commercial regulations and potentially re-restrict recreational use.
- **Philippines**: The International Criminal Court authorized an investigation into Duterte's drug war in 2021, which is estimated to have killed 12,000-30,000 people (Human Rights Watch, 2022). President Marcos Jr. has softened rhetoric but not fundamentally changed policy.
- **Captagon trade**: Syria-based captagon production has become a multi-billion-dollar regional industry, with the Assad regime and affiliated militias controlling much of the production. Saudi Arabia seized 119 million captagon pills in 2022 alone.

### Africa

African drug policy is shaped by the continent's growing role as both a transit zone (for cocaine from South America to Europe) and a consumer market. West African nations, particularly Nigeria and Ghana, have experienced significant increases in drug trafficking. The African Union's Common Position on Drugs (2012) emphasized public health approaches but implementation has been limited.

Key developments:

- **West Africa**: Guinea-Bissau, described as a "narco-state" by the UNODC, serves as a major transit point for Colombian cocaine bound for Europe. An estimated 50-60 metric tons of cocaine transit West Africa annually (UNODC, 2024).
- **South Africa**: Has one of the highest methamphetamine use rates in Africa (concentrated in the Western Cape). A cannabis legalization ruling by the Constitutional Court (2018) decriminalized personal use and home cultivation.
- **Morocco**: The world's largest hashish producer (estimated 47,000 hectares of cannabis cultivation). In 2021, Morocco legalized medical and industrial cannabis, a significant shift that may presage broader reform.

## Recent Trends

- **Synthetic drug dominance**: The global drug market is shifting from plant-based to synthetic production. Fentanyl and its analogs have displaced heroin in much of the U.S. market, while methamphetamine production has expanded globally. This shift fundamentally undermines crop eradication strategies.
- **Cannabis legalization wave**: Five nations have legalized recreational cannabis at the national level (Uruguay, Canada, Germany, Malta, Luxembourg), and over 60 nations permit medical cannabis. The INCB has not formally sanctioned any legalizing nation.
- **Afghanistan opium collapse**: The Taliban's 2022 ban produced the most dramatic supply disruption in modern drug control history, reducing Afghan cultivation by 95%. However, global heroin markets have not collapsed, suggesting stockpiles and alternative sources (particularly Myanmar) are filling the gap.
- **U.S.-China fentanyl diplomacy**: The Biden administration secured commitments from China in November 2023 to crack down on fentanyl precursor chemical exports, but enforcement has been uneven and seizure data suggests limited impact through 2024.
- **Latin American reform momentum**: Colombia elected President Gustavo Petro in 2022, who has explicitly called for ending the War on Drugs and pivoting to a "total peace" approach. Mexico under President Claudia Sheinbaum continues to resist U.S. pressure for aggressive military counter-narcotics operations.
- **Europe's cocaine flood**: Record cocaine seizures at European ports and rising cocaine purity suggest that the European market is expanding rapidly, creating new pressures on European drug policy frameworks.
- **Nitazenes and novel opioids**: A new class of synthetic opioids (isotonitazene, metonitazene, protonitazene) even more potent than fentanyl is appearing in drug supplies in the UK, continental Europe, and North America. These substances are not yet scheduled under international conventions.

## Emerging Challenges

- **Precursor chemical proliferation**: As controls tighten on known fentanyl precursors, manufacturers shift to "pre-precursors" -- chemicals one or two steps earlier in the synthesis chain that are not scheduled under international law. China's 2024 scheduling of three fentanyl precursors was followed by the emergence of at least six new pre-precursor chemicals within months (DEA, 2024).
- **Dark web drug markets**: Online marketplaces using cryptocurrency enable direct-to-consumer drug sales that bypass traditional trafficking networks, complicating interdiction. The closure of major markets (Silk Road, AlphaBay, Hydra) has not reduced the number of operational dark web drug markets, which numbered approximately 30 in 2024 (EMCDDA, 2024).
- **Nitazenes and novel synthetic opioids**: A new class of synthetic opioids even more potent than fentanyl is emerging in European and North American markets, outpacing scheduling and detection capabilities
- **Treaty strain**: The growing number of nations pursuing legalization and decriminalization is creating unprecedented strain on the UN convention system, which was not designed to accommodate regulatory diversity
- **Climate change and drug cultivation**: Changing climate patterns are shifting the geographic viability of coca and opium cultivation, potentially opening new production regions and complicating eradication planning. UNODC has documented coca cultivation expanding to higher altitudes in the Andes and new opium poppy cultivation in previously unsuitable areas of Myanmar.
- **Cryptocurrency and drug finance**: The use of cryptocurrency for drug transactions -- both retail and wholesale -- is growing rapidly, complicating traditional anti-money laundering approaches. The Chainalysis 2024 report estimates that cryptocurrency-based drug transactions exceeded $30 billion globally in 2023.
