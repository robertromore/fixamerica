# International Drug Policy: What You Can Do

International drug policy may seem distant from daily life, but U.S. foreign drug programs are funded by American taxpayers, shaped by American voters, and have profound consequences for millions of people in producer and transit countries. Citizen engagement can shift the political calculus that keeps failed policies in place.

---

## Individual Actions

### Learn and Stay Informed

- Subscribe to the **International Drug Policy Consortium (IDPC) Alert** for monthly updates on global drug policy developments: <https://idpc.net/>
- Follow the **UNODC World Drug Report** releases (annual, typically June) for the most comprehensive global data: <https://www.unodc.org/wdr/>
- Read the **Washington Office on Latin America (WOLA)** drug policy blog for accessible analysis of U.S.-Latin America drug policy: <https://www.wola.org/program/drug-policy/>
- Follow the **Global Commission on Drug Policy** reports for high-level reform advocacy: <https://www.globalcommissionondrugs.org/>
- Read the State Department's annual **International Narcotics Control Strategy Report (INCSR)** to understand how the U.S. government assesses its own programs: <https://www.state.gov/international-narcotics-control-strategy-reports/>
- Follow **InSight Crime** for on-the-ground analysis of cartel dynamics and drug violence in the Americas: <https://insightcrime.org/>
- Read **Filter Magazine** for harm reduction-focused journalism and policy analysis: <https://filtermag.org/>

### Understand the Key Numbers

Effective advocacy requires knowing the basic facts. Memorize these:

| Fact | Number | Source |
|------|--------|--------|
| U.S. annual international drug control spending | $5.5 billion | ONDCP, 2024 |
| Share going to supply-side enforcement | ~80% | ONDCP, 2024 |

| Countries that have decriminalized | 30+ | Talking Drugs, 2024 |

### Make Personal Changes

- Educate yourself on how U.S. drug demand drives violence abroad -- an estimated 400,000+ people have been killed in Mexico's drug war since 2006, driven in significant part by U.S. demand for illicit drugs
- If you use substances, learn about harm reduction practices and share knowledge with your community. Drug checking services, naloxone access, and safer use practices save lives.
- Support fair-trade and alternative-development products from former drug-producing regions when available. Colombian specialty coffee, Peruvian cacao, and Bolivian quinoa from former coca-growing regions represent direct alternatives to the drug economy.
- Read at least one book on international drug policy (see [Resources](09-resources.md)) to build deeper understanding. Recommended starting points: Bewley-Taylor's *International Drug Control: Consensus Fractured* for policy analysis or Grillo's *El Narco* for narrative journalism.

### Use Your Voice

- **Contact Your Representatives**: Urge them to support rebalancing international drug spending from enforcement to health and development programs. Specify that you support the recommendations of the Western Hemisphere Drug Policy Commission (2020).
- **Submit Public Comments**: When the ONDCP publishes its National Drug Control Strategy for public comment, advocate for health-centered international programs. The strategy is typically published for comment in February-March each year.
- **Write Letters to the Editor**: When news stories cover drug violence in Latin America, drug seizures at the border, or fentanyl precursor diplomacy, write letters connecting these issues to the failure of supply-side enforcement and the need for reform. Tips for effective letters:
    - Keep it under 200 words
    - Reference the specific article and connect to international drug policy
    - Include one specific data point (e.g., "$12 billion spent on Plan Colombia, yet coca cultivation is at a record high")
    - Propose a specific alternative (e.g., "Redirect funding to evidence-based treatment and alternative development")
- **Share Information**: Share reports from the Global Commission on Drug Policy and WOLA on social media. Data-driven content about the failure of the War on Drugs consistently gains traction across political lines.
- **Write Op-Eds**: If you have relevant expertise (public health, international relations, Latin American studies, law enforcement), submit op-eds to local newspapers and online platforms. Drug policy reform is underrepresented in opinion pages.

## Community Actions

### Organize Locally

- Form a local study group on international drug policy using materials from the Drug Policy Alliance or Transform Drug Policy Foundation. Suggested curriculum:
    - Week 1: UNODC World Drug Report overview (free online)
    - Week 2: Portugal decriminalization case study (Greenwald, Cato Institute, 2009)
    - Week 3: Plan Colombia and Latin American drug war (WOLA reports)
    - Week 4: Fentanyl and synthetic drugs (RAND "Future of Fentanyl" report)
    - Week 5: Reform proposals and what citizens can do
- Host a screening of documentaries on the War on Drugs followed by discussion:
    - *Breaking the Taboo* (2011) -- narrated by Morgan Freeman, features Global Commission members
    - *The House I Live In* (2012) -- Eugene Jarecki's examination of the domestic and international drug war
    - *Kingdom of Silence* (2020) -- drug policy dimensions of U.S.-Mexico relations
    - *Killing Them Softly: The War on Drugs* (2016) -- BBC documentary on drug policy failures
- Partner with immigrant communities from Latin America -- many of whom have direct experience with drug violence -- to amplify their voices in drug policy debates. Community organizations serving Central American, Mexican, and Colombian immigrants can connect policy reform to lived experience.
- Invite speakers from WOLA, DPA, IDPC, or LEAP (Law Enforcement Action Partnership) to community events. LEAP speakers -- current and former law enforcement officers who support reform -- are particularly effective with audiences skeptical of reform.
- Host a "Know Your Rights" workshop focused on drug policy, connecting domestic and international reform issues

### Build Coalitions

- Connect drug policy reform with immigration advocacy -- drug violence is a primary driver of migration from Central America and Mexico, making these natural allies. Organizations like the American Immigration Lawyers Association and the National Immigration Forum can be engaged on this connection.
- Engage faith communities through the moral case for ending policies that cause violence and suffering abroad while failing to reduce drug use at home. The National Council of Churches and Catholic Charities have both expressed concern about drug war violence in Latin America.
- Build common ground with fiscal conservatives who oppose wasteful government spending -- $5.5 billion/year on international drug programs with no measurable reduction in drug supply is a powerful fiscal argument. The Cato Institute, R Street Institute, and Americans for Prosperity have all published analyses supporting drug policy reform on fiscal grounds.
- Connect with veterans' organizations, as many veterans served in counter-narcotics operations and can speak to the limitations of military approaches. Organizations like Veterans for Responsible Drug Policy provide platforms for veteran voices.
- Engage public health organizations -- the American Public Health Association, the National Association of County and City Health Officials, and state public health associations have passed resolutions supporting harm reduction and evidence-based drug policy.
- Partner with environmental organizations -- aerial fumigation of coca with glyphosate damages ecosystems, and deforestation driven by drug cultivation destroys rainforests. The Sierra Club and Natural Resources Defense Council have identified drug policy as an environmental issue.

## Political Actions

### Contact Elected Officials

**When to Contact**:

- During annual appropriations debates (typically March-September), when INL, DOD counter-narcotics, and foreign assistance budgets are set
- When the ONDCP publishes its annual National Drug Control Strategy (typically February-March)
- When international drug policy bills are introduced or scheduled for committee hearings
- When drug-related violence escalates in Latin America and the media covers the story
- Before and after annual CND sessions in Vienna (typically March), when U.S. positions on international drug policy are set
- When fentanyl-related legislation is being considered (an opportunity to advocate for precursor diplomacy and demand reduction alongside enforcement)

**Who to Contact**:

| Committee | Chamber | Jurisdiction | Key Issues |
|-----------|---------|-------------|------------|
| Foreign Relations | Senate | International drug programs, treaties, bilateral agreements | INL funding, certification, treaty reform |
| Foreign Affairs | House | Same as above | Same as above |
| Appropriations (State-Foreign Ops subcommittee) | Both | INL, USAID, and international drug control funding | Budget allocation, spending rebalancing |
| Judiciary | Both | DEA operations, drug scheduling, extradition | DEA international operations, scheduling reform |
| Armed Services | Both | DOD counter-narcotics programs | Military counter-narcotics funding |
| Homeland Security | Both | CBP interdiction, border enforcement | Border policy, fentanyl response |

**What to Say - Template Email**:

```text
Subject: Reform U.S. International Drug Control Programs

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

I am a constituent from [City, State] writing to urge you to support reforming U.S. international drug control programs. The United States spends approximately $5.5 billion annually on international drug enforcement, yet global coca cultivation hit record highs in 2022 and fentanyl seizures continue to increase.

The Western Hemisphere Drug Policy Commission -- a bipartisan Congressional commission -- recommended in 2020 that the U.S. shift toward health-based approaches in international drug policy. I urge you to support:

1. Rebalancing international drug spending from enforcement (currently ~80%) to health, development, and alternative livelihood programs (at least 60%)
2. Replacing the unilateral drug certification process with cooperative bilateral agreements
3. Supporting evidence-based approaches including harm reduction and decriminalization at the UN Commission on Narcotic Drugs
4. Establishing a Global Drug Harm Reduction Initiative modeled on PEPFAR

The current approach has failed by its own metrics. We need the courage to try evidence-based alternatives.

Thank you for your service and consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your City, State]
```

**What to Say - Phone Call Script**:

```text
"Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [City]. I'm calling about U.S. international drug policy. I'd like the [Representative/Senator] to support rebalancing our $5.5 billion in international drug spending from enforcement toward health and development programs, as recommended by the Western Hemisphere Drug Policy Commission. The current approach has not reduced drug supply. Can you please note my position?"
```

**What to Say - Fentanyl-Specific Phone Call Script**:

```text
"Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [City]. I'm calling about the fentanyl crisis. While I support interdiction efforts, I believe we also need to invest in precursor chemical diplomacy with China and India, expand treatment access, and fund harm reduction programs internationally. Interdiction alone cannot solve the fentanyl crisis -- we need a comprehensive approach. Can you please note my position?"
```

### Support Legislation

Current or proposed legislation to support:

- **Global Drug Harm Reduction Act** (proposed): Would establish a PEPFAR-style harm reduction initiative for international drug programs -- contact your representatives to request they introduce or co-sponsor
- **Appropriations amendments**: Support amendments to the State-Foreign Operations appropriations bill that increase funding for USAID alternative development and reduce funding for aerial eradication
- **Fentanyl precursor legislation**: Support bills that strengthen multilateral precursor chemical controls while avoiding militarized enforcement approaches
- **Certification reform**: Support legislation that replaces the unilateral drug certification process with cooperative bilateral frameworks
- **INL reauthorization**: Support reauthorization language that mandates evidence-based evaluation of all international drug programs and requires health-outcome metrics

### Electoral Engagement

**Questions to Ask Candidates**:

1. "The U.S. spends $5.5 billion per year on international drug control, yet coca cultivation is at record highs and fentanyl continues to flow. What would you do differently?"
2. "Would you support shifting international drug spending from military and law enforcement approaches toward health and development programs?"
3. "What is your position on the UN drug treaty framework and whether the U.S. should support international drug policy reform?"
4. "Would you support a PEPFAR-style program for international harm reduction -- investing in naloxone, treatment, and public health programs in partner countries?"
5. "Do you support replacing the unilateral drug certification process with cooperative bilateral agreements that hold both the U.S. and partner countries accountable?"

**Voting Considerations**:

- Look for candidates who reference evidence-based drug policy, health-centered approaches, and the need to reform the War on Drugs internationally
- Key committees: Senate Foreign Relations, House Foreign Affairs, Senate/House Appropriations (State-Foreign Operations subcommittee), Senate/House Judiciary
- Check candidate positions on the Drug Policy Alliance's voter guide: <https://drugpolicy.org/>
- Review candidates' voting records on international drug policy through GovTrack or OpenSecrets

## Professional Actions

### By Profession

- **For Public Health Professionals**: Join the American Public Health Association's Alcohol, Tobacco, and Other Drug Section, which has passed resolutions supporting drug policy reform including international harm reduction. Submit research on international drug policy outcomes to peer-reviewed journals. Engage with the WHO Expert Committee on Drug Dependence through professional channels.

- **For Legal Professionals**: Support the American Bar Association's resolutions on drug policy reform; provide pro bono assistance to organizations challenging extradition practices or human rights abuses in counter-narcotics operations. The International Bar Association's Human Rights Institute has documented drug war-related human rights violations. Contribute to legal scholarship on treaty flexibility and reform pathways.

- **For Academics/Researchers**: Submit research on international drug policy outcomes to peer-reviewed journals; participate in UNODC and CND side events; sign open letters from the academic community supporting evidence-based reform. Apply for grants from the Open Society Foundations, the Beckley Foundation, or the International Centre for Science in Drug Policy. Organize academic panels on international drug policy at professional conferences (American Political Science Association, American Society of Criminology, International Society for the Study of Drug Policy).

- **For Foreign Service and Development Professionals**: Advocate within your agencies for health-centered approaches; contribute to internal policy discussions; connect with the reform community through professional channels. If you have experience with counter-narcotics programs, document outcomes and share (appropriately) with oversight bodies and researchers.

- **For Journalists**: Investigate the effectiveness of U.S. international drug programs; report on the human costs of eradication and enforcement in producer countries; cover reform developments in Portugal, Uruguay, Canada, and elsewhere. Key story angles include: the gap between drug spending and outcomes, human rights abuses in U.S.-funded counter-narcotics operations, alternative development success stories, and the experiences of drug crop farmers. InSight Crime, WOLA, and TNI are reliable sources for international drug policy reporting.

- **For Business Professionals**: The legal cannabis industry ($43 billion globally) has a direct interest in treaty reform. Business leaders can advocate for regulatory frameworks that enable legal international trade. Financial institutions can support de-risking cannabis businesses and advocate for banking reform.

- **For Military and Law Enforcement**: Current and former military and law enforcement professionals carry unique credibility in drug policy debates. Law Enforcement Action Partnership (LEAP) provides a platform for current and former officers to advocate for evidence-based reform. Veterans who served in counter-narcotics operations (Plan Colombia, Afghanistan, Merida Initiative) can speak to the limitations of military approaches.

- **For Students**: Join Students for Sensible Drug Policy (SSDP) or start a chapter at your university. SSDP operates in over 250 campuses in 30+ countries. Organize campus events on international drug policy. Apply for internships at WOLA, DPA, IDPC, or Transform.

## International Solidarity Actions

- **Support Latin American communities affected by drug violence**: Organizations like the Mexican Commission for the Defense and Promotion of Human Rights (CMDPDH) and the Colombian Truth Commission accept international support and amplify affected communities' voices
- **Advocate against the death penalty for drug offenses**: 35 countries retain the death penalty for drug offenses, with Iran, China, Saudi Arabia, and Singapore among the most frequent executors. Harm Reduction International's campaign against the death penalty for drug offenses provides petitions and advocacy tools: <https://www.hri.global/>
- **Support fair trade products from former drug-producing regions**: Coffee from Colombia's former coca regions, cacao from Peru's San Martin valley, and quinoa from Bolivia's coca-growing areas represent direct economic alternatives to drug cultivation. Fair trade purchasing supports the viability of alternative development.
- **Connect with international reform networks**: IDPC's global network includes organizations in 70+ countries. Connecting with international partners strengthens the global reform movement and provides perspective on how U.S. policy affects communities worldwide.
- **Monitor U.S. military operations abroad**: U.S. counter-narcotics military operations in Latin America, West Africa, and Southeast Asia sometimes involve human rights concerns. Organizations like WOLA and the Center for International Policy monitor these operations and provide channels for citizen oversight.

## Advocacy Toolkit

### Key Talking Points

When discussing international drug policy with others, emphasize:

1. **Fiscal waste**: "The U.S. spends $5.5 billion per year on international drug enforcement, but global coca cultivation is at record highs and fentanyl keeps flowing. We're throwing money at approaches that don't work."
2. **Human cost**: "The War on Drugs has killed over 400,000 people in Mexico since 2006 and destabilized communities across Latin America -- while doing nothing to reduce drug use in the United States."
3. **Evidence-based alternatives**: "Portugal decriminalized all drugs in 2001 and saw drug deaths and HIV infections plummet. We should be supporting evidence-based approaches, not doubling down on prohibition."
4. **Bipartisan issue**: "The bipartisan Western Hemisphere Drug Policy Commission recommended shifting U.S. international drug policy toward health-based approaches. This isn't a left-right issue -- it's about what works."
5. **Fentanyl response**: "Fentanyl can be made anywhere from legal chemicals. Crop eradication and border walls can't stop it. We need precursor diplomacy, treatment, and harm reduction -- not more of the same."
6. **Farmer livelihoods**: "Coca farmers earn $2,000-$4,000 a year. We spend billions destroying their crops without providing alternatives. Seventy-eight percent of them would switch to legal crops if they could earn a living."

### Common Misconceptions to Counter

| Misconception | Reality | Source |
|---------------|---------|--------|
| "We just need to enforce harder and the drug supply will dry up" | The U.S. has spent $50+ billion on international drug enforcement since 1980 with no sustained reduction in drug supply. Coca cultivation hit record highs in 2022. | UNODC World Drug Report, 2023 |
| "Decriminalization causes drug use to skyrocket" | Portugal decriminalized all drugs in 2001 and drug use rates did not significantly increase over 20+ years; drug-related deaths fell dramatically | EMCDDA, 2023 |
| "Legalizing drugs would empower cartels" | Prohibition is the source of cartel profits. Canada's legal cannabis market captured ~70% of sales by 2023, eroding the black market | Statistics Canada, 2023; Health Canada, 2023 |
| "Crop eradication is the best way to reduce supply" | Decades of eradication have failed due to the balloon effect -- production simply shifts elsewhere. Treatment is 7-10x more cost-effective | RAND Corporation, 1994/2005 |
| "International drug policy doesn't affect ordinary Americans" | U.S. drug policy drives violence that fuels migration, costs taxpayers $5.5B/year, and fails to prevent overdose deaths at home | ONDCP, 2024; CDC, 2024 |
| "The UN drug conventions prevent reform" | 30+ countries have decriminalized; 5 have legalized cannabis nationally; none have been sanctioned. Treaty flexibility exists | UNODC Treaty Database, 2024 |
| "Alternative development doesn't work" | Thailand eliminated opium production through 30 years of sustained alternative development. Peru's San Martin region reduced coca by 70% with a development-first approach | UNODC, 2015; DEVIDA, 2016 |
| "Harm reduction enables drug use" | Syringe service program participants are 5x more likely to enter treatment. Switzerland's HAT program reduced crime by 60% and new heroin initiations by 82% | CDC, 2019; Swiss FOPH, 2023 |
