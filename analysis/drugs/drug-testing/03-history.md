# Drug Testing: History

## Historical Development

Drug testing in the United States has evolved from a narrow military application in the 1970s to a vast, multi-billion-dollar system that permeates employment, welfare, education, and criminal justice. Its expansion has been driven less by scientific evidence of effectiveness than by political imperatives, moral panics, catastrophic accidents, and the commercial interests of a growing testing industry. Understanding this history is essential to evaluating whether the current system serves its stated goals.

---

### Origins: Military Drug Testing (1960s-1970s): From Vietnam to the Pentagon

- **Vietnam War and Heroin Use**: The modern era of drug testing began with the U.S. military's response to widespread heroin use among soldiers in Vietnam. In 1971, the military implemented Operation Golden Flow, which required all service members to test negative for opiates before returning to the United States. Approximately 5% of returning soldiers tested positive.
- **Nixon's War on Drugs**: President Richard Nixon declared drug abuse "public enemy number one" in June 1971 and signed the Comprehensive Drug Abuse Prevention and Control Act (1970), which established the drug scheduling system. Nixon's drug policy was explicitly political: aide John Ehrlichman later acknowledged that the administration's drug policy targeted "the antiwar left and black people."
- **Early Technology**: Testing in this era relied on thin-layer chromatography (TLC) and early immunoassay techniques. The technology was rudimentary, with high false positive rates and limited substance coverage.
- **Military Expansion**: Following initial heroin screening, the Department of Defense expanded its testing program in the late 1970s to include cannabis, cocaine, and amphetamines, eventually creating the largest drug testing program in the world.
- **EMIT Immunoassay (1972)**: Syva Company introduced the Enzyme Multiplied Immunoassay Technique (EMIT), the first commercially available immunoassay drug screening test. This technology -- cheap, fast, and easily automated -- would become the foundation of mass drug testing. However, the technology's limitations (cross-reactivity, false positive rates of 5-10%, detection of metabolites rather than active compounds) would shape the controversies that followed for decades.
- **Limited Civilian Application**: Throughout the 1970s, civilian drug testing remained rare. Employer testing was generally limited to the nuclear industry, which adopted testing under Atomic Energy Commission (later NRC) regulations, and a small number of safety-sensitive private employers. Public debate about civilian drug testing was minimal.

---

### The Reagan Era (1981-1989): Drug-Free America and the Workplace Testing Explosion

- **Executive Order 12564 (1986)**: Following the crash of a Navy jet on the USS *Nimitz* in 1981 -- in which autopsies revealed marijuana metabolites in several deceased crew members -- and the derailment of a Conrail train in Chase, Maryland in 1987 (linked to cannabis and PCP use by the engineer), President Reagan signed Executive Order 12564, declaring the federal government a "Drug-Free Workplace" and authorizing testing of federal employees in "sensitive positions."
- **Drug-Free Workplace Act (1988)**: Congress enacted the Drug-Free Workplace Act, requiring all federal contractors and grantees to certify drug-free workplace policies. While the Act did not mandate testing, it created powerful incentives for employers to adopt testing programs.
- **SAMHSA Guidelines (1988)**: The Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA, originally NIDA) published Mandatory Guidelines for Federal Workplace Drug Testing Programs, establishing the technical standards (cutoff levels, confirmation requirements, chain of custody protocols) that remain the foundation of the system.
- **Corporate Adoption Surge**: Private-sector employers rapidly adopted drug testing after 1986. The American Management Association reported that the percentage of large companies testing employees rose from 18% in 1987 to 48% in 1990.
- **"Just Say No" and Moral Panic**: First Lady Nancy Reagan's "Just Say No" campaign and the media-fueled crack cocaine panic created intense public pressure for visible anti-drug measures. Drug testing served a symbolic function -- demonstrating institutional commitment to a drug-free environment -- regardless of its empirical effectiveness.
- **Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1986**: Congress passed the Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1986, which established mandatory minimum sentences for drug offenses and allocated $1.7 billion for anti-drug programs. The Act reflected the punitive philosophy that would also drive workplace testing expansion: drug use was a moral and criminal matter, not primarily a health concern.
- **Laboratory Certification**: NIDA (later SAMHSA) established the National Laboratory Certification Program (NLCP) to certify laboratories conducting federal workplace drug testing. The program required laboratories to meet rigorous quality standards, including proficiency testing, chain-of-custody procedures, and GC-MS confirmation of all positive results. By 1989, approximately 50 laboratories had achieved certification.
- **Worker Resistance**: The rapid expansion of workplace testing generated significant worker resistance. In 1986, the Transport Workers Union and other labor organizations challenged testing programs in court, and the ACLU filed numerous lawsuits arguing that suspicionless testing violated the Fourth Amendment. These challenges would eventually reach the Supreme Court in 1989.
- **Insurance Industry Role**: The insurance industry became a significant driver of workplace testing by offering reduced workers' compensation premiums to employers with drug-free workplace programs. This financial incentive -- which persists in many states today -- accelerated private-sector testing adoption independent of federal mandates.

---

### The DOT Era (1989-2000): Mandated Testing for Safety-Sensitive Workers

- **Omnibus Transportation Employee Testing Act (1991)**: Following the Exxon Valdez oil spill in 1989 (in which the ship's captain had a history of alcohol problems) and a series of transportation accidents, Congress passed the Omnibus Transportation Employee Testing Act, mandating pre-employment, random, post-accident, reasonable suspicion, and return-to-duty testing for all transportation workers under DOT jurisdiction.
- **49 CFR Part 40**: The DOT codified comprehensive testing procedures, including the Substance Abuse Professional (SAP) evaluation process, Medical Review Officer (MRO) review of results, and split specimen procedures.
- **Testing Industry Growth**: The mandated testing market created a multi-billion-dollar industry of certified laboratories, collection sites, MROs, SAPs, and testing technology companies. The number of SAMHSA-certified laboratories grew from fewer than 30 in 1988 to over 80 by 2000. The Drug and Alcohol Testing Industry Association (DATIA) was founded in 1995 to represent the growing industry's interests.
- **Medical Review Officer (MRO) Role**: The DOT regulations created the MRO position -- a licensed physician who reviews and interprets drug test results before they are reported to the employer. The MRO system was designed to prevent false positive adverse actions by allowing individuals to provide legitimate medical explanations for positive results (e.g., prescription medications). This procedural safeguard became a model for best practices but was never extended to non-federally-regulated testing.
- **ADA and Drug Testing (1990)**: The Americans with Disabilities Act, enacted in 1990, addressed drug testing in a nuanced way. The ADA explicitly excludes current illegal drug users from disability protections but protects individuals who are in recovery from substance use disorders, are participating in treatment programs, or have been erroneously perceived as using drugs. The ADA also prohibits pre-employment medical examinations (which could include drug tests) before a conditional offer of employment is made.
- **State Drug-Free Workplace Acts**: Many states enacted their own drug-free workplace laws in the late 1980s and 1990s, often modeled on the federal Act but with additional provisions. Some states (notably Florida, Georgia, and Tennessee) created workers' compensation premium discount programs for employers with certified drug-free workplace programs, creating powerful financial incentives for testing.
- **Supreme Court Cases**: The Supreme Court addressed the constitutionality of drug testing in several landmark cases:
    - ***Skinner v. Railway Labor Executives' Association***, 489 U.S. 602 (1989): Upheld post-accident drug testing of railroad employees as a "special needs" exception to the Fourth Amendment's warrant requirement.
    - ***National Treasury Employees Union v. Von Raab***, 489 U.S. 656 (1989): Upheld suspicionless testing of U.S. Customs employees seeking transfer to positions involving drug interdiction or carrying firearms.
    - ***Chandler v. Miller***, 520 U.S. 305 (1997): Struck down Georgia's requirement that candidates for state office pass a drug test, finding the state's interest was "symbolic, not special."

### Welfare and Student Testing (1996-2010): Expanding the Testing Regime

- **Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act (1996)**: Welfare reform legislation signed by President Clinton included a provision (Section 902) explicitly authorizing states to drug test TANF recipients, though it did not require it. This opened the door to state welfare drug testing programs. The provision reflected the era's emphasis on "personal responsibility" and the politically popular assumption that welfare recipients were likely drug users who should be surveilled.
- **Michigan's Failed Experiment**: Michigan became the first state to implement suspicionless drug testing of all welfare recipients in 1999, but the program was struck down by the 6th Circuit in *Marchwinski v. Howard*, 309 F.3d 330 (6th Cir. 2002), which held that blanket testing without individualized suspicion violated the Fourth Amendment. Despite this ruling, multiple states continued to pursue welfare testing, adopting suspicion-based screening models designed to survive constitutional challenge.
- **State Welfare Testing Wave (2009-2015)**: Between 2009 and 2015, a wave of state legislatures enacted TANF drug testing requirements, driven by Tea Party-era politics emphasizing government accountability and personal responsibility. Missouri (2011), Florida (2011), Utah (2012), Tennessee (2014), and others adopted programs. The results were uniformly similar: positive rates far below the general population (typically 0.1-1.4%), high costs per positive result ($200-$7,000+), and minimal savings from denied benefits.
- **Florida's Constitutional Defeat**: Florida's 2011 program, which required all TANF applicants to submit to urinalysis at their own expense (reimbursed upon a negative result), became the highest-profile test case. Governor Rick Scott championed the law, but it was challenged immediately by the ACLU. In *Lebron v. Secretary, FL DCF*, 710 F.3d 1202 (11th Cir. 2013), the 11th Circuit permanently enjoined the program, finding that "there is nothing inherent to the condition of being impoverished that supports the conclusion that there is a concrete danger" of drug use among TANF applicants.
- **Student Athlete Testing**: In ***Vernonia School District 47J v. Acton***, 515 U.S. 646 (1995), the Supreme Court upheld random urinalysis of student athletes, finding that students in school have reduced privacy expectations and that the government interest in deterring drug use among athletes was sufficient.
- **Expansion to All Extracurricular Activities**: In ***Board of Education of Independent School District No. 92 of Pottawatomie County v. Earls***, 536 U.S. 822 (2002), the Supreme Court extended the *Vernonia* ruling to all students participating in extracurricular activities, not just athletes. This decision significantly expanded the scope of permissible student drug testing.
- **ONDCP and Drug-Free Schools**: The Office of National Drug Control Policy (ONDCP) under the George W. Bush administration promoted random student drug testing as a prevention strategy, directing $10-15 million annually in grant funding to schools implementing testing programs through 2008.
- **Hair Testing Controversies (1990s-2000s)**: Hair follicle testing gained popularity among some employers for its 90-day detection window, but controversies over racial bias emerged early. The Boston Police Department adopted hair testing in the late 1990s, but abandoned it in 2004 after an independent scientific review found that the test disproportionately flagged Black officers due to melanin-drug binding differentials. Multiple studies in *Forensic Science International* documented that Black individuals produce 2-4 times higher drug concentrations in hair than white individuals with equivalent drug exposure, raising significant civil rights concerns.
- **Peak of Testing (2000-2006)**: Workplace drug testing reached its peak prevalence in the early 2000s, with approximately 81% of large employers conducting some form of testing, according to the American Management Association. The combination of DOT mandates, state drug-free workplace premium discounts, the ONDCP's promotion of testing, and post-9/11 security concerns drove testing to its highest levels. After 2006, testing rates began a gradual decline driven by cost pressures, labor market tightness, and emerging questions about effectiveness.

### Cannabis Legalization and the Testing Crisis (2012-Present): A System Under Strain

- **Colorado and Washington (2012)**: The passage of cannabis legalization ballot measures in Colorado (Amendment 64) and Washington (Initiative 502) in 2012 created the first direct conflict between state-legal cannabis use and workplace drug testing. For the first time, large populations of workers could legally purchase and consume cannabis under state law but faced termination for positive workplace drug tests. The conflict was particularly acute because urine testing detects cannabis metabolites for days or weeks after use, long after impairment has subsided.
- **Court Rulings Against Workers**: In *Coats v. Dish Network*, 350 P.3d 849 (Colo. 2015), the Colorado Supreme Court ruled that employers could terminate employees for off-duty cannabis use, finding that Colorado's lawful off-duty activities statute did not protect conduct that was illegal under federal law. This ruling -- holding that federal illegality trumped state legalization for employment purposes -- galvanized advocacy for explicit statutory employment protections.
- **Oregon and *Emerald Steel* (2010)**: Even before full legalization, the Oregon Supreme Court in *Emerald Steel Fabricators, Inc. v. Bureau of Labor and Industries*, 348 Or. 159 (2010), held that Oregon employers could fire medical marijuana patients for positive drug tests despite the state's medical marijuana law. The court found that the Controlled Substances Act preempted state medical marijuana protections in the employment context.
- **State Employment Protections (2019-2024)**: Beginning with Nevada (2019) and Maine (2020), a growing wave of states enacted laws prohibiting employers from taking adverse action based on pre-employment or off-duty cannabis tests, with varying exemptions for safety-sensitive positions. These laws represented a fundamental shift: rather than relying on courts to interpret existing off-duty conduct statutes, legislatures explicitly addressed the cannabis-testing conflict through new legislation.
- **The DOT Clearinghouse (2020)**: The FMCSA Drug and Alcohol Clearinghouse, which went live in January 2020, created a centralized database of drug and alcohol testing violations for commercial motor vehicle drivers. By 2024, the Clearinghouse had recorded over 200,000 violations, and an estimated 90,000 drivers were in "prohibited" status (barred from operating commercial vehicles until completing the return-to-duty process). Critics argued that the Clearinghouse was removing desperately needed drivers from the workforce for cannabis metabolite detections that did not indicate impairment, exacerbating the national truck driver shortage.
- **Amazon's Shift (2021)**: Amazon's decision to drop pre-employment cannabis screening for non-DOT positions represented a major corporate inflection point, with the company publicly advocating for the MORE Act (federal cannabis legalization).
- **Fentanyl Test Strip Movement (2018-2024)**: The explosion of fentanyl-related overdose deaths prompted a rapid legislative movement to legalize fentanyl test strips. Maryland and the District of Columbia were among the first jurisdictions to explicitly exempt FTS from paraphernalia laws (2018), and by 2024, 39 states and D.C. had followed.
- **SAMHSA Oral Fluid Guidelines (2019-2024)**: SAMHSA published revised Mandatory Guidelines in 2019 authorizing oral fluid testing as an alternative specimen for federal workplace testing, with full implementation phased in through 2024. Oral fluid tests detect more recent use (24-48 hours for cannabis vs. 1-30 days for urine), partially addressing the impairment detection gap.
- **Drug Checking Evolution (2015-2024)**: While drug checking has existed internationally since the 1990s (with European organizations like Energy Control in Spain and the Loop in the UK providing testing services at music festivals), the U.S. drug checking movement accelerated in response to the fentanyl crisis. DanceSafe, founded in 1998, was the primary U.S. drug checking organization for nearly two decades, operating primarily at music events. The fentanyl crisis transformed drug checking from a niche nightlife service into a mainstream public health intervention, with city health departments (New York, San Francisco, Chicago) operating programs using sophisticated laboratory equipment.
- **The 2023-2024 Wave**: The period from 2023 to 2024 saw the most rapid changes in drug testing policy since the 1980s. California enacted the broadest cannabis employment protection law in the country (AB 2188, effective January 2024), prohibiting employers from discriminating based on off-duty cannabis use. SAMHSA finalized oral fluid testing guidelines. The FMCSA Drug and Alcohol Clearinghouse reported that over 200,000 commercial motor vehicle drivers had at least one drug or alcohol violation since the system launched in 2020, raising questions about the system's impact on the trucking workforce. Cannabis positivity rates in the general workforce reached 4.3%, the highest level ever recorded.

## Growth of the Testing Industry

The drug testing industry's growth from a niche military application to a $12 billion commercial enterprise is itself a significant historical development that has shaped policy outcomes.

### Industry Timeline

| Period | Industry Revenue (est.) | Key Developments |
|--------|------------------------|------------------|
| Pre-1986 | <$100 million | Limited to military, nuclear, a few private employers |
| 1987-1990 | $500 million - $1 billion | Explosive growth following EO 12564 and Drug-Free Workplace Act |
| 1991-2000 | $2-5 billion | DOT mandates create massive testing market; SAMHSA-certified labs grow to 80+ |
| 2001-2010 | $5-8 billion | Peak prevalence of employer testing (~81%); post-9/11 security-driven expansion |
| 2011-2020 | $8-10 billion | Cannabis legalization begins eroding general workforce testing; DOT market stable |
| 2021-present | $10-12 billion | Market adapts: oral fluid growth, drug checking equipment, impairment technology |

### Industry Consolidation

The drug testing industry has undergone significant consolidation. Two companies -- Quest Diagnostics and Clinical Reference Laboratory -- process the majority of federally mandated workplace drug tests. Quest Diagnostics alone processes approximately 11 million workplace drug tests annually, giving it dominant market share and significant influence over the public narrative through its annual Drug Testing Index.

The consolidation of the industry has created powerful economic interests aligned against testing reform. Major testing companies, collection networks, and technology manufacturers lobby through DATIA and individually to maintain testing mandates and oppose cannabis testing reform. The industry's economic model depends on testing volume -- every worker removed from testing panels, every substance removed from testing panels, and every employer that drops testing represents lost revenue.

### The Revolving Door

Personnel movement between government regulatory agencies and the drug testing industry has been a persistent feature. SAMHSA officials who oversee testing standards and laboratory certification move to private-sector positions in testing companies. Industry representatives serve on SAMHSA advisory boards, including the Drug Testing Advisory Board (DTAB), which recommends testing standards. This revolving door raises concerns about regulatory capture and the independence of standard-setting processes.

## International Context

Drug testing policy in the United States developed on a fundamentally different trajectory than in most other industrialized nations:

### European Approach

Most European countries never adopted mass workplace drug testing comparable to the U.S. system. The International Labour Organization's (ILO) 1996 Code of Practice on Management of Alcohol and Drug-Related Issues in the Workplace recommended against random testing and favored impairment observation, employee assistance, and health-based approaches. Key differences include:

- **France**: Workplace drug testing is permitted only for safety-sensitive positions, requires physician authorization, and results are treated as medical confidentiality. Random testing is not generally practiced outside transportation.
- **Germany**: Workplace drug testing is heavily restricted by data protection laws (BDSG) and requires works council consent. Testing is limited to positions with demonstrated safety justification.
- **United Kingdom**: No statutory framework for workplace drug testing. Employers may test under contract terms, but testing is far less prevalent than in the U.S. The UK Drug Testing Laboratory Forum estimates approximately 1-2 million workplace tests annually, compared to 55-60 million in the U.S.
- **Scandinavia**: Norway, Sweden, and Finland permit safety-sensitive testing (particularly in maritime and transportation) but restrict general workplace testing. Norway's roadside oral fluid testing program has become a model for impairment-focused testing.

### Canadian Approach

Canada occupies a middle ground. The Supreme Court of Canada's decision in *Stewart v. Elk Valley Coal Corp.* (2017) permitted testing in safety-sensitive positions but established that employers must accommodate employees with substance use disorders. Canada's 2018 federal cannabis legalization prompted a systematic review of workplace testing policies, with Canadian employers generally shifting toward impairment-based approaches faster than U.S. counterparts. The Canadian Human Rights Commission has published detailed guidance on acceptable testing practices that emphasizes bona fide occupational requirements and accommodation.

### Australian Approach

Australia conducts oral fluid roadside drug testing on a large scale, with New South Wales and Victoria testing over 200,000 drivers annually. Australian workplace testing exists primarily in mining, construction, and transportation, with a stronger emphasis on oral fluid (which detects recent use) than urine testing. Australia's experience with oral fluid testing has provided significant evidence for SAMHSA's decision to authorize oral fluid in federal programs.

### International Drug Checking

Drug checking has a substantially longer history in Europe than in the United States:

- **Netherlands (1992)**: The Drugs Information and Monitoring System (DIMS) was established as the world's first systematic drug checking program, operating through municipal health services across the Netherlands. DIMS has analyzed over 100,000 samples and provides real-time surveillance of the drug supply.
- **Spain (1999)**: Energy Control began providing drug checking services at music festivals and through a mail-in service, using GC-MS and HPLC analysis. The program has served as a model for community-based drug checking worldwide.
- **United Kingdom (2016)**: The Loop began providing drug checking at music festivals, using FTIR spectroscopy and GC-MS with same-day results and brief interventions. The Loop's model -- combining scientific analysis with health counseling -- influenced U.S. programs.
- **Switzerland, Austria, Belgium**: Multiple countries operate drug checking through government-funded programs, typically integrated with broader harm reduction services.

The European experience demonstrates that drug checking can operate at scale over decades without increasing drug use, and that the public health intelligence generated by drug checking (early detection of dangerous adulterants, trend monitoring) provides benefits beyond individual behavior change.

## Lessons from History

### Pattern 1: Political Symbolism Over Scientific Evidence

Drug testing policies have historically been adopted in response to political pressure and public fear rather than scientific evidence of effectiveness. Executive Order 12564 was issued amid the height of the crack cocaine panic. The Omnibus Transportation Employee Testing Act followed high-profile accidents. Welfare drug testing was enacted during the welfare reform movement's focus on personal responsibility. In each case, the policy served a symbolic function -- demonstrating toughness on drugs or personal responsibility -- before (and in some cases, without) evidence that it achieved its stated goals. The 2007 National Research Council review's finding of "no evidence" that workplace testing reduces drug use underscores this pattern.

### Pattern 2: Technology Lag Behind Policy Needs

The testing technologies adopted in the 1980s -- primarily urine immunoassay screening with GC-MS confirmation -- were designed to detect metabolites of substances consumed days or weeks earlier, not to measure current impairment. Thirty-five years later, the system still lacks a validated, widely deployed test for real-time cannabis impairment. This technological lag has become the central obstacle to rational drug testing policy in the era of cannabis legalization.

### Pattern 3: Industry Momentum Creates Policy Inertia

The $12 billion drug testing industry has created powerful constituencies with economic interests in maintaining the current testing regime. SAMHSA-certified laboratories, specimen collection networks, Medical Review Officers, Substance Abuse Professionals, and testing technology companies all depend on the continuation and expansion of testing programs. This economic momentum makes reform difficult even when the evidence base for testing is weak.

### Pattern 4: Courts Expand Government Testing Power Incrementally

From *Skinner* (1989) to *Earls* (2002), the Supreme Court progressively expanded the "special needs" doctrine to permit suspicionless drug testing in an expanding set of contexts: railroad workers, customs agents, student athletes, all extracurricular students. Each ruling narrowed the Fourth Amendment's protection against unreasonable searches in the drug testing context. The *Lebron* decision (2013) represents a rare counterexample, limiting welfare testing to cases with individualized suspicion.

### Pattern 5: Catastrophic Accidents Drive Policy Regardless of Root Cause

A consistent pattern in drug testing history is that high-profile accidents trigger testing mandates even when the causal link between drug use and the accident is ambiguous or tangential. The USS *Nimitz* crash (1981) led to Executive Order 12564 even though marijuana metabolites in deceased crew members did not establish that marijuana caused the crash. The Exxon Valdez spill (1989) accelerated DOT testing mandates even though alcohol, not illegal drugs, was the primary concern in that incident (and alcohol is poorly detected by standard drug testing panels). The Chase, Maryland train derailment (1987) was linked to cannabis and PCP use, but the broader DOT testing mandate that followed applied to all substances across all transportation modes. Each time, the political response was to expand testing broadly rather than to address the specific causal factors.

### Pattern 6: Harm Reduction Testing Emerges as a Counter-Model

The emergence of drug checking as a public health intervention represents a fundamentally different historical trajectory from punitive testing. While workplace and welfare testing grew from the War on Drugs framework (detect, deter, punish), drug checking grew from the European harm reduction movement (inform, reduce harm, connect to services). The two models coexist in contemporary policy but reflect incompatible philosophies. The rapid legalization of fentanyl test strips -- from 3 states in 2020 to 39 + D.C. by 2024 -- suggests that the harm reduction model is achieving political viability faster than reformers anticipated, driven by the sheer scale of fentanyl-related death (73,838 in 2023).

## The National Research Council Assessment (2007)

A pivotal moment in the intellectual history of drug testing was the 2007 National Research Council (NRC) report "Informing America's Policy on Illegal Drugs," commissioned by the Office of National Drug Control Policy. The committee, comprising leading researchers in pharmacology, public health, and criminal justice, reviewed the available evidence on workplace drug testing and concluded:

- There was "no evidence" that workplace drug testing programs reduce drug use among tested populations.
- The relationship between positive drug test results and workplace impairment or accident risk was not established by the available research.
- The research base on drug testing effectiveness was "thin" and "methodologically limited."
- The deterrent effect of testing, while theoretically plausible, had not been demonstrated empirically.

The NRC report received minimal media attention and had virtually no impact on federal testing policy. Neither SAMHSA nor DOT modified their testing programs in response to its findings. The report's conclusions were, however, extensively cited by reform advocates and have been reinforced by subsequent research, including a 2014 systematic review by Pidd and Roche in *Accident Analysis & Prevention* and a 2022 meta-analysis in the *Journal of Occupational and Environmental Medicine*, both of which found weak and inconsistent evidence for testing's safety benefits.

The disconnect between the NRC's findings and continued policy expansion illustrates one of the central historical patterns in drug testing: the system's persistence is driven by political, economic, and cultural factors rather than scientific evidence.

## Evolution of Testing Technology

| Year | Technology | Significance |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| 1966 | Thin-layer chromatography (TLC) | First laboratory drug detection method; labor-intensive and low sensitivity |
| 1972 | EMIT immunoassay (Syva Company) | First commercial screening test; enabled mass testing |
| 1975 | Gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS) | Gold standard confirmation test; highly specific |
| 1980s | Radioimmunoassay (RIA) | Higher sensitivity than EMIT; used primarily in clinical settings |
| 1990s | ELISA immunoassay | Improved automation and throughput for high-volume screening |
| 2000s | Liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) | More versatile confirmation; broader substance coverage |
| 2010s | Oral fluid immunoassay devices | Point-of-collection testing for recent use; shorter detection window |
| 2010s | Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) for drug checking | Portable substance identification; adapted for community drug checking |
| 2020s | Breath-based THC detection (Hound Labs) | Detects cannabis use within 2-3 hours; closest to impairment measurement |
| 2020s | Cognitive impairment testing apps (DRUID, AlertMeter) | Software-based fitness-for-duty assessment; emerging technology |
| 2020s | Fentanyl test strips (repurposed from clinical immunoassay) | Lateral flow immunoassay adapted for drug supply checking; >96% sensitivity |
