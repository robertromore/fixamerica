# Drug Testing: What You Can Do

Drug testing policy affects tens of millions of Americans each year -- as workers, job applicants, benefits recipients, students, and people who use drugs. Reform requires citizen engagement at every level, from individual workplace advocacy to federal legislative campaigns. The fentanyl crisis has added urgency: drug checking services save lives, and removing legal barriers to their use is literally a life-or-death policy issue.

---

## Individual Actions

### Learn and Stay Informed

- Subscribe to *Marijuana Moment* (<https://www.marijuanamoment.net>) for daily updates on cannabis legalization and workplace testing reform
- Follow the Drug Policy Alliance (<https://drugpolicy.org>) for updates on drug checking legalization and harm reduction policy
- Read the Quest Diagnostics Drug Testing Index (<https://www.questdiagnostics.com/dti>) for annual data on workplace testing trends
- Review your state's laws on cannabis employment protections at NORML's state law tracker (<https://norml.org/laws>)

### Know Your Rights

- **If you are asked to take a workplace drug test**: Understand whether the test is required by federal regulation (DOT), state law, or employer policy. Federal employees and DOT-regulated workers have different rights than private-sector employees.
- **If you receive a positive result**: You have the right to request a confirmatory test (GC-MS/LC-MS) in federally regulated programs. In non-regulated programs, check your state law -- some states require confirmation before adverse action.
- **If you use medical marijuana**: At least 38 states have medical marijuana programs, but employment protections vary widely. Some states protect medical marijuana patients from employer discrimination; others do not.
- **If you are a TANF applicant subject to testing**: The 11th Circuit ruled in *Lebron v. Secretary, FL DCF* (2013) that suspicionless testing of TANF applicants violates the Fourth Amendment. If you believe your rights have been violated, contact the ACLU.

### Use Your Voice

- **Contact Your Representatives**: When cannabis employment protection bills or drug checking legalization bills are pending in your state legislature or in Congress, contact your representatives to express support.
- **Submit Public Comments**: When DOT, SAMHSA, or state agencies issue proposed rules related to drug testing, submit public comments during the notice-and-comment period. Federal rulemaking comments can be submitted at <https://www.regulations.gov>.
- **Write Letters to the Editor**: Share your experience with drug testing, especially if you or someone you know has faced job loss or denial of benefits due to a positive test for legal off-duty cannabis use.
- **Share Information**: Distribute fentanyl test strips and information about drug checking services to people who may benefit. Many harm reduction organizations provide free FTS and training.

## Community Actions

### Organize Locally

- Form a local coalition of workers, employers, civil liberties advocates, and harm reduction organizations to advocate for state-level drug testing reform
- Host community information sessions on workers' rights regarding drug testing in your state, particularly in states with new cannabis employment protections
- Partner with local harm reduction organizations to distribute fentanyl test strips and connect people to drug checking services
- Organize a screening and discussion event around drug testing policy using materials from the Drug Policy Alliance or ACLU

### Build Coalitions

- Connect with local chapters of NORML, the ACLU, and the Drug Policy Alliance to coordinate advocacy
- Engage local business groups and chambers of commerce -- many employers, particularly in tight labor markets, support cannabis testing reform to expand their applicant pools
- Partner with labor unions (Teamsters, BLET, transit unions) that represent DOT-regulated workers and can advocate for testing modernization
- Engage faith communities that support addiction treatment over punishment
- Connect with medical professional organizations (local medical societies, nursing associations) that can provide clinical credibility to testing reform advocacy
- Partner with civil rights organizations (NAACP, National Urban League, LatinoJustice) to address the racial equity dimensions of drug testing policy

### Volunteer for Harm Reduction

Drug checking services and fentanyl test strip distribution depend heavily on volunteer labor. Practical ways to get involved:

- **Distribute fentanyl test strips**: Many harm reduction organizations provide free FTS for community distribution. Organizations like DanceSafe, the National Harm Reduction Coalition, and local syringe service programs train volunteers to distribute FTS and explain result interpretation.
- **Volunteer at drug checking events**: DanceSafe and similar organizations operate drug checking services at music festivals and nightlife events, staffed largely by trained volunteers. Training typically involves a 4-8 hour workshop covering immunoassay interpretation, substance identification, and brief intervention techniques.
- **Support syringe service programs**: SSPs are the primary community-based distribution point for fentanyl test strips and other harm reduction supplies. Volunteer roles include supply preparation, distribution, naloxone training, and peer outreach.
- **Organize naloxone training**: Naloxone (Narcan) is available over-the-counter and can reverse opioid overdoses. Organize training sessions in your community on naloxone use, overdose recognition, and connecting with emergency services. Many state health departments provide free naloxone kits for community distribution.
- **Donate to harm reduction organizations**: Financial support enables programs to purchase fentanyl test strips ($0.50-$2.00 each), naloxone kits, and drug checking equipment. See the Organizations to Support table below.

### Engage with Your School District

If you are a parent, teacher, or school board member in a district that conducts random student drug testing:

- **Request effectiveness data**: Ask the school administration to provide data on the testing program's outcomes -- student substance use rates before and after implementation, number of positives, and how results are used. Compare this data to the 2012 IES finding of "no significant differences" in substance use between schools with and without testing.
- **Advocate for evidence-based alternatives**: Propose that the district invest in evidence-based prevention programs (LifeSkills Training, Project ALERT, Good Behavior Game) that have demonstrated 15-30% reductions in substance use initiation, rather than testing programs that have not demonstrated effectiveness.
- **Raise privacy concerns**: Student drug testing involves collection and analysis of bodily fluids from minors. Ensure that the district has adequate privacy protections, including informed parental consent, confidential handling of results, and restrictions on who can access test information.
- **Monitor for deterrence of extracurricular participation**: If the testing program is associated with declines in extracurricular participation, raise this concern with the school board. Extracurricular involvement is associated with lower drug use; a testing program that deters participation may be counterproductive.

## Political Actions

### Contact Elected Officials

**When to Contact**:

- When cannabis employment protection legislation is pending in your state legislature
- When drug checking/fentanyl test strip legalization bills are introduced
- When welfare drug testing bills are introduced or up for renewal
- During federal appropriations season to support funding for SAMHSA drug checking grants and NIDA impairment technology research
- When DOT or SAMHSA issues proposed rulemaking on testing standards

**What to Say - Template Email**:

```text
Subject: Support Evidence-Based Drug Testing Reform

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

I am writing as a constituent from [City, State] to urge your support for evidence-based drug testing reform.

Current workplace drug testing policies are outdated. They rely on metabolite detection that identifies past use of legal substances rather than current impairment. With 24 states having legalized recreational cannabis, millions of Americans risk losing their jobs for legal off-duty conduct. Meanwhile, fentanyl test strips -- which cost $1 and are proven to reduce overdose deaths -- remain illegal in [X] states because they are classified as drug paraphernalia.

I urge you to support:
- Federal employment protections for off-duty cannabis use in non-safety-sensitive positions
- Legalization of fentanyl test strips and drug checking services
- Federal investment in impairment detection technology research
- An end to wasteful welfare drug testing programs

Over 107,000 Americans died from drug overdoses in 2023. Drug checking saves lives. Modernizing workplace testing protects workers and improves safety. These reforms are supported by evidence and endorsed by public health organizations.

Thank you for your service and consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your City, State]
```

**What to Say - Phone Call Script**:

```text
"Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [City]. I'm calling about drug testing reform. I'd like the [Representative/Senator] to support the legalization of fentanyl test strips, federal employment protections for off-duty cannabis use, and investment in impairment detection technology. These are evidence-based reforms that save lives and protect workers. Can you please note my position?"
```

### Support Legislation

Current or proposed legislation to support:

- **Federal Drug Checking Equipment Legalization Act**: Would amend 21 U.S.C. Section 863 to exclude fentanyl test strips and drug checking equipment from the federal paraphernalia definition -- contact your Senators and Representative to co-sponsor
- **State-level cannabis employment protection bills**: Active or proposed in multiple states; check NORML's legislative tracker for your state
- **State fentanyl test strip legalization**: If you live in one of the 11 states where FTS remain illegal (Alabama, Florida, Georgia, Idaho, Indiana, Iowa, Kansas, Kentucky, South Carolina, Texas, Wyoming), contact your state legislators

### Electoral Engagement

**Questions to Ask Candidates**:

1. "Do you support protecting workers from being fired for legal off-duty cannabis use in non-safety-sensitive positions?"
2. "Do you support legalizing fentanyl test strips and funding drug checking services to reduce overdose deaths?"
3. "Do you support ending welfare drug testing programs that cost more than they save and find positive rates of less than 1%?"
4. "Would you support federal investment in impairment detection technology so that safety-sensitive testing can focus on actual fitness for duty?"
5. "Do you support requiring confirmatory testing before any worker faces adverse action based on a drug test, to prevent false positive terminations?"
6. "Would you support prohibiting hair follicle drug testing in employment due to its documented racial bias against Black workers?"

**Voting Considerations**:

- Look for candidates who support evidence-based drug policy and harm reduction
- Check candidate positions on cannabis legalization and employment protections
- Note candidates' positions on the House Energy and Commerce Committee, Senate HELP Committee, and Appropriations subcommittees that fund SAMHSA and NIDA
- Review candidate positions on the MORE Act (federal cannabis legalization), STATES Act (federalism approach to cannabis), and any drug checking legalization bills
- Check endorsements from NORML, DPA, and ACLU, which evaluate candidates on drug policy positions
- Evaluate candidates' records on Fourth Amendment and civil liberties issues, which often correlate with positions on drug testing reform

## Professional Actions

### For Employers and HR Professionals

- **Review your testing policy**: Assess whether pre-employment cannabis screening is necessary for non-safety-sensitive positions. Amazon, Apple, Google, Microsoft, Chipotle, and other major employers have eliminated cannabis screening without safety impacts. Consider the labor market costs of testing: a 2023 SHRM survey found 33% of employers in legal states reported difficulty filling positions due to positive cannabis tests.
- **Implement confirmatory testing**: If your organization conducts drug testing, require GC-MS or LC-MS/MS confirmation before taking any adverse action. This protects workers from false positives (5-10% rate on initial immunoassay screens) and protects the employer from wrongful termination claims.
- **Evaluate hair testing for racial bias**: If your organization uses hair follicle testing, review the evidence on melanin-drug binding disparities. Consider transitioning to oral fluid or other methods that do not have documented racial bias.
- **Adopt impairment-focused approaches**: Consider supplementing or replacing metabolite testing with fitness-for-duty assessments. Pilot emerging technologies (DRUID app, AlertMeter) to evaluate their effectiveness in your workplace.
- **Update policies for state law compliance**: If your state has enacted cannabis employment protections, ensure your testing policies comply. Consult legal counsel on the intersection of state protections and federal requirements (DOT, Drug-Free Workplace Act).
- **Provide Employee Assistance**: If an employee tests positive, consider referral to an Employee Assistance Program (EAP) rather than immediate termination. Evidence shows that treatment and support produce better outcomes than punishment for employees with substance use disorders.

### For Attorneys

- **Take pro bono testing cases**: Represent workers terminated based on false positives or legal off-duty cannabis use. The ACLU and NORML maintain referral networks for workers seeking legal representation.
- **File amicus briefs**: In pending cannabis employment protection cases, workplace testing challenges, and welfare drug testing litigation, file amicus briefs presenting the scientific evidence on testing accuracy, racial disparities, and effectiveness.
- **Challenge unconstitutional welfare testing**: In states where suspicionless welfare drug testing persists, consider Fourth Amendment challenges under *Lebron* and *Marchwinski*.
- **Advise employers on compliance**: Help employers navigate the complex intersection of federal DOT requirements, state cannabis protections, ADA obligations, and employer testing policies.

### For Healthcare Providers

- **Educate patients**: Discuss drug testing rights, false positive risks, and the distinction between metabolite detection and impairment with patients who may be subject to workplace or criminal justice testing.
- **Distribute harm reduction tools**: Make fentanyl test strips and naloxone available at clinical sites. Integrate drug checking information into patient education materials for individuals at risk of overdose.
- **Prescribe with testing awareness**: When prescribing medications that may trigger false positive drug tests (bupropion, sertraline, tramadol, dextromethorphan, quinolone antibiotics), inform patients about cross-reactivity risks and provide documentation that can be presented to a Medical Review Officer.
- **Advocate for MAT access**: For patients in criminal justice or workplace testing contexts who have opioid use disorders, advocate for access to medication-assisted treatment and ensure that MAT medications (buprenorphine, methadone) are not treated as violations in testing programs.

### For Harm Reduction Workers

- **Expand drug checking services**: Apply for SAMHSA and state grants to fund drug checking equipment (FTIR, mass spectrometry) and trained staff. Expand beyond fentanyl test strips to provide comprehensive substance analysis.
- **Document outcomes**: Collect and publish data on drug checking results, behavior change rates, and engagement with treatment services. The evidence base for drug checking is growing but still limited; real-world outcome data from U.S. programs strengthens the case for federal funding and legal reform.
- **Train volunteers and peers**: Develop and deliver training programs for community members on fentanyl test strip use, result interpretation, and overdose response. Peer-delivered education is often more effective than professionally delivered education for people who use drugs.
- **Advocate for legal reform**: In the 11 states where fentanyl test strips remain classified as paraphernalia, organize legislative advocacy campaigns. Connect with state DPA and NHRC chapters to coordinate strategy.

### For Researchers

- **Pursue impairment technology research**: Apply for NIDA and NHTSA grants to develop and validate impairment detection technologies. The $200 million federal R&D program proposed in this roadmap would create significant funding opportunities.
- **Study testing effectiveness**: Conduct rigorous research on the relationship between workplace drug testing and safety outcomes, building on the 2007 NRC report's finding of "no evidence" for testing's effectiveness. Longitudinal studies comparing safety outcomes in tested vs. untested workplaces are needed.
- **Evaluate drug checking programs**: Conduct rigorous evaluations of U.S. drug checking programs, measuring behavior change, overdose reduction, treatment engagement, and cost-effectiveness.
- **Document racial disparities**: Research the racial impacts of drug testing across employment, welfare, criminal justice, and education contexts, including disparate false positive rates by race and the relationship between state racial demographics and testing policy adoption.

### For Labor Union Representatives

- **Negotiate fair testing provisions**: In collective bargaining agreements, negotiate for mandatory confirmatory testing before adverse action, protection for off-duty legal cannabis use (where state law permits), right to union representation during testing-related proceedings, and reasonable notice of testing policy changes.
- **Advocate for oral fluid transition**: For DOT-regulated workers, advocate for the transition from urine to oral fluid testing, which narrows the detection-impairment gap and better serves the safety interests of both workers and the public.
- **Support impairment technology development**: Engage with impairment technology developers to pilot new tools in union workplaces, ensuring that worker interests are represented in the design and deployment of these technologies.
- **Challenge discriminatory testing**: File grievances and unfair labor practice charges when testing policies disproportionately impact workers of color, workers with disabilities, or workers exercising legal rights.

## Measuring Your Impact

Track these indicators to assess whether your advocacy is making a difference:

- **Legislative progress**: Are cannabis employment protection bills advancing in your state? Are FTS legalization bills being introduced?
- **Employer policy changes**: Are local employers dropping pre-employment cannabis screening? Are any adopting impairment-based approaches?
- **Drug checking access**: Is your community served by a drug checking program? Are fentanyl test strips available and distributed?
- **Media coverage**: Is local media covering drug testing reform? Are the issues framed accurately (impairment vs. metabolite detection)?
- **Elected official positions**: Have your representatives stated positions on drug testing reform? Have they co-sponsored relevant legislation?
- **Community awareness**: Do people in your network understand the difference between metabolite testing and impairment detection? Do they know fentanyl test strips exist and save lives?

## Advocacy Toolkit

### Key Talking Points

When discussing drug testing reform with others, emphasize:

1. **Testing detects, it does not prevent**: "Urine drug testing detects metabolites from days or weeks ago, not current impairment. A positive cannabis test tells you nothing about whether a worker is fit for duty. We need impairment testing, not metabolite testing."
2. **Drug checking saves lives**: "Fentanyl test strips cost $1 and are proven to change behavior in 63% of users, reducing overdose risk. Over 107,000 Americans died from drug overdoses in 2023. Drug checking is one of the most cost-effective interventions available."
3. **Welfare testing is wasteful**: "State welfare drug testing programs find positive rates of 0.05-1.4% -- far below the general population rate of 9.4%. They cost $200-$7,000 per positive result. The money would be better spent on voluntary treatment services."
4. **Employers are already reforming**: "Amazon, Apple, Google, and hundreds of other employers have dropped pre-employment cannabis screening. They report no safety impact. The market is ahead of the law."

### Common Misconceptions to Counter

| Misconception | Reality | Source |
|---------------|---------|--------|
| "Drug testing makes workplaces safer" | The National Research Council found "no evidence" that testing reduces drug use or improves safety (2007). A 2022 meta-analysis confirmed weak evidence. | NRC, 2007; JOEM, 2022 |
| "Most welfare recipients use drugs" | Welfare testing programs find positive rates of 0.05-1.4%, far below the 9.4% general population rate. | State program reports; SAMHSA NSDUH, 2024 |
| "Fentanyl test strips enable drug use" | 63% of users who get a positive FTS result modify their behavior to reduce risk. FTS serve as engagement points for naloxone and treatment. | Peiper et al., 2019 |
| "Hair testing is the gold standard" | Hair testing has documented racial bias -- Black individuals produce 2-4x higher concentrations due to melanin binding. | Forensic Science International; Boston PD review, 2004 |
| "You can't test for impairment" | Oral fluid detects recent use (24-48 hrs). Breath-based THC and cognitive impairment testing are in development. | SAMHSA, 2024; Hound Labs; DRUID |

### Conversation Starters

When discussing drug testing reform in casual or professional settings, these framings have proven effective:

- **With employers**: "We're losing qualified candidates because of cannabis testing. Amazon dropped it for 1.5 million employees and had no safety impact. We should focus our testing budget on impairment detection, which actually makes the workplace safer."
- **With conservative-leaning audiences**: "Welfare drug testing costs more than it saves -- $200 to $7,000 per positive result, and positive rates are less than 1%. That's not a responsible use of taxpayer money. The government has no business testing people's bodily fluids to give them food assistance."
- **With safety-conscious colleagues**: "I'm not against testing for safety-sensitive jobs. But current urine testing detects cannabis from two weeks ago, not impairment right now. We need impairment testing that catches people who are actually unfit for duty -- including those impaired by alcohol, fatigue, or prescription drugs, which urine tests miss entirely."
- **With people who use drugs**: "Fentanyl test strips cost a dollar and they work. If your supply tests positive for fentanyl, you can use less, use with someone present, and keep Narcan nearby. That information can save your life."
- **With medical professionals**: "The AMA and ACOEM have both called for evidence-based testing policies. The 2007 National Research Council found no evidence that workplace testing reduces drug use. We should be applying the same evidence standards to testing policy that we apply to medical interventions."
