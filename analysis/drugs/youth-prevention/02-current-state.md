# Youth Drug Prevention: Current State

## Adolescent Substance Use Trends

The overall trajectory of adolescent substance use in the United States represents a paradox: most traditional substances are at or near historic lows, yet emerging threats -- particularly nicotine vaping and fentanyl-contaminated drugs -- have created new and potentially more dangerous patterns.

### Monitoring the Future Survey Data (2024)

The Monitoring the Future (MTF) study, conducted annually by the University of Michigan's Institute for Social Research since 1975 and funded by NIDA, is the primary national surveillance system for adolescent substance use. The 2024 survey included approximately 24,100 students across 8th, 10th, and 12th grades in 235 public and private schools.

| Substance | 8th Grade (Past-Month) | 10th Grade (Past-Month) | 12th Grade (Past-Month) | 5-Year Trend | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Alcohol | 7.3% | 16.6% | 26.4% | Declining | MTF, 2025 |
| Marijuana | 4.8% | 12.1% | 20.4% | Stable/Declining | MTF, 2025 |
| Nicotine vaping | 4.6% | 8.1% | 10.0% | Declining from 2019 peak | MTF, 2025 |
| Cigarettes | 1.1% | 1.6% | 2.2% | Historic low | MTF, 2025 |
| Any illicit drug (past year) | 8.4% | 14.8% | 30.3% | Stable | MTF, 2025 |
| Binge drinking (past 2 weeks) | 2.8% | 6.4% | 11.4% | Declining | MTF, 2025 |

### Youth Risk Behavior Surveillance System (YRBS) Data (2023)

The CDC's Youth Risk Behavior Surveillance System provides complementary data collected biennially from a nationally representative sample of high school students. The 2023 YRBS surveyed approximately 20,100 students across grades 9-12.

### Nicotine Vaping: The Defining Challenge of the 2020s

Nicotine vaping among adolescents represents the most significant shift in youth substance use patterns in decades. After explosive growth from 2017 to 2019, rates have declined but remain elevated.

| Year | 12th Grade Past-Month Vaping (Nicotine) | Source |
|---|---|---|
| 2017 | 11.0% | MTF, 2018 |
| 2018 | 20.9% | MTF, 2019 |
| 2019 | 25.5% (peak) | MTF, 2020 |
| 2020 | 16.4% (COVID-19 disruption) | MTF, 2021 |
| 2021 | 19.6% | MTF, 2022 |
| 2022 | 14.1% | MTF, 2023 |
| 2023 | 12.4% | MTF, 2024 |
| 2024 | 10.0% | MTF, 2025 |

The decline from the 2019 peak reflects a combination of FDA enforcement actions against flavored cartridge-based products (particularly JUUL), the Tobacco 21 law (effective December 2019), COVID-19 school closures, and increased awareness. However, 10.0% past-month vaping among 12th graders still represents approximately 360,000 high school seniors using nicotine vaping products monthly.

The vaping market has shifted from cartridge-based products (JUUL) to disposable devices that are cheaper, more widely available, and harder to regulate. Brands like Elf Bar, Lost Mary, and Flum have replaced JUUL as the most popular products among adolescents. These disposable devices often contain 5% nicotine salt formulations that deliver higher nicotine concentrations than earlier products, and they come in flavors (watermelon, cotton candy, blue razz) specifically appealing to young users. The FDA has issued marketing denial orders for many of these products, but enforcement is hampered by the volume of imports, largely from China, and the rapid pace at which new brands appear (FDA, 2024).

### The Fentanyl Threat to Adolescents

While overall adolescent drug use has declined, the lethality of the drug supply has increased dramatically due to fentanyl contamination. Adolescent overdose deaths, though a small share of total overdose mortality, have risen sharply.

| Metric | Value | Trend | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Adolescent overdose deaths (ages 14-18, 2023) | ~1,800 | Down 20% from 2022 peak | CDC WONDER, 2024 |
| Adolescent overdose deaths (ages 14-18, 2021) | ~1,146 | Doubled from 2019 | CDC WONDER, 2023 |
| Adolescent overdose deaths (ages 14-18, 2022) | ~2,250 (peak) | Up 96% from 2019 | CDC WONDER, 2024 |
| Counterfeit pills containing fentanyl seized by DEA (2023) | 79.3 million | Up 50% from 2022 | DEA, 2024 |
| Percentage of counterfeit pills containing lethal dose of fentanyl | 70% | Stable | DEA, 2024 |

The critical distinction is that adolescents are not dying because they are using drugs more frequently; they are dying because the drugs available are more lethal. Many adolescent fentanyl deaths involve counterfeit pills purchased through social media platforms, with victims unaware they were consuming fentanyl. A 2023 analysis published in *JAMA Pediatrics* found that 77% of adolescent overdose deaths in 2021-2022 involved fentanyl or fentanyl analogs, and that the majority of decedents had no prior documented substance use disorder -- suggesting these were not chronic users but rather experimenters who encountered a contaminated supply.

### Substance Use by Demographics

Significant disparities exist in adolescent substance use patterns across demographic groups:

| Population | Key Finding | Source |
|---|---|---|

| LGBTQ+ youth | 2-3x higher rates of substance use compared to heterosexual/cisgender peers | CDC YRBS, 2023 |
| Rural adolescents | Higher rates of methamphetamine use; comparable alcohol and marijuana rates to urban peers | SAMHSA NSDUH, 2023 |
| Black adolescents | Lower rates of alcohol and tobacco use; comparable marijuana use to white peers | MTF, 2025 |
| Hispanic adolescents | Higher rates of early alcohol initiation; comparable rates of other substance use | MTF, 2025 |
| Youth in foster care | 3-4x higher rates of substance use than peers not in foster care | National Child Welfare Study, 2023 |
| Youth experiencing homelessness | Substance use rates 2-4x higher than housed peers across all substance categories | National Runaway Safeline, 2023 |

### Perception of Risk and Availability

Adolescent perceptions of substance-related risk are a leading indicator of future use patterns. The Monitoring the Future survey tracks these perceptions alongside use data:

| Perception Metric | 12th Grade (2024) | 5-Year Trend | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Perceive "great risk" from regular marijuana use | 19.5% | Declining (was 28.6% in 2019) | MTF, 2025 |
| Perceive "great risk" from trying vaping nicotine | 23.4% | Increasing (was 17.1% in 2019) | MTF, 2025 |
| Perceive "great risk" from binge drinking (5+ drinks) | 38.2% | Stable | MTF, 2025 |
| Report marijuana "fairly easy" or "very easy" to get | 73.6% | Stable | MTF, 2025 |
| Disapprove of regular marijuana use | 52.3% | Declining (was 63.8% in 2014) | MTF, 2025 |

The declining perception of risk associated with regular marijuana use correlates with the expansion of cannabis legalization and is a concern for prevention researchers, who note that perceived risk is one of the strongest predictors of future use at the population level (Johnston et al., 2025).

## Federal Prevention Infrastructure

### Funding Overview

Federal investment in youth drug prevention is distributed across multiple agencies and programs, with SAMHSA and ONDCP serving as primary conduits.

| Program | FY2025 Funding | Administering Agency | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| SAMHSA Substance Abuse Prevention and Treatment Block Grant (prevention set-aside) | $235 million | SAMHSA/CSAP | 20% of SAPT block grant reserved for prevention activities |
| Drug-Free Communities (DFC) Support Program | $110 million | ONDCP | Grants of up to $125,000/year for community anti-drug coalitions |
| SAMHSA Strategic Prevention Framework | $117 million | SAMHSA/CSAP | State-level prevention system development |
| CDC Youth Risk Behavior Surveillance | $7 million | CDC | Biennial school-based survey on health risk behaviors |
| National Youth Anti-Drug Media Campaign | $25 million | ONDCP | Public service advertising targeting youth and parents |
| Department of Education Safe and Drug-Free Schools | $0 (defunded 2010) | N/A | Previously largest federal school-based prevention program |
| ESSA Title IV-A (prevention-eligible portion) | ~$200 million (estimated) | Dept. of Education | Flexible block grant; prevention competes with other uses |
| **Total federal prevention-specific funding** | **~$494 million** | | |

### The Prevention Funding Gap

| Metric | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Federal drug control budget (FY2025) | $46.1 billion | ONDCP, 2024 |
| Prevention share of federal drug control budget | ~2.4% ($1.1 billion including indirect) | ONDCP, 2024 |
| Treatment share | ~41% | ONDCP, 2024 |
| Law enforcement and interdiction share | ~57% | ONDCP, 2024 |
| Estimated cost of universal evidence-based school prevention | $3-5 billion annually | RAND Corporation, 2022 |
| Current federal prevention-specific spending | ~$494 million | Multiple agencies, 2024 |

The United States spends approximately 50 times more on drug enforcement than on youth prevention. Even within the demand reduction budget, prevention receives a small fraction compared to treatment -- despite evidence that prevention generates higher returns on investment.

### Prevention Spending in Historical Context

Federal prevention-specific spending has declined in real terms over the past 15 years. The Safe and Drug-Free Schools program, which funded $435 million in school-based prevention at its peak (FY2009), was eliminated in FY2010. While ESSA Title IV-A nominally replaced some of this funding, prevention must compete with school safety, mental health, and other priorities within the block grant. The Congressional Research Service (2024) estimates that inflation-adjusted federal prevention spending has declined approximately 30% since 2009, while spending on drug enforcement has increased approximately 45% over the same period.

## Program Implementation

### Evidence-Based Program Adoption

The gap between prevention science and prevention practice is the central challenge in the field. Despite decades of research identifying effective programs, adoption rates remain low.

| Implementation Metric | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Schools using any prevention curriculum | ~65% | National Center on Addiction and Substance Abuse, 2023 |
| Schools using evidence-based prevention programs | ~10% | RAND Corporation, 2022 |
| Schools using D.A.R.E. or similar non-evidence-based programs | ~35% | RAND Corporation, 2022 |
| Schools with no structured prevention programming | ~35% | National Center on Addiction and Substance Abuse, 2023 |
| Drug-Free Communities coalitions operating nationwide | ~700 | ONDCP, 2024 |
| Communities using Communities That Care model | ~500 | Social Development Research Group, 2023 |
| States requiring evidence-based prevention curricula | 6 | Education Commission of the States, 2024 |

### Evidence-Based Programs: Key Examples

| Program | Target | Setting | Evidence Rating | Effect Size | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Life Skills Training (LST) | Grades 6-9 | School | Blueprints Model Plus | 25-50% reduction in drug use | Botvin et al., 2023 |
| Strengthening Families Program (SFP 10-14) | Ages 10-14 | Family/Community | Blueprints Model | 30% reduction in substance initiation | Spoth et al., 2023 |
| Good Behavior Game (GBG) | Grades 1-2 | School | Blueprints Model | 50% reduction in drug use at age 19-21 | Kellam et al., 2008 |
| Communities That Care (CTC) | Community-wide | Community | Blueprints Model | 25-33% reduction in delinquency and drug use | Hawkins et al., 2014 |
| PROSPER | Community-wide | School + Family | Blueprints Promising | 10-20% reduction in substance initiation | Spoth et al., 2017 |
| PAX Good Behavior Game | Grades K-5 | School | SAMHSA NREPP | Significant reductions in conduct problems and later substance use | Embry, 2023 |
| Keepin' it REAL | Grades 5-9 | School | SAMHSA NREPP (moderate) | 12-15% reduction in substance use when delivered by trained teachers | Hecht & Krieger, 2023 |
| Too Good for Drugs | Grades K-12 | School | SAMHSA NREPP | Modest reductions in alcohol and marijuana initiation | Mendez Foundation, 2023 |

### Implementation Fidelity: The Quality Gap

Even when schools adopt evidence-based programs, implementation quality is often poor. Research consistently finds that program fidelity -- the degree to which a program is delivered as designed -- is the strongest predictor of whether evidence-based programs produce their intended effects in real-world settings (Fixsen et al., 2019).

| Fidelity Factor | Finding | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Programs delivered with high fidelity produce effect sizes | 2-3x larger than those with poor fidelity | Durlak & DuPre, 2008 |
| Schools reporting adequate training for prevention delivery | 22% | RAND Corporation, 2022 |
| Schools that complete all sessions of adopted prevention curricula | 40% | National Prevention Science Coalition, 2023 |
| Average teacher preparation for prevention delivery | 4 hours (vs. 20+ hours recommended) | Society for Prevention Research, 2023 |

### D.A.R.E.: Legacy and Current Status

D.A.R.E. remains the most widely recognized prevention brand in the United States, though it has undergone significant changes since its evidence failures were documented.

| D.A.R.E. Metric | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Communities with active D.A.R.E. programs | ~6,500 | D.A.R.E. America, 2024 |
| Countries with D.A.R.E. programs | 52 | D.A.R.E. America, 2024 |
| Annual cost estimate (all D.A.R.E. programs, U.S.) | ~$700 million (local + federal) | GAO estimate, 2023 |
| Current curriculum | "keepin' it REAL" (adopted 2012) | D.A.R.E. America |
| "keepin' it REAL" evidence rating | SAMHSA NREPP listed, moderate evidence | SAMHSA, 2023 |

D.A.R.E. replaced its original curriculum in 2012 with "keepin' it REAL," a program developed by researchers at Arizona State University with moderate evidence of effectiveness. However, fidelity of implementation varies widely, and the program remains largely delivered by uniformed police officers -- a delivery model that research suggests is less effective than delivery by trained teachers or prevention specialists. A 2023 implementation study found that D.A.R.E. officers delivered an average of 70% of the keepin' it REAL curriculum content, compared to 88% when delivered by trained classroom teachers (Prevention Science Implementation Center, 2023).

## Regulatory and Legal Framework

### Key Laws and Regulations

| Law/Regulation | Year | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Drug-Free Schools and Communities Act** | 1986 | Required schools receiving federal funds to implement drug prevention programs; no evidence standards |
| **Anti-Drug Abuse Act** | 1988 | Created ONDCP and expanded prevention funding |
| **Drug-Free Communities Act** | 1997 | Established community coalition grant program administered by ONDCP |
| **No Child Left Behind Act (Title IV)** | 2001 | Required "scientifically based" prevention programs; defunded in 2010 |
| **Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA, Title IV-A)** | 2015 | Restored some prevention funding; requires "evidence-based" activities but definition is broad |
| **Family Smoking Prevention and Tobacco Control Act** | 2009 | Gave FDA authority to regulate tobacco products, later extended to e-cigarettes |
| **Tobacco 21 (T21)** | 2019 | Raised federal minimum age for tobacco/nicotine products from 18 to 21 |
| **SUPPORT for Patients and Communities Act** | 2018 | Expanded prevention provisions within broader opioid legislation |

### ESSA Title IV-A: The Current School Prevention Framework

The Every Student Succeeds Act provides the primary current mechanism for federal prevention funding in schools. Title IV-A (Student Support and Academic Enrichment Grants) authorizes funds for "safe and healthy students" activities, including drug prevention. However, the program is structured as a flexible block grant, and districts are not required to spend any specific amount on prevention. In FY2024, Title IV-A was funded at $1.29 billion, but substance abuse prevention competes with school safety, mental health, arts education, and educational technology for these funds.

### State-Level Prevention Requirements

State requirements for school-based drug prevention vary considerably:

| Requirement Category | Number of States | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Mandate drug education in schools | 39 | Most require some form of substance education but do not specify curricula |
| Require evidence-based prevention curricula | 6 | Washington, Iowa, Pennsylvania, Colorado, Vermont, Connecticut |
| Dedicate cannabis tax revenue to youth prevention | 8 | Colorado ($10M/yr), Washington ($8M/yr), Oregon ($5M/yr), others with smaller allocations |
| Have state prevention certification standards | 47 | Standards vary widely; many lack enforcement mechanisms |
| Fund state-level prevention coordinators | 42 | Positions often understaffed; 11 states have no dedicated prevention coordinator |

## Recent Trends

### Positive Developments (2019-2024)

- Adolescent cigarette smoking has declined to historic lows (2.2% past-month among 12th graders, down from 36.5% in 1997)
- Adolescent nicotine vaping has declined from its 2019 peak of 25.5% to 10.0% among 12th graders
- Adolescent alcohol use continues its long-term decline (26.4% past-month among 12th graders, down from 52.7% in 1997)
- Several states have directed cannabis tax revenue toward evidence-based prevention
- The Tobacco 21 law has reduced adolescent access to nicotine products
- FDA's "Real Cost" campaign prevented an estimated 350,000-590,000 youth from initiating smoking (FDA, 2022)
- The DEA's "One Pill Can Kill" campaign has increased fentanyl awareness among parents and youth
- Growing number of states adopting evidence-based prevention requirements

### Concerning Developments (2019-2024)

- Adolescent overdose deaths surged from ~600 (2019) to ~2,250 (2022) before declining to ~1,800 (2023), driven by fentanyl contamination
- Social media platforms (Snapchat, Instagram, TikTok) have become drug distribution channels, with adolescents purchasing counterfeit pills online
- Youth mental health crisis (43% of high school students reporting persistent sadness/hopelessness in 2023, per CDC YRBS) increases substance use vulnerability
- Federal prevention funding has remained flat in real terms for over a decade while enforcement spending has increased
- The defunding of the Safe and Drug-Free Schools program in 2010 was never replaced with an equivalent evidence-based alternative
- Declining adolescent perception of risk from regular marijuana use (19.5% perceive "great risk" in 2024, down from 28.6% in 2019) as legalization expands
- Prevention workforce shortages: an estimated 10,000 additional Certified Prevention Specialists are needed (SAMHSA, 2023)
- COVID-19 disruption to prevention programming has had lasting effects on implementation in many districts

## Emerging Challenges

### Social Media and Drug Normalization

Social media platforms present unprecedented challenges for youth prevention. Adolescents encounter substance-related content through algorithmic promotion, influencer marketing (particularly for alcohol and cannabis brands), and peer-to-peer communication that normalizes use. DEA and CDC investigations have documented numerous adolescent fentanyl deaths linked to pills purchased through Snapchat, Instagram, and other platforms. Prevention programs developed before the smartphone era lack frameworks for addressing these digital-age risks.

Research from the Stanford Digital Health Lab (2023) found that adolescents who reported frequent exposure to substance-related content on social media were 2.7 times more likely to initiate use within the following 12 months compared to those with limited exposure. The algorithmic amplification of substance-related content -- where engaging with one post about drug use leads to increasingly more such content in a user's feed -- creates echo chambers that can shift perceived norms about substance use among vulnerable adolescents.

### Novel Psychoactive Substances

The rapid proliferation of novel psychoactive substances -- including synthetic cannabinoids, designer benzodiazepines, and emerging hallucinogens -- outpaces prevention messaging and regulatory responses. Adolescents may encounter substances with unknown risk profiles before prevention curricula can address them. The DEA's Emerging Threats Report (2024) identified over 30 new psychoactive substances entering the U.S. market annually, most originating from laboratories in China and Mexico.

### Cannabis Legalization and Youth Messaging

As more states legalize recreational cannabis, prevention programs face the challenge of maintaining credible anti-use messaging for adolescents within a legal framework that permits adult use. Research from Colorado, Washington, and Oregon provides early evidence on post-legalization adolescent use trends, but the messaging challenge remains unresolved. The Monitoring the Future data shows declining perceived risk of marijuana among adolescents nationally, though actual use rates in legalization states have not increased significantly -- suggesting that normalization of attitudes may precede changes in behavior (Cerdá et al., 2023).

### Integration with Mental Health

The adolescent mental health crisis and substance use vulnerability are deeply intertwined. Prevention programs increasingly need to address co-occurring mental health and substance use risks, requiring integration with school-based mental health services that are themselves severely underfunded. The CDC's 2023 YRBS data showing that 43% of high school students report persistent feelings of sadness or hopelessness underscores the scope of the challenge. Research consistently finds that depression, anxiety, and trauma exposure are among the strongest predictors of adolescent substance use initiation (SAMHSA, 2023), yet fewer than 20% of prevention programs incorporate mental health screening or intervention components (National Prevention Science Coalition, 2023).

### Adolescent SBIRT Implementation Gap

The American Academy of Pediatrics has recommended universal substance use screening for adolescents at well-child visits since 2011, using validated tools like the CRAFFT screening instrument. Despite this recommendation, implementation remains below 50% in pediatric practice. Barriers include time constraints in clinical encounters (the average well-child visit is 15-20 minutes), lack of provider training in brief motivational interviewing, limited referral resources for adolescents identified as at-risk, and variation in state minor consent laws that create confusion about confidentiality (AAP, 2023).
