# Youth Drug Prevention: What You Can Do

Youth drug prevention is an area where individual, community, and political action can have outsized impact. Because the current system wastes billions on ineffective programs while proven alternatives are underdeployed, informed advocacy can redirect resources toward approaches that actually protect young people. The fentanyl crisis has created urgent bipartisan motivation for reform that citizens can leverage.

---

## Individual Actions

### Learn and Stay Informed

- Read the Monitoring the Future annual survey results (released each January at <https://monitoringthefuture.org>) to understand current adolescent substance use trends
- Follow the Blueprints for Healthy Youth Development registry (<https://www.blueprintsprograms.org>) to understand which prevention programs have strong evidence
- Read NIDA's "Principles of Substance Abuse Prevention for Early Childhood" and "Preventing Drug Use Among Children and Adolescents" research-based guides (<https://nida.nih.gov/research-topics/prevention>)
- Subscribe to the Society for Prevention Research newsletter for updates on prevention science (<https://www.preventionresearch.org>)
- Review the Washington State Institute for Public Policy's benefit-cost database (<https://www.wsipp.wa.gov/BenefitCost>) to understand the economic case for evidence-based prevention
- Follow the CDC's Youth Risk Behavior Surveillance System data releases (<https://www.cdc.gov/yrbs/>) for comprehensive adolescent health risk data

### Make Personal Changes

- **If you are a parent**: Learn about evidence-based family communication strategies. The Strengthening Families Program and the "Parents Who Host Lose the Most" campaign provide practical tools for discussing substance use with children
- **Talk early and honestly**: Research shows that parent-child conversations about drugs are most effective when they begin before age 10, are ongoing rather than one-time events, and acknowledge the reality of peer pressure while building refusal skills. The Partnership to End Addiction provides conversation guides for different developmental stages at <https://drugfree.org>
- **Monitor without micromanaging**: Parental monitoring (knowing where your child is, who they are with, and what they are doing) is one of the strongest protective factors against adolescent substance use (SAMHSA, 2023). Research distinguishes between monitoring (which is protective) and surveillance (which can undermine trust and adolescent autonomy)
- **Secure medications**: Store prescription medications securely and dispose of unused medications through DEA Take Back events or pharmacy disposal programs. Prescription drug diversion from family medicine cabinets remains a pathway to adolescent opioid use
- **Discuss fentanyl risks explicitly**: Ensure your adolescent knows that any pill not prescribed by their doctor may contain lethal fentanyl, including pills that look like legitimate pharmaceuticals. This conversation is not normalization -- it is lifesaving emergency preparedness
- **Monitor digital environments**: Be aware of your adolescent's social media activity. Drug sales occur through Snapchat, Instagram, and other platforms. While respecting adolescent privacy, establish open communication about online risks. The DEA's "One Pill Can Kill" campaign at <https://www.dea.gov/onepill> provides discussion tools
- **Model healthy behavior**: Parental substance use -- including alcohol consumption patterns -- is one of the strongest predictors of adolescent substance use. Modeling responsible behavior has more impact than any verbal message
- **Learn about naloxone**: Consider having naloxone (available over-the-counter as Narcan at pharmacies for $44.99) in your home, especially if your adolescent attends parties or social events. Knowing how to respond to an overdose could save a life
- **Build protective factors**: Invest in your adolescent's participation in organized activities -- sports, arts, music, volunteering. Research from Iceland's Planet Youth model demonstrates that structured recreational activities are among the strongest protective factors against substance use

### Use Your Voice

- **Contact Your Representatives**: Urge them to support increased funding for evidence-based prevention and evidence standards for federal prevention grants
- **Submit Public Comments**: When SAMHSA, FDA, or the Department of Education issue proposed rules related to prevention programming, submit comments supporting evidence-based requirements. Federal Register notices are posted at <https://www.regulations.gov>
- **Write Letters to the Editor**: When local media covers youth substance use, emphasize the importance of evidence-based prevention and the failure of legacy programs
- **Share Information**: Share the evidence on D.A.R.E.'s historical ineffectiveness and the availability of proven alternatives through school parent networks and social media
- **Testify at school board meetings**: Your local school board selects prevention curricula. Public testimony from informed parents can influence these decisions

## Community Actions

### Organize Locally

- **Attend school board meetings**: Ask your school district what prevention curriculum it uses and whether it is rated as effective by the Blueprints registry or SAMHSA. If the district uses D.A.R.E. or another non-evidence-based program, request a transition to an evidence-based alternative. Bring copies of the Blueprints program list and the WSIPP benefit-cost data to support your case.
- **Form a parent prevention coalition**: Connect with other parents interested in evidence-based prevention. Organizations like the Partnership to End Addiction (<https://drugfree.org>) provide resources for parent-led action
- **Support or start a Drug-Free Community coalition**: If your community has a DFC coalition, volunteer. If it does not, explore applying for an ONDCP Drug-Free Communities grant (<https://www.cadca.org>). DFC grants provide up to $125,000/year for community coalitions.
- **Advocate for naloxone in schools**: Request that your school district equip school nurses with naloxone and implement fentanyl awareness education. The National Association of School Nurses provides implementation guidance at <https://www.nasn.org>
- **Engage local pediatricians**: Ask your child's pediatrician whether they conduct SBIRT screening at well-child visits. If not, share the AAP recommendation for universal adolescent screening beginning at age 12. The CRAFFT screening tool is free at <https://crafft.org>
- **Support positive youth development**: Volunteer with or donate to organizations that provide structured recreational activities, mentoring, and afterschool programs for youth. The Iceland Planet Youth model demonstrates that investment in organized activities is among the most effective prevention strategies.

### Build Coalitions

- Connect evidence-based prevention advocates with fiscal conservatives who support accountability for public spending. The $18 return per $1 invested in evidence-based prevention is a message that resonates across political perspectives.
- Partner with local pediatricians and family physicians to advocate for universal adolescent SBIRT (Screening, Brief Intervention, and Referral to Treatment)
- Engage faith communities, which often support youth protection goals even when they differ on specific prevention approaches. Frame fentanyl safety education as consistent with protecting the sanctity of life.
- Reach out to local law enforcement leaders who may support transition from D.A.R.E. to evidence-based programs while maintaining community engagement roles
- Connect with school counselors and psychologists who understand the evidence base and may be allies for reform but lack the political support to advocate independently
- Engage local business leaders who understand the economic costs of substance use disorders on workforce productivity and healthcare costs

### Host Community Events

- **Prevention science presentations**: Invite a prevention researcher from a local university to present the evidence on what works in youth prevention at a PTA meeting, community center, or faith institution
- **Fentanyl awareness events**: Partner with local health departments and law enforcement to host fentanyl awareness events for parents and youth, including naloxone training
- **Film screenings**: Screen documentaries on the opioid crisis (e.g., "The Crime of the Century," "Dopesick") followed by discussions of local prevention needs
- **Youth listening sessions**: Create opportunities for adolescents to share their perspectives on substance use risks and prevention programs. Youth voices can powerfully inform community prevention strategy and identify credibility gaps in current messaging

## Political Actions

### Contact Elected Officials

**When to Contact**:

- During appropriations season (February-September) when prevention funding levels are set
- When prevention-related legislation is introduced or moving through committee
- After incidents of adolescent overdose deaths in your community
- When your school district is selecting or reviewing prevention curricula
- During ESSA reauthorization discussions, to advocate for stronger prevention evidence requirements
- When state legislatures consider cannabis legalization or regulation bills, to advocate for youth prevention funding provisions

**What to Say - Template Email**:

```text
Subject: Support Evidence-Based Youth Drug Prevention Funding

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

I am a constituent from [City], and I am writing about youth drug
prevention. As a [parent/educator/health professional/concerned citizen],
I believe our children deserve prevention programs that actually work.

Research consistently shows that programs like Life Skills Training, the
Strengthening Families Program, and Communities That Care reduce
adolescent substance use by 25-50%. Yet only about 10% of American
schools implement evidence-based prevention programs. Meanwhile, the
federal government spends less than 3% of the drug control budget on
prevention, and much of that funding goes to programs with no evidence
of effectiveness.

With adolescent overdose deaths driven by fentanyl contamination,
effective prevention has never been more urgent. I urge you to:

1. Support increased appropriations for evidence-based youth prevention
2. Require that federal prevention funds flow to programs with
   demonstrated effectiveness
3. Support fentanyl awareness education in all schools
4. Fund prevention workforce development to ensure programs are
   delivered with quality

Every dollar invested in evidence-based prevention returns $18 in
avoided future costs. This is smart fiscal policy and lifesaving
public health policy.

Thank you for your service and consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your City, State]
```

**What to Say - Phone Call Script**:

```text
"Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [City]. I'm
calling about youth drug prevention funding. I'd like the
[Representative/Senator] to support evidence-based prevention programs
and increased funding for proven approaches like Life Skills Training
and Communities That Care. Only 10% of schools use programs that
actually work, and adolescent fentanyl deaths make reform urgent.
Can you please note my position? Thank you."
```

### Support Legislation

Current or proposed legislation to support:

- **Evidence-Based Youth Prevention Act** (proposed): Would establish federal evidence standards for all prevention funding and increase appropriations -- contact your representatives to encourage introduction and co-sponsorship
- **ESSA Title IV-A Strengthening**: Advocate for strengthened evidence requirements and protected prevention funding within the next ESSA reauthorization
- **Drug-Free Communities Act Reauthorization**: Support expanded authorization and appropriation when the DFC Act comes up for reauthorization
- **SBIRT Coverage Act** (proposed): Would require all insurers to cover adolescent substance use screening
- **State-level cannabis legalization bills**: If your state is considering legalization, advocate for provisions dedicating a percentage of cannabis tax revenue to evidence-based youth prevention (Colorado's model dedicates $10 million annually)
- **State-level evidence requirements**: Advocate for state legislation requiring that school-based prevention programs meet evidence standards (only 6 states currently have such requirements)

### Electoral Engagement

**Questions to Ask Candidates**:

1. "What is your position on requiring evidence-based standards for federally funded youth drug prevention programs?"
2. "Do you support increasing federal prevention funding from the current ~$494 million to at least $1.5 billion annually?"
3. "How would you address fentanyl awareness education for adolescents in schools?"
4. "Do you support transitioning from legacy prevention programs like D.A.R.E. to programs with demonstrated effectiveness?"
5. "What is your position on universal adolescent substance use screening (SBIRT) in pediatric settings?"
6. "Do you support holding social media platforms accountable for facilitating drug sales to minors?"

**Voting Considerations**:

- Look for candidates who distinguish between prevention programs that are popular and programs that are effective
- Support candidates who commit to evidence-based spending accountability, regardless of party
- Key committees: Senate HELP Committee, House Energy and Commerce Committee, and Appropriations subcommittees for Labor-HHS-Education and Commerce-Justice-Science
- At the state level, pay attention to education committee members and health committee members who control school-based prevention and public health prevention policy

## Professional Actions

### For Educators

- Advocate within your school and district for evidence-based prevention curricula. Seek training in programs like Life Skills Training or PAX Good Behavior Game.
- Join the National Prevention Science Coalition (<https://www.npscoalition.org>) and the Society for Prevention Research to stay current on prevention evidence.
- If your school uses D.A.R.E. or a non-evidence-based program, initiate conversations with administration about transitioning to evidence-rated alternatives. Bring specific program recommendations with cost estimates and evidence ratings from Blueprints.
- Integrate substance use prevention concepts into existing academic content where appropriate. Health education, biology, and social studies classes can reinforce prevention messages.
- Build classroom environments that promote protective factors -- positive peer relationships, school bonding, academic engagement -- which reduce substance use risk even without specific prevention curricula.

### For School Counselors

- Implement the CRAFFT screening tool for adolescent substance use risk assessment. The tool is free, validated, and takes 5 minutes to administer. Access it at <https://crafft.org>.
- Connect at-risk students with early intervention services. Develop and maintain a referral directory of adolescent substance use treatment providers in your community.
- Advocate for caseload ratios that allow prevention work (250:1 recommended vs. 385:1 average). Present evidence to administration that lower ratios enable prevention activities that reduce behavioral problems and improve school climate.
- Seek training in motivational interviewing for adolescents, which provides the skills for the Brief Intervention component of SBIRT.
- Coordinate with school nurses on naloxone protocols and overdose response planning.

### For Pediatricians and Family Physicians

- Implement universal SBIRT for adolescents at well-child visits beginning at age 12. Use the CRAFFT screening instrument. Bill for SBIRT services using existing Medicaid/insurance codes (CPT codes 99408, 99409; HCPCS codes H0049, H0050).
- Integrate CRAFFT screening into your electronic health record system to prompt screening at every adolescent visit.
- Develop a referral network for adolescents identified as at-risk, including school-based services, outpatient treatment, and family therapy options.
- Join the American Academy of Pediatrics advocacy for universal screening and prevention funding at <https://www.aap.org>.
- Discuss fentanyl risks and naloxone availability with adolescent patients and their parents as a routine component of anticipatory guidance.

### For Public Health Professionals

- Support community coalition development; provide technical assistance for evidence-based program implementation; contribute to surveillance data on adolescent substance use trends.
- Advocate within your agency for evidence-based prevention funding standards and outcome measurement.
- Conduct or support community needs assessments using the CTC Youth Survey or equivalent tools.
- Bridge the gap between prevention researchers and community practitioners by translating research findings into actionable guidance.

### For Researchers

- Prioritize translational research that bridges the science-to-practice gap. Publish findings in formats accessible to practitioners and policymakers, not only academic journals.
- Engage in policy advocacy through the Society for Prevention Research. Researchers who can communicate evidence to policymakers are essential for reform.
- Develop and validate digital prevention interventions that reach adolescents through their preferred communication channels.
- Study the implementation challenges that prevent evidence-based programs from achieving their trial-level effects in real-world settings.
- Contribute to the evidence base for emerging prevention needs, including fentanyl safety education, social media risk factors, and cannabis legalization impacts on youth.

## Advocacy Toolkit

### Key Talking Points

When discussing youth drug prevention with others, emphasize:

1. **The evidence gap**: "Only about 10% of American schools use drug prevention programs that research shows actually work. We're spending billions on programs that feel good but don't reduce drug use."
2. **The return on investment**: "Every dollar invested in evidence-based prevention returns $18 in avoided healthcare, criminal justice, and productivity costs. This is one of the best investments in public policy."
3. **The fentanyl urgency**: "Adolescent overdose deaths more than doubled between 2019 and 2022 because of fentanyl-contaminated pills. Our kids need prevention education that addresses this lethal threat, not curricula designed for the 1980s."
4. **The brain science**: "Neuroscience shows that the adolescent brain is uniquely vulnerable to substance use because the parts that seek rewards develop faster than the parts that control impulses. Prevention that works with this biology is more effective than programs that rely on willpower alone."
5. **The funding disparity**: "The federal government spends 57% of the drug budget on law enforcement and less than 3% on prevention. Redirecting even a small portion would transform youth outcomes."

### Common Misconceptions to Counter

| Misconception | Reality | Source |
|---------------|---------|--------|
| "D.A.R.E. works -- my kids went through it and they're fine" | Multiple rigorous studies including a 10-year follow-up found D.A.R.E. had no statistically significant effect on drug use. Individual outcomes cannot be attributed to the program. | Lynam et al., 1999; Cochrane Review, 2009 |
| "Scare tactics keep kids off drugs" | Research shows that fear-based approaches have minimal effect on behavior and may reduce program credibility with adolescents who observe that scare claims are exaggerated | Faggiano et al., 2014; Cochrane Review |
| "Drug testing in schools prevents drug use" | The most rigorous study (NIDA-funded RCT, 2003) found no significant deterrent effect. The AAP and APHA oppose school-based random drug testing. | Yamaguchi et al., 2003; AAP Policy, 2015 |
| "Teaching kids about drug risks normalizes drug use" | Evidence-based programs that discuss risks realistically reduce substance use more effectively than abstinence-only approaches. Information is not permission. | Faggiano et al., 2014 |
| "Prevention is less important than enforcement" | The federal government spends ~57% of the drug budget on enforcement and ~2.4% on prevention. Evidence shows prevention generates far higher returns per dollar. | ONDCP, 2024; RAND, 2022 |
| "Kids today use more drugs than ever" | Most traditional substance use among adolescents is at or near historic lows. The danger is that the drug supply is more lethal (fentanyl), not that use rates are higher. | Monitoring the Future, 2025 |
| "Legalization has caused youth marijuana use to increase" | Data from legalization states generally shows stable or slightly declining youth marijuana use, though risk perception has declined. | Cerdá et al., 2020; MTF, 2025 |
