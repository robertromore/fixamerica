# Youth Drug Prevention: Stakeholders

The youth drug prevention system is shaped by the interactions of several key groups, each with distinct interests, resources, and perspectives on what effective prevention looks like. The fundamental tension is between those who prioritize scientific evidence and those who prioritize political visibility, institutional preservation, or cultural values.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Adolescents (ages 10-18) | Direct recipients of prevention programming; affected by substance use outcomes | ~42 million (Census, 2023) | Receiving credible, relevant information; not being patronized or scared; access to help when needed |
| Parents and families | Primary influence on adolescent substance attitudes; affected by children's outcomes | ~65 million parents of children under 18 | Confidence that schools address substance risks; practical tools for family conversations; assurance of child safety |
| Young adults (18-25) | Highest-risk age group for substance use escalation; affected by quality of earlier prevention | ~31 million (Census, 2023) | Continuity of prevention messaging into college/early adulthood; harm reduction information; treatment access |
| Adolescents in high-risk environments | Youth exposed to ACEs, poverty, community violence, parental substance use | ~10 million (estimated, SAMHSA, 2023) | Targeted prevention and early intervention; trauma-informed programming; connection to support services |
| Families affected by adolescent substance use | Parents, siblings of youth with substance use disorders | ~4 million families (SAMHSA, 2023) | Early identification; family-based intervention; support services; destigmatization |

### Secondary Stakeholders

- **K-12 educators and school staff**: 3.7 million teachers (NCES, 2023) who are expected to deliver or facilitate prevention programming, often without adequate training, time, or resources. Teachers report that prevention competes with academic content for limited classroom time, and many feel unprepared to deliver curricula addressing substance use.
- **School counselors**: 119,000 school counselors (ASCA, 2023) with an average caseload of 385 students, far exceeding the recommended 250:1 ratio, limiting capacity for prevention work. School counselors are often the de facto prevention coordinators in their buildings but lack the time and specialized training for this role.
- **Pediatricians and family physicians**: 40,000+ pediatricians and family doctors who could conduct Screening, Brief Intervention, and Referral to Treatment (SBIRT) but face time and training barriers. The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends universal substance use screening starting at age 12, but compliance remains below 50%.
- **School nurses**: 78,000 school nurses (NASN, 2023) who serve as first responders for substance-related health events and increasingly carry naloxone. Many school nurses lack training in adolescent substance use assessment and intervention.
- **College and university administrators**: Overseeing campus prevention for the highest-risk age cohort; affected by enrollment, retention, and campus safety. Campus prevention is often focused on alcohol through mandated online programs (AlcoholEdu, EVERFI) whose effectiveness is debated.
- **Communities with high substance use burden**: Rural Appalachian, Native American, and economically distressed urban communities where adolescent substance use rates are highest and prevention resources are scarcest

### Populations with Disproportionate Impact

| Population | Disproportionate Impact | Key Factors | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Native American/Alaska Native youth | 2.5x national average for past-year illicit drug use; highest overdose death rates | Historical trauma; limited prevention infrastructure on reservations; IHS underfunding | SAMHSA NSDUH, 2023; IHS, 2023 |
| LGBTQ+ youth | 2-3x higher rates of substance use | Minority stress; discrimination; family rejection; limited culturally competent prevention | CDC YRBS, 2023 |
| Youth in foster care | 3-4x higher substance use rates | ACEs; placement instability; disrupted family bonds; inconsistent access to services | National Child Welfare Study, 2023 |
| Youth experiencing homelessness | 2-4x higher rates across all categories | Survival needs; trauma exposure; lack of stable school enrollment; limited adult supervision | National Runaway Safeline, 2023 |
| Youth in juvenile justice system | 50-75% meet criteria for at least one SUD | ACEs; co-occurring mental health disorders; peer exposure; limited prevention before system contact | OJJDP, 2023 |
| Rural adolescents | Higher methamphetamine rates; limited treatment access | Fewer prevention providers; greater substance availability; limited recreational alternatives | SAMHSA NSDUH, 2023 |

## Who Has Power

### Decision Makers

| Actor | Role | Authority | Resources | Influence Level |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| U.S. Congress | Authorizes and funds prevention programs | Legislative authority; appropriations power | ~$494 million in direct prevention funding (FY2025) | High |
| SAMHSA/Center for Substance Abuse Prevention (CSAP) | Administers federal prevention grants and technical assistance | Grant administration; evidence standards; technical assistance | $352 million (prevention set-aside + strategic prevention framework, FY2025) | High |
| ONDCP | Coordinates national drug policy including prevention | Policy coordination; Drug-Free Communities program | $110 million (DFC grants, FY2025); policy authority | High |
| State education agencies | Set curriculum standards and administer education funding | Curriculum authority; funding distribution; teacher certification | Varies by state; collectively billions in education funding | High |
| Local school boards and superintendents | Select prevention curricula and allocate school budgets | Curriculum selection; budget allocation; policy implementation | Local education budgets; direct programming decisions | High |
| FDA Center for Tobacco Products | Regulates tobacco and nicotine products including vaping devices | Product approval/denial; marketing restrictions; enforcement | $712 million (FY2024, funded by tobacco user fees) | Medium-High |
| State governors and legislatures | Set state prevention policy, funding, and requirements | Legislation; budget authority; executive action | State budgets; regulatory authority | Medium-High |
| NIDA (National Institute on Drug Abuse) | Primary federal funder of prevention research | Research funding; scientific guidance; survey administration | $1.6 billion total budget (FY2025); funds MTF survey | Medium |

### Key Organizations and Institutions

- **NIDA (National Institute on Drug Abuse)**: The primary federal funder of prevention research, with a budget of $1.6 billion (FY2025). NIDA funds the Monitoring the Future survey and supports prevention science research, but does not directly administer prevention programs. NIDA's research has been essential in documenting both the ineffectiveness of legacy programs and the effectiveness of evidence-based alternatives.
- **Blueprints for Healthy Youth Development (University of Colorado)**: The most rigorous independent registry of evidence-based prevention programs, rating programs on research quality and replication evidence. Widely recognized as the gold standard for prevention evidence ratings. Blueprints currently lists 17 "Model" and "Model Plus" programs and over 80 "Promising" programs.
- **Society for Prevention Research (SPR)**: The leading professional organization for prevention scientists, representing approximately 1,500 researchers who generate the evidence base for prevention policy. SPR issues standards for prevention research and advocates for evidence-based policy. SPR's annual meeting is the primary venue for presenting prevention research findings.
- **D.A.R.E. America**: Nonprofit organization administering the D.A.R.E. program in approximately 6,500 communities. Maintains significant political relationships with law enforcement organizations and Congressional supporters. D.A.R.E. America's annual budget is approximately $12 million (IRS Form 990, 2023), but the total economic footprint including local police department costs is estimated at $700 million.
- **Community Anti-Drug Coalitions of America (CADCA)**: National organization representing over 5,000 community coalitions. CADCA provides training, advocacy, and technical assistance and is a key intermediary between ONDCP and local prevention efforts. CADCA's National Leadership Forum convenes annually with 3,000+ participants and is a major networking event for prevention professionals.
- **Truth Initiative**: Nonprofit organization dedicated to tobacco and nicotine prevention, known for the "truth" anti-smoking campaign that contributed to significant declines in youth smoking. Truth Initiative has expanded its focus to e-cigarettes and vaping, producing educational content and advocacy targeting adolescent nicotine use. Annual budget approximately $140 million (2023), funded primarily by the 1998 Master Settlement Agreement.
- **Washington State Institute for Public Policy (WSIPP)**: Conducts the most widely cited benefit-cost analyses of prevention programs. WSIPP's analyses provide the $18 return-per-dollar-invested figure frequently used to advocate for evidence-based prevention funding. Their regularly updated benefit-cost database is a key resource for policymakers evaluating prevention investments.

### Influencers

- **Prevention science researchers**: Academics at universities including the University of Washington (Social Development Research Group), Penn State (Prevention Research Center), Iowa State, and University of Colorado who develop, test, and advocate for evidence-based programs. Key figures include J. David Hawkins and Richard Catalano (Communities That Care), Gilbert Botvin (Life Skills Training), Richard Spoth (PROSPER), and Sheppard Kellam (Good Behavior Game, deceased 2019).
- **E-cigarette and tobacco industry**: Companies including JUUL (now restructured), Reynolds American, Altria, and numerous disposable vape manufacturers who market products that drive adolescent nicotine use; collectively spend $8.6 billion/year on marketing (FTC, 2023). The industry's lobbying operations focus on opposing flavor bans, marketing restrictions, and tax increases that would reduce youth access.
- **Social media companies**: Meta (Instagram, Facebook), Snap (Snapchat), ByteDance (TikTok), and Alphabet (YouTube) whose platforms shape adolescent substance-related norms and have been used for illicit drug sales targeting youth. Platform policies prohibiting drug sales exist but are inconsistently enforced, particularly on encrypted messaging features.
- **Pharmaceutical industry**: Companies that manufacture addiction treatment medications (buprenorphine, naltrexone) and whose marketing practices contributed to the opioid epidemic that now drives adolescent fentanyl exposure
- **Media and entertainment industry**: Television, film, music, and streaming platforms that portray substance use; alcohol and cannabis industry advertising that reaches adolescents despite age restrictions
- **Parent advocacy organizations**: Partnership to End Addiction (formerly Partnership for a Drug-Free America), Parents Against Vaping E-cigarettes (PAVe), and National PTA chapters that mobilize parent voices on prevention policy. These organizations vary in their commitment to evidence-based approaches.

## Stakeholder Interests and Alignment

### Aligned Interests (Potential Coalition Partners)

| Coalition | Shared Interest | Strength |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| Prevention scientists + SAMHSA/CSAP + Blueprints | Scaling evidence-based programs nationwide | Strong -- shared evidence framework and policy goals |
| Parents + schools + pediatricians | Protecting adolescent health; practical prevention tools | Strong -- aligned goals despite different capacities |
| Public health advocates + fiscal conservatives | Demonstrating prevention ROI to justify investment; reducing downstream treatment and enforcement costs | Moderate -- agreed on cost-effectiveness but different framing |
| Rural communities + Native communities + urban high-need areas | Targeted prevention resources for high-risk populations | Moderate -- shared needs but different contexts and political relationships |
| School counselors + mental health advocates + prevention advocates | Integrating prevention with school-based mental health services | Strong -- increasing recognition of co-occurring risks |
| Truth Initiative + PAVe + Campaign for Tobacco-Free Kids | Reducing youth nicotine/vaping initiation through regulation and education | Strong -- aligned on vaping policy despite different organizational approaches |
| Washington State Institute for Public Policy + budget hawks | Demonstrating that prevention investment reduces long-term government costs | Moderate -- WSIPP data provides evidence, but budgetary politics often override cost-benefit evidence |

### Conflicting Interests

- **D.A.R.E./law enforcement vs. prevention scientists**: D.A.R.E.'s institutional survival depends on continued funding and school access, which evidence-based reform would redirect. Law enforcement organizations view school-based prevention roles as valuable community engagement, while researchers argue that uniformed officer delivery is less effective than trained educator delivery.
- **Tobacco/vaping industry vs. public health**: The e-cigarette industry profits from adolescent nicotine initiation (JUUL's growth was driven disproportionately by youth users). Public health advocates seek stricter regulation, flavor bans, and marketing restrictions that directly reduce industry revenue.
- **Local control advocates vs. evidence standards advocates**: School boards and state legislators who prioritize local curricular autonomy resist federal evidence requirements. Prevention scientists argue that public funds should only support programs with demonstrated effectiveness.
- **Abstinence-focused groups vs. harm reduction advocates**: Religious and conservative organizations insist that prevention messaging must promote complete abstinence from all substances. Harm reduction advocates argue that realistic risk information (including safer use messages for those who will experiment) is more effective and could save lives in the fentanyl era.
- **Cannabis industry vs. youth prevention advocates**: The legal cannabis industry seeks to expand its market while maintaining nominal support for preventing youth use. Prevention advocates argue that industry marketing, product design, and normalization efforts undermine youth prevention even when not directly targeting minors.

### The Prevention-Enforcement Triangle

A self-reinforcing dynamic connects law enforcement agencies, prevention program vendors, and school administrators:

- **Law enforcement** benefits from school-based prevention roles (D.A.R.E. officer positions, community engagement, overtime funding)
- **Prevention vendors** sell curricula and training to schools without outcome accountability
- **School administrators** satisfy parental and political demands for "doing something" with minimal effort

This triangle is sustained by the absence of outcome-based accountability. No party in this relationship has a strong incentive to evaluate whether prevention activities actually reduce substance use. Breaking this dynamic requires external accountability mechanisms -- federal evidence standards, independent outcome measurement, and public reporting of prevention spending effectiveness.

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on prevention policy:

- **Lobbying**: D.A.R.E. America and the International Association of Chiefs of Police (IACP) lobby Congress to maintain D.A.R.E. funding. CADCA advocates for Drug-Free Communities appropriations. The Society for Prevention Research advocates for evidence-based funding requirements. The e-cigarette industry spends $5+ million annually on federal lobbying (OpenSecrets, 2024).
- **Campaign Contributions**: The tobacco/nicotine industry contributed $4.3 million to federal candidates in the 2024 election cycle (OpenSecrets, 2024). Law enforcement unions contribute significantly to state and local candidates who control school policies.
- **Expertise/Information Control**: Prevention scientists control the evidence base through peer-reviewed research and registries. D.A.R.E. and other legacy programs counter with parent satisfaction surveys and anecdotal testimony. The asymmetry between scientific evidence (complex, nuanced, published in academic journals) and political messaging (simple, emotional, delivered through media) consistently favors legacy programs in public discourse.
- **Public Advocacy**: Organizations like the Truth Initiative (anti-tobacco/vaping) and Parents Against Vaping E-cigarettes (PAVe) use media campaigns and grassroots organizing. The "One Pill Can Kill" campaign (DEA, launched 2021) targets fentanyl awareness among parents and youth.
- **Revolving Door**: Limited in prevention compared to other policy areas, but former ONDCP officials have joined advocacy organizations and consulting firms that advise on prevention policy.
- **Litigation**: Tobacco/vaping industry litigation against FDA regulations (flavor bans, marketing restrictions) delays enforcement actions that would protect youth. School districts have begun filing lawsuits against social media companies for contributing to youth mental health and substance use crises, potentially opening a new legal avenue for prevention funding through settlements.
- **Media Framing**: Local news coverage of adolescent overdose deaths creates pressure for action, but the resulting policy response is often reactive (more enforcement, drug testing) rather than evidence-based (scaling proven prevention programs). Prevention advocates struggle to translate complex research findings into compelling media narratives that compete with emotional stories of individual tragedy.
