# Drug Trafficking: Legislation

## Overview

Reforming drug trafficking policy requires a multi-pronged legislative strategy that modernizes interdiction technology, strengthens financial surveillance, invests in demand reduction, and builds sustainable international partnerships. The legislative approach is primarily federal, given that drug trafficking is inherently interstate and international. However, state model legislation plays a supporting role in treatment access and harm reduction. The strategy prioritizes incremental, achievable reforms that can build bipartisan support, rather than attempting comprehensive overhaul in a single bill.

---

## Constitutional Amendments

*No constitutional amendments are required for this reform.* The Commerce Clause (Article I, Section 8, Clause 3), the Necessary and Proper Clause, and the Treaty Power provide ample constitutional authority for federal drug trafficking legislation. The federal government's authority to regulate interstate and international drug trafficking has been consistently upheld, most recently in *Gonzales v. Raich*, 545 U.S. 1 (2005).

## Federal Legislation

### The Counter-Narcotics Modernization and Demand Reduction Act

**Purpose**: Rebalance the federal drug control strategy by modernizing border interdiction technology, strengthening anti-money laundering enforcement, and investing in demand reduction to address the root driver of trafficking -- American drug demand.

**Key Provisions**:

1. Mandates deployment of non-intrusive inspection (NII) technology at all 328 U.S. ports of entry within 5 years
2. Triples FinCEN analytical capacity and establishes a dedicated narcotics financial investigation unit at Treasury
3. Requires the ONDCP to achieve a 50/50 supply/demand budget split within 5 years and a 40/60 split within 10 years
4. Expands SAMHSA treatment block grants by $5 billion annually
5. Establishes outcome-based metrics for all federal counter-narcotics programs

**Draft Text**:

```text
119th CONGRESS, 1st Session

AN ACT

To modernize United States counter-narcotics strategy by deploying advanced
border inspection technology, strengthening anti-money laundering enforcement,
and investing in demand reduction to address the root causes of drug
trafficking.

Be it enacted by the Senate and House of Representatives of the
United States of America in Congress assembled,

SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Counter-Narcotics Modernization and Demand
Reduction Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:

(1) According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (2024),
107,543 Americans died from drug overdoses in 2023, with approximately
75,000 deaths (70%) attributable to synthetic opioids, primarily illicitly
manufactured fentanyl.

(2) The Office of National Drug Control Policy reports that the Federal
Government spent $46.1 billion on drug control in FY2025, with 57% allocated
to supply reduction and 43% to demand reduction.

(3) According to the Drug Enforcement Administration (2023), Federal agencies
interdict only an estimated 5-10% of illicit drugs entering the United
States, and approximately 90% of fentanyl is seized at legal ports of entry.

(4) Customs and Border Protection (2024) reports that only approximately
2% of commercial vehicles crossing the U.S.-Mexico border are scanned
with non-intrusive inspection technology.

(5) RAND Corporation research consistently demonstrates that demand
reduction (treatment, prevention, harm reduction) is 7-10 times more
cost-effective than supply-side enforcement at reducing drug use.

(6) The United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (2023) estimates that
$80-100 billion in drug trafficking proceeds are laundered through the
U.S. financial system annually.

(7) The Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (2024)
reports that approximately 76% of the 48.7 million Americans with
substance use disorders receive no treatment.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:

(1) NII SYSTEM.—The term "NII system" means a non-intrusive inspection
system utilizing X-ray, gamma-ray, neutron, or other imaging technology
capable of detecting concealed contraband without physically opening cargo,
vehicles, or containers.

(2) DEMAND REDUCTION.—The term "demand reduction" means Federal programs
and activities focused on substance use disorder treatment, prevention,
harm reduction, and recovery support services.

(3) SUPPLY REDUCTION.—The term "supply reduction" means Federal programs
and activities focused on drug enforcement, interdiction, international
counter-narcotics operations, and prosecution.

(4) OUTCOME METRIC.—The term "outcome metric" means a measurable indicator
of the results of counter-narcotics programs, including but not limited to
overdose death rates, drug availability estimates, treatment access rates,
and cartel revenue estimates.

SEC. 4. UNIVERSAL NON-INTRUSIVE INSPECTION DEPLOYMENT.

(a) IN GENERAL.—The Commissioner of U.S. Customs and Border Protection
shall deploy NII systems at all United States ports of entry to achieve
scanning of not less than—
    (1) 40% of all commercial vehicles within 2 years of enactment;
    (2) 70% of all commercial vehicles within 4 years of enactment; and
    (3) 90% of all commercial vehicles within 5 years of enactment.

(b) TECHNOLOGY REQUIREMENTS.—NII systems deployed under this section
shall—
    (1) be capable of detecting fentanyl and fentanyl analogs in
    quantities as small as 50 grams;
    (2) incorporate artificial intelligence-assisted image analysis to
    reduce false positive rates below 5%; and
    (3) process vehicles at a rate that does not increase average port
    of entry crossing times by more than 10%.

(c) PERSONNEL.—The Commissioner shall hire not fewer than 1,500
additional CBP officers over 5 years to operate NII systems and conduct
secondary inspections.

(d) PRE-CLEARANCE.—The Secretary of Homeland Security, in coordination
with the Secretary of State, shall negotiate agreements with Mexico for
pre-clearance NII scanning of northbound commercial vehicles at Mexican
departure points.

SEC. 5. ANTI-MONEY LAUNDERING ENHANCEMENT.

(a) FINCEN EXPANSION.—The Director of the Financial Crimes Enforcement
Network shall—
    (1) increase analytical staff from approximately 300 to not fewer
    than 900 within 2 years of enactment;
    (2) deploy real-time transaction monitoring technology capable of
    analyzing not fewer than 80% of suspicious activity reports within
    72 hours of filing; and
    (3) establish a dedicated Narcotics Financial Intelligence Unit
    with not fewer than 200 analysts focused exclusively on drug
    trafficking proceeds.

(b) CRYPTOCURRENCY REGULATION.—
    (1) IN GENERAL.—All cryptocurrency exchanges, mixers, and
    decentralized finance platforms operating in or serving customers in
    the United States shall comply with Bank Secrecy Act reporting
    requirements, including—
        (A) customer identification programs;
        (B) suspicious activity reporting; and
        (C) currency transaction reporting for transactions exceeding
        $10,000.
    (2) PRIVACY COINS.—The Secretary of the Treasury shall promulgate
    regulations addressing enhanced due diligence requirements for
    privacy-focused cryptocurrencies.

(c) REAL ESTATE TRANSPARENCY.—All real property transactions in the
United States involving cash or non-financed purchases exceeding
$300,000 shall be reported to FinCEN, including the identity of the
beneficial owner.

(d) CRIMINAL ACCOUNTABILITY.—The Attorney General shall establish a
dedicated unit within the Department of Justice to prosecute individuals,
including bank executives, who knowingly facilitate drug money laundering.
This unit shall have not fewer than 50 attorneys and support staff.

SEC. 6. DEMAND REDUCTION INVESTMENT.

(a) BUDGET REBALANCING.—The Director of the Office of National Drug
Control Policy shall certify that the National Drug Control Budget
achieves—
    (1) a ratio of not less than 47% demand reduction by FY2028;
    (2) a ratio of not less than 50% demand reduction by FY2029;
    (3) a ratio of not less than 55% demand reduction by FY2031; and
    (4) a ratio of not less than 60% demand reduction by FY2034.

(b) SAMHSA BLOCK GRANT EXPANSION.—The Substance Abuse Prevention and
Treatment Block Grant under title XIX, part B, subpart II of the
Public Health Service Act (42 U.S.C. 300x-21 et seq.) is authorized
to be increased by—
    (1) $2,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
    (2) $3,500,000,000 for fiscal year 2028;
    (3) $5,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2029; and
    (4) $5,000,000,000 for each subsequent fiscal year through 2036.

(c) COMMUNITY HEALTH CENTERS.—The Secretary of Health and Human Services
shall establish not fewer than 500 community health centers in areas
designated as High Intensity Drug Trafficking Areas that lack adequate
substance use disorder treatment capacity.

SEC. 7. OUTCOME-BASED PERFORMANCE METRICS.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Not later than 180 days after the date of enactment,
the Director of the Office of National Drug Control Policy shall
establish outcome-based performance metrics for all Federal
counter-narcotics programs, including—
    (1) annual drug overdose deaths, by substance and geography;
    (2) estimated drug availability (price and purity data);
    (3) substance use disorder treatment gap percentage;
    (4) estimated cartel revenue from U.S. drug sales;
    (5) financial intelligence cases resulting in asset seizure; and
    (6) estimated interdiction rate by drug type.

(b) ANNUAL REPORTING.—Not later than March 1 of each year, the Director
shall submit to Congress a report evaluating each Federal
counter-narcotics program against the metrics established under
subsection (a) and recommending reallocation of resources from
underperforming programs.

(c) PUBLIC AVAILABILITY.—All reports under this section shall be made
publicly available on the ONDCP website within 30 days of submission.

SEC. 8. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated to carry out this Act—
    (1) $4,600,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
    (2) $6,200,000,000 for fiscal year 2028;
    (3) $7,500,000,000 for fiscal year 2029;
    (4) $8,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2030; and
    (5) $8,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2031.

SEC. 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date that is 90 days after the date
of enactment of this Act.
```

**Explanation of Key Provisions**:

- **Section 2 (Findings)**: Establishes the factual basis for legislative action using the most current federal data, including the 107,543 overdose deaths in 2023, the 5-10% interdiction rate, and the 76% treatment gap.
- **Section 4 (NII Deployment)**: Addresses the critical gap that only 2% of commercial vehicles are currently scanned by mandating phased deployment to all ports of entry. The 90% target is ambitious but technically achievable with current technology.
- **Section 5 (AML Enhancement)**: Targets the $80-100 billion in annual drug money laundering by expanding FinCEN, regulating cryptocurrency, mandating real estate transparency, and creating criminal accountability for facilitating banks.
- **Section 6 (Demand Reduction)**: The core rebalancing provision that shifts the drug control budget from 57/43 to 40/60 over approximately 8 years, with $5 billion annually in additional treatment funding.
- **Section 7 (Metrics)**: Forces the federal government to measure success by outcomes (deaths, availability, treatment access) rather than inputs (seizures, arrests, spending).

**Implementation Timeline**:

| Milestone | Deadline | Responsible Party |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| Outcome metrics established | 180 days after enactment | ONDCP |
| FinCEN expansion to 900 staff | 2 years after enactment | Treasury/FinCEN |
| NII at 40% commercial vehicle coverage | 2 years after enactment | CBP |
| Cryptocurrency AML regulations published | 1 year after enactment | FinCEN |
| Budget ratio reaches 50/50 | FY2029 | ONDCP/Congress |
| NII at 90% commercial vehicle coverage | 5 years after enactment | CBP |
| 500 community health centers operational | 5 years after enactment | HHS |
| Budget ratio reaches 40/60 | FY2034 | ONDCP/Congress |

**Regulatory Authority**: CBP implements NII deployment; FinCEN issues AML regulations for cryptocurrency and real estate; ONDCP certifies budget ratios; HHS/SAMHSA administers treatment expansion.

**Challenges**:

- **Cost**: $8 billion annually at full implementation represents a significant increase over current spending
- **Trade impact**: Universal NII scanning may slow border commerce despite mitigation measures
- **Banking industry opposition**: Cryptocurrency and real estate transparency requirements will face strong industry lobbying
- **Institutional resistance**: DEA and enforcement agencies will resist budget rebalancing

**Refinements**:

- **Phase NII deployment** by port volume, prioritizing the 50 highest-traffic crossings first
- **Grandfather existing crypto platforms** with 18-month compliance transition period
- **Create enforcement agency performance incentives** tied to outcome metrics rather than seizure volumes

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Demand Reduction Investment Act

**Purpose**: Ensure that state drug control spending aligns with evidence-based best practices by requiring states to invest a minimum percentage of drug control funds in treatment, prevention, and harm reduction.

**Adaptability Notes**: States should adjust the minimum demand reduction percentage based on their existing budget balance. States with strong existing treatment infrastructure may set higher targets; states building from a low base may set graduated targets.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act shall be known and may be cited as the "[State] Demand Reduction
Investment Act of 2026".

SECTION 2. FINDINGS.

The Legislature finds that:

(a) Evidence from the RAND Corporation and other research organizations
demonstrates that substance use disorder treatment is 7-10 times more
cost-effective than law enforcement at reducing drug use and its consequences.

(b) Approximately 76% of Americans with substance use disorders receive
no treatment (SAMHSA, 2024).

(c) Every person who receives effective treatment for substance use
disorder is one fewer customer for drug trafficking organizations.

SECTION 3. DEMAND REDUCTION MINIMUM.

(a) Not less than 50% of state funds appropriated for drug control
purposes shall be allocated to demand reduction programs, including—
    (1) substance use disorder treatment;
    (2) medication-assisted treatment access;
    (3) harm reduction services;
    (4) overdose prevention programs; and
    (5) evidence-based prevention programs.

(b) The state shall achieve this minimum within 3 fiscal years of
the effective date of this Act.

SECTION 4. REPORTING.

The [state agency] shall submit an annual report to the Legislature
documenting the allocation of drug control funds between supply and
demand reduction and the outcomes achieved by each category of
program, including overdose deaths, treatment admissions, and
recovery rates.

SECTION 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on [date].
```

## Regulatory Framework

### Administrative Implementation

**Primary Agency**: Office of National Drug Control Policy (coordination); CBP (interdiction technology); FinCEN (financial surveillance); SAMHSA (treatment expansion)

**Rulemaking Required**:

1. **Cryptocurrency AML Compliance Standards**: FinCEN must issue final regulations within 12 months of enactment defining BSA compliance requirements for cryptocurrency exchanges, mixers, and DeFi platforms.
2. **Real Estate Reporting Requirements**: FinCEN must issue regulations within 12 months establishing reporting thresholds and procedures for cash real estate transactions exceeding $300,000.
3. **NII Technology Standards**: CBP must publish technical specifications for NII systems within 6 months, including detection sensitivity, processing speed, and AI accuracy requirements.
4. **Outcome Metric Definitions**: ONDCP must publish standardized definitions and data collection methods for all outcome metrics within 180 days.

**Enforcement Mechanisms**:

- **Civil Penalties**: Financial institutions failing to comply with enhanced AML requirements face penalties of up to $1 million per violation per day (consistent with existing BSA penalty structure).
- **Administrative Actions**: FinCEN may revoke the operating license of cryptocurrency exchanges that fail to implement customer identification programs.
- **Criminal Penalties**: Individuals who knowingly facilitate money laundering of drug proceeds face up to 20 years imprisonment under existing 18 U.S.C. Section 1956.
- **Budget Certification**: ONDCP must annually certify that the drug control budget meets the required supply/demand ratio; failure to certify triggers automatic reallocation.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

**Commerce Clause Authority**: Federal authority to regulate drug trafficking is well-established under the Commerce Clause. *Gonzales v. Raich*, 545 U.S. 1 (2005) held that Congress may regulate intrastate drug activity as part of a comprehensive scheme to regulate interstate commerce. The NII deployment, AML regulations, and budget rebalancing provisions all fall within this authority.

**Fourth Amendment -- NII Scanning**: Non-intrusive scanning of vehicles at ports of entry falls within the border search exception to the Fourth Amendment warrant requirement. *United States v. Flores-Montano*, 541 U.S. 149 (2004) held that the government's interest in border security permits routine searches without individualized suspicion.

**First Amendment -- Cryptocurrency Regulation**: Cryptocurrency AML requirements regulate financial transactions, not speech. Courts have consistently held that BSA reporting requirements do not violate the First Amendment (*California Bankers Ass'n v. Shultz*, 416 U.S. 21, 1974).

### Preemption

Federal drug trafficking law preempts conflicting state laws under the Supremacy Clause. The Controlled Substances Act expressly preempts state laws that conflict with federal scheduling. However, this legislation preserves state authority to enact more protective AML regulations and more expansive treatment programs.

### Enforcement and Compliance

Compliance is ensured through a combination of annual ONDCP certification (budget ratios), FinCEN regulatory enforcement (AML compliance), CBP operational audits (NII deployment), and Congressional oversight (annual reporting requirements).

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | How It Could Be Exploited | Severity | Proposed Fix |
|----------|---------------------------|----------|--------------|
| NII vehicle throughput exemption | CBP could exempt high-volume ports from scanning targets, citing processing time | High | Add provision requiring CBP to demonstrate that any exemption is offset by equivalent scanning at alternative inspection points |
| Cryptocurrency DeFi gap | Decentralized finance protocols without identifiable operators may claim exemption from BSA requirements | High | Define "operating in the United States" broadly to include any protocol with U.S. user access; require front-end developers to implement KYC |
| Budget ratio gaming | ONDCP could reclassify enforcement programs as "demand reduction" to meet ratio targets without real rebalancing | High | Require GAO annual audit of budget classifications against standardized definitions; prohibit reclassification of existing programs without GAO concurrence |
| Real estate LLC loophole | Buyers could structure transactions below $300,000 threshold through multiple purchases | Medium | Add anti-structuring provision mirroring BSA anti-structuring rules (31 U.S.C. Section 5324) for real estate |
| NII technology obsolescence | Cartels may develop concealment methods that defeat current NII systems | Medium | Require annual technology assessment and 3-year upgrade cycle; authorize R&D budget for next-generation detection |
| International data sharing gaps | Foreign financial intelligence may not be shared with U.S. authorities due to data protection laws | Medium | Negotiate bilateral information-sharing agreements as a condition of U.S. financial assistance |

### Known Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause | Mitigation |
|-------|--------|------------|------------|
| Budget rebalancing depends on annual appropriations | Congress may not follow ONDCP certification | Congressional spending power cannot be binding on future Congresses | Build bipartisan support; create automatic reallocation trigger if Congress fails to meet targets |
| China cooperation is voluntary | Precursor chemical regulation depends on Chinese government willingness | Sovereign nation cannot be compelled to act | Develop unilateral detection alternatives; use trade leverage; build multilateral coalition |
| Treatment workforce shortage | New community health centers may lack qualified staff | Insufficient addiction medicine training pipeline | Include workforce development provisions; expand addiction medicine residency funding |
| Social media platform resistance | Platforms may resist drug content monitoring as censorship | First Amendment and Section 230 protections | Condition Section 230 liability protection on good-faith drug content monitoring |
| Cartel adaptation | Organizations will modify tactics in response to new measures | Criminal enterprises inherently adaptive | Build adaptive capacity into all programs; require annual threat assessments |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Anti-gaming provision for budget ratios**: Add requirement that GAO independently audit ONDCP budget classifications annually. Any program reclassified from supply to demand reduction must receive GAO approval. Include whistleblower protections for ONDCP staff who report classification manipulation.

2. **DeFi compliance framework**: Direct FinCEN to develop a regulatory framework within 18 months that applies BSA requirements to DeFi protocols through front-end interfaces, node operators, and governance token holders. Provide safe harbor for protocols that implement compliant front-end access controls.

3. **NII upgrade mandate**: Require CBP to conduct annual assessment of NII technology effectiveness against current concealment methods and authorize automatic procurement of upgraded systems when detection rates fall below 80% for any target substance.

4. **Treatment workforce pipeline**: Authorize $500 million over 5 years for addiction medicine residency programs, addiction counselor certification programs, and loan forgiveness for providers practicing in underserved areas.

5. **Sunset and review**: The Act shall sunset after 10 years unless reauthorized. Congress shall receive comprehensive 5-year and 10-year independent evaluations from RAND Corporation or equivalent institution assessing whether the legislation has achieved its stated outcome metrics.

### Sunset and Review Provisions

All major provisions of this Act sunset 10 years after enactment, requiring Congressional reauthorization. Independent evaluation at years 5 and 10 shall assess:

- Whether overdose deaths have decreased relative to the 2023 baseline of 107,543
- Whether the drug control budget ratio has achieved the 40/60 target
- Whether interdiction rates have increased as a result of NII deployment
- Whether AML reforms have produced measurable reductions in identified money laundering
- Whether treatment access has expanded and the treatment gap has narrowed
