# Drug Trafficking: What You Can Do

Drug trafficking policy is shaped by public opinion, political pressure, and citizen engagement. The choices the government makes about how to allocate resources between enforcement and treatment, how to cooperate with international partners, and how to address the fentanyl crisis directly affect whether Americans live or die. Your voice matters.

---

## Individual Actions

### Learn and Stay Informed

- Subscribe to the **DEA National Drug Threat Assessment** annual release to understand current trafficking trends and agency priorities
- Follow the **CDC's Drug Overdose Surveillance** data to track overdose deaths in your state and community: <https://www.cdc.gov/overdose-prevention/>
- Read RAND Corporation drug policy research to understand the evidence base for reform: <https://www.rand.org/well-being/justice-policy/centers/dprc.html>
- Follow **InSight Crime** (<https://insightcrime.org/>) for investigative reporting on cartels and organized crime in the Americas
- Monitor **CBP drug seizure data** (<https://www.cbp.gov/newsroom/stats/drug-seizure-statistics>) to understand interdiction patterns and trends
- Review the **ONDCP National Drug Control Budget** (<https://www.whitehouse.gov/ondcp/budget/>) to understand how your tax dollars are allocated between enforcement and treatment
- Follow the **Senate Caucus on International Narcotics Control** for legislative updates on counter-trafficking policy: <https://www.drugcaucus.senate.gov/>

### Understand the Key Numbers

Effective advocacy requires knowing the basic facts:

| Fact | Number | Why It Matters |
|------|--------|----------------|
| Annual overdose deaths | 107,543 (2023) | More than car accidents, gun violence, or HIV/AIDS at its peak |
| Fentanyl's share of overdose deaths | ~70% | Fentanyl is the central crisis; policy must target it specifically |
| Federal drug control spending | $46.1 billion/year | Taxpayers fund this; demand accountability for outcomes |
| Supply vs. demand budget split | 57/43 | Despite evidence that treatment is 7-10x more cost-effective |
| Treatment gap | 76% untreated | Three in four Americans with SUD receive no treatment |
| Interdiction effectiveness | 5-10% | Only a fraction of drugs are intercepted despite massive spending |
| Fentanyl entry via ports of entry | ~90% | The "border wall" narrative misses where drugs actually cross |

### Make Personal Changes

- Learn to recognize and avoid counterfeit pills -- the DEA warns that 7 out of 10 fentanyl-laced counterfeit pills contain a potentially lethal dose (DEA, 2024). Only use medications prescribed by a licensed physician and dispensed by a licensed pharmacy.
- Carry naloxone (Narcan). Over-the-counter naloxone is available at most pharmacies without a prescription. One dose can reverse an opioid overdose and save a life. The retail cost is approximately $44.99 for a 2-dose package; many community organizations distribute it free.
- Learn to use fentanyl test strips. If you or someone you know uses substances, test strips can detect fentanyl in drug samples. They cost $0.50-$2.00 per strip and are available from harm reduction organizations and some pharmacies. They are legal in most states (30+ states explicitly legalize them as of 2024).
- If you or someone you know has a substance use disorder, contact SAMHSA's National Helpline at **1-800-662-4357** for free, confidential treatment referrals available 24/7.
- Properly dispose of unused prescription medications through DEA Take Back events or pharmacy drop-off programs to prevent diversion. The DEA conducts National Prescription Drug Take Back events twice annually (April and October).
- Talk to your children about the dangers of counterfeit pills, particularly those purchased through social media. The DEA reports that Snapchat and Instagram are primary channels for fentanyl pill sales to minors.

### Use Your Voice

- **Contact Your Representatives**: Urge members of Congress to support demand reduction funding, anti-money laundering reform, and border technology modernization -- not just enforcement escalation.
- **Submit Public Comments**: When DEA proposes drug scheduling actions or FinCEN proposes AML regulations, submit public comments through regulations.gov. Public comments are read by agency staff and can influence rulemaking.
- **Write Letters to the Editor**: Counter simplistic "just enforce harder" narratives with evidence about the cost-effectiveness of treatment and the failure of enforcement-only approaches.
- **Share Information**: Help others understand that the fentanyl crisis is a public health emergency requiring health-focused responses, not just a border security issue.
- **Use social media**: Share evidence-based information about drug trafficking policy on social media platforms. Counter misinformation about border enforcement, demand reduction, and harm reduction. Tag your elected officials in posts about policy reform.

## Community Actions

### Organize Locally

- Form a community coalition to advocate for local treatment capacity. Many communities lack adequate substance use disorder treatment facilities, particularly medication-assisted treatment (MAT) providers. Over 400 U.S. counties have no buprenorphine provider, and 2,400 counties (77%) have no methadone clinic (SAMHSA, 2024).
- Support local naloxone distribution programs. Many health departments and harm reduction organizations distribute free naloxone kits and provide overdose reversal training. Contact your local health department to find out what programs exist.
- Host community education events on recognizing and responding to opioid overdoses. The Good Samaritan laws in most states protect people who call 911 during an overdose from drug possession charges.
- Advocate for your local school district to adopt evidence-based drug prevention curricula rather than ineffective programs like D.A.R.E. Evidence-based alternatives include the Botvin LifeSkills Training program, which has demonstrated 30-60% reductions in drug use initiation in randomized trials.
- Organize community forums with local law enforcement, treatment providers, and public health officials to discuss coordinated responses to drug trafficking and overdose in your area.
- Support supervised consumption site proposals if they are under consideration in your community. OnPoint NYC in New York City has operated since November 2021 with zero overdose deaths and over 1,800 overdose reversals.

### Build Coalitions

- Connect with local harm reduction organizations to support syringe services, drug checking programs, and overdose prevention efforts
- Partner with faith communities that support recovery programs -- many churches, synagogues, and mosques host 12-step meetings and recovery support groups
- Engage business organizations in supporting anti-money laundering reform and beneficial ownership transparency -- legitimate businesses have an interest in clean financial systems
- Build relationships with local law enforcement leaders who support reform. Organizations like Law Enforcement Action Partnership (LEAP) can connect you with reform-minded officers.
- Reach out to local medical societies and hospital associations to advocate for expanded MAT prescribing and overdose response training for healthcare providers
- Connect with immigrant and Latino community organizations, many of which have direct experience with cartel violence and trafficking and can provide perspectives often missing from policy debates
- Work with local libraries, which are increasingly serving as naloxone distribution and overdose education sites in communities with limited harm reduction infrastructure

### Community-Level Anti-Trafficking Actions

- **Report suspicious activity**: If you observe suspected drug trafficking activity, report it to the DEA tip line (1-877-792-2873) or local law enforcement. Do not attempt to intervene directly.
- **Support suspicious activity report compliance**: If you work in banking, real estate, or other industries covered by BSA reporting requirements, ensure your organization is filing SARs as required. Non-compliance enables money laundering.
- **Advocate for local money laundering investigations**: Encourage your local district attorney and law enforcement to prioritize financial investigations of drug trafficking organizations, not just street-level arrests.

## Political Actions

### Contact Elected Officials

**When to Contact**:

- During federal budget debates (January-October annually) -- advocate for drug control budget rebalancing toward demand reduction
- When counter-narcotics legislation is pending in committee (check Congress.gov for current bills)
- After major fentanyl seizures or overdose spikes, when public attention is high
- During election campaigns, when candidates are formulating policy positions
- When your state legislature is considering harm reduction legislation (syringe services, drug checking, supervised consumption)

**Key Congressional Committees**:

| Committee | Jurisdiction | Key Actions |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Senate Judiciary | Drug scheduling, sentencing, DEA oversight | FENTANYL Results Act, sentencing reform, cartel designation |
| Senate Caucus on International Narcotics Control | Counter-narcotics policy, international cooperation | U.S.-Mexico partnership, China precursor diplomacy |
| House Judiciary | Drug scheduling, criminal justice, enforcement | Mandatory minimum reform, RICO application |
| Senate/House Appropriations | Drug control budget, agency funding | Budget rebalancing, FinCEN expansion, treatment funding |
| Senate Banking | Anti-money laundering, financial regulation | Cryptocurrency AML, beneficial ownership, bank accountability |
| Senate/House Foreign Relations | International cooperation, foreign aid | Merida successor programs, China engagement |
| Senate/House Homeland Security | Border security, CBP operations | NII technology funding, port of entry modernization |

**What to Say - Template Email**:

```text
Subject: Rebalance Drug Control Spending to Save Lives

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

I am writing as a constituent from [City/District] to urge you to support
rebalancing our federal drug control budget from enforcement-heavy to
treatment-centered approaches.

The United States currently spends $46.1 billion annually on drug control,
with 57% going to supply reduction (enforcement, interdiction) and only 43%
to demand reduction (treatment, prevention, harm reduction). Despite decades
of increasing enforcement spending, drug availability has not decreased and
107,543 Americans died of overdoses in 2023.

RAND Corporation research consistently shows that treatment is 7-10 times
more cost-effective than enforcement at reducing drug use. I urge you to
support legislation that shifts federal drug control spending to at least
60% demand reduction and 40% supply reduction over the next decade.

I also urge you to support:
- Full deployment of non-intrusive inspection technology at all 328 ports
  of entry (where 90% of fentanyl is seized)
- Tripling FinCEN's staff to combat cartel money laundering
- Expanding SAMHSA treatment block grants to close the 76% treatment gap

Thank you for your service and consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your City, State]
```

### Support Legislation

Current or proposed legislation to support:

- **FENTANYL Results Act reauthorization**: Maintains temporary scheduling of fentanyl-related substances while Congress debates permanent scheduling reform -- contact Judiciary Committee members to ensure reauthorization includes demand reduction provisions
- **Corporate Transparency Act implementation**: Supports FinCEN's beneficial ownership database that helps track cartel money laundering -- contact Financial Services Committee members to ensure full funding
- **SAMHSA Block Grant expansion**: Increases federal funding for substance use disorder treatment -- contact Appropriations Committee members to support increased funding
- **Cryptocurrency Anti-Money Laundering legislation**: Would require crypto exchanges to comply with Bank Secrecy Act reporting -- contact Banking Committee members
- **NII Technology Deployment Act**: Would accelerate non-intrusive inspection deployment at all ports of entry -- contact Homeland Security Committee members
- **Precursor Chemical Accountability Act**: Would strengthen sanctions authority against foreign precursor chemical suppliers -- contact Foreign Affairs Committee members
- **Drug Checking Decriminalization Act**: Would remove federal barriers to fentanyl test strip distribution and advanced drug checking services -- contact Judiciary Committee members
- **Sentencing reform legislation**: Reducing mandatory minimums for non-violent drug offenses to redirect prison resources toward high-level traffickers -- contact Judiciary Committee members

### Electoral Engagement

**Questions to Ask Candidates**:

1. "What is your specific plan to reduce the 107,543 overdose deaths that occurred in 2023? How much would you invest in treatment versus enforcement?"
2. "Do you support rebalancing the federal drug control budget to invest more in treatment and prevention? If not, why not, given the evidence that treatment is 7-10 times more cost-effective?"
3. "What is your strategy for disrupting cartel money laundering, which is estimated at $80-100 billion annually through the U.S. financial system?"
4. "How would you approach diplomatic engagement with China on fentanyl precursor chemicals without jeopardizing cooperation?"
5. "Do you support deploying non-intrusive inspection technology at all 328 ports of entry, given that 90% of fentanyl enters through legal ports of entry?"
6. "Do you support or oppose designating cartels as terrorist organizations? What specific tools would designation provide that are not already available under existing authorities?"

**Voting Considerations**:

- Look for candidates who propose specific, evidence-based counter-trafficking strategies rather than vague "secure the border" rhetoric
- Examine candidates' positions on the Senate Caucus on International Narcotics Control, Senate/House Judiciary Committees, and Appropriations subcommittees that oversee drug policy
- Evaluate whether candidates support both enforcement modernization and demand reduction investment, or only one side
- Check whether candidates accept contributions from private prison companies (GEO Group, CoreCivic), pharmaceutical companies, or defense contractors with border security contracts -- these contributions may influence their policy positions
- Review candidates' voting records on sentencing reform, treatment funding, and AML legislation through organizations like the Drug Policy Alliance's legislative scorecard

## Professional Actions

- **Healthcare professionals**: Advocate for MAT access in your institution. Prescribe buprenorphine for opioid use disorder -- the X-waiver requirement was eliminated in 2023, meaning any DEA-registered practitioner can now prescribe. Train colleagues in addiction medicine. Screen patients for substance use disorders. Report suspicious prescribing patterns to state medical boards and the DEA.
- **Financial professionals**: Report suspicious activity as required by BSA. Advocate for stronger AML compliance within your institution. Support beneficial ownership transparency. If you work in compliance, push for implementation of advanced transaction monitoring analytics that can detect trade-based laundering and Chinese money broker network patterns.
- **Technology professionals**: Support development of drug detection technology, blockchain analytics for tracing laundered funds, and AI tools for identifying counterfeit pill sales on social media. If you work at a social media company, advocate for stronger content moderation policies targeting drug sales, particularly those targeting minors.
- **Legal professionals**: Provide pro bono representation to individuals facing drug charges. Advocate for sentencing reform. Support expungement programs for non-violent drug offenses. If you practice in financial regulation, support stronger AML enforcement and bank accountability.
- **Educators**: Implement evidence-based drug prevention curricula (such as Botvin LifeSkills Training). Train staff in recognizing signs of substance use. Connect students and families to treatment resources. Ensure school naloxone is available and staff know how to administer it. Educate students about the dangers of purchasing pills through social media.
- **Journalists**: Investigate cartel financial networks, enforcement effectiveness, and the gap between drug policy rhetoric and reality. Cover the evidence on demand reduction. Report on the social media-to-overdose pipeline affecting young Americans. Hold government agencies accountable for outcome-based metrics rather than input-based press releases about seizures and arrests.
- **Researchers**: Conduct independent evaluations of counter-trafficking program effectiveness. Publish findings on the relative cost-effectiveness of supply versus demand reduction strategies. Study novel synthetic drug threats and develop early warning indicators.

## Advocacy Toolkit

### Key Talking Points

When discussing drug trafficking policy with others, emphasize:

1. **The enforcement-only approach has failed by its own metrics**: The U.S. has spent over $1 trillion on the War on Drugs since 1971, yet drug availability has not decreased and overdose deaths have reached 107,543 per year. The inflation-adjusted price of cocaine, heroin, and methamphetamine has fallen while purity has increased.

2. **Treatment is 7-10 times more cost-effective than enforcement**: RAND Corporation research demonstrates that every dollar invested in treatment reduces drug consumption more than every dollar invested in enforcement. Demand reduction is the most effective way to "defund the cartels."

3. **Follow the money**: Drug cartels launder an estimated $80-100 billion through the U.S. financial system annually. Stronger anti-money laundering enforcement -- including beneficial ownership transparency and bank executive accountability -- would degrade cartel operations more effectively than additional border agents.

4. **90% of fentanyl enters at legal ports of entry**: Border walls and between-port enforcement do not address the primary entry method. Non-intrusive inspection technology at ports of entry is the most effective interdiction investment.

5. **The treatment gap is the traffickers' best friend**: When 76% of Americans with substance use disorders receive no treatment, the customer base for drug traffickers remains enormous. Closing this gap is the single most effective long-term counter-trafficking strategy.

### Common Misconceptions to Counter

| Misconception | Reality | Source |
|---------------|---------|--------|
| "Most drugs come across the border between ports of entry" | ~90% of fentanyl is seized at legal ports of entry, hidden in commercial vehicles and cars. A border wall does not address the primary entry method. | CBP, 2023 |
| "More enforcement spending will reduce drug availability" | Drug enforcement spending has increased from $1.5 billion (1981) to $26.3 billion (FY2025). Drug prices have fallen and availability has increased over the same period. | ONDCP, 2024; DEA data |
| "Harm reduction enables drug use" | Countries with robust harm reduction (Portugal, Switzerland) have lower overdose rates than the U.S. Harm reduction saves lives while connecting people to treatment. | EMCDDA, 2024 |
| "Drug traffickers would stop if penalties were harsher" | The U.S. already imposes some of the harshest drug penalties in the developed world. Countries with the death penalty for trafficking still have drug problems. Deterrence fails against highly profitable criminal enterprises. | UNODC, 2024 |
| "Arresting cartel leaders stops trafficking" | The arrest or killing of every major cartel leader (Escobar, El Chapo, Zambada) has failed to reduce drug flows and often increased violence as successors compete for control -- the "hydra effect." | CRS, 2024; DEA historical data |
| "China could stop the fentanyl crisis if it wanted to" | China scheduled all fentanyl substances in 2019, which stopped direct shipments. Precursor chemicals are dual-use with legitimate applications and are produced by 400,000+ chemical companies, making comprehensive control extremely difficult. | CSIS, 2024; DEA, 2023 |
