# Pharmaceutical Industry: Root Causes

## Systemic Analysis

The current challenges in pharmaceutical industry regulation are not the result of a single failure but of several long-term, interlocking structural problems. These include a patent system that incentivizes monopoly extension over genuine innovation, a regulatory agency financially dependent on the industry it oversees, a political system saturated with pharmaceutical lobbying, and a healthcare payment structure that insulates pricing decisions from competitive pressure. Together, these factors create a system in which the industry can extract monopoly profits, externalize public health costs, and resist meaningful reform.

---

## Regulatory Capture and Institutional Weakness

### FDA Funding Conflicts

The FDA's Center for Drug Evaluation and Research (CDER) derives approximately 65% of its drug review budget from user fees paid by pharmaceutical companies under PDUFA (FDA, FY2024). This creates a structural dependency in which the agency's capacity to perform its core function -- evaluating drug safety and efficacy -- is contingent on maintaining a cooperative relationship with the industry it regulates.

**Evidence**: Studies have documented that PDUFA-era approvals have been associated with increased rates of post-market safety withdrawals and black box warnings compared to the pre-PDUFA era (Downing et al., *JAMA Internal Medicine*, 2017). The FDA approved aducanumab (Aduhelm) in 2021 for Alzheimer's disease over the near-unanimous objection of its own advisory committee, in a process that an independent investigation found involved "irregularities" in the agency's interactions with manufacturer Biogen (House Energy and Commerce Committee, 2022).

### Revolving Door

Nine of the fifteen most recent FDA commissioners have subsequently taken positions on pharmaceutical or biotech company boards (Piller, *Science*, 2018). FDA reviewers and senior staff frequently transition to industry positions, creating career incentives that may affect regulatory decision-making.

**Evidence**: A 2016 study in the *BMJ* found that of 55 FDA drug reviewers who left the agency, 36 (65%) went to work for or consult for the pharmaceutical industry (Piller, 2018). Former Commissioner Scott Gottlieb joined Pfizer's board within two years of leaving the FDA.

### Inadequate Post-Market Surveillance

The FDA's authority and capacity for monitoring drugs after approval are significantly weaker than its premarket review capabilities. The agency has limited ability to require post-market studies, enforce completion of post-market commitments, or rapidly withdraw drugs with emerging safety signals.

**Evidence**: As of 2023, approximately 45% of post-market study commitments required as conditions of accelerated approval remained incomplete or had not been initiated, some dating back more than a decade (FDA, 2023).

## Patent System Manipulation

### Evergreening Strategies

The patent system, intended to provide time-limited monopoly incentives for genuine innovation, has been systematically exploited to extend exclusivity far beyond original patent terms. "Evergreening" refers to a suite of strategies through which manufacturers obtain additional patents on minor modifications -- new formulations, dosing regimens, delivery devices, or chemical polymorphs -- that do not represent meaningful therapeutic advances but extend monopoly pricing.

**Evidence**: The average top-selling drug in the United States is protected by 74 patent applications (I-MAK, 2023). Humira (adalimumab), AbbVie's blockbuster autoimmune drug, was protected by more than 150 patents, creating a "patent thicket" that delayed biosimilar competition for over five years beyond the original patent expiration. AbbVie generated an estimated $114 billion in U.S. Humira revenue during the period of extended exclusivity (2017-2023) (I-MAK, 2023).

### Pay-for-Delay Settlements

Brand-name manufacturers have paid generic companies billions of dollars to delay launching competing products, a practice the Supreme Court held could violate antitrust law in *FTC v. Actavis* (2013) but which continues in modified forms.

**Evidence**: The FTC estimated that pay-for-delay agreements cost American consumers $3.5 billion annually in higher drug prices (FTC, 2010). Between 2004 and 2020, the FTC identified over 200 such agreements (FTC, Annual Reports on Pharmaceutical Competition).

### Product Hopping

Manufacturers reformulate drugs near patent expiration -- switching from a tablet to a capsule, changing dosage, or creating an extended-release version -- and actively shift patients and prescribers to the new formulation before generic competition for the original product begins.

**Evidence**: Namenda (memantine), an Alzheimer's drug, was switched from twice-daily to once-daily dosing, with the manufacturer attempting to withdraw the original formulation from the market to prevent generic substitution. The Second Circuit Court of Appeals found this practice anticompetitive in *New York v. Actavis* (2015).

## Political Economy

### Who Benefits from the Status Quo

| Actor | Benefit | Economic Value | Incentive to Block Reform |
|-------|---------|----------------|---------------------------|
| Pharmaceutical manufacturers | Monopoly pricing power, limited regulation | $603 billion U.S. revenue (2023) | Price regulation would directly reduce profits; stronger regulation would increase compliance costs |
| PBMs | Opaque rebate retention, spread pricing | $500 billion in aggregate revenue (2022) | Transparency would expose pricing practices; regulation would limit margin extraction |
| Patent attorneys and litigation firms | Complex patent strategies generate fees | Multi-billion dollar annual industry | Patent reform would reduce demand for services |
| Pharmaceutical lobbyists | Industry engagement with policymakers | $374 million annually (2023) | Reform would reduce industry demand for lobbying |
| Congressional recipients | Campaign contributions and fundraising | $90.5 million (2022 cycle) | Antagonizing the industry risks losing major donor |
| Sackler family and opioid-era executives | Extracted personal wealth before accountability | $10-12 billion (Sackler family) | Criminal prosecution would threaten personal liberty and assets |

### Perverse Incentives

The current system creates multiple misaligned incentives:

- **Innovation Misdirection**: The patent system rewards incremental modifications (new formulations, dosing) more reliably than genuine breakthrough innovation, because evergreening is less risky and less expensive than novel drug development
- **Marketing Over Science**: Companies allocate more to sales and marketing than to R&D because marketing generates higher short-term returns. A dollar spent on advertising typically yields $2-4 in additional revenue, while R&D investments have uncertain returns over 10-15 year timelines
- **FDA as Facilitator**: The user fee model incentivizes the FDA to approve drugs quickly and maintain cooperative relationships with companies that fund its operations, rather than serving as a rigorous, independent gatekeeper
- **PBM Conflicts**: PBMs may prefer drugs with higher manufacturer rebates over clinically superior or lower-cost alternatives, because PBM revenue depends on rebate volume rather than patient outcomes
- **Insurance Buffer**: Employer-sponsored and government-funded insurance insulates patients from drug prices at the point of purchase, reducing the price sensitivity that would ordinarily constrain manufacturer pricing in a competitive market
- **Off-Label Promotion Incentives**: The financial reward for off-label promotion far exceeds the penalties for getting caught. Off-label prescribing can expand a drug's addressable market by 200-400%, while fraud settlements typically represent 5-15% of the revenue generated by the off-label sales. GlaxoSmithKline's $3 billion settlement (2012) represented approximately 11% of one year's revenue -- a calculation that makes continued off-label promotion economically rational despite its illegality
- **Stock Buyback Incentives**: Executive compensation tied to stock price creates incentives to prioritize share buybacks and dividends over R&D investment. Between 2016 and 2020, the top 14 pharmaceutical companies spent $577 billion on buybacks and dividends compared to $532 billion on R&D (House Oversight Committee, 2021). This allocation of capital maximizes short-term shareholder returns while undermining the industry's stated justification for high prices
- **Lobbying Return on Investment**: The pharmaceutical industry's $374 million in annual lobbying spending generates returns that dwarf any other capital allocation. The prohibition on Medicare price negotiation (2003-2022) alone preserved an estimated $15-25 billion annually in monopoly pricing above what negotiated prices would have been, representing a return on lobbying investment exceeding 5,000%

## Bayh-Dole Framework and Public Research Commercialization

### The Publicly Funded Pipeline

The Bayh-Dole Act of 1980 fundamentally altered the relationship between public research investment and private pharmaceutical profit. Before Bayh-Dole, inventions made with federal funding generally belonged to the government. After Bayh-Dole, universities and research institutions could patent these inventions and license them exclusively to private companies.

**Evidence**: NIH-funded basic research contributed to every one of the 210 drugs approved by the FDA from 2010 to 2016 (Galkina Cleary et al., *PNAS*, 2018). A 2018 study found that all 210 drugs traced back to more than $100 billion in cumulative NIH funding. The five largest pharmaceutical companies spent more on stock buybacks and dividends ($577 billion, 2016-2020) than on R&D ($532 billion), even as they claimed that high prices were necessary to fund innovation (House Oversight Committee, 2021).

### Unenforced March-In Rights

The Bayh-Dole Act reserved "march-in rights" for the federal government, allowing it to require licensing of federally funded inventions when the patent holder fails to make the invention available on "reasonable terms." In more than four decades, no administration has ever exercised march-in rights, despite repeated petitions from advocacy groups citing unaffordable drug prices.

**Evidence**: NIH has received at least six formal march-in petitions since 1997, all of which were denied. In each case, NIH interpreted "reasonable terms" as referring to availability (whether the drug was being manufactured), not pricing. The pharmaceutical industry and major research universities have consistently lobbied against any interpretation that would include pricing considerations, preserving the one-directional flow of value from public research to private monopoly profit.

### Misalignment of Public Investment and Public Benefit

The root cause is structural: the United States invests more in biomedical research than any other country ($48.6 billion annually through NIH alone, FY2024), but has no mechanism to ensure that the products of this investment are available at affordable prices. Other countries that invest significantly in public research -- including the UK, Germany, and Canada -- combine their research funding with price regulation, ensuring that taxpayer-funded discoveries are available to taxpayers at reasonable cost.

## Direct-to-Consumer Advertising as a Structural Driver

### Creating Demand Rather Than Meeting Need

The United States is one of only two countries that permits broadcast DTC pharmaceutical advertising (the other is New Zealand). DTC advertising creates patient demand for specific branded products, shifting prescribing decisions from evidence-based physician judgment to commercially driven patient requests.

**Evidence**: A 2005 FDA survey found that 65% of physicians felt DTC ads led patients to believe drugs work better than they actually do. A 2019 *JAMA* study found that drugs with higher DTC advertising spending had higher prescribing rates, independent of clinical merit (Schwartz and Woloshin, 2019). DTC advertising spending grew from $791 million in 1996 to $6.58 billion in 2022, with the top-advertised drugs consistently being high-margin branded products in therapeutic areas where equivalent generics or alternative treatments exist.

### First Amendment Shield

The pharmaceutical industry has successfully used First Amendment commercial speech protections to defend DTC advertising against regulatory restriction. The Supreme Court's decision in *Sorrell v. IMS Health Inc.* (2011) struck down a Vermont law restricting the use of prescriber data for marketing purposes, reinforcing the constitutional protection of pharmaceutical marketing activities and making regulatory action more difficult.

## Structural and Institutional Factors

### Information Asymmetry

The pharmaceutical industry controls most of the clinical data about its own products and is not required to disclose negative trial results comprehensively. Despite reforms (such as ClinicalTrials.gov registration requirements), publication bias persists, and the industry maintains substantial control over the evidence base that physicians and regulators rely upon.

**Evidence**: A 2012 study in *PLOS Medicine* found that industry-funded clinical trials were significantly more likely to report positive results than independently funded studies (Bourgeois et al., 2012). A 2017 study found that approximately half of FDA-mandated post-marketing studies were never completed or their results never published (Woloshin et al., *JAMA*, 2017).

### Physician Dependence on Industry

Pharmaceutical companies have cultivated financial relationships with physicians through speaker fees, consulting arrangements, free samples, continuing medical education funding, and meals. These relationships influence prescribing behavior, often in ways physicians do not recognize.

**Evidence**: The Open Payments database (created by the Sunshine Act of 2010) documented $12.1 billion in industry payments to physicians and teaching hospitals between 2014 and 2023 (CMS, 2024). Studies consistently show that industry payments are associated with increased prescribing of the sponsoring company's products (DeJong et al., *JAMA Internal Medicine*, 2016).

### Consumer Vulnerability

Direct-to-consumer advertising creates demand for specific branded products, undermining physician-led prescribing decisions and driving requests for expensive drugs when equivalent or superior alternatives may exist. The United States and New Zealand are the only two developed nations that permit broadcast DTC pharmaceutical advertising.

**Evidence**: DTC advertising spending has grown from $791 million in 1996 to $6.58 billion in 2022 (Kantar Media, 2023). A 2005 FDA survey found that 65% of physicians felt that DTC ads led patients to believe drugs work better than they actually do (FDA, 2005).

### Enforcement Weakness and Impunity

The enforcement mechanisms available to address pharmaceutical industry misconduct are structurally inadequate. Federal agencies (FDA, DOJ, FTC) are outresourced by the industry they regulate, and the consequences of misconduct -- when enforcement occurs at all -- are financial penalties absorbed as costs of business rather than structural reforms or individual accountability.

**Evidence**: Between 2000 and 2024, the pharmaceutical industry paid more than $35 billion in federal fraud settlements, yet no executive at a major pharmaceutical company has been sentenced to significant prison time for marketing fraud. The DOJ's reliance on corporate deferred prosecution agreements and consent decrees -- rather than criminal prosecution of responsible individuals -- has failed to deter recurring misconduct. Pfizer entered into a corporate integrity agreement in 2004 (Neurontin), violated its terms, and entered another agreement in 2009 (Bextra), demonstrating that corporate-level enforcement does not alter institutional behavior.

### PBM Opacity and Vertical Integration

The consolidation of the PBM industry and its vertical integration with insurers and pharmacy chains has created a drug supply chain in which pricing is structurally opaque. PBM contracts with manufacturers and insurers contain confidentiality provisions that prevent independent verification of whether negotiated rebates are passed through to patients or retained as PBM profit.

**Evidence**: The FTC's 2024 interim report found that the three largest PBMs control approximately 80% of the market and that their vertically integrated model creates "perverse incentives." PBMs may prefer drugs with higher manufacturer rebates over clinically superior or lower-cost alternatives, because PBM revenue depends on rebate volume. The decline of independent pharmacies -- from approximately 23,000 in 2010 to fewer than 19,000 in 2023 -- reflects PBM practices that direct patients to affiliated pharmacies and set reimbursement rates below cost for independent competitors (NCPA, 2024).

## Why Reform Has Failed

### Legislative Obstruction

The pharmaceutical industry has successfully blocked or weakened every major reform effort for decades:

- **Medicare price negotiation** was proposed repeatedly from 2003 to 2022 before the Inflation Reduction Act's limited version passed. The industry spent an estimated $450 million on lobbying and advertising against the Build Back Better Act's drug pricing provisions in 2021 alone (OpenSecrets, 2022)
- **Drug importation** from Canada and other countries has been authorized in principle since 2003 but never implemented, because both FDA commissioners and HHS secretaries -- many with industry ties -- have consistently refused to certify the safety of imported drugs
- **Patent reform** bills have been introduced in every Congress for over a decade but have not advanced beyond committee due to opposition from both pharmaceutical and technology industry lobbies

### Incremental vs. Comprehensive Reform

The IRA's drug pricing provisions, while historic, illustrate the limitations of incremental reform: only 10 drugs were selected for the first round of negotiation, and the provisions do not apply to the commercial insurance market that covers approximately 155 million Americans. The pharmaceutical industry is challenging the IRA's negotiation provisions in multiple federal lawsuits, arguing that mandatory negotiations constitute unconstitutional takings.

### Complexity and Public Understanding

The pharmaceutical supply chain -- spanning manufacturers, PBMs, insurers, wholesalers, pharmacies, and multiple federal and state regulators -- is deliberately complex. This complexity serves incumbent interests by making it difficult for patients, policymakers, and journalists to identify who is responsible for high prices. Each actor in the supply chain points to others when questioned about pricing: manufacturers blame PBM rebate demands, PBMs blame manufacturer list prices, insurers blame both, and pharmacies blame PBM reimbursement rates. This diffusion of responsibility is itself a structural root cause, as it prevents the political consensus needed for reform.

### The Self-Reinforcing Cycle

The most fundamental root cause is the self-reinforcing nature of the entire system. Monopoly pricing generates enormous profits. Profits fund lobbying ($374 million annually) and campaign contributions ($90.5 million per election cycle). Lobbying blocks reform that would reduce profits. The cycle repeats and intensifies with each iteration, as each year of unreformed pricing generates additional resources for the industry to deploy against the next reform attempt. Breaking this cycle requires simultaneous action on multiple fronts -- any single reform leaves the reinforcing mechanisms intact.

### Fragmented Advocacy

Patient advocacy groups, which should serve as a counterweight to industry lobbying, are themselves heavily funded by pharmaceutical companies. A 2020 study found that 12 of the top 14 patient advocacy organizations in the U.S. received pharmaceutical industry funding, creating conflicts of interest that complicate their advocacy for lower prices and stronger regulation (McCoy et al., *Journal of General Internal Medicine*, 2020).

### International Comparison: Why the U.S. System Is Uniquely Dysfunctional

The root causes identified above are not inherent to pharmaceutical markets -- they are specific to the American regulatory and political environment. Other developed nations have addressed these challenges through mechanisms the U.S. has not adopted:

| Issue | U.S. Approach | International Approach | Result |
|-------|---------------|----------------------|--------|
| Drug pricing | No government price regulation (until IRA 2022, limited) | Mandatory price negotiation or reference pricing (UK, Germany, Canada, Japan, Australia) | U.S. prices 2-3x higher than OECD average |
| DTC advertising | Permitted (broadcast and digital) | Banned in all developed countries except New Zealand | DTC advertising drives demand for expensive branded drugs |
| FDA funding | 65% from industry user fees | Predominantly government-funded in EU, UK, Japan | U.S. regulator financially dependent on regulated industry |
| Patent manipulation | Limited enforcement; complex patent thickets tolerated | Stricter patent examination; shorter exclusivity periods (EU, Japan) | Longer effective monopoly periods in U.S. |
| Physician-industry relationships | Sunshine Act disclosure; no payment limits | Strict limits or bans on industry payments in many EU countries | Higher levels of industry influence on prescribing |
| Public research commercialization | Bayh-Dole: no price constraints on publicly funded discoveries | Many countries condition public research funding on reasonable pricing | U.S. taxpayers pay twice: for research and for products |

The persistence of these structural differences reflects the pharmaceutical industry's exceptional political influence in the United States -- lobbying expenditures, campaign contributions, and the revolving door -- rather than any principled policy determination that the American approach produces superior outcomes.
