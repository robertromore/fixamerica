# Pharmaceutical Industry: Stakeholders

The pharmaceutical industry is shaped by the interactions of several key groups, each with its own economic interests, political power, and influence over drug development, pricing, marketing, and regulation.

---

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Patients and consumers | Pay highest drug prices in developed world; 29% report not filling prescriptions due to cost | ~332 million (total population); 131 million prescription drug users | Affordable access; safe, effective medications; transparent pricing |
| Medicare beneficiaries | Limited formularies; coverage gaps; high out-of-pocket costs for specialty drugs until 2025 cap | ~67 million | Lower costs; broader formulary access; IRA implementation |
| Uninsured/underinsured Americans | No negotiated discounts; pay list prices or rely on manufacturer assistance programs | ~27 million uninsured; ~40 million underinsured | Affordable access; expanded coverage |
| Physicians and prescribers | Targeted by marketing; limited ability to compare drug costs for patients; opioid crisis liability | ~1.1 million physicians | Evidence-based prescribing information; reduced industry influence; malpractice protection |
| Pharmacists | Declining reimbursements from PBMs; increased administrative burden; drug shortage management | ~330,000 | Fair PBM reimbursement; reduced administrative burden; scope of practice expansion |
| Opioid crisis survivors and families | >700,000 deaths since 1999; millions with opioid use disorder; communities devastated | ~5.6 million with current OUD; millions of family members | Accountability; treatment access; settlement fund allocation |

### Secondary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|---------------|
| Employers | Bear significant drug costs through employer-sponsored insurance; rising pharmaceutical spending drives premium increases affecting competitiveness and worker compensation | PBM transparency; lower drug costs; alternative benefit models |
| State and local governments | Fund Medicaid drug spending (average 19% of state Medicaid budgets); manage opioid crisis response; administer opioid settlement funds | Medicaid drug cost containment; effective settlement fund allocation; PBM regulation authority |
| Taxpayers | Fund NIH research ($48.6 billion, FY2024) that underpins much of the drug development pipeline; subsidize pharmaceutical industry through tax credits, patent exclusivities, and government-funded basic research | Return on public investment; affordable access to publicly funded discoveries |
| Generic and biosimilar manufacturers | Face barriers to market entry from patent thickets, regulatory exclusivities, and PBM contracting practices | Reduced barriers to competition; patent reform; PBM formulary access |
| Academic researchers | Conduct federally funded basic research that pharmaceutical companies commercialize; face conflicts of interest from industry funding | Research funding; academic freedom; conflict of interest management |
| Independent pharmacies | Declining reimbursements from PBMs threaten viability; number declined from ~23,000 in 2010 to <19,000 in 2023 | Fair PBM reimbursement; prohibition on pharmacy steering; scope of practice expansion |
| Pharmaceutical industry employees | Approximately 900,000 U.S. jobs; workers may face ethical conflicts between employer directives and patient welfare | Job security; ethical business practices; whistleblower protections |
| Venture capital and biotech investors | Fund early-stage drug development; require return on high-risk investment | Intellectual property protections; regulatory predictability; exit opportunities |

### Pharmaceutical Industry Executives and Shareholders

Pharmaceutical company executives and institutional shareholders represent a distinct stakeholder category with interests that may diverge from those of the companies they manage and the patients their products serve:

- **Executive Compensation**: CEO compensation at major pharmaceutical companies averages $15-25 million annually, driven primarily by stock options and performance bonuses tied to revenue and share price targets. This compensation structure creates incentives to maximize short-term profitability through pricing strategies, patent extension, and marketing expenditure rather than long-term innovation
- **Institutional Shareholders**: The largest pharmaceutical companies are primarily owned by institutional investors (mutual funds, pension funds, hedge funds) that prioritize quarterly financial performance. Short-term investor pressure drives decisions to allocate capital to share buybacks ($577 billion by top 14 companies, 2016-2020) rather than R&D or price reductions
- **Sackler Family Model**: The Sackler family's extraction of an estimated $10-12 billion from Purdue Pharma before its bankruptcy represents an extreme case of owner-operators prioritizing personal wealth over corporate sustainability and public health

## Who Has Power

### Decision Makers

| Actor | Role | Authority | Resources | Influence Level |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| U.S. Congress | Legislation governing drug pricing, patents, FDA authority | Constitutional legislative power | Staff; CBO analysis; constituent pressure | High |
| FDA (CDER/CBER) | Drug approval, labeling, post-market surveillance, enforcement | Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act | ~$3.6 billion budget (FY2024); ~18,000 employees | High |
| CMS (Centers for Medicare & Medicaid) | Medicare/Medicaid drug coverage, reimbursement, negotiation | Social Security Act; IRA negotiation authority | $1.5 trillion in annual spending | High |
| FTC | Antitrust enforcement, PBM investigation, pay-for-delay litigation | FTC Act; Sherman Act | ~$400 million budget (FY2024) | Medium-High |
| U.S. Patent and Trademark Office | Patent examination and grants | Patent Act (35 U.S.C.) | ~$4 billion budget (self-funded by fees) | Medium |
| State Attorneys General | Consumer protection, antitrust, opioid litigation | State consumer protection statutes | Varies by state | Medium-High |
| President/HHS Secretary | Executive orders, regulatory priorities, IRA implementation | Executive authority; HHS organic statutes | Administrative power | High |

### Key Organizations and Institutions

#### Industry Trade Associations

| Organization | Role | Lobbying (2023) | Membership | Key Activities |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| PhRMA | Primary industry trade association | $29.3 million | ~30 major manufacturers | Lobbying, advertising campaigns, legal strategy, policy advocacy |
| BIO (Biotechnology Innovation Organization) | Represents biotech companies and startups | $12.8 million | ~1,000 companies | Patent protection, regulatory advocacy, venture capital liaison |
| PCMA (Pharmaceutical Care Management Association) | PBM trade group | $7.1 million | Top 6 PBMs | Defends PBM business model, opposes transparency mandates |
| GPhA/AAM (Association for Accessible Medicines) | Generic and biosimilar manufacturers | $5.4 million | ~100 manufacturers | Patent reform, FDA generic approval acceleration |

#### Government Agencies

- **National Institutes of Health (NIH)**: Funds $48.6 billion (FY2024) in biomedical research that forms the foundation of the drug development pipeline; NIH-funded research contributed to every one of the 210 drugs approved by the FDA from 2010-2016 (Galkina Cleary et al., *PNAS*, 2018)
- **Pharmacy Benefit Managers (CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, OptumRx)**: Control drug formularies, negotiate rebates, and determine pharmacy reimbursement for approximately 267 million Americans
- **Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA)**: Regulates controlled substance manufacturing, distribution, and prescribing; oversight role became politically significant during the opioid crisis, when DEA enforcement was weakened by pharmaceutical industry lobbying (documented in the *Washington Post* and *60 Minutes* investigation, 2017)
- **Government Accountability Office (GAO)**: Provides independent analysis and oversight of pharmaceutical industry practices, FDA performance, and drug pricing trends; reports inform congressional action

#### Professional and Advocacy Organizations

- **American Medical Association (AMA)**: Represents physicians; has advocated for reduced industry marketing to physicians, drug pricing reform, and malpractice protections related to prescribing. Voted in 2015 to support a ban on DTC advertising
- **AARP**: Represents 38 million members aged 50+; one of the most influential advocacy organizations for drug pricing reform and Medicare benefit improvements; played a pivotal role in IRA passage
- **Patients for Affordable Drugs**: Founded in 2017 as the first national patient organization focused exclusively on drug pricing reform; does not accept pharmaceutical industry funding, distinguishing it from most other patient advocacy organizations
- **Public Citizen**: Ralph Nader-founded advocacy organization with a dedicated Health Research Group focused on FDA reform, drug safety, and corporate accountability; publishes the "Worst Pills, Best Pills" newsletter
- **National Community Pharmacists Association (NCPA)**: Represents approximately 19,000 independent pharmacies; leading advocate for PBM reform and fair reimbursement practices
- **Association of American Medical Colleges (AAMC)**: Represents academic medical centers; has interests in both research funding (aligned with maintaining the Bayh-Dole framework) and reducing industry conflicts of interest in medical education

### Influencers

#### Patient Advocacy Organizations (with Industry Funding Conflicts)

| Organization | Focus Area | Industry Funding | Conflict of Interest Concern |
|---|---|---|---|
| American Cancer Society | Cancer research and treatment access | Receives pharmaceutical company donations | May be reluctant to oppose high cancer drug prices |
| American Diabetes Association | Diabetes treatment and research | Major insulin manufacturer sponsorships | Delayed advocacy for aggressive insulin pricing reform |
| National Organization for Rare Disorders (NORD) | Rare disease patient access | Significant pharmaceutical industry funding | Opposes reforms that might reduce orphan drug incentives |
| American Pain Foundation | Pain management (defunct 2012) | 90% funding from opioid manufacturers | Promoted aggressive opioid prescribing; shut down after congressional investigation |
| Cystic Fibrosis Foundation | CF treatment access | Received Vertex Pharmaceuticals royalties | Successfully negotiated price reductions but started from conflicted position |

A 2020 study found that 12 of the 14 largest patient advocacy organizations received pharmaceutical industry funding, creating potential conflicts that may limit their advocacy for lower prices and stronger regulation (McCoy et al., *J Gen Intern Med*, 2020).

#### Academic Medical Centers and Key Opinion Leaders

Academic medical centers shape clinical practice through research, training, and guideline development. They receive significant industry funding for clinical trials ($7.6 billion cumulative through Open Payments research payments, 2014-2023) and continuing medical education ($2.3 billion in 2022). Key opinion leaders -- influential physicians identified and cultivated by pharmaceutical companies -- play a particularly significant role in shaping prescribing patterns through conference presentations, guideline committees, and peer influence.

#### Investment Analysts and Shareholders

Pharmaceutical company stock prices and analyst expectations drive corporate strategy, often prioritizing short-term profitability over long-term public health outcomes. Analyst reports on pharmaceutical companies routinely assess pricing power, patent life, and competitive threats as primary valuation drivers, creating pressure on management to maximize monopoly rents during patent exclusivity periods.

#### Investigative Journalists and Media

Investigative journalism has been a critical force in pharmaceutical accountability. Key contributions include:

- **Patrick Radden Keefe** (*New Yorker*, 2017; *Empire of Pain*, 2021): Definitive investigation of the Sackler family and Purdue Pharma
- **Beth Macy** (*Dopesick*, 2018): Ground-level reporting on the opioid crisis
- **STAT News** (founded 2015): First major news organization dedicated to pharmaceutical and biotech coverage
- **ProPublica** "Dollars for Docs" project: Made Open Payments data accessible and searchable for the public
- **Washington Post** and *60 Minutes* DEA investigation (2017): Revealed how pharmaceutical industry lobbying weakened DEA enforcement during the opioid epidemic
- **Scott Higham and Lenny Bernstein** (*Washington Post*): Analyzed DEA pharmaceutical distribution data revealing massive opioid shipments to small towns

## Stakeholder Interests and Alignment

### Aligned Interests (Potential Coalition Partners)

| Coalition | Shared Interest | Strength | Key Challenge |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|---------------|
| Patients + AARP + Employers + State governments | Lower drug prices and greater transparency | Strong | Coordinating diverse actors with different priorities |
| Generic manufacturers + FTC + Consumer advocates | Removing anticompetitive barriers to generic/biosimilar competition | Strong | Generic industry has limited lobbying resources vs. brand manufacturers |
| Opioid survivors + State AGs + Public health organizations | Pharmaceutical industry accountability and effective settlement fund use | Strong | Settlement fund allocation disputes between state and local governments |
| Independent pharmacies + Small insurers + Patient groups | PBM reform and transparency | Moderate-Strong | PBMs are vertically integrated with the largest insurers, limiting insurer participation |
| Academic researchers + FDA career staff + Evidence-based medicine advocates | Strengthened FDA independence and post-market surveillance | Moderate | FDA career staff face career risk in publicly opposing agency leadership |
| Taxpayer advocates + NIH reform advocates + Open science organizations | Bayh-Dole reform and public return on research investment | Moderate | Universities dependent on licensing revenue oppose reforms |
| International health organizations + Comparative policy researchers | Adoption of international best practices (reference pricing, DTC ban) | Moderate | "American exceptionalism" arguments resist international comparisons |

### Conflicting Interests

| Conflict | Party A | Party B | Nature of Conflict |
|----------|---------|---------|-------------------|
| Pricing | Patients needing affordable drugs | Manufacturers maximizing revenue through monopoly pricing | Direct: every dollar of excess pricing is a dollar patients or insurers pay |
| Pharmacy viability | Independent pharmacies (~19,000 remaining) | PBMs steering patients to affiliated pharmacies | PBM reimbursement rates below cost drive pharmacy closures; 4,000+ independent pharmacies lost since 2010 |
| Generic competition | Generic/biosimilar manufacturers seeking market entry | Brand manufacturers using patent thickets and REMS abuse | Patent manipulation delays generic entry by years; costs consumers billions annually |
| Safety vs. speed | FDA career scientists identifying safety concerns | Industry and patient groups demanding faster approval | Accelerated approval pathway creates tension between access and evidence |
| Opioid settlement allocation | Communities needing treatment and prevention funding | State and local officials with discretionary spending authority | Risk that settlement funds are diverted to general budgets rather than evidence-based treatment |
| Research integrity | Independent academic researchers | Industry funding creating publication bias | Industry-funded trials 70% more likely to report positive results |
| CME independence | Physicians needing unbiased continuing education | Industry funding $2.3 billion annually in CME | Financial support creates indirect influence over medical education content |
| Public research commercialization | Taxpayers funding NIH research ($48.6 billion annually) | Companies commercializing publicly funded discoveries at monopoly prices | Bayh-Dole framework provides no public return on investment |

### The Pharmaceutical Iron Triangle

The pharmaceutical industry operates within a reinforcing power structure that connects industry, Congress, and the FDA:

1. **Industry to Congress**: Campaign contributions ($90.5 million in 2022 cycle), lobbying ($374 million annually), and revolving door hiring (>60% of PhRMA lobbyists are former government officials) create sustained influence over legislative outcomes
2. **Congress to FDA**: Congressional appropriations determine FDA resources; user fee reauthorizations give industry negotiating leverage over FDA performance metrics; congressional pressure on FDA decisions (e.g., accelerated approval controversies) reflects industry priorities communicated through lobbying
3. **FDA to Industry**: Drug approvals generate industry revenue; cooperative regulatory relationships facilitate industry operations; former FDA officials transition to industry positions, creating career incentives that influence current regulatory behavior
4. **The Reinforcing Cycle**: Industry profits fund more lobbying, which maintains favorable legislation, which maintains profits. Each iteration of the cycle strengthens the industry's position and makes reform more difficult

This iron triangle is supplemented by additional influence channels:

- **Industry to Academia**: Research funding, consulting fees, and speaker payments to physicians and researchers ($12.1 billion cumulative through Open Payments, 2014-2023) shape the evidence base and clinical practice
- **Industry to Patient Groups**: Pharmaceutical company funding to patient advocacy organizations ($6+ billion annually for DTC plus advocacy funding) creates the appearance of grassroots support for industry positions
- **Industry to Media**: Advertising revenue from DTC campaigns ($6.58 billion annually) creates financial relationships between pharmaceutical companies and media outlets, potentially influencing editorial coverage

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on pharmaceutical policy:

- **Lobbying**: The pharmaceutical industry spent $374 million on federal lobbying in 2023, more than any other industry sector. PhRMA alone employed 1,805 lobbyists, maintaining a ratio of 3.4 lobbyists per member of Congress (OpenSecrets, 2024)
- **Campaign Contributions**: Pharmaceutical and health products companies contributed $90.5 million in the 2022 election cycle, distributed across both parties with a slight Republican lean (OpenSecrets, 2024)
- **Revolving Door**: The movement of officials between government and industry creates networks of influence and shared perspectives. Over 60% of PhRMA's registered lobbyists are former government officials (OpenSecrets, 2024)
- **Astroturf Advocacy**: Industry-funded patient advocacy groups, physician societies, and disease awareness campaigns create the appearance of grassroots support for industry positions on issues such as drug pricing, patent protections, and accelerated approval
- **Direct-to-Consumer Advertising**: $6.58 billion in annual DTC advertising (2022) shapes patient demand and public perceptions of pharmaceutical products, creating political constituencies for industry positions
- **Academic Funding and Key Opinion Leaders**: Industry-funded clinical trials, consulting arrangements, and speaking fees create relationships with influential physicians who shape clinical practice and treatment guidelines. The Open Payments database recorded $12.1 billion in industry payments to physicians between 2014 and 2023 (CMS, 2024)
- **Legal Challenges**: The pharmaceutical industry has filed multiple lawsuits challenging the IRA's price negotiation provisions, arguing they constitute unconstitutional takings, compelled speech, and violations of due process
- **Clinical Trial Control**: By funding and designing the majority of clinical trials, industry controls the evidence base that regulators, physicians, and payers rely upon for decision-making. Publication bias -- the selective reporting of favorable results -- is well-documented (Bourgeois et al., *PLOS Medicine*, 2012)
- **Key Opinion Leader Programs**: The industry identifies and cultivates relationships with influential physicians who shape clinical practice and treatment guidelines. These "key opinion leaders" (KOLs) receive speaker fees, consulting payments, and research funding that create financial incentives aligned with industry marketing objectives
- **Continuing Medical Education (CME) Funding**: The pharmaceutical industry funded $2.3 billion in CME in 2022 (ACCME, 2023), shaping the ongoing education of physicians in ways that may favor industry products. While accreditation standards require independence, industry funding creates indirect influence through topic selection, speaker choice, and case study design

## Stakeholder Power Analysis

### Power Asymmetry

The most striking feature of the pharmaceutical stakeholder landscape is the extreme power asymmetry between the industry and those it affects:

| Dimension | Pharmaceutical Industry | Patients/Public |
|-----------|------------------------|-----------------|
| Lobbying spending | $374 million annually | Limited; Patients for Affordable Drugs annual budget ~$6 million |
| Campaign contributions | $90.5 million (2022 cycle) | Diffuse; no coordinated patient donor network |
| Legal resources | Top law firms on retainer; multiple simultaneous lawsuits | Limited pro bono representation; class action constraints |
| Information control | Controls clinical trial data; employs KOLs | Dependent on industry-funded evidence base |
| Revolving door access | Direct access through former officials | Limited to constituent contacts |
| Time horizon | Long-term lobbying relationships; decades of investment | Episodic engagement driven by personal health crises |

This power asymmetry explains why drug pricing reform consistently polls at 80-90% public support across party lines (KFF, 2023) yet has produced only incremental legislative action over decades. The concentration of industry resources against diffuse public interest is a textbook case of Mancur Olson's theory of collective action failure.

### Critical Reform Coalitions

Successful pharmaceutical reform will likely require coalitions that combine multiple stakeholder groups:

- **Pricing Reform Coalition**: AARP + employers + state governments + generic manufacturers + Patients for Affordable Drugs -- this coalition provides broad political cover and financial resources
- **FDA Independence Coalition**: Academic researchers + FDA career staff + Public Citizen + evidence-based medicine advocates -- this coalition has technical credibility but limited political resources
- **Opioid Accountability Coalition**: State attorneys general + opioid survivors + public health organizations + investigative journalists -- this coalition has moral authority and legal momentum
- **PBM Reform Coalition**: Independent pharmacies + small insurers + patient groups + bipartisan congressional members -- this coalition benefits from rare bipartisan agreement
- **Patent Reform Coalition**: Generic manufacturers + FTC + consumer advocates + some academic patent scholars -- this coalition has economic logic but faces opposition from the broader IP community
- **Marketing Reform Coalition**: AMA + consumer health advocates + public health researchers + international health organizations -- this coalition has medical authority but faces First Amendment legal barriers
- **Bayh-Dole Reform Coalition**: Taxpayer advocacy groups + open science organizations + affordable medicine advocates -- this coalition faces opposition from both industry and research universities

### Stakeholder Engagement Recommendations

For advocates seeking to advance pharmaceutical reform:

1. **Lead with pricing**: Drug pricing is the highest-salience issue with the broadest public support (80-90% across party lines) and the widest potential coalition
2. **Use bipartisan framing**: PBM reform and patent abuse have bipartisan opposition; avoid partisan framing that allows industry to exploit political divisions
3. **Leverage opioid accountability**: Public anger over the opioid crisis provides political cover for broader pharmaceutical reform that might otherwise face insurmountable industry opposition
4. **Engage employers**: Rising pharmaceutical costs directly affect employer-sponsored insurance premiums, making employers a natural but underutilized reform constituency
5. **Support independent journalism**: Investigative reporting has been the most effective force for pharmaceutical accountability; public support for investigative outlets strengthens the information ecosystem
