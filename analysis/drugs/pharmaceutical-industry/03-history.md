# Pharmaceutical Industry: History

## Historical Development

The pharmaceutical industry's relationship with American public health has evolved through distinct phases, from virtually unregulated patent medicine to one of the most powerful and heavily regulated sectors in the economy. At each stage, public health crises have driven regulatory responses, and at each stage, the industry has adapted to new rules while finding ways to preserve and expand its influence.

---

### The Patent Medicine Era (Pre-1906): Caveat Emptor

- **Unregulated Market**: Throughout the 19th century, Americans consumed a vast array of "patent medicines" -- proprietary concoctions containing alcohol, opium, cocaine, and other substances -- marketed with extravagant health claims and no requirement for ingredient disclosure or efficacy evidence
- **Advertising-Driven Industry**: Patent medicine advertising was the largest category of advertising in American newspapers by the late 1800s, establishing a marketing-driven business model that would persist into the modern era
- **Public Harm**: Widespread poisonings, addiction (particularly to opium-containing preparations), and deaths from adulterated products created growing public demand for regulation
- **The Muckrakers**: Samuel Hopkins Adams' series "The Great American Fraud" (1905-1906) in *Collier's Weekly* and Upton Sinclair's *The Jungle* (1906) catalyzed public outrage
- **Industry Opposition to Regulation**: The patent medicine industry fiercely opposed the Pure Food and Drug Act, demonstrating a pattern of industry resistance to regulation that would persist for more than a century. Newspapers dependent on patent medicine advertising revenues also opposed the law, illustrating the entanglement of commercial and editorial interests

---

### The Regulatory Foundation (1906-1961): Safety as the Standard

- **Pure Food and Drug Act (1906)**: The first federal law prohibiting the manufacture, sale, or transportation of adulterated or misbranded food and drugs; established the Bureau of Chemistry (precursor to FDA) as the regulatory body
- **Sulfanilamide Disaster (1937)**: Elixir Sulfanilamide, a liquid formulation of an antibiotic dissolved in toxic diethylene glycol, killed 107 people including many children. The manufacturer was charged only with "misbranding" (calling it an "elixir") because existing law did not require safety testing
- **Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (1938)**: Enacted in direct response to the Sulfanilamide disaster, this landmark law required manufacturers to prove drug safety before marketing -- the first premarket safety requirement in the world
- **Postwar Pharmaceutical Boom**: The development of penicillin, streptomycin, and other antibiotics during and after World War II transformed the pharmaceutical industry from a largely chemical-manufacturing enterprise into a science-driven, research-intensive sector. Federal wartime investment in penicillin mass production established the template for public-private partnerships in drug development
- **Industry Growth**: Between 1940 and 1960, pharmaceutical industry revenues grew from approximately $300 million to $2.3 billion, driven by new drug discovery and the expansion of employer-sponsored health insurance
- **Kefauver Senate Hearings (1959-1962)**: Senator Estes Kefauver's investigation into the pharmaceutical industry documented widespread price-fixing, excessive markups, and misleading advertising. The hearings revealed that some drugs were priced at 1,000-7,000% markups over manufacturing costs and that the industry's primary competitive tool was marketing, not innovation

---

### The Efficacy Era (1962-1991): Proving It Works

- **Thalidomide Crisis (1961-1962)**: The sedative thalidomide, widely prescribed in Europe for morning sickness, caused severe birth defects in more than 10,000 children worldwide. FDA reviewer Frances Kelsey's refusal to approve the drug in the U.S. prevented a domestic catastrophe and became a powerful argument for stronger regulation
- **Kefauver-Harris Amendment (1962)**: Required manufacturers to prove drug efficacy (not just safety) before marketing; established requirements for informed consent in clinical trials and adverse event reporting; gave the FDA authority to require good manufacturing practices
- **Drug Efficacy Study Implementation (DESI)**: The FDA retroactively reviewed approximately 3,400 drug products marketed between 1938 and 1962; found roughly one-third to be ineffective
- **Growing Industry Power**: By the 1970s, pharmaceutical companies had become major political donors and were beginning to build the lobbying infrastructure that would make them the dominant interest group in healthcare policy. PhRMA (then the Pharmaceutical Manufacturers Association) was established in 1958 and became the industry's primary lobbying vehicle
- **NIH Expansion**: Federal biomedical research funding expanded dramatically during this period, with NIH appropriations growing from $67 million in 1955 to $1.6 billion by 1975. This taxpayer-funded research infrastructure would become the foundation upon which the pharmaceutical industry built its product pipeline
- **Orphan Drug Act (1983)**: Provided incentives (seven-year market exclusivity, tax credits, reduced fees) for developing drugs for rare diseases affecting fewer than 200,000 Americans; intended to address market failure in rare disease research, but later exploited by companies to gain extended exclusivity for drugs with broader commercial applications

---

### The Bayh-Dole Revolution and Industry Consolidation (1980-1991)

- **Bayh-Dole Act (1980)**: Permitted universities and small businesses to patent inventions made with federal funding and license them exclusively to private companies. Proponents argued it would accelerate the transfer of publicly funded discoveries to the marketplace; critics noted it created a pipeline through which taxpayer-funded basic research would generate private monopoly profits without public return on investment
- **Stevenson-Wydler Technology Innovation Act (1980)**: Complemented Bayh-Dole by requiring federal laboratories to actively participate in technology transfer to the private sector
- **Patent Term Restoration**: The Drug Price Competition and Patent Term Restoration Act (Hatch-Waxman, 1984) included provisions extending patent terms to compensate for time lost during the FDA review process, establishing the principle that regulatory delay should not reduce the period of effective patent protection
- **Rise of the Blockbuster Model**: During the 1980s, the industry shifted from a diversified product portfolio approach to a model centered on identifying potential "blockbuster" drugs capable of generating more than $1 billion in annual sales. This strategic shift concentrated R&D investment on large-market conditions and high-revenue potential, sometimes at the expense of therapeutic areas with smaller patient populations
- **Early Merger Wave**: Pharmaceutical consolidation accelerated with mergers including SmithKline Beckman and Beecham Group (1989), Bristol-Myers and Squibb (1989), and Roche's acquisition of a majority stake in Genentech (1990), establishing the template for the megamergers that would characterize the 2000s
- **Growing Marketing Infrastructure**: Pharmaceutical companies began investing heavily in sales forces during this period. By 1990, the industry employed approximately 35,000 pharmaceutical sales representatives in the United States, a number that would grow to over 100,000 by 2005

---

### The Deregulation and Marketing Era (1992-2009): Speed, Advertising, and Influence

- **Prescription Drug User Fee Act (PDUFA, 1992)**: Created the user fee system under which pharmaceutical companies pay the FDA to review their applications, dramatically reducing review times from an average of 33 months (1987) to under 12 months by the 2000s. While the acceleration was welcomed by patients awaiting new therapies, critics noted that the fee structure created a financial dependency -- the FDA's drug review capacity became contingent on industry funding, fundamentally altering the regulator-regulated relationship. PDUFA has been reauthorized seven times, most recently in 2022 (PDUFA VII), with each reauthorization expanding the scope of user fee funding and industry negotiating leverage over FDA performance targets
- **Direct-to-Consumer Advertising (1997)**: The FDA Modernization Act of 1997 and a 1997 FDA guidance document relaxed rules on DTC advertising, enabling the broadcast advertising of prescription drugs. DTC ad spending grew from $791 million in 1996 to $4.9 billion by 2007. The American Medical Association called for a ban on DTC advertising in 2015, but the industry's First Amendment arguments and lobbying have prevented any legislative action. The United States and New Zealand remain the only developed nations that permit broadcast DTC pharmaceutical advertising
- **Hatch-Waxman Act (1984) and Generic Growth**: The Drug Price Competition and Patent Term Restoration Act ("Hatch-Waxman") created the Abbreviated New Drug Application (ANDA) pathway for generic drugs while granting brand manufacturers patent term extensions and data exclusivity periods. It increased generic competition but also created mechanisms (such as 30-month stays triggered by patent infringement suits) that brands would later exploit
- **Medicare Part D (2003)**: The Medicare Modernization Act created the Part D prescription drug benefit -- the largest expansion of Medicare since its creation -- but explicitly prohibited the federal government from negotiating drug prices, a provision that the pharmaceutical industry lobbied heavily to include. The "non-interference clause" (42 U.S.C. 1395w-111(i)) was the industry's top legislative priority, and its enactment was facilitated by then-Rep. Billy Tauzin (R-LA), who became PhRMA president two years later. The Congressional vote on the MMA was held open for an unprecedented three hours in a pre-dawn session as Republican leaders pressured reluctant members to switch their votes, an episode that critics described as emblematic of pharmaceutical industry influence over Congress
- **Blockbuster Drug Model**: The industry's business model increasingly centered on "blockbuster" drugs generating over $1 billion in annual sales. By 2000, the top 10 drugs accounted for more than $50 billion in combined annual revenue
- **Pay-for-Delay Settlements**: Brand-name manufacturers began paying generic companies to delay market entry, preserving monopoly pricing. The FTC estimated that these agreements cost consumers $3.5 billion annually (FTC, 2010)
- **Vioxx Scandal (1999-2004)**: Merck's arthritis drug Vioxx (rofecoxib) was withdrawn from the market in 2004 after evidence emerged that it significantly increased the risk of heart attack and stroke. Internal company documents revealed that Merck had been aware of cardiovascular risks as early as 2000 but continued to market the drug aggressively. The withdrawal came after an estimated 27,000-55,000 excess cardiac events. Merck ultimately paid $4.85 billion to settle approximately 27,000 lawsuits (Topol, *NEJM*, 2004)
- **Off-Label Promotion Enforcement**: The DOJ pursued a series of major off-label promotion cases during this period, resulting in some of the largest corporate fraud settlements in American history. Warner-Lambert (acquired by Pfizer) paid $430 million in 2004 for off-label promotion of Neurontin; Eli Lilly paid $1.4 billion in 2009 for off-label promotion of Zyprexa
- **Pharmaceutical Sales Force Peak**: The number of U.S. pharmaceutical sales representatives peaked at approximately 105,000 in 2005, representing one of the largest and most expensive marketing operations in any industry (Cegedim Strategic Data, 2006)
- **PhRMA Voluntary Code (2002, revised 2009)**: Facing growing criticism, PhRMA adopted a voluntary marketing code limiting certain gifts to physicians. However, the code was not legally binding and contained significant loopholes, and compliance was self-reported

---

### The Sunshine Act and Transparency Era (2010-2021)

- **Physician Payments Sunshine Act (2010)**: Section 6002 of the Affordable Care Act required pharmaceutical and medical device manufacturers to report all payments and transfers of value to physicians and teaching hospitals. The resulting Open Payments database, launched in 2013, has documented more than $12.1 billion in cumulative industry payments to physicians through 2023 (CMS, 2024)
- **FTC v. Actavis (2013)**: The Supreme Court held that pay-for-delay settlements could violate antitrust law and be challenged under the "rule of reason," providing the FTC with a legal framework to combat anticompetitive patent settlements
- **ACA Biosimilar Pathway (2010)**: The Biologics Price Competition and Innovation Act, enacted as part of the ACA, created a regulatory pathway for biosimilar approval, intended to bring biologic competition similar to what Hatch-Waxman achieved for small-molecule drugs. However, biosimilar uptake remained slow due to patent thickets, PBM contracting practices, and limited prescriber adoption
- **21st Century Cures Act (2016)**: Expanded accelerated approval pathways and accepted real-world evidence in drug review, intended to speed access to innovative therapies. Critics warned that the law weakened evidentiary standards and reduced the rigor of the approval process
- **Aducanumab Controversy (2021)**: The FDA's approval of aducanumab (Aduhelm) for Alzheimer's disease, over the near-unanimous objection of its own advisory committee, became a focal point for concerns about regulatory capture. An independent congressional investigation found "irregularities" in the agency's interactions with manufacturer Biogen, including collaborative document drafting that blurred the boundary between regulator and regulated party (House Energy and Commerce Committee, 2022)

---

### The Opioid Crisis and Accountability Era (2010-Present): Reckoning

- **Purdue Pharma and OxyContin**: Purdue Pharma launched OxyContin in 1996 with a marketing campaign that claimed the drug's controlled-release formulation made it less addictive than other opioids -- a claim internal documents showed the company knew to be misleading. Purdue spent an estimated $207 million marketing OxyContin from 1996 to 2001, including training and deploying approximately 1,000 sales representatives who targeted the highest-prescribing physicians. Key Purdue marketing tactics included:
    - Claiming that the risk of addiction from OxyContin was "less than 1%" based on a misrepresented letter to the *New England Journal of Medicine* (the "Porter and Jick" letter, a brief paragraph about hospitalized patients, not outpatient chronic pain users)
    - Targeting primary care physicians -- who lacked specialized pain management training -- as the primary prescribing audience
    - Funding pain advocacy organizations, medical education programs, and treatment guidelines that promoted opioid prescribing
    - Offering bonus incentives to sales representatives based on OxyContin prescribing volume in their territories
    - Internal documents, later revealed in litigation, showing that Sackler family members personally approved marketing strategies and revenue targets
- **2007 Federal Guilty Plea**: In 2007, Purdue Pharma and three executives pled guilty to federal charges of misbranding OxyContin and paid $634.5 million in fines -- a penalty that represented a small fraction of the billions in profits the drug generated and that failed to deter continued aggressive marketing
- **Industry-Funded Pain Advocacy**: The pharmaceutical industry funded and coordinated a network of medical societies, patient advocacy groups, and key opinion leaders to promote aggressive opioid prescribing for chronic pain. The American Pain Foundation, which received 90% of its funding from pharmaceutical companies, was shut down in 2012 after a congressional investigation exposed its industry ties. The Federation of State Medical Boards issued prescribing guidelines in 1998 that were funded by Purdue Pharma and discouraged regulatory action against physicians who prescribed opioids aggressively (Senate Finance Committee, 2012)
- **Opioid Prescribing Peak (2012)**: U.S. opioid prescribing peaked at 255 million prescriptions (81.3 per 100 persons), reflecting a two-decade campaign by the pharmaceutical industry, medical societies, and advocacy groups (some industry-funded) to normalize aggressive opioid prescribing for chronic pain
- **Transition to Illicit Opioids**: As regulatory restrictions and prescriber education reduced prescription opioid availability, millions of people with opioid dependence transitioned to heroin and subsequently to illicitly manufactured fentanyl, producing the deadliest phase of the epidemic. This transition demonstrated that the pharmaceutical industry had created a public health crisis whose consequences would extend decades beyond the original corporate misconduct
- **Mass Litigation**: Beginning in 2017, thousands of state and local governments filed lawsuits against pharmaceutical manufacturers, distributors, and pharmacies, producing more than $50 billion in settlements by 2024. The litigation was coordinated through multi-district litigation (MDL) proceedings in the Northern District of Ohio under Judge Dan Polster, who attempted to broker a global settlement
- **Distributor Accountability**: For the first time, pharmaceutical distributors -- McKesson, Cardinal Health, and AmerisourceBergen -- were held liable for failing to maintain effective controls against diversion, resulting in a combined $21 billion settlement. The distributors had shipped billions of opioid doses to small pharmacies in areas with populations too small to justify the volumes, a pattern documented by DEA databases and investigative journalists at the *Washington Post* and *Charleston Gazette-Mail*
- **Sackler Family Accountability**: The Sackler family, which owned Purdue Pharma and personally directed key marketing decisions, extracted an estimated $10-12 billion from the company before it declared bankruptcy. The Supreme Court's 2024 decision in *Harrington v. Purdue Pharma* initially rejected a bankruptcy plan that would have shielded the Sacklers from civil liability, though a revised $6 billion settlement was subsequently approved. No Sackler family member has faced criminal prosecution
- **Insys Therapeutics Prosecution (2019-2020)**: In a rare case of individual criminal accountability, Insys founder John Kapoor was convicted of RICO conspiracy for a scheme involving bribing physicians to prescribe Subsys, a fentanyl sublingual spray, often to patients who did not need it. Kapoor was sentenced to 66 months in prison. Several other Insys executives were also convicted, making the case an outlier in an enforcement landscape dominated by corporate settlements without individual prosecution
- **Inflation Reduction Act (2022)**: After decades of industry lobbying that blocked price negotiation, the IRA authorized Medicare to negotiate prices on a limited number of high-cost drugs, capped insulin copays at $35 for Medicare beneficiaries, and imposed inflation-based rebate penalties
- **FTC PBM Investigation (2024)**: The Federal Trade Commission launched an investigation into PBM practices, releasing an interim report in July 2024 finding "perverse incentives" in the vertically integrated PBM model

## Growth of Direct-to-Consumer Advertising

The relaxation of DTC advertising rules in 1997 produced one of the fastest expansions of commercial advertising in any industry:

| Year | DTC Advertising Spending | Key Developments |
|------|-------------------------|------------------|
| 1993 | $166 million | Pre-relaxation; primarily print advertisements with full prescribing information |
| 1996 | $791 million | FDA beginning to consider broadcast advertising guidance |
| 1997 | $1.1 billion | FDA Modernization Act; FDA draft guidance on broadcast ads |
| 2000 | $2.5 billion | Rapid expansion of television pharmaceutical advertising |
| 2004 | $4.2 billion | Vioxx advertised heavily before withdrawal |
| 2007 | $4.9 billion | Pre-recession peak |
| 2012 | $3.5 billion | Post-recession decline; industry shift toward digital |
| 2016 | $5.6 billion | Recovery and expansion into digital platforms |
| 2020 | $6.3 billion | Pandemic-era increase |
| 2022 | $6.58 billion | Record spending; television and digital combined |

*Source: Kantar Media; JAMA, Schwartz & Woloshin, 2019 (updated)*

The AMA voted in 2015 to support a ban on DTC pharmaceutical advertising, noting that it drives demand for expensive branded drugs when equivalent generics are available. However, the resolution has not produced legislative action, and the pharmaceutical industry has defended DTC advertising as protected commercial speech under the First Amendment.

The pharmaceutical industry's lobbying apparatus has grown steadily over the past four decades, making it the largest single-industry lobbying spender in the United States:

|------|----------------------------------------|---------------------|------------------------|

The industry's 2021 spending peak coincided with the legislative debate over the Build Back Better Act, which included drug pricing provisions significantly stronger than those ultimately enacted in the Inflation Reduction Act. PhRMA and allied groups spent an estimated $450 million on advertising against the bill's drug pricing provisions, contributing to the weakening of the final legislation (OpenSecrets, 2022).

## Lessons from History

### Pattern 1: Crisis-Driven Regulation

Every major pharmaceutical regulation in American history has followed a public health catastrophe: the Pure Food and Drug Act followed patent medicine abuses, the 1938 FD&C Act followed the Sulfanilamide disaster, the 1962 amendments followed thalidomide, and the Inflation Reduction Act followed decades of escalating prices and the opioid crisis. The pharmaceutical industry has successfully blocked prophylactic regulation, forcing the public to endure preventable harm before Congress acts.

### Pattern 2: Regulatory Adaptation and Capture

The pharmaceutical industry has consistently adapted to new regulations by finding creative ways to work within -- and around -- regulatory frameworks. The Hatch-Waxman Act was intended to increase generic competition but created patent-listing and litigation strategies that brands exploited to delay generics. PDUFA was intended to speed review times but created financial dependence of the FDA on industry fees. The orphan drug program was intended to incentivize rare disease research but has been used to gain extended exclusivity for drugs with far broader commercial applications.

### Pattern 3: Marketing Precedes Science

From patent medicines to OxyContin to DTC advertising, the pharmaceutical industry has consistently prioritized marketing over evidence-based communication. The opioid crisis demonstrated that aggressive marketing, combined with inadequate regulatory enforcement, can produce mass public health harm that persists for decades.

### Pattern 4: Accountability Delayed Is Accountability Diminished

The time lag between pharmaceutical industry misconduct and meaningful consequences -- typically 10-20 years -- allows enormous harm to accumulate and responsible individuals to benefit financially before facing any accountability. Purdue Pharma's 2007 guilty plea (eleven years after OxyContin's launch) imposed penalties that were trivial relative to profits and failed to prevent continued aggressive marketing. The Sackler family extracted billions in personal wealth before the company's 2019 bankruptcy.

### Pattern 5: Penalties as Cost of Business

The history of pharmaceutical fraud settlements reveals a pattern in which financial penalties, however large in absolute terms, are absorbed as a cost of doing business. GlaxoSmithKline's $3 billion settlement (2012) represented approximately 11% of annual revenue. Pfizer's $2.3 billion settlement (2009) was approximately 4.7% of annual revenue. In no case has a major pharmaceutical company fundamentally altered its business practices in response to fraud penalties alone. The absence of criminal prosecution for senior executives -- with the rare exception of John Kapoor of Insys Therapeutics -- means that the individuals who direct fraudulent conduct face no personal consequences.

### Pattern 6: Public Investment, Private Profit

The Bayh-Dole Act created a one-directional flow of value: taxpayer-funded basic research is patented by universities, licensed exclusively to private companies, and sold back to the public at monopoly prices. Despite the government's retention of theoretical "march-in rights" to compel licensing when products are not available on reasonable terms, no administration has ever exercised these rights. The pharmaceutical industry and major research universities have consistently opposed any interpretation of "reasonable terms" that includes pricing considerations, ensuring that the public investment-to-private profit pipeline operates without constraint.

## The Revolving Door: Historical Context

The movement of officials between government and the pharmaceutical industry has a long history that has intensified in recent decades:

| Period | Pattern | Examples |
|--------|---------|----------|
| 1970s-1980s | Occasional moves from FDA to industry | FDA commissioners generally returned to academia or private practice |
| 1990s-2000s | Systematic recruitment of regulators | Industry began actively recruiting senior FDA officials; congressional staff moved to pharmaceutical lobbying |
| 2010s-present | Institutionalized revolving door | 9 of 15 most recent FDA commissioners joined pharma/biotech boards; >60% of PhRMA lobbyists are former government officials |

Notable revolving door cases include:

- **Scott Gottlieb**: FDA Commissioner (2017-2019), joined Pfizer's board of directors in 2019, within months of leaving the agency
- **Mark McClellan**: FDA Commissioner (2002-2004), subsequently served on multiple pharmaceutical company boards and advisory panels
- **Billy Tauzin**: Chairman of the House Energy and Commerce Committee, became PhRMA president in 2005 (salary reportedly $2.5 million annually), after steering the Medicare Modernization Act through Congress with its prohibition on drug price negotiation
- **Tom Daschle**: Senate Majority Leader, became a healthcare industry advisor after leaving the Senate, earning substantial income from pharmaceutical industry consulting

The revolving door creates both direct influence (former officials lobbying their former colleagues) and indirect influence (the expectation of future industry employment may affect current regulatory decisions). A 2016 study in the *BMJ* found that 65% of FDA drug reviewers who left the agency went to work for or consult with the pharmaceutical industry (Piller, *Science*, 2018).
