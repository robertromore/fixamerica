# Racial Disparities in Drug Policy: Resources

This section provides a curated list of key research, government reports, legal sources, and advocacy organizations on racial disparities in drug enforcement, sentencing, and outcomes.

---

## Academic Research

### Peer-Reviewed Studies

- Alexander, Michelle. *The New Jim Crow: Mass Incarceration in the Age of Colorblindness*. The New Press, 2010. - Foundational analysis of how the War on Drugs created a racial caste system through mass incarceration.
- Agan, Amanda, and Sonja Starr. "Ban the Box, Criminal Records, and Racial Discrimination: A Field Experiment." *Quarterly Journal of Economics* 133, no. 1 (2018): 191-235. - Found that criminal records reduce job callbacks by 50%, with a larger penalty for Black applicants.
- Berdejo, Carlos. "Criminalizing Race: Racial Disparities in Plea Bargaining." *Boston College Law Review* 59, no. 4 (2018): 1187-1249. - Found that white defendants were 25% more likely to have their most serious charge dropped or reduced.
- Clear, Todd R. *Imprisoning Communities: How Mass Incarceration Makes Disadvantaged Neighborhoods Worse*. Oxford University Press, 2007. - Documented how incarceration destabilizes communities through "coercive mobility."
- Eberhardt, Jennifer L., et al. "Seeing Black: Race, Crime, and Visual Processing." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology* 87, no. 6 (2004): 876-893. - Demonstrated that implicit bias leads to association of Black faces with criminality.
- Lum, Kristian, and William Isaac. "To Predict and Serve?" *Significance* 13, no. 5 (2016): 14-19. - Showed that predictive policing algorithms replicate and amplify racial bias in historical arrest data.
- Mitchell, Ojmarrh. "A Meta-Analysis of Race and Sentencing Research: Explaining the Inconsistencies." *Journal of Quantitative Criminology* 21, no. 4 (2005): 439-466. - Meta-analysis finding significant racial disparities in sentencing, even after controlling for case characteristics.
- Netherland, Julie, and Helena Hansen. "The War on Drugs That Wasn't: Wasted Whiteness, 'Dirty Doctors,' and Race in Media Coverage of Prescription Opioid Misuse." *Culture, Medicine, and Psychiatry* 40 (2016): 664-686. - Compared racialized media framing of crack vs. opioid epidemics.
- Pierson, Emma, et al. "A Large-Scale Analysis of Racial Disparities in Police Stops Across the United States." *Nature Human Behaviour* 4 (2020): 736-745. - Stanford Open Policing Project analysis of 200+ million traffic stops finding racial disparities in stops and searches.
- Ramchand, Rajeev, Rosalie Liccardo Pacula, and Martin Y. Iguchi. "Racial Differences in Marijuana-Users' Risk of Arrest in the United States." *Drug and Alcohol Dependence* 84, no. 3 (2006): 264-272. - Demonstrated that racial disparities in marijuana arrests cannot be explained by differences in use or selling behavior.
- Shannon, Sarah K.S., et al. "The Growth, Scope, and Spatial Distribution of People with Felony Records in the United States, 1948-2010." *Demography* 54, no. 5 (2017): 1795-1818. - Estimated that one-third of Black men have felony convictions.
- Tonry, Michael. *Punishing Race: A Continuing American Dilemma*. Oxford University Press, 2011. - Comprehensive analysis of how racial bias operates throughout the criminal justice system.

### Working Papers and White Papers

- Rehavi, M. Marit, and Sonja B. Starr. "Racial Disparity in Federal Criminal Sentences." *Journal of Political Economy* 122, no. 6 (2014): 1320-1354. - Found that Black defendants receive 10% longer sentences than comparable white defendants in federal cases, largely driven by mandatory minimum charging decisions.
- Doleac, Jennifer. "The Effects of DNA Databases on Crime." *American Economic Journal: Applied Economics* 9, no. 1 (2017): 165-201. - Research on the effects of criminal justice policy on crime and communities.

## Government Reports and Data

### Federal Agencies

- U.S. Sentencing Commission. "Report to Congress: Cocaine and Federal Sentencing Policy." 2007. <https://www.ussc.gov/research/congressional-reports/2007-report-congress-cocaine-and-federal-sentencing-policy> - Key recommendation to eliminate crack/powder cocaine disparity.
- U.S. Sentencing Commission. "Annual Report and Sourcebook of Federal Sentencing Statistics." FY2022. <https://www.ussc.gov/research/sourcebook> - Authoritative data on federal sentencing by race, drug type, and departure rates.
- U.S. Sentencing Commission. "First Step Act of 2018 Resentencing Provisions: Retroactivity Data Report." 2023. <https://www.ussc.gov/research/data-reports> - Data on First Step Act retroactive resentencing beneficiaries (91% Black).
- U.S. Sentencing Commission. "Report to Congress: Mandatory Minimum Penalties in the Federal Criminal Justice System." 2017. <https://www.ussc.gov/research/congressional-reports/2017-overview-mandatory-minimum-penalties> - Documented racial disparities in mandatory minimum charging.
- Department of Justice. "Investigation of the Ferguson Police Department." 2015. <https://www.justice.gov/sites/default/files/opa/press-releases/attachments/2015/03/04/ferguson_police_department_report.pdf> - Found systematic racial bias in policing and municipal courts.
- SAMHSA. "National Survey on Drug Use and Health (NSDUH)." Annual. <https://www.samhsa.gov/data/nsduh> - Primary source for drug use rates by race and ethnicity.
- Federal Bureau of Investigation. "Crime in the United States (UCR/NIBRS)." Annual. <https://crime-data-explorer.fr.cloud.gov/> - Drug arrest data by race.
- Bureau of Justice Statistics. "Prisoners in 2022." NCJ 307149 (2023). <https://bjs.ojp.gov/library/publications/prisoners-2022> - State and federal incarceration data by offense type and race.

### Congressional Reports

- Congressional Research Service. "The Fair Sentencing Act of 2010: Background and Analysis." R41002 (2010). <https://crsreports.congress.gov/product/details?prodcode=R41002>
- Congressional Research Service. "Federal Sentencing Disparities: Selected Issues." R44825 (2022).
- Government Accountability Office. "Federal Criminal Sentencing: Sentences Imposed by Federal District Courts and Racial Disparity." GAO-17-72 (2017). <https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-17-72>

### Agency Data Portals

- U.S. Sentencing Commission Interactive Data Analyzer. <https://ida.ussc.gov/analytics/saw.dll?Dashboard> - Detailed federal sentencing data by offense type, race, gender, and district.
- FBI Crime Data Explorer. <https://crime-data-explorer.fr.cloud.gov/> - National arrest data by offense type and demographics.
- Stanford Open Policing Project. <https://openpolicing.stanford.edu/> - Traffic stop data from 200+ million stops across 21 state patrol agencies.

## Books

- Alexander, Michelle. *The New Jim Crow: Mass Incarceration in the Age of Colorblindness*. The New Press, 2010. - The most influential book on racial disparities in the criminal justice system.
- Baum, Dan. *Smoke and Mirrors: The War on Drugs and the Politics of Failure*. Little, Brown and Company, 1996. - History of the War on Drugs from Nixon through Clinton, including the Ehrlichman revelation.
- Clear, Todd R. *Imprisoning Communities*. Oxford University Press, 2007. - How mass incarceration damages the communities it claims to protect.
- Forman, James Jr. *Locking Up Our Own: Crime and Punishment in Black America*. Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2017. - Pulitzer Prize-winning examination of how Black leaders' support for tough-on-crime policies contributed to mass incarceration.
- Hinton, Elizabeth. *From the War on Poverty to the War on Crime: The Making of Mass Incarceration in America*. Harvard University Press, 2016. - Documents the policy decisions that built the carceral state.
- Mauer, Marc. *Race to Incarcerate*. The New Press, 2006. - Analysis of the political forces driving mass incarceration and its racial dimensions.
- Provine, Doris Marie. *Unequal Under Law: Race in the War on Drugs*. University of Chicago Press, 2007. - Examination of how drug laws have been shaped by racial politics.
- Tonry, Michael. *Malign Neglect: Race, Crime, and Punishment in America*. Oxford University Press, 1995. - Early and influential documentation of racial disparities in drug enforcement.

## Legal Resources

### Case Law

- *Whren v. United States*, 517 U.S. 806 (1996) - Upheld pretextual traffic stops, enabling racially disparate drug enforcement through minor traffic violations.
- *Washington v. Davis*, 426 U.S. 229 (1976) - Established that Equal Protection claims require proof of discriminatory intent, not merely disparate impact.
- *Terry v. Ohio*, 392 U.S. 1 (1968) - Established the "stop and frisk" standard, which has been applied with pronounced racial disparities.
- *Floyd v. City of New York*, 959 F. Supp. 2d 540 (S.D.N.Y. 2013) - Found NYPD's stop-and-frisk program unconstitutional as applied, violating the Fourth and Fourteenth Amendments.
- *United States v. Armstrong*, 517 U.S. 456 (1996) - Set an extremely high bar for selective prosecution claims, requiring defendants to show that similarly situated individuals of a different race were not prosecuted.
- *Kimbrough v. United States*, 552 U.S. 85 (2007) - Held that federal judges may consider the crack/powder cocaine disparity as a factor in sentencing below guidelines.
- *Dorsey v. United States*, 567 U.S. 260 (2012) - Held that the Fair Sentencing Act's reduced penalties apply to defendants sentenced after the Act's passage, even if their offense occurred before it.
- *Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard*, 600 U.S. 181 (2023) - Restricted race-conscious government programs, relevant to cannabis social equity program design.

### Statutory References

- Controlled Substances Act, 21 U.S.C. Sections 801-971 - Federal drug scheduling and penalty framework.
- Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1986, P.L. 99-570 - Established the 100:1 crack/powder cocaine sentencing disparity.
- Fair Sentencing Act of 2010, P.L. 111-220 - Reduced the crack/powder ratio from 100:1 to 18:1.
- First Step Act of 2018, P.L. 115-391 - Made the Fair Sentencing Act retroactive; reformed mandatory minimums.
- 42 U.S.C. Section 14141 (now 34 U.S.C. Section 12601) - Authorizes DOJ pattern-and-practice investigations of law enforcement agencies.
- 26 U.S.C. Section 280E - Prohibits cannabis businesses from deducting ordinary business expenses on federal taxes.
