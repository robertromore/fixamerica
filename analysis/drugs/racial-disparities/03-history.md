# Racial Disparities in Drug Policy: History

## Historical Development

Racial disparities in American drug enforcement are not an unintended byproduct of well-meaning policy -- they are a defining feature of drug prohibition from its origins. From the earliest narcotics laws of the early 1900s through the crack cocaine panic of the 1980s and the mass incarceration era that followed, racial animus, racial stereotyping, and racially selective enforcement have been embedded in the design, rhetoric, and implementation of drug policy. Understanding this history is essential to grasping why disparities persist and why structural reform -- not merely better intentions -- is required.

---

### Early Narcotics Regulation (1875-1937): Race as the Foundation of Prohibition

- **Anti-Chinese Opium Laws (1875-1909)**: The first American drug prohibition -- San Francisco's 1875 ordinance banning opium dens -- was explicitly targeted at Chinese immigrants. The law criminalized smoking opium in dens (associated with Chinese laborers) while leaving oral opium consumption (common among white Americans) legal. The federal Harrison Narcotics Tax Act of 1914 was debated in Congress with explicit racial rhetoric about "drug-crazed" minorities.

- **Anti-Cocaine Legislation and Black Americans (1900-1914)**: Early 20th-century campaigns for cocaine prohibition were fueled by lurid, fabricated newspaper accounts of Black men using cocaine and becoming violent, sexually aggressive, and impervious to bullets. Dr. Hamilton Wright, a key architect of the Harrison Act, testified to Congress in 1910 that cocaine made Black men rape white women. The *New York Times* published an article in 1914 titled "Negro Cocaine 'Fiends' Are a New Southern Menace" that claimed cocaine gave Black men superhuman strength and accuracy with firearms. Some Southern police departments reportedly switched to .32 caliber revolvers in response to these fabricated claims. These racial myths drove the inclusion of cocaine in federal narcotics control and established a template for using racialized drug panics to justify prohibition.

- **Marijuana Prohibition and Mexican Americans (1910-1937)**: The criminalization of marijuana was intertwined with anti-Mexican sentiment following the Mexican Revolution (1910-1920). Harry Anslinger, the first commissioner of the Federal Bureau of Narcotics (1930-1962), promoted marijuana prohibition using racist claims about Mexican and Black people. Anslinger told Congress that marijuana caused white women to seek relations with "Negroes" and that "most are Negroes, Hispanics, Filipinos, and entertainers" who used the drug. The Marihuana Tax Act of 1937 formalized federal prohibition. Notably, the American Medical Association opposed the Act, arguing that marijuana had medical uses and that the legislation was based on sensationalism rather than science.

- **Naming as Racial Strategy**: The use of the Spanish-derived term "marihuana" rather than the English "cannabis" was itself a deliberate strategy to associate the substance with Mexican immigrants and thereby generate public support for prohibition. Anslinger's Bureau consistently used "marihuana" in public communications and Congressional testimony to emphasize the substance's foreign and racialized connotations.

### Mid-Century Enforcement (1937-1970): Selective Enforcement Takes Shape

- **Jazz and Counterculture Targeting**: The Federal Bureau of Narcotics under Anslinger specifically targeted Black jazz musicians including Billie Holiday, who was arrested for heroin possession in 1947 and denied medical treatment while hospitalized, dying handcuffed to her hospital bed in 1959. The FBN used drug laws as tools to control Black cultural figures perceived as threatening racial hierarchies. Author Johann Hari documented in *Chasing the Scream* (2015) that Anslinger maintained personal files on prominent Black musicians and directed agents to surveil them specifically.

- **The Boggs Act (1951) and Daniel Act (1956)**: Congress established mandatory minimum sentences for drug offenses for the first time, setting a 2-year minimum for first-time marijuana possession and up to 10 years for repeat offenses. These laws fell disproportionately on urban communities of color. They were partially repealed in 1970 but established the template for later mandatory minimum regimes.

- **Shift Toward Treatment (1960s)**: President Kennedy's 1963 Community Mental Health Act and the Narcotic Addict Rehabilitation Act of 1966 briefly shifted federal policy toward treatment -- but primarily for white, middle-class heroin users. Black addicts were more likely to be channeled into the criminal justice system. This differential channeling -- treatment for white users, punishment for Black users -- established a pattern that would recur with striking consistency during the opioid crisis decades later.

- **The Controlled Substances Act (1970)**: The Comprehensive Drug Abuse Prevention and Control Act of 1970 consolidated federal drug law and created the scheduling system still in use today. While it repealed the Boggs Act mandatory minimums and included treatment provisions, it also established the framework for the enforcement escalation that would follow under Nixon and Reagan. Marijuana was placed in Schedule I -- the most restrictive category -- despite a recommendation by Nixon's own Shafer Commission in 1972 that marijuana possession be decriminalized.

### The War on Drugs Begins (1971-1985): Racial Targeting by Design

- **Nixon's War on Drugs (1971)**: President Richard Nixon declared drug abuse "public enemy number one" in June 1971 and launched the War on Drugs, creating the Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) in 1973. John Ehrlichman, Nixon's domestic policy advisor, later acknowledged the racial and political motivations in a 1994 interview published by Harper's Magazine in 2016: "We knew we couldn't make it illegal to be either against the war or Black, but by getting the public to associate the hippies with marijuana and Blacks with heroin, and then criminalizing both heavily, we could disrupt those communities."

- **Heroin Crackdown**: Nixon-era enforcement concentrated on heroin, which was associated in public discourse with Black urban communities. Federal funding surged for enforcement in cities while treatment funding, though initially increased, was soon eclipsed by enforcement spending. Notably, the Nixon administration did significantly expand methadone maintenance treatment -- but this expansion was motivated by crime reduction (reducing property crime by heroin addicts) rather than by concern for the health of users, and the treatment expansion was dwarfed by the enforcement apparatus created alongside it.

- **COINTELPRO and Drug Enforcement as Political Suppression**: The FBI's Counter Intelligence Program (COINTELPRO, 1956-1971) used drug enforcement as one tool for suppressing Black political organizations. FBI documents released through FOIA requests revealed that the Bureau targeted Black Panther Party members for drug arrests and used drug informants to infiltrate civil rights organizations. This intersection of political surveillance and drug enforcement established a precedent for the use of drug laws as instruments of political control that persisted beyond COINTELPRO's formal termination.

- **Rockefeller Drug Laws (1973)**: New York Governor Nelson Rockefeller signed the nation's harshest drug sentencing laws, imposing mandatory minimum sentences of 15 years to life for possession of four ounces of narcotics. These laws devastated Black and Latino communities in New York City. By 2002, 94% of those imprisoned under the Rockefeller Laws were Black or Hispanic (Correctional Association of New York, 2003). The Rockefeller Laws served as the template for mandatory minimum drug sentencing across the country. At least 20 states adopted similar mandatory minimum structures in the following two decades.

- **LEAA and the Federalization of Local Drug Enforcement (1970s)**: The Law Enforcement Assistance Administration (LEAA), created by the Omnibus Crime Control and Safe Streets Act of 1968, funneled billions of federal dollars to state and local police departments for drug enforcement. The LEAA funded the creation of specialized drug task forces, provided equipment for undercover operations, and incentivized aggressive drug enforcement metrics. Federal funding transformed local policing: departments that had previously treated drug use as a low-priority offense began dedicating substantial resources to drug arrests, disproportionately in minority communities where enforcement was politically costless. The LEAA was disbanded in 1982 but its successor programs (including the Byrne Justice Assistance Grants) continued the same funding model.

- **Reagan Escalation (1981-1985)**: President Ronald Reagan dramatically escalated the War on Drugs, increasing federal drug enforcement spending from $1.5 billion in FY1981 to $4.2 billion by FY1986. First Lady Nancy Reagan's "Just Say No" campaign simplified drug policy into a moral crusade. The Administration on its own initiative began the military's involvement in drug interdiction.

- **Civil Asset Forfeiture Expansion (1984)**: The Comprehensive Crime Control Act of 1984 expanded federal civil asset forfeiture powers and allowed state and local law enforcement agencies to share in seized assets through "equitable sharing" agreements with the DOJ and Treasury. This created a direct financial incentive for drug enforcement -- agencies could seize cash, vehicles, and property from suspected drug offenders without a criminal conviction, and retain a portion for their own budgets. The program disproportionately targeted communities of color, where cash-based economies and lack of access to legal representation made seized property difficult to recover.

- **Media and the "Crack Baby" Myth**: In the mid-1980s, media outlets amplified fears about "crack babies" -- children allegedly born with irreversible brain damage due to maternal crack cocaine use. These stories, heavily racialized in their framing, were subsequently debunked by longitudinal research. A 2013 review in the *Journal of Pediatrics* by Ackerman et al. found that the effects of prenatal cocaine exposure were modest and largely explained by poverty, malnutrition, and environmental factors. But the myth served its political purpose: building public support for punitive crack cocaine laws.

### The Crack Era (1986-1994): Disparity Codified in Law

- **Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1986**: In the wake of Len Bias's cocaine-related death, Congress passed the Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1986, which established the 100:1 crack-to-powder cocaine sentencing disparity. Under this law, possession of 5 grams of crack cocaine (the form predominantly used by Black Americans) triggered the same 5-year mandatory minimum as 500 grams of powder cocaine (the form predominantly used by white Americans). The law was passed with minimal deliberation -- there were no hearings and no testimony from pharmacological experts, judges, or the Sentencing Commission.

- **Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1988**: This law created the federal "drug kingpin" statute with a potential death penalty for major drug traffickers, established the Office of National Drug Control Policy ("drug czar"), and created the "crack house" statute. It also added the public housing "one-strike" eviction policy, which allowed federally assisted housing authorities to evict entire households if any member or guest was involved in drug activity.

- **The 1994 Crime Bill**: The Violent Crime Control and Law Enforcement Act of 1994 expanded federal death penalty eligibility to 60 additional offenses including large-scale drug trafficking, provided $9.7 billion for prison construction, established "three strikes" mandatory life sentences, and funded 100,000 new police officers. The Congressional Black Caucus voted for the bill, but with significant internal dissent, viewing it as a painful trade-off to secure the bill's Violence Against Women Act provisions and an assault weapons ban.

- **Racial Impact of the Crack Era**: Between 1986 and 2000, the number of Black Americans incarcerated in state prisons for drug offenses increased by a factor of 4.6, from approximately 25,000 to 115,000 (Human Rights Watch, 2000). By 1996, Black Americans constituted 84.5% of federal crack cocaine convictions (USSC, 1997).

- **The Role of the Congressional Black Caucus**: The CBC's initial support for the 1986 Anti-Drug Abuse Act reflected the genuine crisis facing Black communities during the crack epidemic -- soaring violence, addiction, and neighborhood destruction. As historian Michael Javen Fortner documented in *Black Silent Majority* (2015), many Black civic leaders, clergy, and residents actively demanded aggressive law enforcement responses to the crack epidemic. However, the resulting legislation was designed by white lawmakers, with penalty structures that bore no relation to the CBC's preferences. By 1995, when the U.S. Sentencing Commission recommended eliminating the crack/powder disparity, CBC members unanimously supported the recommendation. Congress, however, voted to reject it -- the only time in history it has overridden a Sentencing Commission recommendation.

- **Mass Incarceration at Scale**: The combined effect of the 1986, 1988, and 1994 legislation was the largest expansion of incarceration in American history. The total U.S. prison population grew from approximately 500,000 in 1980 to 2.3 million by 2008 (Bureau of Justice Statistics). Drug offenses accounted for more of this growth than any other category of crime, and Black Americans bore the heaviest burden: by 2001, the Black drug incarceration rate was 12 times the white rate in some states (Human Rights Watch, 2003). Michelle Alexander's *The New Jim Crow* (2010) argued that this system constituted a new form of racial caste, operating through the ostensibly race-neutral mechanism of drug enforcement.

### Reform Begins (1995-2015): Slow, Partial Corrections

- **U.S. Sentencing Commission Reports (1995-2002)**: The U.S. Sentencing Commission issued four reports recommending reduction or elimination of the crack/powder disparity in 1995, 1997, 2002, and 2007. Congress rejected the 1995 recommendation -- the only time it has overridden a Sentencing Commission recommendation.

- **Declining Crime and the "Smart on Crime" Shift**: As crime rates fell sharply from their early-1990s peak, political space opened for reform. Conservative commentators including Pat Nolan and Newt Gingrich joined reform coalitions, arguing that mass incarceration was fiscally unsustainable. The "Right on Crime" initiative, launched in 2010 by the Texas Public Policy Foundation with support from Nolan, Gingrich, and other conservative figures, provided critical political cover for Republican lawmakers to support sentencing reform without appearing "soft on crime."

- **State Marijuana Decriminalization and Medical Cannabis (1990s-2000s)**: Beginning with California's Proposition 215 (1996), which legalized medical marijuana, a growing number of states began relaxing marijuana laws. By 2010, 14 states had legalized medical cannabis, and several had decriminalized possession of small amounts. These state-level changes began to erode the political consensus supporting marijuana prohibition, though they did not immediately reduce arrest disparities -- the ACLU documented that marijuana arrest disparities persisted even in decriminalization states.

- **Federal Crack Cocaine Retroactivity Battles (2007-2008)**: The U.S. Sentencing Commission voted in 2007 to make its crack cocaine guideline amendments retroactive, potentially affecting approximately 19,500 federal prisoners. The retroactive application was implemented in 2008 over the objections of the Bush administration's Department of Justice, which had argued against retroactivity. This represented an important institutional assertion by the Commission and a precursor to the larger retroactivity provisions in the First Step Act a decade later.

- **Fair Sentencing Act of 2010**: After 15 years of advocacy, Congress passed the Fair Sentencing Act, reducing the crack/powder cocaine sentencing disparity from 100:1 to 18:1 and eliminating the mandatory minimum for simple crack possession. President Obama signed it on August 3, 2010. The law was prospective only, leaving approximately 12,000 federal prisoners serving sentences under the old 100:1 ratio.

- **Holder Memoranda (2013-2014)**: Attorney General Eric Holder issued the "Smart on Crime" initiative, directing federal prosecutors to avoid charging mandatory minimum sentences in certain low-level, non-violent drug cases ("charge below the mandatory minimum") and to expand use of compassionate release. This policy reduced the Black-white gap in federal drug sentencing during Holder's tenure but was reversed under Attorney General Jeff Sessions in 2017. The Holder-Sessions reversal demonstrated a critical structural vulnerability: executive-branch charging policies are inherently fragile and reversible. This experience informed the advocacy strategy for statutory codification of charging guidelines.

- **Obama Clemency Initiative (2014-2017)**: President Obama commuted the sentences of 1,715 federal drug prisoners -- more than the previous 12 presidents combined. The overwhelming majority of beneficiaries were Black or Hispanic individuals serving mandatory minimum sentences for nonviolent drug offenses. While the clemency initiative was unprecedented in scale, it affected only a small fraction of the approximately 80,000 federal drug inmates, and the individualized petition process was slow and resource-intensive.

- **State-Level Reforms**: Between 2000 and 2015, numerous states reformed drug sentencing laws. New York partially repealed the Rockefeller Laws in 2004 and 2009, reducing mandatory minimums and expanding judicial discretion in drug sentencing. California voters approved Proposition 47 in 2014, reclassifying drug possession from a felony to a misdemeanor, leading to significant reductions in incarceration. Texas, Georgia, and other Republican-controlled states enacted sentencing reform and expanded drug courts, often motivated by fiscal considerations rather than racial equity.

- **DOJ Pattern-and-Practice Investigations (2009-2016)**: Under the Obama administration, the Department of Justice's Civil Rights Division conducted pattern-and-practice investigations of police departments in Ferguson, Baltimore, Chicago, Cleveland, and other cities, documenting systematic racial disparities in policing including drug enforcement. These investigations produced consent decrees requiring reforms in stop-and-search practices, data collection, and use of force. The investigations provided the most detailed government documentation of how racially biased policing operates at the local level.

- **Colorado and Washington Legalize Cannabis (2012)**: Colorado and Washington became the first states to legalize recreational cannabis through ballot initiatives in 2012. These early legalization states did not include robust social equity provisions, and the resulting markets were dominated by white entrepreneurs. The absence of equity programs in first-mover states provided cautionary examples that shaped later legalization efforts in Illinois, New York, and other states.

### Contemporary Era (2016-Present): Partial Progress, Persistent Disparities

- **First Step Act (2018)**: The most significant federal criminal justice reform in a generation, the First Step Act made the Fair Sentencing Act retroactive, expanded the safety valve for mandatory minimums, allowed for earned time credits, and reformed mandatory sentences for certain drug offenses. Approximately 4,000 people received sentence reductions under the retroactivity provision, 91% of whom were Black (USSC, 2023). The Act passed with broad bipartisan support, 87-12 in the Senate.

- **Cannabis Legalization Wave (2016-2024)**: Beginning with California, Massachusetts, Nevada, and Maine in 2016, a wave of state cannabis legalization created a $33.6 billion legal industry by 2023. However, the economic benefits have accrued overwhelmingly to white entrepreneurs, prompting social equity programs in states including Illinois, New York, New Jersey, and Connecticut.

- **George Floyd and Racial Justice (2020-2021)**: The murder of George Floyd in May 2020 catalyzed the largest racial justice protests in American history and brought renewed scrutiny to racially disparate policing and drug enforcement. Several cities deprioritized low-level drug possession enforcement. President Biden issued an executive order in May 2022 promoting accountability in federal law enforcement.

- **Oregon's Decriminalization Experiment (2020-2024)**: Oregon voters approved Measure 110 in November 2020, decriminalizing personal possession of all drugs. The measure was intended partly to address racial disparities -- Black Oregonians, 2% of the population, comprised 6% of drug possession arrests. In 2024, Oregon partially reversed course, recriminalizing public drug use amid concerns about implementation.

- **Sessions and Trump Reversal**: Attorney General Jeff Sessions in 2017 rescinded the Holder-era charging memoranda, directing prosecutors to charge the most serious readily provable offense in every case. This reversal was associated with a temporary increase in racial disparities in federal charging (USSC, 2019).

- **The Opioid Epidemic as Racial Mirror**: The most revealing historical comparison in the contemporary era is the contrast between policy responses to the crack epidemic (1986-1995) and the opioid epidemic (2010-present). When crack cocaine devastated Black urban communities, the policy response was overwhelmingly punitive: mandatory minimums, prison construction, and militarized policing. When prescription opioids and heroin devastated white rural and suburban communities, the policy response was overwhelmingly medical: expanded treatment access, naloxone distribution, compassion-centered media coverage, and bipartisan legislation (the SUPPORT for Patients and Communities Act, 2018). This comparison -- documented systematically by Netherland and Hansen in *Social Science & Medicine* (2016) -- does not prove that the opioid response was wrong, but that the crack response was racially driven.

- **Biden Administration (2021-2025)**: President Biden issued an executive order in May 2022 on policing reform, including provisions on data collection and use-of-force reporting. The administration supported cannabis rescheduling (DEA rulemaking to move marijuana from Schedule I to Schedule III, initiated in 2024) and issued pardons for federal simple marijuana possession convictions in October 2022, though relatively few individuals were directly affected because most marijuana convictions occur at the state level.

## Statistical Summary: The Arc of Disparity

| Period | Black Drug Imprisonment Rate (per 100,000) | White Rate | Ratio | Key Policy Driver |
|--------|---------------------------------------------|------------|-------|-------------------|
| 1980 | ~150 | ~20 | ~7.5:1 | Pre-crack, early War on Drugs |
| 1990 | ~600 | ~50 | ~12:1 | Crack-era mandatory minimums at full force |
| 2000 | ~891 | ~102 | ~8.7:1 | Peak incarceration era |
| 2010 | ~603 | ~88 | ~6.9:1 | Post-Fair Sentencing Act; state reforms beginning |
| 2020 | ~469 | ~81 | ~5.8:1 | Post-First Step Act; cannabis legalization wave |

*Source: The Sentencing Project, 2016; 2023*

The ratio has declined from its peak of approximately 12:1 around 1990 to 5.8:1 in 2020 -- a significant improvement in relative terms, but a disparity of nearly 6:1 remains far in excess of any plausible difference in drug-related behavior. The absolute Black drug imprisonment rate has declined by approximately 50% from its peak, driven partly by sentencing reform and partly by declining drug arrests overall. However, the white imprisonment rate has remained relatively stable, indicating that the narrowing of the gap is primarily due to reduced Black incarceration rather than any systematic recalibration of enforcement across racial groups.

## Lessons from History

### Pattern 1: Drug Laws as Instruments of Racial Control

From the anti-Chinese opium ordinances of the 1870s through the crack cocaine laws of the 1980s, American drug prohibitions have repeatedly been enacted during periods of racial anxiety and have targeted substances associated with disfavored racial or ethnic groups. The pattern is remarkably consistent: a substance is associated with a minority group through media campaigns and political rhetoric, severe penalties are enacted with minimal deliberation, enforcement concentrates on minority communities, and the resulting disparities are defended as race-neutral law enforcement. This pattern was not hidden from legislators -- it was the explicit rationale for many of these laws.

### Pattern 2: Reform Follows Changing Demographics of Impact

Political will for drug policy reform has historically emerged when the consequences of harsh enforcement begin to affect white communities. The opioid crisis -- which disproportionately affected rural white Americans -- generated a public health framing that had been largely absent from the crack cocaine epidemic. The bipartisan support for the First Step Act reflected, in part, the recognition by Republican legislators that their constituents were now affected by mandatory minimums and inadequate treatment access.

### Pattern 3: Partial Reforms Leave Structural Disparities Intact

Each major reform -- the Fair Sentencing Act, the First Step Act, state-level sentencing reforms -- has reduced disparities at the margins while leaving the structural architecture of racially disparate enforcement in place. Arrest disparities have not declined despite cannabis legalization. Prosecutorial discretion remains unconstrained. Collateral consequences continue to cascade. The lesson is that incremental reform of individual laws is necessary but insufficient; structural transformation of enforcement systems, data transparency requirements, and community investment is required.

### Pattern 4: The Bipartisan Window Is Narrow and Fragile

The First Step Act demonstrated that bipartisan criminal justice reform is achievable, but the political conditions that enabled it -- a Republican president seeking to differentiate himself from his predecessors, a strong economy, and declining crime rates -- were historically unusual. When crime rates increase or when "law and order" rhetoric gains political traction, the bipartisan reform window closes rapidly. Oregon's reversal of Measure 110 and the political backlash against progressive prosecutors in San Francisco, Los Angeles, and other cities illustrate how quickly reform momentum can dissipate.

### Pattern 5: Affected Communities Have Been Systematically Excluded from Policy Design

Throughout this history, the communities most affected by racially disparate drug enforcement have had the least influence over the policies that target them. Felon disenfranchisement -- which affects 1 in 16 Black adults nationally and 1 in 5 in states like Tennessee and Florida (Sentencing Project, 2022) -- removes the political voice of those most directly harmed. The war on drugs has thus been, in significant part, a policy imposed on politically disempowered communities by actors who do not bear its costs.
