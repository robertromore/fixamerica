# Racial Disparities in Drug Policy: Stakeholders

The landscape of racial disparities in drug enforcement is shaped by the interactions of communities directly harmed by disparate enforcement, law enforcement agencies that carry out drug policy, prosecutors who exercise charging discretion, legislators who set sentencing structures, reform organizations, and a growing cannabis industry whose ownership demographics reflect the very disparities reform seeks to address.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Black Americans arrested or incarcerated for drug offenses | Disproportionate arrest, prosecution, and incarceration; collateral consequences in employment, housing, voting | ~330,000 Black individuals arrested annually for drug offenses; ~120,000 incarcerated for drug offenses (federal + state) | Equal enforcement, sentencing reform, record expungement, restoration of rights |
| Latino/Hispanic Americans targeted in drug enforcement | Disproportionate targeting in border enforcement, fentanyl and methamphetamine cases; immigration consequences | ~225,000 Hispanic individuals arrested annually for drug offenses; 42.7% of federal drug offenders | Equal enforcement, immigration consequence relief, equitable sentencing |
| Families of incarcerated individuals | Family disruption, economic hardship, children growing up with incarcerated parents | ~2.7 million children have an incarcerated parent; disproportionately Black and Hispanic | Family reunification, collateral consequence reform, economic support |
| Communities with concentrated drug enforcement | Neighborhood destabilization, erosion of police trust, economic disinvestment | Hundreds of communities, primarily in urban areas, with high per-capita drug arrest rates | Community investment, policing reform, economic opportunity |
| People with drug felony convictions | Lifetime collateral consequences in housing, employment, education, voting | ~14 million Americans with drug felony convictions (cumulative) | Expungement, record sealing, ban-the-box employment policies, restoration of voting rights |

### Secondary Stakeholders

- **Drug users across all racial groups**: Approximately 59.3 million Americans aged 12+ used illicit drugs in the past year (SAMHSA NSDUH, 2023), but only a fraction are arrested, and those arrested are disproportionately people of color. White drug users, who constitute the majority of users in absolute numbers, benefit from a system that directs enforcement elsewhere.
- **Public housing residents**: Drug enforcement in and around public housing affects residents who may face eviction under "one-strike" policies for any household member's drug activity, regardless of personal involvement. Approximately 2 million Americans live in federally assisted public housing, disproportionately Black and Hispanic families.
- **Students**: Young people with drug convictions face barriers to federal financial aid and school enrollment, disproportionately affecting Black and Hispanic students. The school-to-prison pipeline intersects with drug enforcement when school resource officers initiate drug arrests on campus, with documented racial disparities in which students are arrested versus counseled.
- **Employers and the labor market**: Criminal records reduce the employable labor pool and cost the economy an estimated $87 billion annually in lost GDP (Center for Economic and Policy Research, 2016). Employers who rely on criminal background checks unwittingly perpetuate the effects of racially disparate drug enforcement.
- **Tribal Nations and Native Americans**: Native Americans face drug enforcement disparities compounded by jurisdictional complexity (tribal, state, and federal authority overlap), limited treatment resources on reservations, and the Indian Health Service's chronic underfunding. American Indian/Alaska Native individuals have the highest overdose death rate of any racial group (56.1 per 100,000, CDC 2023) but the least access to medication-assisted treatment.
- **Immigrant communities**: Non-citizen residents face deportation as a consequence of drug convictions, including for offenses that would be misdemeanors for citizens. This creates a distinct layer of disparity for Latino immigrants in particular, who face both the criminal justice consequences and the immigration consequences of drug enforcement targeting.

### Tertiary Stakeholders

- **Taxpayers**: The fiscal cost of racially disparate drug enforcement is substantial. Drug-related incarceration costs approximately $47.8 billion annually (Bureau of Justice Statistics, 2022). The RAND Corporation has estimated that drug treatment is seven times more cost-effective than domestic law enforcement at reducing drug consumption.
- **Healthcare systems**: Emergency departments, hospitals, and public health departments bear the costs of untreated substance use disorders in communities where incarceration rather than treatment is the primary response to drug use.
- **Legal profession**: Public defenders, legal aid attorneys, and pro bono lawyers provide representation in drug cases and expungement proceedings. The chronic underfunding of public defense systems means that drug defendants in minority communities receive less effective legal representation than those with private counsel.

## Who Has Power

### Decision Makers

| Actor | Role | Authority | Resources | Influence Level |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| U.S. Congress | Sets federal drug sentencing law, mandatory minimums, funding levels | Legislative authority over Controlled Substances Act, sentencing statutes | $46.1 billion federal drug control budget (FY2025) | High |
| U.S. Attorney General / DOJ | Sets federal prosecution policy, charging guidelines | Directs 93 U.S. Attorneys, oversees DEA, BOP | 113,000+ DOJ employees; $38.6 billion budget (FY2024) | High |
| State legislatures | Set state drug sentencing laws, decriminalization policies, cannabis legalization | Plenary state legislative authority | Varies by state | High |
| Local prosecutors (~2,300 elected DAs) | Charging decisions, plea bargaining, diversion eligibility | Near-total discretion over individual cases | Office budgets; political capital | High |
| Local police departments (~18,000 agencies) | Arrest decisions, patrol allocation, stop-and-search practices | Street-level enforcement discretion | Varies; collectively hundreds of billions in budgets | High |
| Federal judiciary | Sentencing within guidelines, constitutional review | Sentencing authority, judicial review | Lifetime appointment; independent budget | Medium |
| U.S. Sentencing Commission | Recommends sentencing guidelines, publishes disparity data | Advisory; guidelines are non-binding post-*Booker* (2005) | ~$19 million annual budget | Medium |
| State governors | Clemency power, appointment of corrections and public safety officials | Executive authority, pardon/commutation power | Varies by state | Medium |

### Key Organizations and Institutions

- **Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA)**: Federal agency responsible for drug scheduling and enforcement. Budget of $3.3 billion (FY2024). The DEA has historically resisted calls to reschedule marijuana and has defended aggressive enforcement strategies. The agency's organizational culture is deeply enforcement-oriented, and its funding depends on maintaining the current prohibitionist framework.
- **Bureau of Prisons (BOP)**: Manages federal prisons where 45.3% of inmates are serving drug sentences. Budget of $8.9 billion (FY2024). The BOP is a major federal employer, and its institutional interests are tied to maintaining current incarceration levels.
- **Office of National Drug Control Policy (ONDCP)**: Coordinates federal drug policy across agencies. Has shifted rhetoric toward public health under Democratic administrations but retains enforcement-oriented structure. The ONDCP's statutory mandate has historically required opposition to legalization of Schedule I substances.
- **National District Attorneys Association (NDAA)**: Represents elected prosecutors. Has historically opposed mandatory minimum reform and prosecutorial transparency requirements. The NDAA has lobbied against proposals requiring prosecutors to collect and report charging data by race.
- **Fraternal Order of Police (FOP)**: The largest police union in the United States (356,000 members). Has opposed restrictions on pretextual stops, consent searches, and drug-free zone enhancements. The FOP's endorsement is considered critical in many political races, giving it significant leverage over drug policy.
- **State Cannabis Regulatory Agencies**: New regulatory bodies in legalization states (e.g., New York Office of Cannabis Management, Illinois Department of Commerce and Economic Opportunity) are responsible for implementing social equity provisions. Their effectiveness varies widely, with many facing legal challenges, staffing shortages, and pressure from established cannabis operators.
- **Federal Judiciary**: Federal judges have expressed concern about racial disparities in drug sentencing. In *Kimbrough v. United States* (2007), the Supreme Court held that judges may consider the crack/powder disparity in imposing below-guidelines sentences. Individual judges have written opinions highlighting the racial impact of mandatory minimums, though they remain constrained by statutory requirements.
- **Public Defender Organizations**: The National Association for Public Defense and state public defender offices represent the vast majority of drug defendants, who cannot afford private counsel. Chronic underfunding of public defense -- the Brennan Center estimates that 80% of criminal defendants qualify for public defenders, while public defender offices receive a fraction of prosecutors' budgets -- exacerbates racial disparities by ensuring that defendants in minority communities receive less effective representation.

### Influencers

- **ACLU**: The leading organization documenting marijuana arrest disparities. Its 2013 and 2020 reports on marijuana arrest data have been the most cited evidence of racial disparities.
- **The Sentencing Project**: Provides authoritative data on racial disparities in incarceration and sentencing. Its reports are regularly cited by courts, legislators, and media.
- **Drug Policy Alliance (DPA)**: Major reform organization advocating for decriminalization, harm reduction, and racial equity. Played a key role in Oregon's Measure 110 campaign.
- **National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP)**: Has long advocated for criminal justice reform and has specifically targeted racial disparities in drug enforcement since the 1990s.
- **Last Prisoner Project**: Advocates for the release of people incarcerated for cannabis offenses, focusing on racial equity in the context of legalization.
- **Koch Network / Right on Crime**: Conservative criminal justice reform initiative that has supported sentencing reform and reentry programs, providing bipartisan cover for reform.
- **Cannabis industry trade groups (National Cannabis Industry Association, Marijuana Policy Project)**: Advocate for federal legalization and banking access; increasingly support social equity provisions.

## Stakeholder Power Analysis

Understanding who has the most influence at each stage of the drug enforcement pipeline is critical for effective advocacy:

| Stage | Primary Decision Maker | Secondary Influence | Accountability Mechanism |
|-------|----------------------|---------------------|--------------------------|
| Patrol allocation | Police chief / command staff | Mayor, city council | Civilian oversight board (where they exist) |
| Stop and search | Individual officer | Department policy, training | Body cameras, data reporting (limited) |
| Arrest | Individual officer | Department arrest metrics, union contract | Internal affairs, civilian complaint |
| Charging | Line prosecutor | DA office policy, elected DA platform | Elections (every 4 years); virtually no ongoing oversight |
| Plea bargaining | Line prosecutor | Defense counsel capacity, pretrial detention status | None -- plea deals are made behind closed doors |
| Sentencing | Judge | Mandatory minimums (remove discretion), sentencing guidelines | Appellate review (limited) |
| Incarceration | Bureau of Prisons / State DOC | Congressional appropriations | Congressional oversight, litigation |
| Collateral consequences | Legislature (statutory) | Administrative agencies (HUD, Education, USDA) | Litigation, legislative repeal |

The stakeholders with the greatest power to produce or reduce racial disparities at each stage are highlighted in the table above. Notably, the two stages with the greatest discretionary power -- patrol allocation and charging -- are also the stages with the least transparency and accountability.

## Stakeholder Interests and Alignment

### Aligned Interests (Potential Coalition Partners)

| Coalition | Shared Interest | Strength |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| Civil rights organizations + Criminal justice reform groups + Public defenders | Eliminating racial disparities in arrests, sentencing, and collateral consequences | Strong |
| Progressive prosecutors + Defense attorneys + Reentry organizations | Reducing incarceration, expanding diversion, removing collateral consequences | Strong |
| Libertarian policy groups + Civil liberties organizations + Cannabis industry | Ending drug prohibition, reducing government enforcement power | Moderate |
| Conservative fiscal reformers + Criminal justice reform advocates | Reducing incarceration costs, improving public safety outcomes | Moderate |
| Cannabis industry + Social equity advocates | Federal legalization with equity provisions | Moderate (tension over pace and scope) |
| Faith communities + Reentry organizations + Families of incarcerated | Second chances, family reunification, community restoration | Moderate |

### Conflicting Interests

- **Law enforcement unions vs. Reform advocates**: Police unions seek to preserve officer discretion in stops, searches, and arrests, while reform organizations seek to constrain discretion through data collection mandates, consent search bans, and mandatory racial impact analysis. This conflict plays out in every jurisdiction considering policing reform and is one of the most politically potent dynamics in the drug policy landscape.
- **Prosecutors vs. Transparency advocates**: Elected prosecutors resist charging data transparency requirements that would expose racial disparities in their offices, while reformers argue that transparency is essential for accountability. The NDAA has described mandatory charging data collection as an "unfunded mandate" that would "chill prosecutorial independence."
- **Cannabis industry (established operators) vs. Social equity applicants**: Existing cannabis businesses, which are predominantly white-owned, resist equity licensing preferences that limit their market share, while equity advocates argue that communities harmed by prohibition must receive priority access to the legal market. Multi-state operators (MSOs) like Curaleaf, Trulieve, and Green Thumb Industries control the largest share of the legal market and have lobbied for federal legalization without strong equity mandates.
- **Tough-on-crime politicians vs. Data-driven reformers**: Some elected officials continue to advocate for aggressive drug enforcement as a public safety strategy, resisting evidence that enforcement disparities undermine public safety by eroding community trust in police. This conflict intensifies during periods of rising crime or visible drug use, when reform opponents have the strongest political messaging advantage.
- **Federal vs. State authority**: Cannabis legalization creates ongoing tension between state legalization and federal Schedule I classification. The federal government retains authority to enforce federal drug law in legalization states, creating uncertainty for cannabis businesses and equity applicants. The DOJ's non-enforcement posture (the Cole Memo, rescinded in 2018 by AG Sessions, effectively reinstated in practice) could change with any new administration.
- **Harm reduction advocates vs. Abstinence-only proponents**: Harm reduction approaches (MAT, naloxone distribution, supervised consumption) that would disproportionately benefit communities of color with the highest overdose rates face opposition from abstinence-only treatment proponents and some law enforcement officials who view harm reduction as enabling drug use.

### The Public Opinion Landscape

Public opinion on drug enforcement and racial equity has shifted significantly, creating both opportunities and challenges for reform:

| Finding | Value | Source |
|---------|-------|--------|
| Americans who support marijuana legalization | 70% | Gallup, 2023 |
| Black Americans who say the criminal justice system needs major changes | 87% | Pew Research Center, 2020 |
| Americans who say drug addiction should be treated as a disease, not crime | 64% | Pew Research Center, 2021 |
| White Americans who believe Black people are treated less fairly in the criminal justice system | 59% | Gallup, 2021 |
| Americans who support reducing mandatory minimums for drug offenses | 61% | Pew Research Center, 2021 |
| Americans who support expungement of marijuana convictions | 68% | Data for Progress, 2022 |

These polling numbers indicate broad public support for many reform proposals. However, support weakens when reforms are framed as benefiting criminals or as potentially increasing crime rates, and political messaging by opponents can rapidly shift public opinion on specific proposals.

### The Drug Enforcement Iron Triangle

The persistence of racially disparate drug enforcement is reinforced by a self-sustaining triangle of interests:

1. **Law enforcement agencies** benefit from drug arrest metrics that justify budgets, federal grants (Byrne JAG), and civil asset forfeiture revenue.
2. **Elected prosecutors** build careers on high conviction rates, which are easiest to achieve in over-policed minority communities where defendants lack resources for private counsel.
3. **Legislators** (particularly in competitive districts) benefit politically from appearing "tough on crime" and face little electoral cost from enforcement concentrated in minority communities that may not be their constituents.

These three actors reinforce each other: law enforcement provides arrest data that legislators cite to justify funding, prosecutors secure convictions that law enforcement uses to demonstrate effectiveness, and legislators provide the statutory authority and funding that sustain the system.

## Emerging Stakeholders

### Progressive Prosecutors

A new generation of elected prosecutors -- including Kim Foxx (Chicago), Larry Krasner (Philadelphia), Marilyn Mosby (Baltimore, former), and Satana Deberry (Durham) -- has made racial equity in drug enforcement a central platform. These prosecutors have implemented policies including declining prosecution of marijuana possession, expanding diversion programs, and publishing charging data by race. However, they face political backlash: Chesa Boudin was recalled in San Francisco in 2022, and George Gascon in Los Angeles faced sustained recall efforts, partly on the basis of perceived leniency in drug cases. The progressive prosecutor movement represents both the potential and the fragility of reform through existing institutional channels.

### Formerly Incarcerated Advocates

Organizations led by formerly incarcerated individuals -- including JustLeadershipUSA (founded by Glenn E. Martin), the National Council for Incarcerated and Formerly Incarcerated Women and Girls, and the Formerly Incarcerated, Convicted People and Families Movement (FICPFM) -- have become increasingly influential in drug policy debates. These organizations bring experiential authority to advocacy, though they face barriers to political participation where felony disenfranchisement persists.

### Academic Researchers

Scholars including Devah Pager (Harvard, deceased 2018), Bruce Western (Columbia), Michelle Alexander (Ohio State/activist), and Khalil Gibran Muhammad (Harvard) have produced the empirical and theoretical frameworks that structure the public debate. Pager's audit studies demonstrated that a criminal record reduces employer callbacks by 50% for white applicants and by an additional 15% for Black applicants -- meaning that a white man with a criminal record received more callbacks than a Black man without one (Pager, *Marked*, 2007).

### Cannabis Industry Players

The cannabis industry has become a significant stakeholder in drug policy reform, though its interests do not always align with racial equity goals:

| Actor | Market Position | Equity Stance | Tension |
|-------|----------------|---------------|---------|
| Multi-state operators (Curaleaf, Trulieve, Green Thumb) | Control largest market share | Support federal legalization; mixed on equity mandates | May resist licensing caps and equity preferences that limit expansion |
| Social equity licensees | Small, capital-constrained | Central advocates for equity | Face competition from well-capitalized MSOs |
| National Cannabis Industry Association | Trade association | Endorses equity provisions in federal legalization | Balances member interests across equity and established operators |
| National Cannabis Roundtable | Lobbying coalition | Supports legalization with some equity provisions | Primarily focused on banking access and descheduling |
| Cannabis Workers Coalition | Labor advocacy | Supports equity, living wages, diversity in hiring | Advocates for workforce equity beyond ownership |

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on drug enforcement policy:

- **Lobbying**: The private prison industry spent $5.6 million on federal lobbying in 2022 (OpenSecrets). The Fraternal Order of Police and National District Attorneys Association lobby against mandatory data collection, sentencing reform, and prosecutorial accountability measures.
- **Campaign Contributions**: In the 2022 election cycle, law enforcement PACs contributed $5.2 million to federal candidates, predominantly to those opposing criminal justice reform (OpenSecrets, 2023). The cannabis industry contributed $10.1 million, predominantly to reform-supporting candidates.
- **Revolving Door**: Former prosecutors frequently enter politics or join law firms that defend the current system. Former law enforcement officials staff the DEA, ONDCP, and state drug enforcement agencies, perpetuating institutional culture.
- **Public Advocacy**: Civil rights organizations, reform groups, and affected families use media campaigns, protests, and community organizing to build public support for reform. The 2020 racial justice movement demonstrated the power of public advocacy to shift political discourse.
- **Expertise/Information**: The U.S. Sentencing Commission, academic researchers, and organizations like the ACLU and The Sentencing Project produce the data and analysis that inform legislative debates. Control over the framing of data -- whether disparities are presented as evidence of bias or as evidence of different crime rates -- significantly shapes policy outcomes.
- **Ballot Initiatives**: In states that allow citizen-initiated ballot measures, drug policy reform has been advanced directly by voters, bypassing legislative gridlock. Oregon's Measure 110 (2020), California's Proposition 47 (2014), and numerous cannabis legalization measures have demonstrated that public support for reform often exceeds legislative willingness to act. However, the Oregon reversal in 2024 also illustrates the volatility of ballot-driven reform.
- **Litigation**: Civil rights organizations use federal courts to challenge racially disparate enforcement through 42 U.S.C. Section 1983 suits and challenges under Section 14141 (now 34 U.S.C. Section 12601). The DOJ's pattern-and-practice investigations -- in Ferguson, Baltimore, Chicago, and other cities -- have resulted in consent decrees that constrain drug enforcement practices, though compliance and enforcement vary.
