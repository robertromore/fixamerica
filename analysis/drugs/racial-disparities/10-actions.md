# Racial Disparities in Drug Policy: What You Can Do

Racial disparities in drug enforcement are sustained by political choices, institutional inertia, and public indifference. They can be changed by sustained citizen engagement -- through advocacy, electoral action, community organizing, and professional practice. The reforms outlined in this analysis require not only legislative action but also the persistent public pressure that makes such action politically feasible.

---

## Individual Actions

### Learn and Stay Informed

- Read the ACLU's "A Tale of Two Countries" report (2020) for definitive data on marijuana arrest disparities: <https://www.aclu.org/report/tale-two-countries-racially-targeted-arrests-era-marijuana-reform>
- Follow The Sentencing Project's research updates for the most current data on racial incarceration disparities: <https://www.sentencingproject.org>
- Subscribe to The Marshall Project's daily newsletter for ongoing criminal justice reporting: <https://www.themarshallproject.org/subscribe>
- Track federal sentencing data through the U.S. Sentencing Commission's Interactive Data Analyzer: <https://ida.ussc.gov>
- Read Michelle Alexander's *The New Jim Crow* for foundational analysis of how the War on Drugs created a system of racial control

### Make Personal Changes

- Examine your own assumptions about who uses drugs, who sells drugs, and who is dangerous. Research shows that drug use rates are comparable across racial groups (~15% for Black adults, ~14% for white adults, SAMHSA NSDUH 2023). The mental image of a "drug user" or "drug dealer" that comes to mind for most Americans is shaped by decades of racially biased media coverage, not by empirical reality.
- If you serve on a jury in a drug case, be aware of the research on racial bias in drug enforcement and exercise your role as a check on prosecutorial power. Jury nullification -- acquitting a defendant despite evidence of guilt because the law or its application is unjust -- has a long history in American jurisprudence and is particularly relevant in drug cases where enforcement is demonstrably racially biased.
- If you are an employer, evaluate your criminal background check policies. Consider "ban the box" approaches and individualized assessment rather than blanket exclusions for drug convictions. Research by Agan and Starr (2018) found that criminal records reduce job callbacks by 50%, with a larger penalty for Black applicants. The Society for Human Resource Management (SHRM) and the Koch Industries foundation have both endorsed fair-chance hiring practices.
- If you are a consumer in a legal cannabis state, patronize businesses owned by social equity licensees. Several states and cities maintain public directories of equity-licensed businesses.
- If you are a parent, discuss the reality of racially disparate drug enforcement with your children. Understanding that drug laws are applied unequally is an essential component of civic education.

### Use Your Voice

- **Contact Your Representatives**: Urge your Senators to pass the EQUAL Act (crack/powder cocaine equalization). Urge your House member to support the Drug Enforcement Data Transparency Act and federal cannabis legalization with equity provisions.
- **Submit Public Comments**: When the DEA, DOJ, or HUD propose rules related to drug scheduling, enforcement policy, or public housing drug policies, submit comments advocating for racial equity. Federal rulemaking notices are published at <https://www.regulations.gov>.
- **Write Letters to the Editor**: Respond to local media coverage of drug enforcement with data on racial disparities. Local newspapers remain influential in shaping opinions of elected officials.
- **Share Information**: Amplify credible research on drug enforcement disparities from the ACLU, Sentencing Project, and Drug Policy Alliance on social media platforms.

## Community Actions

### Organize Locally

- Attend your local city council or county commission meetings when drug enforcement budgets, police department policies, or marijuana ordinances are on the agenda. Many jurisdictions have deprioritized marijuana enforcement through local ordinances.
- Organize community forums on drug enforcement disparities featuring local data. Contact your police department's public information office to request demographic data on drug arrests in your jurisdiction.
- Partner with local chapters of the NAACP, ACLU, and Drug Policy Alliance to build coalitions for local reform.
- Support or establish a civilian police oversight board with authority to review drug enforcement practices and demographics.

### Monitor Local Drug Enforcement

- Request drug arrest data by race from your local police department using Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) or state public records law requests. Compare arrest demographics to population demographics and drug use survey data. Many departments have never been asked for this data.
- Attend police department community meetings and ask specific questions about drug enforcement strategy: Where are drug enforcement resources concentrated? What criteria determine which neighborhoods receive increased drug enforcement? What data is collected on stops, searches, and arrests by race?
- Monitor local drug court programs for racial equity. Request data on referral rates, admission rates, completion rates, and sanctions by race. Drug courts that are inaccessible to people of color replicate rather than remedy disparities.

### Build Coalitions

- Connect criminal justice reform advocates with faith communities, which can provide moral authority and institutional support for reform efforts. Many religious traditions emphasize redemption, second chances, and justice. Prison Fellowship, the National Association of Evangelicals, and numerous Black church networks have all endorsed criminal justice reform.
- Bridge the gap between cannabis industry entrepreneurs and social equity advocates -- legal cannabis businesses have financial resources and political access that can support equity campaigns. The National Cannabis Industry Association has endorsed social equity provisions as part of federal legalization.
- Engage public health organizations as allies: racial disparities in drug enforcement are a public health issue that undermines treatment access and community well-being. The American Public Health Association, American Medical Association, and National Medical Association have all issued policy statements identifying racial disparities in drug enforcement as a public health concern.
- Partner with reentry organizations that serve people returning from drug-related incarceration. Organizations like the National Reentry Resource Center and local reentry coalitions provide direct services and can connect reform advocates with individuals whose stories illustrate the human impact of disparate enforcement.
- Engage veterans' organizations: veterans are disproportionately affected by substance use disorders and drug-related criminal justice involvement. Organizations like the Veterans Justice Commission of the Council of State Governments have supported drug sentencing reform.

## Political Actions

### Contact Elected Officials

**When to Contact**:

- When the EQUAL Act or cannabis legalization legislation is scheduled for committee markup or floor vote
- During budget season (February-September) when drug enforcement funding is being determined
- After high-profile incidents of racially disparate drug enforcement in your community
- During judicial confirmation hearings for judges who will hear drug cases

**What to Say - Template Email**:

```text
Subject: Support the EQUAL Act and Drug Enforcement Equity Reform

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

I am a constituent from [City, State] writing to urge your support for addressing
racial disparities in drug enforcement -- one of the most well-documented civil
rights issues in our criminal justice system.

Specifically, I ask you to:

1. Co-sponsor and vote for the EQUAL Act to eliminate the crack/powder cocaine
   sentencing disparity (currently 18:1). This bill passed the House in 2021 with
   bipartisan support (361-66).

2. Support federal cannabis legalization with robust equity provisions, including
   automatic expungement of cannabis convictions and community reinvestment in
   neighborhoods most harmed by the War on Drugs.

3. Support mandatory data collection and public reporting on drug enforcement
   outcomes by race, conditioned on federal law enforcement funding.

The data is clear: Black Americans are 3.73 times more likely to be arrested for
marijuana possession despite roughly comparable use rates (ACLU, 2020). Black
individuals constitute 77.1% of federal crack cocaine offenders while representing
13.6% of the population (USSC, 2023). These are not statistics -- they are people
whose lives have been unjustly disrupted.

I would appreciate knowing your position on these issues.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your City, State]
```

**What to Say - Phone Call Script**:

```text
"Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [City]. I'm calling to ask
the [Representative/Senator] to support the EQUAL Act to eliminate the crack/powder
cocaine sentencing disparity, and to support federal cannabis legalization with social
equity provisions. Black Americans are nearly four times more likely to be arrested
for marijuana despite similar use rates. Can you please note my position?"
```

### Support Legislation

Current or proposed legislation to support:

- **EQUAL Act**: Eliminates the crack/powder cocaine sentencing disparity by setting a 1:1 ratio. Passed the House in 2021 with 361 votes. Contact your Senators to co-sponsor and advance to a vote.
- **Cannabis Administration and Opportunity Act (CAOA)**: Comprehensive federal cannabis legalization with social equity provisions, expungement, and community reinvestment. Contact your Representative and Senator to co-sponsor.
- **Clean Slate Act**: Automatic sealing of federal criminal records after a waiting period. Contact your Representative and Senator to co-sponsor.
- **George Floyd Justice in Policing Act**: Includes restrictions on pretextual stops, mandatory data collection, and reforms to qualified immunity. Contact your Senators to overcome the filibuster.
- **State-level cannabis legalization and equity bills**: Check your state legislature's calendar for pending cannabis legalization bills and advocate for inclusion of social equity provisions, expungement, and reinvestment.

### Electoral Engagement

**Questions to Ask Candidates**:

1. "What is your position on the crack/powder cocaine sentencing disparity? Do you support the EQUAL Act to equalize sentencing at 1:1?"
2. "Do you support requiring law enforcement agencies to collect and publicly report drug enforcement data by race as a condition of federal funding?"
3. "Do you support federal cannabis legalization with social equity provisions, automatic expungement, and community reinvestment?"
4. "As prosecutor [if applicable], will you adopt charging guidelines that limit mandatory minimum triggers for low-level, non-violent drug offenses, and will you publicly report charging data by race?"
5. "Do you support eliminating collateral consequences of drug convictions, including barriers to housing, employment, and public benefits?"
6. "Do you support restoring voting rights to individuals with felony convictions upon release from incarceration?"
7. "What specific steps will you take to ensure that drug enforcement resources are allocated equitably across racial and geographic lines?"

**Voting Considerations**:

- Research candidates' positions on criminal justice reform through organizations like the ACLU's voter guide and the Drug Policy Alliance's scorecard
- For local prosecutor races, examine whether candidates commit to data transparency, diversion programs, and reduced reliance on mandatory minimums. Prosecutor races are among the most consequential and least-followed elections in American democracy -- 2,300 elected prosecutors control charging discretion that directly determines racial disparities in drug case outcomes
- Support candidates for state legislature who back cannabis legalization with equity provisions
- Pay attention to judicial candidates' records on drug sentencing and racial equity
- Monitor sheriff races: county sheriffs set drug enforcement priorities and can choose to deprioritize low-level drug enforcement, adopt data collection practices, and implement diversion programs
- Vote in primaries, not just general elections. In many jurisdictions, particularly in the South, the primary is the decisive election for prosecutors and judges. Low primary turnout means that a small number of engaged voters can determine the outcome

## Professional Actions

- **For Attorneys**: Volunteer with public defender offices or legal aid organizations providing expungement assistance. Organizations like the National Expungement Project coordinate pro bono expungement clinics. Consider taking on selective prosecution challenges under *United States v. Armstrong* (1996) -- while the legal standard is extremely high, building a body of case law is essential for eventual doctrinal change. Join or support the National Association of Criminal Defense Lawyers' (NACDL) Racial Justice Committee.
- **For Healthcare Professionals**: Advocate within your institution for treatment-first approaches to substance use disorder that reduce reliance on criminal justice referrals. Support medication-assisted treatment access in communities of color. If you are a physician with a DEA registration, prescribe buprenorphine -- the elimination of the X-waiver requirement in 2023 means any DEA-registered physician can prescribe. Challenge the two-tier treatment system that channels Black patients to methadone clinics while directing white patients to office-based buprenorphine. Advocate within professional societies (AMA, APA, AAFP) for policy positions supporting drug enforcement equity.
- **For Educators**: Incorporate evidence on racial disparities in drug enforcement into criminal justice, public health, and social studies curricula. Support students affected by the collateral consequences of drug convictions. Assign Michelle Alexander's *The New Jim Crow*, James Forman Jr.'s *Locking Up Our Own*, and John Pfaff's *Locked In* to provide students with multiple analytical frameworks. Invite formerly incarcerated individuals to speak in classes about the lived experience of racially disparate enforcement.
- **For Journalists**: Investigate and report on drug enforcement disparities in your local jurisdiction. Request demographic data on drug arrests, charges, and sentences from local law enforcement and courts. The ACLU's methodology for analyzing marijuana arrest disparities can be adapted to any jurisdiction. Report on local prosecutor races and candidate positions on charging transparency. Use the Stanford Open Policing Project's data and methodology to analyze traffic stop disparities in your coverage area.
- **For Business Leaders**: Adopt fair-chance hiring practices that evaluate individuals with drug convictions based on qualifications rather than blanket exclusion. Partner with reentry organizations to create employment pathways. The Business Roundtable's Second Chance Employment Initiative and the Koch Industries fair-chance hiring program provide corporate models. If operating in the cannabis industry, commit to social equity goals including workforce diversity, community reinvestment, and support for equity licensees.
- **For Social Workers and Counselors**: Provide culturally competent support to individuals and families affected by racially disparate drug enforcement. Advocate for policy changes within your professional organizations (NASW, ACA). Screen clients for collateral consequences of drug convictions and provide referrals for expungement, benefits restoration, and voting rights restoration.
- **For Researchers and Academics**: Conduct and publish research on racial disparities in drug enforcement in understudied jurisdictions. Develop and validate methodologies for measuring prosecutorial discretion disparities. Partner with community organizations to ensure that research findings are accessible and actionable for affected communities. Apply for research grants through NIJ, NSF, and private foundations (Arnold Ventures, MacArthur Foundation) to fund disparity research.
- **For Faith Leaders**: Use your moral authority and institutional platform to advocate for drug enforcement equity. Host community forums on racial disparities. Provide meeting space and organizational support for reform coalitions. Visit incarcerated individuals serving drug sentences through prison ministry programs. Many religious denominations -- including the United Methodist Church, Presbyterian Church (USA), and National Baptist Convention -- have adopted official policy positions supporting criminal justice reform.

## Legal Actions for Individuals Affected by Disparate Enforcement

If you or someone you know has been affected by racially disparate drug enforcement, the following resources provide direct assistance:

### Record Clearing and Expungement

- **National Expungement Project**: Coordinates free expungement clinics across the country. Check their website for upcoming events in your area: <https://www.myrecordcleared.org>
- **Legal Aid Societies**: Most states have legal aid organizations that provide free or low-cost expungement assistance. Contact your state bar association for referrals.
- **Clean Slate State Laws**: As of 2024, at least 12 states have enacted automatic or semi-automatic expungement laws. Check whether your state has adopted Clean Slate legislation and whether you are eligible for automatic record clearing.
- **Federal Marijuana Pardons**: President Biden issued pardons for federal simple marijuana possession convictions in October 2022. If you have a federal simple possession conviction, contact the DOJ Office of the Pardon Attorney for information on whether you are covered.

### Sentence Review

- **First Step Act Resentencing**: If you or someone you know is serving a federal sentence for a crack cocaine offense under the pre-2010 sentencing guidelines, they may be eligible for resentencing under the First Step Act's retroactivity provision. Contact the Federal Defenders' office in the relevant district.
- **Compassionate Release**: Federal inmates may petition for compassionate release under expanded criteria in the First Step Act. Contact FAMM (Families Against Mandatory Minimums) for guidance: <https://famm.org>

### Collateral Consequence Relief

- **Voting Rights Restoration**: Check your state's laws on voting rights for people with felony convictions. In many states, voting rights are automatically restored upon completion of sentence. The Sentencing Project maintains a state-by-state guide: <https://www.sentencingproject.org/policy-brief/felony-disenfranchisement-in-the-united-states/>
- **Benefits Eligibility**: If you have been denied SNAP, TANF, or public housing due to a drug conviction, contact your state's legal aid office. Many states have modified or eliminated drug felony bans, and you may be eligible for benefits you were previously denied.

## Advocacy Toolkit

### Key Talking Points

When discussing racial disparities in drug enforcement with others, emphasize:

1. **The data is unambiguous**: Black Americans are 3.73 times more likely to be arrested for marijuana despite comparable use rates (ACLU, 2020). This is not a matter of opinion -- it is documented by the federal government's own data.
2. **Disparities exist at every stage**: From stops and searches (Stanford Open Policing Project) to charging (USSC Mandatory Minimum Report, 2017) to sentencing (USSC Annual Report, FY2022) to incarceration (5.8:1 Black-to-white drug imprisonment ratio, Sentencing Project 2023).
3. **This costs everyone**: Drug-related incarceration costs taxpayers $47.8 billion annually (BJS, 2022). Treatment is seven times more cost-effective than law enforcement at reducing drug use (RAND Corporation).
4. **Reform has bipartisan precedent**: The First Step Act passed the Senate 87-12. The EQUAL Act passed the House 361-66. These are not partisan issues.

### Common Misconceptions to Counter

| Misconception | Reality | Source |
|---------------|---------|--------|
| "Black Americans use drugs at higher rates" | Drug use rates are roughly comparable across races (~15% vs. ~14% past-year illicit drug use) | SAMHSA NSDUH, 2023 |
| "Disparities reflect different crime rates" | Arrest rate disparities far exceed any plausible difference in offending; search hit rates are lower for Black drivers | Stanford Open Policing Project, 2020; ACLU, 2020 |
| "The crack/powder disparity was based on science" | The USSC has repeatedly found the disparity unjustified; crack and powder cocaine are pharmacologically identical | USSC Reports, 1995-2007 |
| "Reducing sentences will increase crime" | States that reduced drug penalties have not seen crime increases; treatment is 7x more effective | National Research Council, 2014; RAND Corporation |
| "Cannabis legalization has fixed the problem" | The 3.73:1 Black-to-white marijuana arrest ratio was unchanged after years of legalization | ACLU, 2020 |
| "Drug enforcement disparities are inevitable" | Other countries (Portugal, Canada, UK) have reduced drug enforcement disparities through policy reform | EMCDDA, 2023; Public Safety Canada, 2022 |
| "The system is race-neutral; outcomes reflect individual choices" | Facially neutral laws produce massively disparate outcomes when applied through discretionary enforcement, prosecution, and sentencing decisions shaped by implicit and structural bias | Rehavi & Starr, *JPE*, 2014; Berdejo, *BCLR*, 2018 |
| "Black communities want more drug enforcement" | Black communities want effective policing AND reform -- these are not contradictory; 87% of Black adults say the criminal justice system needs major changes | Pew Research Center, 2020; Gallup, 2021 |

### Responding to Specific Scenarios

**When someone says "All lives matter in the drug war"**: Acknowledge the universal harm of drug abuse and then redirect to the data -- "You're right that drug abuse harms everyone. That's exactly why it matters that enforcement is so unequal. When Black Americans are nearly four times more likely to be arrested for marijuana despite using it at the same rate, the system isn't protecting everyone equally."

**When someone says "What about Black-on-Black crime?"**: "Drug market violence is a consequence of prohibition, not of race. When alcohol was prohibited, white and Italian-American communities experienced the same market violence. The question is whether arresting low-level drug users -- which is what the racial disparity data measures -- reduces violent crime. The evidence says it doesn't."

**When someone points to Oregon's Measure 110 reversal**: "Oregon's challenges were primarily about implementation -- not enough treatment infrastructure was built before decriminalization took effect. The lesson is that decriminalization must be paired with robust treatment investment, not that the status quo of racially disparate enforcement is acceptable."
