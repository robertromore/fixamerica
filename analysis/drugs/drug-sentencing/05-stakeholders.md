# Drug Sentencing: Stakeholders

The drug sentencing system is shaped by the interactions of several key groups, each with distinct interests, power, and resources. Understanding these stakeholders -- who is affected, who holds power, and where interests align or conflict -- is essential to designing reform strategies that can navigate political and institutional resistance.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| People incarcerated for drug offenses | Direct loss of liberty, collateral consequences (employment, housing, family separation, voting rights) | ~350,000 (all systems, any given day) | Sentence reduction, resentencing, reentry support |
| Families of incarcerated individuals | Economic hardship, family disruption, stigma, children's developmental outcomes | ~2.7 million children with a parent in prison (all offenses); estimated 500,000+ affected by drug sentences | Family reunification, sentence proportionality, visitation access |
| Communities of color | Disproportionate enforcement, mass incarceration, economic devastation, political disenfranchisement | Black Americans: 13.6% of population, 26.2% of federal drug defendants; Hispanic Americans: 19.1% of population, 44.8% of federal drug defendants | Racial equity, elimination of disparities, community investment |
| People with substance use disorders | Criminalization rather than treatment, incarceration that interrupts recovery, post-release barriers | 48.7 million Americans with SUD (SAMHSA, 2024); significant overlap with incarcerated population | Treatment-based alternatives, diversion programs, destigmatization |
| Federal and state judges | Constrained by mandatory minimums, unable to sentence according to individual circumstances | ~1,700 federal judges; ~30,000 state court judges | Judicial discretion, evidence-based sentencing, proportionality |

### Secondary Stakeholders

- **Defense attorneys**: Public defenders handle the vast majority of federal drug cases.
    - Handle >80% of federal drug cases
    - Average federal public defender carries approximately 60-80 cases simultaneously
    - ABA recommended maximum: 150 felony cases per year
    - Federal public defender budget (~$1.3 billion) dwarfed by DOJ ($38.6 billion)
    - Private defense attorneys create a two-tier system where wealth determines representation quality
    - Wealth disparities in representation translate directly into sentencing disparities

- **Communities where drug enforcement is concentrated**: Low-income urban neighborhoods bear the brunt of aggressive drug policing.
    - Subject to surveillance, stop-and-frisk, and no-knock warrants
    - Concentrated incarceration destabilizes communities (Todd Clear, *Imprisoning Communities*, 2007)
    - Removal of working-age men disrupts family structures and depletes social capital
    - Paradoxically increases the conditions that produce drug activity
    - Weakens community institutions (churches, businesses, civic organizations)
    - Creates distrust of law enforcement that undermines legitimate public safety efforts

- **Taxpayers**: The fiscal costs of drug incarceration are enormous.
    - Annual cost of incarcerating federal drug offenders alone: >$2.7 billion (BOP, FY2024)
    - State and local costs add billions more
    - Full taxpayer cost of incarceration across all levels of government: ~$182 billion annually (Vera, 2022)
    - Average annual cost per federal inmate: ~$39,000 (BOP, 2023)
    - Cost of a single year of drug treatment: ~$4,700 (NIDA)
    - Drug offenses account for a significant share of total incarceration costs

- **Victims of drug-related crime**: People affected by drug trafficking violence have a complex stake in sentencing policy.
    - Want sentencing that effectively reduces drug market violence
    - Evidence suggests incarceration-focused approaches do not achieve this
    - Some victims' advocacy organizations support reform (e.g., Crime Survivors for Safety and Justice)
    - Recognition growing that treatment-based approaches produce better long-term outcomes
    - Victims of drug-market violence are disproportionately from the same communities affected by mass incarceration

- **Children of incarcerated parents**: Drug sentencing has devastating multigenerational effects.
    - ~2.7 million children have a parent in prison or jail
    - Parental incarceration produces adverse childhood experiences (Annie E. Casey Foundation, 2016):
        - Increased behavioral problems
        - Academic failure
        - Economic hardship
        - Higher likelihood of involvement in the criminal justice system
    - The Sesame Street initiative on parental incarceration (2013) reflected the scale of the problem
    - Lengthy drug sentences for parents impose costs on children who had no role in the offense

- **Employers and the labor market**: Criminal record barriers to employment have economy-wide impacts.
    - Millions of people with drug convictions face employment barriers
    - Lost GDP from criminal record employment barriers: $78-$87 billion annually (Brennan Center, 2017)
    - Employers face reduced labor pools, particularly in shortage industries
    - "Ban the box" laws in 37 states attempt to reduce barriers but have limited enforcement
    - JPMorgan Chase, Koch Industries, and other major employers have launched Second Chance hiring initiatives

### Stakeholders in the Treatment System

| Group | Role | Stake in Sentencing Reform |
|---|---|---|
| Substance abuse treatment providers | Deliver evidence-based treatment for substance use disorders | Expanded diversion increases referrals and funding; inadequate capacity constrains reform |
| SAMHSA (Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration) | Federal agency overseeing treatment funding and standards | Treatment integration in sentencing requires expanded SAMHSA budgets and coordination |
| Drug courts | Specialized courts that combine treatment with judicial supervision | Sentencing reform that expands diversion increases drug court caseloads and funding needs |
| Medication-assisted treatment (MAT) providers | Prescribe buprenorphine, methadone, and naltrexone | Sentencing reform must be paired with MAT capacity expansion, particularly in rural and underserved areas |
| Health insurance providers | Cover or deny coverage for substance use treatment | Parity enforcement and Medicaid expansion affect treatment capacity that reform depends on |

## Who Has Power

### Decision Makers

| Actor | Role | Authority | Resources | Influence Level |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| U.S. Congress | Sets mandatory minimums and sentencing statutes | Legislative power over federal drug sentencing | N/A | High |
| U.S. Sentencing Commission | Sets and amends federal sentencing guidelines | Quasi-legislative authority; guidelines shape sentences for all federal offenses | ~$19 million annual budget (FY2024) | High |
| Federal prosecutors (DOJ/U.S. Attorneys) | Determine charges, mandatory minimum application, plea offers, substantial assistance motions | Charging discretion effectively controls sentences | 93 U.S. Attorney offices; ~5,800 federal prosecutors | Very High |
| Attorney General | Sets DOJ charging policy, priorities, and enforcement directives | Supervisory authority over all federal prosecutors | DOJ budget: $38.6 billion (FY2024) | High |
| Federal judges | Impose sentences within statutory and guideline frameworks | Sentencing authority, post-*Booker* discretion | N/A | High (constrained) |
| State legislatures | Set state drug sentencing laws, mandatory minimums, and classification | Full authority over state criminal law | Varies by state | High (state level) |
| State governors | Sign/veto sentencing legislation; clemency power | Executive authority; appointment of judges in some states | Varies | High (state level) |
| Bureau of Prisons | Administers federal prison system; implements compassionate release, good-time credits | Administrative authority over ~160,000 inmates | $8.4 billion budget (FY2024) | Medium |

### Key Organizations and Institutions

- **Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA)**:
    - Enforces federal drug laws; recommends scheduling decisions
    - Budget: >$3.3 billion (FY2024); >10,000 employees
    - Historically opposed sentencing reform and supported maintaining or increasing penalties
    - Institutional mission defined by enforcement outputs (arrests, seizures, dismantlements), not public health outcomes
    - Operates 239 domestic offices and 93 foreign offices in 69 countries

- **National District Attorneys Association (NDAA)**:
    - Represents ~2,700 elected prosecutors nationally
    - Generally opposes mandatory minimum reform, arguing minimums are essential tools for cooperation and plea agreements
    - Influence operates primarily at the state level, where elected prosecutors have significant political power
    - Provides training and model legislation to local prosecutors

- **National Association of Assistant United States Attorneys (NAAUSA)**:
    - Represents federal line prosecutors
    - Actively lobbied against mandatory minimum reform, including opposing the Smarter Sentencing Act and aspects of the First Step Act
    - Influence comes from direct access to congressional judiciary committees
    - Leverages perceived expertise on federal prosecution

- **Federal Public Defenders**:
    - Represent indigent federal defendants in >80% of federal drug cases
    - Consistently advocate for sentencing reform, reduced mandatory minimums, and expanded judicial discretion
    - Budget (~$1.3 billion, FY2024) dramatically lower than prosecutors' ($38.6 billion DOJ)
    - Institutional resource disadvantage translates into representational disparities

- **U.S. Sentencing Commission**:
    - Seven-member bipartisan commission; sets guidelines, analyzes data, advises Congress
    - Increasingly important voice for evidence-based reform
    - Institutional independence from Congress and executive branch gives credibility across partisan lines
    - Vulnerability: can be weakened by vacancies -- lacked a quorum for >2 years (2019-2022), preventing guideline amendments
    - Annual budget: ~$19 million (FY2024)

- **Bureau of Prisons (BOP)**:
    - Administers the federal prison system
    - Budget: $8.4 billion (FY2024); ~36,000 employees; ~160,000 inmates
    - Criticized for slow implementation of First Step Act provisions (earned time credits, compassionate release)
    - DOJ Inspector General has issued multiple reports documenting implementation failures
    - Staff shortages have been identified as a primary barrier to programming and reform implementation

- **Administrative Office of the U.S. Courts**:
    - Provides administrative support to federal courts
    - Manages federal probation and pretrial services system
    - Supervises individuals on post-release supervision and those diverted to drug courts
    - Plays a critical but often overlooked role in reform implementation

### Influencers

- **Reform advocacy organizations**: Conduct research, public advocacy, and direct lobbying.
    - **The Sentencing Project**: Research and advocacy on sentencing and racial disparities since 1986
    - **FAMM (Families Against Mandatory Minimums)**: Centers personal stories of affected individuals; effective in building bipartisan support
    - **ACLU**: Smart Justice Campaign targets reform in all 50 states
    - **Drug Policy Alliance**: Advocates for decriminalization and harm reduction approaches
    - **Brennan Center for Justice**: Research and litigation on sentencing and mass incarceration

- **Conservative reform organizations**: Provide political cover for Republican lawmakers supporting reform.
    - **Right on Crime**: Texas-based initiative framing reform as consistent with conservative principles
    - **Americans for Prosperity (Koch network)**: Lobbied for the First Step Act; instrumental in overcoming Republican opposition
    - **FreedomWorks**: Libertarian-leaning advocacy for reduced government in criminal justice
    - **R Street Institute**: Center-right research on overcriminalization and sentencing reform
    - Charles Koch and Mark Holden of Koch Industries argued publicly that overcriminalization contradicts conservative principles
    - Coalition frames reform in terms of fiscal responsibility, limited government, and religious redemption

- **Academic researchers**: Produce research on sentencing outcomes, deterrence, and racial disparities.
    - **Crystal Yang** (Harvard): Judicial discretion and racial disparities
    - **Sonja Starr and M. Marit Rehavi** (Michigan): Prosecutorial discretion in drug cases
    - **John Pfaff** (Fordham): Prosecutorial power as the primary driver of mass incarceration
    - **University of Minnesota Robina Institute**: Sentencing policy research and comparative analysis
    - **Vera Institute of Justice**: Cost analysis, racial impact studies, and reform evaluation
    - **RAND Corporation**: Drug policy effectiveness and deterrence research
    - **Urban Institute**: Reentry, recidivism, and reform implementation studies

- **Faith-based organizations**: Support reform on moral and redemption grounds.
    - **Prison Fellowship** (founded by Chuck Colson): Largest faith-based reentry program; key advocate for First Step Act earned time credits
    - **National Association of Evangelicals**: Moral argument for proportionality and second chances
    - **U.S. Conference of Catholic Bishops**: Consistent advocacy for sentencing reform based on Catholic social teaching
    - Faith-based support provides critical cover for conservative lawmakers in Southern and Midwestern states

- **Celebrity and public figure advocates**: Bring media attention and public awareness.
    - **Kim Kardashian**: Direct advocacy to President Trump; credited with influencing Alice Marie Johnson clemency and White House support for the First Step Act
    - **Meek Mill / REFORM Alliance** (co-founded with Michael Rubin): Legislative victories in multiple states on probation and parole reform
    - **Van Jones / Cut50**: Bipartisan advocacy to reduce incarceration by 50%
    - **John Legend**: FREEAMERICA campaign focused on ending mass incarceration
    - Celebrity advocacy increases public salience of sentencing reform beyond policy circles

- **Formerly incarcerated advocates**: Bring lived experience to policy advocacy.
    - **JustLeadershipUSA**: Led by formerly incarcerated people; goal of cutting U.S. incarceration in half by 2030
    - **Formerly Incarcerated & Convicted People's Movement (FICPM)**: National coalition
    - **National Council for Incarcerated and Formerly Incarcerated Women and Girls**: Gender-focused advocacy
    - Testimony before Congress and state legislatures has been among the most compelling evidence for reform
    - Increasingly represented in advisory roles in reform organizations and government commissions

## Stakeholder Interests and Alignment

### Aligned Interests (Potential Coalition Partners)

| Coalition | Shared Interest | Strength |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| Civil liberties + fiscal conservatives | Reducing incarceration costs; limiting government overreach | Strong (basis of First Step Act coalition) |
| Racial justice advocates + libertarians | Ending disproportionate enforcement; individual liberty | Moderate (different motivations, same direction) |
| Judges + defense attorneys + reform advocates | Restoring judicial discretion; evidence-based sentencing | Strong (professional expertise alignment) |
| Faith communities + reentry organizations | Second chances; redemption; family reunification | Strong (moral/values alignment) |
| Public health advocates + treatment providers | Treatment alternatives to incarceration; harm reduction | Strong (evidence-based alignment) |
| Fiscal hawks + state governors | Reducing corrections spending; reinvesting savings | Moderate to Strong (budget pressure alignment) |

### Conflicting Interests

- **Prosecutors vs. judges**: Prosecutors want mandatory minimums to preserve plea leverage; judges want discretion to sentence individually. This tension is structural and long-standing -- the Judicial Conference of the United States has formally opposed mandatory minimums since 2013, while the NAAUSA has formally supported them in every congressional hearing.
- **Law enforcement agencies vs. reform advocates**: DEA and drug task forces resist reforms that reduce enforcement budgets and arrest metrics; reform advocates seek to redirect resources to treatment and prevention. The DEA's institutional mission is defined by enforcement, making it a structural opponent of demand-side approaches.
- **Private prison companies vs. incarcerated individuals**: Private prisons profit from high incarceration rates; incarcerated individuals seek sentence reductions. CoreCivic and GEO Group have revenue models that depend directly on maintaining prison populations, creating a financial incentive to oppose sentencing reform.
- **"Tough on crime" politicians vs. evidence-based reform**: Politicians who have built careers on severity resist reforms they fear opponents will use against them. However, this dynamic has weakened significantly since the First Step Act demonstrated that reform could be politically safe.
- **Victims' rights organizations vs. sentencing reform advocates**: Some victims' groups oppose sentence reductions, though this opposition is not universal -- some victim advocates support treatment-based approaches. The emergence of organizations like Crime Survivors for Safety and Justice, which advocates for reform as a public safety strategy, has complicated the narrative that victims universally oppose reform.
- **Federal vs. state priorities**: Federal prosecutors and state prosecutors sometimes have conflicting interests. Federal prosecutors can "adopt" state drug cases to federal court, where mandatory minimums are more severe, effectively overriding state reform efforts. This practice disproportionately affects defendants in states that have reformed their drug sentencing laws.
- **Treatment providers vs. corrections systems**: Treatment providers need adequate referral volumes and funding, which diversion programs provide. Corrections systems lose population (and justification for budgets) when diversion succeeds. This tension can manifest in institutional resistance to treatment alternatives within correctional settings.

### Cross-Cutting Stakeholder Dynamics

Several dynamics cut across traditional stakeholder categories:

- **Urban vs. rural divide**: Urban communities disproportionately bear the brunt of drug enforcement and incarceration, while rural communities increasingly face drug crises (fentanyl, methamphetamine) with limited treatment infrastructure. Both contexts argue for reform but from different angles -- racial equity in urban areas, treatment access in rural areas.
- **Generational shift**: Younger prosecutors, judges, and legislators are more likely to support reform than their predecessors. The generational shift reflects exposure to evidence-based approaches during legal education and professional training, as well as declining public support for "War on Drugs" rhetoric.
- **Partisan realignment**: Drug sentencing reform has partially scrambled traditional partisan alignments. Libertarian-leaning Republicans join progressive Democrats in supporting reform, while some conservative Democrats and moderate Republicans remain cautious. This realignment creates opportunities for unusual coalitions but also makes reform politically unpredictable.

### The Sentencing Iron Triangle

The drug sentencing system features an iron triangle of mutually reinforcing interests:

1. **Prosecutors** benefit from mandatory minimums that give them plea leverage
2. **Law enforcement** benefits from drug enforcement funding, asset forfeiture, and arrest metrics
3. **Corrections industry** (public and private) benefits from the population that prosecutors and law enforcement deliver

These three groups share a structural interest in maintaining high drug sentencing levels, and each reinforces the others' position. Prosecutors advocate for enforcement tools that generate cases; law enforcement delivers defendants to prosecutors; corrections benefits from the resulting incarceration. Reform must either break this triangle by changing incentives or build a coalition powerful enough to overcome it.

### The Emerging Counter-Coalition

The reform coalition that produced the First Step Act demonstrated that the sentencing iron triangle can be overcome. Key elements of this counter-coalition include:

| Coalition Element | Motivation | Key Organizations |
|---|---|---|
| Fiscal conservatives | Reducing government spending on incarceration | Right on Crime, Americans for Prosperity, R Street Institute |
| Libertarians | Limiting government power over individuals | Cato Institute, Reason Foundation, ACLU (on overlap) |
| Religious conservatives | Redemption, forgiveness, family values | Prison Fellowship, NAE, Catholic Charities |
| Racial justice advocates | Ending disproportionate impact on communities of color | NAACP, Sentencing Project, Equal Justice Initiative |
| Progressive reformers | Systemic criminal justice reform | Drug Policy Alliance, Brennan Center, Vera Institute |
| Public health advocates | Treatment over punishment for substance use disorders | American Medical Association, NASW, treatment providers |
| Business leaders | Labor market access, corporate social responsibility | Koch Industries, JPMorgan Chase Second Chance hiring |
| Bipartisan elected officials | Evidence-based policy, constituent support | Senators Booker, Durbin, Grassley, Lee; Representatives Jeffries, Jackson Lee |

This coalition is broader and more ideologically diverse than any previous criminal justice reform effort in American history. Its durability, however, is tested by each new drug crisis (fentanyl) and each election cycle that revives "tough on crime" messaging.

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on drug sentencing policy:

- **Lobbying**:
    - Private prison companies spent $4.6 million on federal lobbying in 2022 (OpenSecrets, 2023)
    - Prosecutors' associations maintain active lobbying presences in Washington and state capitals
    - Reform organizations collectively spend less but have grown significantly since 2015
    - Koch network's Americans for Prosperity and Freedom Partners spent ~$5 million on criminal justice reform lobbying (2015-2019)
    - This provided an unusually well-resourced conservative voice for reform (OpenSecrets, 2020)

- **Campaign Contributions**:
    - CoreCivic and GEO Group contributed $1.9 million to federal candidates and PACs in the 2022 cycle (OpenSecrets, 2023)
    - Law enforcement unions contribute heavily at the state level, particularly to prosecutors and judiciary committee members
    - California Correctional Peace Officers Association (CCPOA) contributed >$2 million to state candidates in the 2022 cycle (California Secretary of State, 2023)
    - CCPOA is one of the largest single donors in California state politics
    - Reform organizations have begun to build campaign contribution capacity but remain outspent by status quo stakeholders

- **Revolving Door**:
    - Former prosecutors serve in Congress, on the Sentencing Commission, and in DOJ leadership
    - Former DOJ officials join law firms representing corporate clients in drug enforcement matters
    - Multiple members of the Senate Judiciary Committee served as prosecutors before entering Congress
    - This revolving door tends to favor the prosecutorial perspective in policy discussions
    - Few formerly incarcerated individuals hold positions of policy influence, though this is slowly changing

- **Public Advocacy**:
    - Reform organizations leverage social media, documentary films, investigative journalism, and personal stories
    - Key media: *13th* (Netflix documentary, 2016), *The House I Live In* (2012), *Clemency* (2019)
    - Bipartisan nature of recent reform coalitions has expanded media coverage and public support
    - REFORM Alliance has been particularly effective at combining celebrity advocacy with policy expertise
    - Achieving legislative changes in multiple states on probation, parole, and sentencing

- **Expertise and Information**:
    - USSC data reports are the most authoritative source on federal sentencing patterns
    - Academic research from Vera Institute, Urban Institute, and university scholars informs debates
    - Both reform advocates and opponents selectively cite research to support their positions
    - The emergence of data dashboards and public-facing sentencing tools has increased transparency

- **Litigation**:
    - Strategic litigation by ACLU, Sentencing Project, and Federal Public Defenders challenges sentencing practices
    - Eighth Amendment proportionality claims, equal protection challenges, habeas corpus petitions
    - Supreme Court has generally not recognized broad proportionality limits on drug sentences (*Harmelin v. Michigan*, 1991)
    - Lower courts have occasionally granted relief in extreme cases
    - Class-action litigation has challenged conditions of confinement for drug offenders in several states

- **State and Local Electoral Politics**:
    - Election of "progressive prosecutors" demonstrates reform platforms can win:
        - Philadelphia (Larry Krasner)
        - Los Angeles (George Gascon)
        - Chicago (Kim Foxx)
        - San Francisco (Chesa Boudin, later recalled)
    - Fair and Just Prosecution supports >70 elected prosecutors committed to reducing drug incarceration
    - Recall campaigns against reform prosecutors (Boudin, Gascon attempted) demonstrate political counter-pressure
    - State ballot initiatives (California Prop 47, Oklahoma SQ 780) have been effective vehicles for reform

|---|---|---|---|---|
| Federal prosecutors | Very High | Status quo / oppose reform | Charging discretion; DOJ institutional support | AG policy directives; public opinion shifts |
| U.S. Congress | High | Mixed (bipartisan reform possible) | Legislative authority | Electoral dynamics; fentanyl crisis narrative |
| U.S. Sentencing Commission | High | Pro-reform (evidence-based) | Data authority; guideline-setting power | Congressional override; vacancies |
| Federal judges | High (constrained) | Pro-reform (majority) | Sentencing authority post-*Booker* | Mandatory minimums; statutory limits |
| DEA | Medium-High | Oppose reform | $3.3 billion budget; media access | Congressional oversight; administration policy |
| Private prison companies | Medium | Oppose reform | Lobbying budget; campaign contributions | Public scrutiny; declining federal contracts |
| Reform advocacy coalition | Medium (growing) | Pro-reform | Research; public advocacy; bipartisan coalition | Funding disparities; fentanyl backlash |
| State legislatures | High (state level) | Increasingly pro-reform | State legislative authority | Varies dramatically by state political composition |
| Public opinion | Medium (indirect) | Increasingly pro-reform | Electoral pressure on legislators | Low salience for most voters outside crisis moments |
