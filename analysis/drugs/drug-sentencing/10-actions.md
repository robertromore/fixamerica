# Drug Sentencing: What You Can Do

Drug sentencing reform is one of the rare policy areas where bipartisan coalitions have achieved meaningful change -- the First Step Act demonstrated that citizen engagement, personal stories, and cross-partisan advocacy can move Congress to act. Continued progress depends on sustained public attention and organized advocacy at the federal, state, and local levels.

---

## Individual Actions

### Learn and Stay Informed

- Read the U.S. Sentencing Commission's annual reports and data tables to understand federal sentencing patterns: <https://www.ussc.gov/research/annual-reports-sourcebooks>
- Follow FAMM (Families Against Mandatory Minimums) for updates on federal and state sentencing reform: <https://famm.org/>
- Subscribe to The Marshall Project's newsletter for criminal justice reporting: <https://www.themarshallproject.org/subscribe>
- Read Michelle Alexander's *The New Jim Crow* for historical context on drug sentencing and racial equity
- Track your state's sentencing laws and reform efforts through the Vera Institute's state profiles: <https://www.vera.org/>
- Explore the USSC's Interactive Data Analyzer to understand sentencing patterns in your federal district: <https://ida.ussc.gov/>
- Read the National Research Council's *The Growth of Incarceration in the United States* (2014) for the most comprehensive governmental review of mass incarceration
- Sign up for FAMM's action alerts to receive notifications when sentencing reform legislation is moving: <https://famm.org/take-action/>

### Make Personal Changes

- Examine your own assumptions about drug offenses, sentencing, and public safety; research shows that most people overestimate the deterrent effect of sentence length and underestimate the costs of incarceration
- If you serve on a jury, understand that jury nullification is a legal right -- jurors can acquit if they believe a law or sentence is unjust, though judges are not required to inform jurors of this right
- If you are an employer, consider adopting "ban the box" hiring practices that do not ask about criminal history on initial applications. Over 37 states and 150 cities have adopted some form of ban-the-box policy for public employment (National Employment Law Project, 2023). The federal government adopted ban-the-box for federal hiring in 2016 through the Fair Chance Act.
- Support reentry in your community: volunteer with local reentry organizations, mentor returning citizens, or participate in employer reentry hiring programs such as the Second Chance Business Coalition (which includes JPMorgan Chase, Walmart, and other major employers)
- If you are a landlord, consider adopting fair-chance housing policies that do not automatically screen out applicants with drug convictions. HUD guidance (2016) indicates that blanket criminal record bans in housing may violate the Fair Housing Act.
- Educate yourself about the collateral consequences of drug convictions in your state using the National Inventory of Collateral Consequences of Conviction (NICCC): <https://niccc.nationalreentryresourcecenter.org/>

### Use Your Voice

- **Contact Your Representatives**: Call or write your U.S. Senators and Representative when sentencing reform legislation is pending, including the EQUAL Act and any mandatory minimum reform bills. Phone calls are generally more effective than emails -- ask to speak with the judiciary or criminal justice staffer.
- **Submit Public Comments**: When the U.S. Sentencing Commission proposes guideline amendments, public comment periods are open -- submit comments supporting evidence-based sentencing reform at <https://www.ussc.gov/>. The USSC publishes proposed amendments annually, typically in January, with a public comment period through March.
- **Write Letters to the Editor**: Respond to local media coverage of drug crime by providing data on sentencing effectiveness and the costs of mass incarceration. Local newspapers remain influential with state legislators.
- **Write Op-Eds**: Draft opinion pieces with data and personal stories for local and regional publications. Many outlets actively seek perspectives on criminal justice issues.
- **Testify at Public Hearings**: Both the USSC and state sentencing commissions hold public hearings where citizen input is welcomed. Prepare 2-3 minute statements with specific data and, if possible, personal stories.
- **Share Information**: Use social media to amplify stories of people affected by harsh drug sentencing and to share data from the USSC and reform organizations
- **Engage in Public Conversations**: When drug sentencing comes up in personal conversations, community events, or online discussions, use the key statistics and talking points in the Advocacy Toolkit section below to provide accurate, evidence-based information

## Community Actions

### Organize Locally

- Form or join a local criminal justice reform group focused on sentencing issues; many faith communities, civic organizations, and community groups are active in this space
- Host a screening of documentaries on mass incarceration and the War on Drugs (*13th*, *The House I Live In*, *Time: The Kalief Browder Story*, *Crack: Cocaine, Corruption & Conspiracy*) followed by guided discussion on policy implications
- Partner with local reentry organizations to support people returning from incarceration for drug offenses -- volunteer for mentoring, job training, or housing assistance programs
- Organize town halls with local prosecutors and judges to discuss sentencing practices in your jurisdiction
- Attend local court proceedings to observe drug sentencing hearings firsthand -- federal and most state court proceedings are open to the public
- Create a local "sentencing watch" group that monitors and reports on drug sentencing patterns in your jurisdiction using publicly available court data
- Organize "know your rights" workshops for communities most affected by drug enforcement, in partnership with local legal aid organizations

### Build Coalitions

- Connect with both progressive (ACLU, Drug Policy Alliance) and conservative (Right on Crime, Americans for Prosperity) organizations working on sentencing reform; bipartisan coalitions are the proven pathway to legislative success
- Engage faith communities, which have been powerful voices for sentencing reform -- Prison Fellowship, the National Association of Evangelicals, and Catholic Charities all support reform. Faith-based advocacy was critical to the First Step Act's passage.
- Build relationships with local defense attorneys and public defenders, who have direct experience with sentencing disparities and can provide expert testimony at public events
- Partner with local chapters of the NAACP, Urban League, and other civil rights organizations that prioritize criminal justice reform as a racial equity issue
- Engage veterans' organizations: veterans are disproportionately represented among drug offenders, and organizations like the National Veterans Legal Services Program support sentencing reform for veterans with substance use disorders connected to service
- Connect with local business groups and chambers of commerce: employers increasingly support sentencing reform and reentry hiring as solutions to labor shortages

### Support Directly Affected People

- Write letters to incarcerated individuals through organizations like Letters to Prisoners or Books to Prisons
- Attend sentencing hearings in your local federal or state court to understand how drug sentencing works in practice. Federal court proceedings are generally open to the public.
- Support commissary and communication funds for incarcerated individuals through organizations like The Bail Project or National Bail Fund Network
- Volunteer to drive family members to visit incarcerated loved ones, particularly in rural areas where federal prisons are often located far from communities of origin
- Support children of incarcerated parents through organizations like Sesame Workshop's "Little Children, Big Challenges: Incarceration" program or Big Brothers Big Sisters

## Political Actions

### Contact Elected Officials

**When to Contact**:

- When the EQUAL Act or other sentencing reform bills are scheduled for committee markup or floor vote
- During annual budget and appropriations season (February-September), when drug court and treatment funding is determined
- When your state legislature considers mandatory minimum reform bills
- After high-profile drug sentencing cases that illustrate the need for reform

**What to Say - Template Email**:

```text
Subject: Support Drug Sentencing Reform -- Pass the EQUAL Act

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

I am a constituent from [City, State], and I am writing to urge you to support drug sentencing reform, including the EQUAL Act, which would eliminate the remaining crack-to-powder cocaine sentencing disparity.

The 18-to-1 crack-to-powder ratio has no scientific basis -- crack and powder cocaine are pharmacologically identical -- and disproportionately harms Black Americans, who constitute 77% of federal crack defendants. The U.S. Sentencing Commission has recommended eliminating this disparity in four separate reports to Congress.

More broadly, I urge you to support eliminating mandatory minimum sentences for drug offenses, which research shows do not deter drug use or trafficking but impose enormous costs on families, communities, and taxpayers. The federal government spends $39,158 per year to incarcerate each prisoner, and federal drug inmates cost over $2.7 billion annually. This money would be far more effectively spent on treatment, prevention, and reentry programs.

The First Step Act proved that bipartisan sentencing reform is possible and popular. I urge you to build on that success.

Thank you for your service and consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your City, State]
```

**What to Say - Phone Call Script**:

```text
"Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [City]. I'm calling to urge [Representative/Senator] [Name] to support drug sentencing reform, including the EQUAL Act. The crack-to-powder cocaine disparity has no scientific basis and disproportionately harms Black Americans. I also support eliminating mandatory minimums for drug offenses, which cost taxpayers billions without reducing drug use. Can you please note my position? Thank you."
```

### Support Legislation

Current or proposed legislation to support:

- **EQUAL Act**: Eliminates the crack-to-powder cocaine sentencing disparity by establishing a 1-to-1 ratio with retroactive application. Contact members of the Senate and House Judiciary Committees. The EQUAL Act has had bipartisan co-sponsors in multiple Congresses and passed the House Judiciary Committee in 2021 with a 36-5 vote.
- **Smarter Sentencing Act**: Reduces (but does not eliminate) mandatory minimums for drug offenses and expands the safety valve. Bipartisan sponsors in previous Congresses including Senators Durbin (D-IL) and Lee (R-UT).
- **State mandatory minimum reform bills**: Many states consider mandatory minimum reform annually. Check your state legislature's session calendar and contact state representatives. Use the National Conference of State Legislatures (NCSL) bill tracking database to find pending legislation: <https://www.ncsl.org/>
- **Drug court and treatment funding**: Support appropriations for the Byrne JAG program, SAMHSA treatment grants, and drug court expansion. Drug courts currently serve only an estimated 10-15% of eligible individuals due to funding constraints (NADCP, 2024).
- **Clean Slate / automatic expungement legislation**: Support state and federal bills that automatically seal or expunge drug conviction records after a period of time without reoffense. Pennsylvania (2018), Utah (2019), Michigan (2020), and Connecticut (2021) have enacted Clean Slate laws with bipartisan support.
- **Collateral consequences reform**: Support bills that remove drug-conviction barriers to SNAP, TANF, public housing, student financial aid, and occupational licensing. The REDEEM Act (federal) and various state bills address specific collateral consequences.

### State-Level Advocacy Guide

State reform is where much of the actionable change occurs. Here is a guide to engaging at the state level:

| Action | How | When | Resources |
|---|---|---|---|
| Track state sentencing bills | Sign up for legislative alerts from your state legislature's website or from FAMM's state page | Ongoing; state sessions typically January-June | FAMM state pages: <https://famm.org/our-work/state-reform/> |
| Testify at committee hearings | Contact committee staff to sign up for public testimony; prepare 2-3 minute statement with personal or data-driven arguments | When sentencing reform bills are in committee | FAMM testimony guidelines; local ACLU chapter support |
| Meet with your state legislator | Request in-person meeting at district office; bring data and personal stories | Outside of session (July-December) for relationship building; during session for bill advocacy | League of Women Voters meeting guides |
| Attend state sentencing commission meetings | Most state sentencing commissions hold public meetings; attend and provide public comment | Varies by state; check commission website | National Center for State Courts: <https://www.ncsc.org/> |
| Support reform-minded DA candidates | Research candidates' positions on mandatory minimums, diversion, and charging policies; volunteer or donate | Election cycles; many DA races are in odd years | Fair and Just Prosecution: <https://fairandjustprosecution.org/> |

### Electoral Engagement

**Questions to Ask Candidates**:

1. "Do you support eliminating mandatory minimum sentences for drug offenses? If not, why do you believe they are effective despite research showing they do not deter drug use?"
2. "Will you support the EQUAL Act to eliminate the crack-to-powder cocaine sentencing disparity?"
3. "What is your plan to address racial disparities in drug sentencing, given that Black men receive sentences 17.7% longer than similarly situated white men?"
4. "Do you support expanding drug courts and treatment-based alternatives to incarceration for drug offenses?"
5. "How would you address the fentanyl crisis -- through public health measures or additional mandatory minimums?"
6. "Do you support retroactive resentencing for individuals serving sentences that would be lower under current law?"

**Voting Considerations**:

- Look for candidates who endorse specific reform legislation rather than offering vague statements about "smart on crime" approaches
- Check whether candidates have accepted campaign contributions from private prison companies or prosecutors' associations that oppose reform
- Review candidates' records on past sentencing votes if they are incumbents -- the First Step Act vote record is publicly available
- For prosecutor elections (DAs and state AGs), examine candidates' positions on mandatory minimums, diversion programs, and charging policies
- Research whether judicial candidates have records of imposing sentences above or below guidelines for drug offenses

**Engaging in Prosecutor Elections**:

- District attorney elections are among the most impactful opportunities for drug sentencing reform at the local level
- In most jurisdictions, prosecutors are elected with very low voter turnout, meaning engaged citizens have outsized influence
- Ask DA candidates about their policies on:
    - Charging at mandatory minimum thresholds vs. using discretion
    - Drug diversion and treatment court referrals
    - Racial impact analysis of charging decisions
    - Restorative justice alternatives for drug offenses
- Support candidates who commit to data-driven, evidence-based prosecution

## Professional Actions

- **For Attorneys**: Volunteer with federal public defender offices or legal aid organizations that handle resentencing petitions under the First Step Act. Join the National Association of Criminal Defense Lawyers' sentencing reform committee.
- **For Healthcare Professionals**: Advocate within your professional associations (AMA, APA, NASW) for treatment-based sentencing alternatives. Provide expert testimony on addiction as a medical condition in sentencing hearings.
- **For Educators**: Incorporate criminal justice data and sentencing policy into courses on government, social studies, and public policy. Invite speakers from reform organizations.
- **For Social Workers and Counselors**: Support reentry programs for individuals released through resentencing. Advocate for treatment integration in sentencing frameworks.
- **For Researchers**: Conduct and publish research on sentencing outcomes, racial disparities, and reform effectiveness. Present findings to sentencing commissions and legislative committees.
- **For Journalists**: Cover sentencing reform beyond individual cases -- report on systemic patterns, data trends, and policy impacts. Use USSC data to quantify disparities. The USSC's Interactive Data Analyzer (<https://ida.ussc.gov/>) provides searchable sentencing data by district, drug type, race, and other variables.
- **For Business Leaders**: Join the Second Chance Business Coalition or similar employer networks committed to hiring people with criminal records. Advocate within your industry for removal of blanket criminal record hiring exclusions. Provide testimony to legislatures on the business case for sentencing reform and workforce reintegration.
- **For Veterans and Military Families**: Connect with organizations like the National Veterans Legal Services Program, which provides legal assistance to veterans facing drug charges. Advocate for veterans treatment courts, which combine drug treatment with judicial supervision tailored to the needs of veterans with service-connected substance use disorders. As of 2024, there are approximately 700 veterans treatment courts operating nationwide (Justice for Vets, 2024).
- **For Faith Leaders**: Organize congregational letter-writing campaigns when sentencing reform legislation is pending. Host "second chance" Sunday events to raise awareness. Participate in prison ministry programs. The National Association of Evangelicals' resolution on criminal justice reform provides a framework for faith-based advocacy.
- **For Public Health Professionals**: Advocate for treatment-based alternatives to incarceration in your professional capacity. Provide data on the public health costs of mass incarceration for drug offenses. Support harm reduction initiatives (naloxone distribution, drug checking, syringe services) that reduce the harms associated with drug use without relying on criminal sanctions.
- **For Elected Officials**: Champion sentencing reform legislation at the federal, state, or local level. Use your platform to educate constituents about the costs and ineffectiveness of current drug sentencing. Support drug court and diversion program funding in budget and appropriations processes. Commission racial impact analyses of existing sentencing laws in your jurisdiction.
- **For Philanthropists**: Direct funding to organizations working on drug sentencing reform, particularly those led by formerly incarcerated people and directly affected communities. Fund reentry programs that provide housing, employment, and treatment support to people released from drug sentences. Support research on sentencing outcomes and reform effectiveness.

## Direct Support for Incarcerated Individuals and Returning Citizens

### Supporting People Seeking Resentencing

- **Federal public defender offices** handle resentencing petitions under the First Step Act but face overwhelming caseloads. Pro bono attorneys can volunteer through the Federal Pro Bono Program to assist with resentencing petitions: contact your local federal public defender office.
- **FAMM's Compassionate Release Guide**: FAMM provides a free guide for families of federal inmates seeking compassionate release: <https://famm.org/our-work/compassionate-release/>
- **The Sentencing Resource Counsel Project**: Provides training and resources for attorneys representing clients in federal sentencing proceedings: available through the Federal Public Defenders.

### Supporting Returning Citizens

- **Employment**:
    - The Second Chance Business Coalition (<https://secondchancebusinesscoalition.org/>) connects employers committed to hiring people with criminal records
    - Over 40 major employers are members, including JPMorgan Chase, Walmart, Target, and Koch Industries
    - The Federal Bonding Program provides free fidelity bonds for employers who hire people with criminal records, reducing employer risk
    - Encourage local employers to adopt fair-chance hiring practices

- **Housing**:
    - Contact your local continuum of care or reentry coalition for transitional housing resources
    - The National Reentry Resource Center maintains a directory: <https://nationalreentryresourcecenter.org/>
    - Advocate for local fair-chance housing ordinances that limit blanket criminal record screening
    - HUD guidance (2016) indicates blanket criminal record bans in housing may violate the Fair Housing Act

- **Education**:
    - People returning from drug sentences may be eligible for Pell Grants (eligibility restored for incarcerated individuals in 2023)
    - Encourage enrollment in community college or vocational training programs
    - Connect returning citizens with local workforce development programs

- **Healthcare**:
    - In Medicaid expansion states, formerly incarcerated individuals can enroll in Medicaid upon release
    - In non-expansion states, federally qualified health centers provide services on a sliding fee scale
    - SUD treatment should begin or resume immediately upon release; pre-release treatment planning is critical
    - Connect returning citizens with peer support specialists who have lived experience with both incarceration and recovery

## Advocacy Toolkit

### Key Talking Points

When discussing drug sentencing reform with others, emphasize:

1. **Cost-effectiveness**: Incarcerating one federal prisoner costs $39,158 per year (BOP, FY2023). Treatment is 7 times more cost-effective than enforcement at reducing drug use (RAND Corporation). We are spending billions on an approach that does not work.
2. **Racial justice**: Black men receive federal drug sentences 17.7% longer than similarly situated white men, even after controlling for offense severity and criminal history (USSC, 2017). The remaining crack-to-powder disparity has no scientific basis and primarily impacts Black Americans.
3. **Proven reform success**: The First Step Act has resentenced over 30,000 people with minimal public safety impact. Over 33 states have reformed mandatory minimums since 2000 without increases in drug crime.
4. **Bipartisan support**: The First Step Act passed with overwhelming bipartisan support (87-12 in the Senate). Sentencing reform is supported by organizations from the ACLU to Right on Crime, from Prison Fellowship to the Drug Policy Alliance.

### Common Misconceptions to Counter

| Misconception | Reality | Source |
|---|---|---|
| "Most drug prisoners are violent criminals" | 36.2% of federal drug trafficking offenders have no prior convictions (Criminal History Category I) | USSC, FY2023 |
| "Mandatory minimums deter drug use" | Research finds no statistical relationship between mandatory minimums and drug use rates | Brennan Center, 2016 |
| "Drug offenders have high recidivism" | Federal drug trafficking offenders have a 37.2% recidivism rate over 8 years -- lower than most other federal offense categories | USSC, 2022 |
| "Crack is more dangerous than powder cocaine" | Crack and powder cocaine are pharmacologically identical; the USSC, NIDA, AMA, and ABA all agree the disparity is not scientifically justified | USSC reports (1995, 1997, 2002, 2007) |
| "Sentencing reform increases crime" | States that reduced drug sentences since 2000 have not experienced increases in drug crime | Vera Institute, 2023 |
| "Drug sellers are outsiders preying on communities" | Most drug selling occurs within social networks; a significant portion of sellers are themselves users supporting their own addiction | Sevigny and Caulkins, 2004 |
| "Mandatory minimums target kingpins" | Most federal drug defendants are low-to-mid-level participants; kingpins often negotiate shorter sentences through cooperation | USSC, 2011 |
| "We can't reduce drug sentences during the fentanyl crisis" | Fentanyl overdose deaths quadrupled between 2016 and 2023 *during* a period of severe mandatory minimums; incarceration has not reduced fentanyl supply | CDC WONDER, 2024 |
| "Reform means no consequences for drug offenses" | All major reform proposals maintain penalties for drug trafficking; reform focuses on proportionality, judicial discretion, and treatment alternatives | EQUAL Act; Smarter Sentencing Act |

### Building Public Support: Key Statistics for Conversations

When discussing drug sentencing reform in everyday conversations, these statistics are most compelling:

- **Cost**: It costs $39,158 per year to incarcerate one federal prisoner (BOP, FY2023). The federal government spends over $2.7 billion per year incarcerating drug offenders alone. Drug treatment costs $5,000-$7,000 per year per participant and is 7 times more effective at reducing drug use than enforcement (RAND Corporation).
- **Scale**: Approximately 350,000 Americans are incarcerated for drug offenses on any given day (Sentencing Project, 2024). The United States incarcerates more people for drug offenses than the entire prison populations of most European nations.
- **Racial impact**: Black Americans are incarcerated for drug offenses at nearly 6 times the rate of white Americans despite similar rates of drug use (National Academy of Sciences, 2020). Black men receive drug sentences 17.7% longer than similarly situated white men (USSC, 2017).
- **Reform success**: Over 30,000 people have benefited from the First Step Act with minimal public safety impact (USSC, 2024). More than 33 states have reformed drug mandatory minimums since 2000 without increases in drug crime (Vera Institute, 2023).
- **Public opinion**: 68% of Americans support eliminating mandatory minimum sentences for nonviolent drug offenses (Pew Research Center, 2021). Support for sentencing reform spans party lines, with 57% of Republicans and 81% of Democrats favoring reduced drug sentences (Gallup, 2022).
