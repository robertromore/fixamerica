# Drug Sentencing: Current State

## Federal Drug Sentencing

The federal drug sentencing system is governed by a combination of statutory mandatory minimums, the U.S. Sentencing Guidelines, and judicial discretion. Following the Supreme Court's decision in *United States v. Booker*, 543 U.S. 220 (2005), the federal sentencing guidelines became advisory rather than mandatory, though judges must still consult them and explain departures. Despite this shift, mandatory minimum statutes continue to constrain judicial discretion for a majority of federal drug cases.

### Mandatory Minimum Application

More than half of all federal drug offenders are sentenced under mandatory minimum provisions. The rate varies by offense type and role in the offense.

| Category | Subject to Mandatory Minimum (%) | Received Relief (%) | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| All drug offenders | 56.2% | 24.8% | USSC, FY2023 |
| Drug traffickers | 67.4% | 28.3% | USSC, FY2023 |
| Simple possession (federal) | 12.1% | 5.3% | USSC, FY2023 |
| Offenders with substantial assistance | -- | 100% (by definition) | USSC, FY2023 |
| Safety-valve eligible | -- | 100% (by definition) | USSC, FY2023 |

Relief from mandatory minimums comes through three primary pathways:

1. **Substantial Assistance (18 U.S.C. Section 3553(e))**: The prosecutor files a motion certifying that the defendant provided substantial assistance to the government in investigating or prosecuting others. In FY2023, 22.6% of drug offenders received this departure.
2. **Safety Valve (18 U.S.C. Section 3553(f))**: Defendants who meet specific criteria -- including limited criminal history -- can receive sentences below the mandatory minimum. The First Step Act of 2018 expanded eligibility by broadening the criminal history threshold.
3. **Compassionate Release (18 U.S.C. Section 3582(c)(1)(A))**: Federal inmates can petition for sentence reduction based on extraordinary and compelling circumstances. The First Step Act allowed inmates to petition courts directly rather than relying solely on BOP motions.

## Crack-Powder Cocaine Disparity

### Current Disparity

The crack-to-powder cocaine sentencing disparity remains one of the most racially significant features of federal drug sentencing law.

## Racial Disparities in Drug Sentencing

Racial disparities pervade every stage of the drug sentencing process, from arrest through sentencing.

### Federal Drug Sentencing by Race/Ethnicity

| Race/Ethnicity | Share of Federal Drug Defendants (FY2023) | Average Sentence (months) | Mandatory Minimum Rate | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| White | 22.8% | 60 | 47.3% | USSC, FY2023 |
| Black | 26.2% | 82 | 63.5% | USSC, FY2023 |
| Hispanic | 44.8% | 71 | 57.8% | USSC, FY2023 |
| Other | 6.2% | 54 | 41.2% | USSC, FY2023 |

### Disparity Analysis

Research consistently finds that racial disparities in drug sentencing cannot be fully explained by legally relevant factors such as offense severity and criminal history:

- Black men receive federal drug sentences approximately 17.7% longer than similarly situated white men after controlling for offense level, criminal history, and other legally relevant factors (USSC, 2017)
- Black defendants are 21% more likely than white defendants to receive a mandatory minimum sentence (USSC, 2017)
- Prosecutors are significantly less likely to file substantial assistance motions for Black defendants than for white defendants, even controlling for case characteristics (Starr and Rehavi, 2013)
- The "trial penalty" -- the difference between post-trial and plea-bargain sentences -- is substantially larger for Black and Hispanic defendants (National Association of Criminal Defense Lawyers, 2018)

## State Sentencing Variation

State drug sentencing policies vary enormously. Some states have moved aggressively to reform mandatory minimums and reduce incarceration for drug offenses, while others maintain extremely harsh sentencing structures.

### State Approaches to Drug Sentencing

| Category | States (Selected) | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| No mandatory minimums for drug possession | CT, MA, NY (reformed), NJ, CA | Judicial discretion for possession offenses |
| Reduced mandatory minimums | MI, IN, SC, KY, LA, MS | Reforms since 2000 reducing or narrowing mandatories |
| Drug-quantity-based mandatories | FL, TX, VA, OH | Trigger weights determine minimum sentences |
| Three-strikes/habitual offender enhancements | AL, FL, GA, TX, WA | Prior drug felonies trigger enhanced sentences |
| Defelonization of possession | OR, CO (some drugs), OK (reformed) | Reclassified possession from felony to misdemeanor |

### Key State Reform Examples

| State | Reform | Year | Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| New York | Drug Law Reform Act (rolled back Rockefeller Drug Laws) | 2009 | Restored judicial discretion; reduced prison population by ~35% for drug offenses |
| California | Proposition 36 (Three Strikes reform) | 2012 | Resentenced ~3,000 inmates; saved ~$150 million annually |
| California | Proposition 47 (felony-to-misdemeanor reclassification) | 2014 | Reduced drug possession to misdemeanor; ~13,000 resentencings |
| Connecticut | Drug sentencing reform package | 2015 | Eliminated mandatory minimums for most drug offenses |
| Mississippi | Criminal justice reform package (HB 585) | 2014 | Revised drug sentencing, expanded parole; saved $40 million |
| Oklahoma | SQ 780 (State Question 780) | 2016 | Reclassified simple drug possession as misdemeanor |
| Louisiana | Justice Reinvestment Act | 2017 | Reduced drug sentences; projected savings of $262 million over 10 years |

## First Step Act Implementation

The First Step Act of 2018 (P.L. 115-391) is the most significant federal sentencing reform legislation in a generation. Its drug sentencing provisions include:

### Key Provisions and Impact

| Provision | Description | Beneficiaries (through 2024) | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Retroactive Fair Sentencing Act | Applied the 2010 crack-powder ratio reduction retroactively | ~2,660 resentenced | USSC, 2024 |
| Expanded Safety Valve | Broadened eligibility for sentences below mandatory minimums | ~3,500 additional per year | USSC, 2024 |
| Stacking Reform (Section 924(c)) | Eliminated mandatory consecutive sentences for first-time offenders | ~950 resentenced | DOJ, 2024 |
| Good-Time Credit Reform | Increased credit from 47 to 54 days per year served | ~4,000 released early (drug offenses) | BOP, 2024 |
| Earned Time Credits (EBRR) | Credits for participating in recidivism-reduction programs | >20,000 (all offenses) | BOP, 2024 |
| Compassionate Release | Allowed direct petitions to courts | >7,000 grants (all offenses, FY2020-2024) | USSC, 2024 |

## Sentencing Commission and Guidelines

The U.S. Sentencing Commission (USSC) plays a central role in federal drug sentencing. It sets the sentencing guidelines, collects and analyzes sentencing data, and advises Congress on sentencing policy.

### Recent Guidelines Actions

| Action | Year | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Amendment 782 (Drug Quantity Table reduction) | 2014 | Reduced drug guidelines by 2 offense levels; made retroactive |
| Amendment 788 (retroactive application of 782) | 2014 | ~46,000 offenders eligible for sentence reduction |
| First Step Act implementation guidance | 2019-2020 | Clarified safety-valve expansion, stacking reform |
| Compassionate release policy statement | 2023 | Expanded categories of extraordinary and compelling reasons |
| Criminal History Report and Recommendations | 2024 | Recommended reducing weight of certain criminal history factors |

## Emerging Challenges

### Fentanyl Sentencing

The emergence of fentanyl and its analogs has created new sentencing challenges. Fentanyl's extreme potency means that very small quantities trigger severe mandatory minimums. A single kilogram of fentanyl triggers a 10-year mandatory minimum under 21 U.S.C. Section 841(b)(1)(A), the same threshold that applies to 5 kilograms of powder cocaine or 1,000 kilograms of marijuana. This potency-to-weight dynamic means that low-level couriers or users may face sentences designed for high-level traffickers.

### Fentanyl Analog Scheduling

The Temporary Scheduling Order for fentanyl-related substances, first issued in 2018 and extended multiple times, imposes Class-wide scheduling of all fentanyl analogs as Schedule I substances. Critics argue this approach is scientifically imprecise, may capture substances with therapeutic potential, and imposes the same harsh penalties on novel analogs regardless of their actual potency or danger.

### Intersection with Immigration Enforcement

Drug offenses trigger severe immigration consequences, including mandatory deportation for most noncitizens. As Hispanic individuals constitute 44.8% of federal drug defendants (USSC, FY2023), the intersection of drug sentencing and immigration enforcement compounds existing disparities.
