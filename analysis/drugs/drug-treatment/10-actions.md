# Drug Treatment: What Citizens Can Do

## Overview

Expanding access to effective drug treatment requires action at every level--from individual conversations that reduce stigma to organized advocacy that changes laws. This section provides concrete, actionable steps for citizens who want to help close the treatment gap and save lives.

---

## Immediate Actions (This Week)

### Learn and Share

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| Learn about addiction as a medical condition | Read NIDA's *Principles of Drug Addiction Treatment* (free online) | Informed advocacy; reduced personal stigma |
| Use person-first language | Say "person with substance use disorder" not "addict" or "junkie" | Language shapes attitudes; reduces stigma |
| Learn about naloxone | Visit getnaloxone.org or nextdistro.org | Preparedness to save a life |
| Carry naloxone | Free from many pharmacies, health departments, and mail-order programs | Every year, naloxone reverses 50,000+ overdoses |
| Share treatment resources | Save the SAMHSA helpline: 1-800-662-4357 (24/7, free, confidential) | Connect someone in need to help |

### Support Someone You Know

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| Have a non-judgmental conversation | Express concern; ask how you can help; avoid ultimatums | People are more likely to seek help when supported |
| Research local treatment options | Use findtreatment.gov (SAMHSA treatment locator) | Remove the "I don't know where to go" barrier |
| Offer practical support | Transportation, childcare, help navigating insurance | Practical barriers prevent many from seeking treatment |
| Connect with a family support group | Partnership to End Addiction helpline: 1-855-378-4373; Al-Anon; Nar-Anon | Families need support too; burnout is real |
| Learn about MOUD | Understand that buprenorphine and methadone are evidence-based treatments, not "replacing one drug with another" | Counter misinformation that prevents people from seeking effective treatment |

## Community-Level Actions (This Month)

### Support Local Treatment Access

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| Attend zoning hearings for treatment facilities | Contact your city/county planning department | NIMBYism is a top barrier; showing up matters |
| Support recovery housing in your neighborhood | Write letters of support; attend community meetings | Recovery housing is among the most effective and least disruptive community services |
| Volunteer at a treatment or recovery organization | Contact local SUD treatment providers; recovery community organizations | Direct service; builds understanding |
| Support a local recovery community organization | Donate time or resources; attend events | Strengthens recovery infrastructure |
| Advocate for naloxone availability | Ask your pharmacy, workplace, and school if they stock naloxone | Expand lifesaving access |

### Engage Your Healthcare System

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| Ask your doctor about addiction screening | Does your primary care practice screen for SUD? Offer MOUD? | Patient demand drives provider behavior |
| Ask your employer about EAP services | Does your Employee Assistance Program cover SUD treatment? How many sessions? | Workplace programs are underutilized |
| Review your insurance plan's SUD coverage | Check prior authorization requirements, network adequacy, coverage limits | Identify potential parity violations |
| File a parity complaint if coverage is inadequate | Contact your state insurance department or DOL | Enforcement depends on consumer complaints |
| Support your local hospital's ED-initiated treatment program | Advocate to hospital leadership if your ED does not offer buprenorphine initiation | Only 18% of EDs currently offer this lifesaving service |

## Advocacy Actions (This Quarter)

### Contact Elected Officials

**Federal level:**

| Issue | Ask | Target |
|-------|-----|--------|
| SUPPORT Act reauthorization | Reauthorize and expand all treatment provisions | U.S. Senators and Representatives |
| SAPT Block Grant funding | Increase from $1.86B to $3.5B | Appropriations committee members |
| Pharmacy-based methadone | Support DEA's proposed rule; urge finalization | Senators; HHS Secretary |
| Parity enforcement | Fund DOL parity enforcement division; support private right of action | Senators and Representatives |
| Telehealth permanence | Make COVID-era SUD telehealth flexibilities permanent | Senators and Representatives |
| Medicaid expansion | Encourage holdout states to expand; support federal incentives | State and federal representatives |

**State level:**

| Issue | Ask | Target |
|-------|-----|--------|
| Medicaid SUD benefits | Expand covered services; remove barriers | State legislators, governor |
| Opioid settlement fund transparency | Require public reporting of settlement spending; mandate treatment allocation | State legislators, attorney general |
| MOUD in corrections | Require all state prisons and jails to offer MOUD | State legislators, corrections officials |
| Insurance parity enforcement | Strengthen state parity laws; fund enforcement | State legislators, insurance commissioner |
| Recovery housing protections | Enact zoning preemption for recovery housing | State and local legislators |
| Counselor workforce | Increase Medicaid SUD reimbursement rates | State legislators, Medicaid director |

**How to contact officials effectively:**

1. **Phone calls**: Most effective constituent communication. Call district offices (not DC). Keep it brief: "I'm a constituent. I'm calling to urge [specific action]."
2. **In-person meetings**: Request meetings during recess periods. Bring personal stories and data.
3. **Written letters**: Handwritten letters receive more attention than emails. Be specific about the policy ask.
4. **Town halls**: Attend and ask questions about SUD treatment policy. Bring data and personal stories.

## Professional Actions (Ongoing)

### For Healthcare Professionals

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| Get trained to prescribe buprenorphine | Complete 8-hour training (SAMHSA resources available) | Any DEA-licensed prescriber can now prescribe |
| Screen every patient for SUD | Use validated screening tools (AUDIT, DAST, NIDA Quick Screen) | Most SUD goes undetected in primary care |
| Integrate addiction into your practice | Offer MOUD; connect patients to behavioral health | Primary care is the front line for SUD |
| Join Project ECHO | Free mentoring and support for MOUD prescribers | Build competence; join a community of practice |
| Mentor a colleague | Share your experience prescribing MOUD | Peer influence is the most effective way to change practice |

### For Employers

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| Implement a recovery-friendly workplace policy | See recoveryathwork.org for resources | Retain employees; reduce disability costs |
| Review your health plan's SUD coverage | Audit for parity compliance; demand improvements | Improve employee access to treatment |
| Eliminate pre-employment drug testing for cannabis | Focus on impairment, not metabolites | Remove barriers to employment for people in recovery |
| Offer paid leave for SUD treatment | Treat SUD treatment like any other medical leave | Remove fear of job loss as a barrier to seeking help |
| Support second-chance hiring | Consider applicants with SUD history | Employment is the strongest predictor of sustained recovery |

### For Attorneys and Legal Professionals

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| File parity complaints and lawsuits | Represent individuals denied SUD coverage | Litigation drives parity compliance |
| Challenge drug court MOUD restrictions | Legal challenges under ADA and MHPAEA | End unconstitutional medication restrictions |
| Support criminal justice diversion | Advocate for treatment alternatives to incarceration | Reduce the criminalization of addiction |
| Pro bono representation for SUD discrimination | Employment, housing, and public accommodation cases | Enforce existing anti-discrimination protections |

### For Educators

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| Teach evidence-based SUD prevention | Implement programs like Life Skills Training, Too Good for Drugs | Reduce youth substance use initiation |
| Train school counselors on SUD identification | Ensure schools can identify and refer students with SUD | Early intervention dramatically improves outcomes |
| Advocate for naloxone in schools | Work with school boards to stock and train on naloxone | Schools are increasingly seeing overdose events |
| Reduce stigma in school communities | Educate parents and staff about addiction as a medical condition | Shape the next generation's understanding |
