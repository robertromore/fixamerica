# Psychedelics Policy: History

## Historical Development

The history of psychedelics in America spans millennia of indigenous use, a brief period of extraordinary scientific promise in the mid-20th century, decades of prohibition driven by political rather than scientific considerations, and a modern renaissance that is attempting to reclaim these substances for medicine and therapy. Understanding this history is essential for designing policy that avoids repeating past mistakes.

---

### Indigenous and Pre-Modern Use (Pre-1938): Ancient Roots

- **Indigenous ceremonial use**: Psilocybin mushrooms have been used in Mesoamerican religious ceremonies for at least 3,000 years. The Aztecs called them *teonanacatl* ("flesh of the gods"). Archaeological evidence from Saharan Africa suggests mushroom use dating back 7,000-9,000 years.
- **Peyote traditions**: The Native American Church and its predecessors have used peyote (containing mescaline) in religious ceremonies for centuries. The Huichol people of Mexico have used peyote for at least 2,000 years. Peyote use spread to North American indigenous communities in the 19th century.
- **Ayahuasca ceremonies**: Indigenous communities in the Amazon Basin have used ayahuasca (containing DMT and MAO inhibitors) for healing and spiritual purposes for an estimated 1,000+ years.
- **Western discovery of mescaline**: German pharmacologist Arthur Heffter first isolated mescaline from peyote in 1897. Aldous Huxley's *The Doors of Perception* (1954) popularized mescaline experiences in Western culture.
- **Iboga traditions in West Africa**: The Bwiti religion of Gabon and Cameroon has used ibogaine-containing iboga root bark in initiation ceremonies for centuries. These traditions inform contemporary interest in ibogaine for opioid addiction treatment.
- **Global context**: Psychoactive plant use in spiritual and healing contexts is documented across virtually every inhabited continent. These traditions demonstrate that human engagement with psychedelic substances is not a modern phenomenon but a deep and persistent cross-cultural practice.

### Early Legal Framework (Pre-1960)

Before the 1960s, there was no federal prohibition on psychedelic substances. The Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act of 1938 regulated drugs for safety but did not prohibit specific psychoactive compounds. Mescaline was first restricted under state-level poison control laws in some jurisdictions, but there was no comprehensive federal framework targeting psychedelic compounds. This permissive legal environment enabled the initial wave of clinical research.

### The First Wave of Research (1938-1965): Scientific Promise

- **LSD synthesis**: Swiss chemist Albert Hofmann synthesized LSD at Sandoz Laboratories in 1938 and discovered its psychoactive properties in 1943 when he accidentally absorbed a small amount through his fingertips. His intentional self-experiment on April 19, 1943 ("Bicycle Day") confirmed the compound's extraordinary potency.
- **Psychiatric research explosion**: Between 1950 and 1965, more than 1,000 peer-reviewed papers were published on LSD, involving more than 40,000 research subjects. LSD was investigated as a treatment for alcoholism, depression, anxiety, and other conditions, with promising results.
- **Sandoz distribution**: Sandoz Pharmaceuticals distributed LSD under the trade name Delysid to researchers and psychiatrists worldwide, marketing it as an aid to psychotherapy and as a tool for understanding psychosis.
- **CIA and military programs**: The CIA's MKUltra program (1953-1973) conducted covert experiments with LSD and other psychoactive substances, often on unwitting subjects, including prisoners, mental patients, and members of the public. The Army also tested LSD at Edgewood Arsenal. These programs were unethical, illegal, and ultimately damaging to the scientific legitimacy of psychedelic research.
- **Psilocybin identification**: R. Gordon Wasson's 1957 *Life* magazine article about his participation in a Mazatec mushroom ceremony in Oaxaca, Mexico introduced psilocybin to mainstream Western awareness. Albert Hofmann subsequently isolated and synthesized psilocybin and psilocin in 1958.
- **Harvard Psilocybin Project**: Timothy Leary and Richard Alpert (later Ram Dass) conducted psilocybin experiments at Harvard University from 1960 to 1963, including the Concord Prison Experiment and the Marsh Chapel Experiment. Their increasingly evangelical promotion of psychedelics and departure from scientific protocols led to their dismissal from Harvard in 1963.
- **Saskatchewan LSD research**: Canadian psychiatrists Humphry Osmond and Abram Hoffer conducted pioneering LSD research for alcoholism at Saskatchewan's Weyburn Mental Hospital from 1953 to 1960. Their results -- approximately 50% of chronic alcoholics showed significant improvement after a single LSD session -- were among the most promising early clinical findings (Krebs and Johansen, *Journal of Psychopharmacology*, 2012, meta-analysis).
- **Czechoslovak psycholytic therapy**: Stanislav Grof and colleagues in Czechoslovakia developed "psycholytic therapy" using LSD in psychotherapy from the late 1950s through the 1960s, treating thousands of patients. Grof's work would later influence the therapeutic models used in modern psychedelic research.
- **Scope of research**: Between 1950 and 1965, the U.S. government itself -- through the National Institute of Mental Health and other agencies -- funded more than 100 studies on psychedelics. The research was supported across the political spectrum and considered mainstream psychiatry.

### The Scale of First-Wave Research

The first wave of psychedelic research was far larger than is commonly understood:

| Metric | Value | Period |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Published papers on LSD | >1,000 | 1950-1965 |
| Human research subjects (LSD) | >40,000 | 1950-1965 |
| Therapeutic indications studied | Alcoholism, depression, anxiety, PTSD (then called "combat neurosis"), end-of-life distress, autism | 1950-1965 |
| Countries with active LSD research programs | >20 | 1950-1965 |
| U.S. government-funded psychedelic studies | >100 | 1950-1965 |

### Prohibition and the Culture Wars (1965-1990): Political Backlash

- **Counterculture association**: By the mid-1960s, LSD had become a symbol of the counterculture movement, anti-Vietnam War protests, and generational rebellion. Timothy Leary's exhortation to "Turn on, tune in, drop out" became a cultural flashpoint. The Grateful Dead, the Beatles (*Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band*, 1967), and Ken Kesey's Merry Pranksters became public symbols of psychedelic culture.
- **Media sensationalism**: Major media outlets published sensationalized stories about LSD-related harms, including the widely reported (and likely apocryphal) claim that LSD users stared at the sun until blinded. Art Linkletter blamed his daughter's 1969 suicide on LSD use, becoming a prominent anti-LSD advocate. These narratives shaped public opinion and created political pressure for prohibition.
- **State-level bans**: California became the first state to ban LSD in 1966. Nevada, New York, and other states followed rapidly. By 1968, most states had enacted LSD prohibition.
- **Federal criminalization**: The Drug Abuse Control Amendments of 1965 placed restrictions on LSD, psilocybin, and other hallucinogens. The Staggers-Dodd Bill (1968) made possession of LSD a misdemeanor and sale a felony.
- **The Shafer Commission (1972)**: President Nixon appointed the National Commission on Marihuana and Drug Abuse, chaired by former Pennsylvania Governor Raymond Shafer. While focused on marijuana, the commission's recommendation for decriminalization of personal drug use -- which Nixon rejected -- reflected a broader recognition that prohibition was not an evidence-based policy.
- **Controlled Substances Act (1970)**: President Nixon signed the Comprehensive Drug Abuse Prevention and Control Act, which created the five-schedule classification system. LSD, psilocybin, mescaline, and DMT were placed in Schedule I -- defined as having "high potential for abuse," "no currently accepted medical use," and "lack of accepted safety for use under medical supervision." This classification effectively ended clinical research for decades. The Act did not include a formal scientific review process for scheduling decisions; substances were placed into schedules by political determination.
- **Nixon's political motivations**: Nixon aide John Ehrlichman later acknowledged in a 1994 interview (published by Harper's Magazine in 2016) that the drug war was designed to target political opponents: "We knew we couldn't make it illegal to be either against the war or black, but by getting the public to associate the hippies with marijuana and blacks with heroin, and then criminalizing both heavily, we could disrupt those communities."
- **Impact on Native American peyote use**: The Controlled Substances Act technically prohibited peyote use, placing mescaline in Schedule I. This created legal jeopardy for Native American Church members until the American Indian Religious Freedom Act (1978) and subsequent amendments (1994) provided explicit protections. For eight years (1970-1978), traditional peyote ceremonies existed in a legal gray zone.
- **Reagan-era escalation**: The Reagan administration's "Just Say No" campaign (launched 1982) and the D.A.R.E. program (Drug Abuse Resistance Education, established 1983) cemented a cultural framework that made no distinction between psychedelics and highly addictive substances. The Anti-Drug Abuse Acts of 1986 and 1988 dramatically increased penalties for drug offenses and established mandatory minimum sentences.
- **MDMA therapeutic emergence (1970s-1980s)**: After chemist Alexander Shulgin resynthesized MDMA and introduced it to psychotherapist Leo Zeff in 1977, MDMA spread through an underground network of therapists who used it as an adjunct to psychotherapy. An estimated 4,000 therapists administered MDMA to patients between 1977 and 1985, particularly for couples therapy, trauma processing, and end-of-life anxiety. The substance was legal throughout this period.
- **MDMA scheduling (1985)**: The DEA emergency-scheduled MDMA as Schedule I in 1985, responding to its spread as a recreational drug ("Ecstasy") rather than to its therapeutic use. DEA Administrative Law Judge Francis Young recommended Schedule III placement, which would have permitted medical use, but DEA Administrator John Lawn overruled the recommendation. This decision -- a law enforcement official overriding a judicial recommendation based on scientific evidence -- established a precedent that continues to shape psychedelic policy.
- **Anti-Drug Abuse Acts (1986, 1988)**: Congress passed sweeping anti-drug legislation that dramatically increased penalties for drug offenses, including mandatory minimum sentences. These laws reinforced Schedule I prohibitions and created the carceral infrastructure that continues to enforce psychedelic criminalization.
- **Research shutdown**: Between 1970 and 1990, virtually no clinical research with psychedelics was conducted in the United States. The number of published papers on LSD dropped from over 1,000 per year in the 1960s to near zero by the late 1970s. An entire generation of psychiatric researchers was lost to the field. Institutional knowledge about psychedelic therapy protocols, dosing, and patient management was preserved only in underground clinical networks and in the archives of researchers like Stanislav Grof.
- **International suppression**: The 1971 United Nations Convention on Psychotropic Substances extended psychedelic prohibition globally, requiring signatory nations to control LSD, psilocybin, mescaline, and DMT. This international framework continues to constrain policy reform in the United States and abroad.

### The Underground Period (1990-2006): Quiet Persistence

- **MAPS founded (1986)**: Rick Doblin, a then-23-year-old activist, founded the Multidisciplinary Association for Psychedelic Studies with the explicit goal of making MDMA a legal prescription medicine. Doblin pursued a Ph.D. in public policy from Harvard's Kennedy School of Government specifically to gain the institutional credibility needed to navigate the FDA approval process. MAPS became the primary organizational driver of the psychedelic research revival, raising over $130 million over the following decades.
- **First modern trial approved (1990)**: The FDA approved Rick Strassman's DMT study at the University of New Mexico, the first federally approved human psychedelic research in over two decades. Results published in *Archives of General Psychiatry* (1994). Strassman documented the extraordinary consistency of DMT experiences across subjects, including reports of encounters with non-human entities, in his book *DMT: The Spirit Molecule* (2000).
- **Employment Division v. Smith (1990)**: The Supreme Court ruled in a case involving Native American Church members fired for peyote use that the Free Exercise Clause does not require religious exemptions from generally applicable laws. This decision threatened indigenous peyote use and prompted Congress to pass the Religious Freedom Restoration Act (1993) and the American Indian Religious Freedom Act Amendments (1994).
- **Peyote protections (1994)**: Congress passed the American Indian Religious Freedom Act Amendments, codifying the right of Native American Church members to use peyote in religious ceremonies (42 U.S.C. Section 1996a). The protections applied only to enrolled members of federally recognized tribes, leaving non-enrolled indigenous people and non-indigenous NAC members in legal uncertainty.
- **Heffter Research Institute (1993)**: Founded by a group of scientists to support rigorous scientific research on psychedelics, the Heffter Research Institute funded early psilocybin studies at Johns Hopkins and NYU that would become the foundational studies of the modern renaissance.
- **Alexander Shulgin's legacy (1991, 1997)**: Pharmacologist Alexander Shulgin published *PiHKAL* (Phenethylamines I Have Known and Loved, 1991) and *TiHKAL* (Tryptamines I Have Known and Loved, 1997), documenting the synthesis and subjective effects of hundreds of psychoactive compounds. These works became essential references for both the scientific community and the underground psychedelic community.
- **UDV Supreme Court case (2006)**: In *Gonzales v. O Centro Espirita Beneficente Uniao do Vegetal*, 546 U.S. 418 (2006), the Supreme Court unanimously ruled that the federal government could not prohibit the UDV church from using ayahuasca in religious ceremonies under the Religious Freedom Restoration Act. Chief Justice John Roberts wrote the opinion, giving the ruling bipartisan institutional credibility.
- **Underground therapy networks**: Throughout the 1990s and 2000s, an underground network of psychedelic therapists continued to practice -- primarily with MDMA, psilocybin, and LSD -- serving patients unable to access these substances through legal channels. These networks preserved therapeutic knowledge but operated without safety oversight, informed consent protections, or accountability structures. Reports of therapist misconduct (including sexual abuse) during this period would later surface and complicate the reform movement.
- **Ibogaine treatment emergence**: In the 1990s, practitioners in Mexico, New Zealand, and other countries began using ibogaine to treat opioid addiction, based on anecdotal reports of its anti-addictive properties. Howard Lotsof, who accidentally discovered ibogaine's anti-addictive effects in 1962, advocated for its medical use until his death in 2010. Despite remaining Schedule I in the United States, ibogaine clinics in Mexico now treat thousands of American patients annually.

### The Psychedelic Renaissance (2006-Present): Scientific Revival

- **Johns Hopkins landmark study (2006)**: Roland Griffiths and colleagues published "Psilocybin Can Occasion Mystical-Type Experiences Having Substantial and Sustained Personal Meaning and Spiritual Significance" in *Psychopharmacology*, the first rigorously controlled psilocybin study in the modern era. This study is widely credited with catalyzing the current research revival. Griffiths, a respected pharmacologist who had spent his career studying caffeine and drug abuse, brought mainstream scientific credibility to psychedelic research.
- **Imperial College London psilocybin study (2012)**: Robin Carhart-Harris and David Nutt published the first modern neuroimaging study of psilocybin's effects on the brain, using fMRI to demonstrate that psilocybin reduced activity in the brain's default mode network -- a finding with significant implications for understanding depression and the mechanism of psychedelic therapy (*PNAS*, 2012).
- **NYU and Hopkins cancer anxiety studies (2016)**: Two landmark randomized controlled trials published simultaneously in the *Journal of Psychopharmacology* demonstrated that psilocybin produced rapid and sustained reductions in anxiety and depression in patients with life-threatening cancer diagnoses. The simultaneous publication in the same journal was coordinated to maximize scientific and media impact -- a deliberate strategic choice by the research community.
- **MAPS Phase 2 MDMA trials (2010-2017)**: MAPS completed a series of Phase 2 trials of MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD at multiple sites, demonstrating consistent efficacy and laying the groundwork for Phase 3 trials. These studies were funded entirely by private donations, as federal funding was unavailable.
- **FDA Breakthrough Therapy designations (2017-2019)**: The FDA granted Breakthrough Therapy designation to MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD (2017), psilocybin for treatment-resistant depression (Compass Pathways, 2018), and psilocybin for major depressive disorder (Usona Institute, 2019).
- **Denver decriminalization (2019)**: Denver voters passed Initiative 301 (50.6%), making Denver the first U.S. city to decriminalize psilocybin mushrooms. Oakland followed weeks later with a broader resolution covering all entheogenic plants.
- **Oregon Measure 109 (2020)**: Oregon voters approved the first-in-the-nation regulated psilocybin therapy program (55.8%). The same ballot included Measure 110, which decriminalized personal possession of all drugs.
- **Colorado Proposition 122 (2022)**: Colorado voters approved the Natural Medicine Health Act (53.6%), decriminalizing and creating a regulated access framework for psilocybin, DMT, ibogaine, and mescaline (excluding peyote).
- **FDA MDMA decision (2024)**: The FDA declined to approve Lykos Therapeutics' NDA for MDMA-assisted PTSD therapy, requesting an additional clinical trial. The FDA advisory committee voted 9-2 against recommending approval in June 2024, citing concerns about trial methodology (blinding challenges, therapist adherence), insufficient diversity in trial populations, and questions about the psychotherapy component. While a setback, the decision did not reject the scientific basis for psychedelic therapy. Lykos announced it would conduct an additional Phase 3 trial to address the FDA's concerns.
- **Roland Griffiths' legacy (2023)**: Roland Griffiths, the Johns Hopkins pharmacologist who is widely considered the most important figure in the psychedelic research revival, died of cancer in October 2023 at age 77. Before his death, he spoke publicly about using psilocybin in preparation for his own death -- a poignant connection between his research on end-of-life psilocybin therapy and his personal experience.
- **Expanding federal research funding**: NIH and VA began funding psychedelic research directly, with NIDA launching psilocybin-focused initiatives in 2023 and the VA funding studies on MDMA and psilocybin for veterans with PTSD.
- **Imperial College Centre for Psychedelic Research (2019)**: The world's first academic center dedicated to psychedelic research was established at Imperial College London, led by Robin Carhart-Harris. The center's work on psilocybin for depression and its neuroimaging studies of psychedelic brain states have been central to the scientific renaissance.
- **Australia reschedules psilocybin and MDMA (2023)**: The Australian Therapeutic Goods Administration became the first national regulatory body to formally reschedule psilocybin and MDMA for therapeutic use, allowing specially licensed psychiatrists to prescribe these substances.
- **Michael Pollan's *How to Change Your Mind* (2018)**: The publication of this bestselling book (and later Netflix documentary, 2022) brought psychedelic research to mainstream audiences, shifting public discourse from counterculture associations to medical and scientific framing. The book is credited by researchers and advocates as a significant cultural turning point.
- **Ketamine clinic proliferation (2015-present)**: The off-label use of ketamine for depression, enabled by its Schedule III classification, created a legal psychedelic-type therapy industry of approximately 800 clinics. In 2019, the FDA approved esketamine (Spravato) for treatment-resistant depression, creating the first FDA-approved psychedelic-type therapy. The ketamine industry provided proof of concept for psychedelic therapy delivery but also demonstrated the risks of inadequate regulation.

## The CIA's MKUltra Program: A Detailed Accounting

The CIA's involvement with psychedelics requires specific historical attention because it continues to shape public perception and policy debates. MKUltra was a covert CIA program (1953-1973) that conducted experiments with LSD and other psychoactive substances for potential use in interrogation and mind control.

### Key Facts

| Metric | Detail | Source |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Duration | 1953-1973 (20 years) | Church Committee Report, 1975 |
| Number of sub-projects | 149+ | CIA Inspector General Report, 1963 |
| Institutions involved | 80+ (universities, hospitals, prisons) | Church Committee Report, 1975 |
| Estimated subjects | Thousands (many unwitting) | Church Committee testimony |
| Known deaths | At least 2 confirmed (Frank Olson, Harold Blauer) | DOJ investigations |
| Records destroyed | Bulk of records destroyed by CIA Director Richard Helms in 1973 | Church Committee findings |

### Documented Abuses

- **Frank Olson**: Army biological weapons researcher given LSD without his knowledge during a CIA retreat in 1953. Olson died nine days later after falling from a 13th-floor hotel window. Initially ruled a suicide, a 1994 exhumation and autopsy found evidence consistent with homicide. The CIA paid his family $750,000 in settlement and President Ford personally apologized in 1975.
- **Operation Midnight Climax**: CIA-operated safe houses in San Francisco and New York where unwitting subjects were given LSD and observed through one-way mirrors. The operation was run from 1954 to 1963.
- **Prison experiments**: LSD was administered to prisoners at the Addiction Research Center in Lexington, Kentucky, often under coercive conditions that violated basic informed consent principles.
- **Harold Blauer**: A tennis professional who died in 1953 after being injected with mescaline derivatives at the New York State Psychiatric Institute as part of an Army-funded experiment. His death was covered up for decades.

### Legacy for Policy

The MKUltra revelations -- exposed by the Church Committee hearings in 1975 and subsequent investigations -- created lasting damage to public trust in both government and psychedelic research. The program's abuses are still cited by opponents of psychedelic reform as evidence that these substances are inherently dangerous or susceptible to misuse. Modern reform advocates must actively distinguish therapeutic use (informed consent, clinical oversight) from covert government experimentation (unwitting subjects, no consent, no oversight). The lesson is not that psychedelics are too dangerous to use, but that any policy framework must include robust protections against institutional abuse.

## Lessons from History

### Pattern 1: Political Decisions Override Scientific Evidence

The scheduling of psychedelics in 1970 and the emergency scheduling of MDMA in 1985 were driven by political considerations rather than scientific assessment. In both cases, the substances were placed in the most restrictive category despite evidence of therapeutic potential and, in MDMA's case, an administrative law judge's explicit recommendation against Schedule I. This pattern -- political interference overriding scientific evidence -- is the central failure that modern reform must address. Any successful reform framework must insulate scheduling decisions from political pressure.

### Pattern 2: Cultural Backlash Can Destroy Legitimate Science

The association of psychedelics with the 1960s counterculture and anti-war movement made them political targets. The resulting prohibition destroyed a legitimate and promising field of psychiatric research for decades. The modern psychedelic movement has deliberately distanced itself from counterculture aesthetics and centered institutional science, clinical data, and medical applications -- a strategic lesson from history that explains the emphasis on "psychedelic-assisted therapy" rather than "psychedelic liberation."

### Pattern 3: State Innovation Precedes Federal Reform

As with cannabis, state-level experimentation with psychedelic policy (Oregon, Colorado, municipal decriminalization) is creating real-world evidence and building political momentum before federal reform. This federalism-driven pattern suggests that federal change is most likely after states demonstrate workable regulatory models.

### Pattern 4: Covert Government Programs Undermine Public Trust

The CIA's MKUltra program and military experiments -- which used psychedelics on unwitting subjects -- created lasting public distrust that continues to complicate reform efforts. Any policy framework must include robust informed consent protections and transparency requirements to rebuild trust.

### Pattern 5: Private Philanthropy Fills the Gap When Government Fails

The modern psychedelic research revival was funded almost entirely by private philanthropy and nonprofit organizations (MAPS, Heffter Research Institute, Tim Ferriss's donations to Johns Hopkins) because federal funding was unavailable or hostile. This pattern -- private capital stepping in when government fails to fund promising research -- is both a strength (it demonstrates conviction) and a weakness (it makes research dependent on individual donors and market cycles). The recent market correction in psychedelic biotech stocks demonstrates the fragility of private funding.

### Pattern 6: The Medical Model Is the Gateway to Reform

In both the psychedelic context and the cannabis context, medical use has preceded broader reform. The FDA Breakthrough Therapy designation (2017-2019) was essential for legitimizing psychedelic research in the eyes of mainstream medicine and politics. State programs in Oregon and Colorado were politically viable in part because they were framed as therapeutic access, not recreational legalization. This pattern suggests that FDA approval of psychedelic-assisted therapy is the single most important enabler of broader reform.

### Pattern 7: Indigenous Rights Are Consistently Marginalized

Throughout the history of psychedelic policy -- from the suppression of Native American peyote use in the 19th century, to the belated protection of the American Indian Religious Freedom Act Amendments (1994), to the current tensions over commercialization and peyote conservation -- indigenous communities' interests have been subordinated to those of dominant institutions. The peyote exemption was won only after decades of advocacy and litigation. The modern psychedelic industry has largely developed without indigenous input or benefit-sharing. This pattern must be actively reversed in any reform framework.

### Pattern 8: Public Opinion Follows Scientific Legitimacy

Public attitudes toward psychedelics have shifted dramatically in response to institutional scientific research. A 2023 Pew Research Center poll found that 61% of Americans support further research on psychedelics for therapeutic use -- a figure that would have been unimaginable in the 1990s. This shift was driven not by counterculture advocacy but by publications in elite medical journals (*NEJM*, *JAMA*, *Nature Medicine*), FDA Breakthrough Therapy designations, and mainstream media coverage (Michael Pollan's *How to Change Your Mind*, Netflix documentary, *60 Minutes* segments). The lesson is that scientific legitimacy -- not political activism alone -- is the most effective driver of public opinion change on drug policy.

## The Human Cost of Prohibition

The decades of psychedelic prohibition have imposed measurable costs:

- **Lost research time**: Approximately 30 years (1970-2000) of potential psychiatric research was lost, during which treatment-resistant depression, PTSD, and substance use disorders went untreated by potentially effective therapies
- **Criminal justice burden**: While psychedelic-related arrests are less common than cannabis arrests, tens of thousands of Americans have been arrested, prosecuted, and incarcerated for psychedelic possession since 1970
- **Underground therapy harms**: Prohibition drove psychedelic therapy underground, where it operated without safety oversight, informed consent protections, or accountability, leading to documented cases of sexual abuse, psychological harm, and medical emergencies
- **Veteran suffering**: Veterans with treatment-resistant PTSD have been denied access to MDMA-assisted therapy for decades while the evidence base accumulated. During this period, 17-22 veterans have died by suicide every day (VA data, 2012-2023)
- **Pharmaceutical monopoly on mental health treatment**: The absence of psychedelic alternatives left the pharmaceutical industry's daily-medication model (SSRIs, SNRIs, benzodiazepines) as the dominant treatment paradigm, despite well-documented limitations in efficacy for treatment-resistant populations
- **Medical tourism**: American patients who could afford it traveled to Jamaica, Costa Rica, Peru, Mexico, and the Netherlands for legal psychedelic experiences, creating an international medical tourism industry that benefits foreign economies while American patients assume the risks of unregulated care abroad
- **Peyote conservation crisis**: Decades of prohibition prevented the development of legal peyote cultivation programs, while wild peyote populations in South Texas continued to decline due to habitat loss, overharvesting, and climate change. The conservation crisis was exacerbated by prohibition, not mitigated by it
- **Workforce atrophy**: The generation of psychiatrists trained in psychedelic therapy protocols in the 1950s and 1960s retired and died without passing their knowledge to successors. Reconstructing this expertise has required decades of effort, starting virtually from scratch in the 2000s

## Comparative International History

While the United States pioneered both psychedelic research and psychedelic prohibition, other countries have followed divergent paths that provide important context for U.S. policy reform.

### Countries That Maintained or Restored Research Access

- **Switzerland**: Never fully abandoned psychedelic therapy. Swiss psychiatrists maintained a compassionate use pathway for LSD and psilocybin therapy from the 1960s onward, with periodic restrictions and reopenings. The Swiss Federal Office of Public Health currently authorizes individual physicians to prescribe psychedelics through the Sonderbewilligung (Special Authorization) program. Hundreds of patients have received legal psychedelic therapy in Switzerland.
- **Brazil**: Ayahuasca has been legal for religious use in Brazil since 1992, with the Brazilian National Drug Policy Council formally authorizing its use in religious ceremonies. Brazil's experience -- more than 30 years of legal ayahuasca use with minimal public health problems -- provides the longest modern dataset on regulated psychedelic access.
- **Netherlands**: While psilocybin mushrooms were banned in 2008 after a tourist death, psilocybin-containing truffles (sclerotia) remain legal and are sold openly in "smart shops." The Netherlands also hosts legal psilocybin retreat centers serving international clients.
- **Jamaica**: Psilocybin mushrooms are not scheduled under Jamaican law, enabling a legal retreat industry that serves thousands of international participants annually.

### Countries That Led Therapeutic Reform

- **Australia (2023)**: The Therapeutic Goods Administration rescheduled psilocybin and MDMA for therapeutic use, making Australia the first country to formally authorize these substances for medical treatment. Early implementation data shows limited uptake due to high costs ($10,000-$25,000 AUD per treatment course), a shortage of authorized psychiatrists, and lack of insurance coverage.
- **Canada (2020-present)**: Health Canada has granted individual exemptions under Section 56 of the Controlled Drugs and Substances Act for psilocybin therapy, primarily for end-of-life patients. The Special Access Programme was expanded in 2022 to allow broader clinical access. Multiple Canadian psilocybin therapy companies are conducting clinical trials.
- **Israel**: Israeli researchers have conducted significant MDMA and psilocybin research. The Israeli Ministry of Health has been supportive of psychedelic clinical trials, and Israel was a major site for MAPS Phase 2 MDMA studies.
