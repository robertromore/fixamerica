# Psychedelics Policy: Stakeholders

The psychedelics policy landscape is shaped by the interactions of patients, researchers, clinicians, indigenous communities, government agencies, pharmaceutical companies, and advocacy organizations, each with distinct interests, resources, and levels of power.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Patients with treatment-resistant depression | Denied access to potentially effective therapy by Schedule I barriers | ~8.9 million adults (STAR*D estimates, NIMH) | Access to FDA-approved psilocybin therapy; insurance coverage; affordable treatment |
| Individuals with PTSD | Limited to conventional therapies with high failure/dropout rates | ~13 million adults in any year (NIMH, 2023) | Access to MDMA-assisted therapy; VA coverage for veterans |
| Veterans with PTSD | High suicide rates; conventional treatment dropout rates ~50% | ~1.7 million receiving VA PTSD care (VA NCPTSD, 2024) | VA research funding; therapeutic access; coverage through VA healthcare |
| Patients with substance use disorders | Promising psilocybin evidence for alcohol and tobacco addiction | 48.7 million with SUD (SAMHSA NSDUH, 2024) | Expanded research; treatment access for addiction |
| Patients with end-of-life anxiety | Psilocybin shows durable benefit for cancer-related distress | ~1.9 million new cancer diagnoses/year (ACS, 2024) | Compassionate access; palliative care integration |
| Indigenous communities | Cultural practices affected by scheduling, commercialization threats | NAC membership: 250,000-400,000; broader indigenous psychedelic traditions | Protection of ceremonial use; peyote conservation; benefit-sharing; cultural sovereignty |

### Secondary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Estimated Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|----------------|---------------|
| Family members of patients | Caregiving burden; directly affected by treatment outcomes | 40-60 million Americans have close family member with treatment-resistant mental illness | Effective treatments for loved ones; reduced caregiving burden |
| Employers and the economy | Mental illness costs U.S. employers ~$210 billion annually (NAMI, 2023) | All U.S. employers; particularly defense, first responder, healthcare sectors | Reduced absenteeism; improved productivity; lower healthcare costs |
| Criminal justice system | Psychedelic-related arrests and prosecutions | Federal and state law enforcement, prosecutors, courts | Reduced caseloads (decriminalization); maintaining enforcement authority (prohibition) |
| General public | Public safety; access to treatments; taxpayer stake in research funding | 330+ million Americans | Safe communities; effective mental health system; responsible use of public funds |
| Psychedelic therapy practitioners | Career opportunities; licensing and training requirements | ~300 Oregon facilitators; growing workforce | Clear regulatory framework; recognized credentials; sustainable business model |
| Insurance industry | Reimbursement framework development; actuarial risk assessment | Major health insurers (UnitedHealth, Anthem, Cigna, Aetna) | Manageable costs; clear billing codes; utilization data |
| Universities and research institutions | Research opportunities; reputation; funding | 50+ institutions with active or planned psychedelic research programs | Federal funding; streamlined DEA licensing; academic freedom |
| Pharmaceutical industry (conventional) | Market competition from psychedelic therapies | Antidepressant market: ~$15 billion annually | Protect market share; potential investment in psychedelic sector |
| State regulators | Implementation of state programs; public safety oversight | Oregon, Colorado, and emerging state programs | Effective regulation; federal-state coordination; program revenue |

### Tertiary Stakeholders

- **Drug testing industry**: The $5.7 billion U.S. drug testing market (2023) has an interest in maintaining broad Schedule I classification, as rescheduling or decriminalization reduces demand for workplace drug testing
- **Private prison industry**: CoreCivic and GEO Group (combined revenue: $3.8 billion, 2023) benefit from continued criminalization, though psychedelic-related incarcerations are a small share of their business
- **International stakeholders**: Other countries monitoring U.S. psychedelic policy for precedent-setting effects on their own regulatory frameworks
- **Agricultural and cultivation interests**: As legal psychedelic cultivation expands (Colorado permits personal cultivation of psilocybin mushrooms), agricultural stakeholders enter the picture
- **Tourism and retreat industry**: Jamaica, Costa Rica, Peru, and the Netherlands host American psychedelic tourists; domestic legalization could shift this market

## Who Has Power

### Decision Makers

| Actor | Role | Authority | Resources | Influence Level |
|-------|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) | Scheduling authority; research licensing | Can schedule/reschedule substances (21 U.S.C. Section 811); issues research registrations | $3.3 billion budget (FY2024); 10,000+ employees | High |
| Food and Drug Administration (FDA) | Drug approval; clinical trial oversight | Approves NDAs; grants Breakthrough Therapy designation; issues guidance | $6.9 billion budget (FY2024) | High |
| U.S. Congress | Legislation; appropriations; oversight | Can amend CSA; direct agencies; appropriate research funding | Legislative authority | High |
| National Institutes of Health (NIDA/NIMH) | Research funding | Fund grants; operate Drug Supply Program | NIDA: $1.6 billion; NIMH: $2.4 billion (FY2024) | Medium-High |
| Department of Veterans Affairs | Veteran healthcare; research | Can fund and conduct psychedelic research for veterans | $325 billion total budget (FY2024) | Medium-High |
| State legislatures | State-level scheduling, regulation | Can create state regulatory programs (Oregon, Colorado model) | Varies by state | Medium |
| Oregon Health Authority | Psilocybin services regulation | Licenses service centers, facilitators; sets program rules | Oregon program budget | Medium (within Oregon) |

### Key Organizations and Institutions

- **MAPS (Multidisciplinary Association for Psychedelic Studies)**: Founded 1986 by Rick Doblin. The single most important organizational driver of psychedelic research revival. Sponsored MDMA Phase 3 trials. Raised over $130 million for MDMA research. Created Lykos Therapeutics as its pharmaceutical arm. Influence: Very High.
- **Compass Pathways**: UK-based pharmaceutical company developing psilocybin therapy (COMP360) for treatment-resistant depression. Funded by investors including Peter Thiel. Controversial for seeking broad patents on psilocybin therapy protocols (including patents on room settings, music, and hand-holding). Market cap fluctuated from $1.5 billion peak to ~$300 million (2024). Has faced criticism from open-access advocates for its aggressive intellectual property strategy. Influence: High.
- **Usona Institute**: Nonprofit funding psilocybin research for major depressive disorder. Received FDA Breakthrough Therapy designation in 2019. Operates on a public benefit model (no patents on psilocybin itself). Represents the alternative to the for-profit pharmaceutical approach. Influence: Medium-High.
- **Lykos Therapeutics** (formerly MAPS Public Benefit Corporation): The pharmaceutical arm of MAPS, which filed the MDMA-assisted therapy NDA with the FDA. The August 2024 FDA rejection was a significant setback. Lykos has indicated it will conduct an additional Phase 3 trial. Its organizational relationship with MAPS (nonprofit parent, for-profit subsidiary) is complex and has generated internal governance debates. Influence: High (though diminished post-FDA decision).
- **Johns Hopkins Center for Psychedelic and Consciousness Research**: Premier academic research center. Founded 2019 with $17 million in private donations. Led by Matthew Johnson; Roland Griffiths (d. 2023) was its founding director. Published foundational studies that catalyzed the modern renaissance. Influence: High (scientific credibility).
- **Imperial College Centre for Psychedelic Research**: London-based academic center led by Robin Carhart-Harris (now at UCSF). Pioneered neuroimaging studies of psychedelic brain states. Published foundational work on psilocybin for depression. Influence: High (international scientific leadership).
- **Drug Policy Alliance**: Major drug policy reform advocacy organization. Supports broad decriminalization and has been involved in state ballot initiatives including Oregon Measure 110 and Colorado Proposition 122. Influence: Medium-High.
- **Heroic Hearts Project**: Nonprofit connecting veterans with psychedelic therapy. Founded by Jesse Gould, an Army Ranger veteran. Key bridge between veteran advocacy and psychedelic reform. Has organized psychedelic retreats for veterans in legal jurisdictions (Jamaica, Peru). Influence: Medium (growing rapidly due to veteran political credibility).
- **Decriminalize Nature**: Grassroots advocacy organization that has driven municipal decriminalization campaigns in Oakland, Santa Cruz, Ann Arbor, Seattle, and other cities. Uses a model of community education and city council resolutions rather than ballot initiatives. Influence: Medium (at local level).
- **New Approach PAC**: Political action committee that has funded psychedelic and cannabis ballot initiatives, including Oregon Measure 109 and Colorado Proposition 122. A primary source of campaign funding for psychedelic reform. Influence: Medium-High (in ballot initiative campaigns).

### Coalition-Building Opportunities

Several untapped or emerging coalitions could accelerate psychedelic reform:

- **Faith-based recovery + psychedelic therapy**: Some Christian, Buddhist, and interfaith recovery communities have expressed interest in psychedelic-assisted spiritual and therapeutic practices. The Federalist Society and religious liberty scholars have published sympathetic analyses of psychedelic religious freedom claims.
- **End-of-life care + psilocybin**: Hospice and palliative care organizations are a natural constituency for psilocybin therapy for end-of-life distress. The American Academy of Hospice and Palliative Medicine has not yet taken a formal position but individual members have been supportive.
- **Employer coalitions + mental health treatment**: Employers bearing the $210 billion annual cost of mental illness (NAMI, 2023) have an economic interest in effective new treatments, including psychedelic-assisted therapy.
- **First responders + PTSD treatment**: Police officers, firefighters, and EMTs experience PTSD at rates comparable to military veterans. First responder unions could become allies in psychedelic PTSD therapy advocacy.
- **Criminal justice reform + decriminalization**: Organizations focused on reducing mass incarceration (ACLU, Vera Institute, Sentencing Project) could support psychedelic decriminalization as part of broader drug policy reform.

### Conflicting Interests

| Conflict | Parties | Nature of Conflict | Resolution Approach |
|----------|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Medical model vs. broad access | Pharmaceutical companies, FDA vs. decriminalization advocates | Whether psychedelics should be channeled through the medical system or available without medical gatekeeping | Oregon's service center model as middle ground; tiered access framework |
| Indigenous rights vs. commercialization | NAC, indigenous communities vs. psychedelic companies, reform advocates | Commercialization threatens limited resources (peyote) and appropriates traditional knowledge | Exclude peyote from commercial access; benefit-sharing agreements; indigenous representation |
| Patent holders vs. open access | Compass Pathways, Atai vs. Usona, open-source advocates | Broad patents on therapy protocols could enable monopoly pricing | Patent challenges; open-source therapy protocols; compulsory licensing |
| DEA enforcement vs. therapeutic access | DEA vs. patients, researchers, clinicians | DEA's institutional interest in maintaining scheduling authority conflicts with therapeutic access | Legislative reform removing DEA scheduling discretion for FDA-approved substances |
| For-profit vs. nonprofit models | Compass Pathways, Atai vs. MAPS/Usona, community advocates | For-profit model prioritizes investor returns; nonprofit model prioritizes access | Hybrid approaches; insurance mandates; community-based delivery |
| Speed vs. safety | Patients, veterans vs. FDA, cautious psychiatrists | Urgency of mental health crisis vs. need for rigorous clinical evidence | Expanded access programs; state pilot programs alongside FDA process |
| Research diversity vs. speed | Equity advocates vs. trial sponsors | Diverse enrollment takes longer and costs more but is essential for legitimacy | Federal funding for diversity-focused trials; recruitment pipeline development |

### The Research-Access Power Structure

The psychedelic policy landscape features an unusual power dynamic that distinguishes it from most pharmaceutical policy debates:

- **Gatekeepers** (DEA, FDA, Congress) hold formal authority but are subject to increasing political pressure from stakeholders. The DEA is the most resistant gatekeeper; the FDA has been more receptive (Breakthrough Therapy designations) but constrained by its own methodological standards.
- **Knowledge producers** (researchers, clinicians) generate the evidence that drives policy change but lack direct regulatory authority. The concentration of research leadership at a small number of institutions (Johns Hopkins, NYU, UCSF, Imperial College) creates both credibility and fragility.
- **Patients and advocates** generate political will but lack institutional power. Veterans are the exception -- their political credibility gives them disproportionate influence relative to other patient populations.
- **Private capital** (pharmaceutical companies, investors) funds the research that feeds the FDA pathway but makes decisions based on return on investment rather than public health need. The 2023-2024 market correction demonstrated the volatility of private funding.
- **State governments** are creating regulatory facts on the ground that pressure federal reform. Oregon's program data and Colorado's emerging framework provide the real-world evidence that federal policymakers need to justify action.
- **Indigenous communities** hold moral authority based on centuries of traditional use but lack economic and political power to shape the commercial psychedelic market. Their interests are frequently acknowledged but rarely centered in policy design.

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on psychedelic policy:

### Influence Budget Comparison

| Sector | Annual Lobbying Spending | Annual Campaign Contributions | Influence Ratio vs. Psychedelics |
|--------|-------------------------|-------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| Pharmaceutical/health products | $374 million (OpenSecrets, 2024) | $65 million (2022 cycle) | ~75:1 to ~190:1 |
| Psychedelic reform sector | $2-5 million (estimated) | $1-3 million (2022 cycle) | 1:1 (baseline) |
| Alcohol industry | $30 million (OpenSecrets, 2024) | $18 million (2022 cycle) | ~6:1 to ~15:1 |
| Tobacco industry | $25 million (OpenSecrets, 2024) | $12 million (2022 cycle) | ~5:1 to ~12:1 |

The massive spending disparity between the psychedelic reform sector and the pharmaceutical industry underscores the structural disadvantage faced by reform advocates in federal legislative battles. However, the psychedelic sector compensates partially through high-impact media coverage, veteran advocacy, and state-level ballot initiatives where direct democracy bypasses lobbying-intensive legislative processes.

- **Lobbying**: The psychedelic sector's lobbying presence is growing but still small relative to the pharmaceutical industry. In 2023, psychedelic-related lobbying spending was estimated at $2-5 million, compared to the pharmaceutical industry's $374 million in total lobbying (OpenSecrets, 2024). Key lobbying entities include MAPS, Compass Pathways, and newer entrants like the Psychedelic Medicine Coalition.
- **Campaign Contributions**: Psychedelic reform PACs are emerging. New Approach PAC (which supported cannabis and psychedelic ballot initiatives) has spent millions on Oregon and Colorado campaigns.
- **Scientific Publications**: The primary influence mechanism for the research community. High-impact publications in *NEJM*, *JAMA*, *Nature Medicine*, and *Lancet Psychiatry* shape FDA decision-making, public opinion, and legislative attention.
- **Media and Public Advocacy**: Michael Pollan's *How to Change Your Mind*, Netflix documentaries, and mainstream media coverage have shifted public opinion. Major outlets (New York Times, Washington Post, 60 Minutes) have produced favorable coverage of psychedelic research.
- **Veteran Advocacy**: The American Legion's 2022 resolution supporting psychedelic research and the Heroic Hearts Project's Congressional testimony have created bipartisan political cover for reform. Veterans are among the most politically sympathetic constituencies in American politics.
- **State Ballot Initiatives**: Direct democracy has been the most effective mechanism for psychedelic policy change to date (Denver Initiative 301, Oregon Measure 109, Colorado Proposition 122).
- **Legal Challenges**: Religious freedom litigation (UDV, Santo Daime) has created legal precedents for psychedelic use outside the medical model. Right-to-try and compassionate use legal challenges are emerging.
- **Clinical Trial Enrollment**: The patient experience in clinical trials -- dramatic improvements in conditions that conventional treatments failed to address -- creates powerful personal testimonies that shape media coverage and political advocacy. Trial participants and their families are emerging as an influential advocacy voice.
- **Underground Practitioners**: An estimated network of unlicensed psychedelic therapists operates outside the legal system, serving clients who cannot access legal options. While operating illegally, this community generates both positive (patient testimonies) and negative (safety incidents, abuse reports) influence on public perception.

### Key Power Dynamics

The psychedelic policy landscape features several distinctive power dynamics that distinguish it from other drug policy reform efforts:

1. **The scientific establishment is aligned with reform**: Unlike cannabis (where the medical evidence was contested for decades), the psychedelic evidence base is published in the most prestigious medical journals (*NEJM*, *JAMA*, *Nature Medicine*) and endorsed by the FDA's own Breakthrough Therapy designations. This gives the reform movement scientific institutional support that cannabis reform lacked at comparable stages.

2. **Veterans provide bipartisan political cover**: The veteran suicide crisis and the clear clinical evidence for MDMA-assisted PTSD therapy create a politically compelling case that crosses partisan lines. Conservative lawmakers who would never support "drug legalization" can support "veteran access to breakthrough PTSD treatment."

3. **The DEA holds disproportionate structural power**: Despite lacking scientific expertise, the DEA's statutory authority over scheduling gives it effective veto power over psychedelic reform at the federal level. This structural imbalance is the single most important power dynamic in the stakeholder landscape.

4. **State regulators are creating facts on the ground**: Oregon and Colorado are generating real-world data on regulated psychedelic access that federal policymakers cannot ignore. This state-level action shifts the burden of proof from reformers ("prove it can work") to prohibitionists ("explain why states should be overridden").

5. **Private capital is a double-edged sword**: Venture capital and pharmaceutical investment have funded the clinical research pipeline, but the sector's market correction (2023-2024) demonstrates the fragility of private funding. If investor interest wanes, the research pipeline may stall absent increased federal funding. Conversely, excessive private investment creates incentives for patent monopolies and premium pricing that undermine access equity.

## Stakeholders Most at Risk

Some stakeholders bear disproportionate risk in the current policy landscape:

### Patients Currently Suffering

The most directly harmed stakeholders are patients with treatment-resistant conditions who lack access to potentially effective therapies:

- **Treatment-resistant depression patients**: An estimated 8.9 million adults who have not responded to two or more antidepressant trials. Many cycle through medications for years or decades without meaningful improvement.
- **Veterans with treatment-resistant PTSD**: Approximately 1.7 million veterans receiving VA PTSD care, with 50% dropout rates from conventional therapy. An average of 17.5 veteran suicides per day (2021).
- **End-of-life patients**: Approximately 1.9 million new cancer diagnoses per year; psilocybin therapy for end-of-life distress has produced some of the most dramatic clinical results but remains unavailable outside clinical trials and Oregon service centers.
- **Opioid addiction patients**: Early evidence for psilocybin and ibogaine in treating opioid use disorder is promising but access is extremely limited. Meanwhile, over 70,000 Americans die from synthetic opioid overdoses annually.

### Indigenous Communities

Indigenous communities face a unique combination of risks:

- **Peyote depletion**: Wild peyote populations in South Texas are declining. The National Council of Native American Churches has warned that increased demand from broader legalization could threaten the supply available for traditional ceremonies.
- **Cultural appropriation**: The commercialization of practices rooted in indigenous traditions, without consent or benefit-sharing, constitutes a form of cultural harm.
- **Economic exclusion**: Indigenous communities generally lack the capital to participate in the licensed psychedelic therapy market, meaning they could be excluded from the economic benefits of an industry built on their traditional knowledge.
- **Legal vulnerability**: While NAC peyote use is federally protected, the protection applies only to enrolled members of federally recognized tribes, leaving non-enrolled indigenous people vulnerable.

### Underground Practitioners and Their Clients

Individuals operating in the underground psychedelic therapy space face legal, safety, and ethical risks:

- **Practitioners**: Risk of federal prosecution, state drug charges, and loss of any existing professional licenses
- **Clients**: Lack of safety oversight, informed consent protections, adverse event reporting, or recourse in cases of harm or misconduct
- **Vulnerable populations**: Individuals seeking underground psychedelic therapy are disproportionately those who have exhausted conventional treatment options and are desperate for alternatives, making them vulnerable to exploitation
