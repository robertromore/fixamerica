# Psychedelics Policy: Root Causes

## Systemic Analysis

The current restrictive framework for psychedelics in the United States is not the result of a single policy failure but of several interlocking structural, political, and institutional factors. Despite growing clinical evidence that psilocybin, MDMA, and other psychedelics have significant therapeutic potential, these substances remain in Schedule I -- the most restrictive federal category, reserved for substances with "no currently accepted medical use" and "high potential for abuse." The persistence of this classification in the face of contradictory evidence reveals deep-rooted systemic problems in how the United States regulates controlled substances, funds mental health research, and responds to political rather than scientific signals.

## Structural Deficiencies in the Controlled Substances Act

### The Schedule I Catch-22

The most fundamental root cause of the psychedelics policy failure is a structural flaw in the Controlled Substances Act itself. Schedule I classification creates a self-reinforcing barrier to the research needed to change the classification:

- **Schedule I substances are defined as having "no currently accepted medical use"** -- yet the scheduling itself makes it extremely difficult to conduct the research that would demonstrate medical use
- Researchers must obtain a DEA Schedule I Researcher Registration (6-12 month average processing time), use only DEA-approved laboratory facilities, store substances in DEA-compliant safes, and submit to periodic inspections
- Research-grade psychedelics must be obtained through the NIDA Drug Supply Program or DEA-licensed manufacturers, creating supply bottlenecks
- Institutional Review Boards (IRBs) at universities and hospitals are often reluctant to approve Schedule I research due to legal liability concerns
- NIH grant reviewers have historically scored psychedelic proposals lower due to perceived political risk, creating a funding disadvantage

**Evidence**: A 2022 Government Accountability Office report found that Schedule I research barriers were "substantial" and that the DEA registration process was "cumbersome and time-consuming," deterring researchers. Between 1970 and 2000, fewer than a dozen human psychedelic studies were approved in the United States (Nutt et al., *Lancet Psychiatry*, 2020).

### Comparison with Other Scheduling Systems

The structural flaws of the U.S. scheduling system become clear when compared with other countries' approaches:

| Country | Scheduling Body | Scientific Review Requirement | Override Mechanism |
|---------|----------------|-------------------------------|-------------------|
| United States | DEA (law enforcement) | HHS recommendation required but not binding | DEA Administrator can override |
| United Kingdom | Home Secretary (advised by ACMD) | Advisory Council on the Misuse of Drugs provides independent scientific assessment | Minister can overrule but must publicly explain |
| Australia | Therapeutic Goods Administration (health agency) | Mandatory scientific review by health experts | None; health agency decision is final |
| Netherlands | Ministry of Health | Required risk assessment by independent committee | Limited political override |

The U.S. system is unique in placing final scheduling authority with a law enforcement agency rather than a health or scientific body. This structural choice is the root cause of the disconnect between scientific evidence and drug classification.

### DEA Administrative Power Without Scientific Accountability

The DEA holds scheduling authority under the CSA (21 U.S.C. Section 811) and has repeatedly exercised this power in ways that override scientific recommendation:

- In 1985, DEA Administrative Law Judge Francis Young recommended that MDMA be placed in Schedule III, which would have permitted medical use. DEA Administrator John Lawn rejected the recommendation and placed MDMA in Schedule I.
- In 1988, Judge Young ruled that marijuana should be rescheduled to Schedule II, calling it "one of the safest therapeutically active substances known to man." The DEA rejected this recommendation as well.
- The DEA has denied every rescheduling petition for psychedelics filed since 1970

**Evidence**: The structural problem is that the DEA -- a law enforcement agency -- has final scheduling authority. Its institutional mission is drug enforcement, not therapeutic access. This creates an inherent conflict of interest when evaluating substances for medical potential. Under 21 U.S.C. Section 811, the Attorney General (delegated to the DEA) must request a scientific and medical evaluation from HHS before scheduling a substance, and HHS's recommendation regarding scientific and medical matters is binding. However, the DEA retains discretion over the final scheduling decision and has historically interpreted its authority broadly. In practice, no substance has been moved out of Schedule I through the DEA petition process in the history of the Controlled Substances Act.

### The "Accepted Medical Use" Standard

The DEA applies a five-factor test to determine whether a substance has "currently accepted medical use":

1. The drug's chemistry must be known and reproducible
2. Adequate safety studies must exist
3. Adequate and well-controlled studies proving efficacy must exist
4. The drug must be accepted by qualified experts
5. Scientific evidence must be widely available

This standard was developed by the DEA itself (not by Congress) and is more restrictive than the FDA's standard for drug approval. Critics argue that the DEA's test creates a higher bar for rescheduling than for initial FDA approval, effectively requiring that a drug be approved before it can be rescheduled -- but Schedule I barriers prevent the research needed for approval (Johnson et al., *Neuropsychopharmacology*, 2018).

## Political Economy

### Who Benefits from the Status Quo

| Actor | Benefit | Economic Value | Incentive to Block Reform |
|-------|---------|----------------|---------------------------|
| DEA and drug enforcement agencies | Budget justification, scheduling authority, enforcement missions | DEA budget: $3.3 billion (FY2024) | Rescheduling reduces enforcement mandate and budget justification |
| Pharmaceutical companies (conventional) | Antidepressant and anxiolytic markets maintained | U.S. antidepressant market: ~$15 billion annually (IQVIA, 2023) | Psychedelic therapies could displace long-term daily medication revenue |
| Private prison industry | Continued criminalization ensures prisoner pipeline | CoreCivic and GEO Group combined revenue: $3.8 billion (2023) | Drug offenses remain a significant share of federal inmates (45% of BOP population) |
| Drug testing companies | Schedule I status ensures testing demand | U.S. drug testing market: $5.7 billion (2023) | Rescheduling reduces testing demand |
| Professional licensing boards | Gatekeeping role reinforced by prohibition | N/A | Reform requires new credentialing frameworks |

### Perverse Incentives

The current system creates several perverse incentives that perpetuate the status quo:

1. **Enforcement agencies benefit from maintaining Schedule I status** because it justifies larger budgets, broader surveillance authority, and more personnel
2. **Pharmaceutical companies profit more from daily medications** (SSRIs average $200-400/month) than from one-to-three-session psychedelic therapies that could produce lasting improvement
3. **Academic researchers face career risk** from associating with Schedule I substances, creating a chilling effect on the research pipeline
4. **Insurance companies have no reimbursement framework** for psychedelic-assisted therapy, which requires multi-hour sessions with trained therapists -- a model that does not fit standard billing codes
5. **Politicians face asymmetric risk**: A legislator who supports psychedelic reform and faces a subsequent adverse incident (a constituent harmed during a psychedelic experience) will be attacked as "pro-drug." A legislator who maintains the status quo faces no political cost even though patients die every day from untreated conditions. This asymmetry biases political incentives toward inaction.
6. **The veteran advocacy pathway is limited**: While veteran-focused psychedelic reform enjoys bipartisan support, framing reform exclusively around veterans may limit the broader coalition needed for comprehensive policy change. Veterans constitute a sympathetic but narrow entry point.

### The Pharmaceutical Industry's Structural Position

The pharmaceutical industry's relationship to psychedelic reform is more complex than simple opposition:

- **Market disruption threat**: The U.S. antidepressant market generates approximately $15 billion annually (IQVIA, 2023). A psychedelic therapy that produces lasting improvement after 1-3 sessions directly threatens the recurring-revenue model of daily medications (SSRIs average $200-400/month). Major pharmaceutical companies (Pfizer, Eli Lilly, AbbVie) have not publicly opposed psychedelic reform but have also not supported it.
- **Selective investment**: Some pharmaceutical companies have made small investments in psychedelic startups while maintaining their core antidepressant portfolios. Johnson & Johnson's esketamine (Spravato) represents a hedge -- a psychedelic-adjacent product marketed within the existing pharmaceutical distribution model.
- **Patent strategy**: Companies like Compass Pathways have pursued aggressive patent strategies on psychedelic therapy protocols, attempting to secure monopoly positions. If successful, these patents could enable pharmaceutical-style pricing ($10,000-$30,000 per treatment course) rather than the lower costs that would follow from open-access models.
- **Lobbying absence**: The pharmaceutical industry spent $374 million on lobbying in 2023 (OpenSecrets, 2024). The psychedelic reform sector spent an estimated $2-5 million. This 75-to-1 spending disparity reflects the pharmaceutical industry's structural advantage in shaping federal drug policy.

## Institutional and Bureaucratic Inertia

### FDA Regulatory Framework Not Designed for Psychedelic Therapy

The FDA's drug approval pathway was designed for pharmaceutical products (pills, injections) administered independently by patients, not for therapist-assisted experiences requiring multi-hour supervised sessions:

- **No standard regulatory category** for drug-plus-therapy combination treatments
- **Blinding challenges**: Psychedelics produce recognizable effects, making double-blind placebo-controlled trials -- the FDA gold standard -- difficult to design
- **Therapist variable**: Treatment outcomes depend partly on therapist skill, rapport, and adherence to protocols, introducing a variable that pharmaceutical trials do not typically address
- **Session duration**: Psilocybin therapy sessions last 6-8 hours; MDMA sessions last 8-12 hours. The FDA's standard clinical trial design does not easily accommodate this format

**Evidence**: The FDA's June 2023 draft guidance on psychedelic drug development acknowledged these challenges explicitly, noting the need for "novel approaches" to clinical trial design, including potentially unblinded studies with appropriate controls.

### NIDA Funding Bias Toward Abuse Research

The National Institute on Drug Abuse (NIDA), which is the primary NIH funder of research on controlled substances, has an institutional mandate focused on drug abuse and addiction, not therapeutic applications:

- NIDA's budget (~$1.6 billion in FY2024) is oriented toward understanding and preventing substance abuse
- Research proposals framing psychedelics as therapeutic agents rather than drugs of abuse have historically received lower priority
- Until 2023, NIH/NIDA had not issued a specific funding opportunity announcement (FOA) for psychedelic therapy research

**Evidence**: Between 2000 and 2020, NIDA funded fewer than 20 grants related to psychedelic therapy research, compared to hundreds for opioid and stimulant abuse research (NIMH portfolio analysis, 2023). NIMH has begun to fill this gap, but funding remains a fraction of the need.

### Insurance Industry Structural Barriers

The insurance industry's reimbursement framework is structurally incompatible with psychedelic-assisted therapy:

- **Session duration**: Psilocybin therapy sessions last 6-8 hours; MDMA sessions last 8-12 hours. Standard psychiatric billing codes (CPT codes) are designed for 30-60 minute sessions. No billing codes exist for multi-hour drug-assisted psychotherapy sessions.
- **Preparation and integration**: Effective psychedelic therapy requires 1-3 preparation sessions before the dosing session and 2-4 integration sessions afterward. This multi-session model does not fit the single-visit reimbursement structure.
- **Therapist ratios**: Many psychedelic therapy protocols require two therapists per patient during the dosing session (typically one male and one female), doubling the personnel cost compared to standard therapy.
- **Actuarial uncertainty**: Insurance companies lack data to estimate utilization rates, session costs, and long-term outcomes for psychedelic therapy, making them reluctant to develop coverage policies.
- **Competitive threat to pharmaceutical revenue**: Psychedelic therapy that produces lasting improvement after one to three sessions competes directly with the daily-medication model that generates ongoing pharmaceutical revenue. Insurance companies may face pressure from pharmaceutical partners to resist coverage of competing therapies.

**Evidence**: Oregon's psilocybin service center model, which operates entirely outside the insurance system at $1,500-$3,500 per session, demonstrates the access consequences of insurance industry non-participation. Without insurance coverage, psychedelic therapy is accessible only to patients who can afford to pay out of pocket.

## Cultural and Ideological Factors

### The Stigma Legacy of the War on Drugs

The deliberate association of psychedelics with the 1960s counterculture, anti-war movement, and social disruption created a stigma that persists in public attitudes, media coverage, and political discourse:

- **Public perception**: Many Americans still associate psychedelics primarily with recreational drug use, "bad trips," and the counterculture, rather than with clinical medicine
- **Media framing**: Despite the growing evidence base, media coverage often defaults to sensationalized narratives about psychedelic experiences rather than clinical outcomes
- **Political framing**: "Tough on drugs" rhetoric remains politically potent in many constituencies, making elected officials reluctant to champion psychedelic reform

**Evidence**: A 2023 Pew Research Center poll found that 61% of Americans support further research on psychedelics for therapeutic use, but only 36% support full legalization. The gap between support for "research" and support for "legalization" reflects lingering stigma. A 2022 UC Berkeley survey found that 56% of Americans agreed that "psilocybin should be available as a therapeutic option if prescribed by a doctor," indicating broad support for medical access even among those who oppose broader legalization (UC Berkeley Center for the Science of Psychedelics, 2022).

### "Just Say No" Cultural Inertia

The Reagan-era "Just Say No" campaign and the D.A.R.E. program (Drug Abuse Resistance Education) instilled a generation-spanning association between all illegal drugs -- regardless of pharmacological properties -- and moral failure. This cultural framework:

- Makes no distinction between addiction-prone substances (opioids, methamphetamine) and psychedelics (which produce tolerance rapidly and have low addictive potential)
- Treats any positive claim about an illegal substance as "pro-drug" advocacy rather than scientific information
- Creates political risk for elected officials who support evidence-based reform

### Professional Medical Conservatism

The medical profession itself has been slow to embrace psychedelic therapy, even as the evidence base has grown:

- **Psychiatric establishment caution**: The American Psychiatric Association has not issued a formal position supporting psychedelic therapy research or rescheduling. Many academic psychiatrists remain cautious, viewing the evidence as promising but preliminary
- **Medical school curriculum**: Psychedelic pharmacology and therapy are not taught in most U.S. medical schools, meaning that new physicians enter practice with no exposure to the clinical evidence
- **Confirmation bias**: Psychiatrists who have built careers around prescribing SSRIs and cognitive behavioral therapy may be resistant to evidence that an alternative paradigm produces superior outcomes for treatment-resistant populations
- **Liability concerns**: Physicians worry about malpractice exposure from prescribing or supervising therapies that remain federally illegal under Schedule I classification

## Regulatory Fragmentation

### Federal-State Conflict

The absence of a coherent federal framework creates a patchwork system with contradictory incentives:

- Oregon and Colorado have created state-regulated psychedelic programs that exist in legal tension with federal Schedule I classification
- State-licensed practitioners and service centers technically violate federal law
- Banks and financial institutions are reluctant to serve psychedelic businesses due to federal illegality (the same problem that plagues cannabis businesses)
- Interstate commerce in psychedelic substances is federally prohibited, preventing efficient supply chains

### No Single Federal Agency Owns the Issue

Psychedelics policy responsibility is fragmented across multiple federal agencies with different missions:

- **DEA**: Scheduling and enforcement
- **FDA**: Drug approval and safety
- **NIDA/NIMH/NIH**: Research funding
- **SAMHSA**: Treatment services
- **VA**: Veteran healthcare
- **HHS**: Overall health policy coordination

No single agency has a mandate to develop a comprehensive psychedelic policy, and the agencies with the most institutional power (DEA) have the least incentive to reform.

### Professional Licensing and Credentialing Gaps

The mental health profession itself lacks a framework for psychedelic therapy:

- **No professional specialty**: Neither the American Psychological Association nor the American Psychiatric Association has established psychedelic therapy as a recognized subspecialty
- **No accredited training programs**: While several institutions offer psychedelic therapy training (California Institute of Integral Studies, Naropa University, MAPS), none are accredited by mainstream medical or psychological accreditation bodies
- **Licensing board uncertainty**: State licensing boards for psychologists, social workers, and counselors have not issued clear guidance on whether existing licensees can provide psychedelic-assisted therapy within their scope of practice
- **Malpractice insurance gaps**: Most professional liability insurance policies do not explicitly cover psychedelic-assisted therapy, creating risk exposure for practitioners

### Racial and Socioeconomic Inequity in the Reform Movement

The psychedelic reform movement itself reflects structural inequities:

- **Clinical trial diversity**: Major psilocybin and MDMA trials have enrolled 70-90% white participants, limiting the generalizability of findings and the political legitimacy of reform among communities of color (Michaels et al., *BMC Psychiatry*, 2018)
- **Industry demographics**: The psychedelic pharmaceutical and therapy industry is overwhelmingly white, male, and affluent. People of color are underrepresented in research leadership, company boards, and practitioner training programs
- **War on Drugs legacy**: Communities of color have borne the disproportionate burden of drug criminalization. Psychedelic reform that benefits predominantly white, affluent populations while leaving communities of color criminalized for other substances would reproduce rather than challenge existing inequities
- **Access patterns**: Oregon's psilocybin program, at $1,500-$3,500 per session with no insurance coverage, is de facto accessible only to affluent clients. Early demographic data from Oregon service centers confirms that clients are disproportionately white and college-educated

## Why Reform Has Failed

Despite growing scientific evidence and shifting public attitudes, federal psychedelic reform has not yet succeeded for several compounding reasons:

1. **Congressional inaction**: No psychedelic reform bill has reached a floor vote in either chamber of Congress. Bills introduced (such as the Breakthrough Therapies Act and the Right to Try Clarification Act for psychedelics) have stalled in committee.
2. **DEA institutional resistance**: The DEA has not initiated a rescheduling review for any classical psychedelic substance, and its track record of rejecting rescheduling petitions suggests institutional resistance to reform.
3. **FDA setback**: The August 2024 MDMA decision, while based on methodological concerns rather than rejection of psychedelic therapy, delayed the first potential federal approval and dampened reform momentum.
4. **Pharmaceutical industry ambivalence**: While psychedelic biotech companies support reform, the larger pharmaceutical industry (which profits from daily medications that psychedelic therapy could displace) has not actively lobbied for scheduling reform.
5. **Divided advocacy**: The psychedelic reform movement includes both medical model advocates (who favor FDA-approved therapy) and broader decriminalization advocates (who favor personal use access), creating strategic tensions that dilute political pressure.
6. **Political polarization**: Drug policy remains a polarized issue. While bipartisan support exists for veteran-focused psychedelic research, broader reform proposals face reflexive opposition from "tough on drugs" constituencies.
7. **Oregon Measure 110 backlash**: Oregon's 2020 drug decriminalization measure (Measure 110), passed on the same ballot as the psilocybin therapy Measure 109, faced significant public backlash and was partially repealed in 2024 after being blamed for increased visible drug use in Portland. This backlash has created political headwinds for drug policy reform broadly, including psychedelic reform, despite the fact that psychedelic therapy (Measure 109) and drug decriminalization (Measure 110) are substantively different policies.
8. **FDA advisory committee process**: The June 2024 FDA advisory committee vote of 9-2 against recommending MDMA approval demonstrated that even when clinical evidence is substantial, the regulatory process can be derailed by methodological concerns (blinding challenges, therapist adherence) that are inherent to the psychedelic therapy model and may be impossible to fully resolve within the existing FDA framework.

## The Interaction of Root Causes

The root causes identified above do not operate in isolation. They interact in a system of mutually reinforcing barriers:

- **Schedule I status** prevents research, which prevents the evidence base from growing, which prevents rescheduling -- the catch-22 loop
- **NIDA funding bias** compounds the Schedule I barrier by directing available research funding toward abuse studies rather than therapeutic studies
- **Cultural stigma** reduces political will for reform, which allows DEA institutional resistance to go unchallenged
- **Insurance industry non-participation** ensures that even successful state programs (Oregon) reach only affluent patients, limiting the political constituency for reform
- **Professional credentialing gaps** slow the development of a trained workforce, which limits the scalability of therapy even where legal access exists
- **Racial inequity** in the reform movement reduces support among communities of color and their political representatives, narrowing the coalition available for legislative action
- **Pharmaceutical industry ambivalence** removes a potential lobbying force that could counterbalance DEA resistance

This system of interlocking barriers explains why reform has been so slow despite the strength of the scientific evidence. Breaking the cycle requires simultaneous action on multiple fronts: expanding research funding (to overcome the evidence gap), reforming DEA licensing (to remove bureaucratic barriers), building workforce capacity (to enable scaled access), and mandating insurance coverage (to ensure equity). No single intervention is sufficient; the systemic nature of the problem requires a systemic response.
