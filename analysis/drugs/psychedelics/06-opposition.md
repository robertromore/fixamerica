# Psychedelics Policy: Opposition to Reform

Proposals to reform psychedelic policy -- whether through federal rescheduling, expanded research access, or regulated therapeutic frameworks -- face resistance from a range of actors who believe that the risks of liberalizing access outweigh the therapeutic benefits, that existing regulatory frameworks are adequate, or that psychedelics present unique dangers that justify continued prohibition.

---

## Who Opposes Reform

### Organized Opposition

| Actor | Interest | Resources | Tactics |
|-------|----------|-----------|---------|
| Drug Enforcement Administration (institutional) | Maintain scheduling authority; enforcement mission | $3.3 billion budget (FY2024); 10,000+ employees | Deny rescheduling petitions; slow research licensing; Congressional testimony emphasizing abuse potential |
| Some psychiatric professional organizations | Concern about safety, training standards, premature adoption | Professional membership, institutional credibility | Publish cautionary statements; lobby against rapid regulatory change |
| Law enforcement lobbying groups (FOP, NAPO) | Oppose drug liberalization broadly | Political influence, union resources | Congressional lobbying; public messaging on drug risks |
| Pharmaceutical companies (conventional) | Protect antidepressant and anxiolytic market share | Pharmaceutical lobbying: $374 million annually (OpenSecrets, 2024) | Indirect opposition through funding of research emphasizing risks; lobbying against scheduling reform |
| Drug-free workplace advocates | Maintain zero-tolerance drug policies | Employer coalitions, drug testing industry ($5.7 billion market) | Lobby against decriminalization; emphasize workplace safety |
| Some religious conservative organizations | Moral objection to psychoactive drug use | Political networks, voter mobilization | Frame psychedelics as moral threat; oppose ballot initiatives |

### Ideological Opposition

- **Social conservatives**: View psychedelic use as morally problematic, associated with counterculture values antithetical to traditional family structures and religious norms. Concerned about normalization of drug use and its perceived impact on social cohesion. Key voices include the Family Research Council and some evangelical leaders who frame psychedelic reform as part of a broader cultural decline.
- **Law-and-order conservatives**: Believe drug prohibition is essential to public safety. Oppose any policy perceived as "soft on drugs," regardless of substance-specific evidence. View rescheduling as a slippery slope toward broader drug legalization. Notable proponents include former DEA administrators and prosecutors who built careers on drug enforcement.
- **Cautious scientific voices**: Some psychiatrists and pharmacologists argue that the evidence base, while promising, is not yet sufficient to justify major policy changes. They emphasize the need for larger, more diverse clinical trials, longer follow-up periods, and better understanding of adverse effects before expanding access. This position was reflected in the FDA advisory committee's 9-2 vote against recommending MDMA approval in June 2024.
- **Anti-commercialization advocates (from the left)**: Some progressive critics worry that the psychedelic therapy movement is being captured by venture capital and pharmaceutical companies that will create expensive, patented therapies accessible only to wealthy patients, reproducing rather than challenging health inequities. Organizations like the Chacruna Institute have raised concerns about the erasure of indigenous contributions.
- **FDA purists**: Some regulatory advocates argue that the FDA's standard drug approval process -- including rigorous randomized controlled trials, adequate blinding, and diverse enrollment -- must not be compromised for psychedelics. They view state-level programs (Oregon, Colorado) as dangerous experiments that circumvent the safety protections of the federal drug approval process.
- **Parents' advocacy groups**: Some parent organizations have expressed concern about the message that psychedelic reform sends to young people, arguing that relaxing restrictions on any controlled substance normalizes drug experimentation among adolescents.

### Opposition Funding and Resources

| Opposition Actor | Estimated Resources | Primary Mechanism of Influence |
|-----------------|--------------------|-----------------------------|
| DEA | $3.3 billion budget (FY2024) | Regulatory authority; Congressional testimony; research licensing delays |
| Pharmaceutical industry (indirect) | $374 million lobbying annually | Funding of research emphasizing risks; quiet legislative opposition |
| Law enforcement unions (FOP, NAPO) | $10-20 million lobbying annually | Congressional lobbying; public messaging on drug risks |
| Drug testing industry | $5.7 billion market | Employer coalition lobbying; advocacy for zero-tolerance workplace policies |
| Social conservative organizations | $50+ million combined (FRC, Heritage Foundation, etc.) | Voter mobilization; media; legislative lobbying |

The combined resources of opposition actors significantly exceed those of the psychedelic reform movement, though opposition is often passive (maintaining status quo) rather than active (organized campaigns against reform). This asymmetry is critical: maintaining prohibition requires only inaction, while reform requires affirmative legislative and regulatory action. The structural advantage of the status quo means that opposition does not need to "win" debates -- it merely needs to prevent action.

### State-Level Opposition Patterns

Opposition to psychedelic reform has manifested differently at the state level:

- **California SB 58 veto (2023)**: Governor Gavin Newsom vetoed a bill that would have decriminalized psilocybin, DMT, and mescaline for adults 21+, citing the need for a "regulated therapeutic framework" rather than broad decriminalization. This demonstrates that even in progressive states, opposition can focus on the distinction between therapeutic and recreational access.
- **Oregon local opt-outs**: Approximately 100 of Oregon's 278 cities and counties (36%) opted out of the psilocybin services program, reflecting local opposition driven by safety concerns, moral objections, and political dynamics. Opt-outs are concentrated in rural and conservative jurisdictions.
- **Massachusetts stalled legislation**: Multiple psychedelic therapy bills have been introduced in Massachusetts but have failed to advance through committee, despite the state's generally progressive politics and its history of municipal decriminalization (Somerville, Cambridge, Northampton, Easthampton).
- **Active opposition campaigns**: In some localities, anti-psychedelic campaigns have been organized by law enforcement groups, conservative religious organizations, and neighborhood associations concerned about the impact of psychedelic service centers on community character.

### Shifting Opposition Landscape

The opposition landscape is not static. Several trends are reshaping the dynamics:

1. **Generational shift**: Younger Americans are significantly more supportive of psychedelic reform than older Americans. A 2023 YouGov poll found that 47% of adults aged 18-34 support psychedelic legalization, compared to 23% of adults aged 65+. As the electorate ages, opposition will naturally decline.
2. **Scientific consensus**: As the evidence base grows, purely anti-scientific opposition becomes less tenable. The shift from "there is no evidence" to "the evidence is insufficient" to "the evidence needs more diversity" represents a progressive retreat of opposition.
3. **International precedent**: Australia's 2023 rescheduling makes it harder for U.S. opponents to argue that therapeutic psychedelic use is too radical. The "other countries are doing it safely" argument is increasingly available to reformers.
4. **Conservative defections**: The involvement of conservative figures (Sen. Rand Paul, Sen. Ted Cruz, Rep. Dan Crenshaw) in psychedelic reform legislation has fractured the law-and-order conservative opposition. When prominent Republicans support psychedelic research, it becomes harder for the GOP to maintain unified opposition.
5. **Patient advocacy growth**: As more patients receive psychedelic therapy (in clinical trials, Oregon service centers, and underground settings), the number of people with positive personal experiences grows, creating an expanding constituency for reform that is difficult for opponents to dismiss.

## Opposition Arguments and Rebuttals

### Argument 1: Psychedelics Are Dangerous and Have High Abuse Potential

**Claim**: Schedule I classification is appropriate because psychedelics have a high potential for abuse and pose significant risks to users, including psychotic episodes, flashbacks, and psychological harm.

**Evidence Cited**: Opponents point to emergency department visits involving psychedelics (estimated 7,000-8,000 annually involving hallucinogens, SAMHSA DAWN, 2021), cases of lasting psychological disturbance (hallucinogen persisting perception disorder, or HPPD), and deaths associated with psychedelic use in uncontrolled settings.

**Reality**: The pharmacological evidence does not support classifying classical psychedelics as having "high potential for abuse" comparable to Schedule I substances like heroin. Psychedelics produce rapid tolerance (making daily abuse impractical), do not cause physical dependence or withdrawal, and are not associated with compulsive use patterns. The 2017 Global Drug Survey found that psilocybin mushrooms had the lowest rate of emergency medical treatment of any recreational drug surveyed (0.2% of users), compared to 1.0% for cannabis and 3.2% for synthetic cannabis. The DEA's own administrative law judge found in 1985 that MDMA did not meet Schedule I criteria. While psychological risks exist (anxiety, psychotic episodes in predisposed individuals), they are manageable with proper screening and clinical protocols.

**Rebuttal**: The argument conflates uncontrolled recreational use with regulated therapeutic administration. Clinical trials involving more than 40,000 research subjects since the 1950s, and thousands of modern trial participants, have demonstrated a favorable safety profile when psychedelics are administered in controlled settings with appropriate screening, preparation, and support. No clinical trial participant has died from psilocybin or LSD administration. Risk mitigation through proper screening (excluding individuals with psychotic disorders or family history of schizophrenia) and clinical oversight addresses the legitimate safety concerns. The safety profile supports rescheduling to Schedule II or III, not continued Schedule I status.

**Comparative context**: The DEA's own scheduling system undermines this argument. Cocaine is Schedule II (accepted medical use). Methamphetamine is Schedule II (marketed as Desoxyn). Fentanyl is Schedule II. These substances have far higher abuse potential and toxicity than classical psychedelics, yet they are classified less restrictively. The inconsistency demonstrates that Schedule I classification of psychedelics is based on political history, not pharmacological evidence.

### Argument 2: The Evidence Is Insufficient for Major Policy Changes

**Claim**: While early clinical results are promising, the evidence base is not yet large enough, diverse enough, or long-term enough to justify rescheduling, FDA approval, or state-level regulated access programs.

**Evidence Cited**: Phase 2 and Phase 3 trials have relatively small sample sizes (typically 100-300 participants); trial populations have been disproportionately white and educated; long-term follow-up data (beyond 12-18 months) is limited; the FDA's 2024 MDMA rejection cited methodological concerns.

**Reality**: This argument has some validity. The psychedelic evidence base, while growing rapidly, is still smaller than that for established psychiatric medications. The MDMA Phase 3 trials enrolled approximately 200 participants across two studies. Long-term safety data beyond 2 years is limited. Trial populations have indeed lacked racial and socioeconomic diversity.

**Rebuttal**: However, the insufficiency argument must be evaluated against the regulatory standard, not against an impossible standard of perfection. The FDA Breakthrough Therapy designation is specifically designed for drugs that show "substantial improvement" over existing treatments for serious conditions -- a standard that psilocybin and MDMA have met. The evidence base for psychedelics is now comparable to or stronger than that which existed when many current psychiatric medications were approved. Moreover, the Schedule I classification itself is the primary barrier to generating larger, more diverse studies. The argument that "more evidence is needed" while maintaining the very regulatory framework that prevents evidence from being gathered is circular. Reform advocates argue that rescheduling (to Schedule II or III) would be the most effective way to generate the additional evidence that cautious critics correctly identify as needed.

### Argument 3: State Programs Like Oregon Lack Adequate Safety Controls

**Claim**: Oregon's Measure 109 psilocybin program allows access without a medical prescription or diagnosis, creating risks of inappropriate use, adverse events, and harm to vulnerable populations.

**Evidence Cited**: Oregon's program does not require a medical referral, psychiatric evaluation, or diagnosis. Facilitators receive only 120-160 hours of training (compared to years for licensed psychologists). Early media reports highlighted high costs, inconsistent service quality, and concerns about inadequate screening.

**Reality**: Oregon's program does lack some safety features that the medical model would provide. There is no requirement for a diagnostic assessment or medical clearance (though a "preparation session" is required). Facilitator training is substantially less than clinical psychologist training. The absence of medical gatekeeping means that individuals with contraindications (e.g., family history of psychosis, certain cardiac conditions) may not be adequately screened.

**Rebuttal**: Oregon's program represents a deliberate policy choice to lower access barriers, reflecting evidence that medical gatekeeping has historically restricted access disproportionately for marginalized communities. The program includes a mandatory preparation session where facilitators can identify risk factors, and facilitators are trained to screen for contraindications. The appropriate policy response to identified safety gaps is to strengthen screening and training requirements within the existing framework, not to return to prohibition. Colorado's program, informed by Oregon's experience, includes stronger safety provisions.

### Argument 4: Commercialization Will Create Inequitable Access

**Claim**: The psychedelic therapy model -- requiring multi-hour sessions with trained therapists in licensed facilities -- will inevitably be expensive and accessible primarily to affluent, white, urban populations, reproducing existing mental health care disparities.

**Evidence Cited**: Oregon psilocybin sessions cost $1,500-$3,500 per session; insurance does not cover psychedelic therapy; clinical trial populations have been disproportionately white (70-90% in major studies); psychedelic therapy companies are venture capital-funded and profit-driven.

**Reality**: This concern is well-founded. Current psychedelic therapy costs are prohibitive for most Americans. The Oregon program has no insurance coverage, no Medicaid reimbursement, and no sliding-scale mandate. Clinical trial participants have been overwhelmingly white and college-educated. The pharmaceutical model (patents, investor returns, premium pricing) could entrench these disparities.

**Rebuttal**: Inequitable access is a problem to solve, not a reason to deny access to everyone. The solution is to build equity into the regulatory framework: require insurance and Medicaid coverage for FDA-approved therapies, fund community-based delivery models, mandate diverse clinical trial enrollment, create training programs for therapists from underrepresented communities, and prevent patent monopolies on naturally occurring compounds. Maintaining prohibition does not serve equity -- it ensures that access is limited to those with the resources to travel to legal jurisdictions or access underground therapists.

**Precedent**: The same equity argument was used to oppose Medicare, Medicaid, and the Affordable Care Act -- that expanding access would primarily benefit some groups over others. In each case, the answer was to design equity into the program, not to deny access to everyone. The Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act of 2008 provides a legal framework for requiring equitable insurance coverage of psychedelic therapies once they are FDA-approved.

### Argument 5: Psychedelic Reform Is a Slippery Slope to Broader Drug Legalization

**Claim**: Rescheduling psychedelics or creating regulated access programs will normalize drug use and pave the way for legalization of more dangerous substances.

**Evidence Cited**: Opponents point to the trajectory from medical cannabis to recreational cannabis legalization, suggesting that psychedelic therapy is a Trojan horse for broader drug liberalization.

**Reality**: The slippery slope argument assumes that policy distinctions cannot be maintained, which is contradicted by experience. Alcohol legalization did not lead to heroin legalization. Medical cannabis legalization has been followed by recreational cannabis legalization in some states, but this progression was driven by specific political and economic dynamics, not by an inevitable logical chain.

**Rebuttal**: Psychedelic reform advocates are not arguing for legalization of all drugs. They are arguing that specific compounds with demonstrated therapeutic value and favorable safety profiles should be classified based on scientific evidence rather than political stigma. The scheduling system already makes distinctions: cocaine is Schedule II (medical use accepted), while heroin is Schedule I. Reclassifying psilocybin to Schedule II would apply the same evidence-based logic. Policy decisions should be made substance by substance based on evidence, not foreclosed by speculative slope arguments.

### Argument 6: Indigenous Practices Will Be Harmed by Commercialization

**Claim**: Commercial psychedelic therapy appropriates indigenous spiritual practices, threatens limited natural resources (particularly peyote), and excludes indigenous communities from economic benefits.

**Evidence Cited**: The National Council of Native American Churches has expressed concern that decriminalization could increase demand for wild peyote, which is already threatened in its native South Texas habitat. Indigenous leaders have criticized the psychedelic industry for profiting from traditional knowledge without consent or compensation.

**Reality**: This concern is legitimate and substantive. Peyote grows slowly (taking 10-15 years to mature) and faces genuine conservation threats. The commercialization of ayahuasca has already created problematic dynamics in South American indigenous communities. The psychedelic industry has generally not established benefit-sharing agreements or meaningful inclusion of indigenous perspectives.

**Rebuttal**: Indigenous concerns should be addressed through policy design, not by opposing reform entirely. Specific protections include: excluding peyote from decriminalization measures (as Colorado has done); funding peyote conservation and cultivation programs; requiring benefit-sharing agreements; including indigenous representatives in regulatory bodies; and establishing cultural competency requirements for psychedelic therapy training programs.

**Positive example**: Colorado's Natural Medicine Health Act (Proposition 122) explicitly excluded peyote from its scope, responding directly to concerns raised by the National Council of Native American Churches. This demonstrates that indigenous protections can be incorporated into reform legislation. The Nagoya Protocol on Access and Benefit Sharing (2010) provides an international framework for addressing traditional knowledge claims that could inform domestic policy.

## The FDA MDMA Decision as a Case Study in Opposition Dynamics

The FDA's August 2024 decision not to approve MDMA-assisted therapy illustrates how opposition dynamics operate within regulatory institutions:

### Timeline of the FDA MDMA Decision

| Date | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|--------------|
| Late 2023 | Lykos Therapeutics files NDA for MDMA-assisted PTSD therapy | First psychedelic NDA submitted to FDA |
| March 2024 | FDA staff briefing documents published | Raised concerns about trial methodology, blinding, therapist adherence |
| June 4, 2024 | FDA Advisory Committee meets | 9-2 vote against recommending approval |
| August 2024 | FDA issues Complete Response Letter | Declined approval; requested additional clinical trial |

### How Opposition Arguments Manifested in the FDA Process

- **Blinding concerns**: Advisory committee members emphasized that MDMA's recognizable effects make true blinding impossible, raising questions about placebo response. This is a legitimate methodological concern but one that is inherent to all psychoactive substance trials (including antidepressants, where side effects often reveal treatment assignment).
- **Therapist adherence**: Reports that therapists in MDMA trials deviated from protocols were cited as evidence that the therapy component is not standardizable. This concern reflects the FDA's discomfort with drug-plus-therapy combination treatments that do not fit the standard pharmaceutical model.
- **Diversity concerns**: The advisory committee noted insufficient racial and socioeconomic diversity in trial populations. This is a valid concern that reform advocates share.
- **Safety allegations**: Media reports (including *New York Magazine* coverage) of therapist misconduct in MAPS-affiliated settings were raised during the advisory committee proceedings. While these reports addressed serious issues, they pertained to training and oversight practices rather than to the pharmacological safety of MDMA itself.

### Lessons for Future Reform

The MDMA decision demonstrates that opposition to psychedelic reform can operate through procedural and methodological channels even when outright political opposition would be ineffective. Advocacy strategies must engage with FDA regulatory science, not just political messaging. Future NDA submissions should proactively address blinding challenges, therapist training standardization, diverse enrollment, and safety monitoring.

### Argument 7: Psychedelic Reform Will Worsen the Fentanyl Crisis

**Claim**: Loosening restrictions on any controlled substance signals that the government is weak on drug enforcement, which will embolden drug trafficking organizations and worsen the fentanyl and opioid crisis.

**Evidence Cited**: The fentanyl crisis has killed over 70,000 Americans per year since 2021. Opponents argue that any relaxation of drug scheduling sends a dangerous signal during the worst overdose crisis in American history.

**Reality**: There is no pharmacological or policy connection between psychedelic therapy reform and the fentanyl crisis. Psychedelics are not opioids, are not addictive, and are not produced by drug trafficking organizations. In fact, emerging evidence suggests that psilocybin and ibogaine may be effective treatments for opioid use disorder.

**Rebuttal**: Conflating psychedelic therapy with fentanyl trafficking is a rhetorical strategy, not a policy argument. Rescheduling psilocybin from Schedule I to Schedule II or III does not affect DEA enforcement resources or capabilities for fentanyl interdiction. The substances are pharmacologically unrelated, produced by different supply chains, and used by different populations. Moreover, psychedelic-assisted therapy for opioid addiction could actually help address the fentanyl crisis by providing a new treatment pathway for patients with opioid use disorder.

### Argument 8: The Ketamine Industry Shows What Will Go Wrong

**Claim**: The unregulated ketamine clinic industry -- with its at-home prescribing, safety incidents, and Matthew Perry's death in 2023 -- demonstrates the dangers of allowing psychedelic-type therapies outside the strictest regulatory framework.

**Evidence Cited**: Approximately 800 ketamine clinics operate with inconsistent safety protocols. Telehealth companies have prescribed compounded ketamine for unsupervised home use. Matthew Perry died in October 2023 from acute ketamine effects.

**Reality**: This concern has validity. The ketamine industry has demonstrated real risks of under-regulation.

**Rebuttal**: The appropriate response to the ketamine industry's problems is better regulation, not prohibition. The ketamine experience is an argument for proactive regulatory design for psychedelic therapy -- establishing training standards, safety protocols, adverse event reporting, and restrictions on unsupervised use before the market develops -- rather than an argument against psychedelic therapy itself. Oregon's service center model and the FDA's REMS framework both represent more structured approaches than the ketamine industry's off-label prescribing model.

## Historical Patterns of Opposition

Opposition to psychedelic reform follows patterns established during the War on Drugs:

1. **Conflation of all drug use**: Opponents group psychedelics with highly addictive substances (opioids, methamphetamine), ignoring fundamental pharmacological differences. This strategy is effective because most Americans do not know the pharmacological distinctions between substance classes.
2. **Moral framing over scientific evidence**: Opposition often relies on moral objections ("drugs are wrong") rather than evidence-based risk assessment. This framing is particularly effective with social conservative constituencies and in electoral contexts.
3. **Institutional self-interest disguised as public safety**: DEA opposition to rescheduling serves the agency's enforcement mission, but is framed as protecting public safety. The DEA has never voluntarily rescheduled a Schedule I substance based on new therapeutic evidence.
4. **Fear of the unknown**: Much opposition is driven by stigma and unfamiliarity rather than specific safety data. The word "psychedelic" itself triggers associations with the 1960s counterculture that are difficult to overcome with clinical data alone.
5. **"More research needed" as permanent delay tactic**: The call for "more research" is valid in principle but becomes a bad-faith argument when paired with maintaining the Schedule I barriers that prevent that research. This circular logic has been used to delay cannabis reform for decades and is now being applied to psychedelics.
6. **Oregon Measure 110 backlash as a political weapon**: The backlash against Oregon's 2020 drug decriminalization measure (Measure 110) -- which was partially repealed in 2024 -- has been used by opponents to discredit all drug policy reform, including psychedelic therapy (Measure 109), despite the substantive differences between the two policies.

## Counter-Strategies

### Political Strategies

- **Lead with veterans**: Veteran advocates are the most politically powerful constituency supporting psychedelic reform. Bipartisan veteran-focused legislation (research funding for PTSD treatment) is the most viable entry point for federal reform. The American Legion's Resolution 11 (2022) provides institutional cover for conservative lawmakers.
- **Build bipartisan coalitions**: Frame psychedelic therapy as a medical and scientific issue, not a drug liberalization issue. Conservative support exists for evidence-based veteran care and for reducing government barriers to medical innovation. The Breakthrough Therapies Act (co-sponsored by Sen. Cory Booker and Sen. Rand Paul) exemplifies successful bipartisan framing.
- **State-level momentum**: Continue building state regulatory models that generate evidence and normalize regulated psychedelic access. Each successful state program reduces the political risk of federal reform by demonstrating workable regulatory frameworks.
- **Engage the FDA constructively**: Rather than attacking the FDA for the MDMA decision, work with the agency to design clinical trials that meet its methodological standards while accommodating the inherent challenges of psychedelic therapy research.
- **Leverage the ketamine precedent**: Point to the existing ketamine clinic industry (Schedule III, ~800 clinics) as evidence that psychoactive therapy is already operating in the U.S. healthcare system. Rescheduling psychedelics to Schedule II or III would place them in the same regulatory category as ketamine, not create a novel regulatory situation.

### Communication Strategies

- **Center patients, not substances**: Frame the debate around patients suffering from treatment-resistant depression, PTSD, and addiction rather than around psychedelic substances themselves. Patient testimonies are the most powerful communication tool.
- **Use clinical language**: "Psilocybin-assisted therapy for treatment-resistant depression" is more politically effective than "psychedelic legalization." Language choice matters enormously in shaping media coverage and legislative debate.
- **Highlight institutional science**: Emphasize Johns Hopkins, NYU, FDA Breakthrough Therapy designations, and *NEJM*/*JAMA*/*Nature Medicine* publications rather than counterculture associations. The scientific credibility of the evidence base is the reform movement's strongest asset.
- **Address safety proactively**: Acknowledge risks and present concrete mitigation strategies rather than dismissing concerns. The most credible advocacy position is: "These substances have real risks that require careful management through screening, training, and oversight -- and that is exactly what regulated access provides."
- **Distinguish from Oregon Measure 110**: Proactively distinguish psychedelic therapy (Measure 109) from drug decriminalization (Measure 110) in public communication. The backlash against Measure 110 should not be allowed to contaminate public perception of psychedelic therapy programs.
- **Use data visualization**: Translate clinical trial results and treatment gap statistics into accessible visual formats. The comparison between MDMA-assisted therapy's 71.2% PTSD remission rate and conventional therapy's outcomes is a powerful data point that should be widely disseminated.

### Policy Design Strategies

- **Medical model entry point**: Begin with FDA-approved therapeutic access under REMS, which addresses safety concerns within an established regulatory framework
- **Exclude peyote**: Respect indigenous concerns by excluding peyote from broader reform measures
- **Include equity provisions**: Build sliding-scale access, insurance coverage mandates, and diverse workforce training into legislation to preempt inequity arguments
- **Sunset and review provisions**: Include mandatory program evaluations and sunset clauses to address concerns about moving too fast
- **Proactive safety design**: Establish therapist training standards, adverse event reporting systems, and patient screening protocols that address the legitimate concerns of cautious scientific voices
- **Learn from ketamine**: Explicitly reference the ketamine industry's regulatory gaps as problems that proactive psychedelic regulation would prevent
- **Cost-effectiveness arguments**: Present the economic case for psychedelic therapy. Treatment-resistant depression costs $17,000-$25,000 more per patient per year than treatment-responsive depression (Amos et al., *Journal of Clinical Psychiatry*, 2018). A one-to-three-session psychedelic therapy that achieves remission is far more cost-effective than years of daily medication and repeated hospitalizations.
- **Incremental approach**: Frame reform as a series of evidence-based steps (research expansion, FDA approval, rescheduling, insurance coverage) rather than a single dramatic policy change. Each step is individually defensible and builds on the previous one.
- **Comparative scheduling**: Emphasize that rescheduling psychedelics from Schedule I to Schedule II or III would place them in the same category as substances already used in medicine (cocaine, methamphetamine, ketamine). This framing normalizes rescheduling as a routine regulatory adjustment, not a radical policy change.

### Engagement Strategies for Specific Opposition Groups

| Opposition Group | Engagement Strategy | Key Message |
|-----------------|--------------------|----|
| DEA | Legislative mandate removing scheduling discretion for FDA-approved substances | "Science, not law enforcement, should determine medical classifications" |
| Cautious psychiatrists | Emphasize FDA Breakthrough Therapy designation and rigorous trial data | "The evidence meets the same standard as other approved psychiatric medications" |
| Social conservatives | Frame around veteran care, suffering reduction, and medical innovation | "Supporting our veterans means supporting access to breakthrough PTSD treatments" |
| Law enforcement | Emphasize that rescheduling does not affect enforcement against trafficking | "Medical rescheduling is about patients, not criminals" |
| Anti-commercialization left | Support public-benefit models, insurance mandates, and equity provisions | "We share the goal of equitable access -- that requires reform, not prohibition" |
| Parents' groups | Emphasize that regulated access includes age restrictions and clinical oversight | "Regulation is safer for young people than prohibition, which drives use underground" |
| FDA purists | Acknowledge FDA's important role while noting structural barriers | "We support rigorous science -- that is why Schedule I must be reformed to enable it" |
