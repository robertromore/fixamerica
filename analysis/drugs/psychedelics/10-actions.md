# Psychedelics Policy: What You Can Do

Psychedelic policy reform is advancing rapidly at the state and local level, but federal change requires sustained citizen engagement. The gap between scientific evidence and federal policy creates an urgent need for informed advocacy. Whether you are a patient, veteran, healthcare professional, or concerned citizen, there are concrete actions you can take to support evidence-based psychedelic reform.

---

## Individual Actions

### Learn and Stay Informed

- Read Michael Pollan's *How to Change Your Mind* (2018) for an accessible introduction to psychedelic science and history
- Follow the MAPS Bulletin (quarterly publication from the Multidisciplinary Association for Psychedelic Studies) for updates on clinical trials and policy developments
- Review the FDA's 2023 draft guidance on psychedelic drug development to understand the federal regulatory pathway
- Track clinical trial results on ClinicalTrials.gov by searching "psilocybin" or "MDMA"
- Follow STAT News psychedelics coverage for rigorous, independent health journalism on the topic

### Make Personal Changes

- If you or a family member has treatment-resistant depression, PTSD, or substance use disorder, discuss psychedelic-assisted therapy research with your healthcare provider. Bring specific clinical trial data (e.g., the Goodwin et al. *NEJM* 2022 psilocybin study, the Mitchell et al. *Nature Medicine* 2023 MDMA study) to the conversation.
- Ask your healthcare provider whether clinical trial enrollment might be appropriate (ClinicalTrials.gov lists active studies with eligibility criteria and enrollment locations). As of 2024, more than 100 psilocybin trials and dozens of MDMA trials are registered, with sites across the country.
- If you are a veteran, contact the Heroic Hearts Project (<https://www.heroicheartsproject.org>) or Veterans Exploring Treatment Solutions (VETS, <https://vfrfoundation.org>) for information on psychedelic therapy access programs. The VA is also funding psychedelic research at multiple medical centers -- ask your VA provider about enrollment opportunities.
- Educate yourself about the risks and safety considerations of psychedelics before forming policy opinions -- the Multidisciplinary Association for Psychedelic Studies and the Johns Hopkins Center for Psychedelic and Consciousness Research publish accessible safety information
- If you live in Oregon, consider the state psilocybin service center program. Sessions cost $1,500-$3,500 and do not require a medical prescription or diagnosis, only a preparation session with a licensed facilitator. Information is available at the Oregon Health Authority's Psilocybin Services page.
- If you or someone you know is considering underground psychedelic therapy, research safety precautions thoroughly. The Zendo Project (a MAPS initiative) provides harm reduction information for psychedelic experiences. Be aware that underground therapy lacks safety oversight, informed consent protections, and accountability structures.

### Understand the Legal Landscape

Before taking any action related to psychedelics, understand the current legal framework:

| Situation | Legal Status | Risk Level |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| Participating in a clinical trial | Legal (FDA-approved research) | None |
| Receiving psilocybin at an Oregon service center | Legal under Oregon law; technically federal Schedule I violation | Low (federal enforcement unlikely) |
| Personal use in a city that has decriminalized | Lowest law enforcement priority; still technically illegal under state/federal law | Low (but not zero) |
| Possession in a state without decriminalization | Federal and state criminal offense | Moderate to High |
| Traveling to Jamaica, Netherlands, or other legal jurisdictions | Legal in those jurisdictions; risk upon return if bringing substances | Low (if no substances transported) |
| Growing psilocybin mushrooms in Colorado | Legal under Colorado law for adults 21+; federal Schedule I violation | Low (federal enforcement unlikely for personal cultivation) |

### Use Your Voice

- **Contact Your Representatives**: Urge your U.S. Senators and Representative to support federal psychedelic research funding, DEA research licensing reform, and evidence-based scheduling policy. The most effective advocacy is specific: reference particular bills, funding levels, or policy actions.
- **Submit Public Comments**: When the FDA, DEA, or HHS issue proposed rules or guidance related to psychedelics, submit public comments. The FDA's public comment process is accessible at Regulations.gov.
- **Write Letters to the Editor**: Local newspapers and online publications are influential in shaping public opinion. Write about the therapeutic potential of psychedelics, citing clinical trial data, or share a personal story about treatment-resistant mental illness.
- **Share Information**: Share credible research findings and policy developments with your social networks. Emphasize clinical evidence and patient stories rather than recreational use narratives.

## Community Actions

### Organize Locally

- Form or join a local psychedelic society chapter -- organizations like Students for Sensible Drug Policy (SSDP) and Decriminalize Nature have active local chapters in many cities
- Host educational events featuring researchers, clinicians, or patients who have participated in psychedelic therapy clinical trials
- Partner with local mental health advocacy organizations (NAMI chapters, Depression and Bipolar Support Alliance) to raise awareness of psychedelic therapy research
- If you live in a city that has not yet addressed psychedelic decriminalization, organize a community education campaign modeled on successful efforts in Denver, Oakland, and other cities

### Build Coalitions

- Connect psychedelic reform advocacy with veteran service organizations -- many American Legion posts have passed resolutions supporting psychedelic research
- Engage faith communities -- some religious leaders have supported psychedelic research as a means of alleviating suffering, and indigenous religious use provides a bridge to religious liberty advocates
- Build relationships with local healthcare providers and mental health professionals who may be supportive of psychedelic therapy research
- Work with local government officials to understand the decriminalization process in your jurisdiction
- Volunteer as a peer support worker at psychedelic harm reduction events -- the Zendo Project (a MAPS initiative) trains volunteers to provide psychological support at festivals and events where psychedelic use occurs
- Organize community screenings of psychedelic-related documentaries (*How to Change Your Mind*, *Fantastic Fungi*) followed by discussion panels with local researchers or clinicians

### Engage with Local Media

- Write letters to the editor of your local newspaper when psychedelic policy news occurs (FDA decisions, state legislation, clinical trial results)
- Contact local TV and radio stations to suggest stories on psychedelic therapy research, particularly if there are clinical trial sites or psychedelic therapy practitioners in your area
- Pitch op-eds to local and regional publications combining personal experience with clinical evidence
- Correct misinformation when you see sensationalized or inaccurate media coverage of psychedelics -- provide specific citations to clinical trial data

## Political Actions

### Contact Elected Officials

**When to Contact**:

- When psychedelic-related bills are introduced or moved through committee in Congress or your state legislature
- During the annual federal appropriations process (advocate for NIH and VA psychedelic research funding)
- When the FDA or DEA issue guidance, proposed rules, or make scheduling decisions related to psychedelics
- During election season -- ask candidates about their positions on psychedelic therapy and drug scheduling reform

**What to Say -- Template Email**:

```text
Subject: Support Evidence-Based Psychedelic Therapy Research and Reform

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

I am writing as your constituent from [City, State] to urge you to support federal policy reforms that would expand access to psychedelic-assisted therapy for Americans with treatment-resistant mental health conditions.

The FDA has granted Breakthrough Therapy designation to psilocybin for treatment-resistant depression and to MDMA for PTSD, reflecting the significant clinical evidence that these therapies can help patients who have not responded to conventional treatments. An estimated 8.9 million Americans have treatment-resistant depression, and approximately 13 million adults have PTSD in any given year. Veterans are disproportionately affected: 1.7 million veterans receive VA care for PTSD, and 17.5 veterans die by suicide every day.

I urge you to:
1. Support increased federal funding for psychedelic therapy research through NIH and the VA
2. Co-sponsor legislation to streamline DEA research licensing for psychedelic studies
3. Support evidence-based scheduling reform so that substances with demonstrated therapeutic value are not trapped in Schedule I

Thank you for your service and consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your City, State]
```

**What to Say -- Phone Call Script**:

```text
"Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [City]. I'm calling to urge [Representative/Senator] [Last Name] to support federal psychedelic therapy research funding and scheduling reform. The FDA has recognized the breakthrough potential of psilocybin and MDMA for treatment-resistant depression and PTSD. I'd like the [Representative/Senator] to support increased NIH and VA research funding and evidence-based scheduling reform. Can you please note my position? Thank you."
```

### Support Legislation

Current or proposed legislation to support:

- **Breakthrough Therapies Act** (Sen. Cory Booker, D-NJ / Sen. Rand Paul, R-KY): Would require expedited DEA review of scheduling for substances with FDA Breakthrough Therapy designation -- contact your Senators and Representative to co-sponsor
- **Veterans Access to Psychedelic-Assisted Therapy Act**: Would fund VA research on psychedelic therapies for PTSD -- especially important for members of the Senate and House Veterans Affairs Committees
- **Right to Try Psychedelics Act** (Rep. Dan Crenshaw, R-TX): Would clarify that the federal Right to Try Act applies to psychedelic therapies in development -- contact House Energy & Commerce Committee members
- **MDMA Therapy Access Act** (Sen. Ted Cruz, R-TX / Sen. Kirsten Gillibrand, D-NY): Would expand access to MDMA-assisted therapy for veterans through the VA system
- **Psychedelic Research Expansion Act**: Would appropriate $200 million over 5 years for NIH and VA psychedelic research
- **State-level legislation**: If your state is considering psychedelic therapy or decriminalization legislation, contact your state legislators with information about Oregon and Colorado's programs. As of 2024, legislation has been introduced in Connecticut, Massachusetts, New Jersey, California, Vermont, Hawaii, and Utah, among other states.

### Appropriations Advocacy

Federal research funding is determined through the annual Congressional appropriations process. This is one of the most impactful areas for citizen advocacy:

- **When**: The annual appropriations cycle typically runs from February (President's Budget Request) through September (end of fiscal year). The Labor-HHS-Education Subcommittee handles NIH and SAMHSA funding.
- **What to ask for**: Specific appropriations language directing NIMH, NIDA, and VA to increase funding for psychedelic therapy research. Reference specific dollar amounts (e.g., "$40 million per year for psychedelic therapy research across NIH institutes").
- **Who to contact**: Members of the Senate and House Appropriations Committees, particularly the Labor-HHS-Education Subcommittee. These members have direct control over research funding levels.
- **How**: Submit written testimony during appropriations hearings. Send letters to subcommittee members during the markup period. Join coordinated advocacy campaigns organized by MAPS, Drug Policy Alliance, or Heroic Hearts Project during appropriations season.

### Electoral Engagement

**Questions to Ask Candidates**:

1. "Do you support increased federal funding for psychedelic-assisted therapy research through NIH and the VA?"
2. "Would you support rescheduling psychedelics that receive FDA approval from Schedule I to a schedule that permits medical use?"
3. "What is your position on Oregon's and Colorado's regulated psychedelic therapy programs?"
4. "How would you address the mental health treatment gap for the millions of Americans with treatment-resistant depression and PTSD?"

**Voting Considerations**:

- Look for candidates who support evidence-based drug policy and mental health reform
- Key committees: Senate HELP Committee, House Energy & Commerce Committee, Senate and House Veterans Affairs Committees, Senate and House Appropriations Committees (Labor-HHS-Education Subcommittee)
- Bipartisan support is essential -- psychedelic reform has drawn support from both parties, particularly on veteran-focused research

## Professional Actions

### For Healthcare Providers

- Stay informed about psychedelic therapy research by reading reviews in *American Journal of Psychiatry* (Reiff et al., 2020), *Neuropsychopharmacology* (Carhart-Harris and Goodwin, 2017), and monitoring ClinicalTrials.gov
- Consider incorporating knowledge of psychedelic-assisted therapy into clinical practice -- preparation and integration therapy can be provided within existing licensure even before psychedelic substances are FDA-approved
- Discuss psychedelic therapy research with patients who have treatment-resistant depression, PTSD, or substance use disorders, and help them evaluate clinical trial enrollment opportunities
- Support colleagues pursuing psychedelic research by serving as co-investigators, referring patients for trial enrollment, and advocating within your institution
- If you are interested in providing psychedelic-assisted therapy, pursue training through CIIS, Naropa, MAPS, or Fluence training programs (see [Resources](09-resources.md) for details)

### For Researchers

- Apply for NIH/NIDA/NIMH funding for psychedelic research -- NIH issued its first dedicated funding opportunity announcement for psychedelic therapy research in 2023
- Collaborate with established psychedelic research centers (Johns Hopkins, NYU, UCSF, Imperial College) to build on existing protocols and institutional knowledge
- Include diverse populations in study designs -- address the racial and socioeconomic diversity gap that has weakened the evidence base and political legitimacy of reform
- Publish in high-impact journals (*NEJM*, *JAMA*, *Nature Medicine*, *Lancet Psychiatry*) to maximize scientific and public impact
- Apply for DEA Schedule I Researcher Registration if your institution does not already have one. The process takes 6-12 months but is essential for conducting psychedelic research.

### For Mental Health Professionals

- Pursue psychedelic therapy training through programs at California Institute of Integral Studies (CIIS), Naropa University, MAPS training program, or Fluence. Training programs range from 80-160 hours.
- Advocate within professional organizations (APA, AMA, NASW) for evidence-based positions on psychedelic therapy. Submit resolutions, serve on committees, and contribute to position statement development.
- Offer preparation and integration therapy services for patients who are participating in clinical trials, receiving psilocybin therapy in Oregon, or planning to access legal psychedelic experiences in other jurisdictions. Preparation and integration therapy is within existing scope of practice for licensed mental health professionals.
- Educate yourself about contraindications, safety considerations, and adverse event management for psychedelic therapy. The primary contraindications are personal or family history of psychotic disorders (schizophrenia, schizoaffective disorder, bipolar I) and certain cardiovascular conditions.

### For Attorneys

- Provide pro bono legal support to psychedelic reform organizations, ballot initiative campaigns, and legislative drafting efforts
- Assist with religious liberty cases -- the *Gonzales v. O Centro* (2006) and *Church of the Holy Light of the Queen v. Mukasey* (2009) precedents provide a foundation for RFRA-based psychedelic religious freedom claims
- Support Right to Try legal challenges for psychedelic therapies with FDA Breakthrough Therapy designation
- Contribute to patent challenge efforts -- organizations like Porta Sophia are filing inter partes review petitions against overbroad psychedelic therapy patents
- Advise healthcare providers on scope of practice, liability, and regulatory compliance for psychedelic-adjacent services (preparation, integration therapy)

### For Veterans

- Share your experience with elected officials -- veteran personal testimonies are the most politically powerful advocacy tool in psychedelic reform
- Participate in clinical trials at VA medical centers. The VA is funding MDMA and psilocybin trials for PTSD at multiple sites. Contact your VA provider or check ClinicalTrials.gov for enrollment opportunities.
- Advocate through veteran service organizations -- introduce resolutions at your American Legion post, VFW chapter, or other veteran organization supporting psychedelic research
- Contact the Heroic Hearts Project or VETS for information on legal psychedelic therapy options, including retreats in legal jurisdictions and clinical trial enrollment
- Testify before Congressional committees -- the Senate and House Veterans Affairs Committees hold regular hearings on veteran mental health where personal testimony is impactful

### For Educators

- Incorporate psychedelic pharmacology and therapy evidence into medical school, nursing school, and graduate psychology curricula
- Host continuing education workshops for practicing clinicians on psychedelic therapy research and clinical considerations
- Support student advocacy -- Students for Sensible Drug Policy (SSDP) chapters exist at hundreds of campuses and provide a pipeline for the next generation of reform advocates

## Advocacy Toolkit

### Key Talking Points

When discussing psychedelic policy reform with others, emphasize:

1. **Clinical evidence is substantial**: The FDA granted Breakthrough Therapy designation to psilocybin (2018, 2019) and MDMA (2017), recognizing "substantial improvement" over existing treatments. Phase 2 and 3 trials at Johns Hopkins, NYU, and other leading institutions demonstrate significant efficacy for treatment-resistant depression and PTSD.
2. **Millions of Americans need better treatments**: An estimated 8.9 million adults have treatment-resistant depression and ~13 million have PTSD. Current treatments fail a substantial share of these patients. Psychedelic therapy offers a new option.
3. **Veterans are a priority population**: 1.7 million veterans receive VA care for PTSD, and 17.5 veterans die by suicide every day. MDMA-assisted therapy showed 71.2% of participants no longer meeting PTSD diagnostic criteria in Phase 3 trials.
4. **This is a medical and scientific issue**: Psychedelic reform is about evidence-based medicine, not counterculture politics. Leading medical institutions and the FDA itself recognize the therapeutic potential.

### Common Misconceptions to Counter

| Misconception | Reality | Source |
|---------------|---------|--------|
| "Psychedelics are highly addictive" | Classical psychedelics (psilocybin, LSD) produce rapid tolerance, do not cause physical dependence, and have among the lowest addiction potential of any psychoactive substances | Nutt et al., *Lancet*, 2010 (drug harm ranking) |
| "Psychedelics are extremely dangerous" | Psilocybin mushrooms had the lowest rate of emergency medical treatment (0.2%) of any recreational drug surveyed by the Global Drug Survey (2017). No deaths have occurred from psilocybin or LSD in clinical trials. | Global Drug Survey, 2017 |
| "There is no medical evidence for psychedelics" | The FDA itself granted 3 Breakthrough Therapy designations for psychedelic therapies (2017-2019), a designation reserved for substances showing "substantial improvement" over existing treatments | FDA, 2017-2019 |
| "Legalizing psychedelics means everyone would use them unsupervised" | Reform proposals focus on regulated therapeutic access (FDA-approved therapy, state service center models) with trained practitioners, not unregulated recreational use | Oregon Measure 109; Colorado Prop 122 |
| "Psychedelic reform is just about getting high" | Modern psychedelic reform is driven by institutional science (Johns Hopkins, NYU, VA) and focused on treating treatment-resistant depression, PTSD, addiction, and end-of-life distress | Goodwin et al., *NEJM*, 2022; Mitchell et al., *Nature Medicine*, 2023 |

### Effective Storytelling

When advocating for psychedelic reform, personal stories are more persuasive than statistics. If you or someone you know has been affected by treatment-resistant mental health conditions, consider sharing your story:

- **With elected officials**: Personal constituent stories are among the most impactful forms of advocacy. A veteran who cannot access MDMA therapy for PTSD, or a cancer patient who benefited from psilocybin for end-of-life anxiety, personalizes the policy debate.
- **With media**: Local newspapers, radio stations, and online publications are receptive to personal stories about mental health treatment gaps. Op-eds combining personal experience with clinical data are particularly effective.
- **At community events**: Psychedelic societies, mental health advocacy organizations, and veteran service organizations host events where personal testimonies can educate and motivate.
- **Through social media**: Share your story on platforms where it can reach broader audiences. Use clinical language and cite specific research to maintain credibility.

### Dos and Don'ts of Advocacy

| Do | Do Not |
|----|--------|
| Center patients and their suffering | Center the psychedelic experience itself |
| Use clinical language ("psilocybin-assisted therapy") | Use recreational language ("tripping," "psychedelic trip") |
| Cite specific clinical trial results with sources | Make vague claims about psychedelics "curing" conditions |
| Acknowledge risks and present mitigation strategies | Dismiss safety concerns or minimize risks |
| Emphasize institutional science (Johns Hopkins, FDA, VA) | Reference counterculture figures or recreational use |
| Frame reform as bipartisan and evidence-based | Frame reform as partisan or ideological |
| Address opponents' concerns respectfully | Attack or dismiss opponents |
| Highlight the veteran constituency and suicide crisis | Use shock tactics or sensationalized imagery |
