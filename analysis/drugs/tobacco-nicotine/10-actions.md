# Tobacco and Nicotine Policy: What You Can Do

Tobacco use kills 480,000 Americans every year -- more than any other preventable cause of death. While the regulatory framework has grown stronger over the past two decades, critical policy battles remain, from the stalled menthol ban to the nicotine reduction standard to cessation funding. Citizen engagement has been pivotal at every stage of tobacco control progress, from the campaign for smoke-free workplaces to the passage of Tobacco 21.

---

## Individual Actions

### Learn and Stay Informed

- Subscribe to the **Campaign for Tobacco-Free Kids** email list for federal and state policy updates: <https://www.tobaccofreekids.org/>
- Follow **Truth Initiative** research and prevention updates: <https://truthinitiative.org/>
- Read the **American Lung Association's "State of Tobacco Control"** annual report to understand how your state ranks on tobacco control policies: <https://www.lung.org/research/sotc>
- Explore the **UCSF Tobacco Industry Documents Archive** to understand the industry's history of deception: <https://www.industrydocuments.ucsf.edu/tobacco/>
- Monitor FDA tobacco regulatory actions through the **FDA Center for Tobacco Products** page: <https://www.fda.gov/tobacco-products>
- Review your state's tobacco control spending at the **Campaign for Tobacco-Free Kids "Broken Promises"** report to see how your state uses MSA and tobacco tax revenue: <https://www.tobaccofreekids.org/what-we-do/us/statereport>
- Track state tobacco legislation through the **Public Health Law Center's** policy database: <https://www.publichealthlawcenter.org/>

### Make Personal Changes

- If you smoke, call **1-800-QUIT-NOW** (the national quitline) or visit **smokefree.gov** to access free cessation support, including text-based coaching and information about FDA-approved medications
- Talk to your healthcare provider about cessation options, including combination therapy (medication plus counseling), which triples quit rates compared to unaided attempts
- Ask your insurer about cessation coverage -- under the ACA, most private plans must cover cessation without cost-sharing; if you are being charged, file a complaint with your state insurance department or CMS
- If you use e-cigarettes and are not using them to quit smoking, consider talking to a healthcare provider about nicotine dependence treatment options
- Make your home and car completely smoke-free to protect family members from secondhand smoke exposure
- If you are a parent or educator, have evidence-based conversations with young people about nicotine addiction -- avoid scare tactics and focus on the neuroscience of addiction and industry manipulation
- If you are a young person who vapes, **This Is Quitting** (text DITCHVAPE to 88709) provides free, anonymous quit-vaping support from Truth Initiative
- Reduce exposure to tobacco retail advertising by supporting tobacco-free pharmacy policies (CVS stopped selling tobacco in 2014, setting an industry precedent)

### Use Your Voice

- **Contact Your Representatives**: Urge your U.S. Senators and Representative to support tobacco tax increases, cessation funding, and FDA enforcement authority
- **Submit Public Comments**: When FDA proposes tobacco regulations (menthol ban, nicotine standard, graphic warnings), submit comments during the public comment period via regulations.gov. Individual comments matter -- the FDA received over 300,000 comments on the menthol rule
- **Write Letters to the Editor**: Respond to local coverage of tobacco and vaping issues with evidence-based perspectives; emphasize the 480,000 annual deaths and the industry's history of targeting vulnerable populations
- **Share Information**: Use social media to amplify tobacco control research and advocacy, particularly around key dates like the Great American Smokeout (third Thursday of November) and World No Tobacco Day (May 31)
- **Testify at Public Hearings**: When your city council, county board, or state legislature considers tobacco-related measures, attend and testify. Personal stories from former smokers, families affected by tobacco, and healthcare providers are particularly effective

## Community Actions

### Organize Locally

- Attend local city council and county board meetings to advocate for smoke-free ordinances in public spaces, parks, and multi-unit housing
- Partner with local health departments to conduct tobacco retail compliance checks and report violations to the FDA via the CTP Tip Line
- Organize a community screening of tobacco control documentaries (e.g., *Merchants of Doubt*, *Addiction Incorporated*, *Broken Promises*) followed by discussion
- Work with local schools to ensure they are implementing evidence-based tobacco and nicotine prevention curricula
- Support community-based cessation programs, particularly in underserved areas with high smoking rates
- Advocate for tobacco-free policies at community events, festivals, and public gatherings
- Map tobacco retail density in your neighborhood using tools from **Counter Tools** (<https://countertools.org/>) and advocate for density reduction through local zoning and licensing

### Build Coalitions

- Connect with local chapters of the American Cancer Society, American Lung Association, and American Heart Association to join their tobacco control advocacy networks
- Engage faith communities in tobacco control advocacy -- many denominations have policy positions supporting tobacco regulation, and churches, mosques, and synagogues can serve as cessation support sites
- Partner with parent-teacher organizations to advocate for tobacco-free school zones and youth prevention programs
- Collaborate with environmental justice organizations to address tobacco retail density in low-income communities and communities of color
- Build bridges with healthcare provider organizations (medical societies, nursing associations) to advocate for cessation coverage and provider training
- Engage local business groups in supporting smoke-free policies by sharing evidence that smoke-free laws do not harm business revenue
- Partner with community health centers (FQHCs) to expand cessation services in underserved areas

### Address Health Equity

- Support organizations like the **African American Tobacco Control Leadership Council** (AATCLC) that advocate for the menthol ban and racial equity in tobacco control
- Advocate for targeted cessation resources in communities with the highest smoking rates: low-income neighborhoods, tribal communities, LGBTQ+ community centers, and behavioral health treatment facilities
- Support culturally appropriate tobacco prevention and cessation programs for American Indian/Alaska Native communities, distinguishing between commercial tobacco addiction and traditional ceremonial tobacco use
- Work with local re-entry programs to provide cessation support for formerly incarcerated individuals, who have smoking rates 2-3 times the national average
- Advocate for tobacco retailer density reduction in neighborhoods where outlet concentrations are highest, which are disproportionately low-income and minority communities

## Political Actions

### Contact Elected Officials

**When to Contact**:

- When the FDA proposes or finalizes tobacco regulations (menthol ban, nicotine reduction standard, graphic health warnings)
- During Congressional budget deliberations that affect tobacco control funding (CDC Office on Smoking and Health, FDA CTP)
- When tobacco tax legislation is introduced or debated at federal or state level
- When your state legislature considers tobacco-related bills (flavor bans, tobacco taxes, clean air laws, cessation funding)
- When your local government considers tobacco retail licensing, zoning restrictions, or smoke-free ordinances

**What to Say - Template Email**:

```text
Subject: Support FDA Tobacco Regulation to Save Lives

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

As your constituent in [City, State], I am writing to urge your support for strong tobacco and nicotine regulation. Tobacco use kills 480,000 Americans every year and costs our healthcare system $240 billion. In our state, [X]% of adults still smoke, and tobacco-related healthcare costs total $[X] billion annually.

I urge you to:
1. Support the FDA's authority to finalize the menthol cigarette ban, which would prevent an estimated 654,000 deaths
2. Increase the federal cigarette excise tax, which has not been raised since 2009 and has lost 30% of its real value to inflation
3. Fund tobacco prevention and cessation programs at CDC-recommended levels ($3.3 billion/year)
4. Support FDA enforcement against the thousands of unauthorized e-cigarettes being sold to youth

These are evidence-based policies that will save lives and reduce healthcare costs. I ask you to stand with public health and not with the tobacco industry.

Thank you for your service and consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your City, State]
```

**What to Say - Phone Call Script**:

```text
"Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [City]. I'm calling about tobacco regulation. I urge the [Representative/Senator] to support the FDA's menthol cigarette ban and to increase funding for tobacco cessation programs. Tobacco kills 480,000 Americans every year, and we need stronger action. Can you please note my position?"
```

**What to Say - State Legislator Script (Flavor Ban)**:

```text
"Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [City/District]. I'm calling to urge [Representative/Senator] to support [bill number], the flavored tobacco products ban. Flavored products are the primary driver of youth nicotine addiction -- 88% of youth e-cigarette users use flavored products. [State] should follow the lead of California and Massachusetts, which have enacted comprehensive flavor bans that are reducing youth tobacco use. Please note my support for this bill."
```

### Support Legislation

Current or proposed legislation to support:

- **Menthol cigarette ban (FDA proposed rule)**: Contact the White House and your members of Congress to urge finalization -- the rule has been delayed since 2022
- **Federal tobacco tax increase**: Support legislation to increase the federal cigarette excise tax from $1.01 to $3.00+ per pack and index to inflation
- **Federal e-cigarette excise tax**: Support legislation establishing the first federal tax on e-cigarettes and nicotine pouches
- **Tobacco-Free Youth Act**: Support any legislation strengthening FDA enforcement against unauthorized e-cigarettes and enhancing Tobacco 21 compliance
- **QUIT Act** (or similar cessation coverage legislation): Support legislation requiring comprehensive cessation coverage under all federal health insurance programs without barriers
- **State-level flavor bans**: Support comprehensive flavored tobacco product bans in your state legislature
- **State tobacco tax increases**: Many states have not raised tobacco taxes in years; advocate for increases with revenue dedicated to cessation and prevention
- **Local smoke-free ordinances**: Support comprehensive smoke-free workplace and public space ordinances in your city or county
- **Tobacco retail licensing**: Support state or local legislation requiring tobacco retail licenses with compliance obligations

### Electoral Engagement

**Questions to Ask Candidates**:

1. "Do you support the FDA's authority to ban menthol cigarettes and flavored cigars? What is your plan to address tobacco companies' targeting of Black communities?"
2. "Will you support increasing the federal cigarette excise tax, which has not been raised since 2009?"
3. "Do you commit to funding tobacco cessation and prevention programs at CDC-recommended levels?"
4. "What will you do to enforce existing laws against unauthorized e-cigarettes being sold to youth?"
5. "Do you accept campaign contributions from tobacco companies? If so, will you commit to recusing yourself from tobacco-related votes?"
6. "Do you support FDA authority to set a nicotine reduction standard for cigarettes that would prevent addiction?"

**Voting Considerations**:

- Check candidates' positions on tobacco industry campaign contributions -- candidates who accept tobacco money may be less likely to support strong regulation. You can search candidate tobacco money at <https://www.opensecrets.org/industries/indus?ind=A02>
- Look for endorsements from public health organizations (American Cancer Society CAN, American Lung Association)
- Pay attention to positions on the House Energy and Commerce Committee and Senate HELP Committee, which have primary jurisdiction over FDA tobacco regulation
- In state races, check whether candidates support your state's tobacco control program or have proposed cutting tobacco prevention funding

## Professional Actions

### For Healthcare Providers

- Implement the "5 A's" (Ask, Advise, Assess, Assist, Arrange) for tobacco cessation at every patient encounter
- Prescribe combination cessation therapy (nicotine replacement plus varenicline or bupropion) as first-line treatment
- Advocate through your professional society for cessation training and coverage
- Screen all patients for tobacco and nicotine use, including e-cigarettes and nicotine pouches
- Ensure your practice's electronic health record system includes tobacco use assessment and cessation referral workflows
- Refer patients to state quitlines (1-800-QUIT-NOW) and digital cessation resources (smokefree.gov)
- If you practice in behavioral health settings, integrate tobacco cessation into all treatment plans -- people with mental illness and substance use disorders smoke at 2-3 times the rate of the general population

### For Educators

- Integrate evidence-based tobacco and nicotine prevention into health education curricula, including specific content on e-cigarettes and nicotine pouches
- Report youth vaping to school administrators using established protocols
- Connect students who use nicotine products to cessation resources, including the **This Is Quitting** text program (text DITCHVAPE to 88709)
- Implement the Stanford REACH Lab's free e-cigarette and nicotine prevention curriculum in your classroom: <https://med.stanford.edu/tobaccopreventiontoolkit.html>
- Advocate for tobacco-free school policies that include e-cigarettes and all nicotine products

### For Employers

- Offer comprehensive cessation coverage in employee health plans, including all seven FDA-approved medications and counseling at no cost-sharing
- Implement tobacco-free campus policies that include e-cigarettes and vaping
- Provide time off for cessation counseling appointments
- Offer incentives for employees who participate in cessation programs
- Ensure that employee assistance programs (EAPs) include tobacco cessation referrals

### For Researchers

- Submit public comments on FDA proposed rules with scientific evidence
- Conduct research on tobacco control policy effectiveness, particularly regarding equity impacts and novel nicotine products
- Publish and disseminate findings through both academic journals and public-facing media
- Decline tobacco industry research funding -- major journals (e.g., *Tobacco Control*, *Nicotine & Tobacco Research*) have policies restricting publication of industry-funded research
- Participate in FDA and NIH tobacco regulatory science funding opportunities

### For Attorneys

- Support tobacco litigation efforts -- state and local governments continue to file suits against e-cigarette companies and tobacco companies
- Provide pro bono legal support to tobacco control organizations
- Monitor MSA compliance in your state and report violations to the state attorney general
- Assist communities in drafting tobacco retail licensing ordinances, flavor restriction policies, and smoke-free regulations

### For Public Health Professionals

- Conduct community health assessments that include tobacco retail environment mapping
- Implement evidence-based tobacco control interventions in your jurisdiction
- Collect and share local data on tobacco use, retail density, and health outcomes to support policy advocacy
- Build relationships with elected officials and educate them about tobacco's impact on community health and healthcare costs
- Connect with the **National Network of Public Health Institutes** (NNPHI) for capacity-building resources

## Advocacy Toolkit

### Key Talking Points

When discussing tobacco policy with others, emphasize:

1. **The death toll**: Tobacco kills 480,000 Americans every year -- that is more than COVID-19 killed in its worst year, and it happens every single year. No other consumer product comes close.

2. **Industry deception**: The tobacco industry lied about the health effects of smoking for 50 years, engineered cigarettes to be more addictive, and deliberately targeted children, Black Americans, and other vulnerable populations. This history demands robust regulation.

3. **Solutions exist**: We know how to reduce tobacco use -- through price increases, flavor restrictions, cessation support, marketing limitations, and product standards. What has been lacking is political will, not scientific knowledge.

4. **The investment case**: Every $1 invested in tobacco cessation returns $2-3 in reduced healthcare costs. The 480,000 annual deaths and $300+ billion in annual costs are preventable with adequate investment.

5. **The equity imperative**: Tobacco's harms are concentrated among the most vulnerable: low-income Americans, Black Americans targeted by menthol marketing, people with mental illness, tribal communities, and LGBTQ+ populations. Stronger regulation is a matter of health equity.

6. **States are not spending the money**: States collect $27.3 billion annually from tobacco taxes and MSA payments but spend only $745 million (22.6% of CDC-recommended levels) on tobacco prevention and cessation. Demand that your state invest tobacco revenue in tobacco control.

### Common Misconceptions to Counter

| Misconception | Reality | Source |
|---------------|---------|--------|
| "Smoking is a free choice" | 95% of smokers started before age 21; 70% want to quit but cannot due to addiction designed by the industry | SAMHSA, 2024; CDC, 2024 |
| "E-cigarettes are just as dangerous as cigarettes" | While not risk-free, e-cigarettes expose users to significantly fewer toxicants than combustible cigarettes; the vast majority of tobacco-related death comes from smoking | National Academies, 2018 |
| "The menthol ban will lead to Prohibition-style enforcement" | The proposed rule targets manufacturers and retailers, not individual consumers; Canada banned menthol in 2017 with no significant enforcement issues | FDA, 2022; Health Canada, 2023 |
| "Tobacco taxes don't work because people are addicted" | A 10% price increase reduces adult smoking by 4% and youth smoking by 7%; low-income smokers are the most price-responsive | Chaloupka et al., 2012 |
| "The tobacco problem is solved -- smoking rates are low" | 28.8 million Americans still smoke, and 480,000 die every year; smoking remains the #1 preventable cause of death | CDC, 2024 |
| "The government should not regulate what adults put in their bodies" | Tobacco products are uniquely lethal -- killing up to half of long-term users when used as intended. No other legal product has this characteristic. Regulation protects both users and bystanders exposed to secondhand smoke | Surgeon General, 2014 |
| "Nicotine pouches like Zyn are safe" | While likely less harmful than cigarettes, nicotine pouches deliver addictive doses of nicotine and have not completed FDA review. Long-term health effects are unknown | FDA CTP, 2024 |
