# Tobacco and Nicotine Policy: Opposition to Reform

Proposals to strengthen tobacco and nicotine regulation face resistance from a coalition of economic interests, political ideologies, and -- uniquely in this policy space -- some harm reduction advocates who believe that overly restrictive regulation may do more harm than good. The opposition landscape is complex because different actors oppose different reforms for different reasons, and some groups that support certain tobacco control measures actively oppose others.

### Ideological Opposition

- **Libertarian**: Opposes tobacco regulation as government paternalism. Adults should be free to make informed choices about nicotine use. Taxation of tobacco is regressive and punitive. The FDA should not be in the business of deciding which products adults can use. Libertarian-leaning think tanks (Reason Foundation, Competitive Enterprise Institute, R Street Institute) have published extensive critiques of FDA tobacco regulation and flavor bans.

- **Conservative (limited government)**: Skeptical of expanding federal regulatory authority. Concerned about the economic impact on businesses and tobacco-growing communities. Favors personal responsibility over government mandates. Opposes tax increases as general principle. Some conservative media figures have championed nicotine pouches (particularly Zyn) as a culture-war symbol of individual freedom.

- **Some harm reduction advocates**: Oppose flavor bans and strict e-cigarette regulation as counterproductive, arguing that these policies will prevent adult smokers from switching to less harmful products and drive consumers to illicit markets. This opposition comes from a public health orientation rather than an industry or libertarian one. Prominent voices include researchers at NYU, Georgetown, and some former government health officials.

- **Criminal justice reform advocates**: Some oppose the menthol ban specifically due to concerns that enforcement will create a new category of prohibited products subject to racially discriminatory policing. The ACLU has raised concerns about enforcement mechanisms, even as it supports addressing tobacco-related health disparities through other means.

### Industry-Funded Opposition

Investigative journalism and academic research have documented that the tobacco industry funds organizations that oppose specific tobacco control measures, particularly the menthol ban. A 2022 Reuters investigation found that Reynolds American and Altria provided financial support to civil rights groups, law enforcement organizations, and community organizations that then publicly opposed the menthol ban on enforcement and equity grounds (Reuters, "How Big Tobacco Funds the Fight Against Menthol Bans," 2022). This complicates the policy debate by making it difficult to distinguish genuine community concerns from industry-manufactured opposition.

## Opposition Arguments and Rebuttals

### Argument 1: Tobacco Regulation Is Nanny-State Paternalism

**Claim**: The government should not tell adults what products they can or cannot use. Tobacco regulation infringes on individual liberty and personal responsibility. Adults are capable of making informed choices about their health.

**Evidence Cited**: Philosophical arguments about bodily autonomy; comparisons to alcohol and other legal products; concerns about regulatory overreach and the "slippery slope" toward broader restrictions on personal choices.

**Reality**: Tobacco is a fundamentally different consumer product. Nicotine addiction typically begins in adolescence -- 95% of adult smokers started before age 21 (SAMHSA, 2024). The overwhelming majority of smokers (70%) wish they could quit but are unable to do so because of the addictive properties deliberately engineered into cigarettes. Tobacco products kill up to half of their long-term users when used exactly as intended -- no other legally available consumer product has this characteristic. The "informed choice" argument fails because addiction undermines the voluntariness of continued use.

**Rebuttal**: Tobacco regulation does not criminalize personal use. The proposed menthol ban, for example, would apply only to manufacturers, distributors, and retailers -- not individual consumers. Existing tobacco regulation (age restrictions, advertising bans, clean indoor air laws) has saved millions of lives without creating a prohibition regime. The question is not whether the government should regulate tobacco but how comprehensively, given that half a million Americans die annually from a product designed to addict its users.

### Argument 2: Flavor Bans Will Create Illicit Markets

**Claim**: Banning menthol cigarettes or flavored e-cigarettes will not eliminate demand but will drive consumers to illicit markets, creating public safety risks, lost tax revenue, and enforcement challenges similar to alcohol Prohibition.

**Evidence Cited**: The growth of illicit cannabis markets alongside legal markets; cross-border cigarette smuggling between high-tax and low-tax states; the experience of Massachusetts's flavor ban, where sales shifted to neighboring states and illicit sources.

**Reality**: Illicit market concerns are legitimate and must be addressed in policy design, but the Prohibition analogy is misleading. Unlike alcohol Prohibition, a menthol ban does not prohibit the underlying product (cigarettes) -- it prohibits one flavor additive. Research on state-level menthol restrictions in Massachusetts and California shows that a significant proportion of menthol smokers switch to non-menthol cigarettes or quit rather than seeking illicit menthol cigarettes (Asare et al., 2023). The FDA's modeling projects that a menthol ban would lead 923,000 smokers to quit within the first year. Canada banned menthol cigarettes nationally in 2017, and the sky did not fall -- smoking rates continued to decline, and no significant illicit menthol market emerged (Health Canada, 2023).

**Rebuttal**: Policy design can mitigate illicit market risks through robust enforcement, retail licensing, and penalties targeted at manufacturers and distributors rather than individual consumers. The existence of some illicit activity does not justify maintaining a legal product that kills 480,000 Americans annually. By this logic, no harmful product could ever be restricted because some people would obtain it illegally. The European Union's 2020 menthol ban across all member states similarly did not produce the predicted wave of illicit activity (European Commission, 2023).

### Argument 3: E-Cigarette Restrictions Will Prevent Adult Smokers from Quitting

**Claim**: Flavored e-cigarettes help adult smokers switch from combustible cigarettes. Banning flavors will push smokers back to more dangerous products. The FDA's PMTA process is unworkable and has left adult smokers without access to products that could save their lives.

**Evidence Cited**: Survey data showing that adult e-cigarette users prefer non-tobacco flavors (approximately 80% use flavored products); the UK's more permissive approach to e-cigarettes and its continued decline in smoking rates; studies showing that flavored e-cigarettes are more effective cessation aids than tobacco-flavored products (Friedman and Xu, 2020).

**Reality**: The relationship between e-cigarette flavors and adult cessation is real but nuanced. While some adult smokers benefit from flavored e-cigarettes, the same flavors are the primary driver of youth initiation -- 88% of youth e-cigarette users use flavored products (NYTS, 2024). The challenge is designing policy that preserves adult access while preventing youth uptake. Additionally, only a small fraction of adult e-cigarette users are actually using the products as intended cessation tools; many are dual users who continue to smoke cigarettes while also vaping, which may provide no health benefit (PATH Study, 2024).

**Rebuttal**: Restricting flavored e-cigarette sales to adult-only venues (tobacco specialty shops with age verification) rather than banning all flavored products could balance adult access with youth protection. The UK experience involves not only permissive e-cigarette regulation but also robust National Health Service cessation support -- a context very different from the U.S. Flavor bans should be paired with expanded cessation services and clear pathways for FDA-authorized reduced-risk products. Furthermore, seven FDA-approved cessation medications with proven efficacy already exist; the argument that flavored e-cigarettes are essential for adult cessation ignores these established treatments.

### Argument 4: Tobacco Taxes Are Regressive and Unfairly Burden the Poor

**Claim**: Tobacco excise taxes are among the most regressive taxes in existence, disproportionately burdening low-income smokers who are least able to afford them. Tax increases punish addiction rather than helping addicted people quit.

**Evidence Cited**: Data showing that low-income smokers spend a significantly higher proportion of their income on cigarettes than affluent smokers; analysis of tobacco tax incidence by income quintile (Tax Policy Center, 2023).

**Reality**: Tobacco taxes are indeed regressive in their direct incidence, but their overall impact is progressive in health terms. Low-income smokers are the most price-sensitive demographic, meaning they are the most likely to quit or reduce consumption in response to price increases. A 10% increase in cigarette prices reduces consumption by approximately 4% overall and by 6-8% among low-income smokers (Chaloupka et al., 2012). When tax revenues are invested in cessation programs that serve low-income populations, the combined effect is a net health benefit for the most disadvantaged.

**Rebuttal**: The regressivity concern can be addressed by dedicating a portion of tobacco tax revenue to cessation services for low-income smokers. The truly regressive outcome is leaving tobacco cheap and accessible while low-income people die from tobacco-related disease at nearly six times the rate of the most educated Americans. Price policy is one of the most effective tools for reducing smoking among the populations that bear the greatest burden. The World Health Organization, World Bank, and International Monetary Fund have all concluded that tobacco taxation is one of the most cost-effective public health interventions available (WHO, 2021).

### Argument 5: The Menthol Ban Will Lead to Racially Discriminatory Enforcement

**Claim**: A federal menthol cigarette ban will inevitably result in police encounters with Black smokers who possess menthol cigarettes. Given the history of racially discriminatory drug enforcement -- including the War on Drugs and stop-and-frisk policing -- a menthol ban will create a new vector for racist policing.

**Evidence Cited**: The disproportionate enforcement of drug laws against Black Americans; the Eric Garner case (killed during an arrest related to selling untaxed cigarettes); the ACLU's analysis of racial disparities in marijuana enforcement.

**Reality**: The enforcement concern is legitimate and deeply felt, particularly in Black communities. However, the FDA's proposed rule explicitly states that enforcement would target manufacturers, distributors, and retailers -- not individual consumers. No individual would be subject to arrest or citation for possessing or using menthol cigarettes. The tobacco industry has amplified and funded enforcement concerns to protect its menthol market, a product category that generates billions in annual revenue from Black consumers.

**Rebuttal**: The AATCLC, the NAACP (national organization), the National Medical Association, and more than 120 health and civil rights organizations support the menthol ban. They argue that the tobacco industry's concern about Black communities is hypocritical given its 60+ years of predatory menthol marketing targeting those same communities. The enforcement risk can be mitigated through statutory language prohibiting individual possession penalties, community-based education campaigns, and equitable enforcement oversight. The alternative -- maintaining a product that kills 45,000 Black Americans annually -- is the greater racial injustice.

### Argument 6: The Nicotine Reduction Standard Will Destroy the Tobacco Economy

**Claim**: An FDA nicotine reduction standard that makes cigarettes non-addictive would devastate the tobacco industry, destroy jobs in tobacco-growing states, eliminate billions in tax revenue, and create an enormous illicit market for full-nicotine cigarettes.

**Evidence Cited**: Economic impact analyses commissioned by the tobacco industry; employment data from tobacco-growing regions; tax revenue projections under reduced consumption scenarios.

**Reality**: A nicotine reduction standard would be phased in over several years, allowing economic adjustment. The tobacco industry's own investments in alternative nicotine products (e-cigarettes, pouches, heated tobacco) suggest that companies have already anticipated a post-cigarette future. Tax revenue would decline, but this decline would be offset by the $240 billion in annual healthcare costs attributable to smoking. Illicit market concerns are legitimate and would require robust enforcement infrastructure.

**Rebuttal**: The tobacco economy argument applies to every effective tobacco control policy -- taken to its logical conclusion, it argues against any regulation that reduces smoking. The question is whether the U.S. should maintain a product that kills 480,000 people annually to protect an industry's revenue. Economic transition programs, including job training, crop conversion assistance, and revenue replacement mechanisms, should be part of any nicotine reduction standard implementation. New Zealand's tobacco endgame proposals included transition assistance for affected businesses and workers.

### Argument 7: The PMTA Process Is Unworkable and Blocks Innovation

**Claim**: The FDA's premarket tobacco product application (PMTA) process is prohibitively expensive, scientifically unclear, and biased against small companies. It effectively gives large tobacco companies a regulatory moat while destroying small e-cigarette businesses that could provide harm reduction alternatives.

**Evidence Cited**: The cost of a single PMTA application ranges from $100,000 to $20 million depending on complexity. The FDA received over 26 million product applications but has authorized only 34 e-cigarette products. The Fifth Circuit has repeatedly found that the FDA's scientific standards were unclear and changed without notice. Thousands of small vape shops have closed due to regulatory uncertainty.

**Reality**: The PMTA process has genuine structural problems. The FDA was overwhelmed by the volume of applications and gave inconsistent guidance about evidentiary standards, particularly regarding comparative population-level evidence. Small companies have been disproportionately burdened. However, the solution is to fix the process, not to abandon premarket review. The existence of 9,000+ unauthorized flavored e-cigarettes on the market demonstrates what happens without effective regulatory gatekeeping.

**Rebuttal**: Reforms to the PMTA process -- including clearer guidance documents, tiered review based on product category, and reduced fees for small manufacturers -- can address workability concerns while preserving the public health purpose of premarket review. The UK's notification system (which requires manufacturers to submit product information and meet safety standards without the full PMTA burden) provides a model for a more streamlined approach that still protects consumers.

## Historical Patterns of Opposition

The tobacco industry's opposition playbook has remained remarkably consistent over seven decades:

1. **Manufacture doubt** about the science (1950s-1990s: doubt that smoking caused cancer; 2010s-present: doubt about e-cigarette risks to youth)
2. **Propose inadequate alternatives** to meaningful regulation (voluntary marketing codes instead of advertising bans; Tobacco 21 instead of flavor bans)
3. **Exploit political divisions** (use the menthol ban to divide public health and civil rights communities; use harm reduction to divide public health researchers)
4. **Litigate aggressively** to delay and weaken regulation (dozens of lawsuits challenging FDA PMTA denials, menthol rule, warning label requirements)
5. **Co-opt the language of opponents** (industry now uses "harm reduction" and "smoke-free future" -- concepts developed by public health advocates -- as marketing and lobbying strategies)
6. **Fund third-party opposition** (finance organizations that oppose regulation on grounds that appear independent of industry interests)
7. **Invoke unintended consequences** (illicit markets, job losses, enforcement disparities) to delay action on products that cause 480,000 deaths annually

### The Industry's Current Strategy

As of 2024-2025, the tobacco industry's opposition strategy has evolved beyond simple obstruction. Major companies are pursuing a dual-track approach:

**Track 1 -- Delay and Litigate on Combustible Tobacco Regulation**:

- Challenge the menthol ban through industry-funded litigation
- Contest every FDA enforcement action against authorized products
- Fund third-party organizations to raise enforcement concerns about the menthol ban
- Lobby Congressional allies to defund FDA enforcement activities
- Invoke the "major questions doctrine" to challenge FDA authority on transformative regulations

**Track 2 -- Shape the Alternative Nicotine Product Market**:

- Position company-owned products (Vuse, NJOY, Zyn) for FDA authorization while competitors face denial
- Acquire or develop authorized alternatives (Altria acquired NJOY for $2.75 billion in 2023)
- Market the "smoke-free future" narrative to regulators, investors, and the public
- Seek modified risk designations for company products (PMI's MRTP application for Zyn, 2023)
- Support harm reduction advocacy that benefits company products while opposing regulation of competitors

This dual-track strategy allows companies to profit from both their legacy combustible businesses (which remain highly profitable) and the emerging alternative nicotine market, while positioning themselves as responsible actors transitioning away from the most harmful products.

## Counter-Strategies

### Political Strategies

- **Build broad coalitions** that include cancer, lung, and heart disease organizations, pediatricians, faith communities, racial justice advocates, and economic fairness groups
- **Use state-level victories** to demonstrate that comprehensive tobacco regulation is achievable and popular (California's Proposition 31 passed with 63% support in 2022)
- **Frame tobacco regulation as protecting children** -- the most politically potent frame, as demonstrated by the rapid passage of Tobacco 21 in 2019
- **Engage tobacco-state allies** by offering economic transition support for tobacco-growing communities and emphasizing the healthcare cost burden
- **Expose industry funding** of organizations that oppose specific tobacco control measures, undermining the credibility of industry-funded opposition

### Communication Strategies

- **Center data and stories**: Lead with the 480,000 annual deaths figure and personal stories of tobacco's impact, particularly on vulnerable communities
- **Expose industry tactics**: The tobacco industry's history of deception is well-documented and politically toxic. Connecting current industry arguments to this history undermines opposition credibility.
- **Address enforcement concerns directly**: Proactively commit to non-criminalization of individual consumers and equity-centered enforcement design
- **Distinguish between products**: Emphasize that the primary target is combustible tobacco, which causes the overwhelming majority of tobacco-related death
- **Use international evidence**: Point to successful implementation of menthol bans (Canada, EU), nicotine reduction research, and tobacco endgame strategies in other countries to demonstrate feasibility

### Legal and Litigation Strategies

- **Prepare for industry litigation proactively**: Build extensive administrative records during rulemaking; engage constitutional law scholars; anticipate major questions doctrine challenges
- **Support FDA enforcement resources**: Advocate for increased CTP enforcement budgets and personnel to address the gap between FDA authority and enforcement capacity
- **File amicus briefs**: Public health organizations should file amicus briefs in FDA-related litigation to provide the court with scientific and public health perspectives
- **Pursue state attorney general enforcement**: State AGs can pursue enforcement actions under state consumer protection laws, MSA compliance, and deceptive advertising statutes
- **Support FTC enforcement**: Advocate for FTC action against deceptive marketing by tobacco and nicotine product companies, including the ongoing FTC suit against JUUL

### Policy Design Strategies

- **Phase in regulations** to allow adjustment and reduce market disruption
- **Pair restrictions with cessation expansion** to address the needs of current users
- **Design enforcement to target the supply chain**, not individual consumers
- **Include robust illicit market monitoring** and enforcement provisions
- **Fund economic transition programs** for affected communities and businesses
- **Create authorized pathways** for less harmful products to maintain adult alternatives while restricting youth access
- **Codify individual possession exemptions** in statute to address enforcement concerns definitively
- **Use international evidence proactively**: Cite Canadian menthol ban, EU menthol ban, and Australian plain packaging outcomes to preempt opposition claims about infeasibility and illicit markets
- **Engage directly with affected communities**: Ensure that Black communities, tribal communities, and other disproportionately affected populations are centered in policy design conversations, not just consulted after the fact
