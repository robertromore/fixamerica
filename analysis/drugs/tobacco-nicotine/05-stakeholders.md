# Tobacco and Nicotine Policy: Stakeholders

The tobacco and nicotine policy landscape is shaped by the interactions of several key groups, each with distinct interests, resources, and levels of political power. The dynamics among tobacco companies, regulators, public health organizations, retailers, and affected populations determine the pace and direction of policy change.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| Current adult smokers | Health consequences, addiction, financial burden | ~28.8 million adults (11.6% of adult population) | Access to cessation support, affordable alternatives, freedom of choice |
| Youth and young adults (12-25) | Nicotine initiation risk, brain development impacts | ~74 million | Protection from addictive products, accurate health information |
| People with mental illness | Disproportionate smoking rates (~25% vs. 11.6% general) | ~13 million adults with serious mental illness | Integrated cessation services, accessible treatment |
| Black/African American smokers | Menthol targeting, higher lung cancer mortality | ~4.5 million Black adults who smoke | End of menthol cigarette marketing, equitable enforcement |
| Low-income smokers | Higher smoking rates, financial burden of addiction | ~8 million adults below poverty who smoke | Affordable cessation, reduced cost barriers |
| Families of smokers | Secondhand smoke exposure, caregiver loss | Tens of millions (including ~5 million children exposed to secondhand smoke) | Smoke-free environments, cessation support for family members |
| E-cigarette and nicotine pouch users | Regulatory uncertainty, product access | ~10 million adult e-cigarette users; growing pouch user base | Continued access to less harmful alternatives, product safety standards |

### Secondary Stakeholders

- **Healthcare providers**: Physicians, nurses, dentists, and pharmacists who treat smoking-related disease and provide cessation counseling. The healthcare system spends $240 billion annually on smoking-attributable medical care (CDC, 2024). An estimated 40% of physicians do not consistently ask patients about tobacco use or offer cessation interventions, despite USPSTF "A" recommendations (CDC, 2023).

- **Employers**: Bear productivity losses estimated at $185 billion annually from smoking-related absenteeism, presenteeism, and premature mortality (CDC, 2024). Employers increasingly offer cessation benefits as part of wellness programs, but coverage quality varies widely.

- **Military and veteran populations**: Historically high smoking rates due to military culture, stress, and tobacco company targeting. The VA spends approximately $6.4 billion annually on smoking-related healthcare (VA, 2024). Active-duty smoking rates have declined to approximately 12% (DoD Health Survey, 2023), but rates remain higher among combat-deployed personnel.

- **LGBTQ+ communities**: Higher smoking rates (~20%) due to targeted marketing, minority stress, and social environments. Tobacco industry documents reveal deliberate marketing strategies targeting LGBTQ+ consumers through bar promotions and community event sponsorships.

- **Tobacco farmers**: Approximately 10,000 U.S. tobacco farms, primarily in Kentucky, North Carolina, Virginia, Tennessee, and Georgia, employ workers and support rural economies. The end of the federal tobacco price support program (2004) has already reduced the tobacco farming labor force, but the crop remains economically important in these regions.

- **Tribal communities**: American Indian/Alaska Native populations have the highest smoking rates (22.6%) among any racial/ethnic group. Traditional ceremonial tobacco use is culturally distinct from commercial tobacco addiction. Tribal public health programs face unique jurisdictional and funding challenges in addressing commercial tobacco use while respecting traditional practices.

- **Prisoners and incarcerated populations**: Smoking rates among incarcerated individuals are 2-3 times the national average. Many correctional facilities have banned smoking, creating enforcement challenges and black-market tobacco economies. Post-release relapse rates are high, and cessation support during and after incarceration remains inadequate (Binswanger et al., 2011).

### Key Organizations and Institutions

#### Tobacco Industry

- **Altria Group (Philip Morris USA)**: The largest U.S. tobacco company, with approximately 44% of the domestic cigarette market (Marlboro). Altria also holds a 35% stake in JUUL Labs (written down to near-zero) and investments in cannabis company Cronos. Revenue: $20.5 billion (2023). Altria acquired NJOY, an e-cigarette company with FDA-authorized products, for $2.75 billion in 2023, positioning itself in the authorized e-cigarette market. Altria is the most politically active tobacco company in the U.S., with extensive lobbying operations in Washington and state capitals.

- **Reynolds American (British American Tobacco subsidiary)**: Second-largest U.S. cigarette company with approximately 25% market share (Camel, Newport, Natural American Spirit). Also markets Vuse e-cigarettes, which hold the largest share of the authorized e-cigarette market. Parent company BAT is headquartered in London. BAT's global "multi-category" strategy aims to transition consumers from cigarettes to Vuse, Velo (nicotine pouches), and Glo (heated tobacco).

- **Philip Morris International (PMI)**: The international tobacco company (separated from Altria in 2008) that markets IQOS heated tobacco globally. PMI acquired Swedish Match (maker of Zyn nicotine pouches) for $16 billion in 2022, making it a major player in the U.S. smokeless and pouch market. PMI has publicly committed to a "smoke-free future" but continues to sell combustible cigarettes in most markets. PMI's U.S. presence is primarily through Zyn, which has become the company's fastest-growing product globally.

- **JUUL Labs**: Once the dominant e-cigarette company (75% U.S. market share in 2018), JUUL has been reduced to a much smaller market presence after regulatory actions, litigation, and public backlash. The company has paid over $2.4 billion in settlements related to youth marketing claims.

- **Independent e-cigarette and vape shop industry**: Thousands of small and medium-sized companies that manufacture e-liquids, sell vaping devices, and operate approximately 14,000 specialty vape shops in the U.S. This segment of the industry generally lacks the resources to navigate the PMTA process and opposes both strict FDA regulation and the dominance of tobacco-industry-owned e-cigarette brands.

#### Public Health Organizations

- **Truth Initiative (formerly American Legacy Foundation)**: Created by the Master Settlement Agreement, Truth Initiative operates the truth anti-tobacco campaign, conducts research, and advocates for tobacco control policy. Funded by MSA proceeds and grants. Its research arm publishes influential reports on youth vaping, tobacco industry marketing, and health disparities.

- **Campaign for Tobacco-Free Kids**: The leading U.S. tobacco control advocacy organization, active in federal and state policy, international tobacco control, and FDA regulatory proceedings. Receives funding from Bloomberg Philanthropies and other foundations. Leads federal advocacy on tobacco tax increases, flavor bans, and FDA enforcement.

- **American Cancer Society / Cancer Action Network**: Major health advocacy organization that lobbies for tobacco control legislation, cessation funding, and cancer research. The Cancer Action Network is its political advocacy arm, conducting lobby days and mobilizing grassroots support.

- **American Lung Association**: Advocates for clean air laws, cessation coverage, and FDA regulatory action. Publishes the annual "State of Tobacco Control" report grading each state on tobacco control policies.

- **American Heart Association**: Advocates for smoke-free environments, cessation coverage, and nicotine reduction policies. Active at both federal and state levels.

### Influencers

- **Tobacco harm reduction advocates**: Organizations like the Consumer Advocates for Smoke-free Alternatives Association (CASAA) and researchers at institutions like the New York University College of Global Public Health argue for policies that facilitate switching from combustible to less harmful nicotine products. They oppose blanket flavor bans and support risk-proportionate regulation.

- **African American Tobacco Control Leadership Council (AATCLC)**: Advocates specifically for the menthol cigarette ban, arguing that menthol has been used as a tool of racial exploitation. Led by Dr. Phillip Gardiner, AATCLC has been the most prominent voice connecting tobacco control to racial justice. AATCLC's advocacy was instrumental in securing the FDA's 2022 proposed menthol rule.

- **Convenience store and retail associations**: The National Association of Convenience Stores (NACS) and state retail associations lobby against flavor bans, tax increases, and regulations that affect tobacco retail. Tobacco products account for approximately 36% of in-store sales at convenience stores. NACS has been a lead opponent of state-level menthol bans and flavor restrictions.

- **Tobacco-state Congressional delegations**: Members of Congress from Kentucky, Virginia, North Carolina, and other tobacco-growing states often oppose aggressive tobacco regulation, regardless of party affiliation. These delegations wield outsized influence through committee assignments (particularly the House Energy and Commerce Committee and Senate HELP Committee).

- **Academic researchers**: Tobacco regulatory science researchers, many funded through FDA and NIH grants, provide the evidence base for tobacco policy. Key research institutions include the University of California San Francisco (UCSF) Center for Tobacco Control Research and Education, the Johns Hopkins Institute for Global Tobacco Control, and the Schroeder Institute at Truth Initiative.

- **Bloomberg Philanthropies**: Michael Bloomberg's philanthropic organization is one of the largest private funders of tobacco control globally, providing hundreds of millions of dollars to support tobacco control advocacy, research, and policy campaigns in the U.S. and internationally.

## Stakeholder Interests and Alignment

### Aligned Interests (Potential Coalition Partners)

| Coalition | Shared Interest | Strength |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| Public health organizations + AATCLC + FDA | Menthol cigarette ban to reduce deaths and address racial disparities | Strong |
| Pediatricians (AAP) + parents + schools + FDA | Youth nicotine prevention, e-cigarette restrictions | Strong |
| Cancer/lung/heart associations + cessation advocates + insurers | Expanded cessation coverage, reduced smoking rates | Strong |
| Harm reduction advocates + some public health researchers + nicotine product manufacturers | Risk-proportionate regulation that permits less harmful alternatives | Moderate |
| State AGs + public health groups + tax advocates | Higher tobacco taxes to fund prevention and reduce consumption | Moderate |
| Environmental justice organizations + AATCLC + public health groups | Reducing tobacco retail density in over-saturated communities | Moderate |

### Conflicting Interests

- **Menthol ban divide**: Public health organizations and AATCLC strongly support a menthol ban, but some civil liberties organizations (e.g., ACLU) and African American advocacy groups (e.g., National Association of Black Law Enforcement Officers, Rev. Al Sharpton's National Action Network) have expressed concern about enforcement creating new vectors for racially discriminatory policing of individual users. Notably, several of these opposing organizations have received funding from tobacco industry sources, complicating the debate (Reuters, 2022).

- **Harm reduction vs. abstinence**: Public health organizations are deeply divided on whether to embrace nicotine harm reduction (permitting e-cigarettes and pouches as less harmful alternatives) or maintain a traditional abstinence-oriented framework. Groups like the Campaign for Tobacco-Free Kids oppose flavored e-cigarettes and most novel nicotine products, while others argue these products can save lives if properly regulated.

- **Industry "transformation" skepticism**: Tobacco companies claim to be transitioning toward "smoke-free" futures, but public health advocates are deeply skeptical given the industry's documented history of deception. Whether to engage with or resist the industry's harm reduction pivot divides the tobacco control community.

- **State revenue vs. public health**: State governments benefit financially from tobacco revenue ($27.3 billion/year) while also bearing the healthcare costs of tobacco use. This tension manifests in inadequate tobacco control funding -- states spend only 22.6% of CDC-recommended levels on prevention and cessation.

- **Small vape shop owners vs. Big Tobacco**: Independent vape shops and small e-liquid manufacturers oppose the PMTA process (which they view as unaffordable and designed to favor large companies) while also opposing tobacco industry dominance of the e-cigarette market. This creates unusual alignments where small businesses and consumer advocates sometimes find themselves at odds with both public health groups and tobacco companies.

### The Tobacco Iron Triangle

The tobacco policy space features a classic "iron triangle" of mutually reinforcing relationships:

1. **Tobacco industry** provides campaign contributions and lobbying dollars to legislators while paying user fees that fund the FDA and excise taxes that fund state budgets
2. **Legislative allies** protect the industry from aggressive regulation, maintain favorable tax structures, and influence FDA oversight priorities
3. **Regulatory agencies** operate within the constraints set by Congress and are funded (in the case of the FDA CTP) by the industry they regulate, creating structural incentive for managed regulation rather than transformative action

## Influence Mechanisms

How stakeholders exert influence on tobacco and nicotine policy:

- **Lobbying**: The tobacco industry spent approximately $24.7 million on federal lobbying in 2023 across all companies and trade associations. Key lobbying firms include Covington & Burling (Altria), Squire Patton Boggs (RAI), and Arnold & Porter. State-level lobbying is extensive but less well tracked (OpenSecrets, 2024).

- **Campaign Contributions**: Tobacco industry political action committees and employees contributed approximately $5.2 million to federal candidates in the 2024 election cycle, with roughly 70% going to Republican candidates. State-level contributions are significant in tobacco-growing states (OpenSecrets, 2024).

- **Revolving Door**: Former government officials regularly transition to tobacco company or trade association positions. Former FDA officials have joined tobacco companies, and former state legislators have become industry lobbyists.

- **Litigation**: The tobacco and e-cigarette industries aggressively litigate against FDA regulatory actions. Multiple circuit courts have vacated or stayed FDA marketing denial orders, and the threat of litigation shapes FDA regulatory decisions. Industry-funded legal challenges have delayed the menthol ban, modified PMTA standards, and created regulatory uncertainty. The industry's litigation capacity is virtually unlimited relative to government enforcement budgets.

- **Public Advocacy and Astroturfing**: The tobacco industry funds organizations that present industry positions as grassroots advocacy. Examples include the National Smokers Alliance (defunct) and some harm reduction organizations that receive industry funding. The e-cigarette industry has cultivated online communities of adult vapers who advocate against flavor bans and strict regulation. Industry funding of organizations that oppose the menthol ban on criminal justice grounds has been documented by investigative journalists (Reuters, 2022).

- **Expertise and Information Control**: Tobacco companies fund research, sponsor academic conferences, and provide data to regulators. While the Tobacco Control Act prohibited tobacco companies from making health claims without FDA authorization, the industry continues to shape the scientific discourse through funded research and expert witnesses.

- **Retail Channel Control and Trade Programs**: Tobacco companies exert influence through their retail relationships, offering trade discounts, slotting fees, and promotional payments to retailers who comply with marketing programs. These "contracts" create financial incentives for retailers to oppose regulation and maintain prominent tobacco displays and advertising in stores.

## Emerging Stakeholders

Several relatively new actors are becoming increasingly important in the tobacco and nicotine policy landscape:

- **Social media platforms**: TikTok, Instagram, and YouTube play a growing role in both promoting and discouraging nicotine product use among youth. Nicotine pouch content (particularly Zyn) has gone viral on social media, raising questions about platform responsibility for nicotine product promotion. At the same time, Truth Initiative has effectively used social media for counter-marketing campaigns.

- **Chinese e-cigarette manufacturers**: Companies such as Shenzhen-based SMOORE International and its subsidiaries manufacture the vast majority of disposable e-cigarettes sold in the U.S., including unauthorized products. These manufacturers operate largely beyond the reach of U.S. enforcement and have become significant (if largely invisible) stakeholders in the U.S. nicotine market.

- **Private equity and venture capital investors**: Investment firms have poured capital into emerging nicotine product companies, including e-cigarette brands, nicotine pouch manufacturers, and cannabis/nicotine convergence products. These investors have financial interests in regulatory outcomes that may diverge from both traditional tobacco company interests and public health goals.

- **International regulatory bodies**: The World Health Organization (FCTC), the European Commission (Tobacco Products Directive), and Health Canada increasingly influence U.S. tobacco policy debates through their regulatory precedents. The EU's menthol ban and the UK's Tobacco and Vapes Bill both inform U.S. policy discussions.

- **State cannabis regulators**: As cannabis and nicotine vaping technologies converge, state cannabis regulatory agencies are becoming relevant stakeholders in nicotine policy, particularly around shared device regulation and product safety standards.
