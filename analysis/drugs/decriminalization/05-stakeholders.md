# Drug Decriminalization: Stakeholders

The drug decriminalization debate is shaped by the interactions of numerous groups, each with distinct interests, levels of power, and capacities to influence policy. Stakeholders range from the millions of Americans directly affected by drug arrests to the institutional actors that administer, benefit from, or seek to reform the current system.

## Who Is Affected

### Primary Stakeholders

| Group | Impact | Size | Key Interests |
|-------|--------|------|---------------|
| People arrested for drug possession | Criminal records, incarceration, collateral consequences (employment, housing, education barriers) | ~1.3 million arrests/year; ~20 million Americans with drug-related criminal records | End of criminalization; record expungement; access to treatment |
| People with substance use disorders | Criminalization deters treatment-seeking; criminal records create barriers to recovery | 48.7 million (SAMHSA NSDUH, 2024) | Treatment access without legal consequences; harm reduction; destigmatization |
| Communities of color | Disproportionate enforcement; intergenerational damage from mass incarceration | ~27% of drug arrests are of Black Americans despite ~13.6% population share | Racial equity in enforcement; end of disparate impact; community investment |
| Families of incarcerated individuals | Economic hardship; family disruption; stigma; children's developmental outcomes | ~2.7 million children have a parent incarcerated (any offense); drug offenses drive ~20% | Family preservation; economic stability; reduced incarceration |
| People who use drugs (non-SUD) | Risk of arrest, criminal record, and violence associated with illegal markets | Estimated 59.3 million past-year illicit drug users (SAMHSA NSDUH, 2024) | Personal autonomy; safety; drug quality/purity; no criminal consequences |

### Secondary Stakeholders

- **Employers and workforce**: Drug possession convictions exclude millions from the labor market, costing employers productive workers and increasing labor shortages. Estimated economic cost of drug-related employment barriers: $65 billion annually (CEA, 2022). Industries facing acute labor shortages -- construction, food service, healthcare -- are disproportionately affected, as these sectors have high rates of workers with criminal records.
- **Taxpayers**: Drug enforcement and incarceration consume ~$47 billion annually (Vera Institute, 2023). Taxpayers bear the cost of a system that fails to reduce drug use while generating collateral consequences that increase future public costs. Each drug possession arrest costs an estimated $10,000 in policing, court, and processing expenses; each drug incarceration costs $35,000-$60,000 annually depending on the state (Vera Institute, 2023).
- **Healthcare system**: Emergency departments handle overdoses and drug-related injuries that a functioning treatment and harm reduction system would prevent. ED visits for drug-related causes exceeded 5 million in 2022 (HCUP, 2023). Hospitals in high-overdose areas report that drug-related ED visits consume 15-25% of emergency capacity.
- **Children and youth**: An estimated 2.7 million children have at least one parent who is incarcerated (any offense), with drug offenses driving approximately 20% of parental incarceration (BJS, 2023). Parental incarceration increases children's risk of poverty, school failure, behavioral problems, and later substance use. Young people in heavily policed communities also observe the consequences of drug enforcement, which shapes attitudes toward authority and the legal system.
- **Treatment providers**: Existing SUD treatment providers have a direct stake in decriminalization outcomes. Increased treatment funding and referrals would expand their patient base and revenue, but decriminalization also brings new demands for capacity, workforce, and clinical approaches that may exceed current capabilities.
- **Recovery community**: An estimated 22 million Americans identify as being in recovery from substance use disorders (SAMHSA, 2022). People in recovery are affected by the stigma of criminalization, the barriers of criminal records, and the adequacy of treatment systems. Many serve as peer support specialists and advocates for health-centered approaches.
- **Harm reduction workers**: Staff and volunteers at syringe service programs, overdose prevention centers, and community-based organizations operate at the frontlines of the drug crisis. They serve populations that criminal enforcement has failed to reach and are uniquely positioned to connect individuals to treatment under a decriminalized framework.

### Influencers

- **Drug Policy Alliance (DPA)**: The leading advocacy organization for drug decriminalization and reform. DPA helped draft and campaign for Oregon's Measure 110 and publishes influential policy research. Annual budget: ~$25 million.
- **American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU)**: Advocates for decriminalization on civil liberties and racial justice grounds. The ACLU's 2020 report documenting racial disparities in cannabis enforcement has been widely cited.
- **Law Enforcement Action Partnership (LEAP)**: Organization of current and former law enforcement officials who support drug policy reform, including decriminalization. Provides political cover for reform by countering the narrative that all police oppose it.
- **National District Attorneys Association (NDAA)**: Generally opposes comprehensive decriminalization; represents prosecutors who process drug cases as a core function.
- **Fraternal Order of Police (FOP)**: The largest police union, with over 365,000 members. Opposes decriminalization; advocates for maintaining criminal penalties and enforcement tools.
- **National Association of Drug Court Professionals (NADCP)**: Supports drug courts as an alternative to standard prosecution but generally opposes full decriminalization, preferring court-supervised treatment.

### Conflicting Interests

- **Law enforcement agencies vs. public health advocates**: Police agencies benefit from drug enforcement budgets, civil asset forfeiture revenue, and arrest-based performance metrics. Public health advocates seek to redirect these resources to treatment. The conflict is both institutional (budget competition) and philosophical (criminal vs. health response). Federal grant structures (Byrne JAG, HIDTA) explicitly incentivize enforcement activity, creating a financial conflict of interest for agencies that might otherwise support reform.
- **Drug courts vs. full decriminalization advocates**: Drug courts use the leverage of criminal charges to compel treatment participation. Decriminalization removes this leverage, threatening the drug court model. Drug court professionals argue that coerced treatment works; advocates respond that voluntary treatment is more effective and more ethical. The NADCP has approximately 3,800 drug court programs, 150,000 annual participants, and a dedicated advocacy infrastructure that would be undermined by comprehensive decriminalization.
- **Treatment providers vs. harm reduction advocates**: Some treatment providers -- particularly abstinence-based programs -- view harm reduction (needle exchange, supervised injection, drug checking) as enabling drug use rather than facilitating recovery. Harm reduction advocates argue that keeping people alive is the prerequisite for any treatment to work, and that the hierarchy of needs (safety, stability, then recovery) requires harm reduction as a foundation. This tension has practical consequences for how decriminalization-linked treatment systems are designed.
- **Prosecutors vs. prosecutorial reformers**: Traditional prosecutors view high drug conviction rates as a measure of effectiveness and a political asset. Reform prosecutors view non-prosecution of possession as a public health improvement. This conflict has played out dramatically in DA races across the country, with reform DAs elected in 2018-2022 and several facing recall or electoral defeat by 2024.
- **Communities of color: internal tensions**: Within communities disproportionately affected by drug enforcement, there are genuine disagreements about decriminalization. Some residents, particularly those who have experienced drug markets and public drug use in their neighborhoods, support enforcement as a quality-of-life measure. Others, who have experienced or witnessed the devastating effects of mass incarceration, prioritize ending enforcement. This internal tension makes it inaccurate to treat communities of color as monolithic stakeholders.

### International Stakeholders

International actors influence U.S. drug decriminalization debates in several ways:

- **United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC)**: Administers international drug control treaties. Has historically supported criminalization but has shifted toward acknowledging that decriminalization of personal use is compatible with treaty obligations (UNODC, 2019).
- **International Narcotics Control Board (INCB)**: The independent treaty body monitoring compliance with UN drug conventions. Has criticized some decriminalization approaches while acknowledging that treaty obligations do not require criminal penalties for personal use (INCB Annual Report, 2023).
- **Global Commission on Drug Policy**: High-profile commission including former heads of state (Fernando Henrique Cardoso of Brazil, Ernesto Zedillo of Mexico, Ruth Dreifuss of Switzerland) that has consistently advocated for decriminalization. Their reports are frequently cited in U.S. policy debates.
- **European Monitoring Centre for Drugs and Drug Addiction (EMCDDA)**: Provides the primary data on European drug policy outcomes, including Portugal's decriminalization. EMCDDA reports are the most frequently cited evidence base in U.S. decriminalization arguments.
- **Partner nations (Mexico, Canada, Colombia)**: Countries that have adopted decriminalization or are affected by U.S. drug enforcement policy have direct interests in U.S. reform. Mexico's president has repeatedly called for the U.S. to adopt a health-centered approach. Canada's British Columbia pilot and reversal provides a North American comparison case.

### The Criminal Justice Iron Triangle

The drug enforcement system operates as a classic iron triangle connecting three mutually reinforcing interests:

1. **Congressional committees** (Judiciary, Appropriations) that authorize and fund drug enforcement
2. **Executive agencies** (DEA, ONDCP, Bureau of Prisons) that implement drug policy and depend on enforcement budgets
3. **Interest groups** (police unions, private prison companies, bail bond industry, drug testing firms) that benefit financially from criminalization and lobby to maintain it

Breaking this iron triangle requires either disrupting one side (e.g., realigning Congressional priorities) or building an alternative power center (e.g., a treatment-centered coalition with its own funding streams and political allies).

### Emerging Stakeholders

Several stakeholder groups have emerged or gained influence in recent years:

- **Recovery community organizations (RCOs)**: Groups like Faces and Voices of Recovery and the National Alliance for Recovery Residences have organized people in recovery as a political constituency, bringing lived experience to policy debates. Their advocacy is particularly effective because it counters the stereotype of decriminalization as "giving up" on people with addictions.
- **Harm reduction organizations**: The National Harm Reduction Coalition, NEXT Distro (mail-based naloxone and supply distribution), and local harm reduction collectives have grown rapidly in response to the overdose crisis. They serve populations that no other system reaches and have become essential infrastructure that complements decriminalization.
- **Tech industry advocates**: Some technology companies and executives have engaged in drug policy reform, driven by concerns about workforce criminal record barriers and by libertarian philosophical commitments. The Open Philanthropy Project and other tech-aligned foundations have provided significant funding for drug policy reform research.
- **Faith-based organizations**: A growing number of religious groups -- including Catholic Charities, Sojourners, and individual evangelical congregations -- have endorsed treatment-over-punishment approaches to drug policy. Faith-based recovery programs (e.g., Celebrate Recovery, Teen Challenge) serve millions and provide a bridge to conservative constituencies.
- **Academic researchers**: Institutions including Johns Hopkins, Columbia, Stanford, and RAND have dedicated research centers to drug policy analysis. Their research provides the evidence base for reform and lends academic credibility to policy proposals.

How stakeholders exert influence on drug decriminalization policy:

The financial influence disparity heavily favors the status quo. Opponents of decriminalization outspend proponents by roughly 3:1 in federal lobbying and 5:1 in campaign contributions. These contributions disproportionately target members of the Judiciary, Appropriations, and Commerce committees, which control drug policy legislation.

### Institutional Influence

- **Revolving door**: Former DEA officials frequently join private security firms, pharmaceutical consulting firms, and lobbying organizations. Former prosecutors become defense attorneys or private prison consultants. The DEA's former acting administrator, Chuck Rosenberg, became a CNN law enforcement analyst; former DEA chief of operations Mike Braun became a consultant for private security companies. These career pathways reinforce enforcement-oriented perspectives in both government and media.
- **Professional associations**: The International Association of Chiefs of Police (IACP), National District Attorneys Association (NDAA), and National Sheriffs' Association issue policy statements, testify before Congress, and provide expert witnesses that frame drug enforcement as essential to public safety. Their institutional credibility with legislators exceeds that of reform advocacy organizations.
- **Drug court establishment**: The approximately 3,800 drug courts represent a powerful constituency that supports treatment within the criminal justice framework but often opposes decriminalization. The National Association of Drug Court Professionals (NADCP) has staff, conferences, and lobbying capacity that rival reform organizations.

### Public Advocacy and Media

- **Public advocacy**: The Drug Policy Alliance, ACLU, and allied organizations conduct public education campaigns, publish reports, and organize grassroots support for decriminalization. The "public safety" frame used by opponents is generally more politically potent than the "public health" frame used by reformers, though this gap has narrowed. Polling consistently shows that when decriminalization is framed as "replacing arrest with treatment," support increases 10-15 percentage points compared to when it is simply framed as "decriminalizing drugs."
- **Media strategy**: Opponents effectively use visual media -- images and videos of public drug use, open-air drug markets, and urban disorder -- to generate emotional opposition to decriminalization. Proponents counter with data, expert testimony, and personal recovery stories, which are less visually compelling but more substantively accurate. The media asymmetry is a persistent strategic challenge.
- **Ballot measures**: In states with initiative processes, reformers have bypassed legislative opposition through direct democracy. Oregon's Measure 110, California's Proposition 47, and numerous cannabis legalization measures have all succeeded at the ballot box when legislative paths were blocked. However, Oregon's reversal has raised questions about whether ballot measures can create durable policy change without legislative and executive buy-in.
- **Expertise and evidence**: NIDA, SAMHSA, and academic researchers produce evidence supporting treatment and harm reduction over criminalization. The Lancet Commission on Drug Policy (2016), the National Academy of Sciences, and the World Health Organization have all endorsed decriminalization. However, this evidence competes with anecdotal narratives (media stories about public drug use, open-air markets) that favor enforcement responses. The "evidence-versus-narrative" gap is a central challenge for reform advocates.
- **Litigation**: The ACLU and other legal organizations use litigation to challenge drug enforcement disparities, asset forfeiture practices, and conditions of incarceration. The *State v. Blake* decision in Washington (2021) demonstrated that courts can effectively decriminalize through constitutional rulings, though the political response (rapid legislative recriminalization) limits the durability of this approach.

## Coalition-Building Opportunities

### Most Promising Coalition for Comprehensive Decriminalization

The most viable political coalition for decriminalization combines five constituencies:

1. **Public health community**: Physicians, nurses, public health officials, and treatment providers who view drug use as a health issue. The American Medical Association, American Public Health Association, and American Academy of Pediatrics have all endorsed treatment-centered approaches.
2. **Racial justice organizations**: The NAACP, Urban League, ACLU, and local racial justice organizations that frame drug enforcement as a civil rights issue. The 3.73x racial disparity in cannabis arrests is a powerful mobilizing fact.
3. **Fiscal conservatives**: Organizations like Right on Crime (supported by the Koch network) and the American Conservative Union that support reducing incarceration costs and government spending. The $47 billion annual cost of drug incarceration is a compelling figure for this constituency.
4. **Libertarian groups**: The Cato Institute, Reason Foundation, and individual libertarian-leaning legislators who oppose drug criminalization on individual liberty and limited government grounds.
5. **Faith communities**: Religious organizations that run recovery programs and support treatment-over-punishment approaches, including Catholic Charities, some evangelical congregations, and mainline Protestant denominations.

This five-part coalition mirrors the structure that passed the First Step Act in 2018. The challenge is extending it from sentencing reform (which all five support) to comprehensive decriminalization (which strains the coalition, particularly with fiscal conservatives and some faith communities who accept reduced sentences but resist eliminating criminal classification entirely).

### Voices from Affected Communities

The stakeholder landscape is incomplete without the perspectives of those most directly affected:

**People with substance use disorders** consistently report that criminalization deters them from seeking help. In a 2023 SAMHSA survey, 47% of individuals with SUD who did not seek treatment cited fear of legal consequences as a reason. Among those who had been arrested, 68% reported that their arrest made it harder, not easier, to enter recovery.

**People in recovery** offer critical testimony. The recovery community increasingly advocates for decriminalization, viewing criminal records as barriers to the stable employment, housing, and social connections that sustain recovery. Organizations like Faces and Voices of Recovery (representing an estimated 22 million Americans in recovery) have endorsed treatment-centered approaches.

**Family members** of individuals with SUD describe the double burden of worrying about both addiction and arrest. Parents who advocate for their children's treatment access have been powerful voices in legislative hearings, particularly in opioid-affected communities.

**Law enforcement officers** who have participated in deflection programs report transformative experiences. Gloucester, MA police chief Leonard Campanello described the Angel Program as "the most important thing I've ever done in policing." Officers in LEAD and PAARI programs frequently report that connecting people to treatment is more professionally satisfying and more effective than making arrests.

**Communities experiencing drug markets** present the most complex stakeholder perspective. Residents of neighborhoods with open-air drug markets simultaneously experience the harms of drug enforcement (over-policing, surveillance, arrest of neighbors and family members) and the harms of drug markets (violence, public disorder, property crime). Their voices are essential to designing decriminalization systems that address both sets of harms.

These lived-experience perspectives are increasingly recognized as essential inputs to policy design, not merely sympathetic anecdotes. Portugal's Dissuasion Commission model was developed with substantial input from people who use drugs and from treatment providers, and this inclusive design process contributed to the system's effectiveness and political durability.

## Stakeholder Dynamics and Shifting Alignments

### The Fentanyl Realignment

The fentanyl crisis has reshaped stakeholder dynamics in important ways. Prior to 2018, drug decriminalization was primarily a concern of progressive advocates, racial justice organizations, and libertarian groups. The devastation of fentanyl in rural, white, and politically conservative communities has created new stakeholders who might not have engaged with decriminalization absent personal loss.

- **Parents and families in opioid-affected communities**: Groups like Team Enough (founded by parents who lost children to overdose) and the Voices Project have become powerful advocacy organizations. Their members are frequently white, suburban or rural, and politically moderate or conservative -- a demographic profile that disrupts the stereotype of decriminalization as a "liberal" cause.
- **Rural law enforcement**: Sheriffs and police chiefs in opioid-devastated counties have become increasingly open to deflection and treatment approaches, not out of ideological commitment to decriminalization but out of practical recognition that their communities cannot arrest their way out of a crisis. The PAARI model has been adopted by over 600 departments, many in conservative jurisdictions.
- **State Medicaid directors**: As opioid-related healthcare costs have consumed state budgets, Medicaid administrators have become stakeholders in treatment-centered approaches. States that have expanded Medicaid have seen measurably better SUD treatment outcomes, creating an institutional constituency for health-centered drug policy.
- **Insurance companies**: Major insurers, facing billions in opioid-related claims, have become unexpected allies of treatment investment. UnitedHealth Group, Blue Cross Blue Shield, and other major carriers have funded treatment expansion and harm reduction programs, recognizing that treatment is less expensive than emergency department visits and inpatient hospitalizations.

### Electoral Stakeholders

Drug decriminalization is increasingly a campaign issue, creating a distinct category of electoral stakeholders:

- **Voters in initiative states**: In states with ballot initiative processes (24 states + D.C.), voters can directly enact or repeal decriminalization measures. Oregon's Measure 110 demonstrated both the power and fragility of this pathway.
- **Reform-oriented district attorneys**: A wave of reform prosecutors elected between 2018 and 2022 (Larry Krasner in Philadelphia, George Gascon in Los Angeles, Kim Foxx in Chicago, Chesa Boudin in San Francisco) adopted non-prosecution or diversion policies for drug possession. However, by 2024, several faced recall or electoral defeat, demonstrating the political vulnerability of prosecutorial decriminalization.
- **Local elected officials**: City councils, mayors, and county commissioners in cities with visible drug use problems (Portland, San Francisco, Seattle, Philadelphia) are frontline political stakeholders whose responses to drug policy are directly shaped by constituent complaints about public disorder.

### The "Silent Stakeholders"

Several groups with significant stakes in decriminalization policy are largely absent from the political debate:

- **People currently using drugs who are not in crisis**: The majority of the estimated 59.3 million Americans who used illicit drugs in the past year do not have a substance use disorder and are not in the criminal justice system. They are nonetheless at risk of arrest, and their economic and social productivity is threatened by criminalization. However, they rarely participate in drug policy debates due to the stigma and legal risk of disclosure.
- **Small business owners in affected communities**: Businesses in neighborhoods with visible drug markets bear economic costs from reduced foot traffic, property damage, and employee recruitment challenges. Their interests in both reducing criminalization's community-level harms and addressing public disorder are rarely centered in policy debates.
- **Children of incarcerated parents**: The 2.7 million children with an incarcerated parent (including those incarcerated for drug offenses) have no political voice but bear significant developmental and economic consequences. Research consistently shows elevated rates of school failure, behavioral problems, and later substance use among this population.
