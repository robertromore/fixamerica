# Drug Decriminalization: Resources

This section provides a curated list of key research, government reports, legal sources, and organizational resources on drug decriminalization policy.

---

## Academic Research

### Peer-Reviewed Studies

- Hughes, C.E., & Stevens, A. "What Can We Learn from the Portuguese Decriminalization of Illicit Drugs?" *British Journal of Criminology* 50, no. 6 (2010): 999-1022. <https://doi.org/10.1093/bjc/azq038> - Foundational study of Portugal's decriminalization outcomes.
- Greenwald, G. "Drug Decriminalization in Portugal: Lessons for Creating Fair and Successful Drug Policies." *Cato Institute White Paper* (2009). <https://www.cato.org/white-paper/drug-decriminalization-portugal> - Early analysis of Portugal model.
- Eastwood, N., Fox, E., & Rosmarin, A. "A Quiet Revolution: Drug Decriminalization Across the Globe." *Release* (2016). <https://www.release.org.uk/publications/drug-decriminalisation-2016> - Comparative analysis of decriminalization in 25+ countries.
- Csete, J., et al. "Public Health and International Drug Policy." *The Lancet* 387, no. 10026 (2016): 1427-1480. <https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(16)00619-X> - Comprehensive Lancet Commission on drug policy reform.
- Stevens, A., et al. "The Effects of Drug Decriminalization: A Systematic Review." *The Lancet Public Health* 5, no. 11 (2020): e620-e630. <https://doi.org/10.1016/S2468-2667(20)30228-9> - Systematic review of 22 studies finding no significant increase in drug use from decriminalization.
- Laqueur, H., Zwald, M., & Brantingham, P.J. "Effects of Reclassifying Drug Possession from a Felony to a Misdemeanor in California." *JAMA Internal Medicine* 180, no. 6 (2020): 884-889. <https://doi.org/10.1001/jamainternmed.2020.1394> - Evaluation of California Proposition 47's impact on drug possession cases.
- Collins, S.E., et al. "Seattle's Law Enforcement Assisted Diversion (LEAD): Program Effects on Criminal Justice and Legal System Utilization and Costs." *Journal of Experimental Criminology* 13 (2017): 145-164. <https://doi.org/10.1007/s11292-017-9278-7> - Evaluation of the LEAD deflection model.

### Working Papers and White Papers

- Drug Policy Alliance. "It's Time for the U.S. to Decriminalize Drug Use and Possession." 2017. <https://drugpolicy.org/resource/its-time-us-decriminalize-drug-use-and-possession> - Policy brief arguing for comprehensive decriminalization.
- RAND Corporation. "How Should Marijuana Be Legalized?" 2015. <https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_briefs/RB9825.html> - Analysis of regulatory design options.
- Caulkins, J.P., et al. "Considering Marijuana Legalization: Insights for Vermont and Other Jurisdictions." RAND Corporation RR-864-RC (2015). <https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RR864.html>

## Government Reports and Data

### Federal Agencies

- Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA). "National Survey on Drug Use and Health (NSDUH)." Annual. <https://www.samhsa.gov/data/data-we-collect/nsduh-national-survey-drug-use-and-health> - Primary data source for U.S. drug use prevalence, SUD rates, and treatment gap.
- Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC). "National Vital Statistics System: Drug Overdose Deaths." Updated monthly. <https://www.cdc.gov/nchs/nvss/vsrr/drug-overdose-data.htm> - Real-time overdose death data.
- Office of National Drug Control Policy (ONDCP). "National Drug Control Budget." Annual. <https://www.whitehouse.gov/ondcp/> - Federal drug control spending by category.
- Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI). "Uniform Crime Reporting (UCR) Program." Annual. <https://www.fbi.gov/services/cjis/ucr> - Drug arrest statistics.
- Bureau of Justice Statistics (BJS). "Drug and Crime Facts." Updated periodically. <https://bjs.ojp.gov/drugs-and-crime-facts> - Criminal justice data on drug offenses.

### Congressional Reports

- Congressional Research Service. "Drug Enforcement in the United States: History, Policy, and Trends." CRS Report R43749 (2023). <https://crsreports.congress.gov/product/pdf/R/R43749> - Overview of federal drug enforcement.
- Government Accountability Office. "Drug Misuse: Most States Have Good Samaritan Laws and Research Indicates They May Have Positive Effects." GAO-21-248 (2021). <https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-21-248> - Analysis of Good Samaritan law effectiveness.
- Congressional Budget Office. "The Federal Budget in Fiscal Year 2024." (2024). <https://www.cbo.gov> - Budget context for drug policy spending.

### Agency Data Portals

- CDC WONDER. <https://wonder.cdc.gov> - Detailed mortality data including drug overdose deaths by substance, state, and demographic group.
- SAMHSA N-SSATS (National Survey of Substance Abuse Treatment Services). <https://www.samhsa.gov/data/data-we-collect/n-ssats-national-survey-substance-abuse-treatment-services> - Treatment facility data.

## Books

- Alexander, M. *The New Jim Crow: Mass Incarceration in the Age of Colorblindness*. The New Press, 2010. - Foundational text on racial disparities in drug enforcement.
- Hari, J. *Chasing the Scream: The First and Last Days of the War on Drugs*. Bloomsbury, 2015. - Journalistic account of drug war history and reform movements worldwide.
- Hart, C. *Drug Use for Grown-Ups: Chasing Liberty in the Land of Fear*. Penguin Press, 2021. - Neuroscientist's argument for decriminalization based on personal autonomy and scientific evidence.
- Courtwright, D.T. *Dark Paradise: A History of Opiate Addiction in America*. Harvard University Press, 2001. - Historical analysis of opiate use and regulation in the United States.
- Baum, D. *Smoke and Mirrors: The War on Drugs and the Politics of Failure*. Little, Brown, 1996. - Political history of the War on Drugs.
- Maia, S. *Drug Decriminalization in Portugal: Setting the Record Straight*. Open Society Foundations, 2011. - Detailed analysis of Portuguese implementation and outcomes.
- Provine, D.M. *Unequal Under Law: Race in the War on Drugs*. University of Chicago Press, 2007. - Academic analysis of racial dimensions of drug enforcement.

## Legal Resources

### Case Law

- *Robinson v. California*, 370 U.S. 660 (1962). - Supreme Court held that criminalizing the status of addiction violates the Eighth Amendment's prohibition on cruel and unusual punishment.
- *State v. Blake*, 197 Wn.2d 170 (2021). - Washington Supreme Court struck down the state felony drug possession statute for lacking a mens rea requirement.
- *Gonzales v. Raich*, 545 U.S. 1 (2005). - Supreme Court upheld federal authority to prohibit marijuana under the Commerce Clause, even in states where medical use was legal.
- *City of Grants Pass v. Johnson*, 603 U.S. ___ (2024). - Supreme Court upheld enforcement of anti-camping ordinances, relevant to public drug use enforcement context.
- *Safehouse v. DOJ*, 985 F.3d 225 (3d Cir. 2021). - Third Circuit held that the operation of a supervised injection site would violate the federal "crack house statute" (21 U.S.C. Section 856).

### Statutory References

- 21 U.S.C. Section 844 - Federal drug possession penalties (simple possession misdemeanor)
- 21 U.S.C. Section 841 - Drug manufacturing and distribution penalties
- 21 U.S.C. Section 812 - Controlled substance scheduling criteria
- 21 U.S.C. Section 856 - "Crack house statute" (maintaining drug-involved premises)
- 18 U.S.C. Section 983 - Civil asset forfeiture procedures
- Oregon Measure 110 (2020) / Oregon HB 4002 (2024) - State decriminalization and recriminalization
- Portugal Law 30/2000 - Portuguese drug decriminalization statute
- Czech Republic Government Order No. 467/2009 - Substance-specific possession thresholds
