# Drug Decriminalization: Root Causes

## Systemic Analysis

The continued criminalization of drug possession in the United States -- despite decades of evidence that it fails to reduce drug use, disproportionately harms communities of color, and diverts resources from effective treatment -- is not the result of a single failure. It is the product of several interlocking structural, institutional, political, and cultural forces. These include the political economy of the criminal justice system, the moral framing of drug use, institutional inertia, racial dynamics, federalism constraints, and the genuine complexity of implementation.

## Structural and Institutional Causes

### The Criminal Justice System as Default Response

The United States has built an enormous institutional infrastructure around drug enforcement. Approximately 1.3 million drug possession arrests per year (FBI UCR, 2023) flow through a system of police departments, prosecutors' offices, courts, jails, prisons, and probation agencies that collectively employ millions of people and consume tens of billions of dollars annually. This infrastructure creates path dependency: once built, the criminal justice apparatus generates its own demand for continued activity.

**Evidence**: Federal, state, and local governments spend approximately $47 billion annually on drug incarceration alone (Vera Institute, 2023). The Drug Enforcement Administration employs over 10,000 personnel with an annual budget of $3.3 billion (FY2024). State and local drug enforcement units, drug courts, drug testing companies, bail bond agents, and prison phone service providers all depend on the continued criminalization of drug possession.

### Absence of Treatment Infrastructure

The U.S. treatment system is severely underdeveloped relative to need. With 48.7 million Americans experiencing a substance use disorder and only 24% receiving any treatment (SAMHSA NSDUH, 2024), the criminal justice system functions as the de facto response to addiction by default -- not because it works, but because treatment alternatives are unavailable. Oregon's experience demonstrated this vividly: decriminalization without treatment capacity produced public drug use without pathways to recovery.

**Evidence**: The average wait time for publicly funded drug treatment is 25 days (NSDUH supplement, 2023). Only 63% of treatment facilities accept Medicaid (SAMHSA N-SSATS, 2023). Rural areas in particular lack treatment infrastructure, with many counties having zero buprenorphine providers. As of 2024, approximately 400 counties (13% of all U.S. counties) had no buprenorphine provider at all, and 2,400 counties (77%) had no opioid treatment program (methadone clinic) (SAMHSA Behavioral Health Mapping, 2024).

The treatment workforce shortage compounds the infrastructure problem. The addiction medicine field lacks an estimated 50,000 counselors and therapists nationally. Average annual turnover at treatment facilities exceeds 30%, driven by low wages ($35,000-45,000 median for addiction counselors vs. $55,000 for clinical social workers), high caseloads, and burnout (BLS, 2024). Even where treatment facilities exist, they frequently lack capacity to serve the population that would be diverted under decriminalization. This creates a structural Catch-22: treatment infrastructure cannot be built without funding, but funding requires political support that depends on demonstrating that alternatives to criminalization work -- which requires treatment infrastructure.

### Fragmented Healthcare System

Unlike Portugal, which decriminalized within a universal healthcare system where every citizen has access to treatment as a right, the United States has a patchwork of public and private insurance, Medicaid (in states that have expanded it), and significant uninsured populations. As of 2024, 10 states had not expanded Medicaid under the Affordable Care Act, leaving millions of low-income adults -- disproportionately people with substance use disorders -- without insurance coverage for treatment. Even in expansion states, Medicaid reimbursement rates for SUD treatment are often too low to sustain quality programs. Private insurance, while required to cover SUD treatment at parity with other medical conditions (Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act, 2008), frequently imposes prior authorization requirements, limited provider networks, and treatment day caps that functionally restrict access.

**Evidence**: A 2023 study in *Health Affairs* found that Medicaid expansion states had 30% higher rates of SUD treatment utilization than non-expansion states. Among the uninsured, only 7% of individuals with SUD received any treatment, compared to 28% of those with private insurance (SAMHSA NSDUH, 2024).

### Federal-State Misalignment

The Controlled Substances Act of 1970 establishes federal drug scheduling and penalties, but approximately 95% of drug arrests occur at the state and local level. This creates a fragmented system in which federal policy sets the framework of prohibition while state and local enforcement determines who is actually arrested and prosecuted. States that wish to decriminalize face the structural constraint that their actions cannot override federal law, creating legal uncertainty for individuals, businesses, and government agencies.

**Evidence**: Oregon's Measure 110 decriminalized drug possession under state law, but possession remained a federal offense. This created confusion among law enforcement, service providers, and the public about the legal status of drug possession.

## Political Economy

### Who Benefits from the Status Quo

| Actor | Benefit | Economic Value | Incentive to Block Reform |
|-------|---------|----------------|---------------------------|
| Private prison companies (CoreCivic, GEO Group) | Revenue from housing drug offenders | ~$4 billion combined annual revenue (FY2023) | Drug inmates represent significant share of population; decriminalization reduces census |
| Law enforcement agencies | Drug enforcement justifies budgets, equipment, personnel | Federal Byrne JAG grants ($442 million FY2024) tied to drug enforcement metrics | Loss of civil asset forfeiture revenue; reduced justification for staffing levels |
| Police unions | Drug arrests are high-volume, low-risk enforcement activity | Union dues from large officer complements | Decriminalization reduces officer demand and enforcement discretion |
| Bail bond industry | Drug possession charges generate bail revenue | ~$14 billion annual industry | Decriminalization eliminates arrest and pre-trial detention for possession |
| Drug testing companies | Workplace, court-ordered, and probation drug testing | ~$5 billion annual market | Decriminalization reduces court-ordered testing |
| Prosecutors' offices | Drug cases are high-volume, easily resolved through plea bargains | N/A (institutional prestige and political capital) | Drug cases provide conviction statistics used in reelection campaigns |
| Pharmaceutical companies | Criminalization of competitors (illicit drugs) maintains prescription monopoly | Opioid market: ~$19 billion annually | Legal substitutes for illicit drugs threaten market share |

### Perverse Incentives

The current system contains multiple incentive misalignments that perpetuate criminalization:

- **Civil asset forfeiture**: Law enforcement agencies can seize cash and property suspected of involvement in drug offenses without a criminal conviction. The DOJ's Assets Forfeiture Fund held $4.3 billion in net assets in FY2023, creating a direct financial incentive for drug enforcement. A 2020 Institute for Justice study found that the majority of cash seizures involved amounts under $1,000, suggesting that forfeiture primarily targets low-level users and small-time dealers rather than major trafficking operations.
- **Arrest metrics**: Police performance is often measured by arrest volume and clearance rates. Drug possession arrests are relatively easy to make -- they require minimal investigation, involve compliant evidence (the drugs themselves), and pose less physical risk to officers than violent crime enforcement -- and inflate these statistics, disincentivizing a shift to non-arrest responses. A Police Executive Research Forum (PERF) study (2020) found that departments using arrest-based performance metrics had 40% higher drug arrest rates than those using outcome-based metrics.
- **Plea bargain efficiency**: Drug possession cases are typically resolved through plea bargains, allowing prosecutors to process high volumes of cases with minimal trial resources. In many jurisdictions, 95% or more of drug possession cases are resolved by guilty plea within weeks. This efficiency would be lost under decriminalization, creating institutional resistance from prosecutors' offices whose workload management depends on drug case volume.
- **Federal grant incentives**: Programs like the Edward Byrne Memorial Justice Assistance Grant ($442 million, FY2024) and the High Intensity Drug Trafficking Areas (HIDTA) program ($309 million, FY2024) tie federal funding to drug enforcement activity, rewarding states and localities for maintaining criminalization. Grant applications typically require demonstration of enforcement activity, creating a bureaucratic incentive structure that favors arrests over treatment.
- **Political incentive misalignment**: Elected prosecutors and sheriffs -- who run for reelection in most states -- have political incentives to demonstrate "toughness" through conviction statistics and sentencing outcomes. A 2019 study in the *Journal of Law and Economics* found that prosecutors facing reelection increased drug prosecution rates by 12% in the year before elections compared to non-election years.

## Political and Cultural Causes

### Moral Framing of Drug Use

American political culture has historically treated drug use as a moral failing rather than a health condition. This framing -- rooted in Protestant temperance traditions, amplified by War on Drugs rhetoric, and reinforced by media portrayals -- creates a political environment in which punishment feels intuitively appropriate and medical responses feel like "letting people off."

**Evidence**: In a 2023 Pew Research poll, 34% of Americans still said drug possession should always be treated as a crime. The phrase "tough on crime" remains a politically potent frame, and candidates who support decriminalization risk being characterized as "soft on drugs" -- a label that has derailed political careers.

### Racial Dynamics

The War on Drugs was designed, at least in part, as a tool of racial control. Because communities of color bear the disproportionate burden of drug enforcement (Black Americans are 3.73x more likely to be arrested for cannabis possession per the ACLU, 2020), the political costs of criminalization fall primarily on populations with less political power. White suburban voters -- who are more politically influential in most electoral systems -- experience drug enforcement less directly, reducing their motivation to demand reform.

**Evidence**: The crack-powder cocaine sentencing disparity (100:1 from 1986 to 2010, 18:1 thereafter) encoded racial disparities into law. Crack, used primarily in Black communities, carried penalties 100 times more severe than powder cocaine, used primarily by white individuals, for the same weight of substance.

### Fear of the "Sending a Message" Reversal

Politicians across the spectrum fear that supporting decriminalization will be interpreted as endorsing drug use. The "sending the wrong message" argument -- that decriminalizing possession signals societal approval -- persists despite evidence from Portugal and other countries that decriminalization does not increase drug use. This fear is amplified during overdose crises, when politicians feel pressure to appear to be "doing something" about drugs, and enforcement is more visible and politically legible than treatment investment. The "message" argument operates on an intuitive level that is difficult to counter with data: the idea that laws reflect and shape social norms is deeply embedded in American political culture.

**Evidence**: Oregon's recriminalization in 2024 was explicitly framed by legislators as "sending a message" that drug use is unacceptable, despite the lack of evidence that the message sent by Measure 110 had increased drug use rates in Oregon relative to other states. Oregon's drug use rates increased during Measure 110's tenure, but so did national rates -- and Oregon's increase was not statistically different from the national trend (Oregon Health Authority, 2023).

### "Tough on Crime" Electoral Dynamics

The political calculus around drug policy is heavily influenced by electoral dynamics. Candidates who support decriminalization face attack ads characterizing them as "soft on drugs" or "pro-crime." This is particularly powerful in competitive districts and in primary elections where turnout skews older and more conservative.

**Evidence**: In the 2022 and 2024 election cycles, opposition to drug decriminalization was used as a campaign weapon against reform candidates in multiple states. San Francisco DA Chesa Boudin's 2022 recall campaign centered heavily on drug policy. Los Angeles DA George Gascon's 2024 defeat was driven in part by criticism of his non-prosecution policies for drug possession. These electoral outcomes create a chilling effect: even politicians who privately support reform may avoid the issue publicly.

### Federalism as Obstacle

The distribution of drug enforcement authority between federal, state, and local levels creates structural barriers to reform. State decriminalization cannot override federal law, meaning that a person whose possession is a civil violation under state law may still be subject to federal prosecution (however rare). This legal uncertainty discourages comprehensive state reform and creates confusion among law enforcement, service providers, and the public. Additionally, immigration law ties drug convictions to deportation risk, meaning that non-citizens face federal immigration consequences for state-level drug conduct regardless of state decriminalization.

**Evidence**: Oregon's Measure 110 generated confusion among law enforcement agencies about how to handle drug encounters. Some officers continued to arrest under federal authority; others followed state law; others stopped engaging with drug-related situations entirely. The lack of consistent enforcement during this period contributed to the perception of lawlessness that fueled backlash.

## Institutional Inertia

### The Controlled Substances Act Framework

The CSA has been the foundation of U.S. drug policy for over 50 years. It establishes five schedules of controlled substances, with Schedule I (including heroin, LSD, cannabis, and psilocybin) defined as having "high potential for abuse" and "no currently accepted medical use." This framework is remarkably resistant to change because it is embedded in thousands of federal and state statutes, regulations, and institutional practices. The CSA is referenced in immigration law (drug convictions trigger deportation under INA Section 237(a)(2)(B)), education law (drug offenses historically affected federal student aid eligibility), housing law (drug convictions can bar access to public housing under HUD regulations), and employment law (federal contractors may be required to maintain drug-free workplaces under 41 U.S.C. Section 8101). Decriminalization requires not just a policy shift but a structural reorganization of how the government classifies, regulates, and responds to controlled substances across multiple legal domains.

### Professional and Institutional Cultures

Law enforcement agencies, prosecutors' offices, and judicial systems have developed professional cultures organized around drug enforcement. Officers are trained to detect and arrest drug offenders; prosecutors specialize in drug cases; judges manage drug dockets. Police academies devote significant training hours to drug identification, field testing, and enforcement tactics. DEA and state drug task forces have created specialized career tracks for drug enforcement that have no equivalent in a decriminalized system. Decriminalization asks these professionals to abandon core competencies and institutional identities.

**Evidence**: The National Association of Police Organizations, the National District Attorneys Association, and the National Sheriffs' Association have all opposed comprehensive drug decriminalization. The DEA itself has opposed rescheduling of cannabis and has publicly stated that decriminalization "sends the wrong message." These positions reflect not only ideological commitments but institutional self-preservation -- agencies whose mission is drug enforcement have a structural interest in maintaining the enforcement framework.

### Regulatory Complexity

The federal scheduling system interacts with international treaty obligations under the 1961 Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs, the 1971 Convention on Psychotropic Substances, and the 1988 Convention Against Illicit Traffic. The United States has historically been a driving force behind these treaties, which require signatory nations to maintain criminal penalties for drug trafficking and, in some interpretations, for personal possession. While international bodies have increasingly acknowledged that decriminalization of personal possession is compatible with treaty obligations (INCB interpretive guidance, 2015), treaty constraints provide a legal and diplomatic argument against comprehensive reform.

## Media and Information Environment

### Sensationalism and Narrative Framing

Media coverage of drug policy has historically emphasized disorder, crime, and individual tragedy over systemic analysis and evidence. The coverage pattern follows a predictable arc: sensationalized stories about drug-related crime and disorder generate public fear, which creates political pressure for enforcement responses, which generates arrests and prosecutions that become the next cycle's stories. This "drug scare" dynamic has been documented by scholars from Craig Reinarman's work on crack in the 1980s to contemporary analyses of fentanyl coverage.

**Evidence**: A 2023 analysis by the Columbia Journalism Review found that news coverage of Oregon's Measure 110 focused overwhelmingly on public drug use, homelessness, and disorder (82% of stories) rather than on treatment outcomes, implementation challenges, or the evidence base for decriminalization (18% of stories). Coverage of Portugal's decriminalization, by contrast, received minimal attention in U.S. media. This asymmetry means that the American public is far more familiar with a single implementation failure than with 20+ years of successful decriminalization in Portugal.

### Social Media Amplification

Social media amplifies the disorder narrative through viral videos and images of public drug use, particularly from cities like Portland, San Francisco, and Philadelphia. These images create a perception that decriminalization has produced chaos, even when the underlying causes are more complex (homelessness, the fentanyl supply, pandemic-related service disruptions). A 2024 study by the Shorenstein Center at Harvard found that social media posts about urban drug use received 5x more engagement than posts about treatment outcomes or policy evidence, creating an information environment heavily skewed toward enforcement-oriented responses.

## Causal Chain

```text
Moral framing of drug use + racial politics
    ↓
War on Drugs creates massive criminal justice infrastructure
    ↓
Infrastructure generates economic interests (private prisons, police budgets, bail bonds)
    ↓
Economic interests lobby to maintain criminalization
    ↓
Treatment infrastructure remains underfunded (resources directed to enforcement)
    ↓
Without treatment alternatives, criminal justice remains the default response
    ↓
Criminal records trap individuals in cycles of poverty and substance use
    ↓
Continued drug use and visible public disorder reinforce moral framing
    ↓
[Cycle reinforces itself]
```

### Reinforcing Feedback Loops

The causal chain above contains several self-reinforcing loops:

**Loop 1 -- The Criminalization-Poverty Cycle**: Drug arrest leads to criminal record. Criminal record leads to employment and housing barriers. Employment and housing barriers lead to poverty and instability. Poverty and instability increase risk of continued drug use. Continued drug use leads to further drug arrests. A 2023 study in *Criminology and Public Policy* estimated that a drug possession arrest reduces lifetime earnings by $120,000 on average, with the effect concentrated in the first five years post-arrest.

**Loop 2 -- The Resource Diversion Cycle**: Drug enforcement consumes resources. Resource allocation to enforcement reduces funding available for treatment. Underfunded treatment means criminal justice remains the only response. Continued criminal justice response justifies continued enforcement budgets. Federal drug control spending allocates approximately 51% to enforcement and 49% to demand reduction (ONDCP, FY2025), despite evidence that demand reduction produces higher returns per dollar invested.

**Loop 3 -- The Political Fear Cycle**: Visible public drug use generates media coverage. Media coverage increases public fear. Public fear creates political pressure for enforcement. Enforcement (arrest, removal from public spaces) temporarily reduces visibility. Underlying drug use continues. Drug use resurfaces in public view. Coverage and fear cycle repeat. This loop explains why enforcement-oriented responses feel politically satisfying despite their lack of long-term effectiveness.

**Loop 4 -- The Stigma-Isolation Cycle**: Moral framing of drug use creates stigma. Stigma deters people from seeking treatment. Untreated substance use disorders worsen over time. Worsening addiction reinforces public perception of drug users as morally deficient. A 2023 Johns Hopkins study found that public stigma toward people with substance use disorders exceeds stigma toward people with mental illness, and that this stigma is a primary barrier to treatment-seeking.

## Underlying Social and Economic Causes

### The Demand Side: Why People Use Drugs

The continued criminalization of drug use rests implicitly on the premise that enforcement can reduce demand. But the root causes of drug use and addiction -- which drive the demand that drug policy must address -- are largely social and economic, not legal.

| Factor | Mechanism | Evidence |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| Economic despair and inequality | Communities with high unemployment, stagnant wages, and limited economic opportunity have higher rates of substance use disorder | Counties in the top quartile of poverty have 2.3x higher overdose death rates than bottom quartile (CDC, 2023) |
| Adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) | Trauma, abuse, neglect, and household dysfunction in childhood dramatically increase the risk of later substance use | Adults with 4+ ACEs are 7x more likely to develop alcohol use disorder and 4.7x more likely to use illicit drugs (Felitti et al., CDC-Kaiser ACE Study, 1998) |
| Mental health co-occurrence | Untreated mental illness -- depression, anxiety, PTSD, bipolar disorder -- drives self-medication with drugs and alcohol | 38% of Americans with SUD also have a mental health disorder; among those with serious mental illness, 25% also have SUD (SAMHSA NSDUH, 2024) |
| Social isolation and disconnection | Loss of community bonds, family breakdown, and social isolation increase vulnerability to addiction | The "deaths of despair" framework (Case & Deaton, 2015, 2020) identifies rising mortality from drugs, alcohol, and suicide in communities experiencing social fragmentation |
| Chronic pain | Inadequate access to effective, non-addictive pain management drives opioid dependence | An estimated 50 million Americans live with chronic pain; 20 million have high-impact chronic pain (CDC, 2023) |
| Housing instability | Homelessness and housing insecurity make recovery extremely difficult and increase exposure to drug use environments | People experiencing homelessness have overdose rates 16-38x higher than housed populations (NHCHC, 2023) |

Criminal penalties do not address any of these underlying causes. Arresting someone for drug possession does not resolve the trauma, poverty, mental illness, or social disconnection that drives their drug use. It does, however, impose additional burdens -- a criminal record, loss of housing eligibility, loss of employment -- that exacerbate the very conditions that caused the drug use.

### Geographic Concentration of Harm

Drug criminalization and drug harm are not distributed evenly across the country. They are concentrated in specific communities:

- **Appalachian region**: West Virginia, Kentucky, Ohio, and Tennessee have the highest per-capita overdose death rates in the nation, driven by prescription opioid addiction transitioning to heroin and fentanyl in communities devastated by coal industry decline and deindustrialization.
- **Urban communities of color**: Black and Latino neighborhoods in cities like Baltimore, Philadelphia, Chicago, and St. Louis experience both the highest drug arrest rates and the highest overdose rates, compounded by decades of disinvestment and concentrated poverty.
- **Rural America**: Rural counties have fewer treatment facilities, fewer harm reduction services, and higher per-capita overdose rates than urban areas. The lack of public transportation in rural areas makes accessing existing treatment difficult even where it nominally exists.
- **Native American communities**: American Indian/Alaska Native populations have the highest overdose death rate of any racial/ethnic group (56.1 per 100,000 in 2023, CDC WONDER), compounded by chronic underfunding of the Indian Health Service and jurisdictional complexities on tribal lands.

This geographic concentration means that the costs of continued criminalization -- and the benefits of decriminalization -- are not abstract national statistics but are experienced acutely by specific communities. It also means that decriminalization must be designed with geographic variation in mind, ensuring that treatment infrastructure reaches the communities where it is needed most.

## Why Reform Has Failed

### Oregon's Measure 110 Failures

Oregon's 2020 decriminalization and 2024 reversal provides the clearest case study of reform failure. The core failures were:

1. **Implementation lag**: Cannabis tax revenue designated for treatment was delayed 18+ months by bureaucratic processes, leaving decriminalization in place without services
2. **Civil citation design failure**: The $100 citation with a phone call opt-out produced a near-zero engagement rate (~1% called the health assessment hotline)
3. **No accountability mechanism**: Without either criminal consequences or effective civil alternatives, there was no mechanism to connect individuals to treatment
4. **Political timing**: The measure took effect during the fentanyl surge, causing rising overdose deaths to be attributed (incorrectly but understandably) to decriminalization

### Broader Reform Challenges

- **Bipartisan coalitions are fragile**: The First Step Act coalition of progressives and libertarian-leaning conservatives holds on sentencing reform but fragments on decriminalization. Libertarians support decriminalization on individual liberty grounds but may not support the treatment infrastructure spending that makes it work. Progressives support both but may prioritize racial equity framing that alienates moderate and conservative allies.
- **Public opinion is ahead of legislators on principle but behind on specifics**: 66% support ending arrest for drug possession (Pew, 2024), but support drops 20-30 percentage points when specific drugs (heroin, methamphetamine, fentanyl) are named. This "substance specificity gap" means that general support for decriminalization does not translate into specific legislative majorities.
- **Media coverage amplifies disorder**: Stories of public drug use and open-air drug markets dominate coverage, while the quieter benefits of treatment expansion receive less attention. Visual media (video, photographs) is particularly powerful in shaping perceptions, and the visual imagery of public drug use is inherently more compelling than the visual imagery of a treatment clinic.
- **The fentanyl timing problem**: The most dangerous period in American drug history -- 107,543 overdose deaths in 2023, driven largely by illicitly manufactured fentanyl -- coincides with the peak of decriminalization advocacy. This timing makes it politically difficult to argue for reducing enforcement even when the evidence strongly supports doing so. Politicians who support decriminalization during a lethal overdose crisis face the accusation that they are "giving up" on drug enforcement.
- **Implementation capacity varies dramatically**: Even if federal decriminalization were enacted, state and local capacity to implement civil citation systems, health assessment panels, and treatment-on-demand varies enormously. States with robust Medicaid programs, treatment infrastructure, and administrative capacity (e.g., Massachusetts, California, Oregon) could implement quickly; states without these resources (e.g., Mississippi, West Virginia, rural areas) would face years of infrastructure buildout before decriminalization could function as intended.

## Why the Status Quo Persists: Summary of Root Causes

The persistence of drug criminalization despite overwhelming evidence of its failure is the product of mutually reinforcing structural factors:

| Category | Root Cause | Self-Reinforcing Mechanism |
|----------|-----------|---------------------------|
| Institutional | Criminal justice infrastructure built over 50 years | Infrastructure generates employment, budgets, and political constituencies that resist reform |
| Economic | Financial interests in enforcement (forfeiture, private prisons, bail, testing) | Financial beneficiaries lobby to maintain the status quo; spending on lobbying exceeds reform advocacy by 3:1 |
| Political | Moral framing of drug use as individual failure | "Tough on crime" rhetoric is politically safer than evidence-based nuance |
| Racial | Disparate enforcement concentrated in communities with less political power | Those most harmed by criminalization have the least political voice to change it |
| Cultural | Stigma toward people who use drugs | Stigma deters treatment-seeking and reduces public sympathy for reform |
| Structural | Absence of treatment infrastructure | Without treatment alternatives, the criminal justice system is the only available response |
| Legal | Federal CSA framework embedded in thousands of statutes | Changing one law requires cascading changes across multiple legal domains |
| Media | Sensationalized coverage amplifies disorder narratives | Enforcement looks like "doing something"; treatment is less visible and photogenic |
| International | Treaty obligations interpreted as requiring criminal penalties | Provides legal and diplomatic cover for maintaining criminalization |
| Temporal | Fentanyl crisis timing | The deadliest drug supply in history coincides with peak decriminalization advocacy, making reform seem reckless |

No single intervention can break this network of causes. Effective reform requires a multi-pronged strategy that simultaneously builds treatment infrastructure, shifts political incentives, reframes public discourse, and creates evidence through well-designed pilot programs. The challenge is not a lack of evidence or policy design -- it is the difficulty of overcoming deeply embedded institutional, economic, and cultural resistance to change.

### The Generational Transmission Problem

The root causes of continued criminalization are not static; they reproduce themselves across generations. Children who grow up in communities heavily policed for drug offenses internalize both the stigma of drug use and the normalization of enforcement as the primary governmental response to addiction. Parents with drug convictions transmit economic disadvantage -- reduced earnings, housing instability, limited educational access -- to their children, increasing the next generation's risk factors for substance use. A 2022 study in *Pediatrics* found that children of parents with drug convictions had 2.4 times the rate of adverse childhood experiences compared to peers, and ACEs are themselves among the strongest predictors of future substance use disorder. This intergenerational cycle means that the harms of criminalization compound over time: each decade of the War on Drugs has produced not only direct victims but a larger population of people structurally vulnerable to the next generation's drug crisis.

## Comparison: Why Portugal Succeeded Where the U.S. Has Not

The contrast between Portugal's sustained success and the U.S.'s repeated reform failures illuminates the structural conditions necessary for decriminalization to work.

| Factor | Portugal (2001) | United States (2020-2024) |
|--------|----------------|--------------------------|
| Healthcare system | Universal public healthcare; treatment accessible to all citizens | Fragmented; 26 million uninsured; Medicaid varies by state |
| Treatment infrastructure at time of reform | Pre-existing network expanded simultaneously with decriminalization | Oregon treatment infrastructure delayed 18+ months; national treatment gap of 76% |
| Population and governance | 10.3 million; centralized unitary state | 330 million; 50 states with independent criminal justice systems |
| Civil engagement mechanism | Mandatory in-person Dissuasion Commission appearance | Oregon: $100 fine with optional phone call (1% engagement) |
| Political support for public health approach | Broad consensus following national drug crisis | Highly polarized; decriminalization coded as partisan issue |
| Drug supply context | Heroin-dominated; relatively stable supply | Fentanyl-dominated; unprecedented lethality and volatility |
| Implementation planning | Multi-year commission process; coordinated rollout | Ballot measure with implementation delegated to state agencies |
| Law enforcement integration | Police retrained as first responders in civil system | Oregon police confused about role; many disengaged entirely |

The comparison suggests that the root cause of U.S. reform failure is not that decriminalization is wrong in principle but that the structural preconditions for its success -- universal healthcare, treatment capacity, political consensus, effective civil engagement mechanisms, and coordinated implementation -- are absent in the United States and must be deliberately built before or alongside decriminalization. The root cause is not the policy; it is the system within which the policy must operate.

Portugal's experience also demonstrates a critical point about political sequencing: the country decriminalized after a national commission spent two years building expert and political consensus, during which treatment infrastructure was simultaneously expanded. In the United States, Oregon's ballot measure bypassed this consensus-building process and preceded infrastructure development, producing the opposite sequence -- policy change without institutional readiness. This sequencing failure is itself a root cause of reform failure, distinct from the policy design failures of Measure 110.

The structural comparison also extends to the role of stigma. Portugal's decriminalization was accompanied by sustained public education campaigns that reframed drug addiction as a health condition. The United States has undertaken no comparable effort. A 2023 Johns Hopkins survey found that 62% of Americans view addiction as at least partly a "moral failing," compared to 28% in Portugal. Without addressing the cultural substrate of stigma, even well-designed institutional reforms face political headwinds that can reverse progress, as Oregon demonstrated.
