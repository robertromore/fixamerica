# Drug Decriminalization: History

## Historical Development

The history of drug decriminalization in the United States is inseparable from the history of drug criminalization itself. Understanding why drugs were criminalized -- and who benefited from that criminalization -- is essential to understanding the contemporary push for decriminalization. The trajectory moves from a largely unregulated era through progressive-era moralism, mid-century racial politics, the War on Drugs, and the slow emergence of evidence-based alternatives.

---

### Pre-Prohibition Era (Before 1914): Open Markets and Patent Medicines

- **Unregulated access**: Throughout the 19th century, opiates, cocaine, cannabis, and other substances were legally available in the United States. Opium-based medicines were sold in pharmacies, general stores, and by mail order. The Sears, Roebuck catalog advertised syringes and cocaine. Coca-Cola contained cocaine until 1903. Mrs. Winslow's Soothing Syrup, marketed for teething infants, contained morphine.
- **Widespread use**: An estimated 300,000 Americans were addicted to opiates by 1900, primarily middle-class white women who used patent medicines containing opium or morphine (Courtwright, *Dark Paradise*, 2001). The typical opiate user of this era bore no resemblance to the racialized stereotype that would later drive criminalization -- she was a white, middle-class housewife who had become dependent on medications prescribed or recommended by physicians.
- **Early racial targeting**: The first drug restrictions were explicitly racial. San Francisco banned opium dens in 1875, targeting Chinese immigrants while leaving opium-based patent medicines (used primarily by white Americans) legal. Southern states criminalized cocaine in the early 1900s, citing fabricated fears of Black men under the influence -- the *New York Times* published a 1914 article headlined "Negro Cocaine 'Fiends' Are a New Southern Menace." Cannabis prohibition in the Southwest targeted Mexican immigrants, with newspapers using the Spanish word "marihuana" to associate the drug with foreigners.
- **Medical profession's role**: The American Medical Association began advocating for physician control of drug dispensing, framing addiction as a medical concern but supporting regulatory rather than criminal approaches. Several states established municipal clinics that provided opiates to addicted individuals under medical supervision -- an early form of harm reduction that would be shut down by federal enforcement after 1914.
- **Pure Food and Drug Act (1906)**: Required labeling of patent medicines containing opiates, cocaine, alcohol, and cannabis. This was a transparency regulation, not a prohibition -- consumers could still purchase these substances, but manufacturers had to disclose their contents.

### The Criminalization Era (1914-1970): From Regulation to Prohibition

- **Harrison Narcotics Tax Act (1914)**: Technically a tax and registration measure, the Harrison Act was interpreted by Treasury Department enforcement agents and courts as effectively prohibiting physicians from prescribing opiates to addicts for maintenance. The Supreme Court upheld this interpretation in *Webb v. United States* (1919) and *Jin Fuey Moy v. United States* (1920). Between 1919 and 1938, approximately 25,000 physicians were prosecuted under the Act, and an estimated 3,000 served prison sentences for prescribing opiates to addicted patients (King, *The Drug Hang-Up*, 1972). Municipal maintenance clinics -- which had provided regulated opiate access to approximately 15,000 patients -- were closed under federal pressure by 1925.
- **Marihuana Tax Act (1937)**: Federal Bureau of Narcotics Commissioner Harry Anslinger led a campaign to criminalize cannabis, relying on racial stereotypes and fabricated claims. The Act effectively prohibited cannabis through a prohibitive tax structure. Anslinger's Congressional testimony included claims that cannabis caused interracial socializing, insanity, and murder. The American Medical Association opposed the Act, with legislative counsel Dr. William Woodward testifying that the bill was unnecessary and based on sensationalism rather than science.
- **Uniform State Narcotic Drug Act (1932-1940s)**: The Federal Bureau of Narcotics pressured states to adopt uniform drug control legislation, creating the patchwork of state criminal statutes that persists today. By 1937, every state had enacted some form of cannabis prohibition.
- **Boggs Act (1951) and Narcotics Control Act (1956)**: Introduced mandatory minimum sentences for drug offenses, including first-offense possession. The 1956 Act prescribed a minimum of 2 years for first-offense marijuana possession and permitted the death penalty for selling heroin to a minor. These laws represented the first wave of mandatory minimums, a mechanism that would be dramatically expanded in the 1980s.
- **1960s counter-culture**: The widespread use of marijuana and psychedelics by the anti-war movement and counterculture created a political dynamic in which drugs became associated with political dissent and social upheaval. Timothy Leary's arrest for marijuana possession led to *Leary v. United States* (1969), in which the Supreme Court struck down the Marihuana Tax Act as unconstitutional, necessitating the Controlled Substances Act of 1970.
- **Controlled Substances Act (1970)**: Replaced the patchwork of prior drug laws with a comprehensive scheduling system. Schedule I -- defined as having "high potential for abuse" and "no currently accepted medical use" -- included heroin, LSD, and cannabis. The Act established the framework that still governs U.S. drug policy.
- **Nixon and racial politics**: In a 1994 interview published by Harper's Magazine in 2016, former Nixon domestic policy advisor John Ehrlichman stated: "The Nixon campaign in 1968, and the Nixon White House after that, had two enemies: the antiwar left and black people... We knew we couldn't make it illegal to be either against the war or black, but by getting the public to associate the hippies with marijuana and blacks with heroin, and then criminalizing both heavily, we could disrupt those communities."

### The War on Drugs (1971-2000): Escalation and Mass Incarceration

- **Nixon declares war (1971)**: President Nixon declared drug abuse "public enemy number one" and created the Special Action Office for Drug Abuse Prevention. Initially, Nixon's approach included significant treatment funding -- the 1971 budget allocated two-thirds to treatment and one-third to enforcement. Nixon expanded methadone maintenance programs, which had been pioneered by Drs. Vincent Dole and Marie Nyswander at Rockefeller University in the 1960s. This balance shifted rapidly under subsequent administrations.
- **Creation of the DEA (1973)**: The Drug Enforcement Administration consolidated federal drug enforcement into a single agency with 2,775 agents and a budget of $75 million (approximately $530 million in 2024 dollars). The DEA merged the Bureau of Narcotics and Dangerous Drugs, the Office of Drug Abuse Law Enforcement, and several other federal enforcement entities.
- **Carter administration (1977-1981)**: President Carter endorsed federal cannabis decriminalization in 1977, stating, "Penalties against possession of a drug should not be more damaging to an individual than the use of the drug itself." His drug policy advisor, Peter Bourne, supported harm reduction and treatment expansion. However, a scandal involving Bourne's prescribing practices and reports of cocaine use among White House staff derailed the initiative.
- **Reagan escalation (1981-1989)**: President Reagan and First Lady Nancy Reagan elevated the War on Drugs with the "Just Say No" campaign, which relied on fear-based messaging despite evidence that such approaches were ineffective at preventing drug use (D.A.R.E. program evaluations consistently found no lasting effect on drug use rates). Federal anti-drug spending increased from $1.5 billion (FY1981) to $6.6 billion (FY1989). The Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1986 created the 100:1 crack-powder cocaine sentencing disparity, mandating a 5-year minimum for 5 grams of crack (used disproportionately by Black Americans) versus 500 grams of powder cocaine. The 1988 Anti-Drug Abuse Act created the Office of National Drug Control Policy (ONDCP) and its director, the "Drug Czar."
- **Bush and Clinton escalation (1989-2000)**: President George H.W. Bush addressed the nation from the Oval Office in September 1989, holding up a bag of crack cocaine purchased near the White House. Federal drug control spending continued to rise, reaching $17.7 billion by FY2000 (ONDCP). President Clinton signed the 1994 Crime Bill, which included $9.7 billion for prison construction and expanded the federal death penalty to include drug-related offenses. Clinton's ONDCP director, Barry McCaffrey, opposed harm reduction and needle exchange funding.
- **Prison population explosion**: The number of Americans incarcerated for drug offenses rose from approximately 41,000 in 1980 to 458,000 in 2000 -- a more than tenfold increase (Sentencing Project, 2023). By 2000, drug offenders constituted approximately 20% of state prisoners and over 50% of federal prisoners. The United States became the world's largest jailer, with a total incarceration rate of 684 per 100,000 -- far exceeding any other industrialized nation.
- **1994 Crime Bill**: The Violent Crime Control and Law Enforcement Act expanded federal drug penalties and provided $9.7 billion for prison construction, further embedding criminalization. The bill received bipartisan support and was signed by President Clinton, who later acknowledged it "cast too wide a net" (Clinton, 2015).
- **Asset forfeiture expansion**: The Comprehensive Crime Control Act of 1984 allowed federal law enforcement to share seized assets with state and local agencies through "equitable sharing," creating powerful financial incentives for drug enforcement. By 2000, federal asset forfeiture funds exceeded $1 billion (DOJ Inspector General, 2002).
- **Drug courts emerge (1989)**: The first drug court was established in Miami-Dade County, Florida in 1989, offering treatment as an alternative to incarceration under judicial supervision. By 2000, approximately 700 drug courts operated nationwide, representing a partial acknowledgment that criminal punishment alone was insufficient (NADCP, 2000).
- **Harm reduction emerges in the U.S. (1988-1990s)**: The first needle exchange program in the United States was established by activist Jon Parker in New Haven, Connecticut in 1988, initially operating illegally. By the mid-1990s, dozens of syringe exchange programs operated, mostly in major cities, often in legal gray areas. The National Academy of Sciences recommended federal funding for needle exchange in 1995, finding that programs reduced HIV transmission without increasing drug use, but Congress maintained a ban on federal funding for syringe purchase.
- **Oregon decriminalizes cannabis (1973)**: In a counter-trend, Oregon became the first state to decriminalize cannabis possession in 1973, reducing possession of less than one ounce to a civil violation with a maximum $100 fine. Ten states followed by 1978 (Alaska, California, Colorado, Maine, Minnesota, Mississippi, Nebraska, New York, and Ohio), but the movement stalled during the Reagan era as "parent power" groups advocated for stricter enforcement. The National Academy of Sciences studied Oregon's decriminalization in 1982 and found no significant increase in cannabis use -- a finding that was politically ignored.
- **International developments**: The United Kingdom's Misuse of Drugs Act (1971) established a tiered classification system. The Netherlands formalized its cannabis tolerance policy in 1976. Italy decriminalized personal possession in 1975 through a referendum. These international experiments created reference points that would inform later U.S. debates.

### The First Wave of Reform (2000-2015): Cracks in the Consensus

- **Portugal decriminalizes (2001)**: Facing a severe heroin crisis -- with the highest rate of drug-related AIDS in the European Union and an estimated 1% of the population addicted to heroin -- Portugal decriminalized personal possession and use of all drugs, effective July 1, 2001. The decision was driven by a Commission for the National Strategy Against Drugs, appointed in 1998 and chaired by Dr. Jose de Sousa e Brito, which concluded that criminalization was counterproductive and recommended a health-centered approach. Trafficking remained criminal. The law (Law 30/2000) created 18 regional Dissuasion Commissions (CDTs) as the primary response mechanism. Each commission comprised three members -- a legal professional, a social worker, and a psychologist -- who assessed individuals and could impose sanctions, recommend treatment, or suspend proceedings. Simultaneously, Portugal dramatically expanded its treatment infrastructure, including methadone maintenance, therapeutic communities, and outreach programs. Initial results showed declining drug-related deaths, plummeting HIV rates, and stable or declining drug use among young people.
- **Czech Republic decriminalizes (2010)**: Following a period of de facto tolerance, the Czech Republic formally decriminalized possession of small amounts of all drugs through Government Order No. 467/2009, effective January 1, 2010. The Czech model used substance-specific quantity thresholds (10g cannabis, 1g cocaine, 1.5g heroin, 2g methamphetamine) below which possession was classified as a misdemeanor punishable by fine rather than imprisonment.
- **State sentencing reforms**: Beginning in the early 2000s, states including New York (2004 reform of the Rockefeller Drug Laws), California (Proposition 36, 2000; Proposition 47, 2014), and Texas (2007 diversion programs) began reducing penalties for drug possession.
- **New York reforms Rockefeller Drug Laws (2004, 2009)**: New York's Rockefeller Drug Laws, enacted in 1973, had imposed some of the harshest drug possession penalties in the nation -- up to 15 years for possession of 4 ounces of cocaine or heroin. After decades of advocacy, the legislature reduced mandatory minimums in 2004 and further reformed the laws in 2009, allowing judges to divert drug offenders to treatment and retroactively reducing sentences for incarcerated individuals.
- **Medical marijuana expansion**: Between 2000 and 2015, the number of states with medical marijuana laws grew from 8 to 23, gradually normalizing the concept that cannabis had legitimate medical uses and challenging Schedule I classification. California had been the first state to legalize medical marijuana by ballot initiative (Proposition 215, 1996).
- **Fair Sentencing Act (2010)**: Reduced the crack-powder cocaine sentencing disparity from 100:1 to 18:1, the first Congressional acknowledgment that the disparity was unjust. The original 100:1 ratio had been established in the 1986 Anti-Drug Abuse Act and resulted in Black defendants receiving sentences averaging 49% longer than white defendants for equivalent conduct (U.S. Sentencing Commission, 2010).
- **Colorado and Washington legalize (2012)**: The first two states to legalize recreational cannabis through ballot measures, fundamentally shifting the political landscape. The federal government, under Attorney General Holder's "Cole Memorandum" (2013), adopted a policy of non-interference with state cannabis legalization as long as states maintained regulatory frameworks.
- **Holder Memo (2013)**: Attorney General Eric Holder directed federal prosecutors to avoid charging mandatory minimums for low-level, non-violent drug offenders, signaling a shift in federal enforcement posture. The "Smart on Crime" initiative acknowledged for the first time from a sitting Attorney General that mandatory minimums for drug offenses were counterproductive.
- **California Proposition 47 (2014)**: Voters reclassified drug possession from a felony to a misdemeanor, the most significant state-level depenalization in U.S. history. The measure reduced California's prison population by approximately 10,000 inmates and generated an estimated $150 million in annual savings, which were redirected to treatment, mental health, and victim services. The Public Policy Institute of California (2018) found no measurable increase in crime attributable to the change.

### The Modern Decriminalization Movement (2016-Present): Breakthrough and Backlash

- **UNGASS 2016**: The United Nations General Assembly Special Session on drugs acknowledged for the first time that the global drug control framework needed to incorporate public health approaches, marking a shift from the enforcement-only consensus. Several Latin American leaders -- including the presidents of Colombia, Guatemala, and Mexico -- publicly criticized the War on Drugs and called for decriminalization. The UNGASS outcome document represented a compromise but opened space for national experimentation.
- **Cannabis legalization wave (2016-2024)**: Between 2016 and 2024, the number of states with legal recreational cannabis grew from 4 to 24 plus D.C. This mainstreaming of cannabis reform created political space for broader decriminalization discussions. Key milestones included California (2016, Proposition 64), Illinois (2019, first state to legalize through legislature), and Virginia (2021, first Southern state).
- **Prosecutorial non-enforcement emerges (2017-2022)**: A wave of reform-minded district attorneys -- including Larry Krasner (Philadelphia, 2017), Rachael Rollins (Boston, 2018), Chesa Boudin (San Francisco, 2019), George Gascon (Los Angeles, 2020), and Alvin Bragg (Manhattan, 2022) -- adopted policies declining to prosecute low-level drug possession cases, achieving de facto decriminalization through prosecutorial discretion. This approach produced rapid policy change but proved politically fragile, as several reform DAs faced recalls or electoral defeats.
- **First Step Act (2018)**: Bipartisan federal legislation signed by President Trump reduced mandatory minimums, expanded good-time credits, and made the Fair Sentencing Act retroactive. While focused on sentencing rather than decriminalization, it represented conservative engagement with criminal justice reform. The Act was supported by an unusual coalition including the Koch brothers' network, the ACLU, and the Trump White House.
- **Oregon Measure 110 (2020)**: Approved by 58.5% of voters in November 2020, Oregon became the first U.S. state to decriminalize personal possession of all drugs. The measure replaced criminal penalties with $100 civil citations and funded treatment through cannabis tax revenue. Key provisions included: reclassification of all personal possession to Class E violation (maximum $100 fine); creation of Behavioral Health Resource Networks (BHRNs) in each county; allocation of approximately $300 million over two years from cannabis tax; and establishment of a health assessment hotline as the primary engagement mechanism.
- **Washington *State v. Blake* (2021)**: The Washington Supreme Court struck down the state's felony drug possession statute as unconstitutional (it lacked a mens rea requirement), effectively decriminalizing drug possession for approximately 18 months before the legislature passed a new misdemeanor statute (SB 5536). The *Blake* decision was unusual -- an accidental decriminalization caused by a constitutional defect rather than policy design -- and the legislature's rapid recriminalization reflected the political difficulty of maintaining decriminalization without deliberate infrastructure building.
- **British Columbia pilot (2023-2024)**: Canada's British Columbia received a federal exemption from the Controlled Drugs and Substances Act in January 2023, decriminalizing personal possession of up to 2.5 grams of specified drugs. The pilot was the first jurisdiction-wide decriminalization in Canada. However, visible public drug use generated public backlash, and British Columbia requested re-prohibition of public drug consumption in May 2024, while maintaining decriminalization of private possession.
- **Oregon recriminalizes (2024)**: Governor Tina Kotzee signed HB 4002 in March 2024, restoring misdemeanor penalties for drug possession. The legislation passed with broad bipartisan support amid public concern about rising overdose deaths, visible public drug use, and the slow rollout of treatment services. Critically, HB 4002 created a "deflection" pathway: charges could be dismissed if the individual engaged with treatment, and the treatment funding established by Measure 110 was preserved. The reversal was thus partial -- a hybrid of recriminalization and treatment diversion.
- **Australia Capital Territory decriminalizes (2023)**: The Australian Capital Territory became the first Australian jurisdiction to decriminalize personal possession of small amounts of all drugs, effective October 2023. The law established substance-specific thresholds and replaced criminal penalties with civil penalties and diversion to treatment, providing another real-time experiment alongside Oregon and British Columbia.
- **Global reform momentum continues**: As of 2024, more than 30 countries maintain some form of drug decriminalization (Transform Drug Policy Foundation, 2024). Germany legalized cannabis in April 2024. Thailand legalized cannabis in 2022 (with ongoing regulatory debate). The global trend toward decriminalization continues despite the setbacks in Oregon and British Columbia, driven by the accumulating evidence that criminalization fails to reduce drug use while imposing enormous human and economic costs.

### The Opioid Crisis and its Impact on Decriminalization Politics (1996-Present)

The opioid epidemic has reshaped the politics of drug policy in ways that both support and complicate decriminalization.

- **Prescription opioid wave (1996-2010)**: Purdue Pharma's aggressive marketing of OxyContin beginning in 1996, combined with a broader pharmaceutical industry campaign to normalize opioid prescribing, created the initial wave of opioid addiction. The American Pain Society's campaign to make pain "the fifth vital sign" and the Joint Commission's pain management standards pressured physicians to prescribe more aggressively. Opioid prescriptions quadrupled between 1999 and 2010, from 86 million to over 300 million annually (CDC, 2017). Prescription opioid overdose deaths rose from approximately 3,400 in 1999 to 16,900 in 2010 (CDC WONDER). Because the victims were disproportionately white, suburban, and middle-class, the political response differed markedly from the crack epidemic -- calls for treatment rather than incarceration became more prominent. Purdue Pharma eventually pleaded guilty to criminal charges in 2007 and again in 2020, paying over $8 billion in penalties, and filed for bankruptcy in 2019.
- **Heroin transition (2010-2015)**: As prescription opioid access tightened through prescriber education, prescription drug monitoring programs (PDMPs), and reformulation of OxyContin to abuse-deterrent formulation (2010), many individuals with opioid dependence transitioned to cheaper, more available heroin. A NIDA study (2014) found that approximately 80% of new heroin users had previously misused prescription opioids. Heroin overdose deaths tripled between 2010 and 2015, from approximately 3,000 to 12,989 (CDC WONDER). This transition exposed a fundamental flaw in supply-side drug policy: restricting access to one substance without addressing underlying addiction simply drives users to more dangerous alternatives.
- **Fentanyl surge (2013-present)**: Illicitly manufactured fentanyl entered the drug supply beginning around 2013, primarily through Mexican cartels using precursor chemicals sourced from China (and, after Chinese regulatory crackdowns in 2019, from India and other sources). Fentanyl is approximately 50-100 times more potent than morphine by weight, making inadvertent overdose far more likely. Even experienced users cannot gauge dosage by sight or taste, and contamination of non-opioid drugs (cocaine, methamphetamine, counterfeit pills) with fentanyl has expanded the population at risk far beyond traditional opioid users. Synthetic opioid deaths skyrocketed from 3,105 in 2013 to 73,838 in 2023 (CDC WONDER). The fentanyl crisis transformed the political landscape: it made the overdose emergency undeniable, generating support for treatment and harm reduction, but it also created fear-based opposition to any policy perceived as reducing enforcement.
- **SUPPORT Act (2018)**: The Substance Use-Disorder Prevention that Promotes Opioid Recovery and Treatment for Patients and Communities Act provided $8 billion over five years for opioid treatment, prevention, and research. The bipartisan legislation expanded Medicaid coverage of SUD treatment, allowed Medicare reimbursement for methadone, and supported the development of non-addictive pain treatments. While not a decriminalization measure, it represented the largest federal treatment investment in a generation and demonstrated bipartisan willingness to fund demand-side responses.
- **Political crosscurrents**: The opioid crisis created an unusual bipartisan alignment -- Republicans representing affected rural districts joined Democrats in supporting treatment funding (the SUPPORT Act, 2018) and reduced sentences (First Step Act, 2018). However, this alignment has not extended to comprehensive decriminalization, as the perceived lethality of fentanyl makes "softening" enforcement politically risky. Oregon's Measure 110 was conceived during the prescription opioid era but implemented during the fentanyl era, and this timing mismatch contributed to its political failure.
- **Drug policy as public health narrative**: The opioid crisis accelerated a broader reframing of drug policy from criminal justice to public health. Surgeon General Vivek Murthy's 2016 report, *Facing Addiction in America*, was the first Surgeon General's report dedicated to substance use disorders and explicitly recommended a health-centered approach. The Biden administration's 2022 National Drug Control Strategy, while not endorsing decriminalization, was the first to include harm reduction as a formal pillar of national drug policy alongside prevention, treatment, and supply reduction. These institutional shifts, while incremental, represent a historical departure from the enforcement-only consensus that dominated from 1981 through approximately 2010.

## History of Alternative Responses in the United States

Even within the U.S.'s predominantly punitive framework, alternative responses to drug use have a significant history.

### Drug Courts (1989-Present)

- **Miami-Dade County (1989)**: Judge Herbert Klein established the first drug court, offering treatment and judicial monitoring as an alternative to incarceration for drug-involved defendants. The model spread rapidly.
- **Drug Courts Program Office (1995)**: The DOJ created a federal office to fund and support drug court expansion, signaling federal endorsement.
- **Growth**: From 1 court in 1989 to approximately 700 by 2000, 2,800 by 2015, and 3,800 by 2024 (NADCP). Drug courts now operate in all 50 states.
- **Significance for decriminalization debate**: Drug courts demonstrated that treatment could be integrated into the justice system, but they preserved criminal charges as leverage. Decriminalization proponents argue this leverage is unnecessary and harmful; drug court proponents argue it is essential for engagement.

### Law Enforcement Deflection (2011-Present)

- **Seattle LEAD (2011)**: The first formalized pre-arrest diversion program allowing officers to redirect low-level drug and prostitution offenders to case management without arrest. A University of Washington evaluation (2017) found 58% reduction in recidivism and $2.40 return per $1 invested.
- **Gloucester Angel Program (2015)**: Police Chief Leonard Campanello announced that anyone who walked into the Gloucester, MA police station seeking help for addiction would be connected to treatment without arrest. The program went viral and inspired the national PAARI network.
- **PAARI Network (2015-present)**: The Police Assisted Addiction and Recovery Initiative expanded the deflection model to 600+ police departments by 2024, connecting over 20,000 individuals to treatment.
- **Madison CARES (2017)**: An active outreach model where officers proactively identified individuals in crisis and offered services, reducing repeat emergency contacts by 38%.
- **Quick Response Teams (2016-present)**: Teams of first responders and peer specialists follow up with overdose survivors within 24-72 hours to offer treatment. Operating in 200+ communities, primarily in Appalachia.
- **Significance**: These programs represent a bridge between criminalization and decriminalization, maintaining police involvement while shifting the response from arrest to engagement. They have generated bipartisan support and created a constituency of law enforcement officials who support treatment-centered approaches.

### Prosecutorial Reform (2017-2024)

- Reform-minded district attorneys in major cities adopted non-prosecution policies for drug possession, achieving de facto decriminalization through discretionary non-enforcement.
- The movement peaked between 2018 and 2022, with reform DAs serving in Philadelphia, Boston, San Francisco, Los Angeles, Manhattan, Baltimore, and other jurisdictions.
- Backlash followed: San Francisco DA Chesa Boudin was recalled in 2022; Los Angeles DA George Gascon was replaced in 2024; Baltimore State's Attorney Marilyn Mosby faced federal charges (unrelated to drug policy) and was replaced by a more traditional prosecutor.
- The prosecutorial reform movement demonstrated both the potential and the fragility of de facto decriminalization. Without statutory backing, policy changes can be reversed with each election cycle.

### The Harm Reduction Movement in the United States (1988-Present)

- **Early needle exchange (1988-1995)**: Activist-run syringe exchange programs operated in legal gray areas in several major cities. The federal government banned funding for syringe purchase, but the Clinton administration allowed SAMHSA to fund other SSP services (counseling, testing, referrals).
- **Naloxone expansion (2001-present)**: New Mexico became the first state to authorize pharmacist prescribing of naloxone without a physician's prescription in 2001. By 2017, all 50 states had naloxone access laws. Community distribution programs expanded dramatically after 2010 as opioid overdose deaths accelerated.
- **Good Samaritan laws (2007-present)**: New Mexico enacted the first 911 Good Samaritan law in 2007, providing legal protection for individuals who call emergency services during an overdose. By 2024, 48 states and D.C. had adopted some form of Good Samaritan protection, reducing a key barrier to seeking help during overdose emergencies.
- **Supervised consumption (2021-present)**: New York City opened the first legally sanctioned supervised consumption sites in the United States in November 2021 (OnPoint NYC, two locations). The sites reversed over 1,800 overdoses with zero deaths in their first three years, providing the first U.S.-based evidence for supervised consumption.
- **Fentanyl test strips (2018-present)**: Initially classified as drug paraphernalia in most states, fentanyl test strips were gradually legalized as states recognized their public health value. By 2024, 30 states and D.C. had explicitly legalized them, and federal guidance (DOJ, 2021) clarified that personal use of test strips was not a federal offense.

The harm reduction movement has operated alongside and often in advance of decriminalization, creating infrastructure and political support for health-centered drug policy. Many harm reduction interventions -- naloxone distribution, syringe exchange, Good Samaritan laws -- were initially opposed as "enabling drug use" but are now widely accepted across the political spectrum. This trajectory suggests that broader decriminalization may follow a similar path from controversy to consensus, though the timeline remains uncertain.

| 2015 | $25.4 billion | ~57% | ~43% | ONDCP |
| 2020 | $34.6 billion | ~54% | ~46% | ONDCP |

The budget trajectory shows that enforcement has consistently commanded the majority of federal drug spending. Even in 2025, after decades of reform advocacy and the worst overdose crisis in American history, enforcement receives a slight majority. However, the gap has narrowed significantly from the Reagan-era peak of 71% enforcement. The goal of decriminalization advocates -- a "60-40" rebalance toward demand reduction -- has historical precedent only in the Nixon administration's initial (and short-lived) treatment-centered approach.

## Lessons from History

### Pattern 1: Racial Politics Drive Criminalization

At every stage of U.S. drug history, the criminalization of specific substances has been linked to racial anxieties: opium to Chinese immigrants, cocaine to Black Americans, cannabis to Mexican immigrants, crack to Black urban communities. This pattern is not incidental -- it reflects a deliberate political strategy. As Michelle Alexander documented in *The New Jim Crow* (2010), the War on Drugs functioned as a mechanism of racial control after explicit racial discrimination became legally impermissible. The racial pattern has a corollary: when drug crises affect predominantly white populations (the opioid epidemic), the political response shifts toward treatment and compassion. This dual standard suggests that decriminalization advocacy must explicitly address racial equity to succeed politically and to avoid reproducing disparities in civil penalty systems.

### Pattern 2: Treatment Infrastructure Determines Decriminalization Success

Portugal's success rested on a pre-existing national health service and a deliberate, simultaneous investment in treatment infrastructure. The Portuguese government, acting on the recommendations of the Commission for the National Strategy Against Drugs, expanded methadone maintenance, therapeutic communities, and outreach programs concurrently with decriminalization. Oregon's difficulties stemmed largely from the failure to deploy treatment services quickly enough to serve decriminalized populations -- cannabis tax revenue earmarked for treatment was delayed 18+ months by bureaucratic grant processes. The historical pattern is clear: removing criminal penalties without providing alternative pathways to care produces political backlash. Every successful decriminalization has been accompanied by treatment expansion; every failure has been characterized by a gap between penalty removal and service availability.

### Pattern 3: Reform Follows Crisis, Not Evidence

The most significant drug policy reforms in U.S. history have been driven by crises -- the opioid epidemic motivated the First Step Act; overdose deaths motivated Measure 110; the crack epidemic, paradoxically, both intensified criminalization in the 1980s and eventually produced the Fair Sentencing Act. Evidence-based arguments alone have rarely been sufficient; political will has typically required visible suffering among politically sympathetic populations. Portugal's decriminalization was catalyzed by a heroin crisis that produced the highest drug-related AIDS rate in the EU. The Czech Republic's reform followed years of growing drug use that existing criminal penalties had failed to prevent. This pattern means that reformers must be prepared to act when crises create political openings, with fully developed policy designs ready for implementation.

### Pattern 4: Federalism Enables Experimentation but Limits Scale

The U.S. federal system has allowed states to serve as "laboratories of democracy" for drug policy -- from Oregon's 1973 cannabis decriminalization to Colorado's 2012 legalization to Oregon's 2020 Measure 110. However, federalism also limits the scale of reform, as federal law (the Controlled Substances Act) remains the baseline, and federal penalties, banking restrictions, and scheduling classifications constrain state innovation.

### Pattern 5: The Pendulum Swings

Drug policy history reveals a recurring pattern of reform followed by retrenchment. The 1970s decriminalization wave was reversed by the Reagan-era War on Drugs. The 2000s sentencing reform movement was partially checked by tough-on-crime politics. Oregon's 2020 decriminalization was reversed by 2024. Each reform cycle builds on the previous one -- the political space for drug policy reform in 2024 is vastly larger than in 1990 -- but the pendulum dynamic means that reform advocates must design policies that are robust against backlash, not just effective in ideal conditions.

### Pattern 6: Implementation Quality Determines Political Durability

Portugal's decriminalization has survived 20+ years and multiple changes of government -- including conservative administrations that had initially opposed it -- because it was well-implemented and produced measurable positive outcomes that transcended partisan framing. Oregon's lasted four years because implementation was poor and visible public disorder increased. British Columbia's pilot lasted approximately 18 months before being partially reversed for similar reasons. The lesson for future reforms is that political sustainability requires operational excellence, not just policy correctness. Treatment must be available before criminal penalties are removed. Civil engagement mechanisms must produce high participation rates. Data must be collected and reported transparently. Without these operational foundations, even sound policy will be reversed.

### Pattern 7: Incremental Reform Accumulates

The overall trajectory of drug policy history, despite oscillations, has moved toward reduced punitiveness. Cannabis has gone from Schedule I prohibition to legal in 24 states over 50 years. Mandatory minimums for drug offenses have been reduced at both federal and state levels. Drug courts, deflection programs, and prosecutorial non-enforcement have created alternatives to incarceration that did not exist a generation ago. Public support for treatment over punishment has grown from minority to supermajority position. Each incremental reform creates constituencies, generates evidence, and normalizes the idea that drug use is a health issue. Even Oregon's reversal preserved treatment funding and created a deflection pathway. The trajectory is not linear, but the direction is consistent: away from pure criminalization and toward health-centered responses.

### Historical Summary

The arc of U.S. drug policy history can be summarized in five phases:

1. **Open market (pre-1914)**: Drugs were legal and widely available; addiction was common but was treated primarily as a medical matter
2. **Criminalization (1914-1970)**: Racial politics drove the progressive criminalization of specific substances, with penalties escalating over time
3. **War on Drugs (1971-2000)**: Enforcement budgets, mandatory minimums, and mass incarceration produced a tenfold increase in drug prisoners without reducing drug use
4. **Reform emergence (2000-2020)**: Portugal's model, state sentencing reforms, cannabis legalization, and the opioid crisis created political space for alternatives
5. **Contested transition (2020-present)**: Oregon's experiment and reversal, the fentanyl crisis, and shifting public opinion define a period in which the direction of reform is established but the pace and scope remain deeply contested

Each phase has built on the previous one: the failures of prohibition enabled the War on Drugs; the failures of the War on Drugs enabled reform; and the challenges of early reform are shaping the next generation of policy design. The central historical question is whether the United States will complete the transition from criminal to health-centered drug policy within the current generation, or whether additional cycles of reform and retrenchment will be required.
