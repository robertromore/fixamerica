# Drug Decriminalization: Overview

## Overview

Drug decriminalization is the policy of removing criminal penalties for the personal possession and use of controlled substances, replacing them with civil sanctions such as fines, mandatory health assessments, or referrals to treatment services. Unlike legalization, which creates a regulated legal market for production and sale, decriminalization maintains the underlying prohibition -- drugs remain illegal -- but reclassifies personal-quantity possession from a criminal offense to a civil or administrative violation. This distinction is critical: decriminalization targets the demand side of drug markets by reducing punishment for users while preserving criminal penalties for manufacturing, trafficking, and distribution.

Within the decriminalization spectrum, policy scholars distinguish several gradations. *De jure* decriminalization formally removes criminal penalties through statute, as Portugal did in 2001 with Law 30/2000. *De facto* decriminalization occurs when prosecutors or police systematically decline to enforce existing criminal statutes, as in the non-enforcement policies adopted by district attorneys in Philadelphia, Manhattan, and Baltimore. *Depenalization* reduces penalties -- typically from felony to misdemeanor -- without eliminating criminal classification entirely, as California achieved through Proposition 47 in 2014. Each approach carries different implications for individual liberty, institutional incentives, and political durability. De jure decriminalization provides the most consistent protection but requires legislative action; de facto decriminalization can be reversed by a single election; depenalization reduces harm incrementally but preserves criminal records and their collateral consequences.

The case for decriminalization rests on decades of evidence demonstrating that criminal penalties for drug possession have failed to reduce drug use, have imposed enormous costs on individuals and communities, and have perpetuated racial disparities in the criminal justice system. The United States arrests approximately 1.5 million people annually for drug offenses (FBI UCR, 2023), with roughly 87% of those arrests -- about 1.3 million -- for simple possession rather than sales or manufacturing (BJS, 2023). These arrests generate criminal records that impede access to employment, housing, education, and public benefits, creating barriers to stability that paradoxically increase the risk of continued substance use. The racial impact is stark: Black Americans are 3.73 times more likely than white Americans to be arrested for cannabis possession despite comparable usage rates (ACLU, 2020).

The most comprehensive evidence for decriminalization comes from Portugal, which in 2001 decriminalized the personal possession and use of all drugs through Law 30/2000. Over two decades, Portugal has seen drug-related deaths fall from 80 in 2001 to 74 in 2022, HIV infections among people who inject drugs decline by over 90%, and drug use rates remain below the European average (EMCDDA, 2023). Portugal's system centers on Dissuasion Commissions (Comissoes para a Dissuasao da Toxicodependencia, or CDTs) -- three-member panels comprising a legal expert, a social worker, and a psychologist -- that assess cited individuals and recommend treatment, sanctions, or both. The Czech Republic adopted a different model in 2010, establishing substance-specific quantity thresholds (e.g., 10 grams of cannabis, 1 gram of cocaine) below which possession is a misdemeanor with a maximum fine of approximately $650 (Government Order No. 467/2009).

In the United States, Oregon became the first state to decriminalize personal possession of all drugs through Measure 110 in 2020, though the state partially recriminalized in 2024 amid concerns about implementation failures and rising public drug use. The measure's core design flaw was a $100 civil citation with a phone-call opt-out that produced a near-zero engagement rate -- only about 1% of cited individuals called the health assessment hotline (Oregon Health Authority, 2023) -- while treatment funding was delayed 18 months by bureaucratic processes. This reversal -- driven more by execution failures than by the decriminalization concept itself -- underscores the critical importance of simultaneously building robust treatment infrastructure and designing effective civil engagement mechanisms.

Globally, more than 30 countries have adopted some form of drug decriminalization (Transform Drug Policy Foundation, 2021). Beyond Portugal and the Czech Republic, Uruguay decriminalized personal possession in 1974, Italy adopted and then partially reversed administrative penalties in the 2000s, Colombia's Constitutional Court recognized personal-use possession as constitutionally protected in 1994 (*Sentencia C-221/94*), and the Netherlands has maintained a "tolerance policy" (*gedoogbeleid*) for cannabis since 1976 that operates as de facto decriminalization. In 2020, the Lancet published a systematic review of 22 studies across multiple countries, finding "no significant increase in drug use" associated with decriminalization (Stevens et al., *The Lancet Public Health*, 2020). The accumulated international evidence provides a substantial basis for evaluating outcomes across different legal traditions, healthcare systems, and cultural contexts.

The political landscape in the United States is shifting, albeit unevenly. Public support for ending arrest for drug possession has grown from 52% in 2014 to 66% in 2024 (Pew Research, 2024). Cannabis decriminalization or legalization now covers the majority of the U.S. population. Yet Oregon's reversal and the fentanyl crisis have created countervailing headwinds, making the path to broader decriminalization narrower and more dependent on demonstrating that health-based systems can provide both accountability and compassion simultaneously.

Drug decriminalization sits at the intersection of criminal justice reform, public health, racial equity, and individual liberty, making it one of the most politically complex yet potentially transformative policy shifts available. The stakes are high: 107,543 Americans died from drug overdoses in 2023 (CDC WONDER, 2024), and 1.3 million were arrested for simple possession -- a system that simultaneously fails to prevent death and produces lasting harm through criminal records. The core question is not whether drug use should be encouraged -- no serious decriminalization proposal advocates that -- but whether the criminal justice system or the public health system is the more effective and humane mechanism for responding to it.

## Key Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Decriminalization** | Removal of criminal penalties for personal possession and use of drugs; drugs remain illegal but possession is treated as a civil or administrative matter |
| **Legalization** | Creation of a regulated legal market for the production, sale, and possession of drugs (as with alcohol or, increasingly, cannabis) |
| **Depenalization** | Reduction of criminal penalties (e.g., from felony to misdemeanor) without removing criminal classification |
| **De facto decriminalization** | Systematic non-enforcement of criminal drug laws through prosecutorial discretion or police policy |
| **Dissuasion Commission** | Panel (Portugal model) that assesses individuals cited for drug possession and recommends treatment, sanctions, or suspension of proceedings |
| **Deflection** | Law enforcement practice of redirecting individuals to treatment rather than arresting them at the point of contact |
| **Possession threshold** | Quantity limit distinguishing personal use (civil violation) from intent to distribute (criminal offense) |
| **Treatment on demand** | Policy goal of ensuring any person seeking substance use disorder treatment can begin treatment within a specified timeframe (typically 72 hours) |

## Scope

This analysis covers the key aspects of drug decriminalization:

- **Decriminalization vs. Legalization**: The critical distinctions between removing criminal penalties (decriminalization), creating regulated legal markets (legalization), and maintaining full prohibition -- and the policy implications of each approach
- **International Models**: Evidence from Portugal's comprehensive decriminalization, the Czech Republic's threshold system, the Netherlands' tolerance policy, and other national approaches
- **State and Local Reforms**: Oregon Measure 110 and its 2024 reversal, state cannabis decriminalization, municipal citation programs, and prosecutorial non-enforcement policies
- **Civil Penalty Systems**: Design of alternative response frameworks including civil fines, mandatory health assessments, treatment referrals, and administrative sanctions
- **Possession Thresholds**: Establishing quantity limits that distinguish personal use from distribution, including weight-based and dose-based approaches
- **Treatment Infrastructure**: The health system capacity required to make decriminalization work, including treatment on demand, harm reduction services, and peer support networks
- **Racial Equity**: How drug criminalization has disproportionately harmed communities of color and how decriminalization can -- or may fail to -- address these disparities
- **Criminal Record Expungement**: Policies to address the legacy of criminalization by clearing records for conduct that is no longer criminal
- **Police Discretion and Deflection**: The role of law enforcement culture, training, and incentive structures in transitioning from arrest-based to health-based responses
- **Public Opinion and Political Strategy**: How public attitudes toward drug policy are shifting and what communication strategies have proven effective in building support for decriminalization

## Core Tensions and Tradeoffs

- **Public Safety vs. Individual Liberty**: Criminalization aims to deter drug use through punishment, but imposes significant liberty costs on individuals whose drug use may harm primarily themselves. Decriminalization accepts some risk of increased visibility of drug use in exchange for reduced incarceration and improved access to treatment.
- **Deterrence vs. Evidence**: Many policymakers believe criminal penalties deter drug use, but decades of research -- including the National Research Council's 2014 review -- find that the certainty of punishment, not severity, drives deterrence, and that arrest and incarceration do not meaningfully reduce drug consumption rates.
- **Implementation Sequencing**: Oregon's experience demonstrates that decriminalization without adequate treatment infrastructure can produce backlash. The tension between moving quickly to end harmful criminalization and building capacity slowly and carefully is a genuine policy dilemma.
- **Threshold Design**: Setting possession quantity thresholds that distinguish personal use from distribution requires balancing inclusivity (higher thresholds protect more users) against concerns about enabling de facto low-level dealing (lower thresholds limit this risk but snare more users).
- **Federal vs. State Authority**: Drug scheduling and the Controlled Substances Act are federal, but 95% of drug arrests occur at the state and local level. Comprehensive decriminalization requires action at multiple levels of government, creating coordination challenges and potential federal-state conflicts.
- **Racial Equity vs. Enforcement Neutrality**: Drug criminalization has been enforced with stark racial disparities. Decriminalization addresses this by removing the mechanism of arrest, but critics argue it does not address the underlying causes of disparate enforcement and may reduce pressure for deeper structural reform.
- **Voluntary vs. Coerced Treatment**: Drug courts and criminal justice mandates compel treatment participation, and proponents cite their 50-75% completion rates (National Drug Court Institute, 2023). Decriminalization removes this leverage. The debate hinges on whether coercion produces lasting recovery or merely temporary compliance, and whether voluntary treatment -- when accessible -- produces better long-term outcomes.
- **Local Context vs. Universal Policy**: Portugal's success occurred within a universal healthcare system serving 10.3 million people. The United States has 330 million people, no universal healthcare, enormous geographic variation in treatment capacity, and a federal system that distributes authority across 50 states. Whether a single model can be adapted to this complexity or whether decriminalization must be designed jurisdiction by jurisdiction is a genuine open question.

## Key Questions

1. Can the United States achieve the public health benefits Portugal demonstrated -- reduced overdose deaths, lower HIV transmission, stable drug use rates -- without replicating Portugal's specific institutional context, including its universal healthcare system?
2. What possession thresholds and civil penalty structures best balance the goals of diverting users from the criminal justice system, maintaining deterrence against dealing, and ensuring connection to treatment?
3. How should decriminalization be sequenced with treatment infrastructure expansion to avoid the implementation failures that led to Oregon's 2024 recriminalization?
4. What federal policy changes are necessary to enable state-level decriminalization, and is federal decriminalization politically achievable?
5. How can civil penalty and health assessment systems be designed to achieve high engagement rates -- avoiding Oregon's ~1% hotline call rate -- without replicating the coercive dynamics of the criminal justice system?
6. What role should police discretion play in a decriminalized system, and how can deflection programs be structured to minimize racial and socioeconomic disparities in who gets diverted versus who gets cited?
7. Can the political coalition for decriminalization survive the fentanyl crisis, or does the lethality of the current drug supply make the politics of reduced enforcement permanently more difficult?

## Related Policy Areas

- **Criminal Justice Reform**: Decriminalization is one component of a broader shift from punitive to evidence-based criminal justice responses. See [Justice analysis](../../justice/01-overview.md).
- **Healthcare Access**: Decriminalization without treatment access produces the Oregon outcome. Universal healthcare or robust Medicaid expansion is a precondition for success. See [Healthcare analysis](../../healthcare/01-overview.md).
- **Housing Policy**: Stable housing is the strongest predictor of sustained recovery. Drug conviction barriers to housing must be removed. See [Housing analysis](../../housing/01-overview.md).

## Vision of Success

A successful drug decriminalization policy in the United States would feature:

- **Elimination of Arrest for Personal Possession**: No American arrested, prosecuted, or incarcerated solely for possessing personal-use quantities of any controlled substance, with civil responses (health assessments, referrals, graduated fines) replacing criminal penalties nationwide
- **Treatment on Demand**: Every person identified through the civil response system able to access evidence-based treatment -- including medication-assisted treatment, behavioral therapy, and harm reduction services -- within 72 hours of request, with no financial barriers to care
- **Measurable Public Health Outcomes**: Drug overdose deaths reduced by at least 30% within five years through expanded treatment access, harm reduction, and removal of barriers that deter people from seeking help; HIV and hepatitis C infections among people who inject drugs reduced by at least 50%
- **Racial Disparity Elimination**: The 3.73x racial disparity in drug arrests effectively eliminated by removing the arrest mechanism, with civil responses monitored for equitable application across racial and socioeconomic groups
- **Criminal Record Relief**: Automatic expungement of prior drug possession convictions for conduct that has been decriminalized, restoring access to employment, housing, education, and public benefits for the estimated 20 million Americans with drug-related criminal records (BJS, 2022)
- **Sustainable Political Support**: Public understanding that decriminalization is a health-centered accountability system -- not permissiveness -- producing durable bipartisan support and preventing the kind of backlash that reversed Oregon's Measure 110
- **Integrated Data and Accountability**: A national drug policy data infrastructure tracking citations, health assessments, treatment enrollment, overdose deaths, and racial equity metrics in real time, enabling continuous improvement and transparent public reporting that sustains political confidence in the system
- **International Alignment**: U.S. drug policy aligned with the growing international consensus -- reflected in WHO, UNODC, and Global Commission on Drug Policy recommendations -- that treating drug use as primarily a health issue rather than a criminal one produces better outcomes for individuals, communities, and public safety
