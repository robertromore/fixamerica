# Economic Inequality: Solutions

## Guiding Principles

### Criteria for Evaluating Reforms

| Criterion | Definition | Why It Matters |
|-----------|------------|----------------|
| **Reduces inequality** | Measurably narrows income/wealth gaps | Direct metric of success |
| **Promotes mobility** | Increases movement between income levels | Restores American Dream |
| **Supports growth** | Compatible with economic dynamism | Avoids zero-sum framing |
| **Politically viable** | Can build majority support | Perfect solutions must pass |
| **Enforceable** | Can be implemented and monitored | Avoids loopholes and evasion |
| **Durable** | Can withstand political changes | Long-term impact |

### The Framework

There's no single solution to inequality. A comprehensive approach addresses multiple causes:

1. **Pre-distribution**: Fix markets to produce more equal outcomes
2. **Redistribution**: Transfer resources after the fact
3. **Opportunity**: Invest in education, healthcare, infrastructure
4. **Power**: Change who makes decisions

## Policy Proposals

### Tier 1: Tax Reform

**Progressive Income Tax Reform**

*Key Provisions*

- Raise top marginal rate to 50-70%
- Tax capital gains as ordinary income
- Eliminate stepped-up basis at death
- Close carried interest loophole
- Strengthen earned income tax credit

*Evidence*

- Top rates were 70-91% during Great Compression
- Economic growth was strong with higher rates
- Most developed nations tax capital gains higher
- EITC proven poverty reducer

*Advantages*

- Direct revenue for public investment
- Constitutional (16th Amendment)
- Administrative capacity exists
- Progressive distribution

*Challenges*

- Requires congressional action
- Evasion and avoidance strategies
- Capital flight concerns (overstated)
- Political opposition

**Wealth Tax**

*How It Works*

- Annual tax on net worth above threshold (e.g., $50 million)
- 2% on wealth $50M-$1B
- 3%+ on wealth above $1B
- Applies to all assets

*Evidence*

- Multiple countries have had wealth taxes
- Switzerland maintains one successfully
- U.S. had de facto wealth tax on real estate
- Concentration has increased without it

*Advantages*

- Directly addresses wealth concentration
- Substantial revenue ($3 trillion over 10 years per Warren proposal)
- Affects only ultra-wealthy
- Reduces political power of billionaires

*Challenges*

- Constitutional questions (some scholars say permissible)
- Valuation difficulties
- Enforcement and evasion
- Political opposition

**Corporate Tax Reform**

*Key Provisions*

- Raise corporate rate (21% â†’ 28% or higher)
- Minimum corporate tax (book income)
- End offshoring incentives
- Close depreciation loopholes

*Evidence*

- Corporate tax share of revenue has fallen
- Effective rates often near zero
- Other countries pursuing minimum taxes
- Pre-2017 rate was 35%

### Tier 2: Labor Market Reforms

**Strengthen Collective Bargaining**

*PRO Act Provisions*

- Prohibit captive audience meetings
- Penalties for labor law violations
- Card check election option
- Limit misclassification
- Ban right-to-work nationally

*Evidence*

- Union wage premium: 10-20%
- Union decline correlates with inequality rise
- Other countries have higher unionization and less inequality
- Unions compress wage distribution

*Advantages*

- Directly increases worker power
- Market-based (not government transfers)
- Creates countervailing power to corporations
- Raises non-union wages too

*Challenges*

- Blocked by Senate filibuster
- Employer resistance
- Changing economy (gig, remote work)
- Some union decline due to sectoral shifts

**Minimum Wage Increase**

*How It Would Work*

- Raise federal minimum to $15-17/hour
- Index to inflation or median wages
- Phase in over 3-5 years
- Apply to all workers (eliminate tipped, youth exceptions)

*Evidence*

- Minimum wage has lost 40%+ real value
- Research shows modest increases don't kill jobs
- 30+ states/cities above federal minimum
- Other countries have higher minimums

*Advantages*

- Direct wage increase for millions
- Reduces poverty
- No government spending required
- Popular

*Challenges*

- Employment effects in some contexts
- Regional cost of living variation
- Small business concerns
- Congressional gridlock

**Sectoral Bargaining**

*How It Would Work*

- Industry-wide wage and benefit standards
- All employers in sector must comply
- Worker representatives negotiate standards
- Government extends agreements

*Evidence*

- Common in Europe (Germany, France, Nordics)
- Reduces competition on wages
- Levels playing field for employers
- Compresses wage distribution

### Tier 3: Public Investment and Services

**Universal Pre-K and Childcare**

*How It Would Work*

- Free pre-K for all 3-4 year olds
- Subsidized childcare for working families
- Pay childcare workers living wages
- Cap costs at % of income

*Evidence*

- High-quality early education has strong returns
- Childcare costs are major burden for families
- Other countries provide universal care
- Enables parental workforce participation

*Advantages*

- Equalizes early childhood opportunity
- Reduces poverty
- Increases labor force participation
- Long-term economic returns

**College Affordability**

*Options*

- Free community college
- Debt-free public university
- Student loan forgiveness
- Income-driven repayment reform
- Pell Grant expansion

*Evidence*

- College was nearly free 50 years ago
- Education premium has increased
- Debt burden affects wealth-building
- Other countries have free/low-cost higher education

**Healthcare Reform**

*Options*

- Medicare for All (single payer)
- Public option
- ACA expansion
- Prescription drug price negotiation

*Connection to Inequality*

- Medical costs are major cause of bankruptcy
- Healthcare tied to employment creates insecurity
- Health outcomes correlate with income
- Universal coverage reduces inequality

### Tier 4: Social Insurance and Safety Net

**Child Allowance**

*How It Would Work*

- Monthly payment per child (e.g., $300/month)
- Universal or means-tested
- Phase out at higher incomes
- No work requirements

*Evidence*

- Expanded CTC (2021) cut child poverty in half
- Most developed countries have child benefits
- Directly addresses child poverty
- Administrative simplicity

**Guaranteed Basic Income**

*Options*

- Universal basic income (all adults)
- Negative income tax (targeted)
- Job guarantee alternative
- Combination approaches

*Evidence*

- Pilot programs show positive results
- Alaska Permanent Fund provides model
- Could replace inefficient programs
- Provides economic security

### Tier 5: Anti-Monopoly Policies

**Antitrust Enforcement**

*Reforms*

- Strengthen merger review
- Break up dominant platforms
- Lower barriers to entry
- Reduce labor market monopsony

*Connection to Inequality*

- Monopoly profits flow to shareholders
- Monopsony suppresses wages
- Concentration increases executive pay
- Reduces entrepreneurship

**Corporate Governance Reform**

*Options*

- Worker representation on boards (codetermination)
- Stakeholder governance requirements
- Executive pay ratio disclosure
- Share buyback restrictions

*Evidence*

- Germany has codetermination with strong economy
- Shareholder primacy is recent invention
- Stakeholder models exist and work
- Reduces extreme executive pay

**The Connection**

## What Has Worked Elsewhere

### Nordic Countries

| Policy | Approach | Result |
|--------|----------|--------|
| Taxes | High, progressive | Gini: 0.25-0.28 |
| Labor | Strong unions, sectoral bargaining | Low wage inequality |
| Services | Universal healthcare, childcare, education | Opportunity equality |
| Safety net | Robust, universal | Low poverty |

### Germany

| Policy | Approach | Result |
|--------|----------|--------|
| Codetermination | Worker board representation | Compressed wage distribution |
| Training | Vocational apprenticeships | Middle-skill jobs preserved |
| Industrial policy | Support manufacturing | Less deindustrialization |
| Unions | Sectoral bargaining | Wage compression |

### Historical United States

| Period | Policies | Result |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1945-1975 | High taxes, strong unions, GI Bill | Great Compression |
| New Deal | Social Security, labor rights | Middle class creation |

## Trade-offs

### Growth vs. Equality

| Claim | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| "Redistribution hurts growth" | IMF, OECD: inequality harms growth |
| "High taxes reduce investment" | No evidence of threshold effect |
| "Other countries grow slower" | Germany, Nordics competitive |

**Reality**: Trade-off is overstated. Inequality harms growth; shared prosperity promotes it.

### Efficiency vs. Equity

| Consideration | Analysis |
|---------------|----------|
| Some inequality incentivizes work | Yes, but extreme inequality unnecessary |
| Transfers create disincentives | Evidence of strong work disincentives is weak |
| Programs have overhead | But market failures have costs too |

**Reality**: Some trade-off exists but magnitude often overstated

### Universal vs. Targeted

| Approach | Pros | Cons |
|----------|------|------|
| Universal | Political durability, no stigma | Higher cost |
| Targeted | Lower cost, focused on need | Cliff effects, stigma |

**Recommendation**: Generally favor universal with some targeting through taxation.

## Recommendations

### Priority 1: Labor Power (Immediate)

**Focus on Worker Organizing**

- PRO Act passage (or components by executive action)
- NLRB enforcement
- Support organizing campaigns
- State-level reforms

### Priority 2: Tax Fairness (Near-Term)

**Progressive Tax Reform**

- Close capital gains preference
- Raise top rates
- Corporate minimum tax
- IRS enforcement funding

### Priority 3: Universal Services (Medium-Term)

**Build Universal Programs**

- Universal pre-K
- Affordable childcare
- College affordability
- Healthcare access

### Priority 4: Structural Change (Long-Term)

**Address Root Causes**

- Campaign finance reform
- Antitrust enforcement
- Corporate governance reform
- Wealth tax (if constitutional)

### The Package

Comprehensive inequality reduction requires:

1. **Pre-distribution**: Labor law, minimum wage, antitrust
2. **Redistribution**: Progressive taxes, transfers
3. **Services**: Education, healthcare, childcare
4. **Power**: Campaign finance, labor organizing
