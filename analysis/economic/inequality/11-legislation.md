# Economic Inequality: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Addressing economic inequality requires a multi-pronged legislative approach: tax reform to reduce wealth concentration, labor law reform to rebuild worker power, expanded social insurance, and corporate governance reform. This document provides draft legislation targeting the key drivers of inequality, working within current constitutional constraints while identifying where constitutional amendment might be needed.

## Constitutional Amendments

### Economic Rights Amendment

**Purpose**: Establish economic rights as constitutional principles, building on FDR's Second Bill of Rights concept.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. Every person in the United States shall have the right to:
(a) employment at a living wage;
(b) freedom from poverty and economic deprivation;
(c) healthcare;
(d) education;
(e) housing; and
(f) economic security in old age.

SECTION 2. Congress and the States shall have the power to
enforce this article by appropriate legislation, including but
not limited to taxation and direct provision of services.

SECTION 3. The rights enumerated in this article shall not be
construed to diminish other rights retained by the people.

SECTION 4. This article shall take effect five years after the
date of ratification.

```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 1**: Establishes positive economic rights not currently in Constitution
- **Section 2**: Provides clear authority for implementing legislation
- **Section 3**: Non-diminishment clause protects other rights
- **Section 4**: Phase-in period for implementation

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Justiciability | Courts can develop standards over time |
| Cost | Rights don't mandate specific programs |
| Federalism | Both Congress and states empowered |
| Political feasibility | Would require mass movement |

### Wealth Taxation Amendment

**Purpose**: Remove constitutional doubts about wealth taxation.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. Congress shall have power to lay and collect taxes on
accumulated wealth, including but not limited to net worth, assets,
property, and intangible property, whether directly or through any
method of assessment Congress deems appropriate.

SECTION 2. Such taxes need not be apportioned among the States
according to population.

SECTION 3. This article shall take effect upon ratification.

```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 1**: Explicit authority for wealth taxes
- **Section 2**: Removes apportionment requirement from Article I
- Addresses conservative arguments against wealth tax constitutionality

## Federal Legislation

### Inequality Reduction Act

**Purpose**: Comprehensive tax reform to reduce income and wealth inequality through progressive taxation and closing loopholes.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Inequality Reduction Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) Economic inequality in the United States has reached
    levels not seen since before the Great Depression.
(2) The top 1 percent of Americans own more wealth than the
    entire middle class.
(3) Tax policy since 1980 has systematically favored wealthy
    taxpayers and corporations, contributing to inequality.
(4) Rising inequality threatens economic growth, social
    cohesion, and democratic governance.
(5) Other developed nations achieve more equitable outcomes
    through progressive taxation.

SEC. 3. INCREASED TOP MARGINAL INCOME TAX RATES.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 1 of the Internal Revenue Code of 1986
    is amended to add the following brackets for taxable income
    over $1,000,000:
    (1) 50 percent on taxable income over $1,000,000 but not
        over $5,000,000;
    (2) 60 percent on taxable income over $5,000,000 but not
        over $25,000,000;
    (3) 70 percent on taxable income over $25,000,000.

(b) INDEXING.—The thresholds in subsection (a) shall be adjusted
    annually for inflation.

SEC. 4. CAPITAL GAINS TAX REFORM.

(a) TAXATION AS ORDINARY INCOME.—For taxpayers with adjusted
    gross income over $1,000,000, long-term capital gains and
    qualified dividends shall be taxed as ordinary income.

(b) PHASE-IN.—For taxpayers with AGI between $500,000 and
    $1,000,000, the preferential rate shall be phased out
    proportionally.

(c) MARK-TO-MARKET FOR BILLIONAIRES.—Taxpayers with net worth
    over $1,000,000,000 shall recognize gains on tradable assets
    annually, regardless of sale.

SEC. 5. CARRIED INTEREST ELIMINATION.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 1061 of the Internal Revenue Code is
    amended to provide that applicable partnership interests
    shall be treated as ordinary income in all cases.

(b) EFFECTIVE DATE.—This section applies to taxable years
    beginning after December 31 following enactment.

SEC. 6. STEPPED-UP BASIS ELIMINATION.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 1014 of the Internal Revenue Code is
    amended to provide that property acquired from a decedent
    shall have a basis equal to the decedent's basis (carryover
    basis).

(b) RECOGNITION AT DEATH.—Alternatively, unrealized gains shall
    be recognized at death for estates exceeding $5,000,000.

(c) EXEMPTION.—The first $1,000,000 of unrealized gains shall be
    exempt from recognition at death.

(d) FAMILY FARM AND BUSINESS PROTECTION.—Gains on family farms
    and closely held businesses may be deferred until sale,
    subject to a lien on the property.

SEC. 7. CORPORATE MINIMUM TAX.

(a) IN GENERAL.—A corporation with book income exceeding
    $1,000,000,000 shall pay a minimum tax of 21 percent of
    book income, with credit for taxes paid.

(b) STOCK BUYBACK TAX.—Stock repurchases shall be subject to
    an excise tax of 4 percent.

SEC. 8. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) IRS FUNDING.—There is authorized to be appropriated to the
    Internal Revenue Service $80,000,000,000 over 10 years for
    enforcement activities focused on high-income taxpayers.

(b) REPORTING.—Financial institutions shall report account
    inflows and outflows over $600 to the IRS annually.

SEC. 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect for taxable years beginning after
December 31 following enactment.

```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Restores top rates toward historical norms (top rate was 70% until 1981)
- **Section 4**: Ends preferential treatment of investment income for wealthy
- **Section 5**: Closes carried interest loophole used by hedge fund managers
- **Section 6**: Ends "angel of death" loophole that allows untaxed wealth transfer
- **Section 7**: Ensures profitable corporations pay taxes
- **Section 8**: Provides enforcement resources

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Capital flight | Evidence shows limited mobility; enforcement provisions |
| Economic harm | Historical high rates coincided with growth |
| Revenue estimates | Joint Committee on Taxation scoring |
| Political opposition | Strong public support for taxing wealthy |

**Refinements**:

- Could phase in rate increases over multiple years
- Carve-outs for specific situations (small business, farmers)
- Coordinate with international minimum tax efforts

### Wealth Building Act

**Purpose**: Create pathways to wealth-building for low and middle-income Americans through baby bonds, expanded homeownership, and retirement security.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Wealth Building Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) The median American family has less than $100,000 in
    wealth, while the top 1 percent owns over 30 percent of
    all wealth.
(2) Racial wealth gaps persist, with Black families having
    one-eighth the wealth of white families.
(3) Most Americans cannot afford a $400 emergency expense.
(4) Wealth begets wealth through compound returns, while
    those without assets cannot build wealth.
(5) Government policy has historically subsidized wealth-
    building for some while excluding others.

SEC. 3. AMERICAN OPPORTUNITY ACCOUNTS (BABY BONDS).

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary of the Treasury shall establish
    an American Opportunity Account for each child born in the
    United States or who becomes a citizen.

(b) INITIAL DEPOSIT.—Upon establishment, the Secretary shall
    deposit $1,000 in each Account.

(c) ANNUAL DEPOSITS.—Based on household income relative to the
    federal poverty level, the Secretary shall make annual
    deposits as follows:
    (1) Households below 100% FPL: $2,000 annually
    (2) Households at 100-200% FPL: $1,500 annually
    (3) Households at 200-300% FPL: $1,000 annually
    (4) Households at 300-400% FPL: $500 annually
    (5) Households above 400% FPL: $0 annually

(d) INVESTMENT.—Funds in Accounts shall be invested in a
    diversified portfolio managed by the Treasury, targeting
    returns comparable to the Thrift Savings Plan L Funds.

(e) ACCESS.—Account holders may access funds upon reaching age 18
    for the following purposes:
    (1) Post-secondary education expenses
    (2) Home purchase (first-time buyer)
    (3) Starting a business
    (4) Retirement savings (transfer to IRA)

(f) PROJECTED VALUE.—A child born into poverty would accumulate
    approximately $50,000 by age 18.

SEC. 4. FIRST-TIME HOMEBUYER ASSISTANCE.

(a) DOWN PAYMENT ASSISTANCE.—First-time homebuyers with household
    income below 150% of area median income shall be eligible for
    grants of up to $25,000 for down payment and closing costs.

(b) PRIORITY.—Priority shall be given to buyers purchasing in
    areas with historical patterns of discrimination or
    disinvestment.

(c) AFFORDABILITY REQUIREMENT.—Assistance shall be available only
    for homes priced below 150% of area median home price.

SEC. 5. RETIREMENT SECURITY.

(a) AUTOMATIC IRA.—Employers not offering a retirement plan shall
    automatically enroll employees in an Individual Retirement
    Account with a default contribution rate of 3% of wages.

(b) EMPLOYEE OPT-OUT.—Employees may opt out at any time.

(c) SAVER'S CREDIT EXPANSION.—The saver's credit under Section
    25B of the Internal Revenue Code is amended to:
    (1) Make the credit fully refundable
    (2) Increase the maximum credit to $2,000 per individual
    (3) Expand income eligibility to 200% of poverty level

SEC. 6. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated such sums as may be
necessary to carry out this Act.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on January 1 of the second calendar
year following enactment.

```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Baby bonds provide capital to those born without it
- **Section 4**: Addresses homeownership gap, key wealth-building mechanism
- **Section 5**: Ensures all workers can save for retirement

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Cost | Long-term investment; reduces future inequality |
| Administrative complexity | Build on existing SSA, IRS systems |
| Targeting accuracy | Income-based; automatic |
| Political support | Universal program builds broad support |

### Fair Pay Act

**Purpose**: Address pay inequality through wage transparency, pay equity enforcement, and CEO pay ratio requirements.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Fair Pay Act".

SEC. 2. PAY TRANSPARENCY.

(a) SALARY RANGE DISCLOSURE.—Employers with more than 15 employees
    shall include in any job posting the salary range for the
    position.

(b) PAY HISTORY BAN.—Employers shall not inquire about or rely
    upon a job applicant's salary history in determining
    compensation.

(c) EMPLOYEE DISCUSSION.—No employer shall prohibit employees from
    discussing their compensation with other employees or
    inquiring about the compensation of other employees.

SEC. 3. PAY EQUITY.

(a) EQUAL PAY.—No employer shall pay wages to employees at a rate
    less than the rate paid to employees of another sex, race,
    or national origin for substantially similar work.

(b) BURDEN OF PROOF.—Upon a prima facie showing of pay disparity,
    the burden shall shift to the employer to demonstrate that
    the disparity is based on bona fide factors other than sex,
    race, or national origin.

(c) PAY AUDITS.—Employers with more than 100 employees shall
    conduct annual pay audits and submit summary data to the
    EEOC.

SEC. 4. CEO PAY RATIO.

(a) DISCLOSURE.—Public companies shall disclose annually the
    ratio of CEO total compensation to median worker
    compensation.

(b) TAX INCENTIVE.—The corporate income tax rate shall be
    reduced by 2 percentage points for companies with CEO-to-
    median-worker pay ratios below 50:1.

(c) TAX PENALTY.—The corporate income tax rate shall be
    increased by the following amounts:
    (1) 2 percentage points for ratios between 100:1 and 200:1
    (2) 4 percentage points for ratios between 200:1 and 300:1
    (3) 6 percentage points for ratios above 300:1

SEC. 5. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—Employees may bring civil actions
    for violations of this Act.

(b) DAMAGES.—Prevailing plaintiffs shall be entitled to back pay,
    compensatory damages, and reasonable attorney's fees.

(c) EEOC AUTHORITY.—The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission
    shall have authority to investigate complaints and bring
    enforcement actions.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 180 days after enactment.

```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 2**: Transparency reduces information asymmetry in wage setting
- **Section 3**: Strengthens equal pay enforcement
- **Section 4**: Creates incentives to limit extreme pay ratios

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Business burden | Phases in; builds on existing requirements |
| Definition of "substantially similar" | Courts have developed standards |
| Ratio manipulation | Median worker, not average |
| Constitutional concerns | Commerce Clause authority |

## State Model Legislation

### State Millionaire Tax Act

**Purpose**: Model legislation for states to implement progressive taxes on high incomes.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Fair Share Tax Act".

SECTION 2. ADDITIONAL TAX ON HIGH INCOMES.

(a) IMPOSITION.—In addition to any other tax imposed by this
    chapter, there is imposed on every resident individual a
    tax equal to:
    (1) 2 percent of taxable income over $1,000,000; and
    (2) An additional 2 percent of taxable income over $5,000,000.

(b) NONRESIDENTS.—For nonresident individuals, the tax imposed
    by this section shall apply to income derived from sources
    within this State.

SECTION 3. USE OF FUNDS.

Revenues from the tax imposed by Section 2 shall be deposited
in the [Education/Infrastructure/General] Fund and used for
[specified purposes].

SECTION 4. INFLATION ADJUSTMENT.

The thresholds in Section 2 shall be adjusted annually for
inflation based on the Consumer Price Index.

SECTION 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect for taxable years beginning after
December 31, [year].

```

**Adaptations**:

- Tax rates can be adjusted based on state needs
- Revenue can be dedicated to specific purposes (education, healthcare)
- Thresholds can be adjusted for state income levels

### State Minimum Wage Enhancement Act

**Purpose**: Model legislation for states to establish living wages with indexing.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Living Wage Act".

SECTION 2. MINIMUM WAGE.

(a) RATE.—The minimum wage for all employees in this State shall
    be:
    (1) $15.00 per hour effective [date]
    (2) Increased annually thereafter as provided in Section 3

(b) TIPPED EMPLOYEES.—The separate minimum wage for tipped
    employees is eliminated. All employees shall be paid the
    full minimum wage.

(c) YOUTH WAGE.—There shall be no separate minimum wage for
    employees under 20 years of age.

SECTION 3. ANNUAL ADJUSTMENT.

(a) Beginning on January 1, [year+1], and annually thereafter,
    the minimum wage shall be adjusted based on the greater of:
    (1) The percentage change in the Consumer Price Index; or
    (2) The percentage change in median wages in this State.

(b) The minimum wage shall not decrease.

(c) The [Labor Department] shall publish the adjusted rate by
    October 1 of each year.

SECTION 4. LOCAL AUTHORITY.

Nothing in this Act shall be construed to preempt or limit the
authority of local governments to establish minimum wages higher
than the State minimum wage.

SECTION 5. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) The [Labor Department] shall enforce this Act.

(b) Employees may bring civil actions for violations, with
    recovery of unpaid wages, liquidated damages, and
    attorney's fees.

SECTION 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect on [date].

```

## Regulatory Framework

### Department of Labor Regulations

**Existing Authority**: Fair Labor Standards Act, NLRA

**Draft Regulation**:

```text
PART 541—DEFINING AND DELIMITING THE EXEMPTIONS FOR EXECUTIVE,
ADMINISTRATIVE, PROFESSIONAL, COMPUTER AND OUTSIDE SALES EMPLOYEES

Sec. 541.100 Salary Threshold for Exemptions.

(a) The salary threshold for the executive, administrative, and
    professional exemptions shall be $75,000 annually, updated
    annually based on the 40th percentile of full-time salaried
    workers.

(b) Employers shall not require exempt employees to work more
    than 50 hours per week without additional compensation.

PART 795—EMPLOYEE OR INDEPENDENT CONTRACTOR CLASSIFICATION

Sec. 795.100 Employee Status.

(a) For purposes of the Fair Labor Standards Act, a worker shall
    be considered an employee unless the hiring entity
    demonstrates that the worker:
    (1) Is free from control and direction in performing work;
    (2) Performs work outside the usual course of the hiring
        entity's business; and
    (3) Is customarily engaged in an independently established
        trade or business of the same nature.

(b) Economic dependence on a single hiring entity shall create
    a presumption of employee status.

```

### SEC Regulations

**Existing Authority**: Securities Exchange Act

**Draft Regulation**:

```text
PART 229—STANDARD INSTRUCTIONS FOR FILING FORMS

Item 402—Executive Compensation

(a) CEO PAY RATIO.—In addition to existing disclosure
    requirements, registrants shall disclose:
    (1) The ratio of total CEO compensation to median worker
        compensation, including all employees worldwide;
    (2) The methodology used to identify median worker;
    (3) Changes in the ratio over the past five years.

(b) WORKFORCE COMPENSATION.—Registrants shall disclose:
    (1) Total workforce compensation expense;
    (2) Median, 25th percentile, and 75th percentile worker
        compensation;
    (3) Percentage of workforce earning below $15/hour;
    (4) Percentage of workforce classified as contractors.

```

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

**Taxing Power**

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| Progressive rates | Clearly constitutional (16th Amendment) |
| Capital gains as ordinary income | Constitutional |
| Wealth tax | Debated; some scholars say constitutional without amendment |
| Mark-to-market | Likely constitutional (realization not required) |

**Commerce Clause**

| Legislation | Authority |
|-------------|-----------|
| Minimum wage | Established under FLSA precedent |
| Pay equity | Civil Rights Act model |
| Labor standards | Commerce Clause |

### Preemption Questions

| Area | Federal vs. State |
|------|-------------------|
| Minimum wage | States can exceed federal |
| Labor organizing | NLRA preempts most state law |
| Taxes | States have broad authority |
| Pay equity | States can go beyond federal |

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Law | Enforcement |
|-----|-------------|
| Tax provisions | IRS enforcement |
| Labor standards | DOL Wage and Hour Division |
| Pay equity | EEOC and private lawsuits |
| Securities disclosure | SEC enforcement |

### Sunset and Review Provisions

**Recommended**:

- GAO study of inequality trends every 4 years
- Automatic adjustment of thresholds for inflation
- Congressional review of effectiveness after 10 years
- Data collection requirements for evaluation

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Inequality Reduction Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Income timing | Defer income to years with lower rates | High |
| Trust structures | Use trusts to reduce taxable income | High |
| Offshore arrangements | Move assets offshore | High |
| Charitable deductions | Use charitable contributions to reduce AGI | Medium |
| Pass-through entities | Restructure income as business income | High |
| Cryptocurrency | Digital assets harder to track | Medium |
| Valuation games | Undervalue assets for mark-to-market | High |
| State tax arbitrage | Move to low-tax states | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address existing wealth | Only affects new income | Constitutional constraints |
| Evasion infrastructure exists | Reduces effectiveness | Decades of loophole creation |
| IRS capacity limited | Enforcement gaps | Underfunding |
| International coordination needed | Offshore escape routes | Sovereignty limits |
| Political reversibility | Next administration could repeal | Legislative nature |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Income timing**: Implement averaging provisions; tax deferred compensation when earned
2. **Trust structures**: Grantor trust reforms; attribution rules; trust minimum tax
3. **Offshore arrangements**: Exit taxes; information exchange agreements; deemed repatriation
4. **Charitable deduction gaming**: Cap deductions at 30% AGI; limit appreciated property donations
5. **Pass-through entity abuse**: Limit Section 199A deduction; strengthen reasonable compensation rules
6. **Cryptocurrency**: Require broker reporting; third-party information requirements
7. **Valuation manipulation**: Default to formula valuation; penalties for undervaluation; independent appraisals required
8. **State tax arbitrage**: Source rules for income; domicile requirements; information sharing

### Wealth Building Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Income manipulation | Reduce reported income to get larger deposits | Medium |
| Multiple accounts | Attempt to open accounts for same child | Low |
| Misuse of funds | Use for non-qualifying purposes | Medium |
| Front organizations | Create businesses to access funds improperly | Medium |
| Family gaming | Transfer assets to appear lower income | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Takes 18 years to mature | Doesn't help current adults | Program design |
| Modest amounts | $50K may not close racial wealth gap | Budget constraints |
| Doesn't address existing barriers | Discrimination continues | Limited scope |
| Investment risk | Market downturns could reduce value | Investment-based approach |
| Administrative burden | Tracking millions of accounts | Scale |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Income manipulation**: Use prior-year income; verification requirements; audits
2. **Multiple accounts**: SSN-based unique identifier; cross-reference at enrollment
3. **Misuse of funds**: Verification of qualifying use; clawback provisions
4. **Front organizations**: Business verification requirements; SBA coordination
5. **Family gaming**: Look-back periods; asset tests in addition to income
6. **Immediate need**: Create parallel program for current adults (IDA matching)
7. **Amount adequacy**: Index deposits to inflation; increase deposits if resources allow
8. **Investment risk**: Age-based glidepath; minimum guarantee provisions
9. **Existing barriers**: Pair with anti-discrimination enforcement

### Fair Pay Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Salary range manipulation | Post extremely broad ranges | Medium |
| Job title games | Create unique titles to avoid comparisons | Medium |
| Contractor classification | Classify workers as contractors | High |
| Outsourcing | Contract out low-wage work to reduce ratio | High |
| Stock-based compensation | Exclude stock from worker pay calculations | Medium |
| Geographic differentials | Claim location-based pay differences | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Private companies exempt from ratio | Doesn't cover all employers | Constitutional limits on disclosure |
| Enforcement burden on employees | Many won't bring claims | Litigation-based model |
| Doesn't set maximum ratios | Only disclosure and incentives | Political compromise |
| "Bona fide factors" loophole | Employers can justify disparities | Legal standard |
| Contractor exclusion | Easy to manipulate ratio | Definitional limits |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Broad salary ranges**: Require ranges within 20% spread; justification for wider ranges
2. **Job title games**: Focus on job duties and functions, not titles; comparator pools
3. **Contractor classification**: Include contractor pay in calculations; ABC test for classification
4. **Outsourcing**: Include outsourced workers in ratio calculations; franchise rules
5. **Stock compensation**: Include fair value of equity in all calculations
6. **Geographic claims**: Require specific justification; limit to cost-of-living differentials
7. **Private company gap**: State-level requirements; contractor disclosure
8. **Enforcement**: Shift more burden to EEOC; pattern-or-practice authority
9. **Mandatory ratios**: Build political support; start with incentives, move to caps

### State Model Legislation

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Residency manipulation | Claim residency in lower-tax state | High |
| Income sourcing games | Characterize income as earned elsewhere | High |
| Business structures | Use entities to reduce individual income | Medium |
| Tip credit restoration | Pressure to restore tipped minimum | Medium |
| Preemption | State or federal preemption of local laws | High |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| State-by-state patchwork | Uneven coverage | Federalism |
| Business relocation threats | Political pressure | Capital mobility |
| Underfunded enforcement | Violations go unchecked | Budget constraints |
| Regional variation | One-size-fits-all may not fit all areas | Geographic diversity |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Residency manipulation**: Multi-factor residency tests; audit programs
2. **Income sourcing**: Strong source rules; information sharing between states
3. **Business structures**: Attribution rules; look-through provisions
4. **Tip credit pressure**: Constitutional dedication; supermajority for changes
5. **Preemption**: Legal challenges; build coalitions to prevent
6. **Patchwork**: Work toward federal standards; interstate compacts
7. **Enforcement**: Dedicated funding; whistleblower programs
8. **Regional variation**: Allow regional adjustments; cost-of-living indexing

### Cross-Cutting Issues

#### Systemic Vulnerabilities

| Vulnerability | Affects | Mitigation |
|---------------|---------|------------|
| Political reversibility | All reforms | Constitutional amendments; build public support |
| Judicial rollback | Regulatory and some legislative | Careful drafting; litigation strategy |
| Enforcement capacity | All provisions | IRS/DOL funding; private enforcement |
| International competition | Tax provisions | Coordinate internationally; exit taxes |
| Inflation erosion | Thresholds and amounts | Automatic indexing |
| Administrative burden | Implementation | Phase-in; use existing systems |

#### Monitoring Requirements

| Metric | Frequency | Entity |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Gini coefficient | Annual | Census Bureau |
| Top 1%/0.1% share | Annual | IRS Statistics of Income |
| Racial wealth gap | Annual | Federal Reserve SCF |
| CEO pay ratios | Annual | SEC filings |
| Minimum wage workers | Quarterly | BLS |
| Union density | Annual | BLS |
| Mobility metrics | Decennial | Academic research |

#### Amendment Procedures

1. **Threshold adjustments**: Automatic inflation indexing; periodic review
2. **Rate adjustments**: Require super-majority for reduction; sunset increases
3. **Loophole closure**: Treasury regulatory authority; annual anti-abuse reports
4. **Program evaluation**: GAO mandatory review every 5 years; sunset if ineffective
5. **Coordination**: Federal-state information sharing; international cooperation

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| IRS capacity | Major funding increase; multi-year commitment |
| Tax complexity | Simplification where possible; but complexity sometimes necessary |
| Political sustainability | Build broad coalitions; design for durability |
| Economic transition | Phase-in periods; adjustment assistance |
| Measurement | Invest in data collection; require reporting |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- All provisions should include automatic review after 10 years
- Threshold adjustments every 5 years based on effectiveness
- GAO evaluation of each major program
- Required congressional testimony by implementing agencies
