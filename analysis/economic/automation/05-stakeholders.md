# Automation and the Future of Work: Stakeholders

## Who Benefits from the Status Quo

### Technology Companies

**Who They Are**

- AI/ML developers (OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Meta)
- Robotics manufacturers (Boston Dynamics, Fanuc, ABB)
- Automation software companies (UiPath, Automation Anywhere)
- Cloud computing providers enabling AI

**What They Gain**

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Massive revenue growth | AI/automation market expanding 25%+ annually |
| Market dominance | Network effects and data advantages |
| Stock appreciation | Investors rewarding automation leaders |
| Regulatory freedom | Minimal constraints on development |
| Labor cost arbitrage | Replace expensive workers with software |

**Level of Opposition to Worker-Focused Reform**: **High**

- Significant lobbying resources
- Ideological commitment to unfettered technology
- Financial stake in rapid deployment

### Large Corporations (Automation Adopters)

**Who They Are**

- Major retailers (Amazon, Walmart, Target)
- Logistics companies (FedEx, UPS, XPO)
- Manufacturing (auto, electronics, consumer goods)
- Financial services (banks, insurance, investment)

**What They Gain**

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Labor cost reduction | Fewer workers needed |
| Increased productivity | Output per worker rises |
| Reduced labor disputes | Machines don't organize |
| Competitive advantage | First-movers gain scale |
| Stock market rewards | Layoffs often boost stock price |

**Level of Opposition to Worker-Focused Reform**: **High**

- Business lobby well-organized
- Direct financial interest in automation freedom
- Oppose taxes, mandates, and worker rights

### Capital Owners and Investors

**Who They Are**

- Shareholders in automation companies
- Venture capitalists funding AI startups
- Institutional investors (pension funds, endowments)
- Private equity automating portfolio companies

**What They Gain**

- Returns from productivity gains
- Capital gains taxed at lower rates
- Wealth concentration accelerating
- Power over corporate decisions

**Level of Opposition to Worker-Focused Reform**: **Very High**

- Robot taxes directly affect returns
- Wealth taxes threaten position
- Worker voice reduces shareholder primacy

### Some Consumers

**Who They Are**

- Beneficiaries of lower prices from automation
- Users of convenient automated services
- Higher-income consumers less affected by displacement

**What They Gain**

- Lower prices (potentially)
- Convenient services (self-checkout, autonomous delivery)
- 24/7 availability of automated services

**Ambivalent on Reform**: Support varies by perceived trade-offs

## Who Is Harmed

### Workers in High-Risk Occupations

**Transportation Workers**

| Occupation | Workers | Risk Level | Timeline |
|------------|---------|------------|----------|
| Truck drivers | 3.5 million | Very High | 5-15 years |
| Delivery drivers | 1.5 million | High | 5-15 years |
| Taxi/rideshare drivers | 1 million | Very High | 5-10 years |

**What They Lose**

- Jobs that provide middle-class income without college
- Pathway to entrepreneurship (owner-operators)
- Community and identity tied to occupation

### Communities Facing Concentrated Impacts

**Geographic Vulnerability**

| Region | Primary Exposure | Impact Severity |
|--------|------------------|-----------------|
| Midwest manufacturing towns | Industrial automation | Severe |
| Truck stop corridors | Autonomous vehicles | Severe |
| Rural retail centers | Store automation | High |
| Suburban office parks | Knowledge work automation | Medium |

**What Communities Lose**

- Tax base from employment
- Anchor institutions and businesses
- Young people (brain drain)
- Social cohesion
- Healthcare and services

### Low-Income and Marginalized Groups

**Disproportionate Impact**

| Group | Risk Factor |
|-------|-------------|
| Workers without college degrees | Higher automation exposure |
| Black and Hispanic workers | Concentrated in at-risk occupations |
| Older workers | Harder to retrain, age discrimination |
| Rural workers | Fewer alternative opportunities |
| Immigrants | Often in automatable jobs |

**Compounding Disadvantages**

- Less access to retraining
- Weaker safety net
- Limited savings for transitions
- Discrimination in hiring for new roles

### The Broader Economy

**Systemic Risks**

| Risk | Mechanism |
|------|-----------|
| Demand collapse | Displaced workers can't buy goods/services |
| Tax base erosion | Fewer workers paying income/payroll taxes |
| Social instability | Economic insecurity fuels extremism |
| Political dysfunction | Automation losers lose political voice |

## Who Has Power to Change Things

### Congress

**Power Level**: High (but largely inactive)

**What They Could Do**

- Pass automation tax legislation
- Fund workforce development
- Mandate automation disclosure
- Create transition assistance programs
- Reform unemployment insurance
- Pilot UBI programs

**Why They Don't**

- Tech/business lobbying
- Ideological opposition
- Lack of public pressure
- Divided government

### Executive Branch

**Power Level**: Medium

**What They Can Do**

- Executive orders on federal contractors
- Regulatory enforcement
- Research funding priorities
- Convening and agenda-setting
- Agency guidance on AI

**Limitations**

- Major changes need legislation
- Limited regulatory authority over automation
- Reversible by next administration

### State Governments

**Power Level**: Medium (significant in their jurisdiction)

**What They Can Do**

- State unemployment insurance modernization
- Workforce development programs
- Tax incentives/disincentives
- Automation disclosure requirements
- Pilot programs for UBI, retraining

**Variation**

- Blue states more likely to act
- Red states more likely to prioritize business
- Creates patchwork

### Labor Movement

**Power Level**: Low-Medium (weakened but organizing)

**What They Can Do**

- Organize workers facing automation
- Negotiate automation provisions in contracts
- Advocate for policy change
- Build public awareness
- Partner with communities

**Challenges**

- Low union density
- Difficult to organize automated workplaces
- Employer resistance
- Legal barriers

### Technology Workers

**Power Level**: Emerging

**What They Can Do**

- Internal organizing at tech companies
- Refuse to build harmful applications
- Whistleblowing
- Public advocacy
- Union organizing

**Notable Actions**

- Google walkouts
- Amazon organizing
- Tech worker advocacy groups
- AI ethics concerns raised internally

### Research and Advocacy Community

**Power Level**: Indirect but significant

**Key Players**

| Organization | Focus |
|--------------|-------|
| MIT Work of the Future | Research, recommendations |
| Economic Policy Institute | Worker-focused analysis |
| Brookings | Policy development |
| Economic Security Project | UBI advocacy |
| AI Now Institute | AI ethics and accountability |

**Role**

- Shape public understanding
- Develop policy options
- Counter industry narratives
- Inform policymakers

## Potential Allies

### Cross-Class Coalition

**Small Businesses**

- Compete against automated large companies
- Face pressure to automate
- Some aligned with worker concerns

**Professional Workers**

- Newly at risk from AI
- May join broader coalition
- Have political voice

**Faith Communities**

- Catholic social teaching on work
- Interfaith worker justice
- Community rootedness

### Unexpected Allies

**Some Business Leaders**

- Concerned about demand collapse
- Recognize need for stability
- Long-term business interest in healthy economy

**Moderate Conservatives**

- Concerned about community disruption
- National security implications
- Work ethic values

**Technology Critics**

- Within tech community
- AI safety researchers
- Tech workers organizing

### The Power Imbalance

| Factor | Reform Coalition | Opposition Coalition |
|--------|------------------|----------------------|
| Numbers | Millions affected | Small elite |
| Money | Very limited | Billions available |
| Organization | Fragmented | Well-coordinated |
| Political access | Limited | Extensive |
| Narrative control | Challenging | Well-established |
| Time horizon | Urgent need | Can delay indefinitely |

## Strategic Implications

### Building Worker Power

**Short-Term**

- Support automation-affected worker organizing
- Build coalitions across affected occupations
- Connect with tech workers raising concerns
- Engage affected communities

**Long-Term**

- Rebuild labor movement capacity
- Create cross-sectoral solidarity
- Develop political power
- Change public narrative

### Coalition Strategy

| Partner | Common Interest | Engagement Strategy |
|---------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Displaced workers | Direct interest | Organizing |
| Tech workers | Ethics concerns | Alliance building |
| Small business | Competition concerns | Policy coalition |
| Faith communities | Values alignment | Mobilization |
| Researchers | Evidence base | Information sharing |

### Political Strategy

**Electoral**

- Support candidates with automation policy
- Make automation a campaign issue
- Build constituent pressure
- Target key districts

**Legislative**

- Build champions in Congress
- Develop specific legislation
- Counter industry lobbying
- Create proof points at state level
