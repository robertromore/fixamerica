# Monetary Policy: History

## Timeline of American Monetary Policy

### Pre-Federal Reserve Era (1776-1913)

**Early Republic**

| Period | System | Issues |
|--------|--------|--------|
| 1776-1791 | No central bank | Chaotic currency |
| 1791-1811 | First Bank of US | Hamilton's creation, charter expired |
| 1811-1816 | No central bank | War of 1812 financing chaos |
| 1816-1836 | Second Bank of US | Jackson killed re-charter |
| 1836-1863 | "Free banking" | Thousands of different currencies |
| 1863-1913 | National Banking System | Periodic panics |

**Financial Panics**

| Year | Crisis | Impact |
|------|--------|--------|
| 1819 | First major depression | Land speculation collapse |
| 1837 | Panic | Bank failures, depression |
| 1857 | Panic | Railroad speculation |
| 1873 | "Long Depression" | 6 years of contraction |
| 1893 | Panic | Gold standard strains |
| 1907 | Panic | JP Morgan's private bailout |

### Creation of the Federal Reserve (1913)

**The 1907 Panic's Aftermath**

| Development | Outcome |
|-------------|---------|
| JP Morgan's intervention | Showed need for lender of last resort |
| Aldrich Plan | Drafted by bankers at Jekyll Island |
| Democratic opposition | Feared Wall Street control |
| Federal Reserve Act (1913) | Compromise structure |

**Original Design**

| Feature | Purpose |
|---------|---------|
| 12 regional banks | Decentralize power |
| Private bank ownership | Banker buy-in |
| Board of Governors | Public oversight |
| Real bills doctrine | Conservative lending |
| Gold standard | Anchor currency |

### The Fed's Early Years (1913-1929)

**Learning to Operate**

| Period | Policy | Outcome |
|--------|--------|---------|
| 1914-1918 | WWI financing | Accommodated war spending |
| 1919-1920 | Post-war inflation | Prices doubled |
| 1920-1921 | Sharp deflation | Brutal recession |
| 1921-1929 | Stable growth | "Roaring Twenties" |
| Late 1920s | Stock speculation | Failed to restrain bubble |

### The Great Depression (1929-1941)

**The Fed's Greatest Failure**

| Year | Fed action | Consequence |
|------|------------|-------------|
| 1929 | Crash occurs | Limited response |
| 1930 | Raised rates | Worsened contraction |
| 1930-1933 | Let banks fail | 9,000 banks collapsed |
| 1931 | Defended gold | Chose gold over economy |
| 1932-1933 | GDP fell 30% | Unemployment 25% |

**Lessons Learned**

| Mistake | Understanding |
|---------|---------------|
| Passive response | Lender of last resort essential |
| Gold standard priority | Domestic economy should come first |
| Bank failures | Systemic risk is real |
| Deflation | Can be as dangerous as inflation |

### World War II and Bretton Woods (1941-1971)

**War Finance**

| Policy | Purpose |
|--------|---------|
| Interest rate pegging | Keep war borrowing costs low |
| Credit controls | Direct lending |
| Fed-Treasury coordination | War effort priority |

**Post-War System**

| Feature | Structure |
|---------|-----------|
| Bretton Woods | Dollar pegged to gold at $35/oz |
| Other currencies | Pegged to dollar |
| Capital controls | Limited international flows |
| Financial repression | Negative real rates |

**Treasury Accord of 1951**

| Before | After |
|--------|-------|
| Fed subordinate to Treasury | Fed gains independence |
| Pegged interest rates | Flexible rates |
| War finance mode | Normal operations |

### The Great Inflation (1965-1982)

**Origins**

| Factor | Contribution |
|--------|--------------|
| Vietnam War spending | Fiscal stimulus |
| Great Society programs | More spending |
| Nixon's pressure on Fed | Political interference |
| Oil shocks (1973, 1979) | Supply disruption |
| Wage-price spirals | Embedded expectations |

**Inflation Timeline**

| Year | Inflation rate | Fed Funds Rate |
|------|----------------|----------------|
| 1965 | 1.6% | 4.1% |
| 1970 | 5.7% | 7.2% |
| 1974 | 11.0% | 10.5% |
| 1979 | 11.3% | 11.2% |
| 1980 | 13.5% | 13.4% |

**Paul Volcker's Disinflation (1979-1982)**

| Action | Consequence |
|--------|-------------|
| Raised rates to 20% | Crushed inflation |
| Deep recession | Unemployment hit 10.8% |
| Political pressure | Reagan supported Volcker |
| Credibility restored | Inflation expectations anchored |
| Distributional impact | Workers bore the cost |

### The Great Moderation (1982-2007)

**Characteristics**

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Lower inflation | 2-4% typical |
| Milder recessions | 1990-91, 2001 brief |
| Financial deregulation | Banks expanded |
| Asset bubbles | Stocks (2000), housing (2006) |
| Increasing inequality | Wages stagnated |

**Key Fed Chairs**

| Chair | Era | Legacy |
|-------|-----|--------|
| Volcker (1979-1987) | Defeated inflation | Hero to markets |
| Greenspan (1987-2006) | "Maestro" reputation | Later criticized for bubbles |
| Bernanke (2006-2014) | Crisis response | Unconventional policy |

**The "Greenspan Put"**

| Concept | Practice |
|---------|----------|
| Asymmetric response | Cut rates on downturns |
| Asset bubble tolerance | Let them inflate |
| Moral hazard | Markets expected rescue |
| Deregulation support | Opposed derivatives oversight |

### The Global Financial Crisis (2007-2009)

**Crisis Timeline**

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| August 2007 | Subprime problems emerge |
| March 2008 | Bear Stearns rescued |
| September 2008 | Lehman Brothers fails |
| September 2008 | AIG bailout |
| October 2008 | TARP passed |
| December 2008 | Rates cut to zero |

**Fed Emergency Actions**

| Program | Purpose | Scale |
|---------|---------|-------|
| Rate cuts | Zero lower bound | 0-0.25% |
| QE1 | Asset purchases | $1.7 trillion |
| Term lending facilities | Bank liquidity | Trillions |
| AIG rescue | Systemic risk | $182 billion |
| Dollar swap lines | Global liquidity | Unlimited |

### Post-Crisis Era (2009-2019)

**Quantitative Easing Programs**

| Program | Dates | Size | Focus |
|---------|-------|------|-------|
| QE1 | 2008-2010 | $1.7T | MBS, Treasuries |
| QE2 | 2010-2011 | $600B | Treasuries |
| Operation Twist | 2011-2012 | $400B | Long-dated |
| QE3 | 2012-2014 | $85B/month | Open-ended |

**Slow Recovery**

| Metric | Reality |
|--------|---------|
| Growth | 2% average (below trend) |
| Unemployment | Slow decline |
| Inflation | Below 2% target |
| Wages | Stagnant |
| Inequality | Continued rising |

**Normalization Attempt**

| Year | Action |
|------|--------|
| 2015 | First rate hike |
| 2017-2018 | Gradual increases |
| 2018-2019 | Balance sheet runoff |
| Late 2019 | Repo market stress, cuts began |

### COVID-19 Crisis (2020-Present)

**Emergency Response**

| Action | Scale | Speed |
|--------|-------|-------|
| Rate cuts | To zero | Days |
| QE | $120B/month | Immediate |
| Corporate bond buying | New authority | First time ever |
| Main Street lending | Small business | Limited uptake |
| Municipal bonds | State/local support | Limited |

**Inflation Surge**

| Factor | Contribution |
|--------|--------------|
| Supply chain disruptions | Major |
| Fiscal stimulus | Significant |
| Monetary stimulus | Contributed |
| Energy prices | Substantial |
| Wage pressures | Growing |

**Policy Reversal**

| Period | Action |
|--------|--------|
| 2021 | "Transitory" view |
| Late 2021 | Acknowledged persistence |
| 2022 | Fastest hikes in 40 years |
| 2023-2024 | High rates maintained |

## Evolution of Fed Doctrine

### Shifting Frameworks

| Era | Framework | Focus |
|-----|-----------|-------|
| 1913-1930s | Real bills doctrine | Self-liquidating loans |
| 1930s-1950s | Fiscal dominance | Support Treasury |
| 1950s-1970s | Fine-tuning | Manage business cycle |
| 1979-1990s | Monetarism influence | Control money supply |
| 1990s-2020 | Inflation targeting | 2% implicit target |
| 2020-present | Flexible average IT | Make up for undershoots |

### Key Intellectual Shifts

| Shift | Old view | New view |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Phillips curve | Stable tradeoff | Shifts over time |
| Money supply | Key target | Less reliable |
| Expectations | Secondary | Central |
| Financial stability | Separate issue | Core mandate |
| Communication | Minimal | Strategic |
