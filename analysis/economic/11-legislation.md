# Economic Reform: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Economic reform requires legislative action at multiple levels: constitutional amendments to address fundamental structural issues, federal legislation to establish national standards and protections, state legislation to advance reform where federal action is blocked, and regulatory frameworks to implement and enforce the law. This document provides an overview of the legislative landscape for economic policy reform and links to detailed legislation in each of the 14 subtopic areas.

Economic legislation faces particular challenges due to:

- First Amendment protections limiting campaign finance regulation
- Commerce Clause and federalism constraints
- Supreme Court doctrines hostile to economic regulation
- Intense lobbying from affected industries
- Constitutional requirements for revenue legislation

### Anti-Corruption Amendment

**Purpose**: Enable Congress to regulate money in politics and restore democratic control over economic policy.

**Draft Text**:

```text
Section 1. To advance democratic self-government and political
equality, and to protect the integrity of government and the
electoral process, Congress and the States may regulate and set
reasonable limits on the raising and spending of money by
candidates and others to influence elections.

Section 2. Congress and the States shall have power to implement
and enforce this article by appropriate legislation, and may
distinguish between natural persons and corporations or other
artificial entities created by law, including by prohibiting
such entities from spending money to influence elections.

Section 3. Nothing in this article shall be construed to grant
Congress or the States the power to abridge the freedom of the
press.
```

**Explanation**: This amendment, based on proposals from groups like American Promise, would overturn Citizens United and allow comprehensive campaign finance regulation. Economic policy reform is extremely difficult when wealthy interests can spend unlimited amounts on elections.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Constitutional amendments require supermajority support
- Framing as limiting "free speech" by opponents
- Entrenched interests will spend heavily against

**Refinements**: Build broad coalition including campaign finance reformers, labor, small business, and cross-partisan anti-corruption advocates.

### Key Regulatory Actions

**FTC Non-Compete Rule**

Existing authority under Section 5 of FTC Act to ban non-compete agreements as unfair methods of competition. Would free workers to seek better opportunities.

**DOL Overtime Rule**

Update salary threshold for overtime eligibility, potentially covering millions more workers.

**NLRB Joint Employer Standard**

Restore standard making franchisors and contractor employers jointly responsible for labor law.

**Treasury Tax Enforcement**

Prioritize wealthy taxpayer audits; issue guidance closing loopholes.

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Wealth tax avoidance | Hard-to-value assets, trusts, expatriation | High |
| Corporate inversions | Relocating headquarters abroad | Medium |
| Gig economy classification | Workers misclassified as contractors | High |
| Antitrust consent decree violations | Weak enforcement of agreements | Medium |
| Regulatory arbitrage | Moving activity to less-regulated sectors | High |
| Tax shelter evolution | New schemes emerge as old ones closed | High |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Constitutional constraints | Wealth tax may face legal challenge | Judicial interpretation |
| Enforcement capacity | IRS, antitrust agencies underfunded | Appropriations |
| Preemption | Federal inaction while blocking state action | Federalism doctrine |
| Judicial hostility | Courts may strike down reforms | Conservative judiciary |
| Regulatory capture | Agencies may favor regulated industries | Revolving door, lobbying |
| Filibuster | Senate rules block most legislation | Institutional rules |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Wealth Tax Enforcement**: Include comprehensive reporting requirements, valuation rules, exit taxes, and IRS enforcement funding. Consider constitutional amendment if courts strike down.

2. **Anti-Avoidance Provisions**: Include general anti-avoidance rules in tax legislation; sunset provisions requiring Congressional reauthorization; regular review and closure of emerging loopholes.

3. **Agency Independence**: Protect CFPB, NLRB, FTC from political interference through for-cause removal protection, independent funding where possible, and whistleblower protections.

4. **Enforcement Funding**: Tie enforcement funding to mandatory rather than discretionary appropriations; create dedicated revenue streams.

5. **Private Rights of Action**: Enable individuals to enforce economic protections through private lawsuits when agencies fail to act.

6. **State Backup**: Design federal legislation to enable rather than preempt stronger state action; preserve state attorney general authority.

### General Implementation Concerns

- Draft legislation with constitutional challenges in mind
- Include severability clauses
- Build implementing agency capacity before effective date
- Phase in requirements to allow adjustment
- Create robust reporting and transparency requirements
- Establish regular review and updating mechanisms
- Fund enforcement adequately
- Protect against regulatory capture

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Authority

| Reform Area | Primary Authority | Limitations |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Taxation | Art. I, § 8 (taxing power); 16th Amendment | Direct tax issues for wealth tax |
| Antitrust | Commerce Clause | Some state action doctrine issues |
| Labor regulation | Commerce Clause | State action on public employees |
| Financial regulation | Commerce Clause | First Amendment (disclosure) |
| Trade | Art. I, § 8 (regulate commerce) | Treaty obligations |
| Corporate governance | Commerce Clause; state chartering | Federalism limits |

### Key Precedents

| Case | Holding | Impact on Reform |
|------|---------|------------------|
| *NFIB v. Sebelius* (2012) | Commerce Clause limits | May affect regulatory mandates |
| *Citizens United v. FEC* (2010) | Corporate speech protected | Blocks some campaign finance reform |
| *Lochner v. New York* (1905) | Due process limits regulation | Overruled but echoes in property rights |
| *West Coast Hotel v. Parrish* (1937) | Minimum wage constitutional | Supports labor standards |
| *Hylton v. United States* (1796) | Carriage tax not direct | Informs wealth tax debate |
| *Pollock v. Farmers' Loan* (1895) | Income tax unconstitutional | Led to 16th Amendment |

### Preemption Questions

| Area | Federal Preemption Issue |
|------|--------------------------|
| Labor law | NLRA preempts some state labor laws |
| Banking | OCC may preempt state banking regulation |
| Employee benefits | ERISA preempts state benefit mandates |
| Securities | Federal securities law may preempt state actions |
| Corporate governance | Internal affairs doctrine (state law) |

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Application |
|-----------|-------------|
| Agency enforcement | DOJ, FTC, SEC, CFPB, IRS |
| Private rights of action | Antitrust, securities, civil rights |
| State attorney general | Consumer protection, antitrust |
| Qui tam actions | Whistleblower suits (some areas) |
| Criminal prosecution | Willful tax evasion, antitrust |
