# Taxation: Stakeholders

## Overview

Tax policy affects virtually everyone in American society, but not equally. This analysis identifies the key groups with stakes in tax policy, their interests, their power to influence outcomes, and their positions on reform.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Working and Middle-Class Taxpayers

**Profile**:

- ~150 million households
- Primary income from wages subject to withholding
- Limited deductions, pay full marginal rates
- Payroll taxes take significant share

**Interests**:

| Interest | Priority |
|----------|----------|
| Lower tax burden | High |
| Fairness (wealthy pay fair share) | High |
| Adequate public services | Medium-High |
| Simplicity | Medium |
| Economic opportunity | High |

**Current Treatment**:

- Bear disproportionate share of payroll taxes
- Limited access to preferential rates
- Automatic withholding ensures compliance
- Few planning opportunities

**Political Power**: High in theory (voting numbers), low in practice (diffuse interests, limited organization).

### Wealthy Individuals and Families

**Profile**:

- Top 1%: ~1.3 million households
- Top 0.1%: ~130,000 households
- Income primarily from capital (investments, business)
- Access to sophisticated planning

**Interests**:

| Interest | Priority |
|----------|----------|
| Lower tax rates | High |
| Preferential capital gains rates | Very High |
| Preservation of stepped-up basis | Very High |
| Estate tax minimization | High |
| Pass-through advantages | High |

**Current Treatment**:

- Access to preferential capital rates
- Stepped-up basis eliminates lifetime gains
- Complex planning reduces effective rates
- Lower audit probability despite higher non-compliance

**Political Power**: Very high (campaign contributions, lobbying, elite networks).

### Small Business Owners

**Profile**:

- ~30 million small businesses
- Diverse: sole proprietors to S-corps
- Mix of labor and capital income
- Varying sophistication in tax planning

**Interests**:

| Interest | Priority |
|----------|----------|
| Simplified compliance | High |
| Lower rates | High |
| Pass-through deduction | High |
| Self-employment tax relief | Medium |
| Clear rules | High |

**Current Treatment**:

- Complex compliance requirements
- Section 199A deduction (20%) benefits many
- Self-employment tax burdensome
- Limited resources for planning

**Political Power**: Moderate (organized through NFIB, chambers of commerce).

### Large Corporations

**Profile**:

- Fortune 500 and major multinational corporations
- Sophisticated tax departments
- Global operations enabling profit shifting
- Significant political influence

**Interests**:

| Interest | Priority |
|----------|----------|
| Low statutory rate | High |
| International tax minimization | Very High |
| Specific industry preferences | High |
| Predictability | Medium |
| Weak enforcement | High |

**Current Treatment**:

- 21% statutory rate (down from 35%)
- Extensive planning opportunities
- Territorial system for foreign income
- Effective rates far below statutory rate

**Political Power**: Very high (lobbying, campaign contributions, revolving door).

### Financial Services Industry

**Profile**:

- Banks, hedge funds, private equity, asset managers
- Highly dependent on tax policy
- Carried interest major benefit
- Sophisticated in policy influence

**Interests**:

| Interest | Priority |
|----------|----------|
| Carried interest preservation | Very High |
| Capital gains preference | Very High |
| Complex product tax treatment | High |
| Light-touch enforcement | High |
| Tax-advantaged products | High |

**Current Treatment**:

- Carried interest taxed as capital gains
- Manages trillions in tax-advantaged accounts
- Creates complex structures for clients
- Significant tax arbitrage opportunities

**Political Power**: Very high (major campaign donors, revolving door with Treasury).

### Tax Professionals

**Profile**:

- CPAs, tax attorneys, enrolled agents
- Big Four accounting firms to sole practitioners
- Industry dependent on tax complexity
- Provides expertise to all stakeholders

**Interests**:

| Interest | Priority |
|----------|----------|
| Complexity (demand for services) | High |
| Professional standards | Medium |
| Certainty (ability to advise clients) | High |
| Enforcement (drives compliance work) | Mixed |

**Current Treatment**:

- Thriving industry due to complexity
- Preparer penalties increase standards
- Return preparer registration debated
- Big Four firms have enormous influence

**Political Power**: High (AICPA, tax bar associations, Big Four lobbying).

## Government Stakeholders

### Internal Revenue Service

**Profile**:

- ~78,000 employees
- Responsible for collection and enforcement
- Underfunded and understaffed
- Technology modernization challenges

**Interests**:

| Interest | Priority |
|----------|----------|
| Adequate funding | Very High |
| Clear legal authority | High |
| Technology modernization | High |
| Staff retention | High |
| Public trust | High |

**Current Challenges**:

- Budget cut 20%+ in real terms since 2010
- Workforce aging, difficulty hiring
- Outdated technology systems
- Complex returns outpace resources

**Political Power**: Limited (politically vulnerable target).

### Treasury Department

**Profile**:

- Sets tax policy in executive branch
- Writes regulations implementing tax law
- International tax negotiation
- Revenue estimation

**Interests**:

| Interest | Priority |
|----------|----------|
| Revenue adequacy | High |
| Administrable rules | High |
| International cooperation | Medium |
| Economic growth | High |

**Role**: Shapes policy through guidance and regulatory interpretation.

### Congressional Tax Committees

**Profile**:

- House Ways and Means Committee
- Senate Finance Committee
- Joint Committee on Taxation

**Interests**: Vary by member ideology, constituency, and donor base.

**Role**: Originate tax legislation, conduct oversight.

### State Governments

**Profile**:

- 50 states with varying tax structures
- Compete for businesses and residents
- Depend on federal conformity decisions

**Interests**:

| Interest | Priority |
|----------|----------|
| Revenue adequacy | High |
| Economic competitiveness | High |
| Administrative simplicity | Medium |
| SALT deduction preservation | High (high-tax states) |

**Current Challenges**:

- Federal conformity choices
- Interstate competition
- Tax base erosion
- Remote work implications

## Advocacy Organizations

### Progressive Tax Reform Advocates

| Organization | Focus | Approach |
|--------------|-------|----------|
| Americans for Tax Fairness | Comprehensive reform | Grassroots, coalition |
| Institute on Taxation and Economic Policy | Research, analysis | Technical expertise |
| Tax Policy Center | Nonpartisan analysis | Distributional modeling |
| Patriotic Millionaires | Wealthy supporting fairness | Moral authority |
| Oxfam | Global inequality | International coordination |

**Positions**: Higher taxes on wealth, corporate reform, IRS funding.

### Conservative/Anti-Tax Organizations

| Organization | Focus | Approach |
|--------------|-------|----------|
| Americans for Tax Reform | No tax increases | Taxpayer Protection Pledge |
| Heritage Foundation | Limited government | Policy research |
| Cato Institute | Libertarian tax policy | Academic influence |
| Tax Foundation | Business-friendly analysis | Modeling, ratings |
| Club for Growth | Primary challengers | Electoral pressure |

**Positions**: Lower rates, limited government, oppose enforcement.

### Industry Groups

| Organization | Focus | Members |
|--------------|-------|---------|
| Business Roundtable | Corporate tax | Major corporations |
| U.S. Chamber of Commerce | Broad business | Wide membership |
| National Association of Manufacturers | Manufacturing | Industrial companies |
| Private Equity Council | Carried interest | PE firms |
| Securities Industry Association | Financial services | Banks, brokers |

**Positions**: Lower corporate rates, preserve preferences, limited enforcement.

## Affected Populations

### Low-Income Households

**Profile**:

- ~40 million households
- May receive EITC, Child Tax Credit
- Disproportionately affected by regressive taxes
- Often targeted by predatory preparers

**Interests**:

| Interest | Priority |
|----------|----------|
| Refundable credits | Very High |
| Free/low-cost filing | High |
| Simple compliance | High |
| Adequate public services | High |

**Current Treatment**:

- EITC and CTC provide significant benefits
- High audit rates relative to tax owed
- Regressive payroll and consumption taxes
- Limited representation in policy debates

### Retirees

**Profile**:

- ~50 million Social Security recipients
- Taxable Social Security income for some
- Dependent on estate tax for inherited wealth
- Medicare funded by payroll taxes

**Interests**:

| Interest | Priority |
|----------|----------|
| Social Security benefit taxation | High |
| Medicare funding | High |
| Investment income rates | Medium |
| Estate tax (for wealthy retirees) | Varies |

### Future Generations

**Profile**:

- Non-voting constituency
- Bear burden of current fiscal choices
- Inherit both wealth and debt

**Interests**:

| Interest | Priority |
|----------|----------|
| Fiscal sustainability | Very High |
| Climate investment (funded by taxes) | High |
| Fair inheritance system | High |
| Adequate public investment | Very High |

**Representation**: Limited; some advocacy by deficit hawks.

## Key Dynamics

### Asymmetric Organization

- Pro-status quo groups are well-organized and funded
- Reform supporters are diffuse and under-resourced
- Policy knowledge concentrated among beneficiaries
- Public attention episodic

### Information Advantages

- Wealthy understand their interests clearly
- Working families face complex trade-offs
- Technical complexity obscures distributional effects
- Framing shapes public perception

### Access Disparities

| Stakeholder | Congressional Access |
|-------------|---------------------|
| Corporate lobbyists | Daily meetings |
| Wealthy donors | Fundraiser access |
| Small business | Occasional hearings |
| Working families | Rare town halls |
| Low-income | Almost none |

## Implications for Reform

### Building Reform Coalitions

**Essential Partners**:

- Labor unions (organized workers)
- Progressive advocacy groups
- Academics and think tanks
- Sympathetic wealthy (Patriotic Millionaires)
- State and local officials

**Potential Partners**:

- Small business (some interests align)
- Deficit hawks (revenue focus)
- Good government groups

### Overcoming Opposition

| Opponent | Approach |
|----------|----------|
| Wealthy individuals | Moral framing, public pressure |
| Corporations | International coordination |
| Financial services | Narrow, targeted reforms |
| Tax professionals | Ensure continued role |
| Anti-tax groups | Change narrative |
