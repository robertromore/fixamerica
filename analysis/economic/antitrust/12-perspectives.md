# Antitrust: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

Antitrust policy occupies a distinctive position in American political debate because it draws concern from across the ideological spectrum, yet perspectives diverge sharply on what the problem is and how to fix it. Conservatives and libertarians historically championed the Chicago School's consumer welfare standard, but rising populist concern about Big Tech has fractured that consensus. Progressives and democratic socialists view concentration as a structural threat to both democracy and economic fairness. Centrists seek evidence-based enforcement modernization. This analysis evaluates how nine political perspectives assess market concentration, proposed solutions ranging from breaking up Big Tech to expanding agency resources, and specific legislation including the Competition and Antitrust Law Enforcement Reform Act, the American Innovation and Choice Online Act, the Prohibition on Anticompetitive Mergers Act, and the Workforce Mobility Act.

## Perspective Analyses

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 3/5 - Mixed Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Claim to defend free markets, but historically protect incumbent firms from enforcement |
| Principle consistency | Inconsistent: support antitrust when targeting Big Tech censorship, oppose it for traditional industries |
| Goalpost stability | Shifting: some conservatives now embrace antitrust action against tech companies |
| Zero-sum behavior | Moderate: willing to engage on Big Tech but resist broader competition reform |

**Justification:** Conservative antitrust positions have become internally divided. Traditional pro-business conservatives defend the consumer welfare standard and oppose expanded enforcement, while a newer faction targets Big Tech for alleged anti-conservative bias. This creates unpredictability regarding which antitrust proposals conservatives will support.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Heritage Foundation traditionally opposed antitrust expansion; some fellows now support Big Tech regulation
- Republican senators split on American Innovation and Choice Online Act (some co-sponsored, others opposed)
- Conservative state AGs joined Google and Meta antitrust suits while opposing FTC rulemaking authority

| Root Cause Agreement | 3/10 | Reject Chicago School critique; attribute concentration to innovation and efficiency, not regulatory failure |

#### Solution Evaluations

| Solution | Score | Stated Position | Underlying Concerns |
|----------|-------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Competition standard (replacing consumer welfare) | 3/10 | Too vague; invites political manipulation of enforcement | Fear of subjective enforcement targeting Republican-aligned industries |
| Increased agency resources | 3/10 | Government agencies are already too powerful | Concern about regulatory overreach and political weaponization |

#### Alternative Proposals

- Targeted enforcement against specific Big Tech platforms engaged in content moderation bias
- Deregulation to lower barriers to entry instead of enforcement against incumbents
- Tort reform allowing private parties to sue monopolists more easily
- Voluntary codes of conduct for dominant platforms

#### Coalition Potential

- **Natural allies:** Libertarian (on deregulation), Populist (on Big Tech)
- **Potential bridges:** Centrist (on targeted enforcement), Constitutionalist (on limited federal power)
- **Unlikely partners:** Progressive, Democratic Socialist (on structural reform)

### Libertarian Perspective

**Justification:** Libertarians hold the most intellectually consistent anti-regulation position on antitrust. They argue that government intervention causes more harm than market concentration, and they apply this principle uniformly. Their challenge is that this consistency leads them to oppose even popular reforms.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Cato Institute consistently opposes antitrust expansion across administrations
- Libertarian scholars like Richard Epstein argue against most antitrust intervention
- Libertarian Party platform opposes antitrust laws as government overreach

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The Liberal-Progressive-Centrist-Populist coalition offers the strongest path to antitrust reform, with the Workforce Mobility Act and American Innovation and Choice Online Act representing the most achievable legislative priorities. Adding Democratic Socialist support broadens the coalition, while targeted outreach to reform-minded Conservatives and Libertarians (on non-compete issues) can build bipartisan credibility.

### Key Obstacles

The primary obstacle is the Conservative-Libertarian bloc that opposes expanded government authority on principle. However, this bloc is fractured on Big Tech issues, creating an opening for targeted reforms. The Religious Right's low consistency makes them an unreliable coalition partner despite shared Big Tech concerns. Industry lobbying remains a cross-cutting obstacle affecting all perspectives.

### Low Consistency Partners

Conservative and Populist perspectives (both rated 3/5) require careful coalition management. Their support for Big Tech regulation is genuine but narrowly motivated. Building durable coalitions requires framing reforms in terms they can sustain: economic freedom (Conservatives), anti-elite accountability (Populists). The Religious Right (2/5) should not be relied upon for coalition building beyond content-specific legislation.

### Recommended Approach

Pursue a three-track strategy: (1) Lead with the non-compete ban and Big Tech regulation, which command the broadest support; (2) Build toward merger review modernization through administrative action and bipartisan fee legislation; (3) Lay the groundwork for comprehensive reform through public education about concentration harms and international examples. This sequencing builds momentum while expanding the coalition for more ambitious reform.
