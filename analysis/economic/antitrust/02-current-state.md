# Antitrust: Current State

## Market Concentration Today

### Overview

Corporate concentration in the United States has reached its highest levels since the Gilded Age. Across industry after industry, a handful of firms dominate markets that were once competitive. This concentration affects prices, wages, innovation, and the distribution of economic and political power.

## Labor Market Concentration

### Employer Market Power

Research reveals significant labor market concentration:

**Concentration Statistics**

- 60% of labor markets are "highly concentrated" under merger guidelines[^2]
- Wages are 17% lower in highly concentrated markets
- Workers in concentrated markets have fewer outside options
- Rural and specialized labor markets especially affected

**Mechanisms of Control**

| Practice | Prevalence | Effect |
|----------|------------|--------|
| Non-compete agreements | 18% of workers | Reduces mobility |
| No-poach agreements | Common in franchises | Suppresses wages |
| Information sharing | Through HR data vendors | Wage coordination |
| Occupational licensing | 25% of workforce | Barriers to entry |

### Wage Suppression

**Effects of Monopsony Power**

| Factor | Estimated Wage Impact |
|--------|----------------------|
| Employer concentration | -15 to -25% |
| Non-compete enforcement | -4 to -10% |
| No-poach agreements | -3 to -5% |
| Information sharing | Unknown but significant |

## Economic Effects

### Prices

**Research Findings**

| Sector | Price Effect of Concentration |
|--------|------------------------------|
| Airlines | +30% where merger reduced carriers |
| Hospitals | +20-40% after mergers |
| Cable TV | Highest prices with least competition |
| Beer | +8% after InBev/SABMiller |

### Innovation

**Research Findings**

- Startup formation declined 50% since 1980
- Incumbent acquisition of startups ("killer acquisitions") common
- R&D spending flat relative to concentration
- Breakthrough innovation may be declining

### Wages

**Labor Market Effects**

| Factor | Wage Impact |
|--------|-------------|
| Employer concentration | -17% average |
| Merger-induced concentration | -3-7% |
| Monopsony across economy | $1+ trillion/year transfer |

### Inequality

**Concentration and Inequality**

- 60% of rising profit share due to rising markups
- Markups transferred from workers/consumers to shareholders
- Corporate concentration drives wealth concentration
- Regional inequality as headquarters concentrate

## Key Trends

### Increasing Concentration

- Concentration rising in 75% of industries since 1990
- Mergers continue at high levels
- Digital markets create new monopolies
- Private equity roll-ups accelerate

### Enforcement Revival

- Biden administration most aggressive since 1970s
- New merger guidelines (2023)
- Big Tech focus across agencies
- Bipartisan interest in some areas

### Legal Uncertainty

- Chicago School still dominant in courts
- New theories being tested
- Supreme Court cases pending
- Legislative reform debated

## Key Takeaways

1. **Concentration is pervasive**: Most major industries dominated by few firms
2. **Tech giants unprecedented**: Market power exceeds historical monopolies
3. **Workers affected**: Labor market concentration suppresses wages
4. **Enforcement reviving**: But decades of under-enforcement to overcome
5. **Legal framework outdated**: Current law may not address modern monopolies
6. **International divergence**: US has been less aggressive than other jurisdictions
7. **Bipartisan concern**: Left and right agree on Big Tech, differ on solutions
8. **Reform contested**: Both diagnosis and remedies still debated
