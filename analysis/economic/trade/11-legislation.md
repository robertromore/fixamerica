# Trade Policy: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Trade policy reform requires a combination of new legislation, executive action within existing authority, and international negotiation. This document provides draft legislation addressing worker protection, trade enforcement, supply chain security, and industrial policy, along with analysis of potential loopholes and rectification procedures.

## Federal Legislation

### Fair Trade Act

**Purpose**: Establish a comprehensive framework for worker-centered trade policy, including enhanced adjustment assistance, enforceable labor standards, and currency manipulation countermeasures.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Fair Trade Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) Trade liberalization over the past three decades has produced
    aggregate economic gains while imposing concentrated costs on
    manufacturing workers and communities.
(2) Existing trade adjustment assistance programs have proven
    inadequate to help displaced workers and affected communities.
(3) Trading partners have engaged in currency manipulation,
    intellectual property theft, and labor exploitation that
    undermine fair competition.
(4) Critical supply chain dependencies threaten national security
    and economic resilience.
(5) A new approach to trade policy must balance economic efficiency
    with worker protection, strategic resilience, and fair competition.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
(1) TRADE-AFFECTED WORKER.—The term "trade-affected worker" means
    any worker who has lost employment or experienced significant
    wage reduction due to—
    (A) increased imports;
    (B) relocation of production to a foreign country;
    (C) loss of business to foreign competition; or
    (D) supply chain disruptions affecting domestic production.

(2) COVERED TRADING PARTNER.—The term "covered trading partner"
    means any country with which the United States has—
    (A) a bilateral trade deficit exceeding $20,000,000,000 annually;
    (B) documented currency manipulation;
    (C) systematic intellectual property violations; or
    (D) persistent labor rights violations.

(3) CRITICAL SECTOR.—The term "critical sector" means any sector
    designated by the Secretary of Commerce as essential to—
    (A) national defense;
    (B) public health;
    (C) critical infrastructure; or
    (D) economic security.

SEC. 4. UNIVERSAL TRADE ADJUSTMENT ASSISTANCE.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established the Universal Trade
    Adjustment Assistance Program, to be administered by the
    Secretary of Labor.

(b) ELIGIBILITY.—Any trade-affected worker is eligible for
    benefits under this section, without requirement to demonstrate
    that trade was the primary cause of job loss.

(c) BENEFITS.—Eligible workers shall receive—
    (1) INCOME SUPPORT.—Weekly income support equal to 70 percent
        of the worker's average weekly wage, for a period not to
        exceed 156 weeks;
    (2) HEALTH COVERAGE.—Full premium subsidy for continuation
        coverage under COBRA or enrollment in marketplace plans,
        for a period not to exceed 36 months;
    (3) TRAINING.—Access to approved training programs, including—
        (A) occupational skills training;
        (B) apprenticeship programs;
        (C) community college programs; and
        (D) on-the-job training;
    (4) WAGE INSURANCE.—For workers aged 50 or older who accept
        reemployment at lower wages, payment of 50 percent of the
        wage differential for a period not to exceed 104 weeks,
        up to a maximum of $15,000 per year;
    (5) RELOCATION ALLOWANCE.—Reimbursement for relocation
        expenses up to $15,000 for workers who must relocate
        for employment.

(d) COMMUNITY INVESTMENT.—The Secretary shall establish a
    Community Investment Fund to provide grants to communities
    experiencing significant trade-related job losses, for—
    (1) infrastructure investment;
    (2) business development;
    (3) workforce training facilities;
    (4) healthcare facilities; and
    (5) economic diversification.

(e) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There are authorized to be
    appropriated—
    (1) $3,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2025 for worker benefits;
    (2) $1,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2025 for the Community
        Investment Fund; and
    (3) such sums as may be necessary for subsequent fiscal years.

SEC. 5. CURRENCY MANIPULATION COUNTERMEASURES.

(a) DESIGNATION.—The Secretary of the Treasury shall designate
    a country as a currency manipulator if such country—
    (1) has a significant bilateral trade surplus with the
        United States exceeding $20,000,000,000;
    (2) has a material current account surplus exceeding 3 percent
        of GDP; and
    (3) has engaged in persistent one-sided intervention in
        currency markets, with net purchases of foreign assets
        exceeding 2 percent of GDP over a 12-month period.

(b) AUTOMATIC COUNTERVAILING DUTIES.—Upon designation under
    subsection (a)—
    (1) the Secretary of Commerce shall calculate the estimated
        currency undervaluation as a percentage; and
    (2) countervailing duties equal to the percentage of
        undervaluation shall be applied to all imports from
        such country.

(c) EXCEPTION.—Countervailing duties under this section may be
    waived by the President upon certification to Congress that—
    (1) the designated country has ceased currency manipulation; and
    (2) imposition of duties would cause severe harm to national
        security.

SEC. 6. ENFORCEABLE LABOR STANDARDS.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—No trade agreement entered into by the United
    States after the date of enactment of this Act shall take
    effect unless it includes enforceable provisions requiring
    trading partners to—
    (1) adopt and maintain laws providing for freedom of
        association and collective bargaining;
    (2) prohibit forced labor and child labor;
    (3) eliminate employment discrimination; and
    (4) provide acceptable conditions of work with respect to
        minimum wages, hours of work, and occupational safety.

(b) ENFORCEMENT MECHANISM.—Each trade agreement shall include
    a rapid response mechanism providing for—
    (1) facility-level enforcement, allowing investigation of
        specific factories or worksites;
    (2) expedited investigation within 90 days of complaint;
    (3) independent labor expert panels to adjudicate disputes;
    (4) denial of entry to goods produced by facilities found
        in violation; and
    (5) monetary penalties for persistent violations.

(c) DOMESTIC ENFORCEMENT.—The Secretary of Labor shall establish
    an Office of Trade Labor Enforcement with authority to—
    (1) receive and investigate complaints;
    (2) conduct independent investigations;
    (3) recommend enforcement actions to the Trade Representative;
    (4) monitor compliance with labor provisions.

SEC. 7. SUPPLY CHAIN SECURITY.

(a) CRITICAL SECTOR DESIGNATION.—The Secretary of Commerce, in
    consultation with the Secretary of Defense and relevant agency
    heads, shall—
    (1) identify sectors critical to national defense, public
        health, critical infrastructure, and economic security;
    (2) assess supply chain vulnerabilities in each critical
        sector; and
    (3) develop strategies for supply chain diversification
        and domestic production.

(b) RESHORING INCENTIVES.—For production in designated critical
    sectors, there shall be allowed—
    (1) an investment tax credit of 25 percent for qualified
        domestic manufacturing equipment;
    (2) accelerated depreciation for manufacturing facilities;
    (3) priority access to federal procurement; and
    (4) technical assistance grants.

(c) DIVERSIFICATION REQUIREMENTS.—Federal agencies shall ensure
    that procurement of goods in critical sectors—
    (1) does not rely on any single foreign source for more than
        50 percent of supply;
    (2) maintains domestic production capability; and
    (3) includes ally-nation alternatives.

SEC. 8. TRADE ADVISORY COMMITTEE REFORM.

(a) COMPOSITION.—The membership of each trade advisory committee
    shall include—
    (1) not less than 25 percent representatives of labor
        organizations;
    (2) not less than 20 percent representatives of public
        interest, consumer, and environmental organizations;
    (3) not less than 15 percent representatives of small
        businesses; and
    (4) not more than 40 percent representatives of large
        corporations.

(b) TRANSPARENCY.—All trade advisory committees shall—
    (1) conduct meetings in public whenever possible;
    (2) publish meeting minutes within 30 days;
    (3) disclose all member conflicts of interest; and
    (4) accept public comment on negotiating objectives.

SEC. 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 180 days after the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 4**: Creates universal adjustment assistance, eliminating burdensome causation requirements and providing comprehensive support including wage insurance and community investment.

- **Section 5**: Establishes automatic currency manipulation countermeasures, removing Treasury discretion that has historically prevented action.

- **Section 6**: Requires enforceable labor standards with facility-level rapid response mechanism modeled on USMCA.

- **Section 7**: Addresses supply chain security through critical sector designation and reshoring incentives.

- **Section 8**: Reforms trade advisory committees to reduce corporate dominance.

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| WTO compatibility | Design duties as countervailing measures |
| Cost concerns | Funded through tariff revenue |
| Business opposition | Worker/community coalition |
| Implementation complexity | Phase-in period |

**Refinements**:

- **Stronger version**: Mandatory reshoring quotas for critical sectors
- **Fallback version**: Enhanced TAA without universal coverage
- **Addition**: Border carbon adjustment provisions

### Trade Enforcement and Worker Protection Act

**Purpose**: Strengthen enforcement of existing trade laws and provide enhanced remedies for unfair trade practices, with particular focus on intellectual property protection and subsidies.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Trade Enforcement and Worker
Protection Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) Current trade enforcement mechanisms have proven inadequate
    to address unfair trade practices by major trading partners.
(2) Intellectual property theft costs American businesses and
    workers hundreds of billions of dollars annually.
(3) Foreign subsidies distort competition and harm domestic
    producers.
(4) Trade enforcement resources are insufficient to address
    the scale of violations.
(5) Enhanced enforcement is necessary to ensure fair competition
    and protect American workers.

SEC. 3. ENHANCED SECTION 301 AUTHORITY.

(a) SYSTEMATIC INVESTIGATIONS.—The Trade Representative shall—
    (1) conduct annual systematic reviews of trade practices
        by covered trading partners;
    (2) maintain a priority watch list of countries with
        significant unfair trade practices;
    (3) initiate Section 301 investigations for any country
        on the priority watch list for two consecutive years.

(b) EXPANDED REMEDIES.—In addition to existing authorities, the
    Trade Representative may—
    (1) impose tariffs on a sectoral basis;
    (2) restrict imports of specific products;
    (3) limit investment by foreign state-owned enterprises;
    (4) restrict access to U.S. financial markets;
    (5) coordinate remedies with allied nations.

(c) TIMELINE.—Investigations under this section shall be
    completed within 12 months, with remedies imposed within
    30 days of determination.

SEC. 4. INTELLECTUAL PROPERTY ENFORCEMENT.

(a) IP ENFORCEMENT OFFICE.—There is established within the
    Office of the United States Trade Representative an Office
    of Intellectual Property Enforcement, headed by a Director
    appointed by the President with the advice and consent of
    the Senate.

(b) AUTHORITY.—The Director shall—
    (1) maintain a database of intellectual property violations
        by country and sector;
    (2) coordinate enforcement actions across agencies;
    (3) recommend trade remedies for IP violations;
    (4) negotiate IP protection provisions in trade agreements;
    (5) provide technical assistance to IP holders.

(c) MANDATORY ACTION.—Upon determination that a country has
    engaged in systematic intellectual property violations,
    the Trade Representative shall—
    (1) impose tariffs on goods produced using stolen IP;
    (2) restrict technology exports to violating entities;
    (3) limit access to U.S. markets for repeat offenders.

(d) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—Any U.S. person whose intellectual
    property has been stolen may bring a civil action in federal
    court against—
    (1) any entity that has acquired or used such property; and
    (2) any entity that imports goods produced using such property.

SEC. 5. ANTI-SUBSIDY ENFORCEMENT.

(a) ENHANCED COUNTERVAILING DUTIES.—The Secretary of Commerce
    shall—
    (1) expand investigation of foreign subsidies to include
        all forms of government support, including below-market
        financing, land grants, tax preferences, and regulatory
        exemptions;
    (2) calculate countervailing duties based on actual subsidy
        value rather than net subsidies;
    (3) apply duties on a country-wide basis when subsidies are
        pervasive.

(b) UPSTREAM SUBSIDIES.—Countervailing duties may be applied to
    goods that incorporate subsidized inputs, calculated based on
    the subsidy value of such inputs.

(c) STATE-OWNED ENTERPRISE PRESUMPTION.—For goods produced by
    state-owned enterprises, there shall be a rebuttable
    presumption that such goods have received countervailable
    subsidies.

SEC. 6. ENFORCEMENT RESOURCES.

(a) USTR STAFFING.—The Office of the United States Trade
    Representative shall increase enforcement staff by not less
    than 100 positions within 2 years of enactment.

(b) COMMERCE DEPARTMENT.—The International Trade Administration
    shall increase staff for trade remedy investigations by not
    less than 50 positions within 2 years of enactment.

(c) CUSTOMS AND BORDER PROTECTION.—CBP shall increase staff for
    trade enforcement by not less than 200 positions within 2
    years of enactment.

(d) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated—
    (1) $100,000,000 for fiscal year 2025 for USTR;
    (2) $75,000,000 for fiscal year 2025 for Commerce;
    (3) $150,000,000 for fiscal year 2025 for CBP; and
    (4) such sums as may be necessary for subsequent years.

SEC. 7. ALLIED COORDINATION.

(a) MULTILATERAL ENFORCEMENT.—The Trade Representative shall
    establish mechanisms for coordinated enforcement with—
    (1) the European Union;
    (2) Japan;
    (3) the United Kingdom;
    (4) Australia;
    (5) Canada; and
    (6) other allied nations.

(b) JOINT INVESTIGATIONS.—The Trade Representative may conduct
    joint investigations with allied trade authorities and impose
    coordinated remedies.

(c) INFORMATION SHARING.—The Trade Representative shall establish
    secure channels for sharing enforcement information with
    allied trade authorities.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect upon enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Makes Section 301 investigations systematic rather than case-by-case, with expanded remedy options.

- **Section 4**: Creates dedicated IP enforcement office with mandatory action requirements and private right of action.

- **Section 5**: Strengthens anti-subsidy enforcement including for upstream subsidies and SOE goods.

- **Section 6**: Provides resources necessary for meaningful enforcement.

- **Section 7**: Enables allied coordination for greater leverage.

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| WTO challenges | Designed within WTO rules where possible |
| Retaliation risk | Allied coordination reduces risk |
| Resource constraints | Phased implementation |
| Private right of action scope | Carefully defined standing |

### Industrial Competitiveness and Resilience Act

**Purpose**: Establish a comprehensive industrial policy framework including a development finance institution, strategic sector investment, and manufacturing workforce development.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Industrial Competitiveness and
Resilience Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) American industrial capacity has declined significantly over
    past decades due to offshoring and underinvestment.
(2) Strategic competitors have used industrial policy to develop
    dominant positions in critical sectors.
(3) Supply chain vulnerabilities exposed during the COVID-19
    pandemic and semiconductor shortage demonstrate the need for
    domestic production capacity.
(4) Market forces alone have proven insufficient to maintain
    industrial capacity essential to national security and
    economic resilience.
(5) A comprehensive industrial policy is necessary to rebuild
    American manufacturing.

SEC. 3. AMERICAN INDUSTRIAL FINANCE CORPORATION.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established the American Industrial
    Finance Corporation (the "Corporation"), which shall be an
    independent agency of the United States.

(b) PURPOSE.—The Corporation shall provide patient capital for
    domestic manufacturing in strategic sectors through—
    (1) direct loans;
    (2) loan guarantees;
    (3) equity investments;
    (4) technical assistance.

(c) BOARD OF DIRECTORS.—The Corporation shall be governed by
    a Board of Directors consisting of—
    (1) the Secretary of Commerce, as Chair;
    (2) the Secretary of Defense;
    (3) the Secretary of Energy;
    (4) the Secretary of Labor;
    (5) three members appointed by the President with expertise
        in manufacturing, finance, and labor; and
    (6) two members appointed by the President representing
        labor organizations.

(d) CAPITAL.—
    (1) INITIAL CAPITALIZATION.—There is authorized to be
        appropriated $50,000,000,000 for initial capitalization.
    (2) BORROWING AUTHORITY.—The Corporation may issue bonds
        guaranteed by the full faith and credit of the United
        States in an amount not to exceed $100,000,000,000.
    (3) RETAINED EARNINGS.—The Corporation may retain earnings
        from its investments for future lending.

(e) INVESTMENT CRITERIA.—Investments shall be made based on—
    (1) strategic importance to national security;
    (2) supply chain resilience;
    (3) job creation potential;
    (4) community impact;
    (5) commercial viability over reasonable time horizon.

(f) LABOR STANDARDS.—Any entity receiving support from the
    Corporation shall—
    (1) pay prevailing wages;
    (2) provide health and retirement benefits;
    (3) maintain neutrality in union organizing;
    (4) prohibit mandatory arbitration for employment disputes.

SEC. 4. STRATEGIC SECTOR DESIGNATION AND INVESTMENT.

(a) DESIGNATION.—The Secretary of Commerce shall designate
    strategic sectors based on—
    (1) importance to national defense;
    (2) public health significance;
    (3) critical infrastructure dependence;
    (4) supply chain vulnerability;
    (5) technology leadership potential.

(b) INITIAL DESIGNATIONS.—The following sectors are designated
    as strategic sectors:
    (1) Semiconductors and advanced electronics;
    (2) Pharmaceuticals and medical devices;
    (3) Critical minerals and materials;
    (4) Clean energy technology;
    (5) Advanced manufacturing equipment;
    (6) Aerospace and defense;
    (7) Biotechnology.

(c) SECTOR-SPECIFIC INVESTMENTS.—
    (1) SEMICONDUCTORS.—In addition to funding under the CHIPS
        and Science Act, there is authorized $25,000,000,000
        for additional semiconductor manufacturing incentives.
    (2) PHARMACEUTICALS.—There is authorized $15,000,000,000
        for domestic pharmaceutical and API manufacturing.
    (3) CRITICAL MINERALS.—There is authorized $10,000,000,000
        for domestic mining and processing of critical minerals.
    (4) CLEAN ENERGY.—There is authorized $30,000,000,000 for
        clean energy manufacturing beyond IRA provisions.

SEC. 5. MANUFACTURING WORKFORCE DEVELOPMENT.

(a) MANUFACTURING SKILLS INITIATIVE.—The Secretary of Labor
    shall establish a Manufacturing Skills Initiative to—
    (1) expand registered apprenticeship programs in
        manufacturing;
    (2) support community college manufacturing programs;
    (3) provide grants for employer-based training;
    (4) establish regional manufacturing training centers.

(b) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
    $5,000,000,000 for fiscal years 2025 through 2030 for the
    Manufacturing Skills Initiative.

(c) PARTNERSHIP REQUIREMENTS.—Training programs funded under
    this section shall be developed in partnership with—
    (1) employers in the sector;
    (2) labor organizations;
    (3) educational institutions; and
    (4) community organizations.

SEC. 6. BUY AMERICAN MODERNIZATION.

(a) DOMESTIC CONTENT REQUIREMENT.—The domestic content
    requirement for federal procurement under the Buy American
    Act is increased to—
    (1) 75 percent for fiscal years 2025 and 2026;
    (2) 80 percent for fiscal years 2027 and 2028; and
    (3) 85 percent for fiscal year 2029 and thereafter.

(b) WAIVER LIMITATION.—Waivers of Buy American requirements
    shall be granted only upon—
    (1) certification that domestic products are not available
        at any price;
    (2) determination that domestic procurement would exceed
        150 percent of foreign price; or
    (3) national security necessity certified by agency head.

(c) PUBLIC REPORTING.—Each agency shall publish quarterly
    reports on Buy American compliance and waivers granted.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect upon enactment, except that—
(a) the American Industrial Finance Corporation shall begin
    operations within 180 days; and
(b) Buy American requirements shall take effect at the beginning
    of fiscal year 2025.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Creates development finance institution modeled on successful international examples (Germany's KfW, etc.) with labor standards.

- **Section 4**: Designates and funds strategic sectors beyond existing programs.

- **Section 5**: Invests in manufacturing workforce to ensure labor supply.

- **Section 6**: Strengthens Buy American with higher thresholds and limited waivers.

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Government picking winners | Focus on sectors, not companies |
| Cost/deficit concerns | Investment creates returns |
| Trade agreement conflicts | Carve-outs, renegotiation |
| Implementation capacity | Phase-in, existing agencies |

## State Model Legislation

### State Buy American Act (Model)

**Purpose**: Model legislation for states to establish domestic content preferences in state procurement.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Buy American Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Domestic product" means a product that—
    (1) is manufactured in the United States; and
    (2) contains at least 60 percent domestic content by value.

(b) "Domestic content" means components manufactured in the
    United States or a country with which the United States
    has a reciprocal procurement agreement.

(c) "State agency" means any agency, department, board,
    commission, or authority of [State] government.

SECTION 3. PREFERENCE FOR DOMESTIC PRODUCTS.

(a) Each state agency shall, in procuring goods, give preference
    to domestic products.

(b) A domestic product shall be deemed the lowest responsible
    bid if its bid does not exceed by more than [10-15] percent
    the lowest bid for a foreign product.

(c) For goods essential to public health or safety, domestic
    products shall be required unless the agency head certifies
    that—
    (1) domestic products are not available; or
    (2) domestic procurement would exceed 150 percent of
        foreign price.

SECTION 4. REPORTING.

Each state agency shall report annually on—
(a) total procurement spending;
(b) percentage of domestic products purchased;
(c) waivers granted and reasons; and
(d) projected domestic procurement for following year.

SECTION 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect [date].
```

### Trade-Affected Community Investment Act (Model)

**Purpose**: Model legislation for states to provide economic development support to communities affected by trade-related job losses.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Trade-Affected Community
Investment Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Trade-affected community" means a county or municipality
    that has experienced—
    (1) loss of [500] or more manufacturing jobs within [5] years;
    (2) manufacturing employment decline of [20] percent or more; or
    (3) closure of a major manufacturing facility.

(b) "Community investment" means expenditure for—
    (1) infrastructure;
    (2) workforce training;
    (3) business development;
    (4) healthcare facilities;
    (5) education facilities.

SECTION 3. TRADE-AFFECTED COMMUNITY FUND.

(a) There is established the [State] Trade-Affected Community
    Fund.

(b) The Fund shall provide grants to trade-affected communities
    for community investment.

(c) Grant applications shall demonstrate—
    (1) trade-related job losses;
    (2) proposed investments;
    (3) community matching funds; and
    (4) expected job creation or retention.

SECTION 4. PRIORITY.

Priority in awarding grants shall be given to—
(a) communities with highest job losses;
(b) communities with limited alternative employers;
(c) applications with significant community matching; and
(d) investments with highest job creation potential.

SECTION 5. APPROPRIATION.

There is appropriated from the General Fund $[amount] for
fiscal year [year] for the Fund.

SECTION 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect [date].
```

## Regulatory Framework

### USTR Enforcement Regulations

**Existing Authority**: 19 U.S.C. Section 2411 (Section 301).

**Draft Regulation**:

```text
PART 2001—SECTION 301 INVESTIGATIONS

§ 2001.1 Systematic Reviews.

(a) Annual Review. The Trade Representative shall conduct an
    annual systematic review of trade practices by major trading
    partners, focusing on:
    (1) Market access barriers;
    (2) Intellectual property violations;
    (3) Subsidies and state support;
    (4) Currency practices;
    (5) Labor and environmental standards.

(b) Priority Watch List. Based on the annual review, the Trade
    Representative shall designate countries for the priority
    watch list if practices meet statutory thresholds.

(c) Mandatory Initiation. For countries on the priority watch
    list for two consecutive years, the Trade Representative
    shall initiate a Section 301 investigation.

§ 2001.2 Investigation Procedures.

(a) Timeline. Investigations shall be completed within 12 months.

(b) Public Participation. The Trade Representative shall—
    (1) Publish notice of investigation in Federal Register;
    (2) Accept public comments for 60 days;
    (3) Hold public hearing;
    (4) Consider all relevant evidence.

(c) Interagency Coordination. The Trade Representative shall
    coordinate with Commerce, Treasury, Labor, and other
    relevant agencies.

§ 2001.3 Remedies.

(a) Available Remedies. Upon affirmative determination, the
    Trade Representative may—
    (1) Impose tariffs on products of the investigated country;
    (2) Restrict imports in specific sectors;
    (3) Recommend investment restrictions;
    (4) Coordinate remedies with allied nations.

(b) Implementation. Remedies shall be implemented within 30 days
    of determination.

(c) Duration. Remedies shall remain in effect until the Trade
    Representative determines that unfair practices have ceased.
```

### Commerce Department Supply Chain Regulations

**Existing Authority**: Defense Production Act, Commerce authorities.

**Draft Regulation**:

```text
PART 700—SUPPLY CHAIN SECURITY

§ 700.1 Critical Sector Designation.

(a) Criteria. A sector may be designated as critical if it is
    essential to:
    (1) National defense;
    (2) Public health;
    (3) Critical infrastructure; or
    (4) Economic security.

(b) Designation Process. The Secretary shall:
    (1) Conduct interagency review;
    (2) Accept public comment;
    (3) Publish designation in Federal Register.

(c) Review. Designations shall be reviewed every 3 years.

§ 700.2 Supply Chain Assessment.

(a) For each critical sector, the Secretary shall conduct an
    assessment of:
    (1) Domestic production capacity;
    (2) Import dependence by source country;
    (3) Concentration of supply;
    (4) Vulnerability to disruption;
    (5) Alternative sources.

(b) Assessment reports shall be submitted to Congress annually.

§ 700.3 Diversification Requirements.

(a) Federal agencies shall ensure that procurement in critical
    sectors:
    (1) Does not rely on any single foreign source for more than
        50 percent of supply;
    (2) Maintains domestic or allied-nation alternative; and
    (3) Includes strategic reserve where appropriate.

(b) Waivers may be granted only upon certification of national
    security necessity.
```

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| Tariff authority | Clear congressional power |

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| Section 301 authority | Upheld by courts |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Fair Trade Act

**Potential Loopholes**:

| Loophole | How Exploited | Severity |
|----------|---------------|----------|
| Trade-affected worker definition | Employers dispute causation to deny eligibility | High |
| Currency manipulation thresholds | Countries stay just under thresholds | Medium |
| Covered trading partner criteria | Manipulation through data reporting | Medium |
| Critical sector designation process | Lobbying delays designations | Medium |
| Labor standards enforcement | Foreign governments obstruct facility access | High |
