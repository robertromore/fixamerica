# Economic Mobility: Stakeholders

## Overview

Economic mobility reform affects virtually everyone in American society, though in different ways. Understanding who benefits from the status quo, who suffers from it, and who has the power to change it is essential for building effective coalitions and overcoming resistance.

## Those Harmed by Low Mobility

### Low-Income Families

**Who they are**: 37 million Americans living below the poverty line; 40 million more classified as "near-poor"

| Impact | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Trapped in poverty | 33% of those born poor remain poor; 60% stay in bottom two quintiles |
| Limited options | Cannot afford to move to opportunity, invest in education, take risks |
| Health consequences | Lower life expectancy, higher chronic disease rates |
| Intergenerational transmission | Children inherit disadvantages |

**Interests**: Pathways out of poverty, accessible education, affordable housing in good neighborhoods, living wages, protection from exploitation

### Black Americans

**Who they are**: 42 million Americans (13% of population) facing persistent racial mobility gaps

| Impact | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Wealth gap | Median Black wealth is 13% of white wealth |
| Downward mobility | Black children from wealthy families often fall to lower quintiles |
| Geographic concentration | 60% live in high-poverty, hypersegregated neighborhoods |
| Discrimination compounding | Every barrier is worse for Black Americans |

**Interests**: Addressing structural racism, reparations or targeted investment, criminal justice reform, ending discrimination

### Hispanic/Latino Americans

**Who they are**: 63 million Americans (19% of population), the largest minority group

| Impact | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Immigration status | 11 million undocumented face unique barriers |
| Language barriers | Limit access to services and employment |
| Educational gaps | Lower college completion rates |
| Convergence potential | Second generation shows mobility gains |

**Interests**: Immigration reform, language access, educational opportunity, legalization pathways

### Native Americans

**Who they are**: 5.2 million Americans with lowest mobility outcomes nationwide

| Impact | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Reservation poverty | 25% poverty rate (38% on reservations) |
| Worst outcomes | Counties with reservations have lowest mobility in America |
| Historical trauma | Ongoing effects of displacement and forced assimilation |
| Sovereignty limits | Federal jurisdiction constrains economic development |

**Interests**: Tribal sovereignty, federal trust responsibilities, economic development, educational investment

### Children

**Who they are**: 73 million Americans under 18, with no political voice but highest stakes

| Impact | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Poverty rate | 16% of children live in poverty (11 million) |
| Outcome determination | Childhood circumstances predict 50%+ of adult outcomes |
| No choice in circumstances | Born into conditions they did not create |
| Long-term effects | Disadvantages compound across lifetime |

**Interests**: Early childhood programs, family support, quality schools, nutrition, healthcare

### Working-Class and Middle-Class Families

**Who they are**: 50% of Americans earning between $30,000 and $100,000

| Impact | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Stagnant wages | Real wages flat for 40 years |
| Cost increases | Housing, healthcare, education outpace income |
| Downward mobility fear | Anxiety about children's prospects |
| Precarity | One crisis away from poverty |

**Interests**: Higher wages, affordable education, healthcare security, housing affordability, retirement security

### First-Generation College Students

**Who they are**: 33% of college students whose parents did not attend college

| Impact | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Lower completion rates | 27% graduate in 6 years vs. 60% for continuing-generation |
| Higher debt | Borrow more, from less wealthy families |
| Less support | Navigate system without parental guidance |
| Imposter syndrome | Social and cultural barriers in addition to academic |

**Interests**: Support services, affordable college, financial aid, mentorship

## Those Benefiting from the Status Quo

### Wealthy Families

**Who they are**: Top 10% of wealth holders (approximately 13 million households)

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Wealth transmission | Estate tax exemption allows tax-free inheritance |
| Opportunity hoarding | Exclusive neighborhoods, elite schools |
| Network advantage | Connections yield opportunities |
| Political influence | Wealth translates to policy influence |
| Safety nets | Private insurance, savings buffer any crisis |

**Interests in status quo**: Preserving advantages for children, low taxes on wealth, exclusive institutions, zoning that maintains segregation

### Elite Educational Institutions

**Who they are**: Ivy League, elite private schools, selective state universities

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Legacy admissions | Maintains alumni donor relationships |
| Selectivity | Exclusivity is marketing advantage |
| Endowment growth | Wealthy alumni give more |
| Prestige | Scarcity creates perceived value |

**Interests in status quo**: Legacy admissions, admissions discretion, minimal oversight, large endowments

### Suburban Property Owners

**Who they are**: Homeowners in exclusive suburban communities (approximately 40 million households)

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Property values | Exclusion maintains scarcity |
| School quality | Property taxes fund local schools |
| Neighborhood composition | Zoning keeps "undesirables" out |
| Political control | Local government serves homeowner interests |

**Interests in status quo**: Exclusionary zoning, local school control, low-density requirements, opposition to affordable housing

### Private Prison Industry

**Who they are**: CoreCivic, GEO Group, and related contractors

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Per-prisoner payments | Profit increases with incarceration |
| Lobbying for tough sentences | Longer sentences = more revenue |
| Immigration detention | Growing profit center |
| Limited accountability | Less oversight than public facilities |

**Interests in status quo**: Mass incarceration, mandatory minimums, immigration detention, bail requirements

### For-Profit Education

**Who they are**: For-profit colleges, test prep companies, private tutoring

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Anxiety monetization | Parents pay for any perceived advantage |
| Targeting vulnerable populations | For-profit colleges recruit low-income students |
| Testing industry | SAT/ACT create coaching market |
| Limited regulation | Weak oversight allows predatory practices |

**Interests in status quo**: Competitive admissions, standardized testing, inadequate public schools, weak regulation

## Institutions with Mixed Interests

### Higher Education (Broadly)

| Interest in Reform | Interest in Status Quo |
|-------------------|------------------------|
| Mission to educate all | Selectivity as marker of quality |
| Federal funding tied to outcomes | Endowment model benefits wealthy institutions |
| Diversity goals | Legacy admissions maintain donor relations |
| Public service mission | Tuition revenue from wealthy families |

### State Governments

| Interest in Reform | Interest in Status Quo |
|-------------------|------------------------|
| Economic development | Race to bottom for business (low taxes) |
| Workforce development | Limited resources for programs |
| Medicaid costs (poverty) | Federal matching limits expansion |
| Prison costs | Private prison lobbying |

### Local Governments

| Interest in Reform | Interest in Status Quo |
|-------------------|------------------------|
| Economic development | Wealthy residents oppose change |
| Housing affordability | NIMBYism from constituents |
| School improvement | Wealthy districts resist equalization |
| Revenue needs | Property tax base from exclusive housing |

### Employers

| Interest in Reform | Interest in Status Quo |
|-------------------|------------------------|
| Skilled workforce | Low wages benefit short-term |
| Reduced turnover | Large labor pool keeps wages down |
| Innovation | Network hiring easy, familiar |
| Diverse perspectives | Discrimination is tolerated |

## Power Analysis

### Who Has Power to Change Policy

| Actor | Power Source | Mobility Interest |
|-------|--------------|-------------------|
| Federal government | Taxation, regulation, spending | Mixed (depends on administration) |
| State legislatures | Education, zoning preemption, criminal justice | Generally status quo |
| Local governments | Zoning, schools, policing | Generally status quo |
| Wealthy donors | Campaign finance, lobbying | Strongly status quo |
| Media | Narrative framing | Generally status quo |
| Unions | Collective action | Pro-mobility |
| Civil rights organizations | Advocacy, litigation | Pro-mobility |
| Research institutions | Evidence, legitimacy | Generally pro-mobility |

### Power Imbalance

| Pro-Mobility Forces | Anti-Mobility Forces |
|--------------------|---------------------|
| Millions of affected families (diffuse) | Wealthy homeowners (organized) |
| Civil rights groups (under-resourced) | Real estate industry (well-funded) |
| Child advocates (no voting constituency) | Suburban voters (high turnout) |
| Academic researchers (limited influence) | Think tanks funded by wealthy |

**The fundamental power imbalance**: Those who benefit from immobility are wealthy, organized, and politically active. Those harmed are poor, diffuse, and often unable to participate in politics.

## Coalition Possibilities

### Natural Allies

| Group | Shared Interest |
|-------|-----------------|
| Civil rights organizations | Racial equity in mobility |
| Labor unions | Higher wages, worker power |
| Education advocates | Equal opportunity in schools |
| Housing advocates | Desegregation, affordability |
| Child welfare organizations | Early childhood investment |
| Public health advocates | Social determinants of health |
| Faith communities | Moral imperative of opportunity |

### Potential Allies (with right framing)

| Group | Appeal |
|-------|--------|
| Business community | Workforce development, talent pool |
| Moderate suburbanites | Opportunity for their children too |
| Libertarians | Criminal justice reform, occupational licensing |
| Fiscal conservatives | Reduce poverty-related spending |
| Military | Recruit from all communities |

### Opposition that May Be Persuaded

| Group | Persuasion Strategy |
|-------|---------------------|
| Suburban homeowners | Emphasize benefits of regional prosperity |
| Elite universities | Pressure from faculty, students, donors |
| Some wealthy individuals | Appeal to fairness, legacy, philanthropy |

## Stakeholder Map Summary

| Category | Groups | Political Power | Mobility Interest |
|----------|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Harmed, Low Power | Low-income families, children, incarcerated | Low (voting barriers, no resources) | Strongly pro-reform |
| Harmed, Moderate Power | Working/middle class, minorities with political organizations | Moderate | Pro-reform |
| Benefits, High Power | Wealthy families, elite institutions, suburban homeowners | High | Anti-reform |
| Mixed | Governments, employers, universities | High | Depends on incentives |
| Potential Allies | Business, moderate suburbanites, libertarians | Moderate to high | Persuadable |
