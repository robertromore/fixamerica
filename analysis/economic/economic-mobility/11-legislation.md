# Economic Mobility: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Restoring economic mobility in America requires a comprehensive legislative agenda addressing early childhood, education, housing, wealth-building, and criminal justice. This document provides draft legislation at federal and state levels, along with analysis of potential loopholes and implementation challenges.

## Constitutional Amendments

### Right to Education Amendment

**Purpose**: Establish education as a fundamental right, enabling courts to require school funding equalization.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. All persons within the United States shall have the
right to a public education of adequate quality sufficient to
prepare them for civic participation and economic self-sufficiency.

SECTION 2. Congress and the States shall have the power to enforce
this article by appropriate legislation. Such legislation may
include standards for educational adequacy and mechanisms for
equalization of educational resources.

SECTION 3. Nothing in this article shall be construed to limit
the right of parents to direct the education of their children
through private schooling or home education.

SECTION 4. This article shall take effect five years after the
date of ratification.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 1**: Creates affirmative right to "adequate quality" education. "Adequate quality" language tracks successful state constitutional litigation while allowing for evolving standards.

- **Section 2**: Empowers both Congress and states to enforce. "Equalization of educational resources" explicitly authorizes school finance reform.

- **Section 3**: Preserves private education to address religious liberty and parental rights concerns.

- **Section 4**: Five-year delay allows time for states to develop compliance plans.

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| State sovereignty | Education is state responsibility, but federal standards exist (IDEA, Title I) |
| Adequacy is undefined | Courts have developed standards in state litigation |
| Enforcement difficulty | Federal funding conditions, judicial enforcement |
| Private school concerns | Section 3 addresses directly |

**Refinements**:

- **Stronger version**: Include specific funding adequacy formula
- **Fallback version**: Apply only to federal education programs
- **Alternative**: Pursue state constitutional amendments (more achievable)

## Federal Legislation

### American Opportunity Act

**Purpose**: Establish baby bonds, universal pre-K, and expanded earned income support to increase economic mobility.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "American Opportunity Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) Economic mobility in the United States has declined
    significantly over the past four decades.
(2) A child born into the bottom fifth of the income distribution
    has only a 7.5% chance of reaching the top fifth.
(3) Early childhood investments show returns of 7:1 or greater.
(4) Wealth inequality is a primary driver of opportunity
    inequality.
(5) Universal programs build broad political support and avoid
    stigmatization.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
(1) ELIGIBLE CHILD.—The term "eligible child" means any child
    who is a citizen or lawful permanent resident of the United
    States.
(2) OPPORTUNITY ACCOUNT.—The term "Opportunity Account" means
    a savings account established under section 4.
(3) MEDIAN FAMILY WEALTH.—The term "median family wealth" means
    the median net worth of families in the United States as
    determined by the most recent Survey of Consumer Finances.

TITLE I—AMERICAN OPPORTUNITY ACCOUNTS

SEC. 101. ESTABLISHMENT OF AMERICAN OPPORTUNITY ACCOUNTS.

(a) IN GENERAL.—The Secretary of the Treasury shall establish
    an American Opportunity Account for each eligible child born
    on or after January 1, 2027.

(b) INITIAL DEPOSIT.—Upon the birth or naturalization of an
    eligible child, the Secretary shall deposit into the child's
    Opportunity Account an amount equal to—
    (1) $1,000 for all eligible children; plus
    (2) an additional amount based on the family's wealth
        relative to median family wealth, as follows:
        (A) for families with wealth below the 25th percentile:
            $50,000;
        (B) for families with wealth at or above the 25th
            percentile but below the 50th percentile: $35,000;
        (C) for families with wealth at or above the 50th
            percentile but below the 75th percentile: $20,000;
        (D) for families with wealth at or above the 75th
            percentile but below the 90th percentile: $10,000;
        (E) for families with wealth at or above the 90th
            percentile: $0.

(c) ANNUAL CONTRIBUTIONS.—Beginning in the year after the
    initial deposit, the Secretary shall deposit annually into
    each Opportunity Account an amount equal to $500, adjusted
    for inflation.

(d) INVESTMENT.—Funds in Opportunity Accounts shall be invested
    in a diversified portfolio of low-cost index funds selected
    by the Secretary.

SEC. 102. ACCOUNT CHARACTERISTICS.

(a) TAX TREATMENT.—
    (1) Deposits and earnings in Opportunity Accounts shall not
        be subject to federal income tax.
    (2) Qualified distributions shall not be subject to federal
        income tax.

(b) PROTECTION FROM CREDITORS.—Funds in Opportunity Accounts
    shall be protected from claims of creditors of the account
    holder or the account holder's parents.

(c) BENEFIT EXCLUSION.—Funds in Opportunity Accounts shall not
    be considered assets or resources for purposes of determining
    eligibility for any federal means-tested program.

SEC. 103. DISTRIBUTIONS.

(a) ELIGIBILITY.—Distributions may be made to account holders
    who have attained the age of 18.

(b) QUALIFIED USES.—Distributions may be used only for—
    (1) costs of attendance at an eligible institution of higher
        education (as defined in section 102 of the Higher
        Education Act of 1965);
    (2) the purchase of a primary residence, including down
        payment and closing costs;
    (3) startup costs for a qualified small business; or
    (4) contributions to a retirement account.

(c) NON-QUALIFIED DISTRIBUTIONS.—Distributions for purposes
    other than those listed in subsection (b) shall be subject
    to a penalty of 10% of the distribution amount, in addition
    to applicable income taxes.

TITLE II—UNIVERSAL PRE-KINDERGARTEN

SEC. 201. ESTABLISHMENT OF GRANT PROGRAM.

(a) IN GENERAL.—The Secretary of Health and Human Services shall
    establish a program of grants to States for the provision of
    high-quality, universal pre-kindergarten for children ages 3
    and 4.

(b) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated—
    (1) $20,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
    (2) $40,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2028;
    (3) $60,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2029; and
    (4) such sums as necessary for subsequent fiscal years.

SEC. 202. GRANT CONDITIONS.

(a) UNIVERSAL ACCESS.—As a condition of receiving a grant, a
    State shall ensure that all children ages 3 and 4 who reside
    in the State have access to pre-kindergarten at no cost to
    families.

(b) QUALITY STANDARDS.—Pre-kindergarten programs funded under
    this title shall meet the following standards:
    (1) Class size of no more than 17 children.
    (2) Staff-to-child ratio of at least 1:8.
    (3) Lead teachers with a bachelor's degree in early childhood
        education or a related field.
    (4) Compensation for lead teachers at parity with K-12
        teachers in the same jurisdiction.
    (5) Developmentally appropriate curriculum aligned with
        state early learning standards.
    (6) Full-day programs (at least 6 hours).

(c) MAINTENANCE OF EFFORT.—States shall not reduce State spending
    on early childhood education as a result of receiving funds
    under this title.

TITLE III—EARNED INCOME TAX CREDIT EXPANSION

SEC. 301. EXPANSION OF CREDIT FOR INDIVIDUALS WITHOUT QUALIFYING
           CHILDREN.

(a) INCREASE IN CREDIT PERCENTAGE.—Section 32(b)(1) of the
    Internal Revenue Code is amended by striking "7.65 percent"
    and inserting "15.3 percent".

(b) INCREASE IN CREDIT AMOUNT.—Section 32(b)(2)(A) is amended
    by striking "$6,164" and inserting "$12,500".

(c) EXPANSION OF AGE RANGE.—Section 32(c)(1)(A)(ii)(II) is
    amended by striking "age 25" and inserting "age 19" and
    by striking "age 65" and inserting "age 67".

SEC. 302. MONTHLY PAYMENTS.

The Secretary of the Treasury shall establish procedures to
allow taxpayers to receive the earned income tax credit in
monthly installments rather than as an annual lump sum.

TITLE IV—FUNDING

SEC. 401. FUNDING.

This Act shall be funded through—
(1) adjustments to the estate tax as provided in section 402;
(2) closing of the stepped-up basis loophole as provided in
    section 403; and
(3) such other revenues as Congress may appropriate.

SEC. 402. ESTATE TAX REFORM.

(a) REDUCTION OF EXEMPTION.—The applicable exclusion amount under
    section 2010(c) of the Internal Revenue Code is reduced to
    $3,500,000.

(b) INCREASE IN RATE.—The rate applicable under section 2001 is
    increased to 55% for estates exceeding $10,000,000 and 65%
    for estates exceeding $50,000,000.

SEC. 403. ELIMINATION OF STEPPED-UP BASIS.

Section 1014 of the Internal Revenue Code is amended to treat
property acquired from a decedent as having a basis equal to the
decedent's basis immediately before death, with an exemption for
the first $1,000,000 of unrealized gains.

SEC. 404. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on January 1, 2027.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Title I**: Baby bonds scaled by family wealth, addressing the core driver of intergenerational inequality. The $50,000 maximum for the poorest families would nearly eliminate the wealth gap over one generation.

- **Title II**: Universal pre-K with quality standards based on research showing early childhood is the highest-return investment.

- **Title III**: EITC expansion for childless workers addresses coverage gap and makes work pay for all.

- **Title IV**: Funding through estate tax reform creates a progressive funding mechanism.

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Cost | 10-year cost ~$1T; funded by estate tax reform |
| Wealth measurement | Use IRS and Census data; self-attestation with penalties |
| Program complexity | Phase in over 5 years |
| Constitutional questions | Within taxing and spending power |

### Baby Bonds Act (Alternative/Simpler Version)

**Purpose**: Standalone baby bonds legislation for separate consideration.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "American Opportunity Accounts Act"
or the "Baby Bonds Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) The median wealth of Black families is $24,100, compared to
    $188,200 for white families—a ratio of 13 cents on the dollar.
(2) This wealth gap perpetuates inequality across generations.
(3) Providing every child with a financial foundation at birth
    can break the cycle of intergenerational poverty.
(4) Simulations suggest baby bonds would dramatically reduce the
    racial wealth gap within two generations.

SEC. 3. ESTABLISHMENT OF OPPORTUNITY ACCOUNTS.

(a) IN GENERAL.—The Secretary of the Treasury shall establish an
    American Opportunity Account for each child born on or after
    January 1, 2027, who is a citizen of the United States.

(b) INITIAL DEPOSIT.—Upon the birth of an eligible child, the
    Secretary shall deposit into the child's Account—
    (1) $1,000 for all children; plus
    (2) an additional amount ranging from $0 to $46,215, based
        on household income relative to the federal poverty
        level, as determined by the Secretary.

(c) SCALE.—The Secretary shall establish a sliding scale such
    that—
    (1) children in households below 100% of the federal poverty
        level receive the maximum additional amount;
    (2) children in households above 500% of the federal poverty
        level receive no additional amount; and
    (3) children in households between 100% and 500% of the
        federal poverty level receive prorated amounts.

SEC. 4. ACCOUNT MANAGEMENT.

(a) INVESTMENT.—Funds shall be invested in Treasury securities
    earning the greater of—
    (1) the rate of inflation; or
    (2) a rate determined by the Secretary, not to exceed 3%.

(b) GROWTH.—At current projections, accounts for children from
    the lowest-income families would grow to approximately
    $50,000 by age 18.

SEC. 5. DISTRIBUTIONS.

(a) ACCESS.—Account holders may access funds upon reaching age 18.

(b) PERMISSIBLE USES.—Funds may be used for—
    (1) higher education or vocational training;
    (2) homeownership;
    (3) starting a small business; or
    (4) retirement savings.

(c) RESTRICTIONS.—The Secretary shall establish procedures to
    verify permissible uses.

SEC. 6. PROGRAM PROTECTIONS.

(a) NOT CONSIDERED INCOME OR ASSETS.—Funds in Opportunity
    Accounts shall not be considered income or assets for any
    means-tested federal program.

(b) PROTECTION FROM CREDITORS.—Funds shall be exempt from claims
    of creditors.

(c) NO REDUCTION IN OTHER BENEFITS.—Receipt of an Opportunity
    Account shall not reduce eligibility for any other federal
    benefit.

SEC. 7. AUTHORIZATION.

There are authorized to be appropriated such sums as necessary
to carry out this Act, estimated at $60,000,000,000 per year.
```

### Homeownership Opportunity Act

**Purpose**: Address barriers to homeownership for first-generation buyers and communities historically denied access.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Homeownership Opportunity and
Mobility Enhancement Act" or "HOME Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) Homeownership is a primary wealth-building mechanism for
    American families.
(2) The Black homeownership rate (44%) is 30 percentage points
    below the white rate (74%).
(3) Discriminatory policies including redlining, restrictive
    covenants, and predatory lending have systematically denied
    homeownership to Black and brown communities.
(4) First-generation homebuyers face unique barriers including
    lack of family wealth for down payments and limited access
    to financial guidance.
(5) Expanding homeownership access is essential for economic
    mobility.

SEC. 3. FIRST-GENERATION HOMEBUYER DOWN PAYMENT ASSISTANCE.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary of Housing and Urban Development
    shall establish a program to provide down payment assistance
    to qualified first-generation homebuyers.

(b) ELIGIBILITY.—An individual is eligible for assistance if—
    (1) neither the individual nor the individual's spouse has
        previously owned a home;
    (2) neither of the individual's parents owned a home during
        the individual's lifetime; and
    (3) the individual's household income is at or below 150%
        of area median income.

(c) ASSISTANCE AMOUNT.—Eligible individuals may receive—
    (1) up to $25,000 for down payment and closing costs; or
    (2) up to $20,000 if combined with other federal assistance.

(d) FORM OF ASSISTANCE.—Assistance shall be in the form of a
    grant that converts to a forgivable loan if the individual
    maintains the home as a primary residence for 5 years.

(e) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated
    $10,000,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2036.

SEC. 4. SPECIAL PURPOSE CREDIT PROGRAMS.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—The Equal Credit Opportunity Act (15 U.S.C.
    1691 et seq.) is amended to explicitly authorize special
    purpose credit programs designed to benefit economically
    disadvantaged communities.

(b) REGULATORY CLARITY.—The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau
    shall issue regulations clarifying that creditors may offer
    programs that provide more favorable terms to—
    (1) first-generation homebuyers;
    (2) residents of historically redlined communities;
    (3) recipients of special purpose credit under this section.

SEC. 5. FAIR HOUSING ENFORCEMENT.

(a) AFFIRMATIVELY FURTHERING FAIR HOUSING.—The Secretary shall
    issue regulations requiring all recipients of federal
    housing funds to affirmatively further fair housing through—
    (1) assessment of fair housing barriers;
    (2) development of action plans to address identified
        barriers; and
    (3) reporting on progress toward fair housing goals.

(b) TESTING PROGRAM.—The Secretary shall establish a national
    housing discrimination testing program with funding of not
    less than $50,000,000 annually.

(c) PENALTIES.—Civil penalties for fair housing violations are
    increased to—
    (1) $150,000 for first offense;
    (2) $300,000 for subsequent offenses.

SEC. 6. APPRAISAL FAIRNESS.

(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that appraisal bias systematically
    undervalues homes in majority-Black and majority-Latino
    neighborhoods.

(b) REQUIREMENTS.—The Appraisal Subcommittee shall issue
    standards requiring—
    (1) appraiser training on fair housing and implicit bias;
    (2) use of comparable sales from integrated neighborhoods;
    (3) prohibition on considering neighborhood racial
        composition;
    (4) recourse procedures for homeowners alleging bias.

(c) RECONSIDERATION OF VALUE.—Homeowners who believe their
    appraisal is affected by bias may request a reconsideration
    of value at no cost, with the second appraisal conducted by
    an appraiser from a different firm.

SEC. 7. HOUSING MOBILITY.

(a) HOUSING CHOICE VOUCHER MOBILITY SERVICES.—The Secretary shall
    establish a housing mobility demonstration program in 20
    metropolitan areas providing—
    (1) housing counseling;
    (2) security deposit assistance;
    (3) landlord recruitment in high-opportunity areas;
    (4) post-move support services.

(b) SMALL AREA FAIR MARKET RENTS.—Within 2 years of enactment,
    the Secretary shall apply Small Area Fair Market Rents in all
    metropolitan areas with populations exceeding 250,000.

(c) SOURCE OF INCOME PROTECTION.—
    (1) It shall be unlawful for any person to discriminate in
        housing because of a person's source of income, including
        housing vouchers.
    (2) The Fair Housing Act is amended to include source of
        income as a protected class.

SEC. 8. AUTHORIZATION.

There are authorized to be appropriated such sums as necessary
to carry out this Act.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: $25,000 grants for first-generation buyers directly addresses wealth gap for homeownership.

- **Section 4**: Clarifies that targeted lending programs are legal, addressing bank reluctance.

- **Section 5**: Restores AFFH enforcement dismantled in prior administration.

- **Section 6**: Addresses documented appraisal bias that undervalues homes in minority neighborhoods.

- **Section 7**: Expands housing mobility programs with proven effectiveness.

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Cost | $10B/year is modest given housing subsidies to wealthy |
| "Reverse discrimination" claims | First-generation is race-neutral criterion |
| Real estate industry opposition | Increased transactions benefit industry |
| Implementation complexity | Build on existing HUD programs |

## State Model Legislation

### Universal Pre-K Act (Model)

**Purpose**: Model legislation for states to establish universal pre-kindergarten.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Universal Pre-Kindergarten
Act".

SECTION 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.—The Legislature finds that:
    (1) High-quality pre-kindergarten produces returns of 7:1 or
        higher over the lifetime of participants.
    (2) Quality pre-kindergarten reduces achievement gaps,
        increases graduation rates, and improves adult earnings.
    (3) Access to pre-kindergarten in this State is uneven,
        with only [X]% of eligible children enrolled.
    (4) Universal access ensures all children, regardless of
        family income, have equal opportunity.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to establish a system
    of high-quality, voluntary, universal pre-kindergarten for
    all children ages 3 and 4.

SECTION 3. UNIVERSAL PRE-KINDERGARTEN PROGRAM.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established the [State] Universal
    Pre-Kindergarten Program to provide access to pre-kindergarten
    for all children ages 3 and 4.

(b) ELIGIBILITY.—All children who are residents of this State
    and are age 3 or 4 as of September 1 of the school year are
    eligible to participate.

(c) VOLUNTARY PARTICIPATION.—Participation in the program is
    voluntary for families.

SECTION 4. QUALITY STANDARDS.

(a) PROGRAM REQUIREMENTS.—Pre-kindergarten programs shall meet
    the following standards:
    (1) CLASS SIZE.—Maximum class size of 18 children.
    (2) STAFF RATIO.—Staff-to-child ratio of at least 1:9.
    (3) TEACHER QUALIFICATIONS.—Lead teachers shall hold a
        bachelor's degree in early childhood education or a
        related field, or shall be enrolled in a program to
        obtain such degree within 5 years.
    (4) COMPENSATION.—Lead teachers shall be compensated at
        not less than the salary of a public school teacher
        with equivalent education and experience.
    (5) CURRICULUM.—Programs shall use evidence-based,
        developmentally appropriate curricula aligned with
        State early learning standards.
    (6) HOURS.—Programs shall operate for a minimum of 6 hours
        per day, 5 days per week, following the public school
        calendar.
    (7) FAMILY ENGAGEMENT.—Programs shall include a family
        engagement component.

(b) QUALITY IMPROVEMENT.—The [Department] shall establish a
    quality improvement system to assist programs in meeting
    standards.

SECTION 5. DELIVERY SYSTEM.

(a) MIXED DELIVERY.—Pre-kindergarten shall be delivered through
    a mixed system including:
    (1) Public schools;
    (2) Licensed child care centers;
    (3) Head Start programs;
    (4) Private pre-kindergarten programs meeting quality
        standards.

(b) EQUITABLE ACCESS.—The [Department] shall ensure that programs
    are geographically distributed to provide access to all
    families, including in rural areas.

SECTION 6. FUNDING.

(a) APPROPRIATION.—There is appropriated from the General Fund
    to the [Department] $[amount] for fiscal year [year], and
    such sums as necessary for subsequent fiscal years.

(b) PER-PUPIL ALLOCATION.—The per-pupil allocation shall be not
    less than $[amount], adjusted annually for inflation.

(c) SUPPLEMENT, NOT SUPPLANT.—Funds appropriated under this Act
    shall supplement, not supplant, existing State and local
    funding for early childhood education.

SECTION 7. PHASE-IN.

(a) TIMELINE.—The program shall be phased in as follows:
    (1) Year 1: All 4-year-olds from families at or below 200%
        of poverty.
    (2) Year 2: All 4-year-olds.
    (3) Year 3: All 4-year-olds and 3-year-olds from families
        at or below 200% of poverty.
    (4) Year 4 and thereafter: All 3- and 4-year-olds.

SECTION 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on July 1, [year].
```

### Zoning Reform Act (Model)

**Purpose**: Model legislation for states to preempt exclusionary local zoning.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Housing Opportunity and
Mobility Enhancement Act".

SECTION 2. FINDINGS.

The Legislature finds that:
(1) Exclusionary zoning practices have created severe housing
    shortages and affordability crises across this State.
(2) Single-family-only zoning excludes low- and moderate-income
    households from access to high-opportunity neighborhoods.
(3) Restrictive zoning perpetuates racial and economic
    segregation.
(4) Local land use decisions have regional and statewide
    impacts on housing availability and economic development.
(5) State action is necessary to ensure adequate housing for
    all residents.

SECTION 3. ACCESSORY DWELLING UNITS.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—Notwithstanding any local ordinance, accessory
    dwelling units shall be a permitted use on any lot where
    single-family residential use is permitted.

(b) STANDARDS.—Local jurisdictions may impose reasonable
    standards on ADUs including:
    (1) Maximum size of 1,000 square feet or 50% of the primary
        unit, whichever is greater.
    (2) Setback requirements no greater than those for the
        primary unit.
    (3) One parking space (waived within 1/2 mile of transit).
    (4) Owner-occupancy requirement for either the primary or
        accessory unit.

(c) PROHIBITED RESTRICTIONS.—Local jurisdictions may not:
    (1) Require minimum lot size beyond that for the primary
        unit.
    (2) Impose design review beyond basic safety standards.
    (3) Limit ADUs based on neighborhood character.
    (4) Cap the number of ADUs within a jurisdiction.

SECTION 4. MIDDLE HOUSING.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—Notwithstanding any local ordinance, the
    following housing types shall be permitted uses on any lot
    where single-family residential use is permitted:
    (1) Duplexes.
    (2) Triplexes and fourplexes on lots of 5,000 square feet
        or larger.
    (3) Cottage clusters.
    (4) Townhouses.

(b) DENSITY.—Maximum density for middle housing shall be:
    (1) No less than 2 units per lot for duplexes.
    (2) No less than 4 units per lot for triplexes, fourplexes,
        and cottage clusters.
    (3) No limits on townhouse density beyond lot coverage and
        setback requirements.

(c) DESIGN STANDARDS.—Local jurisdictions may impose reasonable
    design standards provided they do not:
    (1) Discourage the development of middle housing through
        unreasonable cost or delay.
    (2) Impose standards more restrictive than those for
        single-family homes.

SECTION 5. TRANSIT-ORIENTED DEVELOPMENT.

(a) ZONING REQUIREMENTS.—Within 1/2 mile of a rail transit
    station or 1/4 mile of a high-frequency bus stop:
    (1) Residential density of at least 30 units per acre shall
        be permitted.
    (2) Mixed-use development shall be permitted.
    (3) Parking minimums are eliminated.

(b) HEIGHT.—Maximum building heights within transit zones shall
    be at least 55 feet.

SECTION 6. AFFORDABLE HOUSING.

(a) DENSITY BONUS.—Developments that include affordable units
    shall receive a density bonus as follows:
    (1) 10% affordable units: 25% density bonus.
    (2) 20% affordable units: 50% density bonus.
    (3) 30% affordable units: 75% density bonus.

(b) DEFINITION.—"Affordable unit" means a unit affordable to
    households at or below 80% of area median income.

(c) BY-RIGHT APPROVAL.—Affordable housing developments meeting
    zoning standards shall be approved by right, without
    discretionary review.

SECTION 7. COMPLIANCE.

(a) DEADLINE.—Local jurisdictions shall amend their zoning codes
    to comply with this Act within 2 years of enactment.

(b) ENFORCEMENT.—If a local jurisdiction fails to comply:
    (1) The State shall approve housing developments meeting
        the standards of this Act.
    (2) The State may withhold transportation and housing funds.

(c) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—Any person may bring an action to
    enforce this Act, with prevailing plaintiffs entitled to
    reasonable attorney's fees.

SECTION 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on January 1, [year].
```

### Economic Mobility Tax Credit Act (Model)

**Purpose**: Model state legislation for state-level earned income tax credits.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Working Families Tax
Credit Act".

SECTION 2. ESTABLISHMENT OF CREDIT.

(a) IN GENERAL.—There is established a refundable [State] Working
    Families Tax Credit for individuals eligible for the federal
    earned income tax credit.

(b) AMOUNT.—The credit shall equal 40% of the federal earned
    income tax credit for which the taxpayer is eligible.

(c) REFUNDABILITY.—The credit shall be fully refundable. If the
    credit exceeds the taxpayer's tax liability, the excess shall
    be refunded to the taxpayer.

SECTION 3. CHILDLESS WORKER SUPPLEMENT.

    individuals without qualifying children who are eligible

    annually for inflation.

(c) RATIONALE.—This supplement addresses the inadequacy of the
    federal credit for childless workers.

SECTION 4. EXPANSION OF ELIGIBILITY.

(a) AGE.—For purposes of this credit, eligibility shall extend
    to individuals age 18 and older, regardless of student status.

(b) ITIN FILERS.—Individuals filing with an Individual Taxpayer
    Identification Number (ITIN) who would otherwise qualify for
    the federal credit shall be eligible for the State credit.

SECTION 5. ADMINISTRATION.

(a) The [Department of Revenue] shall administer this credit.

(b) The Department shall conduct outreach to ensure eligible
    taxpayers are aware of and claim the credit.

(c) The Department shall provide for advance payment of the
    credit on a quarterly basis for taxpayers who elect it.

SECTION 6. APPROPRIATION.

There is appropriated from the General Fund such sums as
necessary to carry out this Act.

SECTION 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall apply to taxable years beginning on or after
January 1, [year].
```

## Regulatory Framework

### HUD Housing Mobility Program Regulations

**Existing Authority**: Housing Choice Voucher program under Section 8.

**Draft Regulation**:

```text
PART 983—HOUSING MOBILITY SERVICES

§ 983.1 Purpose.

This part establishes requirements for Housing Mobility Services
funded under the Housing Choice Voucher program to assist
voucher holders in moving to high-opportunity neighborhoods.

§ 983.2 Definitions.

(a) "High-opportunity area" means a census tract meeting criteria
    established by the Secretary, including:
    (1) Poverty rate below 20%;
    (2) School proficiency in the top half of the metropolitan
        area;
    (3) Access to employment opportunities.

(b) "Housing mobility services" means counseling, search
    assistance, and support services to help voucher holders
    locate and maintain housing in high-opportunity areas.

§ 983.3 Program Requirements.

(a) PHAs receiving mobility funding shall provide:
    (1) One-on-one housing counseling;
    (2) Information about high-opportunity neighborhoods;
    (3) Transportation for housing search;
    (4) Security deposit assistance;
    (5) Landlord outreach and recruitment;
    (6) Post-move support for 12 months.

(b) PHAs shall track and report:
    (1) Number of families receiving mobility services;
    (2) Number of families moving to high-opportunity areas;
    (3) Lease-up rates;
    (4) Housing stability outcomes.

§ 983.4 Small Area Fair Market Rents.

(a) All metropolitan PHAs shall use Small Area Fair Market Rents
    (SAFMRs) rather than metropolitan-wide FMRs.

(b) SAFMRs shall be calculated at the ZIP code level.

(c) Exception payment standards up to 120% of SAFMR are
    authorized for high-opportunity areas.

§ 983.5 Source of Income Protections.

(a) PHAs shall require, as a condition of receiving mobility
    funds, that participating landlords not discriminate based
    on source of income.

(b) PHAs shall maintain and make public a list of landlords
    participating in the mobility program.
```

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

**Spending Clause**:
- Baby bonds, pre-K, housing programs are within spending power
- Conditions on state funding must be related to purpose

**Commerce Clause**:
- Zoning preemption may rely on housing market effects
- Fair housing has established Commerce Clause basis

**Equal Protection**:
- Programs targeting first-generation buyers (race-neutral) are permissible
- Race-conscious programs face strict scrutiny post-SFFA

**Takings**:
- Zoning changes generally not takings if value preserved
- Inclusionary zoning upheld in most jurisdictions

### Preemption Questions

| State Law | Federal Impact |
|-----------|----------------|
| State baby bonds | Supplements federal program |
| State pre-K | Federal funds supplement, not supplant |
| State zoning | Federal housing programs may require compliance |
| State EITC | Supplements federal credit |

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Provision | Enforcement |
|-----------|-------------|
| American Opportunity Act | Treasury administration, IRS for tax provisions |
| HOME Act | HUD enforcement, private right of action |
| State pre-K | State education agency |
| State zoning | State approval, private right of action |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### American Opportunity Act (Baby Bonds)

**Potential Loopholes**:

| Loophole | How Exploited | Severity |
|----------|---------------|----------|
| Wealth measurement gaming | Families could temporarily reduce wealth before birth | Medium |
| Qualified use restrictions circumvented | Create shell businesses; buy properties through relatives | Medium |
| Non-citizen children excluded | Creates two-tiered system for immigrant families | High |
| Inflation erosion | Fixed amounts lose value over time if not indexed | Medium |
| State clawback | States could reduce other benefits for account holders | High |

**Shortcomings**:

| Issue | Description | Impact |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| 18-year delay | Children born today don't benefit until adulthood | Significant |
| Doesn't address existing adults | Current generation excluded | Significant |
| Qualified use limits | May not address most pressing needs (healthcare, debt) | Moderate |
| Wealth-based not race-based | May not fully address racial wealth gap | Moderate |
| Administrative complexity | Tracking millions of accounts | Moderate |

**Rectification Procedures**:

| Issue | Rectification Approach |
|-------|----------------------|
| Wealth gaming | Use 3-year average wealth; penalties for manipulation |
| Qualified use circumvention | Verification requirements; fraud penalties; clawback provisions |
| Immigrant exclusion | Extend to all lawful residents; create pathway for undocumented |
| Inflation erosion | Automatic annual adjustment tied to CPI |
| State clawback | Federal preemption of state benefit reductions |
| 18-year delay | Supplement with adult programs (matched savings, etc.) |
| Existing adults excluded | Separate program for wealth-building in current generation |

### Homeownership Opportunity Act

**Potential Loopholes**:

| Loophole | How Exploited | Severity |
|----------|---------------|----------|
| "First-generation" definition | Claim parents never owned home falsely | Medium |
| Program capture by better-off | Higher-income eligible families outcompete | Medium |
| Geographic limitation | Funds used in low-opportunity areas | High |
| Residency requirement evasion | Rent out property while claiming residence | Medium |
| Appraisal bias persists | Second appraiser from same network | Medium |

**Shortcomings**:

| Issue | Description | Impact |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| Doesn't address supply | Down payment help without supply = price increases | Significant |
| Homeownership not always optimal | Forces wealth-building into one asset class | Moderate |
| Credit barriers remain | Down payment doesn't solve credit score issues | Moderate |
| Market timing risk | Buyers enter at market peaks | Moderate |
| Maintenance costs | New owners may struggle with ongoing costs | Moderate |

**Rectification Procedures**:

| Issue | Rectification Approach |
|-------|----------------------|
| False claims | Verification through tax records; fraud penalties |
| Program capture | Lottery system; priority for lower-income applicants |
| Geographic concentration | Bonus for high-opportunity areas; counseling requirement |
| Residency evasion | Annual verification; clawback for non-compliance |
| Appraisal bias | Require second appraiser from different network; blind review |
| Supply constraints | Pair with zoning reform; prioritize new construction |
| Credit barriers | Credit-building programs; alternative scoring |
| Market timing | Financial counseling; consider shared equity models |

### Universal Pre-K (State Model)

**Potential Loopholes**:

| Loophole | How Exploited | Severity |
|----------|---------------|----------|
| Quality standards waived | States grant exceptions to avoid costs | High |
| Teacher pay parity undermined | Define "parity" narrowly; exclude benefits | High |
| Mixed delivery gaming | Funds flow to low-quality private providers | Medium |
| Phase-in delays | States slow-walk implementation | High |
| Supplanting | States reduce other early childhood funding | High |

**Shortcomings**:

| Issue | Description | Impact |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| Ages 3-4 only | Critical 0-3 period not addressed | Significant |
| Workforce shortage | Not enough qualified teachers | Significant |
| Summer gap | School-year only leaves summer uncovered | Moderate |
| Special needs | May not adequately serve children with disabilities | Moderate |
| Rural access | Harder to serve low-density areas | Moderate |

**Rectification Procedures**:

| Issue | Rectification Approach |
|-------|----------------------|
| Quality waivers | Federal minimum standards; funding contingent on quality |
| Pay parity erosion | Define parity to include benefits; annual verification |
| Provider quality | Quality rating system required; funding tied to ratings |
| Phase-in delays | Automatic implementation triggers; penalties for delay |
| Supplanting | Maintenance of effort requirements with teeth |
| 0-3 gap | Expand Early Head Start; add 0-3 component |
| Workforce | Loan forgiveness; training programs; pay increases |
| Summer | Extend program year; summer programming |

### Zoning Reform (State Model)

**Potential Loopholes**:

| Loophole | How Exploited | Severity |
|----------|---------------|----------|
| "Reasonable standards" interpreted restrictively | Excessive design review, delays | High |
| Parking requirements maintained | Parking near transit lines | Medium |
| Height limits in transit zones | 55 feet minimum gamed with mezzanines | Low |
| Affordability definition | 80% AMI is not affordable for many | Medium |
| Local non-compliance | Cities refuse to update codes | High |

**Shortcomings**:

| Issue | Description | Impact |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| Doesn't mandate construction | Zoning allows but doesn't require | Significant |
| Infrastructure burden | New housing needs schools, services | Moderate |
| Displacement pressure | New housing may raise surrounding rents | Moderate |
| Implementation timeline | 2 years is long; delays possible | Moderate |
| Single-family conversion limits | May not work for older housing stock | Low |

**Rectification Procedures**:

| Issue | Rectification Approach |
|-------|----------------------|
| "Reasonable standards" abuse | Define prohibited standards explicitly; state review |
| Parking workarounds | Eliminate parking minimums statewide near transit |
| Height limit gaming | Define floor-to-floor requirements |
| Affordability threshold | Lower to 60% AMI; deeper affordability bonuses |
| Non-compliance | Automatic state approval of compliant projects |
| Infrastructure | Impact fees capped; state infrastructure support |
| Displacement | Tenant protections; right to return; affordable set-asides |

### Cross-Cutting Issues

**Systemic Vulnerabilities**:

| Vulnerability | Applies To | Mitigation |
|---------------|------------|------------|
| Funding instability | All programs | Mandatory appropriations; trust funds |
| Political backlash | Baby bonds, zoning | Universal design; build constituency |
| Implementation variation | State programs | Federal minimum standards |
| Judicial challenges | Zoning, affirmative programs | Careful constitutional design |
| Measurement problems | Wealth, first-generation status | Multiple verification methods |

**Ongoing Monitoring Requirements**:

| Requirement | Frequency | Responsible Entity |
|-------------|-----------|---------------------|
| Mobility statistics | Annual | Opportunity Insights partnership |
| Program participation | Quarterly | Implementing agencies |
| Quality standards compliance | Annual | State/federal oversight |
| Disparate impact analysis | Annual | Civil rights offices |
| Cost-benefit evaluation | Every 5 years | GAO, academic researchers |

**Amendment Procedures**:

For federal legislation:

1. GAO biennial report on program effectiveness
2. Automatic adjustment triggers for thresholds
3. Congressional reauthorization every 10 years
4. Regulatory updates through notice-and-comment

For state legislation:

1. Annual legislative review of participation data
2. Sunset provisions requiring affirmative reauthorization
3. Automatic inflation adjustments
4. Local compliance reporting

### General Implementation Concerns

**Administrative Capacity**:

| Concern | Mitigation |
|---------|------------|
| Treasury managing millions of accounts | Partner with existing 529 infrastructure |
| HUD implementing mobility programs | Build on existing voucher administration |
| States implementing pre-K | Phase-in with capacity building |
| Localities updating zoning | State technical assistance |

**Coordination Requirements**:

| Programs | Coordination Need |
|----------|-------------------|
| Baby bonds + savings accounts | Prevent duplication; allow supplementation |
| Pre-K + Head Start | Mixed delivery without gaps |
| Housing mobility + vouchers | Seamless integration |
| EITC + state credits | Coordinated administration |

**Equity Considerations**:

| Consideration | Approach |
|---------------|----------|
| Rural access | Additional funding for rural areas |
| Disability access | Universal design requirements |
| Language access | Translation, interpretation requirements |
| Undocumented families | Clarify eligibility; firewall from immigration enforcement |
