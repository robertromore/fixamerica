# Economic Mobility: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

Economic mobility -- the ability to rise from the circumstances of one's birth -- is perhaps the most unifying value in American political life. Nearly every political tradition claims to support "opportunity," yet the meaning of that word and the policies it implies vary dramatically. Conservatives emphasize removing government barriers and rewarding individual effort. Progressives focus on structural inequities that stack the odds against disadvantaged children. Libertarians argue that free markets are the engine of mobility, while populists blame elites for hoarding opportunity. These differences produce sharply divergent views on whether programs like baby bonds, universal pre-K, housing mobility vouchers, and zoning reform represent genuine opportunity-creation or counterproductive government overreach. With absolute mobility declining -- only 50% of children born in 1980 earn more than their parents, compared to 92% for the 1940 cohort -- the urgency of this debate is difficult to overstate.

## Perspective Definitions

| Perspective | Core Values | Policy Orientation |
|-------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Individual responsibility, family, free enterprise | Reduce regulation, school choice, tax incentives for saving |
| Liberal | Equal opportunity, social investment, inclusion | Universal pre-K, housing mobility, anti-discrimination enforcement |
| Progressive | Structural equity, racial justice, systemic reform | Baby bonds, wealth redistribution, reparations study, zoning overhaul |
| Libertarian | Individual liberty, minimal government, free markets | Deregulate housing, eliminate subsidies, end occupational licensing |
| Constitutionalist | Enumerated powers, federalism, limited central authority | State-led solutions, oppose federal education mandates |
| Populist | Anti-elite, pro-worker, economic nationalism | Anti-corporate, community investment, restrict immigration |
| Centrist | Pragmatism, evidence-based policy, bipartisan reform | Targeted programs with proven ROI, public-private partnerships |
| Religious Right | Faith, family, moral responsibility, stewardship | Faith-based initiatives, family stability, charitable sector |
| Democratic Socialist | Economic democracy, public goods, worker power | Universal programs, wealth caps, public housing, free education |

## Perspective Analyses

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 3/5 - Mixed Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Rhetoric emphasizes "opportunity for all" but policy priorities often favor existing wealth holders through capital gains and estate tax preferences. |
| Principle consistency | Consistently supports school choice and deregulation; inconsistency between opposing government spending and supporting mortgage interest deduction and 529 plans that subsidize upper-income wealth-building. |
| Goalpost stability | Moderate; shifts focus from "opportunity" to "dependency" rhetoric depending on which programs are under discussion. |
| Zero-sum behavior | Often frames mobility programs as taking from the successful to give to the undeserving. |

**Justification:** Conservatives genuinely value upward mobility but define it primarily through individual effort and market participation. The tension between supporting tax preferences that disproportionately benefit the wealthy and opposing programs that directly invest in disadvantaged children creates a consistency gap.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Heritage Foundation proposals emphasizing school choice and deregulation as mobility drivers.
- Conservative opposition to expanded EITC and child tax credit despite evidence of mobility effects.

### Constitutionalist Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Consistent focus on enumerated powers and federalism. |
| Principle consistency | Consistently opposes federal programs; supports state-level experimentation. |

**Justification:** Constitutionalists apply a consistent framework: if the Constitution does not authorize it, the federal government should not do it. This produces reliable opposition to most federal mobility programs while leaving space for state-level action. The tension is that their framework may block effective interventions even when states fail to act.

**Evidence for assessment:**

## Compromise Proposals

### Compromise 1: The "Opportunity Starts Early" Compact

**Bridges:** Liberal + Centrist + Conservative (partial) + Religious Right (partial)

**Core Agreement:** Early childhood is the highest-return investment window for mobility; children should not be penalized for their parents' circumstances.

**Policy Description:**

A universal pre-K program delivered through a mixed system of public schools, community organizations, and faith-based providers. Includes quality standards tied to research but preserves parental choice among providers. Funded through a combination of federal grants and state matching funds, with quality standards but local flexibility in curriculum and delivery.

**Concern Mapping:**

### Compromise 2: The "Unlock Neighborhoods" Agreement

**Bridges:** Libertarian + Liberal + Progressive + Centrist

**Core Agreement:** Government restrictions on housing construction reduce supply, increase costs, and trap people in low-opportunity neighborhoods.

**Policy Description:**

State-level zoning reform that legalizes accessory dwelling units, middle housing (duplexes through fourplexes), and transit-oriented development while pairing deregulation with inclusionary requirements (10-15% affordable units in new developments over 10 units). Eliminates single-family-only zoning while preserving height and setback standards. Includes source-of-income anti-discrimination protections for voucher holders.

**Concern Mapping:**

**Concessions:**

|-------------|-------------------|
| Libertarian | Accepts modest inclusionary requirements. |
| Progressive | Accepts market-rate development alongside affordable units. |

### Compromise 3: The "Work and Save" Partnership

**Bridges:** Conservative + Centrist + Liberal + Populist

**Core Agreement:** People who work hard should be able to build wealth; the tax code should reward work and saving, not just existing wealth.

**Policy Description:**

Combines EITC expansion (doubled credit for childless workers, monthly payment option) with Children's Savings Accounts (universal accounts at birth with progressive matching up to $500/year for low-income families). Funded by closing the stepped-up basis loophole for estates over $5 million. Includes expanded Opportunity Zones with stricter accountability requirements.

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The **"Opportunity Starts Early" coalition** built around universal pre-K represents the most politically viable near-term opportunity. It has the broadest support, the strongest evidence base, and bipartisan state-level precedents. Framing it as an economic investment (7:1 returns) rather than a social program helps secure centrist and moderate conservative support.

### Key Obstacles

1. **Fiscal opposition:** The comprehensive American Opportunity Act costs roughly $1 trillion over 10 years. Breaking it into component pieces (pre-K first, then EITC, then baby bonds) is more politically feasible.
2. **Racial framing:** Programs explicitly tied to racial equity face stronger opposition. Race-neutral designs (income-based, geography-based) achieve similar distributional effects with broader support.
3. **Federalism tensions:** Conservative and Constitutionalist opposition to federal programs is structural. State-level model legislation may be more productive than federal mandates.

### Low Consistency Partners

**Populists** (3/5 consistency) and **Conservatives** (3/5 consistency) are the most persuadable but unpredictable partners. Populists respond to "hardworking families" framing and anti-elite funding mechanisms (closing loopholes for billionaires). Conservatives can be engaged through work requirements, parental choice, and local flexibility provisions.

### Recommended Approach

Pursue a **phased "Opportunity Agenda"** strategy:

1. **Phase 1:** Universal pre-K through mixed delivery (broadest coalition; proven returns).
2. **Phase 2:** EITC expansion and Children's Savings Accounts (work-based; bipartisan appeal).
3. **Phase 3:** Zoning reform at state level (libertarian-progressive alliance; housing supply).
4. **Phase 4:** Baby bonds and comprehensive wealth-building (requires larger political shift).
