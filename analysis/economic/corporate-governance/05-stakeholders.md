# Corporate Governance Reform: Stakeholders

## Overview

Corporate governance affects virtually everyone in the economy, though in vastly different ways. Understanding who gains and loses under the current system is essential to understanding the politics of reform.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Shareholders

**Who They Are**

| Type | Characteristics |
|------|-----------------|
| Institutional investors | Pension funds, mutual funds, hedge funds |
| Retail investors | Individual stock owners |
| Index fund holders | Passive investors in broad market |
| Employee shareholders | Workers with stock through 401(k) or options |
| Ultra-wealthy | Top 1% owns 54% of stocks |

**Interests**

| Interest | Current System |
|----------|----------------|
| Stock price appreciation | Maximized under shareholder primacy |
| Dividends | Prioritized |
| Buybacks | Inflates share value |
| Governance rights | Nominal but rarely exercised |

**Distribution of Stock Ownership**

| Group | Share of Stock Ownership |
|-------|-------------------------|
| Top 1% | 54% |
| Top 10% | 89% |
| Bottom 50% | 1% |

### Corporate Executives

**Who They Are**

| Level | Number |
|-------|--------|
| Fortune 500 CEOs | 500 |
| C-suite executives | ~5,000 |
| Senior executives | ~50,000 |
| Upper management | ~500,000 |

**Interests**

| Interest | Current System |
|----------|----------------|
| Compensation | Aligned with shareholders, extremely high |
| Job security | Precarious but lucrative exit packages |
| Decision authority | Broad discretion under business judgment rule |
| Status and prestige | High social position |

**CEO Compensation Breakdown**

| Component | Average Fortune 500 |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Base salary | $1.4 million |
| Stock awards | $8.2 million |
| Option awards | $2.1 million |
| Bonus | $2.8 million |
| Other | $2.2 million |
| Total | $16.7 million |

### Workers

**Who They Are**

| Category | Number |
|----------|--------|
| Private sector workers | 130 million |
| Public company workers | 35+ million |
| Fortune 500 workers | 30+ million |
| Unionized workers | 6% of private sector |

**Interests**

| Interest | Current System |
|----------|----------------|
| Wages | Suppressed relative to productivity |
| Job security | Undermined by cost-cutting |
| Benefits | Declining pensions, healthcare |
| Voice in decisions | Virtually none |
| Training and development | Underfunded |

**Worker Outcomes Under Shareholder Primacy**

| Metric | Trend |
|--------|-------|
| Real wages (1979-2023) | +17% |
| Productivity (1979-2023) | +64% |
| Defined benefit pensions | -70% |
| Union membership | -50% |
| Job tenure | Declining |

**Who They Are**

| Characteristic | Value |
|----------------|-------|
| Fortune 500 directors | ~5,000 |
| Average age | 63 years |
| Average tenure | 8 years |
| Interlocking directorships | Common |
| Former CEOs | 25% |

**Interests**

| Interest | Current System |
|----------|----------------|
| Compensation | $300,000-$400,000 average |
| Time commitment | 250 hours/year |
| Liability protection | Strong |
| Reputation | Tied to company performance |

**Director Demographics**

| Category | Share |
|----------|-------|
| Male | 70% |
| Female | 30% |
| White | 79% |
| Minority | 21% |
| Worker representative | <1% |

## Secondary Stakeholders

### Communities

**Who They Are**

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| Company towns | Dependent on single employer |
| Manufacturing regions | Industrial midwest, south |
| Corporate headquarters cities | HQ relocation impacts |
| Local governments | Depend on corporate tax base |

**Interests**

| Interest | Current System |
|----------|----------------|
| Local employment | Subject to relocation threats |
| Tax revenue | Eroded by tax avoidance |
| Community investment | Reduced |
| Environmental protection | Often sacrificed |

**Community Impact Examples**

| Event | Community Impact |
|-------|------------------|
| Plant closure | Devastated local economy |
| Headquarters relocation | Tax base loss |
| Bankruptcy | Pension defaults |
| Merger/acquisition | Job consolidation |

### Suppliers and Contractors

**Who They Are**

| Category | Characteristics |
|----------|-----------------|
| Small suppliers | Dependent on large buyers |
| Contractors | Absorb labor cost pressures |
| Franchisees | Subject to franchisor control |
| Gig workers | Classified as independent |

**Interests**

| Interest | Current System |
|----------|----------------|
| Fair payment terms | Often extended to 90-120 days |
| Contract stability | Precarious |
| Negotiating power | Weak |
| Pass-through of cost pressure | Absorb wage cuts |

### Customers and Consumers

**Who They Are**

| Type | Stake |
|------|-------|
| Individual consumers | Product quality, safety, prices |
| Business customers | Supplier reliability |
| Government purchasers | Contractor performance |

**Interests**

| Interest | Current System |
|----------|----------------|
| Product quality | May be compromised for profits |
| Safety | Minimum compliance |
| Fair pricing | Market power exploitation |
| Customer service | Often reduced for costs |

### Taxpayers and Government

**Who They Are**

| Level | Role |
|-------|------|
| Federal government | Corporate tax collection, enforcement |
| State governments | Incorporation, regulation |
| Local governments | Property taxes, services |
| Taxpayers | Bear costs of corporate externalities |

**Interests**

| Interest | Current System |
|----------|----------------|
| Tax revenue | Eroded by avoidance, offshoring |
| Regulatory compliance | Minimal |
| Bailout liability | Socialized losses |
| Environmental cleanup | Often taxpayer-funded |

## Political Alignment

### Who Supports Reform

| Group | Reform Priority |
|-------|-----------------|
| Progressive Democrats | Comprehensive reform |
| Labor unions | Worker representation |
| Some institutional investors | Long-term value |
| Impact investing community | ESG, stakeholder focus |
| Some business leaders | Stakeholder capitalism |

### Who Opposes Reform

| Group | Opposition Reason |
|-------|-------------------|
| Business associations | Threatens shareholder returns |
| Republican Party | Free market ideology |
| Some Democrats | Corporate donor influence |
| Hedge funds | Activist investing |
| Corporate management | Status quo benefits them |
| Delaware interests | Revenue, legal industry |

## Power Analysis

### Who Has Power

| Actor | Power Source |
|-------|--------------|
| Institutional investors | Voting power, capital allocation |
| Corporate executives | Operational control |
| Delaware courts | Legal interpretation |
| SEC | Regulatory authority |
| Congress | Legislative power |
| Business lobbyists | Political influence |

### Who Lacks Power

| Actor | Power Gap |
|-------|-----------|
| Workers | No board representation, declining unions |
| Communities | No legal standing |
| Small shareholders | Collective action problems |
| Future stakeholders | Not represented in decisions |

## Coalition Dynamics

### Potential Reform Coalition

| Member | Motivation |
|--------|------------|
| Workers | Voice, wages, security |
| Long-term investors | Sustainable returns |
| Communities | Local investment |
| Environmentalists | Climate accountability |
| Small business | Fair treatment by large corporations |

### Obstacles to Coalition

| Challenge | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Fragmented interests | Many stakeholders, different priorities |
| Power imbalance | Opponents have resources |
| Collective action | Hard to organize dispersed stakeholders |
| Ideological barriers | Free market narrative |
