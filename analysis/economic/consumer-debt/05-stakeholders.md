# Consumer Debt: Stakeholders

## Overview

The consumer debt system involves multiple stakeholders with vastly different interests, resources, and political power. Understanding these stakeholders helps explain why the system persists and what coalitions might achieve reform.

## Those Burdened by Debt

### Student Loan Borrowers

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Data |
|----------------|------|
| Total borrowers | 45.3 million |
| With federal loans | 43.4 million |
| With private loans | 3.5 million (overlap exists) |
| Average age | 37 |
| % who have defaulted ever | 25% |
| Median balance | $23,000 |

**Subgroups**

| Group | Population | Unique Challenges |
|-------|------------|-------------------|
| Dropouts | 10 million | Debt without degree benefits |
| For-profit attendees | 5 million | Often defrauded, limited outcomes |
| Graduate borrowers | 15 million | High debt ($100K+), good outcomes variable |
| Parent PLUS borrowers | 3.7 million | Borrowed for children, retirement threatened |
| Black borrowers | 5 million | Higher debt, worse outcomes |
| First-generation college | 15 million | Less family support |

**Interests**

- Debt cancellation or relief
- Lower interest rates
- Improved income-driven repayment
- Bankruptcy discharge access
- Accountability for predatory schools

**Political Power**

- Growing organization (Debt Collective, Student Debt Crisis Center)
- Vocal constituency for Democrats
- Strike actions emerging
- But: diffuse, stigmatized

### Medical Debt Holders

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Data |
|----------------|------|
| Americans with medical debt | 100+ million |
| Medical debt in collections | 23 million people |
| Average amount | $2,000 |
| Uninsured with debt | 47% |
| Insured with debt | 18% |

**Most Vulnerable Groups**

| Group | Medical Debt Rate | Average Amount |
|-------|------------------|----------------|
| Cancer survivors | 45% | $12,000 |
| Chronically ill | 38% | $8,000 |
| Uninsured | 47% | $5,000 |
| Low-income | 31% | $3,000 |
| Rural residents | 25% | $4,000 |

**Interests**

- Medical debt forgiveness
- Hospital financial assistance enforcement
- Surprise billing protection
- Universal coverage
- Credit reporting removal

**Political Power**

- Less organized than student borrowers
- Illness makes advocacy difficult
- RIP Medical Debt provides relief
- Connected to healthcare reform movement

### Credit Card Debtors

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Data |
|----------------|------|
| Americans with credit card debt | 84 million (revolving) |
| Total cardholders | 175 million |
| Average balance (revolvers) | $6,500 |
| Paying minimum only | 35% of revolvers |
| Paying full balance monthly | 55% of cardholders |

**Interests**

- Interest rate caps
- Fee limitations
- Better disclosure
- Bankruptcy access
- Credit counseling resources

**Political Power**

- Not organized as debt holders
- Individual consumer advocacy groups
- Less sympathy ("chose to borrow")

### Payday Loan Users

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Data |
|----------------|------|
| Annual users | 12 million |
| Repeat users | 80% |
| Average borrower income | $30,000 |
| Average loans per year | 8 |
| Average fees paid annually | $520 |

**Demographics**

- 70% female
- 55% minorities
- 58% have children
- 69% used for recurring expenses (rent, utilities)

**Interests**

- Access to affordable small-dollar credit
- Rate caps
- Ability to repay requirements
- Payday lending alternatives (postal banking)

**Political Power**

- Very low: poor, stigmatized, dispersed
- Some organization through community groups
- Center for Responsible Lending advocates

### Auto Loan Borrowers

**Subprime Auto Borrowers**

| Characteristic | Data |
|----------------|------|
| Subprime auto borrowers | 35 million |
| Average APR | 18-21% |
| Annual repossessions | 2 million |
| Negative equity rate | 25% |

**Interests**

- Fair lending practices
- Rate caps
- Repossession protections
- Vehicle reliability standards

### Debt Collection Industry

**Major Players**

| Company | Revenue | Accounts |
|---------|---------|----------|
| Encore Capital | $1.4 billion | 10+ million |
| PRA Group | $1.1 billion | 8+ million |
| Jefferson Capital | $500 million | 5+ million |
| Midland Credit | $800 million | 7+ million |

**Industry Structure**

- Original creditor collection departments
- Third-party collectors (work for fee)
- Debt buyers (purchase debt for pennies)

**Industry Totals**

- Revenue: $13+ billion annually
- Accounts in collection: 67 million
- Average purchase price: 4 cents per dollar
- Recovery rate: 20-30 cents per dollar

**Interests**

- Preserve collection powers
- Maintain wage garnishment access
- Oppose stricter regulations
- Keep legal processes accessible

**Political Power**

- ACA International lobbying
- Campaign contributions
- Industry-friendly regulation historically
- But: CFPB oversight increasing

## Regulatory Bodies

### Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)

**Role**

- Primary consumer protection agency
- Student loan oversight
- Credit card regulation
- Debt collection rules
- Credit reporting supervision

**Authority**

| Power | Scope |
|-------|-------|
| Rulemaking | Consumer financial products |
| Enforcement | Civil penalties up to $1M/day |
| Supervision | Large banks, servicers |
| Consumer complaints | Database and resolution |

**Political Vulnerability**

- Director appointment contested
- Funding attacked
- Business lobby opposition
- Constitutional challenges

### Department of Education

**Role**

- Federal student loan programs
- Student loan servicer contracts
- Income-driven repayment
- Loan forgiveness programs
- For-profit college oversight

**Challenges**

- Servicer accountability
- Political pressure on forgiveness
- IT infrastructure limitations
- Staffing constraints

### Federal Trade Commission

**Role**

- Debt collection enforcement
- Credit reporting oversight
- Payday lending (limited)
- General consumer protection

### State Attorneys General

**Role**

- State consumer protection enforcement
- Can exceed federal standards
- Class action litigation
- Settlement negotiations

**Key AGs for Consumer Debt**

- California, New York, Illinois (aggressive enforcement)
- Coalition letters and multistate actions

## Stakeholder Power Analysis

### Power Imbalance

| Stakeholder | Resources | Organization | Political Access | Power Rating |
|-------------|-----------|--------------|-----------------|--------------|
| Major banks | Enormous | Excellent | Excellent | Very High |
| Credit card companies | Enormous | Excellent | Excellent | Very High |
| Credit bureaus | High | Good | Good | High |
| Debt collectors | Moderate | Good | Good | Moderate |
| Payday lenders | Moderate | Good | Good (state) | Moderate |
| Student loan servicers | Moderate | Good | Good | Moderate |
| Consumer advocates | Low | Good | Moderate | Low-Moderate |
| Debtors | Low | Poor | Poor | Low |

### Coalition Possibilities

**Reform Coalition**

- Consumer advocacy organizations
- Labor unions
- Civil rights groups
- Progressive legislators
- Academic researchers
- Some religious organizations

**Opposition Coalition**

- Financial industry
- Business groups
- Conservative think tanks
- Deregulation advocates
- Some deficit hawks

## Key Takeaways

1. **Power imbalance severe**: Industry resources dwarf consumer advocates
2. **Debtors poorly organized**: Stigma and diffusion limit political power
3. **Industry well-coordinated**: Lobbying, campaign finance, revolving door
4. **CFPB is contested terrain**: Key battleground for consumer protection
5. **State level matters**: Significant variation in protection
6. **Coalition building necessary**: Consumer groups must ally with civil rights, labor
7. **Some industry divisions exist**: Credit unions vs. payday lenders, etc.
