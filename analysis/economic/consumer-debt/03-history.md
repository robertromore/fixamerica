# Consumer Debt: Historical Context

## Overview

The story of consumer debt in America is one of transformation from a society where debt was stigmatized and tightly regulated to one where borrowing for consumption is normalized, necessary, and often predatory. Understanding this history reveals how policy choices, not inevitable market forces, created the current debt crisis.

## Timeline of Key Developments

```text
1900s   | Debt stigmatized, usury laws enforced
1920s   | Installment credit for consumer goods
1950s   | Credit card invention, suburban mortgages
1960s   | Credit card adoption accelerates
1970s   | Inflation and deregulation begin
1980s   | Interest rate deregulation, student loan expansion
1990s   | Subprime lending emergence, credit card boom
2000s   | Housing bubble, student loan crisis begins
2010s   | Deleveraging, then new credit growth
2020s   | Pandemic disruption, debt relief debates
```

## Pre-20th Century: Debt as Last Resort

### Colonial and Early American Period

**Cultural Attitudes**

- Debt viewed as moral failing
- Debtors' prisons existed until 1830s
- Community shame for indebtedness
- Religious teachings against usury

**Limited Debt Forms**

- Store credit (tabs) for basic goods
- Merchant financing for farmers
- Mortgages rare and short-term
- No consumer credit industry

### 19th Century Developments

**Usury Laws**

| State | Maximum Rate | Enforcement |
|-------|--------------|-------------|
| Most states | 6-10% | Strong |
| Some western | 12-15% | Moderate |
| Few exceptions | Higher | Weak |

**Emerging Credit**

- Pawnbrokers served working class
- Installment sales for sewing machines (1850s)
- Furniture store credit
- Farm equipment financing

## Early 20th Century: Consumer Credit Emerges

### 1900s-1910s: The Credit Foundation

**Installment Credit Begins**

- Piano dealers pioneer installment plans
- Department stores offer store credit
- "Easy payment plans" marketed
- Credit becomes aspirational, not shameful

**Early Regulation**

- Russell Sage Foundation studies loan sharks (1907)
- Model Uniform Small Loan Law drafted
- States license small lenders
- Rate caps established (36% typical)

### 1920s: Mass Consumption Era

**Credit Fuels Prosperity**

- Automobile installment financing transforms economy
- 75% of cars purchased on credit by 1929
- Household goods (radios, appliances) financed
- Middle class participates in credit economy

**Key Statistics (1929)**

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Consumer credit outstanding | $7.6 billion |
| Installment credit | $3.5 billion |
| % of durable goods on credit | 60-90% |

### The Great Depression: Credit Collapse

**1929-1933 Crisis**

- Credit contracted 50%
- Mass defaults and repossessions
- Bank failures eliminated savings
- Debt deflation spiral

**Policy Response**

- FHA mortgage insurance (1934)
- Home Owners' Loan Corporation refinancing
- Regulation of consumer credit
- Social Security as alternative to debt

## Post-War Era: Credit Society Construction

### 1945-1960: The Credit Card Revolution

**Technological Innovation**

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 1946 | First bank card (Flatbush National) |
| 1950 | Diners Club card (travel/entertainment) |
| 1958 | American Express, BankAmericard (later Visa) |
| 1966 | Master Charge (later Mastercard) |

**Suburban Mortgage Expansion**

- VA loans for veterans (no down payment)
- FHA loans for suburban homebuyers
- Redlining excluded minorities
- Homeownership rate: 43% (1940) to 62% (1960)

**Consumer Credit Growth**

| Year | Consumer Credit | % of Income |
|------|-----------------|-------------|
| 1945 | $5.7 billion | 4% |
| 1950 | $21.5 billion | 10% |
| 1960 | $56.1 billion | 15% |

### 1960s: Credit Democratization

**Credit Card Proliferation**

- Unsolicited card mailings (millions sent)
- Bank cards spread nationally
- Retail store cards multiply
- Credit as status symbol

**Early Consumer Protection**

| Law | Year | Purpose |
|-----|------|---------|
| Truth in Lending Act | 1968 | APR disclosure |
| Consumer Credit Protection Act | 1968 | Wage garnishment limits |
| Fair Credit Reporting Act | 1970 | Credit bureau regulation |

**Student Loans Begin**

- National Defense Education Act (1958)
- Higher Education Act (1965)
- Guaranteed Student Loan program
- Initial scale modest: $600 million (1968)

## Deregulation Era: 1970s-1980s

### Interest Rate Deregulation

**The Marquette Decision (1978)**

Supreme Court ruling transformed credit:

- Allowed banks to export interest rates nationwide
- Home state's rate cap applied anywhere
- Banks relocated to Delaware, South Dakota
- Effectively eliminated usury caps

**Before Marquette**

- States set maximum interest rates
- Most caps: 12-18%
- Payday lending largely illegal
- Credit rationing for risky borrowers

**After Marquette**

- No effective federal cap
- Rates limited only by competition
- Subprime lending becomes possible
- Predatory lending incentivized

### Inflation and Credit Transformation

**1970s Economic Crisis**

| Year | Inflation | Prime Rate | Impact |
|------|-----------|------------|--------|
| 1973 | 8.7% | 10.0% | Credit squeeze |
| 1979 | 13.3% | 15.5% | Negative real rates |
| 1980 | 12.5% | 21.5% | Credit market crisis |
| 1982 | 6.1% | 14.9% | Beginning of decline |

**Regulatory Response**

- Depository Institutions Deregulation Act (1980)
- Removed interest rate ceilings
- Preempted state usury laws
- Created conditions for credit expansion

### 1980s: Student Loan Expansion

**Key Legislative Changes**

| Year | Change | Effect |
|------|--------|--------|
| 1978 | MISAA | Middle-class eligibility |
| 1980 | PLUS loans | Parents can borrow |
| 1981 | Needs analysis changes | Broader eligibility |
| 1992 | Unsubsidized Stafford | Universal access |

**College Cost Explosion Begins**

| Year | Average Tuition (Public 4-Year) | Student Debt Total |
|------|--------------------------------|-------------------|
| 1980 | $2,100 (2024 dollars) | $12 billion |
| 1985 | $2,900 | $20 billion |
| 1990 | $3,500 | $34 billion |

## Subprime Era: 1990s-2000s

### Credit Card Industry Transformation

**1990s Growth**

- Direct mail marketing explodes
- Risk-based pricing implemented
- Universal default clauses added
- Fee income becomes major revenue source

**Industry Practices**

| Practice | Impact |
|----------|--------|
| Teaser rates | Hook borrowers, then raise rates |
| Universal default | Any late payment triggers rate increase |
| Over-limit fees | Charge for exceeding limit they approved |
| Payment allocation | Apply to lowest-rate balances first |
| Two-cycle billing | Charge interest on paid balances |

### Payday Lending Explosion

**Industry Growth**

| Year | Payday Loan Stores | Annual Volume |
|------|-------------------|---------------|
| 1990 | ~200 | Minimal |
| 1997 | 2,000 | $2 billion |
| 2000 | 10,000 | $9 billion |
| 2005 | 22,000 | $25 billion |

**How Industry Expanded**

- Exploited state law loopholes
- Partnered with out-of-state banks
- Lobbied for favorable legislation
- Targeted low-income communities

### Subprime Mortgage Crisis

**Predatory Mortgage Lending**

| Year | Subprime Share of Mortgages | Predatory Features |
|------|----------------------------|-------------------|
| 1994 | 5% | Prepayment penalties |
| 1999 | 8% | Balloon payments |
| 2003 | 8% | Negative amortization |
| 2006 | 24% | NINJA loans |

**The Bubble**

- Home prices doubled (2000-2006)
- Lending standards collapsed
- Securitization obscured risk
- Regulators failed to act

**The Collapse (2007-2009)**

| Impact | Scale |
|--------|-------|
| Home foreclosures | 10 million |
| Wealth destroyed | $7 trillion |
| Jobs lost | 8.7 million |
| Households underwater | 25% |

### Student Loan Crisis Develops

**2000s Expansion**

| Year | Total Student Debt | Borrowers |
|------|-------------------|-----------|
| 2000 | $200 billion | 20 million |
| 2005 | $500 billion | 30 million |
| 2008 | $640 billion | 35 million |
| 2010 | $830 billion | 38 million |

**Key 2005 Changes**

- Bankruptcy Abuse Prevention Act
- Made private student loans non-dischargeable
- Eliminated statute of limitations on federal loans
- Strengthened collection powers

## Post-Crisis Era: 2010s

### Deleveraging and Recovery

**Initial Debt Reduction**

- Total household debt fell 12% (2008-2013)
- Mortgage debt dropped significantly
- Foreclosures cleared bad loans
- Consumers paid down credit cards

**New Debt Accumulation**

| Debt Type | 2010 | 2019 | Change |
|-----------|------|------|--------|
| Mortgage | $10.2T | $10.3T | +1% |
| Student | $0.83T | $1.51T | +82% |
| Auto | $0.74T | $1.33T | +80% |
| Credit Card | $0.69T | $0.93T | +35% |

### Regulatory Response

**Dodd-Frank Act (2010)**

- Created Consumer Financial Protection Bureau
- Ability-to-repay mortgage standards
- Qualified mortgage safe harbor
- Mortgage originator compensation rules

**CFPB Actions**

| Action | Year | Impact |
|--------|------|--------|
| Credit card rule enforcement | 2011- | Reduced deceptive practices |
| Payday lending rule | 2017 | Limited (later weakened) |
| Debt collection rule | 2020 | Updated practices |
| Student loan servicer oversight | 2012- | Increased scrutiny |

### Student Loan Crisis Intensifies

**Growth Continues**

| Year | Total Debt | Average Debt/Borrower |
|------|------------|----------------------|
| 2010 | $830 billion | $22,000 |
| 2015 | $1.2 trillion | $30,000 |
| 2019 | $1.5 trillion | $35,000 |

**Income-Driven Repayment Expansion**

- PAYE (2012) capped payments at 10% discretionary income
- REPAYE (2015) expanded eligibility
- Enrollment grew from 1 million to 8 million
- But created "negative amortization" problem

## 2020s: Crisis and Transformation

### Pandemic Disruption

**Relief Measures**

| Measure | Duration | Impact |
|---------|----------|--------|
| Student loan pause | March 2020 - Oct 2023 | 45 million benefited |
| Eviction moratorium | 2020-2021 | Prevented foreclosures |
| Stimulus payments | 2020-2021 | Enabled debt paydown |
| Enhanced unemployment | 2020-2021 | Income replacement |

**Debt Trends During COVID**

- Credit card debt dropped initially (spending down, payments up)
- Student loan payments stopped
- Mortgage forbearance protected millions
- Savings rate spiked temporarily

### Return to Growth

**2022-2024 Trends**

| Debt Type | Change 2021-2024 | Driver |
|-----------|------------------|--------|
| Credit cards | +47% | Inflation, rate increases |
| Auto loans | +18% | Vehicle prices |
| Student loans | +5% | Interest accrual |
| Total debt | +14% | All factors |

### Policy Debates

**Student Loan Forgiveness**

- Biden administration attempted $430B broad forgiveness
- Supreme Court struck down (June 2023)
- Alternative programs pursued
- $169 billion discharged through various means

**Interest Rate Concerns**

- Credit card rates reached 27%+
- Renewed calls for rate caps
- Late fee cap proposed ($8)
- Junk fee focus

## Historical Lessons

### What Changed

| Era | Debt Attitude | Regulation | Outcome |
|-----|--------------|------------|---------|
| Pre-1920s | Shameful | Strong usury laws | Limited debt |
| 1920s-1960s | Acceptable | Moderate regulation | Controlled growth |
| 1970s-2000s | Normalized | Deregulation | Explosive growth |
| 2010s-present | Necessary | Mixed | Crisis and debate |

### Key Turning Points

1. **Marquette Decision (1978)**: Ended effective usury caps
2. **1980 Deregulation**: Removed interest rate ceilings
3. **2005 Bankruptcy Act**: Made student loans permanent
4. **2008 Financial Crisis**: Exposed predatory lending
5. **2010 CFPB Creation**: First comprehensive consumer protection
6. **2023 Supreme Court**: Limited executive debt relief authority

### International Comparison

| Country | Household Debt/GDP | Key Difference |
|---------|-------------------|----------------|
| United States | 78% | No universal healthcare, expensive education |
| Germany | 54% | Free university, universal healthcare |
| France | 67% | Similar public goods |
| Japan | 65% | Savings culture stronger |
| Canada | 107% | Housing costs, similar credit access |
| Australia | 120% | Housing bubble |

## Key Takeaways

1. **Debt growth was policy choice**: Deregulation enabled explosion
2. **Usury laws worked**: Effective until preempted
3. **1970s-80s pivotal**: Interest rate deregulation transformed lending
4. **Student loans unique**: Created new category of permanent debt
5. **Predatory lending profitable**: Industry fought regulation
6. **CFPB represented shift**: First comprehensive federal consumer protection
7. **Crisis creates opportunity**: Major reforms follow major failures
