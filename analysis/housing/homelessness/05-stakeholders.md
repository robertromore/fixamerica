# Homelessness: Stakeholders

## Overview

The homelessness crisis involves a complex web of stakeholders with varying interests, power, and perspectives. Understanding these stakeholders is essential for building coalitions and navigating opposition.

## People Experiencing Homelessness

### Demographics and Diversity

The homeless population is not monolithic. Different subpopulations have distinct needs and interests.

| Subpopulation | Estimated Count | Key Characteristics | Primary Needs |
|---------------|-----------------|---------------------|---------------|
| Chronically homeless individuals | 143,000+ | Long-term; disabilities | Permanent supportive housing |
| Families with children | 186,000 | Temporary episodes; working poor | Rapid rehousing; childcare |
| Veterans | 35,000+ | Service-connected issues | VA services; housing |
| Unaccompanied youth | 35,000+ | Aging out; family conflict | Youth-specific services |
| Domestic violence survivors | 74,000+ | Safety needs; trauma | Safe housing; support |
| Recently homeless | Variable | First episode; often employed | Prevention; rapid rehousing |

### Interests and Concerns

**Common interests**:

- Safe, stable, affordable housing
- Dignity and respect
- Access to services without burdensome requirements
- Protection from criminalization
- Voice in policy decisions

**Concerns with current system**:

- Shelter conditions (safety, rules, restrictions)
- Loss of autonomy in programs
- Treatment requirements as barriers
- Criminalization and police harassment
- Being moved without being housed

### Political Power

- **Limited formal power**: No organized voting bloc; many cannot vote
- **Voice through advocates**: Organizations represent interests
- **Media power**: Stories personalize the issue
- **Direct action**: Encampment organizing, protests

## Service Providers

### Shelter Operators

| Type | Examples | Interests |
|------|----------|-----------|
| Faith-based | Salvation Army, Gospel Missions, local churches | Mission-driven; may include religious elements |
| Nonprofit | Local homeless coalitions | Funding stability; outcomes measurement |
| Government-run | Municipal shelters | Public accountability; cost management |

**Perspectives**:

- Support for increased funding
- Varying views on Housing First (some prefer treatment-first)
- Concerns about maintaining capacity during transitions
- Mixed views on low-barrier approaches

### Housing Providers

| Type | Focus | Position |
|------|-------|----------|
| Permanent supportive housing developers | Chronically homeless | Strong Housing First advocates |
| Affordable housing developers | Low-income housing broadly | Support homeless housing within broader mission |
| Property managers | PSH operations | Operational sustainability concerns |

**Interests**:

- Stable funding streams
- Development incentives and subsidies
- Streamlined approval processes
- Adequate services funding alongside housing

### Outreach Workers

**Role**: Street engagement, relationship building, service connection

**Perspectives**:

- Deep understanding of homeless individuals' needs
- Often advocate for harm reduction approaches
- Skeptical of enforcement-only strategies
- Support client choice and autonomy

### Healthcare Providers

| Type | Role | Interests |
|------|------|-----------|
| Healthcare for the Homeless programs | Primary care, behavioral health | Funding; integration with housing |
| Hospital systems | Emergency care; discharge planning | Reducing ED utilization; liability |
| Mental health providers | Treatment and crisis response | Adequate capacity; housing for patients |
| Substance use treatment | Recovery services | Housing stability supports recovery |

## Government Stakeholders

### Federal Agencies

| Agency | Role | Current Approach |
|--------|------|------------------|
| HUD | Primary homeless funding; CoC program | Housing First; coordinated entry |
| HHS | Healthcare; SAMHSA grants; ACF | Supportive services; prevention |
| VA | Veteran homelessness programs | SSVF; HUD-VASH; strong outcomes |
| FEMA | Emergency Food and Shelter | Disaster and emergency response |
| USICH | Interagency coordination | Federal strategic plan |

### State Governments

**Roles**:

- Mental health and Medicaid administration
- State housing finance agencies
- Supplemental homeless funding
- Regulatory authority over land use

**Perspectives vary**:

- Blue states: Often more housing funding; varied effectiveness
- Red states: Often less funding; lower costs may mean less crisis
- All states: Tension between state and local responsibility

### Local Governments

| Actor | Role | Interests |
|-------|------|-----------|
| Mayors | Political accountability; priority setting | Visible results; constituent satisfaction |
| City councils | Funding; land use decisions | Neighborhood concerns; electoral pressure |
| County governments | Mental health; social services | Service provision; cost management |
| Continuums of Care | HUD-funded coordination | Funding; system performance |
| Public housing authorities | Voucher administration; public housing | Waitlist management; fair housing |

**Local government challenges**:

- Pressure from businesses and residents to "do something"
- Limited resources relative to need
- NIMBY opposition to siting facilities
- Competing priorities for limited funds

### Law Enforcement

**Roles in homelessness**:

- Encampment enforcement
- Response to mental health crises
- Trespassing, quality-of-life enforcement
- Some jurisdictions: homeless outreach teams

**Perspectives**:

- Many officers recognize enforcement does not solve homelessness
- Pressure from community to "clear" visible homelessness
- Concerns about officer safety and training
- Growing interest in diversion and co-responder models

## Advocates and Activists

### National Organizations

| Organization | Focus | Approach |
|--------------|-------|----------|
| National Alliance to End Homelessness | Policy; best practices | Evidence-based; Housing First |
| National Coalition for the Homeless | Advocacy; civil rights | Grassroots; anti-criminalization |
| National Low Income Housing Coalition | Affordable housing | Housing policy advocacy |
| Corporation for Supportive Housing | Supportive housing | Technical assistance; development |

### Local Advocacy

**Types**:

- Homeless coalitions and federations
- Legal aid organizations
- ACLU affiliates (criminalization litigation)
- Tenant organizing groups
- Faith-based advocacy networks

**Interests**:

- Increased affordable housing
- Protection from criminalization
- Homeless voices in policy
- Accountability for outcomes

### People with Lived Experience

**Increasingly recognized**:

- "Nothing about us without us" principle
- Lived experience councils
- Peer support specialists
- Community advisory boards

**Contributions**:

- Real-world policy insight
- Trust building with homeless individuals
- Accountability for providers
- Humanizing the issue

## Business Interests

### Downtown Business Associations

**Interests**:

- Property values and commercial rents
- Customer and employee comfort
- "Clean and safe" streetscapes
- Liability concerns

**Positions**:

- Often support enforcement approaches
- May support housing solutions if coupled with visible change
- Oppose siting shelters/housing in commercial districts
- Increasingly recognize enforcement alone fails

### Developers and Real Estate

**Interests**:

- Development approval processes
- Property values
- Affordable housing mandates (often opposed)
- Tax incentives for housing development

**Positions vary**:

- Market-rate developers often oppose affordable housing requirements
- Some developers specialize in affordable/supportive housing
- YIMBY movement includes some developer support

### Healthcare Industry

**Interests**:

- Reducing uncompensated care
- Emergency department overcrowding
- Discharge planning liability
- Medicaid reimbursement for homeless services

**Positions**:

- Generally support housing solutions (reduce ED use)
- Investment in medical respite, recuperative care
- Advocacy for Medicaid housing flexibilities

## Neighborhood Residents

### NIMBY Opposition

**Concerns driving opposition**:

- Property values
- Safety (often based on stereotypes)
- "Character of the neighborhood"
- School quality
- Traffic and parking

**Political power**:

- Dominant voice at local meetings
- Homeowner associations
- Electoral pressure on local officials
- Litigation to block projects

### YIMBY and Allied Residents

**Positions**:

- Support housing development broadly
- Affordable housing as part of supply solution
- Opposition to exclusionary zoning
- Moral arguments for accepting homeless services

**Growing but still minority voice**

## Researchers and Experts

### Academic Researchers

**Contributions**:

- Evidence base for Housing First
- Cost studies demonstrating savings
- Evaluation of interventions
- Understanding of causes

**Key institutions**:

- University of Pennsylvania (Dennis Culhane)
- University of Southern California
- NYU Furman Center
- Multiple urban policy centers

### Think Tanks

| Organization | Orientation | Contribution |
|--------------|-------------|--------------|
| Urban Institute | Center | Research; policy analysis |
| RAND Corporation | Center | Cost-benefit studies |
| Center on Budget and Policy Priorities | Center-left | Housing policy; safety net |
| Manhattan Institute | Center-right | Critical of current approaches |

## Coalitions and Alliances

### Pro-Housing Coalitions

- Affordable housing developers + advocates + some business
- Housing First supporters across sectors
- Homeless rights + civil liberties groups
- Healthcare + housing integration advocates

### Enforcement Coalitions

- Business associations + some residents
- Some law enforcement + local officials
- Property rights advocates
- Groups frustrated with visible homelessness

### Potential Bridge Issues

- Housing development (business + advocates agree on supply)
- Mental health funding (broad support)
- Veteran homelessness (bipartisan)
- Prevention (cost-effective; less controversial siting)
