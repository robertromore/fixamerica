# Rural Housing: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Addressing rural housing challenges requires legislative action at federal and state levels. This document presents draft legislation, regulatory frameworks, and legal analysis for transforming rural housing, including identification of potential loopholes and shortcomings.

## Constitutional Framework

No constitutional amendments are required for rural housing legislation. Congress has established authority under:

- **Commerce Clause**: Regulation of housing finance and interstate commerce
- **Spending Power**: Conditional grants and direct lending programs
- **Taxing Power**: Tax credits for affordable housing development
- **Treaty Power and Trust Responsibility**: Authority for tribal housing programs

**Constitutional limitations**:

- Takings Clause: Constraints on manufactured housing community regulations
- Federalism: Primary land use authority rests with states
- Tribal Sovereignty: Government-to-government relationship requirements

## Federal Legislation

### Rural Housing Revitalization Act

**Purpose**: Dramatically expand and reform USDA rural housing programs to meet actual need.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Rural Housing Revitalization Act of 2025".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.
(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that—
    (1) approximately 60 million Americans live in rural areas where housing
        challenges differ fundamentally from urban markets;
    (2) USDA rural housing programs have been underfunded for decades,
        with Section 502 direct loans serving 93 percent fewer households
        than in 1975 in inflation-adjusted terms;
    (3) the Section 515 rural rental housing portfolio has lost over 100,000
        units since 1990 with no new construction since 2011;
    (4) 7.2 percent of rural housing is moderately or severely inadequate,
        compared to 4.8 percent of urban housing;
    (5) private housing markets cannot adequately serve rural areas due to
        thin markets, limited economic bases, and geographic dispersion.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to ensure all rural Americans have
    access to safe, decent, affordable housing by—
    (1) dramatically expanding USDA direct lending programs;
    (2) reviving rural rental housing production;
    (3) preserving existing affordable rural housing;
    (4) reforming programs to meet 21st century needs.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.
In this Act:
(a) RURAL AREA.—The term "rural area" means—
    (1) any area described in section 520 of the Housing Act of 1949; and
    (2) any other area with a population of 35,000 or less that—
        (A) is rural in character; and
        (B) has demonstrated housing needs.

(b) VERY LOW INCOME.—The term "very low income" means income not
    exceeding 50 percent of area median income.

(c) LOW INCOME.—The term "low income" means income not exceeding
    80 percent of area median income.

SEC. 4. SECTION 502 DIRECT LOAN EXPANSION.
(a) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated for Section 502
    direct loans under section 502(a) of the Housing Act of 1949—
    (1) $5,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2026;
    (2) $8,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
    (3) $12,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2028;
    (4) $15,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2029; and
    (5) $18,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2030 and each fiscal year thereafter.

(b) PROCESSING REQUIREMENTS.—The Secretary of Agriculture shall—
    (1) process complete loan applications within 45 days of submission;
    (2) establish pre-approval procedures for qualified applicants;
    (3) provide applicants written notification of approval, denial, or
        additional requirements within 30 days of application.

(c) MANUFACTURED HOUSING.—
    (1) Not less than 25 percent of Section 502 direct loan funds shall be
        available for manufactured housing.
    (2) Manufactured housing shall be eligible regardless of whether
        classified as real or personal property under state law.
    (3) The Secretary shall establish underwriting standards appropriate
        for manufactured housing including consideration of land ownership.

(d) GEOGRAPHIC TARGETING.—The Secretary shall ensure equitable geographic
    distribution of loans, with particular attention to—
    (1) persistent poverty counties;
    (2) tribal areas;
    (3) areas with limited private lending activity.

SEC. 5. SECTION 515 REVITALIZATION.
(a) NEW CONSTRUCTION.—There are authorized to be appropriated for new
    Section 515 construction—
    (1) $300,000,000 for fiscal year 2026;
    (2) $400,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
    (3) $500,000,000 for fiscal year 2028 and each fiscal year thereafter.

(b) PRESERVATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated for preservation
    of existing Section 515 properties—
    (1) $400,000,000 for fiscal year 2026;
    (2) $500,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
    (3) $600,000,000 for fiscal year 2028 and each fiscal year thereafter.

(c) REGULATORY REFORM.—Within 1 year of enactment, the Secretary shall—
    (1) issue regulations streamlining Section 515 development requirements;
    (2) establish procedures for combining Section 515 with LIHTC financing;
    (3) reduce processing time for new developments to 180 days.

(d) TRANSFER TO MISSION OWNERS.—The Secretary shall prioritize transfer of
    Section 515 properties to nonprofit organizations, housing authorities,
    and tenant organizations, and shall provide—
    (1) technical assistance for transfers;
    (2) rehabilitation funding for transferred properties;
    (3) extended affordability requirements for transferred properties.

SEC. 6. SECTION 504 HOME REPAIR EXPANSION.
(a) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated for Section 504
    loans and grants—
    (1) $100,000,000 for fiscal year 2026;
    (2) $150,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
    (3) $200,000,000 for fiscal year 2028 and each fiscal year thereafter.

(b) GRANT LIMITS.—The Secretary shall increase grant limits to—
    (1) $15,000 for general repairs;
    (2) $25,000 for accessibility modifications; and
    (3) $30,000 for climate resilience improvements.

(c) MANUFACTURED HOUSING.—Section 504 funds shall be available for
    manufactured housing repairs regardless of land ownership status.

SEC. 7. FARM LABOR HOUSING.
(a) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated for Sections 514
    and 516—
    (1) $100,000,000 for fiscal year 2026;
    (2) $150,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
    (3) $200,000,000 for fiscal year 2028 and each fiscal year thereafter.

(b) YEAR-ROUND USE.—The Secretary shall permit Section 514/516 housing to
    serve non-farmworkers during off-season periods when farmworker demand
    is reduced.

(c) STANDARDS.—Within 180 days of enactment, the Secretary shall issue
    regulations establishing minimum habitability standards for all
    federally-assisted farm labor housing including—
    (1) minimum square footage per occupant;
    (2) privacy requirements;
    (3) kitchen and sanitation facilities;
    (4) climate control requirements.

SEC. 8. RURAL HOMELESSNESS.
(a) FUNDING SET-ASIDE.—Of amounts appropriated for the Continuum of Care
    program, not less than 10 percent shall be available for rural CoCs.

(b) METHODOLOGY.—The Secretary of HUD shall develop and implement rural
    homelessness counting methodology that captures—
    (1) individuals doubled up with family or friends;
    (2) individuals living in vehicles or RVs;
    (3) individuals in locations not captured by traditional street counts.

(c) RURAL ADAPTATION.—The Secretary shall issue guidance for adapting
    Housing First and rapid rehousing models for rural contexts.

SEC. 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect on October 1, 2025.
```

**Explanation**: This comprehensive bill dramatically expands all major USDA rural housing programs, addresses critical gaps in farm labor housing and rural homelessness, and requires administrative reforms to improve program effectiveness.

**Challenges**: Significant budget impact; competition with farm programs in USDA appropriations; political support for rural housing underdeveloped.

**Refinements**: Could phase in funding increases more gradually; could link to broader rural economic development initiatives to build coalition support.

### Manufactured Housing Tenant and Consumer Protection Act

**Purpose**: Reform manufactured housing financing and protect community residents from displacement.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Manufactured Housing Tenant and Consumer
Protection Act of 2025".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.
Congress finds that—
    (1) manufactured housing provides affordable homeownership for over
        22 million Americans, predominantly in rural areas;
    (2) manufactured housing residents often face chattel loan financing
        at interest rates 3-6 percentage points higher than conventional
        mortgages;
    (3) approximately 66 percent of manufactured homes are located on
        rented land in manufactured housing communities;
    (4) residents face displacement through community closures, lot rent
        increases, and investor acquisition with no federal protection;
    (5) manufactured housing is essential affordable housing that
        warrants appropriate consumer and tenant protections.

SEC. 3. MANUFACTURED HOUSING FINANCING REFORM.
(a) GSE DUTY TO SERVE.—Section 1335 of the Federal Housing Enterprises
    Financial Safety and Soundness Act of 1992 is amended to require
    that Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac—
    (1) purchase or securitize not less than $10,000,000,000 annually
        in manufactured housing loans beginning in fiscal year 2027;
    (2) develop loan products appropriate for manufactured housing
        including chattel loans on leased land; and
    (3) establish underwriting standards that appropriately account for
        manufactured housing characteristics.

(b) INTEREST RATE LIMITATIONS.—
    (1) No creditor may make a loan secured by a manufactured home with
        an interest rate exceeding the applicable rate for conventional
        mortgages by more than 2 percentage points.
    (2) The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau shall issue regulations
        implementing this subsection within 1 year of enactment.

(c) CONSUMER DISCLOSURES.—Any creditor offering manufactured housing
    financing shall provide written comparison to conventional mortgage
    terms including—
    (1) total interest cost over loan term;
    (2) difference in monthly payments;
    (3) explanation of loan classification (real property vs. chattel).

SEC. 4. COMMUNITY RESIDENT PROTECTIONS.
(a) RIGHT OF FIRST REFUSAL.—
    (1) Before selling a manufactured housing community, the owner shall—
        (A) provide written notice to the community resident association
            or, if none exists, to all residents;
        (B) provide 90 days for residents to form a purchasing entity; and
        (C) allow the resident entity to match any bona fide purchase offer.
    (2) The resident entity shall have 180 days to secure financing.
    (3) The owner may not close on a sale until the right of first
        refusal period has expired.

(b) COMMUNITY CLOSURE NOTICE.—
    (1) An owner intending to close a manufactured housing community
        shall provide not less than 2 years written notice to—
        (A) each resident;
        (B) the local government; and
        (C) the state housing agency.
    (2) The notice shall include—
        (A) the anticipated closure date;
        (B) information about relocation assistance; and
        (C) contact information for housing resources.

(c) RELOCATION ASSISTANCE.—Upon community closure, the owner shall provide
    each resident household—
    (1) relocation assistance of not less than $10,000; or
    (2) payment of actual moving costs up to $20,000.

(d) RENT INCREASE NOTICE.—
    (1) An owner shall provide not less than 90 days written notice
        before any lot rent increase.
    (2) The notice shall include—
        (A) the amount of the increase;
        (B) the effective date; and
        (C) justification for the increase.

SEC. 5. HUD CODE UPDATE.
(a) REQUIREMENT.—Within 2 years of enactment, the Secretary of HUD shall
    update the Federal Manufactured Home Construction and Safety Standards
    to include—
    (1) energy efficiency standards equivalent to the current IECC;
    (2) enhanced wind resistance standards appropriate for climate change
        projections; and
    (3) improved durability requirements.

(b) INSTALLATION STANDARDS.—Within 1 year of enactment, the Secretary
    shall establish uniform national installation standards with
    mandatory inspection requirements.

SEC. 6. COMMUNITY PURCHASE SUPPORT.
(a) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated $100,000,000
    annually for loans and grants to resident organizations, nonprofits,
    and housing authorities for acquisition of manufactured housing
    communities.

(b) TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE.—The Secretary shall provide technical assistance
    to resident organizations pursuing community ownership.

SEC. 7. ENFORCEMENT.
(a) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—Any person aggrieved by a violation of
    Sections 3 or 4 may bring an action in federal or state court for—
    (1) actual damages;
    (2) injunctive relief;
    (3) reasonable attorney's fees; and
    (4) statutory damages of $5,000 per violation.

(b) STATE ENFORCEMENT.—State attorneys general may bring actions to
    enforce this Act.

(c) FEDERAL ENFORCEMENT.—The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau and
    HUD may bring enforcement actions with civil penalties up to
    $50,000 per violation.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect 1 year after enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the two major gaps in manufactured housing policy: predatory financing and tenant insecurity in communities. It creates meaningful protections while preserving manufactured housing as affordable housing option.

**Challenges**: Industry opposition; state preemption concerns; implementation complexity.

### Native American Housing Assistance Improvement Act

**Purpose**: Fulfill trust responsibilities through adequate tribal housing investment and administrative reforms.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Native American Housing Assistance
Improvement Act of 2025".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.
Congress finds that—
    (1) there is an estimated shortage of 68,000 housing units on tribal
        lands;
    (2) 40 percent of housing on tribal lands is overcrowded or lacks
        adequate facilities;
    (3) NAHASDA funding has been essentially flat since 1996, representing
        a 50 percent decline in purchasing power;
    (4) construction costs on tribal lands average 30-40 percent higher
        than off-reservation due to remoteness;
    (5) the United States has a trust responsibility to ensure adequate
        housing for Native Americans.

SEC. 3. NAHASDA FUNDING INCREASE.
(a) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated for the Native
    American Housing Assistance and Self-Determination Act—
    (1) $1,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2026;
    (2) $1,300,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
    (3) $1,600,000,000 for fiscal year 2028;
    (4) $1,800,000,000 for fiscal year 2029; and
    (5) $2,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2030 and each fiscal year thereafter.

(b) GEOGRAPHIC COST ADJUSTMENT.—The NAHASDA formula shall be amended to
    include adjustments for—
    (1) construction cost differentials;
    (2) transportation and logistics costs;
    (3) climate-related construction requirements.

SEC. 4. LAND TITLE RESOLUTION.
(a) BIA STAFFING.—The Secretary of the Interior shall double the number
    of land title staff within 2 years of enactment.

(b) PROCESSING TIMELINE.—The Secretary shall establish a 90-day maximum
    processing time for housing-related land title determinations.

(c) TRIBAL ASSUMPTION.—Tribes may assume title processing authority
    subject to federal standards and oversight.

(d) APPROPRIATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated $50,000,000
    annually for land title processing.

SEC. 5. INFRASTRUCTURE COORDINATION.
(a) INTERAGENCY COORDINATION.—The Secretary of HUD, Secretary of the
    Interior, and Director of the Indian Health Service shall establish
    a joint planning process to coordinate—
    (1) water and sewer infrastructure;
    (2) road access;
    (3) electricity and telecommunications; and
    (4) housing development.

(b) INFRASTRUCTURE SET-ASIDE.—Not less than 10 percent of NAHASDA funds
    may be used for infrastructure directly supporting housing development.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect on October 1, 2025.
```

**Explanation**: This bill dramatically increases NAHASDA funding to begin addressing the tribal housing backlog while also addressing administrative barriers that delay housing construction.

## State Model Legislation

### Manufactured Housing Community Tenant Protection Act

**Purpose**: Protect manufactured housing community residents from displacement and exploitation.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "[State] Manufactured Housing Community
Tenant Protection Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.
(a) "Manufactured housing community" means a parcel of land on which 4 or
    more manufactured homes are located on a permanent or semi-permanent
    basis for residential purposes.
(b) "Resident" means a person who owns a manufactured home located in a
    manufactured housing community.
(c) "Lot rent" means the periodic payment for the use of a lot in a
    manufactured housing community.

SECTION 3. LEASE REQUIREMENTS.
(a) Community owners shall offer all residents a written lease of not
    less than 1 year duration.
(b) Leases shall include—
    (1) lot rent amount and any additional fees;
    (2) services included in lot rent;
    (3) rules and regulations;
    (4) procedures for rent increases;
    (5) grounds for eviction.

SECTION 4. RENT INCREASE LIMITATIONS.
(a) Lot rent may not be increased more than once per year.
(b) Written notice of any rent increase shall be provided not less than
    90 days before the effective date.
(c) Rent increases shall not exceed [5 percent or CPI, whichever is greater]
    per year unless justified by documented cost increases.

SECTION 5. JUST CAUSE EVICTION.
(a) No resident may be evicted except for—
    (1) nonpayment of rent after 30 days written notice and opportunity
        to cure;
    (2) material violation of lease terms after written notice and
        opportunity to cure;
    (3) violation of community rules that substantially affect health
        or safety; or
    (4) community closure as provided in Section 7.
(b) Retaliation for exercise of rights under this Act is prohibited.

SECTION 6. RIGHT OF FIRST REFUSAL.
(a) Before selling a manufactured housing community, the owner shall—
    (1) notify the resident association or all residents in writing;
    (2) provide a 90-day period for residents to form a purchasing entity;
    (3) allow the resident entity to match any bona fide purchase offer.
(b) The resident entity shall have 180 days to secure financing.
(c) Violation of this section renders any sale voidable.

SECTION 7. COMMUNITY CLOSURE.
(a) An owner intending to close a community shall provide not less than
    2 years written notice to each resident.
(b) Upon closure, the owner shall provide each household relocation
    assistance of not less than [$10,000].

SECTION 8. ENFORCEMENT.
(a) Residents may bring private actions for violations with recovery of
    actual damages, injunctive relief, and reasonable attorney's fees.
(b) The [Attorney General/Consumer Protection Agency] may enforce this Act.

SECTION 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect on [date].
```

**Adaptations**: States may adjust rent increase caps; some states may face constitutional challenges to rent limitations; local governments may adopt similar ordinances where enabled.

### Rural Housing Trust Fund Act

**Purpose**: Create dedicated state funding source for rural housing.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "[State] Rural Housing Trust Fund Act".

SECTION 2. ESTABLISHMENT.
There is established in the State Treasury the Rural Housing Trust Fund.

SECTION 3. REVENUE SOURCES.
The Fund shall receive—
(a) appropriations from the General Fund as the Legislature may provide;
(b) [percentage] of real estate transfer tax revenue from non-rural
    transactions;
(c) repayments from loans made by the Fund;
(d) donations, grants, and other contributions.

SECTION 4. USES.
The Fund shall be used exclusively for—
(a) loans and grants for affordable housing development in rural areas;
(b) rehabilitation of existing rural housing;
(c) down payment assistance for rural homebuyers;
(d) manufactured housing purchase and improvement;
(e) capacity building for rural housing organizations;
(f) administration of the Fund (not to exceed 5 percent).

SECTION 5. RURAL AREA DEFINITION.
"Rural area" means any area with a population of [25,000] or less that
is outside a metropolitan statistical area.

SECTION 6. PRIORITY USES.
The [State Housing Agency] shall prioritize—
(a) housing for households below 50 percent of area median income;
(b) persistent poverty counties;
(c) areas with demonstrated housing shortages;
(d) projects that leverage federal rural housing programs.

SECTION 7. ANNUAL APPROPRIATION.
There shall be appropriated to the Fund not less than [$X million] annually.

SECTION 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect on July 1 following enactment.
```

## Regulatory Framework

### USDA Regulations

**Section 502 Processing Reform**

**Authority**: Housing Act of 1949, Section 502

**Key provisions**:

- Maximum 45-day processing timeline
- Pre-approval procedures
- Manufactured housing underwriting standards
- Geographic equity requirements

**Section 515 Streamlining**

**Authority**: Housing Act of 1949, Section 515

**Key provisions**:

- Combined processing with LIHTC applications
- Reduced documentation requirements
- Expedited transfer procedures for mission owners
- Preservation priority framework

### HUD Regulations

**Manufactured Housing Standards Update**

**Authority**: National Manufactured Housing Construction and Safety Standards Act

**Key provisions**:

- Energy efficiency equivalent to IECC
- Wind resistance for 150+ mph zones
- Installation inspection requirements
- Durability and material standards

**Rural CoC Requirements**

**Authority**: McKinney-Vento Homeless Assistance Act

**Key provisions**:

- Alternative enumeration methods for rural areas
- Reduced administrative requirements for rural CoCs
- Transportation as eligible supportive service
- Mobile outreach funding eligibility

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

**Takings Clause**:

- Manufactured housing community regulations may face takings challenges
- Economic impact on community owners must be balanced
- Rent limitations more vulnerable than disclosure/notice requirements

**Due Process**:

- Eviction protections must provide adequate process
- Notice requirements are clearly constitutional
- Community closure requirements should survive due process review

**Commerce Clause**:

- Federal manufactured housing finance reform clearly constitutional
- Preemption of state chattel loan laws permissible

### Preemption Questions

| Issue | Federal | State |
|-------|---------|-------|
| Manufactured housing standards | HUD Code preempts state codes | Limited state additions |
| Consumer finance | CFPB primary authority | State can exceed federal |
| Land use | Limited federal role | Primary state/local authority |
| Tenant protections | No federal standard | State legislation needed |

### Enforcement Mechanisms

**Federal enforcement**:

- USDA: Program compliance, civil rights
- HUD: Manufactured housing standards, fair housing
- CFPB: Consumer finance violations
- DOJ: Civil rights enforcement

**State enforcement**:

- Attorney general enforcement of consumer protection
- State housing agency program oversight
- Civil rights agencies for discrimination

**Private enforcement**:

- Private right of action under tenant protection laws
- Fair housing litigation
- Consumer protection claims

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Rural definition gaming | Communities reclassify to access programs | Medium |
| Manufactured housing exclusion | Programs exclude manufactured housing in practice | High |
| Right of first refusal avoidance | Owners structure sales to avoid resident rights | High |
| Section 515 prepayment | Owners find ways to exit affordability restrictions | High |
| Rent increase justification | Owners inflate cost documentation | Medium |
| Community closure relocation | Inadequate enforcement of relocation payments | High |
| NAHASDA reporting | Limited oversight of tribal program outcomes | Medium |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Funding remains inadequate | Even expanded programs reach fraction of need | Budget constraints |
| Market conditions limit voucher use | Rural areas lack rental housing for vouchers | Supply shortage |
| State variation in protections | Uneven manufactured housing tenant protections | Federalism |
| Implementation capacity | Rural organizations lack development expertise | Capacity deficit |
| Infrastructure preconditions | Housing development blocked by infrastructure gaps | Coordination failure |
| Private equity adaptation | Investors find workarounds to regulations | Profit motive |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Rural definition gaming**: Annual review of community eligibility; disqualification for communities that become metropolitan; population and character-based definition.

2. **Manufactured housing exclusion**: Mandatory set-asides in all programs; explicit manufactured housing eligibility language; staff training requirements.

3. **Right of first refusal avoidance**: Broad definition of sale including change of control; civil penalties for evasion; voiding of sales that violate requirements.

4. **Section 515 prepayment**: Extended use agreements for preservation funding; right of first refusal for mission owners; rehabilitation linked to extended affordability.

5. **Rent increase justification**: Standardized cost documentation requirements; administrative review of large increases; resident access to documentation.

6. **Community closure relocation**: Escrow requirements before closure; state enforcement capacity; private right of action for unpaid relocation.

7. **NAHASDA reporting**: Outcome-based performance measures; third-party evaluation; public reporting of results.

### General Implementation Concerns

- **Capacity building**: Rural organizations need technical assistance and staffing
- **Market conditions**: Supply-side programs needed alongside demand-side
- **Political sustainability**: Rural housing advocacy infrastructure underdeveloped
- **Coordination**: Housing programs must integrate with healthcare, infrastructure, economic development
- **Climate adaptation**: All programs must incorporate resilience requirements
