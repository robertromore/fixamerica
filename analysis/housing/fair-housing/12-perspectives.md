# Fair Housing: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes fair housing policy reform -- specifically strengthening enforcement of anti-discrimination laws, restoring Affirmatively Furthering Fair Housing (AFFH) requirements, codifying disparate impact doctrine, expanding protected classes, and addressing algorithmic discrimination in housing -- through nine distinct political perspectives. The core questions are: how aggressively should government enforce anti-discrimination mandates in the housing market, who bears responsibility for persistent residential segregation, how should disparate impact and disparate treatment be weighed, and what role should government play in affirmatively overcoming patterns of segregation? Each perspective receives engagement-consistency ratings and position scores, culminating in compromise proposals that bridge multiple viewpoints.

Fair housing is a distinctive policy domain because the principle of non-discrimination in housing commands near-universal rhetorical support, yet the mechanisms for achieving it divide sharply along ideological lines. More than 55 years after the Fair Housing Act, America remains deeply segregated, with an estimated 4 million annual acts of housing discrimination, fewer than 25,000 complaints filed, and fewer than 5% of those resulting in meaningful relief. The Black-white homeownership gap is 30 percentage points, and the wealth ratio stands at 8:1. These persistent disparities are driven by a mutually reinforcing system of weak enforcement, exclusionary zoning, algorithmic bias, industry practices, and the compounding effects of historical discrimination. The fair housing debate is further complicated by the tension between color-blind and race-conscious approaches: whether the government should simply prohibit overt discrimination or actively work to undo segregation patterns created by decades of explicit government policy.

### Engagement Consistency (1-5)

This metric assesses how reliably a perspective's stated positions align with its underlying principles, and how stable those positions are when concerns are addressed. Higher scores indicate more predictable negotiating partners.

| Level | Label | Indicators |
|-------|-------|------------|
| 5 | Highly Consistent | Stated positions reflect underlying principles; positions stable when concerns addressed |
| 4 | Mostly Consistent | Principles generally align with positions; minor strategic flexibility |
| 3 | Mixed Consistency | Some positions principled, others reflect coalition pressures or unstated concerns |
| 2 | Low Consistency | Positions shift frequently; stated concerns differ from underlying priorities |
| 1 | Unpredictable | Positions appear driven primarily by opposition dynamics rather than principles |

**Coalition-Building Considerations:**

- Alignment gap: When stated concerns differ from underlying priorities, addressing stated concerns may not secure support
- Position stability: Perspectives with shifting positions require ongoing negotiation
- Principle consistency: Perspectives that apply principles uniformly are more predictable partners
- Internal diversity: Some perspectives contain factions with different priorities

## Perspective Analyses

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 2/5 - Low Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Stated commitment to equal treatment and individual rights is genuine in the abstract but frequently overridden by opposition to government regulation of property rights, protection of real estate and lending industry donors, and resistance to any race-conscious government policy |
| Principle consistency | "Individual responsibility" and "property rights" principles should support the right of individuals to be free from discrimination in housing markets, yet these principles are applied selectively; property rights of homeowners and landlords to exclude are prioritized over the rights of minority families to access housing without discrimination |
| Goalpost stability | Low; conservatives supported the Fair Housing Act in 1968 and early enforcement efforts but have shifted to treating enforcement itself as government overreach; earlier acceptance of disparate impact has given way to efforts to eliminate the doctrine; AFFH requirements were supported under bipartisan consensus before being repealed under the Trump administration |
| Zero-sum behavior | Significant; fair housing enforcement is frequently treated as a proxy battle against government social engineering rather than evaluated on its civil rights merits; reforms that would expand HUD authority are opposed even when they address documented discrimination |

**Justification:** Conservatives face a fundamental credibility challenge on fair housing. The party that supported the original Fair Housing Act and early enforcement now regularly opposes AFFH requirements, challenges disparate impact doctrine, and underfunds enforcement. Stated concerns about "government overreach" and "property rights" are genuine for some conservative thinkers but are frequently deployed as cover for maintaining the status quo of segregation that economically benefits suburban homeowner constituencies. The score of 2 reflects the degree to which conservative positions on fair housing are driven by real estate and lending industry interests, suburban homeowner constituency preferences, and opposition to race-conscious government policy, rather than consistent application of equal treatment principles. When discrimination affects politically favored constituencies (religious groups, veterans), conservatives readily support protections; when enforcement would challenge residential patterns that benefit their base, enforcement becomes "social engineering."

**Evidence for assessment:**

- The Trump administration suspended the AFFH rule in 2020 and proposed replacing it with a weaker "Preserving Community and Neighborhood Choice" rule
- Heritage Foundation has opposed disparate impact doctrine as incompatible with constitutional equal protection
- Conservative state legislatures have preempted local source of income discrimination protections
- Senate Republicans blocked the Fair Housing Improvement Act in multiple sessions
- Conservative support for religious liberty protections in housing contrasts with opposition to source of income and LGBTQ+ protections

| Root Cause Agreement | 3/10 | Reject the structural and systemic framing; view enforcement failures as government incompetence rather than deliberate underfunding; attribute segregation primarily to individual preferences and economic sorting rather than discrimination; reject the premise that facially neutral zoning constitutes discrimination; view algorithmic bias claims with skepticism |

#### Alternative Proposals

- Voluntary fair housing compliance programs with incentives for participation
- Market-based approaches: increase housing supply through deregulation to naturally reduce discrimination
- Education and outreach rather than enforcement-heavy approaches
- Protect religious liberty in housing decisions
- Remove government barriers to housing development rather than mandate integration
- Color-blind enforcement focusing only on intentional discrimination, not disparate impact
- Opportunity zones and economic development in underserved areas rather than mobility programs

#### Coalition Potential

- **Natural allies:** Libertarian (on property rights and deregulation), Constitutionalist (on limiting federal authority and preserving local control)
- **Potential bridges:** Centrist (on targeted enforcement and market-based solutions), Populist (on opposing federal mandates on local communities)
- **Unlikely partners:** Progressive, Democratic Socialist (on AFFH, disparate impact, and structural reform)

| Goalpost stability | Highly stable; has consistently advocated for transformative fair housing policy including reparative approaches, affirmative integration, and structural reform of housing markets |
| Zero-sum behavior | None; explicitly rejects the framing that fair housing enforcement harms property rights or local control; argues that segregation harms everyone by undermining social cohesion and economic efficiency |

**Justification:** Progressives have the most structurally coherent analysis of fair housing. Their argument that segregation persists because economic incentives, political structures, and institutional practices maintain it -- and that comprehensive structural reform is required to dismantle it -- is directly supported by the evidence in the root causes analysis. The progressive framework connects housing discrimination to broader patterns of racial capitalism, wealth inequality, and the failure to treat housing as a right rather than a commodity. Every position follows logically from core principles of racial justice and democratic accountability.

**Evidence for assessment:**

### Libertarian Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 3/5 - Mixed Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Stated commitment to individual liberty and non-discrimination is principled but undermined by practical alliance with real estate and lending interests who benefit from the status quo; libertarian analysis often functions as intellectual cover for deregulation without addressing the government role in creating segregation |
| Principle consistency | Property rights and non-aggression principles should support the right of individuals not to have their housing choices constrained by government-created segregation patterns -- the government actively built segregation through FHA redlining, VA loan discrimination, and public housing policy; yet libertarians focus exclusively on removing current government regulation rather than remedying government-caused harm |
| Goalpost stability | Moderate; the theoretical framework (property rights, voluntary association, no government regulation) has been stable; but the practical agenda focuses entirely on deregulation while ignoring the libertarian case for remedying government-created segregation |
| Zero-sum behavior | Moderate; opposition to fair housing regulation is principled but the failure to equally champion remedies for government-created segregation reveals alignment with beneficiaries of the status quo |

**Justification:** Libertarians have a genuine intellectual case against government regulation of private housing decisions. The argument that property owners should be free to transact with whomever they choose is consistent with libertarian principles. However, this analysis is incomplete because it ignores the massive role of government in creating segregation -- FHA redlining, VA loan discrimination, public housing segregation, highway construction through minority neighborhoods, and racially restrictive covenant enforcement were all government actions. A consistent libertarian position would support remedying government-created harm alongside opposing new government intervention. The score of 3 reflects the gap between the principled framework (which should support both deregulation and remedy for government-created segregation) and the practical agenda (which supports only deregulation).

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Cato Institute publications argue against fair housing enforcement but rarely address the government's role in creating segregation
- Reason Foundation housing publications focus on deregulation but not on remedying government-caused harm
- Libertarian opposition to AFFH ignores that the need for AFFH arose from government-sponsored segregation
- Some libertarian scholars (e.g., David Bernstein, Ilya Somin) have acknowledged the government role in creating segregation
- Koch network opposition to fair housing regulation aligns with real estate industry interests

### Constitutionalist Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Genuine commitment to federalism and enumerated powers; constitutional framework provides principled basis for analyzing fair housing authority at each governmental level |
| Principle consistency | 13th and 14th Amendment analysis is applied with some consistency; acknowledge that the Civil War amendments provide federal authority over racial discrimination while maintaining that housing regulation beyond anti-discrimination has limited constitutional basis |
| Goalpost stability | Stable; constitutional text provides consistent reference point; accept the Fair Housing Act under Commerce Clause and 13th Amendment authority while questioning expansions beyond original statutory scope |
| Zero-sum behavior | Low; constitutional analysis is principled rather than partisan; would apply same federalism framework regardless of the political valence of the housing regulation |

**Justification:** Constitutionalists bring a coherent framework to fair housing that distinguishes between constitutional provisions. The 13th Amendment, as interpreted in *Jones v. Alfred H. Mayer Co.*, provides broad federal authority over racial discrimination in housing. The Commerce Clause supports regulation of interstate housing and lending markets. The Spending Clause authorizes conditions on federal housing grants, including AFFH requirements. This multi-textual analysis produces more nuanced positions than simple opposition to fair housing law. The slight reduction from a perfect score reflects the tension between originalist interpretations of the 14th Amendment (which was specifically motivated by housing discrimination against freed slaves) and the reluctance of some constitutionalists to accept the full implications of that original understanding.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Federalist Society housing panels have discussed the constitutional basis of fair housing law without categorically opposing it
- *Jones v. Mayer* and *Trafficante* are broadly accepted as sound constitutional precedent
- *Texas Department of Housing v. Inclusive Communities Project* (2015) upheld disparate impact under the FHA with a 5-4 opinion; constitutionalist analysis focuses on the scope limitations rather than categorical opposition
- State constitutional provisions on housing and property rights inform constitutionalist analysis
- *West Virginia v. EPA* major questions doctrine applied by analogy to question AFFH regulatory scope

### Compromise 1: Housing Opportunity and Mobility Act

**Bridges:** Liberal + Progressive + Constitutionalist + Populist + Centrist + Religious Right + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** Families who receive housing assistance should be able to use it in any neighborhood, and the program infrastructure should be reformed to make mobility practical rather than theoretical. All perspectives agree that concentrating subsidized housing in high-poverty areas is wasteful policy that harms families and communities.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation requires Small Area Fair Market Rents (SAFMRs) in all metropolitan areas, setting payment standards at the ZIP code level so that voucher holders can afford housing in high-opportunity neighborhoods. A $100 million annual Mobility Counseling Program provides search assistance, security deposit help, moving cost support, and 12-month post-move services to voucher holders in metropolitan areas with significant segregation. Portability is streamlined by eliminating administrative barriers between public housing authorities, with automatic funding transfer and prohibition on receiving-PHA refusal. Landlord incentive programs are authorized including signing bonuses, damage mitigation funds, and holding fees for landlords in opportunity areas. Federal source of income protection is enacted, prohibiting refusal to rent based on lawful source of income including housing vouchers, with a small-landlord exemption for owner-occupied buildings with four or fewer units. Religious organizations providing housing are exempt only from protections that would directly conflict with religious exercise. A 5-year pilot program tests regional voucher administration eliminating jurisdictional boundaries. All components include robust data collection and evaluation requirements.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Liberal | Source of income protection enables voucher access to all neighborhoods; mobility counseling produces demonstrated outcomes; SAFMRs make high-opportunity areas affordable |
| Progressive | Structural reform of voucher program enables desegregation; source of income protection eliminates a key tool of exclusion; mobility counseling empowers families |
| Constitutionalist | Housing Choice Voucher program administration is within federal Spending Clause authority; source of income protection rests on Commerce Clause; small-landlord exemption respects property rights |
| Populist | Helps working families access better neighborhoods and schools; landlord incentives are voluntary; small-landlord exemption protects mom-and-pop operators |
| Centrist | Evidence-based mobility programs with demonstrated effectiveness; cost-effective desegregation; data-driven evaluation; phased implementation |
| Religious Right | Protects families; religious exemption preserves faith-based housing mission; mobility counseling can include faith-based providers; supports family stability |
| Democratic Socialist | Structural reform reducing segregation; source of income protection removes landlord gatekeeping; step toward housing as a right |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Liberal | Accept small-landlord exemption; accept religious organization exemption |
| Progressive | Accept incremental mobility approach rather than comprehensive integration mandate; accept landlord exemptions |
| Constitutionalist | Accept new federal protected class (source of income); accept federal program expansion |
| Populist | Accept source of income mandate on most landlords; accept federal program |
| Centrist | Accept that some provisions may face implementation challenges; accept ambitious funding levels |
| Religious Right | Accept source of income mandate with religious exemption; accept federal program expansion |
| Democratic Socialist | Accept that mobility programs are insufficient without public housing investment; accept landlord and religious exemptions |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 7/9 perspectives (Conservative opposes source of income mandate and program expansion; Libertarian opposes government housing programs)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 5/7 coalition members rated 4+ consistency (Liberal, Progressive, Constitutionalist, Centrist, Democratic Socialist)
- **Durability:** High; mobility programs produce measurable, visible results for families; evidence base is strong; source of income protection has been enacted in 21+ states without political backlash; SAFMRs are already required in some markets; the coalition holds because each perspective can point to specific provisions advancing their priorities
- **Implementation Path:** Build on existing HCV program infrastructure; source of income legislation introduced in multiple Congresses; SAFMR expansion already proceeding administratively; mobility counseling pilots funded through appropriations; bipartisan sponsorship possible through family stability and economic mobility framing

### Compromise 2: Fair Housing Testing and Transparency Act

**Bridges:** Liberal + Progressive + Libertarian + Constitutionalist + Populist + Centrist + Religious Right + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** Regardless of disagreements about the scope of fair housing law, all perspectives agree that documented, evidence-based information about discrimination is essential -- whether to enforce the law, inform market participants, empower communities, or evaluate whether existing protections are adequate.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation authorizes $50 million annually for a national systematic paired testing program in all major metropolitan housing markets, conducted on a 3-year cycle by HUD through grants to local fair housing organizations. Testing covers rental, sales, lending, insurance, and online advertising markets. Results are published annually in a National Fair Housing Testing Report disaggregated by metropolitan area, housing market segment, and protected class. A Fair Housing Data Transparency Initiative requires annual public reporting of HMDA data with enhanced analysis, appraisal data by race and neighborhood, tenant screening outcomes by protected class, and algorithmic housing decision audit summaries. An online Fair Housing Data Portal integrates testing results, complaint data, segregation indices, lending patterns, and appraisal data at the metropolitan and neighborhood level -- building on existing HUD tools with improved public accessibility. Private fair housing testing organizations may be certified by HUD, and testing results from certified organizations are admissible in enforcement proceedings. A $25 million Community Fair Housing Research Grant Program supports academic and community-based research on discrimination patterns and the effectiveness of fair housing interventions. All data is open access with a standardized API for researchers, journalists, and advocacy organizations.

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Liberal | Systematic testing produces actionable evidence for enforcement; data transparency enables public accountability; research funding strengthens the evidence base |
| Progressive | Community-based testing empowers fair housing organizations; data transparency challenges information asymmetries; research documents structural discrimination |
| Libertarian | Information enables market-based decisions by consumers and investors; private testing certification creates market for fair housing services; data access empowers individual tort remedies |
| Constitutionalist | Testing program within existing HUD statutory authority; data reporting within existing regulatory frameworks; community grants within Spending Clause |
| Populist | Documents corporate discrimination against working families; public data holds institutions accountable; community organizations empowered |
| Centrist | Evidence-based foundation for all policy decisions; cost-effective investment; data improves enforcement efficiency; bipartisan transparency principle |
| Religious Right | Communities can identify and respond to discrimination; faith-based organizations can use data for advocacy; transparency supports moral accountability |
| Democratic Socialist | Community-controlled testing shifts information power; public data challenges corporate opacity; research documents systemic discrimination patterns |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Liberal | Accept that testing and transparency alone do not constitute enforcement reform |
| Progressive | Accept that information production is not structural change |
| Libertarian | Accept government spending on testing infrastructure; accept expanded data reporting requirements on industry |
| Constitutionalist | Accept federal testing program expansion; accept data reporting mandates |
| Populist | Accept federal program rather than local control; accept academic and technocratic data management |
| Centrist | Accept that transparency alone does not solve discrimination; accept funding levels |
| Religious Right | Accept government spending on fair housing programs |
| Democratic Socialist | Accept that testing without enforcement is insufficient; accept private sector role in testing |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 8/9 perspectives (Conservative opposes at the margin due to concerns about testing being used for enforcement expansion, but does not fundamentally oppose transparency)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 5/8 perspectives rated 4+ consistency (Liberal, Progressive, Constitutionalist, Centrist, Democratic Socialist)
- **Durability:** Very high; transparency and information access are foundational principles that no perspective can oppose without appearing to favor discrimination; the coalition holds because each perspective views better data as serving their preferred approach; testing is established fair housing practice with bipartisan history; costs are modest relative to housing programs
- **Implementation Path:** Build on existing HUD testing programs and data infrastructure; fair housing testing has bipartisan legislative history; transparency provisions align with broader government data modernization; can be included in appropriations or standalone legislation; does not require resolving broader debates about AFFH or disparate impact

### Compromise 3: State Fair Housing Innovation Framework

**Bridges:** Liberal + Progressive + Constitutionalist + Centrist + Religious Right + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** State-level fair housing action can advance protections beyond federal minimums while respecting federalism, allowing diverse approaches across states, and building an evidence base for what works. States have historically led on fair housing innovation, and a federal incentive framework can encourage and support state action without mandating specific approaches.

**Policy Description:**

Model state legislation establishes three tiers of fair housing enhancement. Tier 1 (baseline): source of income protection, enhanced damages ($5,000 minimum for prevailing plaintiffs), mandatory fee-shifting, and state fair housing agency enforcement authority. Tier 2 (intermediate): criminal history screening limitations with individualized assessment requirements, state AFFH assessment for state housing programs, and algorithmic housing decision audit requirements. Tier 3 (advanced): state zoning preemption allowing accessory dwelling units and middle housing by right, regional fair share housing allocation, and state-level disparate impact codification. Federal incentive legislation provides $2 billion annually in housing block grants to states adopting Tier 1 or above, with additional allocations for Tier 2 and Tier 3 adoption. States retain full authority over program design within the tier framework. A national clearinghouse of state fair housing innovations is established to share best practices and evaluation results. States may apply for waivers to test alternative approaches that achieve equivalent fair housing outcomes.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Liberal | Comprehensive state protections going beyond federal law; source of income protection, enhanced damages, and AFFH advance fair housing goals; federal incentives encourage adoption |
| Progressive | State zoning preemption and fair share housing address structural segregation; disparate impact codification protects against facially neutral discrimination; multiple states moving simultaneously creates momentum |
| Constitutionalist | State legislation is the constitutionally proper vehicle; federal incentives voluntary under Spending Clause; states retain implementation authority; tiered structure allows states to advance at their own pace |
| Centrist | Evidence-based tiered framework; state innovation produces evaluation data; federal incentives align state action with national goals; phased approach allows learning |
| Religious Right | State action respects community values; religious exemptions can be tailored at state level; family protections advance family values; state control over implementation |
| Democratic Socialist | State action advances fair housing beyond inadequate federal protections; zoning reform and fair share housing challenge structural segregation; source of income protection reduces landlord gatekeeping |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Liberal | Accept state-by-state approach rather than uniform federal mandate; accept that non-adopting states will lag behind |
| Progressive | Accept state-level rather than national framework; accept tiered approach allowing states to advance incrementally |
| Constitutionalist | Accept federal incentive grants that influence state policy; accept implicit federal standards through tier requirements |
| Centrist | Accept that voluntary adoption will leave some states behind; accept ambitious funding levels |
| Religious Right | Accept state-level fair housing expansion; accept source of income mandate |
| Democratic Socialist | Accept state-by-state approach rather than national standard; accept incremental tiered structure |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 6/9 perspectives (Conservative opposes substance of most tier requirements; Libertarian opposes government housing regulation at any level; Populist opposes zoning reform and federal incentives for integration)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 5/6 coalition members rated 4+ consistency (Liberal, Progressive, Constitutionalist, Centrist, Democratic Socialist)
- **Durability:** Moderate to high; state-level framing reduces federalism objections; tiered structure allows states to advance at their own pace; federal incentives encourage adoption without mandating it; but voluntary adoption means states with the greatest need and least political will may not participate; strong evidence base from early-adopter states can build case for broader adoption
- **Implementation Path:** Model legislation promoted through National Conference of State Legislatures and state civil rights agencies; federal incentive legislation through housing appropriations; build on existing state source of income protections (21+ states); leverage successful state zoning reform examples (Oregon, California, Minnesota)

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The most effective coalition for fair housing reform operates on three tracks. The broadest coalition (7-8 perspectives) can advance housing mobility enhancement and fair housing testing -- reforms with strong evidence bases, clear beneficiaries (families seeking housing), and minimal ideological baggage. A center-left coalition (Liberal, Progressive, Centrist, Democratic Socialist) with selective Constitutionalist and Religious Right support can advance source of income protections and state-level fair housing innovation by framing reform as family empowerment and state-led innovation rather than federal integration mandates. AFFH restoration and comprehensive fair housing expansion require a center-left coalition that frames integration as community investment and economic opportunity rather than racial social engineering.

### Key Obstacles

- **The AFFH problem:** Affirmatively Furthering Fair Housing is the most important and most politically difficult reform because it requires race-conscious government action to undo race-conscious government harm; the framing of AFFH as "federal housing mandates" or "neighborhood destruction" activates opposition from conservatives, populists, and some religious right constituencies regardless of the substantive merits
- **The disparate impact divide:** The distinction between prohibiting intentional discrimination (broad support) and prohibiting practices with discriminatory effects (polarized support) is the fundamental fault line in fair housing policy; without disparate impact, most modern discrimination is unreachable; with it, every housing policy becomes a potential discrimination claim
- **Real estate industry influence:** The National Association of Realtors, Mortgage Bankers Association, and National Apartment Association have substantial lobbying resources and campaign contribution capacity that create structural opposition to fair housing expansion in Congress and state legislatures
- **Suburban homeowner constituency:** Homeowners in predominantly white suburban communities have both economic interests (property values, school quality) and political power (high voter turnout, campaign contributions) that create resistance to integration in the communities where integration would be most impactful
- **Racial framing dilemma:** Fair housing enforcement is fundamentally about racial discrimination, yet racial framing activates opposition; reframing as "family opportunity" or "housing choice" may broaden support but risks obscuring the racial justice imperatives driving reform

### Low Consistency Partners

Conservative (2/5) and Populist (2/5) perspectives rate at low consistency on fair housing, the lowest ratings in this analysis. Conservative positions are driven by real estate industry interests, suburban homeowner constituencies, and opposition to race-conscious policy rather than principled engagement with discrimination evidence. Conservative support is most reliable on narrow provisions like appraisal bias reform (identifiable problem, no structural implications) and least reliable on AFFH or disparate impact. Populist positions shift dramatically based on framing: "holding banks accountable for cheating working families" (support) versus "government forcing diversity on your neighborhood" (opposition). Populist support is most accessible when fair housing is framed as anti-corporate accountability and least accessible when framed as integration policy.

Libertarian (3/5) and Religious Right (3/5) perspectives rate at mixed consistency. Libertarian support is accessible only on zoning deregulation, where libertarian property rights principles align with fair housing goals. Religious Right support is most accessible through family protection and disability accommodation framing and least accessible when fair housing expansion includes LGBTQ+ protections.

### Recommended Approach

1. **Lead with mobility and source of income protection:** The Housing Opportunity and Mobility Act (Compromise 1) commands 7/9 perspective support and addresses the practical barrier most directly experienced by families -- the inability to use housing vouchers in opportunity areas; source of income protection has been enacted in 21+ states without significant political backlash, demonstrating viability

2. **Build the evidence base through testing:** The Fair Housing Testing and Transparency Act (Compromise 2) commands 8/9 perspective support and produces the data foundation for all subsequent reforms; systematic testing results document discrimination in ways that are difficult to dismiss; transparency empowers market accountability, community advocacy, and enforcement simultaneously

3. **Pursue fair housing innovation through states:** The State Fair Housing Innovation Framework (Compromise 3) respects federalism while building a portfolio of state-level fair housing innovations; successful state models (Oregon zoning reform, New Jersey fair share, state source of income protections) create evidence and momentum for broader adoption

4. **Separate zoning reform from integration framing:** Zoning deregulation (allowing ADUs, duplexes, and middle housing) commands an unusual coalition including libertarians; framing zoning reform as housing supply and property rights rather than integration accesses support that would otherwise be unavailable; the fair housing effects of zoning reform are substantial even if they are not the stated purpose

5. **Frame fair housing as family opportunity:** The family stability and economic mobility framing -- "every family should be able to access good schools and safe neighborhoods" -- accesses support from centrist, religious right, and populist perspectives that would resist the same policies framed as "racial desegregation" or "overcoming segregation"; the framing is accurate (fair housing is about family opportunity) even though it does not capture the full racial justice imperative

6. **Build toward AFFH through demonstrated results:** AFFH is the most important and most difficult reform; rather than leading with AFFH codification, build toward it by demonstrating that mobility programs produce measurable gains, that testing documents ongoing discrimination, and that state-level fair housing innovation produces tangible results; each success creates political space for the next step toward comprehensive AFFH

7. **Maintain the long game on disparate impact:** Codification of disparate impact is essential to prevent Supreme Court rollback but faces significant opposition; the strongest case for disparate impact is built by documenting specific practices (algorithmic screening, zoning, lending policies) that have clearly discriminatory effects despite facial neutrality; each documented example strengthens the political case for codification
