# Homeownership: Stakeholders

## Overview

Homeownership policy involves diverse stakeholders with varying interests. Understanding each group's motivations, power, and positions is essential for building coalitions and anticipating resistance.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Aspiring Homeowners

**First-Time Homebuyers**

| Characteristic | Profile |
|---------------|---------|
| Number | 4.2 million seeking annually |
| Median age | 36 (up from 29 in 1980s) |
| Median income | $97,000 |
| Married/partnered | 58% |
| College educated | 72% |
| Student debt | 43% carry balances |

**Primary Interests**

- Affordable entry-level homes
- Down payment assistance
- Lower credit requirements
- Protection from predatory practices
- Sustainable mortgage terms

**Barriers Faced**

| Barrier | Share Affected |
|---------|----------------|
| Insufficient down payment | 68% |
| High home prices | 75% |
| Student debt burden | 43% |
| Credit score issues | 35% |
| Outbid by cash buyers | 52% |
| Can't find affordable home | 61% |

**Political Power**: Moderate - Numerous but diffuse, not well-organized as constituency

### First-Generation Homebuyers

**Unique Profile**

| Characteristic | First-Generation | Other First-Time |
|---------------|------------------|------------------|
| Number | 1.4 million annually (estimated) | 2.8 million |
| Median income | $72,000 | $105,000 |
| Family gift available | 0% | 38% |
| Average down payment | $24,000 | $58,000 |
| Success rate | 55% of attempts | 78% |

**Primary Interests**

- Targeted assistance programs
- Process education and guidance
- Credit-building opportunities
- Protection from exploitation
- Smaller, affordable homes

**Political Power**: Low - Often not recognized as distinct category; lacks organized advocacy

### Minority Aspiring Homeowners

**Profile by Group**

| Group | Aspiring Buyers | Current Rate | Target Rate |
|-------|-----------------|--------------|-------------|
| Black | 1.8 million | 45.3% | 60% |
| Hispanic | 2.1 million | 48.6% | 60% |
| Asian | 0.9 million | 63.4% | 70% |
| Native American | 0.2 million | 51.2% | 60% |

**Primary Interests**

- Elimination of appraisal bias
- Fair lending enforcement
- Down payment assistance
- Credit alternatives
- Wealth-building support

**Political Power**: Moderate - Civil rights organizations provide advocacy; growing electoral importance

### Existing Homeowners

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Value |
|---------------|-------|
| Number of households | 86 million |
| Median home equity | $215,000 |
| Median age | 52 |
| Years in home | 13 (median) |
| Mortgage holders | 62% |
| Own outright | 38% |

**Primary Interests**

- Protect home values
- Low property taxes
- Maintain neighborhood character
- Limit new development (often)
- Foreclosure protection

**Political Power**: Very High - Vote at high rates, organized through HOAs, dominate local politics

**Tension with Aspiring Buyers**: Existing owners often support policies that restrict supply and maintain high prices, conflicting with first-time buyer interests.

### At-Risk Homeowners

**Profile**

| Status | Number |
|--------|--------|
| Behind on mortgage | 1.8 million |
| In foreclosure process | 340,000 |
| Underwater | 1.5 million |
| Cost-burdened owner | 19.3 million |

**Primary Interests**

- Foreclosure prevention
- Loan modification access
- Maintenance assistance
- Property tax relief
- Counseling services

**Political Power**: Low - Often in crisis, stigmatized, not organized

## Industry Stakeholders

### Mortgage Lenders

**Industry Structure**

| Lender Type | Market Share | Key Players |
|-------------|--------------|-------------|
| Bank lenders | 35% | Wells Fargo, Chase, Bank of America |
| Non-bank lenders | 52% | Rocket, United Wholesale, loanDepot |
| Credit unions | 8% | Navy Federal, PenFed |
| Community banks | 5% | Various local |

**Primary Interests**

- Profitable lending volume
- Manageable regulatory burden
- Secondary market access
- Limited liability for loan performance
- Ability to sell loans to GSEs

**Position on Reforms**

| Reform | Industry Position | Rationale |
|--------|-------------------|-----------|
| CRA expansion | Oppose | More compliance costs |
| Special Purpose Credit Programs | Cautious support | Liability concerns |
| Down payment assistance | Support | More eligible buyers |
| Credit score alternatives | Mixed | Risk assessment concerns |
| Appraisal reform | Cautious | Liability for valuations |

**Political Power**: Very High - Major lobbying presence, campaign contributions, revolving door with regulators

### Real Estate Agents

**Industry Profile**

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Licensed agents | 1.6 million |
| Active agents | 1.0 million |
| NAR members | 1.5 million |
| Median income | $56,400 |
| Commission rate | 5-6% (split) |

**Primary Interests**

- Maintain commission structure
- Transaction volume
- Exclusive access to listings
- Limited disclosure requirements
- Protect MLS system

**Position on Reforms**

| Reform | Industry Position | Rationale |
|--------|-------------------|-----------|
| Fair housing enforcement | Official support | Required by law |
| Commission reform | Strong opposition | Protects income |
| Inventory transparency | Mixed | Pocket listings profitable |
| First-time buyer programs | Support | More transactions |
| Appraisal reform | Support | Faster closings |

**Political Power**: High - Strong state-level lobbying, local political engagement, NAR is major campaign contributor

### Home Builders

**Industry Profile**

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Home building companies | 750,000 |
| Production builders | 500 (build 80% of homes) |
| Top 10 builders' share | 35% |
| Annual housing starts | 1.4 million |
| Average new home price | $495,000 |

**Key Players**: D.R. Horton, Lennar, PulteGroup, NVR, Toll Brothers

**Primary Interests**

- Fewer regulatory barriers
- Lower impact fees
- Skilled labor availability
- Material cost stability
- Zoning reform (for more development)

**Position on Reforms**

| Reform | Industry Position | Rationale |
|--------|-------------------|-----------|
| Zoning reform | Strong support | More building opportunity |
| Affordable housing mandates | Oppose | Reduces margins |
| Environmental review streamlining | Strong support | Faster approvals |
| Inclusionary zoning | Oppose | Cost burden |
| First-time buyer programs | Support | Creates demand |

**Political Power**: High - NAHB is major lobbyist, local political influence

### Mortgage Servicers

**Industry Profile**

| Servicer Type | Market Share |
|---------------|--------------|
| Bank servicers | 40% |
| Non-bank servicers | 60% |

**Key Players**: Mr. Cooper, Quicken, Nationstar, Wells Fargo

**Primary Interests**

- Servicing fee income
- Efficient portfolio management
- Limited foreclosure liability
- Automated processes
- Clear modification rules

**Political Power**: Moderate - Less visible than originators but significant lobby presence

### Appraisal Industry

**Industry Profile**

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Licensed appraisers | 78,000 |
| Appraisals annually | 10 million |
| Median fee | $500-$600 |
| Demographics | 97% white, 77% male |

**Primary Interests**

- Professional autonomy
- Maintain licensing requirements
- Limit liability for valuations
- Resist AVMs and technology displacement
- Maintain fee levels

**Position on Reforms**

| Reform | Industry Position | Rationale |
|--------|-------------------|-----------|
| Bias training | Reluctant support | Acknowledges problem |
| Diversity requirements | Oppose | "Burdensome" |
| AVM expansion | Oppose | Threatens profession |
| Reconsideration of value rules | Cautious | Liability concerns |
| Automated bias detection | Oppose | External oversight |

**Political Power**: Moderate - Appraisal Institute lobbies; state licensing boards protective

### Local Governments

**Interests**

| Interest | Manifestation |
|----------|---------------|
| Property tax revenue | Prefer higher values |
| Community stability | Support homeownership |
| Political pressure | Respond to existing owners |
| Economic development | Attract workforce |
| Service demands | Balance growth with capacity |

**Common Tensions**

- Want homeownership but also high property values
- Support affordable housing "somewhere else"
- Zoning controlled by existing homeowner voters
- Competing with neighboring jurisdictions

## Investors and Financial Institutions

### Institutional Investors

**Profile**

| Investor Type | Single-Family Homes Owned |
|---------------|--------------------------|
| Large institutions (10,000+ homes) | 500,000 |
| Mid-size (1,000-9,999 homes) | 400,000 |
| Small investors (<1,000 homes) | 18 million |

**Key Players**: Invitation Homes, American Homes 4 Rent, Progress Residential

**Primary Interests**

- Rental income
- Property appreciation
- Limited regulation
- Tax advantages
- Portfolio growth

**Position on Homeownership Policy**

| Reform | Position | Rationale |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| First-time buyer assistance | Neutral/Oppose | Reduces rental demand |
| Rent control | Strong opposition | Limits returns |
| Tenant protections | Oppose | Reduces flexibility |
| Conversion limits | Oppose | Restricts exit options |

**Political Power**: Growing - Major lobbying expenditures, campaign contributions

### Coalition Opportunities

**Pro-Access Coalition**

- First-time and first-generation buyers
- Minority homebuyers
- Fair housing organizations
- Housing counseling agencies
- Some lenders (FHA-focused)
- Builders (for supply reform)

**Points of Agreement**

- Expand down payment assistance
- Increase housing supply
- Improve credit access
- Reduce barriers for qualified buyers

**Tensions Within Coalition**

- Speed of reform
- Role of government
- Targeting vs. universal programs
- Supply vs. demand focus
