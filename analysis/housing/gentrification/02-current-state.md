# Gentrification: Current State

## The Problem Today

Gentrification has accelerated dramatically in American cities since 2000, transforming low-income neighborhoods at unprecedented rates. An estimated 20-25% of urban low-income census tracts experienced significant gentrification between 2000 and 2020, compared to roughly 10% in the 1990s. The COVID-19 pandemic briefly disrupted urban migration patterns, but urban neighborhoods have rebounded with continued gentrification pressure. Meanwhile, displacement remains poorly tracked, leaving the full human cost of neighborhood transformation largely invisible in official statistics.

### Defining Gentrification: Measurement Approaches

| Approach | Indicators | Limitations |
|----------|------------|-------------|
| Income-based | Rising median income relative to metro | Doesn't capture displacement directly |
| Housing cost | Rising rents/prices above metro average | New construction can skew |
| Demographic | Increase in white/college-educated residents | Racializes definition |
| Investment | Building permits, renovation, commercial change | Doesn't measure who benefits |
| Composite | Multiple indicators combined | More comprehensive but complex |

## Displacement Data

### The Measurement Challenge

| Data Source | What It Captures | What It Misses |
|-------------|------------------|----------------|
| Census mobility | Moves out of tract | Reason for move, destination |
| Eviction records | Formal legal evictions | Informal displacement, buyouts |
| Surveys | Self-reported displacement | Small samples, recall bias |
| Qualitative research | Community experience | Hard to quantify |

### Available Displacement Estimates

| Study/Location | Finding | Time Period |
|----------------|---------|-------------|
| NYC Furman Center | 6% annual displacement rate in gentrifying areas | 2002-2014 |
| SF Budget Office | 8,000 residents displaced from Mission annually | 2010-2015 |
| Atlanta Federal Reserve | 4-5% higher outmigration in gentrifying tracts | 2000-2010 |
| Philadelphia | 10% displacement from intensely gentrifying areas | 2000-2014 |
| National (Freeman/Braconi) | Minimal difference in mobility rates | 1990s |
| National (Ding et al.) | Significant displacement when using comprehensive measures | 2000-2010 |

### Who Is Being Displaced

| Population | Displacement Risk | Evidence |
|------------|-------------------|----------|
| Low-income renters | Very high | Direct exposure to rent increases |
| Black residents | Very high | Targeted neighborhoods, historical discrimination |
| Latino residents | High | Immigrant communities, language barriers |
| Elderly residents | High | Fixed incomes, predatory targeting |
| Families with children | High | Larger space needs, school stability |
| Public housing residents | Variable | Depends on HOPE VI/RAD participation |

## Commercial Gentrification

### Small Business Impacts

| Indicator | Gentrifying Areas | Non-Gentrifying | Impact |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|--------|
| Business turnover rate | 40-60% over 5 years | 25-30% | Higher displacement |
| Chain store increase | 15-25% | 5-10% | Homogenization |
| Legacy business closures | 30-50% | 10-15% | Cultural loss |
| Commercial rent increases | 80-150% | 30-50% | Displacement driver |

### Types of Businesses Affected

| Business Type | Vulnerability | Why |
|---------------|---------------|-----|
| Bodegas/corner stores | Very high | Low margins, chain competition |
| Ethnic restaurants | High | Changing customer base, rent |
| Barbershops/salons | High | Customer relationship dependent |
| Hardware stores | High | Big box competition |
| Auto repair | Very high | Land value pressure |
| Churches/religious | High | Congregation displacement |
| Community organizations | Very high | Funding, constituency loss |

## The Benefits Debate

### Potential Benefits of Gentrification

| Claimed Benefit | Evidence | Caveats |
|-----------------|----------|---------|
| Crime reduction | Some studies show declines | May reflect displacement of victims |
| Improved schools | Mixed evidence | Existing families may not benefit |
| Better services | Retail, restaurants improve | Serve new residents primarily |
| Infrastructure investment | Street improvements, parks | Historically disinvested areas |
| Property values for owners | Significant increases | Mostly benefits sellers/investors |
| Economic integration | Some mixed-income creation | Often temporary |

### Evidence on Who Benefits

| Population | Benefits | Costs |
|------------|----------|-------|
| New residents | Affordable entry, improving neighborhood | None direct |
| Long-term homeowners | Wealth appreciation | Property taxes, loss of community |
| Remaining renters | Some amenity improvements | Rent stress, cultural displacement |
| Displaced residents | None | Loss of housing, networks, community |
| City government | Property tax revenue | Displacement costs, service demand shift |

## Current Policy Responses

### Prevalence of Anti-Displacement Policies

| Policy Type | Cities with Policy | Effectiveness Evidence |
|-------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Rent stabilization | ~180 municipalities | Moderate protection for covered units |
| Just cause eviction | ~30 jurisdictions | Reduces formal evictions |
| Community land trusts | 300+ CLTs nationally | Strong for participating households |
| Inclusionary zoning | 500+ jurisdictions | Limited affordable unit production |
| Commercial rent stabilization | Very few (SF pilot) | Untested at scale |
| Right to return | Few (Portland, SF) | Implementation challenges |

### Policy Gaps

| Gap | Impact | Needed Response |
|-----|--------|-----------------|
| No federal anti-displacement policy | Patchwork protection | National framework |
| State preemption of rent control | Limited local options | State legislative reform |
| Weak enforcement | Policies unenforced | Dedicated enforcement resources |
| No displacement tracking | Problem invisible | Mandatory displacement reporting |
| Limited funding | Small-scale programs | Major public investment |

## Recent Developments

### Post-Pandemic Trends (2020-2024)

- Initial urban exodus during COVID reversed by 2022
- Remote work enabled some gentrifiers to work from anywhere
- Sun Belt cities experiencing accelerated gentrification
- Commercial real estate uncertainty affecting neighborhood change
- Rent increases resumed at accelerated pace post-eviction moratoria
