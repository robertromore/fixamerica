# Public Housing and Assisted Housing: Stakeholders

## Overview

Public and assisted housing programs involve a complex ecosystem of stakeholders with varying degrees of power, interest, and influence. Understanding these stakeholders is essential for building effective advocacy coalitions, anticipating opposition, and designing politically viable reforms. This section maps the key actors, their interests, and their relative influence over policy outcomes.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Public Housing Residents

**Population Profile**

| Characteristic | Percentage |
|----------------|------------|
| Extremely low income (<30% AMI) | 72% |
| Elderly (62+) | 34% |
| Disabled (non-elderly) | 23% |
| Families with children | 31% |
| Black/African American | 44% |
| Hispanic/Latino | 22% |

**Key Interests**

- Housing stability and security
- Safe, well-maintained living conditions
- Reasonable rent burdens
- Access to services and amenities
- Voice in management decisions
- Protection from displacement
- Opportunity for economic advancement

**Organized Voice**

| Organization | Focus |
|--------------|-------|
| National Resident Organizing Collaborative | National networking |
| Local resident councils | Site-level representation |
| Citywide resident organizations | Municipal advocacy |
| Coalition for a Just Opportunity (NYC) | Tenant organizing |
| Cabrini-Green Legal Aid | Resident legal services |

**Power Assessment**

- **Formal power**: Limited - advisory roles, resident councils
- **Collective action capacity**: Variable - some strong local organizations
- **Electoral influence**: Often marginalized
- **Voice in policy debates**: Frequently excluded or tokenized
- **Legal rights**: Significant protections exist but enforcement is challenging

### Housing Choice Voucher Holders

**Population Profile**

| Characteristic | Percentage |
|----------------|------------|
| Extremely low income (<30% AMI) | 74% |
| Families with children | 39% |
| Elderly (62+) | 23% |
| Disabled (non-elderly) | 23% |
| Black/African American | 47% |
| Hispanic/Latino | 18% |

**Key Interests**

- Adequate payment standards
- Ability to find accepting landlords
- Protection from discrimination
- Stable housing without frequent moves
- Choice of neighborhood
- Reasonable inspection requirements

**Power Assessment**

- Less organized than public housing residents
- Geographically dispersed
- Often invisible to neighbors (unless disclosed)
- Limited collective voice
- Consumer model emphasizes individual choice over collective action

### People on Waiting Lists

**Estimated Population**: 3-4 million households

**Key Interests**

- Reduced wait times
- Fair and transparent selection processes
- Information about list status
- Expansion of available assistance

**Power Assessment**

- Nearly invisible in policy discussions
- No formal representation
- Extreme difficulty organizing people waiting years
- Interest in expansion not prioritized by political system

## Government Stakeholders

### U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD)

**Role**

- Primary federal agency for housing assistance
- Administers voucher and public housing programs
- Sets regulations and policy
- Provides oversight of PHAs
- Distributes funding

**Key Offices**

| Office | Responsibility |
|--------|----------------|
| Office of Public and Indian Housing | Public housing and vouchers |
| Office of Multifamily Housing | Project-based Section 8 |
| Office of Fair Housing and Equal Opportunity | Civil rights enforcement |
| Office of Policy Development and Research | Research and data |

**Interests**

- Efficient program administration
- Positive outcomes for residents
- Compliance and accountability
- Manageable scope of responsibility
- Policy implementation without political conflict

**Constraints**

- Limited staff and budget for oversight
- Political appointees set priorities
- Congress controls funding and some policy
- Court orders shape some actions
- Variable political support by administration

### Public Housing Authorities (PHAs)

**Scale**: ~3,300 PHAs nationwide

**Governance**

- Typically governed by appointed board
- Executive director manages operations
- Staff ranges from a few to thousands
- Mix of local government agencies and independent authorities

**Major PHAs**

| PHA | Units/Vouchers | Staff |
|-----|----------------|-------|
| NYCHA (New York) | 170,000+ | 11,000 |
| CHA (Chicago) | 50,000+ | 2,000 |
| HACLA (Los Angeles) | 85,000+ | 800 |
| HABC (Baltimore) | 25,000+ | 600 |

**Interests**

- Adequate funding from HUD
- Flexibility in operations
- Manageable regulatory burden
- Capital for improvements
- Positive public perception
- Political support from local government

**Industry Association**: Public Housing Authorities Directors Association (PHADA)

### State Housing Finance Agencies

**Role**

- Administer Low-Income Housing Tax Credits
- Issue housing bonds
- Sometimes administer state-funded programs
- Coordinate with PHAs

**Interests**

- Successful tax credit projects
- Meeting housing production goals
- Compliance with federal and state requirements
- Efficient capital deployment

### Local Government

**Interests**

| Interest | Priority |
|----------|----------|
| Addressing homelessness | High |
| Property tax revenue | High |
| Community stability | Moderate |
| Neighborhood quality | High |
| Avoiding concentrated poverty | Moderate to high |
| Political viability | High |

**Powers**

- Zoning and land use approval
- Local funding contributions
- PHA board appointments
- Code enforcement
- Service provision

## Private Sector Stakeholders

### Landlords

**Voucher-Accepting Landlords**

| Characteristic | Interest |
|----------------|----------|
| Stable rental income | Guaranteed by voucher |
| Tenant quality | Concerns about screening |
| Property inspections | Burden versus quality assurance |
| Payment timeliness | Want fast, reliable payments |
| Administrative requirements | Prefer simplicity |

**Non-Participating Landlords**

- Prefer market-rate tenants
- Avoid inspection requirements
- Perceive voucher tenants as higher risk
- May have racial or class prejudice
- Can legally refuse vouchers in most states

### Developers

**Affordable Housing Developers**

| Type | Interest |
|------|----------|
| Nonprofit developers | Mission-driven, need subsidies |
| For-profit developers | Profit motive, attracted by tax credits |
| Mixed-income developers | Balance affordability with market-rate |

**Key Concerns**

- Availability of tax credits and subsidies
- Zoning and entitlement processes
- Construction costs
- Long-term operating feasibility
- Community opposition (NIMBY)

### Investors

**Low-Income Housing Tax Credit Investors**

- Banks, corporations seeking tax credit equity
- Return expectations
- Risk tolerance
- Compliance requirements
- 15-year commitment periods

**Recent Trends**

- LIHTC market has matured
- Pricing competitive
- COVID-19 disrupted but market recovered
- RAD conversions attracting investors

### Property Management Companies

**Role**

- Manage many RAD-converted properties
- Manage project-based Section 8
- Some manage public housing under contract

**Interests**

- Management fees
- Manageable resident populations
- Clear regulations
- Efficient HUD processes

## Opposition Stakeholders

### Anti-Tax/Small Government Groups

**Organizations**

- Heritage Foundation
- Cato Institute
- American Enterprise Institute
- State-level groups (state policy network affiliates)

**Arguments**

- Government housing distorts markets
- Programs create dependency
- Private sector should provide housing
- Fraud and waste in programs
- Work requirements and time limits needed

### Real Estate Industry

**National Association of Realtors**

- Generally supports homeownership programs
- Less supportive of rental assistance expansion
- Opposes source of income protection mandates

**National Apartment Association**

- Opposes rent control
- Resists source of income protection
- Seeks landlord-friendly voucher rules

**National Multifamily Housing Council**

- Represents larger apartment owners
- Mixed on affordable housing policy
- Prefers voluntary programs

### NIMBY Opponents

**Characteristics**

- Often not organized nationally
- Activate locally when projects proposed
- Concerns about property values, safety, traffic
- Effective at blocking individual projects

**Tactics**

- Public meetings and comment
- Legal challenges
- Political pressure on local officials
- Delay as strategy

## Power Analysis

### Stakeholder Influence Matrix

| Stakeholder | Formal Authority | Resources | Organization | Access | Overall Influence |
|-------------|------------------|-----------|--------------|--------|-------------------|
| HUD | High | Moderate | Strong | High | High |
| Congress | High | High | N/A | High | Very High |
| PHAs | Moderate | Variable | Moderate | Moderate | Moderate |
| Residents | Low | Low | Variable | Low | Low |
| Voucher holders | Low | Low | Weak | Low | Very Low |
| Landlords | Low | Moderate | Strong | Moderate | Moderate |
| Developers | Low | High | Strong | High | High |
| LIHTC investors | Low | High | Strong | High | High |
| Advocacy groups | Low | Low-Moderate | Strong | Moderate | Moderate |
| NIMBY opponents | Low | Variable | Weak nationally, strong locally | Variable | Moderate locally |

### Coalition Dynamics

**Expansion Coalitions**

- Advocacy organizations
- Some PHAs
- Resident organizations
- Civil rights groups
- Some developers
- Progressive politicians

**Transformation Coalitions**

- Developers (HOPE VI, Choice Neighborhoods)
- Investors
- Some PHAs
- Local governments seeking redevelopment
- Some housing advocates (conditional support)

**Status Quo Coalitions**

- Fiscally conservative legislators
- Some PHAs (avoiding change)
- Some resident groups (fearing displacement)

**Reduction Coalitions**

- Anti-government groups
- Some fiscal conservatives
- Real estate interests (for some programs)
