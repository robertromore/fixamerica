# Public Housing and Assisted Housing: Overview

## Executive Summary

Public housing and federal housing assistance programs represent America's primary tools for providing affordable housing to extremely low-income households, yet these programs serve only a fraction of eligible families. While approximately 10 million households qualify for rental assistance, only about 5 million receive it, leaving millions on waiting lists that can stretch for years or even decades in high-demand areas.

The public housing program, once envisioned as providing quality housing for the working class, has been systematically disinvested for decades. The original 1.4 million public housing units have declined to under 1 million, with demolitions outpacing new construction since the 1990s. What remains is often severely underfunded, with an estimated $70+ billion capital backlog that grows each year. Meanwhile, Housing Choice Vouchers (Section 8) have become the dominant form of assistance, but their value is limited by landlord discrimination, inadequate payment standards, and tight rental markets.

The transformation of public housing through HOPE VI, Choice Neighborhoods, and Rental Assistance Demonstration (RAD) has replaced traditional public housing with mixed-income developments and project-based vouchers. While proponents argue this approach addresses concentrated poverty and physical deterioration, critics note that these programs have often resulted in net losses of deeply affordable units and displacement of longtime residents.

This analysis examines the current state of public and assisted housing, the policy decisions that shaped its decline, and the possibilities for expansion and reform.

## The Core Problem

| Metric | Current Reality |
|--------|-----------------|
| Eligible households | ~10 million |
| Households receiving assistance | ~5 million |
| Average voucher waiting list | 2-3 years |
| Longest waiting lists | 5-10+ years (NYC, Los Angeles) |
| Public housing units remaining | ~960,000 (down from 1.4 million) |
| Capital needs backlog | $70-80 billion |
| Voucher success rate | 60% nationally (30-40% in tight markets) |

## Why It Matters

**For Low-Income Households**

- Housing assistance enables economic stability and upward mobility
- Children in assisted housing show improved educational outcomes
- Stable housing supports employment retention
- Without assistance, severe cost burden forces impossible choices
- Long waiting lists mean years of housing insecurity

**For Communities**

- Public housing concentrates poverty when poorly managed
- Mixed-income redevelopments can transform neighborhoods
- Loss of affordable units increases homelessness
- Housing stability reduces demand for emergency services
- Displacement fractures established communities

**For Taxpayers**

- Housing assistance costs approximately $50 billion annually
- Unstable housing creates downstream costs (healthcare, education, justice)
- Public housing maintenance deferred today costs more tomorrow
- Preventing homelessness is more cost-effective than responding to it
- Investment in housing provides economic returns

## Key Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Public Housing | Federally subsidized rental housing owned and operated by Public Housing Authorities |
| Housing Choice Voucher (Section 8) | Tenant-based rental assistance allowing recipients to rent in the private market |
| Project-Based Voucher | Vouchers attached to specific units rather than following the tenant |
| Fair Market Rent (FMR) | HUD-determined payment standard based on area rents |
| Payment Standard | The maximum subsidy a PHA will pay, set between 90-110% of FMR |
| Tenant Contribution | Generally 30% of adjusted income toward rent |
| Public Housing Authority (PHA) | Local or state agency administering public housing and vouchers |
| Capital Fund | Federal funding for public housing repairs and improvements |
| Operating Fund | Federal funding for day-to-day public housing operations |
| HOPE VI | Program (1992-2010) funding demolition and redevelopment of distressed public housing |
| RAD | Rental Assistance Demonstration converting public housing to project-based assistance |
| Mixed-Income Development | Housing combining market-rate, moderate, and low-income units |

## The Scale of the Crisis

**Who Receives Assistance**

| Program | Households Served | Average Income |
|---------|-------------------|----------------|
| Housing Choice Vouchers | 2.3 million | ~$14,500/year |
| Public Housing | 950,000 | ~$15,500/year |
| Project-Based Section 8 | 1.2 million | ~$13,000/year |
| Other HUD programs | ~500,000 | Varies |
| USDA Rural Housing | ~400,000 | ~$12,000/year |

**Who Is Waiting**

- An estimated 3-4 million households on waiting lists
- Many lists are closed, meaning true demand is not measured
- Average wait times of 2-3 years nationally
- Waits of 5-10+ years in high-demand areas
- Many potential applicants do not bother applying given length of waits

## What's at Stake

**Current Reality**

- Only 1 in 4 eligible households receives assistance
- 960,000 public housing units with $70+ billion in unmet capital needs
- Voucher holders face 60% success rate in finding housing
- 100,000+ public housing units demolished under HOPE VI
- Source of income discrimination legal in most states

**Without Reform**

- Continued loss of deeply affordable units
- Growing waiting lists and rationing
- Physical deterioration of remaining stock
- Increasing voucher failure rates in tight markets
- Rising homelessness among lowest-income households

## The Path Forward

Addressing public and assisted housing requires:

1. **Dramatically expand assistance** - Provide vouchers to all eligible households or substantially increase supply
2. **Preserve existing public housing** - Fund capital repairs and prevent further demolitions without replacement
3. **Improve voucher success** - Increase payment standards, provide landlord incentives, mandate source of income protections
4. **Protect residents in transformation** - Ensure right to return, one-for-one replacement, and meaningful participation
5. **Reform housing authority governance** - Increase accountability, resident voice, and professional management
6. **Target investments** - Focus on extremely low-income households with greatest need

## Why This Analysis Matters

Public and assisted housing represent the primary federal commitment to housing the nation's poorest residents. The decisions made about these programs directly affect millions of families' ability to maintain stable housing and economic security.

Understanding the history and dynamics of public housing is essential because:

- **Policy debates often rely on misconceptions** - The "failure" of public housing was largely a policy choice, not inevitable
- **Transformation programs have mixed records** - HOPE VI and RAD have not always served residents' interests
- **The voucher program has serious limitations** - Vouchers only work where housing is available and landlords participate
- **Funding levels are policy choices** - The gap between eligible and served households reflects political priorities
- **Resident voices are often marginalized** - Those most affected have least influence over policy

This analysis aims to equip advocates, policymakers, and citizens with evidence and tools to strengthen housing assistance for those who need it most.
