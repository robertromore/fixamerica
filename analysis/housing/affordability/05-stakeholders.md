# Housing Affordability: Stakeholders

## Overview

Housing affordability involves stakeholders with vastly different interests, power levels, and influence over policy. Understanding these dynamics is essential for designing effective reforms and building coalitions for change.

## Those Harmed by the Crisis

### Cost-Burdened Renters

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Data |
|----------------|------|
| Number | 22.4 million households |
| Median income | $35,000 |
| Median rent burden | 42% of income |
| Severely burdened (50%+) | 11.6 million |
| Children in households | 8.2 million |

**Specific Impacts**

| Impact | Prevalence |
|--------|------------|
| Can't afford move | 68% |
| Delayed healthcare | 34% |
| Food insecurity | 31% |
| Housing instability | 45% experienced in past 5 years |
| Eviction risk | 15% have been evicted |

**Political Power**: Low

- Lower voter turnout than homeowners
- Frequent moves disrupt political engagement
- Dispersed, difficult to organize
- Limited campaign contribution capacity
- Underrepresented in local politics

### Would-Be First-Time Homebuyers

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Data |
|----------------|------|
| Renters wanting to buy | 77% of renters |
| Priced out of market | 58% of would-be buyers |
| Average years saving for down payment | 8-14 years |
| Parents providing down payment help | 40% of first-time buyers |
| Median first-time buyer age | 36 (vs 29 in 1981) |

**Barriers Faced**

| Barrier | Impact |
|---------|--------|
| Down payment | Can't save while paying high rent |
| Student debt | $37,000 average, consumes income |
| Credit requirements | Post-2008 tightening |
| Bidding competition | Cash buyers, investors win |
| Rising prices | Savings can't keep pace |

**Political Power**: Moderate

- Higher education, more likely to vote
- Increasing political mobilization (YIMBY movement)
- Social media organizing capability
- But dispersed, not organized at local level
- Often lack awareness of zoning politics

|------------|-------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Home health aides | $14.50/hr | $754 | 62% |
| Childcare workers | $13.50/hr | $702 | 58% |
| Retail salespersons | $15.00/hr | $780 | 55% |
| Food service workers | $14.00/hr | $728 | 61% |
| Nursing assistants | $16.00/hr | $832 | 48% |
| Teachers (K-12) | $28.00/hr | $1,456 | 35% |
| Police officers | $30.00/hr | $1,560 | 28% |

**Geographic Mismatch**

| Metro Area | Teacher Can Afford Housing Near School? |
|------------|----------------------------------------|
| San Francisco | 7% of listings |
| Los Angeles | 12% of listings |
| New York | 15% of listings |
| Boston | 18% of listings |
| Denver | 22% of listings |
| Austin | 28% of listings |

**Political Power**: Moderate

- Unions provide organizing infrastructure
- Sympathetic public narrative
- But housing not usually union priority
- Essential worker status increased visibility post-COVID

### Low-Income Households

**Profile**

| Category | Number | Housing Challenge |
|----------|--------|-------------------|
| Extremely Low Income (<30% AMI) | 11.6 million | Only 33 affordable units per 100 households |
| Very Low Income (30-50% AMI) | 8.2 million | 57 affordable units per 100 households |
| Fixed-income seniors | 6.7 million | Rising housing costs on fixed income |
| Disabled individuals | 4.8 million | Accessibility + affordability |
| Single parents | 3.4 million | Limited income, need space for children |

**Waitlist Crisis**

| Program | Average Wait Time | Households Waiting |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Section 8 voucher | 2-10 years | 2+ million |
| Public housing | 1-5 years | 1+ million |
| LIHTC unit | 1-3 years | Unknown |

**Political Power**: Very Low

- Limited time and resources for organizing
- Stigma discourages advocacy
- Dependent on government programs
- Least likely to vote
- Often invisible in policy discussions

### Homeless Individuals

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Data |
|----------------|------|
| Point-in-time count | 650,000+ |
| Chronically homeless | 127,000 |
| Veterans | 37,000 |
| Families with children | 48,000 families |
| Unaccompanied youth | 34,000 |

**Political Power**: Minimal

- No address = barriers to voting
- Severe stigma
- Limited organizing capacity
- Advocacy often done by others on their behalf

## Those Who Benefit from Status Quo

### Existing Homeowners

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Data |
|----------------|------|
| Number | 86 million households |
| Median home equity | $185,000 |
| Median net worth (owners) | $396,200 |
| Median net worth (renters) | $10,400 |
| Share with mortgage | 62% |

**Interests in Scarcity**

| Interest | Mechanism |
|----------|-----------|
| Property value protection | Scarcity increases values |
| Neighborhood "character" | Exclusion maintains homogeneity |
| Tax assessment stability | New development could raise assessments |
| School quality | Exclusion maintains socioeconomic segregation |
| Traffic/parking | Less density = less congestion |

**Political Power**: Very High

- Higher voter turnout (65% vs 35% for renters)
- HOAs and neighborhood associations organized
- Political donations capacity
- Local officials often homeowners themselves
- Concentrated geographic interest (local politics)

### Landlords and Property Investors

**Small Landlords**

| Characteristic | Data |
|----------------|------|
| Own 1-4 units | 23 million units |
| Individuals as owners | 72% of rental properties |
| Average annual income from rent | $28,000 |
| Primary income source | 22% rely on rental income |

**Institutional Landlords**

| Entity Type | Units Owned | Business Model |
|-------------|-------------|----------------|
| REITs | 500,000+ SFRs | Rental income + appreciation |
| Private equity | 300,000+ SFRs | Value extraction, sale |
| Pension funds | 200,000+ units | Stable returns |
| Insurance companies | 150,000+ units | Long-term income |

**Investor Interests**

| Interest | Position |
|----------|----------|
| Supply restrictions | Support (increases rents) |
| Rent control | Oppose |
| Tenant protections | Oppose |
| Property taxes | Oppose increases |
| Tax preferences | Protect depreciation, 1031 exchanges |

**Political Power**: High

- Concentrated financial resources
- Trade associations (NAA, NMHC)
- Lobbying capacity
- Campaign contributions
- Access to officials

### Real Estate Industry

**Major Actors**

| Actor | Revenue | Employees |
|-------|---------|-----------|
| National Association of Realtors | $1.4 billion | Represents 1.5 million agents |
| National Association of Home Builders | $150 million | Represents 140,000 builders |
| Mortgage Bankers Association | $100 million | Represents lenders |
| Apartment industry groups | $75 million | Represents landlords |

**Industry Interests**

| Interest | Position |
|----------|----------|
| Transaction volume | Want more sales, higher prices |
| Mortgage interest deduction | Strongly support |
| Homeownership promotion | Support (more transactions) |
| Supply increases | Mixed (more supply = lower prices per unit) |
| Regulation | Oppose restrictions on agents, lending |

**Political Power**: Very High

- NAR is top lobbying spender
- Campaign contributions to all parties
- State licensing boards often controlled by industry
- Strong local presence in every community
- Media presence and public relations capacity

### Local Governments

**Fiscal Interests**

| Factor | Local Government Interest |
|--------|---------------------------|
| Property taxes | High-value homes maximize revenue |
| Sales tax | Commercial over residential |
| Service costs | Low-income residents cost more |
| School costs | Families with children most expensive |
| Fiscal zoning | Maximize revenue, minimize costs |

**Political Interests**

| Factor | Interest |
|--------|----------|
| Re-election | Please homeowner voters |
| Campaign donations | Real estate industry major source |
| Conflict avoidance | New housing causes controversy |
| Home values | Officials often homeowners |

**Political Power**: High

- Direct control over land use
- Zoning authority
- Permitting discretion
- Environmental review decisions

## Mixed Interests

### Developers and Builders

**Large Developers**

| Interest | Position |
|----------|----------|
| Building permits | Want easier approval |
| Density allowances | Want higher density |
| Inclusionary requirements | Oppose or negotiate down |
| Labor costs | Want to minimize |
| Impact fees | Oppose |

**Small Builders**

| Interest | Position |
|----------|----------|
| Entry barriers | Want lower barriers |
| Permitting costs | Want reduction |
| Competition with large firms | Want level playing field |
| Workforce availability | Need workers |

**Political Power**: Moderate to High

- Financial resources for lobbying
- Trade associations organized
- But often at odds with homeowners
- Divided between large and small builders
- Interests not always aligned with affordability

### Employers

**Large Employers in High-Cost Areas**

| Interest | Concern |
|----------|---------|
| Workforce recruitment | Can't hire if workers can't afford housing |
| Wage pressure | Must pay more to compensate for housing |
| Worker productivity | Long commutes reduce productivity |
| Business expansion | Housing costs limit growth |

**Employer Actions**

| Company | Housing Initiative |
|---------|---------------------|
| Google | $1 billion for Bay Area housing |
| Meta | $1 billion for housing investments |
| Microsoft | $500 million for Seattle-area housing |
| Kaiser | Housing for healthcare workers |
| School districts | Teacher housing programs |

**Political Power**: High (but rarely mobilized on housing)

- Economic leverage with local government
- But housing not usually priority
- Chamber of Commerce positions often divided
- Individual company advocacy increasing

### Construction Workers

**Interests**

| Interest | Position |
|----------|----------|
| Job availability | Want more construction |
| Wages | Want high wages, which increase costs |
| Working conditions | Support labor protections |
| Housing affordability | Are themselves cost-burdened |

**Political Power**: Moderate

- Union organizing (declining)
- But also need more construction to work
- Cross-pressured on housing policy

## Stakeholder Map

### Power vs. Interest Matrix

| High Interest in Affordability | High Power | Low Power |
|-------------------------------|------------|-----------|
| **Want Change** | Employers (limited), Developers (for supply) | Renters, Would-be buyers, Homeless |
| **Oppose Change** | Homeowners, Local governments, Landlords | Small landlords |

### Coalition Possibilities

**Pro-Housing Coalition**

| Stakeholder | Common Interest |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Renters | Lower costs |
| Would-be buyers | Access to ownership |
| Employers | Workforce access |
| Builders | More projects |
| Unions | Construction jobs |
| Environmental groups | Infill reduces sprawl |

| Stakeholder | Common Interest |
|-------------|-----------------|
