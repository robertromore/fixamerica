# Long-Term Care: Root Causes

## Systemic Analysis

America's long-term care challenges stem from fundamental design choices made in 1965 when Medicare was explicitly limited to acute care and Medicaid became the default payer for institutional LTC. These foundational decisions, combined with market failures in private insurance, workforce economics, and political constraints, have created a system that serves no one well.

## Historical Design Choices

### The 1965 Medicare-Medicaid Split

| Decision | Consequence |
|----------|-------------|
| Medicare limited to acute care | No universal LTC coverage for elderly |
| Medicaid covers nursing homes | LTC tied to poverty status |
| Nursing home entitlement | Institutional bias embedded |
| HCBS excluded | Home care became afterthought |

Why Medicare excluded long-term care:

- **Cost concerns**: LTC would have doubled Medicare costs
- **Insurance model**: LTC doesn't fit acute care insurance model
- **Political calculation**: Focus on hospital and physician coverage
- **Assumptions**: Expected families to continue providing care

### Medicaid's Institutional Bias

| Factor | How It Creates Bias |
|--------|---------------------|
| **Mandatory nursing home coverage** | States must cover; HCBS optional |
| **Open-ended entitlement** | Unlimited federal matching for institutions |
| **HCBS waivers** | Limited, capped, require applications |
| **Provider infrastructure** | Nursing homes already built; HCBS must be created |
| **Spend-down requirements** | Force impoverishment regardless of setting |

## Insurance Market Failure

### Why Private LTC Insurance Collapsed

| Problem | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| **Adverse selection** | Those who expect to need care buy; healthy don't |
| **Information asymmetry** | Buyers know their health better than insurers |
| **Long time horizon** | 30+ years between purchase and claim |
| **Underpricing** | Early policies too cheap; massive losses |
| **Low interest rates** | Investment returns don't cover claims |
| **Premium spirals** | Healthy drop coverage; sick keep it |

### Market Dynamics

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Voluntary market | Only motivated buyers participate |
| No mandate | Can't spread risk across population |
| Pre-existing conditions | Unhealthy excluded or charged more |
| Lapse rates | Many drop coverage before needing it |
| Benefit design | Complex; mismatched with actual needs |

### Insurers' Response

| Year | Carriers Selling | Market Trend |
|------|------------------|--------------|
| 2000 | 100+ | Growing |
| 2010 | ~20 | Declining |
| 2020 | <15 | Minimal |
| 2024 | ~10 | Near-collapse |

Most insurers exited due to unsustainable losses.

## Workforce Economics

### The Low-Wage Trap

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| **Medicaid reimbursement** | Sets floor for what facilities can pay |
| **State rate-setting** | Politically constrained |
| **Competition for workers** | Can't match retail, fast food |
| **Gendered work** | Historically undervalued "women's work" |
| **Racial composition** | 62% workers of color; reflects wage suppression |

### Economic Dynamics

| Issue | Consequence |
|-------|-------------|
| Low wages ($15.77/hr median) | Workers can't afford to stay |
| No benefits | 15% lack health insurance |
| Part-time schedules | No stability; multiple jobs needed |
| No career ladder | No path to higher pay |
| High turnover (65%) | Constant training costs; quality suffers |

### The Staffing-Quality Connection

```text
Low Medicaid rates
    ↓
Low wages for workers
    ↓
High turnover, vacancies
    ↓
Understaffing
    ↓
Poor quality care
    ↓
Increased regulatory burden
    ↓
Higher costs, same low wages
    ↓
(Cycle continues)
```

## Political Economy

### Industry Interests

| Actor | Interest | Political Power |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| **Nursing home chains** | Protect Medicaid rates; resist staffing mandates | State-level lobbying; PACs |
| **Private equity** | Maximize returns; oppose transparency | Financial resources |
| **Home care agencies** | Expand HCBS; protect margins | Growing but less organized |
| **Insurance industry** | Oppose public option | Broad lobbying capacity |
| **Pharmaceutical companies** | Protect SNF drug spending | Major lobbying force |

### Political Constraints

| Constraint | Effect |
|------------|--------|
| **Federalism** | 50 different Medicaid systems |
| **Budget rules** | LTC expansion requires offsets |
| **Electoral politics** | Seniors vote; support status quo |
| **Industry lobbying** | Blocks meaningful reform |
| **Complexity** | Hard to explain; hard to mobilize |

### Who Benefits from Status Quo

| Beneficiary | How |
|-------------|-----|
| Nursing home industry | Guaranteed Medicaid payments |
| Hospital systems | Discharge destination available |
| States | Federal matching without HCBS requirements |
| Upper-middle class | Can afford private pay; Medicaid safety net |
| Some family members | Nursing home as default; avoid caregiving |

## Structural Causes

### Fragmented Financing

| Payer | What It Covers | Limitation |
|-------|----------------|------------|
| Medicare | Short-term skilled nursing | No true LTC |
| Medicaid | Nursing home, some HCBS | Requires impoverishment |
| Private insurance | Whatever policy covers | 7% have policies |
| Out-of-pocket | Everything else | Devastating for middle class |
| Family | Unpaid care | $600B+ value; no support |

No single payer responsible for the whole person across settings.

### Perverse Incentives

| Incentive | Problem |
|-----------|---------|
| **Hospital discharge** | Push patients to SNFs quickly |
| **Nursing home admissions** | More residents = more revenue |
| **Medicaid spend-down** | Divorce, asset transfer to qualify |
| **HCBS caps** | States limit enrollment to control costs |
| **Quality penalties** | Too small to change behavior |

### Information Asymmetry

| Gap | Effect |
|-----|--------|
| **Families don't know options** | Default to nursing homes |
| **Quality hard to assess** | Star ratings manipulable |
| **Costs hidden** | Discover at crisis point |
| **HCBS availability** | Waitlists not publicized |
| **Caregiver burden** | Not discussed until crisis |

## Social and Cultural Factors

### Expectations and Reality

| Expectation | Reality |
|-------------|---------|
| "Medicare covers nursing homes" | Only 100 days post-acute care |
| "Family will take care of me" | Caregivers burn out, get sick |
| "I'll never need a nursing home" | 70% of 65+ will need LTC |
| "I can afford private pay" | $108,000/year depletes savings |
| "Government will help" | Medicaid requires impoverishment |

### Gendered Caregiving

| Factor | Manifestation |
|--------|---------------|
| Women as default caregivers | 61% of caregivers female |
| Career sacrifice | $304,000 average lifetime lost earnings |
| Health impacts | Caregivers report worse health |
| Social expectation | Daughters more than sons |
| Unpaid labor | No compensation for family care |

### Racial Disparities

| Disparity | Mechanism |
|-----------|-----------|
| Lower LTC insurance ownership | Income gaps; marketing |
| Lower HCBS access | Urban-rural; state variation |
| Nursing home quality | Segregation persists |
| Caregiver burden | Higher for communities of color |
| Workforce exploitation | Care workers disproportionately of color |

## Causal Diagram

### Primary Causal Chain

```text
1965 Medicare/Medicaid design
    ↓
Nursing home entitlement; HCBS optional
    ↓
Institutional bias embedded
    ↓
Private LTC insurance attempts (1980s-2000s)
    ↓
Market failure (adverse selection)
    ↓
No universal financing mechanism
    ↓
Medicaid = default for middle class
    ↓
Spend-down; family burden
    ↓
Workforce crisis (low Medicaid rates)
    ↓
Quality problems
    ↓
(Status quo persists)
```

### Secondary Dynamics

| Dynamic | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Political gridlock | Industry opposition + cost concerns |
| State variation | Federalism prevents national standards |
| Crisis-driven policy | Only COVID forced action |
| Incremental approach | Waivers instead of entitlements |
| Workforce neglect | Low pay = low prestige |

### Feedback Loops

| Loop | Effect |
|------|--------|
| **Low wages → turnover → quality problems → more regulation → higher costs → low wages** | Perpetuates workforce crisis |
| **No insurance → no planning → crisis placement → nursing home → Medicaid spend-down** | Perpetuates institutional bias |
| **HCBS waitlists → nursing home placement → HCBS saves less → caps maintained** | Limits rebalancing |
| **Family caregiving → no policy support → caregiver burnout → institutionalization** | Hidden costs to families |

## Why Problems Persist

### Structural Lock-In

| Lock-In | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|

| Medicaid entitlement | Can't take away without replacement |
| Provider interests | Nursing homes resist HCBS shift |
| State systems | 50 different approaches |

| **Competing priorities** | Healthcare, education, climate |

| **Federalism** | States resist federal mandates |
| **Political timing** | Crisis needed for major reform |

### Information Gaps

| Gap | Effect |
|-----|--------|
| Public misunderstanding | Think Medicare covers LTC |
| Policy complexity | Few understand Medicaid LTSS |
| Hidden caregiving | $600B+ invisible in economy |
| Quality opacity | Hard to evaluate facilities |
| Cost uncertainty | Unknown until crisis |

## Comparative Context

Other countries have addressed these challenges:

| Country | Approach | U.S. Difference |
|---------|----------|-----------------|
| Germany | Social insurance (1995) | U.S. relies on Medicaid |
| Japan | Social insurance (2000) | U.S. has no universal program |
| Netherlands | Mandatory insurance | U.S. insurance voluntary, failed |
| Sweden | Tax-funded municipal services | U.S. federal-state fragmentation |
| Denmark | Universal home care | U.S. HCBS optional |

Common elements in successful systems:

- Universal coverage (everyone pays, everyone covered)
- Home care prioritized (not an afterthought)
- Workforce investment (decent wages, benefits)
- Quality oversight (meaningful enforcement)
- Family support (respite, training, payments)
