# Alzheimer's and Dementia Care: Citizen Actions

## What You Can Do

### For Persons Living with Dementia

| Action | Description | Impact |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| **Share your story** | Speak publicly about living with dementia | Reduces stigma, humanizes the issue |
| **Participate in research** | Join clinical trials, research studies | Advances scientific knowledge |
| **Advocate for yourself** | Express preferences, participate in decisions | Maintains agency and dignity |
| **Connect with peers** | Join support groups, peer networks | Mutual support, reduced isolation |
| **Plan early** | Complete advance directives, legal documents | Ensures wishes are honored |
| **Stay engaged** | Continue activities, social connections | Maintains quality of life |

### For Family Caregivers

| Action | Description | How |
|--------|-------------|-----|
| **Seek support** | Don't caregive alone | Contact Alzheimer's Association (1-800-272-3900) |
| **Get training** | Learn dementia care techniques | Take caregiver courses, use online resources |
| **Use respite** | Take breaks from caregiving | Contact Area Agency on Aging for respite services |
| **Plan financially** | Prepare for care costs | Consult elder law attorney, financial advisor |
| **Advocate** | Push for caregiver support policies | Contact legislators, join advocacy organizations |
| **Care for yourself** | Maintain your own health | Regular checkups, mental health support |

### For Healthcare Professionals

| Action | Description | Impact |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| **Get trained** | Improve dementia care competency | Better patient outcomes |
| **Diagnose early** | Don't dismiss cognitive concerns | Enables planning and treatment |
| **Involve families** | Treat the family as unit of care | Better care coordination |
| **Address stigma** | Use person-first language | Reduces shame and isolation |
| **Support research** | Refer to clinical trials | Advances treatment options |
| **Advocate** | Push for better care systems | Systemic improvement |

### For All Citizens

| Action | Description | How |
|--------|-------------|-----|
| **Learn the facts** | Understand dementia | Read resources, attend programs |
| **Reduce stigma** | Challenge misconceptions | Speak up, share accurate information |
| **Support caregivers** | Help those providing care | Offer specific help, check in regularly |
| **Prepare personally** | Plan for your own brain health | Risk reduction, advance planning |
| **Advocate politically** | Support dementia policy | Contact legislators, vote informed |
| **Give financially** | Support research and services | Donate to reputable organizations |

### Sample Advocacy Scripts

**Phone Script for Research Funding**:

> "Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [City]. I'm calling to ask [Representative] to support increased funding for Alzheimer's research at the NIH. Dementia affects millions of American families, including mine. I urge [Representative] to support $6 billion in annual Alzheimer's research funding. Thank you."

**Email Template for Caregiver Support**:

> Subject: Support Family Caregivers of People with Dementia
>
> Dear [Representative]:
>
> As your constituent, I urge you to support policies that help family caregivers of people with dementia. [Share brief personal story if applicable.]
>
> Over 11 million Americans provide unpaid care to people with Alzheimer's and other dementias, contributing $271 billion in unpaid care annually. These caregivers need:
>
> - Expanded respite care access
> - Paid family leave
> - Tax credits for caregiving expenses
> - Training and support services
>
> Please support legislation that helps these dedicated caregivers.
>
> Respectfully,
> [Name]
> [Address]

**Letter Template for State Dementia Plan**:

> Dear [State Legislator]:
>
> I am writing to urge you to support a comprehensive state dementia plan that addresses the needs of the [number] state residents living with Alzheimer's and related dementias.
>
> A strong state plan should include:
>
> - Expanded home and community-based services
> - Caregiver support programs
> - Workforce training requirements
> - Public awareness campaigns
> - Dementia-friendly community initiatives
>
> [State] families need a coordinated response to this growing challenge.
>
> Sincerely,
> [Name]

## Taking Care of Your Brain

### Risk Reduction Actions

| Factor | Actions |
|--------|---------|
| **Cardiovascular health** | Manage blood pressure, diabetes, cholesterol |
| **Physical activity** | Exercise regularly (150+ minutes/week) |
| **Diet** | Follow Mediterranean or MIND diet |
| **Social engagement** | Stay connected with others |
| **Cognitive activity** | Continue learning, challenging activities |
| **Sleep** | Prioritize quality sleep |
| **Hearing** | Get hearing tested and treated |
| **Avoid head injury** | Wear seatbelts, helmets |
| **Limit alcohol** | Drink moderately if at all |
| **Don't smoke** | Quit or never start |

### Personal Planning

| Action | Why | How |
|--------|-----|-----|
| **Complete advance directive** | Ensures wishes honored | Use state-specific forms |
| **Designate healthcare proxy** | Names decision-maker | Legal document |
| **Financial power of attorney** | Protects finances | Work with attorney |
| **Discuss wishes with family** | Reduces burden | Have ongoing conversations |
| **Organize documents** | Simplifies future | Create accessible records |
| **Consider long-term care insurance** | Financial protection | Research options |

### Know the Warning Signs

| Sign | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Memory loss disrupting daily life | Forgetting recently learned information |
| Difficulty planning or problem-solving | Trouble following plans or working with numbers |
| Difficulty completing familiar tasks | Trouble with routine activities |
| Confusion with time or place | Losing track of dates or locations |
| Trouble understanding visual images | Difficulty reading, judging distance |
| New problems with speaking or writing | Trouble following or joining conversation |
| Misplacing things | Putting things in unusual places |
| Decreased or poor judgment | Bad decisions about money, grooming |
| Withdrawal from activities | Avoiding social or work activities |
| Changes in mood and personality | Confusion, suspicion, depression, anxiety |

**If you notice these signs**: Talk to a doctor. Early detection enables planning and treatment.
