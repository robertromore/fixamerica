# Senior Poverty and Economic Security: Current State

## Present Conditions

Approximately 5.8 million Americans aged 65 and older live in poverty using the official federal measure, representing about 10% of the older adult population. This represents substantial progress from the 35% poverty rate in 1959 before Medicare and the major expansion of Social Security. However, the official measure significantly understates economic hardship among seniors. Using the Supplemental Poverty Measure (SPM), which accounts for medical expenses and other factors, the poverty rate rises to approximately 14.1%â€”the highest of any age group. Nearly half of older adults lack income sufficient to meet basic needs according to more comprehensive measures of economic security.

## Poverty Measurement

### Comparing Poverty Measures

| Measure | Rate (65+) | Threshold | Key Features |
|---------|------------|-----------|--------------|
| **Official Poverty Measure (OPM)** | ~10% | $15,060 (single); $19,020 (couple) | Based on 1960s food costs; pre-tax income |
| **Supplemental Poverty Measure (SPM)** | ~14.1% | Varies by geography | Includes medical costs; post-tax income with benefits |
| **Elder Economic Security Standard Index** | ~45% insecure | Varies by location, health, housing | Full cost of basic needs |
| **200% of poverty** | ~32% | Double OPM threshold | Common "near-poor" threshold |

### Why SPM Shows Higher Senior Poverty

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| **Medical out-of-pocket costs** | Subtracted from income; seniors average $6,800/year |
| **Geographic adjustment** | Higher thresholds in expensive areas |
| **Includes in-kind benefits** | SNAP, housing subsidies count as income |
| **Net effect** | +4 percentage points higher than OPM |

## Recent Trends

### Poverty Rate Trends

| Period | Trend |
|--------|-------|
| **1959** | 35% poverty (before Medicare) |
| **1970** | 25% poverty |
| **1980** | 15.7% poverty |
| **2000** | 10.2% poverty |
| **2010** | 9.0% poverty |
| **2019** | 8.9% poverty (pre-pandemic low) |
| **2020-2022** | 10.2% poverty (pandemic effects) |
| **2023** | ~10% poverty |

### Concerning Trends

| Trend | Implication |
|-------|-------------|
| **Declining pensions** | From 38% coverage (1980) to 15% (2020) for private sector |
| **Rising healthcare costs** | Medical inflation exceeding general inflation |
| **Housing cost increases** | Rents rising faster than Social Security COLAs |
| **Inadequate retirement savings** | 40% approaching retirement with no savings |
| **Social Security solvency** | Trust fund depletion projected ~2033 |
| **Reduced employer benefits** | Shift from defined benefit to defined contribution |
| **Longevity increases** | Savings must last longer; more years of potential poverty |

### Positive Trends

| Trend | Impact |
|-------|--------|
| **ACA Medicaid expansion** | Better health coverage for near-poor 60-64 year olds |
| **Medicare drug negotiation** | Will reduce prescription costs for some |
| **COLA improvements** | Better Social Security inflation adjustments |
| **SSI state supplements** | Some states providing additional income |

## Vulnerable Subpopulations

### Highest-Risk Groups

| Group | Poverty Rate | Key Factors |
|-------|--------------|-------------|
| **Unmarried Black women** | 32% | Lifetime wage gaps; caregiving penalties; shorter marriages |
| **Unmarried Hispanic women** | 28% | Immigration status; language barriers; less Social Security |
| **Oldest old (85+)** | 14% | Asset depletion; widowhood; health costs |
| **Rural seniors** | 12% | Lower lifetime earnings; fewer services |
| **Former caregivers** | 16% | Reduced work history; lower Social Security |
| **Disabled before 65** | 25% | Unable to accumulate savings |

### Hidden Hardship

| Hardship Type | Prevalence |
|---------------|------------|
| **Skipping medications** | 12% of Medicare beneficiaries |
| **Food/medicine tradeoffs** | 8% |
| **Utility shutoffs/threat** | 5% |
| **Delaying medical care** | 11% |
| **Housing instability** | 3% |
