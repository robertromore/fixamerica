# Agriculture Policy: History

## Overview

American agricultural policy has evolved from land distribution policies in the 18th century through New Deal intervention, Cold War export expansion, and modern risk management systems. Understanding this history reveals how today's farm programs, market structures, and environmental challenges emerged from deliberate policy choices and political compromises.

---

## Founding Era and Westward Expansion (1785-1862)

### Land Ordinances

**Northwest Ordinance (1785)**

- Established survey system for public lands
- Grid pattern of townships and sections
- Foundation for orderly settlement
- Set precedent for federal land management

**Early Land Policy**

| Policy | Year | Approach |
|--------|------|----------|
| Cash sales | 1785-1820 | Minimum acreage, auction system |
| Credit system | 1800 | Allowed installment purchases |
| Preemption Act | 1841 | Recognized squatter rights |
| Graduation Act | 1854 | Reduced prices on unsold lands |

### Homestead Era Begins

**Homestead Act (1862)**

- Free 160-acre grants to settlers
- Five-year residence and cultivation requirement
- Opened Great Plains to settlement
- Benefited primarily white male settlers
- Native American land dispossession accelerated

**Concurrent Policies:**

- Morrill Land-Grant Act (1862): Established agricultural colleges
- USDA established (1862): "People's Department"
- Transcontinental Railroad land grants: Opened western markets

## Gilded Age and Progressive Era (1862-1933)

### Agricultural Expansion

**Scale of Settlement:**

| Decade | Homestead Claims | Acres Distributed |
|--------|------------------|-------------------|
| 1870s | 340,000 | 40 million |
| 1880s | 650,000 | 75 million |
| 1890s | 260,000 | 30 million |
| 1900s | 370,000 | 45 million |

### Agrarian Political Movement

**Grange Movement (1867-1890s)**

- Farmers' cooperative organization
- Fought railroad monopolies
- Pushed for state regulation
- Granger Laws regulated grain elevators, railroads

**Populist Party (1890s)**

- Agricultural credit reform demands
- Free silver monetary policy
- Graduated income tax
- Railroad nationalization
- Farmers' alliances foundation

### Early Federal Involvement

**Key Developments:**

| Year | Development | Significance |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| 1862 | USDA created | Federal agricultural agency |
| 1887 | Hatch Act | Agricultural experiment stations |
| 1889 | USDA becomes Cabinet department | Elevated status |
| 1906 | Pure Food and Drug Act | Consumer protection |
| 1914 | Smith-Lever Act | Extension service created |

### World War I Era

**Wartime Production:**

- "Food Will Win the War" campaigns
- Massive production expansion
- High prices encouraged debt
- Land speculation boom
- European markets opened

**Post-War Collapse (1920s):**

- European production recovered
- Commodity prices crashed
- Farm debt crisis began
- 500,000 farm bankruptcies (1920s)
- Rural-urban divide widened

## New Deal Era (1933-1945)

### Agricultural Adjustment Act (1933)

**Key Provisions:**

| Component | Mechanism | Goal |
|-----------|-----------|------|
| Acreage allotments | Paid reductions | Reduce surplus |
| Price supports | Government purchases | Stabilize income |
| Marketing agreements | Industry coordination | Orderly markets |
| Processing taxes | Fund payments | Finance program |

**Initial Results:**

- 10 million acres cotton destroyed
- 6 million pigs slaughtered
- Prices increased 50%+
- Constitutional challenge (struck down 1936)

### Agricultural Adjustment Act (1938)

**Permanent Framework:**

- Replaced unconstitutional 1933 provisions
- Soil conservation payments (constitutional basis)
- Ever-normal granary concept
- Commodity loan programs
- Parity price target (pre-WWI ratio)

### Complementary New Deal Programs

| Program | Year | Purpose |
|---------|------|---------|
| Farm Credit Administration | 1933 | Agricultural lending |
| Resettlement Administration | 1935 | Help displaced farmers |
| Soil Conservation Service | 1935 | Erosion control |
| Rural Electrification Administration | 1935 | Farm electricity |
| Commodity Credit Corporation | 1933 | Price stabilization |
| Federal Crop Insurance Corporation | 1938 | Risk management |

### Labor and Race

**Exclusions:**

- Agricultural Adjustment Act excluded sharecroppers from benefits
- Southern Democrats protected plantation system
- National Labor Relations Act (1935) excluded farmworkers
- Fair Labor Standards Act (1938) excluded farmworkers
- Social Security Act (1935) initially excluded farmworkers
- Black farmers systematically denied USDA loans and programs

### World War II

**Production Response:**

- "Victory Gardens" and rationing
- Farm labor shortages
- Bracero Program (1942): Mexican guest workers
- Women entered agricultural workforce
- Mechanization accelerated
- Price controls with inflation

---

## Post-War Abundance (1945-1970)

### Surplus Management

**The Problem of Plenty:**

- Wartime production capacity exceeded peacetime demand
- Technology (tractors, fertilizers, hybrid seeds) increased yields
- Fixed costs encouraged overproduction
- Government stockpiles grew massive

**Policy Responses:**

| Program | Year | Mechanism |
|---------|------|-----------|
| School lunch program expansion | 1946 | Surplus distribution |
| PL-480 Food for Peace | 1954 | Export subsidies |
| Soil Bank | 1956 | Land retirement |
| Food stamps pilot | 1961 | Domestic consumption |

### Agricultural Act of 1954

- Introduced flexible price supports
- Allowed market adjustment
- Political compromise between high/low support advocates
- Set pattern for decades of farm bill debates

### Farm Consolidation Begins

| Year | Number of Farms | Average Size |
|------|-----------------|--------------|
| 1940 | 6.1 million | 175 acres |
| 1950 | 5.6 million | 216 acres |
| 1960 | 3.7 million | 303 acres |
| 1970 | 2.9 million | 374 acres |

**Drivers:**

- Mechanization replaced labor
- Chemical inputs increased yields
- "Get big or get out" economics
- Rural-to-urban migration
- African American exodus from Southern agriculture

### Civil Rights Era

**USDA Discrimination:**

- Systematic denial of loans to Black farmers
- Extension services segregated
- Program payments to white landlords, not Black tenants
- *Pigford v. Glickman* (1999) documented decades of discrimination
- 90%+ of Black-owned farmland lost (1910-1997)

---

## Market Orientation Era (1970-1996)

### Agricultural Act of 1970

- Target prices and deficiency payments
- Tied payments to production
- Set-aside acreage requirements
- Reduced government storage

### Export Boom (1970s)

**Key Events:**

| Year | Event | Impact |
|------|-------|--------|
| 1971 | Nixon ends gold standard | Dollar devaluation boosts exports |
| 1972 | Soviet grain purchases | Record sales, price surge |
| 1973 | Global food crisis | High prices, export expansion |
| 1973 | Agriculture Act | Market-oriented reforms |

**"Fence Row to Fence Row" Planting:**

- Secretary Butz encouraged maximum production
- Acreage restrictions relaxed
- Land prices soared
- Farm debt expanded dramatically
- Set stage for 1980s crisis

### 1980s Farm Crisis

**Causes:**

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Federal Reserve rate hikes | Interest costs soared |
| Strong dollar | Exports collapsed |
| Soviet grain embargo (1980) | Lost markets |
| Land value collapse | Equity destroyed |
| Debt levels | Record defaults |

**Consequences:**

- Farm bankruptcies highest since 1930s
- 300,000 farm families forced out
- Rural bank failures
- Agricultural Credit Act (1987) bailout
- Suicide and mental health crisis

### Food Security Act (1985)

**Reforms:**

- Conservation Reserve Program (CRP) created
- Sodbuster/Swampbuster provisions
- Linked conservation compliance to program benefits
- Export Enhancement Program
- Dairy herd buyout

### Agricultural Trade and Development Act (1990)

- Continued payment limits debates
- Flexibility in planting decisions
- Environmental compliance strengthened
- Trade focus intensified

---

## Freedom to Farm and Beyond (1996-2014)

### Federal Agriculture Improvement and Reform Act (1996)

**"Freedom to Farm" Provisions:**

| Change | Old System | New System |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| Payments | Tied to production | Fixed, declining ("AMTA") |
| Planting | Restricted by base acres | Full flexibility |
| Price supports | Target prices | Eliminated |
| Conservation | Compliance required | Compliance required |

**Expectations vs. Reality:**

- Predicted transition to full market orientation
- Asian financial crisis (1997) collapsed exports
- Commodity prices crashed
- Emergency payments enacted every year (1998-2001)
- Total government payments higher than before reform

### Farm Security and Rural Investment Act (2002)

**Retreat from Market Orientation:**

- Counter-cyclical payments restored
- Target prices reinstated
- Conservation programs expanded
- Payment limits raised
- Fixed payments continued

### Food, Conservation, and Energy Act (2008)

**Key Provisions:**

| Title | Major Provisions |
|-------|-----------------|
| Commodities | ACRE revenue program option |
| Conservation | CSP created; CRP maintained |
| Trade | Export programs continued |
| Nutrition | SNAP benefits increased |
| Energy | Biofuel mandates and credits |
| Credit | Loan limits adjusted |

**Financial Crisis Response:**

- Farm programs provided stability during 2008 recession
- Commodity prices initially spiked (2008)
- Land values continued rising
- Farm debt manageable relative to assets

## Modern Era (2014-Present)

### Agricultural Act of 2014

**Major Changes:**

| Old Program | New Program | Mechanism |
|-------------|-------------|-----------|
| Direct payments | Eliminated | Budget savings |
| ACRE | Price Loss Coverage (PLC) | Price support |
| ACRE | Agricultural Risk Coverage (ARC) | Revenue support |
| SURE disaster | Supplemental coverage option | Enhanced insurance |

**Crop Insurance Expansion:**

- Premium subsidies increased
- Shallow loss coverage added
- Private delivery, public subsidy model entrenched
- Spending shifted from payments to insurance

### Agriculture Improvement Act (2018)

**Key Provisions:**

| Area | Change |
|------|--------|
| Hemp | Legalized production |
| Conservation | CRP cap reduced |
| SNAP | Work requirements debated (not enacted) |
| Dairy | Margin protection program reformed |
| Trade | Export programs maintained |
| Research | Increased funding |

### Recent Developments (2019-2025)

**Trade War (2019-2020):**

- China retaliatory tariffs
- Market Facilitation Program: $28 billion bailout
- Largest farm payments in history
- Long-term market loss concerns

**COVID-19 (2020-2021):**

- Processing plant shutdowns
- Supply chain disruptions
- CFAP payments: $46 billion
- Food insecurity spike
- Revealed market concentration vulnerabilities

**Inflation Reduction Act (2022):**

- $20 billion for conservation programs
- Climate-smart agriculture focus
- Debt relief for socially disadvantaged farmers
- Supply chain investments

**Current Farm Bill Debates (2023-2025):**

- Reference price increases
- Climate provisions contested
- SNAP expansion vs. restrictions
- Conservation funding levels
- Crop insurance reform

## Historical Patterns

### Recurring Themes

| Pattern | Historical Examples |
|---------|-------------------|
| Crisis-driven reform | 1930s depression, 1980s farm crisis, 2020 pandemic |
| Bipartisan coalitions | Farm-nutrition alliance, rural-urban compromise |
| Unintended consequences | Freedom to Farm led to higher payments |
| Concentration acceleration | Every era sees fewer, larger farms |
| Environmental externalities | Erosion, runoff, monoculture |
| Labor exploitation | Slavery, sharecropping, guest workers |

### What Has Changed

1. **Scale**: Average farm 10x larger than 1940
2. **Technology**: Mechanization, chemicals, genetics, data
3. **Markets**: Global integration, commodity speculation
4. **Environment**: Conservation requirements, climate focus
5. **Nutrition**: SNAP largest program in farm bill

### What Hasn't Changed

1. **Consolidation pressure**: Economies of scale persist
2. **Political geography**: Rural states overrepresented
3. **Commodity focus**: Row crops dominate programs
4. **Labor exclusions**: Farmworkers lack federal protections
5. **Boom-bust cycles**: Prices remain volatile

## Lessons from History

### Successful Reforms

| Reform | Why It Worked |
|--------|---------------|
| Conservation compliance | Tied benefits to behavior |
| Crop insurance expansion | Addressed real farmer need |
| SNAP | Built urban-rural coalition |
| Rural electrification | Transformative infrastructure |

### Failed or Abandoned Reforms

| Reform | Why It Failed |
|--------|---------------|
| Freedom to Farm | Crisis payments restored |
| Payment limits | Circumvented through entities |
| Supply management | Politically unsustainable |
| Direct farmer payments | Budget pressure eliminated |

### Reform Windows

Major agricultural policy changes have historically occurred:

- After economic crises affecting farmers
- When unusual political coalitions form
- When public attention focuses on agriculture
- When budget pressures force choices
