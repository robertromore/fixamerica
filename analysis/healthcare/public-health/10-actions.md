# Public Health: Citizen Actions

## What You Can Do

### Individual Actions

- **Get vaccinated**: Protect yourself and your community through immunization
- **Follow public health guidance**: Especially during outbreaks and emergencies
- **Know your health department**: Understand what services are available locally
- **Report health concerns**: Notify health departments of disease outbreaks or environmental hazards
- **Support contact tracing**: Cooperate when contacted about disease exposure
- **Practice prevention**: Hand hygiene, food safety, injury prevention
- **Share accurate information**: Combat health misinformation in your networks
- **Advocate for public health**: Support funding and programs in public communications

### Community Actions

- **Attend health board meetings**: Local boards of health make important decisions
- **Support community health events**: Vaccination clinics, health fairs, screening events
- **Volunteer**: Many public health programs need volunteers
- **Advocate for local investment**: Speak at county commissioner and city council meetings
- **Support safety net services**: Community health centers, free clinics
- **Community health worker roles**: Become trained as community health worker
- **Share information**: Help distribute accurate health information to your networks

### Political Actions

- **Contact elected officials**: Advocate for public health funding at federal, state, local levels
- **Testify at hearings**: Share perspectives on public health needs
- **Support public health candidates**: Vote for officials who prioritize public health
- **Oppose funding cuts**: Resist efforts to reduce public health budgets
- **Support public health authority**: Oppose efforts to limit public health powers
- **Join advocacy campaigns**: Participate in organized public health advocacy

## Staying Informed

### Key Dates and Processes

- **Federal budget cycle**: October - September (appropriations affect CDC, HRSA)
- **State budget cycles**: Vary by state (affect state/local health departments)
- **Public health week**: First week of April (awareness opportunity)
- **Flu vaccination season**: September - March (annual opportunity)
- **Open enrollment**: Health insurance enrollment affects coverage

### Information Sources

- **CDC.gov**: [cdc.gov](https://www.cdc.gov) - Official federal guidance
- **State health department**: Your state's public health information
- **Local health department**: Community-specific information
- **KFF**: [kff.org](https://www.kff.org) - Policy analysis
- **STAT News**: [statnews.com](https://www.statnews.com) - Health journalism

### Warning Signs to Monitor

- **Funding cuts**: Budget proposals reducing CDC or state/local funding
- **Authority limitations**: Legislation restricting public health powers
- **Workforce losses**: Health department layoffs or hiring freezes
- **Vaccination declines**: Dropping immunization rates
- **Disease outbreaks**: Local or regional disease increases
- **Political interference**: Scientific guidance being overridden

**What works**:

- Personal stories about public health impact
- Economic data (ROI of prevention)
- Local health department needs
- Comparison to other states/countries
- Specific funding requests

5. "Public health workers deserve competitive compensation"

### Building Coalitions

**Natural allies**:

- Healthcare providers and hospitals
- Business community (pandemic preparedness)
- Parent organizations (child health)
- Environmental organizations
- Senior advocacy groups
- Faith communities

### Media Engagement

- Write letters to the editor on public health issues
- Share factual public health information on social media
- Counter health misinformation when you see it
- Support local health journalism
- Participate in public health awareness campaigns

## Special Circumstances

### During Outbreaks

- Follow official guidance
- Get tested/vaccinated as appropriate
- Cooperate with contact tracing
- Avoid spreading misinformation
- Support vulnerable community members

### If Your Health Department Is at Risk

- Attend public meetings
- Organize community support
- Contact elected officials
- Engage media
- Document impact of potential cuts

### If You Work in Healthcare

- Report diseases as required
- Support public health partnerships
- Advocate for prevention focus
- Share public health messaging with patients

### If You're a Student

- Consider public health career
- Public health courses available at many institutions
- CDC and state health departments offer internships
- Epidemic Intelligence Service for medical/doctoral graduates
