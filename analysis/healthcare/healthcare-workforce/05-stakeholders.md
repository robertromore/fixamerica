# Healthcare Workforce: Stakeholders

## Stakeholder Landscape

Healthcare workforce policy involves a complex array of stakeholders with overlapping and sometimes conflicting interests. Understanding these interests is essential for building coalitions and anticipating opposition.

## Those Most Affected

### Patients in Underserved Areas

**Impact**: Direct harm from workforce shortages
- Delayed diagnoses and treatment
- Long travel distances for care
- Reduced preventive care access
- Higher mortality rates in shortage areas
- Limited specialist availability

**Voice**: Generally unorganized and politically weak; represented indirectly by advocacy groups and community health centers.

### Healthcare Workers Experiencing Burnout

**Impact**: Unsustainable working conditions
- Excessive patient loads
- Administrative burden
- Inadequate support staff
- Mental health challenges
- Work-life imbalance

**Voice**: Professional associations increasingly advocate for wellness; unions represent some workers; individual exit from profession is primary response.

### Medical Students and Residents

**Impact**: Training pipeline constraints and debt burden
- Competition for limited residency slots
- Unmatched graduates face uncertain futures
- Debt influences specialty and location choices
- Long training hours and low resident pay
- Career flexibility limited by training structure

**Voice**: Student organizations (e.g., AMSA) advocate for debt relief and residency expansion; limited political power.

### Rural and Underserved Communities

**Impact**: Healthcare deserts and facility closures
- Hospital closures eliminate local care
- Emergency response times increase
- Economic impact of healthcare job loss
- Population health declines
- Outmigration accelerates

**Voice**: Rural health associations, National Rural Health Association, state rural health offices; politically underrepresented despite geographic spread.

## Healthcare Professionals and Organizations

### Physicians and Medical Societies

**American Medical Association (AMA)**:
- Historically controlled physician supply
- Now supports GME cap increase
- Opposes expanded scope of practice for NPs/PAs
- Advocates for administrative burden reduction
- Represents ~15% of physicians; influence exceeds membership

**Specialty Societies**:
- Protect specialty income and market share
- Influence RUC payment recommendations
- Generally oppose primary care prioritization in GME
- Support residency expansion in their specialties

**Primary Care Societies (AAFP, ACP, AAP)**:
- Advocate for primary care payment increases
- Support GME redistribution toward primary care
- Often at odds with procedural specialty societies
- Less political influence than specialty societies

### Nurses and Nursing Organizations

**American Nurses Association (ANA)**:
- Represents RN interests
- Advocates for safe staffing ratios
- Supports nursing education funding
- Addresses workplace violence and burnout

**American Association of Nurse Practitioners (AANP)**:
- Advocates for full practice authority in all states
- Opposes physician supervision requirements
- Promotes NP role in addressing shortages
- Active at state legislatures

**Nursing Unions**:
- National Nurses United (largest RN union)
- Advocate for staffing ratios
- Strong on workplace safety
- Support single-payer as systemic solution

### Advanced Practice Providers

**Physician Assistants (AAPA)**:
- Advocate for "Optimal Team Practice" (reduced supervision)
- Support scope of practice expansion
- Generally allied with NP advocacy efforts

**Other Advanced Practice**:
- Pharmacists seeking prescribing authority
- Psychologists seeking prescribing privileges
- Physical therapists seeking direct access

### International Medical Graduates

**Educational Commission for Foreign Medical Graduates (ECFMG)**:
- Certifies IMGs for U.S. practice
- Administers USMLE examinations
- Gatekeeping role in IMG pipeline

**IMG Advocacy**:
- Less organized than U.S. graduate groups
- Often represented by hospital associations that employ IMGs
- Visa reform advocacy through immigration organizations

## Educational Institutions

### Medical Schools

**Association of American Medical Colleges (AAMC)**:
- Primary advocate for GME cap increase
- Publishes workforce projections
- Accreditation influence
- Data collection on medical education

**Osteopathic Schools (AACOM)**:
- Fastest-growing medical school sector
- Often more primary care-oriented
- Rural training emphasis in many programs
- Advocate for GME parity

**For-Profit and Caribbean Schools**:
- Significant producers of U.S.-citizen physicians
- Lower match rates raise quality concerns
- Students face debt with uncertain outcomes

### Nursing Schools

**American Association of Colleges of Nursing (AACN)**:
- Advocates for nursing education funding
- Promotes BSN as entry-level degree
- Addresses faculty shortage
- Curricular standard-setting

**Community College Programs**:
- Train majority of RNs through ADN programs
- More accessible to diverse and working-class students
- Bridge programs to BSN expanding

### Residency Programs and Teaching Hospitals

**Association of American Medical Colleges (AAMC)**:
- Represents teaching hospitals
- Strong advocate for GME funding increase
- Lobbying presence in Washington

**Teaching Health Centers**:
- Community-based residency programs
- Primary care and rural focus
- Separate funding stream (THCGME)
- Chronically underfunded

## Government Stakeholders

### Federal Agencies

**Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS)**:
- Administers GME payments
- Sets payment policies affecting workforce
- Telehealth regulations
- Quality reporting requirements

**Health Resources and Services Administration (HRSA)**:
- Operates National Health Service Corps
- Designates HPSAs
- Loan repayment programs
- Primary care training grants

**Bureau of Health Workforce**:
- Workforce projections and data
- Training grant administration
- Shortage area designation

### Congress

**Relevant Committees**:
- Senate HELP Committee
- Senate Finance Committee (Medicare)
- House Energy and Commerce Committee
- House Ways and Means Committee (Medicare)

**Key Congressional Interests**:
- Budget implications of GME expansion
- Rural hospital access (bipartisan concern)
- Immigration policy (partisan divide)
- Scope of practice (state vs. federal tension)

### State Governments

**State Health Workforce Centers**:
- Data collection and planning
- State-specific projections
- Coordination with educational institutions

**State Licensing Boards**:
- Physician and nursing licensure
- Scope of practice enforcement
- Disciplinary authority
- Often physician-dominated

**State Legislatures**:
- Scope of practice legislation
- State loan repayment programs
- Medicaid policy (affects rural economics)

## Industry Stakeholders

### Hospital Systems

**American Hospital Association (AHA)**:
- Supports GME cap increase (hospitals receive GME funding)
- Addresses nursing shortage through staffing advocacy
- Opposes staffing ratio mandates
- Concerned about rural hospital viability

**Hospital Employers**:
- Increasingly employ physicians (74%+ now employed)
- Control practice conditions
- Set compensation levels
- Influence on work environment and burnout

### Insurance Industry

**Health Insurers**:
- Network adequacy requirements create demand
- Generally support workforce expansion (more providers = more network options)
- Prior authorization requirements contribute to burnout
- Telehealth policies affect access

### Staffing Industry

**Travel Nursing Agencies**:
- Profit from nursing shortage
- Grew dramatically during pandemic
- Create financial stress for hospitals
- Mixed incentives on permanent workforce

**Locum Tenens Agencies**:
- Fill temporary physician gaps
- Enable flexibility but at high cost
- Benefit from licensing fragmentation

### Pharmaceutical and Device Industries

**Pharmaceutical Companies**:
- Train detailers who interact with providers
- Influence continuing education
- Limited direct stake in workforce numbers

## Coalition Opportunities

**Broad Coalition for GME Expansion**:
- Teaching hospitals, medical schools, nursing organizations, rural advocates, patient groups all support
- Budget concerns are primary obstacle, not stakeholder opposition

**Scope of Practice Coalition**:
- NPs, PAs, rural advocates, FQHCs, insurers
- Opposed by physician organizations

**Student Debt Relief Coalition**:
- Medical and nursing students, schools, rural advocates
- Cross-cuts other healthcare and education coalitions
