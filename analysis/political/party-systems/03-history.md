# Party Systems: History

## Origins

### The Founding Era

**Founders' Views on Parties**

| Founder | Position |
|---------|----------|
| Washington | Warned against "factions" in Farewell Address |
| Madison | Federalist 10 on managing factions |
| Jefferson | Initially anti-party, then founded one |
| Hamilton | Organized first party system |

**Constitutional Silence**

- Constitution doesn't mention parties
- Electoral College assumed non-partisan selection
- Founders expected notable individuals, not party tickets
- System quickly evolved beyond founders' vision

### First Party System (1790s-1820s)

**Federalists vs. Democratic-Republicans**

| Party | Leaders | Base |
|-------|---------|------|
| Federalists | Hamilton, Adams | Commercial, urban, elite |
| Democratic-Republicans | Jefferson, Madison | Agrarian, rural, democratic |

**Key Features**

- Emerged from policy disputes
- Elite-driven organization
- Limited mass participation
- Ended with Federalist collapse

### Second Party System (1830s-1850s)

**Democrats vs. Whigs**

| Party | Leaders | Coalition |
|-------|---------|-----------|
| Democrats | Jackson | Western farmers, urban workers |
| Whigs | Clay, Webster | Commercial interests, reformers |

**Key Developments**

- Mass party organization
- Party conventions
- Voter mobilization
- Patronage systems
- Ended with slavery crisis

## Key Turning Points

### Third Party System (1850s-1890s)

**Republicans vs. Democrats**

| Party | Base | Region |
|-------|------|--------|
| Republicans | Anti-slavery, industry | North |
| Democrats | States' rights, agriculture | South |

**Key Developments**

- Civil War realignment
- Reconstruction politics
- Industrial era patronage
- Machine politics
- Solid South emerges

### Fourth Party System (1890s-1930s)

**Progressive Era Changes**

| Reform | Effect on Parties |
|--------|-------------------|
| Primary elections | Reduced machine control |
| Secret ballot | Reduced coercion |
| Civil service reform | Limited patronage |
| Direct election of senators | Reduced state party control |

**Key Features**

- Republican dominance
- Progressive movements in both parties
- Business-party alignment
- Immigration and industrialization reshape coalitions

### Fifth Party System (1930s-1960s)

**New Deal Realignment**

| Party | Coalition |
|-------|-----------|
| Democrats | Labor, urban, ethnic, South |
| Republicans | Business, rural, suburban |

**Key Features**

- FDR coalition dominance
- Democratic congressional control (40 years)
- Ideological diversity within parties
- Bipartisan coalitions on many issues
- Conservative coalition (Southern Dems + GOP)

### Sixth Party System (1960s-Present?)

**Civil Rights Realignment**

| Development | Effect |
|-------------|--------|
| Civil Rights Act (1964) | Democrats lose South |
| Southern Strategy | GOP gains South |
| Urban/rural divide | Geographic sorting |
| Culture wars | New cleavages |

## Timeline

### 1970s-1980s: Seeds of Polarization

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 1972 | McGovern reforms democratize nominations |
| 1974 | Post-Watergate Democratic wave |
| 1978 | Gingrich enters Congress |
| 1980 | Reagan revolution |
| 1987 | Fairness Doctrine eliminated |

### 1990s: Partisan Warfare Begins

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 1992 | Ross Perot gets 19% as independent |
| 1994 | Gingrich revolution, Contract with America |
| 1995 | Government shutdowns |
| 1998 | Clinton impeachment |

**Key Changes**

- Partisan media emerges (Fox News, talk radio)
- Ideological sorting accelerates
- Bipartisan cooperation declines
- Primary challenges shape behavior

### 2000s: Acceleration

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 2000 | Bush v. Gore, 537 votes decide |
| 2004 | "Red state/blue state" enters lexicon |
| 2008 | Obama coalition |
| 2009 | Tea Party emerges |

**Key Trends**

- Geographic polarization
- Media polarization
- Primary radicalization
- Negative partisanship rises

## Lessons from the Past

### What Enables Third Parties

**Historical Success Factors**

| Factor | Example |
|--------|---------|
| Major party failure | Whig collapse → Republicans |
| Regional concentration | Populists in agrarian areas |
| Charismatic leader | Ross Perot, Teddy Roosevelt |
| Single issue | Prohibition Party, Free Soil |

### Why Third Parties Fail

**Historical Patterns**

| Pattern | Example |
|---------|---------|
| Co-optation | Major party adopts platform |
| Spoiler effect | Nader 2000, Perot 1992 |
| Ballot access | Ongoing barrier |
| Winner-take-all | Structural exclusion |

### Party System Changes

**How Realignments Happen**

| Condition | Historical Example |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Major crisis | Depression → New Deal |
| New issues | Slavery → Republicans |
| Demographic change | Immigration waves |
| Institutional change | Primary reforms |

### Lessons for Reform

**Historical Insights**

| Insight | Application |
|---------|-------------|
| Rules shape parties | Electoral system matters |
| Parties adapt | Will resist change |
| Change takes time | Generational process |
| Crisis creates opportunity | Windows for reform |
