# Executive Reform: Stakeholders

## Affected Populations

### All Americans

Executive power affects every citizen:

- Executive orders shape immigration, environment, labor, and civil rights policy
- Emergency declarations affect economic and civil liberties
- War powers determine who fights and dies in conflicts
- Pardon decisions affect justice and accountability
- Presidential misconduct undermines democratic legitimacy

### Military Personnel and Families

Those most directly affected by commander-in-chief authority (2024 snapshot):

- 1.3 million active duty service members
- 760,000 Selected Reserve forces
- Approximately 2.6 million military family members
- Decisions about deployment, engagement rules, and casualties

*Source: Department of Defense Demographics Profile (FY 2023).*

### Federal Employees

2.95 million civilian federal workers (2024 snapshot) affected by:

*Source: Office of Personnel Management (OPM) Federal Workforce Statistics.*

- Executive orders on employment policies
- Agency reorganizations
- Hiring freezes
- Schedule F and politicization threats
- Whistleblower protections (or lack thereof)

### Regulatory Subjects

Businesses and individuals subject to agency action:

- Environmental regulations
- Financial rules
- Workplace safety standards
- Consumer protections
- Immigration enforcement

### Marginalized Communities

Groups historically targeted by executive overreach:

- Japanese Americans (internment)
- Muslim Americans (travel ban, surveillance)
- Immigrants (detention, deportation)
- Civil rights activists (surveillance)
- Journalists and whistleblowers

## Power Holders

### The President and White House

**Interest**: Maximum flexibility and authority

**Resources**:

- Constitutional powers and claims
- Bully pulpit and media attention
- Party loyalty
- Appointment power
- Classification authority
- Executive privilege claims

**Position on Reform**: Generally oppose constraints; support reforms that expand power

### Executive Branch Agencies

**Interest**: Varies by agency—some value independence, others presidential direction

**Resources**:

- Expertise and information
- Implementation authority
- Regulatory power
- Congressional relationships

**Position on Reform**: Mixed; career officials often support independence protections

### Congressional Leadership

**Interest**: Institutional authority vs. partisan loyalty

**Resources**:

- Legislation and appropriations
- Oversight and subpoena power
- Confirmation authority
- Impeachment power

**Position on Reform**: Support constraints on opposite party presidents; protect own party

### Judiciary

**Interest**: Maintaining judicial authority and legitimacy

**Resources**:

- Judicial review
- Constitutional interpretation
- Contempt powers
- Life tenure independence

**Position on Reform**: Generally support justiciability but defer on national security

### Political Parties

**Interest**: Winning elections and implementing agenda

**Party in Power**: Opposes constraints on current president
**Party out of Power**: Supports constraints but reverses when they gain power

### National Security Establishment

**Interest**: Operational flexibility and secrecy

**Actors**:

- Department of Defense
- Intelligence community (17 agencies)
- National Security Council
- Defense contractors

**Position on Reform**: Generally oppose constraints on commander-in-chief and intelligence authorities

## Reform Skeptics and Opponents

### Unitary Executive Advocates

**Argument**: Constitution vests all executive power in president; constraints are unconstitutional

**Organizations**:

- Federalist Society (influential conservative legal network)
- Heritage Foundation
- Claremont Institute

**Key Figures**:

- Steven Calabresi (Northwestern)
- John Yoo (Berkeley)
- Former OLC officials

### National Security Hawks

**Argument**: Executive flexibility essential for security; constraints endanger nation

**Concerns**:

- War powers reform would hamstring military
- Intelligence oversight compromises sources and methods
- Emergency power limits prevent crisis response

### Presidential Loyalists

**Argument**: Reform efforts are partisan attacks on legitimate authority

**Position**:

- Constraints appropriate for other party's president
- Own party's president should have broad authority
- Reform efforts are power grabs by political opponents

## Institutional Stakeholders

### Inspectors General

**Interest**: Independence and authority to investigate

**Resources**:

- Statutory investigation authority
- Access to agency information
- Reporting to Congress

**Vulnerabilities**:

- Presidential appointment and removal
- Budget dependent on agency
- No enforcement power

**Position**: Support independence protections

### Government Accountability Office

**Interest**: Audit authority and access

**Resources**:

- Congressional agency with independence
- Broad audit authority
- Public reporting

**Position**: Support transparency and accountability reforms

### Office of Government Ethics

**Interest**: Ethics enforcement authority

**Resources**:

- Limited—advisory role, not enforcement
- Financial disclosure review

**Position**: Support strengthening of enforcement powers

### Congressional Support Agencies

**Congressional Research Service**: Provides analysis supporting oversight
**Congressional Budget Office**: Independent analysis of executive proposals

## State and Local Officials

### Governors

**Interest**: Federal-state balance; may support federal executive constraints to protect state authority

**Concerns**:

- Executive orders that preempt state law
- Emergency powers affecting states
- Federal enforcement priorities

### State Attorneys General

**Interest**: Ability to challenge federal executive action

**Resources**:

- Standing to sue in many cases
- Coalition litigation
- State enforcement authority

**Position**: Opposition party AGs actively challenge executive overreach

## International Stakeholders

### Allied Governments

**Interest**: Predictability in U.S. foreign policy

**Concerns**:

- Executive agreements that can be revoked by successors
- Unilateral military action
- Trade policy by executive action

### International Organizations

**Interest**: U.S. compliance with international commitments

**Concerns**:

- Executive withdrawal from treaties and agreements
- Unilateral action bypassing international institutions
