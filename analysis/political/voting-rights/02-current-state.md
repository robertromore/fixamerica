# Voting Rights: Current State

## The Problem Today

### The Post-Shelby Landscape

Since the Supreme Court's 2013 decision in *Shelby County v. Holder* gutted the Voting Rights Act's preclearance requirement, states have enacted hundreds of new voting restrictions. What was once subject to federal review before implementation now takes effect immediately, requiring litigation after the factâ€”often after elections have already been affected.

**The Wave of Restrictions**
Following Shelby County:

- Texas implemented strict photo ID within hours of the ruling
- North Carolina passed sweeping voting restrictions within months
- States previously covered by preclearance moved quickly
- Previously blocked measures took immediate effect
- New restrictions continue to be enacted each legislative session

**Current State of Play**

| Category | Trend |
|----------|-------|
| Voter ID laws | Expanding and becoming stricter |
| Early voting | Reduced in many states |
| Voter roll purges | More aggressive |
| Polling place closures | Continuing, especially in minority areas |
| Mail voting access | Restricted in some states, expanded in others |
| Same-day registration | Expanding in some states |
| Automatic registration | Growing adoption |

## Impact Analysis

### On Turnout

**Turnout Disparities**

| Election | White Turnout | Black Turnout | Latino Turnout |
|----------|--------------|---------------|----------------|
| 2020 Presidential | 71% | 63% | 54% |
| 2022 Midterm | 52% | 44% | 36% |
| Typical local | 20-30% | Lower | Lower |

**Effect of Restrictions**

| Restriction Type | Estimated Turnout Impact |
|-----------------|-------------------------|
| Strict voter ID | -2-3% overall, -5-7% for affected groups |
| Polling place closures | -2-4% in affected areas |
| Reduced early voting | -1-3% for working voters |
| Purges | Varies; many affected don't return |

### On Representation

**The Participation Gap**

- Voters are older, whiter, wealthier than non-voters
- Policy preferences differ between voters and non-voters
- Representatives respond to those who vote
- Non-voters' interests are underrepresented

**Policy Implications**

| Issue | Voter Position | Non-Voter Position |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| Minimum wage increase | Support | Stronger support |
| Social safety net | Support | Stronger support |
| Criminal justice reform | Support | Stronger support |
| Healthcare access | Support | Stronger support |

### On Specific Communities

**Black Voters**

- Disproportionately affected by ID laws
- Disproportionately affected by polling place closures
- Disproportionately affected by felony disenfranchisement
- Targeted by some restrictions (Sunday voting cuts)

**Latino Voters**

- Lower registration rates
- Language access barriers
- Citizenship documentation concerns
- Targeted in some states

**Young Voters**

- Student ID often not accepted
- Mobility creates registration challenges
- Less likely to have driver's license
- Different documentation patterns

**Elderly Voters**

- More likely to lack current ID
- Mobility issues with polling place access
- May need assistance voting
- Affected by some ID requirements

**Voters with Disabilities**

- Physical accessibility challenges
- Electronic voting access needs
- Assistance requirements
- Transportation barriers

### Federal Legislation Status

**John Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act**

- Would restore VRA preclearance with updated formula
- Passed House
- Blocked in Senate by filibuster
- Remains priority for voting rights advocates

**Freedom to Vote Act**

- Would establish national voting standards
- Automatic registration, same-day registration
- Early voting and mail voting requirements
- Passed House (as part of larger bill)
- Blocked in Senate

**Electoral Count Reform Act**

- Passed and signed into law (2022)
- Clarifies procedures for counting electoral votes
- Response to January 6 events
- Does not address voter access

### Reform Progress

**Same-Day Registration**

## Key Takeaways

1. **Restrictions continue post-Shelby**: The VRA's weakening has enabled new barriers
2. **State variation is extreme**: Voter experience differs dramatically by state
3. **Disparate impact persists**: Restrictions disproportionately affect minorities
4. **Progress is uneven**: Some states expand access while others restrict
5. **Federal action stalled**: Senate filibuster blocks comprehensive reform
6. **Litigation is essential**: Courts remain key battleground
7. **Reform is achievable**: AVR and other reforms spreading
8. **2024 and beyond**: Stakes remain high for voting access
