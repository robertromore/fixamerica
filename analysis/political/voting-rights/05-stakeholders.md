# Voting Rights: Stakeholders

## Who Benefits from Voting Restrictions

### Politicians Who Benefit from Lower Turnout

**Partisan Beneficiaries**

- Incumbents in safe seats (both parties)
- Party that benefits from restricted electorate
- Politicians who won with suppressed turnout
- Those who would lose with higher turnout

**The Political Calculation**

| Voter Group | Typical Lean | Effect of Suppression |
|-------------|--------------|----------------------|
| Young voters | Democratic | Lower D turnout |
| Urban voters | Democratic | Lower D turnout |
| Black voters | Democratic | Lower D turnout |
| Latino voters | Democratic | Lower D turnout |
| Rural voters | Republican | Lower R turnout (some restrictions) |

### State Officials with Partisan Interests

**Secretaries of State**

- Often partisan elected officials
- Control election administration
- Make voter roll decisions
- Set polling place locations

**State Legislators**

- Pass voting restriction laws
- Draw districts (gerrymandering)
- Control election administration
- Benefit from status quo

### Ideological Organizations

**Conservative Groups Supporting Restrictions**

| Organization | Role |
|--------------|------|
| Heritage Foundation | Policy advocacy, model legislation |
| ALEC | Drafts model restrictive legislation |
| True the Vote | "Election integrity" activism |
| Judicial Watch | Litigation demanding purges |

**Their Interest**

- Ideological commitment to small electorate
- Belief that restrictions protect elections
- Partisan advantage from lower turnout
- Donor interests served by restricted access

## Who Is Harmed by Restrictions

### Voters Facing Barriers

**Communities of Color**

| Group | Key Barriers |
|-------|--------------|
| Black voters | ID laws, purges, Sunday voting cuts, polling place closures |
| Latino voters | Language barriers, ID requirements, proof of citizenship |
| Native American voters | ID requirements (address issues), distance to polls |
| Asian American voters | Language access, ID requirements |

**Young Voters**

- Student IDs often not accepted
- Mobility creates registration problems
- Less likely to have driver's license
- Campus polling places limited

**Elderly Voters**

- May lack current ID
- Mobility limitations
- Long lines difficult
- Complex requirements confusing

**Voters with Disabilities**

- Physical accessibility of polling places
- Accessible voting equipment
- Assistance requirements
- Transportation barriers

**Low-Income Voters**

- Cost of ID and underlying documents
- Time costs of compliance
- Transportation challenges
- Work schedule conflicts

**Rural Voters**

- Distance to polling places
- Limited early voting options
- Polling place closures affect them too
- Less access to DMV for ID

### Citizens with Convictions

**Felony Disenfranchisement**

| Category | Number Affected |
|----------|-----------------|
| Total disenfranchised | 4.6+ million |
| Completed sentence but can't vote | 1.1+ million |
| Black Americans | 1 in 16 |
| Some states | Lifetime ban |

**Impact**

- Disproportionately affects Black communities
- Undermines reintegration after serving time
- Creates second-class citizenship
- Often affects families and communities

### Democracy Itself

**Systemic Harms**

- Elected officials don't represent all citizens
- Policy outcomes skewed toward participating voters
- Legitimacy of government undermined
- Civic culture weakened

## Who Has Power to Change Things

### Federal Government

**Congress**

| Power | Current Status |
|-------|----------------|
| VRA restoration | Blocked by filibuster |
| National voting standards | Blocked by filibuster |
| Constitutional amendment | Requires supermajorities |

**President**

| Power | Status |
|-------|--------|
| Executive orders | Limited on voting |
| DOJ enforcement | Section 2 cases, limited resources |
| Bully pulpit | Can raise awareness |

**Federal Courts**

| Court | Current Posture |
|-------|-----------------|
| Supreme Court | Deference to states on restrictions |
| Circuit courts | Mixed; depends on composition |
| District courts | First line of defense |

### State Government

**State Legislatures**

- Primary venue for both restriction and expansion
- Control over registration, voting procedures
- Can pass same-day registration, early voting, AVR
- Can also pass restrictions

**Governors**

- Veto power over legislation
- Executive orders on election administration
- Appointment power
- Public leadership

**Secretaries of State**

- Day-to-day election administration
- Voter roll maintenance
- Polling place decisions
- Implementation of laws

### Local Officials

**County Election Officials**

- Run elections on the ground
- Allocate polling places
- Manage voter rolls
- Hire and train poll workers

**Municipal Officials**

- Some local election authority
- Resource allocation
- Polling place locations
- Early voting sites

### Courts

**State Courts**

- Interpret state constitutions
- Can strike down restrictions
- Vary by state composition
- State constitutional provisions matter

**Federal Courts**

- VRA Section 2 enforcement
- Constitutional claims
- Limited post-Shelby
- Composition determines outcomes

## Key Relationships

### Power Dynamics

```text

Federal Government
      ↓ (limited post-Shelby)
State Government ←→ State Courts
      ↓
Local Administration
      ↓
Individual Voters ←→ Civil Rights Organizations

```text

### Coalition Building

**Effective Voting Rights Coalitions**

1. Civil rights organizations (legal expertise, legitimacy)
2. Community organizations (local mobilization)
3. Faith communities (moral authority, networks)
4. Labor (resources, GOTV capacity)
5. Youth organizations (energy, future voters)
6. Bipartisan validators (political cover)

### Opposition Relationships

**Who Coordinates Against Reform**

- State legislators passing restrictions
- Conservative legal organizations
- "Election integrity" groups
- Some party organizations

**How They Coordinate**

| Actor | Role |
|-------|------|
| ALEC | Model legislation |
| Heritage Foundation | Policy development |
| Judicial Watch | Litigation for purges |
| True the Vote | Grassroots organizing |
| State legislatures | Pass restrictions |

## Stakeholder Engagement Strategy

### Building Support

**Priority Stakeholders**

| Stakeholder | Engagement Strategy |
|-------------|---------------------|
| Congress | Constituent pressure, lobbying |
| State legislatures | Grassroots mobilization, coalition pressure |
| Courts | Strategic litigation |
| Election officials | Education, relationship building |
| Public | Awareness campaigns |

### Neutralizing Opposition

**Approaches**

| Opponent | Strategy |
|----------|----------|
| Legislators passing restrictions | Electoral pressure, litigation |
| "Election integrity" groups | Debunk fraud myths, expose motives |
| Partisan officials | Transparency, accountability |
| Courts | Appoint better judges where possible |

### Managing Key Relationships

**With Federal Government**

- Lobby for legislation despite filibuster
- Support judicial nominations
- Press for DOJ enforcement
- Build record for future action

**With State Government**

- State-by-state advocacy
- Target winnable reforms
- Defend against restrictions
- Support reform candidates

**With Local Officials**

- Build relationships with election administrators
- Provide resources and best practices
- Monitor for compliance
- Document problems

**With Affected Communities**

- Voter education and mobilization
- Community organizing
- Legal assistance
- Sustained engagement beyond elections
