# Direct Democracy: Legislation

## Overview

This document provides draft legislation to expand and improve direct democracy mechanisms in the United States. Reforms range from state-level initiative process improvements to ambitious federal innovations.

## Constitutional Amendments

### National Popular Vote Amendment

**Purpose**: Elect the president by national popular vote, ensuring every vote counts equally.

**Draft Text**:

```text
AMENDMENT XXIX

Section 1. The President and Vice President shall be elected by
the direct vote of the citizens of the United States. The persons
having the greatest number of votes for President and Vice President
shall be elected.

Section 2. Each State shall provide for the appointment of
presidential electors in a manner consistent with Section 1. Electors
shall cast their votes for the candidates receiving the most votes
nationwide.

Section 3. In the event that no candidate receives a majority of
votes cast, a runoff election shall be held within thirty days
between the two candidates receiving the most votes.

Section 4. Congress shall have the power to enforce this article
by appropriate legislation, including provisions for vote counting,
certification, and dispute resolution.

Section 5. This article shall take effect for the first
presidential election occurring more than one year after
ratification.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 1 establishes direct national popular vote
- Section 2 preserves state administration while requiring consistency
- Section 3 provides for runoff if no majority
- Section 4 grants Congressional enforcement authority
- Section 5 allows transition time for implementation

**Alternative: Interstate Compact Approach** (No Amendment Needed):

The National Popular Vote Interstate Compact achieves similar effect through state legislation. See Federal Legislation section below for model state bill.

### Federal Initiative Amendment

**Purpose**: Allow citizens to propose and vote on federal legislation directly.

**Draft Text**:

```text
AMENDMENT XXX

Section 1. The people reserve the power to propose laws and
amendments to this Constitution and to adopt or reject them at
the ballot.

Section 2. An initiative shall be placed on the ballot upon
petition signed by citizens equal in number to four percent of
votes cast in the most recent presidential election, with
signatures from at least two-thirds of the States.

Section 3. An initiative receiving a majority of votes cast shall
take effect as law or constitutional amendment as specified in the
initiative, subject to judicial review.

Section 4. No initiative shall:
(a) Restrict rights protected by this Constitution;
(b) Declare war or ratify treaties;
(c) Appropriate funds exceeding one percent of the annual federal
    budget without identifying offsetting revenue or reductions;
(d) Target specific individuals or corporations by name.

Section 5. Congress shall provide by law for initiative
procedures, including signature verification, ballot language
review, voter information, and election administration.

Section 6. This article shall take effect two years after
ratification.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 1 establishes the initiative power
- Section 2 sets a high but achievable threshold with geographic distribution
- Section 3 makes passed initiatives law, subject to courts
- Section 4 limits subject matter to protect rights and prevent abuse
- Section 5 delegates procedural details to Congress
- Section 6 provides transition time

**Challenges**:

- Constitutional amendments extremely difficult to pass
- No precedent for federal direct democracy
- Congress has no incentive to empower citizens to bypass it
- Would require sustained multi-decade campaign

### Citizen Assembly Amendment

**Purpose**: Establish permanent deliberative body of randomly selected citizens.

**Draft Text**:

```text
AMENDMENT XXXI

Section 1. There shall be a National Citizens' Assembly composed
of five hundred citizens selected by lot from the eligible
electorate. Members shall serve staggered terms of two years and
may not serve consecutive terms.

Section 2. The Assembly shall deliberate on matters of national
importance and may, by two-thirds vote, place advisory measures
on the ballot for the people's consideration.

Section 3. Congress shall respond to Assembly recommendations
within one hundred eighty days by vote on the substance of each
recommendation.

Section 4. Selection shall be administered to ensure the Assembly
reflects the demographic composition of the nation by age,
geography, and other relevant characteristics as Congress shall
provide.

Section 5. Members shall receive compensation equal to the median
household income during their service and shall be protected from
employment discrimination related to their service.

Section 6. Congress shall have the power to enforce this article
by appropriate legislation.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 1 establishes the Assembly with rotating membership
- Section 2 grants advisory power and ballot access
- Section 3 requires Congressional response (not compliance)
- Section 4 ensures demographic representativeness
- Section 5 compensates members and protects employment
- Section 6 grants enforcement authority

## Federal Legislation

### National Citizens' Assembly Act

**Purpose**: Establish a national citizen assembly by statute (without constitutional amendment).

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "National Citizens' Assembly Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
(1) Public trust in governmental institutions has declined
    significantly over recent decades;
(2) Deliberative bodies of randomly selected citizens have
    demonstrated capacity to address complex issues thoughtfully;
(3) International experience in Ireland, France, and elsewhere
    demonstrates the value of citizen assemblies;
(4) A national deliberative body could improve public discourse
    and inform Congressional action.

SEC. 3. ESTABLISHMENT.

There is established the National Citizens' Assembly (hereinafter
"the Assembly") as an independent deliberative body.

SEC. 4. COMPOSITION AND SELECTION.

(a) COMPOSITION.—The Assembly shall be composed of 300 members
    selected by lot from the eligible electorate.

(b) SELECTION PROCESS.—
    (1) The Comptroller General shall administer a random selection
        process from voter registration rolls and other sources to
        ensure inclusion of unregistered eligible citizens.
    (2) Selection shall be stratified to ensure the Assembly reflects
        the demographic composition of the nation by—
        (A) geographic region;
        (B) age;
        (C) gender;
        (D) race and ethnicity;
        (E) educational attainment; and
        (F) income level.
    (3) Selected citizens may decline to serve without penalty.

(c) TERMS.—Members shall serve terms of eighteen months, with
    selection occurring every six months to ensure continuity.

(d) LIMITATION.—No person may serve more than one term in any
    ten-year period.

SEC. 5. COMPENSATION AND SUPPORT.

(a) COMPENSATION.—Each member shall receive compensation at a rate
    equal to the national median household income, prorated for
    the term of service.

(b) EMPLOYMENT PROTECTION.—No employer may terminate, demote, or
    otherwise discriminate against an employee selected for
    Assembly service.

(c) CHILDCARE AND DEPENDENT CARE.—The Assembly shall provide or
    reimburse reasonable childcare and dependent care expenses for
    members during service.

(d) TRAVEL AND ACCOMMODATION.—The Assembly shall provide travel
    expenses and accommodation for members required to attend
    in-person sessions.

SEC. 6. OPERATIONS.

(a) MEETINGS.—The Assembly shall meet for at least four sessions
    annually, each session lasting not less than five days.

(b) AGENDA.—
    (1) The Assembly shall set its own agenda by majority vote.
    (2) Congress may refer matters to the Assembly for deliberation.
    (3) Members of the public may petition the Assembly to consider
        matters, with petitions signed by 100,000 citizens requiring
        Assembly consideration.

(c) DELIBERATION.—
    (1) The Assembly shall hear testimony from experts representing
        diverse perspectives on each matter.
    (2) Deliberations shall be facilitated by professional
        facilitators trained in deliberative democracy methods.
    (3) Members shall have access to research staff and nonpartisan
        policy analysis.

(d) RECOMMENDATIONS.—The Assembly may, by majority vote, issue
    recommendations to Congress, the President, or the public.

SEC. 7. CONGRESSIONAL RESPONSE.

(a) REQUIRED RESPONSE.—Within 180 days of an Assembly recommendation,
    the relevant committee of jurisdiction shall hold a hearing on
    the recommendation and report to the full chamber.

(b) FLOOR CONSIDERATION.—Recommendations receiving two-thirds
    support of the Assembly shall receive floor consideration in
    both chambers within one year.

(c) NO BINDING EFFECT.—Assembly recommendations shall not bind
    Congress or any governmental body.

SEC. 8. TRANSPARENCY.

(a) PUBLIC PROCEEDINGS.—Assembly deliberations shall be open to the
    public and broadcast online, except that members may request
    closed sessions for sensitive matters by two-thirds vote.

(b) RECORDS.—All Assembly records, including testimony, research,
    and deliberations, shall be preserved and made publicly available.

SEC. 9. INDEPENDENCE.

(a) NONPARTISAN STAFF.—Assembly staff shall be nonpartisan and
    prohibited from engaging in partisan political activity.

(b) NO LOBBYING.—Members may not accept gifts or compensation from
    lobbyists or entities with matters before the Assembly during
    service and for two years after.

(c) ETHICS.—The Assembly shall adopt a code of ethics governing
    member conduct.

SEC. 10. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated $50,000,000 annually to
carry out this Act.

SEC. 11. REVIEW AND RENEWAL.

(a) GAO REVIEW.—The Comptroller General shall conduct a comprehensive
    review of the Assembly's operations and impact five years after
    establishment.

(b) SUNSET.—The authority for the Assembly shall expire ten years
    after enactment unless reauthorized by Congress.
```

### Federal Advisory Referendum Act

**Purpose**: Authorize Congress to place advisory questions on the national ballot.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Federal Advisory Referendum Act".

SEC. 2. AUTHORIZATION.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Congress may, by joint resolution, place advisory
    questions on the ballot for consideration by the people during
    federal general elections.

(b) FORM.—Advisory referendum questions shall be framed as yes/no
    questions or multiple-choice options as specified in the
    authorizing resolution.

(c) EFFECT.—Advisory referendum results shall inform but not bind
    Congress or any governmental body.

SEC. 3. REQUIREMENTS.

(a) BALLOT LANGUAGE.—Referendum questions shall be written in clear,
    neutral language reviewed by the Comptroller General for accuracy
    and clarity.

(b) VOTER INFORMATION.—The Election Assistance Commission shall
    prepare a voter information guide for each referendum including—
    (1) an explanation of the question;
    (2) arguments in favor and against;
    (3) background information; and
    (4) statements from proponents and opponents.

(c) TIMING.—Referendum questions must be finalized at least 90 days
    before the election.

SEC. 4. ADMINISTRATION.

(a) STATE ADMINISTRATION.—States shall administer referendum voting
    consistent with their election procedures.

(b) RESULTS CERTIFICATION.—The Election Assistance Commission shall
    certify national referendum results within 30 days of the
    election.

(c) REPORTING.—Results shall be reported nationally and by state.

SEC. 5. LIMITATIONS.

No advisory referendum may—
(a) inquire about specific individuals;
(b) address matters solely within state jurisdiction; or
(c) seek to influence judicial proceedings.
```

### National Popular Vote Interstate Compact Act (Model State Bill)

**Purpose**: Model legislation for states to join the NPV compact.

**Draft Text** (State Legislation):

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] National Popular Vote Act".

SECTION 2. AGREEMENT AMONG THE STATES.

[State] hereby enters into the Agreement Among the States to Elect
the President by National Popular Vote.

SECTION 3. AGREEMENT TEXT.

Article I – Membership

This agreement shall take effect when states cumulatively
possessing a majority of the electoral votes have enacted this
agreement in substantially the same form.

Article II – Pledge of Presidential Electors

Each member state shall conduct a statewide popular election for
President and Vice President. Each member state shall appoint
presidential electors who shall cast their votes for the
presidential slate that received the largest national popular vote
total.

Article III – Determination of National Popular Vote

The chief election official of each member state shall determine
the national popular vote total by aggregating official results
from all states and the District of Columbia.

Article IV – Certification

Each member state shall certify its slate of electors consistent
with Article II at least six days before the meeting of electors.

Article V – Withdrawal

A member state may withdraw from this agreement, except that
withdrawal during the six-month period prior to the end of a
President's term shall not take effect until the next President
is inaugurated.

Article VI – Enforcement

Any member state may bring action to compel compliance by any
other member state.

SECTION 4. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect upon the agreement taking effect as
specified in Article I.
```

### Citizen Initiative Review Act

**Purpose**: Establish citizen review panels for ballot measures.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Citizen Initiative Review Act".

SECTION 2. ESTABLISHMENT.

There is established the Citizen Initiative Review process for
ballot measures in [State].

SECTION 3. PANEL SELECTION.

(a) For each statewide ballot measure, a Citizens' Panel shall be
    selected by lot from registered voters.

(b) Each panel shall consist of not fewer than 20 and not more than
    24 citizens, selected to be demographically representative.

(c) The Secretary of State shall contract with an independent
    organization to conduct selection and facilitation.

SECTION 4. PANEL DELIBERATION.

(a) Each panel shall meet for not fewer than four days to review
    the assigned ballot measure.

(b) Panels shall—
    (1) receive testimony from measure proponents and opponents;
    (2) hear from nonpartisan experts;
    (3) deliberate in facilitated sessions;
    (4) develop a Citizens' Statement.

(c) Professional facilitators trained in deliberative methods shall
    guide panel proceedings.

SECTION 5. CITIZENS' STATEMENT.

(a) Each panel shall produce a Citizens' Statement including—
    (1) a summary of the measure;
    (2) key findings agreed upon by the panel;
    (3) arguments for and against the measure as evaluated by
        the panel.

(b) Findings shall indicate the number of panelists supporting each
    finding.

(c) The Statement shall not recommend a vote for or against.

SECTION 6. VOTER GUIDE INCLUSION.

The Citizens' Statement shall be included in the official voter
pamphlet distributed to all voters.

SECTION 7. FUNDING.

The Citizen Initiative Review shall be funded through ballot measure
filing fees and appropriations as the legislature shall provide.

SECTION 8. EVALUATION.

The Secretary of State shall conduct biennial evaluations of the
process including voter awareness, usage, and satisfaction.
```

### Participatory Budgeting Enabling Act

**Purpose**: Enable and encourage participatory budgeting in municipalities.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Participatory Budgeting Enabling Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

As used in this Act:
(a) "Participatory budgeting" means a process by which residents
    directly decide how to spend part of a public budget.
(b) "Municipality" means any city, town, county, or school district.

SECTION 3. AUTHORIZATION.

Any municipality may establish a participatory budgeting process
for any portion of its discretionary capital or operating budget.

SECTION 4. MINIMUM STANDARDS.

Any participatory budgeting process shall:
(a) Allow all residents aged 14 or older to participate regardless
    of citizenship or voter registration status;
(b) Provide accessible participation opportunities including
    in-person meetings, online platforms, and paper ballots;
(c) Ensure materials are available in languages spoken by at least
    5% of the population;
(d) Implement the winning projects within two years unless
    infeasible;
(e) Report annually on process participation and project outcomes.

SECTION 5. STATE SUPPORT.

(a) The [Department of Local Government] shall provide technical
    assistance to municipalities implementing participatory
    budgeting.
(b) The state shall maintain a clearinghouse of best practices
    and model ordinances.

SECTION 6. INCENTIVE GRANTS.

(a) There is established a Participatory Budgeting Grant Program.
(b) Grants shall be available to municipalities establishing or
    expanding participatory budgeting programs.
(c) Priority shall be given to programs serving low-income communities.

SECTION 7. LIABILITY PROTECTION.

Municipalities operating participatory budgeting programs in good
faith compliance with this Act shall not be liable for project
decisions made through the participatory process.
```

### Initiative Process Reform Act

**Purpose**: Improve state initiative process quality and accessibility.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Initiative Process Reform Act".

SECTION 2. DRAFTING ASSISTANCE.

(a) The [Legislative Counsel] shall provide free drafting assistance
    to initiative proponents upon request.
(b) Assistance shall include legal drafting and constitutional review.
(c) Proponents are not required to accept recommended changes.

SECTION 3. PRE-CIRCULATION REVIEW.

(a) Before circulation for signatures, proposed initiatives shall be
    submitted to the [Attorney General] for review.
(b) Within 30 days, the [Attorney General] shall provide:
    (1) An opinion on constitutionality;
    (2) Identification of potential single-subject violations;
    (3) A fiscal impact estimate.
(c) Proponents may revise the initiative in response to review.

SECTION 4. SIGNATURE GATHERING.

(a) Paid signature gatherers shall be prohibited from receiving
    compensation on a per-signature basis.
(b) Signature gatherers shall be [state] residents.
(c) Online signature gathering may be permitted subject to
    verification procedures established by the Secretary of State.

SECTION 5. BALLOT TITLE AND SUMMARY.

(a) An independent Ballot Title Board shall draft ballot titles
    and summaries.
(b) The Board shall consist of [members appointed by various
    officials to ensure balance].
(c) Ballot titles shall be clear, neutral, and not exceed 25 words.
(d) Either proponents or opponents may challenge ballot language
    within 10 days.

SECTION 6. FISCAL ANALYSIS.

Each qualified initiative shall include in the voter guide:
(a) An independent fiscal impact analysis;
(b) Revenue sources if the measure requires funding;
(c) Programs affected if the measure redirects funds.

SECTION 7. SUPERMAJORITY FOR CONSTITUTIONAL AMENDMENTS.

Constitutional amendments proposed by initiative shall require
approval by 55% of votes cast.
```

## Regulatory Framework

### Election Assistance Commission Regulations

For federal advisory referenda and citizen assembly ballot measures:

1. **Ballot Design**: Standard format across states for federal questions
2. **Voter Information**: Comprehensive guides with balanced arguments
3. **Results Certification**: National tabulation and certification process
4. **Accessibility**: Language access and disability accommodations

### State Implementation Guidelines

For states implementing direct democracy reforms:

1. **Selection Protocols**: Random selection methodologies for citizen panels
2. **Facilitation Standards**: Training requirements for deliberation facilitators
3. **Transparency Requirements**: Open meetings, recording, and record retention
4. **Evaluation Metrics**: Standardized measures for program assessment

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Questions

**Federal Initiative**:

- Article IV, Section 4 (Republican Form) traditionally not justiciable
- *Pacific States Telephone v. Oregon* (1912): Initiative doesn't violate
- Statutory federal initiative may face different analysis
- Amendment approach more constitutionally secure

**National Popular Vote Compact**:

- Compact Clause (Article I, Section 10, Clause 3): May require congressional consent
- *Virginia v. Tennessee* (1893): Only compacts affecting federal supremacy need consent
- State authority over electors well-established
- Likely to face Supreme Court challenge when activated

**Citizen Assemblies**:

- Advisory bodies generally permissible
- Binding authority would raise more questions
- Delegation doctrine concerns for binding decisions
- Statutory approach safer than constitutional

### Preemption Issues

- Federal advisory referendum: Clear federal authority over federal elections
- State initiatives on federal policy: Limited by preemption
- Participatory budgeting: State/local authority clear

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- NPV Compact: State enforcement against other member states
- Citizen assembly recommendations: Political pressure only (without amendment)
- Initiative process reforms: State court enforcement of procedures

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| NPV compact withdrawal | States could withdraw when unfavorable result likely | High |
| Assembly composition manipulation | Selection process could be influenced | Moderate |
| Signature verification fraud | False or paid signatures corrupt process | Moderate |
| Advisory referendum ignored | Congress could simply ignore results | High |
| Ballot language manipulation | Misleading titles despite reform | Moderate |
| PB elite capture | Well-organized groups dominate process | Moderate |
| CIR panel bias | Selected panel may not be representative | Low |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Federal initiative requires amendment | Extremely difficult to achieve | Constitutional structure |
| NPV doesn't eliminate Electoral College | Could be challenged or repealed | Not constitutional change |
| Citizen assembly only advisory | Limited power without amendment | Separation of powers |
| Money still dominates initiative campaigns | Wealthy interests can outspend | First Amendment limitations |
| Low participation in deliberative processes | Unrepresentative outcomes | Barriers to participation |
| State variation creates patchwork | Unequal access to direct democracy | Federalism |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **NPV Withdrawal Prevention**
    - Include substantial withdrawal notice period (6+ months)
    - Make withdrawal effective only after next presidential term
    - Build political costs into withdrawal
    - Pursue constitutional amendment as backup

2. **Assembly Integrity Protection**
    - Independent administration (GAO or similar)
    - Transparent, auditable selection process
    - Diverse oversight committee
    - Regular process evaluation

3. **Initiative Process Integrity**
    - Ban per-signature payment
    - Require residency for signature gatherers
    - Robust verification procedures
    - Criminal penalties for fraud

4. **Ensuring Government Response**
    - Mandatory response requirements with deadlines
    - Public reporting of government action/inaction
    - Political accountability mechanisms
    - Escalating procedural requirements for ignored recommendations

5. **Campaign Finance in Initiatives**
    - Public financing for citizen-initiated measures
    - Enhanced disclosure requirements
    - Contribution limits where constitutional
    - Matching funds for grassroots campaigns

6. **Participation Equity**
    - Adequate compensation for citizen panel service
    - Childcare and accessibility support
    - Multiple participation channels (in-person, online)
    - Targeted outreach to underrepresented communities

7. **Quality Deliberation**
    - Professional facilitation requirements
    - Balanced expert testimony mandates
    - Information quality standards
    - Evaluation and continuous improvement

### General Implementation Concerns

1. **Coordination Challenge**
    - Federal, state, and local reforms need alignment
    - Different jurisdictions may conflict
    - Timing coordination for elections
    - Recommendation: Establish intergovernmental coordinating body

2. **Public Education**
    - New mechanisms unfamiliar to voters
    - Risk of confusion or disengagement
    - Need for sustained civic education
    - Recommendation: Fund comprehensive public education campaigns

3. **Judicial Review**
    - Courts may invalidate reforms
    - Constitutional questions uncertain
    - Different circuits may reach different conclusions
    - Recommendation: Careful legal drafting, prepare for litigation

4. **Political Opposition**
    - Incumbents threatened by direct democracy
    - Partisan concerns about outcomes
    - Lobbying against reforms
    - Recommendation: Build bipartisan coalitions, emphasize process over outcomes
