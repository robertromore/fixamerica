# Direct Democracy: History

## Ancient and Classical Foundations

### Athenian Democracy (508-322 BCE)

**Direct Participation**:

- All male citizens participated in Assembly (Ecclesia)
- 6,000 quorum for major decisions
- 40 Assembly meetings per year
- Decisions by majority vote

**Key Institutions**:

- **Assembly**: All citizens voted on laws, war, treaties
- **Council of 500**: Selected by lot, set Assembly agenda
- **Courts**: Large juries (201-501) decided cases
- **Magistrates**: Selected by lot, one-year terms

**Sortition (Selection by Lot)**:

- Most offices filled randomly from eligible citizens
- Prevented concentration of power
- Assumed all citizens capable of governance
- Exception: generals elected (expertise needed)

**Limitations**:

- Excluded women, slaves, foreigners (majority of population)
- Limited to city-state scale
- Collapsed under Macedonian conquest

### Roman Republic

**Direct Elements**:

- Tribal and centuriate assemblies
- Citizens voted on laws and magistrates
- But weighted by wealth and social class

**Mixed System**:

- Senate (elite deliberation)
- Assemblies (popular ratification)
- Magistrates (executive)

### Swiss Cantons (Medieval to Present)

**Landsgemeinde** (open-air assemblies):

- Citizens gather in public square
- Raise hands to vote
- Still practiced in two cantons (Glarus, Appenzell Innerrhoden)
- Origin of referendum tradition

## American Founding Era

### Founding Debates

**Anti-Federalist Position**:

- Favored smaller republics with more direct participation
- Concerned about distant federal government
- Wanted more frequent elections
- Supported recall and instruction of representatives

**Federalist Position** (Madison's Federalist 10):

- Large republic as check on "faction"
- Representatives would "refine and enlarge public views"
- Rejected direct democracy as prone to passion
- Designed system to filter popular opinion

**Constitutional Design**:

- No national referendum mechanism
- No recall of federal officials
- Indirect election of senators (until 1913)
- Electoral College for president
- Deliberate distance from direct popular control

### Early State Experiments

**Town Meetings** (New England):

- Direct citizen participation in local governance
- Originated in Massachusetts (1630s)
- Continue today in New England
- Model for participatory democracy

**State Constitutional Ratification**:

- Massachusetts (1780): First constitution ratified by popular vote
- New Hampshire (1784): Popular ratification
- Established precedent for citizen approval

## Progressive Era (1890-1920)

### Rise of Direct Democracy

**Context**:

- Industrial concentration and monopolies
- Corrupt state legislatures ("railroad legislatures")
- Limited responsiveness to public concerns
- Reform movements seeking popular power

**South Dakota (1898)**: First state to adopt initiative and referendum

**Oregon System** (1902):

- Initiative, referendum, and recall adopted
- Became model for other states
- Promoted by William U'Ren and Direct Legislation League
- Called "Oregon System" nationally

### Progressive Era Issues

**Successful Initiatives**:

- Women's suffrage (multiple states before 19th Amendment)
- Prohibition (state-level)
- Workers' compensation
- Primary election systems
- Railroad regulation

**The Recall**:

- California (1911): Applied to all elected officials
- First statewide recall: North Dakota Governor Lynn Frazier (1921)
- Primarily used at local level

### Federal Proposals

**National Initiative Amendment**:

- Introduced repeatedly 1907-1916
- Never passed Congress
- Supported by Progressive Party (1912 platform)
- Theodore Roosevelt endorsed
- William Jennings Bryan supported

## Mid-20th Century (1920-1970)

### Decline of Initiative Use

**Factors**:

- New Deal addressed many Progressive concerns
- Trust in representative government higher
- Court decisions limited some initiatives
- Complexity of modern governance

**Usage Statistics**:

- 1910s: 269 statewide initiatives
- 1920s: 197
- 1930s: 177
- 1940s: 96
- 1950s: 105

### Notable Developments

**California's Continuing Use**:

- Remained active initiative state
- Ham and Eggs pension initiative (1938, 1939)
- Oil taxation battles
- Housing discrimination (Prop 14, 1964)

**Alaska and Hawaii**:

- Both adopted initiative systems at statehood (1959)

## California's Tax Revolt (1970s-1980s)

### Proposition 13 (1978)

**Background**:

- Rapidly rising property values
- Property taxes increasing dramatically
- Elderly homeowners facing displacement
- Anti-tax sentiment building

**Provisions**:

- Capped property tax at 1% of assessed value
- Limited assessment increases to 2% annually
- Required 2/3 vote for state tax increases
- Reset assessment upon sale

**Impact**:

- Passed 65%-35%
- Cut property taxes by 57%
- Starved local governments of revenue
- Shifted power to state level
- Sparked national "tax revolt"

**Legacy**:

- Still in effect (with modifications)
- Model for similar measures nationwide
- Showed initiative power to reshape government
- Demonstrated risks of direct democracy

## Contemporary Era (1990-Present)

### Initiative as Policy Tool

**Term Limits Movement (1990s)**:

- California Prop 140 (1990): Term limits for state legislators
- Spread to 21 states by 1995
- Congressional term limits struck down by Supreme Court (1995)
- Showed initiative power for structural reform

**Criminal Justice Initiatives**:

- Three strikes laws (California 1994)
- Mandatory minimums
- Recent reversal: California Props 36, 47, 57

**Drug Policy**:

- California Prop 215 (1996): Medical marijuana
- Colorado, Washington (2012): Recreational marijuana
- Oregon Measure 110 (2020): Drug decriminalization

**Minimum Wage**:

- Multiple states passed minimum wage increases
- Often surpassing federal level
- Florida (2020): $15 minimum wage

### Marriage Equality Battle

**Anti-Same-Sex Marriage**:

- Defense of Marriage initiatives (2004): 11 states
- California Prop 8 (2008): Banned same-sex marriage
- 30+ states passed marriage bans

**Reversal**:

- Prop 8 overturned by courts (2013)
- Supreme Court legalized same-sex marriage (2015)
- Showed limits of initiative to restrict rights

### Recent Innovations

**Citizen Initiative Review** (Oregon, 2011):

- Randomly selected panels evaluate measures
- "Citizens' Statement" in voter pamphlet
- Improves information quality

**Independent Redistricting**:

- California Prop 11 (2008): Citizens Redistricting Commission
- Arizona Prop 106 (2000): Independent commission
- Michigan Prop 2 (2018): Voters Not Politicians

**Medicaid Expansion**:

- Idaho, Nebraska, Utah (2018): Voters approved Medicaid expansion
- Oklahoma (2020): Medicaid expansion
- Bypassed legislature opposition

### Participatory Budgeting Spread

**Brazil Origin** (Porto Alegre, 1989):

- Worker's Party innovation
- 15% of municipal budget
- Spread throughout Brazil
- International model

**U.S. Adoption**:

- Chicago Ward 49 (2009): First U.S. PB
- NYC (2011): Largest U.S. program
- Spread to 400+ municipalities
- Growing scale and sophistication

### Citizen Assembly Revival

**British Columbia (2004)**:

- 160 citizens studied electoral reform
- Recommended STV
- Lost referendum (57%, needed 60%)

**Ireland's Success** (2012-2018):

- Constitutional Convention
- Citizens' Assembly
- Led to marriage equality, abortion access
- Global model for deliberative democracy

**France Climate Assembly (2019-2020)**:

- 150 randomly selected citizens
- Climate policy recommendations
- High-profile deliberative experiment

## Lessons from History

### Conditions for Success

1. **Public frustration** with representative institutions
2. **Clear, understandable** issues
3. **Organized campaigns** with resources
4. **Elite allies** providing legitimacy
5. **Media attention** raising awareness

### Recurring Challenges

1. **Money influence** on campaigns
2. **Complexity** of policy issues
3. **Minority rights** vulnerable to majority
4. **Implementation** depends on government cooperation
5. **Courts** can overturn initiatives

### Evolution of Use

- **Progressive Era**: Structural and reform measures
- **Mid-century**: Decline in use
- **1970s-1990s**: Tax revolts and social issues
- **2000s-present**: Diverse issues, professional campaigns
