# Direct Democracy: Stakeholders

## Affected Populations

### General Public

**All Citizens**:

- Direct stake in governance quality
- Affected by policy outcomes
- Variable engagement with direct democracy
- Diverse preferences on participation level

**Impact of Current System**:

- Limited voice between elections
- Frustration with unresponsive government
- Feeling of powerlessness
- Disconnect between preferences and policy

**Potential Benefits of Reform**:

- Greater sense of political efficacy
- Direct influence on specific issues
- More responsive government
- Increased civic engagement

**Potential Concerns**:

- Complexity of ballot measures
- Time demands of informed participation
- Manipulation by well-funded interests
- Majority potentially threatening minority

### Marginalized Communities

**Historically Disenfranchised Groups**:

- Communities targeted by past ballot measures
- Anti-immigrant initiatives (California Prop 187)
- Anti-LGBTQ measures (numerous states)
- Measures restricting affirmative action

**Dual Experience**:

- Sometimes victimized by majority tyranny
- Also bypassed representative barriers (marijuana, minimum wage)
- Depends on issue and coalition

**Specific Concerns**:

| Community | Historical Harm | Potential Benefit |
|-----------|----------------|-------------------|
| LGBTQ | Marriage bans, anti-trans measures | Equality initiatives where legislatures hostile |
| Immigrants | English-only, Prop 187 | DREAM Act-type initiatives possible |
| Communities of color | Affirmative action bans | Criminal justice reform measures |
| Low-income | Benefit cuts | Minimum wage, Medicaid expansion |

**Safeguard Needs**:

- Constitutional protections for minority rights
- Review mechanisms for discriminatory measures
- Funding equity in campaigns
- Language access and participation supports

### Civic Engaged Citizens

**Already Participating**:

- Vote on ballot measures
- Follow initiative campaigns
- May contribute or volunteer
- Higher political knowledge

**Potential Super-Participants**:

- Would engage more with expanded opportunities
- Participate in citizen assemblies
- Attend participatory budgeting
- Use digital democracy tools

**Characteristics**:

- Higher education levels
- More time availability
- Greater political interest
- Often already privileged

### Disengaged Citizens

**Current Non-Participants**:

- Don't vote or vote only top of ticket
- Skip ballot measures
- Limited political knowledge
- Often younger, lower income

**Barriers to Participation**:

- Time constraints (work, family)
- Transportation and access
- Information gaps
- Feeling of futility

**Reform Implications**:

- Could be re-engaged with accessible processes
- Need lower-barrier participation options
- Digital tools may help
- Outreach essential for representativeness

## Power Holders

### State Legislators

**Position**: Mixed to hostile toward direct democracy

**Interests**:

- Protect legislative prerogative
- Avoid accountability for controversial issues
- Sometimes use referendum for cover
- Fear being bypassed

**Current Behavior**:

- Some states restrict initiative process
- Legislatures modify/repeal voter-passed measures
- Use referendum strategically
- Control implementation of initiatives

**Recent Actions**:

| State | Action | Effect |
|-------|--------|--------|
| Florida | Supermajority requirement for amendments | Harder to pass initiatives |
| South Dakota | Restricted initiative process after voter-passed measures | Less direct democracy |
| Ohio | Raised threshold for constitutional amendments | Blocks abortion access initiative |
| Arizona | Legislative referrals to compete with initiatives | Confuses voters |

### Congress

**Position**: No interest in federal direct democracy

**Interests**:

- Protect congressional power
- Avoid national referendum
- Maintain gatekeeping role
- No partisan consensus for reform

**Structural Protection**:

- Amendment process requires 2/3 Congress
- No mechanism to bypass
- Both parties benefit from status quo
- No serious reform proposals

### Governors and State Executives

**Position**: Mixed

**Interests**:

- May support initiatives bypassing hostile legislature
- Oppose measures limiting executive power
- Implementation responsibility for passed measures
- Political positioning on popular initiatives

**Use of Direct Democracy**:

- Can propose legislative referenda
- Campaign for/against measures
- Sometimes use initiative for political goals
- Control implementation

### Courts

**Position**: Skeptical oversight role

**Interests**:

- Protect constitutional rights
- Maintain judicial review
- Ensure procedural compliance
- Limit initiative scope

**Current Role**:

- Review initiative constitutionality
- Enforce single-subject rules
- Interpret ballot measure language
- Sometimes overturn voter-passed measures

**Key Decisions**:

- *Pacific States Telephone v. Oregon* (1912): Initiative doesn't violate republican form guarantee
- *Romer v. Evans* (1996): Anti-gay initiative violated equal protection
- *Hollingsworth v. Perry* (2013): Prop 8 proponents lacked standing

### Political Parties

**Position**: Strategic and situational

**Democratic Party**:

- Generally more supportive of direct democracy
- Uses initiatives for progressive policies
- Concerns about money in initiative campaigns
- Platform often includes reform support

**Republican Party**:

- Generally more skeptical
- Concerns about majority rule
- Uses initiatives strategically (tax limits, affirmative action)
- Supports local control but not always direct democracy

**Third Parties**:

- Generally supportive (bypasses two-party lock)
- Limited resources for initiative campaigns
- Use initiatives for ballot access and electoral reform
- Libertarian and Green parties active

### Wealthy Donors and Corporations

**Position**: Use and oppose strategically

**Use of Initiative Process**:

- Fund qualification campaigns ($1-5 million)
- Dominate advertising ($100+ million on major measures)
- Hire professional signature gatherers
- Draft favorable legislation

**Corporate Examples**:

| Company/Industry | Initiative Use | Purpose |
|-----------------|----------------|---------|
| Uber/Lyft | California Prop 22 | Preserve gig worker classification |
| Tobacco industry | Various states | Preempt local smoking restrictions |
| Casino/gambling | Multiple states | Expand/protect gambling |
| Oil/gas | Colorado Measure 2018 | Block drilling restrictions |

**Opposition to Direct Democracy**:

- Support restrictions on initiative process
- Fund opposition to reform measures
- Lobby legislators against direct democracy
- Legal challenges to passed measures

## Reform Advocates

### Good Government Organizations

**Fix Democracy First Coalition**:

- Multiple organizations
- Focus on process improvements
- Research and advocacy
- State and federal reform

**Specific Organizations**:

| Organization | Focus | Activities |
|--------------|-------|-----------|
| Common Cause | Democracy reform | Research, advocacy, litigation |
| League of Women Voters | Voter education | Initiative guides, forums |
| Brennan Center | Democracy and justice | Research, policy development |
| Public Citizen | Corporate accountability | Campaign finance in initiatives |

### Academic Institutions

**Ballot Initiative Research**:

- Initiative and Referendum Institute (USC)
- National Conference of State Legislatures
- Ballotpedia (nonpartisan tracking)
- Academic journals and research centers

**Citizen Assembly Experts**:

- Deliberative democracy scholars
- Sortition advocates
- Participatory democracy researchers
- International comparative studies

### Progressive Organizations

**Issue Advocates Using Initiatives**:

- Minimum wage campaigns
- Criminal justice reform
- Environmental measures
- Healthcare access

**Process Reformers**:

- Democracy reform as foundational
- Connect policy to process
- Coalition building across issues
- State-level focus

### Technology Innovators

**Digital Democracy Developers**:

- Platform builders (Decidim, etc.)
- Civic tech organizations
- Online participation tools
- Government technology modernizers

**Interests**:

- Expand participation through technology
- Lower barriers to engagement
- Increase transparency
- Scale deliberation

## Reform Opponents

### Status Quo Beneficiaries

**Incumbent Politicians**:

- Prefer representative control
- Fear being bypassed
- Protect gatekeeping power
- Bipartisan resistance

**Special Interests with Legislative Access**:

- Lobbying relationships threatened
- Prefer back-room deals
- Initiative process less controllable
- May lose on popular vote

### Ideological Opponents

**Traditional Conservatives**:

- Concerned about majority tyranny
- Prefer constitutional republic
- Value deliberation over popular vote
- Protect minority (wealthy) interests

**Arguments Made**:

- "Republic not democracy" framing
- Founding fathers' intent
- Complexity requires expertise
- Mob rule concerns

### Minority Rights Advocates

**Legitimate Concerns**:

- History of anti-minority initiatives
- Majority can oppress minority
- Constitutional protections essential
- Some rights shouldn't be voted on

**Position**:

- Support robust judicial review
- Want safeguards built in
- May support some forms (citizen assemblies)
- Oppose plebiscites on fundamental rights

## Stakeholder Interests Matrix

| Stakeholder | Interest in More Direct Democracy | Concerns | Position |
|-------------|----------------------------------|----------|----------|
| General public | Higher engagement, influence | Complexity, manipulation | Generally supportive |
| Marginalized communities | Bypass hostile legislators | Majority tyranny history | Cautious, depends on safeguards |
| State legislators | Avoid controversial votes | Loss of power | Mixed to hostile |
| Congress | None | Loss of power | Opposed |
| Courts | None | Workload, conflicts | Skeptical |
| Wealthy donors | Control through spending | Could lose on popular vote | Strategic |
| Good government groups | Democratic legitimacy | Poor implementation | Supportive with safeguards |
| Progressive organizations | Policy wins | Can also lose | Generally supportive |
| Conservatives | Limited | Majority rule concerns | Generally opposed |
