# Government Transparency: Stakeholders

## Affected Populations

### General Public

**Stakes**:

- Access to information about government activities
- Ability to hold government accountable
- Informed participation in democracy
- Protection from government overreach

**Current Impact**:

- Limited access to meaningful information
- Years-long waits for FOIA responses
- Unable to understand government decisions
- Dependent on press and advocates for information

### Journalists and Media

**Stakes**:

- Core mission depends on access
- Investigative reporting requires records
- FOIA essential tool for accountability journalism
- Source protection for whistleblowers

**Current Impact**:

| Challenge | Effect on Journalism |
|-----------|---------------------|
| FOIA delays | Stories outdated by release |
| Excessive redactions | Incomplete reporting |
| Whistleblower prosecutions | Sources afraid to talk |
| Classification | National security stories blocked |

**Recent Examples**:

- AP had to sue for drone strike records
- Immigration reporting hampered by DHS delays
- COVID-19 data inconsistent and delayed
- Pentagon Papers to Snowden: Reliance on leaks

### Researchers and Academics

**Stakes**:

- Historical research requires government records
- Policy research needs government data
- Scientific research needs agency data
- Social science requires administrative data

**Current Impact**:

- Declassification backlog blocks historical research
- FOIA costs prohibitive for academics
- Data quality insufficient for rigorous analysis
- Classification prevents national security research

### Whistleblowers

**Stakes**:

- Personal and professional safety
- Ability to expose wrongdoing
- Protection from retaliation
- Career and livelihood

**Current Impact**:

- High risk, low protection
- Career destruction common
- Criminal prosecution possible
- Years of legal battles

**Profile of Whistleblowers**:

- Often long-serving, committed employees
- Report through channels first (usually)
- Face retaliation regardless of channel
- Rarely achieve satisfying outcome

### Advocacy Organizations

**Stakes**:

- Organizational mission depends on information
- Litigation requires government records
- Advocacy informed by data
- Accountability campaigns need evidence

**Key Organizations**:

| Organization | Focus |
|--------------|-------|
| ACLU | Civil liberties, surveillance |
| EFF | Digital rights, technology |
| Project on Government Oversight | Waste, fraud, abuse |
| Government Accountability Project | Whistleblower support |
| Reporters Committee | Press freedom |
| National Security Archive | Declassification |
| OpenTheGovernment | Coalition coordination |

## Power Holders

### Executive Branch Agencies

**Position**: Generally resist transparency

**Interests**:

- Protect deliberative processes
- Avoid embarrassment
- Maintain operational security
- Minimize workload

**Current Behavior**:

- Broad exemption interpretation
- Slow processing
- Excessive classification
- Resistance to proactive disclosure

### Intelligence Community

**Position**: Maximum secrecy

**Interests**:

- Protect sources and methods
- Maintain operational advantage
- Prevent oversight interference
- Control information flow

**Key Agencies**:

- CIA: Minimal disclosure, heavy redaction
- NSA: Near-total secrecy
- FBI: National security and law enforcement exemptions
- DNI: Coordinates community-wide secrecy

### White House

**Position**: Protect executive privilege, varies by administration

**Interests**:

- Shield presidential deliberations
- Control narrative
- Protect political interests
- Avoid accountability for failures

**Pattern**: Every administration claims to support transparency while resisting in practice

### Congress

**Position**: Mixed

**Interests**:

- Oversight of executive branch
- But: Own records largely exempt
- Some members champion transparency
- Others protect executive allies

**Key Committees**:

- House Oversight: FOIA jurisdiction
- Senate Judiciary: Whistleblower protections
- Intelligence Committees: Classification oversight
- Appropriations: Transparency funding

### Courts

**Position**: Generally deferential to executive

**Interests**:

- Apply legal standards
- But: National security deference strong
- State secrets doctrine expands
- FOIA litigation often favors agencies

**Key Doctrines**:

- State secrets privilege
- Glomar response ("neither confirm nor deny")
- Mosaic theory (aggregate harmf from disclosure)
- Deference to classification decisions

- Training and resources

## Reform Opponents

### National Security Establishment

**Position**: Secrecy essential for security

**Arguments**:

- Disclosure endangers operations
- Sources and methods must be protected
- Enemies benefit from openness
- Classification prevents harm

**Organizations**:

- Intelligence agencies
- Defense Department
- National Security Council
- Classification authorities

### Agency Administrators

**Position**: Discretion necessary

**Arguments**:

- Deliberative process needs confidentiality
- FOIA burdensome and expensive
- Exemptions protect legitimate interests
- Current system works adequately

### Some Business Interests

**Position**: Protect proprietary information

**Arguments**:

- Trade secrets at risk
- Competitive information exposed
- FOIA used by competitors
- Exemption 4 essential

### Law Enforcement

**Position**: Investigative secrecy essential

**Arguments**:

- Disclosure compromises investigations
- Witness safety concerns
- Techniques would be evaded
- Exemption 7 critical
