# Redistricting Reform: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Redistricting reform primarily occurs at the state level, as states control how congressional and state legislative districts are drawn. This document provides model legislation for independent redistricting commissions, fair criteria, and transparency requirements.

## State Model Legislation

### Independent Redistricting Commission Act (Model)

**Purpose**: Establish an independent commission to draw legislative and congressional districts, removing partisan control.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Independent Redistricting
Commission Act".

SECTION 2. FINDINGS.

The [Legislature/People] find(s) that:
(a) Partisan gerrymandering distorts representation.
(b) Voters should choose their representatives, not the reverse.
(c) Independent commissions produce fairer maps.
(d) Transparency in redistricting increases public trust.

SECTION 3. ESTABLISHMENT.

There is established the [State] Independent Redistricting Commission.

SECTION 4. COMPOSITION.

(a) MEMBERS.—The Commission shall consist of 15 members:
    (1) 5 members affiliated with the largest political party;
    (2) 5 members affiliated with the second-largest political party;
    (3) 5 members not affiliated with either of the two largest
        political parties.

(b) QUALIFICATIONS.—To be eligible for membership, a person—
    (1) must be a registered voter in [State];
    (2) must have voted in at least 2 of the last 3 statewide
        general elections;
    (3) may not have held partisan elective office within the
        preceding 10 years;
    (4) may not have served as a partisan legislative or
        congressional staff member within the preceding 5 years;
    (5) may not have been a registered lobbyist within the
        preceding 5 years;
    (6) may not have served as a paid campaign worker or
        consultant within the preceding 5 years;
    (7) may not be a relative of any of the above within the
        third degree of consanguinity.

(c) DISQUALIFICATION AFTER SERVICE.—A person who serves on the
    Commission may not—
    (1) hold partisan elective office in [State] for 10 years;
    (2) be employed by the Legislature for 5 years.

SECTION 5. SELECTION PROCESS.

(a) APPLICATION.—The [State Auditor/Nonpartisan Agency] shall—
    (1) solicit applications from eligible voters;
    (2) review applications for qualifications;
    (3) create a pool of at least 60 qualified applicants
        (20 per category).

(b) RANDOM SELECTION.—
    (1) The [Auditor] shall randomly select 8 commissioners
        (3 + 3 + 2) from the qualified pool.
    (2) The 8 selected commissioners shall select the remaining
        7 commissioners (2 + 2 + 3) from the remaining pool.

(c) CHAIR.—The Commission shall select a chair from among its
    members not affiliated with either major party.

(d) TIMELINE.—Selection shall be completed by March 1 of each
    year ending in 1.

SECTION 6. OPERATIONS.

(a) QUORUM.—A quorum consists of 9 members, including at least
    2 from each category.

(b) VOTING.—
    (1) Adoption of maps requires 9 votes, including at least
        3 from each category.
    (2) Other actions require a majority of members present.

(c) COMPENSATION.—Members shall receive per diem compensation
    and reimbursement of expenses.

(d) STAFF.—The Commission may hire staff and consultants.

(e) BUDGET.—The Legislature shall appropriate sufficient funds
    for Commission operations.

SECTION 7. REDISTRICTING CRITERIA.

(a) MANDATORY CRITERIA (in order of priority)—
    (1) Districts shall comply with the U.S. Constitution and the
        Voting Rights Act;
    (2) Districts shall be contiguous;
    (3) Districts shall be equal in population to the extent
        practicable (for congressional districts) or as nearly
        equal as practicable (for legislative districts);
    (4) Districts shall comply with the [State] Constitution.

(b) DISCRETIONARY CRITERIA (no priority order)—
    (1) Districts should be reasonably compact;
    (2) Districts should respect city and county boundaries;
    (3) Districts should keep communities of interest together;
    (4) Districts should be geographically compact.

(c) PROHIBITED CONSIDERATIONS.—Districts may not be drawn with
    the intent to—
    (1) favor or disfavor any political party;
    (2) favor or disfavor any incumbent or candidate;
    (3) favor or disfavor any member of the Commission.

(d) DATA.—The Commission may not consider—
    (1) addresses of incumbents or candidates;
    (2) political party registration data (except for VRA
        compliance);
    (3) previous election results (except for VRA compliance).

SECTION 8. PROCESS.

(a) PUBLIC HEARINGS.—The Commission shall hold at least 10 public
    hearings across [State]—
    (1) at least 5 before draft maps are released;
    (2) at least 5 after draft maps are released.

(b) DRAFT MAPS.—The Commission shall—
    (1) publish draft maps at least 30 days before final vote;
    (2) provide online tools for public comment;
    (3) respond in writing to substantive public comments.

(c) TRANSPARENCY.—
    (1) All meetings shall be open to the public;
    (2) All data and software used shall be made public;
    (3) All communications with commissioners shall be public.

(d) FINAL MAPS.—The Commission shall adopt final maps by
    [deadline, e.g., November 1] of each year ending in 1.

SECTION 9. FAILURE TO ADOPT.

If the Commission fails to adopt maps by the deadline, the
[State Supreme Court] shall appoint a special master to draw
maps using the criteria in Section 7.

SECTION 10. JUDICIAL REVIEW.

(a) Any registered voter may challenge final maps in [State
    Supreme Court].

(b) The Court shall review de novo whether maps comply with
    Section 7.

(c) If maps are found invalid, the Commission shall adopt new
    maps within 30 days.

SECTION 11. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect immediately and applies to redistricting
following the [next census].

```text

**Explanation**:

- **Section 4**: 5-5-5 structure ensures no party controls.
- **Section 5**: Random selection reduces political influence.
- **Section 6(b)**: Supermajority with all categories prevents partisan maps.
- **Section 7(c)-(d)**: Prohibits partisan data to prevent gerrymandering.

### Redistricting Transparency Act (Model)

**Purpose**: For states that retain legislative redistricting, require transparency and public input.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Redistricting Transparency Act".

SECTION 2. PUBLIC HEARINGS.

(a) Before introducing any redistricting plan, the Legislature
    shall hold at least 3 public hearings in each congressional
    district.

(b) After introducing any redistricting plan, the Legislature
    shall hold at least 1 public hearing in each congressional
    district before final passage.

SECTION 3. DATA AND SOFTWARE.

(a) All data used to develop redistricting plans shall be made
    publicly available.

(b) All software or tools used shall be identified and their
    methodologies disclosed.

(c) The public shall have access to the same redistricting
    software used by the Legislature.

SECTION 4. WAITING PERIOD.

(a) No redistricting plan may be voted upon less than 14 days
    after public introduction.

(b) Any amendment to a redistricting plan restarts the 14-day
    period.

SECTION 5. ALTERNATIVE PLANS.

(a) Any legislator may introduce an alternative redistricting plan.

(b) All plans meeting constitutional requirements shall receive
    a recorded vote.

SECTION 6. CRITERIA DISCLOSURE.

(a) When introducing a redistricting plan, the sponsor shall
    include a report explaining—
    (1) the criteria used in drawing districts;
    (2) how the plan complies with the Voting Rights Act;
    (3) any communities of interest considered;
    (4) measures of compactness.

SECTION 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect immediately.

```text

### Fair Maps Standards Act (Model)

**Purpose**: Establish criteria that any redistricting plan must meet.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Fair Maps Standards Act".

SECTION 2. REQUIRED CRITERIA.

Any redistricting plan for [State] legislative or congressional
districts shall:

(a) POPULATION EQUALITY.—
    (1) Congressional districts shall have equal population.
    (2) Legislative districts shall be as nearly equal in
        population as practicable.

(b) VOTING RIGHTS ACT.—Comply with the Voting Rights Act of 1965,
    including creating majority-minority districts where required.

(c) CONTIGUITY.—All districts shall be contiguous by land.

(d) COMPACTNESS.—Districts shall be reasonably compact, as measured
    by at least two of the following metrics:
    (1) Polsby-Popper;
    (2) Reock;
    (3) Convex Hull.

(e) POLITICAL SUBDIVISIONS.—District lines shall follow city and
    county boundaries where practicable.

(f) COMMUNITIES OF INTEREST.—Districts shall keep communities
    of interest together where practicable.

SECTION 3. PROHIBITED CONSIDERATIONS.

No redistricting plan may be drawn with the intent to:

(a) Unduly favor or disfavor any political party;

(b) Protect or harm any incumbent or candidate;

(c) Dilute the voting power of any racial or language minority.

SECTION 4. METRICS.

(a) Any redistricting plan submitted for enactment shall include
    quantitative analysis of—
    (1) population deviation;
    (2) compactness scores;
    (3) county and city splits;
    (4) partisan fairness metrics (efficiency gap, mean-median
        difference, partisan bias);
    (5) competitiveness (number of competitive districts).

(b) Plans failing to meet reasonable standards on these metrics
    shall be presumed to violate Section 3.

SECTION 5. JUDICIAL REVIEW.

(a) Any registered voter may challenge a redistricting plan as
    violating this Act.

(b) Courts shall give deference to the factual findings of the
    redistricting body but review compliance with this Act de novo.

(c) If a plan is found invalid, the redistricting body shall have
    60 days to adopt a compliant plan.

SECTION 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act applies to all redistricting following its enactment.

```text

## Federal Legislation

### Freedom to Vote Act (Redistricting Provisions)

**Purpose**: Establish federal standards for congressional redistricting.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. CONGRESSIONAL REDISTRICTING STANDARDS.

(a) CRITERIA.—In establishing congressional districts, each State
    shall apply the following criteria (in order of priority):
    (1) Population equality;
    (2) Compliance with the Voting Rights Act;
    (3) Contiguity;
    (4) Prohibition on partisan gerrymandering;
    (5) Respect for political subdivisions and communities of
        interest;
    (6) Compactness.

(b) PARTISAN GERRYMANDERING PROHIBITION.—
    (1) No congressional map shall be drawn with the intent or
        effect of unduly favoring or disfavoring any political party.
    (2) Effect shall be measured by established metrics including
        efficiency gap, mean-median difference, and partisan bias.

SEC. 2. TRANSPARENCY.

(a) PUBLIC INPUT.—States shall hold public hearings before adopting
    any congressional map.

(b) DATA DISCLOSURE.—All data and software used shall be publicly
    available.

(c) WAITING PERIOD.—Maps shall be public for at least 10 days
    before adoption.

SEC. 3. INDEPENDENT REDISTRICTING.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Each State shall establish an independent
    redistricting commission or other independent entity to
    develop congressional maps.

(b) INDEPENDENCE.—The entity shall be independent of the
    legislature and political parties.

(c) STATE FLEXIBILITY.—States may design commissions consistent
    with this section and state law.

SEC. 4. JUDICIAL REVIEW.

(a) Any citizen may bring an action in federal court challenging
    a congressional map as violating this Act.

(b) Courts shall use the metrics in section 1(b) to evaluate
    claims of partisan gerrymandering.

(c) If a map is found invalid, the court shall order a new map
    within 60 days.

SEC. 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This section applies to redistricting following the 2030 census.

```text

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

**State Authority**:

- Article I, Section 4: States prescribe "time, place, and manner" of elections
- *Rucho v. Common Cause* (2019): Partisan gerrymandering claims not justiciable in federal courts—but state courts and state laws remain available

**State Constitutional Provisions**:

Many states have adopted redistricting criteria through constitutional amendments, which are beyond legislative override.

**Voting Rights Act**:

All redistricting must comply with Section 2 (prohibition on vote dilution) and any remaining Section 5 preclearance requirements.

| Issue | Constitutional Status |
|-------|----------------------|
| Independent commissions | Constitutional (*Arizona State Legislature v. Arizona Independent Redistricting Commission*, 2015) |
| Partisan gerrymandering limits | State courts can enforce; federal courts won't |
| Racial gerrymandering | Unconstitutional (*Shaw v. Reno*, 1993) |
| VRA compliance | Required |

### Implementation Considerations

| Consideration | Approach |
|---------------|----------|
| Census data | Available in fall of year ending in 0 |
| Commission selection | Should begin early in year ending in 1 |
| Public hearings | Multiple rounds required |
| Deadlines | Maps needed for candidate filing |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

This section identifies potential weaknesses in the proposed legislation and provides mechanisms for addressing them.

### Independent Redistricting Commission Act (Model)

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Application pool manipulation | Partisan actors may flood application pool with aligned "independents" | Critical |
| Staffing influence | Commission staff and consultants may have undisclosed partisan ties | High |
| Communities of interest definition | Vague term can be exploited to justify partisan outcomes | High |
| Data prohibition workarounds | Commissioners may access prohibited political data through personal knowledge | High |
| Third-party category gaming | Voters may register as "independent" while maintaining strong partisan ties | Medium |
| Budget manipulation | Legislature may underfund commission to limit effectiveness | Medium |
| Supermajority deadlock | 9-vote requirement with 3 from each category may produce stalemate | Medium |
| Selection timeline | March deadline may be too late for adequate map development | Low |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Geographic knowledge limits | Commissioners may not understand local communities | Lay commission design |
| Technical complexity | Map-drawing requires technical expertise commissioners lack | Expertise vs. independence tradeoff |
| Legal vulnerability | Maps may be overturned if commissioners misunderstand VRA requirements | Non-expert judgment |
| Public participation limits | 10 hearings may be insufficient for diverse state | Resource constraints |
| Special master uncertainty | Court-appointed fallback has own biases and limitations | Backstop necessity |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Application vetting**: Require social media and donation history review for applicant screening
2. **Staff independence**: Require staff disclosure of partisan history; prohibit recent political employees
3. **Communities of interest definition**: Establish concrete criteria (school districts, media markets, economic zones)
4. **Data firewall**: Create secure data environment where commissioners cannot access political data
5. **Budget protection**: Establish minimum funding formula based on state population
6. **Deadlock resolution**: Allow tie-breaking through ranked consideration of multiple proposed maps

### Redistricting Transparency Act (Model)

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Hearing location manipulation | Hearings may be held at inconvenient times/locations to suppress participation | High |
| Software disclosure limits | "Methodology" disclosure may be vague while hiding actual algorithms | High |
| 14-day window gaming | Minor amendments may restart clock repeatedly to exhaust opponents | Medium |
| Alternative plan obstruction | Majority may prevent alternative plans from receiving fair consideration | Medium |
| Report manipulation | Sponsor reports may use misleading metrics or selective analysis | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Transparency without power | Public may participate but legislature ignores input | Legislative control unchanged |
| Technical complexity | Public may lack expertise to provide meaningful input | Knowledge asymmetry |
| Time constraints | Census-to-election timeline limits meaningful public process | Constitutional deadlines |
| Partisan majority control | Transparency doesn't change underlying partisan map-drawing | Structural issue |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Hearing standards**: Require geographic distribution, evening/weekend hours, and virtual options
2. **Software access**: Mandate public access to identical tools and training, not just disclosure
3. **Amendment streamlining**: Allow grouped minor amendments without resetting waiting period
4. **Alternative plan process**: Require scored comparison of all qualifying alternative plans
5. **Independent analysis**: Fund nonpartisan analysis of all proposed maps

### Fair Maps Standards Act (Model)

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Metric selection**: Map-drawers may choose compactness/fairness metrics that favor their preferred maps | High |
| "Reasonably compact" subjectivity | Vague standard allows wide interpretation | High |
| Communities of interest definition | Selectively defined communities may mask partisan intent | High |
| "Practicable" escape clauses | Multiple practicability exceptions enable workarounds | Medium |
| Threshold selection | Metric thresholds for "reasonable standards" undefined | Medium |
| Political subdivision gaming | Splitting some subdivisions may be justified while benefiting one party | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Criteria ordering ambiguity | "No priority order" for discretionary criteria creates litigation uncertainty | Compromise language |
| VRA-partisan tension | VRA compliance may require some consideration of political data | Legal requirements |
| Metric limitations | No single metric captures gerrymandering; manipulation possible with any set | Statistical complexity |
| Enforcement timing | Challenges take years while elections proceed under disputed maps | Judicial timelines |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Metric specification**: Require disclosure of all metrics considered with explanation for choices
2. **Compactness standards**: Establish minimum scores on multiple metrics
3. **Communities of interest registry**: Require advance submission and documentation of communities of interest
4. **Threshold establishment**: Set specific metric thresholds based on historical norms
5. **Expedited enforcement**: Mandate court decisions within 90 days with automatic stay pending resolution

### Freedom to Vote Act (Redistricting Provisions)

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| "Independent entity" definition | States may create nominally independent bodies controlled by partisans | Critical |
| Metric selection | Efficiency gap, mean-median can be gamed with knowledge of thresholds | High |
| 60-day remedy window | Insufficient time for meaningful new map development | Medium |
| State flexibility exploitation | Loose standards allow wide interpretation of independence | Medium |
| 2030 application | Delayed implementation allows another decade of gerrymandering | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Constitutional uncertainty | Federal courts may strike down federal redistricting requirements | Rucho precedent |
| Preclearance alternative | Effect-based standard less protective than preclearance | Political feasibility |
| State legislative maps excluded | Only addresses congressional redistricting | Federalism limits |
| Partisan intent proof | Proving intent remains difficult even with metrics | Evidentiary challenges |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Independence standards**: Establish detailed criteria for what constitutes independent entity
2. **Multi-metric requirement**: Require maps to pass multiple fairness metrics, not just one
3. **Expedited remediation**: Require new maps within 30 days; provide for special master if missed
4. **Immediate application**: Apply to current redistricting cycle through emergency provisions
5. **State legislative extension**: Use spending power to incentivize state-level compliance

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Rucho precedent | Focus on state courts and state constitutional provisions |
| Technical expertise gap | Fund nonpartisan redistricting resources and training |
| Census timing constraints | Advocate for earlier census data release |
| Incumbent self-interest | Constitutional amendments to remove legislative map-drawing authority |
| Judicial politicization | Merit selection for judges hearing redistricting cases |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

All provisions should include:

- **Post-cycle evaluation** after each redistricting analyzing competitiveness, representation, and process
- **Commission performance review** assessing commissioner selection, public input, and map quality
- **Criteria effectiveness assessment** evaluating whether specified criteria prevented gerrymandering
- **Legal challenge analysis** reviewing litigation outcomes and identifying needed clarifications
- **Technology update requirements** ensuring mapping technology and data access remain current
