# Political Violence: History

## Early Republic (1787-1860)

### Violence in Early American Politics

Political violence was common in the early republic:

**Dueling**:

- Hamilton-Burr duel (1804): Sitting Vice President killed former Treasury Secretary
- Numerous other political duels
- Andrew Jackson multiple duels
- Gradually declined by mid-19th century

**Congressional Violence**:

- Numerous physical altercations in Congress
- Matthew Lyon and Roger Griswold fight (1798)
- Violence escalated as slavery tensions rose

**Political Mobs**:

- Destruction of abolitionist printing presses
- Mob attacks on political opponents
- Election day violence common

### Preston Brooks and Charles Sumner (1856)

Watershed moment:

- Rep. Brooks (SC) beat Sen. Sumner (MA) nearly to death on Senate floor
- Sumner's anti-slavery speech triggered attack
- Brooks became hero in South, villain in North
- Symbolized sectional breakdown preceding Civil War

### Slave Resistance and Abolition Violence

- Nat Turner's Rebellion (1831)
- John Brown's raid on Harpers Ferry (1859)
- Bleeding Kansas (1854-1858)
- Violence integral to slavery conflict

## Civil War and Reconstruction (1861-1877)

### Civil War as Political Violence

The ultimate political violence:

- 620,000+ deaths
- Resulted from failure of political accommodation
- Resolved slavery question by force

### Reconstruction Violence

Systematic violence against Black political participation:

**Ku Klux Klan (First Era)**:

- Founded 1865-1866
- Systematic terrorism against freedpeople
- Assassinations of Republican officials
- Destruction of Black political power

**Major Incidents**:

| Incident | Year | Deaths |
|----------|------|--------|
| Colfax Massacre | 1873 | 100+ |
| Coushatta Massacre | 1874 | 20+ |
| Hamburg Massacre | 1876 | 7 |
| New Orleans Massacre | 1866 | 40+ |

**Federal Response**:

- Ku Klux Klan Acts (1870-1871)
- Federal troops deployed
- Initial success in suppressing Klan
- Abandoned after 1877 Compromise

### Violence Succeeded

Reconstruction violence achieved political goals:

- Restored white Democratic control in South
- Ended Black political participation
- Established Jim Crow system
- Violence rewarded, not punished

## Gilded Age and Progressive Era (1877-1920)

### Labor Violence

Political violence in labor conflicts:

| Event | Year | Casualties |
|-------|------|------------|
| Great Railroad Strike | 1877 | 100+ |
| Haymarket Affair | 1886 | 11 |
| Homestead Strike | 1892 | 16 |
| Pullman Strike | 1894 | 30+ |
| Ludlow Massacre | 1914 | 25 |

**State and Private Violence**:

- National Guard deployed against strikers
- Pinkerton agents used by companies
- Workers also used violence

### Assassinations

Presidential assassinations:

- Lincoln (1865): Confederate sympathizer
- Garfield (1881): Disappointed office-seeker
- McKinley (1901): Anarchist

**Other Political Assassinations**:

- Numerous state and local officials
- Violence against labor organizers
- Lynching as political terrorism

### Lynching and Racial Terror

Systematic violence continued:

| Period | Estimated Lynchings |
|--------|---------------------|
| 1882-1892 | 1,600+ |
| 1893-1903 | 1,400+ |
| 1904-1914 | 800+ |

**Purposes**:

- Political intimidation
- Economic control
- Social terror
- Enforcing racial hierarchy

### Anarchist Violence

Anarchist movement included violent wing:

- Assassination of President McKinley (1901)
- Bombing campaigns
- Led to early federal security measures
- Palmer Raids (1919-1920) as response

## Interwar Period (1920-1945)

### Klan Revival (Second Era)

Second Klan (1915-1944):

- Millions of members
- Politically powerful in many states
- Violence against Catholics, Jews, immigrants, Black Americans
- Eventually declined due to scandals

### Labor and Political Violence

Continued labor conflict:

- Battle of Blair Mountain (1921)
- Memorial Day Massacre (1937)
- Various strikes and confrontations

### Red Scare Violence

Anti-communist violence:

- Palmer Raids
- Vigilante actions
- State repression

## Civil Rights Era (1954-1970s)

### Violence Against Civil Rights Movement

Systematic terror against integration efforts:

**Major Incidents**:

| Event | Year | Deaths/Impact |
|-------|------|---------------|
| Emmett Till murder | 1955 | 1 |
| Birmingham church bombing | 1963 | 4 |
| Freedom Summer murders | 1964 | 3 |
| Selma | 1965 | Multiple injuries, deaths |
| MLK assassination | 1968 | 1 |

**Patterns**:

- State complicity (local law enforcement)
- Federal intervention required
- Violence galvanized support for civil rights
- Eventual federal protection

### Civil Rights Movement Response

Movement's commitment to non-violence:

- Strategic choice
- Moral position
- Effective politically
- Contrasted with opponents' violence

### Late 1960s-1970s Violence

Rise of political violence from multiple sources:

**Left-Wing Violence**:

- Weather Underground bombings
- Black Liberation Army
- Symbionese Liberation Army
- Various other groups

**Right-Wing Violence**:

- Continued KKK terrorism
- Anti-abortion violence begins
- Various militia precursors

**Assassinations and Attempts**:

- RFK (1968)
- George Wallace attempt (1972)
- Gerald Ford attempts (1975)

## Late 20th Century (1980-2000)

### Domestic Terrorism Waves

**Anti-Abortion Violence**:

- Clinic bombings
- Assassinations of doctors
- Army of God and others

**White Supremacist Violence**:

- The Order (1983-1984)
- Various attacks
- Ruby Ridge (1992)
- Waco (1993)

**Oklahoma City Bombing (1995)**:

- 168 killed
- Domestic terrorism watershed
- Anti-government extremism
- Timothy McVeigh executed

### Militias and Patriot Movement

1990s militia movement:

- Response to federal actions (Ruby Ridge, Waco)
- Fear of gun control
- New World Order conspiracy theories
- Peaked mid-1990s, declined after Oklahoma City

### Hate Crimes Recognition

Development of hate crime framework:

- Federal hate crime laws
- State hate crime laws
- Recognition of political motivation
- Data collection begun

## Post-9/11 Era (2001-2016)

### Focus on International Terrorism

Resources shifted to international threats:

- 9/11 attacks
- War on Terror
- Domestic terrorism de-emphasized
- FBI resources redirected

Domestic terrorism continued but received less attention:

| Incident | Year | Deaths |

| Oak Creek Sikh temple | 2012 | 6 |

### Rise of New Movements

New extremist movements emerged:

- Alt-right development
- Online radicalization
- New militia activity
- Sovereign citizen movement

Political violence escalated dramatically:

**Major Incidents**:

| Event | Year | Deaths/Impact |
|-------|------|---------------|
| Charlottesville | 2017 | 1 killed, many injured |
| Pittsburgh synagogue | 2018 | 11 |

### Threats to Officials

Unprecedented threats:

- Kavanaugh attempted assassination (2022)
- Paul Pelosi attack (2022)
- Thousands of threats to members of Congress
- Election worker threats

### January 6, 2021

Watershed moment in American political violence:

- Violent attack on Capitol
- Attempt to prevent certification
- Multiple deaths
- Hundreds prosecuted
- Ongoing political dispute

### Current Threat Assessment

FBI and DHS assess:

- Domestic terrorism highest threat
- Racially motivated violent extremists
- Anti-government extremists
- Growing threat environment

## Patterns and Lessons

### Violence and Political Change

| Pattern | Examples |
|---------|----------|
| Violence to maintain power | Reconstruction, Jim Crow |
| Violence against change | Civil rights opponents |
| Violence for change | Some movements |
| Violence backfiring | Oklahoma City |

### State Response Patterns

- Initial tolerance or complicity
- Crisis triggers response
- Federal intervention often required
- Sustained commitment necessary

### Warning Signs

Historical patterns that preceded escalation:

- Dehumanizing rhetoric
- Delegitimizing institutions
- Conspiracy theories
- Organized preparation
- Failure to condemn
