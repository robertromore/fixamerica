# Political Violence: Legislation

## Overview

This document provides model legislation to address political violence, including protection for public officials and election workers, domestic terrorism response, prevention programs, and platform accountability. Legislation must carefully balance security needs with civil liberties protections.

## Federal Legislation

### Election Worker Protection Act

**Purpose**: Protect election workers from threats, harassment, and violence.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Election Worker Protection Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
(1) Election workers perform essential duties for democratic
    elections;
(2) Threats against election workers have increased dramatically;
(3) Many election workers have resigned due to safety concerns;
(4) Current federal protections are inadequate;
(5) Strong protections are necessary to ensure election
    administration.

SEC. 3. FEDERAL PENALTIES FOR ELECTION WORKER THREATS.

(a) OFFENSE.—Whoever knowingly and willfully threatens to
    injure or intimidate any election worker, or their immediate
    family, with the intent to influence or retaliate for the
    performance of official duties, shall be fined under this
    title, imprisoned for not more than 5 years, or both.

(b) ENHANCED PENALTY.—If the threat involves a firearm or
    other dangerous weapon, or if the threat causes physical
    injury, the penalty shall be imprisonment for not more
    than 10 years.

(c) DEFINITIONS.—
    (1) "Election worker" means any individual who performs
        or assists in performing election administration duties,
        including election officials, poll workers, vote counters,
        and election volunteers.
    (2) "Election administration duties" includes registration,
        voting, counting, canvassing, certifying, and auditing.

SEC. 4. PERSONAL INFORMATION PROTECTION.

(a) PROHIBITION.—It shall be unlawful to knowingly publish
    the personal information of an election worker with the
    intent to facilitate threats or harassment.

(b) PERSONAL INFORMATION.—Includes home address, personal
    phone numbers, personal email, family member information,
    and daily schedule.

(c) PENALTY.—Violation shall be punished by fine under this
    title, imprisonment for not more than 3 years, or both.

SEC. 5. ELECTION SECURITY GRANTS.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated
    $100,000,000 annually for grants to states and localities
    for election worker security.

(b) USE OF FUNDS.—
    (1) Physical security improvements;
    (2) Threat assessment and response training;
    (3) Personal information protection systems;
    (4) Worker support services;
    (5) Coordination with law enforcement.

(c) ALLOCATION.—Grants shall be distributed based on:
    (1) Number of election workers;
    (2) Documented threat levels;
    (3) Geographic considerations.

SEC. 6. DOJ ELECTION THREATS TASK FORCE.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Attorney General shall maintain a
    task force dedicated to investigating and prosecuting
    threats against election workers.

(b) REPORTING.—The task force shall report annually to
    Congress on threats investigated, prosecutions, and
    recommendations.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 90 days after enactment.
```

### Domestic Terrorism Prevention Act

**Purpose**: Strengthen federal capacity to address domestic terrorism while protecting civil liberties.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Domestic Terrorism Prevention
Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
(1) Domestic terrorism constitutes a significant threat to
    national security;
(2) Federal resources dedicated to domestic terrorism have
    historically been inadequate;
(3) Enhanced capacity is needed while protecting civil
    liberties;
(4) Coordinated federal response is essential.

SEC. 3. DOMESTIC TERRORISM OFFICES.

(a) FBI.—The Director of the FBI shall maintain a Domestic
    Terrorism Unit with responsibility for:
    (1) Investigation of domestic terrorism;
    (2) Intelligence analysis;
    (3) Coordination with state and local agencies;
    (4) Prevention initiatives.

(b) DHS.—The Secretary of Homeland Security shall maintain
    an Office of Domestic Terrorism Prevention with
    responsibility for:
    (1) Targeted violence prevention;
    (2) Information sharing;
    (3) Grant administration;
    (4) Training and technical assistance.

(c) DOJ.—The Attorney General shall maintain a Domestic
    Terrorism Counsel position with responsibility for:
    (1) Prosecution coordination;
    (2) Policy development;
    (3) Civil liberties compliance;
    (4) Congressional liaison.

SEC. 4. REPORTING REQUIREMENTS.

(a) ANNUAL REPORT.—The FBI, DHS, and DOJ shall jointly
    submit to Congress an annual report on domestic terrorism,
    including:
    (1) Number and nature of incidents;
    (2) Investigations and prosecutions;
    (3) Prevention activities;
    (4) Threat assessment;
    (5) Recommendations.

(b) PUBLIC RELEASE.—An unclassified summary shall be made
    publicly available.

SEC. 5. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated:
(a) FBI Domestic Terrorism Unit: $100,000,000 annually;
(b) DHS Prevention Programs: $75,000,000 annually;
(c) DOJ Prosecution Support: $25,000,000 annually;
(d) Joint Terrorism Task Force Support: $50,000,000 annually.

SEC. 6. CIVIL LIBERTIES PROTECTIONS.

(a) PROHIBITION ON IDEOLOGY-BASED INVESTIGATIONS.—
    (1) No investigation may be initiated solely on the basis
        of activities protected by the First Amendment.
    (2) Investigations must be predicated on articulable facts
        indicating potential criminal conduct.

(b) OVERSIGHT.—
    (1) The Inspector General of DOJ shall conduct annual
        audits of domestic terrorism activities.
    (2) The Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board shall
        review programs for civil liberties compliance.

(c) TRAINING.—All personnel assigned to domestic terrorism
    duties shall receive training on civil liberties
    protections and First Amendment limitations.

SEC. 7. DEFINITIONS.

"Domestic terrorism" has the meaning given in 18 U.S.C.
§ 2331(5).

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 180 days after enactment.
```

### Public Official Threat Enhancement Act

**Purpose**: Enhance penalties for threats against public officials at all levels of government.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Public Official Threat
Enhancement Act".

SEC. 2. ENHANCED PENALTIES.

(a) THREATS AGAINST FEDERAL OFFICIALS.—Section 115 of title
    18 is amended to increase maximum penalties for threats
    against federal officials and their families.

(b) NEW OFFENSE.—It shall be a federal offense to threaten
    state or local officials with the intent to intimidate
    or retaliate for the performance of official duties,
    when the threat uses interstate communications or the
    mails.

(c) PENALTIES.—
    (1) Base offense: Fine and/or imprisonment up to 5 years
    (2) If using dangerous weapon: Up to 10 years
    (3) If causing physical injury: Up to 20 years

SEC. 3. FEDERAL JURISDICTION.

(a) INTERSTATE THREATS.—Federal jurisdiction shall apply
    when threats are made using:
    (1) Interstate or foreign communications;
    (2) The mails;
    (3) Interstate travel.

(b) CONCURRENT JURISDICTION.—Federal jurisdiction shall be
    concurrent with state jurisdiction, not exclusive.

SEC. 4. VICTIM SERVICES.

(a) GRANT PROGRAM.—The Attorney General shall make grants
    to support services for public officials targeted by
    threats, including:
    (1) Security consultation;
    (2) Counseling services;
    (3) Legal assistance;
    (4) Relocation assistance in extreme cases.

(b) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated
    $25,000,000 annually.

SEC. 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 90 days after enactment.
```

### Targeted Violence Prevention Act

**Purpose**: Establish and fund comprehensive prevention programs.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Targeted Violence Prevention
Act".

SEC. 2. GRANT PROGRAM.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary of Homeland Security shall
    establish a grant program to support targeted violence
    prevention.

(b) ELIGIBLE RECIPIENTS.—
    (1) State and local governments;
    (2) Educational institutions;
    (3) Nonprofit organizations;
    (4) Community-based organizations;
    (5) Mental health providers.

(c) USE OF FUNDS.—
    (1) Threat assessment programs;
    (2) Intervention services;
    (3) Mental health integration;
    (4) Exit programs for extremists;
    (5) Community resilience building;
    (6) Research and evaluation.

(d) AUTHORIZATION.—$100,000,000 annually.

SEC. 3. NATIONAL PREVENTION RESOURCE CENTER.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary shall establish a National
    Targeted Violence Prevention Resource Center.

(b) FUNCTIONS.—
    (1) Develop best practices;
    (2) Provide technical assistance;
    (3) Support research;
    (4) Disseminate information;
    (5) Training for prevention practitioners.

SEC. 4. CIVIL LIBERTIES PROTECTIONS.

(a) VOLUNTARY PARTICIPATION.—Prevention programs shall be
    voluntary except where court-ordered.

(b) PRIVACY.—Programs shall protect participant privacy
    consistent with applicable law.

(c) NON-DISCRIMINATION.—Programs shall not target
    individuals based on race, religion, ethnicity, or
    political viewpoint.

SEC. 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 180 days after enactment.
```

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Election Worker Protection Act

**Purpose**: State-level protection for election workers.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Election Worker
Protection Act".

SECTION 2. OFFENSES.

(a) THREATS.—Any person who threatens an election worker
    with the intent to influence or retaliate for the
    performance of official duties commits a [Class C
    felony/equivalent].

(b) HARASSMENT.—Any person who engages in a course of
    conduct directed at an election worker that would
    cause a reasonable person to feel intimidated commits
    a [Class A misdemeanor/equivalent].

(c) DOXXING.—Any person who publishes the personal
    information of an election worker with intent to
    facilitate harassment commits a [Class B misdemeanor/
    equivalent].

SECTION 3. PROTECTIVE ORDERS.

(a) Election workers may obtain protective orders against
    individuals who have threatened or harassed them.

(b) Courts shall expedite consideration of such orders.

SECTION 4. SECURITY PROGRAM.

(a) The [Secretary of State/election authority] shall
    establish a program to provide security assistance
    to election workers.

(b) The program may include:
    (1) Security assessments;
    (2) Personal information protection;
    (3) Training;
    (4) Emergency response coordination.

SECTION 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on [date].
```

### Model State Domestic Terrorism Data Collection

**Purpose**: Improve state-level data on domestic terrorism.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Domestic Terrorism
Data Collection Act".

SECTION 2. REPORTING.

(a) The [State Police/relevant agency] shall compile annual
    statistics on domestic terrorism-related incidents.

(b) Data shall include:
    (1) Number of incidents;
    (2) Type of incident;
    (3) Ideological motivation where identifiable;
    (4) Casualties and injuries;
    (5) Prosecution outcomes.

(c) An annual report shall be submitted to the Governor
    and Legislature.

SECTION 3. DEFINITIONS.

"Domestic terrorism" means activities that:
(a) Involve acts dangerous to human life that violate
    criminal laws;
(b) Appear intended to intimidate or coerce a civilian
    population, influence government policy by intimidation,
    or affect government conduct by mass destruction,
    assassination, or kidnapping; and
(c) Occur primarily within [State].

SECTION 4. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on [date].
```

## Regulatory Framework

### DOJ Guidelines on Domestic Terrorism

Provisions for Attorney General guidance:

1. **Investigation Standards**
    - Predication requirements
    - First Amendment limitations
    - Civil liberties training
    - Oversight mechanisms

2. **Prosecution Priorities**
    - Election-related threats
    - Mass casualty plots
    - Organized extremism
    - Hate crimes

3. **Coordination**
    - FBI-DHS coordination
    - State-local coordination
    - Information sharing protocols
    - Task force structure

### Platform Content Policies

Model provisions for platform regulation:

1. **Transparency Requirements**
    - Regular reporting on violent extremist content
    - Algorithm disclosure
    - Moderation decision explanations
    - Researcher access

2. **Enforcement Standards**
    - Clear prohibition on incitement
    - Rapid response to threats
    - Appeals processes
    - Consistency in enforcement

## Legal Considerations

### First Amendment Limitations

**Protected Activity**:

- Political advocacy
- Criticism of officials
- Inflammatory rhetoric (short of true threats)
- Association with political groups

**Unprotected Activity**:

- True threats (specific, credible)
- Incitement to imminent lawless action
- Criminal conspiracy
- Harassment

### Due Process Requirements

- Clear statutory definitions
- Mens rea requirements
- Notice to defendants
- Fair procedures

### Federalism Concerns

- Federal jurisdiction appropriately limited
- Concurrent state authority preserved
- Local law enforcement role respected
- Preemption avoided where possible

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Rhetorical cover | Coded language avoids prosecution | High |
| Interstate gaps | Purely intrastate threats harder to prosecute federally | Moderate |
| Platform evasion | Movement to unregulated platforms | High |
| Resource limitations | Not all threats investigated | Moderate |
| Selective enforcement | Disparate application across ideologies | High |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| First Amendment limits | Cannot address all violent rhetoric | Constitutional |
| Gun access | Violence remains lethal | Political opposition |
| Root causes unaddressed | Prevention limited | Systemic factors |
| Platform immunity | Limited accountability | Section 230 |
| International connections | Hard to address | Jurisdiction limits |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Enforcement Consistency**
    - Clear guidelines
    - Regular audits
    - Civil liberties oversight
    - Transparent reporting
    - Training requirements

2. **Gap Closure**
    - Expand federal jurisdiction where appropriate
    - Strengthen state laws
    - Platform accountability measures
    - International cooperation

3. **Prevention Enhancement**
    - Sustained prevention funding
    - Evidence-based programs
    - Community partnerships
    - Mental health integration
    - Exit program expansion

4. **Oversight Mechanisms**
    - Inspector General audits
    - PCLOB review
    - Congressional oversight
    - Civil liberties reporting
    - Public transparency

### General Implementation Concerns

1. **Political Will**
    - Requires bipartisan commitment
    - Consistent application essential
    - Leadership tone matters
    - Electoral incentives complicated

2. **Resource Adequacy**
    - Sustained funding needed
    - Competition with other priorities
    - State/local capacity varies
    - Training requirements

3. **Civil Liberties Balance**
    - Constant vigilance required
    - Clear guidelines essential
    - Oversight must be meaningful
    - History of abuse must inform

4. **Coordination Challenges**
    - Multiple agencies involved
    - Information sharing barriers
    - Jurisdictional disputes
    - Technology limitations
