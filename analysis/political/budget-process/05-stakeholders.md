# Budget Process Reform: Stakeholders

## Federal Government Stakeholders

### Congress

**Budget Committee Members**

| Interest | Position | Influence |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| Relevance of committee | Support process reform | Medium |
| Reconciliation power | Protect current authority | Medium |
| Budget resolution passage | Support enforcement | Medium |

*Note*: Budget committees have lost influence as resolutions are bypassed. Have interest in reforms that restore their role.

**Appropriations Committee Members**

| Interest | Position | Influence |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| Spending authority | Protect annual process | High |
| Earmarks | Generally supportive | Medium |
| Biennial budgeting | Generally opposed | High |
| Regular order | Support in principle | Medium |

*Key Dynamic*: Appropriators have the most to lose from biennial budgeting and the most to gain from regular order. Power concentrated in "cardinals" (subcommittee chairs).

**Congressional Leadership**

| Interest | Position | Influence |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| Centralized control | Prefer omnibus/CRs | Very High |
| Leverage in negotiations | Oppose automatic CRs | Very High |
| Avoiding bad votes | Prefer packaged bills | Very High |

*Key Dynamic*: Leadership benefits from crisis governance because it concentrates power. Regular order disperses power to committees and rank-and-file.

**Rank-and-File Members**

| Interest | Position | Influence |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| Input on spending | Support regular order | Low |
| Avoid tough votes | Mixed on process reform | Low |
| Local priorities | Support earmarks | Low |
| Electoral concerns | Avoid shutdown blame | Low |

### Executive Branch

**Office of Management and Budget (OMB)**

| Interest | Position | Influence |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| Budget execution authority | Maximize flexibility | High |
| Impoundment power | Expand executive control | High |
| Apportionment control | Maintain current authority | Medium |

**Federal Agencies**

| Interest | Position | Influence |
|----------|----------|-----------|

| Multi-year planning | Support biennial budgeting | Low |
| Avoiding CRs | Support automatic CRs | Low |

*Key Dynamic*: Agencies suffer most from dysfunction but have almost no voice in budget process decisions.

**Federal Employees**

| Interest | Position | Influence |
|----------|----------|-----------|

| Pay continuity | Support No Shutdown Act | Medium |
| Planning stability | Support biennial budgeting | Low |

**Key Groups**:

- AFGE (American Federation of Government Employees)

- NTEU (National Treasury Employees Union)

- NFFE (National Federation of Federal Employees)

## Beneficiaries and Victims

### Who Benefits from Dysfunction

**Leadership of Both Parties**:

- Crisis concentrates negotiating power

- Rank-and-file excluded from decisions

- Omnibus packages leadership priorities

**Minority Party**:

- Leverage over majority

- Can extract concessions

- Crisis benefits opposition

**Fiscal Hawks**:

- Shutdown threat enables demands

- Debt ceiling creates leverage

- CRs prevent spending increases

**Lobbyists**:

- Access to final negotiations critical

- Expertise valuable in crisis

- Clients pay more for influence

### Who Suffers from Dysfunction

**Federal Employees**:

- Furloughs during shutdowns

- Delayed paychecks

- Career uncertainty

- Morale damage

**Federal Contractors and Their Employees**:

- Work stoppages

- Layoffs during shutdowns

- No back pay guarantee

- Business planning impossible

**Program Beneficiaries**:

- Service interruptions

- Delayed benefits

- Program uncertainty

- Reduced effectiveness

**Taxpayers**:

- Inefficient government

- Higher borrowing costs

- Economic damage

- Wasted resources

**The Economy**:

- GDP losses from shutdowns

- Market volatility

- Reduced confidence

- Higher interest rates

## Stakeholder Dynamics

### Coalition for Reform

**Potential Supporters of Comprehensive Reform**:

- Federal employee unions

- Good government groups

- Business community (especially contractors)

- Financial markets

- Moderate legislators (both parties)

- Former budget officials (both parties)

- Academic experts

### Coalition Against Reform

**Potential Opponents of Comprehensive Reform**:

- Fiscal hawks (lose leverage)

- Party leadership (lose power)

- Those benefiting from crisis governance

- Ideological groups (prefer confrontation)

### Key Swing Stakeholders

| Stakeholder | Determinant | Swing Factor |
|-------------|-------------|--------------|
| Business community | Specific reforms proposed | Economic impact |
| Moderate Republicans | Deficit impact | Fiscal conservatism |
| Moderate Democrats | Spending protection | Program priorities |
| Appropriators | Committee power | Institutional turf |
