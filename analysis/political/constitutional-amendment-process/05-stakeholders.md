# Constitutional Amendment Process: Stakeholders

## Government Actors

### Congress

**Interest**: Maintain gatekeeping power over constitutional change while responding to political pressure for amendments.

**Key Roles**:

- Propose amendments (2/3 vote)
- Set ratification method
- Call convention (on state applications)
- Potentially set convention rules
- Count ratifications

**Perspectives by Position**:

| Group | General View |
|-------|--------------|
| House leadership | Resist amendments limiting power |
| Senate leadership | Protect Senate structure |
| Rank-and-file members | Varies by issue and party |
| Former state officials | May favor state role |

**Key Committees**:

- House Judiciary Committee
- Senate Judiciary Committee

### State Legislatures

**Interest**: Preserve state role in federal system; use amendment process for policy goals.

**Key Roles**:

- Ratify amendments
- Apply for Article V convention
- Potentially elect convention delegates
- Amend state constitutions

**Organizations**:

- National Conference of State Legislatures (NCSL)
- American Legislative Exchange Council (ALEC)
- State Innovation Exchange (SiX)

**Perspectives**:

| Group | Tendency |
|-------|----------|
| Republican legislatures | Convention of States, balanced budget |
| Democratic legislatures | Campaign finance, voting rights |
| Bipartisan | Term limits has some cross-party support |

### Governors

**Interest**: State interests in federal structure; policy preferences.

**Key Roles**:

- Influence state legislative action
- Public advocacy on amendments
- Some states require gubernatorial role

**Organizations**:

- National Governors Association (NGA)

### State Attorneys General

**Interest**: Legal interpretation of amendment process; litigation.

**Key Roles**:

- Advise on amendment process
- Bring or defend litigation
- Multistate coordination

### Courts

**Interest**: Interpret constitutional provisions, including amendment process.

**Key Roles**:

- Resolve disputes over ratification
- Interpret amendment provisions
- Political question doctrine limits involvement

**Key Concerns**:

- *Coleman v. Miller*: Ratification questions are political
- Courts historically defer to Congress
- May be drawn into ERA dispute
- Convention questions likely justiciable

## Advocacy Organizations

### Conservative Amendment Advocates

**Convention of States Project**:

- Purpose: Convention for federalism amendments
- Approach: State legislature applications
- Leadership: Mark Meckler, Michael Farris
- Support: Conservative grassroots, ALEC

**Balanced Budget Amendment Task Force**:

- Purpose: Single-issue convention for balanced budget
- Approach: State applications (claims near threshold)
- Support: Fiscal conservatives

**American Legislative Exchange Council (ALEC)**:

- Role: Model legislation, coordination
- Convention support: Yes, with limits
- Orientation: Conservative

**Compact for America**:

- Approach: Interstate compact for amendment
- Innovative: Combined application and ratification
- Status: Limited success

### Progressive Amendment Advocates

**Wolf-PAC**:

- Purpose: Convention for campaign finance
- Founded: 2011 (from Young Turks network)
- States passed: 5
- Approach: Grassroots, state legislature focus

**American Promise**:

- Purpose: 28th Amendment (campaign finance)
- Approach: Cross-partisan outreach
- Focus: Congressional path plus convention

**Free Speech for People**:

- Purpose: Overturn *Citizens United*
- Approach: Multiple strategies
- Litigation and advocacy

**Equal Rights Amendment Coalition**:

- Purpose: ERA ratification recognition
- Litigation: Multiple cases
- Advocacy: Congressional action

### Bipartisan/Non-Partisan

**National Constitution Center**:

- Role: Education, dialogue
- Programming: Constitutional discussions
- Resources: Amendment information

**ConstitutionDAO / Constitutional reform groups**:

- Various groups exploring reform
- Academic conferences
- Public education

### Labor

## Public Opinion

### General Public

**Support for Various Amendments**:

| Amendment | Public Support |
|-----------|---------------|
| Term limits | 75-80% |
| Balanced budget | 60-70% |
| Campaign finance reform | 70%+ |
| ERA | 70%+ |
| Electoral College reform | 50-60% |

**Views on Process**:

- Low awareness of Article V convention option
- General support for making process easier
- Concern about runaway convention

**Engagement**:

- Public rarely engaged on process
- Specific amendment campaigns can mobilize
- Social media increases awareness

### Opposition Groups

**Convention Opponents**:

- John Birch Society: Fears runaway convention
- Some progressives: Same fears
- Common Cause: Cautious
- Various scholars: Process concerns

**Specific Amendment Opponents**:

- Each proposed amendment has organized opposition
- Balanced budget: Fiscal policy concerns
- ERA: Social conservatives
- Electoral College: Small state interests

## Power Analysis

### Most Influential Stakeholders

| Stakeholder | Influence | Source |
|-------------|-----------|--------|
| Congress | Very High | Gatekeeping role |
| State legislatures | High | Ratification/application |
| Organized amendment campaigns | Moderate | Mobilization capacity |
| Academic experts | Low-Moderate | Expertise but limited reach |
| Courts | Uncertain | May be forced to rule |
| Public opinion | Low | Indirect, through representation |

### Whose Interests Current Process Serves

- Small states (veto power)
- Congress (gatekeeping)
- Status quo beneficiaries
- Those with resources for long campaigns

### Whose Interests Current Process Fails

- Large state populations
- Those seeking structural reform
- Those without long-term organizing capacity
- Democratic majorities blocked by supermajority requirements
