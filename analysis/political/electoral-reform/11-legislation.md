# Electoral Reform: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Electoral reform requires action at multiple levels: constitutional amendments to guarantee voting rights, federal legislation to enable alternative voting systems, and state legislation to implement them. This document provides draft legal text for the key reforms identified in [Solutions](07-solutions.md).

## Constitutional Amendments

### Right to Vote Amendment

**Purpose**: The U.S. Constitution does not explicitly guarantee the right to vote. This amendment would establish an affirmative right, provide clear standards for any restrictions, and empower Congress to protect voting rights.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. Every citizen of the United States, who is eighteen
years of age or older, shall have the right to vote in any public
election held in the jurisdiction in which the citizen resides.

SECTION 2. The right of citizens of the United States to vote
shall not be denied or abridged by the United States or by any
State except for conviction of a felony for which the person is
currently incarcerated.

SECTION 3. Congress shall have the power to enforce this article
by appropriate legislation, including establishing uniform
standards for the time, place, and manner of elections.

SECTION 4. This article shall take effect two years after the
date of ratification.

```text

**Explanation**:

- **Section 1**: Creates an affirmative right to vote (unlike current Constitution which only prohibits certain restrictions). "Public election" covers federal, state, and local elections. "Jurisdiction in which the citizen resides" prevents states from imposing unreasonable residency requirements.

- **Section 2**: Sets the standard for restrictions. Only incarceration for felony conviction can disenfranchise—not awaiting trial, not after release. This is more permissive than some advocate (full enfranchisement) but more achievable.

- **Section 3**: Empowers Congress to enforce uniformly, addressing the patchwork of state election laws. "Time, place, and manner" language echoes existing Elections Clause.

- **Section 4**: Two-year implementation period allows states to update registration systems.

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| State sovereignty | Elections Clause already allows federal regulation; this extends it |
| Felon voting controversy | Language allows restriction during incarceration only—compromise position |
| Implementation costs | Two-year delay and congressional power to phase in |

**Refinements**:

- **Stronger version**: Remove felon exception entirely ("shall not be denied or abridged for any reason")
- **Fallback version**: Apply only to federal elections initially
- **Technical addition**: Include language requiring paper ballot backup

### Congressional Representation Amendment

**Purpose**: Enable proportional representation by removing constitutional barriers to multi-member districts and alternative voting systems.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. The House of Representatives shall be composed of
Members chosen every second Year by the People of the several
States, using such methods as Congress may by law provide.

SECTION 2. Each State shall be entitled to such number of
Representatives as Congress shall by law determine, provided
that the total number of Representatives shall not be less
than one per 500,000 persons as determined by the Census.

SECTION 3. Representatives need not be chosen from single-member
districts. Congress may provide for multi-member districts,
ranked-choice voting, proportional representation, or other
methods designed to ensure fair representation.

SECTION 4. This article shall take effect upon ratification.

```text

**Explanation**:

- **Section 1**: Modernizes Article I, Section 2 language while preserving two-year terms and popular election.

- **Section 2**: Allows for House expansion (currently capped at 435 since 1929). One per 500,000 would yield approximately 660 representatives—more representative but still manageable.

- **Section 3**: Explicitly authorizes the reforms in the Fair Representation Act without requiring constitutional amendment for each change.

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Tradition of single-member districts | Many democracies use alternatives successfully |
| Complexity | Implementation details left to Congress |
| House expansion unpopular | Made optional ("not less than") |

**Note**: This amendment is aspirational. The Fair Representation Act (below) can achieve most goals without amending the Constitution, since the 1967 single-member requirement is statutory, not constitutional.

## Federal Legislation

### Fair Representation Act

**Purpose**: Implement multi-member districts with ranked-choice voting for the U.S. House of Representatives, ending gerrymandering and enabling proportional representation.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Fair Representation Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) The use of single-member congressional districts, combined
    with partisan gerrymandering, has produced a House of
    Representatives that fails to reflect the political diversity
    of the American people.
(2) Multi-member districts using ranked-choice voting would
    eliminate the incentive for gerrymandering, as district
    lines cannot determine outcomes when multiple representatives
    are elected proportionally.
(3) Ranked-choice voting eliminates the spoiler effect, allows
    voters to express their true preferences, and ensures that
    representatives have broad support.
(4) Multi-member districts have been used successfully in
    American history and are used by most developed democracies.

SEC. 3. REPEAL OF SINGLE-MEMBER DISTRICT REQUIREMENT.

Section 2c of title 2, United States Code, is repealed.

SEC. 4. MULTI-MEMBER DISTRICTS REQUIRED.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Each State entitled to more than one Representative
    shall establish a number of districts equal to the number of
    Representatives to which such State is entitled divided by five,
    rounded to the nearest whole number, but in no case fewer than one.

(b) DISTRICT MAGNITUDE.—Each district established under subsection
    (a) shall elect no fewer than three and no more than five
    Representatives.

(c) SMALL STATES.—A State entitled to fewer than three Representatives
    shall elect all Representatives at-large using ranked-choice voting.

(d) ODD APPORTIONMENTS.—If a State's number of Representatives is
    not evenly divisible, the State shall adjust district magnitudes
    to ensure the total equals the State's apportionment.

SEC. 5. RANKED-CHOICE VOTING REQUIRED.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Each State shall conduct elections for Representative
    in Congress using ranked-choice voting with single transferable vote.

(b) DEFINITION.—For purposes of this section, "ranked-choice voting
    with single transferable vote" means a method of casting and
    tabulating votes in which—
    (1) voters rank candidates in order of preference;
    (2) a candidate is elected upon receiving a threshold number
        of votes equal to the total votes cast divided by one more
        than the number of seats to be filled, plus one;
    (3) if a candidate exceeds the threshold, excess votes transfer
        to voters' next-ranked choices proportionally;
    (4) if no candidate meets the threshold, the candidate with
        fewest votes is eliminated and those votes transfer to
        voters' next-ranked choices;
    (5) this process continues until all seats are filled.

SEC. 6. REDISTRICTING.

(a) INDEPENDENT COMMISSIONS.—Each State shall establish an independent
    redistricting commission to draw district lines.

(b) CRITERIA.—Districts shall be drawn to—
    (1) comply with the Voting Rights Act;
    (2) be contiguous and reasonably compact;
    (3) respect existing political subdivisions and communities
        of interest;
    (4) not favor or disfavor any political party.

(c) COMMISSION COMPOSITION.—Each commission shall consist of an equal
    number of members affiliated with each of the two largest parties
    in the State, plus at least one member not affiliated with either,
    who shall serve as chair.

SEC. 7. IMPLEMENTATION.

(a) EFFECTIVE DATE.—This Act shall apply to elections for Representative
    in Congress beginning with the first election held after the
    redistricting following the 2030 decennial census.

(b) STATE PLANS.—Each State shall submit a plan for implementing this
    Act to the Federal Election Commission not later than January 1, 2028.

(c) FEC GUIDANCE.—The Federal Election Commission shall issue
    regulations implementing this Act not later than January 1, 2027.

SEC. 8. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated such sums as may be necessary—
(1) to assist States in implementing ranked-choice voting;
(2) for voter education programs; and
(3) for upgrading election infrastructure.

```text

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Repeals 2 U.S.C. § 2c, the 1967 single-member district mandate—the only federal barrier to multi-member districts.

- **Section 4**: Creates 3-5 member districts. Five-member districts maximize proportionality; three is minimum for meaningful proportionality. States retain flexibility within this range.

- **Section 5**: Requires STV (the multi-winner form of RCV). The threshold formula (Droop quota) is the standard method used internationally.

- **Section 6**: Mandates independent redistricting, addressing partisan gerrymandering even though multi-member districts reduce its impact.

- **Section 7**: 2030 implementation gives states a full census cycle to prepare.

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Constitutional authority | Elections Clause (Art. I, § 4) explicitly authorizes |
| Complexity | Phased implementation, voter education funding |
| Voting Rights Act interaction | Explicit VRA compliance requirement (Sec. 6(b)(1)) |
| State resistance | Federal elections regulable by Congress |

**Refinements**:

- **Phased version**: Apply to states with 6+ representatives first; expand later
- **Opt-in version**: Allow states to voluntarily adopt before 2030
- **Stronger VRA protection**: Require majority-minority opportunity districts where possible

### Voter Choice Act

**Purpose**: Establish federal standards for ranked-choice voting in federal elections, providing a framework for states using RCV for single-winner offices.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Voter Choice Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) Several States have adopted or are considering ranked-choice
    voting for federal elections.
(2) Uniform standards for ranked-choice voting would ensure
    consistency and voter confidence across jurisdictions.
(3) Ranked-choice voting reduces negative campaigning, eliminates
    the spoiler effect, and ensures majority support for winners.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
(1) RANKED-CHOICE VOTING.—The term "ranked-choice voting" means
    a method of casting and tabulating votes in which—
    (A) voters rank candidates in order of preference;
    (B) tabulation proceeds in rounds;
    (C) in each round, if no candidate has a majority, the
        candidate with the fewest votes is eliminated;
    (D) votes for eliminated candidates transfer to voters'
        next-ranked choices who remain in the contest; and
    (E) tabulation continues until a candidate receives a majority.

(2) EXHAUSTED BALLOT.—The term "exhausted ballot" means a ballot
    that can no longer be counted because all ranked candidates
    have been eliminated.

SEC. 4. FEDERAL STANDARDS FOR RANKED-CHOICE VOTING.

(a) STATE OPTION.—Any State that uses ranked-choice voting for
    elections for Senator, Representative, or presidential electors
    shall comply with this section.

(b) BALLOT REQUIREMENTS.—
    (1) Ballots shall allow voters to rank at least five candidates.
    (2) Ballots shall include clear instructions on ranking.
    (3) Ranking fewer than all candidates shall not invalidate a ballot.
    (4) Skipped rankings shall be permitted without penalty.

(c) TABULATION STANDARDS.—
    (1) Tabulation shall use the method described in section 3(1).
    (2) Detailed tabulation reports shall be published after each
        round showing votes received, votes transferred, and
        votes exhausted.
    (3) Cast vote records shall be made available for independent
        verification within 48 hours of certification.

(d) RECOUNTS.—States shall provide for recounts using the same
    ranked-choice methodology, with retabulation of all ballots.

SEC. 5. VOTER EDUCATION.

(a) GRANT PROGRAM.—The Election Assistance Commission shall establish
    a grant program to assist States and localities in educating
    voters about ranked-choice voting.

(b) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated
    $50,000,000 for fiscal year 2025 and such sums as may be
    necessary for subsequent fiscal years.

SEC. 6. STUDIES.

(a) EFFECTS STUDY.—The Comptroller General shall conduct a study of
    the effects of ranked-choice voting on—
    (1) voter participation and turnout;
    (2) campaign conduct and tone;
    (3) representation of underrepresented groups;
    (4) voter satisfaction; and
    (5) election administration costs.

(b) REPORT.—Not later than 2 years after the date of enactment,
    and every 4 years thereafter, the Comptroller General shall
    submit a report to Congress on the findings of the study.

```text

**Explanation**:

- **Section 4(b)**: Allows at least five rankings to accommodate crowded fields while permitting strategic partial ranking. "Skipped rankings" provision is permissive—some jurisdictions penalize skips.

- **Section 4(c)**: Transparency requirements allow independent verification and build public trust.

- **Section 6**: Built-in study provision provides evidence base for future expansion.

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| State authority | Framed as optional standards when states choose RCV |
| Cost | Federal grants available for implementation |
| "Exhausted ballots" concern | Transparency requirements expose this issue |

### National Popular Vote Statutory Alternative

**Purpose**: If the National Popular Vote Interstate Compact stalls, this legislation offers an alternative using Congress's power to set the time, place, and manner of elections.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Every Vote Counts Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) The Electoral College, as currently implemented, results in
    presidential campaigns focusing on a small number of
    competitive States.
(2) Four times in American history, candidates have won the
    presidency while losing the national popular vote.
(3) The winner-take-all method of allocating electors is not
    constitutionally required and was not the original intent.
(4) Congress has authority under Article II, Section 1 to
    determine the time of choosing electors.

SEC. 3. NATIONAL POPULAR VOTE WINNER.

(a) CERTIFICATION.—The Archivist of the United States shall certify,
    not later than the first Monday after the second Wednesday in
    December following a presidential election, the national popular
    vote total for each candidate based on certifications from all
    fifty States and the District of Columbia.

(b) CONDITIONAL ALLOCATION.—This section shall take effect only upon
    enactment of consistent legislation by States whose electors
    constitute a majority of the Electoral College.

SEC. 4. SENSE OF CONGRESS.

It is the sense of Congress that—
(1) States should allocate their electoral votes to the national
    popular vote winner; and
(2) the National Popular Vote Interstate Compact represents a
    constitutional method of achieving this goal.

```text

**Explanation**: This is a modest bill expressing Congressional support for NPV and establishing federal certification machinery. It does not mandate state action but creates infrastructure.

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Limited impact | Framework for future action |
| Constitutional questions | Uses clear congressional authorities |

## State Model Legislation

### Ranked-Choice Voting Act (Model)

**Purpose**: Model legislation for states to adopt RCV for state and/or federal elections.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Ranked-Choice Voting Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

As used in this Act:
(a) "Ranked-choice voting" means the method of casting and
    tabulating votes described in Section 4.
(b) "Ranking" means the number assigned by a voter to a candidate
    to express the voter's preference for that candidate.
(c) "Overvote" means an instance in which a voter gives the same
    ranking to more than one candidate.
(d) "Undervote" means an instance in which a voter has not assigned
    a ranking to any candidate.
(e) "Exhausted ballot" means a ballot that cannot be advanced
    because no further valid rankings remain.

SECTION 3. APPLICABILITY.

This Act applies to elections for:
(a) Governor and Lieutenant Governor;
(b) Attorney General;
(c) Secretary of State;
(d) [Other statewide offices];
(e) State Senator and State Representative;
(f) United States Senator and Representative; and
(g) Presidential preference primary.

SECTION 4. RANKED-CHOICE VOTING METHOD.

(a) BALLOT.—The ballot shall allow voters to rank candidates in
    order of preference. Voters may rank as many or as few
    candidates as they choose, up to the total number of candidates.

(b) TABULATION.—Votes shall be counted in rounds as follows:
    (1) In the first round, every ballot counts as one vote for
        its highest-ranked candidate.
    (2) If a candidate receives a majority of votes, that candidate
        is elected.
    (3) If no candidate receives a majority:
        (A) The candidate with the fewest votes is eliminated.
        (B) Each ballot counting for the eliminated candidate
            transfers to its next-highest-ranked active candidate.
        (C) Ballots with no remaining valid rankings are exhausted.
    (4) Rounds continue until a candidate receives a majority of
        active ballots or only two candidates remain.

(c) TIE FOR ELIMINATION.—If two or more candidates are tied for
    the fewest votes, the candidate with the fewest first-choice
    votes shall be eliminated. If still tied, elimination shall
    be determined by lot.

SECTION 5. BALLOT DESIGN.

(a) The ballot shall include:
    (1) Instructions on how to rank candidates;
    (2) A statement that ranking is optional and voters may rank
        as many or as few candidates as they choose;
    (3) Columns or spaces for voters to indicate rankings.

(b) The Secretary of State shall approve ballot designs before
    use and shall issue guidelines for ranked-choice voting ballots.

SECTION 6. TABULATION AND REPORTING.

(a) TABULATION.—Tabulation of ranked-choice voting contests shall
    be conducted at a central location.

(b) REPORTING.—The election authority shall publish:
    (1) First-choice vote totals by precinct;
    (2) Complete round-by-round results showing eliminations
        and transfers;
    (3) Cast vote records sufficient for independent verification.

SECTION 7. VOTER EDUCATION.

(a) The Secretary of State shall conduct a voter education campaign
    before the first election using ranked-choice voting.

(b) There is appropriated the sum of $[amount] for voter education.

SECTION 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect for elections held on or after [date].

```text

**Adaptations**:

| State Situation | Modification |
|-----------------|--------------|
| Initiative state | Format as ballot measure |
| Home rule state | Allow local opt-in or opt-out |
| No presidential primary | Remove Section 3(g) |
| Multi-member districts exist | Add STV provisions |

### Open Primary with RCV General Election (Alaska Model)

**Purpose**: Implement the combined reform used by Alaska—nonpartisan open primary with ranked-choice general election.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Open Elections Act".

SECTION 2. OPEN PRIMARY.

(a) SINGLE PRIMARY.—There shall be a single primary election for
    each office, in which all candidates appear on the same ballot.

(b) NO PARTY REGISTRATION.—Voters need not be registered with any
    party to vote in the primary.

(c) TOP-FOUR ADVANCE.—The four candidates receiving the most votes
    in the primary shall advance to the general election, regardless
    of party affiliation.

(d) PARTY DESIGNATION.—Candidates may indicate their party
    preference on the ballot, but this is optional.

SECTION 3. RANKED-CHOICE GENERAL ELECTION.

(a) BALLOT.—In the general election, voters shall rank the four
    candidates in order of preference.

(b) TABULATION.—Votes shall be tabulated using the ranked-choice
    voting method as described in [reference to RCV statute].

SECTION 4. WRITE-IN CANDIDATES.

(a) PRIMARY.—Write-in candidates may participate in the primary.
(b) GENERAL.—Write-in candidates may not participate in the general
    election.

SECTION 5. APPLICABILITY.

This Act applies to elections for:
(a) State Legislature;
(b) Governor and Lieutenant Governor;
(c) United States Senate and House of Representatives.

SECTION 6. POLITICAL PARTIES.

(a) Nothing in this Act restricts the right of political parties
    to nominate or endorse candidates.

(b) Parties may indicate their endorsed candidate in voter guides
    and other communications.

SECTION 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect for elections held on or after [date].

```text

**Explanation**:

- **Top-four** (vs. top-two): Ensures sufficient candidates for meaningful ranking while reducing to manageable number.

- **Optional party designation**: Preserves voter information without party control over ballot access.

- **Section 6**: Protects party association rights while removing party control over primaries.

## Regulatory Framework

### FEC Ranked-Choice Voting Regulations

**Existing Authority**: Federal Election Commission regulates federal elections under FECA; additional authority under Voter Choice Act.

**Draft Regulation**:

```text
PART 9000—RANKED-CHOICE VOTING

§ 9000.1 Purpose.

This part implements the uniform standards for ranked-choice voting
in federal elections established by the Voter Choice Act.

§ 9000.2 Definitions.

(a) "Active ballot" means a ballot with at least one ranking for
    a candidate who has not been eliminated.
(b) "Batch elimination" means the simultaneous elimination of
    multiple candidates whose combined votes cannot mathematically
    affect the outcome.
(c) "Majority" means more than half of active ballots.

§ 9000.3 Ballot requirements.

(a) Ballots shall provide for ranking at least five candidates.
(b) Each ranking shall be associated with a single candidate.
(c) The ballot shall contain the following statement:
    "Rank candidates in order of preference (1st, 2nd, 3rd, etc.).
    You may rank as many or as few candidates as you wish."
(d) Instructions shall not suggest that voters must rank all
    candidates.

§ 9000.4 Tabulation standards.

(a) Tabulation shall proceed in rounds until a candidate receives
    a majority of active ballots.
(b) In each round:
    (1) Ballots are counted for the highest-ranked active candidate;
    (2) If no candidate has a majority, the candidate with the
        fewest votes is eliminated;
    (3) Ballots for eliminated candidates transfer to the next
        highest-ranked active candidate.
(c) Batch elimination is permitted where it cannot affect the
    outcome of any round.
(d) Cast vote records shall be published in a standardized format.

§ 9000.5 Reporting requirements.

(a) Within 24 hours of completion of tabulation, election
    authorities shall publish:
    (1) First-choice vote totals by precinct;
    (2) Round-by-round results;
    (3) Number of exhausted ballots per round.
(b) Within 48 hours, cast vote records shall be published in
    the format prescribed by the Commission.

§ 9000.6 Recounts.

(a) Recounts shall retabulate all ballots using the ranked-choice
    method.
(b) Recounts may not be limited to first-choice votes only.

```text

**Explanation**: Creates uniform standards without dictating implementation details. Cast vote record publication enables independent verification.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

**First Amendment**:

- RCV does not burden speech—voters have more expressive options
- Ballot access requirements remain subject to Anderson-Burdick balancing

**Fourteenth Amendment (Equal Protection)**:

- All votes counted equally under RCV
- Potential challenge: exhausted ballots treated as abstentions
- Response: Voter chose to rank limited candidates; similar to abstention

**Article I, Section 4 (Elections Clause)**:

- Congress may regulate "time, place, and manner" of federal elections
- Clear authority for Voter Choice Act and Fair Representation Act
- States implement but cannot contradict federal standards

**Article II, Section 1 (Presidential Electors)**:

- State legislatures determine "manner" of appointment
- Congress sets "time" of choosing electors
- NPV Compact uses state legislative authority

### Preemption Questions

| State Law | Federal Impact |
|-----------|----------------|
| State RCV statute | Federal standards supplement, not replace |
| State ballot access laws | Must accommodate federal candidate requirements |
| State recount procedures | Federal minimum standards apply to federal races |

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Provision | Enforcement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Fair Representation Act | DOJ enforcement, private right of action |
| Voter Choice Act | FEC oversight, state implementation |
| State RCV statutes | State election officials, courts |

### Sunset and Review Provisions

**Recommended additions**:

- GAO study of RCV effects every 4 years
- Congressional reauthorization of voter education funding
- 10-year review of Fair Representation Act implementation

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Fair Representation Act

**Potential Loopholes**:

| Loophole | How Exploited | Severity |
|----------|---------------|----------|
| Commission composition gaming | State legislatures could manipulate applicant pool or selection criteria | High |
| Gerrymandering within multi-member districts | Drawing district boundaries to pack/crack communities even with 3-5 members | Medium |
| Threshold manipulation | Setting exact district magnitudes (3 vs 5) strategically | Medium |
| VRA compliance as pretext | Over-claiming VRA requirements to justify partisan maps | Medium |
| Delayed implementation | States could slow-walk implementation, miss deadlines | High |

**Shortcomings**:

| Issue | Description | Impact |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| Senate unchanged | Act only affects House; Senate remains malapportioned | Significant |
| No presidential reform | Electoral College untouched | Significant |
| Complexity concerns | Multi-member RCV harder to explain than single-winner | Moderate |
| Transition disruption | Incumbents lose "their" districts; resistance guaranteed | High |
| Local representation diluted | Larger districts mean less constituent connection | Moderate |
| Doesn't address primaries | Primary systems still controlled by states | Moderate |

**Rectification Procedures**:

| Issue | Rectification Approach |
|-------|----------------------|
| Commission gaming | Amend to require federal oversight of applicant pools; random selection audited by GAO |
| Multi-member gerrymandering | Add explicit partisan fairness metrics with mathematical thresholds |
| Threshold manipulation | Require 5-member districts as default; exceptions only with justification |
| Implementation delays | Add automatic court-drawn maps if state misses deadline; penalties for non-compliance |
| Complexity | Mandate federally-funded voter education; require ballot design testing |
| Senate disparity | Separate constitutional amendment for Senate reform (long-term) |

### Voter Choice Act

**Potential Loopholes**:

| Loophole | How Exploited | Severity |
|----------|---------------|----------|
| Minimum ranking insufficient | States could allow only 3 rankings in crowded fields, causing exhausted ballots | Medium |
| Tabulation opacity | States could use proprietary software without full transparency | High |
| Ballot design manipulation | Confusing ballots could increase invalid votes in targeted communities | High |
| Recount standards weak | States could interpret recount provisions narrowly | Medium |

**Shortcomings**:

| Issue | Description | Impact |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| Voluntary adoption | States can choose not to use RCV at all | Significant |
| No mandate for primaries | Partisan primaries could still produce extreme candidates | Moderate |
| Exhausted ballot problem | Voters who don't rank enough candidates effectively abstain | Moderate |
| No proportionality | Single-winner RCV still produces winner-take-all outcomes | Significant |

**Rectification Procedures**:

| Issue | Rectification Approach |
|-------|----------------------|
| Ranking limits | Amend to require rankings for all candidates or at least N-1 |
| Tabulation opacity | Require open-source tabulation software; mandatory cast vote record publication |
| Ballot manipulation | Federal ballot design standards; pre-clearance for ballot design in covered jurisdictions |
| Exhausted ballots | Consider "final two" or "final three" tabulation to ensure majority of all ballots |
| Voluntary adoption | Future legislation mandating RCV for federal elections (requires political will) |

### State RCV Model Legislation

**Potential Loopholes**:

| Loophole | How Exploited | Severity |
|----------|---------------|----------|
| Centralized tabulation delays | Could be used to delay results, undermine confidence | Medium |
| Voter education underfunding | Legislature could appropriate insufficient funds | High |
| Selective implementation | Apply only to some offices, creating voter confusion | Medium |
| Repeal vulnerability | Simple majority could repeal after one election | High |

**Shortcomings**:

| Issue | Description | Impact |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| No federal office coverage | Model doesn't address congressional races (separate federal law needed) | Moderate |
| Transition period risks | First elections may have higher error rates | Moderate |
| Equipment costs | Older voting systems may not support RCV | Moderate |

**Rectification Procedures**:

| Issue | Rectification Approach |
|-------|----------------------|
| Tabulation delays | Require preliminary results within 24 hours; detailed results within 72 |
| Underfunding | Tie voter education to automatic appropriations; constitutional dedication |
| Selective implementation | Require comprehensive implementation across all applicable offices |
| Repeal vulnerability | Consider constitutional amendment or supermajority requirement for repeal |
| Equipment costs | Include state appropriation or seek federal Election Assistance Commission grants |

### Open Primary + RCV General (Alaska Model)

**Potential Loopholes**:

| Loophole | How Exploited | Severity |
|----------|---------------|----------|
| Top-four manipulation | Strategic candidate filing to crowd out opponents | Medium |
| Party designation abuse | Misleading party labels (e.g., "Republican" when not endorsed) | Medium |
| Write-in prohibition circumvention | Pressure campaigns for write-ins despite prohibition | Low |
| Voter confusion exploitation | Parties could run disinformation about ranking process | High |

**Shortcomings**:

| Issue | Description | Impact |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| Party endorsement ambiguity | Voters may not know party's preferred candidate | Moderate |
| Four candidates may be too many | For simple races, four choices could confuse | Low |
| Doesn't apply to presidential | Different system for presidential elections | Moderate |
| Implementation complexity | Two different systems (primary vs general) | Moderate |

**Rectification Procedures**:

| Issue | Rectification Approach |
|-------|----------------------|
| Strategic filing | Add filing fees or signature requirements to deter frivolous candidates |
| Party label abuse | Require party authorization for using party name, or use "prefers [Party]" language |
| Voter confusion | Mandatory nonpartisan voter education; media literacy requirements |
| Party endorsement | Allow official party endorsement to appear in voter guides |
| Presidential exclusion | Separate legislation for presidential primaries with similar structure |

### Cross-Cutting Issues

**Systemic Vulnerabilities**:

| Vulnerability | Applies To | Mitigation |
|---------------|------------|------------|
| Judicial invalidation | All legislation | Draft with clear constitutional basis; include severability clauses |
| Administrative sabotage | Implementation | Federal oversight; penalties for non-compliance |
| Voter suppression through confusion | RCV adoption | Require multi-year voter education before implementation |
| Technological failure | Electronic tabulation | Mandate paper ballots; hand-count audits |
| Foreign interference | All elections | Include election security provisions; CISA coordination |

**Ongoing Monitoring Requirements**:

| Requirement | Frequency | Responsible Entity |
|-------------|-----------|---------------------|
| Voter understanding survey | Each election | Election Assistance Commission |
| Error rate analysis | Each election | State election officials, GAO |
| Participation rate comparison | Annually | Census Bureau |
| Partisan fairness metrics | After redistricting | Independent commissions |
| Exhausted ballot analysis | Each election | Academic researchers with data access |

**Amendment Procedures**:

For federal legislation:

1. GAO or EAC identifies issue in regular report
2. Congressional committee holds hearings
3. Technical corrections bill introduced
4. Standard legislative process

For state legislation:

1. State election officials report to legislature
2. Post-election audit identifies problems
3. Amendment bill introduced next session
4. Consider automatic review triggers (e.g., if error rate exceeds 2%)
