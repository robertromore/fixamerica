# Civic Education: Stakeholders

## Affected Populations

### Students

**Current Students (K-12)**:

- ~50 million K-12 students
- Uneven access to quality civic education
- Facing unprecedented information challenges
- Will inherit democratic institutions

**Stakes**:

- Preparation for civic participation
- Skills to navigate information environment
- Understanding of rights and responsibilities
- Connection to democratic community

**Current Experience**:

- Often receive superficial civics instruction
- May find content irrelevant
- Limited opportunities for authentic engagement
- Significant variation by school and district

### Young Adults (18-29)

**Recent High School Graduates**:

- Products of current civic education system
- Now eligible to vote and participate
- Lowest civic knowledge in decades
- High rates of engagement on some issues

**Stakes**:

- Ability to participate effectively
- Vulnerability to manipulation
- Connection to democratic processes
- Efficacy and empowerment

### Teachers

**Social Studies Teachers**:

- ~400,000+ K-12 social studies teachers
- Varying preparation for civics instruction
- Face pressure and scrutiny
- Often passionate about civic mission

**Stakes**:

| Stake | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Professional preparation | Need training for current challenges |
| Autonomy | Freedom to teach controversial issues |
| Support | Resources and professional development |
| Protection | Shield from politically motivated attacks |

**Current Challenges**:

- Fear of controversy and complaint
- Limited professional development
- Inadequate preparation for media literacy
- Marginalization within school priorities

### Parents and Families

**Parent Perspectives**:

- Want children prepared for citizenship
- May have varying views on content
- Concerned about "indoctrination" (both directions)
- Important reinforcement of civic learning

**Family Role**:

- Primary civic socialization
- Model civic engagement
- Discuss current events
- Connect to community

### Communities

**Local Communities**:

- Need engaged citizens for local democracy
- Benefit from civic-minded young people
- Can provide authentic civic learning opportunities
- Partners in civic education

**Democratic Society**:

- Depends on informed, engaged citizenry
- Requires citizens capable of self-governance
- Needs shared civic understanding
- Benefits from effective deliberation

## Power Holders

### State Education Agencies

**Role**: Set standards, requirements, and policy

**Position**: Varies widely by state

**Current Actions**:

| State Approach | Examples |
|----------------|----------|
| Strong civics leadership | Illinois, Massachusetts |
| Moderate engagement | California, Florida |
| Limited attention | Many states |

**Leverage Points**:

- Standards development
- Assessment requirements
- Teacher certification
- Curriculum guidance

### School Districts

**Role**: Implement curriculum, allocate resources

**Position**: Respond to state requirements and local pressures

**Challenges**:

- Competing priorities
- Resource constraints
- Political pressures
- Variation within districts

### Teachers Unions

**Position**: Generally supportive of civic education

**Interests**:

- Teacher autonomy
- Professional development
- Job protection
- Curriculum input

**Concerns**:

- Political attacks on teachers
- Unfunded mandates
- Standardized testing pressure
- Teacher evaluation systems

### Education Publishers and Organizations

**Role**: Develop curriculum materials

**Position**: Follow state standards and market demands

**Key Players**:

- Major textbook publishers
- iCivics and similar nonprofits
- AP Government/Politics programs
- News literacy organizations

## Reform Opponents

### Testing and Accountability Advocates

**Position**: Focus on measurable academic outcomes

**Arguments**:

- Reading and math are foundational
- Civic education is "soft"
- Limited time requires prioritization
- Accountability drives improvement

**Impact**:

- Resist expanding assessed subjects
- Question civic education value
- Prioritize "core" academics

### Anti-"Indoctrination" Activists

**Position**: Schools should avoid political content

**Arguments**:

- Action civics is political activism
- Teachers push liberal agenda
- "CRT" pervades curriculum
- Parents should control civic formation

**Activities**:

- School board campaigns
- Legislative restrictions
- Parent protests
- Teacher targeting

### States' Rights Advocates

**Position**: Oppose federal involvement in curriculum

**Arguments**:

- Education is state/local function
- National standards threaten local control
- Diversity of approaches is strength
- Federal "mandates" inappropriate

### Some Teacher Groups

**Concerns**:

- Civic education assessments could be punitive
- Unfunded mandates burden teachers
- Political scrutiny of teaching
- Prefer voluntary approaches
