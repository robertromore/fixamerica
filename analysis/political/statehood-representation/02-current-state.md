# Statehood and Representation: Current State

## Washington, D.C.

### Population and Demographics

- **Population**: Approximately 700,000 (more than Wyoming or Vermont)
- **Demographics**: 46% Black, 41% White, 11% Hispanic, 4% Asian
- **Economy**: Federal government, professional services, tourism
- **Federal Taxes**: DC residents pay more federal taxes per capita than any state

### Current Status

- **Constitutional Basis**: Article I, Section 8 grants Congress exclusive jurisdiction
- **Local Government**: Home rule since 1973, but Congress can override any local law
- **Representation**: One non-voting delegate in the House; no Senate representation
- **Presidential Vote**: 23rd Amendment (1961) grants three electoral votes

### Recent Developments

- 2016 statehood referendum: 86% voted for statehood
- 2020: House passed HR 51 (statehood bill) 232-180
- 2021: House passed HR 51 again, 216-208
- Senate has not held a vote on DC statehood

### Key Issues

| Issue | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Congressional Override | Congress can block any DC law (e.g., gun laws, marijuana) |
| Budget Autonomy | DC cannot spend its own locally-raised funds without congressional approval |
| National Guard | President, not mayor, controls DC National Guard |
| Judicial | Federal courts handle local felonies |

## Puerto Rico

### Population and Demographics

- **Population**: Approximately 3.2 million (more than 20 states)
- **Demographics**: 99% Hispanic/Latino
- **Economy**: Manufacturing, pharmaceuticals, tourism
- **Citizenship**: U.S. citizens since 1917

### Current Status

- **Constitutional Basis**: Territory clause (Article IV, Section 3)
- **Local Government**: Commonwealth with elected governor and legislature
- **Representation**: One non-voting Resident Commissioner in House
- **Presidential Vote**: None (despite U.S. citizenship)
- **Federal Programs**: Excluded or capped in many programs (Medicaid, SSI, EITC)

### Recent Status Votes

| Year | Result |
|------|--------|
| 2012 | 54% rejected current status; of alternatives, 61% chose statehood |
| 2017 | 97% chose statehood (23% turnout after boycott) |
| 2020 | 52% voted for statehood (55% turnout) |

### Ongoing Crises

- **Debt Crisis**: $70+ billion in debt, PROMESA oversight board since 2016
- **Hurricane Maria (2017)**: 3,000+ deaths, slow federal response
- **Earthquakes (2020)**: Ongoing infrastructure damage
- **Population Decline**: 500,000+ residents left since 2010

## Other Territories

### Guam

- **Population**: 170,000
- **Status**: Unincorporated territory since 1898
- **Representation**: One non-voting delegate
- **Issues**: Military presence (30% of island), self-determination efforts

### U.S. Virgin Islands

- **Population**: 100,000
- **Status**: Unincorporated territory since 1917
- **Representation**: One non-voting delegate
- **Issues**: Economic struggles, hurricane recovery

### American Samoa

- **Population**: 55,000
- **Status**: Unincorporated, unorganized territory
- **Unique Feature**: Residents are U.S. nationals, not citizens
- **Issues**: Citizenship question, traditional land ownership

### Northern Mariana Islands

- **Population**: 50,000
- **Status**: Commonwealth since 1978
- **Representation**: One non-voting delegate since 2009
- **Issues**: Immigration, labor, economic development

## House of Representatives

### The 435 Cap

- **Origin**: Apportionment Act of 1929 froze House at 435 members
- **Original Ratio**: Constitution suggested 30,000 per representative
- **Current Ratio**: Average 760,000 per representative
- **Variation**: Ranges from 580,000 (Montana) to 990,000 (Delaware)

### Consequences of Frozen Size

| Effect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Reduced Representation | Each representative serves more constituents |
| Increased Inequality | Population differences among districts grow |
| Electoral College | Smaller states gain relative Electoral College weight |
| Accessibility | Representatives less accessible to constituents |

### Comparative Perspective

| Country | Lower House Size | Population per Member |
|---------|------------------|----------------------|
| United Kingdom | 650 | 102,000 |
| Germany | 736 | 113,000 |
| France | 577 | 117,000 |
| United States | 435 | 760,000 |

## Senate Representation

### Structural Reality

- Each state has exactly two senators regardless of population
- Wyoming (580,000): 2 senators = 290,000 per senator
- California (39 million): 2 senators = 19.5 million per senator
- Ratio: 67:1 population disparity with equal representation

### Political Implications

- 18% of population can elect Senate majority
- Rural, less diverse states overrepresented
- Affects judicial confirmations, treaties, legislation
- Insulated from change (Article V protects equal suffrage)

### Historical Context

- Great Compromise of 1787
- Originally state legislatures chose senators
- 17th Amendment (1913): Direct election
- No amendment has changed equal state representation

## Legal Framework

### Statehood Process

The Constitution (Article IV, Section 3) gives Congress power to admit new states:

1. Territory organizes and requests statehood
2. Congress passes enabling act
3. Territory drafts constitution
4. Congress approves admission
5. President signs
