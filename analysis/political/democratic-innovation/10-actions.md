# Democratic Innovation: Actions

## What You Can Do

Democratic innovation requires building demand from citizens and capacity in institutions. Here's how to contribute at every level.

## Individual Actions

### Educate Yourself

**Learn About Innovations**:

| Resource Type | Examples |
|---------------|----------|
| Books | Fishkin's *Democracy When the People Speak*; Gastil's *Legislature by Lot* |
| Websites | Participedia.net; newDemocracy.com.au |
| Podcasts | Democracy in Danger; How to Fix Democracy |
| Videos | TED talks by Fishkin, Tang, Van Reybrouck |

**Time Investment**: 2-5 hours to get basics; ongoing learning

### Participate When Opportunities Arise

**If You're Randomly Selected**:

- Say yes to citizens' assembly, jury duty, deliberative poll invitations
- Participate fully; it's a civic duty
- Share your experience with others

**If You're Not Selected**:

- Attend public hearings and town halls
- Participate in online consultations
- Join participatory budgeting votes
- Engage with digital democracy platforms

### Spread Awareness

**Talk to People**:

- Explain innovations to friends and family
- Share news stories about successful assemblies
- Counter misconceptions about citizen competence

**Use Social Media**:

- Follow and share from reform organizations
- Post about participation opportunities
- Amplify success stories

### Support Reform Organizations

**Donate**:

| Organization | Focus | Donation Link |
|--------------|-------|---------------|
| Healthy Democracy | Citizens' assemblies | healthydemocracy.org/donate |
| Participatory Budgeting Project | PB expansion | participatorybudgeting.org/donate |
| newDemocracy Foundation | Sortition research | newdemocracy.com.au |

**Volunteer**:

- Many organizations need volunteer facilitators
- Help with outreach and events
- Professional skills (design, communications, legal) valuable

## Community Actions

### Advocate for Local Adoption

**Participatory Budgeting**:

1. Research if your city has PB
2. If not, identify supportive council members
3. Gather examples from similar cities
4. Build coalition (community groups, civic organizations)
5. Present proposal to city council
6. Volunteer to help implement if adopted

**Citizens' Panels**:

1. Propose citizen advisory panels for contentious issues
2. Suggest pilot projects with limited scope
3. Offer to help design and facilitate
4. Document results to build case for expansion

### Build Local Coalitions

**Who to Involve**:

| Group | Why They Might Care |
|-------|---------------------|
| League of Women Voters | Civic engagement mission |
| Rotary/Lions Clubs | Community service orientation |
| Neighborhood associations | Want voice in local decisions |
| Faith communities | Justice and participation values |
| Parent-teacher groups | Schools affect families |
| Environmental groups | Want citizen input on environmental decisions |

**How to Approach**:

- Start with listening; understand their priorities
- Show how innovation serves their goals
- Propose specific, doable actions
- Build relationships before asking for commitment

### Organize Deliberative Events

**Community Dialogue**:

- Host facilitated discussions on local issues
- Use structured deliberation methods
- Invite diverse participants
- Document outcomes and share with officials

**Resources**:

- National Issues Forums Institute provides materials
- Living Room Conversations offers guides
- Better Angels/Braver Angels for cross-partisan dialogue

### Engage Local Media

**Pitch Stories**:

- Successful participation processes elsewhere
- Local officials' views on citizen engagement
- Upcoming opportunities for participation
- Problems that could benefit from citizen input

**Write Opinion Pieces**:

- Local newspaper letters to editor
- Community newsletter articles
- Social media commentary

## State-Level Actions

### Contact State Legislators

**Key Ask**: Support democratic innovation legislation

**Talking Points**:

1. Other states (Oregon) show it works
2. Citizens can handle complex issues (evidence from Ireland, France)
3. Builds trust in government
4. Bipartisan appeal (regular people having a voice)
5. Cost-effective compared to benefits

**Sample Message**:

> "I'm writing to ask you to support democratic innovation in [State]. Oregon's Citizens' Initiative Review shows that randomly selected citizens can evaluate ballot measures fairly and effectively. I'd like to see [State] adopt similar processes. Would you consider sponsoring legislation to pilot citizens' assemblies or participatory budgeting?"

### Support Ballot Initiatives

**Where Available**:

In initiative states, support measures that:

- Create citizens' assemblies
- Establish participatory budgeting
- Expand citizen engagement requirements

**How to Help**:

- Sign petitions
- Donate to campaigns
- Volunteer for signature gathering
- Talk to neighbors about voting yes

### Engage State Organizations

**Groups to Contact**:

| Organization Type | Why Engage |
|-------------------|------------|
| State League of Women Voters | Natural ally on civic engagement |
| State municipal league | Represents cities that could adopt |
| Good government groups | Aligned goals |
| Academic institutions | Research and legitimacy |
| State bar association | Legal expertise for design |

### Run for Office

If you're willing, running for local or state office lets you champion innovations from inside.

**Potential Platforms**:

- "I'll create real opportunities for citizen input"
- "Let's try participatory budgeting in our city/state"
- "Citizens should have a voice between elections"

## National Actions

### Contact Federal Representatives

**Key Asks**:

1. Support federal democratic innovation pilot program
2. Improve e-rulemaking and public comment processes
3. Study innovations for potential federal adoption
4. Fund research on what works

**When to Contact**:

- When relevant legislation is introduced
- During budget processes
- When democracy reform is in the news

## Professional Actions

### If You Work in Government

**At Any Level**:

- Propose pilot participation programs
- Improve existing public input processes
- Learn facilitation skills
- Connect with innovation practitioners
- Share examples from other jurisdictions

**Specific Roles**:

| Role | Opportunity |
|------|-------------|
| City manager | Propose PB or citizen panels |
| Planning department | Deliberative planning processes |
| Budget office | Participatory budget elements |
| Communications | Citizen engagement improvements |
| IT | Digital participation platforms |

### If You're an Academic

**Research**:

- Study local participation processes
- Evaluate innovations rigorously
- Publish accessible findings
- Present to practitioner audiences

**Teaching**:

- Include democratic innovation in courses
- Engage students in real participation
- Connect students with organizations

**Service**:

- Advise local governments
- Serve on citizen panels when asked
- Help design processes

### If You're a Journalist

**Coverage**:

- Report on local participation efforts
- Cover innovations elsewhere
- Profile citizens who participate
- Examine barriers and solutions

**Analysis**:

- Evaluate process quality
- Compare outcomes to traditional methods
- Hold governments accountable for commitments

### If You're in Philanthropy

**Funding Priorities**:

- Pilot programs with rigorous evaluation
- Capacity building and training
- Research and documentation
- Policy advocacy
- Infrastructure (platforms, networks)

**Approach**:

- Long-term commitment (5+ years)
- Coordinated funding with other foundations
- Learning agenda across grantees

### If You're a Facilitator

**Skills Development**:

- Get trained in deliberative facilitation
- Learn multiple methods (World Cafe, Open Space, etc.)
- Study successful cases
- Practice in low-stakes settings

**Opportunities**:

- Contract with governments and organizations
- Volunteer for civic organizations
- Train others

## Organizational Actions

### For Civic Organizations

**Internal Governance**:

- Use deliberative methods for organization decisions
- Experiment with liquid democracy for internal votes
- Model good participation practice

**External Advocacy**:

- Add democratic innovation to advocacy agenda
- Partner with innovation organizations
- Mobilize members to participate

### For Business Groups

**Chamber of Commerce and Similar**:

- Support participatory processes that affect business
- Participate constructively when invited
- Fund local innovation experiments
- Advocate for efficient, effective processes

### For Faith Communities

**Internal**:

- Use deliberative methods for congregation decisions
- Study democratic values in religious tradition

**External**:

- Advocate for citizen voice
- Host community dialogues
- Support marginalized voices in participation

## Key Messages

### For General Audiences

- "Regular people can make good decisions when given good information and time to deliberate."
- "Citizens' assemblies have solved problems that politicians couldn't."
- "Participatory budgeting lets you decide how your tax dollars are spent."
- "Other democracies do this successfully. Why not America?"

### For Conservative Audiences

- "This is about local control and citizen authority, not big government."
- "Random citizens, not bureaucrats or special interests, making decisions."
- "It's the ultimate term limitâ€”every citizen gets a turn."
- "The Founders trusted juries. We can trust citizens for more."

### For Progressive Audiences

- "True representation means everyone has a voice, not just donors."
- "Marginalized communities get included through random selection."
- "Deliberation produces more progressive outcomes on many issues."
- "This is deeper democracy, not just voting."

### For Officials

- "This builds trust and legitimacy for your decisions."
- "Citizens can help you navigate tough issues."
- "Other jurisdictions show it works and is cost-effective."
- "You can be a leader in democratic innovation."
