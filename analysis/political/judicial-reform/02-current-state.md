### Tenure Trends

Average tenure has increased dramatically:

| Era | Average Tenure |
|-----|----------------|
| 1789-1900 | 15 years |
| 1900-1970 | 15 years |
| 1970-2000 | 22 years |
| 2000-present | 25+ years (projected) |

Longer tenures result from:

- Appointment of younger justices
- Improved healthcare and longevity
- Strategic retirement timing
- High stakes of each vacancy

### Partisan Deterioration

The confirmation process has become increasingly partisan:

- Last justice confirmed with 90+ votes: Anthony Kennedy (1988)
- Last justice confirmed with 70+ votes: Stephen Breyer (1994)
- Recent confirmations decided along party lines
- Filibuster eliminated for Supreme Court nominees (2017)

### Merrick Garland Precedent

The refusal to consider Merrick Garland's nomination established:

- No obligation to hold hearings on nominations
- Senate majority controls whether vacancy is filled
- Rules can change based on partisan advantage
- Precedent easily reversed (Barrett confirmation 2020)

## Judicial Ethics

### Current Framework

**Lower Federal Courts**:

- Bound by Code of Conduct for United States Judges
- Financial disclosure requirements
- Recusal governed by 28 U.S.C. ยง 455
- Complaints investigated by judicial councils

**Supreme Court**:

- Not bound by any code of conduct (until 2023)
- Self-policing on recusal
- Financial disclosure but limited enforcement
- No mechanism for ethics complaints

### 2023 Ethics Code

In November 2023, the Court adopted its first code of conduct:

- Largely restates existing law
- No enforcement mechanism
- Justices decide their own recusal
- Critics call it inadequate

### Recent Ethics Controversies

| Justice | Issue | Status |
|---------|-------|--------|

### Recusal Standards

Current recusal practice:

- Each justice decides individually
- No appeal of recusal decisions
- No explanation required
- Standards vaguely defined
- Spouse activities rarely trigger recusal

### Judicial Vacancies

Vacancy rates fluctuate with political control:

- High vacancies during divided government
- Rapid filling when same party controls Senate and White House
- "Judicial emergencies" declared when vacancies create backlogs
- Some districts chronically understaffed

### Caseload Crisis

Federal courts face increasing caseloads:

- District court filings: ~400,000 annually
- Appeals court filings: ~50,000 annually
- Judge-to-case ratios strained
- Time to disposition increasing

### Magistrate Judges

Magistrate judges handle significant caseload:

- Appointed by district judges, not president
- 8-year terms, renewable
- Handle pretrial matters, some trials by consent
- Reduce Article III judge workload

### Pathway to Judgeship

Typical path to federal bench:

1. Elite law school
2. Federal clerkship (often Supreme Court)
3. BigLaw or DOJ experience
4. Federalist Society or ACS involvement
5. Political connections
6. Senate confirmation

### Role of Outside Groups

Interest groups shape judicial selection:

- Federalist Society vets conservative nominees
- American Constitution Society supports progressives
- Judicial Crisis Network runs confirmation campaigns
- Dark money funds advocacy for/against nominees

## Court Transparency

### What's Public

- Opinions and orders
- Oral argument transcripts (same day)
- Oral argument audio (same day, as of 2020)
- Financial disclosure reports (delayed)
- Docket information

### What's Not Public

- Conference deliberations
- Draft opinions
- Vote counts before announcement
- Recusal deliberations
- Ethics complaint handling
- Lobby and influence contacts

### Cameras in the Courtroom

The Supreme Court prohibits cameras:

- Only court not to allow any visual recording
- Audio released same day (recent change)
- Sketches allowed
- Resistance from justices citing various concerns
