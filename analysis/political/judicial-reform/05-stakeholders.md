# Judicial Reform: Stakeholders

## Affected Populations

### All Americans

The federal judiciary affects every citizen:

- Constitutional rights interpretation
- Criminal justice outcomes
- Business and contract disputes
- Immigration decisions
- Civil rights enforcement

### Litigants

Those directly interacting with federal courts:

- 400,000+ district court filings annually
- Criminal defendants (federal)
- Civil plaintiffs and defendants
- Businesses, governments, individuals

### Marginalized Communities

Groups whose rights depend on judicial protection:

- Racial minorities (voting rights, discrimination)
- Women (reproductive rights, workplace)
- LGBTQ+ individuals (marriage, discrimination)
- Immigrants (detention, deportation)
- Criminal defendants (procedural rights)

### Legal Profession

Lawyers, judges, law professors affected by court structure:

- 1.3 million lawyers nationally
- 870+ federal judges
- Law school faculty and students
- Court staff and clerks

## Power Holders

### Current Supreme Court Justices

**Interest**: Preserve authority, independence, and legitimacy

**Resources**:

- Constitutional tenure protection
- Final say on constitutional questions
- Control over Court procedures
- Public platform

**Position on Reform**: Generally oppose changes that reduce power or independence

### Federal Judiciary

**Interest**: Institutional independence, adequate resources

**Resources**:

- Judicial Conference recommendations
- Control over court administration
- Constitutional protections
- Professional prestige

**Position on Reform**: Mixed; support resources, oppose politicization

### Senate Judiciary Committee

**Interest**: Maintain confirmation power, advance partisan goals

**Key Members**:

- Chair: Substantial control over hearings and timing
- Ranking member: Minority voice
- Individual senators: Blue slip tradition (weakened)

**Position on Reform**: Depends on partisan control

### President

**Interest**: Appointment power, judicial support for agenda

**Resources**:

- Nomination power
- DOJ resources for litigation
- Bully pulpit

**Position on Reform**: Varies by president and party

### Department of Justice

**Interest**: Effective litigation, constitutional enforcement

**Resources**:

- Federal litigation authority
- Solicitor General's Supreme Court role
- Opinion-shaping through amicus briefs

**Position on Reform**: Generally supports institutional stability

## Reform Opponents

### Conservative Legal Movement

**Federalist Society**:

- 65,000+ members
- Shapes conservative judicial pipeline
- Opposes court-packing, term limits
- Supports originalism, current structure

**Heritage Foundation**:

- Conservative policy organization
- Opposes structural reforms
- Supports current conservative majority

**Judicial Crisis Network**:

- Dark money advocacy
- Runs campaigns against reform
- Supports conservative nominees

### Business Interests

**U.S. Chamber of Commerce**: Generally supports current Court's business-friendly rulings

**Position**: Oppose reforms that might change pro-business jurisprudence

### Republican Party Leadership

**Interest**: Preserve current conservative majority

**Position**: Strongly oppose court expansion; mixed on ethics reform

### Some Legal Establishment Figures

**Argument**: Reforms threaten judicial independence

**Position**: Even some liberals oppose court-packing as dangerous precedent

## Institutional Stakeholders

### Judicial Conference of the United States

**Role**: Policy-making body for federal courts

**Composition**: Chief Justice (chair), circuit chief judges, district judges

**Interests**: Resources, efficiency, judicial independence

**Position on Reform**: Generally conservative on structural changes

### Administrative Office of the U.S. Courts

**Role**: Administrative support for federal judiciary

**Interests**: Adequate funding, efficient operations

**Position on Reform**: Focus on resources over structure

### Federal Judicial Center

**Role**: Research and education for federal judges

**Interests**: Judicial quality, training

### State Courts

**Interest**: Federal-state court relationships

**Position**: Mixed; some states have reformed successfully

## Professional Stakeholders

### American Bar Association

**Role**: Professional association, rates judicial nominees

**Interests**: Professional standards, access to justice

**Position on Reform**: Supports ethics reform; cautious on structural changes

### Law Schools

**Interest**: Legal education, shaping profession

**Position**: Academic freedom to study and advocate various reforms

### Law Firms

**Interest**: Predictability, access to courts

**Position**: Generally cautious; business depends on current system

## Coalition-Building Opportunities

### Potential Bipartisan Agreement

- **Ethics reform**: Broad public support across parties
- **Transparency**: Few defend current opacity
- **Lower court resources**: Both parties want functioning courts

### Areas of Partisan Divide

- **Court expansion**: Strongly opposed by Republicans
- **Term limits**: Some conservative openness, but mostly partisan
- **Jurisdiction stripping**: Partisan; depends on whose ox is gored

### Key Persuadable Groups

- Moderate legal establishment figures
- Former Republican judges concerned about legitimacy
- Business interests concerned about instability
- State court judges with reform experience
