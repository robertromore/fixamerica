# Federalism Reform: Stakeholders

## State Governments

### Governors

**Interest**: Preserve and expand state authority while ensuring adequate federal resources.

**Key Organizations**:

- **National Governors Association (NGA)**: Bipartisan, represents all governors
- **Republican Governors Association (RGA)**: Partisan advocacy
- **Democratic Governors Association (DGA)**: Partisan advocacy

**Positions**:

| Issue | Generally Favor | Generally Oppose |
|-------|-----------------|------------------|
| Unfunded mandates | Oppose/want funding | Support reform |
| Grant flexibility | More flexibility | Excessive conditions |
| Preemption | Context-dependent | Varies by issue |
| State fiscal aid | Support | N/A |

**Power Resources**:

- Electoral influence in presidential system
- Implementation control over federal programs
- State resistance possibilities
- Media and public attention

### State Legislators

**Interest**: Protect legislative authority from federal mandates and preemption.

**Key Organizations**:

- **National Conference of State Legislatures (NCSL)**: Bipartisan, all states
- **Council of State Governments (CSG)**: Research and convening
- **American Legislative Exchange Council (ALEC)**: Conservative model legislation
- **State Innovation Exchange (SiX)**: Progressive model legislation

**Positions**:

- Generally oppose unfunded mandates
- Favor state policy flexibility
- Divided on specific preemption issues
- Support interstate cooperation mechanisms

**Power Resources**:

- Control over state policy
- Ratification power for amendments
- Federal election administration role
- Constituent services and visibility

### State Attorneys General

**Interest**: Protect state legal interests and pursue state policy goals through litigation.

**Key Organizations**:

- **National Association of Attorneys General (NAAG)**: Bipartisan
- **Republican Attorneys General Association (RAGA)**: Partisan coordination
- **Democratic Attorneys General Association (DAGA)**: Partisan coordination

**Role in Federalism**:

- Bring lawsuits challenging federal overreach
- Defend state laws from federal preemption claims
- Multistate litigation coordination
- Consumer protection enforcement (sometimes preempted)

**Recent Activity**:

- ACA challenges (red states)
- Environmental regulation challenges (red states)
- Immigration policy challenges (blue states)
- Consumer protection enforcement

## Local Governments

### Cities

**Interest**: Preserve local autonomy from both federal and state preemption.

**Key Organizations**:

- **National League of Cities (NLC)**: Represents municipalities
- **U.S. Conference of Mayors**: Focus on larger cities
- **National Association of Towns and Townships**: Smaller municipalities

**Positions**:

| Issue | City Position |
|-------|---------------|
| State preemption | Oppose (minimum wage, sanctuary, etc.) |
| Federal grants | Support direct federal-city funding |
| Unfunded mandates | Oppose from both levels |
| Local autonomy | Strong support |

**Particular Concerns**:

- State preemption of local minimum wage
- State preemption of local anti-discrimination laws
- State interference with sanctuary policies
- State takeover of local functions

### Counties

**Interest**: Adequate resources and authority for county services.

**Key Organizations**:

- **National Association of Counties (NACo)**: Represents all counties

**Role**:

- Often administer federal/state programs
- Bear unfunded mandate costs
- Less visibility than cities
- Rural interests prominent

### School Districts

**Interest**: Flexibility in implementing federal education requirements.

**Key Organizations**:

- **National School Boards Association (NSBA)**
- **Council of the Great City Schools**
- **American Association of School Administrators**

**Federalism Concerns**:

- ESSA implementation flexibility
- Special education mandates (IDEA)
- Civil rights compliance
- Testing and accountability requirements

## Federal Government

### Congress

**Interest**: Maintain federal authority while responding to constituent demands.

**Key Committees**:

- Senate Committee on Homeland Security and Governmental Affairs
- House Committee on Oversight and Accountability
- Authorization committees with state/local programs
- Appropriations subcommittees

**Member Types**:

| Type | Federalism Tendency |
|------|---------------------|
| Former governors | Often more state-friendly |
| Former state legislators | Mixed |
| Former local officials | Local perspective |
| Career federal | Federal orientation |

### Executive Branch

**Interest**: Implement federal programs, often through states.

**Key Actors**:

- **Office of Management and Budget**: Grant conditions, regulatory review
- **Domestic Policy Council**: Policy coordination
- **Agency heads**: Program implementation
- **Regional offices**: State relationships

**Agency Perspectives**:

- Generally favor federal authority
- Prefer uniform national standards
- Sometimes cooperative with states
- Preemption through regulation common

### Federal Courts

**Interest**: Interpret constitutional federalism boundaries.

**Key Doctrines**:

- Commerce Clause interpretation
- Anti-commandeering doctrine
- Spending Clause limits
- State sovereign immunity
- Preemption analysis

**Judicial Philosophies**:

| Approach | Tendency |
|----------|----------|
| Textualist/Originalist | May favor state authority |
| Living Constitution | More flexible, context-dependent |
| Pragmatic | Balancing approach |

## Interest Groups

### Business Groups

**Interest**: Uniform national standards (often at lower level), regulatory certainty.

**Key Organizations**:

- **U.S. Chamber of Commerce**: Generally favors federal preemption
- **National Federation of Independent Business**: More state-focused
- **Industry trade associations**: Sector-specific preemption preferences

**Typical Positions**:

- Favor federal preemption of stricter state consumer protection
- Oppose state regulation beyond federal minimums
- Support preemption of state tort law
- Favor uniform national standards over state patchwork

### Labor Unions

**Interest**: Strong worker protections, whether federal or state.

**Key Organizations**:

- **AFL-CIO**: Federation of unions
- **SEIU**: Service workers
- **AFSCME**: Public employees (state/local)

**Positions**:

- Historically favor federal standards as floor
- Now often support state experimentation (Fight for $15)
- Oppose state preemption of local minimum wage
- Support federal collective bargaining protections

### Consumer and Public Interest Groups

**Interest**: Strong protections at whatever level achievable.

**Key Organizations**:

- **Consumer Federation of America**
- **Public Citizen**
- **Center for Science in the Public Interest**
- **Natural Resources Defense Council**

**Positions**:

- Oppose federal ceiling preemption
- Support federal floor with state flexibility to exceed
- Favor strong enforcement at both levels
- Oppose industry-driven preemption

### Civil Rights Organizations

**Interest**: Federal protection of civil rights as floor.

**Key Organizations**:

- **NAACP**: Racial justice
- **ACLU**: Civil liberties
- **Leadership Conference on Civil and Human Rights**: Coalition
- **Lambda Legal**: LGBTQ+ rights

**Historical Position**:

- Strong federal civil rights standards
- Federal override of discriminatory state laws
- Federal enforcement mechanisms

**Evolution**:

- Still support strong federal standards
- Increasingly use states for advancement
- State-level innovations (marriage equality, drug reform)

## Think Tanks and Academics

### Conservative/Libertarian

**Key Organizations**:

- **Cato Institute**: Libertarian, generally pro-federalism
- **Heritage Foundation**: Conservative, states' rights rhetoric
- **American Enterprise Institute**: Mixed positions
- **Federalist Society**: Judicial federalism

**General Positions**:

- Favor limited federal government
- Support state policy experimentation
- Oppose federal mandates
- But support federal preemption when serving conservative ends

### Progressive/Liberal

**Key Organizations**:

- **Center for American Progress**: Progressive policy
- **Brookings Institution**: Center-left research
- **Economic Policy Institute**: Labor-focused

**General Positions**:

- Historically favor federal standards
- Increasingly recognize state innovation role
- Oppose race-to-bottom federalism
- Support federal floors with state flexibility

### Academic Experts

**Key Voices**:

- Constitutional law scholars
- Public administration researchers
- Political scientists studying federalism
- Economists analyzing fiscal federalism

**Contributions**:

- Theoretical frameworks for federalism
- Empirical analysis of federal-state programs
- Historical analysis of federalism evolution
- Comparative analysis with other federal systems

## Coalitions and Alliances

### "Big Seven" State and Local Organizations

Traditional coalition:

- National Governors Association
- National Conference of State Legislatures
- Council of State Governments
- National League of Cities
- U.S. Conference of Mayors
- National Association of Counties
- International City/County Management Association

**Joint Advocacy**:

- Oppose unfunded mandates
- Support state/local fiscal aid
- Seek grant simplification
- Lobby for flexibility

### Issue-Specific Coalitions

**Examples**:

- State environmental agencies coalition
- State health officials associations
- Education organizations coalition
- Transportation officials organizations

### Cross-Ideological Alliances

**Potential Common Ground**:

| Issue | Possible Alliance |
|-------|-------------------|
| Unfunded mandate reform | Conservatives + state/local officials |
| Grant simplification | Good government reformers |
| Interstate compact facilitation | Regional coalitions |
| Preemption transparency | Consumer groups + states |

## Power Analysis

### Most Influential Stakeholders

| Stakeholder | Influence Level | Source of Influence |
|-------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Business groups | High | Campaign contributions, lobbying |
| Governors | High | Implementation control, visibility |
| State AGs | Moderate-High | Litigation capacity |
| Federal agencies | High | Administrative control |
| Courts | High | Constitutional interpretation |
| State legislators | Moderate | Collective influence, implementation |
| Local governments | Low-Moderate | Limited voice in federal process |
| Advocacy groups | Moderate | Issue-specific influence |

### Who Benefits from Status Quo

- Businesses preferring preemption
- Federal agencies with power
- Interests advantaged by complexity
- Well-resourced states able to navigate system

### Who Loses from Status Quo

- States bearing unfunded mandates
- Local governments preempted by states
- Citizens with poor accountability
- Interests unable to influence federal policy
- Under-resourced governments
