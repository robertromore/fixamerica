# Federalism Reform: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes federalism reform through nine distinct political perspectives, evaluating current state assessments, root cause analyses, proposed solutions, and legislation. Federalism reform raises foundational questions about American governance: How should responsibilities be divided between federal, state, and local governments? When should federal law preempt state law? How can unfunded mandates be curbed? Should states have more flexibility to innovate, and should federal standards set floors or ceilings? The cross-cutting nature of federalism means traditional partisan alignments often break down, with each perspective supporting or opposing federal power depending on the policy area.

**Bad Faith Indicators:**

- Hidden interests (stated vs actual motivations differ significantly)
- Zero-sum mindset (oppose proposals because opponents support them)
- Movable goalposts (shifting objections when concerns are addressed)
- Selective principle application (principles apply only when convenient)
- Pretextual arguments (stated reasons not the real reasons)

## Perspective Analyses

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 2/5 - Low Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Claims to support states' rights but supports federal preemption when industry prefers it |
| Principle consistency | "Federalism" applied extremely selectively; supports state authority on guns and immigration enforcement but federal preemption on banking, telecommunications, and product liability |
| Goalpost stability | States' rights rhetoric shifts dramatically depending on whether states pursue conservative or progressive policies |
| Zero-sum behavior | Significant; opposes state authority when progressive states act but champions it for conservative states |

**Justification:** Conservative engagement with federalism is among the least consistent of any perspective on any issue. While conservatives genuinely value limited federal government in the abstract, the actual application of federalism principles is overwhelmingly policy-driven. Republicans in Congress have supported federal preemption of state consumer protection, environmental regulation, tort law, and financial regulation while opposing federal preemption of state gun laws and immigration enforcement. The "states' rights" claim becomes most vociferous when federal law advances progressive priorities. This pattern is well-documented across decades.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- *Federal preemption for industry*: Republicans supported federal preemption in the Class Action Fairness Act (2005), Federal Arbitration Act expansions, National Bank Act preemption of state banking regulations. See: Buzbee, William, ed. *Preemption Choice* (2009), ch. 3-5.
- *State authority for conservative policies*: Same legislators champion state authority on concealed carry reciprocity, voter ID, and immigration enforcement. See: National Conference of State Legislatures, "Federal Preemption" report (2024).
- *Anti-preemption when states go progressive*: Republicans attacked California's emissions standards and state net neutrality laws using federal preemption. See: *California v. EPA* litigation history.
- *Partisan federalism pattern*: Political scientists have documented that both parties practice "fair-weather federalism," but the conservative claim to consistent federalism makes the inconsistency more notable. See: Bulman-Pozen, Jessica. "Partisan Federalism." *Harvard Law Review* 127 (2014).

### Constitutionalist Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 3/5 - Mixed Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Genuine commitment to enumerated powers doctrine mixed with selective application |
| Principle consistency | "States' rights" invoked inconsistently; supports federal action when it advances preferred policies |
| Goalpost stability | Constitutional principles shift depending on political context |
| Zero-sum behavior | Moderate; constitutional arguments deployed more against progressive federal action |

**Justification:** Constitutionalist engagement with federalism includes genuine commitment to enumerated powers and the Tenth Amendment. However, the selective application is significant: constitutionalists rarely challenge federal drug laws, immigration enforcement, or defense spending on enumerated powers grounds, despite questionable constitutional authority for some of these activities. The "states' rights" argument is most vigorously deployed against federal civil rights protections, environmental regulation, and healthcare programs, creating a partisan pattern.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- *Selective enumerated powers*: Federalist Society scholars challenge ACA, EPA, and OSHA on Tenth Amendment grounds but rarely challenge federal drug scheduling, PATRIOT Act surveillance, or military spending. See: Barnett, Randy. "The Proper Scope of Federal Power" (2003).
- *Historical pattern*: "States' rights" has been invoked primarily to resist federal civil rights enforcement since Reconstruction. See: *New York v. United States*, 505 U.S. 144 (1992); *Printz v. United States*, 521 U.S. 898 (1997).
- *Commerce Clause inconsistency*: Constitutionalists challenge Commerce Clause authority for progressive legislation but not for conservative priorities. See: *NFIB v. Sebelius*, 567 U.S. 519 (2012), briefs filed.

### Religious Right Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 2/5 - Low Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | "Federalism" invoked primarily to protect religious exemptions from federal nondiscrimination requirements |
| Principle consistency | Supports state authority when states protect religious exemptions but federal authority when needed to override progressive states (RFRA-style federal laws) |
| Goalpost stability | Federalism positions shift based on which level of government is more favorable to religious liberty claims |
| Zero-sum behavior | Significant; federalism positions track with culture war alignment |

**Justification:** Religious Right engagement with federalism is almost entirely policy-driven. When state-level protections for religious liberty exceed federal law, the Religious Right champions state autonomy. When federal law provides broader religious exemptions than progressive states would allow, the Religious Right supports federal preemption. This is not principled federalism but rather a strategic calculation about which level of government is more favorable to their policy preferences on any given issue.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- *Federal RFRA support*: Religious Right strongly supports federal Religious Freedom Restoration Act overriding state nondiscrimination laws. See: *Burwell v. Hobby Lobby*, 573 U.S. 682 (2014), Religious Right amicus briefs.
- *State authority for exemptions*: Simultaneously supports state RFRA laws that go beyond federal protections. See: Indiana RFRA (2015) supported by Religious Right.
- *Opposition to state authority*: Religious Right opposed state authority when California, Oregon, and others enacted nondiscrimination laws without religious exemptions. See: Alliance Defending Freedom state-level litigation.

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The cross-cutting nature of federalism creates multiple viable reform coalitions depending on the specific reform:

- **UMRA reform:** Conservative + Libertarian + Centrist + Populist + Liberal (5 perspectives)
- **Preemption transparency:** Progressive + Liberal + Libertarian + Populist + Centrist + Democratic Socialist (6 perspectives)
- **Compact facilitation:** Libertarian + Conservative + Constitutionalist + Centrist + Liberal + Progressive (6 perspectives)

This unusual pattern means federalism reform can proceed on multiple fronts with different coalitions.

### Key Obstacles

1. **Industry opposition** to preemption reform: corporations that benefit from federal preemption of state regulation will lobby intensely
2. **Selective federalism**: Both parties practice "fair-weather federalism," making principled reform difficult
3. **Congressional self-interest**: Congress is reluctant to limit its own power through unfunded mandate reform or preemption constraints
4. **Complexity**: Intergovernmental reform is complex and lacks public salience

### Fair-Weather Federalism Problem

The two lowest-consistency perspectives (Conservative at 2/5, Religious Right at 2/5) claim federalism as a core principle but apply it selectively. This creates two challenges:

1. **Credibility gap**: Inconsistent invocation of "states' rights" undermines the principle
2. **Coalition instability**: Partners who invoke federalism strategically may defect when reform would constrain their preferred policies

**Strategy for handling:**

1. **Expose inconsistencies**: Document cases where federalism is invoked selectively
2. **Build on genuine areas of agreement**: UMRA reform and compact facilitation enjoy authentic support
3. **Frame reforms as non-partisan**: Focus on procedural fairness rather than policy outcomes
4. **Engage governors and state legislators**: Bipartisan state officials are more consistently federalist than federal politicians

### Recommended Approach

1. **Immediate priority:** UMRA reform -- broadest coalition, concrete benefits, bipartisan appeal
2. **Near-term:** Interstate compact facilitation -- broad support, constitutional basis, non-controversial
3. **Medium-term:** Preemption transparency and accountability -- 6-perspective coalition, exposes industry influence
4. **Long-term:** Federal floors framework and local government protection -- require stronger coalition

**Sequence matters:** UMRA reform demonstrates good faith to state/local government partners. Compact facilitation builds institutional cooperation capacity. Preemption transparency creates data that supports the case for stronger reform. Federal floors and local government protection require building public understanding of how corporate preemption harms communities.
