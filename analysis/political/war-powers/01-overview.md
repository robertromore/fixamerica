# War Powers Reform: Overview

## Executive Summary

The power to commit the nation to war is the most consequential decision a government can make. The Constitution divides this authority between Congress (which has the sole power to declare war) and the President (who serves as Commander-in-Chief). Over seven decades, this balance has shifted dramatically toward executive dominance. Presidents now routinely initiate military action without congressional authorization, deploy forces for years under outdated authorizations, and conduct covert operations with minimal oversight. This erosion of democratic accountability over war represents one of the most significant constitutional failures in American governance.

## The Core Problem

| Aspect | Issue |
|--------|-------|
| Constitutional imbalance | Presidents initiate wars without declarations |
| AUMF abuse | 2001 AUMF used for 20+ years across 20+ countries |
| Congressional abdication | Congress avoids war votes to escape accountability |
| Oversight gaps | Covert operations, drones, cyber warfare lack transparency |
| Nuclear authority | Single person can initiate nuclear strike |

## Why It Matters

**For Democracy**

- Citizens have no say in decisions to go to war
- Representatives avoid accountability for war decisions
- Executive power expands unchecked
- Constitutional structure eroded

**For National Security**

- Wars without public debate lack legitimacy
- Open-ended conflicts become endless
- Covert actions evade strategic review
- Nuclear authority lacks safeguards

**For Human Life**

- Military personnel deployed without democratic authorization
- Civilian casualties from drone strikes lack accountability
- Endless wars continue without strategic purpose
- Costs borne by few while most Americans unaffected

## Key Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Declaration of war | Formal congressional act under Article I, Section 8 |
| AUMF | Authorization for Use of Military Force, a statutory authorization short of formal declaration |
| War Powers Resolution | 1973 law attempting to reassert congressional authority |
| 60-day clock | WPR provision requiring withdrawal absent authorization |
| Covert action | Intelligence operations intended to influence events abroad |
| Finding | Presidential authorization required for covert action |
| Gang of Eight | Congressional leaders briefed on sensitive intelligence |
| First use | Initiating nuclear strike without being attacked first |

## The Stakes

**Current Reality**

- No formal declaration of war since 1942
- 2001 AUMF used to justify operations in 20+ countries
- Presidents order strikes with no advance congressional notice
- One person controls nuclear arsenal capable of ending civilization
- Cyber operations conducted with minimal oversight

**What's at Risk**

- Constitutional separation of powers
- Democratic accountability for war
- Strategic coherence of military operations
- Global stability and nuclear deterrence
- American credibility in international law
