# Congressional Reform: History

## The Founding Design

### Constitutional Framework

**Article I: The Legislative Branch**

- First and most detailed article
- Granted most enumerated powers
- "All legislative Powers herein granted"
- Designed as check on executive

**Bicameral Structure**

| Chamber | Design Purpose |
|---------|----------------|
| House | Represent the people, population-based |
| Senate | Represent states, equal representation |
| Compromise | Great Compromise of 1787 |
| Intent | Balance popular will and state interests |

### Original Procedures

**Simple Majority Rule**

- Both chambers operated by majority rule
- No filibuster initially
- Vice President broke ties in Senate
- Relatively simple procedures

**Early Challenges**

- First filibuster-like tactics (1790s)
- Development of committee system
- Evolution of party organization
- Gradual procedural complexity

## The 19th Century

### Development of Procedures

**Senate Evolution**

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 1806 | Previous question motion eliminated (enabling filibuster) |
| 1840s-50s | Filibuster emerges as tactic |
| 1880s | Filibuster used more frequently |

**House Evolution**

| Development | Effect |
|-------------|--------|
| Speaker's power grows | Central authority |
| Committee system develops | Expertise and jurisdiction |
| Rules Committee created | Procedure management |
| Minority protections debated | Ongoing tension |

### Party Development

**Two-Party System Emerges**

- Federalists vs. Democratic-Republicans
- Democrats vs. Whigs
- Republicans vs. Democrats
- Party loyalty and discipline

## The Progressive Era

### Senate Reforms

**17th Amendment (1913)**

- Direct election of senators
- Previously chosen by state legislatures
- Response to corruption and deadlock
- Democratized Senate selection

**Cloture Rule (1917)**

| Feature | Provision |
|---------|-----------|
| Purpose | End filibusters |
| Threshold | 2/3 of senators voting |
| First use | 1919 (Treaty of Versailles) |
| Effect | Theoretically limited filibusters |

### House Reforms

**Cannon Revolt (1910)**

| Before | After |
|--------|-------|
| Speaker Cannon's autocratic rule | Reduced speaker power |
| Rules Committee as speaker's tool | Rules Committee independent |
| Committee chairs appointed by speaker | Seniority system |

## Mid-20th Century: The Modern Congress

### Committee Government Era

**Strong Committees (1920s-1970s)**

| Feature | Effect |
|---------|--------|
| Committee chairs | Powerful, senior members |
| Expertise development | Substantive knowledge |
| Bipartisan coalitions | Cross-party cooperation |
| Deliberative process | Extended consideration |

**Seniority System**

- Chairs determined by length of service
- Often conservative (especially Southern Democrats)
- Stable but not always responsive
- Reduced party leadership power

### Legislative Reorganization Act (1946)

**Key Provisions**

| Reform | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Reduced committee number | Streamlined jurisdiction |
| Professional staff | Increased expertise |
| Registered lobbyists | Transparency (limited) |
| Legislative Reference Service | Research capacity |

### Civil Rights Era

**Filibuster as Obstacle**

| Year | Filibuster Target |
|------|-------------------|
| 1957 | Civil Rights Act (weakened to pass) |
| 1964 | Civil Rights Act (67 days, overcome) |
| 1965 | Voting Rights Act |

**Civil Rights Filibuster Broken (1964)**

- First successful cloture on civil rights
- Required bipartisan coalition
- Demonstrated both filibuster's power and limits
- Changed perception of filibuster

## The Reform Era (1970s)

### Congressional Budget Act (1974)

**Created**

| Institution | Purpose |
|-------------|---------|
| Budget committees | Congressional budget process |
| Congressional Budget Office | Independent analysis |
| Budget resolution | Set spending/revenue targets |
| Reconciliation process | Fast-track budget legislation |

### Sunshine Reforms

**Opening Congress**

| Reform | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Open committee meetings | Public observation |
| Recorded votes | Accountability |
| Financial disclosure | Ethics transparency |
| Open conference committees | Visibility |

### Committee Chair Changes

**Seniority Modified**

| Change | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Secret ballot for chairs | Accountability to caucus |
| Subcommittee bill of rights | Subcommittee autonomy |
| Staff for minority | Some balance |

### Ethics Reforms

**Post-Watergate**

| Reform | Purpose |
|--------|---------|
| Ethics committees | Self-policing |
| Financial disclosure | Transparency |
| Limits on outside income | Reduce conflicts |
| Gift rules | Prevent influence |

### Filibuster Threshold Change (1975)

**Cloture Reduced to 60 Votes**

| Feature | Change |
|---------|--------|
| Old threshold | 2/3 of senators voting |
| New threshold | 3/5 of entire Senate (60) |
| Intent | Make cloture easier |
| Unintended effect | Normalized 60-vote requirement |

## Lessons from History

### Lesson 1: Reform Is Possible

**Examples of Successful Reform**

- 17th Amendment (direct election)
- 1946 reorganization
- 1970s sunshine reforms
- Budget process creation

### Lesson 2: Reforms Have Unintended Consequences

| Reform | Unintended Effect |
|--------|-------------------|
| 1975 cloture reduction | Normalized 60-vote threshold |
| Sunshine reforms | Reduced behind-scenes compromise |
| Primary system | Ideological selection |
| Staff increases | Also more lobbyist access |

### Lesson 3: Institutions Adapt

**Adaptation Patterns**

- Rules exploited beyond original intent
- Minorities find new tactics
- Majorities create workarounds
- Equilibrium shifts over time

### Lesson 4: Context Matters

**Factors Driving Change**

| Factor | Examples |
|--------|----------|
| Crisis | Watergate, 9/11 |
| Leadership | Strong speakers, reform champions |
| Public pressure | Civil rights movement |
| Partisan balance | Periods of change |

### Lesson 5: The Filibuster Has Always Been Contested

**Ongoing Debate**

- Never intended by founders
- Created by accident (1806)
- Used to block civil rights
- Repeatedly reformed and circumvented
