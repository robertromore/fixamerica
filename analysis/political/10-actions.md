# Political Reform: Actions

## For Individuals

### Educate Yourself

**Understand the System**

- [ ] Learn how your state's election system works
- [ ] Research your state's redistricting process
- [ ] Understand how campaign finance works in your district
- [ ] Follow reform organizations to stay informed

**Find Your Information**

- [ ] Look up who funds your representatives (OpenSecrets.org)
- [ ] Check your state's ballot initiative process
- [ ] Learn about reform efforts already underway in your state
- [ ] Research how your representatives vote on reform issues

### Vote and Participate

**Every Election**

- [ ] Vote in primaries (where real decisions often happen)
- [ ] Vote in local elections (often lowest turnout, highest impact)
- [ ] Research candidates' positions on reform
- [ ] Support reform-minded candidates regardless of party

**Beyond Voting**

- [ ] Attend town halls and ask about reform
- [ ] Write letters to editors about reform issues
- [ ] Share reform content on social media (constructively)
- [ ] Talk to friends and family about why reform matters

### Contact Representatives

**Effective Communication**

- [ ] Call congressional offices (most impactful)
- [ ] Write personalized letters (more impact than form emails)
- [ ] Attend town halls and ask questions
- [ ] Request meetings with staff

**What to Say**

- Focus on specific reforms (RCV, redistricting, disclosure)
- Share personal story of why this matters
- Ask for their position and follow up
- Thank them when they take good positions

**Sample Script for RCV Call**
> "Hi, I'm [name] from [city]. I'm calling to ask [Representative] to support ranked-choice voting. I believe voters should be able to rank candidates and not feel forced to choose the lesser evil. Has the Representative taken a position on RCV? Thank you."

### Financial Support

**Where to Give**

- [ ] Donate to reform organizations (FairVote, RepresentUs, etc.)
- [ ] Support ballot initiative campaigns in your state
- [ ] Give to reform-minded candidates
- [ ] Contribute to small-donor matching programs if available

**Maximizing Impact**

- Small recurring donations provide stable funding
- Give early in ballot initiative campaigns (builds momentum)
- Consider giving to races outside your state/district
- Pool resources with like-minded friends

### Modeling Better Discourse

**In Conversations**

- [ ] Listen to people you disagree with
- [ ] Find common ground on process (even if policy differs)
- [ ] Avoid demonizing the "other side"
- [ ] Focus on shared frustrations with the system

**Online**

- [ ] Share constructive content, not outrage
- [ ] Engage respectfully across partisan lines
- [ ] Amplify reform voices
- [ ] Don't feed polarization algorithms

## For Communities

### Local Reform Efforts

**City-Level RCV**

- Many cities can adopt RCV for local elections
- Organize petition campaigns or city council advocacy
- Build coalition across partisan lines
- Document success to build momentum for state adoption

**Local Redistricting**

- Advocate for independent redistricting at city/county level
- Attend redistricting hearings
- Submit public comment on proposed maps
- Challenge unfair maps

**Civic Engagement**

- Organize voter registration drives
- Host candidate forums focused on reform
- Create local civic clubs or chapters
- Partner with schools on civic education

### Building Local Coalitions

**Finding Partners**

- [ ] Identify local reform groups and activists
- [ ] Reach out to civic organizations (Rotary, League of Women Voters)
- [ ] Engage business leaders frustrated with dysfunction
- [ ] Connect with religious/faith communities
- [ ] Partner with labor unions and professional associations

**Coalition Principles**

- Focus on process, not partisan outcomes
- Include voices from across political spectrum
- Respect differences while building on shared goals
- Celebrate local identity and ownership

### Community Education

**Host Events**

- [ ] Screen reform documentaries (Slay the Dragon, etc.)
- [ ] Invite speakers from reform organizations
- [ ] Organize discussion groups on reform topics
- [ ] Host debates between candidates on reform issues

**Media Engagement**

- [ ] Write op-eds for local papers
- [ ] Contact local TV/radio for coverage
- [ ] Create social media presence for local efforts
- [ ] Document and share local success stories

## For Organizations

### Existing Civic Groups

**Integrate Reform Into Mission**

- Add political reform to advocacy agenda
- Educate members about reform issues
- Endorse reform ballot initiatives
- Partner with reform-focused organizations

**Leverage Organizational Capacity**

- Use email lists to mobilize for reform
- Provide volunteer infrastructure
- Offer meeting space for reform events
- Contribute organizational credibility

### Businesses

**Why Businesses Should Care**

- Political dysfunction creates uncertainty
- Polarization is bad for the economy
- Employees want to work for civic-minded companies
- Some industries harmed by regulatory capture

**What Businesses Can Do**

- Sign onto business leader letters supporting reform
- Encourage employee civic participation
- Sponsor nonpartisan voter registration
- Support reform organizations financially
- Use voice to advocate for fair elections

### Academic Institutions

**Research**

- Study reform implementations
- Evaluate reform impacts
- Publish accessible summaries
- Provide expert testimony

**Education**

- Teach about electoral systems and reform
- Host conferences and events
- Train future reformers
- Partner with reform organizations

### Media Organizations

**Coverage**

- Report on reform efforts and successes
- Explain how current system fails
- Investigate money in politics
- Platform diverse voices on reform

**Editorial**

- Take editorial positions on reform
- Endorse ballot initiatives
- Challenge false equivalence
- Model constructive discourse

## For Advocates

### Campaign Strategy

**Ballot Initiative Campaigns**

*Phase 1: Qualify for Ballot*

- [ ] Determine signature requirements
- [ ] Draft ballot language (engage legal experts)
- [ ] Build signature-gathering infrastructure
- [ ] Launch petition campaign
- [ ] Submit signatures and defend against challenges

*Phase 2: Win the Campaign*

- [ ] Develop message and materials
- [ ] Build volunteer field operation
- [ ] Raise funds for advertising
- [ ] Earn media coverage
- [ ] Execute get-out-the-vote effort

*Phase 3: Implementation*

- [ ] Monitor implementation
- [ ] Counter opposition efforts to undermine
- [ ] Document success for other states
- [ ] Defend against legal challenges

### Legislative Advocacy

**Building Support**

- [ ] Identify reform-minded legislators
- [ ] Build relationships with staff
- [ ] Provide research and talking points
- [ ] Generate constituent pressure
- [ ] Offer political cover through coalitions

**Timing**

- Introduce bills early in session
- Build momentum through hearings
- Push for floor votes
- Hold representatives accountable

### Messaging and Communications

**Core Messages**

| Audience | Message Frame |
|----------|---------------|
| Conservatives | "Politicians shouldn't pick their voters" |
| Progressives | "Get big money out of politics" |
| Independents | "Break the two-party stranglehold" |
| All | "Let voters have real choices" |

**Storytelling**

- Use personal stories of voter frustration
- Highlight specific examples of system failure
- Show how reform has worked elsewhere
- Paint vision of functional democracy

**Avoiding Pitfalls**

- Don't predict partisan outcomes ("this helps Democrats/Republicans")
- Don't attack patriotism of opponents
- Don't make reform seem complicated
- Don't promise utopia—be honest about limitations

### Coalition Management

**Maintaining Cross-Partisan Coalitions**

- [ ] Regular communication with all partners
- [ ] Let each side use their own messengers
- [ ] Stay focused on process, not outcomes
- [ ] Navigate disagreements respectfully
- [ ] Celebrate shared victories

**Expanding the Coalition**

- [ ] Identify persuadable opponents
- [ ] Offer face-saving ways to change position
- [ ] Use unexpected validators (e.g., Republican supporting reform)
- [ ] Build relationships before asking for support

| Action | How to Measure |
|--------|----------------|

### Movement Impact

| Indicator | Measurement |
|-----------|-------------|
| State adoption | Number of states with reforms |
| Polling | Public support for reforms |
| Election outcomes | Competitiveness, turnout |

### Long-Term Success Metrics

**Ultimate Goals**

- Trust in government increasing
- Policy reflecting public preferences
- Multiple viable parties
- Reduced polarization
- Problems being addressed

**How We'll Know**

- Gallup/Pew trust metrics improving
- Research showing policy alignment with public
- Election results showing more parties winning
- Survey data showing reduced affective polarization
- Legislative productivity on major issues

## Getting Started Checklist

### Week 1

- [ ] Read one reform book or watch one documentary
- [ ] Sign up for FairVote and RepresentUs newsletters
- [ ] Check OpenSecrets for your representatives' donors
- [ ] Join local reform organization or online community

### Month 1

- [ ] Call your representatives about one reform issue
- [ ] Attend one reform-related event
- [ ] Make first donation to reform organization
- [ ] Have three conversations about reform with friends/family

### Quarter 1

- [ ] Volunteer for a reform campaign
- [ ] Write letter to editor or op-ed
- [ ] Recruit one other person to get involved
- [ ] Attend local government meeting

### Year 1

- [ ] Become regular volunteer or chapter leader
- [ ] Help with ballot initiative signature gathering
- [ ] Build relationships with local elected officials
- [ ] Develop expertise in specific reform area

## The Long Haul

**Sustainable Activism**

- Pace yourself—this is a multi-year effort
- Celebrate small wins
- Build community with fellow reformers
- Take breaks when needed
- Connect to larger purpose and meaning

**Building Power**

- Each action builds capacity for the next
- Each victory creates momentum
- Each connection expands the coalition
- Each reform enables further reform

**The Stakes**

- American democracy is at risk
- Ordinary citizens have power to fix it
- Success requires sustained collective action
- The alternative is continued decline

**Start Now**
The system won't reform itself. Those who benefit from dysfunction will fight to preserve it. Change requires millions of citizens demanding better. Your participation matters. Start where you are, use what you have, do what you can.
