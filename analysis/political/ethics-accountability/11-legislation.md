# Ethics and Accountability: Legislation

## Overview

This document provides model legislation for ethics and accountability reform, including federal statutes to strengthen enforcement, protect inspectors general and whistleblowers, enhance disclosure, and create presidential accountability. Effective ethics reform requires both new statutory authority and strengthening existing mechanisms.

## Federal Legislation

### Inspector General Independence and Empowerment Act

**Purpose**: Protect IGs from political interference and strengthen their authority.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Inspector General Independence
and Empowerment Act".

SEC. 2. REMOVAL OF INSPECTORS GENERAL.

Section 3(b) of the Inspector General Act of 1978 (5 U.S.C.
App.) is amended—
(1) by striking "may be removed from office by the
    President" and inserting "may be removed from office
    by the President only for cause"; and
(2) by adding at the end the following:

"(3) CAUSE DEFINED.—For purposes of this subsection,
    'cause' means—
    (A) permanent incapacity;
    (B) inefficiency;
    (C) neglect of duty;
    (D) malfeasance;
    (E) conviction of a felony; or
    (F) conduct involving moral turpitude.

"(4) NOTICE AND REVIEW.—
    (A) The President shall communicate in writing to the
        Congress the reasons for any removal or transfer of
        an Inspector General not later than 30 days before
        such removal or transfer.
    (B) During the 30-day period, Congress may hold hearings
        and issue a report on the proposed removal.
    (C) Judicial review of removal shall be available in
        federal court.".

SEC. 3. BUDGET PROTECTION.

The Inspector General Act of 1978 is amended by adding:

"SEC. 3A. BUDGET.

"(a) INDEPENDENT SUBMISSION.—Each Inspector General shall
    transmit a budget request to the head of the establishment
    and, concurrently, to Congress.

"(b) MINIMUM FUNDING.—An Inspector General office shall
    receive funding of not less than 0.5 percent of the
    establishment's discretionary budget authority.

"(c) REPROGRAMMING.—No reprogramming of funds appropriated
    to an Inspector General office shall occur without the
    approval of the Inspector General.".

SEC. 4. ACCESS TO INFORMATION.

Section 6 of the Inspector General Act is amended:

"(a) ACCESS.—Each Inspector General shall have—
    (1) access to all records, reports, audits, reviews,
        documents, papers, and recommendations;
    (2) access to interview any officer or employee; and
    (3) subpoena power for documents and testimony.

"(b) LIMITATIONS.—No limitation on access may be imposed
    except by statute.

"(c) PENALTIES.—Obstruction of an Inspector General
    investigation shall be punishable as provided in
    section 1505 of title 18.".

SEC. 5. VACANCIES.

If an Inspector General position is vacant for more than
210 days, the Comptroller General shall appoint an acting
Inspector General.
```

**Explanation**: Provides for-cause removal, budget protection, and information access for IGs.

**Challenges**: Resistance from agencies seeking to limit oversight.

### Comprehensive Whistleblower Protection Act

**Purpose**: Strengthen protections for federal employees who report wrongdoing.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Comprehensive Whistleblower
Protection Act".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

Section 2302(b)(8) of title 5, United States Code, is amended—
(1) by striking "a violation" and inserting "any
    information that the employee reasonably believes
    evidences a violation"; and
(2) by adding "a threat to public health or safety, or
    gross mismanagement, regardless of whether such
    information has been previously disclosed."

SEC. 3. COVERED DISCLOSURES.

(a) EXPANSION.—Protected disclosures include communications
    to—
    (1) Congress or congressional committees;
    (2) an Inspector General;
    (3) the Office of Special Counsel;
    (4) any federal court or grand jury;
    (5) an employee designated by the agency to receive
        such disclosures; and
    (6) in exigent circumstances, the public.

(b) NATIONAL SECURITY.—For disclosures concerning classified
    information—
    (1) protected channels include the Inspector General
        of the Intelligence Community; and
    (2) protection applies to lawful disclosures through
        such channels.

SEC. 4. PROHIBITED PERSONNEL PRACTICES.

Section 2302(b) is amended to add:
"(14) threatening any personnel action against an employee
     in reprisal for a disclosure protected by this section;
"(15) disproportionately denying security clearances to
     employees who have made protected disclosures."

SEC. 5. REMEDIES.

(a) COMPENSATORY DAMAGES.—An employee subjected to a
    prohibited personnel practice shall be entitled to—
    (1) reinstatement to the same position;
    (2) back pay with interest;
    (3) compensatory damages;
    (4) attorney's fees and costs; and
    (5) such other relief as appropriate.

(b) PUNITIVE DAMAGES.—In cases of willful violations,
    punitive damages may be awarded.

(c) PERSONAL LIABILITY.—An employee who commits a
    prohibited personnel practice may be personally liable
    for damages.

SEC. 6. EXPEDITED REVIEW.

(a) TIMELINE.—The Merit Systems Protection Board shall
    adjudicate whistleblower claims within 180 days.

(b) INTERIM RELIEF.—If relief is not granted within 180
    days, an employee may seek relief in federal court.

SEC. 7. ANTI-RETALIATION.

Agencies shall—
(1) train supervisors on anti-retaliation requirements;
(2) investigate allegations of retaliation promptly;
(3) discipline employees who retaliate; and
(4) report retaliation findings to Congress.
```

**Explanation**: Expands protected disclosures, strengthens remedies, and creates expedited process.

### Presidential Ethics Act

**Purpose**: Apply ethics requirements to the President and Vice President.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Presidential Ethics Act".

SEC. 2. CONFLICT OF INTEREST.

(a) APPLICABILITY.—Section 208 of title 18, United States
    Code, shall apply to the President and Vice President.

(b) RECUSAL.—The President and Vice President shall recuse
    from any particular matter in which they have a
    financial interest.

(c) WAIVER.—No waiver of recusal requirements under this
    section shall be granted.

SEC. 3. TAX DISCLOSURE.

(a) CANDIDATES.—Any candidate for President or Vice President
    shall, within 15 days of becoming a candidate, submit
    to the Federal Election Commission copies of the
    candidate's Federal tax returns for the 10 most recent
    taxable years.

(b) INCUMBENT.—The President and Vice President shall
    annually submit tax returns to the Federal Election
    Commission.

(c) PUBLIC DISCLOSURE.—The Commission shall make such
    returns publicly available within 30 days of receipt.

SEC. 4. DIVESTITURE.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—The President and Vice President shall
    divest any financial interest that would create a
    conflict of interest.

(b) TIMELINE.—Divestiture shall occur within 180 days of
    taking office.

(c) BLIND TRUST.—Assets that cannot be divested shall be
    placed in a qualified blind trust meeting the standards
    of 5 C.F.R. 2634.401.

SEC. 5. EMOLUMENTS.

(a) DEFINITION.—For purposes of Article I, Section 9,
    Clause 8, and Article II, Section 1, Clause 7, of the
    Constitution, "emolument" includes any profit, gain,
    or advantage of more than de minimis value, including—
    (1) payments for goods, services, or property;
    (2) payments above fair market value; and
    (3) other financial benefits.

(b) REPORTING.—The President shall report to Congress any
    emolument received from foreign or domestic governments.

(c) CONGRESSIONAL CONSENT.—Any foreign emolument requires
    consent of Congress.

SEC. 6. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) OFFICE.—There is established in the Executive Office
    of the President an Office of Presidential Ethics.

(b) DIRECTOR.—The Office shall be headed by a Director
    appointed by the President with the advice and consent
    of the Senate, for a term of 6 years, removable only
    for cause.

(c) DUTIES.—The Director shall—
    (1) review presidential financial disclosures;
    (2) advise on recusal obligations;
    (3) monitor compliance with this Act;
    (4) report violations to Congress; and
    (5) make public annual compliance reports.
```

**Explanation**: Extends conflict of interest rules to President, requires tax disclosure and divestiture, clarifies emoluments, creates oversight office.

**Constitutional Considerations**: May face separation of powers challenges; designed to survive judicial review.

### Ethics Enforcement Act

**Purpose**: Strengthen the Office of Government Ethics with enforcement authority.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Ethics Enforcement Act".

SEC. 2. DIRECTOR TERM AND REMOVAL.

Section 402 of the Ethics in Government Act is amended—
(1) by striking "5 years" and inserting "7 years";
(2) by adding: "The Director may be removed only for
    cause. Cause includes malfeasance, neglect of duty,
    or incapacity.".

SEC. 3. ENFORCEMENT AUTHORITY.

Section 402 is further amended by adding:

"(f) ENFORCEMENT.—The Director shall have authority to—
    (1) investigate violations of ethics laws and
        regulations by executive branch employees;
    (2) issue civil penalties of up to $100,000 per
        violation;
    (3) order corrective action, including divestiture,
        recusal, or termination;
    (4) refer matters to the Department of Justice
        for criminal prosecution; and
    (5) bring civil actions in federal court to enforce
        compliance.

"(g) SUBPOENA POWER.—The Director may issue subpoenas for
    documents and testimony in connection with investigations.

"(h) INDEPENDENCE.—No officer or employee of the executive
    branch may interfere with an investigation by the
    Office.".

SEC. 4. RESOURCES.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated
    to the Office of Government Ethics $50,000,000 for each
    fiscal year.

(b) MINIMUM.—Appropriations shall be not less than the
    amount appropriated for the previous fiscal year.
```

**Explanation**: Gives OGE real enforcement power with civil penalties and court access.

### Revolving Door Reform Act

**Purpose**: Strengthen post-employment restrictions and close loopholes.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Revolving Door Reform Act".

SEC. 2. EXTENDED COOLING-OFF PERIODS.

Section 207 of title 18, United States Code, is amended—
(1) in striking "2 years" each place it appears and
    inserting "4 years"; and
(2) by striking "1 year" each place it appears and
    inserting "2 years".

SEC. 3. EXPANDED DEFINITION OF LOBBYING.

Section 207 is further amended by adding:

"(m) COVERED ACTIVITIES.—For purposes of this section,
    covered post-employment activities include—
    (1) lobbying contacts as defined in section 3 of the
        Lobbying Disclosure Act;
    (2) strategic consulting or advising on matters
        before the former agency;
    (3) providing advice on government relations to clients
        with matters before the former agency; and
    (4) assisting others in making lobbying contacts.".

SEC. 4. PROCUREMENT BAN.

A former executive branch employee may not, for 4 years
after leaving government—
(1) serve as an officer or director of a contractor that
    received awards from the employee's former agency; or
(2) receive compensation for assisting such contractor
    in obtaining government contracts.

SEC. 5. PRE-EMPLOYMENT RESTRICTIONS.

(a) RECUSAL.—An employee who is negotiating or has an
    arrangement for future employment shall—
    (1) disclose the negotiation or arrangement; and
    (2) recuse from any matter affecting the prospective
        employer for 2 years.

(b) NO WAIVER.—No waiver of this recusal requirement shall
    be granted.

SEC. 6. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) CIVIL PENALTIES.—Violations of this section shall be
    subject to civil penalties of up to $200,000 per
    violation.

(b) DEBARMENT.—An individual who violates this section
    shall be debarred from federal employment for 5 years.
```

**Explanation**: Extends cooling-off periods, captures strategic consulting, adds enforcement.

## Constitutional Amendments

### Emoluments Enforcement Amendment

**Purpose**: Clarify emoluments clause enforcement.

**Draft Text**:

```text
ARTICLE —

Section 1. The term "emolument" in Article I, Section 9,
Clause 8, and Article II, Section 1, Clause 7, includes
any profit, gain, or advantage received from a foreign
state or from the United States or any State.

Section 2. Congress shall establish by law procedures for—
(a) disclosure of potential emoluments;
(b) consent to foreign emoluments; and
(c) enforcement of this article.

Section 3. Any citizen of the United States shall have
standing to bring an action in federal court to enforce
this article.

Section 4. Congress shall have the power to enforce this
article by appropriate legislation.
```

**Note**: Constitutional amendment provides strongest protection but faces high threshold for passage.

## Model State Legislation

### Model State Ethics Commission Act

**Purpose**: Create independent state ethics enforcement.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Ethics Commission Act".

SECTION 2. ESTABLISHMENT.

There is established an independent [State] Ethics Commission.

SECTION 3. COMPOSITION.

(a) The Commission shall consist of 5 members appointed
    as follows:
    (1) 1 member appointed by the Governor;
    (2) 1 member appointed by the Senate President;
    (3) 1 member appointed by the House Speaker;
    (4) 1 member appointed by the minority leaders; and
    (5) 1 member appointed by the other 4 members.

(b) Members shall serve staggered 6-year terms.

(c) Members may be removed only for cause.

SECTION 4. JURISDICTION.

The Commission shall have jurisdiction over—
(1) the Governor and executive branch officials;
(2) members of the legislature;
(3) state employees; and
(4) lobbyists.

SECTION 5. POWERS.

The Commission may—
(1) investigate ethics complaints;
(2) hold hearings;
(3) issue subpoenas;
(4) impose civil penalties up to $50,000;
(5) order corrective action; and
(6) refer matters for prosecution.

SECTION 6. DISCLOSURE.

(a) Public officials shall file annual financial disclosure.

(b) Disclosures shall be publicly available.

SECTION 7. INDEPENDENT COUNSEL.

The Commission may appoint independent counsel for
significant investigations.
```

### Model State Whistleblower Protection Act

**Purpose**: Protect state employee whistleblowers.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Whistleblower
Protection Act".

SECTION 2. PROTECTED DISCLOSURES.

An employee of the State or any political subdivision
is protected from retaliation for disclosing information
evidencing—
(1) violation of law;
(2) waste of public funds;
(3) abuse of authority;
(4) danger to public health or safety; or
(5) gross mismanagement.

SECTION 3. RETALIATION PROHIBITED.

No employer shall take adverse action against an employee
because of a protected disclosure.

SECTION 4. REMEDIES.

An employee subjected to retaliation may—
(1) file a complaint with the [State Ethics Commission];
(2) bring an action in state court;
(3) obtain reinstatement, back pay, and compensatory
    damages; and
(4) recover attorney's fees.

SECTION 5. BURDEN OF PROOF.

The employee need only show that the protected disclosure
was a contributing factor in the adverse action. The
employer must then prove by clear and convincing evidence
that it would have taken the same action absent the
disclosure.
```

## Regulatory Framework

### OGE Regulations

Enhanced regulations needed for:

- Recusal documentation and monitoring
- Divestiture requirements
- Gift and travel disclosure
- Post-employment tracking

### Agency Implementation

Each agency should adopt:

- Ethics office structure
- Training requirements
- Reporting procedures
- Enforcement mechanisms

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Strategic consulting | Expanded definition may still miss some influence | Moderate |
| Family member interests | May not capture all family conflicts | Moderate |
| Beneficial ownership | Hidden interests may escape disclosure | High |
| Independent expenditure coordination | Avoids direct compensation limits | Moderate |
| Nominee hold | Delays in confirmation leave vacancies | Moderate |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Presidential enforcement | Relies on self-compliance and political pressure | Separation of powers |
| DOJ conflicts | DOJ still decides prosecutions | Executive structure |
| Standing barriers | Courts may still limit enforcement suits | Judicial doctrine |
| Resource constraints | Enforcement requires adequate funding | Appropriations |
| Political will | Enforcement depends on commitment | Political culture |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Beneficial Ownership**
    - Cross-reference with Treasury databases
    - Require beneficial ownership disclosure
    - Audit procedures for complex arrangements
    - Penalties for concealment

2. **Family Interests**
    - Extend disclosure to close family
    - Recusal for family financial interests
    - Monitoring of family business activities
    - Clear definitions of covered family

3. **Strategic Consulting**
    - Broad definition with examples
    - Registration requirements
    - Client disclosure
    - Regular refinement of definitions

4. **Enforcement Coordination**
    - Clear referral procedures
    - Interagency coordination
    - Congressional notification
    - Public reporting

### General Implementation Concerns

1. **Political Transitions**
    - Lock in reforms structurally
    - Independent appointment terms
    - Adequate transition funding
    - Documentation requirements

2. **Ongoing Vigilance**
    - Regular review of effectiveness
    - Adaptation to new evasion techniques
    - Public reporting on compliance
    - Congressional oversight

3. **Cultural Change**
    - Leadership commitment
    - Training and education
    - Norm reinforcement
    - Accountability demonstrations

4. **Stakeholder Buy-In**
    - Engage ethics professionals
    - Build bipartisan support
    - Public education
    - Address legitimate concerns
