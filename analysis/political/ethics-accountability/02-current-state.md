# Ethics and Accountability: Current State

## Federal Ethics Framework

### Core Statutes

| Law | Purpose | Coverage |
|-----|---------|----------|
| Ethics in Government Act (1978) | Financial disclosure, OGE, restrictions | Senior officials |
| Criminal Conflict of Interest (18 U.S.C. 208) | Bars participation in matters affecting finances | Most executive employees |
| Post-Employment Restrictions (18 U.S.C. 207) | Limits revolving door | Former officials |
| Bribery and Illegal Gratuities (18 U.S.C. 201) | Prohibits corrupt payments | All federal officials |
| Foreign Gifts and Decorations Act (5 U.S.C. 7342) | Restricts foreign gifts | All federal employees |
| Emoluments Clauses | Constitutional prohibitions | Officers of the United States |

### Office of Government Ethics

**Role**: Executive branch ethics oversight

**Authority**:

- Issue ethics regulations and guidance
- Review financial disclosure forms
- Provide ethics training
- Advise agencies on ethics matters

**Limitations**:

- Advisory only—cannot compel compliance
- No enforcement authority
- Director serves at pleasure of President
- Limited investigative capacity

### Financial Disclosure

**Public Disclosure (OGE 278)**:

- Required for senior officials (SES and above)
- Reports assets, income, liabilities, gifts
- Available for public inspection
- Reviewed by agency ethics officials

**Confidential Disclosure (OGE 450)**:

- Required for officials with decision-making authority
- Not publicly available
- Used for conflict screening

**Gaps**:

| Gap | Description |
|-----|-------------|
| Valuation | Ranges, not precise values |
| Timing | Annual, not real-time |
| Verification | Limited audit capacity |
| Enforcement | Referral to DOJ for criminal matters |

## Conflicts of Interest

### Criminal Statute (18 U.S.C. 208)

Prohibits officials from participating in matters in which they have a financial interest.

**Elements**:

- Official action
- Financial interest (direct or imputed)
- Particular matter
- Knowing participation

**Exemptions**:

- Waivers available
- De minimis exceptions
- President and Vice President exempt

### Civil Service Rules

- Broader ethical standards
- Appearances of conflicts
- Misuse of position
- Outside activities

### Presidential Exemption

The President and Vice President are exempt from:

- 18 U.S.C. 208 (conflicts of interest)
- Many ethics regulations
- Some divestiture requirements

**Rationale**: Separation of powers, breadth of presidential duties

**Problem**: Creates obvious conflicts

## Emoluments Clauses

### Foreign Emoluments Clause

"No Person holding any Office of Profit or Trust under [the United States], shall, without the Consent of the Congress, accept of any present, Emolument, Office, or Title, of any kind whatever, from any King, Prince, or foreign State."

**Issues**:

- Definition of "emolument" disputed
- Enforcement mechanism unclear
- Congressional consent process undefined
- Standing to sue disputed

### Domestic Emoluments Clause

Prohibits President from receiving any emolument from the United States or states beyond compensation.

**Issues**:

- Rarely invoked
- Scope uncertain
- No enforcement mechanism

### Litigation

Multiple suits filed during Trump administration:

- *CREW v. Trump*: Dismissed on standing
- *District of Columbia v. Trump*: Dismissed as moot
- No court reached merits

## Inspectors General

### Structure

**Number**: 74 federal Inspectors General (as of 2024)

**Types**:

- Statutory IGs: Created by IG Act, serve major agencies
- Designated Federal Entity IGs: Smaller agencies
- Special IGs: Specific programs (SIGAR, SIGTARP)

### Authority

- Conduct audits and investigations
- Access to records and information
- Subpoena authority (documents, not testimony)
- Report to Congress and agency head

### Limitations

| Limitation | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| Removal | Serve at pleasure of President/agency head |
| Resources | Depend on agency for funding |
| Scope | Cannot investigate President directly |
| Enforcement | Can only recommend action |
| Classification | Agencies can restrict access |

### Recent Controversies

- Multiple IG firings in 2020
- Concerns about interference
- Gaps in protection

## Whistleblower Protection

### Statutes

| Law | Coverage |
|-----|----------|
| Whistleblower Protection Act | Federal employees |
| Intelligence Community Whistleblower Protection Act | Intelligence community |
| False Claims Act | Private sector (qui tam) |
| Dodd-Frank | Securities, commodities |
| Various sector-specific laws | Varied |

### Protections

- Prohibition on retaliation
- Reinstatement remedies
- Compensatory damages
- Access to Merit Systems Protection Board

### Limitations

| Limitation | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| Coverage gaps | Some employees excluded |
| Burden of proof | Can be difficult to establish |
| Delays | Lengthy adjudication process |
| National security | Special rules limit protections |
| Remedies | Often inadequate |

## Revolving Door

### Current Restrictions

**Post-Employment Bans** (18 U.S.C. 207):

- Permanent ban on switching sides on particular matters
- 2-year ban on matters under official responsibility
- 1-year cooling off for senior officials
- Various specialized restrictions

**Pre-Employment (Executive Order)**:

- Ethics pledges for appointees
- Lobbying bans during service
- Recusals for former employers

### Gaps

| Gap | Description |
|-----|-------------|
| Shadow lobbying | Strategic consulting avoids restrictions |
| Definition of lobbying | Narrow technical definition |
| Enforcement | Difficult to monitor compliance |
| Waivers | Available and frequently granted |
| Congress | Different rules, weaker enforcement |

## Congressional Ethics

### Structure

- House Committee on Ethics
- Senate Select Committee on Ethics
- Member self-regulation
- Office of Congressional Ethics (House, advisory)

### Limitations

- Peer enforcement
- Partisan considerations
- Infrequent sanctions
- Limited transparency

See [Congressional Reform](../congressional-reform/01-overview.md) for detailed analysis.

## State and Local

### Variation

State ethics regimes vary widely:

- Some have independent ethics commissions
- Others rely on attorneys general
- Coverage and penalties differ
- Enforcement ranges from robust to minimal

### Models

| State | Notable Features |
|-------|-----------------|
| California | FPPC with enforcement authority |
| New Jersey | Strong disclosure requirements |
| Various | Independent ethics commissions |

## Recent Developments (as of 2024)

### Trump Administration

- Refusal to divest business interests
- Emoluments litigation
- Multiple cabinet-level ethics controversies
- IG firings
- OGE director resignation

### Biden Administration

- Ethics Executive Order (EO 14019)
- Pledged advance notice to Congress for IG removals
- Supported IG protection legislation
- Strengthened ethics pledges
- Some gaps remain

### Legislative Efforts

- For the People Act (H.R. 1) included ethics provisions
- Protecting Our Democracy Act
- Various standalone bills
- Limited enactment
