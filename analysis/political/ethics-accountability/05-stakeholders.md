# Ethics and Accountability: Stakeholders

## The Public

### As Beneficiaries

The public benefits from effective ethics enforcement:

**Clean Government**: Officials act in public interest, not personal gain.

**Fair Treatment**: Decisions based on merit, not connections.

**Efficient Spending**: Public resources not diverted to private benefit.

**Trust in Institutions**: Confidence that government serves public.

### As Victims

When ethics fail, the public suffers:

| Harm | Example |
|------|---------|
| Policy distortion | Regulations favor connected interests |
| Resource waste | Contracts go to cronies, not best bidders |
| Lost confidence | Declining trust in government |
| Unequal treatment | Connected get favorable treatment |

### Current Engagement

- Strong public support for ethics reform (polls consistently show 80%+ support)
- Limited specific knowledge of ethics rules
- Attention spikes during scandals
- Cynicism about effectiveness of reform

## Government Officials

### Ethics Office Staff

**OGE and Agency Ethics Officials**:

- Generally committed to ethics mission
- Frustrated by limited authority
- Professionalized through training
- Risk-averse due to constraints

**Interests**:

- Stronger enforcement authority
- Clear rules and guidance
- Adequate resources
- Protection from retaliation

### Inspectors General

**Role**: Independent watchdogs within agencies

**Interests**:

- Independence protection
- Adequate resources
- Access to information
- Authority to act on findings

**Challenges**:

- Serve at pleasure of agency heads
- Can be fired without cause
- Resources depend on agency cooperation
- Findings often ignored

### Whistleblowers

**Role**: Report wrongdoing from within

**Interests**:

- Strong protection from retaliation
- Meaningful investigation of complaints
- Remedies when retaliation occurs
- Career preservation

**Vulnerability**:

- Retaliation common despite protections
- Process slow and difficult
- Career damage often irreversible
- Personal costs high

### Political Appointees

**Subject to Ethics Rules**:

- Financial disclosure
- Conflict recusals
- Post-employment restrictions
- Ethics pledges

**Interests**:

| Interest | Tension |
|----------|---------|
| Clear rules | vs. flexibility |
| Reasonable compliance burden | vs. thoroughness |
| Career protection | vs. disclosure |
| Post-government options | vs. restrictions |

### Career Civil Servants

- More extensive ethics training
- Generally committed to ethics
- See political appointees as risk
- Want consistent enforcement

## Congress

### Oversight Role

Congress has ethics oversight responsibility:

**Tools**:

- Hearings
- Investigations
- Confirmation process
- Legislation

**Limitations**:

- Partisan protection of allies
- Limited follow-through
- Competing priorities
- Executive resistance

### Congressional Ethics

Congress also subject to ethics rules:

**House Ethics Committee**:

- Peer enforcement
- Limited transparency
- Infrequent sanctions

**Senate Ethics Committee**:

- Similar structure
- Rare public action

### Key Members

| Role | Examples |
|------|----------|
| Ethics champions | Judiciary, Oversight committee members |
| Reform advocates | Good government caucus members |
| Resistance | Those with own ethics issues |

| Organization | Focus |
|--------------|-------|
| Citizens for Responsibility and Ethics in Washington (CREW) | Ethics enforcement, litigation |
| Project on Government Oversight (POGO) | IG support, waste/fraud |

**Activities**:

- Monitoring and reporting
- FOIA litigation
- Ethics complaints
- Policy advocacy
- Public education

### Academic Researchers

- Study ethics regimes
- Propose reforms
- Provide testimony
- Train practitioners

### Bar Associations

- Legal ethics standards
- Guidance on government ethics
- Professional accountability

## Private Sector

### Regulated Industries

**Interest**: Shape rules to advantage

**Tactics**:

- Lobbying on ethics rules
- Revolving door hiring
- Challenge enforcement
- Weaken restrictions

### Law Firms and Consultants

**Interest**: Serve clients navigating ethics rules

**Role**:

- Ethics compliance advice
- Representation in investigations
- Former government lawyers

### Contractors

**Subject to**:

- Procurement ethics rules
- Debarment risk
- Compliance requirements

**Interest**: Minimize compliance burden while maintaining access

## Beneficiaries of Weak Ethics

### Those with Conflicts

Officials with conflicts benefit from:

- Exemptions
- Weak enforcement
- Information opacity
- Revolving door opportunities

### Connected Interests

Those with access benefit from:

- Favorable treatment
- Insider information
- Contract awards
- Regulatory relief

### Future Employers

Potential employers benefit from:

- Official actions while in government
- Information upon departure
- Relationships and access

## Opposition to Reform

### Arguments Against Stronger Ethics

| Argument | Reality |
|----------|---------|
| "Chilling effect" on service | Strong ethics systems don't prevent recruitment |
| "Already too complex" | Complexity often designed to create loopholes |
| "Criminalizes politics" | Clear rules are navigable |
| "Partisan weapon" | Independent enforcement addresses this |

### Who Benefits from Status Quo

- Officials who want post-government opportunities
- Industries that benefit from capture
- Political actors who use ethics selectively
- Wealthy officials with complex holdings

## Power Dynamics

### Formal Authority

| Actor | Authority |
|-------|-----------|
| Congress | Legislation, oversight, confirmation |
| President | Executive order, appointments, DOJ |
| Courts | Constitutional interpretation |
| OGE | Guidance, disclosure review |
| IGs | Audit, investigation |

### Informal Power

| Actor | Influence |
|-------|-----------|
| Media | Scandal exposure, agenda-setting |
| Watchdog groups | Research, litigation, advocacy |
| Public opinion | Electoral pressure |
| Career officials | Implementation, leaks |

## Coalition Potential

### Reform Coalition

Could unite:

- Good government groups
- Career ethics officials
- Whistleblower advocates
- Academic experts
- Bipartisan reformers

### Challenges

- Partisan divisions on specific cases
- Resistance from those subject to rules
- Limited public attention between scandals
- Complexity of issues
