# Lobbying Reform: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Lobbying reform legislation must balance First Amendment rights to petition the government with the public interest in transparency and preventing corruption. This document provides draft legislative text for strengthening the Lobbying Disclosure Act, closing loopholes, enhancing enforcement, and reforming the revolving door.

## Federal Legislation

### Lobbying Transparency Act

**Purpose**: Close the "shadow lobbying" loophole, expand disclosure requirements, and require real-time reporting.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Lobbying Transparency Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) The Lobbying Disclosure Act of 1995 contains loopholes that
    allow substantial lobbying activity to go unreported.
(2) The 20% time threshold exempts many professional lobbyists
    from registration.
(3) "Strategic consultants" who advise on lobbying campaigns
    without directly contacting officials are not covered.
(4) Quarterly reporting allows months-long delays in public
    disclosure.
(5) Enhanced transparency is essential for government accountability.

SEC. 3. REGISTRATION THRESHOLD.

(a) AMENDMENT.—Section 3(10) of the Lobbying Disclosure Act
    (2 U.S.C. 1602(10)) is amended by striking "20 percent" and
    inserting "5 percent".

(b) STRATEGIC ADVICE.—Section 3(10) is further amended by adding:
    "(C) Any person who provides strategic advice, analysis, or
    preparation for lobbying contacts, regardless of whether
    such person makes lobbying contacts directly."

SEC. 4. EXPANDED COVERED OFFICIALS.

Section 3(4) is amended to include:
(A) All officers and employees of the executive branch at the
    GS-13 level or above;
(B) All members of federal advisory committees;
(C) All contractors with substantial policymaking authority.

SEC. 5. DISCLOSURE REQUIREMENTS.

(a) REAL-TIME CONTACT REPORTING.—Section 5 is amended by adding:
    "(d) CONTACT REPORTS.—Each registrant shall file a report
    within 72 hours of any lobbying contact with a covered
    official, including—
    (1) the name and title of the official contacted;
    (2) the date and method of contact;
    (3) the specific issue discussed;
    (4) the position advocated; and
    (5) any documents or materials provided."

(b) BENEFICIAL OWNERSHIP.—Section 5(b) is amended by adding:
    "(9) For each client, disclosure of beneficial ownership,
    meaning any individual or entity that—
    (A) exercises substantial control over the client;
    (B) owns 10% or more of the equity interests; or
    (C) is entitled to 10% or more of the income."

(c) MONTHLY FINANCIAL REPORTS.—Section 5(b) is amended by
    requiring monthly rather than quarterly financial reports.

SEC. 6. GRASSROOTS LOBBYING DISCLOSURE.

(a) DEFINITION.—The Act is amended by adding:
    "'Grassroots lobbying campaign' means any paid effort to
    generate public communications to covered officials on a
    specific legislative or executive action."

(b) REGISTRATION.—Any person who spends more than $25,000 in any
    quarter on a grassroots lobbying campaign shall register
    under this Act.

(c) DISCLOSURE.—Registrants conducting grassroots lobbying shall
    disclose—
    (1) the issues targeted;
    (2) the amount spent;
    (3) the funding sources;
    (4) the media platforms used.

SEC. 7. FOREIGN AGENTS COORDINATION.

(a) SINGLE REGISTRATION.—The Attorney General and Secretary of
    the Senate shall establish a unified registration system for
    the Lobbying Disclosure Act and the Foreign Agents Registration
    Act.

(b) CROSS-REFERENCING.—All disclosures under either Act shall be
    cross-referenced in a public database.

SEC. 8. PUBLIC DATABASE.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Clerk of the House and Secretary of the
    Senate shall jointly establish and maintain a unified, publicly
    accessible lobbying disclosure database.

(b) FEATURES.—The database shall include—
    (1) real-time publication of all disclosures;
    (2) full-text search capability;
    (3) API access for researchers and journalists;
    (4) visualization tools showing relationships;
    (5) alert systems for monitoring specific issues or lobbyists.

SEC. 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect 180 days after enactment.

```text

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Reduces time threshold from 20% to 5%, capturing more lobbying activity. Adds "strategic advice" to close shadow lobbying loophole.
- **Section 5**: Real-time contact reporting (72 hours) replaces delayed quarterly reports.
- **Section 6**: Requires disclosure of astroturf campaigns.

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| First Amendment | Disclosure requirements upheld in *Buckley* |
| Compliance burden | Modernized electronic filing reduces burden |
| Grassroots speech | Only covers paid campaigns over threshold |

### Close the Revolving Door Act

**Purpose**: Extend and strengthen cooling-off periods for government officials who become lobbyists.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Close the Revolving Door Act".

SEC. 2. CONGRESSIONAL COOLING-OFF PERIOD.

(a) MEMBERS.—Section 207(e)(1) of title 18, United States Code,
    is amended—
    (1) by striking "2 years" and inserting "6 years"; and
    (2) by adding: "including any communication intended to
        influence official action, whether or not such communication
        constitutes a 'lobbying contact' under the Lobbying
        Disclosure Act."

(b) SENIOR STAFF.—Section 207(e)(2) is amended by striking
    "1 year" and inserting "4 years".

(c) COMMITTEE STAFF.—Section 207(e)(7) is amended to add:
    "A person who served on the staff of a congressional committee
    may not, for 4 years, communicate with any member or staff
    of that committee or any agency within its jurisdiction with
    intent to influence official action."

SEC. 3. EXECUTIVE BRANCH COOLING-OFF PERIOD.

(a) SENIOR OFFICIALS.—Section 207(c) is amended by striking
    "2 years" and inserting "4 years".

(b) VERY SENIOR OFFICIALS.—Section 207(d) is amended—
    (1) by striking "1 year" and inserting "4 years"; and
    (2) by expanding the prohibition to include communications
        with any official of the department or agency in which
        the covered person served.

(c) POLITICAL APPOINTEES.—All political appointees at the PAS
    (Presidential appointment with Senate confirmation) or PA
    (Presidential appointment) level shall be subject to a
    6-year cooling-off period.

SEC. 4. REVERSE REVOLVING DOOR.

(a) PROHIBITION.—A person who has been a registered lobbyist
    within the preceding 4 years may not—
    (1) be appointed to a position in the executive branch
        requiring Senate confirmation;
    (2) be appointed to a position with authority over the
        subject matter on which the person lobbied.

(b) WAIVER.—The President may waive this prohibition in writing,
    with the waiver published in the Federal Register.

SEC. 5. SHADOW LOBBYING PROHIBITION.

(a) DEFINITION.—"Shadow lobbying" means advising, supporting, or
    consulting on lobbying activities without registering as a
    lobbyist.

(b) PROHIBITION.—A person subject to a cooling-off period under
    this title may not engage in shadow lobbying related to the
    matters on which the cooling-off period applies.

(c) EMPLOYMENT DISCLOSURE.—A person subject to a cooling-off
    period who accepts employment with any organization that
    employs registered lobbyists shall disclose such employment
    to the Office of Government Ethics within 14 days.

SEC. 6. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) CRIMINAL PENALTIES.—Section 207 is amended to increase the
    maximum fine from $50,000 to $250,000 and the maximum
    imprisonment from 1 year to 5 years.

(b) DEBARMENT.—A person who violates this section may be barred
    from lobbying any federal official for life.

(c) FORFEITURE.—A person who violates this section shall forfeit
    any compensation received for the prohibited activity.

SEC. 7. REPORTING.

(a) ANNUAL REPORT.—The Attorney General shall submit an annual
    report to Congress on enforcement of this section, including—
    (1) number of referrals received;
    (2) investigations initiated;
    (3) prosecutions and outcomes;
    (4) civil penalties imposed.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act applies to persons leaving government service after the
date of enactment.

```text

### Honest Ads Act

**Purpose**: Require disclosure of online political advertising, bringing digital ads under the same rules as broadcast.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Honest Ads Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) The Federal Election Campaign Act requires disclosure of
    political advertising on broadcast, cable, and satellite.
(2) Digital advertising is subject to less regulation despite
    reaching comparable audiences.
(3) Foreign governments and other bad actors have exploited
    this gap.
(4) Transparency in online political advertising is essential
    for informed democratic participation.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
(1) ONLINE PLATFORM.—The term "online platform" means any public-
    facing website, web application, or digital application that—
    (A) has 50,000,000 or more unique monthly visitors; and
    (B) sells qualified political advertisements.

(2) QUALIFIED POLITICAL ADVERTISEMENT.—The term "qualified
    political advertisement" means any advertisement that—
    (A) is made by or on behalf of a candidate for federal office;
    (B) is made by or on behalf of any person and relates to any
        political matter of national importance, including—
        (i) a candidate;
        (ii) an election for federal office; or
        (iii) a national legislative issue of public importance.

SEC. 4. DISCLOSURE REQUIREMENTS.

(a) ADVERTISEMENT CONTENT.—Each qualified political advertisement
    shall include—
    (1) a clear statement identifying the person who paid for the
        advertisement;
    (2) if applicable, a statement that the advertisement was not
        authorized by any candidate or candidate's committee.

(b) PLATFORM REQUIREMENTS.—Each online platform shall—
    (1) maintain a public file of all qualified political
        advertisements purchased by any person for more than $500
        in aggregate during a calendar year;
    (2) make the file available through a searchable database;
    (3) include in the file—
        (A) a copy or description of the advertisement;
        (B) the name and contact information of the purchaser;
        (C) the total cost of the advertisement;
        (D) the dates and times of display;
        (E) a description of the audience targeted.

(c) RETENTION.—Platforms shall retain the information required by
    subsection (b) for not less than 4 years.

SEC. 5. FOREIGN NATIONAL PROHIBITION.

(a) PROHIBITION.—No foreign national may, directly or indirectly,
    purchase or direct the purchase of a qualified political
    advertisement.

(b) PLATFORM VERIFICATION.—Online platforms shall make reasonable
    efforts to verify that purchasers of qualified political
    advertisements are not foreign nationals.

SEC. 6. FEC RULEMAKING.

(a) The Federal Election Commission shall promulgate regulations
    implementing this Act within 180 days.

(b) Regulations shall include—
    (1) format and size requirements for disclaimers;
    (2) procedures for maintaining public files;
    (3) verification standards for foreign national prohibitions.

SEC. 7. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) PLATFORM PENALTIES.—A platform that willfully fails to comply
    with this Act shall be subject to a civil penalty of up to
    $50,000 per violation.

(b) PURCHASER PENALTIES.—A purchaser who fails to provide required
    information shall be subject to penalties under existing
    campaign finance law.

```text

### Corporate Political Activity Disclosure Act

**Purpose**: Require publicly traded corporations to disclose political spending to shareholders.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Corporate Political Activity
Disclosure Act".

SEC. 2. SEC DISCLOSURE REQUIREMENT.

(a) RULEMAKING.—The Securities and Exchange Commission shall
    promulgate rules requiring each issuer of a security
    registered under the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 to
    disclose in any annual report—
    (1) expenditures for political activities during the
        preceding fiscal year;
    (2) the specific activities supported;
    (3) the identities of candidates, parties, or causes supported;
    (4) the issuer's policies and procedures for political activity.

(b) POLITICAL ACTIVITIES DEFINED.—For purposes of this section,
    "political activities" includes—
    (1) independent expenditures;
    (2) contributions to Super PACs;
    (3) contributions to 501(c)(4) organizations that engage in
        political activity;
    (4) payments for electioneering communications;
    (5) lobbying expenditures;
    (6) payments to trade associations used for political purposes.

SEC. 3. BOARD AUTHORIZATION.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—No issuer may make any expenditure for political
    activities unless—
    (1) the board of directors has adopted a policy authorizing
        such expenditures; and
    (2) such policy is disclosed to shareholders.

(b) ANNUAL REVIEW.—The board shall review and vote on the
    political activity policy annually.

SEC. 4. SHAREHOLDER APPROVAL.

(a) ADVISORY VOTE.—Each issuer that makes expenditures for
    political activities shall include in its proxy materials
    a proposal for an annual advisory vote on—
    (1) the issuer's political spending policies; and
    (2) the issuer's political expenditures during the preceding
        year.

SEC. 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect for fiscal years beginning after the date
of enactment.

```text

## State Model Legislation

### State Lobbying Disclosure Act (Model)

**Purpose**: Model legislation for states seeking to strengthen lobbying disclosure.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Lobbying Disclosure Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Lobbying" means any communication with a covered official
    for the purpose of influencing official action, including—
    (1) legislation;
    (2) executive orders or rules;
    (3) procurement or grants;
    (4) appointments.

(b) "Lobbyist" means any person who—
    (1) receives compensation for lobbying; or
    (2) spends more than [hours/dollars threshold] on lobbying
        in any calendar year.

(c) "Covered official" means—
    (1) any elected state official;
    (2) any state employee with policymaking authority;
    (3) any member of a state board or commission.

SECTION 3. REGISTRATION.

(a) Each lobbyist shall register with the [ethics commission]
    within 10 days of first engaging in lobbying.

(b) Registration shall include—
    (1) lobbyist name and contact information;
    (2) employer and client names;
    (3) subject matter areas;
    (4) prior government employment.

SECTION 4. DISCLOSURE.

(a) CONTACT REPORTS.—Each lobbyist shall file reports within
    48 hours of any lobbying contact, including—
    (1) name of official contacted;
    (2) subject discussed;
    (3) position advocated.

(b) EXPENDITURE REPORTS.—Monthly reports of all lobbying-related
    expenditures.

(c) GIFTS.—Disclosure of any gift to a covered official exceeding
    $25 in value.

SECTION 5. GIFT BAN.

(a) No lobbyist may give any gift with a value exceeding $25 to
    any covered official.

(b) No lobbyist may pay for any meal, travel, or entertainment
    for a covered official.

SECTION 6. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) The [ethics commission] shall investigate complaints and may
    impose civil penalties up to [$amount] per violation.

(b) Willful violations may be referred for criminal prosecution.

SECTION 7. PUBLIC DATABASE.

The [ethics commission] shall maintain a searchable public database
of all lobbyist registrations and reports.

```text

## Regulatory Framework

### DOJ Lobbying Enforcement Regulations

**Existing Authority**: Lobbying Disclosure Act enforcement under 18 U.S.C. § 1352.

**Draft Regulation**:

```text
PART 16—ENFORCEMENT OF THE LOBBYING DISCLOSURE ACT

§ 16.1 Purpose.

This part establishes procedures for enforcement of the Lobbying
Disclosure Act and related criminal provisions.

§ 16.2 Referrals.

(a) The Secretary of the Senate and Clerk of the House shall refer
    to the Department of Justice—
    (1) any apparent failure to register;
    (2) any apparent material omission in a registration or report;
    (3) any apparent false statement.

(b) Referrals shall include all relevant documents and a summary
    of the apparent violation.

§ 16.3 Investigation.

(a) Upon receiving a referral, the Department shall initiate an
    investigation within 60 days.

(b) The Department may request additional information from the
    subject of the investigation, who shall respond within 30 days.

§ 16.4 Civil Penalties.

(a) For non-willful violations, the Department may seek civil
    penalties up to $200,000 per violation.

(b) For failure to register, each day of non-compliance constitutes
    a separate violation.

§ 16.5 Criminal Prosecution.

(a) Willful violations shall be referred to the Criminal Division
    for prosecution under 18 U.S.C. § 1001 (false statements) or
    § 1352 (Lobbying Disclosure Act).

(b) The maximum penalty is 5 years imprisonment.

§ 16.6 Annual Reporting.

The Attorney General shall publish an annual report on Lobbying
Disclosure Act enforcement, including number of referrals,
investigations, and outcomes.

```text

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

**First Amendment**:

The Supreme Court has held that lobbying disclosure requirements are constitutional:

- *United States v. Harriss* (1954): Registration requirements valid
- *Buckley v. Valeo* (1976): Disclosure is least restrictive means

**Key Limitations**:

| Restriction | Constitutionality |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Registration requirements | Constitutional |
| Disclosure of contacts | Constitutional |
| Cooling-off periods | Constitutional (reasonable time limits) |
| Lobbying bans | Likely unconstitutional |
| Gift limits | Constitutional |

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Provision | Enforcement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Registration | Referral to DOJ |
| Disclosure | Civil penalties |
| Revolving door | Criminal prosecution |
| Foreign agents | National security authorities |

### Preemption

- Federal lobbying law does not preempt state lobbying laws
- States may impose additional requirements
- Local governments may also regulate

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

This section identifies potential weaknesses in the proposed legislation and provides mechanisms for addressing them.

### Lobbying Transparency Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Coalition structures | Lobbyists may use coalitions to obscure client identity, with the coalition (not ultimate funder) listed as the client | High |
| Foreign subsidiary exception | U.S. subsidiaries of foreign corporations may not trigger FARA requirements even when lobbying for parent company interests | High |
| 72-hour contact reporting | Weekend or holiday contacts could effectively extend reporting windows; bad actors may backdate contacts | Medium |
| "Strategic advice" definition | Term could be interpreted narrowly, allowing consultants to claim they provided general business advice, not lobbying strategy | Medium |
| Grassroots threshold gaming | Campaigns may structure spending to stay just under $25,000 threshold or split across entities | Medium |
| Beneficial ownership gaps | 10% ownership threshold allows significant interests to remain hidden through structured ownership | Medium |
| Database maintenance | No permanent funding mandate could lead to degraded database over time | Low |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No private right of action | Citizens cannot sue for disclosure violations; dependent on DOJ enforcement priorities | Legislative choice to centralize enforcement |
| Limited agency coverage | Advisory committee members and contractors may still fall through definitional gaps | Complex classification systems |
| No compensation disclosure | Public doesn't learn how much lobbyists are paid, only total client spending | Privacy concerns balanced against transparency |
| Delayed compliance | 180-day effective date allows significant lobbying before rules apply | Transition needs vs. immediate transparency |
| No retroactivity | Historical lobbying patterns remain hidden; can't apply to existing shadow lobbying | Constitutional limitations |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Annual loophole review**: Require GAO to report annually on registration compliance gaps
2. **Coalition transparency**: Amend to require disclosure of all entities contributing more than $10,000 to coalition lobbying budgets
3. **Strengthen strategic advice definition**: Add examples in regulatory guidance specifying covered activities
4. **Lower grassroots threshold**: Reduce to $10,000 after 3-year implementation period
5. **Expand beneficial ownership**: Lower threshold to 5% and require disclosure of all controlling persons regardless of ownership percentage

### Close the Revolving Door Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| "Influence" vs "contact" | Former officials may advise on lobbying strategy without making contacts, claiming activity isn't "intended to influence" | Critical |
| Waiver authority | Presidential waivers for reverse revolving door may become routine, undermining the restriction | High |
| Shadow employment | Officials may work as consultants through intermediaries, with lobbying firms paying a third party | High |
| Subject matter interpretation | Narrow interpretation of "subject matter on which the person lobbied" could allow work on related issues | High |
| State and local lobbying | Cooling-off periods only cover federal lobbying; officials may immediately lobby state/local governments | Medium |
| Campaign work exception | Former officials may work on campaigns that coordinate with lobbying operations | Medium |
| Nonprofit exception | Work for 501(c)(3) organizations may not be covered even when those organizations have lobbying affiliates | Medium |
| Enforcement resources | Extended cooling-off periods require longer monitoring; no resources provided | Low |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Wealth penalty | Only affects those who need lobbying income; wealthy officials unaffected | Inherent in income-based restrictions |
| Expertise loss | Extended cooling-off may discourage qualified people from government service | Tension between ethics and recruitment |
| Uneven application | Congress exempted itself from some executive branch ethics rules | Separation of powers |
| Compensation limits | No cap on what former officials can earn in non-lobbying roles at lobbying firms | First Amendment, right to work |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Define "influence" broadly**: Regulatory guidance should specify that any communication about pending matters constitutes influence
2. **Waiver transparency**: Require 60-day advance public notice of proposed waivers with opportunity for public comment
3. **Third-party payment prohibition**: Bar former officials from receiving any compensation from entities that employ lobbyists for 2 years
4. **Subject matter expansion**: Define subject matter to include all issues within the jurisdiction of the official's former agency/committee
5. **State lobbying reporting**: Require disclosure of state/local lobbying by former federal officials during cooling-off period
6. **Independent enforcement**: Create a dedicated revolving door enforcement unit within DOJ

### Honest Ads Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Platform threshold gaming | Foreign actors may use many small platforms that fall below coverage thresholds | High |
| Cryptocurrency payments | Anonymous payments via cryptocurrency may circumvent payment disclosure | High |
| Definitional edge cases | "Political advertisement" may not clearly cover issue ads that don't name candidates | Medium |
| Viral content exception | User-shared content may escape ad disclosure even when originally paid placement | Medium |
| Emerging platforms | New communication technologies (VR, metaverse, AI chatbots) not explicitly covered | Medium |
| Small purchase exemption | Multiple small ad purchases may collectively evade disclosure thresholds | Low |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No content standards | Only requires disclosure, doesn't address deceptive content itself | First Amendment limitations |
| Platform self-regulation | Relies on platforms to implement; no standard technical specification | Avoiding technology mandates |
| International enforcement | Limited ability to enforce against foreign actors on foreign platforms | Jurisdictional limits |
| Rapid evolution | Static legislation struggles to keep pace with digital innovation | Legislative process speed |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Technology-neutral definitions**: Amend to cover "any electronic communication platform" rather than listing specific technologies
2. **Cumulative disclosure**: Require disclosure when cumulative spending from same source exceeds threshold, regardless of individual purchase size
3. **Cryptocurrency tracking**: Require platforms to identify and verify payment sources; prohibit anonymous ad purchases
4. **Expand issue ad coverage**: Define political ads to include any ads that reference federal policy issues during election periods
5. **FEC rulemaking authority**: Grant FEC explicit authority to update platform thresholds and coverage requirements by regulation

### Corporate Political Activity Disclosure Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Trade association passthrough | Corporations may funnel political spending through trade associations, disclosing only association contributions | High |
| 501(c)(4) secondary spending | Disclosed donations to 501(c)(4)s may be passed to undisclosed third parties | High |
| In-kind contribution valuation | Difficult to value in-kind support (employee time, facilities) consistently | Medium |
| Board approval threshold | $50,000 board approval threshold may be gamed through multiple smaller expenditures | Medium |
| Materiality definitions | "Political activities" may be interpreted narrowly to exclude grassroots or issue advocacy | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Public company focus | Private companies escape most requirements despite significant political activity | SEC jurisdictional limits |
| No prohibited activities | Disclosure only; doesn't limit problematic spending | First Amendment |
| Shareholder remedy limits | Shareholders may lack practical ability to act on disclosed information | Corporate governance structure |
| International subsidiaries | Political activities of foreign subsidiaries may not be disclosed | Jurisdictional complexity |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Association flow-through**: Require associations to disclose which member contributed funds used for each political activity
2. **Anti-structuring provisions**: Aggregate all related expenditures for threshold purposes; impose penalties for structuring
3. **Standardized valuation**: SEC should issue rules for valuing in-kind political contributions
4. **Lower board threshold**: Reduce to $10,000 or require annual aggregate board approval
5. **Private company extension**: Extend disclosure requirements to private companies with revenues over $100 million

### State Model Lobbying Reform Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| State variation | Inconsistent state implementation creates arbitrage opportunities | High |
| Local government gap | May not apply to lobbying of cities, counties, special districts | Medium |
| Part-time legislature exception | States with part-time legislatures may have different time thresholds | Medium |
| Executive branch coverage | May focus on legislative lobbying while ignoring executive agencies | Medium |
| Volunteer lobbyists | Uncompensated advocates may fall outside registration requirements | Low |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No uniformity mechanism | Each state adopts different provisions, creating complexity | State sovereignty |
| Resource constraints | Small states may lack enforcement capacity | Budget limitations |
| Multi-state lobbying | Lobbyists active in multiple states face compliance complexity | Federalism |
| Preemption uncertainty | Unclear how federal reforms interact with state requirements | Constitutional structure |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Uniform Law Commission**: Request Uniform Law Commission develop model lobbying act for state adoption
2. **Interstate compact**: Encourage states to enter compacts for shared lobbying databases and enforcement
3. **Local government extension**: Model act should explicitly cover lobbying of all governmental units
4. **Federal technical assistance**: DOJ should provide technical assistance to states on enforcement best practices
5. **Reciprocal registration**: Allow lobbyists registered in one state to file abbreviated registration in others

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Enforcement resource gaps | Dedicate portion of lobbying registration fees to enforcement |
| Technology obsolescence | Require 5-year technology review with mandatory update authority |
| Inter-agency coordination | Create interagency task force on lobbying enforcement |
| Public awareness | Mandate user-friendly public summaries of lobbying activity |
| Whistleblower protection | Strengthen protections for those reporting lobbying violations |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

All provisions should include:

- **5-year GAO review** of effectiveness and unintended consequences
- **Automatic adjustment authority** for dollar thresholds based on inflation
- **Congressional reporting requirements** on enforcement statistics
- **Stakeholder feedback mechanism** for regulated community and public interest groups
- **Technology adaptation clause** allowing regulatory updates without legislation
