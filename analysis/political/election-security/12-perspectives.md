# Election Security: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes election security reform through nine distinct political perspectives, evaluating current state assessments, root cause analyses, proposed solutions, and legislation. Election security occupies a unique position in American political discourse: it is simultaneously an area of potential bipartisan agreement (most Americans want secure elections) and one of intense partisan conflict (the definition of "security" itself is contested). Key fault lines include whether the greater threat comes from foreign interference or domestic fraud, whether federal standards strengthen or undermine elections, and how to balance security with voter access.

|-------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Tradition, limited government, individual responsibility | Incremental change, fiscal restraint, market solutions |
| Liberal | Progress, equality, social responsibility | Government as problem-solver, regulation, safety nets |
| Progressive | Systemic change, economic justice, structural reform | Transformative reform, wealth redistribution, worker power |
| Libertarian | Individual liberty, minimal state, free markets | Deregulation, privatization, voluntary association |
| Constitutionalist | Original intent, enumerated powers, federalism | Strict constitutional limits, states' rights |
| Populist | Anti-elite, pro-worker, economic nationalism | Trade protection, anti-monopoly, immigration restriction |
| Centrist | Pragmatism, compromise, incremental progress | Bipartisan solutions, evidence-based policy |
| Religious Right | Faith-based values, traditional morality | Social conservatism, religious liberty |
| Democratic Socialist | Worker ownership, public goods, democratic control | Public investment, labor rights, universal programs |

### Engagement Consistency (1-5)

This metric assesses how reliably a perspective's stated positions align with its underlying principles, and how stable those positions are when concerns are addressed. Higher scores indicate more predictable negotiating partners.

| Level | Label | Indicators |
|-------|-------|------------|
| 5 | Highly Consistent | Stated positions reflect underlying principles; positions stable when concerns addressed |
| 4 | Mostly Consistent | Principles generally align with positions; minor strategic flexibility |
| 3 | Mixed Consistency | Some positions principled, others reflect coalition pressures or unstated concerns |
| 2 | Low Consistency | Positions shift frequently; stated concerns differ from underlying priorities |
| 1 | Unpredictable | Positions appear driven primarily by opposition dynamics rather than principles |

**Coalition-Building Considerations:**

- Alignment gap: When stated concerns differ from underlying priorities, addressing stated concerns may not secure support
- Position stability: Perspectives with shifting positions require ongoing negotiation
- Principle consistency: Perspectives that apply principles uniformly are more predictable partners
- Internal diversity: Some perspectives contain factions with different priorities

## Perspective Analyses

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 2/5 - Low Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | *Frames election security in terms of voter fraud prevention, but evidence of significant fraud is minimal; actual priority is often voter access restrictions that advantage Republican candidates* |
| Principle consistency | *Limited government principles conflict with support for federal voter ID mandates and restrictive voting laws; inconsistent application of federalism* |
| Goalpost stability | *Security concerns shift: supported CISA pre-2020, then attacked it when CISA confirmed election integrity; security framing changes based on electoral outcomes* |
| Zero-sum behavior | *Treats election security as zero-sum: measures that increase access are framed as reducing security, even when evidence does not support this* |

**Justification:** Conservative election security positions are heavily influenced by partisan electoral strategy. While legitimate security concerns exist, the emphasis on voter fraud (an extremely rare occurrence) over foreign interference and infrastructure vulnerabilities reflects political calculation rather than evidence-based threat assessment.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- *Support for CISA's election security mission reversed after 2020 when CISA confirmed election integrity (Director Krebs fired)*
- *Heritage Foundation Election Fraud Database documents under 1,400 proven cases over 40 years, yet fraud is presented as a major threat*
- *Voter ID laws supported even when studies show they disproportionately affect minority voters without reducing fraud*

**Source references:**

#### Coalition Potential

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The most viable coalition for election security reform centers on the paper ballot consensus (8/9 perspectives support, making it the broadest agreement across all FixAmerica topics). Paper ballot legislation paired with federal funding and state flexibility can attract near-universal support. Building on this foundation, election worker protection legislation with clear speech safeguards can attract 6/9 perspectives. The voluntary security partnership model addresses the mandate vs. sovereignty debate by using incentives rather than requirements.

### Key Obstacles

The primary obstacle to election security reform is the weaponization of security concerns for partisan purposes. The post-2020 election denial movement transformed "election security" from a technical and institutional issue into a proxy for challenging election legitimacy. This makes bipartisan cooperation difficult even on evidence-based measures like paper ballots and audits, because any security discussion activates partisan priors about what constitutes a "threat."

### Low Consistency Partners

Conservative, Populist, and Religious Right perspectives (all 2/5 consistency) present challenges because their election security positions are heavily influenced by partisan electoral outcomes rather than evidence-based security analysis. For coalition-building, this means: (1) focus on areas of genuine agreement (paper ballots) rather than contested terrain (CISA authority); (2) decouple security improvements from partisan election legitimacy debates; and (3) build state-level coalitions where partisan pressures may be less intense.

### Recommended Approach

Pursue a layered strategy: (1) Lead with universal paper ballot legislation, which commands near-universal support and addresses the most fundamental security concern; (2) Follow with election worker protection legislation using clear speech protections to build bipartisan coalition; (3) Use voluntary partnership model for broader security improvements, leveraging federal funding as incentive for state adoption; (4) Reserve comprehensive mandates (SECURE Elections Act) for political conditions where broader coalitions are possible. Throughout, frame election security as national security and democratic infrastructure rather than partisan advantage.
