# Campaign Finance: Roadmap

## Phase 1: Immediate Actions

### Learn and Connect

**Education**

- Understand how money flows in politics
- Learn your state's campaign finance laws
- Study successful reform models (NYC, Seattle, Maine)
- Know the key organizations and resources

**Connect**

- Join End Citizens United, RepresentUs, or similar organization
- Find local reform coalition
- Connect with like-minded people across party lines
- Subscribe to reform newsletters and updates

### Support Active Campaigns

**Current Opportunities**

- State ballot initiatives for disclosure
- City-level public financing campaigns
- Candidates who support reform
- State legislature reform bills

**How to Help**

- Volunteer for campaigns
- Donate to reform organizations
- Phone bank, canvass, digital organizing
- Share information on social media
- Attend public hearings

### Advocate Now

**Contact Representatives**

- Call and write elected officials about reform
- Attend town halls and ask about campaign finance
- Submit letters to the editor
- Testify at hearings when possible

## Phase 2: Short-Term Goals (1-2 Years)

### State and Local Wins

**Target: Expand Public Financing**

| Priority States/Cities | Opportunity |
|------------------------|-------------|
| States with ballot initiative | Small-dollar matching or disclosure |
| Cities with reform-friendly councils | Democracy vouchers or matching |
| States with Democratic trifectas | Legislative public financing |

**Campaign Elements**

- Coalition building (bipartisan essential for some states)
- Ballot initiative preparation (where applicable)
- Legislative lobbying
- Public education

### Disclosure Victories

**State-Level DISCLOSE**

- Pass disclosure requirements in willing states
- Close dark money loopholes
- Real-time reporting requirements
- Build models for federal legislation

**Municipal Disclosure**

- City-level transparency requirements
- Local campaign finance disclosure
- Dark money exposure

### Build Infrastructure

**Organizational Capacity**

- Grow reform organizations
- Train volunteers and staff
- Develop campaign expertise
- Build donor base for reform efforts

**Research and Evidence**

- Document results from existing public financing
- Commission academic studies
- Track spending and corruption
- Counter opposition research

## Phase 3: Medium-Term Goals (3-5 Years)

### Critical Mass of State/Local Reforms

**Target: 10+ States with Public Financing**

- Continued ballot initiatives
- Legislative adoption where possible
- Defend against repeal attempts
- Build demonstration effects

**Target: 20+ Cities with Strong Programs**

- Expand democracy vouchers beyond Seattle
- Replicate NYC matching model
- Create proof points across country

### Federal Legislation Groundwork

**DISCLOSE Act Passage**

- Build Senate support to overcome filibuster
- Document dark money harms
- Create public pressure
- Prepare for window of opportunity

**For the People Act Components**

- Small-dollar matching for federal candidates
- FEC restructuring
- Enhanced disclosure
- Push when political conditions allow

### FEC Reform Progress

**Administrative Improvements**

- Press for commissioner appointments
- Advocate for enforcement action
- Document FEC failures
- Build case for restructuring

### Constitutional Amendment Momentum

**State Resolutions**

- Continue adding states calling for amendment
- Target 2/3 of states (34 for convention call)
- Build sustained national campaign
- Educate public on need for amendment

## Phase 4: Long-Term Transformation (5+ Years)

### Federal Public Financing System

**Goal: Matching Funds for Congressional Races**

- 6:1 or higher match for small donations
- All federal candidates have viable public option
- Reduced dependence on big donors
- More diverse candidates can run

### Effective Enforcement

**Restructured FEC or New Agency**

- End partisan gridlock
- Independent enforcement
- Adequate resources
- Timely action on complaints

### Constitutional Amendment

**28th Amendment**

- Overturn Citizens United
- Establish Congress can regulate spending
- Clarify money is not speech
- Enable comprehensive reform

### System Transformation

**New Political Landscape**

- Candidates spend time with constituents, not donors
- Policy reflects public preferences
- Diverse candidates can run and win
- Trust in government restored

## Risks and Contingencies

### Risk: Court Challenges

**Threat**: Courts strike down public financing or disclosure laws

**Mitigation**

- Design reforms to survive judicial scrutiny
- Build strong record of rationale
- Engage constitutional law experts
- Prepare appeals

**Contingency**: If struck down, adjust design and try again; push for constitutional amendment

### Risk: Opposition Funding

**Threat**: Wealthy interests massively outspend reform campaigns

**Mitigation**

- Build grassroots strength
- Earned media strategy
- Pre-rebut opposition messaging
- Bipartisan coalition for credibility

**Contingency**: If outspent, focus on ground game, localize campaigns, highlight opponent spending

### Risk: Scandal Backfires

**Threat**: Public financing participant involved in scandal, discredits reform

**Mitigation**

- Strong enforcement in public financing programs
- Clear rules and compliance
- Rapid response to problems
- Emphasize systemic benefits over individual cases

**Contingency**: Acknowledge problems, fix them, don't let one case undermine whole program

### Risk: Political Backlash

**Threat**: Reform becomes partisan wedge issue

**Mitigation**

- Maintain bipartisan messengers
- Focus on corruption, not partisan advantage
- Avoid culture war associations
- Republican validators essential

**Contingency**: If captured by one party, work to reclaim bipartisan framing; emphasize shared frustration

### Risk: Reform Fatigue

**Threat**: Public gives up on reform as too hard

**Mitigation**

- Celebrate wins, however small
- Clear communication of progress
- Connect to tangible outcomes
- Show path to victory

**Contingency**: Reframe around local/state success; make reform feel achievable

### Risk: Unintended Consequences

**Threat**: Reforms have unexpected negative effects

**Mitigation**

- Study existing programs carefully
- Build in evaluation and adjustment mechanisms
- Learn from other jurisdictions' mistakes
- Adaptive implementation

**Contingency**: Acknowledge problems, adjust programs, demonstrate commitment to getting it right

## Success Metrics

### Leading Indicators

- Public awareness of campaign finance issues (polling)
- Reform organization membership and engagement
- Media coverage of campaign finance
- Bills introduced in legislatures
- Candidates endorsing reform
- Small-donor participation rates

### Lagging Indicators

- States/cities with public financing
- Total amount raised via public financing
- Decline in dark money spending
- Policy responsiveness to public opinion
- FEC enforcement actions
- Trust in elections (polling)

### Ultimate Success

- Candidates can run without big donors
- Average citizens' voices equal to wealthy
- Policy reflects public preferences
- Trust in democratic institutions restored
- Corruption rare and punished
