# Campaign Finance: Actions

## For Individuals

### Learn First

**Understand the Problem**

- [ ] Read [Current State](02-current-state.md) to understand how money dominates politics
- [ ] Read [Root Causes](04-root-causes.md) to understand Citizens United and its effects
- [ ] Learn about public financing options (see [Solutions](07-solutions.md))
- [ ] Look up your representatives' donors at OpenSecrets.org

**Know Your State**

- [ ] Research your state's campaign finance laws
- [ ] Check if your state has public financing programs
- [ ] Find out if your state has ballot initiative process
- [ ] Identify active reform campaigns in your area

### Take Action

**Contact Representatives**

- [ ] Call your representatives about campaign finance reform
- [ ] Ask about their position on public financing
- [ ] Demand support for disclosure requirements
- [ ] Follow up with letters and emails

**Join the Movement**

- [ ] Sign up with End Citizens United, RepresentUs, or Common Cause
- [ ] Follow reform organizations on social media
- [ ] Subscribe to reform newsletters (see [Resources](09-resources.md))
- [ ] Join local chapter if one exists

**Spread the Word**

- [ ] Share educational content about money in politics
- [ ] Explain the problem to friends and family
- [ ] Post about reform on social media
- [ ] Write letters to the editor

### Vote and Support

**Electoral Action**

- [ ] Research candidates' positions on reform
- [ ] Support candidates who endorse public financing
- [ ] Donate small amounts to reformers (it adds up)
- [ ] Vote YES on reform ballot measures

**Financial Support**

- [ ] Donate to reform organizations
- [ ] Contribute to ballot initiative campaigns
- [ ] Support candidates who refuse Super PAC help
- [ ] Fund local reform efforts

## For Communities

### Local Reform Campaigns

**Assess the Landscape**

1. Research if your city can adopt campaign finance reforms
2. Check city charter for initiative or council adoption paths
3. Identify existing reform supporters
4. Assess city council receptiveness

**Build a Coalition**

1. Recruit bipartisan leadership
2. Engage local civic organizations
3. Connect with business leaders frustrated with the system
4. Partner with local academic institutions
5. Build diverse, visible coalition

**Choose Your Reform**

| Reform | Best For |
|--------|----------|
| **Small-dollar matching** | Cities with budget capacity |
| **Democracy vouchers** | Innovative cities willing to experiment |
| **Enhanced disclosure** | Cities with weaker state laws |
| **Candidate pledge** | Building awareness before formal reform |

### Local Advocacy Actions

**City Council Engagement**

- [ ] Attend city council meetings and speak during public comment
- [ ] Request formal study of public financing for your city
- [ ] Submit written testimony supporting reform
- [ ] Meet with individual council members

**Community Education**

- [ ] Host informational events at libraries, community centers
- [ ] Partner with League of Women Voters for forums
- [ ] Organize candidate forums focused on reform
- [ ] Create local educational materials

**Build Visible Support**

- [ ] Collect signatures supporting reform
- [ ] Get op-eds from local leaders
- [ ] Secure endorsements from community groups
- [ ] Document local examples of money influencing politics

## For Advocates

### Running a Local Reform Campaign

**Phase 1: Preparation**

1. Research legal requirements for reform
2. Study successful models (Seattle, NYC)
3. Build core team and leadership
4. Develop budget and fundraising plan
5. Craft messaging strategy

**Phase 2: Coalition Building**

1. Recruit bipartisan visible leaders
2. Engage civic organizations
3. Build business support
4. Connect with party reformers on both sides
5. Document coalition publicly

**Phase 3: Campaign**

1. If ballot initiative: gather signatures
2. If council adoption: lobby members
3. Public education campaign
4. Earned media strategy
5. GOTV for election (if ballot)

**Phase 4: Implementation**

1. Work with election officials on rollout
2. Voter education about new system
3. Candidate education and encouragement
4. Defend against challenges
5. Document success

### Advocacy Best Practices

**Messaging Principles**

- Lead with anti-corruption, not technical details
- Use specific local examples
- Emphasize bipartisan nature of problem
- Focus on outcomes (who benefits, who's harmed)
- Counter-message opposition talking points

**Coalition Building**

- Bipartisan leadership essential
- Diverse validators (business, civic, faith)
- Don't make it culture war issue
- Give partners ownership and visibility
- Maintain coalition through implementation

**Working with Media**

- Build relationships with local reporters
- Provide clear, quotable soundbites
- Offer data and research
- Respond quickly to opposition claims
- Submit regular op-eds

### State-Level Advocacy

**Legislative Strategy**

1. Identify reform-friendly legislators
2. Draft or support legislation
3. Build constituent pressure
4. Testify at hearings
5. Track and publicize votes

**Ballot Initiative Strategy**

1. Research signature requirements
2. Draft initiative language (legal review)
3. Build signature-gathering operation
4. Run campaign to pass
5. Defend against legal challenges

## Measuring Impact

### Activity Metrics

| Metric | How to Track |
|--------|--------------|
| Volunteers recruited | Organization databases |
| Events held | Event logs |
| Media hits | Press clip tracking |
| Legislators contacted | Contact logs |
| Signatures gathered | Petition counts |

### Outcome Metrics

| Metric | Target |
|--------|--------|
| Cities with public financing | Track adoptions |
| States with disclosure laws | Track legislation |
| Reform ballot measures passed | Win rate, margins |
| Small-donor participation | % of contributors under $200 |
| Diversity of candidates | Demographics of publicly financed candidates |

### Long-Term Success Metrics

- Policy responsiveness to public (research studies)
- Trust in government (polling)
- Voter satisfaction with choices
- Quality and diversity of candidates
- Amount of dark money (declining)

## State-Specific Action Guides

### States with Ballot Initiatives

**Priority Actions**

1. Connect with state-specific reform organization
2. Volunteer for active campaigns
3. Prepare for signature gathering
4. GOTV for ballot measures

### States without Ballot Initiatives

**Strategy**: Legislative path or local adoption

**Actions**

1. Build legislative champions
2. Focus on city-level reforms
3. Create demonstration effects
4. Build pressure for state legislation

### States with Existing Programs

**Maine, Arizona, Connecticut, etc.**
**Actions**

1. Defend against attacks
2. Document and share success
3. Support program expansion
4. Serve as resource for other states
