# Campaign Finance: Stakeholders

## Who Benefits from the Status Quo

### Wealthy Donors and Mega-Donors

**Who They Are**

- Billionaires who fund Super PACs
- Executives who bundle contributions
- Wealthy individuals seeking access
- Family dynasties with political interests

**What They Gain**

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Policy influence | Candidates prioritize donor preferences |
| Direct access | Fundraising events, private meetings |
| Favorable legislation | Tax policy, regulations, subsidies |
| Appointment power | Ambassadorships, boards, administration jobs |

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Very High**

- Direct threat to their influence
- Well-organized to oppose change
- Can fund opposition to reform efforts

### Corporations and Industry Groups

**Who They Are**

- Fortune 500 companies
- Industry trade associations (Chamber of Commerce, etc.)
- Sector-specific PACs (pharma, oil, finance)
- Private equity and hedge funds

**What They Gain**

- Regulatory forbearance
- Tax loopholes and subsidies
- Protection from consumer/environmental laws
- Favorable trade policy
- Government contracts

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **High**

- Corporate spending is their tool
- Business model depends on influence
- Well-resourced to fight reform

### Political Consultants and Campaign Industry

**Who They Are**

- Media buyers (take commission on ad spending)
- Pollsters and data analysts
- Fundraising consultants
- Digital strategists
- Event planners

**What They Gain**

- Commissions on massive ad spending
- Lucrative contracts every cycle
- High-paying jobs in political industry
- More money = more business

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Medium-High**

- Business model depends on expensive campaigns
- Public financing would reduce their market
- Usually quiet opposition

### Incumbent Politicians

**Why They Benefit**

- Won under current system
- Established donor networks
- Party leadership positions tied to fundraising prowess
- Challengers face fundraising disadvantage

**Specific Benefits**

- Incumbents outraise challengers significantly
- Leadership roles reward best fundraisers
- Safe seats reduce need for broad support
- Big donors provide stability vs. small-donor volatility

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Medium-High**

- Self-interest favors status quo
- Some genuine reformers exist
- Fear of empowering challengers

### Party Leadership

**Why They Benefit**

- Control fundraising apparatus
- Distribute resources to loyal members
- Power over nominations partially via money
- Access to biggest donors

**The Patronage System**

| Party Role | Power Source |
|------------|--------------|
| DCCC/NRCC chairs | Assign resources to candidates |
| Party leaders | Access to donor networks |
| Committee chairs | Fundraising expectations from members |
| Whips | Track fundraising compliance |

### Media Companies

**Why They Benefit**

- Political advertising is massive revenue
- Billions in TV ad spending every cycle
- Digital platforms profit from political ads
- Local stations especially dependent

**Conflict of Interest**

- Cover campaigns that also pay their bills
- May be reluctant to expose advertising problems
- More expensive campaigns = more revenue

## Who Is Harmed

### Average Voters

**What They Lose**

- Equal voice in democracy
- Representatives who prioritize their interests
- Policy that reflects their preferences
- Trust in democratic institutions

**The Numbers**

| Metric | Reality |
|--------|---------|
| Donor influence vs. voter influence | Donors' preferences predict policy |
| Average citizen's policy influence | Statistically near zero (Gilens & Page) |
| Small donor contribution share | ~30% of total |
| Perception system is rigged | 77% agree |

### Non-Wealthy Candidates

**Barriers They Face**

- Need access to wealthy networks
- Must spend enormous time fundraising
- Wealthy self-funders have advantage
- Party support goes to proven fundraisers

**Who Doesn't Run**

- Working-class candidates
- Candidates without wealthy connections
- Issue advocates without fundraising experience
- Anyone who can't commit to constant call time

### Policy Beneficiaries Without Lobbying Power

**Groups Whose Issues Are Ignored**

| Group | Why They Lack Power |
|-------|---------------------|
| Poor and working class | Can't donate significantly |
| Children and future generations | Can't vote or donate |
| Environment | Dispersed, long-term interests |
| Consumers (general) | Less organized than industry |
| Criminal justice reform advocates | Often disenfranchised |

### Small Businesses

**The Disadvantage**

- Can't match corporate spending
- Less access to politicians
- Policies favor large corporations
- Trade associations dominated by big firms

### Democracy Itself

**Institutional Harms**

- Legitimacy undermined
- Representation distorted
- Civic engagement discouraged
- Corruption (legal and illegal) normalized

## Who Has Power to Change Things

### Congress

**Power Level**: High (but won't use it)

**What They Could Do**

- Pass disclosure requirements
- Create public financing systems
- Restructure FEC
- Propose constitutional amendment
- Change tax treatment of political spending

**Why They Don't**

- Self-interest opposes reform
- Party pressure against change
- Filibuster blocks in Senate
- Divided views on solutions

### Supreme Court

**Power Level**: Very High

**What They Control**

- Constitutional interpretation of First Amendment
- Definition of corruption
- What regulations are permissible

**Current Stance**

- Conservative majority hostile to regulation
- Money = speech doctrine entrenched
- Would need composition change for new approach

### Voters (via Ballot Initiatives)

**Power Level**: Medium (in some states)

**Mechanism**

- Direct democracy in states with initiative process
- Can pass disclosure, public financing
- Bypass legislature

**Limitations**

- Not all states have initiatives
- Can't address federal elections directly
- Well-funded opposition

### State Legislatures

**Power Level**: Medium

**What They Can Do**

- Public financing for state/local races
- Disclosure requirements
- Independent redistricting (reduces safe seats)
- Electoral reforms that change incentives

**Limitations**

- Can't affect federal elections
- Same self-interest problems as Congress
- Vary widely in reform appetite

### Reform Organizations

**Power Level**: Low-Medium (growing)

**Key Groups**

| Organization | Focus |
|--------------|-------|
| End Citizens United | Political reform, Democratic-aligned |
| Issue One | Bipartisan reform |
| RepresentUs | Anti-corruption, local focus |
| Common Cause | Comprehensive reform |
| Campaign Legal Center | Litigation and legal advocacy |
| Brennan Center | Research and legal strategy |

## Potential Allies

### Cross-Partisan Voter Coalition

**Shared Frustration**

- 77% believe money has too much influence (bipartisan)
- Populist left and populist right both distrust system
- Tea Party and Bernie supporters share this critique
- Common enemy: wealthy special interests

**Messaging That Works**

- "Politicians should work for us, not big donors"
- "End corruption"
- "Level the playing field"
- Avoid partisan framing

### Small Business Owners

**Why They Might Support Reform**

- Tired of competing against corporate influence
- Want fair regulatory treatment
- Some business owners are reform advocates
- Authentic voice for "business supports reform"

### Retiring Politicians

**Why They're Allies**

- Free from re-election pressure
- Some genuinely frustrated with fundraising burden
- Can speak honestly about system's problems
- Credible validators for reform

**Examples**

- McCain (R) championed McCain-Feingold
- Many exit interviews criticize call time
- Bipartisan frustration expressed after leaving

### Good Government Groups

**Organizations**

- League of Women Voters
- Citizens for Responsibility and Ethics in Washington (CREW)
- Sunlight Foundation (transparency)
- OpenSecrets (data and research)

**Role**

- Provide research and data
- Track money flows
- Publicize problems
- Advocate for solutions

### Academic and Legal Experts

**Why They Matter**

- Research demonstrates problems
- Develop and evaluate solutions
- Provide expert testimony
- Counter opposition claims

**Key Institutions**

- Brennan Center for Justice
- Campaign Finance Institute
- Law school programs (Harvard, Stanford)
- Political science departments

### Some Journalists

**Supportive Media**

- Investigative reporters exposing corruption
- Political reporters frustrated with access
- Editorial boards supporting reform
- Nonprofit news organizations

**Their Role**

- Make public aware of problems
- Investigate specific scandals
- Amplify reform arguments
- Counter normalization

### The Power Imbalance

| Factor | Reform Coalition | Opposition Coalition |
|--------|------------------|----------------------|
| Public support | 77%+ | Minority |
| Funding | Limited | Vast |
| Institutional power | Low | High |
| Media access | Limited | Extensive |
| Cohesion | Growing | Stable |

### Strategic Implications

**Advantages for Reform**

1. Overwhelming public support
2. Cross-partisan frustration
3. Bipartisan messengers available
4. State/local wins build momentum

**Challenges for Reform**

1. Opposition well-funded
2. Incumbents control process
3. Courts hostile to regulation
4. Requires sustained effort
