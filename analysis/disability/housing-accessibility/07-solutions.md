# Housing Accessibility for People with Disabilities: Solutions

## Overview

Addressing the housing accessibility crisis requires action on multiple fronts simultaneously: increasing accessible housing supply through building code reform, expanding federal housing programs for disability populations, strengthening FHA enforcement, funding home modifications, reforming zoning laws, and redirecting Medicaid spending from institutions to community housing. No single solution is adequate; the barriers are structural and interconnected.

## Solution 1: National Visitability Standard for New Residential Construction

### Description

Amend the International Residential Code (IRC) and International Building Code (IBC) to require basic visitability features in all new single-family and small multifamily housing: at least one zero-step entrance, minimum 32-inch clear-passage doorways on the main floor, and at least one accessible half-bathroom on the main floor.

### Evidence Base

- Pima County, AZ visitability ordinance (2002) demonstrated feasibility with minimal cost impact
- HUD cost estimates: $200-$1,000 per unit for visitability in new construction (HUD, *Fair Housing Act Design Manual*)
- ICC A117.1 2017 standard includes Type C "visitable" unit classification, providing technical framework
- AARP survey (2024): 77% of adults 50+ want to age in place; visitability features essential for aging in place
- Universal design research showing that accessible homes reduce fall-related injuries and hospitalizations (CDC, 2023)

### Cost Estimate

- **Per unit**: $200-$1,000 for basic visitability in new construction (design phase)
- **Retrofit cost avoided**: $8,000-$50,000 per unit for post-construction accessibility modifications
- **Annual new homes affected**: Approximately 1.4 million single-family housing starts/year (Census Bureau, 2024)
- **Annual national cost**: $280 million-$1.4 billion (new construction) vs. $11-$70 billion in avoided retrofits over building lifetime

### Timeline

- Years 1-2: ICC code development and model code adoption
- Years 2-4: State-by-state building code adoption (begin with early adopter states)
- Years 3-7: Implementation across majority of states
- Year 10+: Critical mass of visitable housing in national stock

### International Models

- **United Kingdom**: Part M of Building Regulations requires all new homes to meet Category 1 (visitable) standards; enhanced accessibility required for a percentage of units since 2015
- **Australia**: National Construction Code (NCC) 2022 includes Livable Housing Design Standards requiring all new homes to meet silver-level accessibility by 2025
- **Japan**: Housing Performance Indication System includes accessibility rating; barrier-free design incentivized through tax benefits

## Solution 2: Expand and Reform Federal Housing Programs

### Description

Significantly expand Section 811 Supportive Housing, Mainstream Vouchers, and Section 8 Housing Choice Voucher funding for disability populations. Reform program design to reduce waiting lists, improve community integration, and align with *Olmstead* requirements.

### Evidence Base

- Section 811 serves 35,000 households against estimated need of 1+ million (HUD, FY 2024)
- Average Section 8 waiting time for people with disabilities: 2-5 years, some areas 7+ years (CBPP, 2024)
- SSI recipients cannot afford market rent in any state; housing subsidies are mathematically necessary (NLIHC, *Priced Out*, 2024)
- Housing First programs demonstrate 80-85% housing retention rates for chronically homeless people with disabilities (National Alliance to End Homelessness, 2024)

### Cost Estimate

- **Section 811 expansion**: Increase from $254 million to $1 billion/year (additional $746 million)
- **Mainstream Voucher expansion**: Increase from $423 million to $1.5 billion/year (additional $1.077 billion)
- **Additional Section 8 vouchers targeted to disability**: $2-$3 billion/year for 200,000-300,000 new vouchers
- **Total additional annual cost**: $3.8-$4.8 billion
- **Offset**: Reduced institutionalization costs; $80,000-$100,000/year per person diverted from nursing home to community

### Timeline

- Year 1: Budget request and appropriations campaign
- Years 1-3: 50,000 new Mainstream Vouchers; 20,000 new Section 811 units
- Years 3-6: Scale to full proposed levels
- Year 10: Section 8 disability waiting lists reduced to under 1 year

### International Models

- **Finland**: Achieved virtual elimination of homelessness through Housing First, providing accessible permanent housing to all homeless individuals including those with disabilities
- **Canada**: National Housing Strategy (2017) includes dedicated funding stream for accessible affordable housing

## Solution 3: Federal Home Modification Grant Program

### Description

Create a dedicated federal grant program for home modifications for people with disabilities, administered through state housing agencies and Centers for Independent Living. Grants of up to $25,000 per household for basic modifications (ramps, grab bars, doorway widening, bathroom renovations) and up to $50,000 for complex modifications (elevators, comprehensive bathroom remodeling, smart home technology).

### Evidence Base

- Average home modification cost: $8,000-$15,000 for basic; $30,000-$50,000 for comprehensive (AARP, 2023)
- Current programs (VA SAH/HISA, state programs, Medicaid waivers) are fragmented and underfunded
- Falls are the leading cause of injury among older adults and people with mobility disabilities; home modifications reduce fall risk by 26-39% (Cochrane Review, Gillespie et al., 2012)
- Every $1 spent on home modifications saves $10 in fall-related healthcare costs (CDC, STEADI program data)

### Cost Estimate

- **Program funding**: $500 million-$1 billion/year
- **Households served**: 33,000-66,000 per year at average $15,000 grant
- **Healthcare offset**: Estimated $5-$10 billion in avoided fall-related healthcare costs over 10 years
- **Institutionalization offset**: Each home modification that prevents nursing home placement saves $80,000-$100,000/year

### Timeline

- Year 1: Legislative authorization and initial appropriation
- Year 2: Program design, state agency agreements, application systems
- Year 3: First grants awarded
- Year 5: Program at scale; 50,000+ modifications annually

### International Models

- **United Kingdom**: Disabled Facilities Grant (DFG) provides up to GBP 30,000 per household for home modifications; mandatory for local authorities to administer
- **Sweden**: Municipal housing adaptation grants cover 100% of medically necessary modifications with no means test

## Solution 4: Strengthen Fair Housing Act Design Compliance

### Description

Enact federal legislation requiring pre-construction plan review for FHA design compliance, increasing penalties for design violations, and creating a dedicated FHA design enforcement unit at HUD FHEO.

### Evidence Base

- 90%+ noncompliance rate for post-1991 multifamily housing (NFHA, 2023)
- Current complaint-driven enforcement has been insufficient over 35 years
- Pre-construction review models exist in other code enforcement areas (fire, structural, energy)
- NFHA testing programs have documented systemic violations in every major metropolitan area

### Cost Estimate

- **HUD enforcement unit**: $30-$50 million/year for dedicated FHA design compliance staff
- **Local plan review training**: $10-$20 million/year (federal grants to localities)
- **Industry compliance**: Minimal if designs are corrected before construction; post-construction remediation costs $5,000-$50,000 per unit
- **Total federal cost**: $40-$70 million/year

### Timeline

- Year 1: Legislation enacted; HUD establishes design enforcement unit
- Years 1-3: Develop pre-construction review standards and training
- Years 3-5: Implement in jurisdictions receiving federal housing funds
- Year 5+: Compliance rates for new construction measurably improve

### International Models

- **Canada**: Ontario Building Code includes accessibility review as part of standard building permit process; compliance rates exceed 90%

## Solution 5: Zoning Reform for Disability Housing

### Description

Enact federal legislation conditioning Community Development Block Grant (CDBG) and HOME funds on elimination of discriminatory zoning barriers: prohibit spacing requirements and occupancy limits for group homes, require as-of-right zoning for supportive housing in residential districts, and preempt local ordinances that violate the Fair Housing Act.

### Evidence Base

- Group homes face discriminatory zoning in communities across the country (NFHA, 2023)
- *City of Edmonds v. Oxford House, Inc.*, 514 U.S. 725 (1995), established that FHA overrides local zoning for group homes
- DOJ has settled numerous FHA zoning cases, but violations continue because enforcement is case-by-case
- YIMBY movement creates potential political allies for zoning reform

### Cost Estimate

- **Federal enforcement**: $15-$25 million/year (embedded in HUD FHEO budget increase)
- **No direct housing cost**: Removes barriers to private and nonprofit development
- **Savings**: Reduced litigation costs for disability organizations and local governments

### Timeline

- Year 1: Executive guidance on FHA zoning obligations
- Year 2: Proposed CDBG/HOME conditions
- Year 3: Final rule; state and local compliance begins
- Year 5: Measurable reduction in group home zoning barriers

### International Models

- **Canada**: Ontario Human Rights Code prohibits zoning restrictions that discriminate against people with disabilities; group homes treated as residential use by right

## Solution 6: LIHTC Accessibility Enhancement

### Description

Amend Section 42 of the Internal Revenue Code to increase accessibility requirements in LIHTC properties from 5% to 15% of units meeting mobility accessibility standards and from 2% to 5% meeting sensory accessibility standards. Require state Qualified Allocation Plans to award additional points for projects exceeding minimum accessibility requirements and for universal design features.

### Evidence Base

- LIHTC is the largest federal affordable housing production program ($10+ billion annual tax expenditure)
- Current 5% accessibility requirement (from Section 504) produces too few accessible units
- Some states already award QAP points for accessibility, demonstrating feasibility
- Universal design features add minimal cost in new construction (1-2% of total cost)

### Cost Estimate

- **Per unit additional cost**: $1,000-$3,000 for enhanced accessibility in new construction
- **Annual impact**: 15,000-20,000 additional accessible LIHTC units per year (based on ~150,000 total annual LIHTC units)
- **Total annual cost**: $15-$60 million in additional construction costs (absorbed by developers as condition of tax credit)

### Timeline

- Year 1: Legislative amendment to Section 42
- Year 2: IRS/Treasury guidance; state QAP revisions
- Year 3: First projects with enhanced requirements under construction
- Year 5+: 75,000-100,000 additional accessible affordable units produced

## Solution 7: Medicaid Housing-Related Services Expansion

### Description

Expand Medicaid authority to cover housing-related services and supports, including housing transition services, tenancy sustaining services, housing search assistance, and limited environmental modifications. Make HCBS a mandatory rather than optional Medicaid benefit, eliminating waiting lists.

### Evidence Base

- CMS Housing-Related Services and Supports guidance (2015, updated 2023) authorizes but does not require housing services
- 650,000+ people on HCBS waiting lists nationally (KFF, 2024)
- Institutional Medicaid is mandatory; HCBS is optional, creating structural institutional bias
- Studies show HCBS costs average 30-50% less than institutional care per person (Eiken et al., *Medicaid Expenditures for Long-Term Services and Supports*, 2018)

### Cost Estimate

- **Making HCBS mandatory**: Estimated $15-$25 billion/year in additional federal Medicaid spending
- **Offset**: Estimated $8-$12 billion/year in reduced institutional Medicaid costs as people transition to community
- **Net additional cost**: $7-$13 billion/year
- **Per person savings**: Community placement saves $20,000-$60,000/year per person vs. institutional care

### Timeline

- Year 1-2: Legislative effort to make HCBS mandatory
- Years 2-5: Phase in mandatory HCBS; eliminate waiting lists
- Years 5-10: Full implementation; institutional populations reduced to those who need or choose institutional care

### International Models

- **Sweden**: Right to personal assistance and community housing for all people with disabilities; no HCBS waiting lists
- **Germany**: Long-term care insurance (Pflegeversicherung) provides community-based supports as an entitlement, not a waiver

## Solution 8: Accessible Housing Data and Accountability

### Description

Create a national registry of accessible housing units, require disclosure of accessibility features in real estate listings, and mandate that federally funded housing programs track and report on accessible unit supply and demand.

### Evidence Base

- No comprehensive data exists on the accessible housing supply in the United States
- People with disabilities cannot efficiently search for accessible housing; search costs are high and information is unreliable
- Real estate listing services (MLS) do not consistently include accessibility information
- HUD does not systematically track accessible unit supply or demand across programs

### Cost Estimate

- **National registry development**: $20-$30 million (one-time)
- **Annual maintenance**: $5-$10 million/year
- **MLS integration**: $5-$10 million (one-time; MLS system modifications)
- **Total**: $30-$50 million first year; $5-$10 million ongoing

### Timeline

- Year 1: Legislation and HUD program design
- Year 2: Registry development and MLS integration
- Year 3: Data collection begins
- Year 5: Comprehensive accessible housing data available for policymaking

## Sequencing and Priority

| Priority | Solution | Rationale |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| 1 | Federal housing program expansion | Most immediate impact on people currently in crisis |
| 2 | Home modification grants | Addresses needs of people in existing housing |
| 3 | FHA design compliance strengthening | Stops ongoing production of inaccessible multifamily housing |
| 4 | LIHTC accessibility enhancement | Leverages largest production program for affordable housing |
| 5 | Visitability standard | Long-term structural change in all new housing |
| 6 | Zoning reform | Removes barriers to group homes and supportive housing |
| 7 | Medicaid HCBS expansion | Addresses institutional bias; largest fiscal impact |
| 8 | Housing data and accountability | Foundation for evidence-based policy and consumer information |
