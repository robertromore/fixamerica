# Housing Accessibility for People with Disabilities: Actions

## Individual Actions

### Educate Yourself

- Learn the basics of the Fair Housing Act's disability protections, including reasonable accommodation and modification rights
- Understand your rights as a tenant or homebuyer with a disability under federal, state, and local law
- Visit HUD's Fair Housing page at <https://www.hud.gov/program_offices/fair_housing_equal_opp> for guidance and complaint information
- Read the National Low Income Housing Coalition's *Priced Out* report to understand the SSI-housing affordability gap
- Follow disability housing organizations (NFHA, NLIHC, TAC) on social media for policy developments
- Learn about visitability features and universal design principles for housing

### Report Violations

- File Fair Housing Act complaints with HUD at <https://www.hud.gov/program_offices/fair_housing_equal_opp/online-complaint>
- Contact your state or local fair housing agency (find yours at <https://nationalfairhousing.org/get-help/>)
- Report FHA design and construction violations in multifamily buildings to the National Fair Housing Alliance
- Document accessibility barriers in housing with photos, dates, and descriptions
- Keep records of all communications with landlords about reasonable accommodation and modification requests
- Contact your state's Protection and Advocacy organization for legal assistance (find yours at <https://www.ndrn.org/about/ndrn-member-agencies/>)
- Report Medicaid HCBS service denials or inadequate services to your state Medicaid ombudsman

### Personal Advocacy

- Make reasonable accommodation requests in writing and keep copies of all correspondence
- Know that landlords must respond to accommodation requests within a reasonable time and cannot charge fees for accommodations
- Understand your right to make reasonable modifications to rental units at your own expense under the FHA
- Request accessible unit transfers in public housing and Section 8 programs
- Write reviews of apartment complexes noting accessibility (or lack thereof)
- Share your housing search experiences publicly to raise awareness
- Attend public housing authority meetings to advocate for accessible unit availability

## Community Actions

### Local Organizing

- Join or start a local disability housing advocacy group through your Center for Independent Living
- Organize accessibility audits of apartment complexes, particularly post-1991 buildings that should meet FHA design requirements
- Attend city council and planning commission meetings to advocate for visitability ordinances in new construction
- Partner with local fair housing organizations for testing of housing discrimination against people with disabilities
- Build coalitions with YIMBY groups, affordable housing advocates, and aging-in-place organizations
- Connect with local Habitat for Humanity chapters to advocate for accessible building practices
- Organize tenant associations in public housing to demand accessible unit maintenance and repairs

### Awareness Campaigns

- Organize community events for Fair Housing Month (April) highlighting disability housing issues
- Create local accessibility scorecards for apartment complexes and property management companies
- Engage local media in covering the accessible housing shortage and its impact on community members
- Use social media to document housing accessibility barriers (#AccessibleHousing, #FairHousing, #Visitability)
- Invite people with disabilities to share their housing search experiences at community forums
- Host home modification fairs connecting people with disabilities to contractors and funding sources
- Document and publicize the gap between SSI income and local fair market rents

### Technical Assistance

- Volunteer to help people with disabilities navigate the Section 8 application process
- Train community members on filing fair housing complaints
- Develop accessible housing guides for your local area, identifying buildings with accessibility features
- Partner with occupational therapists and home modification contractors to create referral networks
- Mentor people with disabilities in homeownership opportunities and processes
- Help aging family members understand home modification options and funding sources
- Assist neighbors in requesting reasonable accommodations from landlords

## Political Actions

### Contact Your Representatives

**Call your members of Congress about housing accessibility:**

- Support increased funding for Section 811 Supportive Housing for Persons with Disabilities
- Support expansion of Mainstream Vouchers for non-elderly people with disabilities
- Support creation of a federal home modification grant program
- Support LIHTC accessibility requirements beyond the current 5% minimum
- Support making Medicaid HCBS a mandatory benefit to eliminate waiting lists
- Oppose cuts to HUD housing programs that serve people with disabilities

```text
beneficiaries cannot afford a one-bedroom apartment at fair
market rent in any state.

I ask you to:

1. Support increased funding for Section 811 Supportive Housing,
   which serves only 35,000 households nationally against a need
   of over 1 million
2. Support expansion of Mainstream Vouchers for non-elderly
   people with disabilities
3. Support creation of a federal home modification grant program
   to help people with disabilities remain safely in their homes
4. Support strengthening Fair Housing Act design compliance for
   new multifamily construction
5. Support making Medicaid home and community-based services a
   mandatory benefit to end waiting lists that force people into
   institutions

An estimated 15.4 million households include a person with a
disability. These families deserve accessible, affordable housing
that allows community living and independence. Every dollar
invested in accessible community housing saves far more in
avoided institutionalization costs.

Thank you for your attention to this important issue.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your Address]
```

### State and Local Advocacy

- Advocate for visitability ordinances in your city or county requiring basic accessibility in new construction
- Support state legislation for visitability standards in building codes
- Advocate for strengthened LIHTC accessibility requirements in your state's Qualified Allocation Plan
- Support zoning reforms that remove barriers to group homes and supportive housing in residential neighborhoods
- Advocate for state home modification grant programs through your state legislature
- Attend state housing finance agency public hearings on housing program design

### Vote and Engage

- Research candidates' positions on disability housing, fair housing enforcement, and HUD funding
- Participate in the REV UP campaign (Register, Educate, Vote, Use your Power)
- Attend town halls and ask candidates about accessible housing policy
- Support candidates who commit to expanding accessible affordable housing
- Volunteer for campaigns of candidates who support housing accessibility reform

## Talking Points

### For General Audiences

- Fewer than 5% of U.S. housing units have basic accessibility features, despite 15.4 million households including a person with a disability
- Disability is the most common basis for fair housing complaints, making up 56% of all complaints filed with HUD
- SSI beneficiaries cannot afford a one-bedroom apartment at fair market rent in any state in the country
- An estimated 40% of people experiencing homelessness have a disability
- Basic visitability features (zero-step entrance, wide doorways, accessible bathroom) cost only $200-$1,000 when included in new construction but $8,000-$50,000 to retrofit later
- An estimated 20-25% of nursing home residents could live in the community with appropriate housing and services

### For Business and Developer Audiences

- Accessible housing serves a growing market: 10,000 Americans turn 65 every day, and most will need accessibility features eventually
- Visitability features add less than 1% to new construction costs when designed from the start
- AARP surveys show 77% of adults 50+ want to age in place, creating strong market demand for accessible features
- Accessible design increases the marketable life of housing units, reducing vacancy risk as the population ages
- Pre-construction FHA compliance review is less expensive than post-construction remediation ($200-$1,000 per unit vs. $5,000-$50,000)
- Smart home technology that improves accessibility also appeals to mainstream buyers

### For Policymaker Audiences

- Section 811 serves 35,000 households against a need of over 1 million, a 3.5% coverage rate
- Medicaid pays $80,000-$100,000/year for nursing home care; community housing with services costs $30,000-$40,000/year
- Housing First programs for chronically homeless people with disabilities save $15,000-$30,000 per person per year in reduced public costs
- Over 90% of post-1991 multifamily housing violates Fair Housing Act design requirements after 35 years of enforcement
- The UK, Australia, and other countries have adopted national visitability standards with minimal cost impact
- Every $1 invested in home modifications saves an estimated $10 in fall-related healthcare costs

## Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Reality |
|--------------|---------|
| "Accessible housing is only for wheelchair users" | Accessibility features benefit people with a wide range of disabilities, older adults, parents with strollers, people with temporary injuries, and delivery workers |
| "Accessibility features are expensive" | Basic visitability costs $200-$1,000 per unit in new construction; retrofitting costs 10-50 times more |
| "The Fair Housing Act already ensures accessible housing" | Over 90% of covered multifamily buildings violate FHA design requirements; the law does not cover single-family homes |
| "People with disabilities can find accessible housing if they look hard enough" | Fewer than 5% of housing units are accessible, and the accessible units that exist are often unaffordable |
| "Group homes reduce property values" | Empirical research consistently finds no negative impact on neighboring property values |
| "The market will provide accessible housing when demand increases" | Market forces have failed to produce adequate accessible housing for decades; this is a well-documented market failure |
| "Home modifications are a personal responsibility" | The cost of modifications ($8,000-$50,000) exceeds the financial capacity of most people living on disability incomes |
| "Nursing homes are safer than community living" | Community living with appropriate supports produces equivalent or better outcomes; COVID-19 killed over 200,000 nursing home residents |
| "Section 8 vouchers solve the housing problem for disabled people" | 2-7 year waiting lists; many PHAs have closed their lists entirely; vouchers do not guarantee accessible units |
| "Visitability requirements are government overreach" | Building codes already mandate plumbing, electrical, fire safety, and energy standards; accessibility is equally a public welfare issue |
