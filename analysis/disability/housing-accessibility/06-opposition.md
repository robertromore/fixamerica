# Housing Accessibility for People with Disabilities: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to housing accessibility reforms comes from the homebuilding industry, local government officials, neighborhood groups, and fiscal conservatives. The homebuilding industry, through the National Association of Home Builders (NAHB), has been the most effective opponent of accessibility mandates, successfully blocking national visitability legislation for decades. Local opposition to group homes and supportive housing adds a geographic dimension, with NIMBY resistance translating into exclusionary zoning that functions as disability discrimination.

## Opposition Landscape

| Opponent | Primary Concern | Strategy | Influence Level |
|----------|----------------|----------|-----------------|
| National Association of Home Builders (NAHB) | Construction cost increases from accessibility mandates | Lobbying against visitability legislation; promoting voluntary standards | Very High |
| National Association of Realtors (NAR) | Property value concerns; regulation burden | Opposition to expanded FHA requirements; preference for voluntary approaches | Very High |
| Local governments/zoning boards | Loss of local control; NIMBY constituent pressure | Restrictive zoning, conditional use permits for group homes, spacing requirements | High |
| Neighborhood associations | Property values; character of neighborhood | Opposition to group homes, supportive housing, and multifamily development | Medium-High |

| Fiscal conservatives | Government spending on housing programs | Opposition to Section 811 expansion, voucher increases, and home modification funding | High |
| Small landlords | Modification costs; liability concerns | Resistance to reasonable modification and accommodation requests | Medium |

## Key Opposition Arguments

### Argument 1: Visitability Mandates Increase Housing Costs

**Claim**: Requiring accessibility features in all new single-family homes adds thousands of dollars to construction costs, making housing less affordable for everyone and pricing first-time homebuyers out of the market.

**Evidence cited**: NAHB estimates that visitability requirements add $2,000-$7,000 per unit in construction costs, depending on topography, lot configuration, and local building conditions. NAHB argues that sloped lots make zero-step entries especially expensive and that mandates are a one-size-fits-all approach to a problem that varies by geography.

**Reality**: Independent cost analyses have consistently found lower figures than NAHB claims. HUD estimated the cost of basic visitability features (zero-step entry, wider doorways, accessible half-bathroom) at $200-$1,000 per unit when incorporated into new construction from the design phase (HUD, *Fair Housing Act Design Manual*, 1997; updated 2013). Concrete Change estimates $200-$600 for basic visitability in typical new construction. Even NAHB's higher estimates represent less than 2% of the median new home price ($420,800 in 2024, Census Bureau). Retrofitting an existing home for accessibility costs $8,000-$50,000, making upfront accessibility far less expensive than after-the-fact modifications.

**Rebuttal**: Visitability features add minimal cost to new construction (under 1% by most independent estimates) and save far more in avoided retrofit costs. Moreover, an aging population means that most homebuyers will eventually benefit from accessibility features. Visitability is no different in principle from other building code requirements (plumbing, electrical, fire safety) that add cost but are universally accepted as necessary.

### Argument 2: The Market Should Determine Housing Features

**Claim**: If consumers wanted accessible housing, they would demand it, and builders would supply it. Government mandates are unnecessary because market forces will naturally produce accessible housing as the population ages and demand increases.

**Evidence cited**: NAHB points to the growth of "universal design" features in higher-end homes as evidence that the market is responding to demand. Some builders voluntarily offer accessibility packages as upgrades.

**Reality**: The market failure in accessible housing is well-documented. People with disabilities typically have lower incomes and less purchasing power than the general population, meaning their demand does not register strongly in housing markets. Most homebuyers do not anticipate future disability and do not pay a premium for accessibility features they do not currently need. By the time a person needs accessibility, they are living in an inaccessible home and face expensive retrofits. This is a classic market failure: the long-term social cost of inaccessible housing (retrofits, institutionalization, healthcare costs from falls) exceeds the minimal upfront cost of building accessible, but individuals making purchasing decisions at the time of construction do not internalize those future costs.

**Rebuttal**: No building code requirement exists because the market demanded it. Building codes exist precisely to address market failures in safety, health, and welfare. The market did not produce lead-free paint, fire sprinklers, or energy-efficient insulation; codes required them. Housing accessibility is a public welfare issue comparable to fire safety, and the market alone will not produce an adequate supply.

### Argument 3: Group Homes Reduce Property Values and Neighborhood Character

**Claim**: Group homes for people with disabilities, particularly psychiatric disabilities, reduce property values, increase traffic, and alter the character of single-family residential neighborhoods. Zoning restrictions are necessary to protect neighborhood quality of life.

**Evidence cited**: Neighborhood opponents frequently cite anecdotal concerns about property values, parking, noise, and "incompatible use." Some real estate appraisers have testified in local hearings that group homes reduce nearby property values.

**Reality**: Empirical research consistently shows that group homes do not reduce nearby property values. A comprehensive review of 19 studies by Galster and Williams found no statistically significant negative impact on property values from group homes for people with disabilities (*Housing Policy Debate*, 1994; confirmed in subsequent studies through 2020). Group homes are residential in character; they are homes, not commercial or institutional uses. Zoning restrictions on group homes often function as proxies for disability discrimination, violating the Fair Housing Act's prohibition on discriminatory land use decisions.

**Rebuttal**: The Fair Housing Act prohibits zoning decisions that discriminate against people with disabilities, and courts have consistently struck down zoning restrictions that single out group homes for special requirements (spacing, conditional use permits, enhanced notification) not applied to other residential uses. *City of Edmonds v. Oxford House, Inc.*, 514 U.S. 725 (1995). The property values argument is empirically unsupported and legally irrelevant as a justification for disability discrimination.

### Argument 4: FHA Compliance Is Unclear and Enforcement Is Unfair

**Claim**: The Fair Housing Act's design and construction requirements are vague, inconsistent with other building codes, and enforced through after-the-fact litigation that punishes builders who made good-faith efforts to comply. The HUD *Fair Housing Act Design Manual* is guidance, not regulation, and creates compliance uncertainty.

**Evidence cited**: Builders cite the existence of multiple safe harbors (HUD Guidelines, ANSI A117.1, ICC A117.1) as evidence of compliance confusion. Some builders and architects argue they followed local building codes, which do not reference FHA requirements, and were unaware of additional federal obligations.

**Reality**: The FHA's design requirements have been in effect since 1991, over 30 years. HUD published detailed technical guidance in 1997. Multiple safe harbors exist precisely to provide compliance flexibility. The argument that builders are unaware of a 35-year-old federal law is not a defense; it is evidence of an industry that has not made compliance a priority. Pre-construction plan review (as proposed by disability advocates) would resolve the complaint that enforcement is after-the-fact, but the industry has opposed this measure as well.

**Rebuttal**: The solution to compliance uncertainty is clearer standards and pre-construction review, not weakened enforcement. The building industry successfully complies with far more complex code requirements for structural, fire, and energy performance. FHA accessibility is less technically complex than many existing code requirements. The real issue is industry willingness, not technical difficulty.

### Argument 5: Institutionalization Is Safer Than Community Living

**Claim**: Nursing homes and other institutional settings provide 24-hour supervision, medical care, and safety that cannot be replicated in community housing. Moving people with significant disabilities into community settings risks their health and safety.

**Evidence cited**: The nursing home industry cites incidents of abuse, neglect, or medical emergencies in community settings as evidence that institutional care is safer. They argue that some individuals require a level of care that only institutional settings can provide.

**Reality**: Institutional settings have well-documented histories of abuse, neglect, and poor quality of care. COVID-19 killed more than 200,000 nursing home residents (CMS, 2023), demonstrating that congregate settings carry their own severe safety risks. Research consistently shows that people with disabilities report higher quality of life, greater satisfaction, and equivalent or better health outcomes in community settings when adequate support services are available (Lakin et al., *Mental Retardation*, 2005; Stancliffe et al., *Journal of Intellectual Disability Research*, 2011). The *Olmstead* decision affirmed that unnecessary institutionalization is a form of discrimination, not a form of care.

**Rebuttal**: Community living with appropriate supports is not less safe than institutional care; it is differently configured to respect autonomy while providing necessary assistance. The question is not whether institutions are safe (they often are not) but whether people with disabilities have the right to choose where they live, consistent with the ADA, *Olmstead*, and fundamental principles of self-determination.

### Argument 6: Federal Housing Programs Are Too Expensive

**Claim**: Expanding Section 811, increasing housing vouchers, and funding home modifications would cost billions of dollars that the federal government cannot afford. Housing programs should be reduced, not expanded, to control the national debt.

**Evidence cited**: Fiscal conservatives cite the federal deficit ($1.7 trillion in FY 2023) and total national debt ($34+ trillion) as reasons to reduce discretionary spending, including housing programs.

**Reality**: Federal housing assistance for people with disabilities is a small fraction of federal spending. The entire HUD budget ($72 billion in FY 2024) represents less than 1.1% of federal spending. Section 811 ($254 million) is 0.004% of federal spending. Meanwhile, Medicaid institutional care for people who could live in the community with housing support costs an estimated $10-$15 billion annually in avoidable institutional placements. Investing in accessible community housing saves money on the far more expensive alternative of institutionalization.

**Rebuttal**: Housing program expansion is an investment, not just an expense. Every dollar spent on Section 811 supportive housing reduces spending on emergency shelter, hospitalization, institutional care, and law enforcement interventions. Housing First programs for chronically homeless individuals with disabilities save $15,000-$30,000 per person per year in avoided public costs (Culhane et al., *Journal of Urban Economics*, 2002; updated analyses through 2023).

## Counter-Strategies

### Reframing Visitability as Universal Design

Connect visitability to the aging population. When AARP's 38 million members understand that visitability features help them age in place, the political calculus for NAHB opposition changes. Frame visitability as "future-proof housing" rather than "disability accommodation."

### Pre-Construction Plan Review

Advocate for local adoption of pre-construction FHA compliance review, resolving the industry's stated concern about after-the-fact enforcement while improving compliance rates. This shifts enforcement from litigation to prevention.

### Documenting Institutional Cost Comparisons

Commission and publicize rigorous cost comparisons between institutional placement and community housing with supports. When legislatures see that a $15,000/year housing voucher plus $20,000/year in services replaces $90,000/year in nursing home costs, the fiscal argument for housing investment becomes compelling.

### NIMBY Litigation

Continue Fair Housing Act enforcement against discriminatory zoning. Federal courts have consistently ruled against group home restrictions. Each successful case establishes precedent and raises the cost of discriminatory zoning for local governments.

### LIHTC Leverage

Advocate for strengthened accessibility requirements in LIHTC Qualified Allocation Plans. Since LIHTC is the largest production program for affordable housing, increasing the accessibility requirement from 5% to 10-15% of units would significantly increase accessible housing supply without new spending.
