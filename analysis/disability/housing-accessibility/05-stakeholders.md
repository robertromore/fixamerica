# Housing Accessibility for People with Disabilities: Stakeholders

## Overview

Housing accessibility involves a broad range of stakeholders spanning disability rights organizations, the housing industry, government agencies at all levels, advocacy coalitions, and the financial institutions that fund housing construction. The disability community's interests are frequently subordinated to those of better-resourced industry actors, particularly homebuilders and real estate developers, whose lobbying power exceeds that of disability advocacy organizations by orders of magnitude.

## Primary Stakeholders: People Affected

### People with Disabilities Seeking Housing

| Group | Population | Key Housing Concerns | Source |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|--------|
| People with mobility disabilities | 13.7 million | Zero-step entry, accessible bathrooms, single-floor living | CDC DHDS (2023) |
| People with cognitive/intellectual disabilities | 7.4 million | Supportive housing, group home availability, community integration | ACL (2023) |
| People with psychiatric disabilities | 14.2 million (serious mental illness) | Supportive housing, discrimination, homelessness | SAMHSA (2023) |
| Older adults with disabilities | 21.1 million (65+) | Aging in place, home modifications, affordable accessible senior housing | Census Bureau (2023) |
| People with sensory disabilities | 18.5 million | Visual/auditory accessibility features in housing (alarms, lighting, signage) | Census Bureau (2023) |
| SSI recipients seeking housing | 7.4 million individuals | Affordability gap; SSI income insufficient for market-rate rent in any state | SSA (2025) |
| People experiencing homelessness with disabilities | Estimated 250,000 | Housing First placement, accessible shelter space, permanent supportive housing | HUD AHAR (2024) |

### Family Members and Caregivers

An estimated 8-10 million family members provide housing or housing-related support to adults with disabilities. Many parents of adults with intellectual disabilities plan housing transitions, often providing housing in their own homes well into the adult child's middle age. Aging caregivers face the dual challenge of their own declining accessibility needs and planning for the housing future of their disabled family member.

## Coalition Dynamics

### Existing Coalitions

**Campaign for Housing and Community Development Funding**: National coalition of approximately 70 organizations advocating for HUD budget increases. Includes disability organizations (The Arc, NCIL), housing organizations (NLIHC, NAHRO), and civil rights groups. Effective at defending existing funding but has not achieved significant budget increases in recent years.

**Disability Housing Coalition**: Informal coalition coordinated through CCD Housing Task Force. Includes disability-specific and housing organizations advocating for Section 811 expansion, *Olmstead* compliance, and accessible housing supply. Limited resources but strong policy expertise.

### Potential Coalitions

| Coalition | Members | Common Interest | Obstacle |
|-----------|---------|-----------------|----------|
| Visitability Alliance | Disability orgs + AARP + aging organizations + universal design professionals | Basic accessibility in all new homes | NAHB lobbying against mandates |
| Community Integration Housing Coalition | Disability orgs + Medicaid reform advocates + anti-institutional advocates | Redirecting Medicaid from institutions to community housing | Nursing home industry opposition; state budget concerns |
| LIHTC Accessibility Coalition | Disability orgs + NLIHC + state HFAs + some developers | Strengthening accessibility in LIHTC allocations | Developer resistance to additional requirements |
| Zoning Reform Coalition | Disability orgs + YIMBY groups + fair housing organizations | Eliminating exclusionary zoning barriers to group homes and multifamily housing | NIMBYs and local government resistance |
| Home Modification Coalition | Disability orgs + AARP + home improvement industry + aging organizations | Federal home modification program | Funding competition; no natural industry champion |

## Power Analysis

### Most Influential Actors

1. **NAHB**: The homebuilding industry's primary lobbying organization has consistently blocked visitability legislation at the national level and in most states. Annual lobbying expenditures exceed $10 million.
2. **HUD**: Controls the largest federal housing programs serving people with disabilities (Section 8, Section 811, public housing). Budget decisions directly determine housing availability.
3. **CMS**: Medicaid HCBS policy shapes whether people with disabilities have the support services needed to live in community housing.
4. **State legislatures**: Control building codes, zoning enabling legislation, and state housing programs.
5. **AARP**: 38 million members provide significant political power on aging-in-place and universal design issues that overlap with disability housing.

### Least Powerful Actors with Greatest Stake

1. **SSI recipients**: The most housing-cost-burdened group in America, with incomes mathematically insufficient for market-rate housing in any state, yet limited political voice.
2. **People experiencing homelessness with disabilities**: Face the most acute housing crisis but are largely excluded from political processes.
3. **People with intellectual disabilities in aging-caregiver families**: Face impending housing crises as caregivers age and die, with inadequate planning and waiting lists for alternatives.
4. **People in nursing homes who could live in the community**: Often lack advocates and face institutional barriers to community transition.
