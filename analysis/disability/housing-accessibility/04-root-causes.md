# Housing Accessibility for People with Disabilities: Root Causes

## Overview

The housing accessibility crisis for people with disabilities results from interlocking structural failures in building codes, federal housing programs, real estate markets, zoning law, enforcement systems, and income supports. These are not independent problems; they form a system where each failure reinforces the others, making inaccessible, unaffordable housing the default condition for millions of Americans with disabilities.

## Systemic Analysis

### 1. Building Codes Do Not Require Accessible Single-Family Housing

The most fundamental root cause of housing inaccessibility is that building codes, both model codes and locally adopted codes, do not require accessibility features in single-family homes or small multifamily buildings (3 units or fewer). The International Residential Code (IRC), which governs the vast majority of housing construction in the United States, contains no accessibility requirements. The Fair Housing Act's design requirements apply only to multifamily housing of 4 or more units first occupied after March 13, 1991. The ADA does not apply to private residential housing at all.

This means that the approximately 80 million single-family homes in the United States, and millions of duplexes and triplexes, have no legal obligation to be accessible. Since single-family homes constitute approximately 65% of U.S. housing stock, the majority of the housing market is entirely outside the scope of any accessibility mandate.

The consequence is stark: accessibility must be retrofitted at significant cost ($8,000-$50,000 per unit), or people with disabilities must compete for the tiny fraction of multifamily units that were built to FHA standards and are actually compliant.

### 2. Fair Housing Act Design Requirements Are Widely Violated

Even within the scope of the FHA's coverage, compliance is extraordinarily poor. The National Fair Housing Alliance and other organizations have documented that over 90% of covered multifamily buildings contain at least one FHA design violation (NFHA, 2023). Common violations include:

- Step-up entrances to ground-floor units
- Bathroom doors too narrow for wheelchair passage
- Kitchens and bathrooms without adequate clear floor space
- Lack of reinforced walls for grab bar installation
- Inaccessible building entrances and common areas

The root causes of this noncompliance include:

- **Builder ignorance**: Many builders and architects are unfamiliar with FHA design requirements, which are distinct from ADA standards
- **No pre-construction review**: No federal, state, or local agency reviews residential plans for FHA compliance before construction permits are issued
- **Post-construction enforcement only**: Violations are discovered only after buildings are occupied, making remediation expensive and disruptive
- **Weak penalties**: FHA penalties for design violations have not been sufficient to change industry behavior
- **Professional liability gaps**: Architects and engineers face limited professional liability for FHA design failures compared to structural or fire safety failures

### 3. Federal Housing Programs Are Massively Underfunded

Federal housing programs serving people with disabilities, particularly Section 811 and Mainstream Vouchers, are funded at levels that serve only a small fraction of the eligible population:

| Program | Current Capacity | Estimated Unmet Need | Coverage Rate |
|---------|-----------------|---------------------|---------------|
| Section 811 | 35,000 units | 1+ million extremely low-income with disabilities | ~3.5% |
| Mainstream Vouchers | 62,000 vouchers | Hundreds of thousands non-elderly disabled | ~10-15% |
| Section 8 (disability share) | 1.3 million households | 3.5+ million eligible disabled households | ~37% |
| Public Housing (accessible) | ~50,000 accessible units | Unknown but substantial | Very low |

The result is waiting lists measured in years, with many public housing authorities closing their waiting lists entirely because they cannot serve current applicants, let alone new ones.

### 4. Medicaid's Institutional Bias Diverts Housing Resources

Medicaid is the largest single funder of long-term services and supports for people with disabilities, spending approximately $218 billion on disability-related services in FY 2024 (CMS Medicaid Actuarial Report). However, Medicaid's structure creates a powerful bias toward institutional placement:

- **Institutional care is a mandatory benefit**: States must cover nursing facility care for eligible individuals
- **Home and community-based services are optional**: States may, but are not required to, provide HCBS waivers
- **HCBS waiting lists**: An estimated 650,000 people are on HCBS waiting lists nationwide, with average waits of 2-5 years (KFF, 2024)
- **No housing benefit**: Medicaid cannot pay for room and board in community settings, creating a gap where Medicaid funds services but not the housing those services support

This structure means that a person who needs accessible housing plus supportive services may be placed in a nursing home at $80,000-$100,000/year because the $20,000/year in community services plus $12,000/year in housing assistance that would enable community living is not available. Institutional placement is the default because it is the funded option.

### 5. Income Supports Are Inadequate for Housing Costs

The SSI maximum federal benefit for an individual in 2025 is $967/month ($11,604/year). The national average fair market rent for a one-bedroom apartment is $1,167/month (HUD, FY 2025). This means that even if an SSI recipient spent 100% of their income on housing, they could not afford the average one-bedroom apartment in the United States.

This is not a new problem; it has worsened steadily. In 2000, the average one-bedroom FMR was 69% of SSI benefits; by 2024, it was 121% (NLIHC, *Priced Out*, 2024). The structural disconnect between disability income levels and housing costs has made independent housing mathematically impossible for millions of SSI recipients without additional subsidies that are available to a small minority.

### 6. Exclusionary Zoning Blocks Community-Based Housing

Local zoning laws create multiple barriers to housing for people with disabilities:

- **Single-family zoning**: Restricts the construction of multifamily housing (which has accessibility requirements under the FHA) in most residential areas
- **Group home restrictions**: Spacing requirements, conditional use permits, and occupancy limits effectively ban or restrict group homes for people with disabilities in many neighborhoods
- **Occupancy limits**: Defining "family" narrowly to exclude unrelated adults sharing a home, which disproportionately affects people with disabilities living in shared supportive housing
- **Accessory dwelling unit (ADU) restrictions**: Many jurisdictions prohibit or severely limit ADUs, which could provide accessible housing options on existing residential lots
- **Height and density restrictions**: Limit the construction of elevator-equipped multifamily buildings that are inherently more accessible than walk-up or single-family structures

### 7. Home Modification Programs Are Fragmented and Underfunded

For people with disabilities who already have housing that is not accessible, home modifications (ramps, grab bars, widened doorways, bathroom renovations, stair lifts) are essential but difficult to fund:

- No single federal home modification program exists; funding is scattered across HUD, VA, state programs, Medicaid waivers, and nonprofit organizations
- Medicaid can fund some modifications through HCBS waivers, but availability varies by state and waiting lists are long
- VA's Specially Adapted Housing (SAH) and Home Improvements and Structural Alterations (HISA) programs serve only veterans
- Most home modification programs require homeownership, excluding the 48% of people with disabilities who rent
- Cost estimates for basic modifications ($8,000-$15,000) exceed the savings capacity of most people living on disability incomes

## Who Benefits from the Status Quo

| Beneficiary | How They Benefit | Mechanism |
|-------------|-----------------|-----------|
| Homebuilders | Avoid additional construction costs from accessibility features | No building code accessibility requirements for single-family homes |
| Nursing home industry | Maintains occupancy and revenue from people who could live in community with accessible housing | Medicaid institutional bias; community housing shortage |
| Landlords of inaccessible properties | Avoid modification costs; maintain full rental market | Weak FHA enforcement; tenants bear modification costs |
| NIMBYs and property owners | Prevent group homes and supportive housing in their neighborhoods | Exclusionary zoning and land use restrictions |
| Real estate developers | Avoid FHA design compliance costs | No pre-construction plan review; weak enforcement |
| States with low HCBS investment | Avoid state spending on community housing services | Institutional care covered by mandatory Medicaid; HCBS is optional |

## Perverse Incentives

### Building More Inaccessibility Is Cheaper

Under current building codes, a builder who constructs an inaccessible single-family home faces no penalty and no additional cost. A builder who voluntarily includes accessibility features incurs additional costs ($200-$1,000 per unit for basic visitability in new construction, per HUD estimates) that may not be recouped in higher sale prices because the market does not yet adequately value accessibility. The economically rational choice under current rules is to build inaccessible housing.

### Institutionalization Is Financially Rewarded

Nursing homes receive guaranteed Medicaid reimbursement for every occupied bed. Community-based housing services, which are less expensive per person, compete for limited and optional HCBS funding. The financial incentive structure rewards institutional placement over community living.

### FHA Violations Are Cost-Effective

The expected cost of FHA design noncompliance (probability of enforcement action multiplied by likely penalty) is far lower than the cost of building to FHA standards for many developers. Without pre-construction review or systematic post-construction auditing, the rational economic calculation favors noncompliance.

### Voucher Shortages Suppress Demand

Because Section 8 vouchers for people with disabilities are severely limited, the apparent demand for accessible rental housing is artificially suppressed. Developers respond to market demand, and when people with disabilities cannot access vouchers, they do not enter the rental market as effective demand signals for accessible units.

## Interconnections with Other Root Causes

### Housing as a Social Determinant of Health

Inaccessible housing is a direct cause of poor health outcomes for people with disabilities. Falls in inaccessible homes are a leading cause of hospitalization among older adults and people with mobility disabilities. Isolation caused by being unable to leave inaccessible housing worsens mental health. Inaccessible kitchens and bathrooms compromise nutrition and hygiene. The healthcare access failures documented in the [Healthcare Access](../healthcare-access/01-overview.md) analysis are compounded by housing failures.

### Disability-Poverty Nexus

Housing unaffordability is both a consequence and a cause of disability-related poverty. People with disabilities who spend 50%+ of their income on housing have less for food, healthcare, transportation, and other necessities. This deprivation worsens health, reduces employment capacity, and deepens poverty, which further constrains housing options.

### Racial Disparities in Disability Housing

Housing accessibility barriers are compounded by racial discrimination in housing markets. Black and Hispanic people with disabilities face both disability and racial discrimination in housing searches, are more likely to live in neighborhoods with older, less accessible housing stock, and are less likely to be homeowners who can make modifications. The intersection of race and disability creates multiplicative housing barriers (National Fair Housing Alliance, 2023).
