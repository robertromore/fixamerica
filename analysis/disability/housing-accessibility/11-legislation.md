# Housing Accessibility for People with Disabilities: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Transforming housing accessibility for people with disabilities requires legislative action across multiple dimensions: building code reform to make accessible design the default, federal housing program expansion, Fair Housing Act enforcement strengthening, home modification funding, zoning reform, and Medicaid restructuring. This document provides draft legislation addressing each dimension, along with analysis of legal considerations, potential loopholes, and implementation challenges.

---

## Federal Legislation

### Bill 1: Accessible Housing for All Act (National Visitability Standard)

**Purpose**: Establish a national visitability standard requiring basic accessibility features in all new federally assisted residential construction, and incentivize state and local adoption of visitability building codes for all new residential construction.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Accessible Housing for All Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.
(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that—
    (1) fewer than 5 percent of United States housing units have
    basic accessibility features, despite 15.4 million households
    including a person with a disability;
    (2) the Fair Housing Act's design requirements apply only to
    multifamily housing of 4 or more units, leaving approximately
    80 million single-family homes and millions of small multifamily
    units outside any accessibility mandate;
    (3) the cost of basic visitability features (zero-step entrance,
    32-inch clear-passage doorways, and accessible half-bathroom on
    the main floor) is $200 to $1,000 per unit when incorporated
    into new construction, compared to $8,000 to $50,000 for
    post-construction modifications;
    (4) an aging population means that the majority of homebuyers
    will eventually need or benefit from accessibility features;
    (5) the United Kingdom, Australia, and other nations have
    adopted national residential accessibility standards with
    minimal cost impact on housing production; and
    (6) visitability in all new housing is essential to community
    integration for people with disabilities and to reducing the
    costs of institutionalization.
(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to—
    (1) require basic visitability features in all new federally
    assisted residential construction;
    (2) incentivize state and local governments to adopt
    visitability building codes for all new residential
    construction; and
    (3) promote universal design in the national housing stock.

SEC. 3. VISITABILITY REQUIREMENTS FOR FEDERALLY ASSISTED HOUSING.
(a) IN GENERAL.—Any residential dwelling unit constructed,
in whole or in part, with federal financial assistance on or
after the date that is 24 months after the date of enactment
of this Act shall include the following visitability features:
    (1) At least one entrance at grade level or accessible by a
    ramp with a slope not exceeding 1:12, on an accessible route
    from a driveway, sidewalk, or street.
    (2) All interior doorways on the main floor providing a
    minimum clear passage width of 32 inches.
    (3) At least one bathroom or half-bathroom on the main floor
    usable by a person in a wheelchair, with clear floor space of
    not less than 30 inches by 48 inches, a door providing 32-inch
    minimum clear passage, and reinforced walls for future grab bar
    installation.
    (4) An accessible route connecting the accessible entrance to
    the main-floor bathroom and at least one main-floor living area.
    (5) Light switches, electrical outlets, and environmental
    controls on the main floor mounted at heights accessible from
    a seated position (switches no higher than 48 inches, outlets
    no lower than 15 inches from the floor).
(b) FEDERAL FINANCIAL ASSISTANCE DEFINED.—For purposes of this
section, "federal financial assistance" includes—
    (1) any grant, loan, loan guarantee, or mortgage insurance
    provided by the Department of Housing and Urban Development,
    the Department of Veterans Affairs, the Department of
    Agriculture, or any other federal agency for residential
    construction;
    (2) any housing constructed, in whole or in part, with
    proceeds from the Low-Income Housing Tax Credit under
    section 42 of the Internal Revenue Code of 1986;
    (3) any housing constructed on land conveyed by the federal
    government below fair market value for residential use; and
    (4) any housing constructed by or for a public housing
    authority.
(c) TOPOGRAPHIC EXCEPTION.—The zero-step entrance requirement
in subsection (a)(1) may be waived for a specific dwelling unit
only where the enforcing authority determines that—
    (1) the existing topography of the specific lot makes a
    zero-step entrance infeasible without extraordinary measures;
    (2) no reasonable alternative site configuration exists; and
    (3) a waiver is granted in writing with documentation of the
    specific topographic conditions.
(d) ENFORCEMENT.—The Secretary of Housing and Urban Development
shall enforce compliance with this section as a condition of
federal financial assistance. Noncompliant construction shall
constitute a violation of section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act
of 1973 (29 U.S.C. 794).

SEC. 4. STATE AND LOCAL VISITABILITY INCENTIVE PROGRAM.
(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary of Housing and Urban Development
shall establish the Visitability Incentive Program to provide
grants to states, units of local government, and Indian tribes
that adopt and enforce visitability building codes meeting or
exceeding the requirements of section 3(a) for all new
residential construction, regardless of federal assistance.
(b) GRANT AMOUNTS.—
    (1) PLANNING GRANTS.—The Secretary may award planning grants
    of up to $500,000 per jurisdiction for development and
    adoption of visitability codes.
    (2) IMPLEMENTATION GRANTS.—The Secretary may award
    implementation grants of up to $2,000,000 per year per
    state and $500,000 per year per locality for enforcement
    of visitability codes.
(c) PRIORITY.—The Secretary shall give priority to jurisdictions
that—
    (1) adopt visitability codes covering all new residential
    construction, not limited to federally assisted housing;
    (2) provide training for builders and building inspectors
    on visitability standards; and
    (3) establish pre-construction plan review processes for
    accessibility compliance.
(d) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There is authorized to be
appropriated $100,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through
2033 to carry out this section.

SEC. 5. VISITABILITY TECHNICAL STANDARDS.
(a) IN GENERAL.—Not later than 18 months after the date of
enactment, the Access Board shall publish technical standards
for visitability in residential construction, consistent with
the requirements of section 3(a).
(b) SAFE HARBORS.—Compliance with the following shall constitute
compliance with the visitability requirements of this Act:
    (1) ICC A117.1 Type C unit standards.
    (2) The technical standards published by the Access Board
    under subsection (a).
    (3) Any state or local standard that meets or exceeds the
    requirements of section 3(a), as certified by the Access Board.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.
(a) FEDERAL ASSISTANCE REQUIREMENT.—Section 3 shall apply to
residential construction for which a building permit is issued
on or after the date that is 24 months after the date of
enactment.
(b) INCENTIVE PROGRAM.—Section 4 shall take effect on the date
of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Establishes visitability as a condition of federal housing assistance, covering LIHTC, FHA-insured mortgages, VA loans, USDA Rural Housing, and public housing. This is the broadest achievable coverage without requiring a standalone building code mandate.
- **Section 4**: Incentivizes state and local adoption of visitability codes for all new construction through competitive grants, creating a pathway to universal visitability.
- **Section 5**: Directs the Access Board to publish technical standards and establishes safe harbors, addressing the industry's stated concern about compliance clarity.
- **Section 6**: Provides a 24-month lead time for builders and code officials to prepare for implementation.

**Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| NAHB lobbying against any mandate | Limited to federally assisted housing; voluntary adoption incentivized for the rest |
| Cost objections | Independent studies show $200-$1,000 per unit; topographic exception for genuine hardship |
| State resistance to building code changes | Incentive program is voluntary; no preemption of less restrictive state codes |
| Enforcement capacity | Integrated into existing Section 504 enforcement framework |

**Refinements**:

- **Stronger version**: Mandate visitability for all new residential construction nationwide (preempt state building codes)
- **Compromise version**: Limit to HUD-assisted housing only; expand incentive program
- **Fallback version**: Executive order requiring visitability in HUD-assisted housing only

### Bill 2: Disability Housing Opportunity Act (Program Expansion)

**Purpose**: Expand Section 811 Supportive Housing, create new Mainstream Vouchers, establish a federal home modification grant program, and strengthen LIHTC accessibility requirements.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Disability Housing Opportunity Act".

SEC. 2. SECTION 811 EXPANSION.
(a) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—Section 811 of the
Cranston-Gonzalez National Affordable Housing Act (42 U.S.C.
8013) is amended by striking "$254,000,000" and inserting
"$1,000,000,000" in the authorization of appropriations.
(b) PROJECT RENTAL ASSISTANCE.—Of funds appropriated under
this section, not less than 60 percent shall be available for
Project Rental Assistance Contracts to integrate accessible
units for people with disabilities into mainstream affordable
housing developments.
(c) REPORTING.—The Secretary shall report annually to Congress
on—
    (1) the number of units produced, under construction, and
    in the pipeline;
    (2) the average time from application to occupancy;
    (3) the geographic distribution of units; and
    (4) tenant demographics, including disability type.

SEC. 3. MAINSTREAM VOUCHER EXPANSION.
(a) IN GENERAL.—The Secretary shall allocate not fewer than
100,000 additional Mainstream Vouchers for non-elderly people
with disabilities over fiscal years 2027 through 2032, at a
rate of not fewer than 20,000 per fiscal year.
(b) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There is authorized to
be appropriated $1,500,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027
through 2032 to carry out this section.
(c) MOBILITY.—Mainstream Vouchers issued under this section
shall be portable across public housing authority jurisdictions.
(d) ACCESSIBLE UNIT PREFERENCE.—Public housing authorities
administering Mainstream Vouchers shall provide preferences for
accessible units and shall assist voucher holders in identifying
accessible housing.

SEC. 4. FEDERAL HOME MODIFICATION GRANT PROGRAM.
(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary shall establish a Home
Accessibility Modification Program to provide grants for home
modifications for people with disabilities and older adults
with functional limitations.
(b) ELIGIBLE MODIFICATIONS.—Eligible modifications include—
    (1) ramps and zero-step entrances;
    (2) grab bars and handrails;
    (3) doorway widening;
    (4) bathroom modifications including roll-in showers,
    accessible fixtures, and adequate clear floor space;
    (5) kitchen modifications for wheelchair accessibility;
    (6) stair lifts and residential elevators;
    (7) smart home technology for environmental controls;
    (8) visual and auditory alert systems; and
    (9) other modifications that enable safe independent living
    as determined by the Secretary.
(c) GRANT AMOUNTS.—
    (1) BASIC MODIFICATIONS.—Grants of up to $25,000 per
    household for basic modifications.
    (2) COMPREHENSIVE MODIFICATIONS.—Grants of up to $50,000
    per household for comprehensive modifications, available
    upon demonstration that basic modifications are insufficient
    to meet the individual's functional needs.
(d) ELIGIBILITY.—Eligible applicants include—
    (1) individuals with disabilities, as defined in section 3
    of the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 (42 U.S.C.
    12102);
    (2) individuals age 62 or older with functional limitations
    affecting mobility, vision, hearing, or cognition; and
    (3) the income of the applicant household does not exceed
    80 percent of area median income.
(e) PRIORITY.—The Secretary shall give priority to applicants
who—
    (1) are at risk of institutionalization without home
    modifications;
    (2) have household income below 30 percent of area median
    income;
    (3) have been on waiting lists for accessible housing for
    more than 2 years; or
    (4) are transitioning from institutional to community settings.
(f) ADMINISTRATION.—The Secretary shall administer the program
through—
    (1) state housing finance agencies;
    (2) Centers for Independent Living; and
    (3) Area Agencies on Aging.
(g) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There is authorized to be
appropriated $750,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through
2033 to carry out this section.

SEC. 5. LIHTC ACCESSIBILITY ENHANCEMENT.
(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 42(d) of the Internal Revenue Code
of 1986 is amended by adding at the end the following:
    "(7) ENHANCED ACCESSIBILITY REQUIREMENTS.—
        (A) IN GENERAL.—A qualified low-income housing project
        shall include—
            (i) not fewer than 15 percent of units meeting the
            requirements for Type A units under ICC A117.1 (or
            successor standard) for mobility accessibility; and
            (ii) not fewer than 5 percent of units meeting the
            requirements for communication accessibility (visual
            alarms, accessible environmental controls, and other
            features for people with hearing or vision
            disabilities).
        (B) VISITABILITY.—All ground-floor units in a qualified
        low-income housing project shall meet the visitability
        requirements of ICC A117.1 Type C (or successor standard).
        (C) QUALIFIED ALLOCATION PLAN.—Each state housing credit
        agency shall include in its qualified allocation plan—
            (i) additional points for projects that exceed the
            minimum accessibility requirements of this paragraph;
            (ii) additional points for projects incorporating
            universal design features; and
            (iii) monitoring and compliance procedures for
            accessibility requirements.
        (D) EFFECTIVE DATE.—This paragraph shall apply to
        buildings placed in service after December 31, 2028.".

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.
Except as provided in section 5(a), this Act shall take effect
on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 2**: Quadruples Section 811 funding to $1 billion/year and prioritizes Project Rental Assistance Contracts that integrate disability housing into mainstream affordable developments rather than segregated settings.
- **Section 3**: Creates 100,000 new Mainstream Vouchers over 6 years, with portability and accessible unit assistance.
- **Section 4**: Establishes the first comprehensive federal home modification program, addressing the fragmented funding landscape with grants of up to $50,000 per household.
- **Section 5**: Triples the LIHTC accessibility requirement from 5% to 15% and adds visitability for all ground-floor units, leveraging the largest federal affordable housing production program.

**Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Appropriations may fall short of authorization | Phased authorization allows partial implementation |
| LIHTC developers resist accessibility increase | Phased effective date (2028); additional QAP points incentivize compliance |
| Home modification program administration | Leverages existing networks (state HFAs, CILs, AAAs) |
| Voucher utilization rates | Accessible unit preferences and search assistance improve utilization |

**Refinements**:

- **Stronger version**: Make LIHTC accessibility 20%; increase home modification grants to $75,000
- **Compromise version**: Phase LIHTC to 10% first; limit home modification program to $500 million
- **Fallback version**: Mainstream Voucher expansion only (lowest political resistance)

### Bill 3: Fair Housing Design Compliance Act

**Purpose**: Require pre-construction plan review for Fair Housing Act design compliance, increase penalties for design violations, and establish a dedicated FHA design enforcement unit at HUD.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Fair Housing Design Compliance Act".

SEC. 2. PRE-CONSTRUCTION PLAN REVIEW.
(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 804(f)(3) of the Fair Housing Act
(42 U.S.C. 3604(f)(3)) is amended by adding at the end the
following:
    "(D) PRE-CONSTRUCTION REVIEW.—
        (i) REQUIREMENT.—Any covered multifamily dwelling, as
        defined in subparagraph (C), for which a building permit
        is applied for on or after the date that is 24 months
        after the date of enactment of the Fair Housing Design
        Compliance Act, shall not receive a building permit unless
        the construction plans have been reviewed and certified
        for compliance with the design and construction
        requirements of this subsection.
        (ii) REVIEWING AUTHORITY.—Plan review and certification
        shall be conducted by—
            (I) the local building code official, if the locality
            has adopted a building code that incorporates the
            design requirements of this subsection and has trained
            inspectors; or
            (II) a qualified accessibility reviewer certified by
            the Access Board.
        (iii) CERTIFICATION.—Upon certification of compliance,
        the reviewing authority shall issue a certificate of
        accessibility compliance that shall accompany the building
        permit.
        (iv) POST-CONSTRUCTION INSPECTION.—The reviewing authority
        shall conduct a post-construction inspection of compliance
        with the certified plans prior to issuance of a certificate
        of occupancy.
        (v) COSTS.—The costs of plan review and post-construction
        inspection shall be borne by the applicant for the
        building permit, at rates not to exceed the costs of
        comparable building code reviews.".

SEC. 3. ENHANCED PENALTIES.
(a) DESIGN VIOLATIONS.—Section 812 of the Fair Housing Act
(42 U.S.C. 3612) is amended—
    (1) by increasing the maximum civil penalty for design and
    construction violations to $150,000 per building for a
    first violation and $300,000 per building for subsequent
    violations; and
    (2) by adding the following:
    "In assessing penalties for design and construction violations,
    the administrative law judge or court shall consider—
        (A) the number of noncompliant units;
        (B) the severity of the violations;
        (C) whether the respondent knew or should have known of
        the design requirements;
        (D) the respondent's prior history of violations; and
        (E) the respondent's good-faith efforts to achieve
        compliance, including whether the respondent obtained
        pre-construction plan review.".

SEC. 4. HUD DESIGN COMPLIANCE UNIT.
(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary shall establish within the
Office of Fair Housing and Equal Opportunity a Design and
Construction Compliance Unit.
(b) DUTIES.—The Unit shall—
    (1) conduct proactive compliance reviews of covered
    multifamily dwellings;
    (2) provide technical assistance to builders, architects,
    and local building officials on FHA design requirements;
    (3) develop and maintain training materials for building
    code officials on FHA accessibility review;
    (4) certify qualified accessibility reviewers under
    section 2;
    (5) maintain a public database of compliance review
    results; and
    (6) report annually to Congress on compliance rates and
    enforcement actions.
(c) STAFFING.—The Unit shall be staffed with not fewer than
50 accessibility specialists, including architects, engineers,
and fair housing investigators.
(d) PROACTIVE REVIEWS.—The Unit shall conduct not fewer than
500 proactive design compliance reviews per fiscal year
beginning in fiscal year 2029.
(e) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There is authorized to
be appropriated $50,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027
through 2033 for the establishment and operation of the Unit.

SEC. 5. BUILDER EDUCATION PROGRAM.
(a) IN GENERAL.—The Secretary, in coordination with the Access
Board, shall establish a builder education program on Fair
Housing Act design and construction requirements.
(b) ELEMENTS.—The program shall include—
    (1) free online training for builders, architects, and
    building inspectors;
    (2) regional workshops conducted in coordination with
    state and local building code organizations;
    (3) model curricula for architecture and construction
    management programs; and
    (4) updated technical guidance documents.
(c) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There is authorized to be
appropriated $15,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through
2033 for the program.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 2**: Transforms FHA design enforcement from reactive complaint-driven to proactive prevention through pre-construction plan review. Addresses the industry's stated concern about after-the-fact enforcement by catching violations before construction.
- **Section 3**: Increases penalties to levels that create meaningful deterrence. Per-building penalties (rather than per-unit) make penalties proportional to the scale of construction.
- **Section 4**: Creates dedicated capacity within HUD for proactive design compliance review and technical assistance.
- **Section 5**: Addresses builder ignorance through education, complementing enforcement with compliance support.

**Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Builders oppose pre-construction review | Review catches problems early, reducing costly post-construction remediation |
| Local building officials lack training | Federal technical assistance and reviewer certification program |
| Increased building permit processing time | Review is concurrent with other code reviews; 14-day processing standard |
| Appropriations for HUD unit | $50 million is modest relative to scope; offset by reduced litigation costs |

### Bill 4: Community Integration Housing Act (Zoning and Medicaid Reform)

**Purpose**: Eliminate exclusionary zoning barriers to disability housing and reform Medicaid to support community-based housing through mandatory HCBS and housing-related services.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Community Integration Housing Act".

SEC. 2. ELIMINATION OF DISCRIMINATORY ZONING.
(a) CDBG AND HOME CONDITIONS.—As a condition of receiving
funds under the Community Development Block Grant program
(42 U.S.C. 5301 et seq.) or the HOME Investment Partnerships
program (42 U.S.C. 12741 et seq.), a jurisdiction shall—
    (1) not impose spacing requirements, conditional use permits,
    or special notification requirements on group homes for
    persons with disabilities that are not imposed on other
    residential uses in the same zoning district;
    (2) not define "family" for zoning purposes in a manner that
    excludes unrelated persons with disabilities sharing a
    dwelling as a functional household;
    (3) permit supportive housing developments in any residential
    zoning district on the same terms as other multifamily
    residential uses;
    (4) not impose maximum occupancy limits on group homes for
    persons with disabilities that are more restrictive than
    limits applied to other residential uses; and
    (5) permit accessory dwelling units in all residential
    zoning districts.
(b) ENFORCEMENT.—The Secretary of Housing and Urban Development
shall—
    (1) review jurisdictions receiving CDBG or HOME funds for
    compliance with subsection (a) as part of the consolidated
    plan review process;
    (2) provide technical assistance to jurisdictions in
    achieving compliance; and
    (3) withhold funds from jurisdictions that fail to achieve
    compliance within 3 years of the effective date.
(c) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—Nothing in this section shall
limit the private right of action under the Fair Housing Act
(42 U.S.C. 3601 et seq.) for discriminatory zoning practices.

SEC. 3. MEDICAID HCBS MANDATORY BENEFIT.
(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 1915 of the Social Security Act
(42 U.S.C. 1396n) is amended to make home and community-based
services a mandatory benefit under the Medicaid program for
individuals who—
    (1) require the level of care provided in an institutional
    setting; and
    (2) can be served safely and appropriately in a
    community-based setting with HCBS.
(b) PHASE-IN.—
    (1) YEAR 1.—States shall provide HCBS to all individuals
    on HCBS waiting lists who have been waiting more than
    3 years.
    (2) YEAR 3.—States shall provide HCBS to all individuals
    on HCBS waiting lists who have been waiting more than
    1 year.
    (3) YEAR 5.—States shall eliminate HCBS waiting lists
    entirely.
(c) HOUSING-RELATED SERVICES.—Home and community-based services
under this section shall include—
    (1) housing transition services, including housing search,
    application assistance, and move-in support;
    (2) tenancy sustaining services, including landlord mediation,
    crisis intervention, and assistance with housing-related
    obligations;
    (3) environmental modifications to the individual's home,
    up to $10,000 per individual per year; and
    (4) housing-related collaborative activities, including
    coordination with housing authorities and providers.
(d) ENHANCED FEDERAL MATCHING.—The federal medical assistance
percentage for HCBS under this section shall be increased by
6 percentage points for the first 5 fiscal years after enactment,
to assist states in eliminating waiting lists.
(e) MAINTENANCE OF EFFORT.—States shall not reduce state funding
for HCBS below the level expended in the fiscal year preceding
enactment.

SEC. 4. OLMSTEAD COMPLIANCE INCENTIVE.
(a) IN GENERAL.—The Secretary of Health and Human Services
shall establish an Olmstead Housing Compliance Program to
provide grants to states for—
    (1) community transition of individuals from institutional
    settings to community housing;
    (2) development of accessible community housing options;
    (3) coordination between Medicaid, housing, and disability
    service agencies; and
    (4) data collection on institutional diversion and
    community placement outcomes.
(b) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There is authorized to be
appropriated $200,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through
2033 to carry out this section.

SEC. 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 2**: Uses federal housing funding as leverage to eliminate the most common discriminatory zoning practices against disability housing, including group home restrictions, discriminatory family definitions, and supportive housing barriers.
- **Section 3**: Makes Medicaid HCBS a mandatory benefit with a 5-year phase-in, eliminating the structural bias toward institutional placement that keeps hundreds of thousands of people waiting for community services.
- **Section 4**: Provides dedicated funding for *Olmstead* compliance, including community transition and accessible housing development.

**Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| State opposition to mandatory HCBS | Enhanced FMAP for 5 years; maintenance of effort prevents cost-shifting |
| Local government resistance to zoning conditions | 3-year compliance timeline; technical assistance; funds at stake |
| Nursing home industry opposition | Phase-in protects existing operations; focus on new admissions diversion |
| Cost of mandatory HCBS | Net savings from reduced institutional costs; enhanced FMAP offsets state burden |

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Visitability Act

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "[State] Visitability Act".

SECTION 2. VISITABILITY REQUIREMENTS.
(a) All new single-family and multifamily residential
construction for which a building permit is issued on or after
[date, 18 months after enactment] shall include the following
visitability features:
    (1) At least one entrance at grade level or accessible by
    a ramp with a slope not exceeding 1:12.
    (2) All interior doorways on the main floor providing a
    minimum clear passage width of 32 inches.
    (3) At least one bathroom or half-bathroom on the main floor
    with clear floor space of not less than 30 inches by 48 inches,
    a door providing 32-inch minimum clear passage, and reinforced
    walls for future grab bar installation.
(b) EXCEPTION.—The zero-step entrance requirement may be waived
where the local building official certifies in writing that the
specific lot topography makes a zero-step entrance infeasible.
(c) ENFORCEMENT.—Local building officials shall review
construction plans for visitability compliance as part of the
standard building permit process and shall inspect for
compliance prior to issuance of a certificate of occupancy.

SECTION 3. HOME MODIFICATION GRANT PROGRAM.
(a) There is established the [State] Home Accessibility
Modification Program to provide grants for home modifications
for people with disabilities and older adults.
(b) Grants shall not exceed $[25,000] per household.
(c) The program shall be administered by the [State housing
finance agency] in coordination with Centers for Independent
Living and Area Agencies on Aging.
(d) There is appropriated $[amount] annually for this program.

SECTION 4. LIHTC ACCESSIBILITY ENHANCEMENT.
The [State housing credit agency] shall amend its Qualified
Allocation Plan to—
    (1) require not fewer than 15 percent of units in LIHTC
    projects to meet Type A accessibility standards;
    (2) award additional points for projects exceeding the
    minimum accessibility requirement; and
    (3) award additional points for projects incorporating
    universal design features.

SECTION 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect on [date].
```

### Model State Group Home Protection Act

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "[State] Community Residential
Housing Protection Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.
(a) "Community residential home" means a dwelling in which
not more than [8] residents with disabilities live together
as a functional household, with or without live-in support
staff.

SECTION 3. ZONING PROTECTIONS.
(a) A community residential home shall be a permitted use in
any residential zoning district, subject only to the same
requirements applied to other single-family or multi-family
residential uses in the same district.
(b) No local government shall—
    (1) require a conditional use permit, special exception, or
    variance for a community residential home that is not
    required for other residential uses in the same district;
    (2) impose spacing or density requirements on community
    residential homes;
    (3) impose notification requirements on community residential
    homes that are not imposed on other residential uses; or
    (4) define "family" in a manner that excludes residents of
    a community residential home from residential zoning
    protection.

SECTION 4. ENFORCEMENT.
(a) Any person aggrieved by a violation of this Act may bring
a civil action for injunctive relief and reasonable attorney
fees.
(b) The [State Attorney General] may bring an action to enforce
this Act.

SECTION 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect on [date].
```

## Regulatory Framework

### HUD Visitability and Design Compliance Rulemaking

| Element | Proposed Approach |
|---------|-------------------|
| Standard | ICC A117.1 Type C visitability for federally assisted housing |
| Scope | All new residential construction receiving federal financial assistance |
| Compliance timeline | 24 months after final rule |

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Authority

- Congress has authority under the Commerce Clause to regulate residential construction standards, as housing markets are substantially connected to interstate commerce
- Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment authorizes legislation to enforce equal protection for people with disabilities (*Tennessee v. Lane*, 2004)
- Spending Clause supports conditions on federal housing assistance (CDBG, HOME, LIHTC, Section 811)
- Medicaid restructuring authorized under the Spending Clause and Commerce Clause

### Preemption

- Federal visitability requirements for federally assisted housing would preempt less protective state standards for covered construction
- States remain free to adopt broader visitability requirements for all new construction
- Zoning conditions do not preempt state zoning enabling acts but condition federal funds on compliance
- LIHTC accessibility requirements preempt less protective state QAP provisions

### Fair Housing Act Relationship

- New visitability requirements supplement, not replace, existing FHA design and construction requirements for covered multifamily housing
- Pre-construction plan review applies to FHA-covered construction specifically
- Group home zoning protections reinforce existing FHA protections with funding conditions

### Medicaid Authority

- Making HCBS mandatory requires amendment to Title XIX of the Social Security Act
- Enhanced FMAP authorized under existing Spending Clause authority
- Housing-related services authority builds on existing CMS guidance (2015, updated 2023)
- Maintenance of effort provisions prevent state cost-shifting

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Availability | Effectiveness |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| Section 504 enforcement (visitability) | Available; integrated with existing framework | High for federally assisted housing |
| CDBG/HOME fund withholding (zoning) | Available; strong leverage | High; loss of CDBG funds creates strong compliance incentive |
| FHA complaint and litigation (design) | Enhanced by pre-construction review | High with proactive enforcement unit |
| Private right of action (zoning) | Available under FHA and state law | Moderate; case-by-case |
| Medicaid plan review (HCBS) | Available through CMS oversight | High; state plan compliance is mandatory |
| LIHTC credit recapture (accessibility) | Available through IRS enforcement | High; loss of credits is severe penalty |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Topographic exception abuse | Builders claim topographic hardship for zero-step entries on lots where alternatives exist | High |
| Federal assistance avoidance | Builders forgo federal assistance to avoid visitability requirements | Medium |
| LIHTC accessibility clustering | Developers concentrate accessible units in least desirable locations within a project | Medium |
| Zoning condition workarounds | Jurisdictions adopt facially neutral restrictions that have discriminatory effect on disability housing | High |
| HCBS waiting list manipulation | States narrow eligibility criteria to technically eliminate "waiting lists" while restricting access | High |
| Pre-construction review rubber-stamping | Local officials issue accessibility certifications without meaningful review | Medium |
| Home modification program delays | Administrative complexity delays grant processing, leaving applicants in unsafe housing for months | Medium |
| Group home definition narrowing | Jurisdictions narrowly define "community residential home" to exclude certain disability populations | Medium |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|-----------|
| Visitability limited to federally assisted housing | Majority of new construction (private, non-assisted) not covered by federal mandate | Political feasibility; limits of federal authority over private construction |
| Home modification grants capped at $50,000 | Some complex modifications (elevators, comprehensive renovations) exceed cap | Budget constraints |
| LIHTC 15% accessibility may be insufficient | Still leaves 85% of affordable units without full accessibility | Developer resistance to higher percentages |
| 5-year HCBS phase-in | People on waiting lists continue waiting years for community services | State capacity and fiscal constraints |
| No rent-to-income cap reform | SSI income remains insufficient for market rent even with expanded vouchers | SSI reform is separate legislative effort |
| Pre-construction review applies only to FHA-covered buildings | Single-family and small multifamily (1-3 units) not covered by FHA design requirements | Scope of FHA coverage |
| Enhanced FMAP expires after 5 years | States may reduce HCBS investment when enhanced matching ends | Budget scoring and fiscal constraints |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Topographic exception abuse**: Require third-party certification by licensed surveyor or civil engineer; HUD to audit a random sample of waivers annually; revoke builder's good-faith status for pattern of unsupported waiver claims
2. **Federal assistance avoidance**: Track visitability adoption in non-assisted housing through Census AHS data; expand incentive program to make non-assisted visitability adoption attractive; long-term strategy: build political support for universal visitability mandate
3. **LIHTC accessibility clustering**: Require accessible units to be distributed proportionally across building types, floor plans, and locations within a project; include in QAP scoring
4. **Zoning condition workarounds**: HUD to publish guidance on facially neutral zoning practices that constitute disability discrimination; maintain FHA private right of action for pattern-or-practice claims
5. **HCBS waiting list manipulation**: Define "waiting list" to include all eligible individuals not receiving services, regardless of administrative categorization; CMS to audit state eligibility determinations
6. **Pre-construction review rubber-stamping**: Access Board certification required for reviewers; HUD to audit local review quality through random sampling of certified plans; decertify reviewers with pattern of inadequate reviews
7. **Home modification delays**: Establish 90-day processing standard; emergency modification pathway (14 days) for safety-critical modifications; allow provisional approvals pending full documentation
8. **Group home definition narrowing**: Federal legislation defines protected categories broadly; DOJ enforcement guidance on covered populations; pattern-or-practice enforcement against jurisdictions with narrow definitions

### General Implementation Concerns

- **Industry opposition**: Pair every mandate with technical assistance and compliance support; phase implementation to allow adjustment
- **State fiscal constraints**: Enhanced FMAP for HCBS transition; maintenance of effort to prevent backsliding; document long-term cost savings from community placement
- **Enforcement capacity**: Build HUD Design Compliance Unit gradually over 3 years; leverage state and local building officials through training and certification
- **Data infrastructure**: National accessible housing registry (from Bill 1 of 07-solutions.md) essential for measuring progress and targeting enforcement
- **Political durability**: Embed requirements in authorizing statutes rather than regulations; build bipartisan coalitions through aging-in-place framing
- **Judicial interpretation**: Clear legislative findings and statements of purpose to guide judicial interpretation; avoid ambiguity in key definitions
