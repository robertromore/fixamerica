# Housing Accessibility for People with Disabilities: Current State

## Overview of Current Conditions

The U.S. housing system fails people with disabilities on two interconnected dimensions: accessibility and affordability. The vast majority of the housing stock is physically inaccessible, and the small fraction that is accessible is often unaffordable for people with disability-level incomes. Federal housing programs targeted at disability populations are massively underfunded relative to need, with waiting lists measured in years. Fair Housing Act design requirements are widely violated, and enforcement has been insufficient to achieve compliance at scale. The result is a system where people with disabilities have severely constrained housing choices, leading to institutionalization, homelessness, unsafe living conditions, and dependence on informal family support.

## Trends

### Positive Developments

- **Aging population driving universal design interest**: As baby boomers age, demand for accessible housing features is increasing, creating market incentives for builders to incorporate universal design. AARP surveys show 77% of adults 50+ want to age in place, driving interest in accessible design (AARP, 2024).
- **Section 811 Project Rental Assistance reform**: HUD's revised Section 811 program (post-2010 Frank Melville Act) integrates disability units into mainstream affordable housing rather than segregated settings, improving community integration.
- **Smart home technology**: Voice-activated controls, automated door openers, smart lighting, and other technologies are reducing the cost and complexity of making homes functionally accessible for people with diverse disabilities.
- **Housing First success**: Evidence-based Housing First programs demonstrating effectiveness for chronically homeless people with disabilities, with housing retention rates of 80-85% (National Alliance to End Homelessness, 2024).

### Negative Trends

- **Worsening affordability crisis**: Housing costs have increased faster than disability incomes. Between 2019 and 2024, national median rent increased 25% while SSI benefits increased 18% (NLIHC, 2024; SSA, 2024).
- **Public housing deterioration**: Estimated $70 billion capital needs backlog in public housing, with accessible units in disrepair (HUD Capital Needs Assessment, 2022).
- **FHA compliance stagnation**: Despite decades of litigation, new multifamily construction continues to violate Fair Housing Act design requirements at high rates (NFHA, 2023).
- **Waiting list expansion**: Section 8 waiting lists have grown in most metropolitan areas; many PHAs have closed their waiting lists entirely (CBPP, 2024).
- **Zoning restrictions on group homes**: Local governments continue to enact or enforce occupancy limits, spacing requirements, and conditional use permits that function as barriers to community-based housing for people with disabilities (National Fair Housing Alliance, 2023).

## Current Political Context

Housing accessibility for people with disabilities receives limited attention in mainstream housing policy debates, which focus on affordability, homelessness, and supply constraints. Key political dynamics as of 2025:

- Federal housing budget faces pressure from competing priorities; HUD discretionary funding has declined in real terms over the past decade
- Bipartisan interest in addressing homelessness creates potential openings for Housing First programs that serve people with disabilities
- State-level visitability legislation has advanced in a few states but faces homebuilder opposition in most
- *Olmstead* enforcement by DOJ has varied by administration; community integration remains a cross-administration priority with inconsistent resourcing
- LIHTC reauthorization debates offer opportunities to strengthen accessibility requirements in the largest federal affordable housing production program
