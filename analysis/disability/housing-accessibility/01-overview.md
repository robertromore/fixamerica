# Housing Accessibility for People with Disabilities: Overview

## Executive Summary

Accessible, affordable housing is a prerequisite for community integration, independence, and health for people with disabilities, yet the United States faces a severe and worsening shortage. An estimated 15.4 million households include a person with a disability, but fewer than 5% of the nation's housing stock is accessible to people with mobility disabilities, and an even smaller fraction accommodates the needs of people with sensory or cognitive disabilities. The result is that millions of Americans with disabilities live in housing that is physically dangerous, functionally inadequate, or so expensive that it consumes the majority of their income.

The legal framework for housing accessibility includes the Fair Housing Act (FHA) Amendments of 1988, which require accessible design in multifamily housing built after 1991; Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act, which requires accessibility in federally funded housing; and the ADA, which covers public housing programs. Despite these laws, compliance is poor, enforcement is weak, and the scope of coverage leaves the vast majority of the housing stock, particularly single-family homes and small multifamily buildings, outside any accessibility mandate.

The intersection of disability and housing affordability creates compounding crises. People with disabilities have median household incomes roughly 60% of the non-disabled population. SSI beneficiaries receiving the maximum federal benefit cannot afford a modest one-bedroom apartment at fair market rent in any state. When the accessible housing that exists is also unaffordable, and the affordable housing that exists is also inaccessible, people with disabilities face a double barrier that pushes them toward institutionalization, homelessness, or dependence on family members who may themselves lack resources.

Addressing housing accessibility requires reforms spanning building codes, federal housing programs, zoning law, home modification funding, anti-discrimination enforcement, and the fundamental relationship between disability income supports and housing costs.

## Core Tensions

### Individual Rights vs. Development Costs

The homebuilding industry argues that mandatory accessibility features increase construction costs and housing prices, ultimately reducing housing affordability. Disability advocates counter that accessible design adds minimal cost when incorporated from the start (estimated 0.5-1% of construction costs for basic visitability features) and that inaccessible housing imposes far greater costs through home modifications, institutionalization, and lost economic participation. This tension manifests in every legislative debate over building code reform and visitability requirements.

### Fair Housing Enforcement vs. Housing Production

Some housing developers and local officials argue that aggressive Fair Housing Act enforcement, particularly design and construction requirements, discourages housing production at a time of severe housing shortages. Disability advocates respond that producing housing that excludes people with disabilities is not solving the housing shortage for those who need accessible units, and that waivers and exemptions undermine civil rights protections.

### Community Integration vs. Neighborhood Opposition

The *Olmstead* decision established the right to community-based living, but people with disabilities seeking to live in residential neighborhoods face opposition from neighbors and local zoning authorities. Group homes, supportive housing developments, and congregate settings for people with psychiatric or intellectual disabilities frequently encounter NIMBY resistance, restrictive zoning, and occupancy limits that function as proxies for disability discrimination.

### Modification Rights vs. Property Owner Interests

The Fair Housing Act gives tenants with disabilities the right to make reasonable modifications at their own expense and to request reasonable accommodations in rules and policies. Landlords resist modifications that they perceive as reducing property values or creating liability, and the ambiguity of "reasonable" in both modification and accommodation contexts generates persistent conflict and litigation.

## Vision of Success

A housing system that serves people with disabilities would be one where accessible design is the norm, not the exception, in new construction. Visitability features (zero-step entrance, wide doorways, accessible bathroom on the main floor) would be standard in all housing, just as plumbing and electrical systems are standard. People with disabilities would have the same range of housing choices as anyone else: homeownership, rental apartments, urban, suburban, or rural locations, living alone or with others. Federal housing programs would be funded at levels that eliminate waiting lists measured in years. Home modification grants would be available without lengthy applications or financial means-testing for basic safety modifications. Landlords would comply with reasonable accommodation requests as routine business practice. Group homes and supportive housing would be welcomed in residential neighborhoods as valued community assets. No one would live in a nursing home or institution because of a lack of accessible community housing options. The connection between disability, poverty, and homelessness would be broken through housing supports that recognize disability as a structural condition requiring structural responses.
