# Housing Accessibility for People with Disabilities: History

## Overview

The history of housing for people with disabilities in the United States is inseparable from the broader history of institutionalization, segregation, and the disability rights movement. For most of American history, people with significant disabilities had two housing options: family homes (almost never accessible) or institutions. The disability rights movement fought to establish community living as both a right and a practical reality, but the infrastructure to make community living viable, accessible and affordable housing, has never been adequately built.

---

## Era 1: Institutionalization as Housing (Pre-1960s)

### The Institutional Default

Before the disability rights movement, housing for people with disabilities was synonymous with institutional placement. State institutions for people with intellectual disabilities, psychiatric hospitals for people with mental illness, and veterans' homes for disabled veterans served as the default "housing" for millions of Americans.

- **1842**: The Perkins Institution in Massachusetts establishes residential facilities for blind students, one of the earliest disability-specific residential programs
- **1848**: First state institution for people with intellectual disabilities opens in Massachusetts
- **1854**: President Franklin Pierce vetoes the "Bill for the Benefit of the Indigent Insane," rejecting federal responsibility for disability housing and care
- **1935**: Social Security Act creates Old-Age Insurance but excludes most people with disabilities from social insurance; no housing provisions for disabled individuals
- **1937**: United States Housing Act creates public housing program with no accessibility requirements
- **1949**: Housing Act declares goal of "a decent home and suitable living environment for every American family" but does not address disability accessibility
- **1956**: Social Security Disability Insurance created; provides income but no housing supports

### Housing Implications

People with disabilities who lived in the community depended on family support in homes designed without consideration of accessibility needs. Multi-story homes without elevators, narrow doorways, bathtubs without grab bars, and stepped entrances were universal. No building code or regulation addressed residential accessibility. People who could not navigate these environments were candidates for institutionalization, regardless of whether they could live independently with appropriate housing modifications.

## Era 2: Emergence of Community Living (1960s-1980s)

### Deinstitutionalization and the Housing Gap

- **1963**: Community Mental Health Centers Act begins shifting mental health care from institutions to communities, but makes no provision for accessible community housing
- **1965**: Medicare and Medicaid enacted; Medicaid covers institutional care but not community housing, creating a structural bias toward institutionalization
- **1968**: Fair Housing Act enacted (Title VIII of the Civil Rights Act); prohibits housing discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, and national origin but *does not include disability*
- **1973**: Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act prohibits disability discrimination in federally funded programs, including public housing
- **1975**: Developmentally Disabled Assistance and Bill of Rights Act affirms rights to community living
- **1978**: Section 202 program expanded to include disabled individuals (not just elderly), creating first federal housing production program for people with disabilities
- **1979**: HUD issues Section 504 regulations requiring 5% of federally funded housing units to be accessible for mobility disabilities and 2% for sensory disabilities

### The Independent Living Movement

The independent living movement, originating in Berkeley, California in the early 1970s, challenged the assumption that people with significant disabilities needed institutional care. Centers for Independent Living (CILs) provided peer support, advocacy, and practical assistance, including help finding and modifying community housing. The movement demonstrated that housing accessibility, not the severity of disability, determined whether community living was possible.

However, the movement also revealed the depth of the housing crisis. Accessible housing was nearly nonexistent, home modification funding was minimal, and no building code required accessible design in new residential construction.

## Era 3: Fair Housing and ADA (1988-2000)

### Fair Housing Act Amendments of 1988

The Fair Housing Amendments Act of 1988 was the most significant housing accessibility law in U.S. history:

- **1988**: Congress amends the Fair Housing Act to add disability (and familial status) as protected classes
- **Key provisions**:
    - Prohibits discrimination in sale, rental, and financing based on disability
    - Requires covered multifamily housing (4+ units, first occupied after March 13, 1991) to meet seven accessibility design requirements: accessible building entrance on accessible route, accessible common areas, usable doors (32-inch clear passage), accessible routes into and through units, accessible light switches/thermostats/outlets, reinforced bathroom walls for grab bars, and usable kitchens and bathrooms
    - Establishes right to reasonable modifications (at tenant's expense) and reasonable accommodations in rules and policies
    - Protects people in recovery from substance use disorders
    - Defines disability broadly, consistent with Rehabilitation Act

### ADA and Olmstead

- **1990**: ADA enacted; Title II requires public housing authorities to make programs accessible; Title III covers housing-related commercial activities
- **1992**: Section 811 Supportive Housing for Persons with Disabilities program created, establishing the first dedicated federal program for supportive housing for extremely low-income people with disabilities
- **1997**: HUD publishes *Fair Housing Act Design Manual* providing detailed technical guidance for FHA accessibility requirements
- **1999**: *Olmstead v. L.C.*, 527 U.S. 581 (1999), establishes that unnecessary institutionalization violates the ADA; housing becomes central to *Olmstead* compliance because people cannot leave institutions without community housing options

### Early Enforcement and Compliance Gaps

Despite the FHA's design requirements, compliance was poor from the beginning. The homebuilding industry objected to costs (though HUD estimated the requirements added only $200-$1,000 per unit for new construction), and the FHA's complaint-driven enforcement model meant that violations were rarely detected. The National Fair Housing Alliance and other organizations began systematic testing of new multifamily buildings in the mid-1990s and found noncompliance rates exceeding 90%.

## Era 4: Modern Developments (2000-Present)

### Program Reforms

- **2003**: HUD issues *Olmstead* guidance directing public housing authorities to promote community integration
- **2010**: Frank Melville Supportive Housing Investment Act reforms Section 811 to integrate disability housing into mainstream affordable housing developments through Project Rental Assistance Contracts
- **2010**: ACA's Community First Choice and Balancing Incentive Programs promote community living but provide no direct housing production funding
- **2012**: HUD adopts Equal Access Rule prohibiting discrimination against LGBTQ+ individuals in HUD programs, including people with disabilities who are also LGBTQ+
- **2015**: HUD publishes Affirmatively Furthering Fair Housing (AFFH) rule requiring jurisdictions to address housing accessibility in fair housing planning
- **2017**: AFFH rule suspended by Trump administration; partially reinstated in 2021; revised rule finalized in 2024
- **2020**: CARES Act provides emergency housing assistance; people with disabilities particularly vulnerable to COVID-19 housing insecurity
- **2024**: HUD updates Section 504 regulations for federally assisted housing

### Visitability Movement

The visitability movement, led by Concrete Change founder Eleanor Smith beginning in the late 1980s, advocated for basic accessibility features (zero-step entrance, wide doorways, accessible half-bathroom) in all new single-family homes. Unlike full ADA accessibility, visitability is a modest standard that makes homes navigable for wheelchair users without requiring full accessibility of all rooms and features.

- **2002**: Pima County, Arizona becomes the first jurisdiction to require visitability in publicly funded single-family homes
- **2003-present**: Multiple cities (Atlanta, Chicago, Bolingbrook IL, and others) adopt visitability ordinances, but no state has adopted a comprehensive visitability requirement for all new construction
- **2022**: International Code Council (ICC) A117.1 standard updated to include Type C "visitable" units, providing a model code provision

### COVID-19 Impact

The pandemic intensified housing accessibility challenges:

- Congregate housing settings (group homes, assisted living, nursing homes) became COVID-19 hotspots, renewing urgency around community-based housing alternatives
- Stay-at-home orders made inaccessible housing more dangerous as people with disabilities were confined to spaces not designed for their needs
- Eviction moratoria temporarily protected tenants, including disabled tenants, but expiration led to increased displacement
- Remote work opportunities created new possibilities for some people with disabilities to live in less urban, more affordable areas with accessible housing

## Lessons from History

### What Has Worked

- **Civil rights framework**: Adding disability to the Fair Housing Act established enforceable rights that, while imperfectly implemented, created legal tools to challenge housing discrimination
- **Dedicated housing programs**: Section 811 and Mainstream Voucher programs demonstrate that targeted resources can produce accessible, affordable housing when funded
- **Visitability ordinances**: Where enacted, visitability laws have produced accessible housing at minimal additional cost, proving that the construction industry's cost objections are overstated
- **Housing First approaches**: Evidence-based models demonstrating that providing housing to homeless people with disabilities is more effective and cost-efficient than treatment-first approaches (Tsemberis, *Pathways to Housing*, 2004)

### What Has Failed

- **Complaint-driven enforcement**: FHA design requirement compliance remains below 10% after 35+ years, demonstrating that complaint-driven enforcement cannot achieve systemic change in construction practices
- **Voluntary accessibility**: Without mandates, the homebuilding industry does not build accessible housing. Market forces alone have not produced adequate accessible housing supply
- **Institutional bias in Medicaid**: Medicaid's structural bias toward institutional care (mandatory institutional coverage vs. optional HCBS) has diverted resources from community housing for decades
- **Underfunded housing programs**: Section 811 serves 35,000 households nationally against an estimated need of 1+ million; the gap has grown rather than closed

### Recurring Patterns

1. **Legal rights without implementation infrastructure**: Every major housing law (Section 504, FHA, ADA, *Olmstead*) has been followed by prolonged implementation failures due to inadequate enforcement, funding, and political will
2. **Production vs. access tension**: Housing debates consistently pit production advocates against accessibility advocates, despite the fact that accessible production is achievable at minimal cost
3. **Local resistance to community integration**: NIMBY opposition to disability housing has been a constant throughout the community living era, requiring ongoing litigation and enforcement
4. **Income-housing gap widening**: The gap between disability incomes and housing costs has grown consistently, making accessible affordable housing an increasingly unattainable goal without structural reform
