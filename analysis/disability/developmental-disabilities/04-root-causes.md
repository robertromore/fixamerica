# Developmental Disabilities: Root Causes

## Overview

The failures of the American developmental disabilities system are not accidents or resource constraints alone. They reflect deeply embedded structural choices in how the nation finances services, organizes government responsibility, values care work, and conceptualizes disability. Understanding these root causes is essential because surface-level reforms that fail to address underlying structures will produce temporary improvements at best.

---

## Structural Root Causes

### 1. Medicaid's Institutional Bias

**The Problem**: Medicaid was designed around institutional care. Institutional services (ICF/IDD, nursing facilities) are mandatory benefits that states must provide to anyone who qualifies. Home and community-based services remain optional, available only through waivers that states may limit by capacity, geography, or budget.

**How This Creates Harm**

- States must fund an institutional bed for anyone who qualifies but can maintain years-long waiting lists for community services
- The waiver structure requires states to demonstrate "budget neutrality," meaning HCBS cannot cost more per person than institutional care would
- CMS approval processes for waiver amendments or new waivers can take 12-18 months
- States have financial incentives to fill institutional beds because the federal match rate (FMAP) for institutional care is often more favorable than for HCBS

**Why It Persists**

- Restructuring Medicaid to make HCBS a mandatory benefit would require legislation (the HCBS Access Act has been introduced but not enacted)
- Congressional Budget Office scoring treats HCBS expansion as new spending even when it would replace more expensive institutional care
- Institutional providers (nursing home chains, ICF/IDD operators) lobby against policies that shift funding to community settings
- Medicaid's structure reflects 1965 assumptions that long-term care means institutional care

### 2. Chronic Underfunding of Community Infrastructure

**The Problem**: The community-based service system was built incrementally on top of an institutional system, never receiving the sustained investment needed to serve the full population of people who need support.

**How This Creates Harm**

- HCBS waiver capacity is determined by state appropriations, not by population need
- The DD Act (State DD Councils, P&A, UCEDDs) has been essentially flat-funded since the early 2000s, losing approximately 25% of purchasing power to inflation
- Vocational Rehabilitation funding has not kept pace with the growing population seeking services
- Housing subsidies (Section 811, HUD programs) serve a small fraction of eligible people with disabilities

**Why It Persists**

- People with developmental disabilities are a relatively small and politically marginalized constituency
- DD services compete with other Medicaid populations (elderly, physically disabled) for limited LTSS funding
- The diffuse nature of community services makes them less visible to policymakers than institutions
- Anti-spending political dynamics affect discretionary programs disproportionately

### 3. The Direct Support Workforce as Market Failure

**The Problem**: The labor market systematically undervalues care work, and the DSP workforce exemplifies this failure. DSPs perform complex, demanding work, including personal care, behavior support, medication administration, community integration, and crisis response, yet earn wages that are barely above (and sometimes below) minimum wage.

**How This Creates Harm**

- Low wages drive chronic turnover (43-54% annually) and vacancy rates (13-17%)
- Turnover disrupts continuity of care, increasing risk of harm and reducing quality of life
- Provider agencies cannot recruit sufficient staff, leading to service reductions, program closures, and new admissions being refused
- DSPs themselves experience poverty, food insecurity, and lack of healthcare while providing essential services

**Why It Persists**

- Medicaid reimbursement rates set DSP wage ceilings: providers cannot pay more than Medicaid reimburses
- Rate-setting processes in most states are opaque, infrequent, and disconnected from labor market data
- The workforce is predominantly female (approximately 72%) and disproportionately people of color (approximately 52%), reflecting broader patterns of devaluing care work performed by women and minorities
- DSP work lacks professional recognition: no national credentialing, no career ladder in most states
- The care work economy generates profit for intermediaries (provider agencies, managed care organizations) rather than direct workers

### 4. Fragmented Federal and State Responsibility

**The Problem**: Responsibility for developmental disability services is fragmented across multiple federal agencies, state departments, and local entities with no single coordinating authority.

**Federal Fragmentation**

| Agency | Role | Coordination |
|--------|------|-------------- |
| CMS (HHS) | Medicaid HCBS waivers, ICF/IDD | Limited coordination with education and employment |
| ACL (HHS) | DD Act programs | No authority over Medicaid |
| DOL | Vocational rehabilitation (RSA) | Separate from Medicaid service system |
| DOEd | IDEA, transition services | Ends at age 21; poor handoff to adult systems |
| SSA | SSI/SSDI | Separate eligibility; benefits cliffs discourage employment |
| HUD | Housing subsidies | Separate application and waiting lists |
| DOJ | ADA enforcement (*Olmstead*) | Enforcement, not service delivery |

**State Fragmentation**

- In most states, IDD services are administered by a state DD agency, but authority is split between Medicaid agencies, education departments, vocational rehabilitation agencies, and housing authorities
- No single state agency has comprehensive authority over the full range of services a person with IDD needs
- County-administered systems (e.g., California regional centers) add another layer of variation

### 5. The Transition Cliff as Systemic Design Failure

**The Problem**: The transition from entitlement-based educational services to capacity-limited adult services is not merely a gap in services; it is a predictable consequence of how the two systems were designed without regard for each other.

**How This Creates Harm**

- IDEA guarantees services through age 21; adult DD services have no entitlement, only capped waiver slots
- School-based transition planning (required by IDEA) often fails to connect to available adult services
- Families are frequently unaware of the need to apply for adult services years before school exit
- State agencies managing waiting lists cannot predict when slots will become available
- Young adults who lose services often experience skill regression that is difficult and costly to reverse

**Why It Persists**

- IDEA and Medicaid were designed by different congressional committees for different purposes
- No federal mandate requires states to ensure continuity between educational and adult services
- Adult service systems lack capacity to absorb the annual cohort of youth aging out of schools
- Transition planning is treated as a school responsibility, but schools lack authority over adult systems

## Ideological Root Causes

### 6. Residual Paternalism and the Competence Question

Despite the self-advocacy movement's gains, dominant cultural assumptions about people with intellectual disabilities continue to shape policy and practice.

**How This Creates Harm**

- Guardianship remains the default response to cognitive disability rather than supported decision-making
- Service systems prioritize safety over choice, restricting autonomy in the name of protection
- People with IDD are excluded from decision-making about their own services and from policy discussions
- Assumed incompetence leads to lower expectations, limiting employment, education, and community participation opportunities

**Why It Persists**

- Deep cultural association of intellectual disability with incapacity
- Professional training emphasizes deficit models rather than strengths and supports
- Legal systems default to guardianship because judges and attorneys lack familiarity with alternatives
- Media representation reinforces stereotypes of incompetence or inspirational exceptionalism

### 7. Segregation as Default Service Model

**How This Creates Harm**

- Sheltered workshops, day programs, and group homes create parallel systems that separate people with IDD from the broader community
- Segregated settings reduce opportunities for social inclusion, community relationships, and economic participation
- "Facility-based" services are often easier to administer and staff than truly individualized community supports
- The congregate model persists even when individual outcomes in integrated settings are demonstrably better

**Why It Persists**

- Segregated services are administratively simpler and more cost-efficient per unit (though not per outcome)
- Provider organizations have business models built around congregate settings
- Families sometimes prefer familiar, supervised settings over the perceived risks of community integration
- Community resistance to group homes and supported living arrangements (NIMBYism) limits options
- Employment First policies exist in most states but lack enforcement mechanisms

## Economic Root Causes

### 8. The Benefits Cliff

People with IDD who want to work face severe disincentives in the benefits system.

| Benefit | Work Disincentive | Impact |
|---------|-------------------|--------|
| SSI | Earnings above $65/month reduce SSI dollar-for-dollar (after $65 exclusion) | Discourages employment |
| Medicaid | Loss of SSI can trigger Medicaid loss | Fear of losing healthcare |
| Section 8 housing | Rent increases with income (30% of adjusted income) | Reduces net income gain from work |
| SNAP | Benefits decrease as income rises | Reduces food security benefit of working |

**Cumulative effect**: Marginal effective tax rates for people with IDD who work can exceed 80-100%, meaning additional earnings result in net income loss when benefits reductions are combined.

### 9. Inadequate Federal Fiscal Architecture

- Federal funding for DD services relies heavily on Medicaid, which is a matching program subject to state fiscal conditions
- During economic downturns, states face Medicaid budget pressures precisely when more people need services
- Discretionary programs (DD Act, vocational rehabilitation) are subject to annual appropriations fights
- The Congressional Budget Office does not model long-term cost savings from community integration, employment, or prevention, making investment-oriented policies appear more expensive than they are

## Summary: Interconnected Root Causes

| Root Cause | Category | Primary Impact |
|------------|----------|---------------|
| Medicaid institutional bias | Structural | Waiting lists; unequal access to HCBS |
| Chronic underfunding | Structural | Service capacity gaps; inadequate infrastructure |
| DSP workforce failure | Structural/Economic | Turnover; service reductions; safety risks |
| Federal/state fragmentation | Structural | Gaps in coordination; duplicative processes |
| Transition cliff | Structural | Young adults lose services at critical juncture |
| Residual paternalism | Ideological | Restricted autonomy; low expectations |
| Segregation as default | Ideological | Limited community participation and employment |
| Benefits cliff | Economic | Employment disincentives; poverty trap |
| Inadequate fiscal architecture | Economic | Procyclical funding cuts; investment barriers |

These root causes interact and reinforce each other. Medicaid's institutional bias drives underfunding of community services, which causes workforce shortages, which leads to service reductions, which pushes families toward institutional options, which reinforces the institutional bias. Breaking these cycles requires addressing multiple root causes simultaneously.
