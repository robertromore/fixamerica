# Developmental Disabilities: Solutions

## Overview

Reforming the developmental disabilities system requires simultaneous action across multiple fronts: restructuring Medicaid financing, investing in the direct support workforce, transforming employment systems, improving transitions, expanding self-determination, and strengthening quality oversight. No single reform will be sufficient because the system's failures are interconnected. The solutions presented here range from immediately achievable executive actions to structural reforms requiring legislation and sustained political will.

---

## Solution 1: Make HCBS a Mandatory Medicaid Benefit

### Problem Addressed

Medicaid's institutional bias: ICF/IDD care is a mandatory benefit; HCBS is optional, resulting in 800,000+ people on waiting lists.

### Proposal

Amend the Social Security Act to make home and community-based services for people with developmental disabilities a mandatory Medicaid benefit, equivalent in legal status to institutional care.

### Key Elements

- Eliminate waiver structure for IDD HCBS; make services available under the state plan
- Maintain person-centered service planning and individualized budgets
- Require states to offer a comprehensive service array including residential supports, employment services, day services, respite, and assistive technology
- Phase in over 5 years to allow states to build capacity
- Provide enhanced FMAP (90% federal match) during implementation period

### Evidence

- States without waiting lists (Arizona, Vermont, New Hampshire) demonstrate feasibility
- Per-person HCBS costs average 30-50% less than institutional care (Braddock et al., 2024)
- *Olmstead* decision establishes legal obligation for community integration
- Community settings produce better outcomes across all measured domains (employment, health, social inclusion, self-determination)

### Challenges

- CBO scoring treats HCBS expansion as new spending (estimated $300-400 billion over 10 years)
- States with large waiting lists face significant ramp-up challenges
- Workforce shortages must be addressed simultaneously or new funding will have no providers to pay
- Political opposition from Medicaid spending hawks

### Precedents

- ACA Medicaid expansion (2010) demonstrated federal capacity to expand Medicaid eligibility
- Community First Choice Option (Section 2401 of ACA) provides 6% enhanced FMAP for community attendant services
- Money Follows the Person demonstration (2005-present) funded transitions out of institutions

## Solution 2: DSP Workforce Investment

### Problem Addressed

Direct support professional wages, benefits, and working conditions are inadequate, driving 43-54% annual turnover and critical service shortages.

### Proposal

Establish a federal DSP workforce investment strategy with mandatory minimum wage requirements, benefits, and career development tied to Medicaid reimbursement.

### Key Elements

- **Wage floor**: Require Medicaid-funded DSP wages to be at least 150% of the applicable minimum wage (state or federal, whichever is higher)
- **Rate transparency**: Require states to publicly report the percentage of Medicaid reimbursement rates allocated to DSP compensation
- **Pass-through requirements**: Mandate that rate increases designated for DSP wages are passed through to worker compensation (not absorbed by provider administration)
- **Benefits**: Require Medicaid-funded providers to offer health insurance and paid leave to DSPs working 20+ hours per week
- **Credentialing**: Fund national DSP credentialing through the National Alliance for Direct Support Professionals (NADSP)
- **Career ladder**: Create a defined career pathway from entry-level DSP to senior DSP, lead DSP, and supervisor with corresponding wage tiers
- **Federal investment**: Dedicate enhanced FMAP for DSP wage and benefit increases

### Evidence

- States that increased DSP wages (Minnesota, Massachusetts) showed significant improvements in turnover and vacancy rates
- ARPA HCBS funding used for DSP wage increases in many states reduced turnover by 15-25% (ANCOR, 2024)
- PHI research demonstrates that wage increases to $15+/hour reduce turnover by approximately 30%
- Credentialing improves service quality, worker satisfaction, and retention (NADSP, 2024)

### Challenges

- Significant federal and state cost ($25-40 billion annually)
- Rate-setting reform requires state-by-state implementation
- Provider industry resistance to pass-through mandates and transparency requirements
- Sustained funding needed; one-time investments (like ARPA) create temporary improvement followed by regression

## Solution 3: Eliminate Subminimum Wages and Transform Employment Services

### Problem Addressed

Only 19% of adults with IDD have competitive integrated employment; approximately 42,000 workers are paid subminimum wages; segregated day programs remain the dominant service model.

### Proposal

Phase out Section 14(c) of the Fair Labor Standards Act and transform employment services to prioritize competitive integrated employment for all.

### Key Elements

- **Phase-out timeline**: Eliminate Section 14(c) certificates over 5 years with mandatory transition planning
- **Employment First mandate**: Require states to offer competitive integrated employment services before facility-based options
- **Service transformation funding**: Provide federal transition grants to states and providers converting from sheltered workshops to community employment programs
- **Supported employment expansion**: Increase Vocational Rehabilitation funding for supported and customized employment by 50%
- **Employer engagement**: Expand tax credits for businesses hiring people with IDD; fund employer technical assistance
- **Benefits alignment**: Implement Medicaid buy-in programs and ABLE account expansion to reduce employment disincentives

### Evidence

- States that eliminated subminimum wage (Vermont, Oregon, Washington) show higher rates of competitive employment without increased unemployment among people with IDD
- Supported employment produces better outcomes than sheltered workshops on every measured dimension: wages, hours, community integration, self-reported satisfaction (Cimera, 2011; Butterworth et al., 2024)
- Customized employment successfully places individuals with the most significant disabilities in competitive jobs (Griffin et al., 2008)
- Federal contractor minimum wage order (Executive Order 14026) already prohibits subminimum wages for federal contract work

### Challenges

- Some families and providers fear loss of day programming without adequate alternatives
- Rural areas have fewer competitive employment opportunities
- Workforce shortages affect supported employment job coaching availability
- Transition requires up-front investment during overlap period

## Solution 4: Fix the Transition Cliff

### Problem Addressed

Young adults with IDD lose educational entitlements at age 21-22 and face multi-year waits for adult services.

### Proposal

Create a seamless transition system that begins planning early and ensures continuity of services.

### Key Elements

- **Transition planning at age 14**: Mandate coordinated transition planning involving the school, state DD agency, vocational rehabilitation, and Medicaid starting at age 14 (current IDEA requirement is 16)
- **Pre-enrollment in adult services**: Allow enrollment in Medicaid HCBS waivers up to 3 years before school exit, with services activating upon school exit
- **Transition coordinators**: Fund state-level transition coordinator positions jointly through DOEd and ACL
- **Summer bridge programs**: Fund summer programming between school exit and adult services to prevent service gaps
- **Data sharing**: Require interagency data sharing between schools and adult service agencies to track transition outcomes

### Evidence

- States with early transition planning (Maryland, Wisconsin) show higher rates of post-school employment and service connection
- Pre-enrollment models reduce the transition gap from years to weeks
- Research shows that the first two years post-school are critical; service gaps during this period lead to skill regression and social isolation

### Challenges

- Requires coordination across IDEA, Medicaid, and Vocational Rehabilitation, each governed by different federal agencies
- Schools already struggle with transition planning; adding earlier requirements without additional resources would be counterproductive
- Adult service capacity must increase to absorb annual cohort of school exiters

## Solution 5: Expand Self-Direction and Supported Decision-Making

### Problem Addressed

Paternalistic service models and guardianship systems restrict the autonomy of people with IDD.

### Proposal

Make self-directed services universally available and replace default guardianship with supported decision-making.

### Key Elements

- **Self-direction mandate**: Require all Medicaid HCBS waivers to offer self-directed service options (budget authority and employer authority)
- **Supported decision-making**: Enact federal legislation recognizing supported decision-making as a less restrictive alternative to guardianship
- **Guardianship reform**: Require courts to consider supported decision-making before granting guardianship; mandate periodic review of existing guardianships
- **Person-centered planning**: Strengthen CMS requirements for person-centered service planning with meaningful individual choice
- **Training**: Fund training for individuals, families, and professionals in self-direction and supported decision-making

### Evidence

- Self-directed services produce equal or better outcomes at equal or lower cost compared to agency-directed services (Caldwell, 2007; Conroy, 2014)
- Supported decision-making pilot projects (Texas, DC, Wisconsin) show that individuals maintain or improve decision-making skills while maintaining safety
- States with robust self-direction (Vermont, Oregon, Washington) demonstrate higher rates of community integration and satisfaction

### Challenges

- Not all individuals or families want self-directed services; must remain optional
- Quality assurance in self-directed models requires different approaches than agency-based oversight
- Guardianship reform faces opposition from probate courts and guardianship attorneys
- Some individuals need intensive support to meaningfully self-direct

## Solution 6: Strengthen Quality and Safety Oversight

### Problem Addressed

People with IDD experience high rates of abuse, neglect, and preventable death, with inadequate monitoring and accountability.

### Proposal

Establish a national quality and safety framework for developmental disability services.

### Key Elements

- **National incident reporting system**: Create a standardized, mandatory incident reporting system across all states
- **Mortality review**: Require all states to conduct independent mortality reviews for all deaths of people receiving DD services
- **Independent ombudsman**: Fund state-level independent ombudsman programs for IDD services (modeled on the Long-Term Care Ombudsman Program)
- **NCI participation**: Require all states receiving Medicaid IDD funding to participate in the National Core Indicators (NCI) program
- **DSP background checks**: Establish national background check requirements for all DSPs (currently fragmented by state)
- **Restraint reduction**: Phase out all forms of restrictive intervention; ban prone restraint and aversive procedures

### Evidence

- States with independent oversight (Connecticut, DC) have lower rates of substantiated abuse and neglect
- National mortality review data reveals patterns of preventable death that localized systems miss
- NCI data enables benchmarking and targeted quality improvement across states

### Challenges

- Quality oversight systems cost money that competes with service delivery funding
- State resistance to federal quality mandates
- Mandatory reporting can be burdensome for providers already stretched thin
- Defining "quality" is contested: medical safety versus quality of life versus rights protection

## Quick Wins (Executive Action or Administrative Reform)

1. **CMS guidance on waiting list management**: Issue guidance requiring states to demonstrate progress toward reducing waiting lists as condition of waiver renewal
2. **Section 14(c) rulemaking**: DOL can phase out subminimum wage certificates through regulatory action
3. **HCBS rate adequacy**: CMS can use HCBS Access Rule authority to require states to demonstrate rate adequacy tied to DSP wage benchmarks
4. **Transition data sharing**: Issue interagency guidance requiring data sharing between IDEA and Medicaid systems
5. **Employment First executive order**: Presidential executive order directing federal agencies to implement Employment First policies in contracting and hiring
