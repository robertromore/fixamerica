# Developmental Disabilities: Overview

## Executive Summary

Developmental disabilities are a group of conditions arising from impairments in physical, learning, language, or behavioral areas that begin during the developmental period, typically before age 22, and usually last throughout a person's lifetime. In the United States, approximately 17% of children aged 3-17 have one or more developmental disabilities, and an estimated 7.4 million Americans across all age groups live with an intellectual or developmental disability (IDD). These conditions include intellectual disability, autism spectrum disorder, cerebral palsy, Down syndrome, fetal alcohol spectrum disorders, and a range of other conditions that affect cognitive, adaptive, or physical functioning.

Despite decades of progress moving away from institutionalization toward community-based living, Americans with developmental disabilities continue to face pervasive barriers to full community participation. Waiting lists for home and community-based services (HCBS) through Medicaid waivers exceed 800,000 people nationally, with average wait times stretching from three to eight years depending on the state. The direct support professional (DSP) workforce that enables community living is in perpetual crisis, with annual turnover rates exceeding 50% and vacancy rates above 15% in many states. Employment outcomes remain dismal: fewer than 20% of adults with IDD participate in competitive integrated employment, and subminimum wage certificates under Section 14(c) of the Fair Labor Standards Act still permit paying workers with disabilities below the federal minimum wage.

The developmental disabilities system sits at the intersection of healthcare, education, employment, housing, and civil rights policy. Fragmentation across these systems creates gaps that families must navigate with inadequate support. The transition from child-serving systems (Part C early intervention, IDEA special education) to adult services is particularly treacherous, often described as "falling off a cliff" when young adults age out of entitlement-based educational services into adult systems with limited capacity and long waiting lists.

## Core Tensions

### Institutional Bias vs. Community Integration

The *Olmstead v. L.C.* (1999) Supreme Court decision established that unnecessary institutionalization constitutes discrimination under the ADA. Yet Medicaid's structural bias toward institutional care persists: Institutional Medicaid (ICF/IDD) is a mandatory benefit, while home and community-based services remain an optional waiver, subject to state budgets and waiting lists. This structural inequity means that states must fund a nursing home or ICF/IDD bed for anyone who qualifies, but can maintain years-long waiting lists for community-based services that are often more cost-effective and strongly preferred by people with disabilities and their families.

### Self-Determination vs. Protection

The disability rights movement's emphasis on autonomy, choice, and self-determination can conflict with societal impulses to protect people perceived as vulnerable. This tension manifests in debates over guardianship versus supported decision-making, managed risk versus safety-first approaches to community living, and the degree to which people with significant intellectual disabilities can and should direct their own services and life decisions.

### Workforce Economics vs. Service Needs

Direct support professionals perform demanding, skilled work that is essential to community living for people with developmental disabilities. Yet their wages remain close to minimum wage in many states, driving chronic shortages and turnover that directly harm the people they support. Increasing DSP wages requires substantial public investment, creating tension between fiscal conservatism and the practical requirements of community-based services.

### Family Burden vs. System Capacity

Families of individuals with developmental disabilities provide the vast majority of care and support across the lifespan. When public systems are inadequate, families absorb the cost through reduced workforce participation, financial strain, and physical and emotional exhaustion. The question of how much responsibility the public system should assume versus how much falls to families remains politically and fiscally contested.

- How does the fragmentation of developmental disability services across federal and state agencies affect outcomes?

## Vision of Success

A reformed developmental disabilities system would ensure that every American with a developmental disability has timely access to individualized, community-based supports that enable them to live, work, and participate in their communities according to their own preferences and goals. Waiting lists for Medicaid HCBS would be eliminated through mandatory coverage parity with institutional care. Direct support professionals would earn living wages with benefits, career advancement opportunities, and the training and recognition their essential work deserves. Competitive integrated employment would be the default expectation, with subminimum wages eliminated and replaced by customized employment, supported employment, and integrated day services. Self-determination, person-centered planning, and supported decision-making would replace paternalistic guardianship as the standard approach to supporting people with intellectual disabilities in making life decisions. Transitions between systems would be seamless, supported by coordinated planning that begins years before each transition point. Families would receive meaningful respite, navigation support, and financial assistance rather than being expected to serve as the primary service system by default.
