# Developmental Disabilities: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to reforming the developmental disabilities system comes from diverse sources with distinct motivations. Unlike many policy areas, overt opposition to disability rights is politically costly; few policymakers openly argue against helping people with disabilities. Instead, opposition tends to manifest as fiscal objections to expanded spending, defense of existing institutional models, resistance to workforce cost increases, and skepticism about the capacity of all individuals with IDD to participate in integrated settings. Understanding these opposition dynamics is essential because they have successfully blocked or delayed reforms for decades despite broad rhetorical support for community integration.

## Fiscal Opposition

### State Budget Hawks

**Who**: State legislators and governors who prioritize fiscal restraint, particularly in Medicaid spending.

**Arguments**

- Medicaid is already the largest line item in most state budgets (29% of state spending on average)
- Eliminating waiting lists would cost billions in additional state matching funds
- HCBS expansion could trigger a "woodwork effect" where previously unknown eligible individuals seek services
- States cannot afford to simultaneously maintain institutional capacity and expand community services
- Federal mandates without full federal funding shift costs to states

**Tactics**

- Maintaining waiting lists as de facto cost containment
- Setting artificially low Medicaid reimbursement rates for IDD services
- Limiting HCBS waiver slots in annual budget processes
- Advocating for Medicaid block grants or per capita caps that would lock in current (inadequate) spending levels
- Opposing HCBS as a mandatory Medicaid benefit

**Assessment**: Fiscal arguments against HCBS expansion are partially valid in the narrow budgetary sense but fail to account for the higher per-person cost of institutional care, the economic benefits of community integration and employment, and the moral and legal obligations established by *Olmstead*. Multiple studies demonstrate that well-designed HCBS programs cost less per person than institutional care.

### Congressional Opponents of Medicaid Expansion

**Who**: Members of Congress, primarily Republican, who oppose expanding Medicaid entitlements.

**Arguments**

- The HCBS Access Act (making HCBS mandatory) would cost an estimated $300+ billion over 10 years
- Medicaid expansion would increase federal deficits
- States should have flexibility to design their own programs
- Medicaid should be reformed to reduce spending, not expanded

**Tactics**

- Blocking floor votes on HCBS legislation
- Proposing Medicaid restructuring (block grants, per capita caps) that would reduce IDD funding
- Framing HCBS expansion as "new entitlement spending" rather than substitution for institutional costs
- Using CBO scoring rules that count HCBS spending as additive (not accounting for institutional savings)

## Institutional Defenders

### Institutional Employee Unions

**Who**: AFSCME and other public employee unions representing workers in state-operated institutions.

**Arguments**

- Institutional closure eliminates good-paying union jobs with benefits
- Some individuals with severe/profound disabilities need intensive 24-hour care that community settings cannot provide
- Community services are understaffed and under-regulated compared to institutions
- Deinstitutionalization has produced homelessness and incarceration for some populations
- Workers have invested careers in institutional employment

**Tactics**

- Lobbying against institutional closures at the state legislative level
- Negotiating contract provisions that make closures more expensive (transfer rights, severance)
- Commissioning studies that question community service quality
- Building coalitions with families who fear community placement
- Using political influence with Democratic legislators who rely on union support

**Assessment**: The tension between union interests and community integration is real. However, institutional employee advocacy should be distinguished from the interests of people with disabilities who live in those institutions. Multiple studies and the overwhelming preference expressed by people with IDD themselves support community placement with adequate supports.

### Private ICF/IDD Operators

**Who**: For-profit and nonprofit operators of Intermediate Care Facilities for Individuals with Intellectual Disabilities.

**Arguments**

- ICFs/IDD provide medically necessary levels of care
- Federal certification ensures quality and safety
- Some individuals' needs exceed what HCBS can safely provide
- Eliminating ICFs/IDD reduces the continuum of care

**Tactics**

- Lobbying state legislatures against rate reductions and bed closures
- Emphasizing safety and medical capacity of institutional settings
- Challenging *Olmstead* compliance plans that would reduce ICF/IDD populations
- Building relationships with families who prefer institutional placement

## Subminimum Wage Defenders

### Sheltered Workshop Operators

**Who**: Approximately 1,200 organizations holding Section 14(c) certificates, including Goodwill Industries locations, Jewish Vocational Services, and other nonprofit organizations.

**Arguments**

- Sheltered workshops provide meaningful activity and social connection
- Some individuals with significant disabilities cannot produce at competitive levels
- Eliminating subminimum wage would result in unemployment, not employment
- Workshops offer a safe, structured environment
- Families rely on workshop attendance for respite and day structure

**Tactics**

- Lobbying Congress against the Transformation to Competitive Integrated Employment Act
- Mobilizing families of workshop participants to oppose changes
- Commissioning surveys showing family satisfaction with workshops
- Arguing for "choice" to include the option of subminimum wage employment
- Highlighting individual stories of workshop participants who prefer current arrangements

### Parent Groups Opposing Section 14(c) Elimination

**Who**: Some parents and family members, particularly of individuals with severe/profound intellectual disabilities, who fear the loss of day programming.

**Arguments**

- "My child cannot work in competitive employment; eliminating workshops leaves them with nothing"
- Community-based alternatives are inadequate or nonexistent in many areas
- Forcing change without building alternatives first is irresponsible
- Parents know their children's capabilities better than advocates

**Assessment**: The concern about inadequate alternatives is legitimate. States that have eliminated subminimum wage (like Vermont and Washington) demonstrate that transition is possible with adequate investment, but states that move too quickly without building alternatives risk leaving people without services. The self-advocacy community's position is clear: subminimum wage is exploitative regardless of disability severity, and alternatives must be built alongside phase-out.

## Guardianship System Defenders

### Probate Courts and Guardianship Attorneys

**Who**: Judges, attorneys, and professional guardians who administer and profit from the guardianship system.

**Arguments**

- Guardianship protects vulnerable people from exploitation and abuse
- Supported decision-making is untested and may leave people at risk
- Some individuals truly lack the capacity to make informed decisions
- Courts provide oversight and accountability

**Tactics**

- Opposing supported decision-making legislation
- Setting high bars for supported decision-making implementation
- Emphasizing cases of exploitation to justify maintaining plenary guardianship
- Framing guardianship reform as endangering vulnerable people

**Assessment**: There are genuine cases where individuals need protective oversight. However, the guardianship system as currently constituted strips rights from hundreds of thousands of people with IDD, many of whom could exercise greater autonomy with appropriate supports. The National Council on Disability and self-advocacy organizations have documented widespread overuse of guardianship and abuse within the system itself.

## Community Opposition (NIMBYism)

### Neighborhood Resistance to Group Homes

**Who**: Residents and homeowner associations in communities where group homes or supported living arrangements are proposed.

**Arguments**

- Group homes reduce property values (generally not supported by evidence)
- Increased traffic and disruption
- Safety concerns about residents with behavioral challenges
- Changing neighborhood character
- Concentration of social services in certain neighborhoods

**Tactics**

- Zoning challenges and appeals
- Petitions and public comment campaigns
- Lobbying local officials to deny permits
- Using restrictive covenants and HOA rules
- In some cases, harassment of residents and staff

**Legal Context**: The Fair Housing Act prohibits disability-based discrimination in housing, including zoning decisions that exclude group homes. However, enforcement is inconsistent, and communities find indirect methods to resist.

## Industry and Employer Opposition

### Small Business Concerns

**Who**: Small businesses facing ADA reasonable accommodation requirements for employees with IDD.

**Arguments**

- Accommodations can be costly for small businesses
- Supported employment requires employer time and flexibility
- Liability concerns about workplace safety
- Productivity concerns about employees with significant disabilities

**Assessment**: Most reasonable accommodations cost $500 or less (Job Accommodation Network data), and tax credits (WOTC, Section 44 tax credit) offset costs. Supported employment models provide job coaches who address productivity and training concerns. Nevertheless, small business anxiety about disability employment requirements is a real barrier to Employment First implementation.

## Counter-Strategies

### Against Fiscal Arguments

- Demonstrate cost-effectiveness of HCBS versus institutional care
- Document economic benefits of competitive employment (reduced benefits dependence, tax revenue)
- Highlight long-term fiscal consequences of waiting lists (emergency placements, crisis services, preventable health costs)
- Build bipartisan support by framing investment as pro-family and pro-work

### Against Institutional Defenders

- Center the voices and preferences of people with IDD themselves
- Document quality outcomes in community versus institutional settings
- Provide transition support for institutional workers (retraining, community job placement)
- Phase closures with adequate community capacity building

### Against Subminimum Wage Defenders

- Highlight state successes in transitioning from workshops to competitive employment
- Provide concrete transition timelines with capacity-building requirements
- Address family concerns with evidence-based community alternatives
- Center self-advocate testimony about dignity and exploitation

### Against NIMBYism

- Enforce Fair Housing Act requirements
- Provide community education about group homes and supported living
- Present property value data showing no negative impact
- Build relationships between group home residents and neighbors
