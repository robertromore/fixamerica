# Developmental Disabilities: History

## The Era of Institutionalization (1850s-1960s)

### Rise of the Institutional Model

The institutional model for people with developmental disabilities emerged in the mid-nineteenth century as an ostensibly benevolent reform. Early institutions were conceived as "training schools" that would educate people with intellectual disabilities and return them to the community. This vision was quickly abandoned in favor of permanent custodial care.

**Key Developments**

- 1848: Samuel Gridley Howe establishes the Massachusetts School for Idiotic Children, the first publicly funded institution for people with intellectual disabilities in the United States
- 1850s-1880s: State-operated institutions proliferate across the country; most begin as small training schools
- 1870s-1900s: Institutions shift from educational to custodial purpose as "incurable" residents accumulate
- 1880: U.S. Census identifies approximately 76,000 "idiots" and "lunatics" in institutions

### The Eugenics Era (1900-1940s)

The eugenics movement fundamentally transformed the purpose of institutions from care to segregation and control. People with intellectual disabilities were recast as threats to the genetic fitness of the nation.

| Year | Development | Impact |
|------|-------------|--------|
| 1907 | Indiana enacts first compulsory sterilization law | Model for 31 states |
| 1912 | Henry Goddard publishes *The Kallikak Family* | Pseudoscientific basis for institutionalization |
| 1924 | Virginia Sterilization Act | Challenged in *Buck v. Bell* |
| 1927 | *Buck v. Bell* (Supreme Court) | Upheld forced sterilization; "Three generations of imbeciles are enough" |
| 1930s | Institutional populations peak at over 190,000 | Overcrowding, abuse, neglect become endemic |
| 1930s-1940s | Forced sterilization reaches peak levels | Estimated 60,000+ Americans sterilized |

**Scale of Institutional Confinement**

| Year | Institutional Population | Average Institution Size |
|------|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| 1900 | 30,000 | 500 |
| 1920 | 68,000 | 800 |
| 1940 | 117,000 | 1,200 |
| 1960 | 195,000 | 1,500 |
| 1967 | 228,500 (peak) | 1,600 |

*Source: Braddock, David. *The State of the States in Developmental Disabilities*. Various editions.*

## Exposing the Institutions (1960s-1970s)

### Public Awareness

A series of exposures brought the horrific conditions in state institutions to public attention.

**Key Exposures**

- 1965: Senator Robert Kennedy visits Willowbrook State School (Staten Island, NY) and publicly describes it as a "snake pit"
- 1966: Burton Blatt and Fred Kaplan publish *Christmas in Purgatory*, a photographic expose of institutional conditions
- 1972: Geraldo Rivera's investigative report on Willowbrook airs on WABC-TV, showing residents naked, unsupervised, and living in filth
- 1972: *Wyatt v. Stickney* (Alabama) establishes constitutional right to adequate conditions in state institutions

### Legal Landmarks

| Case | Year | Holding | Impact |
|------|------|---------|--------|
| *Wyatt v. Stickney* | 1972 | Constitutional right to minimally adequate habilitation in state institutions | First court to establish institutional standards |
| *New York State ARC v. Rockefeller* (Willowbrook) | 1975 | Consent decree requiring deinstitutionalization of Willowbrook | Model for institutional reform litigation |
| *Halderman v. Pennhurst* | 1977 | Community placement is required alternative to institutional care | Later narrowed by Supreme Court on federalism grounds |
| *Youngberg v. Romeo* | 1982 | Constitutional right to safe conditions, freedom from restraint, and minimally adequate training | Supreme Court establishes constitutional floor for institutional care |

### The Developmental Disabilities Act

Congress enacted the Developmental Disabilities Services and Facilities Construction Act in 1970, later renamed the Developmental Disabilities Assistance and Bill of Rights Act (DD Act). Key iterations:

| Year | Action | Significance |
|------|--------|--------------|
| 1970 | DD Act enacted | Created state DD Councils; defined developmental disabilities |
| 1975 | DD Act amended | Added Protection and Advocacy (P&A) system |
| 1978 | DD Act reauthorized | Added University Centers for Excellence in Developmental Disabilities (UCEDDs) |
| 1984 | DD Act reauthorized | Strengthened rights provisions; emphasized community integration |
| 1994 | DD Act reauthorized | Self-determination and inclusion emphasis |
| 2000 | DD Act reauthorized (current) | Employment First principles; self-advocacy |

## Deinstitutionalization and Community Services (1970s-2000s)

### Building the Community System

The movement from institutions to community living required building an entirely new service infrastructure.

**Medicaid and IDD Services**

| Year | Development | Impact |
|------|-------------|--------|
| 1965 | Medicaid enacted | Initially funded only institutional care for people with IDD |
| 1971 | ICF/MR (now ICF/IDD) benefit created | Medicaid optional benefit for intermediate care facilities |
| 1981 | HCBS waiver authority (Section 1915(c)) created | Allowed Medicaid funding for community-based services as alternative to institutions |
| 1990 | Community Supported Living Arrangements Act | Demonstration project for individual supports |
| 1999 | *Olmstead v. L.C.* | Supreme Court rules unnecessary institutionalization is discrimination under ADA |
| 2005 | Deficit Reduction Act | Money Follows the Person demonstration program |
| 2010 | ACA Section 2402(a) | Community First Choice state plan option |
| 2014 | HCBS Settings Rule | CMS defines community integration requirements for HCBS |

### Institutional Closure Timeline

| Period | Institutions Closed | Institutional Population | Key Events |
|--------|---------------------|--------------------------| ------------|
| 1970-1980 | 15 | 195,000 to 150,000 | First wave of institutional reform litigation |
| 1980-1990 | 40 | 150,000 to 88,000 | HCBS waivers created; community infrastructure built |
| 1990-2000 | 50 | 88,000 to 52,000 | *Olmstead* decided; ADA enacted |
| 2000-2010 | 45 | 52,000 to 33,000 | Money Follows the Person; state closures accelerate |
| 2010-2024 | 30 | 33,000 to 22,000 | ACA provisions; HCBS Settings Rule |

*Source: RISP annual reports; Braddock et al., various editions.*

### The Olmstead Decision (1999)

*Olmstead v. L.C.*, 527 U.S. 581 (1999), is the most important legal decision in the history of developmental disability policy. The Supreme Court held:

- Unjustified institutionalization of people with disabilities is discrimination under Title II of the ADA
- States must provide community-based services when: (1) treatment professionals determine community placement is appropriate; (2) the individual does not oppose community placement; and (3) the placement can be reasonably accommodated
- States can invoke a "fundamental alteration" defense but must demonstrate a comprehensive, effectively working plan for community integration

**Impact of Olmstead**

- Became the legal framework for all subsequent deinstitutionalization and HCBS expansion efforts
- DOJ has used *Olmstead* to compel states to expand community-based services through enforcement actions and settlements
- However, the "fundamental alteration" defense and the absence of specific timelines have allowed states to maintain waiting lists for decades

## The Self-Advocacy Movement (1970s-Present)

### Origins

People with intellectual and developmental disabilities began organizing for their own rights in the 1970s, challenging the assumption that others should make decisions on their behalf.

- 1973: People First organized in Salem, Oregon, the first self-advocacy organization run by people with intellectual disabilities
- 1974: People First movement spreads across the United States and internationally
- 1984: Self Advocates Becoming Empowered (SABE) founded as a national self-advocacy organization
- 1991: First national self-advocacy conference organized by self-advocates
- 2000: DD Act reauthorization mandates self-advocate representation on state DD Councils

### Key Self-Advocacy Achievements

| Period | Achievement | Significance |
|--------|-------------|--------------|
| 1970s-1980s | People First movement | Established that people with IDD can and should speak for themselves |
| 1990s | Self-advocates testify before Congress | Directly shaped ADA and DD Act reauthorization |
| 2000s | Self-advocates in state government | Some states hire self-advocates in DD agency roles |
| 2010s | Close the Institutions campaign | SABE launches campaign to close all remaining state institutions |
| 2010s-2020s | Supported decision-making advocacy | Self-advocates lead reform of guardianship laws |
| 2020s | #StopTheShock campaign | Self-advocates lead effort to ban electric shock devices |

## Employment History

### From Sheltered Workshops to Employment First

| Era | Dominant Model | Key Features |
|-----|---------------|-------------- |
| 1920s-1960s | Vocational rehabilitation | Focus on physical disabilities; IDD largely excluded |
| 1960s-1980s | Sheltered workshops | Segregated, subminimum wage "prevocational" programs |
| 1980s-1990s | Supported employment | Job coaches; competitive wages; Paul Wehman model |
| 2000s-2010s | Employment First policies | States adopt policies prioritizing competitive integrated employment |
| 2010s-2020s | Customized employment | Individualized job creation; employer negotiation |

**Section 14(c) Timeline**

- 1938: Fair Labor Standards Act Section 14(c) allows subminimum wage for workers with disabilities
- 1986: FLSA amendments require individual productivity assessments (time studies)
- 2014: WIOA Section 511 restricts new subminimum wage placements for youth
- 2016: National Council on Disability recommends phasing out Section 14(c)
- 2020s: States begin eliminating subminimum wage; 17 states + DC have done so by 2024
- 2024: DOL proposes rule to phase out Section 14(c) certificates nationally

## Family and Community Supports History

### Evolution of Family Support

| Period | Approach | Examples |
|--------|----------|----------|
| Pre-1960s | Families advised to institutionalize children | "Put them away and forget about them" was standard medical advice |
| 1960s-1970s | Parent advocacy organizations | ARC (formerly Association for Retarded Citizens); parent-led deinstitutionalization advocacy |
| 1980s | Family support programs emerge | Cash assistance, respite care, home modifications |
| 1990s | Self-directed services | Families and individuals manage budgets and hire workers |
| 2000s | Person-centered planning | Individual goals and preferences drive service plans |
| 2010s-2020s | Self-direction expansion | More states offer budget authority and employer authority models |

### Guardianship Reform

| Period | Development |
|--------|-------------|
| Pre-2000 | Full guardianship standard; plenary guardianship strips all rights |
| 2000-2010 | Limited guardianship becomes more common |
| 2010-2015 | Supported decision-making emerges as alternative to guardianship |
| 2015-2020 | Texas, DC, and others enact supported decision-making statutes |
| 2020-2024 | 20+ states have enacted or introduced supported decision-making legislation |

*Sources: National Guardianship Association (2024); ACLU Disability Rights Program (2024).*

## Lessons from History

1. **Institutionalization was not inevitable**: It resulted from specific policy choices driven by eugenics ideology, and those choices can be reversed
2. **Legal advocacy has been essential**: Every major advance was supported or driven by litigation
3. **Self-advocacy transforms policy**: When people with developmental disabilities speak for themselves, policy improves
4. **Family advocacy built the system**: Parent organizations created the political constituency for community services
5. **Medicaid is determinative**: The structure of Medicaid financing shapes the entire service system
6. **Progress is not linear**: Periods of reform alternate with retrenchment; gains must be defended
7. **The workforce was never adequately valued**: From institutional attendants to DSPs, the workers who support people with disabilities have always been underpaid
