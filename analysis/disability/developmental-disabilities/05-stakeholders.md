# Developmental Disabilities: Stakeholders

## Overview

The developmental disabilities policy landscape involves an unusually wide range of stakeholders because the population requires supports across virtually every domain of life: healthcare, education, housing, employment, transportation, and social services. Stakeholder interests frequently align on broad goals (community integration, quality of life) but diverge sharply on funding mechanisms, service models, workforce policy, and the pace of system transformation.

---

## People with Developmental Disabilities and Self-Advocates

### Who They Are

Approximately 7.4 million Americans with intellectual and developmental disabilities (IDD), a growing number of whom are organized in the self-advocacy movement.

### Key Organizations

| Organization | Focus | Membership/Reach |
|--------------|-------|------------------|
| Self Advocates Becoming Empowered (SABE) | National self-advocacy network | Chapters in all 50 states |
| People First chapters | Local/state self-advocacy | Thousands of members nationwide |
| Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN) | Autism-specific self-advocacy | National; strong policy presence |
| Green Mountain Self-Advocates (VT) | State model self-advocacy | Highly influential state organization |
| National Youth Leadership Network | Youth self-advocacy | Young people with disabilities |

### Priorities

- Elimination of waiting lists for community-based services
- End of subminimum wages (Section 14(c))
- Supported decision-making over guardianship
- Closure of remaining state institutions
- Competitive integrated employment
- Meaningful choice and self-direction in services
- Full community inclusion and participation
- End of restraint and seclusion practices

### Power and Influence

- Growing political voice through self-advocacy organizations
- Directly represented on state DD Councils (mandated by DD Act)
- Increasingly effective at testifying before Congress and state legislatures
- Limited by communication barriers, transportation access, and systemic exclusion from policy processes
- Historical pattern of decisions being made *about* rather than *with* people with IDD

## Families

### Who They Are

Families provide the majority of support for Americans with developmental disabilities. Approximately 75% of adults with IDD live with family members, and an estimated 700,000 caregiving parents are age 60 or older.

### Key Organizations

| Organization | Focus | Reach |
|--------------|-------|-------|
| The Arc of the United States | Largest IDD family advocacy organization | 600+ state and local chapters |
| National Down Syndrome Society (NDSS) | Down syndrome specific | National; 375+ affiliates |
| National Down Syndrome Congress (NDSC) | Down syndrome advocacy and education | National |
| Autism Speaks | Autism research and advocacy | National; significant funding |
| National Alliance for Direct Support Professionals (NADSP) | DSP workforce advocacy | National |
| Family Voices | Health-related family advocacy | National; state affiliates |

### Priorities

- Adequate funding for Medicaid HCBS waivers
- Reduced waiting lists and timely access to services
- Respite care and family support programs
- Stable, qualified workforce of DSPs
- Quality school-to-adult transition planning
- Residential options that provide safety and community integration
- Planning for when aging parents can no longer provide care

### Internal Tensions

- Some families prefer protective, supervised settings; others prioritize independence and risk
- Autism community is divided between organizations led by autistic adults (ASAN) and family-led organizations (Autism Speaks) over priorities, research focus, and representation
- Families of individuals with severe/profound disabilities sometimes feel their needs are marginalized by "high-functioning" advocacy
- Generational divide: older parents may be more accepting of institutional or sheltered models

## Service Providers

### Who They Are

Organizations and individuals that deliver residential, employment, day, and support services to people with IDD. Includes large nonprofit agencies, small community-based organizations, and individual support workers.

### Key Organizations

| Organization | Role | Membership |
|--------------|------|------------|
| ANCOR (American Network of Community Options and Resources) | National provider trade association | 1,800+ provider organizations |
| CARF International | Accreditation body | Accredits DD service providers |
| National Association of State Directors of DD Services (NASDDDS) | State DD agency directors | All 50 states |

### Priorities

- Adequate Medicaid reimbursement rates
- DSP wage increases funded through rate increases
- Regulatory flexibility in service delivery
- Transition support from congregate to community models
- Technology integration in service delivery
- Workforce recruitment and retention supports

### Interests and Tensions

- Provider business models often depend on congregate settings (group homes, day programs) that are being challenged by integration mandates
- Providers generally support higher wages for DSPs but need rate increases to fund them
- Some providers resist the shift to self-directed services because it reduces their role as intermediaries
- Quality providers are threatened by the same workforce crisis they did not create
- Small community-based providers are being squeezed by inadequate rates while large corporate providers have more capacity to absorb losses

## Direct Support Professionals (DSPs)

### Who They Are

An estimated 1.2-1.4 million workers who provide daily support to people with IDD in residential, employment, and community settings. The workforce is predominantly female (72%), disproportionately people of color (52%), and largely immigrant in some states.

### Key Organizations

| Organization | Role |
|--------------|------|
| NADSP | Professional standards, credentialing, advocacy |
| PHI (formerly Paraprofessional Healthcare Institute) | Research and advocacy for direct care workers |
| SEIU, AFSCME (unions) | Collective bargaining for DSPs in some states |

### Priorities

- Living wages (advocacy for $20+/hour minimum)
- Health insurance and benefits
- Professional recognition and credentialing
- Career advancement pathways
- Safe working conditions
- Manageable caseloads and schedules

### Power and Influence

- Limited collective bargaining: DSPs are unionized in only a handful of states
- Low political visibility despite essential role
- High turnover means workforce is constantly cycling; difficult to sustain advocacy
- Growing attention from federal and state policymakers through workforce initiatives

## Federal Government

### Key Agencies

| Agency | Role | Key Issues |
|--------|------|------------|
| CMS (within HHS) | Medicaid HCBS waivers, ICF/IDD oversight | HCBS Settings Rule implementation; rate adequacy |
| Administration for Community Living (ACL) | DD Act programs; aging and disability | Flat funding; limited authority |
| DOJ Civil Rights Division | ADA/*Olmstead* enforcement | Institutional segregation cases |
| DOL Wage and Hour Division | Section 14(c) oversight | Subminimum wage phase-out |
| DOL Rehabilitation Services Administration | Vocational rehabilitation | Employment services for IDD |
| Department of Education (OSEP) | IDEA enforcement and funding | Transition planning oversight |
| SSA | SSI/SSDI administration | Benefits cliff; employment incentives |

### Priorities

- Implementing HCBS Access Rule
- *Olmstead* enforcement
- HCBS spending accountability (ARPA funds)
- Section 14(c) reform
- HCBS workforce strategy

## State Governments

### Key State Agencies

Each state has a designated DD agency (sometimes standalone, sometimes within a larger HHS department) responsible for administering IDD services.

### Priorities and Tensions

- Balancing Medicaid budgets with service demands
- Reducing waiting lists within fiscal constraints
- Setting adequate provider reimbursement rates
- Complying with CMS HCBS Settings Rule
- Responding to *Olmstead* obligations
- Managing institutional closures

### Variation

State approaches vary enormously, creating a patchwork system.

| Area | Range |
|------|-------|
| Per capita IDD spending | $12,000 (lowest) to $75,000 (highest) per person served |
| Waiting lists | 0 (Arizona, Vermont) to 150,000+ (Texas) |
| DSP wages | $11-$22/hour depending on state |
| Institutional census | 0 (17 states + DC) to several thousand |
| Self-direction availability | Universal (some states) to minimal (others) |

## Opposition and Skeptics

### Institutional Providers and Unions

Some institutional employees' unions and institutional provider organizations resist deinstitutionalization, arguing that some individuals need institutional levels of care and that community services cannot safely serve everyone.

### Fiscal Conservatives

Some policymakers oppose expanding HCBS as unaffordable, arguing that Medicaid spending is already unsustainable and that additional entitlements would accelerate fiscal crisis.

### Business Interests

Employers subject to ADA reasonable accommodation requirements sometimes view disability employment mandates as burdensome, particularly small businesses with limited resources.

### Sheltered Workshop Operators

Some workshop operators and families of workshop participants oppose the elimination of subminimum wages, arguing that workshop settings provide social connection and structured activity for individuals who cannot perform competitive work.
