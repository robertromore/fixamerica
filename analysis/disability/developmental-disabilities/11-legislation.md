# Developmental Disabilities: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative framework for developmental disabilities spans federal entitlement programs (Medicaid), civil rights law (ADA), education law (IDEA), labor standards (FLSA), and specialized authorizing legislation (DD Act). Reform requires action across multiple statutory domains because no single law governs the full range of services and supports that people with developmental disabilities need. This document presents draft legislation addressing the most critical gaps: HCBS access, workforce investment, employment transformation, and transition reform.

---

## Federal Legislation

### The Home and Community-Based Services Access Act

**Purpose**: Eliminate Medicaid's institutional bias by making HCBS a mandatory benefit for people with developmental disabilities, ending waiting lists.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Home and Community-Based Services
Access Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(a) Over 800,000 Americans with developmental disabilities are on
waiting lists for home and community-based services under Medicaid.
(b) The Supreme Court held in Olmstead v. L.C., 527 U.S. 581 (1999),
that unnecessary institutionalization of people with disabilities
constitutes discrimination under the Americans with Disabilities Act.
(c) Home and community-based services are preferred by the vast
majority of people with developmental disabilities and their families
and typically cost 30 to 50 percent less per person than institutional
care.
(d) Medicaid's current structure treats institutional care as a
mandatory benefit while making home and community-based services
optional, creating a structural bias against community integration.

SEC. 3. MANDATORY HCBS BENEFIT.

(a) IN GENERAL.--Title XIX of the Social Security Act (42 U.S.C.
1396 et seq.) is amended by adding the following new section:

"SEC. 1945A. MANDATORY HOME AND COMMUNITY-BASED SERVICES FOR
INDIVIDUALS WITH DEVELOPMENTAL DISABILITIES.

"(a) REQUIREMENT.--Each State plan under this title shall provide
home and community-based services to individuals with developmental
disabilities who meet the institutional level of care criteria
established under section 1915(c), without regard to waiver capacity
limits or waiting lists.

"(b) SERVICES INCLUDED.--Home and community-based services under
this section shall include, at a minimum--
"(1) residential habilitation and supported living services;
"(2) day habilitation and community integration services;
"(3) supported employment and customized employment services;
"(4) respite care;
"(5) personal care services;
"(6) assistive technology services and devices;
"(7) transportation services;
"(8) behavioral support services;
"(9) family support and training;
"(10) service coordination and case management;
"(11) crisis intervention services; and
"(12) self-directed service options, including budget authority
and employer authority.

"(c) PERSON-CENTERED PLANNING.--Services under this section shall
be provided pursuant to an individualized, person-centered service
plan developed with the meaningful participation of the individual
and, where appropriate, the individual's family or chosen supporters.

"(d) PHASE-IN.--
"(1) States shall eliminate waiting lists for services under this
section within 5 years of enactment.
"(2) States shall submit waiting list reduction plans to the
Secretary within 1 year of enactment.
"(3) States that fail to meet annual reduction targets shall
be subject to corrective action plans.

"(e) ENHANCED FEDERAL MEDICAL ASSISTANCE PERCENTAGE.--
"(1) For the first 3 fiscal years following enactment, the Federal
medical assistance percentage for services under this section shall
be increased by 10 percentage points above the otherwise applicable
rate.
"(2) For fiscal years 4 and 5, the enhanced rate shall be 5
percentage points above the otherwise applicable rate.
"(3) Beginning in fiscal year 6, the otherwise applicable Federal
medical assistance percentage shall apply.

"(f) MAINTENANCE OF EFFORT.--States shall not reduce their own
expenditures on home and community-based services for individuals
with developmental disabilities below the level of such expenditures
for the fiscal year preceding enactment.

"(g) SELF-DIRECTION REQUIREMENT.--Each State shall offer a
self-directed service option under this section, including--
"(1) budget authority, allowing the individual or representative
to manage an individualized budget; and
"(2) employer authority, allowing the individual or representative
to recruit, hire, and manage workers who provide services.

"(h) DEFINITIONS.--For purposes of this section--
"(1) DEVELOPMENTAL DISABILITY.--The term 'developmental disability'
has the meaning given such term in section 102(8) of the
Developmental Disabilities Assistance and Bill of Rights Act of
2000 (42 U.S.C. 15002(8)).
"(2) HOME AND COMMUNITY-BASED SERVICES.--The term 'home and
community-based services' means services provided in home and
community-based settings as defined in 42 C.F.R. 441.301(c)(4)."

SEC. 4. CONFORMING AMENDMENTS.

[Conforming amendments to Sections 1902, 1905, and 1915 of the
Social Security Act.]

SEC. 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the first day of the first fiscal year
beginning 1 year after the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill converts HCBS for people with developmental disabilities from an optional waiver benefit to a mandatory state plan benefit, eliminating the legal basis for waiting lists. The enhanced FMAP provides fiscal incentive and transition support for states. The self-direction requirement ensures person-centered implementation.

**Potential Challenges**: CBO scoring will show significant federal cost ($300-400 billion over 10 years). States will argue they lack infrastructure to absorb waiting list populations rapidly. Some states may resist the mandate as an unfunded federal requirement.

**Refinements**: Consider tiered phase-in based on state waiting list size. Allow states flexibility in service array design within a federal floor. Tie enhanced FMAP to workforce investment requirements.

### The Direct Support Professional Workforce Investment Act

**Purpose**: Establish a federal framework for DSP compensation, training, and professional development.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Direct Support Professional Workforce
Investment Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. DSP WAGE FLOOR.

(a) MINIMUM COMPENSATION.--Any provider of home and community-based
services for individuals with developmental disabilities that receives
Medicaid reimbursement shall compensate direct support professionals
at a rate not less than 150 percent of the higher of--
(1) the applicable State minimum wage; or
(2) the Federal minimum wage.

(b) RATE TRANSPARENCY.--Each State shall publicly report, not less
than annually--
(1) the percentage of Medicaid reimbursement rates for developmental
disability services that is allocated to direct worker compensation;
(2) the average hourly wage, benefits cost, and total compensation
of direct support professionals in the State; and
(3) turnover and vacancy rates for direct support professionals.

(c) PASS-THROUGH REQUIREMENTS.--Any increase in Medicaid
reimbursement rates designated for direct support professional
compensation shall be passed through to worker wages and benefits.
Not less than 80 percent of any rate increase designated for
workforce purposes shall be allocated to direct worker compensation.

SEC. 3. DSP BENEFITS.

(a) HEALTH INSURANCE.--Providers receiving Medicaid reimbursement
shall offer health insurance coverage to direct support professionals
working 20 or more hours per week.

(b) PAID LEAVE.--Providers shall provide not less than 5 days of
paid sick leave and 5 days of paid personal leave per year to direct
support professionals.

SEC. 4. PROFESSIONAL DEVELOPMENT.

(a) NATIONAL CREDENTIALING.--The Secretary of Health and Human
Services shall support and recognize a national direct support
professional credentialing program.

(b) CAREER LADDER.--States shall establish career advancement
pathways for direct support professionals, including defined tiers
(entry, experienced, senior, lead, supervisor) with corresponding
compensation increases.

(c) TRAINING FUNDING.--There are authorized to be appropriated
$100,000,000 per year for grants to States to support direct support
professional training, credentialing, and career development programs.

SEC. 5. ENHANCED FEDERAL MEDICAL ASSISTANCE PERCENTAGE.

The Federal medical assistance percentage for expenditures attributable
to direct support professional compensation increases under this Act
shall be increased by 10 percentage points for the first 5 fiscal years
following enactment.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

(a) Wage and benefit requirements shall take effect 2 years after
enactment.
(b) Reporting requirements shall take effect 1 year after enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the DSP workforce crisis by establishing a wage floor, benefits requirements, transparency mandates, and professional development infrastructure. The enhanced FMAP ensures that the federal government shares the cost of wage increases.

**Potential Challenges**: Small provider organizations may struggle to meet new requirements. States with very low current wages face larger cost increases. Workforce supply may not respond immediately to wage increases alone.

**Refinements**: Include small provider transition support. Allow rural wage adjustments. Pair with immigration reform that provides pathways for immigrant care workers.

### The Transformation to Competitive Integrated Employment Act

**Purpose**: Phase out Section 14(c) subminimum wages and transform employment services for people with developmental disabilities.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Transformation to Competitive Integrated
Employment Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. PHASE-OUT OF SUBMINIMUM WAGE CERTIFICATES.

(a) NEW CERTIFICATES.--No new certificates shall be issued under
section 14(c) of the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 (29 U.S.C. 214(c))
after the date of enactment.

(b) EXISTING CERTIFICATES.--
(1) All existing certificates shall expire not later than 5 years
after the date of enactment.
(2) Certificate holders shall submit transition plans to the Secretary
of Labor within 1 year of enactment.

(c) YOUTH PROHIBITION.--No individual under age 25 shall be employed
under a section 14(c) certificate after the date of enactment.

SEC. 3. EMPLOYMENT TRANSFORMATION GRANTS.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.--There are authorized to be appropriated
$300,000,000 per year for 5 years for grants to--
(1) States for developing competitive integrated employment
infrastructure;
(2) current certificate holders for business model transformation;
(3) individuals transitioning from subminimum wage employment
to competitive integrated employment.

(b) REQUIREMENTS.--Grant recipients shall--
(1) develop individualized employment plans for each worker
transitioning from subminimum wage employment;
(2) provide job coaching, supported employment, and customized
employment services;
(3) report employment outcomes annually.

SEC. 4. EMPLOYMENT FIRST.

(a) FEDERAL REQUIREMENT.--All Federal agencies that fund or administer
employment services for individuals with developmental disabilities
shall adopt Employment First policies that--
(1) prioritize competitive integrated employment as the preferred
outcome;
(2) require competitive integrated employment to be explored and
offered before facility-based services;
(3) prohibit the use of Federal funds for new admissions to
facility-based sheltered employment.

SEC. 5. BENEFITS ALIGNMENT.

(a) SSI EARNED INCOME EXCLUSION.--Section 1612(b) of the Social
Security Act is amended to increase the earned income exclusion
from $65 to $500 per month.

(b) MEDICAID BUY-IN.--States shall offer Medicaid buy-in programs
for workers with disabilities with incomes up to 250 percent of
the Federal poverty level.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment, except as
otherwise specified.
```

**Explanation**: This bill phases out subminimum wages over 5 years while providing transformation funding to build competitive employment alternatives. The benefits alignment provisions reduce the benefits cliff that discourages employment.

**Potential Challenges**: Concerns about adequate alternatives during transition. Rural employment markets may have fewer competitive job options. Some individuals and families will oppose the change.

**Refinements**: Include robust monitoring of outcomes during phase-out. Require states to demonstrate adequate community employment capacity before certificate expiration. Exempt individuals currently over age 65 if they choose to remain in current settings.

## State Model Legislation

### Model Supported Decision-Making Act

**Purpose**: Establish supported decision-making as a recognized legal alternative to guardianship.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Supported Decision-Making Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Supported decision-making" means a process by which an adult
with a disability uses trusted supporters to assist in understanding,
making, communicating, and implementing decisions without the
appointment of a guardian.

(b) "Supported decision-making agreement" means a written agreement
between an adult with a disability and one or more supporters setting
forth the scope and terms of the supported decision-making arrangement.

(c) "Supporter" means a trusted individual chosen by the adult with
a disability to assist with decision-making.

SECTION 3. SUPPORTED DECISION-MAKING AGREEMENTS.

(a) Any adult may voluntarily enter into a supported decision-making
agreement.

(b) A supported decision-making agreement shall specify:
(1) the types of decisions for which the adult desires support;
(2) the identity of each supporter;
(3) the scope of each supporter's role;
(4) the duration of the agreement;
(5) the process for termination by the adult.

(c) A supported decision-making agreement does not transfer
decision-making authority to the supporter.

SECTION 4. DUTIES OF SUPPORTERS.

A supporter shall:
(a) act in a manner that promotes the self-determination and
independence of the adult;
(b) respect the adult's right to make decisions;
(c) avoid exerting undue influence on the adult;
(d) keep the adult's personal information confidential.

SECTION 5. RECOGNITION BY THIRD PARTIES.

Third parties presented with a supported decision-making agreement
shall recognize the agreement and allow the supporter to accompany
and assist the adult in decision-making, including accessing records
as specified in the agreement.

SECTION 6. GUARDIANSHIP PROCEEDINGS.

(a) Before appointing a guardian, the court shall consider whether
the alleged incapacitated person's needs can be met through supported
decision-making.

(b) In any proceeding to establish guardianship, the petitioner
shall present evidence that supported decision-making and other
less restrictive alternatives have been considered and are insufficient.

(c) Courts shall review existing guardianships upon request to
determine whether supported decision-making is a viable alternative.
```

**Adaptations**: States should adapt to their existing guardianship statutes. Some states may incorporate supported decision-making into existing probate codes; others may create standalone legislation.

## Regulatory Framework

### CMS HCBS Rate Adequacy Rule

**Existing Authority**: Social Security Act Section 1902(a)(30)(A) (sufficient payment to enlist adequate providers); HCBS Access Rule (42 C.F.R. Section 441.301)

**Draft Regulation**:

CMS should issue a regulation requiring states to:

1. Conduct rate studies for IDD HCBS services at least every 3 years
2. Demonstrate that rates are sufficient to recruit and retain an adequate workforce, as measured by turnover rates below 30% and vacancy rates below 10%
3. Publicly report the percentage of rates allocated to direct care worker compensation
4. Submit corrective action plans when workforce indicators fall below adequacy thresholds

### DOL Section 14(c) Phase-Out Rule

**Existing Authority**: 29 U.S.C. Section 214(c) and implementing regulations at 29 C.F.R. Part 525

DOL can restrict the issuance and renewal of Section 14(c) certificates through regulatory action, even absent legislation.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Spending Clause**: Congress can condition Medicaid funding on HCBS requirements under the Spending Clause, subject to the coercion limits established in *NFIB v. Sebelius* (2012). A mandatory HCBS benefit would need to be structured to avoid the "gun to the head" problem identified in that case.
- **Tenth Amendment**: Federal mandates on state service systems must be implemented through Medicaid conditions rather than direct commands to comply with *New York v. United States* (1992).
- **Due Process**: Section 14(c) phase-out does not raise due process concerns because there is no constitutional right to pay workers below minimum wage.

### Preemption Questions

- Federal HCBS legislation would preempt state waiver capacity limits
- Section 14(c) repeal would preempt state subminimum wage provisions (though most state bans are already more protective)
- Supported decision-making is primarily a state law matter; federal legislation would supplement, not replace, state laws

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- CMS can enforce HCBS requirements through waiver compliance reviews and corrective action plans
- DOJ can enforce *Olmstead* integration mandate through ADA Title II enforcement
- Private right of action under Medicaid Act Section 1983 (subject to ongoing litigation over scope)
- P&A agencies provide legal advocacy for individuals

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### HCBS Access Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Institutional level of care manipulation | States could raise the institutional level of care threshold to reduce the population eligible for mandatory HCBS | Critical |
| Service adequacy evasion | States could offer nominally mandatory HCBS but provide insufficient service hours or types | High |
| Enhanced FMAP sunset exploitation | States could increase spending during enhanced FMAP period and cut after it expires | High |
| Waiting list relabeling | States could relabel waiting lists as "assessment queues" or "pending determination" lists | Medium |
| Definition narrowing | States could adopt narrow definitions of developmental disability to limit eligible population | High |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address workforce supply | Mandatory benefit without sufficient workforce would create unfunded legal rights | Bill does not link to DSP investment |
| CBO scoring barrier | High cost score may prevent passage | CBO methodology does not account for institutional cost offsets |
| State variation in implementation | Quality and access would still vary significantly by state | Federal-state partnership inherently creates variation |
| Housing not addressed | Community living requires affordable, accessible housing | Housing policy is outside Medicaid scope |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Include anti-manipulation provisions prohibiting changes to level-of-care criteria that reduce eligible populations after enactment
2. Tie mandatory HCBS to the DSP Workforce Investment Act to ensure workforce capacity
3. Require CBO to model institutional cost offsets and employment-related savings in scoring
4. Establish federal service adequacy standards (minimum hours, service types) with CMS enforcement
5. Use the DD Act definition of developmental disability to prevent state definitional narrowing
6. Pair with housing legislation (Section 811 expansion, HCBS housing-related services)

### DSP Workforce Investment Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Independent contractor reclassification | Providers could reclassify DSPs as independent contractors to avoid wage and benefit requirements | Critical |
| Hours manipulation | Providers could reduce worker hours below 20/week to avoid benefits requirements | High |
| Pass-through evasion | Providers could increase administrative costs to absorb rate increases | Medium |
| Small provider exemptions | Small providers might seek exemptions that cover significant portions of the workforce | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| 150% of minimum wage may be insufficient in high-cost areas | Workers in expensive metro areas still cannot afford housing | National floor does not account for regional cost variation |
| Does not address working conditions beyond wages | Burnout, stress, and safety risks are not solely wage-related | Scope limited to compensation |
| Five-year enhanced FMAP may be too short | States may cut wages after enhanced match expires | Temporary federal investment |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Include anti-misclassification provisions consistent with DOL worker classification rules
2. Define "hours worked" broadly to prevent manipulation; prorate benefits for part-time workers
3. Require independent auditing of pass-through compliance
4. Tie wage floor to regional Bureau of Labor Statistics cost data rather than a single national multiplier
5. Make enhanced FMAP permanent for DSP compensation above the wage floor
6. Include provisions addressing staffing ratios, workplace safety, and scheduling predictability

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Multi-bill dependency | Package HCBS, workforce, and employment bills for simultaneous consideration |
| State resistance | Provide robust transition support; use carrots (enhanced FMAP) before sticks |
| Federal administration capacity | Increase CMS staffing for HCBS oversight |
| Data infrastructure gaps | Fund state data systems for workforce tracking, waiting list monitoring, and outcome measurement |
| Litigation costs | Provide P&A agencies with increased funding for enforcement litigation |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Require GAO review of HCBS mandatory benefit implementation at years 3, 5, and 10
- Mandate DOL annual reporting on Section 14(c) phase-out progress and employment outcomes
- Require CMS biennial assessment of DSP workforce indicators and rate adequacy
- Establish congressional reauthorization requirement for enhanced FMAP provisions at year 7
