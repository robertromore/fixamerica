# Digital Accessibility: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Digital accessibility legislation must address the regulatory vacuum that has allowed persistent noncompliance despite existing ADA mandates. The legislative approach combines federal bills establishing explicit digital accessibility standards for the private sector, strengthening enforcement mechanisms, reforming education and procurement systems, and providing a regulatory framework that adapts to technological change. No constitutional amendments are required, as Congress has clear authority under the Commerce Clause and the Fourteenth Amendment to regulate digital accessibility.

## Constitutional Amendments

No constitutional amendments are required for digital accessibility reform. Congress has established authority to:

- Regulate interstate commerce, including internet-based services (Commerce Clause)
- Enforce the equal protection and due process guarantees of the Fourteenth Amendment
- Condition federal funding on accessibility compliance (Spending Clause)
- Regulate federal procurement and agency operations (Necessary and Proper Clause)

## Federal Legislation

### Digital Accessibility Act

**Purpose**: Establish explicit, enforceable digital accessibility standards for private-sector websites and applications, filling the regulatory gap that has persisted since the ADA's enactment.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Digital Accessibility Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.
Congress finds that—
(1) 61 million Americans live with disabilities that affect their
    ability to use digital technology;
(2) over 95 percent of the most visited websites in the United
    States fail to meet internationally recognized accessibility
    standards;
(3) the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 prohibits
    discrimination in places of public accommodation, which
    includes websites and digital services;
(4) the absence of explicit federal regulations specifying digital
    accessibility standards has created uncertainty and reliance
    on costly litigation;
(5) the European Union, United Kingdom, Canada, and Australia
    have enacted explicit digital accessibility requirements for
    the private sector; and
(6) accessible digital design benefits all users, including older
    adults, users with temporary impairments, and users in
    constrained environments.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.
In this Act—
(a) COVERED ENTITY.—The term "covered entity" means any
    entity that operates a place of public accommodation as
    defined in section 301(7) of the Americans with Disabilities
    Act (42 U.S.C. 12181(7)) and maintains a website, mobile
    application, or other digital service available to the public.
(b) DIGITAL SERVICE.—The term "digital service" means any
    website, web application, mobile application, electronic
    document, kiosk interface, or other digital content or
    functionality made available to the public by a covered
    entity.
(c) ACCESSIBILITY STANDARD.—The term "accessibility
    standard" means the most recent version of the Web Content
    Accessibility Guidelines published by the World Wide Web
    Consortium that has been designated by the Access Board
    under section 5, at Level AA conformance.
(d) SMALL BUSINESS.—The term "small business" means a
    covered entity with annual gross revenue of less than
    $5,000,000 and fewer than 50 employees.

SEC. 4. DIGITAL ACCESSIBILITY REQUIREMENTS.
(a) IN GENERAL.—No covered entity shall discriminate against
    any individual on the basis of disability in the full and
    equal enjoyment of digital services. A covered entity shall
    ensure that its digital services conform to the accessibility
    standard.
(b) COMPLIANCE TIMELINE.—
    (1) LARGE ENTITIES.—Covered entities that are not small
        businesses shall achieve conformance within 24 months of
        the date of enactment of this Act.
    (2) SMALL BUSINESSES.—Small businesses shall achieve
        conformance within 42 months of the date of enactment
        of this Act.
(c) ACCESSIBILITY STATEMENT.—Each covered entity shall
    publish an accessibility statement on its digital service
    that includes—
    (1) the conformance level achieved;
    (2) known accessibility limitations;
    (3) a mechanism for users to report accessibility barriers;
        and
    (4) contact information for the entity's accessibility
        coordinator or responsible party.
(d) CONFORMANCE REPORTING.—Covered entities with annual
    gross revenue exceeding $25,000,000 shall publish an
    Accessibility Conformance Report annually, documenting
    testing methodology, results, and remediation plans.

SEC. 5. STANDARDS DESIGNATION.
(a) INITIAL STANDARD.—The initial accessibility standard
    under this Act shall be WCAG 2.2 Level AA, as published
    by the World Wide Web Consortium on October 5, 2023.
(b) UPDATE PROCESS.—The Access Board shall, not later than
    12 months after the publication of any new version of the
    Web Content Accessibility Guidelines by the World Wide Web
    Consortium, determine whether to designate the new version
    as the accessibility standard under this Act.
(c) TRANSITION PERIOD.—When a new accessibility standard is
    designated, covered entities shall have 18 months to
    achieve conformance with the updated standard.

SEC. 6. ENFORCEMENT.
(a) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—Any person aggrieved by a
    violation of section 4 may bring a civil action in any
    court of competent jurisdiction.
(b) REMEDIES.—In any action under this section, the court
    may award—
    (1) injunctive relief;
    (2) statutory damages of not less than $1,000 and not more
        than $10,000 per violation;
    (3) reasonable attorney fees and costs; and
    (4) any other relief the court determines appropriate.
(c) DOJ ENFORCEMENT.—The Attorney General may bring a civil
    action to enforce this Act, with civil penalties of up to
    $75,000 for a first violation and $150,000 for subsequent
    violations.
(d) SAFE HARBOR.—A covered entity shall not be liable under
    this section if it demonstrates that—
    (1) it has conducted or commissioned an accessibility
        evaluation within the preceding 12 months;
    (2) it has a documented remediation plan with specific
        timelines for addressing identified barriers;
    (3) it is making measurable progress toward conformance;
        and
    (4) it provides an accessible alternative means for
        individuals with disabilities to access the service.

SEC. 7. PREEMPTION.
Nothing in this Act shall be construed to preempt any State or
local law that provides greater protections for digital
accessibility than this Act.

SEC. 8. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.
There are authorized to be appropriated—
(a) $50,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2032
    for DOJ enforcement under this Act;
(b) $10,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2032
    for the Access Board to support standards development and
    technical assistance; and
(c) $20,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2032
    for small business accessibility grants administered by
    the Small Business Administration.
```

**Explanation**: The Digital Accessibility Act fills the gap left by the absence of explicit ADA regulations for private-sector digital services. It designates WCAG as the compliance standard with an automatic update mechanism, provides a meaningful private right of action with statutory damages, includes a safe harbor for good-faith compliance efforts, and avoids preempting stronger state laws.

**Potential Challenges**: Industry opposition to statutory damages and compliance timelines. Small business concerns about cost. Constitutional challenges unlikely given established Commerce Clause authority.

**Refinements**: The safe harbor provision addresses business concerns about immediate litigation while maintaining accountability. The small business timeline extension and SBA grant program mitigate cost concerns.

### Section 508 Modernization Act

**Purpose**: Update Section 508 to reference current standards, establish a regular update cycle, strengthen enforcement, and extend accessibility requirements to federally funded technology.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Section 508 Modernization Act".

SEC. 2. UPDATED STANDARDS.
(a) Section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act (29 U.S.C. 794d)
    is amended to require that the Access Board update ICT
    accessibility standards not later than 18 months after
    the publication of any new version of the Web Content
    Accessibility Guidelines by the World Wide Web Consortium.
(b) The initial updated standard under this Act shall be
    WCAG 2.2 Level AA.

SEC. 3. PROCUREMENT ENFORCEMENT.
(a) Each federal agency shall include accessibility
    conformance as a mandatory evaluation factor in all
    information and communications technology procurements
    exceeding $25,000.
(b) The General Services Administration shall maintain a
    public registry of ICT products that have been
    independently evaluated for accessibility conformance.
(c) Voluntary Product Accessibility Templates shall be
    replaced by independently verified Accessibility
    Conformance Reports for all products on the GSA
    Schedules.

SEC. 4. COMPLIANCE MONITORING.
(a) The Office of Management and Budget shall establish an
    annual federal website accessibility monitoring program,
    including automated and manual evaluation of agency
    digital services.
(b) Results shall be published in an annual public report
    and included in agency performance assessments.

SEC. 5. EXTENSION TO FEDERALLY FUNDED ENTITIES.
(a) Any entity receiving federal financial assistance
    exceeding $500,000 annually shall ensure that digital
    services funded in whole or in part by federal funds
    conform to the accessibility standard.

SEC. 6. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.
There are authorized to be appropriated $25,000,000 for each
of fiscal years 2027 through 2032 for the implementation of
this Act.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the persistent lag in Section 508 standards, the weakness of self-reported VPATs, and the failure to extend accessibility requirements to federally funded entities beyond federal agencies.

**Potential Challenges**: Agency resistance to increased oversight. Vendor resistance to independent conformance reporting. Cost of federal monitoring program.

**Refinements**: The $25,000 procurement threshold and $500,000 funding threshold exempt small purchases and small grants from full compliance requirements.

### Accessible Technology Education Act

**Purpose**: Integrate accessibility competency into computer science and design education through funding incentives and accreditation engagement.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Accessible Technology Education Act".

SEC. 2. NSF GRANTS.
The National Science Foundation shall establish a grant program,
funded at $20,000,000 annually, to support the integration of
accessibility and inclusive design into undergraduate and
graduate computer science and design curricula.

SEC. 3. ACCREDITATION ENGAGEMENT.
The Secretary of Education shall convene a working group of
accreditation bodies, disability rights organizations, industry
representatives, and academic institutions to develop
recommendations for including accessibility competency in
program accreditation criteria within 24 months.

SEC. 4. SCHOLARSHIP PROGRAM.
The Department of Education shall establish an accessibility
scholarship program providing up to 1,000 scholarships annually
for students with disabilities pursuing degrees in computer
science, design, or related fields.

SEC. 5. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.
There are authorized to be appropriated $35,000,000 for each
of fiscal years 2027 through 2032 for the implementation of
this Act.
```

**Explanation**: Addresses the root cause of workforce skill deficits by embedding accessibility in the education pipeline rather than relying solely on post-hire training.

**Potential Challenges**: Academic freedom concerns about mandating curriculum content. Slow accreditation reform cycles.

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Digital Accessibility Act

**Purpose**: Provide state-level digital accessibility requirements for state and local government services and state-regulated industries.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "[State] Digital Accessibility Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.
(a) "State digital service" means any website, mobile
    application, or electronic document operated by or on behalf
    of any state agency, county, municipality, school district,
    or public institution of higher education.
(b) "Accessibility standard" means WCAG 2.2 Level AA, or the
    most recent version designated by the [State Accessibility
    Office/equivalent].

SECTION 3. REQUIREMENTS.
(a) All state digital services shall conform to the
    accessibility standard within 24 months of the effective
    date of this Act.
(b) Each state agency shall designate an accessibility
    coordinator responsible for digital accessibility
    compliance.
(c) All state technology procurements shall include
    accessibility conformance as a mandatory evaluation factor.

SECTION 4. COMPLAINT PROCESS.
(a) Any individual may file a digital accessibility complaint
    with the [State Human Rights Commission/equivalent].
(b) Complaints shall be investigated within 60 days.
(c) Remedies include injunctive relief, compensatory damages
    up to $5,000, and attorney fees.

SECTION 5. REPORTING.
Each state agency shall publish an annual digital accessibility
report including conformance evaluation results, complaints
received, and remediation plans.
```

**Adaptations**: States can customize thresholds, timelines, enforcement mechanisms, and designated oversight bodies. States with existing disability rights agencies can integrate digital accessibility into existing enforcement structures.

## Regulatory Framework

### DOJ ADA Title III Digital Accessibility Rule

**Existing Authority**: ADA Section 308(b)(1) authorizes the Attorney General to issue regulations to carry out Title III.

**Draft Regulation**:

```text
28 C.F.R. Part 36—Nondiscrimination on the Basis of Disability
in Public Accommodations and Commercial Facilities

Subpart H—Web Content and Mobile Application Accessibility

36.801 Scope.
This subpart applies to any public accommodation that maintains
a website, web application, or mobile application available to
the public.

36.802 Accessibility Standard.
(a) Web content and mobile applications shall conform to WCAG
    2.2 Level AA, or the most current version designated by the
    Access Board.

36.803 Compliance Timeline.
(a) Large entities (annual revenue exceeding $25,000,000):
    18 months from effective date.
(b) Other entities: 36 months from effective date.

36.804 Accessibility Statement.
Each covered entity shall publish an accessibility statement
including conformance status, known limitations, feedback
mechanism, and contact information.
```

**Explanation**: This regulation would provide the regulatory clarity that businesses, courts, and disability advocates have sought for decades.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

Digital accessibility regulation is well within congressional authority. The Commerce Clause supports regulation of internet-based services that constitute interstate commerce. The Fourteenth Amendment supports legislation enforcing equal protection for people with disabilities. First Amendment challenges (treating accessibility requirements as compelled speech) have been uniformly rejected by courts, which hold that accessibility requirements regulate conduct, not expression.

### Preemption Questions

The proposed legislation explicitly preserves stronger state laws (anti-preemption clause). States like California, New York, and Illinois have enacted digital accessibility requirements that exceed minimal federal standards. Federal legislation should create a floor, not a ceiling.

### Enforcement Mechanisms

The proposed framework uses three complementary enforcement mechanisms:
1. **Private right of action** with statutory damages (Digital Accessibility Act)
2. **Federal enforcement** through DOJ civil actions with escalating penalties
3. **Procurement leverage** through Section 508 requirements for federal technology

### Sunset and Review Provisions

The standards update mechanism (Section 5 of the Digital Accessibility Act) provides built-in review through automatic evaluation of new WCAG versions. Appropriations are authorized for 6-year periods, requiring congressional reauthorization.

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Digital Accessibility Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Safe harbor exploitation | Entities may create minimal remediation plans to qualify for safe harbor without making meaningful progress | High |
| Small business definition gaming | Entities may structure operations to fall below revenue/employee thresholds | Medium |
| Outsourced content exclusion | Third-party content embedded on covered entity websites may not be addressed | Medium |
| Automated testing reliance | Conformance claims based solely on automated testing miss 60-70% of barriers | High |
| Mobile application scope | Native mobile apps may be treated differently from web applications | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Cognitive accessibility gaps | WCAG standards inadequately address cognitive disabilities | Standards limitation |
| Emerging technology coverage | AI, XR, and IoT not explicitly covered | Technology outpacing regulation |
| Statutory damages ceiling | $10,000 maximum may be insufficient to motivate large corporations | Political compromise |
| Enforcement resource dependency | Effectiveness depends on DOJ funding levels that may vary | Appropriations uncertainty |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Safe harbor should require independent third-party verification of remediation progress, not self-reporting
2. Small business thresholds should use affiliated entity revenue to prevent artificial structuring
3. Covered entities should be responsible for third-party content they embed or integrate
4. Conformance claims should require combination of automated and manual testing with assistive technology validation
5. WCAG 3.0 (when finalized) should address cognitive accessibility gaps through outcome-based scoring
6. Emerging technology provisions should authorize the Access Board to issue interim guidance before formal standards
7. Statutory damages should be indexed to entity revenue for entities exceeding $100 million annual revenue

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Workforce shortage | Accessible Technology Education Act creates pipeline |
| Small business burden | SBA grants, tax credits, accessible platform defaults |
| Standards evolution | Automatic update mechanism with transition periods |
| Enforcement capacity | Dedicated Digital Accessibility Division with $50M budget |
| International harmonization | Standards alignment with EU EN 301 549 |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Standards designation reviewed within 12 months of each new WCAG publication
- Appropriations authorized in 6-year cycles requiring reauthorization
- GAO audit of enforcement effectiveness required within 3 years of enactment
- Annual OMB report on federal compliance
- 5-year congressional review of small business threshold adequacy
