# Digital Accessibility: Overview

## Executive Summary

Digital accessibility -- the design of websites, applications, electronic documents, and digital services so that people with disabilities can perceive, understand, navigate, and interact with them -- has become one of the most consequential civil rights issues of the twenty-first century. As governments, employers, healthcare providers, retailers, and educational institutions have migrated core functions online, inaccessible digital platforms exclude the 61 million Americans with disabilities from employment, commerce, civic participation, healthcare, and education. The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated this shift, making digital access not merely convenient but essential to daily life.

Despite clear legal mandates under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), Section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act, and Section 255 of the Telecommunications Act, the majority of websites and applications remain inaccessible. A 2024 WebAIM analysis of the top one million homepages found that 95.9% had detectable WCAG 2 failures, with an average of 56.8 errors per page. Federal agencies, which are subject to the most explicit digital accessibility requirements, achieved only 30% full compliance with Section 508 standards according to the Government Accountability Office (GAO, 2023). Private sector compliance is worse. Over 4,600 web accessibility lawsuits were filed in federal courts in 2023 alone, yet this litigation-driven enforcement model has failed to produce systemic change.

The consequences of digital inaccessibility are not abstract. Blind users cannot complete online job applications that rely on unlabeled buttons. Deaf users cannot access emergency information delivered through uncaptioned video. People with motor disabilities cannot navigate websites that require precise mouse movements. Individuals with cognitive disabilities are overwhelmed by complex, inconsistent interfaces. These barriers translate directly into unemployment, social isolation, educational exclusion, and preventable health disparities. Comprehensive reform requires updated federal regulations, enforceable technical standards, procurement mandates, workforce development, and a fundamental shift in how the technology industry approaches inclusive design.

## Core Tensions

### Innovation Speed vs. Accessibility Integration

Technology companies argue that accessibility requirements slow product development and innovation. Disability advocates counter that accessibility must be integrated from the design phase ("shift left"), not retrofitted, and that inclusive design often benefits all users. This tension is particularly acute for startups and emerging technology categories where standards may not yet exist.

### Litigation Enforcement vs. Regulatory Clarity

The absence of explicit DOJ regulations applying the ADA to websites has produced a litigation-driven enforcement model. Businesses face uncertainty about what standards they must meet, while people with disabilities rely on costly and slow lawsuits to enforce their rights. Proposed legislative "fixes" like the ADA Education and Reform Act would impose pre-suit notice requirements that advocates argue would effectively gut enforcement without providing alternative compliance mechanisms.

### Technical Standards vs. Functional Outcomes

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) provide a technical framework for accessibility, but compliance with WCAG does not guarantee a usable experience for all disability types. Cognitive accessibility, in particular, is poorly addressed by current standards. Automated testing tools can detect only approximately 30-40% of WCAG failures, meaning that even organizations conducting regular testing may miss significant barriers.

### Private Sector Autonomy vs. Public Accommodation

Businesses argue that mandating specific design standards interferes with creative and business decisions. Disability rights advocates argue that websites and applications functioning as places of public accommodation must be accessible, just as physical storefronts must have ramps and accessible entrances.

## Vision of Success

A digitally accessible America would be one where every website, application, and digital service is usable by people with all types of disabilities as a matter of course. Federal regulations would provide clear, enforceable standards updated on a predictable cycle. Technology companies would employ accessibility professionals, test with disabled users, and treat accessibility as a core quality metric alongside security and performance. Computer science programs would teach inclusive design as a required competency. Government digital services would be models of accessibility, and procurement requirements would ensure that taxpayer-funded technology is usable by all taxpayers. E-commerce platforms, healthcare portals, educational technology, and civic participation tools would work seamlessly with screen readers, voice controls, switch devices, and other assistive technologies. The 61 million Americans with disabilities would navigate the digital world with the same ease and independence as everyone else, and the economic and social benefits of full digital participation would be realized by all.
