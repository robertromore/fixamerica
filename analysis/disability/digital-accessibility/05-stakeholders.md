# Digital Accessibility: Stakeholders

## Overview

Digital accessibility involves a wide range of stakeholders whose interests sometimes align and sometimes conflict. People with disabilities are the primary affected population, but the stakeholder ecosystem includes technology companies, government agencies, educational institutions, standards bodies, legal professionals, and advocacy organizations. Understanding these relationships is essential for designing reforms that build effective coalitions and anticipate opposition.

## Primary Stakeholders

### People with Disabilities

**Population**: 61 million American adults (26% of population), CDC Disability and Health Data System (2023)

| Disability Category | Population | Key Digital Barriers |
|---------------------|-----------|---------------------|
| Vision (blind, low vision) | 7.6 million | Missing alt text, poor contrast, screen reader incompatibility |
| Hearing (deaf, hard of hearing) | 37.5 million with some hearing difficulty | Uncaptioned video, audio-only content, no visual alerts |
| Motor/mobility | 13.7 million with serious mobility difficulty | Mouse-dependent interfaces, small touch targets, time limits |
| Cognitive/neurological | 16.3 million with cognitive difficulty | Complex navigation, inconsistent layouts, no plain language |
| Speech | 3.6 million | Voice-only interfaces without text alternatives |

**Interests**: Full and equal access to digital services, employment through accessible workplace technology, independent completion of tasks others take for granted (banking, shopping, healthcare, education, civic participation)

**Power**: Significant legal standing through ADA and Section 508; growing political organization; limited economic leverage relative to non-disabled consumer base

### Family Members and Caregivers

**Population**: Estimated 53 million informal caregivers (AARP/NAC, 2020)

**Interests**: Reduced burden from managing inaccessible systems on behalf of disabled family members; increased independence for the people they support

**Power**: Significant voting block; often more politically engaged on disability issues than the general public

## Industry Stakeholders

### Technology Companies (Large)

**Key actors**: Apple, Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Meta, Adobe, Salesforce

**Interests**: Maintain voluntary approach to accessibility; avoid prescriptive regulation; market accessibility as competitive advantage; manage litigation exposure

**Power**: Enormous lobbying influence; shape platform standards that affect entire ecosystems; dominate market for operating systems, browsers, and development tools

**Current position**: Major platforms have invested significantly in built-in accessibility features (VoiceOver, TalkBack, Narrator) but resist mandatory external auditing and reporting requirements. Apple and Microsoft have been industry leaders; others have been more uneven.

### Technology Companies (Startups and SMEs)

**Key actors**: Early-stage startups, small software vendors, web agencies

**Interests**: Minimize compliance costs; avoid litigation exposure; focus resources on growth and feature development

**Power**: Collectively enormous economic footprint; individually lack lobbying power; represented through industry associations

**Current position**: Most lack accessibility expertise and budget; often unaware of obligations until faced with litigation; frequently view accessibility as prohibitively expensive

### Web Development and Design Agencies

**Key actors**: Design firms, web development agencies, freelance developers

**Interests**: Client satisfaction; minimize project scope and cost; avoid liability for inaccessible deliverables

**Power**: Shape the accessibility of thousands of websites and applications through their work; significant influence on industry practices

**Current position**: Accessibility expertise varies widely; some agencies specialize in accessible design, but the majority treat accessibility as an add-on service

### Content Management System and Framework Providers

**Key actors**: WordPress (43% of web), Shopify, Squarespace, Wix, React, Angular, Vue

**Interests**: Market share growth; developer adoption; user satisfaction; manage accessibility of default outputs

**Power**: Shape the accessibility baseline for millions of websites through platform defaults, themes, and templates

**Current position**: WordPress and Shopify have made significant accessibility improvements; many platforms still produce inaccessible default output

## Government Stakeholders

### Department of Justice

**Role**: Primary ADA enforcement authority

**Interests**: Enforce civil rights protections; manage limited resources across all civil rights areas; respond to political direction

**Power**: Can bring enforcement actions, issue regulations, publish guidance, enter consent decrees

**Current actions**: Finalized ADA Title II web accessibility rule (2024); Title III rulemaking pending; continues enforcement through settlements and guidance

### General Services Administration (GSA)

**Role**: Section 508 compliance support; government-wide IT accessibility program

**Interests**: Improve federal technology accessibility; provide tools and guidance to agencies

**Power**: Develops testing tools and guidance; manages Accessibility Requirements Tool (ART); no direct enforcement authority

### Access Board

**Role**: Develops accessibility standards and guidelines including Section 508 technical standards

**Interests**: Set technically sound, implementable standards; harmonize with international standards

**Power**: Standards-setting authority; advisory role; cannot independently enforce compliance

### Federal Agencies (as implementers)

**Key actors**: All federal agencies subject to Section 508

**Interests**: Meet accessibility obligations with limited IT budgets; avoid complaints and enforcement actions; serve all members of the public

**Power**: Each agency controls its own IT procurement and development; OMB provides oversight

### State and Local Governments

**Population affected**: Over 90,000 state and local government entities

**Interests**: Comply with ADA Title II web accessibility rule (2024) within deadline; manage compliance costs; serve constituents with disabilities

**Power**: Control their own digital services; collectively represent a major web presence; face new compliance deadline (April 2026/2027)

## Legal and Advocacy Stakeholders

### Disability Rights Organizations

| Organization | Focus | Power/Influence |
|-------------|-------|----------------|
| National Federation of the Blind (NFB) | Blindness and digital access | Major litigation force; drove Target, AOL cases |
| American Council of the Blind (ACB) | Vision disabilities | Legislative advocacy; federal policy influence |
| National Association of the Deaf (NAD) | Deaf/hard of hearing access | Captioning and communications advocacy |
| Disability Rights Advocates (DRA) | Cross-disability litigation | Impact litigation in federal courts |
| National Disability Rights Network (NDRN) | Protection and advocacy | 57 state/territory P&A systems |
| Center for Accessible Technology (CforAT) | Digital inclusion | Policy advocacy and user testing |
| Disability:IN | Business engagement | Corporate disability inclusion index |
| American Association of People with Disabilities (AAPD) | Cross-disability advocacy | Political advocacy and coalition building |

### Plaintiff's Bar

**Key actors**: Accessibility litigation firms (e.g., Lainey Feingold, Disability Rights Advocates, individual attorneys specializing in ADA digital cases)

**Interests**: Enforce ADA through litigation; recover attorney fees; drive compliance through legal pressure

**Power**: File thousands of lawsuits annually; create case law; economically incentivized to identify violations

**Controversy**: Some serial litigants file hundreds of near-identical complaints, which opponents characterize as abusive. Advocates argue this reflects the scale of noncompliance, not abuse of the legal system.

### Defense Bar and Business Associations

| Organization | Position | Influence |
|-------------|----------|-----------|
| U.S. Chamber of Commerce | Supports litigation reform (pre-suit notice); opposes prescriptive regulation | Major lobbying power |
| National Retail Federation | Seeks regulatory clarity; opposes lawsuit-driven enforcement | Retail industry influence |
| Internet Association (dissolved 2022) | Previously opposed web accessibility regulation | Tech industry voice |
| TechNet | Prefers voluntary standards to mandates | Silicon Valley representation |

## Standards and Technical Stakeholders

### W3C Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI)

**Role**: Develops WCAG, WAI-ARIA, and other web accessibility standards

**Interests**: Create technically sound, internationally harmonized accessibility standards; maintain multi-stakeholder development process

**Power**: WCAG is referenced by laws and regulations worldwide; ISO standard status; shapes global accessibility expectations

### International Association of Accessibility Professionals (IAAP)

**Role**: Certifies accessibility professionals; promotes professional development

**Interests**: Grow the accessibility profession; establish competency standards; increase demand for accessibility expertise

**Power**: Controls professional certification (CPACC, WAS, CPABE); shapes professional practice

### Accessibility Testing Tool Vendors

**Key actors**: Deque Systems (axe), WebAIM (WAVE), Level Access, TPGi, Siteimprove, AudioEye

**Interests**: Market growth; demonstrate value of accessibility; shape testing methodologies

**Power**: Define what organizations can detect and measure; influence compliance understanding; growing market ($800 million, Grand View Research, 2024)

## Academic and Research Stakeholders

### Universities and Research Centers

**Key actors**: University of Washington DO-IT Center, Georgia Tech CIDI, University of Illinois DRES, Trace Center (University of Maryland)

**Interests**: Advance accessibility research; train future professionals; develop assistive technologies

**Power**: Shape curriculum and workforce pipeline; publish research that influences policy; develop tools and techniques

## Coalition Opportunities

### Accessibility-as-Quality Coalition

People with disabilities, large tech companies (Apple, Microsoft), accessibility vendors, and standards bodies share an interest in framing accessibility as a quality attribute rather than a compliance burden. This framing could support market-based incentives alongside regulation.

### Procurement Reform Coalition

Federal agencies, GSA, accessibility vendors, and disability rights organizations share an interest in strengthening Section 508 procurement enforcement. This coalition could advance reform through executive action without congressional legislation.

### Education Pipeline Coalition

Universities, IAAP, tech companies, and disability rights organizations share an interest in integrating accessibility into CS and design curricula. This aligns with industry workforce needs and advocacy goals.
