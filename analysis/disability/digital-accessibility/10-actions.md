# Digital Accessibility: Actions

## Overview

This document outlines concrete actions that individuals, organizations, and communities can take to advance digital accessibility. Actions are organized by actor type and effort level, from immediate individual steps to sustained organizational campaigns.

## Individual Actions

### For All Citizens

#### Immediate (Today)

- **Learn the basics**: Read the W3C WAI "Introduction to Web Accessibility" (free, 30 minutes) at https://www.w3.org/WAI/fundamentals/accessibility-intro/
- **Report inaccessible government websites**: File a Section 508 complaint with the relevant federal agency or a Title II complaint with the DOJ Civil Rights Division at https://www.ada.gov/file-a-complaint/
- **Contact elected officials**: Ask your congressional representative whether they support or oppose the ADA Education and Reform Act, and communicate your position
- **Share accessibility barriers**: When you encounter an inaccessible website or app, contact the organization directly and describe the barrier (most companies have accessibility feedback forms or email addresses)

#### Short-Term (This Month)

- **Test your own digital presence**: Use the WAVE tool (https://wave.webaim.org) to evaluate any website you own or manage
- **Enable accessibility features**: Try using your phone or computer with accessibility features enabled (VoiceOver on Apple, TalkBack on Android, Narrator on Windows) to understand the experience
- **Support accessible businesses**: Seek out and patronize businesses that prominently feature accessibility commitments and conformance reports
- **Attend a webinar or training**: Organizations like Deque, WebAIM, and W3C WAI offer free webinars on digital accessibility topics

#### Ongoing

- **Follow disability rights organizations**: National Federation of the Blind, American Council of the Blind, National Association of the Deaf, and Disability Rights Advocates provide action alerts on digital accessibility legislation and policy
- **Amplify disability voices**: Share content from disabled creators, journalists, and advocates who write about digital accessibility
- **Vote for candidates who support disability rights**: Evaluate candidates' positions on ADA enforcement, technology accessibility, and disability civil rights

### For People with Disabilities

#### Asserting Your Rights

- **Document barriers**: When you encounter an inaccessible website, document the issue (screenshots, screen reader output, description of the barrier). This documentation is valuable for complaints and litigation.
- **File formal complaints**: Contact the DOJ Civil Rights Division for ADA complaints, the relevant federal agency for Section 508 complaints, or the Department of Education OCR for educational technology complaints
- **Engage in structured negotiation**: Lainey Feingold's law office (https://lflegal.com) pioneered structured negotiation as an alternative to litigation for resolving digital accessibility barriers
- **Participate in user testing**: Organizations seek disabled testers for accessibility evaluations; this provides compensation and directly shapes more accessible products
- **Contact your state protection and advocacy organization**: Each state has a P&A system that can assist with disability rights complaints, including digital accessibility (find yours at https://ndrn.org)

#### Advocacy

- **Testify at public hearings**: Share your experience with digital accessibility barriers at city council, state legislature, or congressional hearings
- **Join advocacy organizations**: NFB, ACB, NAD, ADAPT, and state-level disability rights organizations organize campaigns on digital accessibility
- **Contribute to accessibility research**: Participate in university and industry research studies on digital accessibility and assistive technology
- **Run for advisory positions**: Many cities and states have disability advisory committees that influence technology procurement and digital service design

## Professional Actions

### For Web Developers and Designers

#### Immediate

- **Learn WCAG 2.2**: Read the WCAG 2.2 Quick Reference (https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/quickref/) and understand the four principles (Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, Robust)
- **Install accessibility testing tools**: Add axe DevTools or WAVE extension to your browser; integrate Pa11y or axe-core into your CI/CD pipeline
- **Write semantic HTML**: Use proper heading hierarchy, label form elements, provide alt text for images, and use ARIA roles only when HTML semantics are insufficient
- **Test with keyboard navigation**: Ensure every interactive element on your pages is reachable and operable using only a keyboard

#### Short-Term

- **Get certified**: Pursue IAAP certification (CPACC for core competencies, WAS for web accessibility specialization)
- **Add accessibility to your workflow**: Include accessibility acceptance criteria in user stories, conduct accessibility reviews in code review, and test with at least one screen reader
- **Advocate within your organization**: Propose adding accessibility to your team's definition of done, quality standards, and sprint review checklists
- **Build an accessible component library**: Create or adopt a UI component library that meets WCAG 2.2 AA standards

#### Ongoing

- **Mentor others**: Teach accessibility skills to junior developers and designers
- **Contribute to open source**: Improve accessibility in open-source projects you use (file issues, submit PRs, review accessibility documentation)
- **Stay current**: Follow W3C WAI, WebAIM, and accessibility community blogs for updates on standards, techniques, and best practices

### For Technology Leaders and Executives

#### Organizational Steps

- **Hire accessibility expertise**: Create dedicated accessibility roles (accessibility lead, accessibility engineer, accessibility designer) or engage qualified consultants
- **Establish an accessibility policy**: Publish an organizational accessibility commitment, including target conformance level, testing methodology, and feedback mechanism
- **Integrate accessibility into procurement**: Require vendors to provide current Accessibility Conformance Reports and demonstrate accessibility in product evaluations
- **Fund training**: Provide accessibility training for all development and design staff, not just designated accessibility specialists
- **Set measurable goals**: Track accessibility metrics (WCAG conformance, user feedback, assistive technology compatibility) alongside other product quality metrics
- **Engage disabled users**: Include people with disabilities in user research, beta testing, and product advisory groups

### For Educators

#### In the Classroom

- **Teach accessibility**: Integrate accessibility into CS, design, and business curricula as a core competency, not an elective topic
- **Use accessible teaching materials**: Ensure course documents, presentations, and online materials are accessible (captioned videos, tagged PDFs, accessible LMS content)
- **Assign accessibility projects**: Include accessibility audits, accessible design projects, and assistive technology testing in coursework
- **Invite disabled speakers**: Bring people with disabilities into the classroom to share their digital accessibility experiences

#### Institutional Advocacy

- **Propose curriculum changes**: Advocate for accessibility requirements in CS and design program curricula
- **Engage accreditation bodies**: Support ABET, NASAD, and other accreditation organizations in adding accessibility to program criteria
- **Publish accessibility research**: Contribute to the academic literature on accessibility education, testing methodologies, and policy
- **Create accessibility resources**: Develop open educational resources (OERs) for teaching accessibility

## Community and Organizational Actions

### For Disability Rights Organizations

- **Monitor federal rulemaking**: Track and comment on ADA Title III rulemaking, Section 508 updates, and related regulatory proceedings
- **Organize constituent testimony**: Coordinate testimony from disabled individuals at congressional hearings, state legislative sessions, and agency public comment periods
- **Build coalitions**: Partner with civil rights organizations, consumer groups, aging organizations, and technology companies that support accessibility
- **Conduct accessibility audits**: Publicly evaluate the accessibility of government services, major retailers, healthcare portals, and educational platforms
- **Train advocates**: Provide accessibility testing skills to disability rights advocates so they can document and report barriers effectively

### For State and Local Government

- **Adopt accessibility procurement requirements**: Require WCAG 2.1 AA conformance for all technology purchases
- **Conduct website audits**: Evaluate all government websites and digital services against current accessibility standards
- **Develop transition plans**: Create and publish plans with timelines for remediating accessibility barriers in government digital services
- **Train staff**: Ensure all employees who create or manage digital content understand basic accessibility requirements
- **Appoint an accessibility coordinator**: Designate a staff member responsible for digital accessibility compliance and coordination

### For Businesses

- **Publish an accessibility statement**: Describe your accessibility commitment, conformance level, known limitations, and how to provide feedback
- **Commission an accessibility audit**: Hire a qualified accessibility firm to evaluate your website and applications against WCAG 2.1 AA
- **Create a remediation plan**: Based on audit findings, develop a prioritized plan to address accessibility barriers
- **Implement accessible design systems**: Adopt or create design systems and component libraries that produce accessible output by default
- **Report conformance**: Publish a current Accessibility Conformance Report (ACR) for your products and services
