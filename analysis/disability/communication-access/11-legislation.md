# Communication Access: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative landscape for communication access rests on existing civil rights statutes (ADA Titles II, III, and IV; Section 504; CVAA) that establish the right to effective communication but lack specific implementation standards, adequate funding mechanisms, and sufficient enforcement resources. This document proposes legislation to close those gaps: a National Communication Access Fund, healthcare communication standards, interpreter workforce development, TRS modernization, captioning expansion, emergency communication accessibility, AAC access reform, and technology platform standards.

## Constitutional Amendments

No constitutional amendment is necessary for the reforms proposed in this document. Existing congressional authority under the Commerce Clause (Art. I, Section 8, Cl. 3) and the Spending Clause (Art. I, Section 8, Cl. 1) provides sufficient basis for communication access legislation. The Fourteenth Amendment, Section 5 enforcement power supports disability nondiscrimination requirements for state and local governments. Title IV of the ADA (47 U.S.C. Section 225), which established TRS, relies on FCC telecommunications regulatory authority that extends naturally to TRS modernization.

## Federal Legislation

### The National Communication Access Fund Act

**Purpose**: Establish a centralized fund to finance interpreter and captioning services across healthcare, government, and public accommodation settings, modeled on the TRS Fund.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "National Communication Access Fund Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
(a) an estimated 48 million Americans have hearing loss and 7.5
    million have speech or voice disorders;
(b) the Telecommunications Relay Services Fund, established under
    section 225 of the Communications Act of 1934, has successfully
    provided telecommunications access for people with hearing and
    speech disabilities for more than 30 years;
(c) no comparable funding mechanism exists for in-person and video
    interpreting, real-time captioning, or other communication
    access services in healthcare, government, legal, educational,
    and employment settings;
(d) the absence of centralized funding results in inconsistent
    communication access that depends on the willingness and
    financial capacity of individual providers, creating an
    inherently unequal system;
(e) 63 percent of deaf patients report being denied interpreter
    services in healthcare settings, and communication failures in
    healthcare result in adverse events at twice the rate for deaf
    patients compared to hearing patients; and
(f) a centralized communication access fund would distribute costs
    equitably, increase interpreter utilization, and reduce the
    communication access failures that endanger health, safety, and
    civil rights.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act—
(a) QUALIFIED INTERPRETER.—The term "qualified interpreter" means
    an interpreter who is certified by the Registry of Interpreters
    for the Deaf or holds an equivalent credential recognized by
    the Secretary, and who is able to interpret effectively,
    accurately, and impartially, both receptively and expressively,
    using any necessary specialized vocabulary.
(b) QUALIFIED CAPTIONER.—The term "qualified captioner" means a
    person who provides Communication Access Realtime Translation
    (CART) and who holds certification from the National Court
    Reporters Association or equivalent credential.
(c) COMMUNICATION ACCESS SERVICES.—The term "communication access
    services" means sign language interpreting, CART, tactile
    interpreting for deafblind individuals, oral interpreting, and
    other services necessary for effective communication with
    individuals who are deaf, hard of hearing, deafblind, or
    speech-disabled.
(d) COVERED SETTING.—The term "covered setting" means any setting
    in which communication access is required under the Americans
    with Disabilities Act of 1990, Section 504 of the
    Rehabilitation Act of 1973, or any other Federal law.

SEC. 4. ESTABLISHMENT OF THE NATIONAL COMMUNICATION ACCESS FUND.

(a) IN GENERAL.—There is established in the Treasury of the United
    States a fund to be known as the "National Communication Access
    Fund" (hereinafter "the Fund").
(b) PURPOSE.—The Fund shall provide reimbursement for the cost of
    communication access services provided by qualified interpreters
    and qualified captioners in covered settings.
(c) ADMINISTRATION.—The Secretary of Health and Human Services, in
    consultation with the Federal Communications Commission, shall
    administer the Fund.
(d) CONTRIBUTIONS.—
    (1) HEALTHCARE ENTITIES.—Each healthcare entity receiving
        Federal financial assistance shall contribute to the Fund
        an assessment equal to 0.05 percent of annual revenues.
    (2) FEDERAL AND STATE GOVERNMENT.—The Federal Government
        shall contribute $200,000,000 annually. States shall
        contribute matching funds as a condition of Medicaid
        participation.
    (3) LARGE EMPLOYERS.—Employers with 500 or more employees
        shall contribute an assessment determined by the Secretary.
(e) DISBURSEMENT.—The Fund shall reimburse qualified interpreters
    and qualified captioners for services provided in covered
    settings at rates established by the Secretary, not less than
    the prevailing market rate in the geographic area.
(f) PRIORITY SETTINGS.—The Secretary shall prioritize Fund
    disbursements for—
    (1) healthcare settings;
    (2) legal proceedings and law enforcement interactions;
    (3) government services;
    (4) emergency situations; and
    (5) employment settings.

SEC. 5. FUND GOVERNANCE.

(a) ADVISORY COMMITTEE.—The Secretary shall establish an advisory
    committee consisting of—
    (1) representatives of the deaf, hard of hearing, deafblind,
        and speech-disabled communities (majority of members);
    (2) representatives of the interpreting and captioning
        professions;
    (3) representatives of healthcare, government, and employer
        stakeholders; and
    (4) technical experts in communication access.
(b) ANNUAL REPORT.—The Secretary shall submit an annual report to
    Congress on Fund operations, utilization, and outcomes.

SEC. 6. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There is authorized to be appropriated $200,000,000 for each of
fiscal years 2027 through 2037 for the Federal contribution to the
Fund and for administrative costs.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment. The Fund shall
be operational not later than 18 months after the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill creates a centralized funding mechanism for communication access services modeled on the TRS Fund, which has successfully funded telecommunications relay services since 1993. By pooling contributions from healthcare entities, government, and large employers, the Fund distributes costs equitably and eliminates the financial disincentive that currently deters individual providers from offering interpreter services.

**Potential Challenges**: Opposition from healthcare industry and employer organizations to assessments; administrative complexity of fund operations; defining covered settings and eligible services.

**Refinements**: Could include a phased assessment schedule; voluntary opt-in for small businesses with tax credit incentives; integration with existing state interpreter service programs.

### The Healthcare Communication Access Standards Act

**Purpose**: Establish binding federal standards for communication access in healthcare settings, including interpreter response times, VRI technical standards, prohibition on unqualified interpreters, and mandatory communication access plans.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Healthcare Communication Access
Standards Act".

SEC. 2. HEALTHCARE COMMUNICATION ACCESS STANDARDS.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Not later than 1 year after the date of enactment,
    the Secretary of Health and Human Services shall promulgate
    regulations establishing standards for communication access in
    healthcare settings receiving Federal financial assistance.
(b) MINIMUM STANDARDS.—The regulations shall include—
    (1) INTERPRETER RESPONSE TIMES.—
        (A) Emergency departments: qualified interpreter available
            within 30 minutes of patient arrival or identification
            of communication need;
        (B) Inpatient settings: qualified interpreter available
            within 1 hour of request for non-emergency needs;
        (C) Outpatient settings: interpreter arranged at time of
            appointment scheduling.
    (2) VRI TECHNICAL STANDARDS.—Where video remote interpreting
        is used—
        (A) minimum screen size of 18 inches diagonal;
        (B) minimum bandwidth of 5 Mbps dedicated;
        (C) high-quality camera and audio equipment;
        (D) adequate lighting and camera angle;
        (E) interpreter verified as qualified for medical
            interpreting; and
        (F) patient consent to VRI use documented.
    (3) PROHIBITED PRACTICES.—
        (A) Use of family members, friends, or minor children as
            interpreters, except in emergencies when no qualified
            interpreter is available and the patient consents;
        (B) Use of unqualified bilingual staff as interpreters;
        (C) Reliance on written notes for complex medical
            communication including diagnosis, treatment
            discussions, informed consent, and discharge
            instructions.
    (4) COMMUNICATION ACCESS PLANS.—Each covered healthcare
        facility shall develop and maintain a communication access
        plan that includes—
        (A) procedures for identifying patient communication needs;
        (B) contracts with qualified interpreter and captioning
            service providers;
        (C) staff training on communication access requirements;
        (D) protocols for VRI use and its limitations; and
        (E) a process for patients to provide feedback on
            communication access.

SEC. 3. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—Any person aggrieved by a violation
    of this Act may bring a civil action for injunctive relief,
    compensatory damages, and reasonable attorney fees.
(b) FEDERAL ENFORCEMENT.—The HHS Office for Civil Rights shall
    investigate complaints and may impose civil penalties of not
    more than $50,000 for a first violation and $100,000 for
    subsequent violations.
(c) INTEGRATION WITH EXISTING LAW.—The standards established under
    this Act supplement and do not replace existing obligations
    under the Americans with Disabilities Act, Section 504 of the
    Rehabilitation Act, and Section 1557 of the Affordable Care Act.

SEC. 4. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill codifies the communication access standards that have emerged through DOJ settlement agreements into binding regulation applicable to all federally funded healthcare facilities. Specific response times, VRI technical standards, and prohibited practices provide clarity that the ADA's general "effective communication" mandate lacks.

**Potential Challenges**: Healthcare industry opposition to response time mandates; enforcement resource requirements; defining "qualified" interpreter for healthcare settings.

**Refinements**: Could include safe harbor for facilities that demonstrate good-faith compliance; could tie compliance to CMS Conditions of Participation; could establish a healthcare interpreter certification program.

### The Interpreter Workforce Development Act

**Purpose**: Expand the ASL interpreter workforce through training program funding, scholarship programs, mentorship pathways, diversity recruitment, and improved compensation.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Interpreter Workforce Development Act".

SEC. 2. INTERPRETER TRAINING PROGRAM EXPANSION.

(a) GRANTS.—The Secretary of Health and Human Services, acting
    through the Health Resources and Services Administration, shall
    award grants to institutions of higher education for the
    establishment or expansion of interpreter training programs.
(b) PRIORITY.—Priority shall be given to programs that—
    (1) are located in states or regions with critical interpreter
        shortages;
    (2) include specialization tracks for healthcare, legal, and
        mental health interpreting;
    (3) demonstrate partnerships with the deaf community for
        mentorship and practicum supervision;
    (4) actively recruit students from underrepresented racial
        and ethnic groups; and
    (5) include training for Certified Deaf Interpreter candidates.
(c) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
    $100,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2037.

SEC. 3. INTERPRETER SCHOLARSHIP PROGRAM.

(a) The Secretary shall establish a scholarship program for students
    enrolled in accredited interpreter training programs.
(b) Each scholarship shall cover tuition, fees, and a living
    stipend for up to 4 years.
(c) Recipients shall commit to 3 years of interpreting service in
    an underserved area or high-need setting upon completion.
(d) There is authorized to be appropriated $75,000,000 for each of
    fiscal years 2027 through 2037.

SEC. 4. INTERPRETER MENTORSHIP AND SPECIALIZATION.

(a) MENTORSHIP.—The Secretary shall fund supervised practicum
    placements for interpreter trainees in healthcare, legal, and
    educational settings.
(b) SPECIALIZATION CERTIFICATION.—The Secretary, in consultation
    with the Registry of Interpreters for the Deaf, shall develop
    and fund specialized certification programs for—
    (1) healthcare interpreting;
    (2) legal interpreting;
    (3) mental health interpreting; and
    (4) DeafBlind interpreting.
(c) There is authorized to be appropriated $50,000,000 for each of
    fiscal years 2027 through 2037.

SEC. 5. INTERPRETER COMPENSATION STUDY.

(a) Not later than 1 year after the date of enactment, the
    Secretary of Labor shall conduct and publish a study on—
    (1) interpreter compensation relative to comparable
        professions;
    (2) the relationship between compensation levels and
        workforce retention;
    (3) the impact of interpreter agency overhead on interpreter
        income; and
    (4) recommendations for compensation structures that support
        workforce sustainability.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the interpreter shortage at its root by expanding the training pipeline, providing financial support for interpreter trainees, creating specialization pathways for critical settings, and studying the compensation structures that drive workforce attrition.

**Potential Challenges**: Appropriations competition; faculty shortages limiting program expansion capacity; timeline for training pipeline to produce qualified interpreters (4-7 years).

**Refinements**: Could include loan forgiveness for practicing interpreters in shortage areas; could fund interpreter training faculty development; could establish interstate licensure compact framework.

### The TRS Modernization Act

**Purpose**: Modernize telecommunications relay services to encompass modern digital communication platforms and update the TRS Fund contribution mechanism.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Telecommunications Relay Services
Modernization Act".

SEC. 2. EXPANDED SCOPE OF RELAY SERVICES.

(a) Section 225 of the Communications Act of 1934 (47 U.S.C. 225)
    is amended to include within the definition of
    "telecommunications relay services" relay services that enable
    effective communication through—
    (1) video conferencing platforms;
    (2) text-based messaging services;
    (3) social media platforms with voice or video features;
    (4) voice-activated devices and assistants; and
    (5) other digital communication technologies as determined
        by the Commission.
(b) FUNCTIONAL EQUIVALENCE.—The Commission shall ensure that relay
    services for digital communication platforms provide functional
    equivalence to direct communication in those platforms,
    consistent with the functional equivalence standard established
    for traditional TRS.

SEC. 3. UPDATED CONTRIBUTION MECHANISM.

(a) The Commission shall update the TRS Fund contribution
    mechanism to reflect the shift from legacy telephony to digital
    communications.
(b) Contributions shall be assessed on entities providing—
    (1) interstate telecommunications services;
    (2) broadband internet access services;
    (3) video conferencing services with more than 10 million
        monthly active users; and
    (4) voice-activated device platforms with more than 10 million
        active devices.
(c) The Commission shall establish contribution rates that maintain
    the Fund at levels sufficient to provide relay services across
    all covered communication platforms.

SEC. 4. VIDEO CONFERENCING RELAY INTEGRATION.

(a) Not later than 2 years after the date of enactment, the
    Commission shall establish standards for the integration of
    relay services with video conferencing platforms.
(b) Video conferencing providers with more than 10 million monthly
    active users shall integrate relay service functionality into
    their platforms within 3 years of the publication of standards.

SEC. 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill updates the TRS system from its telephone-era design to cover the digital communication platforms that are now essential for employment, education, healthcare, and social participation. The updated contribution mechanism ensures sustainable funding as communication shifts from traditional telephony to digital platforms.

**Potential Challenges**: Technology industry opposition to contribution assessments and integration mandates; technical complexity of relay integration with diverse platforms; TRS Fund cost increases.

**Refinements**: Could include pilot program phase before full mandate; could provide technology development grants to VRS providers for platform integration.

## State Model Legislation

### Model Healthcare Interpreter Access Act

**Purpose**: Provide a template for state-level requirements for interpreter services in healthcare settings.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Healthcare Interpreter Access
Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Healthcare facility" means any facility licensed by the
    [State Department of Health] to provide medical, dental, or
    mental health services.
(b) "Qualified interpreter" means a person who holds certification
    from the Registry of Interpreters for the Deaf, a state
    interpreter license, or equivalent credential, and who has
    demonstrated competency in healthcare interpreting.

SECTION 3. REQUIREMENTS.

(a) Each healthcare facility shall ensure that a qualified
    interpreter is available to any deaf or hard of hearing patient
    within—
    (1) 30 minutes for emergency settings; and
    (2) the time of the scheduled appointment for non-emergency
        settings.
(b) Healthcare facilities shall not use family members, friends,
    minor children, or unqualified bilingual staff as interpreters,
    except in medical emergencies when no qualified interpreter is
    available and the patient provides informed consent.
(c) Healthcare facilities shall maintain communication access
    plans, including interpreter service contracts, VRI protocols,
    and staff training requirements.

SECTION 4. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) The [State Department of Health] shall incorporate
    communication access requirements into facility licensing
    inspections.
(b) Failure to comply shall constitute grounds for corrective
    action under the facility licensing code.
(c) Any person aggrieved may file a complaint with the [State
    Human Rights Commission/Attorney General] and may bring a
    private right of action for injunctive relief and damages.

SECTION 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on [date].
```

**Explanation**: This model legislation uses state licensing authority to enforce healthcare communication access standards at the facility level.

**Adaptations**: States may adjust interpreter response times, define qualification credentials based on state licensure, and specify enforcement agencies.

### Model Interpreter Licensure Act

**Purpose**: Provide a template for states that do not currently have interpreter licensure.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Sign Language Interpreter
Licensure Act".

SECTION 2. LICENSURE REQUIREMENT.

(a) No person shall provide sign language interpreting services for
    compensation in [State] without a license issued by the [State
    Interpreter Licensure Board].
(b) Provisional licenses may be issued to persons enrolled in
    accredited interpreter training programs who work under
    supervision.

SECTION 3. LICENSURE STANDARDS.

(a) Full licensure requires—
    (1) certification from the Registry of Interpreters for the
        Deaf (NIC or equivalent); or
    (2) passing a state interpreter performance assessment
        approved by the Board; and
    (3) a minimum of a bachelor's degree or equivalent experience.
(b) Specialized endorsements may be established for healthcare,
    legal, and educational interpreting.
(c) Licensees shall complete 40 hours of continuing education
    every 2 years.

SECTION 4. RECIPROCITY.

(a) The Board shall grant reciprocal licensure to interpreters
    licensed in other states with substantially equivalent
    standards.

SECTION 5. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) Practicing without a license shall be a [Class B misdemeanor].
(b) The Board may revoke, suspend, or condition licenses for
    cause, including incompetence, ethical violations, or fraud.

SECTION 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on [date].
```

## Regulatory Framework

### HHS Healthcare Communication Access Rulemaking

**Existing Authority**: The ADA (42 U.S.C. Sections 12131-12189), Section 504 (29 U.S.C. Section 794), and Section 1557 of the ACA (42 U.S.C. Section 18116) provide authority for HHS to promulgate specific communication access standards for healthcare settings.

**Draft Regulation**: HHS should issue regulations under Section 1557 specifying minimum communication access standards for covered healthcare entities, including interpreter response times, VRI technical requirements, prohibited practices, and communication access plan requirements.

**Explanation**: This regulatory approach can be pursued through executive action without new legislation, providing faster implementation while legislative campaigns proceed.

### FCC TRS Modernization Rulemaking

**Existing Authority**: Section 225 of the Communications Act (47 U.S.C. Section 225) and the CVAA (P.L. 111-260) provide FCC authority to update TRS to encompass modern communication technologies.

**Draft Regulation**: The FCC should initiate a comprehensive rulemaking to extend TRS to digital communication platforms, establish quality standards for VRS providers, update the TRS Fund contribution mechanism, and develop video conferencing relay service standards.

### CMS AAC Device Coverage Reform

**Existing Authority**: CMS has administrative authority to revise Medicare DME coverage policies for speech-generating devices.

**Draft Regulation**: CMS should revise its SGD coverage policy to permit AAC applications on general-purpose tablets and smartphones, reduce approval timelines to 30 days, and expand coverage to include adequate AAC training for users and communication partners.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Commerce Clause**: Federal authority to regulate communication services and technology platforms is well-established under the Commerce Clause. Communication services are interstate commercial activities.
- **Spending Clause**: Conditioning Medicare, Medicaid, and other federal healthcare funding on communication access compliance is permissible. *South Dakota v. Dole*, 483 U.S. 203 (1987). The conditions relate to the federal interest in healthcare quality and nondiscrimination.
- **Fourteenth Amendment**: Section 5 enforcement power supports ADA communication access requirements for state and local governments. *Tennessee v. Lane*, 541 U.S. 509 (2004) (upheld ADA Title II as applied to court access, a setting directly analogous to communication access).
- **FCC Authority**: The Communications Act provides comprehensive authority for TRS regulation. Extending this to digital platforms is consistent with the FCC's general authority over interstate communications.

### Preemption Questions

- Federal communication access standards would preempt less stringent state standards but not more stringent ones, consistent with the ADA framework (42 U.S.C. Section 12201(b))
- TRS modernization legislation would preempt conflicting state regulation of relay services
- State interpreter licensure would remain valid and complementary to federal standards

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- Private right of action with compensatory damages and attorney fees for healthcare communication access violations (addressing current ADA Title III limitation of injunctive relief only)
- Federal agency enforcement through HHS OCR and DOJ Civil Rights Division
- FCC enforcement for TRS and captioning requirements
- State licensing integration for healthcare communication access
- Financial incentives through the National Communication Access Fund

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Healthcare communication access standards reviewed every 5 years to incorporate evolving best practices
- TRS Fund contribution mechanism reviewed every 3 years to reflect communication technology changes
- GAO evaluation of National Communication Access Fund effectiveness after 3 years
- Interpreter workforce pipeline assessed annually by HRSA

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### National Communication Access Fund Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Revenue threshold gaming | Healthcare entities could structure operations to minimize revenue subject to assessment | Medium |
| Qualified interpreter definition | Entities could argue that bilingual staff meet "equivalent credential" standard | High |
| Covered setting ambiguity | Disputes over whether specific settings are "covered settings" under the Act | Medium |
| Priority setting rigidity | Rigid priority ordering could leave employment and education settings persistently underfunded | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address interpreter supply | Fund availability does not create more interpreters | Workforce pipeline separate from funding mechanism |
| Employer assessment resistance | Large employer contributions may face political opposition | Cost concerns from business community |
| State matching fund burden | Low-income states may struggle to match federal contributions | State budget constraints |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Define "equivalent credential" narrowly in regulations with specific certification requirements
2. Establish clear covered-setting criteria with periodic updates as new settings emerge
3. Pair fund with Interpreter Workforce Development Act to ensure supply matches demand
4. Provide enhanced federal matching for low-income states
5. Include anti-avoidance provisions preventing revenue structure manipulation

### Healthcare Communication Access Standards Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Emergency exception abuse | Facilities could claim "emergency" status to use family members as interpreters routinely | High |
| VRI consent as pressure | Patients may feel pressured to consent to VRI when in-person would be more appropriate | Medium |
| Communication access plan as paperwork compliance | Plans could exist on paper without meaningful implementation | High |
| Small practice exemption claims | Practices could argue compliance is an undue burden | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Response time enforcement difficulty | Verifying 30-minute response time compliance is challenging | Real-time monitoring impractical |
| Does not cover non-federally funded facilities | Some cash-pay and concierge practices escape coverage | Spending Clause authority limitation |
| VRI quality depends on infrastructure | Rural facilities may lack bandwidth for quality VRI | Broadband infrastructure gaps |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Require documentation of every instance where the emergency exception is used, with quarterly reporting to HHS OCR
2. Establish VRI consent protocols that include informing patients of their right to in-person interpreters
3. Require communication access plan audits as part of CMS Conditions of Participation surveys
4. Provide broadband infrastructure grants for rural healthcare facilities
5. Extend coverage to all healthcare facilities through ADA Title III enforcement

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Interpreter shortage limits all reforms | Pair every access mandate with workforce investment |
| Technology advancement outpaces regulation | Build adaptive regulatory framework with regular update cycles |
| Enforcement agency capacity | Dedicated appropriations for communication access enforcement |
| Industry capture of standards development | Require majority deaf/HH/speech-disabled community representation on standard-setting bodies |
| Data gaps preventing accountability | Mandatory communication access reporting as condition of federal funding |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- National Communication Access Fund assessed by GAO after 3 years for utilization, cost-effectiveness, and impact on communication access
- Healthcare communication standards reviewed every 5 years by HHS with stakeholder input
- TRS modernization rules updated every 3 years to reflect technology changes
- Interpreter workforce pipeline assessed annually by HRSA with annual report to Congress
