# Communication Access: Actions

## Overview

This document provides concrete actions that individuals, community groups, and advocates can take to improve communication access for deaf, hard of hearing, deafblind, and speech-disabled Americans. Actions range from individual advocacy in everyday settings to systemic change campaigns.

## Individual Actions

### As a Deaf, Hard of Hearing, or Speech-Disabled Person

- **Know your rights**: The ADA requires "effective communication" in healthcare settings, government services, courts, and public accommodations. This means qualified interpreters, CART services, assistive listening devices, or other auxiliary aids, provided at no cost to you. You have the right to state your preferred communication method, and the covered entity must give "primary consideration" to your preference.
- **Request accommodations in writing**: When scheduling medical appointments, government services, or workplace meetings, request your communication access needs in writing (email or patient portal). Specify whether you need an ASL interpreter, CART, assistive listening device, or other accommodation. Written requests create a record.
- **Report communication access failures**: File complaints when your rights are violated:
    - **Healthcare settings**: HHS Office for Civil Rights (hhs.gov/ocr/complaints)
    - **State and local government**: DOJ ADA Information Line (1-800-514-0301) or file complaint at ada.gov
    - **Public accommodations**: DOJ complaint process
    - **Telecommunications**: FCC Consumer Complaint Center (fcc.gov/consumers/guides/filing-informal-complaint)
    - **Education**: U.S. Department of Education Office for Civil Rights
- **Refuse inadequate accommodations**: You have the right to refuse VRI that is not working (small screen, poor connection, inadequate interpreter), family members being used as interpreters, or written notes for complex communication. Document the refusal and the reason.
- **Connect with Protection and Advocacy agencies**: Every state has a federally funded Protection and Advocacy (P&A) agency that provides free legal assistance for disability rights violations, including communication access denials. Find yours at ndrn.org.

### As a Family Member or Communication Partner

- **Learn ASL or the communication method your family member uses**: Enrolling in ASL classes or AAC training demonstrates respect for your family member's communication and reduces reliance on professional interpreters for daily communication. Community colleges, deaf community organizations, and online programs offer ASL instruction.
- **Do not serve as an interpreter in high-stakes settings**: While helping with daily communication is appropriate, family members should not interpret in healthcare, legal, or government settings. Insist that the facility provide a qualified interpreter. Using family members (especially children) as interpreters violates best practices and risks communication errors.
- **Advocate for communication access in schools**: If your child is deaf, hard of hearing, or uses AAC, ensure their IEP or 504 plan includes specific communication access provisions: qualified educational interpreter, CART, assistive listening system, AAC device and training, and communication goals.
- **Document barriers systematically**: Keep a written log of communication access barriers encountered, including dates, settings, specific problems, staff involved, and outcomes. This documentation supports complaints, legal advocacy, and systemic change efforts.

### As a Healthcare Provider

- **Arrange interpreter services before the appointment**: Do not wait for the patient to request an interpreter. If your patient records indicate deafness or hearing loss, proactively arrange interpreter services for every appointment.
- **Provide qualified interpreters, not workarounds**: Do not use family members, bilingual staff without interpreting credentials, or written notes for medical communication. These approaches produce clinically significant errors and violate ADA requirements.
- **Assess VRI appropriateness for each patient**: VRI is appropriate for some patients in some settings but inappropriate for complex communication, deafblind patients, patients with cognitive disabilities, and situations requiring physical presence. When VRI is used, ensure adequate screen size (minimum 18 inches), camera quality, bandwidth, and lighting.
- **Allow extra time**: Interpreted appointments and appointments with AAC users require additional time. Schedule accordingly and do not rush communication.
- **Learn basic communication strategies**: Even brief training in communicating with deaf, hard of hearing, and speech-disabled patients improves care. Resources are available through AUCD provider training modules and ASHA practice guidelines.

### As an Employer

- **Provide communication access in the workplace**: Under ADA Title I, employers must provide reasonable accommodations including interpreters for meetings, CART for training events, captioned video content, accessible communication platforms, and AAC accommodation.
- **Caption all workplace videos and virtual meetings**: Enable captioning on all video conferencing platforms. For important meetings, hire CART providers rather than relying solely on auto-captioning.
- **Budget for communication access**: Communication access is a cost of inclusive employment, not an exceptional expense. Include interpreter and captioning costs in departmental budgets rather than treating them as ad hoc requests.
- **Do not penalize communication pace**: AAC users communicate more slowly than speaking individuals. Allow adequate time for communication in meetings, performance evaluations, and workplace interactions.

## Community Actions

### Organizing for Change

- **Start or join a local communication access task force**: Work with your local Center for Independent Living (CIL), NAD chapter, HLAA chapter, or deaf community organization to monitor communication access in your community. Assess healthcare facilities, government offices, courts, and emergency services for communication accessibility.
- **Conduct community communication access surveys**: Organize volunteer teams to assess communication access at local healthcare facilities, government offices, and public accommodations. Document whether ASL interpreters are available, captioning is provided, assistive listening devices are offered, and accessible formats exist. Publish results.
- **Attend city council and school board meetings**: Advocate for communication access at public meetings. Request that meetings be captioned and interpreted. Advocate for interpreter services at government offices and emergency communications.
- **Monitor emergency communication accessibility**: Assess whether your local emergency management agency provides accessible emergency alerts, whether emergency press conferences include interpreters and captioning, and whether local 911 accepts text messages. Advocate for improvements.

### Advocacy Campaigns

- **State interpreter licensure**: If your state does not have interpreter licensure (19 states lack it), advocate for legislation establishing licensure standards. If your state has licensure, advocate for strengthening standards and reciprocity with other states.
- **Healthcare interpreter access legislation**: Advocate for state laws requiring healthcare facilities to provide qualified interpreter services within defined response times, prohibiting the use of family members as interpreters, and establishing state interpreter registries.
- **Captioning in public spaces**: Advocate for captioning requirements in movie theaters, live performance venues, houses of worship, and public meeting spaces in your community.
- **FCC comment participation**: Submit public comments on FCC proceedings related to captioning quality, TRS reform, and NG911. The FCC's public comment process is a direct avenue for influencing communication access policy. NAD and HLAA regularly issue action alerts with talking points.

## Template Communications

### Email to Healthcare Provider About Interpreter Services

```
Subject: Request for ASL Interpreter Services for Appointment

Dear [Provider/Practice Manager],

I am a patient [or family member of a patient] who is deaf/hard of
hearing and requires the following communication accommodation for my
appointment on [date]:

- Qualified ASL interpreter (in-person preferred; VRI acceptable only
  if adequate screen size, bandwidth, and camera quality are ensured)
- [OR: CART/real-time captioning services]
- [OR: Other specific accommodation]

Under the Americans with Disabilities Act (42 U.S.C. Section 12182)
and Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act (29 U.S.C. Section 794),
healthcare providers must ensure effective communication with patients
who are deaf or hard of hearing, at no cost to the patient. The use of
family members, friends, or unqualified staff as interpreters does not
satisfy this legal obligation.

Please confirm that the requested accommodation will be arranged for
my appointment. If you are unable to provide the accommodation, please
notify me immediately so that I can seek care elsewhere and determine
whether to file a complaint with the HHS Office for Civil Rights.

Thank you for your attention to this matter.

Sincerely,
[Name]
[Contact Information]
```

### Phone Script for Congressional Office

```
Hello, my name is [name] and I am a constituent from [city/zip code].

I am calling to ask [Representative/Senator name] to support
legislation to improve communication access for deaf, hard of hearing,
and speech-disabled Americans.

[Choose one or more]:

- I urge support for establishing a National Communication Access Fund
  to cover the cost of interpreter and captioning services. Currently,
  there is no centralized funding for interpreters outside of telephone
  relay, and 63% of deaf patients report being denied interpreters in
  healthcare settings.

- I urge support for expanding the interpreter training pipeline.
  There are only 10,000 certified ASL interpreters serving 1 million
  deaf Americans, and 30-50% of interpreter requests go unfilled in
  underserved areas.

- I urge support for TRS modernization. The relay system was designed
  for voice telephone calls and does not work with video conferencing,
  text messaging, or voice-activated devices that are essential for
  modern communication.

- I urge support for accelerating NG911 deployment. 30% of 911
  centers still cannot accept text messages, and most cannot accept
  video calls, putting deaf and speech-disabled people at risk in
  emergencies.

This issue affects 48 million Americans with hearing loss and
7.5 million with speech disabilities. Can I get the
[Representative/Senator]'s position on this issue?

Thank you for your time.
```

### HHS Office for Civil Rights Complaint Points

When filing a complaint at hhs.gov/ocr/complaints for healthcare communication access violations, include:

- Name and address of the healthcare facility
- Date(s) of the incident(s)
- Specific communication barrier: interpreter denied, VRI inadequate (specify: small screen, poor connection, unqualified interpreter), family member used as interpreter, written notes used for complex medical communication, no accommodations provided
- How the barrier affected your healthcare: unable to understand diagnosis, unable to give informed consent, medication instructions unclear, unable to ask questions, felt unsafe
- Whether you requested accommodations in advance and how the facility responded
- Names of staff involved (if known)
- Documentation: emails, patient portal messages, photos of inadequate VRI setup, medical records

## Talking Points

### For Conversations with Elected Officials

- **The interpreter shortage is a civil rights crisis**: Only 10,000 certified ASL interpreters serve 1 million deaf Americans. In rural areas, 30-50% of interpreter requests go unfilled. Without interpreters, deaf Americans cannot access healthcare, courts, government services, or employment on equal terms.
- **Communication failure in healthcare kills**: Deaf patients experience adverse events at twice the rate of hearing patients. 63% of deaf patients report being denied interpreters. Communication access in healthcare is a patient safety issue, not a convenience.
- **The TRS Fund model works**: The $1.5 billion TRS Fund has provided telephone access for deaf Americans for 30+ years. Extending this model to cover interpreter and captioning services in other settings would solve the funding problem that drives communication access failures.
- **48 million Americans have hearing loss**: This is not a niche issue. Nearly 1 in 6 American adults has some degree of hearing loss, and the number grows with the aging population. Captioning, hearing technology, and communication access benefit a vast constituency.

### For Conversations with Healthcare Providers

- **Communication access is a patient safety requirement**: The Joint Commission identifies communication as a core patient safety issue. Providing interpreters is not a courtesy; it is a clinical safety practice comparable to hand hygiene or medication reconciliation.
- **VRI is not always sufficient**: VRI is a valuable tool but inappropriate for complex medical communication, deafblind patients, mental health settings, and situations where it has proven ineffective. In-person interpreters must remain available.
- **Family members are not interpreters**: Using family members to interpret medical communication produces clinically significant errors, violates patient privacy, and creates role conflicts. Qualified medical interpreters are essential.
- **It is the law**: The ADA and Section 504 require effective communication in healthcare. Proactive compliance is less costly than DOJ investigations, settlements, and malpractice claims.

### For Media and Public Communication

- **Frame as civil rights, not charity**: Communication access is a legal right under the ADA, not a favor. Deaf Americans are entitled to the same access to information, services, and participation as hearing Americans.
- **Use real stories**: A deaf patient who receives a wrong medication because no interpreter was provided. A deaf person who cannot call 911 during an emergency. A child who serves as interpreter for a parent during a cancer diagnosis. Concrete examples are more powerful than statistics.
- **Connect to universal experience**: Nearly everyone will experience hearing loss as they age. Captioning benefits people in noisy environments, language learners, and those with auditory processing differences. Communication access is a universal design issue.
