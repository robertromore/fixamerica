# Communication Access: Overview

## Executive Summary

Communication is fundamental to participation in every aspect of American life, from employment and education to healthcare, civic engagement, and social connection. Yet millions of Americans with communication-related disabilities, including people who are deaf or hard of hearing, people with speech disabilities, people who are deafblind, and people who use augmentative and alternative communication (AAC), face pervasive barriers to effective communication across virtually every setting. Despite legal mandates under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act, and the Telecommunications Act, enforcement is weak, compliance is inconsistent, and technological advances have created new access gaps even as they solve old ones.

An estimated 48 million Americans have some degree of hearing loss, including 1 million who are functionally deaf. Approximately 7.5 million Americans have voice or speech disorders. Hundreds of thousands rely on AAC devices, sign language interpreters, real-time captioning, or other communication supports to participate in daily life. For these individuals, communication access is not a convenience but a civil right, as fundamental as physical accessibility for wheelchair users.

The current system of communication access in the United States is characterized by critical shortages of qualified American Sign Language (ASL) interpreters, inconsistent captioning quality, inaccessible emergency communications, barriers in healthcare settings that jeopardize patient safety, and a telecommunications relay system that has not kept pace with the shift from voice telephony to digital communication. Addressing these failures requires strengthening legal requirements, expanding the communication access workforce, leveraging technology appropriately, and ensuring that emerging communication platforms, from video conferencing to artificial intelligence, are designed for accessibility from the outset.

- **Sign language interpreting**: ASL interpreter availability, qualifications, compensation, and deployment across settings (healthcare, legal, education, government, employment)
- **Captioning**: Real-time captioning (CART), broadcast captioning, web and video captioning, and automatic speech recognition (ASR) quality
- **Accessible formats**: Braille, large print, plain language, easy-read, and accessible digital formats
- **Telecommunications relay**: Traditional relay services, Video Relay Service (VRS), Internet Protocol Relay (IP Relay), and emerging technologies
- **Augmentative and alternative communication (AAC)**: Devices, software, funding, and accommodation requirements

- **Emergency communications**: 911 accessibility, emergency alerts, and disaster communication for people with communication disabilities

## Core Tensions

### Interpreter Quality vs. Interpreter Availability

The most critical tension in communication access is between maintaining high quality standards for sign language interpreters and addressing the severe interpreter shortage. Stricter certification requirements improve quality but reduce the available workforce. Relaxing standards increases supply but risks providing interpreters who cannot effectively communicate, particularly in high-stakes settings like healthcare and legal proceedings. This tension is especially acute in rural areas, where the shortage is most severe.

### Technology vs. Human Services

Automatic speech recognition, AI-powered captioning, and machine translation are rapidly improving and offer the promise of cheaper, more widely available communication access. However, current technology remains unreliable in many conditions (background noise, accents, technical vocabulary, multiple speakers) and cannot replace human interpreters and captioners for many purposes. The tension between deploying imperfect technology as a cost-saving measure and maintaining human services at higher cost is central to communication access policy.

### Accommodation Request vs. Proactive Access

The ADA generally operates on a request-based model: individuals with disabilities request accommodations, and covered entities must provide them. For communication access, this model creates problems because requesting accommodations requires effective communication in the first place. People who are deaf may not know they need to request an interpreter for a medical appointment if the scheduling system is not accessible. Proactive access, where communication accommodations are built into systems by default, is more effective but more costly.

### Cost Allocation

Who should pay for communication access remains contested. Covered entities under the ADA bear the cost of providing reasonable accommodations, but many small businesses, healthcare practices, and community organizations argue they cannot afford interpreter services that can cost $75-200+ per hour. The lack of a centralized funding mechanism for interpreter services, unlike the centralized funding for telecommunications relay through the TRS Fund, creates an uneven landscape where access depends on the financial capacity of individual providers.

## Vision of Success

Effective communication access would mean that every deaf, hard of hearing, deafblind, or speech-disabled American can communicate effectively in every setting, from routine medical appointments to emergency situations, from job interviews to town hall meetings. Qualified interpreters would be available within reasonable timeframes in every geographic area. Captioning on all video and audio content would be accurate, synchronized, and complete. Emergency communications would be fully accessible through text, video, and other modalities. Healthcare providers would have seamless access to interpreter services, and communication failures would be recognized as patient safety events. AAC users would be able to communicate as fully and rapidly as speaking individuals. Technology would supplement but not replace human communication support where accuracy and nuance are critical. Compliance with communication access requirements would be the norm, not the exception, driven by clear standards, adequate funding, and meaningful enforcement.
