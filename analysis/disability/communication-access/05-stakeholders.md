# Communication Access: Stakeholders

## Overview

Communication access involves a diverse ecosystem of stakeholders whose interests, power, and influence determine whether people with communication disabilities can participate fully in American life. The people most directly affected, deaf, hard of hearing, deafblind, and speech-disabled Americans, have built significant advocacy infrastructure but continue to face institutional resistance from entities that bear the costs of providing communication access. Understanding these dynamics is essential for building effective reform coalitions.

### Family Members and Communication Partners

An estimated 5-10 million Americans serve as primary communication partners for family members with communication disabilities. These individuals bridge communication gaps in healthcare, education, employment, and daily life. Their concerns include ASL instruction access, communication partner training, respite from constant interpreting demands, and recognition that family members should not serve as substitutes for qualified professional interpreters.

## Coalition Dynamics

### Existing Coalitions

**Coalition of Organizations for Accessible Technology (COAT)**: Cross-disability coalition that advocated for CVAA passage and continues to monitor implementation. Includes NAD, HLAA, TDI, AFB, and other organizations. Key strength: proven track record on telecommunications legislation. Key weakness: limited enforcement capacity.

**Telecommunications Access Advisory Committee (TAAC)**: FCC advisory committee bringing together disability organizations, industry, and government. Key strength: direct access to FCC decision-making. Key weakness: advisory only; recommendations not binding.

**CCD Rights Task Force**: Consortium for Citizens with Disabilities task force addressing communication access within broader ADA enforcement framework. Key strength: broad disability coalition support. Key weakness: communication access competes with many other disability priorities.

## Power Analysis

### Most Influential Actors

1. **FCC**: Controls TRS Fund ($1.5 billion annually), captioning rules, and CVAA enforcement, making it the single most powerful actor in communication access policy
2. **Video conferencing and tech companies**: Their product design decisions determine communication access for millions of deaf and hard of hearing people in workplace, education, and social settings
3. **VRS provider companies**: Five certified providers serve as the primary communication access infrastructure for deaf Americans' telephone communication
4. **DOJ Civil Rights Division**: Sets the tone for ADA communication access enforcement through investigation priorities and settlement terms
5. **State interpreter licensure boards**: Control who can work as an interpreter and under what conditions, directly affecting supply

### Least Powerful Actors with Greatest Stake

1. **DeafBlind individuals**: Face the most severe communication barriers but constitute a very small population with limited political visibility; require highly specialized and expensive services (tactile interpreting, support service providers) that are chronically underfunded
2. **AAC users with significant cognitive disabilities**: Cannot self-advocate effectively; depend entirely on others to ensure their communication access needs are met; often excluded from policy discussions about their own rights
3. **Deaf individuals in rural areas and tribal lands**: Face critical interpreter shortages, limited VRI infrastructure due to poor broadband, and few local advocates; geographic isolation compounds political marginalization
4. **Deaf immigrants and refugees**: Face compound barriers of language (may not know ASL or English), immigration status, cultural unfamiliarity with rights, and interpreter unavailability for non-ASL sign languages
5. **Deaf and hard of hearing people in jails and prisons**: Face systematic denial of interpreter services, inaccessible communication with attorneys, and isolation; limited ability to file complaints or advocate for themselves
