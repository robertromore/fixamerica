# Communication Access: History

## The Era of Exclusion (Pre-1960s)

### Deaf Education and the Oralist-Manualist Debate

The history of communication access for deaf Americans is inseparable from the contentious history of deaf education and the suppression of sign language.

**Key Developments**

- 1817: American School for the Deaf founded in Hartford, Connecticut; used sign language instruction
- 1864: Gallaudet University established as the first institution of higher education for the deaf
- 1880: International Congress on Education of the Deaf (Milan Conference) declared oral education superior to sign language; triggered global suppression of sign language in schools
- 1880-1960s: Oralist approach dominated American deaf education; sign language prohibited in most schools
- ASL survived primarily through residential school dormitories, deaf clubs, and family transmission

**Consequences of Oralism**

- Generations of deaf Americans denied access to their natural language
- Lower educational attainment for deaf students educated through oral-only methods
- Linguistic isolation and mental health consequences
- ASL maintained underground, leading to its later recognition as a complete natural language

### Communication Before Technology

Before telecommunications and modern technology, deaf and hard of hearing Americans faced near-total communication barriers outside their immediate communities:

- Written notes were the primary communication method with hearing people
- No access to telephone communication (invented 1876; remained inaccessible for a century)
- No captioning on film (sound films began 1927) or television (captioning began experimentally in 1970s)
- Employment largely limited to manual labor and deaf-community businesses
- No legal right to interpreters in any setting

## The Civil Rights Foundation (1960s-1980s)

### Recognition of ASL as a Language

William Stokoe's 1960 publication *Sign Language Structure* established that ASL is a complete natural language with its own grammar, not a derivative of English. This linguistic recognition transformed the political and legal landscape:

- Legitimized demands for ASL interpreting as communication access (not just a convenience)
- Challenged oralist educational approaches on scientific grounds
- Provided intellectual foundation for deaf civil rights advocacy

### Section 504 and Early Communication Access (1973)

Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 prohibited discrimination based on disability by recipients of federal funding and included communication access requirements:

- Federal agencies required to provide effective communication
- Hospitals, universities, and other federal fund recipients required to provide auxiliary aids and services
- Implementing regulations (1977) specified interpreters, captioning, and other communication access requirements
- Enforcement was complaint-driven and limited

### Telecommunications Access

| Year | Development | Significance |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| 1964 | TTY (teletypewriter) adapted for deaf use | First telecommunication access for deaf people |
| 1968 | Weitbrecht modem enables TTY communication | Made TTY commercially viable |
| 1975 | First state relay service (Connecticut) | Operators relay calls between TTY and voice users |
| 1982 | Decoder Circuitry Act | Required television manufacturers to include caption decoder capability |
| 1988 | Telecommunications Relay Service (TRS) mandated | Required common carriers to provide interstate relay services |

### Captioning History

| Year | Development | Significance |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| 1971 | First open-captioned television broadcast (PBS) | Demonstrated feasibility of TV captioning |
| 1972 | National Captioning Institute (NCI) founded | Developed captioning technology and standards |
| 1980 | First closed-captioned broadcasts | ABC, NBC, PBS begin closed captioning |
| 1982 | Decoder Circuitry Act | Caption decoders required in TVs with 13"+ screens |
| 1990 | Television Decoder Circuitry Act | All TVs 13"+ must have built-in caption decoder chips |

## The ADA Era (1990-2000s)

### ADA Communication Access Requirements (1990)

The Americans with Disabilities Act established comprehensive communication access obligations:

**Title II (State and Local Government)**

- Effective communication obligation for all state and local government programs and services
- Must provide auxiliary aids and services: interpreters, captioners, assistive listening devices, accessible formats
- Primary consideration given to the individual's preferred communication method
- Cannot charge the individual for the cost of auxiliary aids

**Title III (Public Accommodations)**

- Private businesses must provide effective communication
- Auxiliary aids and services required
- May consider "undue burden" defense
- DOJ regulations (28 C.F.R. Part 36) specify requirements

**Title IV (Telecommunications)**

- Required nationwide telecommunications relay services by July 1993
- FCC to establish TRS standards and certification
- Required captioning on all federally funded public service announcements

### Building the TRS System (1990s)

Following the ADA mandate, the FCC established the modern TRS system:

| Year | Development | Impact |
|------|-------------|--------|
| 1993 | Nationwide TRS operational | All states have relay services |
| 1994 | FCC establishes TRS Fund | Interstate carrier contributions fund relay |
| 1999 | STS (Speech-to-Speech) added | Relay for people with speech disabilities |
| 2000 | Video Relay Service (VRS) pilot | ASL interpreters via video; transformative |
| 2003 | VRS mandatory | FCC requires TRS providers to offer VRS |

**The VRS Revolution**

VRS transformed telecommunications for deaf Americans by allowing natural ASL communication through video interpreters, rather than the slow, text-based TTY relay. Usage grew from near zero in 2000 to 30 million calls annually by 2024.

### Captioning Expansion (1990s-2000s)

| Year | Development | Significance |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| 1996 | Telecommunications Act Section 713 | FCC authority to mandate captioning on all video programming |
| 1998 | FCC captioning rules take effect | Phase-in of captioning for new and existing programming |
| 2000 | Full captioning required for new programming | 100% of new English-language programming |
| 2006 | FCC extends captioning to Spanish-language programming | Phased implementation |

### Key Legal Developments

| Year | Case/Action | Impact |
|------|-------------|--------|
| 1994 | DOJ issues ADA technical assistance on effective communication | Guidance on interpreter and auxiliary aid requirements |
| 1999 | DOJ settles with major hospital systems | Established healthcare interpreter requirements |
| 2003 | *Bates v. UPS* (9th Cir.) | Employer must provide effective communication for deaf employee |
| 2005 | *Duvall v. County of Kitsap* (9th Cir.) | Government must provide effective communication; specific accommodation selection |
| 2008 | DOJ settles with University of California | Video captioning in educational settings |

## The Digital Communication Era (2010-Present)

### Twenty-First Century Communications and Video Accessibility Act (2010)

The CVAA was the most significant communication access legislation since the ADA:

**Key Provisions**

| Provision | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Advanced communications | Accessibility of text messaging, email, video conferencing for people with disabilities |
| Video programming | Captioning on previously broadcast content distributed online |
| Emergency access | Accessible emergency alerts and Next Generation 911 |
| TRS modernization | Authority for FCC to update relay services for new technologies |
| Equipment accessibility | Consumer devices must be accessible |

### Next Generation 911

The transition from legacy 911 to IP-based Next Generation 911 (NG911) represents both an opportunity and an ongoing challenge for communication access:

| Year | Development | Status |
|------|-------------|--------|
| 2012 | FCC begins NG911 proceedings | Rulemaking |
| 2014 | Text-to-911 deployment begins | Gradual state-by-state rollout |
| 2020 | COVID-19 accelerates NG911 investment | Federal funding increased |
| 2024 | 70% of PSAPs accept text-to-911 | 48 states with some coverage |
| 2025-2028 | Full NG911 deployment target | Video, RTT, and multimedia 911 planned |

### Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) and AI

The rapid advancement of ASR technology has transformed captioning and communication access:

**Timeline**

- 2016: Google, Microsoft achieve ASR word error rates below 6% on benchmark tests
- 2018: Major video platforms begin offering auto-generated captions
- 2020: COVID-19 pandemic drives rapid adoption of auto-captioning in video conferencing
- 2022-2024: Large language models improve ASR accuracy, especially for accents and technical vocabulary
- 2024: Auto-captioning accuracy reaches 90-95% in ideal conditions; drops significantly with background noise, accents, or technical content

**Regulatory Status**

- FCC has not formally accepted auto-generated captions as meeting broadcast captioning requirements
- Courts have not ruled definitively on whether AI captioning meets ADA "effective communication" standard
- DOJ has not issued guidance on when auto-captioning is sufficient vs. when human captioning is required

### Recent DOJ Enforcement

| Year | Action | Impact |
|------|--------|--------|
| 2014 | Settlement with H&R Block (accessible tax services) | Communication access in financial services |
| 2016 | Settlement with hospitals (interpreter access) | Healthcare communication standards |
| 2020 | COVID-19 communication access enforcement | Accessible testing, vaccination, and telehealth |
| 2022 | Settlement with Uber (communication access for deaf riders) | Rideshare accessibility |
| 2024 | Title II web accessibility rule | Includes requirements for accessible online communication |

## Lessons from History

1. **Language rights are communication rights**: The suppression of ASL for over a century demonstrates that communication access begins with recognizing the right to communicate in one's natural language
2. **Technology creates both solutions and new barriers**: Each new communication technology (telephone, television, internet, video conferencing) initially excluded people with communication disabilities until advocacy forced access
3. **Funding mechanisms matter**: The TRS Fund model (centralized funding from carrier contributions) has sustained relay services for 30+ years; settings without centralized funding (healthcare interpreting) remain chronically underserved
4. **Legal mandates without enforcement are insufficient**: The ADA has required effective communication since 1990, yet compliance remains inconsistent three decades later
5. **Automation is a tool, not a solution**: ASR and AI captioning are valuable supplements but cannot replace human interpreters and captioners for high-stakes communication
