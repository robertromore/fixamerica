# Communication Access: Solutions

## Overview

Addressing communication access failures in the United States requires coordinated reforms across the interpreter workforce, captioning infrastructure, telecommunications systems, healthcare communication, AAC provision, and technology design. The solutions below target the root causes identified in this analysis: workforce shortages, inadequate funding, weak enforcement, technological gaps, and cultural attitudes that treat communication access as optional. Each solution includes an evidence base, cost estimate, timeline, and international models.

## Solution 1: National Communication Access Fund

### Description

Establish a centralized National Communication Access Fund, modeled on the TRS Fund, to finance interpreter and captioning services across healthcare, government, and public accommodation settings. The fund would be financed through assessments on healthcare entities, government agencies, and large employers, distributed to cover the cost of qualified interpreter and captioner services.

### Evidence Base

- The TRS Fund ($1.5 billion annually) has successfully funded telecommunications relay services for 30+ years through carrier contributions, demonstrating the viability of centralized communication access funding (FCC, 2024)
- The absence of centralized funding for in-person interpreting is identified as a critical root cause of communication access failure across healthcare, legal, and government settings (NAD, 2024)
- Countries with centralized interpreter funding (Sweden, Australia) report significantly higher interpreter availability and utilization rates than the United States (European Forum of Sign Language Interpreters, 2023)
- Healthcare interpreter cost is the most cited barrier to ADA compliance in healthcare provider surveys (MGMA, 2023)

### Cost Estimate

- **Annual fund size**: $800 million-$1.2 billion
- **Funded by**: Healthcare entity assessments (0.05% of revenues), federal/state government contributions, large employer assessments
- **Per-encounter cost**: $75-$200 for interpreter services; fund covers qualified provider costs minus nominal copay
- **Administrative overhead**: 8-12% (comparable to TRS Fund administration)
- **Offset**: Reduced preventable adverse events in healthcare, reduced emergency department utilization, reduced malpractice liability

### Timeline

- Year 1: Legislation establishing fund; regulatory framework development
- Year 2: Fund operational for healthcare settings (highest priority)
- Year 3: Fund expanded to government services
- Year 5: Full coverage of all settings with demonstrated demand

### International Models

- **Sweden**: Swedish National Interpreter Service provides centralized interpreter funding through county councils; deaf individuals receive interpreter services at no cost across all settings
- **Australia**: National Auslan Interpreter Booking and Payment Service provides centralized funding for Auslan interpreters in healthcare, legal, and employment settings
- **United Kingdom**: Access to Work program funds interpreter services for deaf employees; NHS provides interpreter services at no cost in healthcare

## Solution 2: Interpreter Workforce Expansion Pipeline

### Description

Launch a comprehensive Interpreter Workforce Development Initiative including expanded training program capacity, scholarship programs, mentorship pathways, diversity recruitment, specialization training in healthcare and legal interpreting, and improved interpreter compensation to retain qualified professionals.

### Evidence Base

- Net new certified interpreters per year (500-700) falls far short of demand growth (3-5% annually), creating a widening gap (RID, 2024)
- Only 20-30% of ITP graduates achieve RID certification within 3 years (Conference of Interpreter Trainers, 2023)
- 87% of interpreters are white despite growing demand for interpreters of color (RID, 2024)
- Healthcare and legal interpreting specialization programs are scarce; most ITPs provide generalist training only
- Average interpreter income ($52,000-$68,000) is insufficient to attract candidates given the 4-7 year training investment required

### Cost Estimate

- **ITP expansion**: $100-$150 million/year for 50 new or expanded training programs nationally
- **Scholarships**: $50-$75 million/year for 5,000 interpreter training scholarships
- **Mentorship programs**: $25-$40 million/year for supervised practicum placements
- **Specialization training**: $30-$50 million/year for healthcare, legal, and mental health interpreter certification programs
- **Diversity recruitment**: $15-$25 million/year for targeted recruitment of interpreters of color
- **Total**: $220-$340 million/year
- **Offset**: Reduced interpreter shortage costs (delayed services, VRI overreliance, communication failures)

### Timeline

- Year 1: Fund ITP expansion and scholarship programs
- Years 1-3: Launch mentorship and specialization programs
- Years 3-5: First cohorts of new interpreters enter workforce
- Years 5-7: Measurable reduction in interpreter shortage (target: 50% reduction in unfilled requests)
- Year 10: Interpreter-to-deaf-population ratio reaches recommended levels

### International Models

- **Finland**: Government-funded interpreter training with guaranteed employment pathways; interpreter-to-deaf-population ratio is 1:30 (vs. estimated 1:100+ in the U.S.)
- **Germany**: Interpreter training is university-based with government scholarship support; professional compensation exceeds U.S. averages
- **Norway**: National interpreter training and registry system with specialized medical and legal interpreter tracks

## Solution 3: Healthcare Communication Access Standards

### Description

Establish binding federal standards for communication access in healthcare settings, including required response times for interpreter services, minimum VRI technical standards, prohibition on using unqualified interpreters (family members, bilingual staff without interpreting credentials), and mandatory communication access plans for all federally funded healthcare facilities.

### Evidence Base

- 63% of deaf patients report being denied interpreter services in healthcare (Deaf Health Survey, 2023)
- Adverse events for deaf patients are 2x higher than for hearing patients, directly linked to communication failure (Annals of Emergency Medicine, 2022)
- DOJ healthcare communication access settlements have established de facto standards that should be codified in regulation (DOJ, 2016-2024)
- The Joint Commission recognizes communication as a patient safety issue but has not established binding interpreter standards
- Only 15-20% of hospitals provide 24/7 in-person interpreter access (NAD, 2024)

### Cost Estimate

- **Rulemaking**: $5-$10 million (one-time HHS cost)
- **Healthcare facility compliance**: $2-$4 billion/year across the healthcare system for interpreter services meeting standards (currently estimated at $1-$2 billion/year, with significant gaps)
- **VRI infrastructure upgrades**: $200-$400 million (one-time) for compliant equipment
- **Training**: $100-$200 million/year for healthcare facility communication access training
- **Offset**: Reduced adverse events, malpractice liability, emergency department utilization, and readmissions

### Timeline

- Year 1: HHS proposed rulemaking on healthcare communication access standards
- Year 2: Final rule published; compliance timeline begins
- Years 2-3: Large health systems (500+ beds) comply
- Years 3-5: Medium facilities comply
- Years 5-7: Small practices comply (with fund support from Solution 1)

### International Models

- **United Kingdom**: NHS Accessible Information Standard (2016) requires healthcare providers to identify, record, flag, share, and meet patients' communication needs; enforced through Care Quality Commission inspections
- **Australia**: Australian Commission on Safety and Quality in Health Care includes interpreter access in national safety standards

## Solution 4: Captioning Quality and Coverage Expansion

### Description

Strengthen FCC captioning quality standards, extend captioning requirements to original online video content, establish standards for when human captioning is required versus when ASR is acceptable, and fund CART provider workforce development.

### Evidence Base

- Broadcast captioning accuracy averages 95-98% against the FCC's 99% standard, with live programming accuracy as low as 92% (FCC, 2024)
- Original online video content is not covered by CVAA captioning requirements despite representing a growing share of video consumption (HLAA, 2023)
- ASR accuracy of 80-95% is inadequate for educational, healthcare, and legal settings (Stanford HAI, 2024)
- CART provider workforce is declining due to aging workforce and reduced court reporter training programs (NCRA, 2024)
- No federal standard defines when human captioning is required versus when ASR is acceptable

### Cost Estimate

- **FCC rulemaking**: $3-$5 million (one-time)
- **Industry captioning compliance**: $500 million-$1 billion/year for expanded captioning on original online video
- **CART workforce development**: $50-$75 million/year for training programs
- **ASR quality standards development**: $10-$20 million (one-time)
- **Offset**: Improved educational outcomes, workplace inclusion, and information access for 48 million Americans with hearing loss

### Timeline

- Year 1: FCC initiates rulemaking on captioning quality and ASR standards
- Year 2: Final rules on captioning quality standards and ASR acceptable use
- Years 2-4: Phased implementation of online video captioning requirements
- Years 3-5: CART workforce expansion through training programs
- Year 5: Measurable improvements in captioning quality and availability

### International Models

- **European Union**: European Accessibility Act (2025 compliance) requires captioning on audiovisual media services, including online platforms
- **Canada**: CRTC captioning rules require 100% captioning on English- and French-language broadcasting with defined quality standards

## Solution 5: TRS Modernization for Digital Communication

### Description

Modernize the Telecommunications Relay Service system to encompass modern digital communication platforms, including video conferencing integration, text messaging relay, accessible voice-activated systems, and social media accessibility. Update the TRS Fund contribution mechanism to reflect the shift from legacy telephony to digital communications.

### Evidence Base

- TRS was designed for voice telephony and has not adapted to video conferencing, texting, voice-activated devices, or social media (FCC, 2024)
- VRS usage (30 million calls/year) demonstrates high demand for relay services, but VRS does not integrate with Zoom, Teams, or other workplace communication tools (FCC, 2024)
- Voice-activated systems (Alexa, Siri, Google Assistant) are inaccessible to deaf and speech-disabled users, creating a growing digital divide
- The TRS Fund contribution mechanism, based on interstate telecommunications carrier revenues, is eroding as communication shifts away from traditional telephony

### Cost Estimate

- **FCC rulemaking**: $5-$10 million (one-time)
- **TRS Fund expansion**: $500 million-$800 million/year additional (on top of current $1.5 billion)
- **Video conferencing relay integration**: $100-$200 million (one-time technology development)
- **Updated contribution mechanism**: Revenue-neutral transition from carrier-based to platform-based assessments
- **Offset**: Improved employment outcomes, social inclusion, and economic participation for deaf and hard of hearing Americans

### Timeline

- Year 1: FCC initiates comprehensive TRS modernization rulemaking
- Year 2: Updated TRS Fund contribution mechanism
- Years 2-3: Video conferencing relay integration pilot programs
- Years 3-5: Full digital communication relay services operational
- Year 5: Voice-activated device accessibility standards implemented

### International Models

- **Australia**: National Relay Service has integrated video relay with modern communication platforms
- **European Union**: European Electronic Communications Code requires member states to ensure relay services are functionally equivalent to direct communications

## Solution 6: Emergency Communication Accessibility

### Description

Accelerate Next Generation 911 (NG911) deployment, mandate text, video, and real-time text (RTT) capability at all Public Safety Answering Points (PSAPs), establish communication access standards for emergency alerts and disaster communication, and fund training for first responders in communicating with deaf, hard of hearing, and speech-disabled individuals.

### Evidence Base

- 30% of PSAPs still cannot accept text-to-911 messages (FCC, 2024)
- Current text-to-911 limitations (no picture/video, character limits, location accuracy issues) reduce effectiveness for deaf callers
- Emergency press conferences and alerts routinely lack interpreters, captioning, or accessible formats (NAD, 2024)
- First responders rarely receive training in communicating with deaf or speech-disabled individuals
- COVID-19 pandemic exposed critical gaps in emergency communication accessibility

### Cost Estimate

- **NG911 acceleration**: $2-$3 billion (one-time) for remaining PSAP upgrades (partial federal funding already authorized)
- **PSAP communication access training**: $50-$75 million/year
- **Emergency alert accessibility**: $25-$40 million/year for accessible alert infrastructure
- **First responder training**: $30-$50 million/year integrated into existing training programs
- **Total**: $105-$165 million/year recurring; $2-$3 billion one-time

### Timeline

- Year 1: FCC accelerates NG911 deployment timeline; emergency alert accessibility standards issued
- Year 2: All PSAPs accept text-to-911 (100% coverage)
- Years 2-4: NG911 with video and RTT capability deployed to 80% of PSAPs
- Year 5: Full NG911 deployment with video, RTT, and multimedia capability
- Ongoing: First responder communication access training integrated into standard curricula

### International Models

- **European Union**: European Emergency Number 112 requires text and real-time text access in all member states
- **United Kingdom**: Emergency SMS service provides text access to 999 for deaf and speech-disabled individuals; BritishSign Language 999 video relay pilot launched 2022

## Solution 7: AAC Access and Rights Protection

### Description

Reform AAC device funding to allow modern tablet-based AAC systems, streamline Medicaid and Medicare approval processes, establish AAC communication accommodation standards, fund AAC training for users and communication partners, and protect AAC users' right to communicate at their own pace.

### Evidence Base

- Medicare requires SGDs to be "locked down" devices, excluding modern tablet-based AAC solutions that are cheaper and more functional (CMS, 2024)
- Medicaid AAC approval timelines of 3-6 months leave individuals without effective communication (ASHA, 2024)
- AAC users report pervasive accommodation failures: being interrupted, ignored, spoken for, or denied time to communicate (Communication First, 2024)
- SLP workforce with AAC expertise is scarce, particularly in rural areas
- An estimated 2 million+ Americans could benefit from AAC but do not have access to appropriate devices or services

### Cost Estimate

- **Medicare/Medicaid SGD policy reform**: Budget-neutral (cost reduction from allowing cheaper tablet-based systems offsets expanded eligibility)
- **AAC training programs**: $50-$100 million/year for user, family, and professional training
- **AAC accommodation standards development**: $5-$10 million (one-time)
- **SLP workforce expansion for AAC**: $30-$50 million/year for AAC specialization training
- **Total**: $85-$160 million/year

### Timeline

- Year 1: CMS revises SGD coverage policy to allow tablet-based AAC; streamlines approval process
- Year 2: AAC accommodation standards developed through stakeholder process
- Years 2-4: AAC training programs established and operational
- Years 3-5: SLP AAC specialization workforce expansion shows measurable results
- Year 5: AAC approval timelines reduced to 30 days or less

### International Models

- **United Kingdom**: NHS provides AAC assessment and provision through regional specialist AAC services; no cost to user
- **Sweden**: County councils provide AAC devices and comprehensive training at no cost; communication is recognized as a fundamental right
- **Australia**: NDIS funds AAC devices and training as "reasonable and necessary" supports

## Solution 8: Technology Accessibility Standards for Communication Platforms

### Description

Establish binding accessibility standards for video conferencing platforms, voice-activated devices, and AI-powered communication tools used in employment, education, healthcare, and government settings. Standards should address captioning quality, sign language visibility, AAC compatibility, and alternative input methods.

### Evidence Base

- Video conferencing platforms became essential communication infrastructure during COVID-19 but lack consistent accessibility features (HLAA, 2023)
- Voice-activated devices and AI assistants are inaccessible to deaf and speech-disabled users (NAD, 2024)
- No binding federal standards exist for communication platform accessibility beyond the CVAA's requirements for advanced communications services
- Technology companies implement accessibility features voluntarily and inconsistently (W3C WAI, 2024)

### Cost Estimate

- **Standards development**: $10-$20 million (one-time through FCC/Access Board)
- **Industry compliance**: $200-$500 million (one-time) across major technology platforms
- **Ongoing compliance monitoring**: $15-$25 million/year
- **Offset**: Accessible platforms serve 48 million Americans with hearing loss, 7.5 million with speech disabilities, and billions globally

### Timeline

- Year 1: FCC initiates rulemaking on communication platform accessibility standards
- Year 2: Standards published; compliance timeline begins
- Years 2-3: Major platforms (Zoom, Teams, Google Meet) achieve compliance
- Years 3-5: Full compliance across covered platforms
- Ongoing: Standards updated every 3 years to reflect technology evolution

### International Models

- **European Union**: European Accessibility Act requires accessibility of digital communication services including video conferencing
- **Canada**: Accessible Canada Act includes digital communication platforms within accessibility requirements

## Sequencing and Priority

| Priority | Solution | Rationale |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| 1 | Healthcare communication standards | Most immediate life-safety impact; builds on existing DOJ enforcement |
| 2 | National Communication Access Fund | Addresses the root cause of cost as compliance deterrent; enables all other solutions |
| 3 | Interpreter workforce expansion | Without more interpreters, other reforms cannot be fully implemented |
| 4 | Emergency communication accessibility | Life-safety priority; NG911 deployment already underway |
| 5 | Captioning quality and coverage | Addresses the most common communication access need (48 million with hearing loss) |
| 6 | TRS modernization | Extends existing successful model to modern communication |
| 7 | AAC access reform | Critical for underserved population; achievable through CMS rulemaking |
| 8 | Technology platform standards | Addresses growing gap; builds on CVAA framework |
