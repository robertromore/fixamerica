# Communication Access: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to communication access reform comes from entities that bear the costs of providing interpreters, captioners, and accessible communication, including healthcare providers, employers, technology companies, and government agencies facing budget constraints. Unlike overt opposition to civil rights, most resistance to communication access operates through cost arguments, technological substitution, procedural barriers, and framing communication access as an exceptional accommodation rather than a baseline requirement. The result is a system where legal rights exist on paper but are inconsistently realized in practice.

## Opposition Landscape

| Opponent | Primary Concern | Strategy | Influence Level |
|----------|----------------|----------|-----------------|
| American Hospital Association (AHA) | Interpreter costs for healthcare settings | Promote VRI as cost-effective alternative to in-person interpreters; oppose mandated interpreter response times | Very High |
| Healthcare provider organizations (MGMA, AMA) | Small practice interpreter costs ($150-$400 per appointment) | Argue undue burden; support voluntary compliance over mandates | High |
| Insurance industry (AHIP) | AAC device and interpreter service coverage costs | Restrictive prior authorization for AAC devices; narrow coverage criteria | Very High |
| Technology companies (major platforms) | Cost of built-in accessibility features; preference for voluntary standards | Support voluntary accessibility; resist binding accessibility mandates for video conferencing and voice platforms | Very High |
| VRI companies | Market dominance of remote interpreting over costlier in-person services | Lobby for expanded VRI use cases; resist limitations on when VRI can substitute for in-person interpreting | Medium-High |
| Broadcast and streaming industry (NAB, NCTA) | Captioning costs and quality compliance burden | Seek exceptions and delayed timelines for captioning requirements; resist expansion to new platforms | High |
| State and local governments | Budget constraints for interpreter services in government programs | Slow compliance with ADA effective communication requirements; underfund interpreter budgets | High |
| Some employer organizations (SHRM, Chamber of Commerce) | Workplace interpreter and accommodation costs | Support tax credits over mandates; argue undue hardship for small businesses | Medium-High |

## Key Opposition Arguments

### Argument 1: Interpreter Costs Are Prohibitive for Healthcare Providers

**Claim**: Requiring healthcare providers to arrange in-person ASL interpreters for every deaf patient encounter imposes unsustainable costs, particularly on small practices, community clinics, and rural healthcare facilities operating on thin margins.

**Evidence cited**: In-person interpreter services cost $75-$200+ per hour with typical 2-hour minimums. A 30-minute follow-up appointment may cost the practice $150-$400 for interpreter services, exceeding the reimbursement for the visit itself. The AHA notes that interpreter costs for a single hospital system can exceed $2-$5 million annually. Small practices with one or two deaf patients may face interpreter costs that constitute a significant share of their operating margin.

**Reality**: The cost argument, while containing real financial data, fundamentally misframes the issue. Communication access in healthcare is a legal obligation under the ADA and Section 504, not an optional service. Healthcare is a $4.5 trillion annual industry; interpreter services represent a tiny fraction of total healthcare spending. More importantly, the cost of communication failure, including misdiagnosis, adverse drug events, unnecessary emergency department visits, malpractice liability, and loss of life, far exceeds the cost of interpreter services. Deaf patients report being denied interpreters in 63% of healthcare encounters, resulting in adverse event rates twice those of hearing patients. The problem is not that interpreter costs are prohibitive but that the healthcare system treats communication access as disposable.

**Rebuttal**: A centralized healthcare interpreter funding mechanism, similar to the TRS Fund model, could distribute costs across the healthcare system rather than burdening individual providers. In the interim, VRI can supplement in-person interpreting for routine appointments, while in-person interpreters must remain available for complex, sensitive, or emergency situations. Tax credits, Medicaid and Medicare interpreter reimbursement, and grant programs can offset costs for small providers. The cost of preventable adverse events from communication failure dwarfs the cost of interpreter services.

### Argument 2: Automatic Captioning Technology Makes Human Captioners Unnecessary

**Claim**: Advances in automatic speech recognition (ASR) technology have made human captioners and CART providers obsolete for most purposes. AI-powered captioning is fast, cheap, and increasingly accurate, making expensive human captioning services an unnecessary cost.

**Evidence cited**: Major technology companies report ASR accuracy rates of 90-95% in controlled conditions. Auto-captioning is now built into Zoom, Microsoft Teams, Google Meet, YouTube, and other major platforms at no additional cost. The cost differential between human CART ($100-$200/hour) and ASR (near-zero marginal cost) is substantial.

**Reality**: ASR accuracy of 90-95% means 5-10 errors per 100 words. In a typical one-hour meeting producing 8,000-10,000 words, this translates to 400-1,000 errors. These errors are not random; they cluster around proper nouns, technical terminology, accented speech, overlapping speakers, and background noise, precisely the conditions most common in the settings where accurate captioning matters most: healthcare, legal proceedings, academic lectures, and workplace meetings. ASR performs worst for speakers of color, non-native English speakers, and people with speech disabilities, creating equity concerns. In healthcare settings, a single captioning error involving a medication name or dosage instruction can be life-threatening. In legal settings, inaccurate captions violate due process rights. The FCC has not accepted auto-generated captions as meeting broadcast captioning quality standards. Courts have not ruled that ASR meets the ADA "effective communication" standard.

**Rebuttal**: ASR is a valuable supplement that improves access in informal settings and for people who previously had no captioning at all. However, it cannot replace human captioning in high-stakes settings where accuracy is essential. The appropriate standard is not whether ASR is "good enough" for hearing people to follow along, but whether it provides communication access equivalent to what hearing people experience. A 95% accuracy rate does not meet that standard for complex communication. Policy should define the settings where human captioning is required and the settings where ASR is acceptable, based on the consequences of error.

### Argument 3: Video Remote Interpreting (VRI) Is Sufficient for Most Settings

**Claim**: VRI provides adequate interpreter access at lower cost and faster response times than in-person interpreting, effectively solving the interpreter shortage problem. Facilities using VRI are meeting their ADA obligations.

**Evidence cited**: VRI providers offer 24/7 service with connection times of 2-5 minutes, compared to hours or days for scheduling in-person interpreters. VRI costs $3-$5 per minute compared to $75-$200+ per hour for in-person interpreters. Over 75% of large hospital systems have adopted VRI.

**Reality**: VRI is a valuable tool in the right circumstances, but it is inadequate or inappropriate in many settings where it is routinely used as a cost-saving measure. DOJ settlement agreements have repeatedly found that facilities relied on VRI in situations where it was ineffective: small screens, poor camera angles, inadequate bandwidth, noisy environments, patients who could not see the screen, patients who were deafblind, patients with cognitive disabilities, and situations requiring physical presence (surgical consent, mental health assessment, complex medical procedures). The NAD has documented systematic VRI failures including disconnections, interpreter qualifications not verified, and interpreters unfamiliar with medical terminology. Some deaf individuals simply cannot use VRI due to deafblindness, vision impairments, cognitive disabilities, or the need for tactile communication.

**Rebuttal**: VRI should be one tool in a communication access toolkit, not a blanket substitute for in-person interpreting. Clear regulatory standards should define minimum technical requirements for VRI (screen size, bandwidth, camera quality), settings where VRI is appropriate (routine appointments with cooperative deaf patients who consent to VRI), and settings where in-person interpreting is required (emergency care, surgical procedures, mental health, complex medical communication, deafblind patients, and any situation where VRI has proven ineffective for the individual).

### Argument 4: Communication Access Mandates Stifle Technology Innovation

**Claim**: Imposing accessibility mandates on emerging communication technologies (video conferencing, voice assistants, AI-powered platforms) will slow innovation and increase costs, ultimately harming the very populations the mandates are intended to serve.

**Evidence cited**: Technology companies argue that accessibility requirements add development time and cost, potentially delaying product launches. The CVAA's requirements for advanced communications services were criticized by some industry participants as technologically prescriptive. Voluntary industry accessibility initiatives (Microsoft Accessibility, Google Accessibility, Apple Accessibility) demonstrate that market incentives drive innovation without regulation.

**Reality**: History demonstrates precisely the opposite. Every major communication technology, from the telephone to television to the internet to video conferencing, was initially designed without accessibility and required legal mandates or sustained advocacy to become accessible. The CVAA, which industry initially opposed, led to captioning on streaming video that now benefits millions of users, including non-disabled users watching in noisy environments or learning languages. Apple's accessibility features, often cited as evidence that mandates are unnecessary, were developed in significant part in response to legal advocacy and Section 508 requirements for government procurement. Voluntary initiatives are inconsistent: companies add and remove accessibility features based on business priorities, and the absence of standards creates fragmentation that harms users.

**Rebuttal**: Accessibility mandates establish a floor, not a ceiling. Companies remain free to innovate beyond minimum requirements. Universal design, building accessibility from the start, is cheaper than retrofitting. The curb cut effect demonstrates that accessibility innovations benefit all users. What mandates prevent is the cyclical pattern where each new technology excludes disabled users for years or decades until advocacy forces access.

### Argument 5: Interpreter Licensure Restricts Supply and Increases Costs

**Claim**: State interpreter licensure requirements restrict the supply of interpreters by creating bureaucratic barriers to entry, driving up costs and worsening the shortage. Reducing or eliminating licensure would increase the interpreter workforce.

**Evidence cited**: Only 31 states have interpreter licensure. In states without licensure, more interpreters are available in the market. Licensure requirements vary widely, creating portability barriers that prevent interpreters from working across state lines. RID certification has a 40-50% pass rate, keeping qualified interpreters out of the workforce.

**Reality**: The interpreter shortage is not caused by licensure but by the fundamental scarcity of people with the necessary bilingual competency in ASL and English. Reducing quality standards does not create more qualified interpreters; it increases the supply of unqualified interpreters, which is dangerous in healthcare, legal, and educational settings where communication errors have serious consequences. Studies of interpreter errors in healthcare settings show that unqualified interpreters produce significantly more clinically significant errors than certified interpreters. States without licensure have higher rates of complaints about interpreter quality, and deaf individuals in those states report less trust in interpreter-mediated communication.

**Rebuttal**: The solution to the interpreter shortage is expanding the training pipeline, not lowering standards. Investments in interpreter training programs, mentorship, pay improvements, and career advancement will grow the qualified workforce. Provisional licensing for interpreters in training, tiered credentials for different settings, and interstate licensure compacts can increase supply without compromising quality in high-stakes settings.

### Argument 6: AAC Devices Are Too Expensive to Cover Comprehensively

**Claim**: Comprehensive insurance coverage for AAC devices, including frequent upgrades and full training, would significantly increase healthcare costs. Current coverage through Medicare and Medicaid DME programs is adequate.

**Evidence cited**: Dedicated speech-generating devices cost $5,000-$15,000. Medicare and Medicaid cover SGDs as durable medical equipment. Annual upgrade costs could add billions to healthcare expenditures if coverage is expanded.

**Reality**: Current AAC coverage is inadequate by multiple measures. Medicare requires SGDs to be "locked down" devices that cannot function as general-purpose computers, preventing users from accessing modern AAC apps on standard tablets, which cost $300-$800 versus $5,000-$15,000 for dedicated devices. Medicaid coverage varies by state, with approval timelines of 3-6 months, during which users lack effective communication. Coverage for AAC training, essential for effective device use, is limited. Device replacement policies do not account for the rapid evolution of AAC technology. The 300,000+ AAC users represent a significant undercount; the actual population who could benefit from AAC is estimated at 2 million or more.

**Rebuttal**: AAC device costs are modest relative to their impact. A $5,000-$15,000 device enables communication for years, at a daily cost of $4-$12. The cost of not providing AAC, including institutionalization, lost employment, preventable healthcare utilization, and diminished quality of life, vastly exceeds the cost of comprehensive AAC coverage. Allowing AAC apps on standard tablets, streamlining approval processes, and covering adequate training would improve access while potentially reducing costs.

## Counter-Strategies

### Reframing Cost Arguments

Present communication access as an investment in participation, safety, and productivity rather than a cost. A deaf employee who receives adequate interpreter services contributes fully to the workplace. A deaf patient who receives an interpreter avoids the adverse events that result from communication failure. A deaf student with CART services completes their education and enters the workforce. Quantify the costs of inaccessibility: preventable healthcare errors, educational underachievement, employment discrimination, and civic exclusion.

### Building Technology Accountability

Work with technology companies' accessibility teams to develop accessibility standards collaboratively while maintaining regulatory backstops. Recognize companies that lead on accessibility (Microsoft's ASL interpreter feature in Teams, Google's Live Transcribe). Use procurement power: federal and state government technology contracts should require communication accessibility compliance.

### Leveraging the TRS Fund Model

The TRS Fund demonstrates that centralized funding for communication access works. The $1.5 billion TRS Fund provides telecommunications access for millions of deaf Americans through carrier contributions. Advocate for extending this model to other communication access settings, particularly healthcare interpreter services, where the absence of centralized funding is the primary barrier to access.

### Using Patient Safety Data

Document and publicize communication failure as a patient safety crisis. Partner with patient safety organizations (The Joint Commission, National Patient Safety Foundation) to establish communication access as a patient safety standard. When healthcare communication failures result in adverse events, ensure they are reported and tracked as patient safety incidents. The healthcare industry responds to safety data more readily than to civil rights arguments.

### State-by-State Advocacy

Pursue state-level interpreter licensure, healthcare interpreter requirements, and emergency communication accessibility where federal action stalls. States like California, Oregon, and Colorado have enacted communication access protections that exceed federal minimums. State-level successes build momentum for federal action and create models for other states.

### Coalition with Aging Organizations

48 million Americans have hearing loss, and the prevalence increases dramatically with age. Communication access is an aging issue as much as a disability issue. Coalition with AARP, the Gerontological Society of America, and aging services networks broadens the political constituency for captioning, hearing technology, and accessible communication far beyond the traditional disability community.
