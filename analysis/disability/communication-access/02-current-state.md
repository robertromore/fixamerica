# Communication Access: Current State

## Sign Language Interpreting

### Workforce Profile (2024)

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| RID-certified interpreters | Approximately 10,000 |
| State-licensed interpreters (all levels) | Approximately 25,000-30,000 |
| Interpreter demand (annual service hours) | Estimated 15-20 million hours |
| Unfilled interpreter requests | 30-50% in underserved areas |
| Average interpreter hourly rate | $50-$85 (staff); $75-$200+ (freelance/specialized) |
| Average annual income (full-time) | $52,000-$68,000 |
| Workforce demographics | 87% white; 83% female |

*Sources: Registry of Interpreters for the Deaf (2024); BLS Occupational Employment Statistics (2024); National Association of the Deaf (2024).*

### Interpreter Shortage by Setting

| Setting | Shortage Severity | Key Issues |
|---------|-------------------|------------|
| Healthcare | Critical | Patient safety risk; specialized vocabulary; emergency availability |
| Legal/courts | Critical | Constitutional rights at stake; specialized certification required |
| K-12 education | High | IDEA requirements; classroom interpreting demands |
| Higher education | High | Complex academic content; long assignments |
| Mental health | Critical | Therapeutic communication requires specialized skills |
| Government services | Moderate-High | Variable by jurisdiction; federal buildings better served |
| Employment/workplace | Moderate | Employer reluctance to pay; VRI used as inadequate substitute |
| Rural areas | Critical | Few interpreters; long travel distances; limited VRI infrastructure |

### Video Remote Interpreting (VRI)

VRI allows real-time interpreting via video connection, expanding access in areas with interpreter shortages but with significant limitations.

**Usage (2024)**

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Hospitals using VRI | 75%+ of large hospital systems |
| VRI provider companies | 15-20 major national providers |
| Average connection time | 2-5 minutes |
| Average session length | 20-30 minutes (healthcare); varies by setting |
| Accuracy concerns | Screen size, camera angle, bandwidth, and environmental factors affect quality |

**VRI Limitations**

- Inadequate for complex medical procedures, mental health, or situations requiring physical presence
- Technology failures (poor bandwidth, small screens, bad camera angles) degrade communication
- Some deaf individuals cannot use VRI due to deafblindness, cognitive disabilities, or preference
- DOJ settlement agreements have specified when VRI is and is not appropriate
- ADA requires in-person interpreters when VRI is ineffective

*Source: National Association of the Deaf, "Position Statement on VRI" (2024).*

### Geographic Disparities

| Region | Interpreter Availability | Key Factor |
|--------|-------------------------|------------|
| Major metro areas | Moderate-Good | Larger interpreter pools; interpreter training programs |
| Suburban areas | Moderate | VRI supplements limited in-person availability |
| Rural areas | Poor-Critical | Few interpreters; long distances; limited VRI infrastructure |
| Tribal lands | Critical | Almost no interpreters; cultural/linguistic barriers |
| U.S. territories | Critical | Very limited; Puerto Rico has Spanish Sign Language considerations |

## Captioning

### Broadcast Captioning

The FCC requires closed captioning on virtually all television programming under Section 713 of the Communications Act, as amended by the Twenty-First Century Communications and Video Accessibility Act (CVAA) of 2010.

**Quality Metrics (2024)**

| Metric | Standard | Actual Performance |
|--------|----------|-------------------|
| Accuracy | 99% (FCC standard) | 95-98% average; varies by program type |
| Synchronicity | Within 2 seconds | Generally met for pre-recorded; 3-5 seconds for live |
| Completeness | All spoken content | Generally met; some omissions in live programming |
| Placement | Not obscuring content | Variable; improving with technology |

**Live Captioning Challenges**

- Breaking news and unscripted programming have lower accuracy (92-96%)
- Speaker identification errors in multi-speaker content
- Technical terminology, proper nouns, and accents reduce accuracy
- Captioner fatigue during extended coverage (captioners typically work 15-20 minute shifts)

*Source: FCC, Closed Captioning Quality Reports (2024); HLAA survey data (2023).*

### Online Video Captioning

The CVAA requires captioning on video content that was previously broadcast with captions when it is distributed online. Original online video content is not covered by the CVAA but may be required under the ADA.

**Current Landscape**

| Platform/Context | Captioning Status | Quality |
|------------------|-------------------|---------|
| Netflix/major streaming | Full captioning | Good (professional captioning) |
| YouTube (uploaded) | Automatic + user-added | Variable; auto-captions 80-90% accurate |
| Social media video | Inconsistent; growing auto-caption adoption | Low-Moderate |
| Corporate training/webinars | Growing adoption | Variable |
| Educational content | Improving; ADA complaints driving compliance | Moderate-Good |
| Live streaming | Emerging auto-captioning | Low-Moderate accuracy |

### CART (Communication Access Realtime Translation)

CART provides real-time verbatim transcription of spoken language, used in classrooms, meetings, conferences, and other settings.

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| CART providers nationally | Approximately 3,000-5,000 |
| Hourly rate | $100-$200+ |
| Accuracy standard | 98%+ for certified providers |
| Remote CART availability | Growing; pandemic accelerated adoption |
| Shortage severity | High; especially in rural areas and for specialized settings |

## Telecommunications Relay Services (TRS)

### Current System

The FCC administers the Interstate Telecommunications Relay Services (TRS) Fund, which compensates providers for relay services that enable people with hearing or speech disabilities to communicate by telephone.

**Service Types (2024)**

| Service | Description | Usage (2024) |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Traditional TRS | Operator reads/types between parties | Declining (1.5 million calls/year) |
| Video Relay Service (VRS) | ASL interpreter via video | Primary service (30 million calls/year) |
| IP Relay | Text-based relay over internet | Moderate (5 million sessions/year) |
| Captioned Telephone Service (CTS) | Real-time captions on phone calls | Growing (18 million calls/year) |
| Speech-to-Speech Relay | Trained operator re-voices for clarity | Limited (500,000 calls/year) |

**TRS Fund (FY 2024)**

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Total fund size | Approximately $1.5 billion |
| Funded by | Interstate telecom carrier contributions |
| VRS per-minute rate | $5.29 per minute (largest expenditure) |
| Providers (VRS) | 5 certified providers |
| Providers (other TRS) | Multiple certified providers |

*Source: FCC, TRS Fund Annual Report (2024).*

### Gaps in Modern Communication

The TRS system was designed for voice telephone calls and has not fully adapted to modern communication:

| Gap | Description | Impact |
|-----|-------------|--------|
| Video conferencing | No relay integration with Zoom, Teams, etc. | Excludes deaf people from workplace meetings |
| Text messaging/chat | TRS does not cover SMS or instant messaging | Workarounds available but not equivalent |
| Social media/voice messages | No relay for voice-based social media features | Excludes from emerging platforms |
| Voice-activated systems | Alexa, Siri, smart home devices | Inaccessible to deaf and speech-disabled users |
| Telehealth | Limited relay integration with healthcare platforms | Barriers to healthcare access |

### Text-to-911

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| PSAPs accepting text-to-911 | 70% (approximately 4,200 of 6,000) |
| States with statewide text-to-911 | 48 |
| FCC target | 100% of PSAPs |
| Limitations | No picture/video; character limits; location accuracy issues |
| Next Generation 911 (NG911) | Deployment in progress; will support text, video, and data natively |

*Source: FCC, Text-to-911 Master PSAP Registry (2024).*

## Healthcare Communication Access

### The Scale of the Problem

Communication failures in healthcare settings for deaf and hard of hearing patients represent both a civil rights violation and a patient safety crisis.

**Key Statistics**

| Metric | Detail | Source |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Deaf patients denied interpreter in healthcare | 63% report being denied at least once | Deaf Health Survey (2023) |
| Adverse events linked to communication failure | 2x higher for deaf patients | Annals of Emergency Medicine (2022) |
| Hospitals with 24/7 interpreter access | 15-20% (in-person); 75%+ (VRI) | NAD survey (2024) |
| Average wait for in-person interpreter (ER) | 2-4 hours in most hospitals | HLAA report (2023) |
| Healthcare providers trained in deaf communication | Less than 5% of medical schools have significant deaf culture training | AAMC survey (2023) |

**Common Failures**

- Using family members (including children) as interpreters instead of qualified professionals
- Relying on written notes for complex medical communication
- Providing VRI with inadequate equipment (small screens, poor camera angles)
- Failing to provide interpreters for follow-up appointments, discharge instructions, or pharmacy consultations
- No interpreter during informed consent discussions for procedures

### Legal Requirements

| Law | Requirement | Enforcement |
|-----|-------------|-------------|
| ADA Title III | Effective communication in healthcare settings | DOJ; private right of action (injunctive only) |
| Section 504 | Communication access in federally funded healthcare | HHS Office for Civil Rights |
| Section 1557 (ACA) | Nondiscrimination including communication access | HHS OCR |
| State laws (varies) | Some states have specific healthcare interpreter requirements | State agencies |

## AAC (Augmentative and Alternative Communication)

### Current Landscape

AAC encompasses all communication methods used to supplement or replace speech for individuals with speech production or comprehension limitations.

**Users and Technology**

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| AAC users in the U.S. | 300,000+ (significant undercount) |
| Speech-generating device (SGD) users | Approximately 100,000 |
| AAC apps (tablet/phone-based) | Growing market; 50+ commercial products |
| Medicare SGD coverage | Covered as durable medical equipment (DME) |
| Medicaid SGD coverage | Required but implementation varies by state |
| Average SGD cost | $5,000-$15,000 for dedicated devices |
| AAC app cost | $100-$300 for comprehensive apps |

**Access Barriers**

| Barrier | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Funding delays | Medicare/Medicaid approval can take 3-6 months |
| Training gaps | Users and communication partners often lack adequate training |
| Device limitations | Devices may not keep pace with communication needs |
| Professional shortage | SLPs with AAC expertise are scarce, especially in rural areas |
| Accommodation failures | Employers, schools, and service providers often fail to accommodate AAC communication pace |
| Emergency access | AAC users cannot communicate effectively in emergencies |

## Key Takeaways

1. **Interpreter shortage is the central crisis**: Approximately 10,000 certified interpreters serve 1 million+ deaf Americans; the shortage is critical in healthcare, legal, and rural settings
2. **Technology supplements but does not replace human services**: Automatic captioning and VRI improve access but remain inadequate for high-stakes communication
3. **Healthcare communication failures are a patient safety crisis**: The majority of deaf patients report being denied interpreters; adverse events are twice as common
4. **TRS has not kept pace with modern communication**: Relay services designed for voice telephony do not cover video conferencing, text messaging, or voice-activated devices
5. **Captioning quality varies widely**: Broadcast captioning is improving but live programming and online content remain inconsistent
6. **AAC users face pervasive barriers**: Funding delays, training gaps, and accommodation failures limit communication for hundreds of thousands of Americans
7. **Geographic disparities are severe**: Rural areas, tribal lands, and territories face critical shortages across all communication access services
8. **Enforcement is complaint-driven and inconsistent**: Proactive compliance with communication access requirements is the exception rather than the norm
