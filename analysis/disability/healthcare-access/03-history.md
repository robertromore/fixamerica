# Healthcare Access for People with Disabilities: History

## Overview

The history of healthcare for people with disabilities in America is one of exclusion, institutionalization, paternalism, and a slow, incomplete transition toward rights-based access. For most of U.S. history, people with disabilities were either warehoused in institutions where healthcare was custodial at best, or excluded from mainstream medical services that were physically and communicationally inaccessible. The disability rights movement of the 1960s-1990s produced landmark legislation, but the translation of legal rights into actual healthcare access has been halting and uneven.

---

## Era 1: Institutionalization and Exclusion (Pre-1960s)

### The Institutional Period

From the mid-19th century through the 1960s, the dominant approach to disability in America was segregation through institutionalization. State-run facilities housed hundreds of thousands of people with intellectual disabilities, mental illness, and physical disabilities. Healthcare in these institutions was characterized by neglect, abuse, and medical experimentation.

- **1848**: The first state institution for people with intellectual disabilities opens in Massachusetts (Walter E. Fernald State School)
- **1907**: Indiana becomes the first state to pass a compulsory sterilization law targeting people with disabilities
- **1927**: *Buck v. Bell*, 274 U.S. 200 (1927), upholds compulsory sterilization; Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes writes "three generations of imbeciles are enough"
- **1935**: Social Security Act creates federal disability benefits but excludes most people with disabilities from mainstream programs
- **1946**: Hill-Burton Act funds hospital construction but includes no accessibility requirements

### Healthcare Implications

Medical care for institutionalized populations was minimal and often harmful. Residents of state institutions received inadequate nutrition, lived in overcrowded conditions, and were subjected to experimental procedures including lobotomies, electroshock therapy, and involuntary sterilization. An estimated 60,000 Americans with disabilities were forcibly sterilized under eugenics laws between 1907 and 1983 (Lombardo, *Three Generations, No Imbeciles*, 2008).

People with disabilities living in the community faced different but equally severe barriers. Medical offices were physically inaccessible, physicians had no training in disability-related health needs, and many providers simply refused to treat disabled patients. No legal framework required accessibility or nondiscrimination.

## Era 2: Rights Emergence and Deinstitutionalization (1960s-1980s)

### The Disability Rights Movement

The 1960s and 1970s saw the emergence of a disability rights movement inspired by and allied with the civil rights movement. Advocates challenged both institutional segregation and community exclusion, demanding access to mainstream services including healthcare.

- **1963**: Community Mental Health Centers Act begins shifting mental health services from institutions to communities, though implementation is chronically underfunded
- **1965**: Medicare and Medicaid enacted; Medicaid becomes the primary insurer for people with disabilities, but initially covers only institutional care
- **1971**: Medicaid adds optional home and community-based services (HCBS) waiver authority, allowing states to fund community alternatives to institutions
- **1972**: SSI program created, providing income support and Medicaid eligibility for low-income people with disabilities
- **1973**: Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act prohibits disability discrimination in federally funded programs, including hospitals and clinics receiving federal funds
- **1977**: HEW Secretary Joseph Califano delays issuing Section 504 regulations; disability activists stage a 25-day sit-in at the San Francisco Federal Building, the longest occupation of a federal building in U.S. history
- **1979**: Section 504 regulations finally take effect, requiring accessible healthcare facilities and effective communication for deaf and blind patients

### The Deinstitutionalization Crisis

Deinstitutionalization moved hundreds of thousands of people with disabilities from institutions into communities that lacked adequate healthcare infrastructure. Between 1967 and 1990, the population of state psychiatric institutions dropped from 426,000 to 92,000, and state institutions for people with intellectual disabilities from 195,000 to 88,000 (Braddock et al., *The State of the States in Developmental Disabilities*, 2015). Many individuals were discharged without adequate community health services, creating ongoing healthcare access crises.

## Era 3: The ADA Era and Managed Care (1990s-2000s)

### Landmark Legislation

- **1990**: Americans with Disabilities Act enacted, requiring accessible healthcare facilities under Title II (public entities) and Title III (public accommodations)
- **1990**: ADA regulations establish that healthcare providers must provide auxiliary aids and services for effective communication, including sign language interpreters
- **1997**: Balanced Budget Act accelerates Medicaid managed care, shifting many disability populations into managed care plans not designed for complex needs
- **1999**: *Olmstead v. L.C.*, 527 U.S. 581 (1999), rules that unjustified institutional isolation violates the ADA; establishes right to community-based services including healthcare
- **2000**: Developmental Disabilities Assistance and Bill of Rights Act reauthorized, strengthening protections for people with intellectual and developmental disabilities
- **2004**: Surgeon General's report, *Closing the Gap: A National Blueprint to Improve the Health of Persons with Mental Retardation*, documents severe health disparities

### Implementation Gaps

Despite the ADA's requirements, healthcare facility accessibility improved slowly. The law imposed no specific equipment standards, and enforcement relied on complaint-driven processes. The DOJ brought few healthcare accessibility cases, and private litigation was hampered by standing requirements and the difficulty of proving that inaccessibility constituted discrimination rather than mere inconvenience.

Medicaid managed care created new barriers. Managed care organizations (MCOs) applied utilization management techniques developed for general populations to disability populations, resulting in inappropriate denials of specialized services, inadequate provider networks, and care coordination failures. States often contracted with MCOs that had little experience serving people with complex disabilities.

## Era 4: The ACA and Modern Reforms (2010-Present)

### Affordable Care Act Provisions

- **2010**: ACA enacted with multiple disability-relevant provisions:
    - Section 1557 prohibits discrimination on the basis of disability in health programs receiving federal financial assistance
    - Essential Health Benefits include habilitative services for the first time
    - Pre-existing condition protections prevent insurance discrimination based on disability
    - Community First Choice Option provides enhanced Medicaid funding for HCBS
    - Medicaid expansion extends coverage to low-income adults, including many with undiagnosed or undocumented disabilities
- **2010**: ACA Section 4203 requires the U.S. Access Board to develop standards for accessible medical diagnostic equipment
- **2013**: HHS issues proposed standards for accessible medical diagnostic equipment (exam tables, weight scales, mammography equipment)
- **2017**: U.S. Access Board publishes final standards for accessible medical diagnostic equipment, but these remain advisory rather than mandatory
- **2024**: HHS finalizes updated Section 1557 regulations incorporating accessible medical equipment requirements for covered entities
- **2024**: CMS issues guidance on Medicaid managed care network adequacy for disability populations

### COVID-19 Impact (2020-2023)

The COVID-19 pandemic exposed and deepened healthcare access failures for people with disabilities:

- Crisis standards of care in multiple states initially included disability-based triage criteria that would have deprioritized disabled patients for ventilators and ICU beds (National Council on Disability, *Medical Futility*, 2019; updated 2021)
- People with intellectual and developmental disabilities experienced COVID-19 mortality rates 2-3 times higher than the general population (Landes et al., *Disability and Health Journal*, 2021)
- Telehealth expansion provided new access opportunities but excluded people without internet access, those who need in-person physical examination, and those whose assistive technology was incompatible with telehealth platforms
- Congregate care settings (group homes, intermediate care facilities) became COVID-19 hotspots

## Lessons from History

### What Has Worked

- **Civil rights legislation with private enforcement**: Section 504 and the ADA created legally enforceable rights that, while imperfectly implemented, have driven significant improvements in healthcare accessibility
- **Medicaid as coverage backstop**: Despite its limitations, Medicaid provides essential healthcare coverage for millions of people with disabilities who would otherwise be uninsured
- **Disability-led advocacy**: The most significant healthcare access reforms have resulted from sustained activism by people with disabilities and their organizations
- **Community-based care models**: Where adequately funded, home and community-based services have produced better health outcomes than institutional care at comparable or lower cost

### What Has Failed

- **Complaint-driven enforcement**: Relying on individuals to file complaints has produced slow, inconsistent enforcement that leaves systemic barriers largely unchallenged
- **Advisory rather than mandatory standards**: The U.S. Access Board's medical equipment standards remain non-binding after nearly a decade, limiting their impact
- **Deinstitutionalization without community investment**: Moving people out of institutions without building adequate community healthcare infrastructure created new access gaps
- **Medical education neglect**: The failure to integrate disability competency into medical training has perpetuated provider knowledge gaps across generations

### Recurring Patterns

1. **Rights on paper, barriers in practice**: Each major legislative advance (Section 504, ADA, ACA) has been followed by prolonged implementation delays and enforcement failures
2. **Crisis as catalyst**: Major improvements (Medicaid expansion, telehealth, equipment standards) have typically come in response to crises rather than proactive planning
3. **Cost-shifting to individuals**: The system repeatedly shifts the burden of access from institutions to individuals with disabilities, who must self-advocate, file complaints, and navigate complex systems to receive care they are legally entitled to
4. **Separate is not equal**: Programs designed specifically for disability populations (institutional care, sheltered workshops, special clinics) have consistently produced worse outcomes than inclusive mainstream services with appropriate accommodations
