# Healthcare Access for People with Disabilities: Solutions

## Overview

Addressing healthcare access for people with disabilities requires coordinated reforms across facility design, provider education, insurance regulation, workforce development, data infrastructure, and enforcement. No single intervention is sufficient; the barriers are systemic and require systemic responses. The solutions below are ordered by impact potential and sequenced for practical implementation.

## Solution 1: Mandatory Accessible Medical Diagnostic Equipment Standards

### Description

Adopt the U.S. Access Board's 2017 Medical Diagnostic Equipment Accessibility Standards as mandatory requirements for all healthcare facilities receiving federal funds, with phased compliance timelines and financial assistance for small practices.

### Evidence Base

- The U.S. Access Board developed comprehensive standards for exam tables, weight scales, mammography equipment, and exam chairs through a multi-year process including public comment (2017)
- California SB 1379 (2022) demonstrated that state-level equipment mandates are legislatively feasible
- Mudrick et al. (2012) documented that only 8.4% of primary care offices have accessible exam tables, establishing the scope of the problem
- National Council on Disability recommended mandatory standards in its 2023 healthcare equity report

### Cost Estimate

- **Per practice**: $10,000-$60,000 for accessible exam tables and scales (one-time)
- **National total**: $1.8-$3.2 billion across all primary care practices (one-time capital expenditure)
- **Offset**: Tax credits under 26 U.S.C. Section 44 ($5,000/year for small businesses), HHS grant program ($200-$400 million over 5 years)
- **Return**: Reduced ED visits, earlier diagnoses, lower downstream treatment costs

### Timeline

- Year 1: Final rulemaking adopting equipment standards
- Years 1-3: Large health systems and hospitals (500+ beds) comply
- Years 3-5: Medium facilities (community health centers, group practices) comply
- Years 5-7: Small practices comply (with grant/tax credit support)

### International Models

- **United Kingdom**: NHS Accessible Information Standard (2016) requires healthcare providers to identify, record, and meet communication and accessibility needs. Equipment standards enforced through Care Quality Commission inspections.
- **Australia**: National Disability Insurance Scheme includes healthcare equipment accessibility standards; Australian Standards AS 1428 series covers medical facility design.

## Solution 2: Disability Competency in Medical Education Accreditation

### Description

Require the Liaison Committee on Medical Education (LCME) and the Accreditation Council for Graduate Medical Education (ACGME) to include disability competency as an accreditation standard, ensuring all medical students and residents receive training in accessible clinical skills, communication with disabled patients, and disability-aware differential diagnosis.

### Evidence Base

- Iezzoni et al. (2021) found that 82.4% of physicians reported receiving "nothing" or "little" training in disability care
- AAMC published disability competency learning objectives in 2023, providing a curricular framework
- University of South Carolina and University of Michigan have implemented disability curricula demonstrating feasibility with minimal curricular disruption
- Studies show that even brief disability training interventions improve provider attitudes and clinical skills (Shakespeare et al., *Medical Education*, 2009)

### Cost Estimate

- **Per medical school**: $50,000-$150,000 annually for curriculum development, guest faculty, and community partnerships
- **National total**: $8-$25 million/year across 157 accredited medical schools
- **Offset**: Integration into existing courses minimizes marginal cost; some content replaces outdated material

### Timeline

- Year 1: LCME and ACGME adopt disability competency standards
- Years 2-3: Medical schools develop and implement curricula
- Years 4-7: First cohorts of disability-trained physicians enter practice
- Year 10+: Critical mass of disability-competent providers in workforce

### International Models

- **United Kingdom**: General Medical Council includes disability competency in *Outcomes for Graduates* standards. UK medical schools average 20+ hours of disability-related content (compared to fewer than 5 hours in U.S. schools).
- **Canada**: CanMEDS framework includes disability cultural competency as a component of the Health Advocate role.

## Solution 3: Insurance Reform for Disability-Specific Coverage

### Description

Enact federal legislation and regulation addressing disability-specific insurance barriers: prior authorization reform for DME and disability services, elimination of annual therapy visit caps, expanded habilitation coverage, standardized network adequacy requirements for disability specialists, and Medicare dental/vision/hearing coverage.

### Evidence Base

- AMA survey (2023): 93% of physicians report prior authorization delays care; 34% report serious adverse events
- GAO (2023): 30-50% of DME claims initially denied; majority overturned on appeal
- Therapy cap elimination in 2018 Bipartisan Budget Act improved access but was replaced with manual medical review threshold
- State prior authorization reform laws in Texas, Oregon, and others demonstrate feasibility

### Cost Estimate

- **Prior authorization reform**: Net savings estimated at $2-$4 billion/year through reduced administrative costs (Council for Affordable Quality Healthcare, 2023)
- **Medicare dental benefit**: $12-$15 billion/year (CBO estimate, 2021)
- **Habilitation coverage expansion**: $3-$5 billion/year
- **Net impact**: Moderate cost increase offset by reduced emergency utilization and improved health outcomes

### Timeline

- Year 1: Executive order directing CMS to strengthen Medicaid managed care disability protections
- Year 2: Federal prior authorization reform legislation
- Years 2-3: Medicare dental/vision/hearing benefit expansion
- Years 3-5: Comprehensive insurance reform for disability-specific coverage

### International Models

- **Germany**: Statutory health insurance includes habilitation, rehabilitation, and assistive devices as standard benefits without prior authorization for most categories.
- **Japan**: National health insurance covers dental, vision, and hearing as standard benefits; specialized disability rehabilitation is covered without visit limits.

## Solution 4: Disability Health Data Infrastructure

### Description

Require disability status data collection, disaggregation, and reporting across all federal health programs, surveys, and quality measures. Establish standardized disability identifiers in electronic health records and require healthcare facilities to report disability-disaggregated quality metrics.

### Evidence Base

- HHS finalized disability data collection rules in 2024, but implementation is incomplete
- NCD (2023) recommended disability-disaggregated quality measures as essential for accountability
- Race/ethnicity data disaggregation requirements have driven measurable improvements in addressing racial health disparities (Nerenz et al., *Health Affairs*, 2009)
- Section 4302 of the ACA requires demographic data collection in HHS surveys but has been inconsistently implemented for disability

### Cost Estimate

- **EHR modifications**: $500 million-$1 billion nationally for standardized disability data fields (one-time)
- **Survey expansion**: $15-$25 million/year for enhanced disability sampling in NHIS, MEPS, BRFSS
- **Reporting infrastructure**: $30-$50 million/year for CMS quality reporting systems

### Timeline

- Year 1: Finalize standardized disability data definitions and collection instruments
- Years 2-3: EHR vendors incorporate disability data fields
- Years 3-5: Healthcare facilities begin reporting disability-disaggregated quality metrics
- Year 5+: Data-driven accountability identifies and targets persistent disparities

### International Models

- **United Kingdom**: NHS collects disability status data through the Summary Care Record and reports disability-disaggregated quality metrics through the NHS Outcomes Framework.

## Solution 5: Workforce Development for Disability Healthcare

### Description

Expand the pipeline of healthcare providers trained in disability care through targeted residency funding, loan forgiveness programs, community health worker training, and incentives for disability-competent providers in shortage areas.

### Evidence Base

- NRMP data (2024): Only 478 physical medicine and rehabilitation residency positions annually
- HRSA (2024): 83% of rural counties lack adequate rehabilitation services
- Community health worker models have demonstrated effectiveness in improving healthcare access for underserved populations (Perry et al., *Annual Review of Public Health*, 2014)
- Loan forgiveness programs (NHSC) have proven effective at directing providers to shortage areas

### Cost Estimate

- **Residency expansion**: $150-$250 million/year for 200-300 additional positions in disability-related specialties
- **Loan forgiveness**: $100-$200 million/year for disability-competent providers in HPSAs
- **Community health worker training**: $50-$100 million/year for disability-focused CHW programs
- **Total**: $300-$550 million/year

### Timeline

- Year 1: Establish disability-focused NHSC loan repayment tracks
- Years 1-3: Fund residency expansion in physical medicine, developmental medicine, and disability psychiatry
- Years 2-4: Develop and deploy disability-focused community health worker programs
- Years 5-10: Measurable improvement in disability provider distribution

### International Models

- **Sweden**: County councils employ disability liaison nurses who coordinate healthcare for people with complex disabilities, improving care continuity and reducing hospitalizations.
- **Netherlands**: Community disability health teams provide proactive primary care for people with intellectual disabilities, reducing diagnostic delays.

## Solution 6: Accessible Telehealth Standards

### Description

Establish binding accessibility standards for telehealth platforms used by healthcare providers receiving federal funds, ensuring compatibility with screen readers, hearing aids, and alternative input devices; providing real-time captioning; and meeting WCAG 2.2 AA standards at minimum.

### Evidence Base

- 68% of adults with disabilities used telehealth in 2023, up from 11% pre-pandemic (ATA, 2024)
- Studies document that 40-60% of telehealth platforms have significant accessibility barriers for people with visual, hearing, motor, or cognitive disabilities (Lazar et al., *Journal of Medical Internet Research*, 2023)
- WCAG 2.2 provides established technical standards for digital accessibility

### Cost Estimate

- **Platform remediation**: $10-$50 million across major telehealth vendors (one-time)
- **Captioning services**: $3-$5/minute for real-time captioning; estimated $200-$400 million/year for healthcare use
- **Offset**: Accessible telehealth reduces need for costly in-person visits and transportation

### Timeline

- Year 1: HHS issues proposed accessibility standards for telehealth
- Year 2: Final rule adopted; compliance timeline begins
- Years 2-4: Major telehealth platforms achieve compliance
- Year 5: All covered platforms meet accessibility standards

### International Models

- **European Union**: European Accessibility Act (2025 compliance deadline) requires accessibility for digital health services including telehealth.

## Solution 7: Strengthened Enforcement with Proactive Compliance

### Description

Increase HHS OCR and DOJ Civil Rights Division funding dedicated to disability healthcare accessibility enforcement, establish a proactive compliance audit program, and create financial incentives for voluntary compliance.

### Evidence Base

- DOJ resolved only 30-40 healthcare accessibility cases between 2010 and 2024, a fraction of noncompliant facilities
- HHS OCR budget ($39 million, FY 2024) covers all civil rights enforcement, not just disability
- Proactive audit models in other civil rights contexts (EPA environmental justice audits, DOL wage-and-hour investigations) have demonstrated effectiveness
- Voluntary compliance programs with financial incentives (OSHA VPP, EPA audit policy) reduce overall enforcement costs

### Cost Estimate

- **Additional enforcement staff**: $50-$75 million/year for HHS OCR and DOJ disability healthcare teams
- **Proactive audit program**: $30-$50 million/year for facility audits
- **Voluntary compliance incentives**: $100-$200 million/year in tax credits and grants
- **Total**: $180-$325 million/year

### Timeline

- Year 1: Appropriations increase for HHS OCR and DOJ
- Years 1-2: Establish proactive audit protocol and voluntary compliance program
- Years 2-5: Audit 10% of healthcare facilities annually
- Year 5+: Compliance rates measurably increase based on audit findings and enforcement actions

## Solution 8: Mental Health Access Expansion

### Description

Strengthen Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act enforcement, expand Medicaid behavioral health provider networks, fund crisis services alternatives to law enforcement, and establish disability-specific mental health training programs.

### Evidence Base

- 32.9% of adults with disabilities report frequent mental distress, 4x the general population rate (CDC, 2022)
- NSDUH (2023): 15.7% of adults with disabilities could not access needed mental health care
- MHPAEA enforcement has been weak; DOL and CMS conduct few compliance examinations (GAO, 2022)
- 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline expansion provides infrastructure for improved crisis response

### Cost Estimate

- **Parity enforcement**: $30-$50 million/year for additional DOL/CMS staff
- **Medicaid behavioral health expansion**: $5-$8 billion/year in enhanced federal matching
- **Crisis services**: $1-$2 billion/year for disability-accessible crisis centers and mobile teams
- **Training programs**: $50-$100 million/year for disability-specific mental health provider training

### Timeline

- Year 1: Executive order strengthening MHPAEA enforcement
- Years 1-3: Fund crisis services expansion with disability accessibility requirements
- Years 2-5: Expand Medicaid behavioral health networks
- Year 5+: Measure improvements in mental health access and outcomes for disability populations

## Sequencing and Priority

| Priority | Solution | Rationale |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| 1 | Mandatory MDE standards | Most immediate, concrete impact on physical access |
| 2 | Insurance reform | Addresses financial barriers affecting millions |
| 3 | Disability health data | Foundation for accountability and targeted interventions |
| 4 | Medical education reform | Long-term structural change in provider competency |
| 5 | Enforcement strengthening | Ensures compliance with existing and new requirements |
| 6 | Workforce development | Addresses provider shortages in critical specialties |
| 7 | Telehealth accessibility | Builds on pandemic-era telehealth expansion |
| 8 | Mental health expansion | Addresses severe but politically complex access gaps |
