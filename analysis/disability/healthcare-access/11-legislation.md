# Healthcare Access for People with Disabilities: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative landscape for disability healthcare access rests on existing civil rights statutes (ADA, Section 504, ACA Section 1557) that establish the right to accessible healthcare but lack specific implementation standards and adequate enforcement mechanisms. This document proposes legislation to close those gaps: mandatory accessible medical equipment standards, prior authorization reform, medical education requirements, and strengthened enforcement.

## Constitutional Amendments

No constitutional amendment is necessary for the reforms proposed in this document. Existing congressional authority under the Commerce Clause (Art. I, Section 8, Cl. 3) and the Spending Clause (Art. I, Section 8, Cl. 1) provides sufficient basis for healthcare accessibility legislation. The Fourteenth Amendment, Section 5 enforcement power supports disability nondiscrimination requirements for state and local governments.

## Federal Legislation

### The Accessible Medical Equipment Act

**Purpose**: Codify mandatory accessibility standards for medical diagnostic equipment in all healthcare facilities receiving federal financial assistance, with phased compliance timelines and financial assistance for small practices.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Accessible Medical Equipment Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
(a) 61 million adults in the United States live with disabilities;
(b) only 8.4 percent of primary care physician offices have
    examination tables accessible to people with mobility disabilities;
(c) the lack of accessible medical diagnostic equipment prevents
    people with disabilities from receiving routine examinations,
    preventive screenings, and diagnostic procedures;
(d) the U.S. Access Board published Medical Diagnostic Equipment
    Accessibility Standards in 2017, but these standards remain
    advisory; and
(e) existing civil rights laws require accessible healthcare
    services but do not specify equipment standards, creating
    compliance uncertainty.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act—
(a) COVERED ENTITY.—The term "covered entity" means any healthcare
    facility, practice, clinic, hospital, diagnostic imaging center,
    or other entity that provides medical diagnostic services and
    receives Federal financial assistance, including but not limited
    to Medicare, Medicaid, or other Federal health program payments.
(b) MEDICAL DIAGNOSTIC EQUIPMENT.—The term "medical diagnostic
    equipment" means equipment used in, or in conjunction with,
    medical settings for the purpose of diagnosis, including
    examination tables, examination chairs, weight scales,
    mammography equipment, and other imaging equipment.
(c) ACCESSIBILITY STANDARDS.—The term "accessibility standards"
    means the Medical Diagnostic Equipment Accessibility Standards
    published by the U.S. Access Board pursuant to section 4203 of
    the Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act (Public Law
    111-148).
(d) SMALL PRACTICE.—The term "small practice" means a healthcare
    facility with 10 or fewer healthcare providers and annual
    revenues of $5,000,000 or less.

SEC. 4. ACCESSIBLE MEDICAL DIAGNOSTIC EQUIPMENT REQUIREMENTS.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Not later than 1 year after the date of enactment
    of this Act, the Secretary of Health and Human Services shall
    promulgate regulations requiring covered entities to acquire,
    maintain, and make available medical diagnostic equipment that
    meets the accessibility standards.
(b) MINIMUM REQUIREMENTS.—The regulations shall require each
    covered entity to—
    (1) have at least one examination table per facility that meets
        the accessibility standards for height adjustability and
        transfer surface;
    (2) have at least one weight scale per facility that meets the
        accessibility standards for wheelchair accessibility;
    (3) ensure that diagnostic imaging equipment, including
        mammography equipment, meets accessibility standards or
        that the entity has a referral arrangement with a facility
        within 30 miles that has accessible imaging equipment; and
    (4) train clinical staff in the proper use of accessible
        equipment and in safe patient transfer techniques.
(c) COMPLIANCE TIMELINE.—
    (1) LARGE FACILITIES.—Covered entities with more than 50
        healthcare providers shall comply within 2 years of the
        effective date of the regulations.
    (2) MEDIUM FACILITIES.—Covered entities with 11 to 50
        healthcare providers shall comply within 4 years.
    (3) SMALL PRACTICES.—Small practices shall comply within
        6 years, provided that they have applied for financial
        assistance under section 5.

SEC. 5. FINANCIAL ASSISTANCE.

(a) GRANT PROGRAM.—The Secretary shall establish a grant program
    to assist small practices in acquiring accessible medical
    diagnostic equipment. The Secretary shall award grants of up to
    $25,000 per facility.
(b) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
    $200,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2032 to
    carry out the grant program under subsection (a).
(c) TAX CREDIT EXPANSION.—Section 44 of the Internal Revenue Code
    of 1986 is amended by striking "$5,000" each place it appears
    and inserting "$10,000".

SEC. 6. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—Any person aggrieved by a violation
    of this Act may bring a civil action for injunctive relief,
    damages, and reasonable attorney fees.
(b) FEDERAL ENFORCEMENT.—The Secretary may bring a civil action to
    enforce compliance with this Act and may impose civil penalties
    of not more than $50,000 for a first violation and $100,000 for
    subsequent violations.
(c) COMPLAINT PROCESS.—The HHS Office for Civil Rights shall
    investigate complaints under this Act using the procedures
    established for complaints under Section 504 of the
    Rehabilitation Act of 1973 (29 U.S.C. 794).

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill converts the Access Board's existing advisory MDE standards into binding requirements for all federally funded healthcare facilities. The phased timeline and grant program address small practice concerns. The private right of action ensures enforcement does not depend solely on inadequate federal resources.

**Potential Challenges**: Industry opposition citing compliance costs; potential Spending Clause challenge if requirements are deemed coercive; administrative capacity for rulemaking and enforcement.

**Refinements**: Could include safe harbor provisions for entities that demonstrate good-faith compliance efforts; could tie compliance to Medicare/Medicaid participation conditions rather than standalone mandate.

### The Disability Healthcare Prior Authorization Reform Act

**Purpose**: Reform prior authorization requirements for durable medical equipment, habilitative services, and disability-specific healthcare to reduce delays and denials.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Disability Healthcare Prior
Authorization Reform Act".

SEC. 2. PRIOR AUTHORIZATION REQUIREMENTS FOR DISABILITY-RELATED
SERVICES.

(a) ELECTRONIC PRIOR AUTHORIZATION.—All group health plans,
    health insurance issuers, Medicare, and Medicaid shall
    implement electronic prior authorization for durable medical
    equipment, habilitative services, and rehabilitative services
    not later than 1 year after the date of enactment.
(b) DECISION TIMELINES.—
    (1) URGENT REQUESTS.—Decisions on prior authorization requests
        for durable medical equipment and disability-related
        services classified as urgent shall be rendered within
        24 hours.
    (2) STANDARD REQUESTS.—Decisions on standard requests shall
        be rendered within 7 calendar days.
    (3) DEEMED APPROVAL.—Any request not decided within the
        applicable timeline shall be deemed approved.
(c) GOLD CARD EXEMPTION.—Providers with a prior authorization
    approval rate of 90 percent or higher for a category of
    service over a 12-month period shall be exempt from prior
    authorization requirements for that category for the
    subsequent 12 months.
(d) CLINICAL REVIEW CRITERIA.—Prior authorization review criteria
    for disability-related services shall be developed with input
    from disability healthcare specialists and shall be publicly
    available.
(e) CONTINUITY OF CARE.—Prior authorization approvals for ongoing
    disability-related services, including durable medical
    equipment, home health, and habilitative therapies, shall be
    valid for a minimum of 12 months and shall be automatically
    renewed unless the plan demonstrates a material change in
    medical necessity.

SEC. 3. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) The Secretary of Health and Human Services, the Secretary of
    Labor, and the Secretary of the Treasury shall jointly enforce
    this Act through their respective authorities over health plans
    and issuers.
(b) Civil penalties of $1,000 per violation, per affected
    individual, shall apply to plans that fail to meet the
    requirements of Section 2.

SEC. 4. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 180 days after the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the specific prior authorization barriers that disproportionately affect people with disabilities. The gold card exemption incentivizes appropriate authorization practices. The deemed-approval provision creates accountability for timely decisions.

**Potential Challenges**: Insurance industry lobbying; concerns about fraud and waste in DME; ERISA preemption issues for self-insured plans.

**Refinements**: Could include anti-fraud provisions specific to DME; could phase in deemed-approval provision after electronic PA infrastructure is operational.

### The Medical Education Disability Competency Act

**Purpose**: Require medical education accreditation standards to include disability competency, ensuring all physicians receive training in accessible clinical skills and disability-informed care.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Medical Education Disability
Competency Act".

SEC. 2. DISABILITY COMPETENCY ACCREDITATION.

(a) MEDICAL SCHOOLS.—The Secretary of Education, in consultation
    with the Secretary of Health and Human Services, shall require
    that accrediting bodies recognized under section 496 of the
    Higher Education Act of 1965 (20 U.S.C. 1099b) for medical
    education programs include disability competency as a required
    element of accreditation, including—
    (1) clinical skills for examining patients with physical,
        sensory, intellectual, and psychiatric disabilities;
    (2) effective communication methods with patients who are
        deaf, blind, or have communication disabilities;
    (3) recognition and avoidance of diagnostic overshadowing;
    (4) understanding of the social model of disability and
        disability as a component of health equity; and
    (5) involvement of people with disabilities as educators in
        medical training.
(b) GRADUATE MEDICAL EDUCATION.—The Secretary of Health and
    Human Services shall condition Medicare graduate medical
    education payments under section 1886(h) of the Social
    Security Act (42 U.S.C. 1395ww(h)) on compliance with
    disability competency standards in residency training.

SEC. 3. GRANT PROGRAM.

(a) The Secretary of Health and Human Services shall award grants
    to medical schools for the development and implementation of
    disability competency curricula.
(b) There is authorized to be appropriated $25,000,000 for each
    of fiscal years 2027 through 2032 to carry out this section.

SEC. 4. EFFECTIVE DATE.

Accreditation requirements under Section 2 shall take effect not
later than 3 years after the date of enactment of this Act.
```

**Explanation**: By tying disability competency to accreditation, this bill ensures that all future physicians receive training. Using Medicare GME funding as leverage for residency programs ensures compliance without new regulatory structures.

**Potential Challenges**: Medical education lobby resistance; concerns about federal overreach into accreditation; curricular space competition.

**Refinements**: Could include continuing medical education (CME) requirements for practicing physicians; could establish a National Center for Disability Health Education at HRSA.

## State Model Legislation

### Model Accessible Healthcare Facilities Act

**Purpose**: Provide a template for state-level requirements for accessible medical diagnostic equipment.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Accessible Healthcare
Facilities Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Healthcare facility" means any facility licensed by the
    [State Department of Health] to provide medical, dental,
    or diagnostic services.
(b) "Accessible medical equipment" means examination tables,
    weight scales, and diagnostic imaging equipment that meet
    the Medical Diagnostic Equipment Accessibility Standards
    published by the U.S. Access Board.

SECTION 3. REQUIREMENTS.

(a) Each healthcare facility shall, not later than [3/5] years
    after the effective date of this Act, acquire and maintain
    accessible medical equipment sufficient to serve patients
    with disabilities.
(b) Each healthcare facility shall ensure that at least one
    examination room per location is fully accessible, including
    accessible equipment, clear floor space, and accessible
    paths of travel.
(c) Each healthcare facility shall train staff in the use of
    accessible equipment and safe patient handling techniques.

SECTION 4. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) The [State Department of Health] shall incorporate
    accessible equipment requirements into facility licensing
    inspections.
(b) Failure to comply shall constitute grounds for corrective
    action under the facility licensing code.
(c) Any person aggrieved may file a complaint with the
    [State Human Rights Commission/Attorney General].

SECTION 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on [date].
```

**Explanation**: This model legislation provides a state-level complement to federal action, using state licensing authority as the enforcement mechanism.

**Adaptations**: States may adjust compliance timelines, define facility categories, and specify enforcement agencies based on existing regulatory structures.

## Regulatory Framework

### HHS Section 1557 Accessible Equipment Rulemaking

**Existing Authority**: Section 1557 of the ACA (42 U.S.C. Section 18116) prohibits discrimination in health programs receiving federal financial assistance. The 2024 final rule strengthened disability protections.

**Draft Regulation**: HHS should issue a supplemental rule under Section 1557 specifically requiring covered entities to acquire and maintain accessible medical diagnostic equipment meeting Access Board standards, with compliance timelines and technical assistance.

**Explanation**: This regulatory approach can be pursued through executive action without new legislation, providing faster implementation while legislative campaigns proceed.

### CMS Medicaid Managed Care Network Adequacy Rule

**Existing Authority**: 42 C.F.R. Section 438.68 requires Medicaid managed care plans to maintain adequate provider networks.

**Draft Regulation**: CMS should issue guidance or rulemaking specifying that network adequacy for disability populations requires inclusion of providers with accessible medical equipment, disability competency training, and appropriate specialties (physical medicine, developmental medicine, disability psychiatry).

**Explanation**: Current network adequacy standards do not account for disability-specific accessibility needs, allowing managed care plans to technically meet requirements while failing disability populations.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Commerce Clause**: Federal authority to regulate healthcare facilities is well-established under the Commerce Clause. Healthcare is an interstate commercial activity, and disability discrimination in healthcare substantially affects interstate commerce.
- **Spending Clause**: Conditioning Medicare/Medicaid participation on accessibility compliance is a permissible exercise of Spending Clause authority, provided conditions are clearly stated and relate to the federal interest in the program. *South Dakota v. Dole*, 483 U.S. 203 (1987); *NFIB v. Sebelius*, 567 U.S. 519 (2012) (coercion analysis).
- **Fourteenth Amendment**: Section 5 enforcement power supports ADA-style disability nondiscrimination requirements for state and local government healthcare facilities. *Tennessee v. Lane*, 541 U.S. 509 (2004) (upholding ADA Title II as applied to court access).

### Preemption Questions

- Federal accessible equipment standards would preempt less stringent state standards but not more stringent ones, consistent with the ADA's framework (42 U.S.C. Section 12201(b)).
- Prior authorization reform legislation should explicitly address ERISA preemption for self-insured employer plans, which are otherwise exempt from state insurance regulation.

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- Private right of action with attorney fees creates incentives for private enforcement
- Federal agency enforcement through HHS OCR and DOJ provides systemic enforcement capacity
- State licensing integration ensures ongoing compliance monitoring
- Financial incentives (tax credits, grants) encourage voluntary compliance

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Accessible equipment standards should be reviewed by the Access Board every 5 years to incorporate technological advances
- Prior authorization reform provisions should include a 3-year GAO review assessing impact on access, costs, and utilization

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Accessible Medical Equipment Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Federal funding threshold | Facilities could potentially refuse Medicare/Medicaid to avoid compliance | Low (few facilities would forfeit federal payments) |
| Referral arrangement escape | Entities could rely on referral arrangements for imaging equipment rather than purchasing accessible equipment | Medium |
| Small practice grant dependency | Small practices may delay compliance pending grant funding that may be inadequately appropriated | High |
| Staff training without standards | "Training" requirement lacks specificity, allowing perfunctory compliance | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not cover non-federally funded facilities | Some cash-only and concierge practices escape coverage | Spending Clause authority limited to federal funding nexus |
| Equipment acquisition without maintenance | Facilities may acquire equipment but not maintain or use it | Compliance focuses on procurement, not ongoing use |
| No patient outcome measures | Compliance measured by equipment presence, not by patient access improvements | Difficulty measuring access outcomes at facility level |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Add maintenance and utilization reporting requirements to regulations
2. Establish CMS quality measures for disability access outcomes
3. Require referral facilities to be within 15 miles in urban areas and 30 miles in rural areas, with transportation assistance
4. Develop detailed staff training standards through HHS rulemaking
5. Extend coverage to all healthcare facilities through ADA Title III enforcement, independent of federal funding

### Prior Authorization Reform Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Reclassification of services | Plans could reclassify disability services to avoid coverage under the Act | High |
| Gold card gaming | Plans could narrowly define service categories to prevent providers from reaching 90% threshold | Medium |
| Deemed approval reversal | Plans could approve requests under deemed approval then seek retroactive denial | High |
| Clinical criteria manipulation | Plans could develop restrictive clinical criteria that technically comply but functionally deny access | High |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address coverage denials | Prior authorization reform addresses process, not coverage scope | Separate legislative issue |
| ERISA preemption gap | Self-insured employer plans may be partially exempt | Federal structure of employee benefits law |
| Enforcement resource dependency | Civil penalties require agency enforcement capacity | Budget constraints |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Define protected service categories in statute to prevent reclassification
2. Prohibit retroactive denial of deemed-approved services
3. Require gold card calculations at the provider level, not service subcategory level
4. Mandate independent review of clinical criteria by disability healthcare experts
5. Create whistleblower protections for plan employees who report criteria manipulation

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Enforcement agency capacity | Dedicated appropriations for disability healthcare enforcement, not subject to sequestration |
| Industry capture of standards development | Require majority disability community representation on standard-setting bodies |
| Data gaps preventing accountability | Mandatory disability-disaggregated reporting as prerequisite for participation in federal programs |
| Political vulnerability to administration changes | Codify requirements in statute rather than relying on regulation; include anti-rollback provisions |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- All standards reviewed every 5 years by the U.S. Access Board with public comment
- GAO evaluation of enforcement effectiveness after 3 years
- CMS assessment of prior authorization reform impact on access and costs after 2 years
- LCME/ACGME report on disability competency implementation after 5 years
