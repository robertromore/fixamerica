# Healthcare Access for People with Disabilities: Actions

## Overview

This document provides concrete actions that individuals, community groups, and advocates can take to improve healthcare access for people with disabilities. Actions range from personal advocacy in clinical settings to systemic change campaigns.

## Individual Actions

### As a Patient with a Disability

- **Know your rights**: Healthcare facilities receiving federal funds must provide accessible services under Section 504 and the ADA. You have the right to accessible exam tables, effective communication (interpreters, large print, plain language), and reasonable modifications to policies and procedures.
- **Request accommodations in advance**: When scheduling appointments, inform the office of your accessibility needs (accessible exam room, interpreter, extended appointment time). Document these requests in writing (email or patient portal message).
- **File complaints when rights are violated**: If a healthcare provider fails to provide accessible services, file complaints with HHS Office for Civil Rights (hhs.gov/ocr/complaints) and the DOJ ADA Information Line (1-800-514-0301).
- **Request your medical records**: Ensure your records accurately reflect your health conditions separate from your disability. If you suspect diagnostic overshadowing, request a second opinion.
- **Use patient advocates**: Many hospitals have patient advocates or ombudsmen who can intervene when accessibility barriers arise during care.

### As a Family Member or Caregiver

- **Advocate during appointments**: Ensure providers address the patient's health concerns rather than directing all conversation to the caregiver. Support self-advocacy while providing assistance when needed.
- **Document barriers**: Keep a written log of accessibility barriers encountered at healthcare facilities, including dates, locations, and specific problems. This documentation supports complaint filings and advocacy.
- **Connect with peer networks**: Join family advocacy organizations that share strategies for navigating healthcare systems (Family Voices, The Arc local chapters, Parent to Parent programs).

### As a Healthcare Provider

- **Assess your practice's accessibility**: Conduct a self-audit of your facility using the U.S. Access Board's Medical Diagnostic Equipment Standards and the ADA Checklist for Existing Facilities.
- **Invest in accessible equipment**: Purchase at least one height-adjustable exam table, an accessible weight scale, and ensure accessible paths of travel in your facility. Tax credits under 26 U.S.C. Section 44 offset costs for small businesses.
- **Seek disability competency training**: Complete continuing education in disability healthcare. Resources include the Association of University Centers on Disabilities (AUCD) provider training modules.
- **Allow extended appointment times**: Schedule longer appointments for patients with complex communication or mobility needs without penalizing patients through additional charges.

## Community Actions

### Organizing for Change

- **Start or join a disability healthcare access task force**: Work with local Centers for Independent Living, Protection and Advocacy agencies, and disability organizations to monitor healthcare facility accessibility in your community.
- **Conduct community accessibility surveys**: Organize volunteer teams to assess the accessibility of healthcare facilities in your area using standardized checklists. Publish results and share with local media and elected officials.
- **Attend hospital board meetings**: Most nonprofit hospitals have open board meetings. Attend to raise accessibility concerns and advocate for accessible equipment purchases and staff training.
- **Engage with managed care advisory committees**: Many state Medicaid managed care programs have beneficiary advisory committees. Participate to advocate for disability-specific network adequacy and prior authorization reforms.

### Advocacy Campaigns

- **State legislature advocacy**: Support state-level accessible medical equipment mandates modeled on California SB 1379. Contact state representatives and testify at committee hearings.
- **Medical school curriculum advocacy**: Petition medical schools in your state to implement disability competency curricula. Partner with medical students interested in disability health.
- **Insurance commissioner complaints**: File complaints with state insurance commissioners about inadequate provider networks, excessive prior authorization requirements, and DME coverage denials.

## Template Communications

### Email to Healthcare Provider About Accessibility

```
Subject: Request for Accessible Medical Equipment and Services

Dear [Provider/Practice Manager],

I am a patient [or family member of a patient] with [type of disability] who
requires the following accommodations for effective healthcare:

- [Accessible exam table / height-adjustable table]
- [Sign language interpreter / captioned communication]
- [Extended appointment time]
- [Accessible weight scale]
- [Other specific need]

Under the Americans with Disabilities Act (42 U.S.C. Section 12182) and
Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act (29 U.S.C. Section 794), healthcare
providers are required to ensure that people with disabilities have equal
access to medical services, including through the provision of accessible
equipment and auxiliary aids and services.

I would appreciate confirmation that these accommodations will be available
for my appointment on [date]. If your facility is unable to provide these
accommodations, please let me know so that I can seek care elsewhere and
determine whether to file a complaint with the HHS Office for Civil Rights.

Thank you for your attention to this matter.

Sincerely,
[Name]
[Contact Information]
```

### Phone Script for Congressional Office

```
Hello, my name is [name] and I am a constituent from [city/zip code].

I am calling to ask [Representative/Senator name] to support legislation
to improve healthcare access for people with disabilities.

[Choose one or more]:

- I urge support for mandatory accessible medical equipment standards.
  Only 8.4% of doctor's offices have exam tables that a wheelchair user
  can access. This is unacceptable 35 years after the ADA was passed.

- I urge support for prior authorization reform for durable medical
  equipment and disability services. People with disabilities face denial
  rates of 30-50% on initial DME requests, delaying access to wheelchairs,
  communication devices, and other essential equipment.

- I urge support for Medicare dental, vision, and hearing coverage.
  People with disabilities have among the highest rates of unmet dental
  care needs in the country.

- I urge support for disability competency requirements in medical
  education. Fewer than 20% of medical schools require any disability
  training, resulting in providers who do not know how to examine or
  communicate with disabled patients.

This issue affects 61 million Americans with disabilities and their families.
Can I get the [Representative/Senator]'s position on this issue?

Thank you for your time.
```

### HHS Office for Civil Rights Complaint Points

When filing a complaint at hhs.gov/ocr/complaints, include:

- Name and address of the healthcare facility
- Date(s) of the incident(s)
- Specific accessibility barrier encountered (inaccessible equipment, lack of interpreter, inaccessible information, etc.)
- How the barrier prevented or limited your access to healthcare
- Whether you requested accommodations and how the facility responded
- Names of staff involved (if known)
- Any documentation (emails, photos, medical records)

## Talking Points

### For Conversations with Elected Officials

- **The problem is measurable**: Only 8.4% of primary care offices have exam tables accessible to wheelchair users. Women with mobility disabilities are 30% less likely to receive mammograms. People with intellectual disabilities receive cancer diagnoses at later stages and die at higher rates from preventable causes.
- **The law is clear but unenforced**: The ADA has required accessible healthcare since 1990, but enforcement resources are grossly inadequate. HHS OCR has a budget of $39 million to cover all civil rights enforcement in health and human services.
- **The cost is manageable**: The total national cost of accessible medical equipment for all primary care practices is estimated at $1.8-$3.2 billion, a one-time investment of less than 0.1% of annual healthcare spending. Tax credits and grants can reduce the burden on small practices.
- **Prevention saves money**: Accessible primary care reduces emergency department visits (average ED visit costs $2,168 vs. $250 for primary care). Earlier diagnoses through accessible screening reduce treatment costs.

### For Conversations with Healthcare Providers

- **Your patients need you**: 26% of the U.S. population has a disability. Accessible healthcare is not a niche concern; it is a core competency for serving your patient population.
- **Small changes matter**: A height-adjustable exam table costs $5,000-$15,000 and serves not only wheelchair users but also elderly patients, pregnant women, and anyone with mobility limitations.
- **Training is available**: The AUCD, AAMC, and disability organizations offer free and low-cost provider training modules. Even brief training interventions improve clinical skills and patient satisfaction.
- **It is the law**: The ADA and Section 504 require healthcare providers to ensure equal access. Proactive compliance is less costly than responding to complaints and litigation.

### For Media and Public Communication

- **Frame as civil rights, not charity**: Healthcare access is a legal right, not a favor. People with disabilities are entitled to the same quality of care as everyone else.
- **Use real stories**: Concrete examples of access failures (a woman who cannot receive a mammogram because no facility has accessible equipment; a deaf patient who receives a misdiagnosis because no interpreter was provided) are more compelling than statistics alone.
- **Connect to universal concerns**: Everyone will experience disability at some point in their lives, through aging, injury, or illness. Accessible healthcare benefits everyone.
