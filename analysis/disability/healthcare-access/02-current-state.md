# Healthcare Access for People with Disabilities: Current State

## Overview of Current Conditions

Healthcare access for people with disabilities in the United States remains deeply inadequate despite more than three decades of civil rights protections. The system fails disabled individuals at every point of contact: physical barriers prevent entry into facilities, communication barriers impede diagnosis and treatment, provider attitudes and knowledge gaps lead to substandard care, and insurance structures create financial obstacles to necessary services. These failures are not marginal inconveniences; they translate into delayed diagnoses, untreated conditions, preventable hospitalizations, and premature death.

### Current Legal Framework

| Law/Regulation | Year | Key Protections | Enforcement Agency |
|----------------|------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Section 504, Rehabilitation Act | 1973 | Prohibits disability discrimination in federally funded programs including hospitals and clinics | HHS Office for Civil Rights |
| Americans with Disabilities Act | 1990 | Title II (public entities) and Title III (private) require accessible healthcare facilities and services | DOJ Civil Rights Division |
| ACA Section 1557 | 2010 | Prohibits discrimination in health programs receiving federal funds; includes disability | HHS Office for Civil Rights |

## State-Level Variation

### Medicaid Expansion Impact

States that expanded Medicaid under the ACA saw measurable improvements in healthcare access for people with disabilities. However, 10 states had not fully expanded Medicaid as of 2025, leaving an estimated 500,000 adults with disabilities in the coverage gap.

## Trends

### Positive Developments

- **Telehealth expansion**: COVID-19 accelerated telehealth adoption, with 68% of adults with disabilities reporting telehealth use in 2023 (vs. 37% pre-pandemic). Many disability organizations report telehealth as a significant access improvement (American Telemedicine Association, 2024).
- **ACA Section 1557 enforcement**: The Biden administration issued updated Section 1557 regulations in 2024, strengthening nondiscrimination protections and explicitly addressing accessible medical diagnostic equipment.
- **Disability data collection**: HHS finalized rules requiring disability status data collection in federal health surveys and programs, improving the evidence base (HHS, 2024).
- **Medical education reform**: The Association of American Medical Colleges (AAMC) published disability competency learning objectives in 2023, though adoption remains voluntary.

### Negative Trends

- **Medicaid managed care concerns**: As more states shift disability populations into Medicaid managed care, advocacy organizations report increased prior authorization denials for specialized services and equipment (National Health Law Program, 2024).
- **Provider shortages**: Rural areas and underserved communities face worsening shortages of physical medicine, rehabilitation, and disability-competent providers (HRSA, 2024).
- **Mental health crisis**: Demand for behavioral health services among people with disabilities outpaces supply, with average wait times of 6-8 weeks in many states (NAMI, 2024).
- **Dental coverage gaps**: Despite evidence of heightened oral health needs, Medicare still does not cover routine dental care, and many state Medicaid programs offer only emergency dental services (CMS, 2024).
- **Enforcement resource stagnation**: HHS OCR and DOJ Civil Rights Division budgets have not kept pace with complaint volumes or the expansion of covered entities (GAO, 2023).

## Current Political Context

Healthcare access for people with disabilities receives limited attention in mainstream political debates, which tend to focus on insurance coverage and prescription drug costs rather than structural accessibility. The disability community has mobilized effectively around specific threats, such as proposed Medicaid cuts and work requirements, but proactive accessibility reforms have struggled to gain legislative traction.

Key political dynamics as of 2025:

- Congressional proposals to impose Medicaid work requirements would disproportionately affect people with disabilities, who face higher administrative burden in proving exemptions
- Bipartisan support exists for expanding Medicare dental coverage, which would benefit many people with disabilities
- Accessible medical equipment standards have been under development at the U.S. Access Board since 2017, with final rules still pending
- ACA marketplace premium subsidies and cost-sharing reductions remain politically vulnerable to changes in congressional priorities
