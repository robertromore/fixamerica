# Healthcare Access for People with Disabilities: Stakeholders

## Overview

Healthcare access for people with disabilities involves a complex ecosystem of stakeholders whose interests, power, and influence shape policy outcomes. The people most affected, disabled individuals and their families, historically have had the least institutional power. Understanding stakeholder dynamics is essential for building effective reform coalitions.

## Primary Stakeholders: People Affected

### People with Disabilities

| Group | Population | Key Healthcare Concerns | Source |
|-------|-----------|------------------------|--------|
| People with mobility disabilities | 13.7 million | Inaccessible exam tables, equipment, and facilities | CDC DHDS (2023) |
| People who are deaf or hard of hearing | 11.5 million | Interpreter access, communication failures | NIDCD (2023) |
| People with intellectual/developmental disabilities | 7.4 million | Diagnostic overshadowing, provider competency, complex care coordination | ACL (2023) |
| People who are blind or have low vision | 7.0 million | Inaccessible health information, wayfinding barriers | AFB (2023) |
| People with psychiatric disabilities | 14.2 million (serious mental illness) | Treatment access, crisis services, stigma from providers | SAMHSA (2023) |
| People with multiple disabilities | Approximately 15 million | Compounding barriers, fragmented systems | Census Bureau, ACS (2023) |

### Family Members and Caregivers

An estimated 53 million Americans serve as unpaid caregivers for people with disabilities and chronic conditions (AARP, 2020). Caregivers navigate healthcare systems on behalf of disabled family members, manage care coordination, and often experience their own health impacts from caregiving stress. Their concerns include respite care access, inclusion in treatment planning, and healthcare system navigation support.

## Coalition Dynamics

### Existing Coalitions

**Consortium for Citizens with Disabilities (CCD) Health Task Force**: The primary national coalition coordinating disability healthcare advocacy. Includes approximately 100 member organizations spanning all disability types. Key strengths include breadth of representation and established relationships with congressional offices. Key weaknesses include limited enforcement capacity and dependence on volunteer coordination.

**Medicaid Defense Coalition**: Cross-disability coalition focused on protecting Medicaid coverage and opposing work requirements, block grants, and per-capita caps. Has demonstrated significant mobilization capacity around Medicaid threats, including the 2017 ACA repeal efforts.

### Potential Coalitions

| Coalition | Members | Common Interest | Obstacle |
|-----------|---------|-----------------|----------|
| Accessibility Standards Alliance | Disability orgs + Access Board + some AHA members | Mandatory medical equipment standards | AHA and MGMA opposition to unfunded mandates |
| Provider Training Coalition | AAMC + disability orgs + medical specialty societies | Disability competency in medical education | Curriculum time competition, no accreditation mandate |
| Insurance Reform Coalition | Disability orgs + consumer groups + state regulators | Prior authorization reform, network adequacy | AHIP lobbying resources |
| Rural Health Access Coalition | Disability orgs + rural health associations + telehealth companies | Expanding provider access in rural areas | Funding, broadband infrastructure |
| Mental Health Parity Coalition | Disability orgs + NAMI + APA + labor unions | Full mental health parity enforcement | Insurance industry resistance, enforcement capacity |

## Power Analysis

### Most Influential Actors

1. **CMS**: Controls Medicare and Medicaid rules that directly affect the majority of healthcare services used by people with disabilities
2. **AHIP/Insurance industry**: Shapes coverage design, prior authorization practices, and network construction through lobbying and market power
3. **AMA/AHA**: Influence provider behavior, medical education, and healthcare facility standards
4. **State Medicaid Directors**: Determine how Medicaid is implemented for disability populations in each state
5. **Congressional committee chairs**: Control whether disability healthcare legislation advances

### Least Powerful Actors with Greatest Stake

1. **People with intellectual and developmental disabilities**: Face the most severe healthcare access barriers but have the least individual advocacy capacity
2. **Low-income people with disabilities in non-expansion states**: Lack both insurance coverage and political representation in state legislatures hostile to Medicaid expansion
3. **People with disabilities in rural areas**: Face compounding barriers of provider shortages and geographic isolation with limited political influence
4. **Undocumented immigrants with disabilities**: Excluded from most public coverage programs and fearful of seeking care
