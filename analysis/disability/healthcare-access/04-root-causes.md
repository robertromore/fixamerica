# Healthcare Access for People with Disabilities: Root Causes

## Overview

The healthcare access failures experienced by people with disabilities are not random or accidental. They result from deeply embedded structural features of the American healthcare system, medical education, insurance markets, and enforcement mechanisms. These root causes interact and reinforce one another, creating a system where inaccessibility is the default and access requires extraordinary individual effort.

## Systemic Analysis

### 1. Medical Education Fails to Teach Disability Competency

The foundation of healthcare access problems lies in how physicians and other providers are trained. Disability is overwhelmingly taught through a deficit-based medical model that frames it as pathology to be treated or overcome, rather than as a human variation that requires competent, individualized clinical care. Most medical schools devote fewer than 5 hours of their entire curriculum to disability-related content, and much of that time is spent on the pathophysiology of disabling conditions rather than on clinical skills for providing accessible, effective care to disabled patients (Seidel et al., *Academic Medicine*, 2020).

Fewer than 20% of medical schools require disability competency training (Iezzoni et al., *Health Affairs*, 2021). As a result, physicians graduate without knowing how to transfer a patient from a wheelchair to an exam table, communicate effectively with a deaf patient, adapt a pelvic exam for a woman with cerebral palsy, or distinguish between symptoms caused by a disability and symptoms of an independent health condition. This knowledge gap persists throughout careers because continuing medical education rarely addresses disability competency.

### 2. Healthcare Facility Design Assumes Able-Bodiedness

The built environment of healthcare, from primary care offices to hospitals to diagnostic imaging centers, is designed around the assumption that patients can walk, stand on a scale, climb onto an exam table, sit in a standard chair, and communicate verbally. This assumption is baked into architectural standards, equipment procurement, and clinical workflows.

Accessible medical equipment exists but is rarely purchased. Standard exam tables cost $1,500-$3,000 while height-adjustable accessible tables cost $5,000-$15,000. Accessible weight scales cost $2,000-$5,000 versus $200-$500 for standard scales. These cost differentials, though modest relative to total facility budgets, create a persistent bias toward inaccessible procurement, especially in smaller practices.

### 3. Insurance Structures Create Disability-Specific Barriers

Insurance design features that may be neutral on their face create disproportionate barriers for people with disabilities:

- **Prior authorization**: People with disabilities require more authorizations due to complex care needs, creating administrative burdens that delay treatment. An estimated 35% of prior authorization requests for disability-related services are initially denied (American Medical Association, 2023).
- **Step therapy**: Requirements to fail on cheaper medications or treatments before accessing more effective ones disproportionately harm people with disabilities who may have already tried and failed standard treatments.
- **Network adequacy**: Narrow provider networks frequently exclude disability specialists, forcing patients to go out of network or forgo care.
- **Annual and visit limits**: Caps on therapy visits (physical, occupational, speech) may be adequate for acute rehabilitation but are insufficient for people with ongoing disability-related needs.
- **DME coverage**: Durable medical equipment coverage is subject to complex rules, frequent denials, and requirements to use specific suppliers, creating access delays for essential equipment like wheelchairs, communication devices, and respiratory equipment.

### 4. Enforcement Mechanisms Are Structurally Inadequate

Healthcare accessibility laws (ADA, Section 504, ACA Section 1557) have weak enforcement:

- **HHS Office for Civil Rights** handles all health-related civil rights complaints (race, sex, disability, age) with a budget of approximately $39 million (FY 2024). The office is structurally incapable of proactive enforcement.
- **DOJ Civil Rights Division** brings relatively few healthcare accessibility cases. Between 2010 and 2024, DOJ settled or litigated approximately 30-40 healthcare facility accessibility cases, a fraction of the thousands of inaccessible facilities nationwide (DOJ Civil Rights Division, Project Civic Access data).
- **No mandatory equipment standards**: Despite the U.S. Access Board publishing accessible medical diagnostic equipment standards in 2017, these remain advisory. No federal agency has adopted them as binding requirements for healthcare providers.
- **Complaint-driven model**: Enforcement depends on individuals filing complaints, which requires knowledge of rights, ability to navigate bureaucratic processes, and willingness to risk retaliation from providers on whom patients depend for care.

### 5. Fragmented Care Delivery Systems

People with disabilities frequently need care from multiple providers, specialists, and systems (primary care, specialists, therapists, equipment suppliers, home health, behavioral health). The U.S. healthcare system's fragmentation means:

- No single provider is responsible for care coordination
- Electronic health records do not reliably capture or share disability-related information
- Transitions between systems (pediatric to adult, hospital to community, acute to long-term) create gaps where patients are lost to follow-up
- Medicaid, Medicare, and private insurance each have different coverage rules, creating complexity for dual-eligible individuals

## Perverse Incentives

### Pay-for-Volume Disadvantages Complex Patients

Fee-for-service payment systems reward physicians for seeing more patients in less time. People with disabilities typically require longer appointments for communication, transfers, and complex medical decision-making. Under standard payment structures, serving disabled patients is financially penalized. A primary care physician spending 45 minutes with a complex disabled patient earns the same reimbursement as a 15-minute visit with an able-bodied patient.

### Medicaid Reimbursement Discourages Provider Participation

Medicaid reimburses providers at lower rates than Medicare or private insurance, and people with disabilities are disproportionately covered by Medicaid. Many providers limit the number of Medicaid patients they accept, and some refuse Medicaid entirely, shrinking the pool of available providers for disabled patients. In 2023, only 74% of office-based physicians accepted new Medicaid patients, compared to 90% for private insurance (NCHS, 2023).

### DME Competitive Bidding Reduces Quality and Access

Medicare's competitive bidding program for durable medical equipment has driven down reimbursement rates to the point where many suppliers have exited the market, reducing geographic access to essential equipment. Between 2013 and 2023, the number of Medicare-enrolled DME suppliers declined by approximately 40% (CMS data, 2023).

### Diagnostic Overshadowing Is Clinically Efficient

When providers attribute symptoms to a patient's known disability rather than investigating further, they save time and avoid the complexity of diagnostic workups in patients who may be difficult to examine with standard equipment. This clinical shortcut is individually rational but systemically harmful, resulting in missed diagnoses, delayed treatment, and preventable deaths.

## Interconnections with Other Root Causes

### Ableism as Foundational

Underlying all specific root causes is ableism: the pervasive assumption that disabled bodies and minds are inferior and that disability is primarily a medical problem rather than a social and political condition. Ableism shapes medical education (disability as pathology), facility design (bodies that cannot stand or walk are abnormal), insurance design (disability-related needs are "extra" rather than standard), and enforcement priorities (disability access is a nice-to-have rather than a civil right).

### Poverty and Disability Feedback Loop

Disability causes poverty (through employment discrimination, benefit inadequacy, and extra costs of living), and poverty worsens disability (through inadequate healthcare, nutrition, housing, and environmental exposures). Healthcare access barriers sit at the center of this feedback loop: people with disabilities cannot access the healthcare they need to maintain function and independence, which limits their economic participation, which limits their insurance options and financial resources to overcome access barriers.

### Intersection with Race and Geography

Healthcare access barriers are compounded for people with disabilities who are also members of racial and ethnic minorities or who live in rural areas. Black adults with disabilities are 60% more likely to report unmet healthcare needs than white adults with disabilities (NHIS, CDC, 2023). Rural residents with disabilities face both provider shortages and transportation barriers that make healthcare access significantly more difficult than for urban residents.
