# Healthcare Access for People with Disabilities: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to healthcare accessibility reforms for people with disabilities comes primarily from healthcare industry organizations concerned about compliance costs, insurance companies seeking to limit coverage obligations, and political actors who oppose government mandates and spending. Unlike some civil rights issues where opposition is explicit, much disability healthcare opposition operates through procedural mechanisms: delaying regulation, opposing funding, weakening enforcement, and framing accessibility as aspirational rather than mandatory.

## Opposition Landscape

| Opponent | Primary Concern | Strategy | Influence Level |
|----------|----------------|----------|-----------------|
| American Hospital Association (AHA) | Compliance costs for equipment and facility upgrades | Lobbying against mandatory equipment standards; supporting voluntary approaches | Very High |
| America's Health Insurance Plans (AHIP) | Expanded coverage mandates increase costs | Lobbying against habilitation mandates, DME expansion, and prior authorization reform | Very High |
| Medical Group Management Association (MGMA) | Small practice compliance burden | Opposing mandatory accessibility requirements for physician offices | High |
| National Federation of Independent Business (NFIB) | ADA compliance costs for small healthcare businesses | Supporting ADA exemptions and extended compliance timelines | Medium-High |
| Some state governors and legislatures | Medicaid spending growth | Opposing Medicaid expansion, seeking block grants and work requirements | High |
| Pharmaceutical industry (PhRMA) | Tangential; opposes price controls that would benefit disability population | Lobbying against prescription drug pricing reforms | Very High |

## Key Opposition Arguments

### Argument 1: Mandatory Accessibility Standards Are Too Costly

**Claim**: Requiring all healthcare facilities to purchase accessible medical diagnostic equipment would impose unsustainable costs on small practices and rural health clinics, potentially forcing closures.

**Evidence cited**: The American Medical Association estimated in 2018 that equipping a single primary care practice with accessible exam tables and weight scales could cost $15,000-$25,000. MGMA argues that small practices operate on thin margins (average 4-6% net revenue) and cannot absorb additional capital expenditures.

**Reality**: The cost argument overstates the burden and understates the obligation. Accessible exam tables cost $5,000-$15,000 each, and practices typically have 2-4 exam rooms. The total cost of $10,000-$60,000 per practice is a one-time capital expenditure comparable to other routine equipment purchases (an EKG machine costs $3,000-$10,000; a standard ultrasound costs $20,000-$100,000). Moreover, the ADA has required accessible services since 1990; the cost of compliance is a cost of doing business in a field that serves the public. Tax credits under Section 44 of the Internal Revenue Code provide small businesses up to $5,000 annually for ADA compliance costs (26 U.S.C. Section 44).

**Rebuttal**: Healthcare is a $4.5 trillion industry (CMS, 2023). The total national cost of accessible medical equipment for all primary care practices has been estimated at $1.8-$3.2 billion, a one-time investment of less than 0.1% of annual healthcare spending. Phased implementation with tax incentives and grant programs can reduce financial impact on small practices. The cost of inaccessibility, measured in delayed diagnoses, preventable hospitalizations, and health disparities, far exceeds the cost of equipment.

### Argument 2: Provider Training Mandates Overload Medical Education

**Claim**: Adding mandatory disability competency training to medical school curricula would displace other critical content in an already overcrowded curriculum, and should be left to individual schools and specialty programs.

**Evidence cited**: The AAMC curriculum inventory shows that medical schools face competing demands for curricular time from genomics, health informatics, population health, and other emerging fields. The AAMC argues that competency objectives should remain voluntary to preserve institutional flexibility.

**Reality**: Medical schools currently devote fewer than 5 hours to disability content across 4 years of training (Seidel et al., *Academic Medicine*, 2020). The argument that no additional time can be found for the health needs of 26% of the population is not credible. Disability content can be integrated across existing courses (anatomy, clinical skills, ethics, community health) rather than added as a standalone block. The real barrier is institutional inertia and lack of faculty with disability expertise, not curricular capacity.

**Rebuttal**: Medical education accreditation already mandates competency in cultural humility, health equity, and social determinants of health. Disability competency fits naturally within these frameworks. Institutions that have implemented disability curricula (e.g., University of South Carolina, University of Michigan) report that integration requires minimal additional curricular time when embedded across existing courses. The Liaison Committee on Medical Education (LCME) could add disability competency as an accreditation standard, as it has for other competencies.

### Argument 3: Prior Authorization Reform Would Increase Healthcare Costs

**Claim**: Prior authorization is a necessary tool for managing healthcare utilization and preventing unnecessary or low-value care. Weakening prior authorization requirements for disability-related services would increase costs without improving outcomes.

**Evidence cited**: AHIP estimates that prior authorization saves the healthcare system $57 billion annually by preventing unnecessary procedures and ensuring appropriate utilization. Insurance companies argue that removing prior authorization for DME would lead to fraud and waste.

**Reality**: Prior authorization disproportionately burdens people with disabilities, who require more authorizations due to complex care needs. An AMA survey found that 93% of physicians reported that prior authorization delays necessary care, and 34% reported that prior authorization had led to a serious adverse event for a patient (AMA, 2023). For disability-specific services (wheelchairs, communication devices, specialized therapies), prior authorization denial rates of 30-50% on initial submission create dangerous delays. Many denials are overturned on appeal, indicating that the process functions more as a deterrent than as a quality control mechanism.

**Rebuttal**: Prior authorization reform does not mean eliminating all utilization review. Gold card programs that exempt providers with high approval rates, standardized criteria that reduce arbitrary variation, and electronic prior authorization that reduces processing time can maintain appropriate oversight while eliminating barriers to medically necessary care. Several states have enacted prior authorization reform laws demonstrating that cost savings and improved access are not mutually exclusive (Texas SB 3162, 2021; Oregon HB 3046, 2023).

### Argument 4: Medicaid Work Requirements Promote Self-Sufficiency

**Claim**: Requiring Medicaid beneficiaries to work, volunteer, or engage in community activities promotes self-sufficiency, reduces dependency, and ensures that limited Medicaid resources are directed to those who truly need them. Disability exemptions protect people who cannot work.

**Evidence cited**: Proponents cite state-level work requirement proposals in Arkansas, Georgia, and other states that included disability exemptions. They argue that most people with disabilities would be exempt and that requirements target only able-bodied adults.

**Reality**: Disability exemptions in work requirement programs have consistently failed to protect all people who should be exempt. In Arkansas's work requirement program (2018-2019, before judicial reversal), an estimated 18,000 people lost Medicaid coverage, including individuals with disabilities who could not navigate the reporting system, had intermittent conditions not meeting strict exemption criteria, or were awaiting disability determinations (Sommers et al., *New England Journal of Medicine*, 2019). Many disabilities are episodic, invisible, or undiagnosed; rigid categorical exemptions inevitably exclude people who cannot work but do not fit narrow definitions.

**Rebuttal**: The administrative costs of implementing and enforcing work requirements have exceeded any savings from coverage reductions in every state that has attempted them (KFF, 2023). The primary effect is not encouraging work but creating administrative barriers that cause coverage losses among eligible populations, including people with disabilities. People with disabilities who can work face employment discrimination that makes meeting work requirements difficult even when they are able to perform job functions.

### Argument 5: Telehealth Solves Access Problems

**Claim**: The rapid expansion of telehealth eliminates the need for expensive facility accessibility modifications, since patients with mobility barriers can receive care remotely.

**Evidence cited**: Telehealth utilization among people with disabilities increased from 11% pre-pandemic to 68% during and after the pandemic (ATA, 2024). Satisfaction rates for telehealth among disability populations are high in some surveys.

**Reality**: Telehealth is a valuable complement to in-person care but cannot replace it for many medical needs. Physical examinations, diagnostic imaging, blood draws, vaccinations, dental care, and many procedures require in-person visits. Telehealth platforms frequently have accessibility barriers: video interfaces may not work with screen readers, captioning is often inaccurate, and cognitive accessibility features are minimal. People without reliable internet or appropriate devices, who are disproportionately disabled, low-income, and rural, cannot access telehealth. Relying on telehealth to substitute for facility accessibility creates a separate-and-unequal healthcare system.

**Rebuttal**: Telehealth should expand access, not excuse inaccessibility. Both modalities must be accessible. The Americans with Disabilities Act applies to telehealth platforms as well as physical facilities, and accessibility standards for both are necessary and achievable.

### Argument 6: The ADA Already Requires Healthcare Accessibility

**Claim**: Existing ADA requirements are sufficient; the problem is compliance, not law. Additional legislation or regulation is unnecessary and would create duplicative requirements.

**Evidence cited**: The ADA has prohibited disability discrimination in healthcare since 1990. Section 504 has been in effect since 1973. DOJ has enforcement authority.

**Reality**: After 35 years, only 8.4% of primary care offices have accessible exam tables. Enforcement resources are grossly inadequate, and the complaint-driven model places the burden on patients. The ADA's general mandate to provide access has not been translated into specific, enforceable standards for medical equipment, and courts have inconsistently interpreted what access requires in healthcare settings. The law is necessary but insufficient without specific standards, adequate enforcement resources, and proactive compliance monitoring.

**Rebuttal**: The inadequacy of current enforcement proves the need for additional measures, not that the problem is solved. Mandatory accessible medical equipment standards, increased enforcement budgets, and proactive compliance audits are not duplicative; they are the implementation mechanisms that the original ADA framework requires but lacks.

## Counter-Strategies

### Reframing Cost Arguments

Present accessibility as an investment with measurable returns. Accessible primary care reduces emergency department visits ($2,168 average ED visit vs. $250 average primary care visit, MEPS 2022). Preventive screenings catch conditions at earlier, less expensive stages. The economic case for accessibility is strong when lifetime costs are considered rather than just upfront equipment purchases.

### Building Provider Champions

Identify and elevate physicians, nurses, and other providers who champion disability accessibility. Provider voices carry more weight with medical associations and accreditation bodies than external advocacy. Programs like the University of Michigan's disability health curriculum can serve as models that demonstrate feasibility.

### Leveraging State Action

State-level reforms can create momentum and demonstrate that accessibility requirements are workable. California's SB 1379 (accessible medical equipment mandate, 2022) provides a legislative model. Success in early-adopter states builds the evidence base for federal action.

### Using Data to Drive Accountability

Advocate for mandatory disability-disaggregated health outcome data from hospitals, health systems, and managed care organizations. When disparities are measured and reported, institutional pressure to address them increases. The HHS disability data collection rules finalized in 2024 provide a foundation to build upon.

### Coalition Building Beyond Disability

Frame healthcare accessibility as a universal design issue that benefits aging populations, pregnant individuals, people with temporary injuries, and obese patients. The accessible exam table that serves a wheelchair user also serves an elderly patient with mobility limitations. Coalition with aging organizations (AARP), maternal health advocates, and obesity health groups broadens the political constituency for accessible healthcare infrastructure.
