# SSDI Reform: Actions

## What Citizens Can Do

### Immediate Actions (This Week)

1. **Learn your representatives' positions**: Search for your members of Congress at congress.gov and review their voting records on Social Security and disability legislation. The NOSSCR and AAPD websites track congressional positions on SSDI reform.

2. **Contact your members of Congress**: Use the template communications below to urge support for SSDI reform. Phone calls to district offices are most effective, followed by personal emails and letters.

3. **Share your story**: If you or a family member has experience with SSDI -- application delays, benefit inadequacy, work disincentive traps -- share your story with your representatives, advocacy organizations, and local media. Personal narratives drive policy change.

4. **Join an advocacy organization**: See the organizations table below and join at least one that aligns with your priorities.

### Short-Term Actions (This Month)

5. **Attend a town hall or congressional event**: Ask your representative directly about their position on SSA funding, the SGA cliff, and processing delays. Record their response if possible.

6. **Write a letter to the editor**: Local newspapers remain influential with members of Congress. Write about SSDI issues using specific data and personal examples.

7. **Connect with local disability organizations**: Your state's Protection and Advocacy organization (find at ndrn.org) can connect you with local advocacy efforts and may need volunteers.

8. **Educate your network**: Many people confuse SSDI with SSI or welfare. Help friends and family understand that SSDI is earned insurance funded by payroll taxes.

### Ongoing Actions

9. **Vote for candidates who support SSDI reform**: Regardless of party, prioritize candidates who support adequate SSA funding, benefit modernization, and work incentive reform.

10. **Participate in public comment periods**: When SSA proposes regulatory changes, public comments influence outcomes. Sign up for SSA regulatory notices at federalregister.gov.

11. **Support bipartisan reform efforts**: SSDI reform is most likely to succeed as a bipartisan initiative. Support organizations and candidates who seek cross-party solutions.

## Template Email/Letter

**Subject: Support SSDI Reform -- Adequately Fund SSA and Modernize Benefits**

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

As your constituent in [City/District], I am writing to urge your support for meaningful reform of Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI).

SSDI is not welfare -- it is earned insurance that American workers pay for through their payroll taxes. Yet the program that is supposed to protect workers who become disabled is failing them at every stage:

**Processing delays**: The average initial disability determination takes 213 days (FY 2024), and applicants who must appeal wait over 15 months for a hearing. An estimated 10,000 people die each year while waiting for their disability hearings.

**Administrative underfunding**: The Social Security Administration has lost more than 7,000 employees in the past decade while serving a growing population. Congress has consistently appropriated less than what the agency needs to maintain basic service levels.

**Work disincentives**: The Substantial Gainful Activity cliff threatens total benefit loss for beneficiaries who earn more than $1,550 per month, even if they could work part-time. This traps people in poverty who could contribute to our economy.

**Benefit inadequacy**: Average SSDI benefits are $1,537 per month -- barely above the federal poverty line. A quarter of beneficiaries live in poverty.

I urge you to support:

1. Increasing SSA administrative funding to adequate levels
2. Replacing the SGA cliff with a $1-for-$2 benefit offset
3. Eliminating the two-year Medicare waiting period
4. Establishing a benefit floor at 125% of the federal poverty level

These reforms would save lives, reduce dependency, and improve fiscal outcomes. SSDI reform should not be a partisan issue -- it is about keeping a promise to American workers.

I look forward to hearing your position on these issues.

Sincerely,
[Name]
[Address]
[Phone/Email]

## Talking Points

### For Conservative Audiences

- SSDI is earned insurance, not welfare. Beneficiaries worked an average of 22 years and paid into the system through payroll taxes.
- The current system wastes money: multi-year processing delays push applicants onto other programs (SNAP, Medicaid, state assistance) that cost more in aggregate.
- The SGA cliff discourages work and traps people in dependency. A benefit offset would reduce government costs and increase tax revenue.
- Adequate SSA funding is fiscal responsibility: every dollar invested in administration returns $10 in reduced costs elsewhere.

### For Liberal/Progressive Audiences

- Average SSDI benefits are barely above the poverty line. A quarter of beneficiaries live in poverty. This is a social justice crisis.
- The two-year Medicare waiting period is unconscionable: people determined too disabled to work must wait two years for health insurance.
- Processing delays kill people -- an estimated 10,000 die each year waiting for their disability hearings.
- The binary disability standard reflects an outdated understanding of disability that denies dignity and agency to people with partial disabilities.

### For General Audiences

- Any worker can become disabled. One in four of today's 20-year-olds will experience a disability before reaching retirement age.
- The SSDI system is broken at every level: too slow, too rigid, and too stingy. Reform is not about expanding or cutting -- it is about modernizing.
- Other countries have figured this out. The Netherlands, Sweden, and Germany all have graduated disability systems that support work while providing security.
- This should not be partisan. Faster processing, better work incentives, and adequate benefits are goals that everyone should support.
