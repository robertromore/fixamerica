# SSDI Reform: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

SSDI reform requires a combination of statutory changes to the Social Security Act, regulatory modernization by the SSA, and appropriations reform to stabilize the agency's administrative budget. The legislative proposals below address the program's core structural deficiencies: the binary disability standard, the SGA cliff, processing delays, the Medicare waiting period, and benefit inadequacy. Because SSDI is a federal program administered under Title II of the Social Security Act (42 U.S.C. Chapter 7, Subchapter II), reform is almost entirely a federal legislative matter, though state DDS operations and vocational rehabilitation coordination require some state-level action.

## Federal Legislation

### The SSDI Modernization Act

**Purpose**: Comprehensive reform of SSDI's benefit structure, work incentives, and administrative processes to align the program with modern understanding of disability and employment.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Social Security Disability Insurance
Modernization Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds the following:
    (1) SSDI serves 7.3 million disabled workers and 1.4 million
        dependents, providing essential income support funded through
        worker payroll tax contributions.
    (2) The program's binary disability standard, enacted in 1956,
        does not reflect modern understanding of disability as a
        spectrum of functional limitation.
    (3) The Substantial Gainful Activity threshold creates a benefit
        cliff that discourages workforce participation and traps
        beneficiaries in poverty.
    (4) Processing delays averaging over 7 months for initial claims
        and 15 months for hearings cause financial devastation and
        an estimated 10,000 deaths per year among applicants.
    (5) Average monthly benefits of $1,537 leave approximately 25%
        of beneficiaries below the federal poverty level.
(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to—
    (1) replace the SGA cliff with a graduated benefit offset;
    (2) establish processing time standards and streamline
        adjudication;
    (3) modernize the disability definition to recognize partial
        disability;
    (4) increase benefit adequacy; and
    (5) strengthen return-to-work supports.

SEC. 3. BENEFIT OFFSET.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 223 of the Social Security Act (42 U.S.C.
    423) is amended by adding at the end the following new subsection:
    "(k) BENEFIT OFFSET FOR EARNINGS ABOVE SGA.—
        (1) IN GENERAL.—Notwithstanding subsection (d)(4), a
            beneficiary who earns in excess of the applicable
            substantial gainful activity amount shall have the monthly
            benefit reduced by $1 for each $2 of earnings above such
            amount, rather than having benefits terminated.
        (2) MINIMUM BENEFIT.—No beneficiary's monthly benefit shall
            be reduced below $0 under this subsection.
        (3) CONTINUATION OF MEDICARE.—A beneficiary whose cash
            benefits are reduced to $0 under this subsection shall
            retain eligibility for benefits under title XVIII for a
            period of 120 months following the month in which cash
            benefits were first reduced to $0.
        (4) REINSTATEMENT.—A beneficiary whose cash benefits have
            been reduced to $0 under this subsection may have full
            benefits reinstated without a new application upon
            demonstrating that earnings have fallen below the
            substantial gainful activity amount, provided the
            beneficiary continues to meet the disability standard."

SEC. 4. PROCESSING TIME STANDARDS.

(a) INITIAL DETERMINATION.—The Commissioner of Social Security shall
    ensure that an initial disability determination under section 221
    of the Social Security Act is completed within 90 days of the
    date on which a complete application is received.
(b) HEARING.—The Commissioner shall ensure that a hearing under
    section 205(b) of the Social Security Act on a denied disability
    claim is scheduled and conducted within 180 days of the date on
    which a hearing request is received.
(c) EXPEDITED PROCESSING.—
    (1) TERMINAL CONDITIONS.—The Commissioner shall issue a final
        favorable determination within 15 days for any applicant
        with a condition identified on the Compassionate Allowances
        list or determined to be terminal.
    (2) DIRE NEED.—The Commissioner shall establish an expedited
        processing track for applicants who demonstrate imminent
        risk of homelessness, loss of medical care, or other dire
        need, with a target of 30 days for initial determination.
(d) REPORTING.—The Commissioner shall report to Congress quarterly on
    compliance with the processing time standards established under
    this section, including the number and percentage of claims
    exceeding each standard and the average processing time by state.

SEC. 5. ELIMINATION OF RECONSIDERATION.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 221 of the Social Security Act is amended to
    eliminate the reconsideration step for disability determinations.
    An applicant whose initial determination is unfavorable may
    request a hearing directly before an administrative law judge
    under section 205(b).
(b) PRE-HEARING CONFERENCE.—The Commissioner shall establish a
    pre-hearing conference process to narrow issues and develop
    evidence prior to the hearing, to be conducted within 60 days
    of a hearing request.

SEC. 6. MINIMUM BENEFIT FLOOR.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 223(a) of the Social Security Act is amended
    to provide that no monthly disability insurance benefit shall be
    less than 125 percent of the monthly federal poverty guideline for
    an individual, as determined by the Secretary of Health and Human
    Services, divided by 12.
(b) COLA ADJUSTMENT.—The minimum benefit floor established under
    subsection (a) shall be adjusted annually in the same manner as
    other disability insurance benefits under section 215(i).

SEC. 7. ELIMINATION OF MEDICARE WAITING PERIOD.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 226(b) of the Social Security Act (42 U.S.C.
    426(b)) is amended by striking "and has for 24 calendar months
    been entitled to" and inserting "and has for 3 calendar months
    been entitled to".
(b) PHASED IMPLEMENTATION.—
    (1) Effective with respect to individuals who become entitled
        to disability insurance benefits on or after the date that
        is 1 year after the date of enactment, the waiting period
        shall be 12 months.
    (2) Effective with respect to individuals who become entitled
        to disability insurance benefits on or after the date that
        is 2 years after the date of enactment, the waiting period
        shall be 3 months.
    (3) Effective with respect to individuals who become entitled
        to disability insurance benefits on or after the date that
        is 3 years after the date of enactment, the waiting period
        shall be eliminated entirely.

SEC. 8. MANDATORY ADMINISTRATIVE FUNDING.

(a) IN GENERAL.—There is established in the Treasury of the United
    States a Social Security Administration Operations Fund.
(b) ANNUAL TRANSFER.—There shall be transferred to the Fund from the
    Federal Old-Age and Survivors Insurance Trust Fund and the Federal
    Disability Insurance Trust Fund, in each fiscal year, an amount
    equal to 2.0 percent of the total benefit payments made from such
    Trust Funds in the preceding fiscal year.
(c) AVAILABILITY.—Amounts in the Fund shall be available to the
    Commissioner of Social Security, without further appropriation,
    for the administrative expenses of the Social Security
    Administration.
(d) SUPPLEMENT NOT SUPPLANT.—Amounts made available under this section
    shall supplement, not supplant, any amounts appropriated for
    Social Security Administration administrative expenses.

SEC. 9. COST-OF-LIVING ADJUSTMENT.

(a) CPI-E.—Section 215(i) of the Social Security Act is amended to
    require that the cost-of-living adjustment for disability
    insurance benefits be calculated using the Consumer Price Index
    for the Elderly (CPI-E) as published by the Bureau of Labor
    Statistics.

SEC. 10. SUPPORTED EMPLOYMENT SERVICES.

(a) IN GENERAL.—The Commissioner of Social Security, in coordination
    with the Secretary of Labor, shall establish a comprehensive
    supported employment program for disability insurance
    beneficiaries.
(b) AUTOMATIC ENROLLMENT.—Each individual awarded disability
    insurance benefits shall be automatically enrolled in supported
    employment services and provided information about available
    vocational rehabilitation, supported employment, and work
    incentive programs.
(c) INDIVIDUAL PLACEMENT AND SUPPORT.—The Commissioner shall fund
    Individual Placement and Support (IPS) programs in each state
    through grants to state vocational rehabilitation agencies,
    community mental health centers, and other qualified providers.
(d) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There is authorized to be
    appropriated $3,000,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027
    through 2036 to carry out this section.

SEC. 11. EFFECTIVE DATE.

Except as otherwise provided, this Act shall take effect on the date
that is 1 year after the date of enactment of this Act.
```

**Explanation**: This omnibus bill addresses the six core structural deficiencies of SSDI simultaneously. The benefit offset (Section 3) replaces the SGA cliff with a gradual reduction, ensuring that work always pays. Processing time standards (Section 4) and elimination of reconsideration (Section 5) attack the backlog. The minimum benefit floor (Section 6) ensures no beneficiary lives in poverty. The Medicare waiting period elimination (Section 7) is phased to manage fiscal impact. Mandatory administrative funding (Section 8) ends the cycle of chronic underfunding. The CPI-E switch (Section 9) prevents benefit erosion over time. Supported employment (Section 10) provides the infrastructure for beneficiaries to return to work.

**Potential Challenges**:

- **Cost**: Total estimated cost of $15-25 billion per year when fully implemented. Fiscal conservatives may oppose without offsetting revenue.
- **Trust Fund impact**: Medicare waiting period elimination and benefit increases require additional revenue or reallocation.
- **SSA implementation capacity**: The agency must simultaneously implement multiple complex changes.
- **ALJ resistance**: Processing time standards may face opposition from ALJs who view them as threatening decisional independence.

**Refinements**:

- Phase implementation over 5 years to manage costs and administrative burden
- Include a revenue provision (e.g., lifting or raising the payroll tax cap on earnings above $250,000) to fund benefit increases
- Establish an independent advisory board to recommend processing standards rather than imposing rigid deadlines
- Protect ALJ decisional independence while establishing case management timelines

### The Disability Insurance Access Act

**Purpose**: Targeted legislation to eliminate the two-year Medicare waiting period and the five-month SSDI cash benefit waiting period, addressing the most acute hardships faced by newly approved beneficiaries.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Disability Insurance Access Act".

SEC. 2. ELIMINATION OF FIVE-MONTH WAITING PERIOD.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 223(a)(1)(A) of the Social Security Act
    (42 U.S.C. 423(a)(1)(A)) is amended by striking "and has
    been so entitled for not less than five full calendar months."
(b) EFFECTIVE DATE.—The amendment made by subsection (a) shall apply
    to individuals who file applications for disability insurance
    benefits on or after the date that is 6 months after the date
    of enactment of this Act.

SEC. 3. ELIMINATION OF MEDICARE WAITING PERIOD.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 226(b) of the Social Security Act (42 U.S.C.
    426(b)) is amended by striking "and has for 24 calendar months
    been entitled to" and inserting "and is entitled to".
(b) EFFECTIVE DATE.—The amendment made by subsection (a) shall apply
    to individuals who become entitled to disability insurance
    benefits on or after the date that is 1 year after the date of
    enactment of this Act.
```

**Explanation**: This focused bill addresses the two most harmful waiting periods in SSDI without attempting comprehensive reform, making it a potentially easier legislative lift. Eliminating the five-month cash benefit waiting period means beneficiaries receive income support immediately upon being found disabled. Eliminating the Medicare waiting period means they receive health coverage when they need it most.

**Potential Challenges**: Cost (approximately $12-15 billion per year for both provisions). May require offsetting revenue.

**Refinements**: Could be phased (reducing waiting periods to 3 months, then 1 month, then 0) to spread costs over multiple fiscal years.

## Regulatory Framework

### SSA Rulemaking

**Existing Authority**: The Commissioner of Social Security has broad rulemaking authority under 42 U.S.C. Section 405(a) to adopt rules and regulations necessary to carry out the functions of the Social Security Act.

**Proposed Regulatory Changes**:

1. **Modernize Listing of Impairments**: Update 20 C.F.R. Part 404, Appendix 1 to reflect current medical knowledge, including recognition of conditions such as Long COVID, chronic fatigue syndrome, and episodic disabilities that are poorly captured by current listings.

2. **Revise Residual Functional Capacity Assessment**: Amend 20 C.F.R. Section 404.1545 to require assessment of capacity for part-time, flexible, and remote work, rather than only traditional full-time employment.

3. **Standardize DDS Operations**: Issue binding regulations for state DDS agencies establishing minimum staffing ratios, training requirements, processing time targets, and quality standards.

4. **Expand Compassionate Allowances**: Use existing regulatory authority to add conditions to the Compassionate Allowances list, targeting the list's expansion to 500+ conditions.

**Explanation**: These regulatory changes can be implemented under existing statutory authority without new legislation, making them achievable even in a gridlocked Congress. They would improve processing consistency, reduce denial-then-reversal cycles, and update the program for modern conditions.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

SSDI reform legislation is on solid constitutional ground. Congress has broad authority under the Spending Clause and the Commerce Clause to structure social insurance programs. The Supreme Court has upheld Social Security's constitutionality since *Helvering v. Davis*, 301 U.S. 619 (1937). Benefit modifications do not raise due process concerns as long as existing beneficiaries are not retroactively harmed, consistent with *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976), which established the framework for procedural due process in disability benefit determinations.

### Due Process Requirements

Any reforms that affect existing beneficiaries must comply with *Mathews v. Eldridge* due process requirements: notice, opportunity to be heard, and a neutral decision-maker. Transitions to a graduated disability system must include grandfathering provisions for current beneficiaries and adequate notice of changes.

### Trust Fund Financing

Legislation that increases SSDI expenditures must address Trust Fund financing. Options include:

- Raising or eliminating the payroll tax cap (currently $168,600 in 2024)
- Increasing the SSDI payroll tax rate (currently 0.9% employee, 0.9% employer)
- Reallocating revenue between the OASI and DI Trust Funds
- General revenue transfers (used for SSI but not currently for SSDI)

### Preemption

SSDI is exclusively federal. State laws cannot modify SSDI eligibility, benefit amounts, or administrative procedures. However, state DDS operations are governed by cooperative agreements between SSA and state agencies, which could be restructured through legislation or regulation.

### Enforcement Mechanisms

Processing time standards would require enforcement mechanisms. Options include:

- Deemed approvals (automatic approval if processing exceeds time limits) -- strong incentive but risks erroneous payments
- Congressional reporting requirements with automatic funding increases if targets are missed
- Inspector General monitoring with corrective action plans for non-compliant offices

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### SSDI Modernization Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Benefit offset gaming | Beneficiaries could cycle earnings above and below SGA to maximize combined income | Medium |
| Processing time waivers | SSA could seek blanket waivers of processing standards citing resource constraints | High |
| Mandatory funding diversion | Congress could offset mandatory admin funding with discretionary cuts elsewhere | High |
| Supported employment provider capture | Providers could enroll beneficiaries without delivering meaningful services | Medium |
| Compassionate Allowances inflation | Political pressure to add conditions that do not clearly qualify | Low |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address SSI-SSDI interaction | Concurrent beneficiaries face complex rules from both programs | Legislative scope limited to Title II |
| No private disability insurance coordination | Employers may reduce coverage if SSDI benefits increase | Outside SSDI statutory framework |
| ALJ decisional independence tension | Processing standards may conflict with ALJ independence | Constitutional separation of powers concerns |
| Trust Fund financing not fully addressed | Benefit increases without revenue may accelerate depletion | Political difficulty of tax increases |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Include anti-gaming provisions in benefit offset regulations: earnings must be from documented employment, not self-reported; pattern analysis for cycling behavior.
2. Prohibit blanket processing time waivers; allow case-by-case extensions only with documented cause and individual applicant notification.
3. Include "supplement not supplant" language with enforcement mechanism (IG audit requirement) for mandatory administrative funding.
4. Require performance metrics and outcome-based payment for supported employment providers; annual IG audits of provider performance.
5. Establish medical advisory board to review Compassionate Allowances additions based on clinical evidence.

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| SSA workforce cannot implement all reforms simultaneously | Phase reforms over 5 years with clear sequencing |
| State DDS variation produces geographic disparities | Strengthen federal standards; consider pilot federalization |
| Technology modernization risk | Modular procurement; phased deployment; independent oversight |
| Beneficiary confusion during transition | Multi-channel communication campaign; WIPA counselor expansion |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

All provisions of this Act should be subject to a 10-year comprehensive review by the Government Accountability Office, with particular attention to:

- Effect of benefit offset on beneficiary earnings and benefit costs
- Impact of processing time standards on decision accuracy
- Adequacy of the minimum benefit floor relative to poverty thresholds
- Performance of supported employment programs
- Trust Fund solvency under reformed program parameters
