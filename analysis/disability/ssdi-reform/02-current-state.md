# SSDI Reform: Current State

## The Problem Today

### Application Backlog and Processing Delays

The Social Security Administration's disability determination system is overwhelmed. As of September 2024, approximately 547,000 cases were pending at the hearing level, down from a peak of over 1.1 million in 2017 but still representing wait times that destroy lives. The average initial claim takes 213 days to process (FY 2024), but claimants who are denied and appeal face an average of 15.5 months waiting for an Administrative Law Judge (ALJ) hearing. The full appeals process -- initial determination, reconsideration, ALJ hearing, Appeals Council review, and federal court -- can take 3 to 5 years from application to final resolution.

These delays are not mere inconvenience. The Government Accountability Office estimated that more than 10,000 people died while awaiting disability hearings in recent fiscal years (GAO-23-105480, 2023). Applicants who survive the process often exhaust their savings, lose their homes, and experience deteriorating health conditions that become harder and more expensive to treat.

### The Denial-and-Appeal Cycle

SSDI's adjudication system functions as a multi-stage filtration process that denies the majority of applicants at the initial level and then reverses many of those denials on appeal:

| Stage | Approval Rate | Average Processing Time | Source |
|-------|---------------|------------------------|--------|
| Initial determination | 33% | 213 days (FY 2024) | SSA Disability Determination Process (2024) |
| Reconsideration | 13% of denied claims | 120-180 days | SSA Annual Statistical Report (2024) |
| ALJ hearing | 45-50% of denied claims | 15.5 months (FY 2024) | SSA Office of Hearings Operations (2024) |
| Appeals Council | 1-2% of denied claims | 6-12 months | SSA Appeals Council Data (2024) |
| Federal court | Varies | 12-24 months | Administrative Office of U.S. Courts (2024) |

The high reversal rate at the ALJ level -- roughly half of cases that make it to hearing are approved -- raises fundamental questions about the accuracy of initial determinations. If ALJs approve cases that initial examiners denied, either the initial process is systematically too restrictive or the hearing process is too lenient. Research consistently suggests the former: initial determinations are made by state-employed disability examiners (Disability Determination Services) who often lack complete medical records and apply conservative interpretations of eligibility criteria.

### Staffing Crisis at SSA

SSA's administrative capacity has eroded dramatically. The agency employed approximately 57,000 workers in FY 2024, down from over 64,000 a decade earlier, even as the U.S. population grew and aged (SSA FY 2025 Congressional Justification). Field offices have closed or reduced hours, and the ratio of claims to examiners has increased. Disability examiners in state DDS offices face high caseloads and turnover rates exceeding 15% annually in many states (National Council of Disability Determination Directors, 2024).

Congress has chronically underfunded SSA's administrative budget. The agency's operating expenses are funded through discretionary appropriations, not from the Social Security trust funds, meaning SSA must compete annually with every other domestic program for funding. In FY 2024, SSA received $14.2 billion in administrative funding, approximately $1.5 billion less than what the agency requested and what experts estimated was needed to maintain service levels (SSA FY 2024 Budget Justification).

### The SGA Cliff and Work Disincentives

The Substantial Gainful Activity (SGA) threshold creates a sharp benefit cliff that discourages beneficiaries from working. In 2024, a non-blind beneficiary who earns more than $1,550 per month (approximately $9.67/hour at 40 hours/week) during the Extended Period of Eligibility risks losing all cash benefits. For blind individuals, the threshold is $2,590 per month.

SSDI offers some transitional work incentives, but they are complex, poorly understood, and time-limited:

| Work Incentive | Description | Duration | Limitation |
|----------------|-------------|----------|------------|
| Trial Work Period (TWP) | Beneficiary can work and earn any amount while keeping benefits | 9 months within 60-month window | Non-renewable; months need not be consecutive |
| Extended Period of Eligibility (EPE) | Benefits paid in months earnings are below SGA | 36 months after TWP | Benefits cease entirely once SGA exceeded after EPE |
| Expedited Reinstatement | Can request reinstatement without new application | 5 years after benefits stop | Requires proving continuing disability |
| Impairment-Related Work Expenses (IRWE) | Disability-related costs deducted from earnings for SGA calculation | Ongoing | Complex documentation requirements |

The result is that only about 4% of SSDI beneficiaries attempt to return to work, and fewer than 1% earn enough to leave the rolls permanently (SSA, Annual Statistical Report on SSDI, 2024). The Ticket to Work program, which provides vocational rehabilitation services, has had limited participation -- fewer than 4% of eligible beneficiaries use their Ticket (GAO-24-106220, 2024).

## Impact

### On Applicants

The application process itself causes harm. Applicants must demonstrate that they are unable to perform "any substantial gainful activity" considering their age, education, work experience, and residual functional capacity. This requires extensive medical documentation, often at the applicant's expense, during a period when they typically have no income and may have lost employer-sponsored health insurance.

The process incentivizes claimants to emphasize incapacity over capability. Applicants who discuss wanting to work, maintaining social activities, or managing their conditions effectively risk having their claims denied because such statements can be interpreted as evidence of functional capacity. This perverse dynamic teaches people with disabilities to present themselves at their worst -- a psychologically damaging exercise that contradicts rehabilitation goals.

### On Beneficiaries

Once approved, beneficiaries face the SGA cliff, the two-year Medicare waiting period (during which they must rely on Medicaid, COBRA, or go uninsured), and average benefits that barely exceed the federal poverty level. For a single individual in 2025, the poverty threshold is approximately $15,650 annually; the average annual SSDI benefit is approximately $18,444 -- just 18% above the poverty line.

The two-year Medicare waiting period is particularly cruel: people who have been determined to be too disabled to work must wait 24 months from benefit entitlement (29 months from disability onset) before receiving the health insurance they need to manage their conditions. An estimated 1 in 7 SSDI beneficiaries has no health insurance during this waiting period (Commonwealth Fund, 2023).

### On the Workforce and Economy

SSDI's work disincentives remove productive capacity from the economy. Researchers at the National Bureau of Economic Research have estimated that SSDI reduces labor force participation among people with disabilities by 3 to 5 percentage points beyond what their conditions alone would cause (Autor and Duggan, "The Rise in the Disability Rolls and the Decline in Unemployment," *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 2003; updated estimates in Maestas et al., 2021). This represents hundreds of thousands of workers who could contribute to the economy in some capacity if the program were redesigned to support partial work.

### On State and Federal Budgets

SSA's administrative underfunding creates a false economy. Slow processing pushes applicants onto other safety net programs -- state-funded General Assistance, SNAP, Medicaid, homeless services -- that cost taxpayers more in aggregate than adequate SSA staffing would. The National Academy of Social Insurance estimated that every dollar spent on SSA administrative capacity returns $10 or more in reduced costs to other programs and in faster benefit delivery (NASI, "Strengthening Social Security's Administrative Capacity," 2024).

## Recent Developments

### FY 2024-2025 Administrative Developments

- **Hearing backlog reduction**: SSA reduced the hearing-level backlog from a 2017 peak of 1.1 million cases to approximately 547,000 in September 2024, primarily through increased ALJ hiring and video hearings. However, the pace of reduction has slowed, and average wait times remain above 15 months.
- **Telework expansion**: SSA expanded telework for disability examiners and ALJs during the COVID-19 pandemic and has maintained hybrid work models. Video hearings have become standard, reducing some geographic barriers but raising concerns about claimant access to technology.
- **Compassionate Allowances expansion**: SSA added new conditions to the Compassionate Allowances list in 2024, enabling faster processing for clearly severe conditions. The list now covers over 280 conditions.
- **Proposed SGA threshold increase**: Advocates have pushed for indexing the SGA threshold to median wage growth rather than CPI, which would raise it more significantly over time.

### Legislative Activity (119th Congress, 2025-2026)

- **SSDI Benefit Restoration Act**: Introduced to increase benefits for long-term beneficiaries whose purchasing power has eroded due to inadequate COLA adjustments. Would provide a one-time benefit increase and adjust the benefit formula.
- **SSA Modernization Act**: Bipartisan bill to increase SSA administrative funding, mandate technology upgrades, and establish processing time standards.
- **Work Without Worry Act**: Proposed replacement of the SGA cliff with a $1-for-$2 benefit offset, allowing beneficiaries to earn without the all-or-nothing benefit loss.
- **Disability Insurance Access Act**: Would eliminate the five-month waiting period for SSDI cash benefits and the two-year waiting period for Medicare coverage.

### Trust Fund Projections

The 2024 OASDI Trustees Report projected that the SSDI Trust Fund, considered separately, would remain solvent through at least the mid-2090s under intermediate assumptions, a significant improvement from projections a decade earlier that forecast depletion by 2016. This improvement reflects declining SSDI application and award rates since 2010, driven partly by demographic shifts (baby boomers aging onto retirement rolls) and improved labor market conditions. However, combined OASI and DI Trust Fund depletion is projected for 2035, and political dynamics may lead Congress to reallocate funds between the trust funds, potentially re-exposing SSDI to solvency pressure.
