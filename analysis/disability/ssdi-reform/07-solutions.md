# SSDI Reform: Solutions

## Solution 1: Replace the SGA Cliff with a Benefit Offset

### Description

Replace the all-or-nothing SGA threshold with a graduated benefit reduction: for every $2 earned above the SGA level, SSDI benefits would be reduced by $1. This ensures that work always increases total income and eliminates the catastrophic cliff where earning $1 above SGA can result in total benefit loss.

### Evidence

The Benefit Offset National Demonstration (BOND), conducted from 2011 to 2022 and evaluated by Mathematica, found that a $1-for-$2 offset modestly increased beneficiary earnings (approximately $50-100/month on average) without significantly increasing program costs. The Netherlands implemented a similar partial disability system in 2006 (the WIA), which categorizes workers into partial (35-80% disabled) and full (80-100% disabled) tiers and allows partial beneficiaries to work with reduced benefits. Dutch employment rates for people with partial disabilities increased from 40% to 55% within five years of implementation (OECD, "Mental Health and Work: Netherlands," 2014).

### Cost Estimate

The Congressional Budget Office estimated that a benefit offset similar to BOND would cost approximately $1.2 billion over 10 years due to slightly reduced benefit savings from work, but would generate offsetting revenue through increased payroll tax collections from working beneficiaries (CBO, "Options for Reducing the Deficit: Social Security," 2023). Net fiscal impact is approximately neutral to slightly positive over 20 years.

### Timeline

- Year 1: Enact legislation and develop implementation regulations
- Year 2-3: Phase in for new beneficiaries; offer opt-in for existing beneficiaries
- Year 3-5: Universal implementation with evaluation

### International Models

- **Netherlands (WIA)**: Graduated disability categories with partial benefits and work expectations
- **Sweden**: Partial disability pension (25%, 50%, 75%, or 100%) based on work capacity assessment
- **Germany**: Partial and full disability pensions with earnings allowances
- **Australia**: Disability Support Pension with income test that reduces benefits gradually

## Solution 2: Eliminate the Medicare Waiting Period

### Description

Eliminate the 24-month waiting period between SSDI entitlement and Medicare eligibility. Beneficiaries would receive Medicare coverage immediately upon benefit entitlement (or at least within 3 months), ending the period during which 1 in 7 SSDI beneficiaries go uninsured.

### Evidence

The Commonwealth Fund (2023) documented that SSDI beneficiaries without health insurance during the waiting period delay medical care, experience health deterioration, and incur higher long-term costs when they do obtain Medicare. The Medicare Payment Advisory Commission (MedPAC) has noted that beneficiaries who enter Medicare after the waiting period tend to have higher initial costs because conditions have worsened during the gap (MedPAC Annual Report, 2023). Eliminating the waiting period for beneficiaries with ALS was enacted in 2000 and has produced no adverse effects.

### Cost Estimate

CBO estimated that full elimination of the Medicare waiting period would cost approximately $10-12 billion per year in additional Medicare spending (CBO, 2023). Partial elimination (reducing to 6 months) would cost approximately $4-5 billion per year. These costs would be partially offset by reduced Medicaid spending and improved health outcomes.

### Timeline

- Year 1: Enact legislation
- Year 2: Implement for new beneficiaries
- Year 3: Extend to existing beneficiaries in waiting period

### International Models

Most OECD countries with disability benefit programs provide health coverage concurrently with cash benefits. The U.S. Medicare waiting period is an international outlier.

## Solution 3: Adequately Fund SSA Administration

### Description

Establish a dedicated, mandatory funding stream for SSA administrative operations, removing the agency from the annual discretionary appropriations process. Options include dedicating a fixed percentage of OASDI trust fund revenue to administration or creating a separate administrative trust fund with automatic annual adjustments.

### Evidence

The National Academy of Social Insurance estimated that SSA administrative costs represent approximately 1.4% of benefit payments, among the lowest ratios of any comparable program globally (NASI, 2024). Yet this budget is routinely cut. The agency's workforce declined from 64,000 in 2014 to 57,000 in 2024, even as the population it serves grew. Research consistently shows that every dollar spent on SSA administration yields $10+ in benefits: faster processing reduces costs to other safety net programs, better fraud detection reduces overpayments, and improved service increases voluntary compliance (NASI, 2024).

### Cost Estimate

Restoring SSA's administrative budget to adequate levels would require approximately $1.5-2 billion per year in additional funding above current appropriations. This investment would be substantially offset by reduced costs elsewhere: faster processing means fewer applicants on SNAP, Medicaid, and state assistance; better CDRs reduce overpayments; and improved technology reduces per-claim processing costs over time.

### Timeline

- Year 1: Enact mandatory funding legislation
- Years 1-3: Hire and train additional staff; upgrade technology
- Years 3-5: Achieve processing time targets (90 days initial, 180 days hearing)

### International Models

- **Canada**: Service Canada has dedicated administrative funding linked to program workload
- **United Kingdom**: Department for Work and Pensions receives administrative funding proportional to caseload

## Solution 4: Modernize the Disability Definition

### Description

Update SSDI's disability definition to recognize disability as a spectrum rather than a binary condition. Create categories of partial and total disability, with benefit amounts scaled to the degree of work incapacity. Workers assessed as partially disabled would receive reduced benefits and be expected to work to the extent of their capacity, with supported employment services available.

### Evidence

The OECD's cross-country analysis of disability benefit programs found that countries with graduated disability definitions have higher employment rates among people with disabilities and lower rates of permanent dependency than countries with binary systems (*Sickness, Disability and Work: Breaking the Barriers*, OECD, 2010; updated in "Disability, Work and Inclusion," 2022). The binary "all or nothing" definition is increasingly out of step with medical understanding of disability as a dynamic, contextual experience that varies by environment, accommodation, and support.

### Cost Estimate

Implementation costs would depend on design parameters. A revenue-neutral approach would redistribute existing SSDI spending across a broader population of partially disabled workers while reducing individual benefit amounts for those with partial capacity. CBO has not scored a specific proposal, but NASI modeling suggests that a graduated system could serve 15-20% more workers at comparable aggregate cost through a combination of partial benefits and increased work-related tax revenue (NASI, "Restructuring SSDI for the 21st Century," 2023).

### Timeline

- Years 1-2: Legislative enactment and rulemaking
- Years 2-4: Develop assessment tools for partial disability
- Years 3-5: Phase in for new applicants
- Years 5-10: Transition existing beneficiaries through voluntary opt-in

### International Models

- **Netherlands (WIA)**: Three categories -- fully disabled, partially disabled, temporary disability
- **Germany**: Partial disability pension proportional to remaining work capacity
- **Norway**: Graded disability benefits at 20% increments

## Solution 5: Streamline the Adjudication Process

### Description

Redesign the multi-stage adjudication process to reduce redundancy and accelerate decisions. Key elements: (1) strengthen initial determinations by providing DDS examiners with more complete evidence and more time per case, (2) replace the reconsideration stage (which adds months and rarely changes outcomes) with a pre-hearing conference, (3) establish binding processing time limits at each stage, and (4) expand Compassionate Allowances and presumptive disability for clearly qualifying conditions.

### Evidence

The reconsideration stage approves only 13% of denied claims and adds an average of 120-180 days to the process (SSA, 2024). Ten states operated under a "prototype" that eliminated reconsideration, and studies showed no significant impact on decision accuracy but measurable reduction in processing time (SSA Office of Research, 2018). Expanding Compassionate Allowances from the current 280+ conditions has been shown to dramatically reduce processing time for severe conditions without increasing error rates (SSA, 2024).

### Cost Estimate

Eliminating reconsideration would save approximately $200-300 million per year in processing costs (SSA OIG, 2020). Strengthening initial determinations would require an estimated $500-700 million per year in additional DDS funding. Net cost is approximately $200-400 million per year in additional DDS investment, offset by faster processing that reduces costs to other programs.

### Timeline

- Year 1: Enact legislation eliminating reconsideration; expand Compassionate Allowances
- Years 1-3: Hire and train additional DDS examiners; implement processing time standards
- Years 3-5: Evaluate and adjust based on outcomes

### International Models

- **Canada**: Single-stage disability determination with appeal to a tribunal
- **United Kingdom**: Single assessment by independent assessor (Health Assessment Advisory Service) with appeal to tribunal
- **Australia**: Single-stage assessment by Centrelink with review by Administrative Appeals Tribunal

## Solution 6: Expand and Simplify Return-to-Work Programs

### Description

Replace the underperforming Ticket to Work program with an integrated employment support system that is automatic, simple, and adequately funded. Key elements: (1) automatic enrollment in supported employment services upon benefit award, (2) elimination of the Trial Work Period time limits (allow unlimited work attempts without benefit loss risk), (3) extended Medicare coverage for 10 years after return to work (up from current 93 months), and (4) funding for evidence-based supported employment models like Individual Placement and Support (IPS).

### Evidence

The Ticket to Work program's low participation (under 4% of eligible beneficiaries) reflects its complexity, not beneficiaries' unwillingness to work (GAO, 2024). Individual Placement and Support (IPS), an evidence-based supported employment model, has been shown in over 30 randomized controlled trials to be 2-3 times more effective than traditional vocational rehabilitation for people with severe mental illness, with employment rates of 55-65% compared to 20-25% for controls (Drake et al., "IPS Supported Employment: An Evidence-Based Approach," *Psychiatric Services*, 2012; updated meta-analysis in Bond et al., 2020). IPS has also been adapted successfully for other disability populations.

### Cost Estimate

Comprehensive supported employment services for SSDI beneficiaries who want to work (estimated at 20-30% of the population) would cost approximately $2-4 billion per year. This would be partially offset by reduced benefit payments, increased tax revenue from working beneficiaries, and reduced costs to other safety net programs. The Congressional Budget Office has estimated that every $1 spent on effective vocational services for SSDI beneficiaries generates $1.50-2.00 in fiscal savings over 10 years (CBO, 2022).

### Timeline

- Year 1: Enact legislation; develop implementation plan
- Years 1-3: Build supported employment infrastructure; train providers
- Years 3-5: Scale up services; evaluate outcomes
- Years 5-10: Full implementation with ongoing evaluation

## Solution 7: Increase Benefit Adequacy

### Description

Increase SSDI benefits for long-term beneficiaries and those with below-poverty benefits through a combination of: (1) a special minimum benefit floor (e.g., 125% of the federal poverty level for individuals), (2) a supplemental payment for beneficiaries who have been on the rolls for 15+ years whose benefits have eroded in real terms, and (3) annual COLA adjustments based on CPI-E (Elderly) rather than CPI-W (Urban Wage Earners), which better reflects expenditure patterns of disabled and elderly populations.

### Evidence

Approximately 25% of SSDI beneficiaries live below the federal poverty level, and 36% rely on SSDI as their sole income source (Census Bureau, 2024; SSA, 2024). Long-term beneficiaries are particularly vulnerable because their benefits are based on earnings from years or decades earlier, and COLA adjustments have not kept pace with the actual cost increases they face, particularly in healthcare and housing. The Bureau of Labor Statistics' CPI-E shows that inflation for elderly and disabled populations has historically exceeded CPI-W by approximately 0.2 percentage points per year, compounding to significant purchasing power loss over decades (BLS, 2023).

### Cost Estimate

A poverty-level benefit floor would cost approximately $3-5 billion per year. Switching to CPI-E would cost approximately $1-2 billion per year, growing over time. Supplemental payments for long-term beneficiaries would cost approximately $2-3 billion per year depending on design. Total: approximately $6-10 billion per year.

### Timeline

- Year 1: Enact legislation
- Year 2: Implement benefit floor and COLA change for current beneficiaries
- Ongoing: Annual adjustments

## Solution 8: Technology Modernization

### Description

Invest in comprehensive technology modernization at SSA, including: (1) AI-assisted initial medical review to identify claims that clearly meet Compassionate Allowance criteria, (2) electronic health record integration with healthcare providers to reduce documentation burdens, (3) a modern case management system replacing legacy mainframe applications, and (4) predictive analytics to identify claims at high risk of reversal on appeal (indicating need for more thorough initial review).

### Evidence

SSA's core case processing systems run on legacy COBOL code dating to the 1980s. The agency has attempted multiple modernization efforts but has been constrained by inadequate funding. Private disability insurers process claims in 30-90 days using modern technology; SSA takes 213 days for initial claims using systems that are decades behind industry standards. Electronic health record integration alone could eliminate weeks from the claims process by automating medical evidence collection (SSA OIG, "Technology Modernization Challenges," 2023).

### Cost Estimate

A comprehensive technology modernization program would require approximately $3-5 billion over 5-7 years in capital investment, with annual maintenance costs of approximately $500 million. These costs would be offset by long-term reductions in per-claim processing costs, reduced appeals (from better initial decisions), and improved fraud detection.

### Timeline

- Years 1-2: Secure funding; begin procurement and planning
- Years 2-5: Develop and deploy new case management and EHR integration systems
- Years 3-7: Full deployment; decommission legacy systems
- Ongoing: Continuous improvement and maintenance

## Sequencing and Priority

| Priority | Solution | Rationale |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| 1 | Fund SSA administration (Solution 3) | Prerequisite for all other reforms; immediate impact on processing times |
| 2 | Streamline adjudication (Solution 5) | Addresses most immediate harm (processing delays and deaths) |
| 3 | Replace SGA cliff with offset (Solution 1) | Highest-evidence reform with bipartisan appeal |
| 4 | Eliminate Medicare waiting period (Solution 2) | Prevents health deterioration; partially offset by Medicaid savings |
| 5 | Expand return-to-work programs (Solution 6) | Builds on offset by providing services to support work |
| 6 | Technology modernization (Solution 8) | Enables long-term efficiency; requires investment period |
| 7 | Increase benefit adequacy (Solution 7) | Important but most expensive; sequence after cost-saving reforms |
| 8 | Modernize disability definition (Solution 4) | Most complex; requires assessment infrastructure built by other reforms |
