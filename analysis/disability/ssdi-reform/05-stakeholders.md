# SSDI Reform: Stakeholders

## Primary Stakeholders

### People Directly Affected

| Stakeholder Group | Size | Interest | Current Impact |
|-------------------|------|----------|---------------|
| SSDI applicants (pending) | ~2.3 million in process at any time | Timely, fair adjudication | Endure months-to-years of uncertainty; 10,000+ die waiting annually |
| SSDI beneficiaries (disabled workers) | 7.3 million (December 2024) | Adequate benefits, work opportunity, healthcare | Average benefit barely above poverty; SGA cliff traps in dependency |
| SSDI dependents | 1.4 million | Family stability, income support | Benefits tied to disabled worker's status; vulnerable to termination |
| Denied applicants | ~1.2 million denied annually at all levels | Accurate decisions, meaningful appeals | Must navigate complex appeals or go without support |
| Workers with partial disabilities | Millions (uncounted) | Recognition of partial work capacity | Excluded from SSDI; must prove total disability or get nothing |

### Workers and Their Families

Every American worker who pays FICA taxes is a potential SSDI beneficiary. The program is not welfare; it is insurance that workers earn through their payroll tax contributions. Approximately 1 in 4 of today's 20-year-olds will experience a disability before reaching retirement age (SSA, "Disability and Death Probability Tables," 2023). This makes SSDI reform relevant to the entire working population, not just current beneficiaries.

Families of SSDI applicants and beneficiaries bear enormous burdens. Spouses may need to leave employment to provide care. Children of disabled parents face economic instability and educational disruption. The median income of households with an SSDI beneficiary is approximately 60% of the national median (Census Bureau, American Community Survey, 2023).

## Decision Makers

### Federal Government

| Decision Maker | Role | Disposition Toward Reform |
|---------------|------|--------------------------|
| Social Security Administration | Administers SSDI; sets processing procedures; proposes regulations | Generally supports modernization but constrained by budget and institutional culture |
| SSA Commissioner | Appointed by President; sets agency priorities | Varies with administration; recent commissioners have focused on backlog reduction |
| Congress (Ways and Means, Finance) | Authorizes program changes; sets benefit levels; controls SSA budget | Divided; some members support modernization, others focus on cost cutting or benefit expansion |
| Congress (Appropriations) | Sets SSA administrative funding annually | Historically underfunds SSA relative to workload |
| President | Appoints SSA Commissioner; proposes budget; signs legislation | Priority varies by administration |
| Office of Management and Budget | Reviews SSA budget requests; shapes administration proposals | Often reduces SSA administrative budget requests |
| Administrative Law Judges | Conduct SSDI hearings; make benefit decisions | Strong views on decisional independence; resist productivity mandates |

### State Government

| Decision Maker | Role | Disposition |
|---------------|------|------------|
| State DDS agencies | Make initial disability determinations | Vary widely; generally support adequate federal funding for their operations |
| State governors | Oversee DDS operations in their states | Mixed; some advocate for federalizing DDS, others protect state control |
| State Medicaid agencies | Provide healthcare to SSDI applicants during waiting period | Support elimination of Medicare waiting period (would reduce Medicaid costs) |

## Coalition Dynamics

### Reform Coalition (Potential)

A bipartisan SSDI reform coalition could form around shared interests in:

- **Faster processing**: Nearly universal support across the political spectrum
- **Return-to-work incentives**: Supported by both fiscal conservatives (reduces long-term costs) and disability advocates (promotes autonomy)
- **SSA modernization**: Technology and process improvements that improve efficiency appeal broadly
- **Benefit offset**: Replaces cliff with gradual reduction; supported by researchers and policy organizations across the ideological spectrum

### Opposition Coalition

Structural reform faces opposition from actors who benefit from the status quo:

- **Disability attorneys**: Concerned that faster processing or benefit offsets could reduce fee-generating appeals
- **State DDS agencies**: Resist federalization or restructuring of their operations
- **Fiscal absolutists**: Oppose any reform that increases short-term costs, even if it reduces long-term costs
- **Program purists**: Oppose any changes perceived as weakening eligibility standards or benefit levels

### Key Coalition Fractures

The disability community itself is not unified on reform. Some organizations prioritize benefit adequacy (higher payments, elimination of waiting periods) and resist structural changes that could be used to cut benefits. Others prioritize work incentives and graduated benefits, arguing that the binary standard is itself the primary obstacle to economic participation. Bridging this divide requires reform proposals that demonstrably protect existing beneficiaries while improving the system for future applicants.

## Power Analysis

### Who Has Power

| Actor | Power Source | Level of Influence |
|-------|------------|-------------------|
| Congressional appropriators | Control SSA budget | Very High |
| SSA institutional leadership | Administrative discretion, rulemaking | High |
| ALJs and their union (AALJ) | Decisional independence, collective bargaining | Moderate-High |
| NOSSCR and disability attorneys | Lobbying, campaign contributions, expertise | Moderate |
| AARP | Membership size, lobbying power (Social Security broadly) | Moderate |
| Disability rights organizations | Moral authority, legal expertise, grassroots | Moderate |
| OMB | Budget review authority | Moderate |

### Who Lacks Power

| Actor | Barrier to Influence | Consequence |
|-------|---------------------|-------------|
| SSDI applicants and beneficiaries | Poverty, disability, isolation, stigma | Least powerful group most affected by program decisions |
| DDS examiners | Mid-level employees with no policy voice | Bear productivity burden of underfunding |
| Family caregivers | Invisible in policy process | Costs of program dysfunction fall on families |
| Future beneficiaries | Not yet identifiable as a constituency | Reform inertia protects status quo at their expense |
