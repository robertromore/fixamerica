# SSDI Reform: Roadmap

## Phase 1: Administrative Fixes and Quick Wins (Years 1-2)

### Objective

Address the most immediate harms -- processing delays, applicant suffering, and SSA resource constraints -- through measures that can be implemented administratively or through bipartisan legislation with relatively low political friction.

### Actions

| Action | Type | Lead Actor | Timeline | Est. Cost |
|--------|------|-----------|----------|-----------|
| Increase SSA administrative appropriation by $1.5B | Legislative (Appropriations) | Congress | Year 1 | $1.5B/year |
| Expand Compassionate Allowances list to 400+ conditions | Administrative | SSA Commissioner | Months 1-12 | Minimal (administrative) |
| Eliminate reconsideration stage in all states | Legislative | Congress (Ways and Means, Finance) | Year 1 | Net savings $200-300M/year |
| Establish binding processing time standards (90/180/365 days) | Legislative | Congress | Year 1 | Enforcement costs included in admin funding |
| Deploy presumptive disability for terminal conditions | Administrative | SSA | Months 3-9 | Minimal |
| Hire 5,000 additional DDS examiners and SSA staff | Administrative (with funding) | SSA, state DDS agencies | Months 6-24 | ~$500M/year |
| Expand video hearing capacity and technology | Administrative | SSA | Months 3-18 | $50-100M one-time |

### Milestones

- Month 6: Compassionate Allowances list expanded; presumptive disability for terminal conditions operational
- Month 12: Reconsideration elimination enacted; processing time standards established; hiring underway
- Month 18: 2,500+ new staff onboarded; video hearing capacity doubled
- Month 24: Initial processing time reduced to 150 days; hearing wait times reduced to 12 months

## Phase 2: Structural Reform (Years 2-5)

### Objective

Implement the core structural reforms that address SSDI's fundamental design flaws: the SGA cliff, work disincentives, the Medicare waiting period, and benefit adequacy. These changes require legislation and regulatory development but build on the administrative foundation established in Phase 1.

### Actions

| Action | Type | Lead Actor | Timeline | Est. Cost |
|--------|------|-----------|----------|-----------|
| Enact $1-for-$2 benefit offset replacing SGA cliff | Legislative | Congress | Year 2 | ~$1.2B/10yr (CBO) |
| Eliminate Medicare waiting period (phase to 6 months, then 0) | Legislative | Congress | Years 2-3 | $4-5B/year initially, rising to $10-12B |
| Establish minimum benefit floor at 125% FPL | Legislative | Congress | Year 3 | $3-5B/year |
| Extend Medicare coverage post-return-to-work to 10 years | Legislative | Congress | Year 2 | $1-2B/year |
| Replace Ticket to Work with integrated employment support | Legislative + Administrative | Congress, SSA, DOL | Years 2-4 | $2-4B/year |
| Fund IPS supported employment in all states | Legislative + Administrative | Congress, SAMHSA, DOL | Years 3-5 | $1-2B/year |
| Switch COLA to CPI-E | Legislative | Congress | Year 3 | $1-2B/year |
| Begin technology modernization procurement | Administrative (with funding) | SSA | Year 2 | $500M-1B/year capital |

### Milestones

- Year 2: Benefit offset enacted and operational for new beneficiaries; Medicare waiting period reduced to 6 months
- Year 3: Medicare waiting period eliminated; minimum benefit floor implemented; COLA switched to CPI-E
- Year 4: Integrated employment support system operational in 25+ states; first technology modernization deployments
- Year 5: Benefit offset available to all beneficiaries; IPS supported employment available nationwide; processing times at targets (90 days initial, 180 days hearing)

## Phase 3: System Transformation (Years 5-10)

### Objective

Complete the transformation from a binary, reactive disability determination system to a graduated, proactive disability support system that maximizes economic participation while ensuring income security.

### Actions

| Action | Type | Lead Actor | Timeline | Est. Cost |
|--------|------|-----------|----------|-----------|
| Implement graduated disability definition (partial/total) | Legislative + Regulatory | Congress, SSA | Years 5-7 | Revenue-neutral if designed as redistribution |
| Develop and validate partial disability assessment tools | Administrative + Research | SSA, NASI | Years 5-8 | $100-200M R&D |
| Transition existing beneficiaries to graduated system (voluntary) | Administrative | SSA | Years 7-10 | Administrative costs within modernized budget |
| Complete technology modernization; decommission legacy systems | Administrative | SSA | Years 5-7 | Final $1-2B capital |
| Integrate SSDI with vocational rehabilitation and workforce systems | Administrative + Legislative | SSA, DOL, state VR agencies | Years 5-8 | $500M-1B/year |
| Establish dedicated mandatory SSA administrative funding | Legislative | Congress | Year 5 | Budget-neutral (shift from discretionary to mandatory) |
| Implement EHR integration with healthcare providers | Administrative | SSA, CMS | Years 6-9 | $500M-1B capital |

### Milestones

- Year 6: Graduated disability definition enacted; assessment tool development underway
- Year 7: Technology modernization complete; EHR integration pilot in 10 states
- Year 8: Partial disability category operational for new applicants
- Year 9: EHR integration nationwide; integrated workforce system operational
- Year 10: Full graduated system available; SSDI return-to-work rate above 10%; processing times consistently below targets

## Risk Mitigation

### Political Risks

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation Strategy |
|------|------------|--------|---------------------|
| Partisan gridlock prevents legislation | High | High | Build bipartisan coalitions around efficiency/modernization; pursue administrative reforms first |
| Reform framed as benefit cuts | Medium | High | Lead with benefit increases and process improvements; protect existing beneficiaries |
| Change in administration reverses progress | Medium | Medium | Embed reforms in statute rather than regulation; build constituency for reforms |
| Trust Fund solvency concerns crowd out reform | Medium | Medium | Demonstrate that reforms improve solvency through work incentives and efficiency |

### Implementation Risks

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation Strategy |
|------|------------|--------|---------------------|
| SSA cannot hire fast enough | Medium | Medium | Use contractors for surge capacity; expand DDS partnership |
| Technology modernization delayed or over budget | High | Medium | Phased deployment; modular procurement; learn from past failures |
| Partial disability assessments prove unreliable | Medium | High | Extensive pilot testing; international benchmarking; continuous validation |
| Supported employment providers lack capacity | Medium | Medium | Multi-year phase-in; invest in provider training; use IPS fidelity standards |

### Beneficiary Risks

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation Strategy |
|------|------------|--------|---------------------|
| Existing beneficiaries lose benefits during transition | Low (if designed properly) | Very High | Grandfather existing beneficiaries; make transitions voluntary; hold harmless provisions |
| Work incentives used to justify benefit cuts | Medium | High | Separate work incentive reforms from benefit adequacy measures; statutory protections |
| Faster processing reduces decision quality | Medium | Medium | Pair speed improvements with accuracy standards; quality review system |
