# Employment Discrimination: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Closing the disability employment gap requires legislative action on multiple fronts: strengthening EEOC enforcement capacity, eliminating the subminimum wage, regulating AI hiring tools for disability discrimination, reforming benefit system work incentives, and expanding employer incentives for disability hiring. This document provides draft federal legislation, state model legislation, a regulatory framework, legal analysis, and loophole identification for each major reform.

---

## Federal Legislation

### Bill 1: EEOC Disability Enforcement Enhancement Act

**Purpose**: Significantly increase EEOC staffing and funding dedicated to ADA Title I enforcement, create a specialized systemic disability discrimination investigation unit, and establish AI hiring investigation capability.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "EEOC Disability Enforcement
Enhancement Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.
(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that—
    (1) ADA Title I has been in effect for over 35 years, yet the
    employment rate for people with disabilities is only 22.5
    percent, compared to 65.8 percent for people without
    disabilities;
    (2) the EEOC processes approximately 29,000 ADA employment
    charges annually but achieves merit resolution in only 16.4
    percent of cases;
    (3) the EEOC files only approximately 60 ADA lawsuits per year,
    insufficient to produce systemic deterrence;
    (4) hiring discrimination against people with disabilities is
    pervasive, with audit studies documenting 26 to 40 percent
    reductions in employer interest when disability is disclosed;
    (5) artificial intelligence hiring tools pose emerging risks
    of algorithmic disability discrimination that the EEOC
    currently lacks capacity to investigate; and
    (6) the disability employment gap costs the economy an estimated
    $1.8 trillion in lost productivity and consumer spending.
(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to strengthen EEOC
enforcement of ADA Title I through increased resources, specialized
investigation capacity, and AI hiring oversight authority.

SEC. 3. EEOC DISABILITY ENFORCEMENT FUNDING.
(a) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There is authorized to be
appropriated to the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission
$150,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2033, in
addition to any other amounts authorized or appropriated for the
Commission, for the purpose of enhancing ADA Title I enforcement.
(b) ALLOCATION.—Of the amounts appropriated under subsection (a),
the Commission shall allocate—
    (1) not less than $80,000,000 for the hiring and training of
    not fewer than 200 additional field investigators dedicated
    to disability discrimination;
    (2) not less than $30,000,000 for systemic disability
    discrimination investigations;
    (3) not less than $15,000,000 for AI hiring tool investigation
    and enforcement;
    (4) not less than $15,000,000 for disability discrimination
    litigation; and
    (5) remaining amounts for administrative support, training,
    and technology modernization.
(c) STAFFING TIMELINE.—The Commission shall—
    (1) hire not fewer than 75 additional investigators within
    12 months of initial appropriation;
    (2) hire not fewer than 150 additional investigators within
    24 months; and
    (3) achieve full staffing of 200 additional investigators
    within 36 months.

SEC. 4. SYSTEMIC DISABILITY DISCRIMINATION UNIT.
(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Commission shall establish a Systemic
Disability Discrimination Investigation Unit within the Office
of Systemic Investigations.
(b) MISSION.—The Unit shall—
    (1) investigate patterns or practices of disability
    discrimination in hiring, accommodation, and terms and
    conditions of employment;
    (2) conduct industry-wide investigations where evidence
    indicates systemic barriers;
    (3) prioritize investigations of large employers, staffing
    agencies, and hiring platform operators;
    (4) coordinate with OFCCP on Section 503 compliance for
    federal contractors; and
    (5) issue public reports on systemic investigation findings
    and outcomes.
(c) MINIMUM INVESTIGATIONS.—The Unit shall initiate not fewer
than 40 systemic disability discrimination investigations per
fiscal year beginning in fiscal year 2029.

SEC. 5. AI HIRING INVESTIGATION AUTHORITY.
(a) IN GENERAL.—The Commission shall establish an Algorithmic
Fairness Enforcement Unit to investigate the use of artificial
intelligence and algorithmic tools in employment decisions for
compliance with the Americans with Disabilities Act.
(b) AUTHORITY.—The Unit shall have authority to—
    (1) require employers to disclose the use of AI tools in
    hiring, promotion, and termination decisions;
    (2) require employers and AI tool vendors to produce
    documentation of bias testing, validation studies, and
    accommodation procedures;
    (3) conduct audits of AI hiring tools for disability
    discrimination, including disparate impact analysis;
    (4) issue guidance on employer obligations under the ADA
    with respect to AI hiring tools; and
    (5) bring enforcement actions against employers whose AI
    hiring tools produce unlawful disability discrimination.
(c) RECORD RETENTION.—Employers using AI tools in employment
decisions shall retain records of tool design, validation, and
outcomes data disaggregated by disability status for not less
than 3 years.
(d) REASONABLE ACCOMMODATION IN AUTOMATED PROCESSES.—
    (1) IN GENERAL.—Employers shall provide reasonable
    accommodations in automated hiring processes, including but
    not limited to alternative application methods, modified
    assessments, and human review of algorithmically generated
    decisions upon request.
    (2) NOTICE.—Employers using AI tools in hiring shall notify
    applicants of the use of such tools and of the availability
    of reasonable accommodations in the automated process.

SEC. 6. CHARGE PROCESSING STANDARDS.
(a) TIMELINESS.—The Commission shall resolve ADA charges within
180 days of filing, absent documented extraordinary circumstances
requiring extension.
(b) HIRING DISCRIMINATION PRIORITY.—The Commission shall
prioritize investigation of ADA charges alleging hiring
discrimination, recognizing that hiring discrimination is the
most impactful and underreported form of disability employment
discrimination.
(c) DATA COLLECTION AND REPORTING.—The Commission shall collect
and publicly report annual data on—
    (1) ADA charge volume, processing time, and outcomes
    disaggregated by disability type, charge basis, employer
    size, and industry;
    (2) AI-related disability discrimination charges and outcomes;
    (3) systemic investigation activity and results; and
    (4) litigation outcomes and monetary recovery.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Creates a dedicated, substantial funding stream for ADA enforcement that cannot be diverted to other Commission priorities
- **Section 4**: Addresses the critical need for systemic investigations that go beyond individual charge processing
- **Section 5**: Provides the first explicit statutory authority for EEOC to investigate AI hiring tools for disability discrimination
- **Section 6**: Establishes accountability for charge processing speed and data transparency

**Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Appropriation may not match authorization | Authorization of specific amounts creates political expectation; appropriators retain discretion |
| Investigator recruitment at scale | Phased hiring over 3 years; competitive salary authority; coordinate with law schools |
| Business community opposition | Enforcement of existing law, not new mandates; reduces litigation uncertainty |
| AI enforcement technical complexity | Dedicated unit with technology expertise; coordinate with NIST |

**Refinements**:

- **Stronger version**: Mandate minimum penalty amounts for systemic violations; give EEOC subpoena authority for AI tool documentation
- **Compromise version**: Reduce authorization amount; focus on AI enforcement unit and charge processing improvements
- **Fallback version**: AI hiring disclosure requirements only (lowest political resistance)

### Bill 2: Transformation to Competitive Integrated Employment Act

**Purpose**: Phase out Section 14(c) of the Fair Labor Standards Act over 6 years, ending authorization of subminimum wages for workers with disabilities, and fund transition to competitive integrated employment.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Transformation to Competitive
Integrated Employment Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.
Congress finds that—
    (1) Section 14(c) of the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938
    authorizes employers to pay workers with disabilities below
    the federal minimum wage;
    (2) approximately 40,000 workers with disabilities are
    currently paid subminimum wages, some earning less than
    $1.00 per hour;
    (3) 15 States and the District of Columbia have eliminated
    subminimum wages for workers with disabilities without mass
    unemployment;
    (4) the National Council on Disability has recommended
    elimination of Section 14(c) since 2012;
    (5) subminimum wage perpetuates poverty and segregation for
    workers with disabilities; and
    (6) competitive integrated employment with appropriate
    supports produces better outcomes for workers with
    disabilities.

SEC. 3. MORATORIUM ON NEW CERTIFICATES.
(a) IN GENERAL.—The Secretary of Labor shall not issue any new
special minimum wage certificate under section 14(c) of the
Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 (29 U.S.C. 214(c)) after the
date of enactment of this Act.
(b) RENEWALS.—Certificates in effect on the date of enactment
may be renewed during the phase-out period under section 4
subject to the minimum wage floor requirements of that section.

SEC. 4. PHASE-OUT OF EXISTING CERTIFICATES.
(a) WAGE FLOOR SCHEDULE.—
    (1) YEAR 1.—Beginning 1 year after the date of enactment,
    no worker paid under a section 14(c) certificate shall be
    paid less than 50 percent of the applicable minimum wage.
    (2) YEAR 3.—Beginning 3 years after the date of enactment,
    no worker paid under a section 14(c) certificate shall be
    paid less than 75 percent of the applicable minimum wage.
    (3) YEAR 5.—Beginning 5 years after the date of enactment,
    no worker paid under a section 14(c) certificate shall be
    paid less than the applicable minimum wage.
(b) REPEAL.—Section 14(c) of the Fair Labor Standards Act of
1938 (29 U.S.C. 214(c)) is repealed effective 6 years after
the date of enactment of this Act.
(c) APPLICABLE MINIMUM WAGE.—For purposes of this section,
"applicable minimum wage" means the higher of the federal
minimum wage or the applicable state or local minimum wage.

SEC. 5. COMPETITIVE INTEGRATED EMPLOYMENT TRANSITION GRANTS.
(a) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
$300,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2036 for
grants to States, territories, and service providers to
transition from subminimum wage employment to competitive
integrated employment.
(b) PERMISSIBLE USES.—Grant funds may be used for—
    (1) job placement, job coaching, and ongoing supported
    employment services;
    (2) customized employment development;
    (3) training and retraining of provider staff in competitive
    employment models;
    (4) transportation and assistive technology supports;
    (5) employer engagement and technical assistance;
    (6) benefits counseling for workers transitioning to
    competitive employment; and
    (7) evaluation and data collection on transition outcomes.
(c) ALLOCATION.—Of the amounts appropriated—
    (1) 70 percent shall be distributed to States by formula
    based on the number of workers currently paid subminimum
    wages and the number of individuals in segregated
    employment settings;
    (2) 20 percent shall be available as competitive grants for
    innovative transition models; and
    (3) 10 percent shall be reserved for national technical
    assistance, evaluation, and reporting.
(d) STATE PLAN REQUIREMENT.—Each State receiving funds shall
submit a plan to the Secretary of Labor describing—
    (1) the number of workers currently in subminimum wage
    employment;
    (2) the transition strategy and timeline;
    (3) the competitive integrated employment services to be
    provided; and
    (4) performance metrics and evaluation methodology.

SEC. 6. EMPLOYER TRANSITION SUPPORT.
(a) TAX CREDIT.—Employers who hire workers transitioning from
subminimum wage employment to competitive integrated employment
shall be eligible for a tax credit equal to $5,000 per worker
hired and retained for not fewer than 6 months.
(b) TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE.—The Secretary of Labor, in
coordination with the Job Accommodation Network, shall provide
technical assistance to employers hiring former subminimum
wage workers.

SEC. 7. WORKER PROTECTIONS.
(a) NO REDUCTION IN SERVICES.—No worker transitioning from
subminimum wage employment shall experience a reduction in
disability-related services, supports, or benefits as a result
of this transition, except to the extent that competitive
employment earnings render means-tested benefits inapplicable.
(b) CHOICE.—Workers and their guardians shall participate in
transition planning and have meaningful choice in employment
options.
(c) MONITORING.—The Secretary of Labor shall monitor transition
outcomes for a period of not less than 5 years after the
effective date of the section 14(c) repeal and report annually
to Congress.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Immediately stops growth of the subminimum wage system
- **Section 4**: Provides a gradual 6-year phase-out that gives providers time to transition
- **Section 5**: Ensures adequate funding for transition services, the critical component
- **Section 6**: Creates employer incentives to hire transitioning workers
- **Section 7**: Protects workers during transition from loss of services or benefits

**Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Provider organization opposition | Transition funding addresses financial concerns; phase-out timeline allows planning |
| Parent group concerns about service loss | Worker protection provisions; participation in planning; monitoring |
| Transition funding adequacy | $300 million/year for 10 years; formula allocation ensures equity |
| Regional capacity variation | Technical assistance and competitive grants address capacity gaps |

**Refinements**:

- **Stronger version**: Shorter phase-out (3 years); higher wage floors; mandatory provider conversion
- **Compromise version**: 8-year phase-out; additional provider transition funding; pilot competitive employment in states without capacity
- **Fallback version**: Moratorium on new certificates only; voluntary transition incentives

### Bill 3: AI Hiring Fairness Act

**Purpose**: Require employers and AI tool developers to audit hiring algorithms for disability discrimination, provide reasonable accommodations in automated hiring processes, and disclose AI use to applicants.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "AI Hiring Fairness Act".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.
In this Act—
    (1) AUTOMATED EMPLOYMENT DECISION TOOL.—The term "automated
    employment decision tool" means any system that uses
    machine learning, artificial intelligence, statistical
    modeling, or similar techniques to substantially assist or
    replace human decision-making in hiring, promotion,
    termination, discipline, or compensation decisions.
    (2) COVERED EMPLOYER.—The term "covered employer" means any
    employer subject to Title I of the Americans with
    Disabilities Act of 1990 (42 U.S.C. 12111 et seq.).
    (3) BIAS AUDIT.—The term "bias audit" means an independent,
    impartial evaluation of an automated employment decision
    tool that tests for disparate impact on the basis of
    disability status and other protected characteristics.
    (4) DEVELOPER.—The term "developer" means any person or
    entity that designs, develops, or substantially modifies
    an automated employment decision tool.

SEC. 3. BIAS AUDIT REQUIREMENTS.
(a) IN GENERAL.—No covered employer shall use an automated
employment decision tool unless the tool has been subject to a
bias audit within the preceding 12 months.
(b) AUDIT STANDARDS.—
    (1) The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, in
    consultation with the National Institute of Standards and
    Technology, shall issue standards for bias audits not later
    than 18 months after the date of enactment.
    (2) Until such standards are issued, bias audits shall, at
    minimum—
        (A) test for disparate impact against applicants and
        employees with disabilities;
        (B) evaluate whether the tool provides or allows for
        reasonable accommodations;
        (C) assess the accuracy and validity of the tool's
        predictions across disability subgroups; and
        (D) be conducted by an independent auditor with no
        financial interest in the outcome.
(c) AUDIT RESULTS.—
    (1) RETENTION.—Covered employers shall retain bias audit
    results for not less than 4 years.
    (2) AVAILABILITY.—Bias audit results shall be made available
    to the EEOC upon request during an investigation.
    (3) SUMMARY DISCLOSURE.—Covered employers with 100 or more
    employees shall publish an annual summary of bias audit
    findings on their public-facing websites.

SEC. 4. NOTICE AND ACCOMMODATION REQUIREMENTS.
(a) NOTICE.—Before using an automated employment decision tool
to evaluate a candidate, a covered employer shall provide
notice to the candidate that—
    (1) an automated tool will be used in the evaluation;
    (2) the general type and purpose of the tool (e.g., resume
    screening, video analysis, skills assessment); and
    (3) the candidate may request a reasonable accommodation
    or alternative evaluation process.
(b) REASONABLE ACCOMMODATION.—
    (1) IN GENERAL.—Covered employers shall provide reasonable
    accommodations in automated hiring processes upon request,
    including but not limited to—
        (A) an alternative non-automated evaluation method;
        (B) modifications to automated assessments to account
        for disability;
        (C) extended time for timed assessments;
        (D) accessible formats for all automated communications
        and assessments; and
        (E) human review of automated decisions upon request.
    (2) INTERACTIVE PROCESS.—The obligation to engage in an
    interactive process under the ADA extends to
    accommodation requests related to automated hiring tools.

SEC. 5. DEVELOPER OBLIGATIONS.
(a) IN GENERAL.—Developers of automated employment decision
tools shall—
    (1) design tools that are accessible to individuals with
    disabilities, consistent with applicable accessibility
    standards;
    (2) provide covered employers with documentation of
    disability-related bias testing conducted during
    development;
    (3) enable reasonable accommodations within the tool's
    functionality; and
    (4) provide employers with the technical information
    necessary to conduct bias audits.
(b) SHARED LIABILITY.—A developer that fails to meet the
requirements of subsection (a) shall be jointly liable with
the employer for any resulting disability discrimination.

SEC. 6. ENFORCEMENT.
(a) EEOC AUTHORITY.—The Commission shall enforce this Act
through the procedures established in Title I of the
Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990.
(b) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—An individual aggrieved by a
violation of this Act may bring a civil action under the
same terms and conditions as an action under Title I of the
Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990.
(c) PENALTIES.—In addition to remedies available under Title I,
a court may impose civil penalties of not more than $50,000
per violation for failure to conduct a bias audit or provide
required notice.

SEC. 7. COMPLIANCE DEADLINES.
(a) LARGE EMPLOYERS.—Covered employers with 500 or more
employees shall comply with this Act not later than 18 months
after the date of enactment.
(b) MEDIUM EMPLOYERS.—Covered employers with 50 to 499
employees shall comply not later than 30 months after the
date of enactment.
(c) SMALL EMPLOYERS.—Covered employers with 15 to 49 employees
shall comply not later than 42 months after the date of
enactment.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Creates the first federal requirement for disability-specific bias audits of AI hiring tools
- **Section 4**: Ensures that the shift to automated hiring does not eliminate accommodation rights
- **Section 5**: Extends liability to AI tool developers, creating upstream incentives for accessible design
- **Section 6**: Integrates enforcement with existing ADA mechanisms for consistency
- **Section 7**: Phased compliance by employer size gives smaller employers more time

**Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Technology industry opposition | Phased compliance; technical assistance; clear standards reduce uncertainty |
| Rapidly evolving AI landscape | Annual audit requirement ensures ongoing compliance; EEOC rulemaking authority for updates |
| Defining audit standards | EEOC/NIST collaboration; interim standards with subsequent refinement |
| Developer shared liability may chill innovation | Liability only for failure to meet specific requirements; safe harbor for compliant developers |

**Refinements**:

- **Stronger version**: Mandatory pre-deployment testing; annual public reporting for all employers; treble damages for willful violations
- **Compromise version**: Audit requirement for large employers only; voluntary compliance program for smaller employers
- **Fallback version**: Disclosure and accommodation requirements only (no bias audit mandate)

### Bill 4: Disability Employment Incentive Act

**Purpose**: Consolidate and expand existing disability employment tax incentives into a unified credit that rewards hiring, retention, and accessibility investment.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Disability Employment Incentive Act".

SEC. 2. UNIFIED DISABILITY EMPLOYMENT TAX CREDIT.
(a) IN GENERAL.—Subpart D of part IV of subchapter A of chapter
1 of the Internal Revenue Code of 1986 is amended by adding at
the end the following new section:

"SEC. 45BB. DISABILITY EMPLOYMENT CREDIT.
"(a) GENERAL RULE.—For purposes of section 38, the disability
employment credit for any taxable year is the sum of—
    (1) the hiring credit under subsection (b);
    (2) the retention credit under subsection (c); and
    (3) the accessibility credit under subsection (d).
"(b) HIRING CREDIT.—
    (1) IN GENERAL.—The hiring credit is an amount equal to
    $5,000 for each qualified individual with a disability
    hired during the taxable year and retained for not fewer
    than 6 months.
    (2) SMALL BUSINESS ENHANCEMENT.—For eligible small employers,
    the hiring credit under paragraph (1) shall be $7,500.
"(c) RETENTION CREDIT.—
    (1) IN GENERAL.—The retention credit is an amount equal to
    $2,500 for each qualified individual with a disability who
    has been employed by the taxpayer for not fewer than 12
    months as of the close of the taxable year.
    (2) DURATION.—The retention credit shall be available for
    each of the first 5 taxable years of continuous employment.
    (3) SMALL BUSINESS ENHANCEMENT.—For eligible small employers,
    the retention credit under paragraph (1) shall be $3,750.
"(d) ACCESSIBILITY CREDIT.—
    (1) IN GENERAL.—The accessibility credit is an amount equal
    to 50 percent of qualified accessibility expenditures paid
    or incurred during the taxable year, not to exceed $10,000.
    (2) QUALIFIED ACCESSIBILITY EXPENDITURES.—The term 'qualified
    accessibility expenditures' means amounts paid or incurred
    for—
        (A) physical workplace accessibility modifications;
        (B) assistive technology and adaptive equipment;
        (C) digital accessibility improvements to hiring
        platforms, workplace technology, and communications;
        (D) sign language interpretation and communication
        access services; and
        (E) consultation with qualified accessibility
        professionals.
    (3) SMALL BUSINESS ENHANCEMENT.—For eligible small employers,
    the credit percentage under paragraph (1) shall be 75
    percent.
"(e) ELIGIBLE SMALL EMPLOYER.—For purposes of this section, the
term 'eligible small employer' means an employer with fewer than
50 full-time equivalent employees.
"(f) QUALIFIED INDIVIDUAL WITH A DISABILITY.—For purposes of
this section, the term 'qualified individual with a disability'
has the meaning given such term in section 101 of the Americans
with Disabilities Act of 1990 (42 U.S.C. 12111).
"(g) COORDINATION.—This credit shall be in lieu of any credit
available under section 51 (Work Opportunity Tax Credit) with
respect to the same individual. The taxpayer may elect to claim
the credit under this section or section 51, but not both, with
respect to any individual.
"(h) TERMINATION.—This section shall not apply to taxable years
beginning after December 31, 2036."

SEC. 3. REPEAL OF DUPLICATIVE PROVISIONS.
(a) The existing disability-related provisions of the Work
Opportunity Tax Credit under section 51 of the Internal Revenue
Code of 1986 are not repealed but are coordinated under section
2(g) to prevent duplication.
(b) Section 44 of the Internal Revenue Code of 1986 (disabled
access credit) and section 190 of the Internal Revenue Code of
1986 (barrier removal deduction) remain in effect for expenses
not claimed under this section.

SEC. 4. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall apply to taxable years beginning after
December 31, 2026.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 2(b)**: The hiring credit ($5,000/$7,500 for small businesses) substantially exceeds the current WOTC disability credit ($1,200-$9,600)
- **Section 2(c)**: The retention credit addresses the problem of employers hiring for the credit and then terminating
- **Section 2(d)**: The accessibility credit incentivizes proactive workplace accessibility investment
- **Section 2(e)**: Enhanced credits for small businesses address the disproportionate burden of accommodation on smaller employers

**Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Revenue cost ($3-5 billion/year) | Offset by reduced SSDI/SSI payments and increased income tax revenue |
| Potential for abuse (hire-and-fire) | 6-month retention requirement for hiring credit; retention credit rewards long-term employment |
| Complexity of claiming credit | Unified application replaces multiple programs; IRS guidance |
| Measuring effectiveness | Built-in sunset (2036) with evaluation requirement |

### Model State AI Hiring Transparency Act

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "[State] Algorithmic Hiring
Transparency and Accountability Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.
(a) "Automated employment decision tool" means any
computational process derived from machine learning,
statistical modeling, data analytics, or artificial
intelligence that issues simplified outputs, including a
score, classification, or recommendation, that is used to
substantially assist or replace discretionary decision-making
for employment decisions.
(b) "Employment decision" means a decision related to
screening, hiring, promotion, termination, or compensation.
(c) "Bias audit" means an impartial evaluation by an
independent auditor to assess whether an automated employment
decision tool produces disparate impact against any protected
class, including individuals with disabilities.

SECTION 3. REQUIREMENTS.
(a) BIAS AUDIT.—Any employer with [25 or more] employees
that uses an automated employment decision tool shall ensure
the tool has undergone a bias audit within the preceding 12
months.
(b) NOTICE.—Before using an automated employment decision
tool to evaluate a candidate, the employer shall provide
notice that—
    (1) an automated tool will be used;
    (2) the candidate may request an alternative evaluation
    process as a reasonable accommodation; and
    (3) the candidate may request information about the type
    of data collected and the factors used by the tool.
(c) ACCOMMODATION.—Employers shall provide alternative,
non-automated application and evaluation processes upon
request as a reasonable accommodation under [state disability
rights law].
(d) RECORDS.—Employers shall retain bias audit results and
records of accommodation requests for not fewer than 3 years.

SECTION 4. ENFORCEMENT.
(a) The [State Attorney General/Labor Commissioner] may
investigate violations and bring enforcement actions.
(b) Civil penalties of not more than $[amount] per violation
may be assessed.
(c) An individual aggrieved by a violation may bring a civil
action for injunctive relief, compensatory damages, and
reasonable attorney fees.

SECTION 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect [18 months] after enactment.
```

## Regulatory Framework

### EEOC Rulemaking: AI Hiring Tools and the ADA

| Element | Proposed Approach |
|---------|-------------------|
| Scope | All automated employment decision tools used by ADA-covered employers |
| Standard | Disability-specific bias audit annually; accommodation in automated processes |
| Disparate impact analysis | Required testing against disability subgroups |
| Notice | Applicants must be informed of AI tool use and accommodation availability |
| Accommodation | Alternative non-automated processes; human review on request |
| Record retention | 3 years for audit results, accommodation requests, and decision data |
| Enforcement | Existing EEOC charge process plus specialized AI investigation unit |
| Technical assistance | EEOC guidance documents; NIST collaboration on audit standards |

### OFCCP Rulemaking: Section 503 Utilization Goal Increase

| Element | Proposed Approach |
|---------|-------------------|
| Current goal | 7% of each job group |
| Proposed goal | 10% (phased: 8% Year 1, 9% Year 2, 10% Year 3) |
| Self-identification | Enhanced outreach to encourage self-identification |
| Data reporting | Public disclosure of aggregate disability employment data |
| Compliance reviews | Minimum 300 disability-focused compliance reviews per year |

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Authority

- **Commerce Clause**: Congress has clear authority under the Commerce Clause to regulate employment practices that affect interstate commerce, including AI hiring tools and subminimum wage practices
- **Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment**: Provides authority to enforce equal protection through disability anti-discrimination provisions, as upheld in *Tennessee v. Lane*, 541 U.S. 509 (2004) for Title II
- **Taxing and Spending Clause**: Provides authority for tax credit provisions and federal grant programs
- **Note on Title I**: *Board of Trustees v. Garrett*, 531 U.S. 356 (2001), limits monetary damages against state employers under Title I, but does not affect regulation of private employers or federal grant conditions

### Preemption

- **AI hiring regulation**: Federal standards would establish a minimum floor; states remain free to enact stronger AI hiring protections (consistent with Illinois AI Video Interview Act, NYC Local Law 144)
- **Subminimum wage**: Federal elimination of Section 14(c) supersedes all state laws permitting subminimum wages
- **Tax credits**: Federal credits operate independently of state incentive programs; states may enact complementary credits

### Sovereign Immunity

- *Board of Trustees v. Garrett* limits Title I damages against state employers but does not bar injunctive relief
- Federal grant conditions (Section 503, transition grants) avoid sovereign immunity issues through conditional spending
- State model legislation operates under state authority, unaffected by federal sovereign immunity doctrine

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Availability | Effectiveness |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| EEOC charge processing and investigation | Available; capacity-enhanced under Bill 1 | Moderate to high with increased resources |
| EEOC litigation | Available; increased capacity under Bill 1 | High for systemic cases |
| Private litigation under ADA Title I | Available | Moderate; damages capped for large employers |
| OFCCP compliance reviews | Available for federal contractors | Moderate; debarment is powerful deterrent |
| DOL Section 14(c) enforcement | Available during phase-out | High with active monitoring |
| State enforcement | Variable | Depends on state capacity and law |
| Tax incentive compliance | IRS enforcement | Moderate; audit-based |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| AI tool "human review" formality | Employers nominally provide human review but rubber-stamp automated decisions | High |
| Bias audit shopping | Employers select auditors who produce favorable results | High |
| Disability non-disclosure undermines audit data | Workers who do not disclose disability are missing from disparate impact analysis | High |
| Subminimum wage transition service diversion | States use transition grant funds for administrative costs rather than direct services | Medium |
| Tax credit churn | Employers hire workers with disabilities, claim credit, then terminate after minimum retention period | Medium |
| Contractor self-identification gaming | Contractors inflate disability counts through aggressive self-identification campaigns without genuine inclusion | Medium |
| Accommodation request retaliation through AI | Employers use AI systems to flag and disadvantage workers who request accommodations | High |
| Small employer exemption exploitation | Employers structure operations to fall below 15-employee ADA threshold or 50-employee AI audit threshold | Medium |
| Phase-out wage floor avoidance | Sheltered workshops reduce hours rather than increase hourly wages to comply with wage floor | Medium |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|-----------|
| SSDI/SSI work incentive reform not included | Benefit cliffs continue to suppress employment; legislation addresses demand side but not supply side | Social Security Act amendments require separate legislative vehicle with high political difficulty |
| Tax credit revenue cost | $3-5 billion/year may face budget opposition | Political reluctance to increase deficit for disability employment |
| AI audit standards may lag technology | New AI modalities may emerge between standard updates | Regulatory process slower than technological change |
| Phase-out timeline may be too long for current workers | Workers earning $3.34/hour wait up to 6 years for minimum wage | Political compromise to gain provider support |
| No mandatory employer reporting of disability employment data | Limits ability to track progress and enforce | Business opposition to data collection mandates |
| EEOC charge processing goal (180 days) may sacrifice quality | Pressure to close cases quickly may reduce investigation thoroughness | Tension between speed and quality in enforcement |
| Private litigation under Title I has capped damages | Limits deterrent effect on large employers who can absorb capped damages | ADA damages cap (42 U.S.C. 1981a) not addressed in this legislation |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **AI human review formality**: Require documented human review with written justification for departing from or affirming automated recommendation; EEOC authority to investigate sham review processes
2. **Bias audit shopping**: Require auditors to be certified by EEOC-established criteria; rotation requirement (no auditor may audit the same employer for more than 3 consecutive years); public disclosure of audit summaries
3. **Disability non-disclosure in audit data**: Require audits to test with proxy variables (e.g., accommodation requests, employment gaps) in addition to self-identified disability status; voluntary anonymous disability surveys for statistical purposes
4. **Transition service diversion**: Cap administrative costs at 15% of grant funds; require 70% direct service expenditure; annual financial audits; outcome-based performance metrics
5. **Tax credit churn**: Extend minimum retention period to 12 months for full hiring credit; 6-month credit at 50% with clawback if terminated before 12 months; pattern monitoring by IRS
6. **Contractor self-identification gaming**: Require qualitative inclusion metrics alongside quantitative self-identification; OFCCP verification through employee surveys; workplace accommodation data as verification
7. **Accommodation retaliation through AI**: Prohibit AI systems from accessing accommodation request data; require firewalling of accommodation records from performance evaluation systems; specific retaliation provisions with enhanced penalties
8. **Small employer exemption exploitation**: Apply aggregation rules for commonly owned or controlled entities; anti-evasion provisions modeled on FMLA regulations
9. **Phase-out hour reduction**: Require certificate holders to maintain or increase total worker compensation during phase-out; monitor hours worked alongside hourly wage data

### General Implementation Concerns

- **Coordination complexity**: Multiple bills involve multiple agencies (EEOC, DOL, OFCCP, IRS, NIST); Interagency Disability Employment Coordination Council recommended
- **Political durability**: Tax credits and grant programs vulnerable to subsequent budget cuts; embed in mandatory rather than discretionary spending where possible
- **Technology evolution**: AI hiring regulation must be technology-neutral (regulating outcomes, not specific technologies) to remain relevant
- **Data infrastructure**: Effective enforcement requires robust data collection systems; authorize technology investment for EEOC and DOL
- **Provider transition resistance**: Some sheltered workshop providers will resist transition despite funding; enforcement of transition timeline must be firm
- **Benefit system interaction**: Workers transitioning from subminimum wage to minimum wage may face SSDI/SSI benefit reductions; benefits counseling is critical
