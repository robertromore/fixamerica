# Employment Discrimination: Root Causes

## Overview

The persistent 43-point disability employment gap cannot be explained by a single factor. It results from the interaction of employer attitudes, structural barriers in benefit systems, inadequate enforcement, inaccessible workplaces and hiring processes, and an economy that treats disability accommodation as a cost rather than an investment. These root causes reinforce each other in a system that makes exclusion the path of least resistance.

## Structural Causes

### 1. Benefit System Work Disincentives

The Social Security disability system creates powerful economic disincentives for people with disabilities to work, trapping many in poverty.

**SSDI Cliff Effect**

- SSDI beneficiaries can earn up to the Substantial Gainful Activity (SGA) level ($1,550/month in 2024) without losing benefits
- Earnings above SGA trigger a Trial Work Period, after which benefits can be suspended
- After the Extended Period of Eligibility, earning above SGA results in permanent benefit termination
- Loss of SSDI also triggers loss of Medicare after 93 months of benefits
- For many beneficiaries, the risk of losing guaranteed income and healthcare outweighs uncertain employment prospects

**SSI Benefit Reduction**

- SSI reduces benefits by $1 for every $2 earned above $65/month
- Effective marginal tax rate: 50% (income reduction) plus loss of Medicaid and other means-tested benefits
- Can create effective marginal tax rates exceeding 80% when all benefit reductions are combined
- Asset limits ($2,000 individual, $3,000 couple) prevent saving for emergencies or career investments

**Scale of Impact**

- 8.5 million SSDI beneficiaries (SSA, 2024)
- 3.8 million SSI recipients with disabilities (SSA, 2024)
- Fewer than 1% of SSDI beneficiaries successfully exit to work (SSA Annual Statistical Supplement, 2023)

*Source: Social Security Administration, Annual Statistical Supplement (2023); NCD, "Addressing the Needs of People with Disabilities" (2023).*

### 2. Employer Attitudes and Stereotypes

Employer discrimination against people with disabilities persists through both overt prejudice and unconscious bias.

**Evidence from Research**

| Study | Finding | Year |
|-------|---------|------|
| Kessler Foundation National Employment Survey | 46% of employers expressed concerns about cost of accommodations | 2022 |
| Ameri et al. (Rutgers) | Disclosed disability reduced employer interest by 26% | 2018 |
| Employer Perceptions Survey | 34% of employers cited "supervisory challenges" as barrier to hiring | 2023 |
| Cornell University Survey | 40% of employers said they did not know how to recruit workers with disabilities | 2022 |
| National Organization on Disability | Only 20% of employers had formal disability employment programs | 2024 |

**Common Employer Stereotypes**

- Workers with disabilities are less productive (contradicted by DOL research: equal or higher productivity)
- Accommodations are expensive (contradicted by JAN data: median cost $500, 49% cost $0)
- Workers with disabilities will use more sick leave (mixed evidence; depends on disability and accommodation)
- Hiring workers with disabilities increases insurance costs (generally false; ADA prohibits insurer discrimination)
- Workers with disabilities are a litigation risk (self-fulfilling: discrimination causes litigation)

### 3. Inaccessible Hiring Processes

The job application and interview process itself discriminates against people with disabilities at multiple stages.

**Barriers at Each Stage**

| Stage | Barrier | Impact |
|-------|---------|--------|
| Job posting | Unnecessarily restrictive physical requirements | Screens out qualified applicants |
| Online application | Inaccessible websites and application platforms | Excludes applicants who are blind, have motor disabilities |
| Resume screening | Algorithms penalize employment gaps, non-traditional backgrounds | Systematically disadvantages disabled applicants |
| Assessment/testing | Tests not accommodated; personality tests bias against neurodivergent applicants | Screens out qualified candidates |
| Interview | In-person requirements, communication barriers, bias | Discrimination at point of human interaction |
| Background check | Employment gaps flagged as negative | Penalizes disability-related work interruptions |
| Onboarding | Inaccessible orientation, training materials | Barriers begin on first day |

### 4. Subminimum Wage as Structural Trap

Section 14(c) of the FLSA creates a two-tier employment system that channels workers with disabilities into segregated, low-wage settings.

**How It Perpetuates Exclusion**

- Sheltered workshops have financial incentives to retain workers (contracts, government funding)
- Workers in sheltered settings receive little exposure to competitive employment options
- Skills developed in sheltered settings may not transfer to competitive employment
- Family members may fear loss of services if worker leaves sheltered setting
- State VR systems sometimes default to sheltered placement rather than investing in competitive employment supports

**Economic Incentive:**

- Provider organizations receive per-worker funding
- Low wages reduce provider costs
- Contract work for sheltered workshops subsidizes below-market labor
- Transition to competitive employment requires investment in support services

## Legal and Enforcement Causes

### 5. EEOC Enforcement Limitations

The EEOC processes the majority of ADA employment charges but produces relief for only a small fraction of charging parties.

**Why EEOC Enforcement Falls Short**

- Insufficient investigators: ~540 field investigators for all discrimination statutes (race, sex, age, disability, religion, national origin, genetics)
- Priority triage: Charges are categorized and many receive minimal investigation
- "No reasonable cause" determination in 60%+ of charges does not mean no discrimination occurred; it means insufficient evidence was developed
- Litigation resources: EEOC files only ~60 ADA lawsuits per year out of ~29,000 charges
- Systemic cases are resource-intensive and rare (~14 ADA systemic cases, FY 2023)
- Conciliation success rate is low (25-30% of cases where reasonable cause is found)

### 6. Proving Discrimination Is Difficult

Employment discrimination is inherently difficult to prove because employers control the evidence.

**Evidentiary Challenges**

| Challenge | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| At-will employment | Employers can terminate for any non-discriminatory reason; pretext is hard to prove |
| Subjective hiring criteria | "Culture fit," "attitude," "professionalism" mask disability bias |
| Comparator evidence | Hard to identify similarly situated non-disabled employee |
| Accommodation documentation | Interactive process often undocumented; disputes become he-said/she-said |
| Medical privacy | Employee disability information protected but can be used against them internally |
| Implicit bias | Unconscious discrimination produces identical outcomes to intentional discrimination but is harder to prove |

## Political Economy

### Who Benefits from the Status Quo

| Beneficiary | How They Benefit | Scale |
|-------------|-----------------|-------|
| Employers who discriminate | Lower accommodation costs, no enforcement consequences | Millions of employers |
| Sheltered workshop providers | Retain workers, government contracts, low labor costs | ~800 providers |
| Insurance industry | Avoid accommodation-related claims | Significant cost savings |
| Government (short-term) | Lower enforcement costs, fewer services demanded | Budget savings |
| Non-disabled workers | Reduced competition for some positions | Marginal benefit |

### Who Pays the Cost

| Group | Cost Borne | Scale |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| Workers with disabilities | Lost wages, economic insecurity, poverty | 61 million Americans |
| Families | Caregiving burden, lost household income | Tens of millions |
| Taxpayers | SSDI/SSI benefits for excluded workers | $200+ billion/year |
| Economy | Lost productivity, innovation, and consumer spending | $1.8 trillion in disabled disposable income (AIR, 2023) |
| Society | Social isolation, health disparities, inequality | Immeasurable |

## Perverse Incentives

### 1. The Disability Benefits Trap

Workers with disabilities face a cruel choice: guaranteed poverty through benefits or uncertain employment with risk of losing all support.

```text
Worker considers employment
        |
        v
Will earnings exceed SGA ($1,550/month)?
        |               |
        No              Yes
        |               |
        v               v
Keep benefits     Risk losing SSDI/SSI
but remain poor   Medicare/Medicaid
        |               |
        v               v
Employment      If job doesn't work out:
as supplement   Must reapply for benefits
(limited)       Wait 5+ months (SSDI)
                Face uncertainty
```

### 2. Employer Insurance Incentive

Employers may avoid hiring workers with disabilities to keep group health insurance premiums low, even though this is prohibited by the ADA.

- Insurers cannot legally charge higher premiums based on disability status
- But employers may perceive risk and act accordingly
- Self-insured employers bear accommodation costs directly
- Workers' compensation concerns may deter hiring for physical jobs

### 3. The Accommodation Paradox

Requesting accommodation requires disclosing disability, which triggers the very discrimination the accommodation is supposed to prevent.

- Workers who request accommodations are marked as "different"
- Supervisors may view accommodation requests as evidence of inability to do the job
- 38% of ADA charges include retaliation claims (EEOC, FY 2023)
- Fear of retaliation deters accommodation requests, leading to job failure or voluntary departure

## Why Reform Has Failed

### Attempted Reforms and Outcomes

| Reform | Year | Outcome | Why It Failed |
|--------|------|---------|---------------|
| ADA Title I | 1990 | Employment gap persists | Enforcement inadequate; attitudinal barriers persist |
| Ticket to Work | 1999 | <1% of SSDI beneficiaries exit benefits | Complex, burdensome, insufficient incentives |
| ADAAA | 2008 | Improved legal coverage | Did not address employment gap root causes |
| Section 503 reform | 2013 | 7% goal largely unmet | Weak enforcement; self-reporting limitations |
| WIOA | 2014 | Modest improvement in VR outcomes | Underfunded; implementation uneven |
| AbilityOne reform | Ongoing | Mixed | Program entrenched; reform resisted by provider interests |
| ABLE Act (2014) | 2014 | Useful but limited | Does not address fundamental benefit cliffs |

### Structural Barriers to Reform

1. **Benefit system complexity**: SSDI/SSI reform requires Social Security Act amendments, which are politically perilous
2. **Employer lobby power**: Business organizations resist mandate expansion
3. **Provider entrenchment**: Sheltered workshop providers resist competitive employment requirements
4. **Political fragmentation**: Disability employment reform spans multiple committees and agencies
5. **Cultural inertia**: Attitudes toward disability and work change slowly
6. **Data limitations**: Limited data on disability employment outcomes impedes evidence-based reform
7. **Intersectional neglect**: Disabled people of color, disabled women, and other intersectional groups face compounding barriers rarely addressed by single-axis reforms
