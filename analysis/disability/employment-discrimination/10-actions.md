# Employment Discrimination: Actions

## Individual Actions

### Educate Yourself

- Learn the basics of ADA Title I: what it requires of employers, what qualifies as a disability, and what constitutes reasonable accommodation
- Understand your rights under the interactive accommodation process and how to document requests
- Visit the EEOC's disability discrimination page at <https://www.eeoc.gov/disability-discrimination>
- Consult the Job Accommodation Network (JAN) for free, confidential guidance on workplace accommodations at <https://askjan.org/>
- Read the EEOC guidance on AI hiring tools and disability: <https://www.eeoc.gov/ai>
- Follow disability employment organizations on social media for current policy developments
- Learn about your state's disability discrimination laws, which may provide broader coverage than federal law

### Report Violations

- File an EEOC charge of discrimination if you experience employment discrimination: <https://www.eeoc.gov/filing-charge-discrimination>
- Note the 300-day filing deadline (180 days in states without a state/local agency) for EEOC charges
- Contact your state's Protection and Advocacy organization for free legal assistance: <https://www.ndrn.org/about/ndrn-member-agencies/>
- If you work for a federal contractor, file a complaint with OFCCP: <https://www.dol.gov/agencies/ofccp/complaint>
- If you are paid subminimum wage and believe your rights are being violated, contact the DOL Wage and Hour Division: <https://www.dol.gov/agencies/whd/contact/complaints>
- Document discrimination with dates, communications, witness names, and copies of relevant policies
- Keep copies of accommodation requests and employer responses
- Save screenshots of inaccessible online job applications

### Workplace Advocacy

- Request accommodations in writing and keep records of the interactive process
- Know that you do not need to use the words "reasonable accommodation" to trigger employer obligations; describing a need related to your disability is sufficient
- If your accommodation request is denied, ask for the specific reason and request alternatives
- Connect with employee resource groups (ERGs) or disability affinity groups at your workplace
- If you are comfortable, share your experiences to educate colleagues and normalize disability in the workplace
- If you face retaliation for requesting accommodation, document it and file an EEOC retaliation charge

## Community Actions

### Local Organizing

- Join or start a local disability employment advocacy chapter (ADAPT, AAPD local affiliates, local CILs)
- Organize employment discrimination awareness events during National Disability Employment Awareness Month (October)
- Partner with local independent living centers to offer employment rights workshops
- Build coalitions with labor unions, civil rights organizations, and workforce development agencies
- Engage with local workforce development boards to ensure disability employment is a priority
- Volunteer with supported employment providers to help individuals prepare for competitive employment

### Employer Engagement

- Encourage local employers to participate in the Disability Equality Index: <https://disabilityin.org/dei/>
- Organize employer education sessions on the low cost of accommodations (JAN data: median $500, 49% cost $0)
- Promote the Employer Assistance and Resource Network (EARN) resources: <https://askearn.org/>
- Recognize employers with strong disability employment practices through local awards or media coverage
- Encourage small businesses to use the Work Opportunity Tax Credit for hiring workers with disabilities
- Connect employers with the Job Accommodation Network for free accommodation consultation

### Technology Accountability

- Report inaccessible online job application systems to the EEOC and the platform operator
- Organize accessibility audits of local employers' hiring processes and application systems
- Advocate for bias audits of AI hiring tools used by major local employers
- Document instances where automated hiring systems fail to provide accommodation options
- Partner with technology organizations to improve accessibility awareness among hiring platform developers

## Political Actions

### Contact Your Representatives

**Call your members of Congress about disability employment:**

- Support the Transformation to Competitive Integrated Employment Act (eliminate subminimum wage)
- Support increased EEOC funding for disability discrimination enforcement
- Support the AI Civil Rights Act to regulate algorithmic hiring tools
- Support reform of SSDI/SSI work incentives to eliminate benefit cliffs
- Oppose any legislation that weakens ADA employment protections

65.8% for people without disabilities -- a 43-point gap.

This gap is not because people with disabilities cannot work. It
persists because of employer discrimination, benefit system work
disincentives, and inadequate enforcement. I ask you to:

1. Support the Transformation to Competitive Integrated Employment
   Act to end the subminimum wage for workers with disabilities
2. Support increased EEOC funding for ADA employment
   discrimination enforcement
3. Support legislation to regulate AI hiring tools that
   discriminate against applicants with disabilities
4. Support reform of SSDI/SSI work incentives to replace benefit
   cliffs with gradual phase-outs

Closing the employment gap would benefit the economy, reduce
benefit costs, and fulfill the ADA's promise of equal
opportunity. The 61 million Americans with disabilities deserve
the chance to work and contribute.

Thank you for your attention to this critical issue.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your Address]

## Talking Points

### For General Audiences

- The disability employment gap is over 43 percentage points: only 22.5% of people with disabilities are employed, compared to 65.8% of people without disabilities
- This gap has persisted for over 35 years despite the ADA; the law is not enough without strong enforcement
- Most workplace accommodations cost nothing or very little: the median cost is $500, and 49% of accommodations cost $0
- About 40,000 workers with disabilities are still legally paid below the federal minimum wage, some earning less than $1 per hour
- AI hiring tools are creating new forms of discrimination by screening out qualified applicants with disabilities
- The EEOC resolves only about 16% of disability discrimination charges with relief for the worker

### For Business Audiences

- People with disabilities represent $1.8 trillion in disposable income in the United States
- Accommodations are a sound business investment: 89% of employers report that benefits exceed costs (JAN, 2023)
- In tight labor markets, workers with disabilities represent an untapped talent pool of millions
- Companies with strong disability inclusion programs report higher retention rates, lower absenteeism, and improved innovation
- Tax credits are available: the Work Opportunity Tax Credit, Section 44 Small Business Credit, and Section 190 Deduction help offset any costs
- The Disability Equality Index provides a framework for benchmarking and improving disability inclusion practices

### For Policymaker Audiences

- The 43-point employment gap costs the economy an estimated $1.8 trillion in lost productivity and consumer spending
- Federal benefit systems spend over $200 billion annually on SSDI and SSI; moving more beneficiaries into employment reduces costs
- The EEOC's ADA enforcement is under-resourced: approximately 540 investigators handle all discrimination statutes, not just disability
- Only 9% of EEOC disability charges allege hiring discrimination, but audit studies show it is pervasive, suggesting massive underreporting
- Fifteen states plus D.C. have eliminated the subminimum wage without mass unemployment of workers with disabilities
- AI hiring regulation is a bipartisan opportunity: both parties have expressed concern about algorithmic bias

## Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Reality |
|--------------|---------|
| "People with disabilities can't work" | The majority of unemployed people with disabilities want to work and can work with appropriate supports |
| "Accommodations are expensive" | The median accommodation cost is $500 when there is a cost; 49% cost nothing at all |
| "The ADA has solved disability employment discrimination" | The employment gap has barely narrowed in 35 years; enforcement is inadequate |
| "Hiring people with disabilities increases insurance costs" | The ADA prohibits insurance discrimination based on disability; group rates are not affected |
| "Workers with disabilities are less productive" | DOL research shows equal or higher productivity; companies report better retention and innovation |
| "Subminimum wage protects workers with disabilities" | It entrenches poverty; states that have eliminated it have transitioned workers to better outcomes |
| "AI hiring tools are neutral and objective" | They can encode and amplify existing biases against people with disabilities |
| "SSDI recipients don't want to work" | Benefit cliffs create powerful disincentives; the system punishes attempts to work |
| "Small businesses are exempt from the ADA" | Employers with 15 or more employees are covered; the "undue hardship" standard considers resources |
| "Disability employment is a niche issue" | 61 million Americans have disabilities; the employment gap affects the entire economy |
