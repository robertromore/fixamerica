# Employment Discrimination: Overview

## Executive Summary

Despite the Americans with Disabilities Act's prohibition on employment discrimination, people with disabilities remain dramatically underrepresented in the American workforce. As of 2024, the employment rate for people with disabilities was 22.5%, compared to 65.8% for people without disabilities, a gap of more than 43 percentage points that has persisted with only marginal improvement since the ADA's passage in 1990. This employment gap is not explained by inability to work: surveys consistently show that the majority of unemployed people with disabilities want to work and are capable of working with reasonable accommodations.

ADA Title I prohibits discrimination in hiring, firing, promotion, compensation, and other terms and conditions of employment for employers with 15 or more employees. It requires employers to provide "reasonable accommodations" to qualified individuals with disabilities unless doing so would impose an "undue hardship." The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) enforces Title I, processing approximately 25,000-30,000 disability-related charges annually. Yet the EEOC's enforcement has not translated into meaningful workforce integration. Employers continue to discriminate through facially neutral practices, biased hiring algorithms, failure to engage in the interactive accommodation process, and outright refusal to hire qualified applicants with disabilities.

The roots of employment discrimination against people with disabilities extend beyond overt prejudice. Structural barriers include subminimum wage provisions under Section 14(c) of the Fair Labor Standards Act, sheltered workshops that segregate workers with disabilities from competitive employment, employer benefit systems that penalize accommodation costs, inaccessible application and interview processes, and a Social Security disability benefits system that creates work disincentives through income cliff effects. Addressing the employment gap requires both strengthening enforcement of existing law and reforming the broader systems that perpetuate economic exclusion.

## Core Tensions

### Individual Accommodation vs. Universal Design

The ADA's accommodation model places the burden on individual employees to request modifications, creating a system where workers must disclose their disabilities and navigate complex interactive processes. Critics argue this model should be supplemented by universal design requirements that make workplaces accessible by default, reducing the need for individual accommodation requests and the stigma associated with them.

### Work Incentives vs. Benefit Security

People with disabilities who receive SSDI or SSI face dramatic benefit cliffs if they earn above certain thresholds, creating powerful disincentives to work. The tension between providing adequate income support and incentivizing employment has defied decades of reform attempts. Workers with disabilities must choose between guaranteed benefits (health insurance, income) and uncertain employment prospects.

### Employer Flexibility vs. Employee Protection

Employers argue that ADA accommodation requirements are costly, unpredictable, and litigious. Disability advocates counter that most accommodations cost little or nothing and that employer resistance reflects prejudice more than genuine hardship. Finding the right balance between protecting employer prerogatives and ensuring equal opportunity is central to employment discrimination policy.

### Competitive Employment vs. Supported Employment

The disability community is divided on whether sheltered workshops and subminimum wage employment should be eliminated entirely (as the Transformation to Competitive Integrated Employment Act proposes) or preserved as options for individuals who may not succeed in competitive settings. This debate reflects deeper questions about autonomy, capacity, and the role of paternalism in disability policy.

## Vision of Success

A successful disability employment system would produce an employment rate for people with disabilities that approaches the rate for people without disabilities, accounting for those whose disabilities genuinely prevent all work. Employers would compete to recruit and retain workers with disabilities, recognizing their talent and the innovation that diverse workforces produce. Reasonable accommodations would be routine and unremarkable, with universal design reducing the need for individual modifications. The subminimum wage would be eliminated, with all workers receiving at least the minimum wage. Benefit systems would support transitions to employment rather than penalizing them. AI hiring tools would be audited and certified as non-discriminatory. The EEOC would have the resources to investigate charges promptly and the enforcement tools to produce systemic change.
