# Employment Discrimination: History

## The Era of Exclusion (Pre-1920s)

### Institutionalization and Segregation

For most of American history, people with disabilities were excluded from the workforce through a combination of institutionalization, social stigma, and legal barriers. The prevailing view treated disability as a personal tragedy or moral failing rather than a social condition.

**Key Features**

- "Ugly laws" in many cities prohibited people with visible disabilities from appearing in public (1860s-1970s)
- State institutions removed hundreds of thousands of people from communities and productive work
- People with disabilities who lived in communities were often confined to family settings without employment
- No legal prohibition on disability discrimination existed at any level of government
- Charity model dominated: employment of people with disabilities was viewed as charitable act, not economic opportunity

## Rehabilitation and Sheltered Work (1920s-1960s)

### The Rehabilitation Model

**Smith-Fess Act (1920)**

- Created federal-state vocational rehabilitation program
- First federal recognition that people with disabilities could be productive workers
- Focus on physical rehabilitation: restoring function rather than removing barriers
- Limited to veterans and civilians with physical disabilities

**Fair Labor Standards Act, Section 14(c) (1938)**

- Authorized subminimum wage certificates for workers with disabilities
- Intended to encourage employers to hire workers considered less productive
- Sheltered workshops proliferated as providers of segregated employment
- By mid-century, hundreds of thousands worked in sheltered settings at subminimum wages

**Post-World War II**

- Disabled veterans returned in large numbers, demanding employment and services
- Vocational rehabilitation expanded significantly
- President's Committee on Employment of the Handicapped established (1947)
- Focus remained on rehabilitation and "overcoming" disability rather than removing barriers

### The Sheltered Workshop System

| Era | Workers in Sheltered Settings | 14(c) Certificates | Context |
|-----|------------------------------|--------------------:|---------|
| 1940s | ~50,000 | ~500 | Post-FLSA establishment |
| 1960s | ~200,000 | ~3,000 | Expansion of disability services |

## Civil Rights Framework Emerges (1970s-1980s)

### Section 504 and Federal Employment (1973)

**Rehabilitation Act of 1973**

- Section 501: Required federal agencies to affirmatively employ people with disabilities
- Section 503: Required federal contractors to take affirmative action
- Section 504: Prohibited disability discrimination by federal fund recipients (including employment)

**Impact on Employment**

- First time disability discrimination in employment was legally prohibited (for federal and federally-funded employers)
- Established framework of reasonable accommodation
- Created precedent for ADA employment provisions
- Limited coverage: did not reach private employers not receiving federal funds

### Pre-ADA Employment Landscape

| Metric | 1980s Data | Context |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| Disabled employment rate | ~15% | No comprehensive data before BLS tracking |
| Disability discrimination complaints (Section 504) | ~5,000/year | Limited to federal fund recipients |
| Sheltered workshop population | ~350,000 | Growing |
| Supported employment programs | Emerging | Pilot programs in several states |
| State disability discrimination laws | ~45 states | Varying coverage and enforcement |

## The ADA Era (1990-2008)

### ADA Title I: Employment Provisions

**Enacted July 26, 1990; employment provisions effective July 26, 1992**

Key provisions:

- Prohibited discrimination against "qualified individuals with disabilities" by employers with 15+ employees
- Required "reasonable accommodations" unless "undue hardship"
- Prohibited medical examinations and disability-related inquiries before a conditional job offer
- Prohibited qualification standards that screen out people with disabilities unless job-related and consistent with business necessity
- EEOC designated as enforcement agency

**Early Implementation**

| Year | EEOC ADA Charges | Notable Developments |
|------|-----------------|---------------------|
| 1992 | 1,048 (partial year) | Title I takes effect |
| 1993 | 15,274 | First full year |
| 1994 | 18,859 | Growing awareness |
| 1995 | 19,798 | Employer resistance builds |
| 1997 | 18,108 | EEOC guidance documents issued |
| 2000 | 15,864 | Judicial narrowing begins |

*Source: EEOC Charge Statistics (1992-2000).*

### Supreme Court Narrows ADA Employment Protections (1999-2002)

The Supreme Court issued a series of decisions that dramatically narrowed who qualified as "disabled" under the ADA, with devastating effects on employment claims:

| Case | Year | Holding | Employment Impact |
|------|------|---------|-------------------|
| *Sutton v. United Air Lines* | 1999 | Disability assessed with mitigating measures | Twin sisters with corrected vision not disabled; millions excluded |
| *Murphy v. UPS* | 1999 | Applied Sutton to hypertension | Mechanic with controlled hypertension not disabled |
| *Albertsons v. Kirkingburg* | 1999 | Applied to monocular vision | Body's own compensation counted as mitigation |
| *Toyota Motor v. Williams* | 2002 | "Substantially limits" interpreted strictly | Carpal tunnel plaintiff not disabled enough |
| *Board of Trustees v. Garrett* | 2001 | States immune from Title I damages | Eliminated monetary relief against state employers |
| *Chevron v. Echazabal* | 2002 | "Direct threat to self" defense permitted | Employer can refuse to hire if job threatens employee's own health |

**Consequences**

- Plaintiff loss rate at summary judgment exceeded 95% (Ruth Colker, *Harvard Civil Rights-Civil Liberties Law Review*, 1999)
- Courts focused on whether plaintiffs were "disabled enough" rather than whether they were discriminated against
- Employers successfully argued that employees who managed their conditions were not disabled
- The ADA's employment protections were effectively gutted for many workers

## The ADA Amendments Act and Recovery (2008-2020)

### ADAAA Restores Broad Coverage (2008)

Congress responded to judicial narrowing with overwhelming bipartisan legislation:

**Key Employment-Relevant Changes**

| Before ADAAA | After ADAAA |
|-------------|-------------|
| Mitigating measures considered | Mitigating measures not considered |
| "Substantially limits" narrowly interpreted | Broadly interpreted; lower threshold |
| Focus on proving disability status | Focus on whether discrimination occurred |
| Episodic conditions often excluded | Covered if limiting when active |
| "Regarded as" required proving limitation | Only requires showing of impairment-based action |

### Obama Administration Employment Enforcement (2009-2017)

**EEOC Enforcement**

- Increased systemic discrimination investigations
- Updated ADA regulations to reflect ADAAA (effective May 2011)
- Issued guidance on reasonable accommodation and pregnancy-related disabilities
- Wellness program regulations issued (2016)
- Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act (GINA) coordination

**Section 503 Reform (2013)**

- OFCCP updated Section 503 regulations for federal contractors
- Established 7% utilization goal for workers with disabilities
- Required employee self-identification invitations
- Enhanced data collection and reporting requirements
- Effective March 24, 2014

**Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act (WIOA, 2014)**

- Replaced Workforce Investment Act
- Emphasis on competitive integrated employment
- Required states to spend minimum 15% of VR funds on pre-employment transition services
- Limited use of subminimum wage: required documentation that competitive employment was considered first
- Created path toward employment-first policies

### Trump Administration (2017-2021)

**EEOC Under Trump**

- Reduced EEOC enforcement activity
- Attempted to limit collection of pay data (EEO-1 Component 2)
- Fewer systemic ADA cases filed
- Wellness program regulations vacated by courts
- EEOC leadership vacancies

**Section 14(c) and Subminimum Wage**

- No action on subminimum wage reform at federal level
- Executive Order 13950 (2020) restricted diversity training, potentially affecting disability inclusion programs (later revoked)
- State-level bans on subminimum wage continued regardless of federal inaction

## Current Era (2021-Present)

### Biden Administration

**EEOC Enforcement**

- Charlotte Burrows continued as EEOC Chair
- Strategic Enforcement Plan (FY 2024-2028) prioritizes disability discrimination
- AI and algorithmic fairness initiative: EEOC guidance on AI hiring tools (2023)
- Increased emphasis on systemic ADA cases
- Long COVID guidance: recognized as potential ADA disability

**Executive Actions**

- Executive Order 14035 (2021): Diversity, Equity, Inclusion, and Accessibility in the Federal Workforce
- Executive Order 14091 (2023): Further Advancing Racial Equity and Support for Underserved Communities
- Federal hiring of people with disabilities increased to 10%+ of new hires
- Schedule A hiring authority promoted and expanded

**Legislative Activity (119th Congress, 2025-2026)**

| Bill | Purpose | Status |
|------|---------|--------|
| Transformation to Competitive Integrated Employment Act | Phase out Section 14(c) | Committee stage |
| AI Civil Rights Act | Regulate AI in employment decisions | Introduced |
| RAISE Act | Increase competitive integrated employment | Committee stage |
| Disability Employment Incentive Act | Tax incentives for hiring workers with disabilities | Committee stage |

## Key Turning Points

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|--------------|
| 1938 | FLSA Section 14(c) | Created subminimum wage for workers with disabilities |
| 1973 | Rehabilitation Act | First employment discrimination prohibition (federal sector) |
| 1990 | ADA enacted | Comprehensive private-sector employment discrimination prohibition |
| 1992 | ADA Title I effective | EEOC begins processing employment charges |
| 1999-2002 | Supreme Court narrowing | Gutted ADA employment protections for many workers |
| 2008 | ADAAA | Congress restored broad ADA coverage |
| 2013 | Section 503 reform | 7% utilization goal for federal contractors |
| 2014 | WIOA enacted | Employment-first policy framework |
| 2020 | COVID-19 pandemic | Demonstrated viability of remote work as accommodation |
| 2023 | EEOC AI guidance | First federal guidance on algorithmic disability discrimination |

## Lessons from History

1. **Legal protection alone is insufficient**: The ADA's employment provisions have been in effect for over 30 years without closing the employment gap
2. **Courts can undermine legislative intent**: The Supreme Court's 1999-2002 decisions nearly destroyed ADA employment protections; constant legislative vigilance is required
3. **The subminimum wage is a legacy of paternalism**: Section 14(c) treats workers with disabilities as inherently less productive, perpetuating segregation
4. **Technology is a double-edged sword**: Remote work enables participation; AI hiring tools may create new barriers
5. **Federal contractor requirements drive private-sector change**: Section 503's 7% goal, while imperfect, has increased attention to disability hiring
6. **Economic conditions matter**: The employment gap narrows in tight labor markets, suggesting demand-side barriers are significant
7. **Bipartisan action is possible**: The ADAAA passed overwhelmingly, demonstrating that disability employment remains a bipartisan issue when properly framed
