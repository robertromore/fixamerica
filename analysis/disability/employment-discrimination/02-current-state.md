# Employment Discrimination: Current State

## The Employment Gap

### Scale of the Problem

The disability employment gap is one of the most persistent and dramatic disparities in the American labor market. Despite 35 years of ADA enforcement, the gap has narrowed only modestly.

**Employment Status (2024)**

| Metric | People with Disabilities | People Without Disabilities | Gap |
|--------|------------------------|-----------------------------|-----|
| Employment-to-population ratio | 22.5% | 65.8% | 43.3 points |
| Unemployment rate | 7.2% | 3.5% | 3.7 points |
| Labor force participation rate | 24.3% | 68.1% | 43.8 points |
| Full-time employment (of those employed) | 60% | 78% | 18 points |
| Part-time employment (of those employed) | 40% | 22% | 18 points |

*Source: BLS, "Persons with a Disability: Labor Force Characteristics, 2024." Table 1.*

|------|---------------------------|-------------------------------|-----|

*Source: BLS Current Population Survey, Annual Averages (2010-2024).*

## Subminimum Wage and Sheltered Employment

### Section 14(c) of the Fair Labor Standards Act

Section 14(c) allows employers holding special certificates from the Department of Labor to pay workers with disabilities below the federal minimum wage. This practice, enacted in 1938, was intended to encourage employment but has become a mechanism for segregated, low-wage employment.

**Current Status (2023)**

| Metric | Data | Trend |
|--------|------|-------|
| Workers paid subminimum wage | ~40,000 | Down from 420,000 in 2001 |
| Active 14(c) certificates | ~800 | Down from 5,600 in 2001 |
| Average subminimum wage paid | $3.34/hour | Varies widely |
| Lowest wages paid | Under $1.00/hour | Documented by NCD |
| States that have banned subminimum wage | 15 + DC (as of 2024) | Growing |
| Workers in sheltered workshops | ~100,000 | Declining |

*Sources: DOL Wage and Hour Division (2023); National Council on Disability, "Phase-Out of Subminimum Wage" (2023).*

## Reasonable Accommodation

### The Interactive Process

Under ADA Title I, employers must engage in an "interactive process" with employees who request reasonable accommodations. This process frequently fails.

**Common Accommodation Requests and Outcomes**

| Accommodation Type | Frequency | Average Cost | Typical Outcome |
|-------------------|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Schedule modification | Very common | $0 | Usually granted but often contested |
| Remote/telework | Very common (post-COVID) | Varies | Increasingly accepted; still contested |
| Modified duties | Common | $0 | Often leads to conflict |
| Equipment/technology | Common | $500 median | Usually provided |
| Physical workspace modification | Moderate | $200-$2,000 | Usually provided |
| Leave of absence | Common | Varies | Frequently contested |
| Reassignment | Less common | $0 | Often resisted by employers |
| Sign language interpreter | Less common | $150-$300/hour | Usually provided |
| Job coaching | Less common | Varies | More common for intellectual/developmental disabilities |

*Source: Job Accommodation Network (JAN), "Accommodation and Compliance: Low Cost, High Impact" (2023).*

### Accommodation Cost Data

| Metric | Finding | Source |
|--------|---------|--------|
| Accommodations costing $0 | 49.4% | JAN (2023) |
| Accommodations costing under $500 | 43.3% | JAN (2023) |
| Accommodations costing $500-$5,000 | 5.5% | JAN (2023) |
| Accommodations costing over $5,000 | 1.8% | JAN (2023) |
| Median one-time cost (when cost exists) | $500 | JAN (2023) |
| Average ongoing annual cost | $200 | JAN (2023) |
| Employer-reported benefits exceeding costs | 89% of employers | JAN (2023) |

### COVID-19 and Remote Work

The pandemic demonstrated that remote work is a viable accommodation for many jobs, fundamentally changing the reasonable accommodation landscape:

| Pre-COVID Status | Post-COVID Status |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Remote work rarely granted as accommodation | Remote work more commonly available |
| "Essential function" of many jobs required on-site presence | Many jobs can be performed remotely |
| Employers claimed undue hardship for telework | Employers now struggle to justify refusing telework as accommodation |
| EEOC guidance limited on telework | Updated EEOC guidance recognizes telework as potential accommodation |

*Source: EEOC, "The ADA and COVID-19: Resource Guide" (updated 2023).*

## Hiring Discrimination

### Evidence of Hiring Bias

**Audit Studies**

| Study | Year | Finding |
|-------|------|---------|
| Ameri et al. (Rutgers) | 2018 | Applicants who disclosed disabilities received 26% fewer expressions of employer interest |
| Kang et al. | 2016 | Applicants with disabilities were 34% less likely to receive callbacks |
| National Bureau of Economic Research | 2019 | Disclosed disability reduced callbacks by 30-40% depending on disability type |
| Disability Rights California audit | 2022 | Mystery shoppers with visible disabilities received inferior service in 42% of retail encounters |

*Sources: Ameri et al., "The Disability Employment Puzzle," *ILR Review* (2018); NBER Working Paper Series.*

### AI and Algorithmic Discrimination

| Issue | Status | Risk Level |
|-------|--------|-----------|
| Resume screening algorithms | Widely used; limited disability accommodation | High |
| Video interview analysis (HireVue-type) | Growing use; facial analysis may penalize some disabilities | High |
| Personality assessments | Widely used; may screen out neurodiverse applicants | High |
| Skills-based testing | Common; may lack reasonable accommodation provisions | Medium |
| Background check automation | Common; disability-related gaps penalized | Medium |
| Chatbot-based applications | Growing; accessibility varies widely | Medium |

*Source: Center for Democracy and Technology, "Algorithm-Driven Hiring Tools" (2023); EEOC, "Artificial Intelligence and Algorithmic Fairness Initiative" (2023).*

## Recent Developments

### EEOC Strategic Enforcement Plan (FY 2024-2028)

The EEOC's current strategic enforcement plan identifies disability discrimination as a priority:

| Priority | Focus Area |
|----------|-----------|
| Hiring discrimination | Systemic barriers including AI hiring tools |
| Reasonable accommodation | Interactive process failures, particularly post-COVID |
| Retaliation | Increasing focus on retaliation against accommodation requesters |
| Subminimum wage | Supporting transition to competitive employment |
| Emerging issues | Long COVID as disability, gig economy, remote work |

*Source: EEOC, "Strategic Enforcement Plan FY 2024-2028" (2023).*

### Long COVID as Disability

The DOJ and EEOC have issued guidance recognizing Long COVID as a potential disability under the ADA:

- Affects an estimated 17 million adults in the U.S. (CDC, 2023)
- Common symptoms (fatigue, cognitive impairment, pain) may substantially limit major life activities
- Employers must consider reasonable accommodations for employees with Long COVID
- EEOC has received increasing numbers of Long COVID-related charges

*Source: EEOC, "What You Should Know About COVID-19 and the ADA" (updated 2023).*

### Transformation to Competitive Integrated Employment Act

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Purpose | Phase out Section 14(c) subminimum wage over 6 years |
| Status (119th Congress) | Introduced; committee stage |
| Key provisions | End new 14(c) certificates immediately; phase out existing certificates; fund transition services |
| CBO estimate | $250 million over 10 years for transition support |
| Support | Bipartisan; disability community broadly supportive |
| Opposition | Some provider organizations; some parent groups |

*Source: Congress.gov; National Council on Disability (2023).*

### Federal Contractor Requirements

**Section 503 of the Rehabilitation Act** requires federal contractors to take affirmative action to employ people with disabilities:

| Requirement | Status |
|-------------|--------|
| Utilization goal | 7% of workforce for each job group |
| Self-identification | Employers must invite applicants and employees to self-identify |
| Recordkeeping | Detailed employment data by disability status |
| OFCCP enforcement | Active but resources limited |
| Actual contractor disability employment rate | Estimated 5-6% (below 7% goal) |

*Source: OFCCP, Section 503 Implementation Data (2023).*

## Key Takeaways

1. **The employment gap is persistent**: 43+ point gap between disabled and non-disabled employment rates
2. **EEOC enforcement is improving but insufficient**: Merit resolution rate is 16.4%, meaning most charges do not result in relief
3. **Hiring discrimination is the hardest to prove and most impactful**: Only 9% of charges allege hiring discrimination, but audit studies show it is pervasive
4. **Subminimum wage is declining but still legal**: 40,000 workers remain affected; 15 states have banned it
5. **Accommodations are cheap**: Nearly half cost nothing; median cost $500 when there is a cost
6. **COVID changed the landscape**: Remote work is now a more viable accommodation; Long COVID is emerging as a significant ADA issue
7. **AI poses new risks**: Algorithmic hiring tools may introduce new forms of discrimination
8. **Geographic disparities are enormous**: Employment rates range from over 30% in some states to under 16% in others
