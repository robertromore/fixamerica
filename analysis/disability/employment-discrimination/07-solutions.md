# Employment Discrimination: Solutions

## Overview

Closing the disability employment gap requires simultaneous action on multiple fronts: strengthening enforcement of existing anti-discrimination law, eliminating structural barriers like subminimum wage and benefit cliffs, addressing emerging threats from AI hiring tools, and creating positive incentives for employer inclusion. No single solution is sufficient; the gap persists because multiple barriers reinforce each other.

---

## Solution 1: Strengthen EEOC ADA Enforcement Capacity

### Description

Significantly increase EEOC staffing, funding, and authority to investigate and litigate ADA employment discrimination charges, with emphasis on systemic investigations, hiring discrimination, and reasonable accommodation failures.

### Evidence Base

- EEOC merit resolution rate of 16.4% (FY 2023) means most charges do not result in relief
- Only ~60 ADA lawsuits filed per year out of ~29,000 charges
- EEOC pending charge inventory of ~37,000 demonstrates capacity constraints
- GAO has recommended EEOC resource increases (multiple reports)

### Implementation

- Increase EEOC budget by $150 million annually (from $479 million to ~$630 million)
- Hire 200 additional investigators dedicated to disability discrimination
- Create specialized ADA systemic investigation unit
- Reduce average charge processing time to under 6 months
- Increase ADA litigation to 150+ cases per year
- Develop AI hiring tool investigation capability

### Cost

- **Annual cost**: $150 million additional appropriation
- **Return**: Increased monetary recovery and deterrence effect

### Timeline

- Year 1: Appropriation and hiring begins
- Year 2: Investigators deployed; charge backlog declining
- Year 3: Systemic investigation capacity operational
- Year 5: Average processing time under 6 months

### Challenges

- Congressional appropriation required
- Competition with other EEOC enforcement priorities
- Investigator recruitment and training pipeline
- Balancing charge processing speed with investigation quality

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline (2024) | Target (Year 5) |
|--------|-----------------|------------------|
| ADA charges resolved per year | ~29,000 | 35,000+ |
| Merit resolution rate | 16.4% | 25%+ |
| ADA lawsuits filed per year | ~60 | 150+ |
| Average charge processing time | 9 months | Under 6 months |
| Systemic ADA cases per year | ~14 | 40+ |

## Solution 2: Eliminate Subminimum Wage (Section 14(c) Phase-Out)

### Description

Phase out Section 14(c) of the Fair Labor Standards Act over 6 years, ending the authorization of subminimum wages for workers with disabilities. Provide transition funding for competitive integrated employment supports.

### Evidence Base

- 15 states plus D.C. have eliminated subminimum wage without mass unemployment of disabled workers (APSE, 2024)
- NCD has recommended elimination since 2012
- Workers paid subminimum wage earn an average of $3.34/hour, entrenching poverty
- International precedent: EU member states moving toward competitive employment models

### Implementation

- Immediate moratorium on new Section 14(c) certificates
- Phase out existing certificates over 6 years:
    - Year 1-2: Wage floor rises to 50% of minimum wage
    - Year 3-4: Wage floor rises to 75% of minimum wage
    - Year 5-6: Full minimum wage required; Section 14(c) repealed
- Authorize $300 million annually for competitive employment transition services
- Provide technical assistance to providers transitioning to new models
- Create employer incentives (tax credits) for hiring former sheltered workers

### Cost

- **Federal cost**: $300 million/year for transition services (10-year authorization)
- **Offset**: Reduced SSDI/SSI payments as workers earn competitive wages
- **CBO estimate**: $250 million over 10 years net cost (Transformation to Competitive Integrated Employment Act)

### Timeline

- Year 1: Moratorium on new certificates; transition planning
- Year 2-6: Phased wage floor increases
- Year 6: Full minimum wage; Section 14(c) repealed
- Year 7-10: Continued transition support

### Challenges

- Opposition from some provider organizations and family groups
- Need for adequate transition funding (without it, phase-out could result in service loss)
- Regional variation in competitive employment capacity
- Some workers may need ongoing support that current systems cannot provide

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline (2024) | Target (Year 7) |
|--------|-----------------|------------------|
| Workers paid subminimum wage | ~40,000 | 0 |
| Active 14(c) certificates | ~800 | 0 |
| Workers in competitive integrated employment (former sheltered) | Baseline | 75%+ of transitioned workers |
| Average earnings of transitioned workers | $3.34/hour | Minimum wage or higher |

## Solution 3: Regulate AI Hiring Tools for Disability Discrimination

### Description

Require employers and AI tool developers to audit hiring algorithms for disability discrimination, provide reasonable accommodations in automated hiring processes, and disclose the use of AI in hiring decisions.

### Evidence Base

- AI resume screening algorithms penalize employment gaps common among people with disabilities (CDT, 2023)
- Video interview analysis tools may discriminate against applicants with facial differences, speech patterns, or motor disabilities
- Personality assessments used in hiring systematically disadvantage neurodiverse applicants
- EEOC has issued guidance indicating ADA applies to AI hiring but lacks enforcement tools (2023)
- Illinois AI Video Interview Act (2020) and NYC Local Law 144 (2023) provide state/local models

### Implementation

- Federal legislation requiring:
    - Bias audits of AI hiring tools for disability discrimination (annual)
    - Disclosure to applicants when AI is used in hiring decisions
    - Reasonable accommodation in automated hiring processes (alternative application methods)
    - Record retention of AI tool decision data for EEOC investigation
- EEOC rulemaking authority on AI hiring standards
- Technical assistance for employers on accessible AI implementation
- Whistleblower protections for employees who report discriminatory AI systems

### Cost

- **Federal cost**: $15 million/year for EEOC AI enforcement unit
- **Employer cost**: $5,000-50,000 per employer for audits (depending on complexity)
- **Technology industry cost**: Development of accessible AI tools

### Timeline

- Year 1: Legislation enacted
- Year 2: EEOC issues implementing regulations
- Year 3: Compliance deadline for large employers (500+)
- Year 4: Compliance deadline for all covered employers

### Challenges

- Technology industry opposition
- Rapidly evolving AI landscape may outpace regulation
- Defining "bias audit" standards requires technical expertise
- Balancing innovation with fairness
- Enforcement requires EEOC technical capacity

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline (2024) | Target (Year 5) |
|--------|-----------------|------------------|
| Employers using audited AI hiring tools | ~5% | 80%+ |
| AI tool disability bias incidents detected | Unknown | Measurable baseline |
| Accommodation availability in automated hiring | Rare | Universal |
| EEOC AI-related charges resolved | Minimal | 500+ per year |

## Solution 4: Reform Benefit System Work Incentives

### Description

Reform SSDI and SSI programs to eliminate cliff effects and create gradual benefit phase-outs that make work always financially beneficial for people with disabilities. Decouple health insurance from disability benefit status.

### Evidence Base

- Fewer than 1% of SSDI beneficiaries successfully exit to work (SSA, 2023)
- Effective marginal tax rates for SSI recipients can exceed 80% when all benefit reductions are combined
- SSDI's SGA cliff ($1,550/month) creates an all-or-nothing choice
- Ticket to Work program has produced minimal results since 1999
- Countries with gradual benefit phase-outs (Sweden, UK Universal Credit) show higher work participation

### Implementation

- Replace SSDI SGA cliff with gradual benefit reduction:
    - For every $2 earned above SGA, reduce SSDI by $1 (50% reduction rate)
    - No complete benefit termination due to earnings
    - Benefits reach $0 when earnings are high enough
- Decouple Medicare/Medicaid from SSDI/SSI status:
    - Allow Medicaid buy-in for all working people with disabilities regardless of income
    - Extend Medicare eligibility for SSDI beneficiaries who work
- Raise SSI asset limits from $2,000 to $10,000 (individual) and $3,000 to $20,000 (couple)
- Expand ABLE account eligibility and contribution limits

### Cost

- **Transition cost**: $10-20 billion over 10 years (SSA estimates for similar proposals)
- **Offset**: Increased tax revenue from working beneficiaries; reduced benefit payments over time
- **Long-term**: Potentially revenue-neutral or positive as more beneficiaries work

### Timeline

- Year 1-2: Legislation (Social Security Act amendments)
- Year 3: SSA implements new benefit calculations
- Year 4-5: Phase-in of new rules for current beneficiaries
- Year 5-10: Full implementation and evaluation

### Challenges

- Social Security Act amendments are politically difficult
- SSA institutional resistance to change
- Benefit reform may be perceived as benefit cuts
- Complex implementation requiring SSA systems modernization
- Must ensure no current beneficiary is worse off during transition

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline (2024) | Target (Year 10) |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| SSDI beneficiaries with earnings | ~5% | 20%+ |
| Effective marginal tax rate for working beneficiaries | 50-80%+ | Under 50% |
| SSDI beneficiaries exiting to work | <1% | 5%+ |
| SSI recipients with earnings | ~15% | 30%+ |

## Solution 5: Expand Federal Contractor Disability Employment Requirements

### Description

Strengthen Section 503 of the Rehabilitation Act to increase the disability employment utilization goal from 7% to 10%, improve data collection, and establish meaningful penalties for noncompliance.

### Evidence Base

- Current 7% goal has been in effect since 2014; most contractors remain below it (5-6% average)
- OFCCP enforcement is under-resourced
- Federal contractors employ approximately 20% of the U.S. workforce (DOL estimate)
- Contractor requirements have historically driven private-sector change (racial and gender employment)

### Implementation

- Increase utilization goal from 7% to 10% (phased over 3 years)
- Require contractors to report disability employment data publicly
- Increase OFCCP enforcement staff by 50%
- Establish meaningful penalties: contract debarment for persistent noncompliance
- Require accessibility audits of contractor application processes
- Create contractor best practices sharing program

### Cost

- **Federal cost**: $30 million/year for OFCCP expansion
- **Contractor cost**: Variable; depends on current disability employment levels
- **Economic benefit**: Expanded talent pool for contractors

### Timeline

- Year 1: Regulatory amendment (no legislation needed)
- Year 2: 8% utilization goal
- Year 3: 9% utilization goal
- Year 4: 10% utilization goal

### Challenges

- Contractor resistance to higher goals
- Self-identification limitations (many employees do not disclose disability)
- OFCCP enforcement capacity
- Defining and measuring "disability" consistently across contractors
- Anti-affirmative action political environment

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline (2024) | Target (Year 5) |
|--------|-----------------|------------------|
| Average contractor disability employment rate | 5-6% | 10%+ |
| Contractors meeting utilization goal | ~30% | 70%+ |
| OFCCP compliance reviews (disability-focused) | ~100/year | 300+/year |
| Federal contractor workforce with disabilities | ~1 million | 1.5 million+ |

## Solution 6: Create National Disability Employment Tax Incentive

### Description

Consolidate and expand existing disability employment tax incentives into a single, robust tax credit that rewards employers for hiring and retaining workers with disabilities and investing in workplace accessibility.

### Evidence Base

- Current tax incentives (Work Opportunity Tax Credit, Section 44 Small Business Credit, Section 190 Deduction) are fragmented and underutilized
- WOTC for disability is only $1,200-$9,600 per employee, often insufficient incentive
- Countries with stronger employer incentives (Germany's quota-levy system, UK's Access to Work) show higher disability employment
- Employer surveys indicate tax incentives would increase willingness to hire (NOD, 2024)

### Implementation

- Create unified "Disability Employment Tax Credit":
    - $5,000 per new hire with a disability (retained for 6+ months)
    - $2,500 annual retention credit for each employee with a disability (years 2-5)
    - Up to $10,000 credit for accessibility investments (physical or digital)
    - Higher credits for small businesses (under 50 employees): 150% of base credit
- Simplify employer application process (one form, one application)
- Fund technical assistance for employer participation

### Cost

- **Revenue cost**: Estimated $3-5 billion/year (depending on uptake)
- **Offset**: Reduced SSDI/SSI payments, increased income tax revenue from workers
- **Net cost**: Potentially positive over 10 years

### Timeline

- Year 1: Legislation
- Year 2: IRS implements credit; employer outreach
- Year 3: Full utilization begins
- Year 5: Evaluation and adjustment

### Challenges

- Revenue cost in constrained budget environment
- Must prevent abuse (hiring and quickly terminating to claim credit)
- Employer awareness and participation
- Measuring effectiveness

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline (2024) | Target (Year 5) |
|--------|-----------------|------------------|
| Employers claiming disability hiring credit | ~50,000 (WOTC) | 200,000+ |
| Workers with disabilities hired through incentive | ~100,000 (WOTC) | 500,000+ |
| Small business participation | Low | 50%+ of eligible |
| Disability employment rate | 22.5% | 28%+ |

## Solution 7: Establish National Supported Employment Infrastructure

### Description

Create a federally funded national supported employment system that provides ongoing workplace supports (job coaching, assistive technology, workplace modification) for workers with significant disabilities in competitive integrated settings.

### Evidence Base

- Supported employment produces better outcomes than sheltered workshops (Wehman et al., 2018)
- Current supported employment funding is fragmented across Medicaid, VR, and state programs
- Waitlists for supported employment services exist in most states
- Cost per supported employment placement ($4,000-$8,000) is less than sheltered workshop costs ($15,000-$25,000 per person/year)

### Implementation

- Authorize $500 million annually for competitive integrated employment services
- Create standardized supported employment service model
- Fund customized employment, job coaching, and workplace supports
- Eliminate waitlists for supported employment services
- Coordinate with Medicaid HCBS waivers for ongoing support funding
- Require employment-first policy for all federally funded disability services

### Cost

- **Annual cost**: $500 million
- **Offset**: Reduced sheltered workshop funding, reduced SSDI/SSI costs
- **Per-person cost**: $4,000-$8,000 (placement) + $3,000-$6,000/year (ongoing support)

### Timeline

- Year 1: Authorization and state plan development
- Year 2: First grants awarded
- Year 3: Services operational in all 50 states
- Year 5: Waitlists eliminated; full national coverage

### Challenges

- Significant federal investment required
- State capacity varies widely
- Provider workforce development needed
- Must coordinate with existing Medicaid and VR systems
- Ongoing support funding is the critical unmet need

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline (2024) | Target (Year 7) |
|--------|-----------------|------------------|
| Workers in competitive integrated employment | ~500,000 | 750,000+ |
| Supported employment waitlists | Extensive (most states) | Eliminated |
| Average supported employment tenure | 12-18 months | 24+ months |
| Workers transitioning from sheltered to competitive | Slow | 10,000+/year |

## International Models

### Germany: Quota-Levy System

| Feature | German Approach | U.S. Comparison |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|
| Employer quota | 5% of workforce must be disabled (50+ employees) | No quota; 7% aspiration for contractors |
| Penalty for noncompliance | Monthly levy per unfilled quota position | No financial penalty |
| Integration offices | Workplace support services | Limited comparable infrastructure |
| Sheltered workshops | Being reformed; dual focus on transition | 14(c) being phased out at state level |
| Employment rate | ~50% (much higher than U.S.) | 22.5% |

### United Kingdom: Access to Work Program

| Feature | UK Approach | U.S. Comparison |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| Government-funded accommodations | Government pays accommodation costs directly | Employer bears costs (with tax credit) |
| Annual participants | ~50,000 | No equivalent program |
| Average grant | ~$7,000/year | JAN consultation only (no funding) |
| Scope | Physical, communication, transport, support worker | Limited to employer-provided accommodation |

### Australia: Disability Employment Services

| Feature | Australian Approach | U.S. Comparison |
|---------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Funded service providers | 100+ funded DES providers | Fragmented VR, supported employment |
| Outcome-based funding | Payment tied to employment outcomes | Fee-for-service (mostly) |
| Ongoing support | Up to 52 weeks post-placement | Limited post-placement support |
| National coordination | Single federal program | State-by-state VR systems |

## Solution Sequencing

### Phase 1: Immediate (No Legislation Required)

1. EEOC prioritization of disability enforcement within existing resources
2. OFCCP increased focus on Section 503 compliance
3. Executive order on federal hiring of people with disabilities
4. EEOC AI hiring guidance enforcement

### Phase 2: Near-Term (1-2 Years)

1. EEOC enforcement funding increase (Solution 1)
2. Subminimum wage phase-out legislation (Solution 2)
3. AI hiring regulation legislation (Solution 3)
4. Tax incentive consolidation and expansion (Solution 6)

### Phase 3: Medium-Term (2-5 Years)

1. Benefit system reform legislation (Solution 4)
2. Section 503 expansion (Solution 5)
3. National supported employment infrastructure (Solution 7)

### Rationale

- Start with enforcement capacity (most achievable, builds foundation)
- Address subminimum wage and AI simultaneously (different constituencies, different legislative vehicles)
- Benefit reform requires longer legislative process and political coalition-building
- Supported employment infrastructure requires sustained federal investment
