# Institutionalization: Actions

## Overview

This document outlines concrete actions that individuals, organizations, and communities can take to advance deinstitutionalization and community integration. Actions are organized by stakeholder type and level of effort.

## For Individual Citizens

### Learn and Educate

- **Educate yourself**: Read about the Olmstead decision, the institutional bias in Medicaid, and the community living movement. Start with the National Council on Disability's reports on Olmstead implementation.
- **Challenge assumptions**: Question narratives that frame institutional care as "necessary" or "protective." Ask who benefits from institutional placement and who is harmed.
- **Share stories**: Amplify the voices of people with disabilities who have transitioned from institutions to community living. Personal narratives are the most powerful tool for changing public opinion.
- **Know the data**: Familiarize yourself with the fact that HCBS costs less than institutional care and produces better outcomes. Use these facts in conversations and social media.

### Take Political Action

- **Contact your members of Congress**: Urge support for the HCBS Access Act, Money Follows the Person reauthorization, and opposition to bills that weaken Olmstead enforcement
- **Contact your state legislators**: Urge increased HCBS funding, elimination of waiting lists, higher DSP wages, and Olmstead plan compliance
- **Vote**: Support candidates at all levels who commit to community integration and HCBS expansion
- **Attend town halls**: Ask elected officials specifically about their plans for ending unnecessary institutionalization and addressing HCBS waiting lists
- **Submit public comments**: When CMS or state Medicaid agencies propose rules affecting HCBS, submit comments supporting community integration

### Support the Community

- **Hire people with disabilities**: If you are an employer, create inclusive workplaces that employ people with disabilities in competitive integrated employment
- **Welcome group homes**: If a supported living arrangement or group home is proposed in your neighborhood, support it rather than opposing it
- **Volunteer**: Offer your time to organizations that support community integration, such as independent living centers, The Arc chapters, or ADAPT
- **Donate**: Financial support for disability rights organizations funds litigation, advocacy, and direct services

## For People with Disabilities and Families

### Know Your Rights

- **Olmstead rights**: You have a legal right to receive services in the most integrated setting appropriate to your needs. If you or a family member is institutionalized or at risk of institutionalization, contact your state's protection and advocacy agency.
- **HCBS waiver rights**: Apply for HCBS waiver services in your state. If placed on a waiting list, document the wait and its impact on your life.
- **Guardianship alternatives**: If a guardian is making decisions about your placement, explore supported decision-making as an alternative. Contact your state's protection and advocacy agency for assistance.

### Advocate

- **Self-advocacy organizations**: Join People First, Self Advocates Becoming Empowered (SABE), or local self-advocacy groups
- **Family organizations**: Join The Arc, NAMI, Autism Society, or other family advocacy organizations that support community integration
- **Tell your story**: Share your experience with legislators, media, and the public. Personal testimony is essential for policy change.
- **File complaints**: If you experience unnecessary institutionalization or denial of community services, file complaints with your state protection and advocacy agency, the DOJ, and CMS

### Plan for Community Living

- **Person-centered planning**: Insist on person-centered planning that starts with your goals, preferences, and strengths rather than your deficits
- **Housing**: Explore housing options including Section 811 housing, Housing Choice Vouchers, and supported living arrangements
- **Employment**: Pursue competitive integrated employment through your state's vocational rehabilitation agency
- **Healthcare**: Establish community-based healthcare relationships before or during transition from institutional settings
- **Social connections**: Build community relationships through volunteering, community organizations, faith communities, and recreational activities

## For Direct Support Professionals

- **Advocate for wages**: Support campaigns for higher DSP wages and benefits. Join or form a union.
- **Pursue training**: Seek certification and specialization through the National Alliance for Direct Support Professionals
- **Practice self-determination**: Support the people you serve in making their own choices, even when those choices differ from what you might choose
- **Report abuse**: If you witness abuse, neglect, or exploitation in any setting (institutional or community), report it immediately to your state's protective services agency and your employer
- **Stay in the field**: The community services system needs experienced, dedicated workers. Higher wages and better conditions are coming.

## For Healthcare Providers

- **Default to community**: When discharging patients who need long-term services, explore community-based options before recommending nursing home placement
- **Know the resources**: Familiarize yourself with your state's HCBS waivers, independent living centers, and community services agencies
- **Assess for community potential**: Use assessment tools that identify needed supports rather than merely documenting deficits
- **Refer to community services**: Connect patients with HCBS navigators, independent living centers, and supported decision-making programs
- **Train your staff**: Ensure all hospital discharge planners, social workers, and care managers understand Olmstead rights and community alternatives

## For Legal Professionals

- **Bring Olmstead cases**: File litigation challenging unnecessary institutionalization and inadequate community services. Contact the Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law or NDRN for guidance.
- **Challenge guardianship**: Advocate for supported decision-making alternatives in guardianship proceedings. Represent people seeking restoration of rights.
- **Pro bono work**: Offer pro bono legal services to people with disabilities facing institutionalization or seeking community transition
- **File amicus briefs**: Support Olmstead litigation through amicus briefs from bar associations, law school clinics, and legal organizations
- **Monitor compliance**: Help monitor state compliance with Olmstead consent decrees and settlement agreements

## For State and Local Officials

- **Develop Olmstead plans**: Create comprehensive, enforceable Olmstead compliance plans with measurable goals and timelines
- **Eliminate waiting lists**: Invest in HCBS expansion to serve all eligible individuals without waiting lists
- **Raise DSP wages**: Increase Medicaid reimbursement rates and require pass-through to worker wages
- **Reform assessments**: Implement community-first assessment protocols that default to community placement
- **Coordinate housing**: Align Medicaid, housing, and disability agency efforts to ensure accessible affordable housing is available
- **Engage stakeholders**: Include people with disabilities and their families in all policy development processes

## For Researchers

- **Document outcomes**: Conduct rigorous research comparing institutional and community outcomes across quality of life, health, safety, and cost metrics
- **Study transitions**: Research best practices for institutional-to-community transitions, including what supports are most critical for success
- **Analyze disparities**: Investigate racial, geographic, and socioeconomic disparities in institutionalization rates and community services access
- **Evaluate workforce interventions**: Study the effectiveness of wage increases, career ladders, and other strategies for building the DSP workforce
- **Publish and disseminate**: Make research accessible to policymakers, advocates, and the public
