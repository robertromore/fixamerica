# Institutionalization: Current State

## Institutional Census

### Who Remains Institutionalized

Approximately 600,000 Americans with disabilities live in institutional settings, a number that has declined from over 1 million in the 1990s but has plateaued in the last decade. The population spans multiple disability types and facility categories.

**Institutional Population by Facility Type (2024)**

| Facility Type | Estimated Residents | Trend (5-year) | Primary Population |
|---------------|--------------------|-----------------|--------------------|
| Nursing homes (all ages with disabilities) | 370,000 | Declining slowly | Elderly; 145,000+ under 65 |
| Nursing homes (under 65 only) | 145,000 | Stable | Physical, intellectual, psychiatric disabilities |
| ICFs/IID (16+ beds) | 23,000 | Declining | Intellectual/developmental disabilities |
| ICFs/IID (under 16 beds) | 45,000 | Stable | Intellectual/developmental disabilities |
| State psychiatric hospitals | 35,000 | Stable | Psychiatric disabilities |
| Private psychiatric facilities (long-term) | 25,000 | Increasing slightly | Psychiatric and substance use disorders |
| Large state-operated residential facilities | 18,000 | Declining | Intellectual/developmental disabilities |
| Other congregate settings | 40,000 | Varies | Mixed |

*Sources: CMS Nursing Home Compare (2024); University of Minnesota, Residential Information Systems Project (2024); SAMHSA, National Mental Health Services Survey (2024).*

**Key disparities**: Black Americans with disabilities are institutionalized at rates 70% higher than their share of the disability population. This disparity reflects broader patterns of racial inequality in healthcare access, housing, and community services availability.

## Medicaid and the Institutional Bias

### Spending Structure

Medicaid is the dominant funder of long-term services and supports (LTSS) in the United States, covering approximately 60% of all LTSS spending. The program's structure creates a systematic bias toward institutional placement.

**Medicaid LTSS Spending (FY 2024 Estimated)**

| Category | Federal + State Spending | Share of LTSS |
|----------|------------------------|---------------|
| Institutional care (total) | $105 billion | 45% |
| - Nursing homes | $82 billion | 35% |
| - ICFs/IID | $11 billion | 5% |
| - Psychiatric facilities | $12 billion | 5% |
| HCBS (total) | $130 billion | 55% |
| - 1915(c) waivers | $75 billion | 32% |
| - State plan HCBS | $30 billion | 13% |
| - 1115 demonstration waivers | $15 billion | 6% |
| - Other HCBS | $10 billion | 4% |
| **Total LTSS** | **$235 billion** | **100%** |

*Source: Medicaid and CHIP Payment and Access Commission (MACPAC), MACStats (2024).*

**The institutional bias explained**: While HCBS now accounts for a larger share of total spending (55%) than institutional care (45%), the critical distinction is *entitlement status*:

- **Institutional care is a Medicaid entitlement**: Anyone who meets Medicaid eligibility and clinical criteria has a federal right to nursing home or ICF/IID placement. States cannot cap enrollment or create waiting lists.
- **Most HCBS is optional**: Section 1915(c) waivers, which fund the majority of HCBS, allow states to limit enrollment, impose waiting lists, and restrict geographic availability. States can choose which populations to serve and which services to offer.

This asymmetry means that a person who cannot get community services has a guaranteed right to institutional care -- creating a one-way ratchet toward institutionalization.

## HCBS Waiting Lists

### National Scope

Over 800,000 Americans with disabilities are on waiting lists for HCBS waiver services. These are people who have been determined eligible for services but cannot receive them because of state funding or slot limitations.

**HCBS Waiting List Data (2024)**

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Total people on waiting lists nationally | 820,000+ |
| States with waiting lists | 40+ |
| Average wait time nationally | 3-8 years |
| Longest average wait (by state) | Texas: 10+ years for some waivers |
| People who die while on waiting lists (annual est.) | Unknown; Texas reported 700+ deaths in 2022 |
| Most common waiver with waiting list | ID/DD waivers (70% of total waiting list) |

*Sources: Kaiser Family Foundation, HCBS Waiver Waiting List Survey (2024); Texas HHS, Interest List Data (2023).*

**Consequences of waiting**

- People remain in or are placed in institutions because community services are unavailable
- Family caregivers experience burnout, financial hardship, and health deterioration
- Individuals lose skills and social connections during prolonged waits
- Some people die before receiving services
- Others enter the criminal justice system due to lack of community supports

### COVID-19 Impact and Aftermath

The COVID-19 pandemic exposed institutional settings as uniquely dangerous:

| Impact | Data | Source |
|--------|------|--------|
| Nursing home COVID deaths (2020-2022) | Over 200,000 | CMS COVID-19 Nursing Home Data (2023) |
| Share of U.S. COVID deaths in nursing homes | 23% of all U.S. deaths (through 2021) | Kaiser Family Foundation (2022) |
| ICF/IID COVID death rate vs. community | 2.5x higher | University of Minnesota (2022) |
| State psychiatric hospital outbreaks | Documented in 47 states | Treatment Advocacy Center (2022) |

The pandemic prompted federal action through the American Rescue Plan Act (2021), which provided a temporary 10 percentage point increase in Medicaid HCBS matching funds ($37 billion). States used these funds to expand services, increase provider rates, and reduce waiting lists. However, the enhanced funding expired in March 2025, and many states face fiscal cliffs as they attempt to sustain expanded services.

## Olmstead Enforcement

### DOJ Enforcement Activity

The DOJ Civil Rights Division enforces the Olmstead integration mandate through investigations, settlement agreements, and litigation against states and localities.

**Olmstead Enforcement Actions (Cumulative through 2025)**

| Administration | Investigations Opened | Settlement Agreements | Lawsuits Filed | States Affected |
|----------------|-----------------------|-----------------------|----------------|-----------------|
| Obama (2009-2017) | 40+ | 25 | 8 | 20 |
| Trump (2017-2021) | 8 | 6 | 1 | 5 |
| Biden (2021-2025) | 20+ | 12 | 3 | 10 |
| **Cumulative** | **68+** | **43** | **12** | **30+** |

*Source: DOJ Civil Rights Division, Olmstead Enforcement page (2025).*

**Key recent enforcement actions:**

- *United States v. Texas* (ongoing): Challenge to Texas's institutional bias for people with intellectual disabilities
- *United States v. Georgia* (settlement 2023): State agreed to expand community services for people with mental illness
- *United States v. Mississippi* (ongoing): Challenges to inadequate community services for children with disabilities
- *Disability Rights Oregon v. Oregon* (2024): Private Olmstead challenge regarding nursing home placements

### Judicial Developments

| Issue | Current Status | Key Cases |
|-------|----------------|-----------|
| Olmstead applies to "at risk" of institutionalization | Circuit split; most circuits recognize "at risk" claims | *Pashby v. Delia* (4th Cir. 2013); *Steimel v. Wernert* (7th Cir. 2016) |
| Olmstead applies to employment/day services | Expanding; DOJ argues for broad application | *Lane v. Brown* (D. Or. 2012) |
| State "fundamental alteration" defense | States raise costs as defense; courts generally skeptical | *Olmstead* itself recognized this defense |
| Community integration for children | Growing area of enforcement | *United States v. Mississippi* (S.D. Miss., ongoing) |

## Workforce Crisis

### Direct Support Professionals

The community services system depends on direct support professionals (DSPs) who provide personal care, supported employment, residential support, and other services. This workforce is in persistent crisis.

**DSP Workforce Data (2024)**

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Estimated DSP workforce | 4.6 million | PHI National (2024) |
| Median hourly wage | $14.50 | BLS Occupational Employment Statistics (2024) |
| Annual turnover rate | 43% (ID/DD services); 75% (home care) | National Core Indicators Staff Stability Survey (2024) |
| Vacancy rate | 13% (ID/DD services) | ANCOR, "The State of America's Direct Support Workforce" (2024) |
| Workers relying on public assistance | 45% | PHI National (2024) |
| Workers without employer health insurance | 38% | PHI National (2024) |

**Why the crisis persists:**

- Medicaid reimbursement rates set DSP wages; states chronically underfund
- DSPs earn less than retail and food service workers in most markets
- Work is physically and emotionally demanding
- Limited career advancement opportunities
- COVID-19 accelerated departures from the field

## Key Takeaways

1. **The institutional population has plateaued**: After decades of decline, approximately 600,000 people remain in institutional settings
2. **Medicaid's institutional bias persists**: Institutional care remains an entitlement while community services are capped and waitlisted
3. **Waiting lists are enormous**: Over 800,000 people wait years for community services they are entitled to under Olmstead
4. **Racial disparities are severe**: Black Americans are disproportionately institutionalized
5. **The workforce crisis threatens community infrastructure**: DSPs earn poverty-level wages with 43-75% turnover
6. **COVID exposed institutional danger**: Over 200,000 nursing home residents died, yet systemic reform has been limited
7. **Olmstead enforcement is inconsistent**: DOJ enforcement activity varies dramatically by administration
8. **Housing remains the largest barrier**: Without accessible affordable housing, community integration cannot succeed
9. **State variation is extreme**: A person's chance of community living depends heavily on which state they live in
