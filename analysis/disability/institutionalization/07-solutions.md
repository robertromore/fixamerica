# Institutionalization: Solutions

## Overview

Ending unnecessary institutionalization requires a comprehensive approach that simultaneously eliminates the structural bias toward institutional care in Medicaid, builds community infrastructure, strengthens Olmstead enforcement, addresses the workforce crisis, and expands affordable accessible housing. No single reform is sufficient; the interconnected root causes demand interconnected solutions.

## Solution 1: Eliminate the Medicaid Institutional Bias

### Description

Make home and community-based services a Medicaid entitlement on par with institutional care, ending the asymmetry that guarantees institutional placement while making community services optional.

### Mechanism

- Amend the Social Security Act to add HCBS as a mandatory Medicaid benefit (currently 42 U.S.C. Section 1396a(a)(10) mandates nursing facility and ICF/IID services but not HCBS)
- Establish a federal floor of services that all states must offer: personal care, supported employment, residential support, respite, adaptive equipment, transportation, and crisis services
- Require states to serve all eligible individuals without waiting lists within a defined transition period (5-7 years)
- Maintain institutional services as an option but remove the entitlement asymmetry

### Evidence

| Evidence Point | Detail | Source |
|----------------|--------|--------|
| Cost comparison | HCBS costs $30,000-$70,000/person/year vs. $80,000-$250,000 for institutional care | MACPAC (2024) |
| Quality outcomes | People in HCBS report higher satisfaction, greater autonomy, and better health outcomes | NCI Adult Consumer Survey (2024) |
| State examples | Oregon serves 82% of LTSS population in community at lower per-capita cost | Kaiser Family Foundation (2024) |
| International models | Sweden, Norway, Denmark virtually eliminated large institutions by 2000 through HCBS entitlements | European Disability Forum (2023) |
| CBO estimates | HCBS entitlement would cost $150-400 billion over 10 years but reduce institutional spending | CBO, preliminary estimates (2024) |

### Implementation Considerations

- **Phased rollout**: Allow states 5-7 years to build infrastructure before full entitlement takes effect
- **Enhanced FMAP**: Provide 90% federal matching for HCBS expansion in the first 5 years, declining to 80% thereafter (compared to current 50-77% FMAP)
- **Woodwork mitigation**: Phase enrollment increases to manage fiscal impact of newly eligible individuals entering the system
- **Quality standards**: Establish federal HCBS quality metrics before full implementation

## Solution 2: Strengthen Olmstead Enforcement

### Description

Increase the capacity and consistency of federal Olmstead enforcement through dedicated funding, codified enforcement standards, and expanded private enforcement rights.

### Mechanism

- Establish a dedicated Olmstead Enforcement Office within DOJ Civil Rights Division with protected funding
- Codify Olmstead enforcement standards in statute to reduce dependence on executive branch priorities
- Create a private right of action with compensatory damages for individuals subjected to unnecessary institutionalization
- Require states to submit and implement Olmstead compliance plans as a condition of Medicaid participation
- Establish an independent Olmstead Compliance Commission to monitor and report on state progress

### Evidence

- DOJ enforcement activity declined by 75% between the Obama and Trump administrations, demonstrating the need for statutory protections (DOJ Civil Rights Division data, 2009-2021)
- States without active DOJ enforcement or private litigation make significantly less progress on community integration (National Council on Disability, 2023)
- Private enforcement supplements DOJ capacity: disability rights organizations filed over 100 Olmstead-related cases in 2023 (NDRN, 2024)

### Implementation Considerations

- **Funding**: Dedicated appropriation of $50 million/year for Olmstead enforcement (current Disability Rights Section budget covers all ADA enforcement)
- **Judicial capacity**: Federal courts need training on disability rights and Olmstead compliance standards
- **State resistance**: Enforcement must be paired with technical assistance and funding, not purely punitive

## Solution 3: Build the Community Workforce

### Description

Address the direct support professional crisis through minimum wage standards, career development, and Medicaid rate reform.

### Mechanism

- Establish a federal minimum wage for DSPs at 150% of the state or federal minimum wage (whichever is higher), funded through enhanced Medicaid rates
- Create a DSP career ladder with certifications, specializations, and wage progressions
- Fund DSP training programs through a new grant program within the Administration for Community Living
- Require state Medicaid programs to ensure that a minimum percentage (e.g., 80%) of HCBS rate increases pass through to worker wages
- Extend student loan forgiveness and tuition assistance to DSPs
- Create visa pathways for international DSP workers

### Evidence

| Intervention | Evidence | Source |
|-------------|----------|--------|
| Higher wages reduce turnover | A $1/hour wage increase reduces DSP turnover by 8-15% | PHI National research synthesis (2024) |
| Career ladders improve retention | Programs with career progression retain workers 2x longer | National Core Indicators (2024) |
| Pass-through requirements work | States with Medicaid rate pass-through requirements show higher DSP wages | CMS evaluation data (2023) |
| International recruitment | Canada, Australia, and the UK use immigration pathways for care workers | OECD Health Workforce data (2024) |

### Implementation Considerations

- **Cost**: A $15/hour DSP minimum (current median is $14.50) would cost approximately $5-8 billion/year in additional Medicaid spending
- **Provider capacity**: Small community providers may struggle to absorb wage increases; enhanced rates must flow quickly
- **Rural areas**: Workforce challenges are most severe in rural areas where the labor pool is smallest

## Solution 4: Expand Affordable Accessible Housing

### Description

Address the housing shortage that prevents community integration through expanded federal funding, zoning reform, and disability-specific housing programs.

### Mechanism

- Triple Section 811 housing funding from $230 million to $700 million annually
- Create a new HCBS Housing Trust Fund with $2 billion in annual mandatory spending
- Require all federally funded housing to meet visitability standards (one zero-step entrance, accessible bathroom on main floor, 32-inch doorways)
- Preempt local zoning restrictions on group homes and supported living arrangements under the Fair Housing Act
- Expand Housing Choice Voucher program with a disability-specific allocation
- Fund rapid rehousing for people transitioning from institutions

### Evidence

- Section 811 serves only 35,000 units against demand for millions (HUD, 2024)
- 48% of renters with disabilities pay more than 50% of income for housing (ACS, 2023)
- Visitability requirements add less than $500 to new construction costs (Concrete Change, 2023)
- Money Follows the Person program demonstrates that housing is the top barrier to institutional transition (CMS, 2024)

## Solution 5: Reform Guardianship Systems

### Description

Reduce guardianship as a pathway to institutionalization by promoting supported decision-making alternatives and reforming guardianship proceedings.

### Mechanism

- Enact federal supported decision-making legislation establishing supported decision-making as the preferred alternative to guardianship
- Require courts to consider less restrictive alternatives before granting plenary guardianship
- Mandate periodic (at least annual) review of all guardianships with consideration of restoration of rights
- Prohibit institutional staff from petitioning for guardianship of residents
- Fund state supported decision-making pilot programs and training for judges, attorneys, and families
- Require guardians to demonstrate they have explored community living options before consenting to institutional placement

### Evidence

- Over 1.3 million Americans are under guardianship; many are institutionalized (National Guardianship Association, 2023)
- Supported decision-making has been recognized in law in 20+ states with positive outcomes (National Resource Center for Supported Decision-Making, 2024)
- Texas, Maine, and DC have implemented supported decision-making programs showing reduced guardianship and improved self-determination (SABE, 2024)

## Solution 6: Transform Assessment and Placement Systems

### Description

Redesign the processes by which people enter long-term care to default to community placement rather than institutional placement.

### Mechanism

- Require "community-first" assessment protocols for all Medicaid long-term care placements
- Create state-funded community transition teams that intervene at the point of hospital discharge, caregiver crisis, or other placement decision points
- Mandate that all individuals receive information about community alternatives before institutional placement
- Establish 30-day community-first review periods before any institutional admission (with emergency exceptions)
- Fund independent HCBS navigators in every state to assist individuals and families with community service access

### Evidence

- Minnesota's "Return to Community" initiative transitioned 3,500+ people from nursing homes to community settings with 95% satisfaction rates (Minnesota DHS, 2024)
- Washington State's community-first assessment protocol reduced nursing home admissions by 15% in the first two years (Washington DSHS, 2023)
- Money Follows the Person demonstration: 100,000+ successful transitions with lower costs and higher satisfaction (CMS, 2024)

## Solution 7: Ensure HCBS Quality and Accountability

### Description

Build a robust quality assurance system for community-based services to address legitimate safety concerns and counter opposition arguments.

### Mechanism

- Establish federal HCBS quality standards comparable to nursing home quality requirements
- Create an HCBS quality reporting system modeled on Nursing Home Compare
- Fund independent HCBS ombudsman programs in every state
- Require critical incident reporting and investigation for all HCBS providers
- Establish outcome-based quality metrics: community participation, employment, health outcomes, consumer satisfaction
- Fund peer support and self-advocacy programs within the quality assurance system

### Evidence

- National Core Indicators demonstrate that community-based services consistently outperform institutions on quality of life, choice, and satisfaction metrics (NCI, 2024)
- States with strong HCBS quality systems (Oregon, Vermont) have successfully transitioned even individuals with the most significant disabilities (NASDDDS, 2024)
