# Institutionalization: History

## Overview

The history of institutionalization in America is a story of cycles: expansion, reform, neglect, scandal, and partial transformation. From the founding of the first state asylums in the early 19th century through the mass deinstitutionalization movements of the late 20th century to the ongoing struggle for community integration, the treatment of people with disabilities in institutional settings reflects evolving -- and often conflicting -- ideas about disability, dependency, protection, and civil rights.

## Timeline

### The Institutional Era (1840s-1960s)

**1840s-1860s: The Asylum Movement**

- Dorothea Dix campaigns for state-funded institutions to replace jails and poorhouses as settings for people with mental illness and intellectual disabilities
- States begin building large residential institutions, initially framed as therapeutic "asylums" offering moral treatment
- By 1860, 28 of 33 states operate public institutions for people with mental illness
- Institutions for people with intellectual disabilities ("schools for the feebleminded") established in Massachusetts (1848), New York (1851), and Pennsylvania (1853)

**1870s-1920s: Custodialism and Eugenics**

- Institutional populations grow rapidly as states use institutions for social control
- Eugenics movement redefines institutional purpose from treatment to segregation and containment
- Buck v. Bell (1927): Supreme Court upholds compulsory sterilization of institutionalized individuals; Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes writes "three generations of imbeciles are enough"
- Over 60,000 people with disabilities are forcibly sterilized in state institutions (1907-1970s)
- Institutional conditions deteriorate as populations swell beyond capacity
- Average institutional population per facility exceeds 1,000 by 1920

**1930s-1950s: Overcrowding and Neglect**

- Great Depression and World War II divert resources from institutions
- State facilities reach peak populations: over 550,000 people in psychiatric hospitals alone by 1955
- Conditions in many facilities are brutal: overcrowding, physical abuse, forced labor, medical experimentation
- Conscientious objectors serving in institutions during WWII document and publicize abuses
- Albert Deutsch publishes *The Shame of the States* (1948), exposing institutional conditions

### The Reform Era (1960s-1990s)

**1960s: The Beginning of Deinstitutionalization**

- President Kennedy's Panel on Mental Retardation (1961) calls for community-based alternatives
- Community Mental Health Act (1963) creates community mental health centers to replace state hospitals
- Medicaid enacted (1965), creating federal funding for institutional care that would later shape the institutional bias
- Burton Blatt and Fred Kaplan publish *Christmas in Purgatory* (1966), photographic expose of institutional conditions
- Robert Kennedy tours Willowbrook State School (1965), publicly condemns conditions

**1970s: Rights and Litigation**

- *Wyatt v. Stickney* (M.D. Ala. 1971): First federal court ruling establishing constitutional right to adequate treatment in institutions
- Geraldo Rivera's television expose of Willowbrook State School (1972) shocks national audience
- *New York State Association for Retarded Children v. Rockefeller* (Willowbrook case, 1975): Landmark consent decree requiring improved conditions and community placement
- *O'Connor v. Donaldson* (1975): Supreme Court rules that states cannot confine non-dangerous individuals who can live safely in the community
- Education for All Handicapped Children Act (1975): Mandates public education for children with disabilities, reducing one pathway to institutionalization
- Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act (1973): First federal civil rights protection for people with disabilities

**1980s: Accelerating Community Movement**

- Medicaid Home and Community-Based Services waiver program created (1981), allowing states to use Medicaid funds for community services -- but as an optional waiver, not an entitlement
- State institutional populations decline sharply: psychiatric hospital census drops from 550,000 (1955) to 130,000 (1985)
- However, deinstitutionalization without adequate community services contributes to homelessness and incarceration of people with mental illness
- Self-advocacy movement grows: People First organizations founded by people with intellectual disabilities
- *Pennhurst State School v. Halderman* (1981): Supreme Court limits federal courts' ability to order community placement under Developmental Disabilities Act, but allows claims under Medicaid and Section 504

**1990s: The ADA and Olmstead**

- Americans with Disabilities Act enacted (1990): Prohibits discrimination and mandates integration
- ADA Title II integration regulation (28 C.F.R. Section 35.130(d)): Requires public entities to administer services in the most integrated setting appropriate
- *Helen L. v. DiDario* (3d Cir. 1995): Early circuit court ruling applying ADA integration mandate to institutional placement
- *Olmstead v. L.C.* (1999): Supreme Court rules that unjustified institutionalization constitutes discrimination under the ADA; states must provide community-based services when (1) treatment professionals determine community placement is appropriate, (2) the individual does not oppose community placement, and (3) placement can be reasonably accommodated

### Post-Olmstead Era (2000-Present)

**2000s: Implementation Begins**

- President Bush issues Executive Order 13217 (2001): Directs federal agencies to assist states in Olmstead implementation
- States begin developing Olmstead plans (compliance strategies for community integration)
- Money Follows the Person (MFP) demonstration program launched (2007): Federal grants to help states transition people from institutions to community settings
- Over 100,000 people transitioned through MFP by 2024
- New Freedom Initiative promotes community-based alternatives
- DOJ begins systematic Olmstead enforcement investigations

**2010s: Enforcement Expansion**

- Obama administration significantly expands DOJ Olmstead enforcement
- DOJ secures major settlement agreements in Georgia, Virginia, North Carolina, Delaware, Oregon, and other states
- *United States v. Georgia* (2010): Landmark DOJ investigation and settlement on community services for people with mental illness
- CMS issues guidance on HCBS settings requirements (2014): Rules defining what constitutes a "community" setting vs. an institutional one
- Bipartisan support for HCBS grows, but the institutional bias in Medicaid remains unchanged
- ABLE Act (2014) allows tax-advantaged savings accounts for people with disabilities

**2020s: Pandemic and Partial Progress**

- COVID-19 pandemic devastates institutional settings; over 200,000 nursing home residents die (2020-2022)
- American Rescue Plan Act (2021) provides $37 billion in enhanced HCBS Medicaid funding
- States use ARPA funds to increase DSP wages, expand services, reduce waiting lists
- Build Back Better / Inflation Reduction Act efforts to make HCBS expansion permanent fail in Congress
- CMS issues HCBS Access Rule (2023): Requirements for DSP wage transparency and access metrics
- ARPA enhanced HCBS funding expires March 2025; states face fiscal cliffs
- DOJ Olmstead enforcement continues but at reduced pace compared to Obama era

## Key Historical Patterns

### Pattern 1: Scandal-Driven Reform

Throughout history, institutional reform has been catalyzed by media exposes and public scandals rather than systematic policy change. From Dorothea Dix's 1840s campaigns to Willowbrook to COVID-19, the pattern repeats: conditions deteriorate, a scandal emerges, partial reforms follow, attention wanes, and conditions deteriorate again.

### Pattern 2: Deinstitutionalization Without Community Investment

Each wave of institutional closure has been accompanied by inadequate investment in community alternatives. The psychiatric deinstitutionalization of the 1960s-1980s reduced state hospital populations by 80% but failed to build a community mental health system, contributing to homelessness and mass incarceration. Today, the same pattern risks repeating as states close ICFs/IID and large residential facilities without sufficient community services infrastructure.

### Pattern 3: Medicaid as Both Problem and Solution

Medicaid simultaneously sustains institutional care (through its institutional entitlement) and funds community alternatives (through HCBS waivers). Reform efforts have repeatedly attempted to rebalance Medicaid toward community services without eliminating the institutional entitlement, creating an inherently contradictory structure.

### Pattern 4: State Variation as Inequality

Because Medicaid is a federal-state partnership and HCBS is optional, a person's access to community living depends heavily on their state of residence. This geographic lottery creates profound civil rights inequality that federal enforcement has only partially addressed.

### Pattern 5: Slow Legal Progress

From the first right-to-treatment cases in the 1970s through Olmstead in 1999 to ongoing enforcement, legal progress has been real but incremental. Courts have established important principles but lack the institutional capacity to redesign service systems. Enforcement depends on executive branch priorities that shift with each administration.

## Lessons from History

1. **Closing institutions is necessary but not sufficient**: Without community infrastructure, people with disabilities trade one form of neglect for another
2. **Federal funding structures drive outcomes**: Medicaid's design determines whether people live in institutions or communities more than any other single factor
3. **Enforcement depends on political will**: Olmstead enforcement activity has varied by a factor of five across administrations
4. **Self-advocacy matters**: Progress accelerates when people with disabilities lead reform movements
5. **Fiscal arguments align with rights arguments**: Community services cost less than institutions, creating unusual alignment between civil rights and fiscal conservatism
