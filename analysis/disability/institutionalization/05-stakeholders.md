# Institutionalization: Stakeholders

## Overview

The institutionalization landscape involves a complex web of stakeholders with varying degrees of power, resources, and interests. People with disabilities who are institutionalized or at risk of institutionalization are the most directly affected but often have the least political power. Understanding stakeholder dynamics is essential for building effective reform coalitions and anticipating opposition.

## People Directly Affected

### People Currently Institutionalized

- **Population**: Approximately 600,000 individuals in nursing homes, ICFs/IID, psychiatric hospitals, and other congregate facilities
- **Interests**: Autonomy, community integration, quality of life, safety, healthcare, social connection
- **Political power**: Very low; many have guardians who speak for them; limited access to advocacy organizations, communication technology, and voting
- **Barriers to participation**: Physical confinement, communication barriers, guardianship restrictions, institutional staff as gatekeepers, limited access to information about alternatives
- **Key concern**: Many do not know they have a right to community living under Olmstead

### People on HCBS Waiting Lists

- **Population**: Over 800,000 individuals waiting for community-based services
- **Interests**: Timely access to services, avoiding institutionalization, remaining with families and communities
- **Political power**: Low to moderate; some are organized through disability advocacy networks, but many are isolated
- **Key concern**: Waiting lists can last 3-10+ years; some individuals die or are institutionalized while waiting

### People at Risk of Institutionalization

- **Population**: Unknown but estimated in the millions -- anyone with a significant disability whose community supports are inadequate
- **Interests**: Stable community services, affordable housing, employment, healthcare
- **Political power**: Moderate when organized through disability rights and self-advocacy organizations
- **Key concern**: A single crisis (caregiver death, housing loss, service funding cut) can trigger institutional placement

### Family Members and Caregivers

- **Population**: Over 53 million family caregivers in the U.S., a significant portion caring for family members with disabilities
- **Interests**: Support for caregiving, respite services, financial stability, assurance that loved ones will be safe
- **Political power**: Moderate; family advocacy organizations are influential (e.g., The Arc, NAMI, Autism Society)
- **Internal diversity**: Family positions range from strong community integration advocacy to preference for institutional "safety"
- **Key concern**: Many families face a false choice between providing all care themselves or accepting institutional placement

## Government Stakeholders

### Federal Agencies

| Agency | Role | Position | Influence |
|--------|------|----------|-----------|
| CMS | Administers Medicaid, approves HCBS waivers | Supports rebalancing but constrained by statute | Very high -- controls the funding spigot |
| DOJ Civil Rights Division | Enforces Olmstead mandate | Pro-integration; enforcement varies by administration | High when active |
| HHS Administration for Community Living | Funds independent living, protection and advocacy | Strongly pro-community | Moderate; limited budget |
| HUD | Administers Section 811 and other housing programs | Supports accessible housing but underfunded | Moderate |
| SSA | Administers SSI/SSDI | Neutral on institutionalization; benefit rules affect eligibility | High (income/asset limits affect community living) |

### State Governments

| Actor | Interest | Position | Influence |
|-------|----------|----------|-----------|
| Governors | Budget management, political liability | Mixed; some champion reform, others resist costs | Very high |
| State Medicaid agencies | Service design, provider management | Varies widely; many support rebalancing | High |
| State legislatures | Budget appropriations, policy direction | Generally follow governor; fiscal conservatives resist HCBS expansion | High |
| State developmental disabilities agencies | Direct service provision, oversight | Generally pro-community | Moderate to high |
| State protection and advocacy agencies | Legal representation, systemic advocacy | Strongly pro-community | Moderate |

### Local Governments

- **Interests**: Zoning, land use, local service delivery, group home siting
- **Position**: Often resistant to group homes and supported living in residential neighborhoods
- **Influence**: Moderate; zoning decisions affect community living feasibility

## Institutional Providers

### Nursing Home Industry

- **Key organizations**: American Health Care Association/National Center for Assisted Living (AHCA/NCAL), state nursing home associations
- **Population served**: 1.2 million nursing home residents (not all with disabilities)
- **Revenue**: Approximately $170 billion annually, predominantly Medicaid and Medicare funded
- **Position**: Opposes policies that would reduce nursing home census; frames nursing homes as essential safety net
- **Political power**: Very high; major campaign contributors and lobbyists at federal and state levels
- **Key tactic**: Argues for "choice" including institutional choice; opposes mandatory community-first policies

### ICF/IID Providers

- **Key organizations**: State provider associations, VOR (formerly Voice of the Retarded, now rebranded)
- **Position**: Defends ICFs as appropriate for people with the most significant disabilities; opposes closures
- **Political power**: Moderate; concentrated in states with large ICF systems (Texas, Louisiana, Illinois)
- **Key argument**: Claims that some people need the intensive services that only ICFs can provide

### State Psychiatric Hospital Systems

- **Key organizations**: National Association of State Mental Health Program Directors (NASMHPD)
- **Position**: Supports community mental health but defends psychiatric hospitals as necessary for acute and forensic populations
- **Political power**: Moderate; intersects with criminal justice and public safety politics

### State Employee Unions

- **Key organizations**: AFSCME, SEIU (in some states representing institutional workers)
- **Position**: Opposes institutional closures that eliminate union jobs; supports transition only with job guarantees
- **Political power**: High in heavily unionized states (California, New York, Illinois, Connecticut)
- **Key tension**: SEIU also represents community-based workers and supports HCBS expansion; AFSCME historically more protective of institutional jobs

## Community Service Providers

### HCBS Provider Organizations

- **Key organizations**: ANCOR (American Network of Community Options and Resources), state provider associations
- **Position**: Strongly supports HCBS expansion, higher Medicaid rates, workforce investment
- **Political power**: Moderate; growing influence as HCBS spending increases
- **Key concern**: Medicaid reimbursement rates are too low to hire and retain staff

### Independent Living Centers

- **Network**: Over 400 centers nationwide, funded through the Rehabilitation Act
- **Position**: Strongly pro-community; many provide transition assistance to people leaving institutions
- **Political power**: Moderate; well-connected to disability community but underfunded

### Housing Developers and Providers

- **Key organizations**: National Housing Trust, state housing finance agencies
- **Position**: Supports affordable accessible housing development but faces funding constraints
- **Key concern**: Section 811 and Low-Income Housing Tax Credit programs are inadequate to meet demand

## Advocacy Organizations

### Disability Rights Organizations

| Organization | Focus | Position | Influence |
|-------------|-------|----------|-----------|
| The Arc of the United States | Intellectual/developmental disabilities | Strongly pro-community integration | High; largest I/DD advocacy organization |
| ADAPT | Direct action for community living | Strongly pro-community; confrontational tactics | Moderate; highly visible |
| National Disability Rights Network | Protection and advocacy system | Strongly pro-community; legal advocacy | High |
| Disability Rights Education and Defense Fund | Legal advocacy and policy | Strongly pro-community | Moderate to high |
| Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN) | Autistic adults | Strongly pro-community; self-determination | Growing |
| NAMI (National Alliance on Mental Illness) | Mental health | Mixed; supports both community services and some institutional capacity | High |
| National Council on Disability | Federal advisory body | Strongly pro-community; produces influential reports | Moderate |
| Self Advocates Becoming Empowered (SABE) | Self-advocacy by people with I/DD | Strongly pro-community | Moderate |

### Pro-Institutional Advocacy

| Organization | Position | Arguments | Influence |
|-------------|----------|-----------|-----------|
| VOR | Defends institutional options | "Choice" includes institutions; some people need 24/7 care | Low to moderate |
| Some NAMI chapters | Support involuntary treatment and hospital capacity | Community services are inadequate for people with severe mental illness | Moderate |
| Some family organizations | Prefer institutional safety | Concerned about abuse and neglect in community settings | Low to moderate |

## Legal Community

### Disability Rights Attorneys

- **Key actors**: National Disability Rights Network attorneys, Disability Rights Advocates, Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law, state protection and advocacy organizations
- **Position**: Bring Olmstead litigation, challenge institutional conditions, advocate for community services
- **Influence**: High; Olmstead enforcement depends heavily on private litigation alongside DOJ action

### Institutional Defense Attorneys

- **Key actors**: State attorneys general (defending state institutional systems), institutional provider counsel
- **Position**: Defend institutional systems, argue "fundamental alteration" defense, contest Olmstead obligations
- **Influence**: Moderate; can delay reform through prolonged litigation

## Research and Academic Community

- **Key institutions**: University of Minnesota Institute on Community Integration, University of Illinois at Chicago Department of Disability and Human Development, Lurie Institute for Disability Policy at Brandeis
- **Position**: Research overwhelmingly supports community integration as producing better outcomes at lower cost
- **Influence**: Moderate; informs policy but does not drive political decisions

## Stakeholder Power Analysis

| Stakeholder | Power Level | Resources | Position on Reform |
|-------------|-------------|-----------|-------------------|
| CMS | Very High | Controls Medicaid funding | Supportive but constrained |
| Nursing home industry | Very High | Political spending, lobbying infrastructure | Opposed |
| State governments | High | Budget authority, service design | Mixed |
| DOJ | High (when active) | Enforcement authority | Supportive |
| Disability rights organizations | Moderate | Legal expertise, moral authority | Strongly supportive |
| Direct support professionals | Low | Essential labor | Supportive if wages increase |
| People with disabilities | Low | Moral authority, legal rights | Strongly supportive |
| Unions | Moderate | Political power in some states | Mixed |

## Coalition Dynamics

### Pro-Community Integration Coalition

The Arc + ADAPT + NDRN + ASAN + Independent Living Centers + HCBS providers + ANCOR + most Democrats + some Republicans + disability rights attorneys

**Strengths**: Legal mandate (Olmstead), fiscal arguments (community is cheaper), moral authority, bipartisan support for HCBS
**Weaknesses**: Fragmented, underfunded, competing priorities within the coalition

### Status Quo / Pro-Institutional Coalition

Nursing home industry + ICF/IID providers + state employee unions (in some states) + VOR + some family groups + some state governments

**Strengths**: Existing infrastructure, political spending, risk-aversion arguments, unionized workforce
**Weaknesses**: Declining moral authority, high costs, COVID-19 discredited institutional model
