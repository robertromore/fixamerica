# Institutionalization: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Ending unnecessary institutionalization requires legislative action at multiple levels: federal Medicaid reform to eliminate the institutional bias, strengthened enforcement tools for the Olmstead mandate, workforce legislation to build community capacity, and state-level reforms to accelerate community integration. This document presents draft legislation, regulatory frameworks, and legal analysis for a comprehensive reform agenda.

## Constitutional Amendments

No constitutional amendment is required for deinstitutionalization reform. The ADA, Medicaid Act, and Spending Clause provide adequate constitutional authority for federal action. However, the following constitutional considerations are relevant:

- **Fourteenth Amendment**: Equal protection and due process arguments support community integration as a civil right
- **Spending Clause**: Federal conditions on Medicaid funding (including community integration requirements) are constitutionally permissible under *South Dakota v. Dole* and *NFIB v. Sebelius* as long as they are not coercive
- **Tenth Amendment**: States may argue that HCBS mandates exceed federal authority, but Medicaid conditions have consistently been upheld

## Federal Legislation

### Bill 1: HCBS Access Act

**Purpose**: Eliminate the Medicaid institutional bias by making home and community-based services a mandatory Medicaid benefit, ending waiting lists, and establishing a federal floor of community services.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Home and Community-Based Services
Access Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that—
  (1) approximately 600,000 Americans with disabilities live in
      institutional settings, many unnecessarily;
  (2) over 800,000 Americans are on waiting lists for home and
      community-based services;
  (3) the Medicaid program's mandatory coverage of institutional
      care and optional coverage of community-based services
      creates a structural bias toward institutionalization;
  (4) community-based services cost 50-70 percent less per person
      than institutional care;
  (5) community-based services produce better outcomes in quality
      of life, health, employment, and social participation; and
  (6) the Supreme Court held in Olmstead v. L.C. (1999) that
      unjustified institutionalization constitutes discrimination
      under the Americans with Disabilities Act.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to eliminate the
institutional bias in Medicaid by making home and community-based
services a mandatory benefit and ensuring that all eligible
individuals can receive services in the most integrated setting
appropriate to their needs.

SEC. 3. MANDATORY HCBS BENEFIT.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 1902(a)(10)(A) of the Social Security
Act (42 U.S.C. 1396a(a)(10)(A)) is amended to include home and
community-based services as a mandatory benefit for individuals who
meet institutional level of care criteria.

(b) COVERED SERVICES.—Home and community-based services under
this section shall include, at a minimum—
  (1) personal care services;
  (2) habilitation services;
  (3) supported employment services;
  (4) residential support services;
  (5) respite care;
  (6) adaptive equipment and assistive technology;
  (7) transportation;
  (8) community transition services;
  (9) crisis intervention services;
  (10) peer support services; and
  (11) self-directed services, including fiscal intermediary
       services.

(c) ELIMINATION OF WAITING LISTS.—
  (1) No State may maintain a waiting list for home and
      community-based services for any individual who meets
      the eligibility criteria established under this section.
  (2) States shall have 7 years from the date of enactment to
      eliminate all existing waiting lists.
  (3) The Secretary shall establish interim benchmarks for
      waiting list reduction and may withhold Federal matching
      funds from States that fail to meet benchmarks.

SEC. 4. ENHANCED FEDERAL MATCHING.

(a) INITIAL PERIOD.—For the first 5 fiscal years following
enactment, the Federal medical assistance percentage for home
and community-based services provided under this section shall
be 90 percent.

(b) SUBSEQUENT PERIOD.—Beginning in fiscal year 6, the Federal
medical assistance percentage for such services shall be 80 percent.

(c) MAINTENANCE OF EFFORT.—States shall not reduce existing
State spending on home and community-based services as a condition
of receiving enhanced Federal matching funds.

SEC. 5. COMMUNITY-FIRST ASSESSMENT.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Each State shall implement a community-first
assessment protocol under which—
  (1) all individuals seeking or referred for long-term services
      and supports receive an assessment of community-based
      service options before any institutional placement;
  (2) assessors provide individuals with complete information
      about available community services, housing options, and
      support alternatives;
  (3) institutional placement occurs only after community
      options have been explored and documented; and
  (4) individuals placed in institutional settings receive
      reassessment for community transition at least annually.

SEC. 6. QUALITY STANDARDS.

(a) The Secretary shall establish Federal quality standards for
home and community-based services, including—
  (1) person-centered planning requirements;
  (2) health and safety protections;
  (3) community integration metrics;
  (4) employment and community participation outcomes;
  (5) consumer satisfaction measures; and
  (6) critical incident reporting and investigation requirements.

SEC. 7. REPORTING AND ACCOUNTABILITY.

(a) Each State shall submit annual reports to the Secretary on—
  (1) the number of individuals receiving HCBS;
  (2) waiting list data, if any;
  (3) institutional population data;
  (4) quality metrics;
  (5) spending data; and
  (6) workforce data, including direct support professional
      wages, benefits, vacancy rates, and turnover rates.

SEC. 8. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated such sums as may be
necessary to carry out this Act.

SEC. 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on October 1 following the date of
enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the root cause of unnecessary institutionalization by making HCBS a Medicaid entitlement. The enhanced FMAP reduces the fiscal burden on states during transition. The community-first assessment requirement changes the default from institutional to community placement. Quality standards address concerns about community services safety.

**Potential Challenges**: Cost (CBO estimates $150-400 billion over 10 years); state resistance to new mandates; nursing home industry lobbying; "woodwork effect" concerns.

**Refinements**: If full entitlement is not achievable, an intermediate step would be enhanced FMAP for HCBS spending (increasing from current rates to 80%) without full entitlement status, combined with mandatory waiting list reporting and reduction targets.

### Bill 2: Olmstead Enforcement Act

**Purpose**: Strengthen federal enforcement of the Supreme Court's Olmstead decision by creating a dedicated enforcement office, codifying enforcement standards, and expanding private enforcement rights.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Olmstead Enforcement Act".

SEC. 2. DEDICATED OLMSTEAD ENFORCEMENT OFFICE.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established within the Civil Rights
Division of the Department of Justice an Office of Olmstead
Enforcement.

(b) DIRECTOR.—The Office shall be headed by a Director appointed
by the Attorney General.

(c) FUNCTIONS.—The Office shall—
  (1) investigate potential violations of the integration
      mandate of the Americans with Disabilities Act;
  (2) bring enforcement actions against States and local
      governments that fail to provide services in the most
      integrated setting appropriate;
  (3) issue guidance on Olmstead compliance;
  (4) provide technical assistance to States;
  (5) publish annual reports on the state of Olmstead compliance
      nationwide; and
  (6) coordinate with the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid
      Services on Medicaid-related integration issues.

(d) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated
$50,000,000 annually for the Office of Olmstead Enforcement.

SEC. 3. PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.

(a) Any individual who is subjected to unnecessary
institutionalization or denied community-based services in
violation of the integration mandate may bring a civil action
for—
  (1) injunctive relief;
  (2) compensatory damages;
  (3) reasonable attorney fees; and
  (4) costs.

(b) STATUTE OF LIMITATIONS.—An action under this section must
be commenced within 4 years of the violation.

SEC. 4. OLMSTEAD COMPLIANCE PLANS.

(a) Each State participating in Medicaid shall submit to the
Secretary of Health and Human Services and the Attorney General
an Olmstead compliance plan that includes—
  (1) an assessment of the State's institutional population;
  (2) goals and timelines for community transition;
  (3) a plan for building community infrastructure;
  (4) a workforce development strategy;
  (5) a housing plan;
  (6) quality assurance provisions; and
  (7) annual progress benchmarks.

(b) The Secretary may withhold up to 5 percent of Federal
Medicaid matching funds from States that fail to submit or
implement compliance plans.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the inconsistency of Olmstead enforcement across administrations by creating a permanent, dedicated office with protected funding. The private right of action with compensatory damages provides a powerful incentive for compliance. Olmstead compliance plans tied to Medicaid participation create accountability.

**Potential Challenges**: State sovereignty arguments; concerns about litigation costs; DOJ staffing; political resistance to new enforcement mandates.

**Refinements**: If compensatory damages face opposition, limit initial damages to injunctive relief with mandatory attorney fees, with damages added after a transition period.

### Bill 3: Community Integration Workforce Act

**Purpose**: Address the direct support professional workforce crisis through wage standards, career development, and Medicaid rate reform.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Community Integration Workforce Act".

SEC. 2. DIRECT SUPPORT PROFESSIONAL MINIMUM COMPENSATION.

(a) MINIMUM WAGE.—No State may pay a Medicaid reimbursement rate
for home and community-based services that results in a direct
support professional wage below 150 percent of the greater of—
  (1) the Federal minimum wage under Section 6(a)(1) of the
      Fair Labor Standards Act; or
  (2) the applicable State minimum wage.

(b) BENEFITS.—The Secretary shall establish minimum benefit
standards for direct support professionals, including—
  (1) health insurance;
  (2) paid leave; and
  (3) retirement contributions.

SEC. 3. CAREER DEVELOPMENT.

(a) The Secretary, acting through the Administrator of the
Administration for Community Living, shall establish a Direct
Support Professional Career Ladder Program, including—
  (1) standardized certification levels;
  (2) specialized training in areas including behavioral
      support, medical care coordination, and employment
      coaching;
  (3) continuing education requirements; and
  (4) wage progressions tied to certification levels.

(b) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated
$500,000,000 annually for the Career Ladder Program.

SEC. 4. RATE PASS-THROUGH.

(a) As a condition of Medicaid participation, each State shall
ensure that not less than 80 percent of any increase in Medicaid
reimbursement rates for home and community-based services is
passed through to direct support professional compensation.

(b) States shall report annually on rate pass-through compliance.

SEC. 5. STUDENT LOAN FORGIVENESS.

Direct support professionals who have worked in the field for
5 or more consecutive years shall be eligible for student loan
forgiveness of up to $30,000 under a new category added to the
Public Service Loan Forgiveness Program.
```

**Explanation**: The DSP workforce crisis is the primary practical barrier to community integration. This bill addresses it through wage standards tied to Medicaid rates, career development, and retention incentives. The 80% pass-through requirement ensures rate increases reach workers rather than being absorbed by provider overhead.

**Potential Challenges**: Cost ($5-8 billion/year for wage increases); provider compliance with pass-through; interaction with state minimum wage laws.

## State Model Legislation

### Model HCBS Expansion and Olmstead Compliance Act

**Purpose**: State-level legislation to accelerate community integration and Olmstead compliance.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "[State] Community Integration Act".

SECTION 2. COMMUNITY-FIRST POLICY.
(a) It is the policy of this State that all individuals with
    disabilities shall receive services in the most integrated
    setting appropriate to their needs, consistent with the
    Supreme Court's decision in Olmstead v. L.C.
(b) Institutional placement shall be a last resort, available
    only after community-based options have been exhausted.

SECTION 3. WAITING LIST ELIMINATION.
(a) The [State Medicaid Agency] shall eliminate all waiting lists
    for home and community-based waiver services within 5 years
    of the effective date of this Act.
(b) The Agency shall submit quarterly progress reports to the
    Legislature.

SECTION 4. DIRECT SUPPORT PROFESSIONAL WAGES.
(a) Medicaid reimbursement rates for HCBS shall be set at levels
    sufficient to support a minimum direct support professional
    wage of [150% of state minimum wage].
(b) Not less than 80 percent of rate increases shall be passed
    through to worker compensation.

SECTION 5. COMMUNITY TRANSITION TEAMS.
(a) The [State Agency] shall establish community transition teams
    in each region of the State.
(b) Teams shall intervene at the point of institutional
    placement decisions to ensure community alternatives are
    explored.

SECTION 6. OLMSTEAD COMPLIANCE PLAN.
(a) The [Governor/State Agency] shall develop and publish an
    Olmstead compliance plan with measurable goals and timelines.
(b) The plan shall be updated annually and subject to public
    comment.
(c) An independent Olmstead compliance advisory committee,
    including people with disabilities and family members,
    shall monitor implementation.
```

**Adaptations**: States can customize dollar amounts, timelines, and administrative structures. States with larger institutional populations may need longer transition periods. States with existing HCBS infrastructure can implement more quickly.

## Regulatory Framework

### CMS HCBS Access Rule Enhancement

**Existing Authority**: Social Security Act Section 1902(a)(30)(A) requires states to ensure sufficient access to Medicaid services.

**Proposed Regulation**: CMS should strengthen the HCBS Access Rule (finalized 2023) to include:

- Mandatory DSP wage reporting by all HCBS providers
- Minimum network adequacy standards for HCBS (e.g., maximum travel distance, maximum wait time for service initiation)
- Required annual consumer satisfaction surveys
- Public reporting of access metrics by state

### CMS HCBS Settings Rule Enforcement

**Existing Authority**: 42 C.F.R. Section 441.301(c)(4) (HCBS settings requirements, finalized 2014)

**Proposed Action**: CMS should enforce the 2014 settings rule deadline (extended to March 2023, with further extensions granted to many states) by:

- Withholding new waiver approvals for states not in compliance
- Requiring heightened scrutiny for all settings that have characteristics of institutions
- Publishing state compliance data publicly

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Spending Clause**: Federal HCBS mandates as Medicaid conditions are constitutional as long as they are not coercive under *NFIB v. Sebelius*. The enhanced FMAP and transition period mitigate coercion concerns.
- **Tenth Amendment**: States cannot be commandeered to administer federal programs, but Medicaid participation is voluntary. Conditions on voluntary federal funding programs are permissible.
- **Due Process**: Individuals have a liberty interest in freedom from institutional confinement. The proposed legislation strengthens due process protections.

### Preemption Questions

- Federal HCBS entitlement would preempt state laws that limit community-based services availability
- States retain authority over service delivery systems, provider qualifications, and quality standards (within federal minimums)
- Olmstead compliance requirements are already established as federal law; proposed legislation strengthens rather than creates new preemption

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- **DOJ enforcement**: Strengthened through dedicated Olmstead Enforcement Office
- **Private litigation**: Enhanced through private right of action with compensatory damages
- **CMS enforcement**: Medicaid conditions enforceable through fund withholding
- **State enforcement**: State protection and advocacy agencies funded to bring Olmstead cases
- **Judicial oversight**: Consent decrees and court-monitored compliance remain available

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| "Community" redefinition | States could define large congregate settings (16+ beds) as "community-based" to meet requirements without genuine integration | High |
| Provider gaming | HCBS providers could meet minimum wage requirements on paper while reducing hours or benefits | Moderate |
| Assessment manipulation | States could manipulate level-of-care assessments to find individuals ineligible for HCBS | High |
| Voluntary institutionalization | States could pressure individuals to "choose" institutional placement to avoid HCBS obligations | High |
| Compliance plan evasion | States could submit plans with ambitious goals but implement slowly, using "fundamental alteration" defense | Moderate |
| Rate increase capture | Providers could absorb rate increases in overhead rather than passing through to workers | Moderate |
| Transition cherry-picking | States could prioritize transitioning individuals who are easiest to serve, leaving those with complex needs institutionalized | Moderate |
| Geographic concentration | States could concentrate community services in urban areas, leaving rural populations without access | Moderate |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Cost uncertainty | CBO estimates range from $150-400 billion over 10 years; fiscal impact could exceed projections | Uncertainty about "woodwork effect" and service utilization rates |
| Housing dependency | HCBS entitlement alone cannot succeed without parallel housing expansion | Housing markets are beyond Medicaid's scope |
| Workforce timeline | DSP workforce cannot be built overnight; years may pass before adequate staffing is achieved | Labor market constraints, training pipeline |
| State capacity variation | Some states lack administrative infrastructure to implement rapid HCBS expansion | Decades of underinvestment in community systems |
| Quality assurance gaps | Federal HCBS quality standards do not yet exist at the level of detail of nursing home regulations | Regulatory development takes years |
| Guardianship interaction | Even with HCBS entitlement, guardians may still choose institutional placement for wards | Guardianship reform is a separate legislative track |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Anti-redefining provision**: Add explicit statutory definition of "community-based setting" consistent with the 2014 CMS HCBS settings rule. Settings with more than 4 individuals are presumptively institutional and require heightened scrutiny. Settings with more than 8 individuals are categorically institutional.

2. **Wage verification**: Require independent auditing of DSP wages and benefits. Providers that fail pass-through requirements face disqualification from Medicaid participation.

3. **Assessment integrity**: Establish independent assessment entities (not affiliated with institutional or community providers) to conduct level-of-care assessments. Require CMS approval of assessment instruments.

4. **Informed choice protections**: Require documented, witnessed informed consent for any institutional placement. Individuals must receive information about community alternatives from an independent source (not the institutional provider). Consent must be renewed annually.

5. **Compliance plan enforcement**: Establish specific numerical benchmarks with automatic fund withholding for non-compliance. Eliminate subjective "fundamental alteration" defense for states that have not made good-faith efforts.

6. **Rate transparency**: Require public reporting of provider finances, including the share of Medicaid revenue allocated to direct worker compensation.

7. **Complex needs priority**: Require states to demonstrate that transition plans include individuals with the most significant disabilities, not just those easiest to serve.

8. **Rural access standards**: Establish maximum geographic distance standards for HCBS availability. Fund rural HCBS infrastructure through targeted grants.

### General Implementation Concerns

- **Transition period risks**: During the multi-year transition from institutional to community systems, some individuals may experience disruptions in care. Mitigation requires parallel operation of both systems during transition with careful individual transition planning.
- **Political sustainability**: An HCBS entitlement, once enacted, could face future budget cuts or repeal attempts. Building broad political support and demonstrating positive outcomes is essential for long-term sustainability.
- **Regulatory capture**: The community services industry, as it grows, could replicate some of the regulatory capture dynamics currently seen with the nursing home industry. Strong consumer voice and independent oversight are essential safeguards.
- **Data infrastructure**: Effective monitoring requires data systems that many states do not currently have. Federal investment in HCBS data infrastructure must accompany the legislative reforms.
