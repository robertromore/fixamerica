# Institutionalization: Overview

## Executive Summary

Institutionalization -- the practice of placing people with disabilities in segregated residential facilities rather than supporting them in community-based settings -- remains one of the most consequential civil rights issues in American disability policy. Despite decades of federal law, Supreme Court rulings, and bipartisan reform efforts mandating community integration, approximately 600,000 Americans with disabilities continue to live in institutional settings including nursing homes, intermediate care facilities for individuals with intellectual disabilities (ICFs/IID), psychiatric hospitals, and large state-operated residential facilities.

The landmark 1999 Supreme Court decision in *Olmstead v. L.C.* established that unjustified institutionalization constitutes discrimination under the Americans with Disabilities Act. Yet a quarter century later, the Olmstead mandate remains incompletely implemented. Medicaid's institutional bias -- a structural feature that guarantees funding for institutional care while making community-based services optional -- continues to drive placement decisions. Waiting lists for home and community-based services (HCBS) exceed 800,000 people nationwide, meaning that hundreds of thousands of Americans are effectively denied the community living the law promises.

The consequences of unnecessary institutionalization are severe: loss of autonomy, social isolation, elevated rates of abuse and neglect, reduced life expectancy, and violation of fundamental civil liberties. Institutional settings cost two to three times more per person than community-based alternatives, creating a fiscal argument that reinforces the moral and legal case for reform. Transition requires not merely closing institutions but building a robust community services infrastructure -- affordable accessible housing, personal care attendants, employment supports, and healthcare -- that enables genuine independence.

## Core Tensions

### Individual Choice vs. Paternalism

At the heart of institutionalization policy lies a fundamental tension between protecting vulnerable individuals and respecting their autonomy. Some families and guardians argue that institutional settings provide necessary supervision and medical care that community settings cannot replicate. Disability rights advocates counter that this framing substitutes paternalism for self-determination, and that with adequate supports, virtually all people with disabilities can live in integrated community settings. The tension is sharpened by the fact that many institutionalized individuals have never been offered genuine alternatives.

### Fiscal Federalism vs. Civil Rights

The Olmstead mandate requires community integration, but Medicaid -- the primary funding source -- is a federal-state partnership in which states control service design and spending priorities. States vary enormously in their commitment to community-based services, creating a patchwork where a person's right to community living depends heavily on geography. Federal efforts to mandate HCBS expansion face resistance from states citing cost concerns and administrative capacity.

### Closure vs. Transition

Closing institutions without building adequate community infrastructure can leave people with disabilities worse off -- a pattern seen in the premature deinstitutionalization of psychiatric hospitals in the 1960s-1980s that contributed to homelessness and incarceration. Advocates emphasize that the goal is not merely closure but transition: replacing institutions with a comprehensive community services system that actually works.

### Workforce Crisis vs. Service Expansion

Community-based services depend on direct support professionals (DSPs) -- personal care attendants, job coaches, residential support staff -- who are among the lowest-paid workers in the economy. Expanding community services requires dramatically increasing this workforce at higher wages, which raises costs and creates tension with fiscal constraints.

## Vision of Success

Full implementation of the Olmstead mandate would mean that no American with a disability lives in an institutional setting against their will or because community-based alternatives are unavailable. Every state would offer a comprehensive array of HCBS -- personal care, supported employment, accessible housing, transportation, healthcare -- without waiting lists. Direct support professionals would earn a living wage with career advancement opportunities, creating a stable workforce. Medicaid would fund community services as an entitlement on par with institutional care, eliminating the structural bias that currently drives placement decisions. People with disabilities would exercise genuine choice about where they live, with whom, and how they spend their days. Institutions would exist only as a truly voluntary option chosen by individuals with full knowledge of community alternatives -- not as a default imposed by a system that fails to invest in independence.
