# Intersectionality: Stakeholders

## Overview

Intersectional disability policy involves stakeholders from disability rights, racial justice, gender equity, LGBTQ+ advocacy, immigration reform, and anti-poverty movements. The challenge is that people at the intersections are often the least powerful within each of these movements, even as they bear the greatest burden of compounded disadvantage.

## People Directly Affected

### People of Color with Disabilities

- **Population**: Approximately 16 million Black, Latino, Native American, and Asian American adults with disabilities
- **Interests**: Equal access to disability services regardless of race; culturally responsive care; freedom from compounded discrimination; representation in disability advocacy leadership
- **Political power**: Low to moderate; organized through some identity-specific organizations but underrepresented in mainstream disability organizations
- **Key concern**: Face the worst outcomes on every measurable dimension yet have the least voice in shaping disability policy

### Women with Disabilities

- **Population**: Approximately 33 million adult women with disabilities
- **Interests**: Reproductive autonomy, protection from gender-based violence, equal employment, accessible healthcare
- **Political power**: Moderate; women's organizations and disability organizations address gender and disability separately
- **Key concern**: Violence against women with disabilities is pervasive but under-addressed by both domestic violence and disability service systems

### LGBTQ+ People with Disabilities

- **Population**: Estimated 4-6 million LGBTQ+ adults with disabilities (higher disability prevalence in LGBTQ+ population)
- **Interests**: Affirming disability services, inclusive data collection, protection from compound discrimination, accessible LGBTQ+ community spaces
- **Political power**: Low; largely invisible in both LGBTQ+ advocacy and disability advocacy
- **Key concern**: Service providers lack competency in both disability and LGBTQ+ identities simultaneously

### Immigrants with Disabilities

- **Population**: Estimated 4-6 million immigrants with disabilities
- **Interests**: Access to benefits regardless of immigration status, language access, protection from deportation, culturally appropriate services
- **Political power**: Very low; fear of government contact limits advocacy participation
- **Key concern**: Excluded from major benefit programs and afraid to seek services due to immigration enforcement

### People with Multiple Marginalized Identities

- **Example**: A queer Latina immigrant woman with a psychiatric disability faces compounded barriers from disability, gender, race/ethnicity, sexual orientation, immigration status, and mental health stigma
- **Political power**: Extremely low; no single organization fully represents their interests
- **Key concern**: Fall through the cracks of every service system and advocacy movement

## Government Stakeholders

### Federal Agencies

| Agency | Intersectional Role | Current Activity | Influence |
|--------|-------------------|------------------|-----------|
| EEOC | Enforces both Title VII (race/sex) and ADA (disability) | Developing intersectionality guidance | High |
| DOJ Civil Rights Division | Enforces multiple civil rights statutes | Some intersectional enforcement | High |
| CMS | Administers Medicaid/Medicare | Equity plan addressing racial disparities | Very high |
| DOE Office of Civil Rights | Enforces IDEA and Title VI | Monitors racial disproportionality in special education | High |
| HHS Office of Minority Health | Health equity promotion | Limited disability focus | Moderate |
| ACL | Funds disability services | Beginning equity focus | Moderate |
| White House Domestic Policy Council | Executive orders on equity | EO 13985 directs intersectional equity | High |

### State and Local Governments

- **State Medicaid agencies**: Control service design and can incorporate equity requirements
- **State education agencies**: Implement IDEA disproportionality requirements
- **State human rights commissions**: Some recognize intersectional discrimination claims
- **Local government**: Zoning, policing, and social services affect intersectional populations

## Research Community

- **University centers**: University of Minnesota ICI, UIC DHD, Syracuse University Burton Blatt Institute
- **Research organizations**: Mathematica, AIR, RAND
- **Disability justice scholars**: Nirmala Erevelles, Subini Ancy Annamma, Liat Ben-Moshe, Sami Schalk

## Coalition Dynamics

### Intersectional Disability Justice Coalition

Disability justice organizations + identity-specific disability organizations + progressive disability rights organizations + civil rights organizations (selectively) + academic researchers

**Strengths**: Moral authority, sophisticated analysis, growing cultural influence, lived experience
**Weaknesses**: Very limited funding, small organizations, limited political access, policy translation gap

### Traditional Disability Rights Coalition

Major disability organizations + protection and advocacy agencies + HCBS provider organizations + some bipartisan political support

**Strengths**: Institutional resources, political connections, legal expertise, track record of legislative wins
**Weaknesses**: Leadership diversity gaps, single-axis policy focus, slow to incorporate intersectional analysis

### Cross-Movement Potential

The most powerful potential coalition would unite disability rights, racial justice, gender equity, LGBTQ+ rights, and immigrant rights organizations around shared intersectional goals. This coalition has been partially realized in specific campaigns (e.g., opposition to Medicaid cuts, police reform) but has not been sustained as a structural alliance.
