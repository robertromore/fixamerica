# Intersectionality: Actions

## Overview

This document outlines concrete actions that individuals, organizations, and communities can take to advance intersectional disability equity. Actions are organized by actor type and effort level, from immediate individual steps to sustained organizational campaigns.

## Individual Actions

### For All Citizens

#### Immediate (Today)

- **Learn the basics**: Read Kimberle Crenshaw's foundational TED talk transcript or watch the video "The Urgency of Intersectionality" (18 minutes, free) to understand how identity axes interact
- **Listen to intersectional disability voices**: Follow disabled creators of color, LGBTQ+ disabled people, and immigrant disabled people on social media and read their work (see Resources section for starting points)
- **Examine your assumptions**: Reflect on whether your understanding of "disability" centers the experiences of white, physically disabled people and consider whose experiences you may be overlooking
- **Contact elected officials**: Ask your congressional representative whether they support recognizing intersectional discrimination in federal law and whether they support eliminating SSI asset limits

#### Short-Term (This Month)

- **Attend a disability justice event**: Sins Invalid, the Disability Visibility Project, and local disability justice organizations host virtual and in-person events centering intersectional perspectives
- **Read an intersectional disability account**: Start with *Disability Visibility* edited by Alice Wong, *Care Work* by Leah Lakshmi Piepzna-Samarasinha, or *Skin, Tooth, and Bone* by Sins Invalid
- **Support intersectional disability organizations**: Donate to organizations led by people of color, women, LGBTQ+ individuals, and immigrants with disabilities (National Black Disability Coalition, Sins Invalid, Ramp Your Voice!)
- **Report intersectional barriers**: When you observe a disability service, program, or organization that lacks language access, cultural responsiveness, or LGBTQ+ inclusion, communicate the concern in writing

#### Ongoing

- **Amplify intersectional disability voices**: Share content from disabled people of color, LGBTQ+ disabled people, and immigrant disabled advocates
- **Vote for candidates who address intersectional equity**: Evaluate candidates' positions on disability rights, racial justice, LGBTQ+ rights, and immigration reform together, not as separate issues
- **Support cross-movement campaigns**: When disability rights, racial justice, LGBTQ+ rights, or immigrant rights organizations collaborate on shared goals, participate and contribute
- **Challenge single-axis thinking**: When conversations about disability ignore race, or conversations about race ignore disability, raise the intersectional dimension

### For People with Disabilities

#### Asserting Your Rights

- **Document intersectional discrimination**: When you experience discrimination that involves both your disability and your race, gender, sexual orientation, or other identity, document the full experience including all identity factors. This documentation is essential for intersectional claims.
- **File complaints on multiple bases**: When filing a discrimination complaint with the EEOC, DOJ, or a state human rights agency, explicitly allege discrimination based on the combination of your identities, not just one. Even if the agency has not historically recognized intersectional claims, creating a record builds the case for reform.
- **Contact your state protection and advocacy organization**: Each state has a P&A system funded to assist with disability rights complaints. Ask whether they have experience with intersectional discrimination cases. Find yours at https://ndrn.org
- **Seek legal representation with intersectional expertise**: Organizations like DREDF, the ACLU, and Lambda Legal have experience with intersectional discrimination cases
- **Participate in data collection**: When disability surveys ask about your race, ethnicity, gender, sexual orientation, or language, answer these questions. Intersectional data depends on participation.

#### Advocacy and Leadership

- **Join organizations that reflect your full identity**: Seek out disability organizations that explicitly center intersectionality, or racial justice / LGBTQ+ / immigrant rights organizations that include disability
- **Serve on advisory bodies**: Many cities, states, and federal agencies have disability advisory committees. If these bodies lack diversity, advocate for more inclusive membership and apply for positions
- **Testify at public hearings**: Share your experience of intersectional disadvantage at city council, state legislature, or congressional hearings. Personal testimony on how disability intersects with race, gender, or immigration status is uniquely powerful.
- **Mentor emerging advocates**: If you have advocacy experience, mentor people at the intersections who are new to advocacy
- **Run for leadership positions**: Seek leadership roles in disability organizations, civil rights organizations, and political bodies where intersectional perspectives are underrepresented

### For People of Color, LGBTQ+ Individuals, and Immigrants Without Disabilities

#### Building Solidarity

- **Include disability in your analysis**: When advocating for racial justice, LGBTQ+ rights, or immigrant rights, explicitly include disability as an intersecting dimension
- **Make your events and spaces accessible**: Ensure that racial justice, LGBTQ+, and immigrant rights events, meetings, and materials are physically, communicatively, and programmatically accessible
- **Collaborate with disability organizations**: Seek out partnerships with disability rights and disability justice organizations on shared policy goals (Medicaid, housing, criminal justice, employment)
- **Educate your community about disability**: Challenge stigma around disability in your cultural and community contexts, and support community members with disabilities in accessing services

## Professional Actions

### For Disability Service Providers

#### Immediate

- **Assess your cultural responsiveness**: Evaluate whether your services are accessible and appropriate for people of diverse racial, ethnic, linguistic, gender, and sexual orientation backgrounds
- **Collect and review demographic data**: Track who is and is not accessing your services by race, ethnicity, language, gender, and (where appropriate) sexual orientation and gender identity
- **Provide language access**: Ensure intake, service planning, and ongoing services are available in the languages spoken by your service area population
- **Review hiring practices**: Assess whether your staff reflect the demographics of the communities you serve

#### Short-Term

- **Obtain cultural responsiveness training**: Pursue training on serving people of color with disabilities, LGBTQ+ people with disabilities, and immigrants with disabilities. Go beyond one-time training to sustained organizational change.
- **Build referral partnerships**: Establish formal relationships with racial justice organizations, LGBTQ+ service agencies, immigrant service organizations, and domestic violence agencies in your community
- **Revise person-centered planning**: Ensure your planning processes accommodate diverse cultural frameworks for disability, autonomy, family involvement, and decision-making
- **Create welcoming environments**: Review your physical spaces, marketing materials, and organizational culture for signals of inclusion or exclusion across racial, gender, LGBTQ+, and immigrant identities

#### Ongoing

- **Track intersectional outcomes**: Monitor service outcomes disaggregated by race, gender, language, and other demographics. Identify and address disparities.
- **Hire from the communities you serve**: Prioritize recruiting direct support professionals, service coordinators, and leaders who share racial, linguistic, and cultural backgrounds with service recipients
- **Advocate for policy change**: Support policy reforms that address the intersectional barriers your clients face, including benefit reform, language access, and anti-discrimination protections

### For Researchers

#### Immediate

- **Include intersectional analysis in your research design**: When studying disability, design research that captures race, gender, sexual orientation, immigration status, and other identity factors
- **Diversify your participant pool**: Actively recruit participants from communities of color, LGBTQ+ communities, immigrant communities, and rural communities
- **Cite intersectional scholarship**: Include work by scholars of color, disability justice scholars, and intersectional disability researchers in your literature reviews and citations

#### Short-Term

- **Seek intersectional research funding**: Apply for grants from NIH, NIDILRR, and foundations that specifically support intersectional disability research
- **Collaborate with community organizations**: Partner with organizations led by people at the intersections for community-based participatory research
- **Mentor researchers of color with disabilities**: Support the development of the next generation of intersectional disability researchers

#### Ongoing

- **Publish intersectional findings**: Report intersectional findings even when they are secondary to your primary research question. The data gap requires every researcher to contribute.
- **Advocate for research infrastructure**: Support funding for larger sample sizes that enable intersectional analysis, SOGI data collection in disability surveys, and community-based research partnerships

### For Attorneys and Legal Advocates

- **Bring intersectional claims**: When representing clients who face discrimination on multiple bases, plead intersectional theories explicitly. Even in jurisdictions that have not recognized such claims, building a record advances the law.
- **Cite intersectional precedent**: Reference *Lam v. University of Hawaii*, *Frappied v. Affinity Gaming*, and other intersectional decisions in briefs and arguments
- **Support law reform**: Advocate for legislative recognition of intersectional discrimination at federal and state levels
- **Provide legal education**: Train disability rights attorneys on intersectional discrimination theory and practice
- **Partner with civil rights organizations**: Collaborate with racial justice, LGBTQ+ rights, and immigrant rights legal organizations on intersectional cases

## Community and Organizational Actions

### For Disability Rights Organizations

- **Conduct an equity audit**: Assess your organization's board, leadership, staff, membership, and policy priorities for demographic representation and intersectional focus
- **Adopt an intersectional policy platform**: Explicitly commit to addressing racial, gender, LGBTQ+, and immigrant disparities within disability policy
- **Share power**: Create pathways for people of color, women, LGBTQ+ individuals, and immigrants with disabilities to lead your organization -- not just serve as tokens
- **Build cross-movement coalitions**: Establish formal partnerships with racial justice, gender equity, LGBTQ+ rights, and immigrant rights organizations
- **Advocate for intersectional data**: Push federal and state agencies to collect and publish intersectional disability data
- **Fund intersectional work**: Allocate organizational resources to intersectional initiatives, not just single-axis disability programs

### For Civil Rights and Social Justice Organizations

- **Include disability in your mission and work**: If your organization works on racial justice, gender equity, LGBTQ+ rights, or immigrant rights, explicitly include disability as an intersecting dimension
- **Make your organization accessible**: Ensure your offices, events, publications, and digital presence meet accessibility standards
- **Hire and promote people with disabilities**: Include disability in your diversity, equity, and inclusion commitments
- **Collaborate with disability organizations**: Seek out partnerships on shared policy goals and campaigns
- **Collect disability data**: Include disability status in your community needs assessments, membership surveys, and program evaluations

### For State and Local Government

- **Adopt intersectional data collection**: Require state agencies to collect and publish disability data disaggregated by race, ethnicity, gender, and language
- **Enact intersectional anti-discrimination laws**: Adopt state laws recognizing intersectional discrimination as a cognizable legal theory
- **Require cultural responsiveness in disability services**: Establish standards for culturally and linguistically appropriate disability services funded by the state
- **Create intersectional advisory bodies**: Establish advisory committees on disability that reflect the full diversity of the disability community
- **Fund intersectional programs**: Direct state disability funding to address documented disparities by race, gender, and other demographics

### For Foundations and Funders

- **Fund intersectional disability work**: Direct disability grants to organizations led by people of color, women, LGBTQ+ individuals, and immigrants with disabilities
- **Require intersectional analysis**: Make intersectional data collection and analysis a condition of disability-related grants
- **Support cross-movement work**: Fund collaborative projects between disability, racial justice, gender equity, LGBTQ+, and immigrant rights organizations
- **Invest in infrastructure**: Fund organizational capacity building, leadership development, and coalition infrastructure for intersectional disability work
