# Intersectionality: Root Causes

## Overview

The failure of disability policy to address intersectional disadvantage stems from interconnected structural, institutional, legal, and cultural causes. These root causes operate at multiple levels -- from the design of federal law to the practices of individual service providers -- and reinforce each other in ways that make intersectional inequality self-perpetuating.

## Structural Causes

### 1. Single-Axis Legal Architecture

American civil rights law was constructed along single axes of identity, creating legal protections that address one form of discrimination at a time but fail to capture compounded disadvantage.

- **Title VII** (1964): Race, color, sex, religion, national origin -- no disability
- **ADA** (1990): Disability -- no explicit race, sex, or other identity provisions
- **Section 504** (1973): Disability in federally funded programs -- no intersectional framework
- **Fair Housing Act** (1968): Race, color, sex, religion, national origin, familial status, disability -- separate protected classes, rarely analyzed together

**Why this matters**: A Black woman with a disability who is denied employment faces a legal system that can analyze her claim as race discrimination (Title VII), sex discrimination (Title VII), or disability discrimination (ADA) -- but struggles to recognize the unique discrimination she faces at the intersection of all three. Courts have been reluctant to recognize "intersectional" claims, forcing plaintiffs to choose a single axis.

**Why this persists**: Each civil rights statute was the product of a specific movement and political moment. Attempts to create unified anti-discrimination frameworks (like the proposed Equality Act) face resistance from constituencies that fear diluting existing protections.

### 2. Structural Racism in Health and Disability Causation

Racial disparities in disability prevalence are not natural variations but the predictable result of structural racism:

| Mechanism | How It Causes Disability | Racial Impact |
|-----------|-------------------------|---------------|
| Environmental racism | Toxic waste sites, lead paint, air pollution concentrated in communities of color | Black and Latino children have higher rates of lead poisoning, asthma, developmental disabilities |
| Occupational segregation | People of color concentrated in more dangerous jobs | Higher rates of work-related injuries and disabilities |
| Healthcare disparities | Less access to preventive care, chronic disease management | Untreated conditions progress to disability |
| Violence exposure | Higher rates of interpersonal and police violence | Traumatic brain injury, PTSD, physical disability |
| Chronic stress | Weathering effect of lifetime discrimination | Accelerated aging, higher rates of chronic disease |
| Maternal health disparities | Black women 3x more likely to die in childbirth | Higher rates of birth-related disabilities for both mothers and children |

### 3. Poverty as Compounding Mechanism

Poverty operates as a force multiplier for disability disadvantage, and the intersection of poverty with race, gender, and disability creates a vicious cycle:

- **Disability causes poverty**: Employment barriers, healthcare costs, benefit inadequacy
- **Poverty causes disability**: Environmental exposures, occupational hazards, healthcare gaps
- **Race causes poverty**: Wealth gap, employment discrimination, educational inequity
- **Race causes disability**: Structural racism mechanisms listed above
- **Gender causes poverty**: Wage gap, caregiving burden, occupational segregation
- **Poverty worsens disability outcomes**: Inadequate services, inaccessible housing, food insecurity

The intersection of all these factors means that a Black woman with a disability faces compounding disadvantages that cannot be addressed by targeting any single axis.

### 4. Immigration Policy as Exclusion Mechanism

Federal immigration policy systematically excludes immigrants with disabilities from services:

- **SSI exclusion**: Non-citizens are ineligible for SSI during their first 5 years of legal residence; undocumented immigrants are permanently excluded
- **"Public charge" rule**: Federal rule (rescinded 2021, but cultural impact persists) that receiving public benefits could jeopardize immigration status discourages service access
- **Medicaid exclusion**: Undocumented immigrants are excluded from Medicaid except for emergency services
- **Language barriers**: Disability services are overwhelmingly English-language; limited translation services
- **Documentation requirements**: Benefit applications require documentation (medical records, work history) that immigrants may not have

## Institutional Causes

### 5. Service System Design Around Dominant Identities

Disability service systems were designed around the needs of white, English-speaking, middle-class individuals with physical disabilities, and have not been fundamentally redesigned:

- **Person-centered planning**: Reflects Western individualistic values; may not align with collectivist cultural frameworks
- **Self-determination models**: Assume individual autonomy as the primary value; some cultures prioritize family or community decision-making
- **Service navigation**: Requires English literacy, internet access, and familiarity with bureaucratic systems
- **Provider workforce**: Direct support professionals and service coordinators are often culturally dissimilar from the people they serve
- **Geographic distribution**: Services concentrated in urban areas and predominantly white communities

### 6. Data Infrastructure Gaps

The absence of routine intersectional data analysis prevents evidence-based policy:

- **Single-dimension reporting**: Most disability data systems report disability status separately from race, gender, and other demographics
- **Missing categories**: Sexual orientation and gender identity data are absent from virtually all disability surveys
- **Small sample sizes**: When intersectional crossbreaks are available, small sample sizes limit analysis
- **Agency silos**: Disability data (CMS, DOE, DOJ, BLS) is collected by different agencies with different categories, making cross-agency intersectional analysis difficult
- **Research funding**: NIH and other research funders rarely require intersectional analysis in disability research

### 7. Disability Rights Movement Organizational Structure

The disability rights movement's own organizational structure has contributed to intersectional gaps:

- **Leadership demographics**: Major disability organizations have been disproportionately led by white, male, physically disabled individuals
- **Priority setting**: Organizational priorities (physical accessibility, ADA enforcement, employment rights) reflect leadership demographics
- **Funding patterns**: Foundation funding for disability rights flows disproportionately to white-led organizations
- **Board composition**: Boards of major disability organizations have historically lacked racial, gender, and LGBTQ+ diversity
- **Staff demographics**: Professional staff at disability organizations often do not reflect the diversity of the disability community

## Cultural and Attitudinal Causes

### 8. Intersecting Stigma Systems

People at the intersections face compounded stigma that is qualitatively different from single-axis stigma:

| Intersection | Compound Stigma |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Black + disabled | Perceived as both threatening (racial bias) and incompetent (disability bias); higher rates of police violence |
| Female + disabled | Perceived as both helpless (disability) and asexual/undesirable (gendered ableism); reproductive autonomy denied |
| LGBTQ+ + disabled | Perceived as both deviant (heteronormativity) and pitiable (ableism); identity dismissed as "confusion" caused by disability |
| Immigrant + disabled | Perceived as both unwelcome (nativism) and burdensome (ableism); framed as undeserving of services |
| Elderly + disabled | Perceived as both declining (ageism) and incapable (ableism); preferences dismissed as unrealistic |

### 9. Cultural Concepts of Disability

Different cultures understand disability differently, and the dominant American disability framework (rooted in Western medicine and rights-based advocacy) does not universally apply:

- Some cultures view disability through spiritual or moral frameworks that affect willingness to seek services
- Collectivist cultures may resist individualistic service models
- Stigma associated with disability varies across cultures, affecting disclosure and help-seeking
- The English-language concept of "disability" does not translate directly into many languages
- Immigration disrupts cultural support systems that might otherwise provide care

### 10. Research Bias

Academic disability research has historically centered white, middle-class experiences:

- The majority of disability research participants are white (reflecting both researcher networks and institutional review board practices)
- Intersectional disability research is disproportionately unfunded
- Researchers of color with disabilities face compounded barriers in academia
- Publication bias favors research on "mainstream" disability topics over intersectional analysis

## Interaction of Causes

These root causes create a reinforcing cycle:

1. Single-axis legal frameworks fail to capture intersectional discrimination
2. Without legal recognition, intersectional discrimination goes unremedied
3. Data systems do not track intersectional outcomes, so disparities remain invisible
4. Without data, policymakers cannot design targeted interventions
5. Service systems designed for dominant identities fail people at the intersections
6. The disability movement's own leadership gaps prevent internal course correction
7. Structural racism and sexism continue to cause disability disproportionately in marginalized communities
8. People at the intersections face the worst outcomes with the least voice in shaping solutions
