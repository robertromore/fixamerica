# Intersectionality: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to intersectional disability policy takes multiple forms: explicit resistance to equity-focused reforms, institutional inertia within organizations that benefit from single-axis approaches, ideological opposition to intersectional frameworks, and practical concerns about implementation complexity. Some opposition is principled, some is strategic, and some reflects discomfort with power-sharing and organizational change.

## Primary Opposition Forces

### Anti-"Identity Politics" Movement

**Who**: Conservative commentators, some libertarian thinkers, anti-DEI organizations, and politicians who oppose intersectional frameworks as divisive

**Arguments**:

1. **Intersectionality is divisive**: Categorizing people by multiple identity axes fragments society and undermines common citizenship
2. **Merit over identity**: Policy should be identity-blind, rewarding individual merit rather than compensating for group membership
3. **Slippery slope**: Intersectional analysis leads to an endless proliferation of identity categories, each demanding specific accommodations
4. **Anti-free speech**: Intersectionality creates a "victimhood hierarchy" that suppresses dissenting viewpoints
5. **Anti-American**: Intersectional frameworks are rooted in critical theory that rejects American founding principles

**Scale of influence**: Significant; anti-DEI legislation has been enacted in 20+ states affecting education, government contracting, and workforce training (National Conference of State Legislatures, 2025)

**Assessment**: This opposition is politically powerful but does not engage seriously with the empirical evidence of compounded disadvantage. The argument that policy should be "identity-blind" ignores the reality that existing policy already has disparate impacts based on identity. However, the framing of intersectionality as "divisive" resonates with many Americans who prefer universalist approaches.

### Institutional Resistance Within Disability Organizations

**Who**: Some leaders and staff within traditional disability rights organizations who resist intersectional analysis or equity-focused restructuring

**Arguments**:

1. **Mission focus**: Disability organizations should focus on disability, not take on race, gender, or LGBTQ+ issues that dilute their mission
2. **Coalition risk**: Explicitly aligning with racial justice or LGBTQ+ movements may alienate conservative allies and donors
3. **Competence concerns**: Disability organizations lack expertise in racial equity, gender equity, or LGBTQ+ inclusion
4. **Power dynamics**: Intersectional leadership requirements threaten existing leadership positions and organizational culture
5. **Universalism**: Disability is a universal experience; focusing on identity subgroups undermines the power of disability as a unifying category

**Assessment**: This resistance is partly principled (concern about mission dilution) and partly self-interested (resistance to power-sharing). The universalist argument for disability as a unifying identity has merit but ignores the empirical reality that disability outcomes vary dramatically by race, gender, and other factors. Organizations that claim to represent "all people with disabilities" while effectively serving primarily white, middle-class populations are not truly universalist.

### Fiscal Conservatives Opposed to Targeted Programs

**Who**: Budget hawks in Congress and state legislatures, some think tanks, taxpayer advocacy organizations

**Arguments**:

1. **Cost**: Intersectional approaches require additional data collection, targeted programs, cultural competency training, and multilingual services -- all of which cost money
2. **Duplication**: Adding intersectional requirements to existing programs creates bureaucratic overhead without proportional benefit
3. **Efficiency**: Universal programs serve more people at lower administrative cost than targeted programs
4. **Accountability**: Intersectional metrics are complex and difficult to measure, making program accountability harder

**Assessment**: Cost concerns are legitimate but must be weighed against the cost of failing to address disparities. Programs that ignore intersectional barriers often fail the populations they are designed to serve, wasting resources on interventions that do not reach those with the greatest need.

### Some Family Advocacy Organizations

**Who**: Family-led disability organizations, particularly in communities where cultural values conflict with some intersectional priorities

**Arguments**:

1. **Family authority**: Intersectional frameworks that emphasize individual self-determination may conflict with family-centered decision-making in some cultures
2. **Values conflicts**: Some families oppose LGBTQ+ inclusion components of intersectional disability policy on religious or cultural grounds
3. **Complexity resistance**: Families already navigating complex disability service systems resist additional requirements

### Immigration Restrictionists

**Who**: Organizations and politicians opposing immigration who also oppose extending disability benefits to immigrants

**Arguments**:

1. **Resources for citizens first**: Disability services should prioritize U.S. citizens before extending to immigrants
2. **Public charge**: Immigrants who need disability services are "public charges" who should not be admitted
3. **Border security**: Addressing immigrant disability needs diverts attention from border enforcement
4. **Fiscal burden**: Extending disability benefits to immigrants increases Medicaid and SSI costs

**Assessment**: These arguments treat disability services as a zero-sum resource competition rather than a human rights obligation. International law (including the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities, which the U.S. has not ratified) recognizes disability rights regardless of immigration status.

## Opposition Tactics

### Framing Strategies

| Tactic | How It Works | Counter-Argument |
|--------|-------------|------------------|
| "Identity politics" label | Frames intersectional analysis as divisive political ideology | Intersectionality is an empirical observation about how discrimination works, not an ideology |
| "All lives matter" universalism | Claims that focusing on specific groups ignores others | Universal programs consistently produce disparate outcomes; targeted attention is necessary |
| "Mission creep" accusation | Argues that disability organizations adding equity focus lose effectiveness | Organizations that ignore equity fail the majority of the population they claim to serve |
| Anti-DEI legislation | Bans or restricts diversity, equity, and inclusion programs in government and education | DEI restrictions prevent evidence-based approaches to documented disparities |
| "Complexity" argument | Claims intersectional approaches are too complex to implement | Simplicity that ignores reality is not efficiency; it is waste |

### Legislative Tactics

- **Anti-DEI bills**: Restrict equity-focused data collection, training, and programming in government agencies and educational institutions (enacted in 20+ states as of 2025)
- **Budget amendments**: Strip funding for intersectional data collection or targeted programs
- **Reporting requirements**: Impose burdensome reporting requirements on equity programs to make them administratively unsustainable
- **Block grant proposals**: Convert categorical programs to block grants, eliminating equity targeting provisions

### Institutional Tactics

- **Passive resistance**: Organizations agree to intersectional goals on paper but do not change leadership, priorities, or resource allocation
- **Tokenism**: Appoint individuals from marginalized groups to visible positions without sharing actual power or changing organizational culture
- **Data suppression**: Avoid collecting or publishing intersectional data that would reveal disparities
- **Gatekeeping**: Control funding and policy access to prevent intersectional advocates from influencing decisions

## Addressing Opposition

### Responding to "Identity Politics" Critiques

- Emphasize that intersectional analysis is evidence-based, not ideological: data shows compounded disadvantage is real and measurable
- Frame reforms in universalist language: "disability policy that works for everyone" requires addressing disparities
- Highlight fiscal arguments: programs that ignore intersectional barriers waste resources by failing to reach people with the greatest need
- Share individual stories: personal narratives of people at the intersections humanize abstract policy debates

### Addressing Organizational Resistance

- **Internal advocacy**: Support leaders of color, women, LGBTQ+ individuals, and immigrants within disability organizations
- **Accountability metrics**: Require organizations receiving federal funding to report demographic data on leadership, staff, and service recipients
- **Funding equity**: Redirect foundation and government funding toward organizations led by people at the intersections
- **Coalition building**: Build partnerships between disability organizations and racial justice, gender equity, and LGBTQ+ organizations

### Addressing Fiscal Concerns

- Demonstrate that intersectional approaches improve program effectiveness: targeted interventions reach the populations with the greatest unmet need
- Show cost savings from reduced emergency services, criminal justice involvement, and institutional placement when intersectional barriers are addressed
- Propose phased implementation with evaluation to demonstrate return on investment
- Frame as investment, not cost: addressing compounded disadvantage enables economic participation

### Addressing Immigration Concerns

- Emphasize that disability services for immigrants protect public health and community safety
- Highlight that many immigrants with disabilities are taxpayers, workers, and community members
- Frame access to basic services as a humanitarian obligation consistent with American values
- Demonstrate fiscal benefits of integration over exclusion
