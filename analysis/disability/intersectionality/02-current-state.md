# Intersectionality: Current State

## Racial and Ethnic Disparities

### Disability Prevalence by Race

Disability prevalence varies significantly by race and ethnicity, reflecting the cumulative impact of structural racism on health, exposure to environmental hazards, access to healthcare, occupational risks, and violence.

**Disability Prevalence by Race/Ethnicity (2024)**

| Race/Ethnicity | Disability Prevalence (Adults) | Compared to National Average (26%) | Source |
|----------------|-------------------------------|--------------------------------------|--------|
| Native American/Alaska Native | 33% | +7 percentage points | CDC DHDS (2024) |
| Black/African American | 31% | +5 percentage points | CDC DHDS (2024) |
| White (non-Hispanic) | 26% | Average | CDC DHDS (2024) |
| Hispanic/Latino | 20% | -6 percentage points | CDC DHDS (2024) |
| Asian American | 14% | -12 percentage points | CDC DHDS (2024) |

*Note: Hispanic/Latino and Asian American figures likely undercount due to lower survey response rates, language barriers, and cultural differences in disability identification.*

## Gender and Disability

### Prevalence and Economic Data

| Disability prevalence | 27% | 25% | +2 pp |
| Employment rate | 19.5% | 25.5% | -6 pp |

| Poverty rate | 28% | 22% | +6 pp |
| SSI/SSDI recipients | 52% | 48% | +4 pp |
| Uninsured rate | 11% | 10% | +1 pp |

*Sources: BLS Current Population Survey (2024); ACS (2023); SSA Annual Statistical Supplement (2024).*

### Violence Against Women with Disabilities

Women with disabilities experience violence at rates significantly higher than either women without disabilities or men with disabilities.

| Type of Violence | Women without Disabilities | Women with Disabilities | Ratio |
|------------------|---------------------------|------------------------|-------|
| Intimate partner violence (lifetime) | 25% | 40% | 1.6x |
| Sexual assault (lifetime) | 20% | 33% | 1.65x |
| Stalking (lifetime) | 15% | 25% | 1.67x |
| Reported to accessible DV services | N/A | 15% of DV shelters are accessible | -- |

*Sources: Bureau of Justice Statistics, "Crime Against Persons with Disabilities" (2024); National Network to End Domestic Violence (2024).*

### Reproductive Rights

- Women with disabilities face higher rates of forced or coerced sterilization, contraception, and abortion
- Many OB/GYN offices lack accessible examination tables and equipment
- Women with intellectual disabilities are disproportionately denied reproductive autonomy through guardianship
- Maternal mortality is 3x higher for women with physical disabilities (National Institutes of Health, 2023)
- Only 30% of medical schools include disability competency in OB/GYN training (AAMC, 2024)

## LGBTQ+ and Disability

### Prevalence

LGBTQ+ individuals experience disability at higher rates than the general population, likely due to minority stress, discrimination, violence exposure, and healthcare barriers.

| Population | Disability Prevalence | Source |
|------------|----------------------|--------|
| Heterosexual adults | 25% | Gallup/Williams Institute (2024) |
| LGB adults | 36% | Gallup/Williams Institute (2024) |
| Transgender adults | 39% | U.S. Transgender Survey (2023) |
| Non-binary adults | 42% | U.S. Transgender Survey (2023) |

### Compounded Barriers

| Barrier | Impact |
|---------|--------|
| Service provider bias | LGBTQ+ people with disabilities report being turned away from or mistreated in disability services, group homes, and institutional settings |
| Lack of culturally competent services | Disability service providers rarely receive LGBTQ+ competency training; LGBTQ+ service providers rarely have disability competency |
| Healthcare discrimination | Transgender people with disabilities face compounded barriers to healthcare, including denial of transition-related care and disability accommodations simultaneously |
| Homelessness | 40% of LGBTQ+ homeless youth have a disability; shelters often inaccessible and unwelcoming to both identities |
| Family rejection | LGBTQ+ people with disabilities who depend on family for care may face rejection when coming out, losing both housing and caregiving |
| Data invisibility | Most disability surveys do not collect sexual orientation or gender identity data |

*Sources: True Colors United (2024); National LGBTQ Task Force (2024); U.S. Transgender Survey (2023).*

## Immigration and Disability

### Population

An estimated 4-6 million immigrants in the United States have disabilities, including lawful permanent residents, refugees, asylum seekers, temporary visa holders, and undocumented individuals.

### Benefit Exclusions

| Immigration Status | SSI Eligibility | SSDI Eligibility | Medicaid Eligibility | HCBS Waiver Access |
|-------------------|----------------|------------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| U.S. citizen | Yes | Yes (with work history) | Yes | Yes |
| LPR (first 5 years) | No | Yes (with work history) | Varies by state | Varies by state |
| LPR (after 5 years) | Yes | Yes (with work history) | Yes | Yes |
| Refugee/asylee | Yes (7 years) | Yes (with work history) | Yes | Yes |
| Undocumented | No | No | Emergency only | No |
| DACA recipients | No | Limited | Varies by state | Varies by state |
| TPS holders | No | Limited | Varies by state | Varies by state |

*Source: National Immigration Law Center (2024).*

### Additional Barriers

| Barrier | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Language access | Disability services often unavailable in languages other than English; ASL interpretation limited for deaf immigrants who use other sign languages |
| "Public charge" fear | Immigrants avoid seeking disability services for fear of being deemed a "public charge" and jeopardizing immigration status |
| Documentation requirements | Disability benefit applications require documentation (medical records, work history) that immigrants may not have |
| Deportation fear | Undocumented individuals with disabilities avoid any contact with government agencies, including healthcare and disability services |
| Cultural concepts of disability | Disability is understood differently across cultures; Western medical model may not align with immigrants' frameworks |
| Institutional placement | Immigrants with disabilities are disproportionately placed in institutions because community-based services are unavailable to them |

*Sources: Migration Policy Institute (2024); National Immigration Law Center (2024).*

## Poverty and Disability

### The Bidirectional Relationship

Disability causes poverty (through employment barriers, healthcare costs, and benefit inadequacy) and poverty causes disability (through environmental exposures, occupational hazards, inadequate healthcare, and stress).

**Poverty Rates by Disability Status and Other Identities (2023)**

| Group | Poverty Rate |
|-------|-------------|
| All Americans | 12% |
| People without disabilities | 10% |
| All people with disabilities | 25% |
| White people with disabilities | 22% |
| Black people with disabilities | 33% |
| Hispanic people with disabilities | 30% |
| Native American people with disabilities | 38% |
| Women with disabilities | 28% |
| Single mothers with disabilities | 42% |
| LGBTQ+ people with disabilities | 30% (estimated) |
| Children with disabilities | 22% |

*Sources: ACS (2023); Supplemental Poverty Measure data (2023).*

### Asset Limits and the Poverty Trap

SSI's asset limit ($2,000 for individuals, $3,000 for couples) traps people with disabilities in poverty by preventing savings. This disproportionately affects people of color with disabilities who have fewer family wealth resources to draw upon.

- Black families have 1/8 the median wealth of white families (Federal Reserve Survey of Consumer Finances, 2023)
- A Black person with a disability faces both the disability poverty trap and the racial wealth gap
- ABLE accounts (2014) allow limited tax-advantaged savings but have low participation rates among people of color (ABLE National Resource Center, 2024)

## Legal Framework for Intersectional Discrimination

### Current Protections

| Law | Protected Class | Intersectional Coverage |
|-----|----------------|------------------------|
| ADA | Disability | Does not explicitly address intersectional discrimination |
| Title VII | Race, color, sex, religion, national origin | Does not include disability; courts reluctant to recognize intersectional claims |
| Section 504 | Disability (federally funded programs) | No explicit intersectional framework |
| Fair Housing Act | Race, color, sex, religion, national origin, familial status, disability | Separate protected classes; limited intersectional analysis |
| IDEA | Disability (education) | Requires disproportionality analysis by race but enforcement is weak |

### Judicial Treatment of Intersectional Claims

Courts have been inconsistent in recognizing intersectional discrimination claims:

| Approach | Description | Prevalence |
|----------|-------------|------------|
| "Single-axis" analysis | Courts analyze race and disability discrimination separately, ignoring their interaction | Majority approach |
| Intersectional recognition | Courts recognize that discrimination based on the combination of race and disability is distinct from either alone | Growing but minority |
| "Sex-plus" doctrine | Courts recognize compound claims (e.g., race + disability) as a distinct form of discrimination | Developing |

**Key cases:**

- *DeGraffenreid v. General Motors* (1976): Rejected intersectional claim by Black women, establishing single-axis framework
- *Lam v. University of Hawaii* (9th Cir. 1994): Recognized intersectional discrimination claim (race + sex)
- *Frappied v. Affinity Gaming* (9th Cir. 2020): Applied intersectional analysis to compound discrimination claim

### Data Collection Gaps

### Active Initiatives

| Initiative | Focus | Status |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| EEOC intersectionality guidance | Recognizing compound discrimination claims | Under development (2025) |
| CMS equity plan | Addressing racial disparities in Medicaid/Medicare | Implementing |

### Key Advocacy Organizations

- **National Black Disability Coalition**: Addresses racial disparities in disability policy
- **Disability Rights Education and Defense Fund**: Intersectional legal advocacy
- **National Council on Independent Living**: Community living with equity focus
- **Sins Invalid**: Disability justice with intersectional framework centering people of color and LGBTQ+ people with disabilities
- **National Hispanic Council on Disabilities**: Latino disability advocacy
- **National Asian Pacific Center on Aging**: Asian American disability and aging issues
- **Disability Justice movement**: Intersectional disability activism led by Patty Berne, Mia Mingus, and others

## Key Takeaways

1. **Disability disparities compound with race**: Black and Native American people with disabilities face the worst outcomes across every measurable dimension
2. **Gender amplifies disability disadvantage**: Women with disabilities earn less, experience more violence, and face compounded reproductive rights barriers
3. **LGBTQ+ disability data is invisible**: Most disability surveys do not collect sexual orientation or gender identity data
4. **Immigrants face benefit exclusions**: Immigration status determines whether a person with a disability can access basic services
5. **Legal frameworks are inadequate**: Courts and statutes treat discrimination along single axes, failing to recognize compounded disadvantage
6. **Poverty creates a trap**: Disability causes poverty and poverty causes disability, with the cycle worst for people of color
7. **Data infrastructure is insufficient**: Routine intersectional analysis is rare, preventing evidence-based policy
8. **The disability rights movement itself has equity gaps**: Leadership and organizational priorities have not consistently centered people at the intersections
