# Intersectionality: Overview

## Executive Summary

Intersectionality -- the concept that social identities including disability, race, gender, sexual orientation, age, immigration status, and socioeconomic class interact to create distinct experiences of privilege and oppression -- is foundational to understanding disability policy in America. People with disabilities are not a monolithic group. A Black Deaf woman, a low-income white veteran with PTSD, an undocumented immigrant with a developmental disability, and a wealthy elderly man with mobility impairments each encounter fundamentally different barriers, service systems, and life outcomes despite all being "people with disabilities."

Disability policy that ignores intersectionality fails the people it is designed to serve. The Americans with Disabilities Act treats disability as a single axis of identity, creating legal protections that often work well for white, middle-class, English-speaking individuals with physical disabilities but fail those whose identities place them at the intersection of multiple systems of marginalization. Data consistently shows that people of color with disabilities experience worse outcomes across every measurable dimension: employment, income, education, healthcare, housing, criminal justice involvement, and life expectancy. Women with disabilities face compounded gender and disability discrimination. LGBTQ+ individuals with disabilities encounter service systems designed around heteronormative and cisgender assumptions. Immigrants with disabilities face barriers to basic services, including language access, immigration enforcement fear, and exclusion from benefit programs.

Addressing intersectionality in disability policy requires more than adding diversity language to existing programs. It demands structural redesign: data systems that collect and analyze intersectional data, funding formulas that account for compounding disadvantage, enforcement mechanisms that recognize intersectional discrimination, culturally responsive service delivery, and policy development led by people who live at the intersections. Without this transformation, disability policy will continue to serve some while leaving others behind.

- **LGBTQ+ and disability**: How heteronormativity and cisnormativity compound disability barriers

## Core Tensions

### Universal Design vs. Targeted Intervention

Disability policy faces a fundamental tension between universal approaches (designing systems that work for everyone) and targeted interventions (creating programs for specific intersectional populations). Universal design principles suggest that accessibility improvements benefit all; targeted approaches recognize that some groups face unique barriers requiring specific responses. Both are necessary, but resource allocation between them is contested.

### Identity Politics vs. Broad Coalitions

Intersectional analysis can strengthen coalitions by identifying shared interests across movements (disability rights, racial justice, gender equity, LGBTQ+ rights). But it can also fragment coalitions when identity-specific demands compete for limited resources or political attention. The disability rights movement has historically been led by white, physically disabled men; incorporating intersectional perspectives requires power-sharing that challenges existing organizational structures.

### Data Collection vs. Privacy

Effective intersectional analysis requires granular data on race, gender, sexual orientation, disability type, and other identity factors. But collecting such data raises privacy concerns, particularly for marginalized communities wary of government surveillance. The tension between data-driven policy and privacy protection is especially acute for undocumented immigrants with disabilities and LGBTQ+ individuals in unsupportive communities.

### Cultural Competence vs. Cultural Autonomy

Some disability service models (e.g., person-centered planning, self-determination) reflect Western, individualistic cultural values that may not align with collectivist cultures where family and community decision-making is preferred. Designing culturally responsive services requires navigating between imposing dominant cultural frameworks and respecting diverse approaches to disability, caregiving, and autonomy.

## Vision of Success

An intersectional approach to disability policy would mean that a person's race, gender, sexual orientation, immigration status, language, or socioeconomic class does not predict the quality of disability services they receive, the discrimination they face, or the outcomes they experience. Data systems would routinely collect and analyze intersectional information, enabling evidence-based interventions tailored to compounded disadvantage. Legal frameworks would recognize and remedy intersectional discrimination, not just discrimination along single axes. Disability services would be culturally responsive, linguistically accessible, and designed by the communities they serve. The disability rights movement would reflect the full diversity of the disability community, with leadership and decision-making shared by people of color, women, LGBTQ+ individuals, immigrants, and others who have been historically marginalized within the movement. Disparities in employment, income, education, healthcare, housing, and criminal justice between subgroups of people with disabilities would narrow measurably and continuously.
