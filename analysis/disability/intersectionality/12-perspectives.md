# Intersectionality: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes intersectional disability policy through nine distinct political perspectives, evaluating current state assessments, root cause analyses, proposed solutions, and legislation. Each perspective receives engagement consistency ratings and position scores, culminating in compromise proposals that bridge multiple viewpoints. Intersectionality in disability policy is one of the most politically polarized subtopics in the disability domain, as it sits at the nexus of disability rights, racial justice, gender equity, LGBTQ+ rights, and immigration reform -- issues that divide along predictable ideological lines. However, specific proposals (intersectional data collection, culturally responsive services, SSI asset limit elimination) generate more nuanced cross-partisan responses than the overarching "intersectionality" framing might suggest.

| Populist | Anti-elite, pro-worker, economic nationalism | Trade protection, anti-monopoly, immigration restriction |

## Perspective Analyses

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 2/5 - Low Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | States opposition as principled concern about "identity politics" and government overreach, but underlying motivation is often resistance to acknowledging racial disparities in systems conservatives defend. Willing to address veteran disability disparities but resistant to racial framing. |
| Principle consistency | Individual responsibility principle is applied inconsistently: accepts that disabled veterans deserve special support but resists the idea that Black people with disabilities face compounded systemic barriers requiring targeted intervention. |
| Goalpost stability | Positions have shifted from ignoring intersectionality entirely to actively opposing it through anti-DEI legislation. The goalposts have moved from "this is not a real problem" to "addressing it is divisive." |
| Zero-sum behavior | Frames intersectional analysis as inherently zero-sum: attention to racial disparities in disability is framed as taking resources from disability programs generally. |

**Justification:** Conservative engagement is low consistency because the underlying objection -- that structural racism compounds disability disadvantage -- challenges narratives about American meritocracy and limited government that are central to conservative identity. Veterans and aging constituencies with disabilities are served while people of color with disabilities are not, revealing that the objection is to the racial equity framing rather than to disability support per se.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Anti-DEI legislation enacted in 20+ states directly restricts the kind of intersectional analysis proposed. Heritage Foundation and similar organizations have published critiques of intersectionality as an ideology.
- Conservative members of Congress have supported SSI reform for veterans while opposing immigration-based SSI expansions.
- Some conservative governors have supported disability service improvements framed as efficiency rather than equity.

#### Position Scores

| Dimension | Score | Reasoning |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Current State Assessment | 3/10 | Disputes the framing that racial disparities in disability outcomes are caused by structural racism. Attributes disparities to individual factors, cultural differences, or poverty rather than systemic discrimination. Accepts some data on disparities but rejects the causal analysis. |
| Root Cause Agreement | 2/10 | Fundamentally rejects the intersectionality framework as an explanatory model. Views single-axis legal architecture as appropriate, not as a problem. Opposes the premise that disability policy should be redesigned around identity categories. |

**Source references:**

## Compromise Proposals

### Compromise 1: Intersectional Disability Data Infrastructure

**Bridges:** Liberal + Centrist + Conservative (partial) + Populist (partial)

**Core Agreement:** All perspectives accept that better data on disability outcomes is valuable, even if they disagree on the analytical framework. Data collection can be framed as program effectiveness measurement rather than intersectional equity analysis.

**Policy Description:**

Mandate federal agencies to publish disability program outcomes disaggregated by race, ethnicity, gender, age, and language. Frame as "program effectiveness data" demonstrating whether disability services reach all eligible populations. Include privacy protections and opt-out provisions for sensitive categories. Fund expanded survey sample sizes for meaningful analysis.

**Concern Mapping:**

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The intersectional data infrastructure + SSI asset limit elimination combination has the broadest potential coalition, with support from liberal, progressive, centrist, and democratic socialist perspectives and partial openings from conservative and populist perspectives when appropriately framed. These proposals can advance through a combination of executive action (data) and bipartisan legislation (asset limits).

### Key Obstacles

The primary obstacle is the anti-DEI political environment, which has made "intersectionality" itself a politically toxic term in many contexts. The substantive proposals (data collection, service quality, poverty reduction) have broader support than the framing suggests. Advocates must navigate the gap between the concept's analytical power and its political toxicity.

A secondary obstacle is the LGBTQ+ inclusion dimension: the intersectional framework's centering of LGBTQ+ people with disabilities creates a hard barrier for Religious Right and some Conservative and Populist engagement. This is not merely a framing issue; it reflects a genuine values conflict.

### Low Consistency Partners

Conservative, Populist, and Religious Right perspectives (all rated 2/5 consistency) are the most unpredictable and require the most careful engagement. Their positions are driven by coalition pressures and cultural politics more than by consistent principle, making them both harder to predict and potentially movable when the right frame is found.

### Recommended Approach

1. **Lead with data and effectiveness**: Intersectional data collection framed as program effectiveness measurement can be advanced through executive action with minimal political opposition.
2. **Pursue SSI asset limit reform**: Bipartisan support exists and the reform disproportionately benefits people of color with disabilities without requiring racial equity framing.
3. **Build culturally responsive services incrementally**: Voluntary standards and grants can establish infrastructure that evolves toward mandatory requirements.
4. **Advance legal reform when political conditions allow**: Intersectional discrimination recognition requires a favorable political window but builds on growing judicial recognition of intersectional claims.
5. **Separate immigration provisions from other reforms**: Immigration-related disability service expansion faces the strongest opposition and should be pursued through administrative action and state policy rather than bundled with broader reforms.
