# Intersectionality: History

## Overview

The history of intersectionality in disability policy is a story of parallel movements that have only recently begun to converge. The disability rights movement, the civil rights movement, the feminist movement, and LGBTQ+ liberation each developed largely separate frameworks for understanding oppression, with only occasional cross-pollination. The concept of intersectionality -- coined by legal scholar Kimberle Crenshaw in 1989 -- provided a theoretical framework for understanding how these systems interact, but translating that framework into disability policy and practice has been slow and incomplete.

## Timeline

### Pre-Civil Rights Era (Pre-1960s)

**Separate and Unequal Institutionalization**

- State institutions for people with disabilities were racially segregated throughout the Jim Crow era
- Black people with disabilities were placed in the worst-funded, most overcrowded facilities
- *Buck v. Bell* (1927): Eugenics-based forced sterilization disproportionately targeted Black women and women of color with disabilities
- Over 60,000 forced sterilizations, disproportionately affecting Black, Native American, and Latina women with disabilities (1907-1970s)
- Native American children with disabilities removed from families and placed in boarding schools or institutions at rates far exceeding white children

**Exclusion from Benefits**

- Social Security Act (1935) initially excluded agricultural and domestic workers -- occupations disproportionately held by Black and Latino Americans -- from disability benefits
- State vocational rehabilitation programs were racially segregated; Black people with disabilities received inferior services
- Workers' compensation systems provided lower benefits to Black workers, including those who acquired disabilities on the job

### Civil Rights and Disability Rights Parallel Movements (1960s-1980s)

**1960s-1970s: Separate Tracks**

- Civil Rights Act of 1964 prohibited race discrimination but did not include disability
- Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act (1973) prohibited disability discrimination in federally funded programs but did not address race
- Education for All Handicapped Children Act (1975) mandated special education but did not address racial disproportionality
- The disability rights movement was overwhelmingly white and physically disabled; the civil rights movement did not center disability
- Ed Roberts and the independent living movement at UC Berkeley drew inspiration from civil rights but operated separately

**Key tensions:**

- Black disability activists (such as Donald Galloway and Brad Lomax) participated in both movements but found neither fully addressed their experience
- The 504 sit-ins (1977) in San Francisco succeeded partly because of support from the Black Panthers, who provided food to disabled protesters -- a rare moment of cross-movement solidarity
- Feminist movement of the 1960s-1970s largely excluded women with disabilities from leadership and agenda-setting

**1980s: Early Intersectional Awareness**

- Audre Lorde's work on intersecting oppressions (race, sex, class, sexuality) began to influence disability thinking, though Lorde did not center disability until her own cancer experience
- Emergence of disability studies as an academic field, initially dominated by white scholars
- Growing recognition that Black and Latino children were disproportionately placed in special education and institutionalized
- *Larry P. v. Riles* (9th Cir. 1984): Court found that IQ tests used for special education placement were racially biased, disproportionately labeling Black children as "educable mentally retarded"

### The Intersectionality Framework Emerges (1989-2000s)

**1989: Crenshaw's Foundational Work**

- Kimberle Crenshaw publishes "Demarginalizing the Intersection of Race and Sex" in the *University of Chicago Legal Forum*, coining the term "intersectionality"
- Crenshaw's analysis of *DeGraffenreid v. General Motors* (1976) shows how courts failed Black women by treating race and sex discrimination as separate categories
- The framework provides theoretical tools for understanding disability + race, disability + gender, and other compound experiences

**1990s: ADA and Its Limitations**

- ADA (1990) is enacted as a single-axis disability rights law; its drafters do not incorporate intersectional analysis
- ADA's definition of disability and its enforcement mechanisms work best for individuals who face disability discrimination alone
- People of color with disabilities, women with disabilities, and LGBTQ+ people with disabilities find that ADA enforcement does not fully address their experiences
- Patricia Hill Collins's *Black Feminist Thought* (1990) and other intersectional scholarship begin to influence disability studies
- Olmstead v. L.C. (1999): The landmark case involved two Black women with intellectual disabilities and mental illness institutionalized in Georgia -- their race shaped their institutional experience, but the legal analysis focused solely on disability

**2000s: Disability Studies Engages Intersectionality**

- Nirmala Erevelles and other scholars of color begin publishing on race and disability intersection
- Chris Bell's "Introducing White Disability Studies" (2006) critiques the whiteness of disability studies scholarship
- Disability justice movement emerges, explicitly centering intersectionality, led by queer and trans people of color with disabilities
- Sins Invalid performance project (founded 2006) centers disability and sexuality through an intersectional lens
- Growing data on racial disparities in disability outcomes begins to inform policy advocacy

### Disability Justice and Modern Intersectionality (2010s-Present)

**2010s: The Disability Justice Framework**

- Patty Berne, Mia Mingus, Leroy Moore, Stacey Milbern, and others articulate the disability justice framework as an explicitly intersectional alternative to the disability rights framework
- Disability justice principles include: intersectionality, leadership of those most impacted, anti-capitalism, cross-movement solidarity, wholeness, and sustainability
- Black Lives Matter movement (2013-present) increasingly incorporates disability: many police killings involve people with disabilities, disproportionately Black men with mental illness
- #DisabilitySoWhite (2016) social media campaign highlights racial exclusion in disability advocacy
- Sandy Ho, Vilissa Thompson (Ramp Your Voice!), and other advocates of color with disabilities gain national platforms
- Federal data systems begin to publish more disability-by-race crossbreaks, though gaps remain large

**2020s: Convergence and Conflict**

- COVID-19 pandemic disproportionately kills people of color with disabilities in nursing homes and other institutional settings
- Disability justice framework gains mainstream attention within disability advocacy
- Some traditional disability rights organizations face internal reckoning on race and inclusion
- Executive Order 13985 (2021): "Advancing Racial Equity and Support for Underserved Communities" directs federal agencies to address equity, including for people of color with disabilities
- CMS Equity Plan (2022) begins to address racial disparities in Medicaid/Medicare
- EEOC begins developing intersectional discrimination guidance
- Growing scholarship on LGBTQ+ disability, immigrant disability, and other intersections
- Tensions emerge between traditional disability rights focus (ADA enforcement, HCBS expansion) and disability justice focus (anti-capitalism, abolition, decolonization)

## Key Historical Patterns

### Pattern 1: Separate Movements, Shared Opponents

The disability rights movement, civil rights movement, feminist movement, and LGBTQ+ movement have historically operated in parallel, sometimes in solidarity but rarely in structural alliance. Yet the systems they oppose -- ableism, racism, sexism, heteronormativity -- are interconnected and mutually reinforcing. Progress in one movement without attention to the others leaves people at the intersections behind.

### Pattern 2: Law Follows Single Axes

American civil rights law has been constructed along single axes of identity: Title VII covers race and sex, the ADA covers disability, Title IX covers sex in education. This legal architecture makes intersectional discrimination claims difficult to bring and harder to win. Each law was a hard-fought victory, but the cumulative result is a patchwork that does not address compound disadvantage.

### Pattern 3: Data Lags Behind Experience

People at the intersections of disability and other marginalized identities have always known their experiences were distinct. But data systems have only recently begun to capture these experiences through intersectional analysis. The absence of data has been used to deny the existence of disparities and resist targeted interventions.

### Pattern 4: Leadership Reflects Privilege

The disability rights movement has been disproportionately led by white, male, physically disabled individuals. This leadership pattern shaped the movement's priorities (physical accessibility, employment rights) and left other concerns (racial disparities in institutionalization, gender-based violence against women with disabilities, LGBTQ+ inclusion) as secondary issues.

### Pattern 5: Grassroots Innovation Outpaces Policy

The disability justice framework -- developed by queer, trans, and people of color with disabilities -- represents the most sophisticated intersectional analysis of disability in American history. But this framework has had limited impact on federal and state policy, which continues to treat disability as a single-axis issue.
