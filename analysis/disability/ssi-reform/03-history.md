# SSI Reform: History

## Origins

### Pre-SSI Disability Assistance

Before SSI's creation, assistance for disabled and aged individuals with limited income was provided through three separate federal-state programs:

- **Old-Age Assistance (OAA)**: Established by the Social Security Act of 1935, provided state-administered, federally matched grants to needy elderly individuals
- **Aid to the Permanently and Totally Disabled (APTD)**: Established in 1950, provided similar assistance to disabled adults
- **Aid to the Blind (AB)**: Established in 1935 as part of the original Social Security Act

These programs produced extreme variation in benefits, eligibility, and administration across states. In 1973, monthly benefits ranged from under $100 in Mississippi to over $300 in California. Eligibility standards varied so widely that a person eligible in one state might be categorically ineligible in another. The programs were widely criticized for their inconsistency, inadequacy, and administrative dysfunction.

### Creation of SSI (1972)

The Social Security Amendments of 1972 (Public Law 92-603) created the Supplemental Security Income program, effective January 1, 1974. SSI federalized the patchwork of state programs under a single national standard administered by SSA. Key features of the original program:

- **National benefit floor**: $130/month for individuals, $195/month for couples (1974)
- **National eligibility standard**: Uniform definition of disability, income, and resource limits
- **Federal administration**: SSA assumed responsibility from state welfare agencies
- **State supplementation**: States could (and were encouraged to) supplement federal payments
- **Mandatory supplementation**: States required to supplement to prevent benefit reduction for those previously receiving state assistance

The original resource limits were $1,500 for individuals and $2,250 for couples.

### Design Assumptions

SSI's original design reflected several assumptions that have proven problematic:

- **Disability as permanent total incapacity**: The program assumed most disabled recipients would never work, making employment-related provisions an afterthought
- **Family as primary support**: Deeming rules assumed that spouses and parents should support disabled family members, reducing the public obligation
- **Minimal savings needed**: Asset limits reflected an assumption that recipients needed no financial cushion beyond monthly benefits
- **Stability of circumstances**: The monthly accounting system assumed relatively stable income and living arrangements, poorly suited to the reality of fluctuating circumstances

## Key Milestones

### 1974-1980: Implementation and Early Challenges

- **1974**: SSI begins with approximately 3.2 million recipients transferred from state programs; implementation problems include delayed payments, lost records, and confusion among former state program recipients
- **1976**: Congress passes provisions for retrospective monthly accounting and the one-third reduction rule for ISM, adding complexity that would plague the program for decades
- **1977**: First major adjustment to resource limits (raised to $1,500 individual, $2,250 couple from original lower amounts that had been superseded)

### 1980s: Expansion and Retrenchment

- **1980**: Social Security Disability Amendments mandate continuing disability reviews (CDRs) for SSI and SSDI, leading to mass terminations
- **1981-1984**: Reagan administration's accelerated CDRs terminate hundreds of thousands of SSI and SSDI recipients, many with severe mental illness; public backlash and court orders force reversal
- **1984**: Disability Benefits Reform Act restores many terminated beneficiaries, requires consideration of combined impairments, and establishes moratorium on mental impairment CDRs pending new standards
- **1987**: Congress establishes Section 1619(a) and (b), allowing SSI recipients to continue receiving Medicaid even while working above the SGA level -- a critical work incentive
- **1989**: Resource limits raised to current levels: $2,000 (individual) and $3,000 (couple). These have not been updated since.

### 1990s: Children's SSI and Welfare Reform

- **1990**: *Sullivan v. Zebley*, 493 U.S. 521 (1990): Supreme Court rules SSA's childhood disability standard is too restrictive, requiring individualized functional assessments for children. SSI childhood rolls expand significantly.
- **1993-1996**: Political backlash against childhood SSI growth; media coverage focuses on alleged "coaching" of children to appear disabled; Congressional investigations
- **1996**: Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act (welfare reform):
    - Eliminates SSI eligibility for most legal immigrants (partially restored in 1997)
    - Replaces *Zebley* standard for children with stricter "marked and severe functional limitations" standard
    - Eliminates drug addiction and alcoholism as qualifying disabilities
    - Mandates representative payees for beneficiaries with drug/alcohol conditions
    - Result: Approximately 135,000 children lose SSI benefits

### 2000s: Administrative Erosion

- **2001**: SSI modernization proposals surface but gain no legislative traction
- **2005**: SSA begins implementing technology upgrades for SSI processing but progress is slow due to legacy systems
- **2008**: Financial crisis increases SSI applications as people exhaust savings and lose jobs; SSA is ill-equipped to handle the surge
- **2009**: American Recovery and Reinvestment Act provides one-time $250 payment to SSI recipients but no structural reform

### 2010s: Incremental Progress

- **2010**: Affordable Care Act expands Medicaid in many states, partially deinking healthcare coverage from SSI eligibility but creating new administrative complexity
- **2014**: Achieving a Better Life Experience (ABLE) Act passes, creating tax-advantaged savings accounts that do not count as SSI resources (limited to individuals with disability onset before age 26)
- **2015**: Bipartisan Budget Act of 2015 addresses SSDI Trust Fund but ignores SSI
- **2017**: SSI Restoration Act introduced by Sen. Brown (D-OH) and Rep. Grijalva (D-AZ); would raise benefits to poverty level; no hearing held

### 2020s: Renewed Attention

- **2020-2021**: COVID-19 pandemic exposes extreme vulnerability of SSI recipients; pandemic stimulus payments counted as resources, initially threatening SSI eligibility until SSA issued guidance excluding them
- **2021**: American Rescue Plan provides expanded Child Tax Credit, but SSI families face complications with interactions between CTC and SSI deeming rules
- **2022**: SECURE 2.0 Act raises ABLE account age-of-onset limit from 26 to 46 (effective 2026); bipartisan accomplishment but narrow in scope
- **2023**: SSI Savings Penalty Elimination Act introduced with bipartisan support; would raise asset limits to $10,000/$20,000; no floor vote
- **2024**: SSA announces administrative changes to simplify food-related ISM rules; implementation timeline uncertain
- **2025**: SSI Restoration Act reintroduced in 119th Congress; SSI Savings Penalty Elimination Act also reintroduced; prospects depend on budget negotiations

## Lessons from History

### What Has Worked

- **Section 1619(b)**: Medicaid protection for working SSI recipients has been the single most effective work incentive in the program, allowing beneficiaries to earn above SGA without losing healthcare
- **ABLE accounts**: Despite limitations, ABLE accounts have provided a meaningful savings vehicle for eligible individuals; as of 2024, over 170,000 ABLE accounts held more than $1.7 billion in assets
- **State supplementation**: States with substantial supplements demonstrate that higher benefits are administratively feasible and improve outcomes

### What Has Failed

- **Asset limits as policy tool**: The frozen asset limits serve no rational policy purpose; they do not meaningfully target benefits and impose enormous administrative and human costs
- **ISM rules**: The attempt to count in-kind support as income has generated administrative complexity far exceeding any savings; SSA itself has sought to simplify these rules
- **Incremental legislative approach**: Decades of small, isolated reforms have not addressed SSI's fundamental structural problems; piecemeal changes have added complexity without improving adequacy

### Recurring Patterns

- **Crisis-driven attention**: SSI receives legislative attention primarily during crises (welfare reform backlash, pandemic) rather than through systematic review
- **Bipartisan frustration, partisan gridlock**: Members of both parties acknowledge SSI's problems but cannot agree on the scope or cost of solutions
- **Administrative atrophy**: SSA's capacity to administer SSI has declined continuously regardless of which party controls Congress or the White House
- **Beneficiary invisibility**: SSI recipients are among the most politically marginalized populations in the country, lacking the organized advocacy presence of SSDI recipients (who have work history and perceive benefits as "earned")
