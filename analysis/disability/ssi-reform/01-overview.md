# SSI Reform: Overview

## Executive Summary

Supplemental Security Income (SSI) is the federal means-tested income support program for aged, blind, and disabled individuals with limited income and resources. Administered by the Social Security Administration (SSA), SSI provides monthly cash benefits to approximately 7.4 million recipients as of December 2024, including roughly 4.6 million disabled adults, 1.1 million disabled children, and 1.7 million aged individuals. Unlike Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI), which is an earned benefit funded by payroll taxes, SSI is funded from general revenues and serves as a last-resort safety net for people who have little or no work history or whose disabilities prevent them from accumulating sufficient work credits.

The program is profoundly broken. The maximum federal SSI benefit in 2025 is $967 per month for an individual -- approximately 75% of the federal poverty level. No person living exclusively on SSI income can afford basic necessities in any housing market in the United States. The program's asset limits, unchanged in real terms since 1989, cap countable resources at $2,000 for individuals and $3,000 for couples, effectively prohibiting beneficiaries from saving for emergencies, building any financial cushion, or planning for the future. These limits penalize marriage between SSI recipients (a couple receives less than two individuals living separately), creating a de facto "marriage penalty" that has been criticized by disability rights organizations, faith communities, and fiscal conservatives alike.

SSI's administrative complexity rivals its inadequacy. The program's byzantine income-counting rules distinguish between "earned" and "unearned" income, apply different exclusions to each, and impose reporting requirements that trip up beneficiaries and create overpayment debts that can take years to resolve. An estimated $5.3 billion in SSI overpayments were outstanding as of FY 2024, much of it attributable to the program's labyrinthine reporting requirements rather than fraud. Meanwhile, SSA's capacity to administer the program has been hollowed out by decades of flat or declining administrative budgets, resulting in long wait times for applications, delayed processing of changes in circumstances, and inadequate quality review of eligibility determinations.

SSI reform is simultaneously a disability rights issue, a poverty reduction imperative, and an administrative efficiency challenge. The program's outdated design traps millions of Americans in deep poverty while consuming disproportionate administrative resources to enforce rules that serve no rational policy purpose. Modernizing SSI requires updating benefit levels, raising or eliminating asset limits, simplifying income-counting rules, eliminating the marriage penalty, and investing in the administrative infrastructure needed to deliver benefits accurately and efficiently.

## Core Tensions

### Adequacy vs. Cost

The SSI benefit is too low to support basic needs, yet raising it to the poverty level would cost an estimated $10-15 billion annually. Advocates argue the human cost of inadequate benefits -- homelessness, malnutrition, untreated medical conditions -- ultimately costs more through emergency services and institutionalization. Fiscal conservatives counter that expanding benefits without structural reform invites dependency and waste.

### Simplicity vs. Targeting

SSI's complex income-counting and resource rules exist to ensure benefits go to the "truly needy" and adjust to individual circumstances. But this complexity generates enormous administrative costs, creates opportunities for error (driving overpayments), and imposes compliance burdens on beneficiaries with cognitive, mental health, and physical disabilities who are least equipped to navigate bureaucratic systems. Simplification could reduce targeting precision but would improve access and reduce errors.

### Work Incentives vs. Program Integrity

SSI's income rules create steep effective marginal tax rates on earnings, discouraging work. The earned income exclusion phases out benefits at a rate that, combined with loss of Medicaid and other benefit interactions, can produce effective marginal tax rates exceeding 100%. Expanding work incentives risks criticism that the program is paying people who can work, while the current structure traps beneficiaries in poverty.

### Individual Rights vs. Family Obligations

SSI's "deeming" rules count a portion of a spouse's or parent's income and resources against the SSI applicant, reflecting an assumption that family members should support disabled relatives. Critics argue deeming penalizes marriage and family formation, imposes hardship on families with limited resources, and reflects outdated assumptions about family obligation that do not account for the costs of disability.

## Vision of Success

A reformed SSI program would provide benefits at or above the federal poverty level, ensuring that no person with a significant disability lives in destitution. Asset limits would be raised to at least $10,000 (individual) and $20,000 (couple) or eliminated entirely, allowing beneficiaries to save for emergencies and plan for the future. The marriage penalty would be eliminated so that couples receive at least the combined amount two individuals would receive. Income-counting rules would be simplified, with a single earned income exclusion and elimination of in-kind support and maintenance as a category. Overpayments would be prevented through real-time data matching and simplified reporting, and any overpayments that do occur would be recovered through reasonable, hardship-sensitive procedures. SSA would be adequately funded to administer the program with dignity, accuracy, and speed. People with disabilities would no longer be forced to choose between financial survival and marriage, savings, or work.
