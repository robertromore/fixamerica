# SSI Reform: Root Causes

## Why SSI Benefits Remain Below the Poverty Line

### Political Marginalization of Beneficiaries

SSI recipients are among the most politically powerless populations in the United States. Unlike Social Security retirement or SSDI beneficiaries, who perceive their benefits as "earned" through payroll tax contributions and who belong to powerful advocacy organizations (AARP, disability advocacy groups with employed members), SSI recipients:

- Have no payroll tax contribution narrative to invoke ("I paid into this system")
- Are disproportionately poor, non-white, and undereducated, limiting political engagement
- Include a large share of individuals with cognitive, intellectual, and psychiatric disabilities who face barriers to political participation
- Are served by advocacy organizations that prioritize SSDI and ADA enforcement over SSI reform
- Cannot credibly threaten electoral consequences because their voting rates are among the lowest of any demographic group

### The "Welfare" Frame

SSI is funded from general revenues, not a dedicated trust fund. This makes it politically indistinguishable from other "welfare" programs in the minds of most legislators and the public, subjecting it to the same stigma and political vulnerability that characterizes TANF, SNAP, and Medicaid:

- **Deservingness narratives**: Public support for disability benefits correlates with perceptions of "deserving" recipients; SSI's means-tested nature invites scrutiny of whether recipients are "truly" disabled or "truly" poor
- **Fraud narratives**: Media coverage of SSI overwhelmingly focuses on fraud and abuse, despite fraud rates consistently below 1% of program expenditures
- **Budget competition**: General revenue funding means SSI competes with all other discretionary and mandatory spending in budget negotiations

### Cost Sensitivity Without Cost Context

Raising SSI to the federal poverty level would cost an estimated $10-15 billion annually. This figure is routinely cited as prohibitive, without context:

- Federal spending on corporate tax expenditures exceeds $150 billion annually
- The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act reduced revenue by approximately $1.5 trillion over 10 years
- State and federal spending on homelessness, emergency room visits, and institutionalization attributable to SSI inadequacy is not netted against the cost of benefit increases

## Why Asset Limits Have Not Been Updated

### Legislative Inertia

Updating asset limits requires affirmative legislative action. In the absence of a crisis or political champion, inertia favors the status quo:

- No automatic indexing mechanism was included in the 1989 legislation
- Each subsequent year makes the gap larger but also makes the cost of updating larger, creating a vicious cycle
- The issue lacks salience with the general public, who are largely unaware that asset limits exist

### False Precision of Means-Testing

Asset limits serve a symbolic function: they demonstrate that the program is "targeted" to those with the least resources. Policymakers are reluctant to raise limits because:

- Any specific number ($5,000, $10,000, $50,000) invites the question "why not higher/lower?"
- Elimination of asset limits is perceived as converting SSI from a targeted program to an entitlement, which many legislators oppose on principle
- The administrative savings from eliminating asset limits (reduced redetermination workload, fewer overpayments) are diffuse and hard to attribute, while the "cost" of allowing recipients to have savings is easily calculable

### CBO Scoring Dynamics

The Congressional Budget Office scores changes to SSI asset limits as increasing program costs because more people would be eligible. This creates a budget obstacle even though:

- Many currently-eligible people are deterred from applying by the asset test
- Administrative savings from simpler eligibility rules would offset some costs
- Downstream savings (reduced emergency services, reduced institutionalization) are outside CBO's scoring window

## Why Income Rules Are So Complex

### Accretive Complexity

SSI's income-counting rules were not designed as a coherent system. They accumulated through decades of legislative amendments, regulatory interpretations, SSA policy decisions, and court orders, each adding a new exception, exclusion, or special rule without simplifying the underlying structure:

- The earned income exclusion ($65 + 50% reduction) dates from 1972
- ISM rules were modified multiple times through regulation and litigation
- PASS (Plan to Achieve Self-Support) was added as a work incentive but creates its own administrative burden
- Student earned income exclusion, Impairment-Related Work Expenses, and other carve-outs each serve legitimate purposes but add layers of complexity

### Program Integrity vs. Beneficiary Experience

SSA and Congressional oversight committees have historically prioritized program integrity (preventing overpayments and ineligible receipt) over beneficiary experience. This priority ordering produces:

- Rules designed to capture every dollar of income, no matter how small or irregular
- Reporting requirements that assume beneficiaries can track and communicate complex financial information
- A presumption of potential fraud that imposes surveillance and documentation burdens
- Administrative systems optimized for detection and recovery rather than prevention and simplification

### Fear of the Marginal Case

Legislators resist simplification because they fear the marginal case: the hypothetical person who would receive benefits under a simpler system but who "shouldn't." This fear:

- Is amplified by media coverage of outlier cases
- Overweights Type I errors (paying someone who shouldn't receive benefits) relative to Type II errors (denying benefits to someone who should receive them)
- Ignores the aggregate cost of complexity (administrative burden, overpayments, beneficiary hardship)

## Why the Marriage Penalty Persists

### Historical Design Logic

When SSI was created, the couple rate was set lower than double the individual rate based on the assumption that two people living together share housing costs and achieve "economies of scale." This logic:

- Mirrors the treatment of married couples in tax and other benefit programs
- Assumes that marriage reduces per-person living costs proportionally
- Ignores the additional costs of disability, which do not decrease with cohabitation
- Fails to account for the fact that SSI benefits are already below the poverty level, making any reduction catastrophic

### Political Difficulty of Expansion

Eliminating the marriage penalty would cost an estimated $2-3 billion annually. While relatively modest in the context of federal spending, this:

- Requires affirmative legislation
- Is scored by CBO as an increase in mandatory spending
- Cannot be framed as "fixing a problem" in traditional budget terms (it is a benefit increase)
- Faces opposition from those who view SSI as already too generous

### Lack of Champion

The marriage penalty has been criticized by every major disability rights organization, multiple conservative think tanks (on family values grounds), faith communities, and academic researchers. Yet no sustained legislative campaign has focused specifically on this issue because:

- The affected population is small and politically weak
- The issue is overshadowed by larger SSI reform debates
- Advocates are divided on whether to pursue the marriage penalty in isolation or as part of comprehensive reform

## Why SSA Cannot Administer SSI Effectively

### Chronic Underfunding

SSA's administrative budget has been effectively flat or declining in real terms for over a decade. Congress appropriates less than SSA requests and less than is needed to maintain current service levels:

| Fiscal Year | SSA Admin Budget Request | Congressional Appropriation | Gap |
|-------------|--------------------------|----------------------------|-----|
| 2018 | $12.5 billion | $12.4 billion | -$100 million |
| 2020 | $13.0 billion | $12.8 billion | -$200 million |
| 2022 | $14.2 billion | $13.3 billion | -$900 million |
| 2024 | $15.2 billion | $14.2 billion | -$1.0 billion |

### Legacy Technology

SSA's core SSI processing systems run on COBOL-based mainframe platforms dating from the 1980s. Modernization efforts have been repeatedly underfunded, delayed, and restarted:

- Multiple GAO reports have identified SSA's IT infrastructure as high-risk
- The systems cannot easily accommodate rule changes, leading to manual workarounds that increase error rates
- Real-time data matching (which could prevent overpayments) requires modern systems that SSA does not have

### SSI's Disproportionate Administrative Burden

SSI consumes a disproportionate share of SSA's administrative resources relative to its share of total beneficiaries:

- SSI accounts for roughly 15% of SSA beneficiaries but an estimated 30-40% of administrative workload
- Means-testing (income verification, asset checking, living arrangement determination) is labor-intensive
- Frequent changes in beneficiary circumstances require ongoing case management
- Overpayment detection, calculation, notification, and recovery each consume staff time

## Structural vs. Incremental Failure

The root causes of SSI's dysfunction are not isolated problems with individual rules. They reflect a structural failure: the program was designed in the early 1970s based on assumptions about disability, poverty, family, and administration that no longer hold. Incremental reforms over five decades have added complexity without addressing the foundational design:

- Disability is now understood as a spectrum, not a binary
- Poverty is deeper and housing costs are higher relative to benefits than in 1974
- Family structures have changed; the nuclear family model underlying deeming rules is less prevalent
- Technology could enable real-time income verification and benefit adjustment, but SSA lacks the systems
- The administrative state model of paper applications, in-person interviews, and manual calculations is obsolete

No amount of tinkering with individual rules will produce a functional SSI program. Comprehensive reform is required.
