# SSI Reform: Current State

## The Problem Today

### Benefits Below the Poverty Line

The maximum federal SSI benefit for 2025 is $967 per month for an individual and $1,450 for an eligible couple. The federal poverty level for a single individual is $1,255 per month (2025 HHS guidelines), meaning SSI benefits reach only 77% of the poverty threshold. This is not a recent development -- SSI benefits have never reached the federal poverty level since the program's inception in 1974. The gap has widened in real terms as cost-of-living adjustments (COLAs) are calculated using the Consumer Price Index for Urban Wage Earners and Clerical Workers (CPI-W), which does not reflect the spending patterns of people with disabilities, who face higher costs for medical care, transportation, personal assistance, and specialized housing.

Twenty-six states and the District of Columbia provide state supplementation payments that raise total benefits above the federal level, but these supplements vary dramatically:

| State Category | Number of States | Supplement Range (Individual) |
|----------------|-----------------|------------------------------|
| No state supplement | 24 states | $0 |
| Minimal supplement (<$50/month) | 8 states | $10-$49 |
| Moderate supplement ($50-$200/month) | 12 states | $50-$199 |
| Substantial supplement (>$200/month) | 6 states + DC | $200-$450+ |

California provides the largest supplement, bringing total SSI payments to approximately $1,182 per month for an independent living individual (2025), still below the federal poverty level in a state where median rent exceeds $2,000. Even in the most generous states, SSI recipients cannot afford market-rate housing without additional subsidies.

### Frozen Asset Limits

The SSI resource limit is $2,000 for an individual and $3,000 for a couple. These limits have not been updated since 1989. Had they been adjusted for inflation using the CPI, they would be approximately $5,200 and $7,800 respectively in 2025 dollars. The limits were already considered inadequate in 1989; their real value has eroded by more than 60% over three decades.

The asset test excludes certain resources -- a primary home, one vehicle, household goods, life insurance with face value under $1,500, and burial funds up to $1,500 -- but the list of exclusions has not kept pace with modern financial instruments or reality. Retirement savings accounts such as 401(k)s and IRAs are counted as resources, meaning an SSI applicant who accumulated any retirement savings before becoming disabled must spend them down to $2,000 before becoming eligible. ABLE (Achieving a Better Life Experience) accounts, created by Congress in 2014, allow limited tax-advantaged savings without affecting SSI eligibility, but the annual contribution limit ($18,000 for 2025) and total account cap (state 529 limits, often $300,000-$500,000) provide only partial relief, and ABLE accounts are available only to individuals whose disability onset occurred before age 46.

The practical consequences of the asset limit are severe:

- **No emergency savings**: A single unexpected expense -- car repair, appliance replacement, medical bill -- can trigger a crisis for someone with no financial buffer
- **No financial planning**: Beneficiaries cannot save for deposits, moving costs, or any future expense exceeding $2,000
- **Constant financial surveillance**: SSA conducts periodic redeterminations that review bank accounts, and even temporary excess resources (such as receiving an insurance settlement or inheritance) can trigger suspension of benefits
- **Perverse behavior**: Beneficiaries report spending money on unnecessary purchases at the end of each month to stay under the resource limit, a practice that is both economically wasteful and psychologically damaging

### The Marriage Penalty

SSI's treatment of married couples constitutes one of the most criticized features of any federal benefit program. The maximum federal benefit for an eligible couple ($1,450/month in 2025) is less than double the individual rate ($967 x 2 = $1,934). Two disabled individuals living separately each receive $967 ($1,934 combined); if they marry, their combined benefit drops to $1,450 -- a loss of $484 per month, or $5,808 per year.

The marriage penalty extends beyond the cash benefit. When two SSI recipients marry:

- Their combined asset limit is $3,000 (versus $2,000 + $2,000 = $4,000 if unmarried)
- Each spouse's income is "deemed" to the other, potentially reducing or eliminating benefits
- Housing assistance, SNAP benefits, and other means-tested programs may also be affected

The result is a powerful financial incentive against marriage. Disability rights advocates have documented cases of couples maintaining separate residences, declining to formalize relationships, and even divorcing to preserve benefits. This penalty falls disproportionately on people with intellectual and developmental disabilities who may not fully understand the financial implications of marriage but are encouraged by support networks and personal preferences to form committed relationships.

### Income-Counting Complexity

SSI's income rules are among the most complex in any federal program:

- **Earned income**: Subject to a $65 monthly exclusion plus a 50% reduction rate (every $2 earned reduces benefits by $1 after the exclusion)
- **Unearned income**: Subject to a $20 monthly general exclusion, then reduces benefits dollar-for-dollar
- **In-kind support and maintenance (ISM)**: If someone else pays for a beneficiary's food or shelter, SSA treats this as unearned income, reducing benefits by up to one-third of the federal benefit rate plus $20 (the "presumed maximum value" rule) or the actual value of the support, whichever is less
- **Deeming**: Income and resources of ineligible spouses, parents (for children under 18), or sponsors (for certain immigrants) are partially counted against the SSI applicant

The ISM rules are particularly problematic. If a parent allows an adult disabled child to live rent-free, SSA treats the shelter as income and reduces benefits. If a friend buys groceries for a beneficiary, SSA treats the food as income. These rules discourage informal support networks, punish families who help disabled relatives, and generate enormous administrative burden as SSA attempts to track and value every instance of in-kind assistance.

### Overpayments and Debt

SSI overpayments represent a systemic failure of program administration. As of FY 2024, approximately $5.3 billion in SSI overpayments were outstanding. The causes are structural:

- **Reporting lag**: Beneficiaries must report changes in income, resources, and living arrangements, but processing delays mean SSA often continues paying at the old rate for months after a change occurs
- **Complexity-driven errors**: The income-counting rules are so complex that neither beneficiaries nor SSA staff consistently apply them correctly
- **Retrospective calculation**: SSI uses a retrospective monthly accounting system that can take months to reconcile, generating overpayments that accumulate before detection
- **Wage reporting delays**: Employer wage data reaches SSA through quarterly tax filings, creating a built-in delay of 3-9 months

When overpayments are detected, SSA recovers them by withholding 10% of monthly benefits (the default rate), creating additional financial hardship for beneficiaries already living far below the poverty line. Beneficiaries can request a waiver of recovery if they were not at fault and recovery would defeat the purpose of the program or be against equity and good conscience, but the waiver process is itself burdensome and the approval rate is inconsistent across SSA offices.

### Administrative Capacity Crisis

SSA's ability to administer SSI has deteriorated steadily:

| Metric | 2010 | 2024 | Change |
|--------|------|------|--------|
| SSA full-time employees | 67,000 | 57,000 | -15% |
| Field offices | 1,342 | 1,230 | -8% |
| SSI redetermination rate | ~35% of cases/year | ~20% of cases/year | -43% |
| Average SSI initial processing time | ~90 days | ~130 days | +44% |

The decline in redetermination rates is particularly significant for program integrity. Redeterminations are the periodic reviews of SSI recipients' financial eligibility (income, resources, living arrangements). When SSA cannot conduct them regularly, changes in circumstances go undetected, overpayments accumulate, and ineligible individuals continue receiving benefits. GAO has estimated that each dollar spent on redeterminations saves $4-7 in program costs, making the decline in redetermination rates a fiscal failure as well as an administrative one (GAO-23-106178, 2023).

## Impact

### On Beneficiaries

- **Poverty**: The majority of SSI recipients live below or near the poverty line; the benefit structure guarantees poverty
- **Housing instability**: SSI benefits cover less than 50% of fair market rent for a studio apartment in most metropolitan areas and many rural areas
- **Food insecurity**: Despite automatic SNAP eligibility in most states, 35-40% of SSI recipients report food insecurity (USDA Food Security Survey, 2024)
- **Health consequences**: Poverty-level income limits access to nutritious food, safe housing, and disability-related expenses not covered by Medicaid
- **Social isolation**: The marriage penalty and ISM rules discourage formation of relationships and acceptance of informal support
- **Psychological harm**: Constant financial surveillance, overpayment recovery, and the requirement to remain impoverished impose documented mental health costs

### On Families

- **Caregiver financial strain**: Families providing care and support to SSI recipients receive no compensation and may be penalized (through ISM rules) for providing housing or food
- **Childhood disability**: The age-18 redetermination, which applies the adult disability standard to children aging out of childhood SSI, results in approximately 35-40% of children losing benefits upon turning 18
- **Family asset depletion**: Parents of disabled children must limit their own savings (through deeming) while the child is under 18

### On the Economy

- **Work disincentive costs**: Only 4% of disabled adult SSI recipients work, compared to much higher employment rates among comparable populations in countries with graduated benefit systems
- **Administrative waste**: The complexity of SSI rules consumes disproportionate SSA staff time, with SSI cases accounting for a larger share of workload than their share of total beneficiaries
- **Downstream costs**: Inadequate SSI benefits increase costs in emergency rooms, homeless shelters, and institutional settings

## Recent Developments

### Legislative Proposals (118th-119th Congress)

- **SSI Savings Penalty Elimination Act**: Would raise asset limits to $10,000 (individual) and $20,000 (couple); reintroduced in 119th Congress with bipartisan support
- **SSI Restoration Act**: Would raise benefit levels to the federal poverty level, raise asset limits, eliminate ISM rules, eliminate marriage penalty, and update income exclusions; estimated cost of $30+ billion annually
- **ABLE Age Adjustment Act**: Signed into law in December 2022 as part of the SECURE 2.0 Act, raising the age-of-onset requirement for ABLE accounts from 26 to 46, effective 2026

### Administrative Actions

- SSA announced plans in 2024 to simplify ISM rules, specifically the food-related ISM counting, though implementation has been delayed
- SSA updated its technology systems for SSI processing but continues to rely on legacy IT infrastructure for core eligibility calculations
- Congressional appropriations for FY 2025 included a modest increase in SSA's administrative budget, partially reversing years of decline but insufficient to restore pre-2010 service levels

### Judicial Developments

- Multiple federal courts have addressed SSI overpayment recovery procedures, with several finding SSA's collection practices inadequate in protecting due process rights
- Ongoing litigation challenging SSA's interpretation of "holding out" rules for unmarried couples who cohabitate
