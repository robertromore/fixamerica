# SSI Reform: Solutions

## Solution 1: Raise Benefits to the Federal Poverty Level

### Description

Increase the maximum federal SSI benefit to 100% of the federal poverty level (FPL) for individuals and 150% of FPL for couples (approximating two times the individual amount). Index benefits annually to the higher of the CPI-W or the Supplemental Poverty Measure threshold.

### How It Works

- **Individual benefit**: Increase from $967/month to approximately $1,255/month (2025 FPL)
- **Couple benefit**: Increase from $1,450/month to approximately $1,882/month (150% FPL)
- **Annual adjustment**: Apply COLA based on the larger of CPI-W or SPM threshold change, ensuring benefits do not erode relative to actual living costs

### Evidence

- The National Council on Disability has recommended raising SSI to the FPL since 2005 (NCD, *The Social Security Administration's Efforts to Promote Employment for People with Disabilities*, 2005)
- Center on Budget and Policy Priorities analysis shows that raising SSI to FPL would lift approximately 3.3 million people out of poverty (CBPP, 2024)
- Comparable programs in Canada (Ontario Disability Support Program), Australia (Disability Support Pension), and the United Kingdom (Personal Independence Payment + Employment and Support Allowance) provide benefits at or above the poverty level

### Estimated Cost

- $10-15 billion annually (depending on interaction effects with state supplements and other programs)
- CBO score would reflect gross cost; net cost is lower when accounting for reduced use of emergency services, homeless shelters, and institutional care

### Challenges

- Largest cost item in any SSI reform package
- Requires either new revenue or mandatory spending offsets under budget rules
- State supplement interaction: some states may reduce supplements if federal benefits increase, partially offsetting gains

### Implementation

- Phase in over 3-5 years to manage budget impact
- Require maintenance of effort from states that currently supplement
- Include inflation indexing from year one to prevent future erosion

## Solution 2: Modernize Asset Limits

### Description

Raise the SSI resource limit to $10,000 for individuals and $20,000 for couples, with automatic annual inflation indexing. Alternatively, eliminate asset limits entirely.

### How It Works

- **Immediate increase**: From $2,000/$3,000 to $10,000/$20,000
- **Annual indexing**: Adjust limits each January based on CPI-U
- **Exclusion updates**: Add retirement accounts (401(k), IRA), ABLE accounts without separate cap, and one additional vehicle to exclusion list
- **Alternative**: Full elimination of asset test (evaluate income only)

### Evidence

- SSI Savings Penalty Elimination Act (bipartisan) proposes $10,000/$20,000 limits
- GAO has recommended updating asset limits (GAO-23-106178)
- Multiple analyses show administrative savings from simpler asset rules would partially offset increased eligibility costs
- No OECD country with a comparable disability program maintains asset limits as low as SSI's

### Estimated Cost

- **$10,000/$20,000 limits**: $2-4 billion annually (CBO preliminary estimate)
- **Full elimination**: $6-10 billion annually
- **Net cost**: Lower when administrative savings are included (fewer redeterminations, fewer overpayments, simpler processing)

### Challenges

- CBO scores additional eligible individuals as new spending
- Some legislators view any asset limit increase as a slippery slope to elimination
- Transition issues for current beneficiaries who have structured finances around the $2,000 limit

### Implementation

- Effective immediately upon enactment (no phase-in needed for increases)
- SSA systems modification required but relatively straightforward compared to other reforms
- Automatic indexing prevents future legislative inaction

## Solution 3: Eliminate the Marriage Penalty

### Description

Set the SSI couple benefit at 200% of the individual rate (i.e., two individual benefits) and set the couple resource limit at 200% of the individual limit.

### How It Works

- **Couple benefit**: $967 x 2 = $1,934/month (2025 rates), up from $1,450
- **Couple resource limit**: $2,000 x 2 = $4,000 (or $10,000 x 2 = $20,000 if asset limits are also reformed)
- **Eliminate "holding out"**: Remove the rule that treats unmarried cohabitating couples as married for SSI purposes

### Evidence

- National Council on Disability recommended elimination of the marriage penalty (NCD, 2011)
- GAO has documented the marriage penalty's effects on beneficiaries (GAO-14-494R)
- The marriage penalty has no equivalent in SSDI, Social Security retirement, or most other federal programs
- "Economies of scale" justification is empirically unsupported at SSI benefit levels

### Estimated Cost

- $2-3 billion annually
- Relatively modest compared to other SSI reforms

### Challenges

- Requires legislation; cannot be accomplished administratively
- Some legislators view the couple rate reduction as legitimate economies-of-scale policy
- Interactions with other programs (SNAP, housing) that also have couple penalty issues

### Implementation

- Straightforward legislative change
- Effective with next COLA calculation after enactment
- Minimal systems impact (simple rate table change)

## Solution 4: Simplify Income-Counting Rules

### Description

Replace SSI's Byzantine income-counting system with a simplified structure: a single earned income exclusion, elimination of in-kind support and maintenance (ISM) as a category, and simplified deeming rules.

### How It Works

- **Unified earned income exclusion**: Exclude the first $400/month of earned income (up from $65), then reduce benefits by $1 for every $2 earned (maintain 50% phase-out rate)
- **Eliminate ISM for food**: No longer count food provided by others as income (SSA has already proposed this administratively)
- **Simplify shelter ISM**: Replace the presumed maximum value rule with a flat deduction or eliminate shelter ISM entirely
- **Reform deeming**: Reduce the share of spouse/parent income deemed to the SSI applicant; raise deeming thresholds

### Evidence

- SSA itself has acknowledged the need for ISM simplification (SSA, Notice of Proposed Rulemaking, 2024)
- Administrative cost of ISM enforcement exceeds the benefit savings from the rule (CBPP analysis, 2023)
- The earned income exclusion has not been updated since 1972; inflation-adjusting it to 2025 would yield approximately $400

### Estimated Cost

- **ISM elimination**: $1-2 billion annually (higher benefits for those currently subject to ISM reductions)
- **Earned income exclusion increase**: $500 million-$1 billion (higher benefits for working recipients)
- **Administrative savings**: $500 million-$1 billion annually (reduced processing time, fewer errors, fewer overpayments)

### Challenges

- ISM elimination requires legislation or regulation (SSA can modify ISM rules administratively but full elimination may require statutory change)
- Some overpayments are prevented by current ISM rules; elimination requires alternative safeguards
- Earned income exclusion increase must be carefully designed to avoid creating new cliff effects

### Implementation

- ISM food simplification: administrative action (underway)
- ISM shelter simplification: regulation or legislation
- Earned income exclusion: legislation
- Deeming reform: legislation
- 18-24 month implementation period for SSA systems changes

## Solution 5: Modernize Overpayment Prevention and Recovery

### Description

Shift from a detection-and-recovery model to a prevention-first approach, using real-time data matching, simplified rules, and humane recovery procedures.

### How It Works

- **Real-time wage verification**: Connect SSI systems to IRS wage data, state unemployment insurance databases, and financial institution data for real-time verification
- **Prospective monthly accounting**: Replace retrospective accounting with prospective calculations that adjust benefits in real time based on reported income
- **Overpayment threshold**: Do not establish overpayments below $100 (administrative cost exceeds recovery)
- **Recovery reform**: Limit recovery to 5% of monthly benefits (down from 10%), extend hardship waiver criteria, and allow repayment plans of up to 10 years
- **Prevention incentives**: Reward accurate reporting rather than punishing errors

### Evidence

- Real-time wage verification has been demonstrated in other countries (Australia's Centrelink system) and in U.S. programs (EITC advance payment verification)
- GAO has recommended real-time data matching for SSI (GAO-21-341)
- SSA OIG estimates that $1.5-2 billion in overpayments could be prevented annually through better data matching

### Estimated Cost

- **IT investment**: $500 million-$1 billion (one-time system modernization)
- **Annual savings**: $1.5-2 billion (reduced overpayments)
- **Net**: Budget-positive within 2-3 years

### Challenges

- SSA's legacy IT systems require major overhaul to support real-time processing
- Privacy concerns about expanded data matching
- Transition period will be disruptive
- Financial institution data sharing requires new legal authority

### Implementation

- Phase 1 (Years 1-2): Legislation and system design
- Phase 2 (Years 2-4): Pilot real-time verification in select states
- Phase 3 (Years 4-6): National rollout
- Phase 4 (Ongoing): Continuous improvement

## Solution 6: Invest in SSA Administrative Capacity

### Description

Provide SSA with adequate, stable, and predictable funding to administer SSI effectively, including hiring, technology, and training.

### How It Works

- **Staffing**: Authorize and fund hiring to restore SSA workforce to 67,000+ FTEs
- **Technology**: Dedicated SSI modernization fund of $500 million over 5 years
- **Training**: Mandatory training on SSI rules for all claims representatives
- **Field offices**: Halt closures and reopen offices in underserved areas
- **Mandatory funding**: Shift SSA's administrative budget from discretionary to mandatory appropriations to ensure stable, predictable funding

### Evidence

- Every dollar spent on SSI redeterminations saves $4-7 (GAO-23-106178)
- SSA workforce has declined 15% since 2010 while caseloads have grown
- Multiple GAO and OIG reports have identified SSA understaffing as a primary cause of processing delays and error rates

### Estimated Cost

- **Additional annual administrative funding**: $2-3 billion above current levels
- **One-time technology investment**: $500 million over 5 years
- **Net return**: Positive, through reduced overpayments, faster processing, and better program integrity

### Challenges

- Congressional appropriators resistant to mandatory administrative funding
- SSA has struggled to execute technology modernization even when funded
- Hiring at scale takes 2-3 years to produce fully trained staff
- Shifting to mandatory funding reduces Congressional control

## Recommended Reform Package

The following reforms, implemented together, would transform SSI from a poverty-guaranteeing program into a functional safety net:

1. **Raise benefits to FPL** ($10-15 billion/year)
2. **Raise asset limits to $10,000/$20,000** ($2-4 billion/year)
3. **Eliminate marriage penalty** ($2-3 billion/year)
4. **Simplify income rules** ($1-2 billion/year gross; offset by administrative savings)
5. **Modernize overpayment systems** (budget-positive after initial investment)
6. **Fund SSA administration** ($2-3 billion/year; generates net savings)

**Total gross cost**: $17-27 billion/year
**Total net cost** (accounting for savings): $12-20 billion/year
**Context**: Less than 0.5% of the federal budget; less than the annual cost of a single major weapons system
