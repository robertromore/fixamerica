# SSI Reform: Stakeholders

## Who Benefits from the Status Quo

### Federal Budget Hawks

**Who**: Congressional appropriators, OMB budget analysts, fiscal conservative organizations (Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget, Concord Coalition)

**Why They Benefit**:

- Current SSI benefit levels represent a $10-15 billion annual savings compared to poverty-level benefits
- Frozen asset limits keep some potentially eligible people off the rolls
- Complexity creates barriers to enrollment that function as informal rationing

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Moderate**

- Genuine concern about fiscal sustainability
- Many acknowledge SSI inadequacy but prioritize deficit reduction
- Some support targeted reforms (asset limits) while opposing benefit increases

### Program Integrity Establishment

**Who**: SSA Office of Inspector General, Congressional oversight committees (especially House Ways and Means Social Security Subcommittee), Government Accountability Office

**Why They Benefit**:

- Complex rules justify large program integrity operations
- Overpayment recovery generates revenue that offsets some administrative costs
- Reporting requirements provide data for oversight
- The surveillance model sustains jobs and institutional purpose

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Low to Moderate**

- GAO has repeatedly recommended simplification
- OIG focuses on fraud (which is rare) but also recommends administrative improvements
- Opposition is primarily to reforms perceived as weakening program integrity

### States That Avoid Supplementation

**Who**: 24 states that provide no SSI state supplement

**Why They Benefit**:

- Save state funds by relying entirely on federal benefit levels
- Low benefit levels may discourage migration to their states by SSI recipients
- No administrative burden of state supplementation

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Low**

- Federal SSI reform imposes no direct cost on states
- States may support federal increases that reduce demand for state-funded services

## Who Is Harmed by the Status Quo

### SSI Beneficiaries

**4.6 million disabled adults, 1.1 million disabled children, 1.7 million aged individuals**

- Living at 77% of the federal poverty level or below
- Cannot save more than $2,000 without losing benefits
- Face marriage penalty of $5,808/year
- Subject to complex reporting requirements and overpayment risk
- Limited work incentives trap them in poverty

**Political Power**: **Very Low**

- Low voting rates
- Limited organizational representation
- Stigmatized as "welfare recipients"
- Many face cognitive, psychiatric, or physical barriers to advocacy

### Families of SSI Recipients

**Millions of parents, spouses, siblings, and children of SSI recipients**

- Bear financial costs of disability that SSI does not cover
- Subject to deeming rules that count their income against family member's benefits
- Penalized through ISM rules for providing housing or food support
- Parents of SSI children face age-18 redetermination risk

**Political Power**: **Low to Moderate**

- More politically engaged than beneficiaries themselves
- Organized through disability-specific parent groups (The Arc, NAMI family support)
- Some access to state and federal legislators through constituent services

### Disability Rights Organizations

**Key Organizations**:

- **National Council on Disability (NCD)**: Federal advisory body; has issued comprehensive SSI reform recommendations
- **The Arc of the United States**: Advocacy for people with intellectual and developmental disabilities
- **National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI)**: Mental health advocacy
- **Center on Budget and Policy Priorities (CBPP)**: Policy research and advocacy on SSI
- **Justice in Aging**: Advocacy for low-income older adults, including SSI-aged population
- **National Organization of Social Security Claimants' Representatives (NOSSCR)**: Attorney and advocate organization

**Political Power**: **Moderate**

- Effective at raising awareness and producing policy analysis
- Limited lobbying resources compared to industry groups
- Successfully advocated for ABLE Act and incremental reforms
- Struggle to generate sustained media coverage of SSI issues

### SSA Employees

**57,000 federal employees (reduced from 67,000)**

- Overworked and under-resourced
- Processing SSI claims with outdated technology
- Unable to provide quality service due to caseload pressure
- Morale issues related to chronic understaffing

**Political Power**: **Moderate**

- Represented by American Federation of Government Employees (AFGE)
- Can advocate through unions for better resources
- Constrained by limitations on political activity by federal employees

## Coalition Opportunities

### Bipartisan Asset Limit Reform

The strongest bipartisan opportunity is raising asset limits. Support comes from:

- **Disability rights organizations**: Core demand
- **Faith communities**: Marriage penalty linked to family values
- **Fiscal conservatives**: Asset limits are economically irrational; removing them may save administrative costs
- **Libertarians**: Government should not police individual savings
- **Business community**: Better savings = less reliance on public programs

### SSA Funding Coalition

Adequate SSA funding attracts support across the political spectrum:

- **Program integrity advocates**: More staff = more redeterminations = less fraud/overpayment
- **Disability rights organizations**: More staff = faster processing = better service
- **AFGE/labor**: More staff = better working conditions
- **Fiscal conservatives**: Each dollar spent on redeterminations saves $4-7

### Work Incentive Enhancement

Improving SSI work incentives could bridge ideological divides:

- **Conservatives**: Reducing dependency, promoting self-sufficiency
- **Progressives**: Enabling economic participation for marginalized group
- **Business groups**: Expanding labor force participation
- **Disability rights**: Removing barriers to employment
