# SSI Reform: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

SSI reform requires federal legislation because SSI is entirely a federal program (unlike Medicaid or SNAP, which involve state administration). Constitutional authority rests primarily in Congress's spending power and the General Welfare Clause. Most SSI rules are statutory (Title XVI of the Social Security Act, 42 U.S.C. sections 1381-1383f), though SSA has significant regulatory authority under existing law. This document provides draft legal text for the key reforms identified in [Solutions](07-solutions.md).

## Federal Legislation

### SSI Savings Penalty Elimination Act (Asset Limit Reform)

**Purpose**: Raise SSI resource limits from $2,000/$3,000 to $10,000/$20,000 and index for inflation, enabling beneficiaries to save for emergencies and plan for the future.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "SSI Savings Penalty Elimination Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) The resource limits for Supplemental Security Income under
    title XVI of the Social Security Act have not been updated
    since 1989.
(2) If adjusted for inflation, the individual resource limit
    would be approximately $5,200 in 2025 dollars.
(3) The current limits of $2,000 for individuals and $3,000 for
    couples prevent SSI recipients from saving for emergencies,
    deposits, or any future expense.
(4) The resource limits impose administrative costs on the Social
    Security Administration that exceed the savings they generate.
(5) Raising resource limits would reduce overpayments caused by
    inadvertent resource limit violations.

SEC. 3. INCREASE IN RESOURCE LIMITS.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 1611(a)(3) of the Social Security Act
    (42 U.S.C. 1382(a)(3)) is amended—
    (1) inParagraph (A), by striking "$2,000" and inserting
        "$10,000"; and
    (2) in paragraph (B), by striking "$3,000" and inserting
        "$20,000".

(b) INFLATION ADJUSTMENT.—Section 1611(a)(3) of the Social
    Security Act is further amended by adding at the end the
    following:
    "(C) The dollar amounts specified in subparagraphs (A) and (B)
    shall be adjusted annually, beginning January 1 of the second
    calendar year following the date of enactment of this
    subparagraph, by the percentage increase (if any) in the
    Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers (CPI-U) for the
    12-month period ending with September of the preceding year."

SEC. 4. EXCLUSION OF RETIREMENT ACCOUNTS.

Section 1613(a) of the Social Security Act (42 U.S.C. 1382b(a))
is amended by adding at the end the following:
"(15) Any funds held in a retirement account described in section
401(a), 403(b), 408, 408A, or 457(b) of the Internal Revenue
Code of 1986, or in a Thrift Savings Plan account under
subchapter III of chapter 84 of title 5, United States Code."

SEC. 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

The amendments made by this Act shall take effect on the first
day of the first month beginning after the date of enactment of
this Act.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3(a)**: Raises limits to $10,000 (individual) and $20,000 (couple). The couple limit is set at double the individual limit, also addressing a portion of the marriage penalty
- **Section 3(b)**: Automatic CPI-U indexing prevents future erosion
- **Section 4**: Excludes retirement accounts from countable resources, preventing the current requirement that applicants liquidate retirement savings
- **Section 5**: Immediate effective date eliminates the gap between enactment and implementation

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| CBO cost estimate | $2-4 billion/year; modest in context of federal budget |
| "Wealthy people on SSI" concern | Income test still applies; $10,000 in savings does not make someone wealthy |
| Administrative systems changes | SSA system modification is straightforward (simple threshold change) |

**Refinements**:

- **Stronger version**: Eliminate asset test entirely (evaluate income only)
- **Fallback version**: Raise to $5,000/$10,000 as intermediate step
- **Addition**: Exclude 529 education savings accounts

### SSI Marriage Penalty Elimination Act

**Purpose**: Eliminate the SSI marriage penalty by setting the couple benefit at twice the individual rate and the couple resource limit at twice the individual limit.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "SSI Marriage Penalty Elimination
Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) The maximum SSI benefit for an eligible couple ($1,450 per
    month in 2025) is less than twice the individual rate
    ($967 per month), creating a marriage penalty of $484 per
    month or $5,808 per year.
(2) This penalty discourages marriage among people with
    disabilities and forces some couples to maintain separate
    residences or divorce to preserve benefits.
(3) The couple resource limit ($3,000) is less than twice the
    individual limit ($2,000), compounding the penalty.
(4) No comparable marriage penalty exists in Social Security
    retirement or disability insurance benefits.

SEC. 3. ELIMINATION OF MARRIAGE PENALTY IN BENEFITS.

Section 1611(b)(1) of the Social Security Act (42 U.S.C.
1382(b)(1)) is amended by striking the couple benefit amount and
inserting "an amount equal to 200 percent of the amount determined
under subsection (b)(1) for an eligible individual who does not
have an eligible spouse".

SEC. 4. ELIMINATION OF MARRIAGE PENALTY IN RESOURCES.

Section 1611(a)(3)(B) of the Social Security Act (42 U.S.C.
1382(a)(3)(B)) is amended by striking "$3,000" and inserting "an
amount equal to 200 percent of the amount specified in
subparagraph (A)".

SEC. 5. ELIMINATION OF "HOLDING OUT" PROVISION.

Section 1614(d) of the Social Security Act (42 U.S.C. 1382c(d))
is amended by striking paragraph (2) (relating to persons holding
themselves out as married).

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

The amendments made by this Act shall apply with respect to
benefits payable for months beginning after the date of enactment
of this Act.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Sets the couple rate at exactly double the individual rate, eliminating the financial penalty for marriage
- **Section 4**: Sets the couple resource limit at double the individual limit
- **Section 5**: Eliminates the "holding out" provision, which treats unmarried cohabiting couples as married for SSI purposes -- a rule that penalizes intimate relationships regardless of legal status

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| "Economies of scale" argument | At poverty-level benefits, there are no meaningful economies of scale |
| Cost ($2-3 billion/year) | Modest; comparable to single tax provision |
| Holding out elimination concern | Other programs do not apply this rule; enforcement is invasive and inconsistent |

### SSI Modernization Act (Comprehensive Reform)

**Purpose**: Comprehensive reform of SSI benefit levels, income-counting rules, and program administration.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "SSI Modernization Act".

SEC. 2. BENEFIT INCREASE TO FEDERAL POVERTY LEVEL.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 1611(b) of the Social Security Act
    (42 U.S.C. 1382(b)) is amended to provide that the monthly
    benefit for an eligible individual shall not be less than
    one-twelfth of the annual income threshold used by the Census
    Bureau to determine poverty status for a single individual
    under 65 years of age.

(b) PHASE-IN.—The increase required by subsection (a) shall be
    phased in over a 4-year period as follows:
    (1) Year 1: benefit increased by 25 percent of the difference
        between the current benefit and the poverty level amount;
    (2) Year 2: benefit increased by 50 percent of the difference;
    (3) Year 3: benefit increased by 75 percent of the difference;
    (4) Year 4 and thereafter: benefit equal to the poverty level
        amount.

(c) COLA PROTECTION.—The annual cost-of-living adjustment shall
    be computed using the greater of—
    (1) the Consumer Price Index for Urban Wage Earners and
        Clerical Workers (CPI-W); or
    (2) the Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers (CPI-U).

SEC. 3. INCOME SIMPLIFICATION.

(a) EARNED INCOME EXCLUSION.—Section 1612(b)(4) of the Social
    Security Act (42 U.S.C. 1382a(b)(4)) is amended by striking
    "$65" and inserting "$400 (adjusted annually for inflation
    using the CPI-U)".

(b) ELIMINATION OF IN-KIND SUPPORT AND MAINTENANCE FOR FOOD.—
    Section 1612(a)(2) of the Social Security Act is amended to
    exclude from unearned income any food or meals provided to
    an eligible individual by another person.

(c) SIMPLIFICATION OF SHELTER ISM.—Section 1612(a)(2) is further
    amended to replace the presumed maximum value rule for shelter
    with a flat monthly deduction of $100 (adjusted annually for
    inflation), applicable only when the eligible individual pays
    no shelter costs.

(d) DEEMING REFORM.—Section 1614(f) of the Social Security Act
    (42 U.S.C. 1382c(f)) is amended to—
    (1) increase the spousal allocation by 50 percent;
    (2) increase the parental allocation by 50 percent; and
    (3) exempt the first $500 per month of deemed income from
        consideration.

SEC. 4. OVERPAYMENT REFORM.

(a) MINIMUM OVERPAYMENT THRESHOLD.—The Commissioner shall not
    establish an overpayment of less than $100 in any month.

(b) RECOVERY LIMITATION.—The rate of recovery of overpayments
    from continuing benefits shall not exceed 5 percent of the
    monthly benefit amount, unless the beneficiary requests a
    higher rate.

(c) EXPANDED WAIVER.—The Commissioner shall waive recovery of
    an overpayment whenever recovery would—
    (1) reduce the beneficiary's income below 150 percent of the
        federal poverty level; or
    (2) deprive the beneficiary of financial resources needed for
        necessary living expenses, including food, shelter,
        clothing, medical care, and disability-related expenses.

(d) STATUTE OF LIMITATIONS.—No action to recover an overpayment
    shall be taken more than 5 years after the date of the last
    overpayment in the period at issue.

SEC. 5. ADMINISTRATIVE FUNDING.

(a) MANDATORY APPROPRIATION.—There are authorized to be
    appropriated from the general fund of the Treasury, on a
    mandatory basis, such sums as are necessary to ensure—
    (1) average initial processing time for SSI applications does
        not exceed 90 days;
    (2) annual redetermination of eligibility for not less than
        33 percent of all SSI recipients; and
    (3) maintenance of not fewer than 1,300 field offices
        nationwide.

(b) TECHNOLOGY MODERNIZATION.—There is authorized to be
    appropriated $500,000,000 over 5 fiscal years for
    modernization of SSA information technology systems used
    for SSI administration.

SEC. 6. STATE SUPPLEMENT MAINTENANCE OF EFFORT.

No State that provides supplementary payments under section
1616 of the Social Security Act as of the date of enactment
shall reduce such payments below the level in effect on such
date for a period of 5 years following enactment.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

Except as otherwise provided, the amendments made by this Act
shall take effect on January 1 of the first calendar year
beginning after the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 2**: Raises benefits to FPL with a 4-year phase-in and improved COLA indexing
- **Section 3**: Comprehensive income simplification: updates the earned income exclusion to $400, eliminates food ISM, simplifies shelter ISM, and reforms deeming
- **Section 4**: Overpayment prevention and recovery reform, including a $100 minimum threshold, 5% recovery cap, expanded waiver, and 5-year statute of limitations
- **Section 5**: Mandatory administrative funding tied to performance standards
- **Section 6**: Prevents states from reducing supplements in response to federal increases

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Total cost ($15-25 billion/year) | Less than 0.5% of federal budget; phase-in reduces initial-year impact |
| Budget reconciliation eligibility | All provisions relate to spending and revenue; eligible for reconciliation |
| SSA implementation capacity | Section 5 provides dedicated technology and staffing funding |
| PAYGO requirements | Offsets can include modest revenue measures or spending adjustments |

## State Model Legislation

### State SSI Supplement Enhancement Act (Model)

**Purpose**: Model legislation for states to establish or increase SSI supplementation, ensuring that no SSI recipient in the state lives below the federal poverty level.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Supplemental Security
Income Enhancement Act".

SECTION 2. PURPOSE.

The purpose of this Act is to supplement federal Supplemental
Security Income benefits so that no eligible individual in this
State receives combined federal and state benefits below the
federal poverty level.

SECTION 3. STATE SUPPLEMENT.

(a) The [State Agency] shall provide monthly supplementary
    payments to each individual who receives federal Supplemental
    Security Income benefits and resides in this State.

(b) The amount of the supplementary payment shall equal the
    difference between—
    (1) one-twelfth of the annual federal poverty level for the
        applicable household size; and
    (2) the individual's federal SSI benefit (after any reductions
        for income or living arrangements).

(c) The supplementary payment shall be adjusted annually to
    reflect changes in the federal poverty level.

SECTION 4. ADMINISTRATION.

The [State Agency] may enter into an agreement with the Social
Security Administration for federal administration of state
supplementary payments under section 1616 of the Social Security
Act.

SECTION 5. APPROPRIATION.

There is appropriated from the general fund such sums as are
necessary to carry out this Act.

SECTION 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on July 1 following the date of
enactment.
```

## Regulatory Framework

### SSA Administrative Actions (No Legislation Required)

The following reforms can be implemented by the SSA Commissioner under existing statutory authority:

1. **ISM food simplification**: SSA proposed eliminating food as ISM in 2024; finalize this rulemaking
2. **Overpayment recovery procedures**: Reduce default withholding rate from 10% to 5% through administrative directive
3. **Waiver processing**: Expedite overpayment waiver decisions and adopt presumptive waivers for small amounts
4. **Technology improvements**: Implement online SSI application and reporting within existing IT budgets
5. **Field office accessibility**: Improve accessibility of field offices for people with disabilities (ironic that SSA offices serving people with disabilities are themselves sometimes inaccessible)

### Recommended Regulations

| Regulation | Authority | Timeline |
|------------|-----------|----------|
| Final rule eliminating food ISM | 42 U.S.C. 1382a(a)(2) interpretation | 2026 |
| Revised overpayment recovery guidelines | 42 U.S.C. 1383(b) | 2026 |
| Updated ABLE account interaction rules | 42 U.S.C. 1382b(a)(14) | 2026-2027 |
| Simplified shelter ISM calculation | 42 U.S.C. 1382a(a)(2) | Requires legislation for full reform |

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Authority

SSI rests on Congress's Spending Clause power (Article I, Section 8, Clause 1). There are no significant constitutional barriers to any of the proposed reforms. The reforms expand rather than restrict benefits, avoiding Due Process concerns that arise with benefit reductions.

### Preemption

Federal SSI law preempts state law where they conflict. State supplements are permissive, not required (except for the "pass-along" requirement for states that supplemented predecessor programs). New federal legislation should explicitly address:

- Whether states may reduce supplements in response to federal increases
- Whether federal income-counting simplifications apply to state supplement calculations
- Whether state ABLE account rules must conform to federal changes

### Enforcement

SSI reform enforcement mechanisms include:

- **Administrative review**: SSI applicants and beneficiaries can appeal adverse determinations through SSA's administrative process and ultimately to federal court
- **Class action litigation**: Systemic failures in SSI administration (e.g., overpayment collection practices, processing delays) can be challenged through class actions
- **Congressional oversight**: Enhanced reporting requirements in proposed legislation would provide ongoing oversight data

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### SSI Savings Penalty Elimination Act

**Potential Loopholes**:

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Asset sheltering | Individuals just above the old limit could restructure assets to qualify, expanding rolls beyond intended population | Low |
| Retirement account gaming | Shifting countable assets into newly excluded retirement accounts | Medium |
| Inflation indexing lag | CPI-U may not capture actual cost increases for people with disabilities | Low |
| State supplement interaction | States could count newly excluded assets in state supplement calculations | Medium |

**Shortcomings**:

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address benefit adequacy | Saving $10,000 on $967/month income is still not financial security | Narrow scope of bill |
| Does not address marriage penalty | Couples still penalized even with higher asset limits | Not included in bill |
| Does not address income complexity | Income rules remain Byzantine even with higher asset limits | Not included in bill |
| ABLE account interaction unclear | Relationship between $10,000 general limit and ABLE account exclusion may confuse beneficiaries | Drafting gap |

**Rectification Procedures**:

| Issue | Rectification Approach |
|-------|----------------------|
| Asset sheltering | Monitor enrollment data post-implementation; adjust if gaming is significant (unlikely at $10,000) |
| Retirement account gaming | Limit retirement account exclusion to accounts established before SSI application or cap excluded amount |
| Inflation lag | Use disability-specific price index (CPI-D) if BLS develops one; supplement CPI-U with periodic statutory adjustments |
| State supplement interaction | Include preemption clause in federal legislation prohibiting states from counting federally excluded assets |
| ABLE interaction | Issue regulatory guidance clarifying that ABLE and general resource limits are separate and cumulative |

### SSI Marriage Penalty Elimination Act

**Potential Loopholes**:

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Sham marriages | Non-disabled individuals could marry SSI recipients to gain household-level benefits | Low |
| Holding out elimination abuse | Removal of holding-out rule could incentivize cohabitation arrangements designed to avoid deeming | Medium |
| Benefit calculation complexity | Doubling the couple rate creates new edge cases in benefit computation | Low |

**Shortcomings**:

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address deeming of non-SSI spouse income | Marrying a non-SSI spouse still triggers deeming that may reduce or eliminate benefits | Separate issue not addressed |
| Does not address other program marriage penalties | SNAP, housing assistance, and other programs also penalize marriage | Federal program fragmentation |
| Holding out elimination creates verification gap | SSA may have difficulty determining household composition | Removal of enforcement tool |

**Rectification Procedures**:

| Issue | Rectification Approach |
|-------|----------------------|
| Sham marriage concern | Income test still applies; non-disabled spouse's income would be deemed, providing natural check |
| Holding out concerns | Replace with self-attestation of household composition; use periodic verification rather than surveillance |
| Non-SSI spouse deeming | Address in comprehensive reform (SSI Modernization Act deeming provisions) |
| Cross-program penalties | Coordinate with SNAP and housing reforms to align treatment of married couples |

### SSI Modernization Act

**Potential Loopholes**:

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Phase-in gaming | Applicants could delay applications to benefit from higher phase-in amounts | Low |
| Earned income exclusion arbitrage | Restructuring self-employment to maximize the $400 exclusion | Medium |
| Shelter ISM flat deduction exploitation | Individuals paying nominal rent to avoid the deduction while receiving substantial housing subsidy | Medium |
| Deeming threshold circumvention | Families structuring income to stay under $500 deemed income exemption | Low |
| Overpayment statute of limitations | SSA delays in identifying overpayments could eliminate recovery for legitimate overpayments older than 5 years | Medium |

**Shortcomings**:

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Phase-in delays full poverty-level benefits for 4 years | Beneficiaries continue in poverty during transition | Political compromise to reduce initial cost |
| SSA IT systems may not support required changes within timeline | Implementation failures could create new errors and overpayments | Legacy technology debt |
| Mandatory funding mechanism may not survive appropriations politics | SSA could remain underfunded despite mandatory authorization | Congressional budget dynamics |
| No integration with SSDI | Concurrent SSI/SSDI recipients face continued complexity | Programs remain separate |
| Does not address childhood SSI age-18 redetermination | 35-40% of children still lose benefits at 18 | Not addressed in bill |

**Rectification Procedures**:

| Issue | Rectification Approach |
|-------|----------------------|
| Phase-in delay | Provide emergency supplements during phase-in for beneficiaries below 50% FPL |
| IT implementation risk | Require pilot testing in 3 states before national rollout; include fallback manual procedures |
| Mandatory funding enforcement | Include sequestration exemption for SSA administrative funding; create independent monitoring board |
| SSDI integration | Commission study in Year 2 on unified disability benefit; include findings deadline |
| Childhood SSI transition | Add provision for 2-year transition period after age 18 before adult standard applies; separate legislative vehicle |
| Self-employment gaming | Require Schedule C documentation and apply anti-abuse rules from EITC |
| Shelter ISM deduction | Define "no shelter costs" narrowly; require lease or housing authority documentation |
| Overpayment limitations | Allow tolling of statute during periods when beneficiary's whereabouts are unknown |

### Cross-Cutting Issues

**Systemic Vulnerabilities**:

| Vulnerability | Applies To | Mitigation |
|---------------|------------|------------|
| Congressional repeal or erosion | All legislation | Build constituency support; include anti-rider provisions |
| Administrative implementation failure | Comprehensive reforms | Phase in; pilot test; provide adequate funding |
| Interaction effects with other benefit programs | All reforms affecting income/resources | Coordinate with SNAP, Medicaid, housing; issue unified guidance |
| Inflation erosion of fixed dollar amounts | Any provision with fixed thresholds | Include automatic indexing in every provision |
| Judicial narrowing of eligibility changes | Eligibility expansions | Draft with clear legislative intent; include legislative history |

**Ongoing Monitoring Requirements**:

| Requirement | Frequency | Responsible Entity |
|-------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Beneficiary poverty rate measurement | Annual | Census Bureau using SPM |
| Overpayment rate and recovery analysis | Quarterly | SSA OIG |
| Employment rate among SSI recipients | Annual | SSA Office of Research |
| Administrative processing times | Monthly | SSA performance dashboard |
| State supplement adequacy | Annual | CBPP or similar research organization |
| Asset accumulation among beneficiaries | Annual (post-reform) | SSA longitudinal data |
