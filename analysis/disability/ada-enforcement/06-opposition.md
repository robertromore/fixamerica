# ADA Enforcement: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to strengthening ADA enforcement comes primarily from business interests that frame the issue as "lawsuit abuse" rather than civil rights enforcement. While some concerns about litigation practices are legitimate, the dominant opposition strategy is to restrict private enforcement without providing meaningful alternative enforcement mechanisms, effectively weakening the ADA by making it unenforceable.

## Opposition Landscape

| Opposition Group | Primary Objection | Strength | Strategy |
|-----------------|-------------------|----------|----------|
| U.S. Chamber of Commerce | ADA litigation is abusive and costly | Very strong | Lobby for pre-suit notice; fund "reform" campaigns |
| National Federation of Independent Business | Small businesses unfairly targeted | Strong | Frame as small business protection |
| National Retail Federation | Serial litigants abuse Title III | Strong | Support ADA Education and Reform Act |
| American Hotel and Lodging Association | Hospitality industry over-targeted | Moderate | Seek safe harbor for compliant businesses |
| State legislative coalitions | State tort reform agenda | Moderate | Pass state pre-suit notice laws |
| Conservative legal movement | Government overreach via regulation | Moderate | Challenge ADA through court doctrines |
| Property rights advocates | Forced retrofits violate property rights | Moderate | Oppose building modification requirements |

## Ideological Opposition

### Free Market / Anti-Regulation Perspective

**Core belief:** Market forces should determine accessibility; government mandates are inefficient.

**Arguments:**

- Businesses have market incentives to serve customers with disabilities
- Regulatory compliance costs reduce economic competitiveness
- ADA imposes one-size-fits-all standards on diverse businesses
- Voluntary compliance is preferable to coerced compliance

**Weakness:** Three decades of the ADA demonstrate that market forces alone are insufficient. The 40-60% noncompliance rate for existing buildings and 96%+ noncompliance rate for websites (WebAIM, 2024) show that voluntary compliance does not work.

### Small Government / Federalism Perspective

**Core belief:** Federal disability mandates exceed the proper scope of federal power.

**Arguments:**

- States should set their own accessibility standards
- Federal one-size-fits-all regulation ignores local conditions
- ADA creates unfunded mandates on state and local governments
- Federal enforcement agencies are inefficient and unaccountable

**Weakness:** Disability rights are civil rights. The same federalism arguments were used to oppose the Civil Rights Act of 1964. Without federal standards, people with disabilities would face a patchwork of protections that varies by state, undermining equal citizenship.

## Key Arguments Against Stronger Enforcement

### Argument 1: "ADA Lawsuits Are Driven by Serial Litigants, Not People Seeking Access"

**Claim:** A small number of plaintiffs and law firms file thousands of ADA lawsuits for profit, targeting small businesses with technical violations.

**Evidence cited:**

- Top 10 ADA plaintiffs filed over 3,000 cases combined (2018-2023) (Seyfarth Shaw, 2024)
- Some plaintiffs have never visited the establishments they sue (California court records)
- Average Title III settlement is $5,000-$15,000, suggesting shakedown dynamics (NFIB, 2023)

**Reality:**

- The volume of lawsuits reflects the volume of violations: 40-60% of commercial buildings and 96%+ of websites are noncompliant
- Federal ADA lawsuits provide only injunctive relief, not damages, making pure profit motive unlikely under federal law (state law damages vary)
- Many "serial litigants" are people with disabilities who encounter barriers routinely in their daily lives
- The enforcement system depends on private action because federal enforcement is inadequate
- Similar "litigation abuse" arguments were historically used against racial discrimination lawsuits

**Rebuttal:** The problem is not too many lawsuits but too many violations. If businesses complied with the ADA, there would be nothing to sue about. Restricting lawsuits without increasing compliance leaves people with disabilities without recourse.

### Argument 2: "Pre-Suit Notice Will Encourage Compliance Without Litigation"

**Claim:** Requiring plaintiffs to notify businesses of ADA violations before suing would give businesses a chance to fix problems, reducing unnecessary litigation.

**Evidence cited:**

- ADA Education and Reform Act (H.R. 620) would require 60-day notice and 120-day cure period
- Several states have enacted notice requirements with claimed reductions in lawsuits
- Businesses argue they want to comply but need to know what is wrong

**Reality:**

- The ADA has been law for over 35 years; businesses have had ample notice of their obligations
- Pre-suit notice requirements create a "free pass" for first violations, eliminating deterrence
- 120-day cure periods mean that a person in a wheelchair who encounters stairs must wait four months before the law provides any remedy
- Defendants can cure the specific violation, ending the lawsuit, while maintaining systemic noncompliance
- No other federal civil rights statute requires pre-suit notice for injunctive relief claims
- States with notice requirements have seen reduced lawsuits but not increased compliance (NCD, 2023)

**Rebuttal:** Pre-suit notice shifts the burden of enforcement from violators to victims. It effectively tells people with disabilities that they must serve as unpaid compliance consultants before they can access their own rights.

### Argument 3: "ADA Compliance Is Too Expensive for Small Businesses"

**Claim:** Small businesses cannot afford the cost of ADA modifications, and enforcement unfairly burdens them.

**Evidence cited:**

- Average cost of ADA modifications: $10,000-$50,000 for existing buildings (NFIB, 2023)
- Small businesses operate on thin margins
- Accessibility consultants and attorneys add significant costs

**Reality:**

- The ADA requires only "readily achievable" barrier removal for existing buildings, a standard that accounts for business size and resources
- Tax credits (Section 44) provide up to $5,000 annually for small business accessibility expenditures
- Tax deductions (Section 190) allow up to $15,000 annually for barrier removal
- Many accessibility improvements cost little: lowering a counter section, adding grab bars, improving signage
- New construction accessibility adds only 0.5-1% to building costs (Access Board, 2022)
- People with disabilities represent $1.8 trillion in disposable income (AIR, 2023), making accessibility a business opportunity

**Rebuttal:** Cost concerns are legitimate for some businesses, but the ADA already accounts for this through the "readily achievable" standard and tax incentives. The solution is better technical assistance, not weaker enforcement.

### Argument 4: "Web Accessibility Standards Are Unclear"

**Claim:** Businesses cannot comply with web accessibility requirements because the DOJ has not issued formal regulations specifying technical standards.

**Evidence cited:**

- DOJ withdrew its Title III web accessibility ANPRM in 2017
- No formal federal regulation specifies WCAG version or conformance level for private businesses
- Court decisions are inconsistent on what web accessibility requires

**Reality:**

- WCAG 2.1 Level AA is the internationally recognized standard and is referenced in DOJ settlements and consent decrees
- The DOJ issued a Title II web accessibility rule in April 2024 using WCAG 2.1 AA
- Most web accessibility failures involve basic issues: missing alt text, poor color contrast, inaccessible forms
- Numerous resources exist for web accessibility compliance
- The ADA's obligation to provide equal access does not depend on specific regulations; the statute itself creates the obligation

**Rebuttal:** While formal Title III web accessibility regulations would be helpful, the absence of regulations does not excuse basic accessibility failures. Businesses that claim they cannot make their websites accessible are often simply choosing not to.

### Argument 5: "ADA Enforcement Is a Cottage Industry That Benefits Lawyers, Not Disabled People"

**Claim:** The ADA enforcement system primarily benefits plaintiff attorneys who collect fees without meaningfully improving access.

**Evidence cited:**

- Attorney fees in ADA cases can exceed the cost of remediation
- Some law firms specialize in high-volume, low-impact ADA cases
- Settlements may address specific barriers without systemic change

**Reality:**

- Attorney fees are the mechanism by which Congress ensures that civil rights can be enforced by private parties
- Without fee-shifting, people with disabilities could not afford legal representation
- The same fee-shifting model applies to all federal civil rights statutes
- Many ADA attorneys are people with disabilities themselves
- Impact litigation by organizations like DRA and NFB has produced systemic changes affecting millions

**Rebuttal:** The question is not whether lawyers benefit from ADA enforcement, but whether enforcement produces compliance. It does: businesses that are sued overwhelmingly come into compliance. The alternative is no enforcement at all.

### Argument 6: "The ADA Is Being Weaponized for Competitive Advantage"

**Claim:** Some businesses use ADA lawsuits against competitors as a competitive weapon.

**Evidence cited:**

- Anecdotal reports of businesses reporting competitors' ADA violations
- Concern that larger businesses can absorb compliance costs while smaller competitors cannot

**Reality:**

- Evidence of strategic competitive ADA litigation is minimal
- ADA compliance requirements apply equally to all businesses
- If a competitor is noncompliant, the appropriate remedy is compliance, not immunity from enforcement
- This argument could be made about any regulation; it does not justify nonenforcement

**Rebuttal:** If businesses are using ADA enforcement competitively, the solution is not to weaken enforcement but to ensure all businesses comply. The answer to selective enforcement is more enforcement, not less.

## Counter-Strategies for Advocates

### Reframing the Narrative

| Opposition Frame | Advocate Reframe |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| "Lawsuit abuse" | "Civil rights enforcement" |
| "Serial litigants" | "People who encounter barriers every day" |
| "Burdensome regulation" | "Equal access to public life" |
| "Small business protection" | "Customer inclusion and market expansion" |
| "Unclear standards" | "35-year-old obligation with well-established requirements" |
| "Cottage industry for lawyers" | "Justice system that vindicates civil rights" |

### Strategic Approaches

1. **Lead with stories**: Center the experiences of people with disabilities who face barriers, not abstract legal arguments
2. **Emphasize the business case**: Disability market represents $1.8 trillion in spending power; accessibility is profitable
3. **Draw civil rights parallels**: Pre-suit notice is equivalent to telling Black Americans they must notify a restaurant before suing for racial discrimination
4. **Separate legitimate concerns from bad faith**: Offer solutions for small businesses (technical assistance, tax incentives) while opposing enforcement rollbacks
5. **Build bipartisan coalitions**: ADA was signed by a Republican president; frame enforcement as fulfilling a bipartisan promise
6. **Highlight enforcement inadequacy**: Show that the problem is too little enforcement, not too much, using DOJ complaint and resolution data

### Addressing Legitimate Concerns

| Concern | Legitimate Response (Without Weakening Enforcement) |
|---------|-----------------------------------------------------|
| Small business compliance costs | Expand tax credits; provide free technical assistance |
| Unclear web accessibility standards | Support formal rulemaking for Title III web accessibility |
| Frivolous or bad-faith lawsuits | Existing Rule 11 sanctions and vexatious litigant procedures |
| Need for compliance guidance | Fund Access Board and DOJ to provide more guidance |
| Retrofit costs for older buildings | Phase-in compliance with technical assistance and financing |
