# ADA Enforcement: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Strengthening ADA enforcement requires legislative action to address three fundamental gaps: inadequate federal enforcement resources, insufficient private enforcement remedies, and the absence of clear technical standards for emerging accessibility challenges. This document provides draft legislation addressing each gap, along with analysis of legal considerations, potential loopholes, and implementation challenges.

---

## Federal Legislation

### Bill 1: ADA Enforcement Enhancement Act

**Purpose**: Increase DOJ Disability Rights Section capacity, establish regional enforcement offices, create proactive compliance review authority, and fund interagency coordination.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "ADA Enforcement Enhancement Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.
(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that—
    (1) the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 is a landmark
    civil rights statute that prohibits discrimination against
    individuals with disabilities;
    (2) enforcement of the ADA has been chronically inadequate,
    with the Department of Justice resolving fewer than 5 percent
    of complaints through formal enforcement action;
    (3) the Disability Rights Section of the Civil Rights Division
    lacks sufficient resources to fulfill its enforcement mandate;
    (4) fragmented enforcement across multiple federal agencies
    creates coordination failures; and
    (5) people with disabilities continue to face pervasive barriers
    to participation in civic, economic, and social life.
(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to strengthen federal
enforcement of the Americans with Disabilities Act through increased
resources, improved interagency coordination, and proactive
compliance review authority.

SEC. 3. DISABILITY RIGHTS SECTION FUNDING.
(a) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There is authorized to be
appropriated to the Department of Justice, for the Disability Rights
Section of the Civil Rights Division, $75,000,000 for each of
fiscal years 2027 through 2033, in addition to any other amounts
authorized or appropriated for the Civil Rights Division.
(b) STAFFING REQUIREMENTS.—The Attorney General shall ensure
that the Disability Rights Section maintains not fewer than 150
attorneys dedicated to ADA enforcement by the end of fiscal year
2030.
(c) REGIONAL OFFICES.—
    (1) IN GENERAL.—The Attorney General shall establish not fewer
    than 8 regional ADA enforcement offices by the end of fiscal
    year 2030.
    (2) LOCATION.—Regional offices shall be geographically
    distributed to provide coverage to all 50 States and the
    territories.
    (3) STAFFING.—Each regional office shall be staffed with not
    fewer than 8 attorneys and appropriate support staff.

SEC. 4. PROACTIVE COMPLIANCE REVIEW.
(a) IN GENERAL.—The Attorney General shall establish an ADA
Compliance Review Program to conduct proactive reviews of
compliance with Titles II and III of the Americans with
Disabilities Act.
(b) MINIMUM REVIEWS.—The Attorney General shall conduct not
fewer than 200 proactive compliance reviews per fiscal year
beginning in fiscal year 2029.
(c) PRIORITY.—In selecting entities for proactive review, the
Attorney General shall prioritize—
    (1) state and local government entities that have not been
    reviewed in the preceding 10 years;
    (2) industries or sectors with documented patterns of
    noncompliance;
    (3) entities providing essential services including healthcare,
    education, transportation, and voting; and
    (4) entities in geographic areas with limited private
    enforcement activity.
(d) REPORT.—The Attorney General shall submit an annual report
to Congress on compliance review activity and findings.

SEC. 5. INTERAGENCY ADA ENFORCEMENT COUNCIL.
(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established an Interagency ADA
Enforcement Council (in this section referred to as the "Council").
(b) MEMBERSHIP.—The Council shall consist of—
    (1) the Attorney General or designee, who shall serve as Chair;
    (2) the Chair of the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission
    or designee;
    (3) the Secretary of Transportation or designee;
    (4) the Secretary of Health and Human Services or designee;
    (5) the Secretary of Education or designee;
    (6) the Chair of the Federal Communications Commission or
    designee;
    (7) the Chair of the Access Board or designee; and
    (8) the Chair of the National Council on Disability or designee.
(c) DUTIES.—The Council shall—
    (1) coordinate ADA enforcement activities across Federal
    agencies;
    (2) establish a shared complaint intake and referral system;
    (3) develop joint enforcement priorities;
    (4) issue an annual report on Federal ADA enforcement to Congress;
    (5) identify and address gaps in enforcement coverage; and
    (6) recommend legislative and regulatory actions to improve
    enforcement.
(d) MEETINGS.—The Council shall meet not less frequently than
quarterly.

SEC. 6. COMPLAINT PROCESSING STANDARDS.
(a) TIMELINESS.—The Attorney General shall resolve ADA complaints
within 12 months of receipt, absent extraordinary circumstances.
(b) TRANSPARENCY.—The Attorney General shall—
    (1) acknowledge receipt of all complaints within 30 days;
    (2) provide complainants with regular status updates; and
    (3) issue written determinations on all closed complaints.
(c) DATA COLLECTION.—The Attorney General shall collect and
publicly report data on complaint volume, resolution times,
outcomes, and enforcement actions disaggregated by disability
type, complaint category, and geographic location.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Creates a dedicated, separately authorized funding stream for ADA enforcement, preventing it from being crowded out by other Civil Rights Division priorities
- **Section 4**: Shifts from purely reactive enforcement to proactive compliance review, addressing the complaint-driven model's limitations
- **Section 5**: Codifies interagency coordination in statute, preventing it from being eliminated by executive action
- **Section 6**: Establishes accountability standards for complaint processing, addressing the chronic backlog

**Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Appropriation may not match authorization | Authorizes specific amounts; appropriators retain discretion |
| Staffing may be difficult at this scale | Phased hiring over 3 years; competitive salary authority |
| Business community may oppose proactive reviews | Reviews identify compliance gaps, not punish; technical assistance included |
| Interagency Council may lack teeth | Statutory mandate with congressional reporting requirements |

**Refinements**:

- **Stronger version**: Mandate minimum penalty amounts for violations found during compliance reviews
- **Compromise version**: Reduce authorized amount; focus on regional offices and technical assistance
- **Fallback version**: Establish Interagency Council only (lowest political resistance)

### Bill 2: ADA Access to Justice Act

**Purpose**: Add compensatory damages to ADA Title III, strengthen attorney fee provisions, and codify protections against mootness abuse.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "ADA Access to Justice Act".

SEC. 2. COMPENSATORY DAMAGES UNDER TITLE III.
(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 308(a) of the Americans with Disabilities
Act of 1990 (42 U.S.C. 12188(a)) is amended—
    (1) inParagraph (1), by inserting after "injunctive relief"
    the following: ", compensatory damages, and such other relief
    as the court determines to be appropriate"; and
    (2) by adding at the end the following:
    "(3) COMPENSATORY DAMAGES.—
        (A) IN GENERAL.—In any action under paragraph (1), a
        court may award compensatory damages to the prevailing
        plaintiff for harm caused by a violation of this title.
        (B) LIMITATIONS.—
            (i) FIRST VIOLATION.—Compensatory damages for a first
            violation shall not exceed $75,000.
            (ii) SUBSEQUENT VIOLATIONS.—Compensatory damages for
            subsequent violations shall not exceed $150,000.
        (C) GOOD FAITH DEFENSE.—A defendant that demonstrates by
        a preponderance of the evidence that it conducted an
        accessibility audit within the preceding 3 years and was
        implementing a remediation plan in good faith at the time
        of the violation may have damages reduced by not more
        than 50 percent.
        (D) SMALL BUSINESS ADJUSTMENT.—For defendants with fewer
        than 50 employees and gross annual receipts of less than
        $7,500,000, the court shall consider the financial
        resources and size of the defendant in determining the
        amount of damages.".

SEC. 3. ATTORNEY FEES.
Section 505 of the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990
(42 U.S.C. 12205) is amended by adding at the end the following:
"(c) CATALYST THEORY.—A plaintiff who demonstrates that the
filing of an action was a material factor in obtaining the
relief sought shall be considered a prevailing party for
purposes of an award of attorney fees under this section,
regardless of whether a judgment or consent decree was entered.
(d) INTERIM FEES.—The court may award interim attorney fees
to a plaintiff who demonstrates substantial likelihood of
success on the merits, to ensure continued access to legal
representation.".

SEC. 4. MOOTNESS PROTECTIONS.
Section 308 of the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990
(42 U.S.C. 12188) is amended by adding at the end the following:
"(c) MOOTNESS.—
    (1) VOLUNTARY CESSATION.—A defendant's voluntary cessation
    of a challenged practice shall not moot a claim under this
    title unless the defendant demonstrates that—
        (A) the violation has been fully remedied;
        (B) there is no reasonable likelihood of recurrence; and
        (C) the defendant has implemented policies and procedures
        to prevent future violations.
    (2) ATTORNEY FEES.—A plaintiff whose claim is mooted by
    the defendant's voluntary cessation shall be entitled to
    reasonable attorney fees for the period during which the
    claim was pending.".

SEC. 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall apply to violations occurring on or after the
date that is 180 days after the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 2**: Addresses the fundamental weakness of Title III by adding compensatory damages, with caps to address business concerns and a good-faith defense for businesses making genuine compliance efforts
- **Section 3**: Codifies the catalyst theory to prevent defendants from mooting cases without paying fees, and provides for interim fees to sustain plaintiff representation
- **Section 4**: Prevents the common defense tactic of fixing a violation during litigation to moot the case without consequences

**Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Business opposition to any damages | Caps and good-faith defense address concerns |
| May increase litigation volume | Reflects underlying violations; compliance reduces lawsuits |
| Constitutional concerns | Congress has clear authority under Section 5 of 14th Amendment |
| Insurance cost increases | Market will adjust; ADA has existed for 35+ years |

### Bill 3: Online Accessibility Act

**Purpose**: Codify web accessibility standards for private businesses under ADA Title III, with phased compliance deadlines and technical assistance.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Online Accessibility Act".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.
In this Act—
    (1) COVERED ENTITY.—The term "covered entity" means any
    person or entity that operates a place of public accommodation
    as defined in section 301(7) of the Americans with Disabilities
    Act of 1990 (42 U.S.C. 12181(7)) and maintains a website or
    mobile application.
    (2) WEB CONTENT ACCESSIBILITY GUIDELINES.—The term "WCAG"
    means the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines published by
    the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C), or successor guidelines
    adopted by the Access Board.
    (3) CONFORMANCE LEVEL.—The term "conformance level" means
    Level AA as defined in WCAG version 2.1 or the most recent
    version adopted by the Access Board.

SEC. 3. DIGITAL ACCESSIBILITY REQUIREMENTS.
(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 302 of the Americans with Disabilities
Act of 1990 (42 U.S.C. 12182) is amended by adding at the end
the following:
"(c) DIGITAL ACCESSIBILITY.—
    (1) REQUIREMENT.—It shall be a violation of this title for
    a covered entity to maintain a website or mobile application
    that does not conform to the applicable Web Content
    Accessibility Guidelines at the conformance level specified
    by the Access Board.
    (2) INITIAL STANDARD.—Until the Access Board specifies an
    alternative standard, the applicable standard shall be WCAG
    version 2.1 Level AA.
    (3) STANDARD UPDATES.—The Access Board shall review and
    update the applicable standard not less frequently than
    every 5 years.".

SEC. 4. COMPLIANCE DEADLINES.
(a) LARGE ENTITIES.—Covered entities with 500 or more employees
shall comply with the requirements of section 3 not later than
18 months after the date of enactment.
(b) MEDIUM ENTITIES.—Covered entities with 50 to 499 employees
shall comply not later than 30 months after the date of enactment.
(c) SMALL ENTITIES.—Covered entities with fewer than 50 employees
shall comply not later than 42 months after the date of enactment.

SEC. 5. SAFE HARBOR.
(a) IN GENERAL.—A covered entity that achieves and maintains
conformance with the applicable standard, as verified by a
qualified accessibility assessor, shall have an affirmative
defense to any claim under section 302(c) for the period of
conformance.
(b) QUALIFIED ASSESSOR.—The Access Board shall establish
criteria for qualified accessibility assessors and maintain a
registry of qualified assessors.

SEC. 6. TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE.
(a) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
$20,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2033 for
the Access Board to provide technical assistance to covered
entities in achieving digital accessibility.
(b) SMALL BUSINESS PRIORITY.—Not less than 50 percent of
technical assistance funding shall be directed to covered
entities with fewer than 50 employees.
(c) RESOURCES.—The Access Board shall develop and make publicly
available—
    (1) model accessibility policies;
    (2) testing tools and checklists;
    (3) training materials; and
    (4) a help desk for accessibility questions.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Resolves the ambiguity about what web accessibility requires under Title III by codifying WCAG 2.1 AA as the standard
- **Section 4**: Provides phased deadlines that give smaller businesses more time to comply
- **Section 5**: Creates a safe harbor that incentivizes proactive compliance and protects compliant businesses
- **Section 6**: Ensures businesses have access to technical assistance, particularly small businesses

## State Model Legislation

### Model State ADA Enforcement Enhancement Act

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "[State] Disability Access
Enforcement Enhancement Act".

SECTION 2. STATE DISABILITY RIGHTS ENFORCEMENT OFFICE.
(a) There is established within the Office of the Attorney
General a Disability Rights Enforcement Office.
(b) The Office shall—
    (1) investigate complaints of disability discrimination
    under state and federal law;
    (2) conduct proactive compliance reviews of state and local
    government entities and places of public accommodation;
    (3) provide technical assistance to businesses and
    governments seeking to achieve accessibility compliance;
    (4) coordinate with the United States Department of Justice
    and other federal agencies on ADA enforcement; and
    (5) issue an annual report to the Governor and Legislature
    on disability access enforcement.
(c) FUNDING.—There is appropriated to the Office the sum of
$[amount] for each fiscal year, to remain available until
expended.

SECTION 3. STATE DISABILITY ACCESS STANDARDS.
(a) All state agencies and political subdivisions shall
maintain websites and mobile applications that conform to
WCAG 2.1 Level AA or successor standard.
(b) Compliance deadline: [18 months] after the effective
date of this Act.
(c) The Office shall conduct annual audits of state agency
website compliance and publish results.

SECTION 4. PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.
(a) Any person aggrieved by a violation of [state disability
rights law] may bring a civil action for injunctive relief,
compensatory damages, and reasonable attorney fees.
(b) Compensatory damages shall not exceed $[amount] for a
first violation and $[amount] for subsequent violations.
(c) The court shall consider the good faith efforts of the
defendant in determining the amount of damages.

SECTION 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect on [date].
```

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Authority

- Congress has authority under Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment to enforce equal protection through ADA provisions (*Tennessee v. Lane*, 2004)
- Commerce Clause provides independent basis for Title III requirements
- Spending Clause supports conditional federal grants to states

### Preemption

- Federal web accessibility standards would preempt less protective state standards
- States remain free to provide greater protections
- Safe harbor provisions create uniform compliance standard

### Sovereign Immunity

- *Board of Trustees v. Garrett* (2001) limits Title I damages claims against states
- *Tennessee v. Lane* (2004) allows some Title II damages claims
- State enforcement grants do not raise sovereign immunity issues
- Clear congressional intent and congruence-and-proportionality analysis required

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Availability | Effectiveness |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| DOJ enforcement actions | Available but under-resourced | High when deployed |
| Private litigation (injunctive) | Available | Moderate; limited by lack of damages |
| Private litigation (damages, if enacted) | Proposed | High; creates meaningful deterrent |
| Administrative enforcement | Limited | Low; agencies lack capacity |
| State enforcement | Variable | Depends on state capacity and law |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Good-faith defense manipulation | Businesses conduct minimal audits to claim good-faith defense without genuine remediation | High |
| Safe harbor gaming | Achieve certification then allow compliance to lapse between reviews | Medium |
| Small business threshold exploitation | Structure businesses to fall under employee thresholds | Medium |
| Compliance deadline extensions | Seek repeated extensions or phase-in accommodations | Medium |
| Technical standard obsolescence | Comply with outdated standard version while newer versions address emerging barriers | Low |
| Mootness via partial fix | Fix only the specific violation identified while maintaining systemic noncompliance | High |
| State law preemption claims | Argue federal standards preempt stronger state protections | Medium |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|-----------|
| Damages caps may be too low | Insufficient deterrence for large corporations | Political compromise to gain business support |
| No punitive damages | Limited deterrent for willful violators | Legislative caution |
| Voluntary certification may have low uptake | Limited impact on overall compliance | Certification cost and perceived value |
| Interagency Council lacks enforcement authority | Coordination without teeth | Respecting agency independence |
| Phased compliance deadlines delay access | People with disabilities wait years for compliance | Political feasibility |
| No private enforcement of compliance review findings | DOJ controls follow-up; complainants cannot enforce | Preserve DOJ discretion |
| Technology standards lag innovation | New technologies deployed without accessibility | Standard-setting timeline |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Good-faith defense abuse**: Require audits to be conducted by Access Board-certified assessors with detailed remediation timelines; allow courts to pierce good-faith claims when evidence shows minimal effort
2. **Safe harbor gaming**: Require annual compliance monitoring between triennial certifications; automatic safe harbor revocation for documented violations
3. **Small business threshold**: Base thresholds on combined employees of affiliated entities; anti-evasion provisions modeled on FMLA regulations
4. **Mootness abuse**: Statutory mootness provisions (included in Bill 2) require full remedy, prevention policies, and attorney fees
5. **Standard obsolescence**: Mandate Access Board standard updates every 5 years with automatic adoption of successor versions
6. **Interagency Council effectiveness**: Add congressional reporting requirements and GAO review authority
7. **Technology lag**: Grant Access Board authority to issue interim guidance for emerging technologies pending formal standards

### General Implementation Concerns

- **Judicial resistance**: Courts may interpret new provisions narrowly; legislative history must clearly express congressional intent
- **Political durability**: Enforcement reforms may be weakened by subsequent Congresses; embed protections in authorizing statute rather than appropriations
- **Business community backlash**: Pair enforcement measures with technical assistance and compliance support
- **Capacity constraints**: Hiring 100+ attorneys requires pipeline development; coordinate with law schools
- **Data collection**: Effective oversight requires robust data systems; authorize technology investment
