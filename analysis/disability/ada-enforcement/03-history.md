# ADA Enforcement: History

## Pre-ADA Era: The Fight for Recognition

### Early Disability Rights (1800s-1960s)

The history of disability in America is largely a history of exclusion, institutionalization, and invisibility. For most of American history, people with disabilities were segregated from society through "ugly laws," institutional confinement, and social stigma.

**Key Developments**

- 1817: American School for the Deaf founded (Hartford, CT), among the first disability-specific institutions
- 1840s-1900s: State institutions for people with intellectual and psychiatric disabilities proliferate
- 1920: Smith-Fess Act creates federal vocational rehabilitation program
- 1935: Social Security Act excludes most people with disabilities from benefits
- 1945-1960s: Disabled veterans from World War II and Korea push for rehabilitation and access

**The Institutional Model**

- By 1960, over 500,000 Americans were confined in state institutions
- Conditions were frequently abusive and dehumanizing
- People with disabilities had virtually no legal rights
- *Buck v. Bell* (1927): Supreme Court upheld forced sterilization of people with disabilities

## The Civil Rights Model Emerges (1960s-1980s)

### Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act (1973)

**The First Federal Disability Rights Law**

- Prohibited disability discrimination by recipients of federal financial assistance
- Modeled on Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964
- Signed by President Nixon as part of broader rehabilitation legislation
- Initially went unenforced: HEW delayed issuing implementing regulations for four years

**The 504 Sit-ins (1977)**

- Disability activists occupied HEW offices in San Francisco for 25 days
- Longest occupation of a federal building in U.S. history
- Forced HEW Secretary Joseph Califano to sign regulations
- Demonstrated the power of disability civil rights organizing
- Established cross-disability coalition model that would shape ADA advocacy

### Building the Legislative Foundation

| Year | Development | Significance |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| 1975 | Education for All Handicapped Children Act (P.L. 94-142) | Right to public education for children with disabilities |
| 1978 | Amendments to Rehabilitation Act | Expanded Section 504 coverage |
| 1980 | Civil Rights of Institutionalized Persons Act (CRIPA) | DOJ authority to investigate institutional conditions |
| 1983 | National Council on the Handicapped recommends comprehensive civil rights law | Seeds of ADA |
| 1986 | Air Carrier Access Act | Prohibited disability discrimination in air travel |
| 1988 | Fair Housing Amendments Act | Extended Fair Housing Act to cover disability |
| 1988 | National Council on Disability publishes "Toward Independence" | Blueprint for ADA |

## The Americans with Disabilities Act (1990)

### Passage

**Legislative History**

- 1988: First ADA bill introduced by Senator Lowell Weicker (R-CT) and Representative Tony Coelho (D-CA)
- 1989: Revised bill introduced by Senator Tom Harkin (D-IA) and Representative Steny Hoyer (D-MD)
- Bipartisan support: co-sponsored by Senators Bob Dole (R-KS) and Ted Kennedy (D-MA)
- Passed Senate 76-8 (September 7, 1989)
- Passed House 403-20 (May 22, 1990)
- Signed by President George H.W. Bush on July 26, 1990

**Key Enforcement Provisions as Enacted**

| Title | Scope | Enforcement Agency | Remedies |
|-------|-------|-------------------|----------|
| Title I: Employment | Employers with 15+ employees | EEOC | Compensatory and punitive damages (via Civil Rights Act of 1991) |
| Title II: Public Services | State and local governments | DOJ | Injunctive relief, compensatory damages |
| Title III: Public Accommodations | Private businesses open to public | DOJ | Injunctive relief only; DOJ can seek civil penalties |
| Title IV: Telecommunications | Telephone companies | FCC | Administrative enforcement |
| Title V: Miscellaneous | General provisions | Various | Varies |

### Early Enforcement (1990-2000)

**DOJ Enforcement Launch**

- DOJ Disability Rights Section established within Civil Rights Division
- Initial enforcement focused on high-profile public accommodations cases
- 1994: DOJ issued ADA Standards for Accessible Design based on Access Board guidelines
- First consent decrees targeted large hotel chains, restaurant chains, and sports venues

**Early Achievements**

- Curb cuts became standard in new construction
- Public transit systems began wheelchair accessibility programs
- New commercial construction incorporated accessibility requirements
- Telecommunications relay services established nationwide

**Early Limitations**

- Enforcement focused on new construction; existing buildings largely untouched
- Small business compliance was minimal and unenforced
- DOJ lacked resources for proactive compliance reviews
- Private litigation was the primary enforcement mechanism from the beginning

## The Retrenchment Era (2000-2008)

### Supreme Court Narrows the ADA

A series of Supreme Court decisions significantly narrowed ADA coverage and enforcement:

| Case | Year | Effect |
|------|------|--------|
| *Sutton v. United Air Lines* | 1999 | Disability determined with mitigating measures; excluded many from coverage |
| *Murphy v. United Parcel Service* | 1999 | Same principle applied to hypertension |
| *Albertsons v. Kirkingburg* | 1999 | Applied to monocular vision |
| *Toyota Motor Manufacturing v. Williams* | 2002 | Narrowed "substantially limits" standard |
| *Board of Trustees v. Garrett* | 2001 | No compensatory damages against states under Title I (sovereign immunity) |
| *Tennessee v. Lane* | 2004 | Title II access to courts survives sovereign immunity, but narrow holding |
| *Chevron v. Echazabal* | 2002 | Allowed "direct threat to self" defense in employment |

**Impact of Judicial Narrowing**

- Between 1999 and 2008, courts ruled against ADA plaintiffs approximately 95% of the time at summary judgment (ABA study, 2008)
- The "Sutton trilogy" excluded millions of people with manageable conditions from ADA protection
- "Substantially limits" was interpreted so narrowly that many plainly disabled individuals could not establish coverage
- Enforcement became focused on proving disability status rather than discrimination

### Federal Enforcement During Bush Administration (2001-2008)

- DOJ Civil Rights Division shifted priorities away from disability enforcement
- Number of ADA enforcement actions declined
- Consent decrees became less common
- GAO criticized DOJ for ADA enforcement gaps (GAO-06-365, 2006)
- EEOC ADA charge resolutions declined in volume and impact

---

## The ADA Amendments Act and Renewed Enforcement (2008-2020)

### ADA Amendments Act of 2008 (ADAAA)

**Purpose and Key Changes**

Congress responded to the Supreme Court's narrowing decisions with bipartisan legislation:

- Passed Senate 93-1, House voice vote
- Signed by President George W. Bush on September 25, 2008
- Effective January 1, 2009

| Change | Before ADAAA | After ADAAA |
|--------|--------------|-------------|
| "Substantially limits" standard | Narrowly interpreted | Broadly interpreted |
| Mitigating measures | Considered in determining disability | Not considered (except ordinary glasses) |
| "Regarded as" claims | Required showing of substantial limitation | Only requires showing of discrimination based on impairment |
| Episodic conditions | Often excluded | Covered if substantially limiting when active |
| Major life activities | Narrow list | Expanded to include major bodily functions |

### Obama Administration Enforcement (2009-2017)

**Increased Enforcement Activity**

- DOJ filed significantly more ADA enforcement actions
- Landmark settlements with major entities: Legoland, Hilton Hotels, H&R Block (web accessibility)
- First DOJ enforcement actions on website accessibility (2010-2014)
- Increased EEOC enforcement of Title I claims
- 2010: DOJ updated ADA Standards for Accessible Design (effective 2012)

**Proactive Enforcement Initiatives**

- Project Civic Access: DOJ reviewed state and local government ADA compliance
- ADA mediation program expanded
- Compliance reviews of major healthcare systems
- Voting accessibility initiative ahead of 2012 and 2016 elections

### Trump Administration (2017-2021)

**Enforcement Pullback**

- DOJ withdrew pending web accessibility rulemaking (ANPRM, December 2017)
- Fewer ADA enforcement actions initiated
- Civil Rights Division leadership vacancies
- DOJ issued less formal guidance
- Private litigation continued to fill enforcement gap

**Congressional Threats**

- ADA Education and Reform Act (H.R. 620) passed House in 2018, 225-192
- Would have required 60-day notice and 120-day cure period before Title III lawsuits
- Did not advance in Senate
- Disability community rallied opposition

## Current Era (2021-Present)

### Biden Administration

**Enforcement Priorities**

- Appointed Kristen Clarke as Assistant Attorney General for Civil Rights
- Increased DOJ ADA enforcement actions
- April 2024: Issued final rule on web accessibility under Title II (WCAG 2.1 AA)
- Announced Title III web accessibility rulemaking (proceeding)
- COVID-19 response: Addressed accessibility of vaccination sites and telehealth
- Long COVID: DOJ and HHS guidance recognizing Long COVID as potential ADA disability (2021)

**Key Enforcement Actions (2021-2025)**

| Year | Action | Impact |
|------|--------|--------|
| 2021 | Settlement with Rite Aid (web accessibility) | National chain required to make website accessible |
| 2022 | Uber settlement (wheelchair-accessible vehicles) | $2.2 million; policy changes |
| 2022 | Settlement with Louisiana (community integration) | *Olmstead* enforcement |
| 2023 | Settlement with Kroger (self-checkout accessibility) | In-store technology access |
| 2024 | Title II web accessibility final rule | First binding federal web accessibility standard |
| 2024 | Settlement with multiple hospital systems | Medical equipment accessibility |

### Ongoing Challenges

- Transition to new administration (2025): Uncertainty about enforcement priorities
- Persistent resource constraints at DOJ Disability Rights Section
- Growing complaint backlog
- Unresolved Title III web accessibility standards
- Circuit splits on key legal questions
- ADA Education and Reform Act continues to be reintroduced

## Key Turning Points

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|--------------|
| 1973 | Section 504 enacted | First federal disability rights law |
| 1977 | 504 sit-ins | Forced implementation of disability rights regulations |
| 1990 | ADA signed | Comprehensive disability civil rights law |
| 1999 | *Olmstead v. L.C.* | Right to community integration |
| 1999-2002 | Sutton trilogy + *Garrett* | Supreme Court narrows ADA |
| 2008 | ADA Amendments Act | Congress restores broad ADA coverage |
| 2018 | ADA Education and Reform Act passes House | Threat to private enforcement |
| 2023 | *Laufer v. Acheson Hotels* | Supreme Court avoids ruling on tester standing |
| 2024 | DOJ Title II web accessibility rule | First binding federal web accessibility standard |

## Lessons from History

1. **Laws without enforcement are aspirational**: The ADA's promise depends entirely on the willingness and capacity of federal agencies and courts to enforce it
2. **Judicial interpretation matters as much as statutory text**: The Supreme Court nearly gutted the ADA between 1999 and 2008; Congress had to respond legislatively
3. **Private enforcement is essential**: Federal agencies have never had sufficient resources for comprehensive ADA enforcement; private litigation fills critical gaps
4. **Enforcement ebbs and flows with political priorities**: Presidential administration changes produce dramatic shifts in enforcement activity
5. **Disability rights require constant vigilance**: Legislative threats like the ADA Education and Reform Act show that opponents continue to seek weakening of enforcement mechanisms
6. **Bipartisan support is possible but fragile**: The original ADA passed with overwhelming bipartisan support, but enforcement has become increasingly partisan
