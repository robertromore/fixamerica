# ADA Enforcement: Actions

## Individual Actions

### Educate Yourself

- Learn the basics of ADA Titles I, II, and III and what they require
- Understand your rights as a person with a disability or an ally
- Visit <https://www.ada.gov/> for DOJ resources and guidance
- Follow disability rights organizations on social media for current developments
- Read the ADA Standards for Accessible Design to understand physical access requirements

### Report Violations

- File ADA complaints with the DOJ at <https://www.ada.gov/complaint/>
- File employment complaints with the EEOC at <https://www.eeoc.gov/filing-charge-discrimination>
- Contact your state's Protection and Advocacy organization (find yours at <https://www.ndrn.org/about/ndrn-member-agencies/>)
- Document barriers with photos, dates, and descriptions
- Keep records of communications with businesses and governments about accessibility

### Personal Advocacy

- Politely but firmly request accommodations and access
- Write reviews of businesses noting accessibility (or lack thereof)
- Contact businesses directly about accessibility barriers before or instead of filing complaints
- Share your experiences publicly to raise awareness (with consent if involving others)
- Attend public meetings about local accessibility issues

## Community Actions

### Local Organizing

- Join or start a local disability rights chapter (ADAPT, AAPD local affiliates)
- Organize accessibility audits of local businesses and government buildings
- Attend city council and school board meetings to advocate for accessibility
- Partner with local independent living centers for advocacy support
- Build coalitions with other civil rights organizations

### Awareness Campaigns

- Organize community events for ADA anniversary (July 26)
- Create local accessibility scorecards for businesses and public facilities
- Engage local media in covering accessibility stories
- Use social media to document and publicize barriers (#ADAFails, #AccessibilityMatters)
- Invite people with disabilities to share their experiences at community forums

### Technical Assistance

- Volunteer to help small businesses understand accessibility requirements
- Train community members on filing ADA complaints
- Develop accessibility guides for local venues and services
- Partner with technology organizations to improve digital accessibility awareness
- Mentor people with disabilities in navigating legal and administrative systems

## Political Actions

### Contact Your Representatives

**Call your members of Congress about ADA enforcement:**

- Support increased funding for DOJ Disability Rights Section
- Oppose the ADA Education and Reform Act (pre-suit notice requirements)
- Support the Online Accessibility Act (web accessibility standards)
- Support Title III compensatory damages legislation

### Template Email

```text
Subject: Strengthen ADA Enforcement - Oppose Weakening of Disability Rights

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

I am writing as a constituent to urge you to support stronger
enforcement of the Americans with Disabilities Act and to oppose any
legislation that would weaken ADA enforcement.

The ADA was signed into law over 35 years ago with overwhelming
bipartisan support. Yet enforcement remains inadequate: the
Department of Justice resolves fewer than 5% of ADA complaints through
formal enforcement action, and an estimated 40-60% of commercial
buildings still have accessibility barriers.

I ask you to:

1. Support increased funding for the DOJ Disability Rights Section
2. Oppose the ADA Education and Reform Act, which would impose
   pre-suit notice requirements that effectively gut private
   enforcement
3. Support legislation to codify web accessibility standards
   for private businesses
4. Support adding compensatory damages to ADA Title III to align
   it with other federal civil rights statutes

The 61 million Americans with disabilities deserve the full
protection of the law. Stronger enforcement is a civil rights
imperative.

Thank you for your attention to this important issue.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your Address]
```

## Talking Points

### For General Audiences

- The ADA is one of America's most important civil rights laws, but enforcement has not kept up with its promise
- 61 million Americans live with disabilities, and many face daily barriers to full participation
- The DOJ resolves fewer than 5% of ADA complaints through formal enforcement action
- Over 96% of websites fail basic accessibility standards, excluding millions from online services
- Stronger enforcement is not about more lawsuits; it is about ensuring compliance with a 35-year-old law

### For Business Audiences

- People with disabilities represent $1.8 trillion in disposable income in the United States
- Accessible design benefits everyone: parents with strollers, seniors, people with temporary injuries
- ADA compliance reduces litigation risk and improves customer experience
- Tax credits and deductions make accessibility affordable for small businesses
- Early compliance is far less expensive than retrofitting after a lawsuit

### For Policymaker Audiences

- The ADA was passed with overwhelming bipartisan support: 76-8 in the Senate, 403-20 in the House
- Federal enforcement resources have not kept pace with the scope of ADA obligations
- Private enforcement fills critical gaps but faces judicial and legislative threats
- International competitors (EU, UK, Australia) are strengthening their disability access requirements
- Investing in enforcement produces economic returns through increased participation

## Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Reality |
|--------------|---------|
| "The ADA is fully enforced" | The DOJ resolves a small fraction of complaints; most violations go unaddressed |
| "ADA lawsuits are frivolous" | The vast majority address real, documented accessibility barriers |
| "Compliance is too expensive" | The ADA requires only "readily achievable" barrier removal; tax incentives help with costs |
| "Small businesses are exempt" | Small businesses must comply but the "readily achievable" standard considers their resources |
| "Websites don't have to be accessible" | Courts consistently apply the ADA to websites; the DOJ has issued Title II web rules |
| "Pre-suit notice would help businesses comply" | The ADA has been law for 35+ years; businesses have had ample notice |
| "People with disabilities can go somewhere else" | The ADA guarantees equal access to public accommodations, not just alternative options |
| "ADA compliance is a one-time cost" | Accessibility requires ongoing attention, especially for digital content |
| "Only wheelchair users benefit from the ADA" | The ADA covers a wide range of disabilities including vision, hearing, cognitive, and mental health |
| "The ADA is anti-business" | Accessibility expands the customer base and improves the experience for all users |
