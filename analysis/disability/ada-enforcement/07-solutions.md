# ADA Enforcement: Solutions

## Overview

Strengthening ADA enforcement requires a multi-pronged approach: increasing federal enforcement capacity, preserving and expanding private enforcement rights, establishing clear compliance standards, and creating economic incentives for voluntary compliance. The following solutions are designed to work together as a comprehensive enforcement reform package.

---

## Solution 1: Increase DOJ Disability Rights Section Funding and Staffing

### Description

Triple the Disability Rights Section's attorney staffing from approximately 55-60 to 150-180 and create a dedicated ADA enforcement budget line within the DOJ appropriation. Establish regional ADA enforcement offices to improve geographic coverage.

### Evidence Base

- GAO has repeatedly recommended increased DRS staffing and funding (GAO-06-365; GAO-23-105296)
- NCD has documented the relationship between enforcement capacity and compliance rates (NCD, 2023)
- Comparable civil rights enforcement agencies (e.g., the Civil Rights Division's Voting Section) have received proportionally more resources for narrower mandates

### Implementation

- Congressional appropriation of $50-75 million annually for dedicated ADA enforcement (separate from general Civil Rights Division budget)
- Hire 100-120 additional attorneys over 3 years
- Establish 8-10 regional ADA enforcement offices (co-located with U.S. Attorney offices)
- Create an ADA Compliance Review Unit for proactive enforcement
- Implement case management technology to reduce backlog

### Cost

- **Annual cost**: $50-75 million in additional appropriations
- **Current baseline**: Embedded within $189 million Civil Rights Division budget (FY 2024)
- **Per-complaint cost reduction**: Higher volume would reduce per-unit investigation cost

### Timeline

- Year 1: Appropriation and hiring
- Year 2: Regional offices operational
- Year 3: Proactive compliance review program launched
- Year 5: Backlog eliminated; average complaint resolution under 12 months

### Challenges

- Requires congressional appropriation in a constrained budget environment
- Competition with other DOJ priorities
- Need to recruit specialized disability rights attorneys
- Regional office establishment requires space and administrative support

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline (2024) | Target (Year 5) |
|--------|-----------------|------------------|
| Complaints resolved per year | ~4,000 | 10,000+ |
| Formal enforcement actions | ~200 | 500+ |
| Average resolution time | 24-36 months | Under 12 months |
| Pending complaint backlog | 4,800+ | Under 1,000 |
| Proactive compliance reviews | Minimal | 200+ per year |

## Solution 2: Add Compensatory Damages to ADA Title III

### Description

Amend ADA Title III to allow private plaintiffs to recover compensatory damages (not just injunctive relief) when they are denied access to places of public accommodation. This would align Title III with Title II and with other federal civil rights statutes.

### Evidence Base

- Title III's injunction-only remedy is unique among federal civil rights laws and widely recognized as inadequate
- States that allow damages under state disability laws (California, New York, Massachusetts) have higher compliance rates
- The absence of damages means many plaintiff attorneys cannot afford to take ADA cases, reducing enforcement
- Bagenstos, Samuel R. "The Perversity of Limited Civil Rights Remedies." *Michigan Law Review* 97 (1999): 1075-1154

### Implementation

- Congressional amendment to 42 U.S.C. Section 12188 to add compensatory damages
- Include reasonable caps to address business community concerns (e.g., $50,000 first violation, $100,000 subsequent)
- Preserve injunctive relief as primary remedy
- Maintain attorney fee-shifting for prevailing plaintiffs
- Include good-faith compliance defense: businesses that have conducted accessibility audits and are implementing remediation in good faith may have damages reduced

### Cost

- **Federal cost**: Minimal (no appropriation required; enforcement is private)
- **Business cost**: Variable; depends on compliance status
- **Insurance cost**: Some increase in ADA-related insurance claims

### Timeline

- Legislative passage: 1-2 years
- Effective date: 6-12 months after enactment
- Impact on compliance: Measurable within 2-3 years

### Challenges

- Strong business lobby opposition
- Risk of increasing lawsuit volume (though this reflects underlying violations)
- Must balance deterrence with avoiding over-deterrence for small businesses
- Constitutional issues: unlikely (damages are within congressional power)

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline (2024) | Target (Year 5) |
|--------|-----------------|------------------|
| Plaintiff attorney participation in ADA cases | Limited by economics | Significant increase |
| Title III case filing rate | ~9,000/year | May initially increase, then decrease as compliance improves |
| Commercial building compliance rate | 40-60% | 70-80% |
| Settlement values | $5,000-15,000 avg | Reflect actual harm |

## Solution 3: Codify Web Accessibility Standards Under Title III

### Description

Enact federal legislation requiring private businesses that operate websites or mobile applications to conform to WCAG 2.1 Level AA (or successor standard), with phased compliance deadlines based on business size.

### Evidence Base

- 96.3% of top one million website home pages have detectable WCAG 2.1 failures (WebAIM, 2024)
- DOJ issued Title II web accessibility rule in April 2024 (WCAG 2.1 AA) but has not issued Title III rule
- Courts have applied varying standards to web accessibility, creating confusion
- Countries with codified web accessibility standards (EU, Canada, Australia) report higher compliance rates
- European Accessibility Act (2019) establishes comprehensive digital accessibility requirements effective 2025

### Implementation

- Congressional enactment of Online Accessibility Act or equivalent
- Standard: WCAG 2.1 Level AA (with provision for updates to successor standards)
- Compliance deadlines:
    - Large businesses (500+ employees): 18 months
    - Medium businesses (50-499 employees): 30 months
    - Small businesses (under 50 employees): 42 months
- Technical assistance program funded at $20 million annually
- Safe harbor for businesses that achieve and maintain conformance
- Regular standard updates through Access Board rulemaking

### Cost

- **Federal cost**: $20 million/year for technical assistance
- **Business cost**: Average $5,000-50,000 for initial compliance depending on website complexity (WebAIM estimates)
- **Economic benefit**: Access to 61 million disabled consumers; SEO and usability improvements for all users

### Timeline

- Legislation: 1-2 years
- Large business compliance deadline: 18 months after enactment
- Small business compliance deadline: 42 months after enactment
- Full compliance: 5 years

### Challenges

- Business community opposition to new mandates
- Rapidly evolving technology may outpace standards
- Small business compliance costs
- Need for ongoing standard updates
- Enforcement mechanism must be designed carefully

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline (2024) | Target (Year 5) |
|--------|-----------------|------------------|
| Websites meeting WCAG 2.1 AA | 3-5% | 60-75% |
| Web accessibility lawsuits | 4,600+/year | Decrease as compliance increases |
| Disabled users reporting online barriers | ~70% | Under 30% |
| Businesses with accessibility policies | ~15% | 70%+ |

## Solution 4: Establish ADA Compliance Certification Program

### Description

Create a voluntary federal ADA compliance certification program through the Access Board, allowing businesses and government entities to obtain certification that they meet current accessibility standards. Certified entities would receive a safe harbor from damages (not injunctive relief) in ADA litigation.

### Evidence Base

- ISO/IEC 17065 conformity assessment framework provides model
- LEED certification for building sustainability demonstrates viability of voluntary certification
- Business community has expressed willingness to invest in compliance if standards are clear and compliance provides legal protection
- NCD has recommended certification programs as alternative to litigation restriction (NCD, 2023)

### Implementation

- Access Board develops certification criteria for physical and digital accessibility
- Accredited third-party assessors conduct certifications
- Certification valid for 3 years with annual spot-checks
- Certified entities receive safe harbor from compensatory damages (injunctive relief still available)
- Database of certified entities publicly accessible
- Federal procurement preference for certified businesses

### Cost

- **Federal cost**: $15 million/year for Access Board certification program
- **Business cost**: $2,000-20,000 per certification depending on size and complexity
- **Revenue**: Certification fees partially offset federal costs

### Timeline

- Year 1: Access Board develops criteria
- Year 2: Accreditation of assessors; pilot certification program
- Year 3: Full program launch
- Year 5: 10,000+ entities certified

### Challenges

- Must be voluntary; mandatory certification would face opposition
- Certification standards must be rigorous to be meaningful
- Risk of "rubber stamp" certifications if oversight is weak
- Must not become a substitute for enforcement

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline (2024) | Target (Year 5) |
|--------|-----------------|------------------|
| Certified entities | 0 | 10,000+ |
| Compliance rate among certified entities | N/A | 95%+ |
| Litigation against certified entities | N/A | Significantly lower |
| Voluntary accessibility investment | Current levels | Measurable increase |

## Solution 5: Strengthen Interagency ADA Enforcement Coordination

### Description

Establish a White House-level ADA Enforcement Coordinator position and create an Interagency ADA Enforcement Council to coordinate enforcement across DOJ, EEOC, DOT, HHS, FCC, Education, and other federal agencies with ADA responsibilities.

### Evidence Base

- NCD documented significant interagency coordination failures (NCD, 2023)
- GAO identified fragmented enforcement as a barrier to compliance (GAO-23-105296)
- Model: Interagency Working Group on Environmental Justice (established by Executive Order 12898) demonstrates interagency coordination mechanisms
- Complainants regularly report confusion about which agency handles their complaint

### Implementation

- Executive Order establishing ADA Enforcement Council
- White House Domestic Policy Council staffer designated as ADA Enforcement Coordinator
- Quarterly interagency meetings with published agendas and outcomes
- Shared complaint intake system across agencies
- Joint enforcement database
- Coordinated compliance review schedules
- Annual report on ADA enforcement to Congress

### Cost

- **Federal cost**: $5 million/year for coordination staff and systems
- **Efficiency gains**: Reduced duplication and faster complaint routing

### Timeline

- Immediate: Executive Order (no legislation needed)
- Year 1: Council established, first meetings held
- Year 2: Shared complaint system operational
- Year 3: Joint enforcement actions initiated

### Challenges

- Depends on presidential commitment; can be reversed by subsequent administration
- Agency turf battles over enforcement jurisdiction
- Technology challenges of integrating complaint systems
- Must not add bureaucratic layer that slows enforcement

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline (2024) | Target (Year 3) |
|--------|-----------------|------------------|
| Interagency referral resolution time | 6-12 months | Under 3 months |
| Complainant confusion rate | High | Reduced by 50%+ |
| Joint enforcement actions | Rare | 20+ per year |
| Complaints routed to wrong agency | Frequent | Under 5% |

## Solution 6: Create State ADA Enforcement Grants Program

### Description

Establish a federal grant program providing $100 million annually to state attorneys general and state human rights commissions to enhance state-level ADA enforcement capacity. Grants would be conditioned on meeting minimum enforcement standards.

### Evidence Base

- States with strong disability rights enforcement (California, New York, Massachusetts) have higher compliance rates
- Many states lack resources for disability rights enforcement
- Federal-state partnerships have proven effective in other civil rights contexts (VAWA grants, hate crimes enforcement)
- Geographic disparities in ADA enforcement correspond to state capacity differences

### Implementation

- Congressional authorization and appropriation of $100 million/year
- Formula-based grants to state AGs and human rights commissions
- Conditions: minimum staffing, training, complaint processing standards
- Technical assistance from DOJ to state enforcement agencies
- Annual reporting requirements on enforcement activity and outcomes
- Matching fund requirements (50% match) to incentivize state investment

### Cost

- **Federal cost**: $100 million/year
- **State matching**: $50 million/year (aggregate)
- **Total investment**: $150 million/year in state enforcement

### Timeline

- Year 1: Legislation and appropriation
- Year 2: First grants awarded
- Year 3: All 50 states participating
- Year 5: Measurable increase in state enforcement activity

### Challenges

- Federal funding constraints
- Some states may resist federal conditions
- Varying state legal frameworks require flexible grant design
- Risk of states substituting federal funds for existing state spending

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline (2024) | Target (Year 5) |
|--------|-----------------|------------------|
| States with active ADA enforcement programs | ~20 | 45+ |
| State-level ADA enforcement actions | ~2,000/year | 5,000+/year |
| Rural area enforcement | Very low | Measurable increase |
| State compliance review capacity | Limited | Every state has review capability |

## Solution 7: Expand Access Board Authority and Standards

### Description

Convert the U.S. Access Board from an advisory body to an enforcement-capable agency with authority to issue binding accessibility standards for emerging technologies and conduct compliance monitoring.

### Evidence Base

- The Access Board develops technical standards but has no enforcement authority
- Gap between standards development and enforcement creates compliance uncertainty
- Emerging technologies (AI, autonomous vehicles, kiosks) need accessibility standards before deployment
- Other countries (UK, Australia) have standards bodies with enforcement capability

### Implementation

- Congressional legislation expanding Access Board authority
- Grant rulemaking authority for emerging technology accessibility
- Create compliance monitoring division within Access Board
- Increase Access Board budget from $9.5 million (FY 2024) to $35 million
- Require accessibility impact assessments for new federal technology procurement

### Cost

- **Annual cost**: $25 million increase to Access Board budget
- **Economic benefit**: Early standards reduce costly retrofitting

### Timeline

- Year 1: Legislation
- Year 2: Expanded Access Board operational
- Year 3: First emerging technology standards issued
- Year 5: Comprehensive standards framework for new technologies

### Challenges

- Resistance to creating new regulatory authority
- Technology industry opposition to prescriptive standards
- Need to balance innovation with accessibility
- Standards development timeline vs. technology deployment speed

### Impact Metrics

| Metric | Baseline (2024) | Target (Year 5) |
|--------|-----------------|------------------|
| Emerging technology accessibility standards | Few | Comprehensive framework |
| Pre-deployment accessibility reviews | Rare | Required for federal procurement |
| Access Board budget | $9.5 million | $35 million |
| Technology accessibility disputes | Growing | Reduced through clear standards |

## International Models

### United Kingdom: Equality Act 2010 and EHRC

| Feature | UK Approach | U.S. Comparison |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| Enforcement body | Equality and Human Rights Commission (EHRC) | DOJ + EEOC (fragmented) |
| Damages | Compensatory damages available for all claims | Title III: injunctive only |
| Proactive duty | "Anticipatory duty" to make reasonable adjustments | Reactive complaint-driven |
| Web accessibility | Mandatory standards (Public Sector Bodies Accessibility Regulations 2018) | Title II rule only (2024); no Title III rule |
| Enforcement resources | EHRC budget approximately $30 million (2023) | Comparable to DRS alone, but narrower mandate |

### Australia: Disability Discrimination Act 1992

| Feature | Australian Approach | U.S. Comparison |
|---------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Enforcement body | Australian Human Rights Commission (AHRC) | DOJ + EEOC |
| Conciliation process | Mandatory conciliation before litigation | No exhaustion requirement (Titles II/III) |
| Standards | Disability Standards for Accessible Public Transport (2002), Education (2005) | More limited sector-specific standards |
| Web accessibility | Disability Discrimination Act applies; WCAG adopted | Evolving through litigation |
| Compliance incentive | Disability Action Plans encouraged | No formal incentive program |

### European Union: European Accessibility Act (2019)

| Feature | EU Approach | U.S. Comparison |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| Scope | Products and services including digital | Broader product coverage |
| Standards | EN 301 549 (harmonized standard) | WCAG referenced but not codified for Title III |
| Timeline | Member state implementation by June 2025 | No comparable timeline |
| Enforcement | National enforcement bodies with market surveillance | Complaint-driven federal enforcement |
| Penalties | "Effective, proportionate and dissuasive" penalties required | Title III: injunctive only |

## Solution Sequencing

### Phase 1: Immediate (Executive Action, No Legislation Required)

1. Executive Order establishing Interagency ADA Enforcement Council (Solution 5)
2. DOJ prioritization of ADA enforcement within existing resources
3. Initiate Title III web accessibility rulemaking

### Phase 2: Near-Term (1-2 Years, Legislation Required)

1. Increase DOJ Disability Rights Section funding (Solution 1)
2. Codify web accessibility standards (Solution 3)
3. Create state enforcement grants program (Solution 6)

### Phase 3: Medium-Term (2-4 Years)

1. Add compensatory damages to Title III (Solution 2)
2. Establish ADA compliance certification program (Solution 4)
3. Expand Access Board authority (Solution 7)

### Rationale for Sequencing

- Start with executive actions that require no legislation and demonstrate commitment
- Build enforcement capacity before expanding legal tools
- Establish clear standards before strengthening penalties
- Create compliance pathways before increasing litigation exposure
- Build political momentum through early wins
