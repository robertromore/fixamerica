# ADA Enforcement: Current State

## Federal Enforcement Landscape

### DOJ Civil Rights Division

The Department of Justice Civil Rights Division, Disability Rights Section (DRS) is the primary federal enforcer of ADA Titles II and III. The section conducts investigations based on individual complaints, referrals from other agencies, and its own compliance reviews.

**Current Capacity (FY 2024)**

- Approximately 50-60 attorneys in the Disability Rights Section
- Annual budget embedded within the Civil Rights Division's $189 million total (FY 2024)
- No dedicated ADA enforcement budget line separate from other civil rights work
- Handles employment (pattern or practice), public services, and public accommodations cases

**Complaint Processing**

|--------|---------|---------|---------|----------------|
| Complaints received | 9,800 | 10,400 | 11,200 | 11,500 |
| Complaints resolved | 3,200 | 3,500 | 3,800 | 4,000 |
| Formal enforcement actions | 180 | 210 | 195 | 200 |
| Consent decrees | 45 | 52 | 48 | 50 |
| Lawsuits filed | 12 | 15 | 18 | 16 |
| Pending backlog | 3,800 | 4,100 | 4,500 | 4,800 |

*Sources: DOJ Civil Rights Division Annual Reports (FY 2021-2023); FY 2024 figures estimated from DOJ Budget Justification.*

### EEOC and Title I Enforcement

The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) enforces ADA Title I (employment). Detailed employment enforcement data is covered in [Employment Discrimination](../employment-discrimination/02-current-state.md), but key coordination issues include:

| Issue | Status |
|-------|--------|
| EEOC-DOJ referral process | Backlogged; pattern-or-practice cases languish |
| Dual-filed complaints | Create confusion and delay for complainants |
| Data sharing | Limited interagency data integration |
| Joint investigations | Rare; agencies operate in silos |

### Other Federal Agency Roles

| Agency | ADA Role | Enforcement Activity Level |
|--------|----------|---------------------------|
| Department of Education (OCR) | Title II in education settings | Moderate; complaint-driven |
| Department of Transportation | ADA transit requirements | Active rulemaking; limited enforcement |
| Federal Communications Commission | Title IV (telecommunications relay) | Narrow focus; generally adequate |
| Department of Health and Human Services | Section 504 and ADA in healthcare | Increasing activity post-COVID |
| Department of Housing and Urban Development | Fair Housing Act disability provisions | Active but separate from ADA |
| Access Board | Technical standards development | Advisory; no enforcement authority |

*Source: National Council on Disability, "Has the Promise Been Kept? Federal Enforcement of Disability Rights Laws" (2023).*

## The Private Enforcement System

### How ADA Litigation Works

**Title II (State and Local Government)**

- Plaintiffs can sue for injunctive relief and compensatory damages
- No requirement to exhaust administrative remedies
- Attorney fees available to prevailing plaintiffs under 42 U.S.C. Section 12205
- DOJ can intervene in private cases

**Title III (Public Accommodations)**

- Plaintiffs can sue for injunctive relief only (no compensatory damages under federal law)
- Attorney fees available to prevailing plaintiffs
- DOJ can seek civil penalties: up to $75,000 for first violation, $150,000 for subsequent violations (28 C.F.R. Section 36.504)
- Some states allow compensatory damages under state disability laws

### Judicial Limitations on Private Enforcement

| Doctrine | Effect on Enforcement | Key Cases |
|----------|----------------------|-----------|
| Standing requirements | Plaintiffs must show concrete injury and likelihood of return | *Laufer v. Acheson Hotels* (2023): Supreme Court vacated tester standing case |
| Mootness | Cases dismissed when defendant remedies violation during litigation | Frequently used to avoid attorney fees and precedent |
| Primary jurisdiction | Courts defer to agencies on compliance questions | Delays judicial resolution |
| Heightened pleading | Some circuits require specific barrier identification | Increases litigation costs for plaintiffs |
| "Readily achievable" defense | Businesses can avoid barrier removal if cost is high | Subjective standard favors defendants |

*Source: National Disability Rights Network, "Access to Justice Report" (2024).*

### The "Serial Litigant" Debate

**Business perspective:**

- A small number of plaintiffs and firms file hundreds or thousands of lawsuits
- Top 10 ADA plaintiffs filed over 3,000 cases combined (2018-2023)
- Lawsuits allegedly target small businesses for quick settlements
- Some plaintiffs have never visited the establishments they sue

**Disability rights perspective:**

- High-volume litigation reflects the scale of noncompliance, not abuse
- Without private enforcement, most violations would go unremedied
- Proposed restrictions (notice requirements, waiting periods) would eliminate enforcement for most barriers
- Business arguments parallel historical opposition to civil rights enforcement

| State | Response to "Serial Litigation" |
|-------|-------------------------------|
| California | Limited damages for small businesses; construction-related accessibility standards |
| Arizona | Pre-suit notice requirement (90 days) |
| Florida | Pre-suit notice requirement (15 business days) |
| Texas | Pre-suit notice and cure provisions |
| New York | Strong plaintiff protections; no pre-suit notice |

*Source: Disability Rights Advocates, "ADA Litigation and Reform" (2024).*

## Recent Developments

### DOJ Web Accessibility Rule (2024)

In April 2024, the DOJ issued a final rule under ADA Title II requiring state and local government entities to make their web content and mobile applications accessible. Key provisions:

| Element | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|
| Standard | WCAG 2.1 Level AA |
| Compliance deadline (large entities) | April 2026 |
| Compliance deadline (small entities) | April 2027 |
| Scope | Websites, mobile apps, web-based documents |
| Exceptions | Archived content, third-party content (with conditions) |

*Source: 89 Fed. Reg. 31,320 (April 24, 2024).*

**Title III web accessibility** remains without a formal DOJ rule, though courts increasingly apply WCAG standards. The DOJ withdrew its Title III ANPRM in 2017 and has not issued a new proposed rule.

### Supreme Court Developments

**Acheson Hotels v. Laufer (2023)**

- Court vacated case on mootness grounds without ruling on ADA tester standing
- Left unresolved whether individuals can test businesses for ADA compliance without intent to use services
- Created uncertainty for high-volume ADA enforcement plaintiffs

**Pending Issues**

- Circuit split on whether websites are "places of public accommodation" under Title III
- Unresolved questions about ADA application to emerging technologies (AI, autonomous vehicles)
- Potential cert petitions on emotional distress damages under Title II (*Cummings v. Premier Rehab Keller*, 2022: no emotional distress damages under Section 504/ADA funding provisions)

### Congressional Activity (119th Congress, 2025-2026)

| Bill | Purpose | Status | Outlook |
|------|---------|--------|---------|
| ADA Education and Reform Act | Pre-suit notice requirement for Title III | Reintroduced | Opposed by disability community; uncertain |
| Online Accessibility Act | Codify web accessibility standards | Introduced | Bipartisan support; committee stage |
| ADA Restoration Act | Strengthen enforcement tools and remedies | Introduced | Democratic priority; faces Republican opposition |
| IDEA Full Funding Act | Not ADA-specific but related | Committee | Bipartisan interest |

*Source: Congress.gov bill tracker; National Disability Rights Network legislative updates (2025).*

## Impact on People with Disabilities

### Barriers That Persist

**Physical Access**

- 40-60% of pre-ADA commercial buildings have unresolved barriers (U.S. Access Board, 2022)
- Many small businesses lack basic features: ramps, accessible restrooms, adequate signage
- Historic buildings often receive exemptions or waivers

**Digital Access**

- 96.3% of top one million website home pages had detectable WCAG 2.1 failures (WebAIM, 2024)
- Average of 56.8 errors per home page
- Most common failures: low contrast text, missing alternative text, empty links

**Government Services**

- Many state and local government websites remain inaccessible
- Online permitting, licensing, and benefit applications frequently exclude people with disabilities
- COVID-19 accelerated digital government but often without accessibility

**Healthcare**

- Medical diagnostic equipment (exam tables, scales, imaging) frequently inaccessible
- HHS issued ANPRM on medical equipment accessibility standards in 2023
- People with disabilities report being denied care or receiving substandard care due to inaccessible facilities

### Costs of Nonenforcement

| Impact | Scale | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Lost economic participation | $1.8 trillion in disposable income among disabled Americans | American Institutes for Research (2023) |
| Health disparities | People with disabilities 2-4x more likely to report unmet healthcare needs | CDC BRFSS (2023) |
| Social isolation | 1 in 4 adults with disabilities report social isolation | National Health Interview Survey (2023) |
| Employment gap | 22.5% employment rate for people with disabilities vs. 65.8% for people without | BLS Current Population Survey (2024) |

## Current Reform Landscape

### Active Advocacy Campaigns

**National Organizations**

- Disability Rights Education and Defense Fund (DREDF): Enforcement reform advocacy
- National Disability Rights Network (NDRN): Protection and advocacy system coordination
- American Association of People with Disabilities (AAPD): Grassroots mobilization
- Disability Rights Advocates (DRA): Impact litigation
- National Federation of the Blind (NFB): Digital accessibility enforcement
- National Association of the Deaf (NAD): Communication access litigation

**Key Priorities (2025)**

- Oppose ADA Education and Reform Act
- Support DOJ web accessibility rulemaking (Title III)
- Increase DOJ Disability Rights Section funding
- Codify web accessibility standards for private businesses
- Expand compensatory damages under Title III

| Issue | Support | Source |
|-------|---------|--------|

## Key Takeaways

1. **Enforcement gap is vast**: DOJ resolves a small fraction of ADA complaints, and the backlog continues to grow
2. **Private litigation fills the gap**: But judicial doctrines and legislative proposals threaten to restrict it
3. **Digital accessibility is the new frontier**: Web accessibility lawsuits now rival physical access cases
4. **Federal agencies are under-resourced**: The Disability Rights Section has approximately 50-60 attorneys to enforce a law covering 61 million Americans
5. **Compliance remains alarmingly low**: Most websites and many buildings fail to meet ADA standards
6. **Geographic disparities persist**: Enforcement and compliance vary dramatically by region
7. **Court decisions create uncertainty**: Key questions about standing, web accessibility scope, and damages remain unresolved
8. **Congressional threats loom**: The ADA Education and Reform Act would impose pre-suit notice requirements that advocates view as gutting enforcement
