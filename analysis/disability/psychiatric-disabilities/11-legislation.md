# Psychiatric Disabilities: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Psychiatric disability legislation must address the interconnected failures that produce mass incarceration, homelessness, unemployment, and premature death for people with serious mental illness. The legislative approach combines federal bills establishing community treatment infrastructure, strengthening ADA protections specific to psychiatric disabilities, reforming the criminal justice response to mental illness, modernizing disability benefits, and funding anti-stigma interventions. Constitutional amendments are not required for most reforms, though an Olmstead codification provision strengthens the constitutional foundation for community integration mandates.

## Constitutional Amendments

No constitutional amendments are required for the core psychiatric disability reform agenda. Congress has established authority to:

- Regulate employment practices affecting interstate commerce (Commerce Clause; ADA Title I)
- Enforce the equal protection and due process guarantees of the Fourteenth Amendment (ADA Title II and community integration mandates)
- Condition federal funding on compliance with disability rights requirements (Spending Clause; Section 504, Medicaid)
- Administer and reform federal benefit programs (SSDI, SSI, Medicaid)
- Fund public health programs (General Welfare Clause)

The Supreme Court's *Olmstead v. L.C.* (1999) established the community integration mandate under existing ADA authority. Codification through legislation would strengthen this foundation against future judicial narrowing.

## Federal Legislation

### Community Mental Health Infrastructure Act

**Purpose**: Permanently authorize and fund the Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinic (CCBHC) model as the foundation of the community mental health system, eliminate the Medicaid IMD exclusion, and establish a federal mental health infrastructure fund.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Community Mental Health
Infrastructure Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.
Congress finds that—
(1) 14.2 million American adults live with serious mental
    illness, and 55 percent of adults with mental illness did
    not receive treatment in the past year;
(2) the community mental health system envisioned by the
    Community Mental Health Act of 1963 was never built, and
    cumulative state mental health budget cuts since 2009
    exceed $5.6 billion in inflation-adjusted terms;
(3) Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinics have
    demonstrated reductions of 60 percent in emergency
    department visits, 40 percent in hospitalizations, and
    30 percent in criminal justice involvement among
    participants;
(4) the Medicaid exclusion for Institutions for Mental
    Diseases prevents Medicaid reimbursement for short-term
    psychiatric hospitalization in facilities with more than
    16 beds, contributing to acute bed shortages;
(5) 60 percent of United States counties have no practicing
    psychiatrist; and
(6) jails and prisons now house approximately 550,000
    people with serious mental illness, exceeding the state
    psychiatric hospital census by a factor of 15 to 1.

SEC. 3. PERMANENT CCBHC AUTHORIZATION.
(a) IN GENERAL.—Title XIX of the Social Security Act is
    amended to permanently establish Certified Community
    Behavioral Health Clinics as a distinct Medicaid provider
    type with Prospective Payment System reimbursement.
(b) REQUIRED SERVICES.—Each CCBHC shall provide, directly
    or through contractual arrangement—
    (1) crisis mental health services, including 24-hour
        mobile crisis teams;
    (2) outpatient mental health and substance use
        treatment;
    (3) psychiatric rehabilitation services;
    (4) peer support and counseling services;
    (5) supported employment services using the Individual
        Placement and Support model;
    (6) supported housing services, including tenancy
        support;
    (7) primary care screening and monitoring; and
    (8) services for members of the Armed Forces and
        veterans, as appropriate.
(c) ACCESSIBILITY.—Each CCBHC shall serve all individuals
    regardless of ability to pay, residency, or insurance
    status.
(d) EXPANSION TARGET.—The Secretary shall establish a grant
    program to expand the number of CCBHCs to not fewer than
    2,500 by fiscal year 2035.

SEC. 4. IMD EXCLUSION REFORM.
(a) Section 1905(a) of the Social Security Act is amended
    to permit Medicaid reimbursement for inpatient
    psychiatric services in institutions for mental diseases
    for stays of up to 30 days per admission and no more
    than 90 days per calendar year.
(b) Facilities receiving Medicaid reimbursement under this
    section shall—
    (1) be accredited by the Joint Commission or equivalent
        body;
    (2) comply with patient rights protections under 42
        C.F.R. Part 482;
    (3) demonstrate active discharge planning beginning at
        admission; and
    (4) maintain connections with community-based treatment
        providers.

SEC. 5. MENTAL HEALTH INFRASTRUCTURE FUND.
(a) There is established a Mental Health Infrastructure Fund
    within the Department of Health and Human Services.
(b) AUTHORIZED USES.—Funds shall be used for—
    (1) construction and renovation of community mental
        health facilities;
    (2) psychiatric workforce development, including loan
        repayment and training programs;
    (3) health information technology for mental health
        service integration; and
    (4) crisis stabilization center construction.
(c) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There are authorized
    to be appropriated $1,000,000,000 for each of fiscal
    years 2027 through 2036 for the Fund.

SEC. 6. PSYCHIATRIC WORKFORCE DEVELOPMENT.
(a) The Secretary shall expand the National Health Service
    Corps to include a dedicated mental health track
    providing loan repayment of up to $250,000 over 5 years
    for psychiatrists, psychologists, and psychiatric nurse
    practitioners serving in designated shortage areas.
(b) There are authorized 5,000 additional psychiatric
    residency positions funded through Medicare Graduate
    Medical Education.
(c) The Secretary shall establish a national certification
    and Medicaid reimbursement standard for peer specialists.
(d) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There are authorized
    to be appropriated $500,000,000 for each of fiscal years
    2027 through 2036 for workforce development under this
    section.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the central structural failure in psychiatric disability policy: the community mental health system that was promised but never built. CCBHCs provide the comprehensive, integrated service model that community mental health centers were supposed to deliver. IMD exclusion reform addresses the acute psychiatric bed shortage. The infrastructure fund provides capital for facility construction and workforce development.

**Potential Challenges**: Cost ($1.5 billion annually for infrastructure and workforce alone). State participation variability. Workforce development takes years to produce results. IMD exclusion reform requires careful safeguards against re-institutionalization.

**Refinements**: Prospective Payment System ensures financial sustainability for CCBHCs. IMD exclusion reform includes stay limits and discharge planning requirements to prevent long-term institutionalization. Expansion targets create accountability.

### Mental Health Crisis Response Act

**Purpose**: Establish a fully funded national mental health crisis response system as an alternative to law enforcement response to psychiatric emergencies.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Mental Health Crisis Response Act".

SEC. 2. NATIONAL CRISIS RESPONSE SYSTEM.
(a) IN GENERAL.—The Secretary of Health and Human Services,
    acting through the Administrator of SAMHSA, shall
    establish a national mental health crisis response system
    comprising—
    (1) the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline;
    (2) mobile crisis response teams;
    (3) crisis stabilization facilities; and
    (4) crisis receiving and stabilization services.
(b) 988 LIFELINE.—The 988 Lifeline shall—
    (1) operate 24 hours per day, 7 days per week, 365 days
        per year;
    (2) provide services by phone, text, and chat in English,
        Spanish, and other languages as determined by the
        Secretary;
    (3) achieve average response times of less than 60
        seconds; and
    (4) integrate with 911 dispatch systems to enable
        appropriate routing of mental health crisis calls.
(c) MOBILE CRISIS TEAMS.—
    (1) REQUIREMENT.—Each community with a population
        exceeding 50,000 shall have access to a mobile crisis
        team available 24 hours per day.
    (2) COMPOSITION.—Mobile crisis teams shall include
        licensed mental health professionals and certified
        peer specialists. Law enforcement participation shall
        be available upon request but not required.
    (3) RESPONSE TIME.—Mobile crisis teams shall achieve
        response times of less than 15 minutes in urban
        areas and less than 30 minutes in rural areas.
(d) CRISIS STABILIZATION FACILITIES.—
    (1) The Secretary shall fund the construction and
        operation of not fewer than 500 crisis stabilization
        facilities nationwide by fiscal year 2032.
    (2) Crisis stabilization facilities shall provide up to
        23 hours of observation and stabilization without
        requiring formal hospital admission.

SEC. 3. 911-988 INTEGRATION.
(a) The Federal Communications Commission, in coordination
    with SAMHSA, shall issue regulations requiring 911 call
    centers to implement protocols for identifying and
    routing mental health crisis calls to the 988 system.
(b) States shall receive grants to implement 911-988
    integration technology and training.

SEC. 4. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.
There are authorized to be appropriated—
(a) $3,000,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through
    2036 for the 988 Lifeline and crisis response system;
(b) $500,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2032
    for crisis stabilization facility construction; and
(c) $100,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2032
    for 911-988 integration grants.
```

**Explanation**: This bill replaces law enforcement as the default response to psychiatric crisis with a purpose-built crisis system. The 988 Lifeline provides the national entry point; mobile crisis teams provide on-scene response; crisis stabilization facilities provide short-term safe alternatives to emergency departments and jails.

**Potential Challenges**: $3 billion annual cost. Rural implementation challenges. 911-988 integration requires cooperation between federal, state, and local agencies. Workforce availability for mobile crisis teams.

**Refinements**: Population thresholds for mobile crisis teams ensure resources are directed where demand is highest. Response time standards create accountability. FCC involvement ensures technical integration with existing emergency systems.

### Psychiatric Disability Employment Equity Act

**Purpose**: Strengthen ADA protections specifically for psychiatric disabilities in employment and expand evidence-based supported employment.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Psychiatric Disability
Employment Equity Act".

SEC. 2. EEOC GUIDANCE AND ENFORCEMENT.
(a) The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission shall, within
    12 months of enactment, issue updated enforcement
    guidance on psychiatric disability under the Americans
    with Disabilities Act, addressing—
    (1) common reasonable accommodations for psychiatric
        disabilities, including flexible scheduling, remote
        work, modified supervision, sensory accommodations,
        and leave for treatment;
    (2) a rebuttable presumption that common psychiatric
        disability accommodations are reasonable, placing the
        burden on the employer to demonstrate undue hardship;
    (3) the application of the "direct threat" standard to
        psychiatric disabilities, requiring individualized,
        objective assessment based on current behavior and
        prohibiting reliance on diagnosis alone;
    (4) protections against retaliation for disclosing a
        psychiatric disability or requesting accommodation;
        and
    (5) confidentiality protections for psychiatric
        disability information disclosed during the
        accommodation process.
(b) The EEOC shall establish a dedicated psychiatric
    disability investigation unit with investigators trained
    in psychiatric disability issues.
(c) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated
    $25,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2032
    for EEOC psychiatric disability enforcement under this
    section.

SEC. 3. SUPPORTED EMPLOYMENT EXPANSION.
(a) The Secretary of Health and Human Services shall
    establish a national grant program to expand Individual
    Placement and Support (IPS) Supported Employment programs
    to not fewer than 2,000 programs by fiscal year 2032.
(b) State vocational rehabilitation agencies receiving
    federal funding shall offer IPS as the primary employment
    service for individuals with serious mental illness.
(c) The Secretary shall issue guidance permitting Medicaid
    reimbursement for IPS employment support as a mental
    health rehabilitation service.
(d) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated
    $200,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2032
    for IPS expansion under this section.

SEC. 4. EMPLOYER INCENTIVES.
(a) There is established a Psychiatric Disability Employment
    Tax Credit equal to 25 percent of wages paid during the
    first 2 years of employment, up to $5,000 per year, for
    employers who hire individuals with psychiatric
    disabilities referred through IPS or state vocational
    rehabilitation programs.
(b) The Small Business Administration shall provide
    technical assistance to small businesses on psychiatric
    disability accommodation.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the 22% employment rate for people with SMI by strengthening the legal framework (EEOC guidance, enforcement resources) and scaling the most effective employment intervention (IPS Supported Employment). The employer tax credit incentivizes hiring while the accommodation presumption reduces the burden on employees to justify common requests.

**Potential Challenges**: Employer opposition to accommodation presumption. Cost of IPS expansion. State vocational rehabilitation agency resistance to IPS mandate.

**Refinements**: The rebuttable presumption still allows employers to demonstrate undue hardship for specific accommodations. The tax credit incentivizes voluntary participation. IPS expansion is phased over 5 years.

### Psychiatric Disability Benefits Modernization Act

**Purpose**: Reform SSDI and SSI to accommodate the episodic nature of psychiatric disabilities and eliminate benefits cliffs that trap recipients in poverty.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Psychiatric Disability Benefits
Modernization Act".

SEC. 2. EPISODIC DISABILITY RECOGNITION.
(a) Title II and Title XVI of the Social Security Act are
    amended to establish an "episodic disability" category
    for individuals whose disability is characterized by
    periods of impairment alternating with periods of
    functional capacity.
(b) Individuals classified under the episodic disability
    category may—
    (1) suspend benefits during periods of employment
        without requiring a new disability determination
        upon return;
    (2) resume benefits within 60 days of cessation of
        employment due to recurrence of disability; and
    (3) maintain Medicaid or Medicare eligibility
        continuously regardless of employment status.

SEC. 3. BENEFITS OFFSET.
(a) For individuals receiving SSDI with a psychiatric
    disability, benefits shall be reduced by $1 for every $2
    earned above the Substantial Gainful Activity threshold,
    rather than terminated upon exceeding the threshold.
(b) The Trial Work Period for individuals with psychiatric
    disabilities shall be extended from 9 months to 24
    months.

SEC. 4. SSI MODERNIZATION.
(a) The SSI individual asset limit is increased from $2,000
    to $25,000, effective upon enactment, and shall be
    indexed to inflation annually thereafter.
(b) The SSI couple asset limit is increased from $3,000 to
    $37,500, effective upon enactment, and shall be indexed
    to inflation annually thereafter.
(c) Earned income disregards under SSI are increased from
    $65 to $400 per month, indexed to inflation annually.

SEC. 5. MEDICAID CONTINUITY.
(a) States participating in Medicaid shall provide a
    Medicaid buy-in option for individuals with psychiatric
    disabilities whose earnings exceed SSI or Medicaid
    eligibility limits.
(b) Premiums for Medicaid buy-in programs shall be set on a
    sliding scale based on income and shall not exceed 5
    percent of gross income.

SEC. 6. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.
There are authorized to be appropriated such sums as may be
necessary to carry out this Act.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the economic disincentives that trap people with psychiatric disabilities in poverty. The episodic disability category recognizes that psychiatric conditions fluctuate, allowing benefits to be suspended and resumed without the lengthy re-application process. The benefits offset eliminates the cliff, and SSI modernization updates rules that have not changed since 1989.

**Potential Challenges**: SSA implementation complexity. Cost concerns from fiscal conservatives (though studies show offsets from increased employment). Political difficulty of expanding entitlement programs.

**Refinements**: The $1-for-$2 offset was tested in the BOND demonstration and shown to increase earnings without increasing program costs. Asset limit increases are calibrated to inflation-adjusted 1989 levels plus a reasonable margin. Medicaid buy-in premium caps protect against hardship.

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Mental Health Crisis Response Act

**Purpose**: Establish state-level mental health crisis response systems with mobile crisis teams, crisis stabilization facilities, and 911-988 integration.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "[State] Mental Health Crisis
Response Act".

SECTION 2. CRISIS RESPONSE SYSTEM.
(a) The [State Mental Health Authority] shall establish a
    statewide mental health crisis response system
    comprising—
    (1) mobile crisis teams in every county or service area;
    (2) crisis stabilization facilities distributed to
        ensure access within 60 minutes of travel time for
        all residents;
    (3) integration between 911 dispatch and 988 crisis
        services.

SECTION 3. MOBILE CRISIS TEAMS.
(a) Mobile crisis teams shall be available 24 hours per day
    in all service areas with populations exceeding 25,000.
(b) Mobile crisis teams shall include at least one licensed
    mental health professional and one certified peer
    specialist.
(c) Law enforcement shall not be dispatched as the primary
    responder to mental health crisis calls unless there is
    an imminent threat of serious physical harm.

SECTION 4. CRISIS STABILIZATION.
(a) The [State] shall fund the construction and operation of
    crisis stabilization facilities sufficient to serve each
    region.
(b) No individual shall be charged for crisis stabilization
    services.
(c) Crisis stabilization facilities shall not be operated
    within correctional facilities.

SECTION 5. FUNDING.
This Act shall be funded through [state appropriation and/or
Medicaid matching funds and/or 988 implementation grants].
```

**Adaptations**: States can customize population thresholds, response time standards, and funding mechanisms based on geography and existing infrastructure. Rural states may establish regional crisis teams covering multiple counties.

### Model State Involuntary Treatment Reform Act

**Purpose**: Reform involuntary treatment laws to balance treatment access with individual rights, requiring voluntary community treatment as a prerequisite to involuntary intervention.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "[State] Psychiatric Treatment
Rights and Access Act".

SECTION 2. VOLUNTARY TREATMENT FIRST.
(a) No court shall order involuntary outpatient or inpatient
    treatment unless the petitioner demonstrates by clear and
    convincing evidence that—
    (1) voluntary community-based treatment was offered and
        the individual was unable or unwilling to engage; or
    (2) no voluntary treatment option is available within
        the individual's community within a reasonable time
        period.

SECTION 3. RIGHTS PROTECTIONS.
(a) Every individual subject to involuntary treatment
    proceedings shall have the right to—
    (1) representation by counsel at all proceedings;
    (2) an independent psychiatric evaluation;
    (3) judicial review within 72 hours of any involuntary
        hold;
    (4) periodic judicial review every 30 days for the
        duration of any involuntary order; and
    (5) a psychiatric advance directive that shall be
        followed to the maximum extent consistent with
        safety.
(b) Involuntary treatment orders shall not result in loss
    of voting rights, parental rights, or other civil rights
    beyond those directly related to the treatment order.

SECTION 4. DATA COLLECTION.
The [State Mental Health Authority] shall collect and publish
annual data on involuntary treatment orders including
demographics, duration, outcomes, and racial and ethnic
disparities.
```

**Adaptations**: States with existing AOT (Assisted Outpatient Treatment) programs can integrate these protections into current frameworks. States without AOT can adopt this model as a stand-alone reform.

## Regulatory Framework

### EEOC Updated Enforcement Guidance on Psychiatric Disabilities

**Existing Authority**: ADA Section 107 authorizes EEOC to issue substantive regulations and guidance.

**Key Provisions**:

- Rebuttable presumption that common psychiatric accommodations (flexible scheduling, remote work, modified break schedule, quiet workspace, temporary leave) are reasonable
- Prohibition on "direct threat" determinations based on diagnosis or stereotypes; requirement for individualized, objective assessment
- Confidentiality standards for psychiatric disability information disclosed during accommodation process
- Guidance on episodic conditions: employers cannot penalize absences or performance fluctuations that are disability-related and subject to accommodation
- Anti-retaliation protections: documentation requirements for any adverse action taken within 12 months of accommodation request

### CMS Medicaid Guidance on Community Mental Health Services

**Existing Authority**: Medicaid state plan amendment and waiver authority.

**Key Provisions**:

- Guidance permitting Medicaid reimbursement for peer specialist services as a distinct provider category
- State plan amendment template for IPS Supported Employment as a Medicaid rehabilitation service
- Guidance on Medicaid coverage of crisis stabilization services in non-hospital settings
- Home and Community-Based Services (HCBS) waiver template for psychiatric disability community integration services

### HUD Guidance on Psychiatric Disability Fair Housing

**Existing Authority**: Fair Housing Act enforcement authority.

**Key Provisions**:

- Updated guidance on reasonable accommodation for psychiatric disabilities in housing, including emotional support animals, flexible lease terms, and modified behavioral rules
- Prohibition on housing discrimination based on mental health treatment history, medication use, or prior hospitalization
- Guidance on landlord obligations when tenants experience psychiatric crisis
- Strengthened enforcement protocols for psychiatric disability fair housing complaints

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

The legislative proposals operate within well-established constitutional authority. The ADA's coverage of psychiatric disabilities has been upheld repeatedly since 1990. *Olmstead v. L.C.* (1999) confirmed that unjustified institutionalization violates the ADA. The ADAAA (2008) restored broad coverage for episodic psychiatric conditions. SSDI/SSI reform operates under Congress's plenary authority over federal benefits programs. Medicaid reforms use the Spending Clause to condition federal funds. Crisis response funding uses the general welfare power.

The most significant constitutional issue involves involuntary treatment reform. The Supreme Court has held that civil commitment requires "clear and convincing evidence" (*Addington v. Texas*, 1979) and that non-dangerous individuals capable of community living cannot be confined (*O'Connor v. Donaldson*, 1975). State involuntary treatment reforms must comply with these due process requirements.

### Preemption Questions

Federal legislation should establish a floor for psychiatric disability protections, not a ceiling. States should remain free to provide stronger protections, including broader involuntary treatment rights protections, more generous benefits, and more comprehensive crisis response systems. The proposed legislation includes explicit anti-preemption clauses.

### Enforcement Mechanisms

The proposed framework uses four complementary enforcement mechanisms:

1. **EEOC enforcement** of updated psychiatric disability guidance with dedicated investigation unit
2. **DOJ Olmstead enforcement** through civil rights investigations and settlements
3. **HUD fair housing enforcement** with strengthened psychiatric disability complaint handling
4. **Private right of action** through existing ADA, Fair Housing Act, and Section 504 litigation

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- CCBHC expansion progress reviewed by GAO every 3 years
- 988 crisis system performance evaluated annually by SAMHSA
- EEOC psychiatric disability guidance reviewed every 5 years
- SSDI/SSI episodic disability provisions reviewed by SSA after 5 years of implementation
- State involuntary treatment data published annually for public review

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Community Mental Health Infrastructure Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| CCBHC scope narrowing | States may certify CCBHCs that do not provide the full range of required services, meeting technical requirements while failing to deliver comprehensive care | High |
| IMD exclusion workaround | The 30-day/90-day stay limits may be circumvented through revolving admissions (discharge and readmission cycles) | Medium |
| Workforce diversion | Loan repayment funds may be used by providers who leave shortage areas after the minimum service period | Medium |
| Prospective payment gaming | CCBHCs may adjust patient mix to maximize reimbursement while underserving high-need populations | Medium |
| Rural coverage gaps | Expansion targets may be met by concentrating CCBHCs in urban areas while leaving rural areas underserved | High |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Workforce pipeline lag | Treatment expansion requires workforce that takes years to develop | Training timeline inherent in healthcare professions |
| State participation variability | Not all states will participate equally, creating geographic disparities | Federalism; cannot mandate state participation |
| Housing integration gap | Treatment infrastructure without housing leaves patients unstable | Separate housing and healthcare funding streams |
| Dual diagnosis complexity | Co-occurring substance use disorders require integrated treatment that not all CCBHCs provide effectively | Workforce training gaps; separate licensing systems |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. CCBHC certification should require annual demonstration of full-service delivery, with decertification for failure to meet standards
2. IMD exclusion reform should include an annual utilization cap per patient to prevent revolving-door admissions
3. NHSC loan repayment should require a 5-year service commitment with pro-rated repayment for early departure
4. Prospective payment rates should be risk-adjusted to incentivize serving high-need populations
5. Expansion targets should include rural-specific goals (minimum of one CCBHC per 100,000 population in rural areas)
6. A cross-agency housing-treatment integration mandate should require CCBHCs to partner with local housing authorities

### Mental Health Crisis Response Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Law enforcement override | Police may continue responding to mental health calls despite 911-988 routing protocols, citing safety concerns | High |
| Staffing substitution | Mobile crisis teams may be staffed with undertrained personnel who do not meet the licensed professional/peer specialist requirement | Medium |
| Crisis stabilization as gatekeeping | Facilities may be used to detain individuals involuntarily without adequate rights protections | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Rural response times | 30-minute rural response time may not be achievable in remote areas | Geography and workforce distribution |
| After-hours coverage | 24/7 mobile crisis teams are expensive and difficult to staff in smaller communities | Workforce and cost constraints |
| Follow-up care gaps | Crisis response without connection to ongoing treatment creates a revolving door | Fragmented service systems |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. 911-988 protocols should include clear criteria for law enforcement dispatch, with mental health team dispatch as the default for calls without imminent physical danger
2. Mobile crisis team staffing requirements should be enforced through SAMHSA grant conditions with regular compliance audits
3. Crisis stabilization facilities should be subject to the same rights protections as involuntary psychiatric holds, including judicial review and right to counsel
4. Crisis teams should be required to provide warm handoffs to community treatment within 48 hours of crisis contact

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Advocacy community divisions | Inclusive policy development with representation from both treatment and rights perspectives |
| Stigma undermining implementation | National anti-stigma campaign running concurrently with system reforms |
| Political vulnerability of mental health funding | Bipartisan framing (crisis response, criminal justice savings, employment) to build durable coalitions |
| Data gaps | Mandatory federal data collection on psychiatric disability outcomes across all systems |
| Implementation variability across states | Federal standards with flexibility in implementation, combined with DOJ enforcement |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- CCBHC expansion progress: GAO review every 3 years
- 988 system performance: annual SAMHSA evaluation with public reporting
- IPS expansion: DOL evaluation of employment outcomes at 3 and 5 years
- SSDI/SSI reforms: SSA report to Congress at 5 years with cost-benefit analysis
- Involuntary treatment data: annual state reporting to SAMHSA with demographic analysis
- Anti-stigma campaign: independent evaluation at 5 and 10 years measuring attitude change
