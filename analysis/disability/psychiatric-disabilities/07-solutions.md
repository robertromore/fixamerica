# Psychiatric Disabilities: Solutions

## Overview

Addressing the systemic failures faced by people with psychiatric disabilities requires a multi-layered approach spanning community mental health investment, criminal justice diversion, employment and housing reform, anti-stigma interventions, legal framework updates, and benefits modernization. No single reform will reverse decades of disinvestment and criminalization; the proposals below are organized from highest-impact structural reforms to targeted interventions, with each building on the others to create a self-reinforcing system of community support and civil rights protection.

## Community Mental Health System Solutions

### 1. Fully Fund Community-Based Treatment Infrastructure

**Problem addressed**: The community mental health system promised by the 1963 Community Mental Health Act was never built. Only 789 of 1,500 planned centers were constructed, and cumulative state budget cuts since 2009 total $5.6 billion (inflation-adjusted).

**Proposal**: Establish a federal-state partnership to build a comprehensive community mental health infrastructure, modeled on the Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinic (CCBHC) program.

**Key elements**:

- Permanently authorize and expand CCBHCs to all 50 states, requiring Medicaid prospective payment to ensure financial sustainability
- Fund 1,500 additional CCBHCs by 2035, ensuring every county has access to comprehensive community mental health services
- Require CCBHCs to provide the full range of services: crisis stabilization, outpatient treatment, psychiatric rehabilitation, peer support, supported employment, and supported housing
- Eliminate the Medicaid IMD exclusion (Institutions for Mental Diseases) to allow Medicaid payment for short-term psychiatric hospitalization in larger facilities
- Establish a federal Mental Health Infrastructure Fund of $10 billion over 10 years for facility construction, workforce development, and technology

**Evidence**: CCBHCs have demonstrated reduced emergency department visits (60%), reduced hospitalizations (40%), and reduced criminal justice involvement (30%) in evaluation studies (SAMHSA, 2024). States participating in the CCBHC demonstration have shown improved access and outcomes across multiple measures.

**Estimated impact**: Would provide comprehensive community treatment access for an additional 5-8 million people with serious mental illness and co-occurring disorders.

### 2. Build a National Crisis Response System

**Problem addressed**: Only 6% of communities have non-police mental health crisis response teams, and people with mental illness are 16 times more likely to be killed during police encounters.

**Proposal**: Establish a national mental health crisis response system through full funding and expansion of the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, coupled with community-based mobile crisis teams and crisis stabilization facilities.

**Key elements**:

- Fund 988 at $3 billion annually (current funding: $502 million) to ensure adequate call center capacity and follow-up services
- Require every community to have a mobile crisis team available 24/7, staffed by mental health professionals and peer specialists
- Fund 500 crisis stabilization units nationwide (23-hour and short-stay facilities) as alternatives to emergency departments and jails
- Establish crisis response standards through SAMHSA, requiring response times comparable to EMS (under 15 minutes in urban areas, under 30 minutes in rural areas)
- Integrate 988 with 911 dispatch systems to enable appropriate routing of mental health calls

**Evidence**: The CAHOOTS program (Eugene, Oregon) responded to 24,000 calls in 2019 with no use of force and only 150 requiring police backup. Similar programs in Denver, Houston, and New York have demonstrated reduced use of force, reduced arrests, and reduced emergency department visits.

### 3. Expand the Psychiatric Workforce

**Problem addressed**: 60% of U.S. counties have no practicing psychiatrist; the mental health workforce is insufficient to meet demand at every level.

**Proposal**: Implement a comprehensive psychiatric workforce development strategy addressing psychiatrists, psychologists, social workers, counselors, peer specialists, and psychiatric nurses.

**Key elements**:

- Expand the National Health Service Corps to include dedicated mental health tracks with enhanced loan repayment ($250,000 over 5 years for psychiatrists serving in shortage areas)
- Fund 5,000 new psychiatric residency positions through Graduate Medical Education reform
- Create a federal certification and payment standard for peer specialists, recognizing lived experience as a professional qualification
- Expand telehealth flexibilities for psychiatric services permanently, including cross-state licensing compacts
- Fund training programs for community health workers to provide basic mental health screening and navigation

**Evidence**: States that have invested in peer specialist programs (Georgia, Texas, Pennsylvania) have demonstrated improved treatment engagement, reduced hospitalization, and improved recovery outcomes (SAMHSA, 2023).

## Criminal Justice Diversion Solutions

### 4. Divert People with Mental Illness from the Criminal Justice System

**Problem addressed**: Approximately 550,000 people with serious mental illness are incarcerated in jails and prisons, most for nonviolent offenses driven by untreated illness.

**Proposal**: Establish a comprehensive diversion system that intercepts people with psychiatric disabilities at every stage of criminal justice involvement.

**Key elements**:

- **Pre-arrest diversion**: Fund mobile crisis teams and co-responder programs in every jurisdiction with populations above 50,000
- **Pre-booking diversion**: Establish crisis triage centers where police can bring individuals in mental health crisis instead of jail (modeled on San Antonio's Restoration Center)
- **Post-booking diversion**: Require mental health screening at jail intake and create mental health courts in every judicial district
- **Reentry support**: Fund Forensic Assertive Community Treatment (FACT) teams to provide intensive support for people with SMI leaving jails and prisons
- **Decriminalize survival behaviors**: Eliminate criminal penalties for low-level offenses (trespassing, disorderly conduct, public intoxication) that primarily affect people experiencing homelessness and mental health crisis

**Evidence**: The Stepping Up Initiative (National Association of Counties) has demonstrated 15-30% reductions in jail populations of people with SMI in participating counties. Mental health courts reduce recidivism by 20% compared to traditional courts (SAMHSA, 2023).

### 5. Reform Involuntary Treatment with Rights Protections

**Problem addressed**: The tension between treatment access and individual autonomy creates a policy impasse that results in both untreated illness and coercive practices.

**Proposal**: Reform involuntary treatment laws to ensure that expanded treatment authority is accompanied by robust civil rights protections and that voluntary community treatment is always the first option.

**Key elements**:

- Require that community-based voluntary treatment options be exhausted before any involuntary intervention
- Establish a right to counsel in all civil commitment proceedings (currently not guaranteed in most states)
- Require judicial review every 72 hours for involuntary holds (many states allow longer holds without review)
- Fund supported decision-making programs as alternatives to guardianship and court-ordered treatment
- Prohibit involuntary treatment from resulting in loss of civil rights (voting, parenting, firearms) beyond the period of the treatment order
- Require data collection and public reporting on involuntary treatment demographics, outcomes, and racial disparities

**Evidence**: States that have invested in voluntary community treatment (e.g., Connecticut's intensive outpatient programs) have reduced involuntary commitment rates while maintaining or improving outcomes (Phelan et al., 2022).

## Employment Solutions

### 6. Strengthen ADA Protections for Psychiatric Disabilities

**Problem addressed**: People with serious mental illness have a 22% employment rate (vs. 65% for the general population), and EEOC data shows persistent accommodation denials and workplace discrimination.

**Proposal**: Update EEOC guidance and ADA enforcement to address the unique challenges of psychiatric disability in the workplace.

**Key elements**:

- Issue comprehensive EEOC guidance on psychiatric disability accommodation, including specific guidance on flexible scheduling, remote work, modified supervision, and sensory accommodations
- Establish a presumption that common psychiatric accommodations (flexible scheduling, modified break schedule, quiet workspace) are reasonable, shifting the burden to employers to demonstrate undue hardship
- Prohibit employer use of "direct threat" assessments based on diagnosis alone; require individualized, objective assessment based on current behavior
- Protect employees from retaliation for disclosing psychiatric disabilities or requesting accommodation
- Increase EEOC psychiatric disability investigation resources with dedicated investigators trained in psychiatric disability issues

**Evidence**: JAN data shows that psychiatric accommodations are among the least costly (average $0-$500) and produce positive ROI through reduced turnover and improved productivity. Supported Employment (Individual Placement and Support) achieves competitive employment rates of 55-65% for people with SMI, compared to 20-25% in traditional vocational rehabilitation (Bond et al., 2023).

### 7. Scale Supported Employment (IPS)

**Problem addressed**: Traditional vocational rehabilitation achieves only 20-25% competitive employment for people with SMI, while the Individual Placement and Support (IPS) model achieves 55-65%.

**Proposal**: Make IPS Supported Employment available to every person with serious mental illness who wants to work.

**Key elements**:

- Require state vocational rehabilitation agencies to offer IPS as the first-line employment service for people with psychiatric disabilities
- Fund IPS expansion through Medicaid waiver authority, making employment support a reimbursable mental health service
- Establish 2,000 IPS programs nationwide (current: approximately 700)
- Create employer incentive programs (tax credits, technical assistance) for hiring and accommodating people with psychiatric disabilities
- Reform SSDI/SSI work incentives to eliminate benefits cliffs (see Solution 10)

**Evidence**: Over 30 randomized controlled trials across 14 countries demonstrate IPS superiority over traditional vocational rehabilitation for people with SMI. The model has been endorsed by SAMHSA, NAMI, and the Cochrane Collaboration.

## Housing Solutions

### 8. Expand Permanent Supportive Housing

**Problem addressed**: 195,000-230,000 people with serious mental illness experience homelessness, and psychiatric disability is the most common basis for fair housing complaints.

**Proposal**: Scale permanent supportive housing (PSH) and strengthen fair housing enforcement for people with psychiatric disabilities.

**Key elements**:

- Fund 150,000 new permanent supportive housing units over 10 years through HUD and Medicaid partnerships
- Integrate mental health services with housing through Medicaid-funded tenancy support services
- Strengthen fair housing enforcement for psychiatric disability complaints, including dedicated investigators and increased penalties
- Prohibit housing discrimination based on mental health treatment history, medication use, or prior hospitalization
- Fund housing first programs that provide immediate housing without treatment compliance prerequisites

**Evidence**: Housing First programs reduce homelessness by 80-90% for participants and reduce emergency department visits and hospitalizations by 50-60% (Tsemberis, 2010). Permanent supportive housing produces net savings of $8,000-$15,000 per person per year through reduced emergency and criminal justice costs (Urban Institute, 2023).

## Anti-Stigma Solutions

### 9. Launch a National Anti-Stigma Campaign

**Problem addressed**: Stigma against psychiatric disabilities is a structural force that undermines policy, enforcement, and individual outcomes across every domain.

**Proposal**: Fund a sustained, evidence-based national anti-stigma campaign combining public education, media reform, and contact-based interventions.

**Key elements**:

- Fund a 10-year national anti-stigma campaign at $200 million annually, modeled on successful international campaigns (UK's Time to Change, Australia's Beyond Blue, Canada's Opening Minds)
- Prioritize contact-based interventions (programs where people with lived experience share their stories) over education-only approaches, as research shows contact-based programs produce stronger and more durable attitude change
- Develop media guidelines for reporting on mental illness (expanding AP Stylebook guidance) and establish a monitoring program for media portrayals
- Require anti-stigma training for key professionals: law enforcement, judges, healthcare providers, employers, and housing providers
- Fund peer-led organizations to develop and deliver anti-stigma programming in their communities
- Target specific forms of stigma: dangerousness assumptions, competence doubts, and social distance preferences

**Evidence**: The UK's Time to Change campaign (2007-2021) achieved a 10-percentage-point improvement in public attitudes toward mental illness and a 5-percentage-point reduction in reported discrimination. Contact-based anti-stigma programs produce larger effect sizes than education-only programs (Corrigan et al., 2021).

## Benefits Reform Solutions

### 10. Modernize SSDI/SSI for Psychiatric Disabilities

**Problem addressed**: SSDI/SSI rules create benefits cliffs that trap people with psychiatric disabilities in poverty and discourage employment. The episodic nature of psychiatric conditions is poorly accommodated by rules designed for stable conditions.

**Proposal**: Reform SSDI and SSI to accommodate the episodic nature of psychiatric disabilities and eliminate benefits cliffs.

**Key elements**:

- Establish an "episodic disability" category in SSDI/SSI that allows benefits to be suspended and resumed without full re-application when a person's condition fluctuates
- Extend the SSDI Trial Work Period from 9 months to 24 months for psychiatric disabilities
- Raise the SSI asset limit from $2,000 to $25,000 and index to inflation
- Delink Medicaid eligibility from SSI income limits so that working does not result in loss of mental health treatment
- Create a gradual benefits offset ($1 reduction for every $2 earned above the disregard) instead of the current cliff structure
- Expand Medicaid buy-in programs for working people with psychiatric disabilities in all states

**Evidence**: The Benefit Offset National Demonstration (BOND) showed that a $1-for-$2 offset increased earnings among SSDI recipients without increasing program costs (SSA, 2022). Current SSI asset limits have not been updated since 1989 and would be over $10,000 if adjusted for inflation.
