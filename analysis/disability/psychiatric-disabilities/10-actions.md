# Psychiatric Disabilities: Actions

## Overview

This document outlines concrete actions that individuals, organizations, and communities can take to advance the rights and well-being of people with psychiatric disabilities. Actions are organized by actor type and effort level, from immediate individual steps to sustained organizational campaigns.

## Individual Actions

### For All Citizens

#### Immediate (Today)

- **Learn about psychiatric disability rights**: Read the Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law's "Know Your Rights" guides (free) at https://www.bazelon.org
- **Save 988 in your phone**: The 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline is the national mental health crisis number. Knowing it can save a life.
- **Contact elected officials**: Ask your congressional representative to support permanent CCBHC authorization and full 988 funding
- **Challenge stigma in conversation**: When you hear someone use mental illness as an insult or associate mental illness with violence, offer a factual correction

#### Short-Term (This Month)

- **Take a Mental Health First Aid course**: The 8-hour course teaches how to recognize and respond to mental health crises. Find a course at https://www.mentalhealthfirstaid.org
- **Support community mental health**: Donate to or volunteer with a local community mental health center, NAMI affiliate, or peer-run organization
- **Attend a NAMI support group or education program**: NAMI chapters offer free Family-to-Family, Peer-to-Peer, and support group programs in most communities
- **Review your workplace policies**: Check whether your employer's reasonable accommodation process addresses psychiatric disability and whether anti-discrimination training includes mental health

#### Ongoing

- **Follow psychiatric disability advocacy organizations**: NAMI, Bazelon Center, Mental Health America, and DBSA provide action alerts on legislation and policy
- **Amplify lived experience voices**: Share content from people with psychiatric disabilities who write and speak about their experiences and policy priorities
- **Vote for candidates who support mental health reform**: Evaluate candidates' positions on mental health funding, criminal justice diversion, and disability rights enforcement
- **Support anti-stigma efforts**: Participate in mental health awareness events, share accurate information about mental illness, and model openness about mental health

### For People with Psychiatric Disabilities

#### Asserting Your Rights

- **Know your employment rights**: The ADA protects people with psychiatric disabilities from employment discrimination and entitles you to reasonable accommodation. The JAN (Job Accommodation Network) provides free, confidential guidance at https://askjan.org or 1-800-526-7234
- **Know your housing rights**: The Fair Housing Act prohibits housing discrimination based on psychiatric disability and entitles you to reasonable accommodation, including emotional support animals. File complaints with HUD at https://www.hud.gov/program_offices/fair_housing_equal_opp/online-complaint
- **Document discrimination**: Keep records of accommodation requests and denials, discriminatory statements, and adverse actions. This documentation is essential for complaints and legal action.
- **Contact your state Protection and Advocacy (P&A) organization**: Every state has a federally funded P&A system that provides free legal assistance for disability rights issues. Find yours at https://ndrn.org
- **Request your records**: You have a right to access your mental health treatment records and to request corrections to inaccurate information
- **Know your rights regarding involuntary treatment**: State laws vary, but you generally have the right to counsel, the right to refuse medication (with exceptions), and the right to judicial review. Your P&A organization can explain your state's laws.

#### Advocacy

- **Share your story**: Personal stories from people with psychiatric disabilities are the most powerful tool for changing public attitudes (contact-based anti-stigma research confirms this). NAMI, MHA, and state advocacy organizations facilitate storytelling programs.
- **Testify at public hearings**: Your lived experience is expert testimony. State legislatures, city councils, and federal agencies hold hearings on mental health policy where your voice matters.
- **Join a peer-led organization**: Peer-run organizations (DBSA chapters, Hearing Voices groups, psychiatric survivor organizations) provide community and advocacy opportunities
- **Serve on advisory boards**: Mental health agencies, hospitals, and community organizations increasingly seek consumer/peer representation on advisory boards. Apply for these positions.
- **Participate in research**: Universities and advocacy organizations conduct research on psychiatric disability policy and treatment. Your participation shapes better evidence.

## Professional Actions

### For Mental Health Professionals

#### Immediate

- **Learn about rights-based approaches**: Read the Bazelon Center and SAMHSA materials on recovery-oriented, rights-respecting practice
- **Ask patients about their accommodation needs**: Many people with psychiatric disabilities have never been asked what accommodations would help them in treatment, employment, or housing
- **Use person-first or identity-first language as preferred by your patients**: Ask which terminology individuals prefer (person with a mental illness, person with a psychiatric disability, psychiatric survivor, etc.)
- **Screen for social determinants**: Ask about housing stability, employment, criminal justice involvement, and benefits -- not just symptoms

#### Short-Term

- **Get trained in evidence-based supported employment (IPS)**: If you work with people with SMI, the IPS model is the most effective employment intervention. Training is available through the IPS Employment Center (https://ipsworks.org)
- **Advocate for parity within your organization**: Review whether your practice or hospital applies more restrictive treatment limitations to mental health than physical health, and raise concerns through quality improvement channels
- **Develop advance directive capacity**: Help patients create psychiatric advance directives that document their treatment preferences in case of future crisis when they may not be able to communicate

#### Ongoing

- **Support peer specialist integration**: Advocate for hiring peer specialists within your organization and treat them as valued professional colleagues
- **Challenge clinical stigma**: Address negative attitudes toward people with psychiatric disabilities within your professional community
- **Engage in policy advocacy**: Professional organizations (APA, APA, NASW) have advocacy arms. Use your professional credibility to support mental health policy reform.

### For Employers

#### Immediate

- **Review accommodation policies**: Ensure your reasonable accommodation process specifically addresses psychiatric disability, including flexible scheduling, remote work, modified supervision, sensory accommodations, and leave for treatment
- **Train managers on psychiatric disability**: Managers are the front line of accommodation. Training should cover legal obligations, common accommodations, and anti-stigma messaging.
- **Remove mental health screening questions from hiring**: Do not ask about mental health history, medication use, or hospitalization during the hiring process (this violates the ADA)

#### Short-Term

- **Implement an Employee Assistance Program (EAP)**: EAPs provide confidential mental health support and can serve as an early intervention tool
- **Adopt a return-to-work protocol for mental health leave**: Develop a supportive process for employees returning from psychiatric hospitalization or extended leave that focuses on accommodation rather than surveillance
- **Partner with IPS programs**: Contact your state vocational rehabilitation agency or local CCBHC about hosting IPS employment specialists in your workplace

#### Ongoing

- **Track accommodation outcomes**: Monitor accommodation requests, approvals, denials, and outcomes by disability type to identify patterns of psychiatric disability discrimination
- **Participate in employer networks**: Disability:IN (https://disabilityin.org) and the Employer Assistance and Resource Network on Disability Inclusion (EARN) provide resources and peer support for inclusive employers
- **Champion mental health at leadership level**: Executive-level commitment to mental health inclusion signals organizational values and reduces stigma

### For Law Enforcement

#### Immediate

- **Complete Crisis Intervention Team (CIT) training**: The 40-hour CIT program teaches officers to recognize mental health crises and de-escalate safely. Contact the CIT International center (https://citinternational.org)
- **Learn about 988**: Know how to connect individuals in mental health crisis with the 988 Lifeline instead of defaulting to arrest
- **Stop criminalizing homelessness and mental illness**: Exercise discretion to avoid arresting people for survival offenses (trespassing, disorderly conduct) when the behavior is driven by mental illness and homelessness

#### Short-Term

- **Support co-responder programs**: Advocate within your department for co-responder models pairing officers with mental health clinicians
- **Build relationships with local mental health providers**: Develop referral pathways to crisis stabilization centers and community mental health centers
- **Review use-of-force policies**: Ensure policies address interactions with individuals in mental health crisis, including prohibitions on escalation tactics

### For Educators

#### In the Classroom

- **Teach about psychiatric disabilities**: Include psychiatric disability in diversity, equity, and inclusion curricula. Social work, nursing, law, criminal justice, and public policy programs should address psychiatric disability rights.
- **Use accessible and trauma-informed teaching practices**: Recognize that students with psychiatric disabilities may need accommodations (extended deadlines, attendance flexibility, reduced-distraction testing environments)
- **Invite speakers with lived experience**: Contact NAMI, DBSA, or local peer organizations to arrange classroom presentations by people with psychiatric disabilities

#### Institutional Advocacy

- **Strengthen campus mental health services**: Advocate for adequate staffing of campus counseling centers (recommended ratio: 1 counselor per 1,000-1,500 students)
- **Improve campus disability services for psychiatric conditions**: Many campus disability offices are better equipped for physical and learning disabilities than psychiatric disabilities. Advocate for staff training and process improvements.
- **Support psychiatric disability research**: Contribute to the academic literature on psychiatric disability policy, treatment, and outcomes

## Community and Organizational Actions

### For Disability Rights Organizations

- **Include psychiatric disability in cross-disability advocacy**: Ensure that ADA enforcement, Olmstead compliance, and disability rights campaigns explicitly address psychiatric disability
- **Bridge the treatment-rights divide**: Create dialogue spaces where treatment advocacy and rights advocacy organizations can find common ground
- **Train advocates on psychiatric disability issues**: Provide training on the unique legal, policy, and practical challenges of psychiatric disability
- **Monitor Olmstead compliance**: Track state progress on community integration for people with psychiatric disabilities and report findings publicly
- **Support peer-led organizations**: Provide funding, technical assistance, and platform access to organizations led by people with psychiatric disabilities

### For State and Local Government

- **Fund community mental health services**: Increase state mental health budgets to at least pre-2009 levels (inflation-adjusted)
- **Establish or expand mental health courts**: Mental health courts reduce recidivism and connect people with treatment instead of incarceration
- **Implement 988 and crisis response systems**: Develop state 988 plans, fund crisis stabilization centers, and establish mobile crisis teams
- **Enforce Olmstead**: Develop and implement comprehensive Olmstead plans with measurable benchmarks for community integration
- **Reform zoning to allow community treatment facilities**: Eliminate zoning restrictions that prevent community mental health facilities and supportive housing in residential neighborhoods
- **End criminalization of mental illness**: Reform local ordinances that criminalize behaviors associated with mental illness and homelessness (loitering, trespassing, disorderly conduct)

### For Healthcare Organizations

- **Achieve parity compliance**: Conduct a self-audit of non-quantitative treatment limitations and ensure mental health services are not subject to more restrictive criteria than comparable physical health services
- **Integrate psychiatric and physical healthcare**: People with psychiatric disabilities die 15-25 years earlier than the general population, largely from treatable physical conditions. Integrated care addresses both.
- **Hire peer specialists**: Integrate peer specialists into care teams in hospitals, clinics, and emergency departments
- **Adopt trauma-informed practices**: Train all staff in trauma-informed care principles, recognizing that many people with psychiatric disabilities have experienced trauma, including within the mental health system
