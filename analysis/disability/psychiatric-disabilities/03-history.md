# Psychiatric Disabilities: History

## The Institutional Era (1750s-1960s)

### Colonial and Early American Approaches

In colonial America, people with psychiatric conditions were cared for by families, confined in poorhouses, or placed in jails. There was no concept of mental illness as a treatable condition, and no distinction between psychiatric disability and other forms of social deviance.

- 1752: Pennsylvania Hospital opens the first dedicated ward for people with mental illness (Philadelphia)
- 1773: Eastern State Hospital (Williamsburg, Virginia) becomes the first freestanding psychiatric hospital in America
- Early 1800s: Dorothea Dix campaigns for humane treatment and state-funded asylums

### The Asylum Movement (1840s-1950s)

Dorothea Dix's advocacy led to the construction of state psychiatric hospitals across the country. Initially conceived as therapeutic environments, these institutions rapidly became overcrowded warehouses.

**Growth of Institutionalization**

| Year | State Hospital Census | Per Capita Rate (per 100,000) |
|------|----------------------|-------------------------------|
| 1850 | 4,700 | 20 |
| 1903 | 150,000 | 190 |
| 1940 | 420,000 | 320 |
| 1955 | 559,000 | 337 (peak) |
| 1970 | 310,000 | 150 |
| 1990 | 72,000 | 29 |
| 2010 | 43,000 | 14 |
| 2023 | 35,000 | 11 |

*Sources: Grob, Gerald N. *The Mad Among Us* (1994); Treatment Advocacy Center (2023).*

**Conditions in State Hospitals**

- Overcrowding: By the 1940s, many hospitals held double or triple their intended capacity
- Abuse: Physical restraints, solitary confinement, and physical abuse were routine
- Lack of treatment: Most patients received custodial care, not therapy
- Forced labor: Patients performed unpaid work maintaining the institutions
- Forced sterilization: Over 60,000 institutionalized people were forcibly sterilized under eugenics laws; *Buck v. Bell* (1927) upheld the practice

### Exposes and Reform (1940s-1960s)

A series of journalistic exposes and advocacy campaigns drew public attention to institutional conditions:

| Year | Event | Impact |
|------|-------|--------|
| 1946 | Albert Deutsch publishes *The Shame of the States* | Photos of asylum conditions shocked the public |
| 1948 | Albert Q. Maisel, "Bedlam 1946" in *Life* magazine | Compared institutions to concentration camps |
| 1961 | Joint Commission on Mental Illness and Health final report | Recommended community-based treatment |
| 1963 | Geraldo Rivera exposes Willowbrook State School (aired 1972) | Galvanized public support for deinstitutionalization |

## Deinstitutionalization and Its Aftermath (1960s-1990s)

### The Community Mental Health Act (1963)

President John F. Kennedy signed the Community Mental Health Act on October 31, 1963, envisioning a network of 1,500 community mental health centers that would replace state hospitals. The law authorized construction funding but not ongoing operational support.

**What Was Promised**

- 1,500 community mental health centers (CMHCs) nationwide
- Comprehensive services: inpatient, outpatient, emergency, partial hospitalization, consultation
- Federal construction and staffing grants

**What Was Delivered**

- Only 789 CMHCs were ever built
- Federal funding was cut repeatedly, particularly under the Nixon and Reagan administrations
- The Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1981 (Reagan) converted CMHC funding into block grants, cutting total federal mental health funding by 25%
- State hospital populations declined rapidly, but community infrastructure was never built to replace them

### The Transinstitutionalization Problem

As state hospitals closed, people with serious mental illness did not move to community treatment programs. Instead, they moved to:

| Destination | Approximate Population | Status |
|-------------|----------------------|--------|
| Jails and prisons | 550,000 (current) | Criminalization of mental illness |
| Homelessness | 195,000-230,000 (current) | Untreated, unsupported |
| Nursing homes | 200,000-300,000 (estimated) | Institutional care without psychiatric expertise |
| Boarding homes and SROs | Unknown (declining) | Minimal support; often exploitative |
| Family care | Millions | Burden on families without public support |
| Community treatment | 35,000 (state hospital level) | The intended destination; the smallest group |

### Legal Developments

| Year | Development | Significance |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| 1971 | *Wyatt v. Stickney* | Right to treatment in least restrictive environment |
| 1975 | *O'Connor v. Donaldson* | Cannot confine a non-dangerous mentally ill person capable of surviving in community |
| 1979 | *Addington v. Texas* | Civil commitment requires "clear and convincing evidence" |
| 1982 | *Youngberg v. Romeo* | Institutionalized persons have right to minimally adequate training |
| 1990 | ADA enacted | Psychiatric disabilities explicitly included in disability definition |
| 1992 | *Heller v. Doe* | Lower standard of proof for commitment of intellectually disabled (controversial) |
| 1999 | *Olmstead v. L.C.* | Unjustified institutionalization is discrimination under ADA |

## The ADA and Psychiatric Disability (1990-2008)

### ADA Coverage of Psychiatric Disabilities

The ADA explicitly includes mental illness within its definition of disability. The legislative history references psychiatric conditions, and DOJ and EEOC guidance has consistently interpreted the ADA to cover conditions such as major depression, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, PTSD, OCD, and anxiety disorders.

**Early Implementation Challenges**

- Courts frequently ruled against plaintiffs with psychiatric disabilities at the "substantially limits" threshold
- Employers successfully argued that psychiatric conditions were not "disabilities" if controlled by medication
- The "direct threat" defense was invoked more frequently against people with psychiatric disabilities than other disability groups
- Stigma affected judicial attitudes: some courts applied heightened skepticism to psychiatric disability claims

### Judicial Narrowing

The Supreme Court decisions that narrowed the ADA between 1999 and 2008 had a disproportionate impact on people with psychiatric disabilities:

- *Sutton v. United Air Lines* (1999): Required consideration of mitigating measures -- people whose psychiatric conditions were controlled by medication were excluded from ADA coverage
- *Toyota Motor Manufacturing v. Williams* (2002): Narrowed "substantially limits" -- episodic psychiatric conditions often failed this test
- Result: Courts dismissed many psychiatric disability claims at the threshold disability-determination stage

### ADA Amendments Act of 2008

The ADAAA significantly improved coverage for psychiatric disabilities:

- Eliminated consideration of mitigating measures (medication) in determining disability
- Broadened "substantially limits" interpretation
- Explicitly covered episodic conditions (e.g., bipolar disorder, recurrent depression)
- Expanded "regarded as" claims, making it easier to challenge discrimination based on perceived mental illness

## Modern Era (2000-Present)

### Olmstead Implementation

The Supreme Court's *Olmstead v. L.C.* decision (1999) established that unjustified institutionalization of people with disabilities violates the ADA. For psychiatric disabilities, this meant states had an obligation to provide community-based treatment rather than confining people in institutions.

**Implementation Timeline**

| Period | Development |
|--------|-------------|
| 2001-2008 | Bush administration: Limited DOJ Olmstead enforcement; New Freedom Commission on Mental Health (2003) recommended community integration |
| 2009-2017 | Obama administration: Aggressive DOJ Olmstead enforcement; major settlements in Georgia, North Carolina, Delaware, Mississippi |
| 2017-2021 | Trump administration: Reduced Olmstead enforcement activity; some ongoing settlements continued |
| 2021-2025 | Biden administration: Renewed Olmstead enforcement; new investigations in multiple states |

### Mental Health Parity

The Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act (MHPAEA, 2008) and the Affordable Care Act (2010) established that insurance coverage for mental health conditions must be comparable to coverage for physical conditions. Implementation has been challenging:

- 2008: MHPAEA enacted (parity in financial requirements and treatment limitations)
- 2010: ACA requires coverage of mental health as an essential health benefit
- 2014: Final MHPAEA regulations issued
- 2024: Updated MHPAEA regulations strengthen enforcement of parity (particularly non-quantitative treatment limitations)

### 988 and Crisis Response Reform

- 2020: National Suicide Hotline Designation Act designates 988 as the universal mental health crisis number
- 2022: 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline launches (July 16)
- 2023-2025: Gradual expansion; states developing 988 implementation plans; still in early stages

### Anti-Stigma Initiatives

- NAMI's "StigmaFree" campaign (ongoing)
- SAMHSA's anti-stigma initiatives
- Media guidelines for reporting on mental illness (AP Stylebook updates)
- Contact-based anti-stigma programs showing effectiveness in research

## Lessons from History

1. **Deinstitutionalization without community investment produced catastrophe**: The closure of state hospitals without building community mental health infrastructure led directly to mass incarceration and homelessness of people with psychiatric disabilities
2. **Legal rights without funded services are hollow**: The ADA, *Olmstead*, and parity laws establish rights to community-based treatment, non-discrimination, and insurance coverage, but without adequate funding, these rights are unenforceable for millions
3. **Stigma shapes law and policy**: Judicial decisions narrowing ADA coverage for psychiatric disabilities reflected societal stigma about mental illness; the ADAAA was necessary to correct this
4. **The medical-legal divide persists**: The tension between treating psychiatric conditions as medical problems and treating them as civil rights issues has never been resolved and shapes every policy debate
5. **Federal leadership is essential but inconsistent**: Enforcement of psychiatric disability rights varies dramatically by presidential administration
6. **People with lived experience must lead**: The most effective advocacy has come from people with psychiatric disabilities themselves, not from clinicians or government officials
