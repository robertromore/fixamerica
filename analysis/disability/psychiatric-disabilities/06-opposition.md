# Psychiatric Disabilities: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to psychiatric disability rights and services takes several distinct forms: those who oppose expanded rights for people with psychiatric disabilities, those who oppose the cost of mental health services, those who view mental illness through a moral rather than disability lens, and those within the mental health field who disagree about the appropriate balance between treatment and autonomy. Uniquely, some opposition comes from within the disability rights movement itself, where debates over involuntary treatment and the medical model create unusual alliances and conflicts.

## Opposition to Psychiatric Disability Rights

### Employer Opposition

**Organizations**: U.S. Chamber of Commerce, Society for Human Resource Management (SHRM), industry-specific employer groups

**Arguments**

- **Accommodation burden**: Employers argue that psychiatric disability accommodations (flexible scheduling, modified supervision, reduced workload) are more disruptive and harder to define than physical accommodations
- **Performance standards**: Employers contend that psychiatric conditions that affect interpersonal skills, emotional regulation, or concentration go to the core of job performance, making accommodation impractical
- **Safety concerns**: Employers invoke the "direct threat" defense more frequently for psychiatric disabilities than for other disability types, citing workplace safety and the perceived unpredictability of mental illness
- **Accommodation gaming**: Some employers argue that employees use psychiatric disability claims strategically to avoid discipline or performance management
- **Documentation difficulty**: Employers complain that psychiatric disability documentation is more subjective and harder to verify than physical disability documentation

**Counter-Arguments**

- JAN data shows that psychiatric accommodations are among the least costly (average $0 to $500)
- People with psychiatric disabilities who receive effective accommodation have retention and performance rates comparable to other employees
- The "direct threat" standard requires objective, individualized assessment, not stereotyped assumptions about mental illness
- Psychiatric disability discrimination claims succeed at rates comparable to other ADA claims

### Insurance Industry Opposition

**Organizations**: AHIP, Blue Cross Blue Shield Association, UnitedHealth Group

**Arguments**

- **Cost**: Mental health treatment is open-ended and difficult to define in terms of medical necessity
- **Overutilization**: Parity requirements may lead to overutilization of mental health services
- **Moral hazard**: Insurance coverage of mental health treatment creates incentives for overdiagnosis
- **Clinical uncertainty**: Mental health diagnoses are more subjective than physical health diagnoses, making utilization management more difficult

**Policy Positions**

- Support narrow interpretation of parity requirements
- Resist elimination of non-quantitative treatment limitations (NQTLs)
- Support prior authorization and utilization management for mental health services
- Oppose mandated coverage of specific mental health services or provider types

**Counter-Arguments**

- Mental health parity has not produced the cost increases insurers predicted (Milliman actuarial studies)
- NQTLs are frequently applied more restrictively to mental health than physical health, violating parity law
- Restricting mental health treatment increases downstream costs (emergency rooms, hospitalization, incarceration)
- The 2024 MHPAEA final rules specifically target NQTL disparities

### NIMBY Opposition

**Description**: Residents and community organizations that oppose the siting of mental health treatment facilities, group homes, and supportive housing in their neighborhoods.

**Arguments**

- Mental health facilities will reduce property values
- Residents of mental health programs are dangerous
- Mental health facilities bring crime and disorder to neighborhoods
- Communities have a right to control what facilities are in their neighborhoods

**Policy Positions**

- Zoning restrictions on group homes and treatment facilities
- Community opposition to supportive housing projects
- Requirements for community notification and approval before facility siting

**Counter-Arguments**

- Research consistently shows no effect on property values from well-managed mental health facilities (Urban Institute, 2022)
- People with mental illness are more likely to be victims of crime than perpetrators
- The Fair Housing Act prohibits zoning discrimination against people with disabilities
- *Olmstead* requires community-based treatment; NIMBYism blocks compliance

## Opposition to Expanded Treatment Authority

### Disability Rights Opposition to Involuntary Treatment

**Organizations**: Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law, NDRN, MindFreedom International, NCIL

This represents a unique form of opposition -- from within the disability rights community -- to policies that treatment advocacy organizations support.

**Arguments Against Expanded Involuntary Treatment**

- Involuntary treatment violates fundamental autonomy and liberty rights
- Coercion is counterproductive: it increases distrust of the mental health system and reduces voluntary treatment engagement
- Involuntary treatment disproportionately affects Black and other minority individuals
- Assisted outpatient treatment (AOT) is court-ordered control that may violate the ADA
- The evidence base for AOT effectiveness is contested (research shows mixed results)
- Resources spent on coercive treatment should be redirected to voluntary community services

**Counter-Position (Treatment Advocacy Center, NAMI)**

- Some people with serious mental illness lack insight into their condition (anosognosia) and cannot make informed treatment decisions
- Without involuntary treatment authority, people cycle through homelessness, jails, and emergency rooms
- AOT research shows reduced hospitalization, incarceration, and homelessness for participants (Swartz et al., 2010)
- The right to refuse treatment is meaningless if the person is incarcerated or homeless

### Medical Model Opposition

**Groups**: Psychiatric survivor movement, critical psychiatry, some disability rights organizations

**Arguments**

- Psychiatric diagnoses are social constructs, not objective medical conditions
- Medication-centered treatment causes harm (side effects, dependency, metabolic syndrome)
- The disease model of mental illness increases stigma rather than reducing it
- Peer support and non-medical alternatives are more effective and less harmful
- Forced medication is a form of violence

## Opposition to Mental Health Spending

### Fiscal Conservative Opposition

**Arguments**

- State mental health systems are inefficient and should be reformed before receiving additional funding
- Mental health is a personal and family responsibility, not a government obligation
- Federal mental health programs (SAMHSA block grants, 988) create dependency on government
- Private sector and faith-based organizations can provide mental health support more effectively
- Medicaid expansion has increased costs without proportionate improvement in outcomes

**Policy Positions**

- Block-grant mental health funding to states with flexibility to reduce spending
- Reduce federal mental health mandates
- Privatize mental health service delivery
- Oppose Medicaid expansion

### Law Enforcement Community (Mixed)

**Position**: Law enforcement organizations have mixed views on psychiatric disability policy. Some support expanded mental health services (particularly Crisis Intervention Teams and 988 diversion) because they reduce the burden on police. Others oppose reforms that reduce law enforcement authority over individuals in crisis.

**Supporting reforms**: International Association of Chiefs of Police (IACP) supports CIT and co-responder models
**Opposing reforms**: Some police unions oppose restrictions on involuntary holds, mandatory diversion requirements, or reduction of police role in crisis response

## Opposition Effectiveness

### Where Opposition Has Succeeded

| Area | Opposition Success | Result |
|------|-------------------|--------|
| Mental health funding | Fiscal conservatives have repeatedly cut state mental health budgets | $5.6 billion in cumulative cuts since 2009 |
| Parity enforcement | Insurance industry lobbying has delayed and weakened MHPAEA implementation | NQTLs persisted for over a decade despite parity law |
| NIMBY | Community opposition blocks group homes and treatment facilities | Concentration of facilities in poor neighborhoods |
| Employer accommodation resistance | Psychiatric disability accommodation denials are common | 35-40% denial rate for many accommodation types |
| ADA Education and Reform Act | Business lobbying produced House passage in 2018 | Threatened private enforcement of psychiatric disability rights |

### Where Opposition Has Been Less Effective

- **ADA coverage of psychiatric disabilities**: Despite attempts to exclude mental illness from ADA coverage, the ADAAA (2008) broadened coverage
- **Olmstead enforcement**: Despite state resistance, DOJ settlements have produced incremental progress in community integration
- **988 Lifeline**: Bipartisan support for crisis intervention has overcome fiscal conservative opposition
- **CCBHC expansion**: Bipartisan support for community mental health clinic model continues to grow
- **Public opinion**: Support for mental health treatment access has increased significantly (85% support treatment access, Pew, 2024)
