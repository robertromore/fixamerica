# Education and IDEA: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Reforming IDEA implementation requires legislative action to address five interconnected failures: chronic federal underfunding, critical workforce shortages, inequitable dispute resolution, inadequate inclusion and equity protections, and outdated statutory provisions that have not been reauthorized since 2004. This document provides draft legislation addressing each failure area, along with analysis of legal considerations, potential loopholes, and implementation challenges.

---

## Federal Legislation

### Bill 1: IDEA Full Funding Act

**Purpose**: Phase in the federal government's 40% cost share commitment over 10 fiscal years by converting IDEA Part B funding to a mandatory spending category, eliminating dependence on annual appropriations.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "IDEA Full Funding Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.
(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that—
    (1) the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act authorizes
    federal funding at 40 percent of the national average per-pupil
    expenditure multiplied by the number of children with
    disabilities served;
    (2) Congress has never appropriated funding at the authorized
    level, with the federal share at approximately 15 percent of
    excess costs as of fiscal year 2024;
    (3) the gap between authorized and appropriated funding exceeds
    $23 billion annually;
    (4) chronic underfunding produces teacher shortages, excessive
    caseloads, inadequate services, and poor outcomes for
    approximately 7.5 million children with disabilities;
    (5) states and local school districts absorb 85-88 percent
    of excess special education costs, creating unfunded mandate
    burdens that harm both special and general education; and
    (6) fulfilling the federal funding commitment is essential to
    achieving the purposes of the Individuals with Disabilities
    Education Act.
(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to phase in full federal
funding of the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act at
40 percent of the national average per-pupil expenditure over
10 fiscal years.

SEC. 3. MANDATORY FUNDING FOR IDEA PART B.
(a) RECLASSIFICATION.—Section 611(i) of the Individuals with
Disabilities Education Act (20 U.S.C. 1411(i)) is amended—
    (1) by striking "such sums as may be necessary" and
    inserting "the amounts determined under subsection (j)";
    and
    (2) by adding at the end the following:
"(j) MANDATORY FUNDING.—
    (1) IN GENERAL.—For each of fiscal years 2027 through 2036,
    the amount appropriated under subsection (i) shall be the
    amount calculated under paragraph (2), and such amount shall
    be classified as mandatory spending not subject to
    discretionary spending limits.
    (2) CALCULATION.—
        (A) TARGET AMOUNT.—The target amount for each fiscal
        year is 40 percent of the national average per-pupil
        expenditure multiplied by the number of children with
        disabilities aged 3 through 21 receiving special
        education and related services, as reported by the
        Secretary for the most recent fiscal year for which
        data are available.
        (B) PHASE-IN.—The amount for each fiscal year shall
        be calculated as follows:
            (i) FY 2027: the FY 2026 appropriation plus
            one-tenth of the difference between the FY 2026
            appropriation and the target amount;
            (ii) FY 2028 through FY 2035: the prior year
            amount plus one-tenth of the difference between
            the FY 2026 appropriation and the FY 2027 target
            amount, adjusted for changes in per-pupil
            expenditure and child count;
            (iii) FY 2036 and thereafter: the full target
            amount.
    (3) ADJUSTMENT.—Beginning in FY 2037, the mandatory
    appropriation shall be automatically adjusted each fiscal
    year based on changes in the national average per-pupil
    expenditure and the child count.".

SEC. 4. ACCOUNTABILITY PROVISIONS.
(a) OUTCOME REPORTING.—As a condition of receiving funds under
this Act, each State shall annually report to the Secretary—
    (1) graduation rates for students with IEPs, disaggregated
    by race, ethnicity, language, income, and disability category;
    (2) post-secondary enrollment and competitive employment
    rates within 2 years of exit;
    (3) inclusion rates (percentage of students in general
    education 80 percent or more of the school day);
    (4) disproportionality indices for identification, placement,
    and discipline; and
    (5) dispute resolution data including volume, outcomes, and
    demographic characteristics of filing families.
(b) PERFORMANCE TARGETS.—The Secretary shall establish national
performance targets for each metric in subsection (a), with
improvement benchmarks for states not meeting targets.
(c) CORRECTIVE ACTION.—States that fail to meet performance
targets for 3 consecutive years shall develop and implement
corrective action plans approved by the Secretary.

SEC. 5. POVERTY ADJUSTMENT.
(a) SUPPLEMENTAL ALLOCATION.—In addition to the amounts
calculated under section 3, an additional 5 percent of total
IDEA Part B funding shall be distributed to states based on
the proportion of children with disabilities living in
poverty, as determined by the most recent Census data.
(b) USE OF FUNDS.—Supplemental poverty adjustment funds shall
be used for—
    (1) additional staffing in high-poverty schools;
    (2) parent advocacy services;
    (3) culturally responsive evaluation and services; and
    (4) evidence-based interventions for students experiencing
    poverty-related educational disadvantage.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect on October 1, 2026.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Converts IDEA Part B from discretionary to mandatory spending, the single most important structural change to ensure funding stability. The 10-year phase-in spreads the fiscal impact.
- **Section 4**: Links increased funding to outcome accountability, addressing the concern that more money without accountability will not improve results.
- **Section 5**: Targets additional resources to high-poverty districts where special education needs are greatest and service gaps are widest.

**Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| $23 billion annual cost at full implementation | Phased over 10 years; offset by reduced state/local burden and long-term savings |
| CBO scoring of mandatory spending conversion | Significant score; requires budget offset or waiver |
| Appropriations Committee jurisdiction loss | May face procedural resistance; authorizing committee jurisdiction |
| Accountability measures may be too rigid | Allow state flexibility in meeting targets; focus on improvement trajectories |

**Refinements**:

- **Stronger version**: Reach full funding in 7 years; add maintenance of effort requirements to prevent state funding substitution
- **Compromise version**: Phase in over 15 years; maintain discretionary classification with statutory floor
- **Fallback version**: Increase IDEA appropriations by $5 billion with targeted accountability requirements

### Bill 2: Special Education Workforce Investment Act

**Purpose**: Address critical special education workforce shortages through federal scholarships, loan forgiveness, preparation program investment, and caseload standards.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Special Education Workforce
Investment Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.
Congress finds that—
    (1) 44 states report critical shortages of special education
    teachers;
    (2) an estimated 39,000 special education positions are
    unfilled nationally;
    (3) special education teachers experience an annual attrition
    rate of 16 percent, double the rate for general education
    teachers;
    (4) 12 percent of special education teachers work on
    emergency credentials;
    (5) excessive caseloads and paperwork burden are primary
    drivers of attrition; and
    (6) related service personnel shortages (school psychologists,
    speech-language pathologists, occupational therapists) further
    undermine service quality.

SEC. 3. SPECIAL EDUCATION TEACHER SCHOLARSHIP PROGRAM.
(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary of Education shall establish
a Special Education Teacher Scholarship Program to award
scholarships to individuals enrolled in accredited special
education teacher preparation programs.
(b) AWARD AMOUNTS.—Each scholarship shall cover—
    (1) full tuition and required fees; and
    (2) a living stipend of not less than $15,000 per academic
    year.
(c) SERVICE OBLIGATION.—Each scholarship recipient shall teach
in a public school special education position for a period of
not less than 5 years after completion of the preparation
program.
(d) PRIORITY.—The Secretary shall give priority to applicants
who—
    (1) commit to teaching in high-need schools, as defined by
    the Secretary;
    (2) are members of underrepresented racial or ethnic groups
    in the teaching profession;
    (3) demonstrate proficiency in a language other than English;
    or
    (4) have personal experience with disability.
(e) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
$500,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2033 to
carry out this section.

SEC. 4. SPECIAL EDUCATION LOAN FORGIVENESS.
(a) IN GENERAL.—The Secretary shall cancel $10,000 of federal
student loan debt per year for each year of service (up to 5
years, $50,000 total) for individuals employed as—
    (1) special education teachers;
    (2) school psychologists serving students with disabilities;
    (3) speech-language pathologists in public schools;
    (4) occupational therapists in public schools; or
    (5) Board Certified Behavior Analysts serving public school
    students.
(b) HIGH-NEED BONUS.—Individuals serving in high-need schools
or geographic areas, as defined by the Secretary, shall receive
an additional $5,000 per year in loan cancellation.
(c) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
$1,000,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2033 to
carry out this section.

SEC. 5. PREPARATION PROGRAM INVESTMENT.
(a) GRANTS.—The Secretary shall award competitive grants to
institutions of higher education to—
    (1) establish or expand special education teacher preparation
    programs;
    (2) develop dual certification programs (general and special
    education);
    (3) create residency-based preparation models with
    school-based clinical experience;
    (4) integrate evidence-based practices, including structured
    literacy, applied behavior analysis, and universal design
    for learning, into preparation curricula; and
    (5) recruit and prepare diverse candidates reflecting the
    student population served.
(b) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
$200,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2033 to
carry out this section.

SEC. 6. RECOMMENDED CASELOAD STANDARDS.
(a) DEVELOPMENT.—Not later than 1 year after the date of
enactment, the Secretary shall convene a panel of experts to
develop recommended national caseload standards for special
education teachers, based on disability category and service
delivery model.
(b) GUIDELINES.—Recommended caseload standards shall not
exceed—
    (1) 8 students for teachers serving students with significant
    cognitive disabilities;
    (2) 12 students for teachers serving students with emotional
    or behavioral disabilities;
    (3) 15 students for teachers providing resource room or
    pull-out services; and
    (4) 25 students for teachers providing consultative or
    co-teaching services.
(c) INCENTIVE GRANTS.—The Secretary shall award grants to
states that adopt and implement caseload standards meeting or
exceeding the recommended standards.

SEC. 7. PARAPROFESSIONAL CAREER LADDER.
(a) PROGRAM.—The Secretary shall establish a grant program to
support career advancement for special education
paraprofessionals, including—
    (1) tuition assistance for paraprofessionals pursuing
    teaching certification;
    (2) credentialing programs for specialized paraprofessional
    roles;
    (3) wage supplements for paraprofessionals meeting advanced
    training requirements.
(b) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
$100,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2033 to
carry out this section.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Creates a direct pipeline of qualified special education teachers through substantial scholarships with a service commitment
- **Section 4**: Addresses retention by reducing financial burden on current special education professionals
- **Section 5**: Improves preparation quality, which research shows is a key factor in both teacher effectiveness and retention
- **Section 6**: Establishes evidence-based caseload standards to reduce the workload that drives attrition
- **Section 7**: Creates advancement pathways for paraprofessionals, addressing the most severe shortage area

**Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| $1.8 billion annual cost | Offset by reduced attrition costs and improved outcomes; investment framing |
| Service obligation enforcement | Model on existing TEACH Grant and National Health Service Corps structures |
| Caseload standards may conflict with local control | Recommended, not mandated; incentive grants encourage adoption |
| Union concerns about credential requirements | Involve unions in standard development; grandfather existing professionals |

### Bill 3: IDEA Dispute Resolution Equity Act

**Purpose**: Equalize access to IDEA procedural safeguards by establishing federally funded independent educational advocacy, expanding facilitated IEP meetings, and creating state ombudsman offices.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "IDEA Dispute Resolution Equity Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.
Congress finds that—
    (1) due process hearings under IDEA cost families an average
    of $10,000 to $50,000, creating a two-tier system in which
    well-resourced families can challenge inadequate services
    while low-income families cannot;
    (2) White families file due process complaints at twice the
    rate of Black families, and families earning over $100,000
    file at 5 times the rate of families earning under $40,000;
    (3) non-English-speaking families file complaints at less
    than half the rate of English-speaking families;
    (4) the procedural safeguards that are IDEA's hallmark are
    functionally inaccessible to the majority of families; and
    (5) equalizing access to dispute resolution is essential
    to ensuring FAPE for all children.

SEC. 3. INDEPENDENT EDUCATIONAL ADVOCACY PROGRAM.
(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary of Education shall establish
a grant program to fund independent educational advocates in
every state.
(b) ELIGIBILITY.—Grants shall be awarded to—
    (1) Protection and Advocacy organizations designated under
    the Developmental Disabilities Act;
    (2) legal aid organizations; or
    (3) nonprofit organizations with demonstrated expertise in
    special education advocacy.
(c) SERVICES.—Independent educational advocates shall provide,
at no cost to families—
    (1) assistance understanding evaluation results, IEP
    documents, and procedural rights;
    (2) accompaniment to IEP meetings as a support and advocate
    for the family;
    (3) assistance preparing for and participating in mediation
    and resolution sessions;
    (4) referral to legal representation when due process is
    necessary; and
    (5) outreach to underserved communities, including
    non-English-speaking families and families in rural areas.
(d) STAFFING.—Each state shall maintain sufficient independent
educational advocates to serve all requesting families, with
a minimum of 1 advocate per 5,000 students receiving special
education services.
(e) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
$300,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2033 to
carry out this section.

SEC. 4. IEP FACILITATION PROGRAM.
(a) IN GENERAL.—Each state receiving IDEA Part B funds shall
make trained IEP facilitators available for any IEP meeting
at the request of either the parent or the school district.
(b) FACILITATOR QUALIFICATIONS.—IEP facilitators shall—
    (1) be independent of the school district;
    (2) complete training approved by the Secretary; and
    (3) have knowledge of IDEA requirements and special
    education practices.
(c) FUNDING.—The Secretary shall provide grants to states to
establish and maintain IEP facilitation programs.

SEC. 5. STATE SPECIAL EDUCATION OMBUDSMAN.
(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—Each state receiving IDEA Part B funds
shall establish an Office of the Special Education Ombudsman
within the state education agency.
(b) DUTIES.—The Ombudsman shall—
    (1) receive and investigate complaints from families
    regarding special education services;
    (2) mediate disputes informally before formal complaint
    or due process proceedings;
    (3) monitor patterns of complaints to identify systemic
    issues;
    (4) issue public reports on complaint trends and
    resolutions; and
    (5) make recommendations to the state education agency
    for policy and practice improvements.
(c) INDEPENDENCE.—The Ombudsman shall operate independently
of the special education division of the state education
agency and shall not be involved in compliance monitoring
decisions.

SEC. 6. COMPLAINT PROCESS IMPROVEMENTS.
(a) TIMELINE.—State complaint investigations shall be
completed within 45 days of filing, reduced from the
current 60-day requirement.
(b) ACCESSIBILITY.—State complaint forms shall be available
in the 10 most common languages in the state and in
accessible formats.
(c) CORRECTIVE ACTION.—States shall require school districts
found in violation to implement corrective action within 30
days and verify implementation through follow-up review.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Provides the single most important reform for equitable dispute resolution: free independent advocacy for every family that needs it
- **Section 4**: IEP facilitation prevents disputes from escalating to formal proceedings, saving money and preserving relationships
- **Section 5**: An ombudsman function creates accountability and identifies systemic issues without the adversarial costs of due process
- **Section 6**: Faster complaint resolution with linguistic accessibility ensures the free complaint process actually works

**Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| School districts may resist external advocates | Advocates improve IEP quality and reduce costly due process proceedings |
| $300 million annual cost for advocacy program | Far less than current due process costs borne by families and districts |
| State ombudsman independence may be compromised | Statutory independence requirements; external oversight |
| Facilitator neutrality concerns | Training standards, independence requirements, conflict of interest rules |

### Bill 4: IDEA Inclusion and Equity Act

**Purpose**: Strengthen LRE requirements, address racial disproportionality, and improve transition services for students with disabilities.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "IDEA Inclusion and Equity Act".

SEC. 2. PRESUMPTION OF INCLUSION.
(a) AMENDMENT.—Section 612(a)(5) of the Individuals with
Disabilities Education Act (20 U.S.C. 1412(a)(5)) is amended
by adding at the end the following:
"(C) PRESUMPTION.—In making placement decisions under this
paragraph, the IEP Team shall begin with the presumption that
the child will be educated in the general education classroom
with supplementary aids and services. Placement in a setting
other than the general education classroom shall be made only
when the IEP Team documents, based on individualized data,
that—
    (i) the child cannot receive a free appropriate public
    education in the general education classroom even with the
    full range of supplementary aids and services; and
    (ii) the IEP Team has considered and documented the specific
    supplementary aids and services that were attempted or
    considered and the reasons they were insufficient.".

SEC. 3. DISPROPORTIONALITY REFORM.
(a) STRENGTHENED MONITORING.—Section 618(d) of the
Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (20 U.S.C. 1418(d))
is amended by adding at the end the following:
"(4) ENHANCED REVIEW.—
    (A) The Secretary shall identify significant
    disproportionality based on race and ethnicity in—
        (i) the identification of children as children with
        disabilities, including by disability category;
        (ii) the placement of children in particular
        educational settings; and
        (iii) the incidence, duration, and type of disciplinary
        actions, including suspensions and expulsions.
    (B) A state identified with significant disproportionality
    under subparagraph (A) shall—
        (i) redirect not less than 15 percent of IDEA Part B
        funds to provide comprehensive coordinated early
        intervening services in the districts identified;
        (ii) conduct root cause analyses of disproportionality
        in identified districts;
        (iii) implement evidence-based corrective action
        including culturally responsive evaluation training,
        implicit bias training, and multi-tiered systems of
        support; and
        (iv) report annually to the Secretary on progress
        toward reducing disproportionality.".

SEC. 4. CULTURALLY RESPONSIVE EVALUATION.
(a) REQUIREMENT.—Section 614(b) of the Individuals with
Disabilities Education Act (20 U.S.C. 1414(b)) is amended
by adding at the end the following:
"(7) CULTURAL AND LINGUISTIC COMPETENCE.—
    (A) Each evaluation team shall include at least one member
    with demonstrated competence in the cultural and linguistic
    background of the child being evaluated.
    (B) Evaluation instruments shall be validated for use with
    the child's cultural and linguistic population.
    (C) Evaluation reports shall document how cultural and
    linguistic factors were considered in interpreting results.".

SEC. 5. TRANSITION SERVICES EXPANSION.
(a) AGE REQUIREMENT.—Section 614(d)(1)(A)(i)(VIII) of the
Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (20 U.S.C.
1414(d)(1)(A)(i)(VIII)) is amended by striking "beginning
not later than the first IEP to be in effect when the child
turns 16" and inserting "beginning not later than the first
IEP to be in effect when the child turns 14, or upon entering
the 9th grade, whichever occurs first".
(b) TRANSITION REQUIREMENTS.—Section 614(d)(1)(A)(i)(VIII)
is further amended by adding at the end the following:
"(cc) WORK-BASED LEARNING.—Each student's transition plan
shall include participation in competitive integrated
employment, paid work experience, or structured work-based
learning prior to exit from special education.
(dd) INTERAGENCY COORDINATION.—The IEP Team for transition
planning shall include a representative of the state
vocational rehabilitation agency, and may include
representatives of developmental disability services,
independent living centers, and post-secondary education
institutions.
(ee) POST-SCHOOL OUTCOME TRACKING.—Each state shall track
and report post-school outcomes for students who exit
special education, including employment, post-secondary
enrollment, and independent living status, for a period of
not less than 5 years after exit.".

SEC. 6. NATIONAL INCLUSION TARGET.
(a) TARGET.—It is the goal of the United States that by 2035,
not less than 80 percent of students with disabilities shall
be educated in the general education classroom for 80 percent
or more of the school day.
(b) STATE PLANS.—Each state shall include in its IDEA state
plan a strategy for achieving the national inclusion target,
including timelines, resource allocation, and professional
development plans.
(c) ANNUAL REPORTING.—The Secretary shall report annually to
Congress on national and state progress toward the inclusion
target.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 2**: Codifies the presumption of inclusion that courts have developed, shifting the burden to districts to justify restrictive placements
- **Section 3**: Strengthens disproportionality monitoring and enforcement, requiring states to take meaningful action when significant disproportionality is identified
- **Section 4**: Addresses assessment bias by requiring culturally and linguistically competent evaluation practices
- **Section 5**: Expands transition services to begin earlier and include work-based learning, interagency coordination, and post-school outcome tracking
- **Section 6**: Establishes a national goal for inclusion with state planning and reporting requirements

**Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Inclusion presumption may alarm families who prefer specialized settings | Preserves continuum of services; presumption is rebuttable with individualized data |
| Disproportionality root cause analysis is complex | Fund technical assistance centers; model on existing OSEP TA infrastructure |
| Transition at age 14 increases IEP complexity | Earlier planning leads to better outcomes; additional staffing addressed in workforce bill |
| National inclusion target may be seen as one-size-fits-all | Target is aspirational with individualized exceptions; state flexibility in strategies |

## State Model Legislation

### Model State IDEA Funding Supplement Act

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "[State] Special Education
Funding Equity Act".

SECTION 2. STATE SPECIAL EDUCATION FUNDING FORMULA.
(a) The [state education funding formula] is amended to
include a special education weight of not less than [2.0]
times the base per-pupil funding amount for each student
receiving special education services.
(b) Students with significant disabilities requiring
extensive support shall receive a weight of not less than
[3.5] times the base per-pupil funding amount.
(c) The special education weight shall be adjusted annually
based on changes in the cost of special education services.

SECTION 3. SPECIAL EDUCATION TEACHER RECRUITMENT.
(a) There is established a [State] Special Education
Teacher Scholarship Program providing full tuition for
[number] candidates per year who commit to teaching
special education in [state] public schools for 5 years.
(b) The [state education agency] shall administer a loan
forgiveness program providing $[amount] per year for
special education teachers in high-need districts.

SECTION 4. PARENT ADVOCACY SERVICES.
(a) The [state education agency] shall fund independent
educational advocacy services available at no cost to
families in all school districts.
(b) Priority shall be given to serving families in
high-poverty areas, non-English-speaking families, and
families in rural areas.

SECTION 5. CASELOAD LIMITS.
(a) Special education teacher caseloads shall not exceed—
    (1) [8] students for teachers of students with significant
    disabilities;
    (2) [12] students for teachers of students with emotional
    or behavioral needs;
    (3) [15] students for resource or pull-out services; and
    (4) [25] students for co-teaching or consultative services.
(b) Districts exceeding caseload limits shall submit
corrective action plans to the [state education agency].

SECTION 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect on [date].
```

## Regulatory Framework

### OSEP Regulatory Priorities

| Area | Current Status | Recommended Action |
|------|---------------|-------------------|
| Results-Driven Accountability | Implementation ongoing since 2014 | Finalize outcome metrics; establish performance thresholds |
| Discipline protections | Proposed rule published 2024 | Finalize strengthened manifestation determination and behavior intervention requirements |
| Disproportionality methodology | Updated 2023 | Enforce significant disproportionality findings with mandatory corrective action |
| AT in IEPs | Guidance issued 2024 | Require documented AT consideration in every IEP with specific AT assessment protocol |
| Virtual IEP meetings | Guidance issued 2023 | Formalize standards for virtual participation ensuring parent access and participation rights |
| Transition services | Current regulations at 34 C.F.R. 300.320(b) | Update regulations to require earlier transition planning and interagency coordination |
| Evidence-based practices | No current requirement | Issue guidance on integrating structured literacy, UDL, and other evidence-based practices into IEPs |

### Department of Education Enforcement Actions

| Action | Current Approach | Recommended Improvement |
|--------|-----------------|------------------------|
| State monitoring | Annual determinations based on compliance indicators | Shift to outcome-based determinations with consequences for persistent poor performance |
| IDEA fund withholding | Rarely used; harms children more than administrators | Create targeted intervention authority: require states to redirect funds to corrective action |
| Technical assistance | OSEP-funded TA centers | Expand TA capacity; create inclusion-focused TA center |
| Data collection | Section 618 annual data | Add post-school outcome tracking and parent satisfaction data |

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Authority

- Congress has authority under the Spending Clause (Article I, Section 8, Clause 1) to condition federal education funds on compliance with IDEA requirements
- IDEA is a voluntary program: states accept federal funds in exchange for compliance, satisfying the *South Dakota v. Dole* (1987) framework
- Converting to mandatory spending raises no additional constitutional issues; Congress has broad discretion over spending classification
- *Cedar Rapids Community School District v. Garret F.* (1999) confirmed IDEA's broad scope of services required under FAPE

### Preemption

- IDEA establishes a federal floor; states may provide greater protections (20 U.S.C. Section 1407)
- State special education laws that exceed IDEA requirements remain in effect
- Full funding legislation does not alter the federal-state relationship; it fulfills existing federal obligations

### Federalism

- IDEA is a cooperative federalism statute: federal funding in exchange for state compliance
- Mandatory spending conversion strengthens the federal commitment without expanding federal authority
- Caseload standards are recommended, not mandated, preserving state discretion
- State model legislation is offered for voluntary adoption

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Availability | Effectiveness |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| OSEP state monitoring | Available; limited capacity | Moderate; determinations often lack consequences |
| Due process hearings | Available to families | Effective but inequitable (cost barrier) |
| State complaints | Available; free | Moderate; variable quality and enforcement |
| OCR disability discrimination complaints | Available | Moderate; backlog and processing delays |
| Federal court | Available after administrative exhaustion | High but expensive; *Fry v. Napoleon* clarified exhaustion |
| Corrective action plans | Available under IDEA | Low; often not enforced rigorously |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| State funding substitution | States reduce state special education funding as federal funding increases, negating the benefit of full funding | High |
| Inclusion presumption exceptions | Districts document boilerplate justifications for restrictive placements to circumvent inclusion presumption | High |
| Disproportionality data manipulation | States adjust methodology or thresholds to avoid triggering significant disproportionality findings | Medium |
| Caseload gaming | Districts assign students to consultative model to reduce paper caseloads while providing less service | Medium |
| Transition compliance without substance | Schools create transition plans that meet procedural requirements but include no meaningful services or connections | High |
| Advocate independence compromise | Advocacy organizations become dependent on state funding, compromising independence | Medium |
| Outcome metric gaming | States set low performance targets to avoid corrective action triggers | Medium |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|-----------|
| Mandatory spending conversion is politically difficult | Full funding remains uncertain if mandatory classification fails | Budget process resistance; competing spending priorities |
| 10-year phase-in delays relief | Students with disabilities today do not benefit from Year 10 funding levels | Political feasibility requires phase-in |
| Recommended caseloads are not mandated | States can ignore recommendations without consequence | Federalism concerns limit federal mandates on staffing |
| Post-school outcome tracking lacks enforcement | Data collection without consequences does not drive improvement | Resistance to outcome-based accountability |
| Cultural competence requirements are difficult to implement | Qualified culturally responsive evaluators are scarce | Workforce pipeline issues |
| Dispute resolution reforms do not address attorney fee barriers | Families who need legal representation still face cost barriers beyond advocacy support | IDEA fee-shifting provisions are limited by *Arlington Central* (2006) |
| IDEA reauthorization not included | Comprehensive statutory update remains necessary | Standalone bills are more politically feasible than omnibus reauthorization |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **State funding substitution**: Include maintenance of effort provisions requiring states to maintain at least the prior year's state and local special education spending as a condition of receiving increased federal funds. Model on IDEA's existing MOE provisions but strengthen enforcement.
2. **Inclusion presumption abuse**: Require districts to document specific supplementary aids and services considered, attempted, or rejected, with individualized data showing why each was insufficient. OSEP to issue guidance on acceptable documentation.
3. **Disproportionality data manipulation**: Establish uniform federal methodology for calculating significant disproportionality; prohibit states from setting thresholds above federal standards. Require independent verification of state calculations.
4. **Caseload gaming**: Define caseload standards by actual contact hours and service delivery model, not just student count. Require districts to report both caseload numbers and service hours per student.
5. **Transition compliance without substance**: Require post-school outcome data to be linked to transition planning quality. Districts with poor post-school outcomes must modify transition practices.
6. **Advocate independence**: Establish multi-year grant cycles (5 years minimum) with statutory independence protections. Prohibit state education agencies from influencing advocate recommendations.
7. **Outcome metric gaming**: Secretary to establish minimum performance thresholds based on national data; states cannot set targets below federal minimums. Require year-over-year improvement for states below national averages.

### General Implementation Concerns

- **Phase-in timeline**: Students currently in school will age out before full funding is achieved. Supplemental appropriations for immediate needs should accompany the phase-in.
- **Workforce pipeline lag**: Scholarship and preparation programs take 4-5 years to produce fully certified teachers. Interim strategies (emergency retention bonuses, virtual service delivery, cross-district sharing) are needed.
- **Political durability**: Mandatory spending classification is more durable than appropriations, but future Congresses can reclassify. Build bipartisan support to protect funding.
- **Data infrastructure**: Outcome tracking and disproportionality monitoring require data systems that many states lack. Fund data system development alongside reporting requirements.
- **Stakeholder resistance**: School administrators may resist accountability provisions; engage administrator organizations in implementation design.
