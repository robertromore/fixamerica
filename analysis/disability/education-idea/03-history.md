# Education and IDEA: History

## Origins

### Exclusion Era (Pre-1970)

Before federal legislation, children with disabilities were routinely excluded from public education:

- **State exclusion laws**: Many states had laws explicitly permitting or requiring schools to exclude children with disabilities
- **Institutionalization**: An estimated 1 million children were placed in state institutions with minimal or no education (Congressional Record, 1975)
- **No legal right**: The U.S. Constitution does not mention education, and no federal law guaranteed educational access for children with disabilities
- **Parent-run schools**: Families of children with disabilities created private schools and advocacy organizations (The Arc, United Cerebral Palsy) because public schools refused to serve their children

**Scale of Exclusion (1970 estimates)**

| Category | Estimated Number |
|----------|------------------|
| Children with disabilities in the U.S. | 8 million |
| Receiving appropriate public education | 3.5 million (44%) |
| Excluded from public school entirely | 1.75 million (22%) |
| In institutions with minimal education | 1 million (12.5%) |
| In public school but without appropriate services | 1.75 million (22%) |

*Source: Senate Report 94-168 (1975); Bureau of Education for the Handicapped data.*

### The Civil Rights Foundation (1950s-1970s)

The disability education movement built on civil rights precedents:

**Key Legal Developments**

| Year | Development | Significance |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| 1954 | *Brown v. Board of Education* | Established that separate education is inherently unequal; applied by analogy to disability |
| 1966 | Elementary and Secondary Education Act amendments | First federal funding for children with disabilities in schools |
| 1971 | *PARC v. Commonwealth of Pennsylvania* | Consent decree requiring Pennsylvania to educate all children with intellectual disabilities |
| 1972 | *Mills v. Board of Education* | Federal court ruled DC must educate all children with disabilities regardless of cost |
| 1973 | Rehabilitation Act, Section 504 | Prohibited disability discrimination by federal fund recipients, including schools |

## Key Turning Points

### The Education for All Handicapped Children Act (1975)

**P.L. 94-142: The Original IDEA**

President Gerald Ford signed the Education for All Handicapped Children Act on November 29, 1975, establishing the federal framework for special education that persists today.

**Core Requirements**

| Requirement | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE) | Every child with a disability entitled to education at no cost |
| Individualized Education Program (IEP) | Written plan for each child with goals, services, and placement |
| Least Restrictive Environment (LRE) | Educate children with disabilities alongside nondisabled peers to maximum extent appropriate |
| Procedural safeguards | Parent notice, consent, participation, and due process rights |
| Nondiscriminatory evaluation | Assessment in child's native language; no single measure determines eligibility |
| Child Find | States must identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities |

**Funding Promise**: Congress authorized federal funding at 40% of the national average per-pupil expenditure, recognizing that special education costs more than general education. This promise has never been fulfilled.

**Initial Impact**

- Schools opened to approximately 1 million previously excluded children
- States scrambled to develop special education infrastructure
- Institutional populations began declining as children moved to public schools
- Parent advocacy organizations became permanent fixtures in education policy

### Amendments and Reauthorizations

**1986: Education of the Handicapped Act Amendments (P.L. 99-457)**

- Created Part H (now Part C): early intervention for infants and toddlers (birth to age 3)
- Extended FAPE to children ages 3-5
- Established Individualized Family Service Plan (IFSP) for early intervention

**1990: IDEA Name Change (P.L. 101-476)**

- Renamed law "Individuals with Disabilities Education Act"
- Changed language from "handicapped children" to "children with disabilities" (person-first language)
- Added autism and traumatic brain injury as separate disability categories
- Required transition planning beginning at age 16

**1997: IDEA Reauthorization (P.L. 105-17)**

- Strengthened LRE requirements
- Added discipline protections ("manifestation determination" before removal)
- Required general education teacher participation in IEP meetings
- Added mediation as required dispute resolution option
- Addressed disproportionality: required states to collect and report racial/ethnic data

**2004: Individuals with Disabilities Education Improvement Act (P.L. 108-446)**

- Aligned IDEA with No Child Left Behind accountability requirements
- Changed eligibility process: allowed Response to Intervention (RTI) as alternative to discrepancy model
- Reduced paperwork requirements (pilot programs)
- Strengthened transition planning: began at age 16 (some states at 14)
- Changed attorney fees provisions
- Added "highly qualified" teacher requirements for special education

## Evolution of Key Concepts

### Least Restrictive Environment

| Era | Dominant Practice | Rationale |
|-----|-------------------|-----------|
| Pre-1975 | Exclusion and institutionalization | Children with disabilities seen as uneducable |
| 1975-1985 | Self-contained classrooms in public schools | Compliance with FAPE but minimal inclusion |
| 1985-1995 | "Mainstreaming" for some | Students placed in general education for non-academic subjects |
| 1995-2010 | "Inclusion" movement grows | Research shows benefits of general education placement |
| 2010-present | Presumption of inclusion | 66% of students in general education 80%+ of day; still short of full inclusion |

**Key Court Cases on LRE**

| Case | Year | Holding |
|------|------|---------|
| *Roncker v. Walter* | 1983 | Services must be provided in integrated setting unless they cannot be provided satisfactorily |
| *Daniel R.R. v. State Board of Education* | 1989 | Two-part test: Can education be achieved in general education with supplementary aids? If not, is child mainstreamed to maximum extent? |
| *Sacramento City Unified v. Rachel H.* | 1994 | Four-factor test for inclusion: academic benefits, nonacademic benefits, effect on teacher and students, cost |
| *Oberti v. Board of Education* | 1993 | Strong presumption in favor of inclusion; school must demonstrate it considered full range of supplementary aids |
| *Endrew F. v. Douglas County* | 2017 | IEP must be "reasonably calculated to enable a child to make progress appropriate in light of the child's circumstances" (raised floor above de minimis) |

### FAPE Standard

The meaning of "appropriate" education has evolved through litigation:

| Case | Year | Standard |
|------|------|----------|
| *Board of Education v. Rowley* | 1982 | FAPE requires "some educational benefit" but not "the best possible education"; procedural compliance emphasis |
| *Endrew F. v. Douglas County* | 2017 | Raised standard: IEP must be "reasonably calculated to enable a child to make progress appropriate in light of the child's circumstances"; rejected de minimis standard |

### Transition Services

| Era | Focus |
|-----|-------|
| 1990 (IDEA amendment) | Transition planning required at age 16 |
| 2004 (IDEA reauthorization) | Results-oriented transition; postsecondary goals required |
| 2014 (WIOA) | Pre-employment transition services beginning at age 14 through VR |
| 2020s | Focus on competitive integrated employment outcomes |

## Lessons from History

### What Has Worked

- **Legal mandate**: IDEA's enforceable right to FAPE transformed education access for millions
- **Procedural safeguards**: Due process rights give families a meaningful (if imperfect) tool to challenge inadequate services
- **Early intervention**: Part C services have demonstrated positive outcomes for infant and toddler development
- **Data collection requirements**: IDEA's Section 618 data have enabled identification of disparities and trends
- **Endrew F. decision**: Raised the FAPE standard from de minimis to meaningful progress

### What Has Failed

- **Funding promise**: 50 years of underfunding have undermined every aspect of implementation
- **Compliance focus**: Emphasis on procedural compliance over educational outcomes has allowed schools to check boxes without serving children well
- **Workforce strategy**: No sustained federal investment in the special education workforce pipeline
- **Disproportionality**: Decades of data collection on racial disparities have not produced meaningful reductions
- **Reauthorization delay**: IDEA has not been reauthorized since 2004, leaving the law two decades out of date

### Recurring Patterns

1. **Courts drive progress**: Major advances (PARC, Rowley, Endrew F.) come through litigation, not legislation
2. **Promises exceed implementation**: Congress creates rights but does not fund them
3. **Advocacy sustains progress**: Parent and disability organizations are the primary engine of enforcement
4. **Crises create temporary action**: COVID-19 produced one-time funding that has already expired
5. **Inclusion advances slowly**: Each decade brings modest increases in general education placement
