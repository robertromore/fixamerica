# Education and IDEA: Current State

## Federal Funding

### The Funding Gap

IDEA Part B authorizes federal funding to cover 40% of the national average per-pupil expenditure (APPE) multiplied by the number of children with disabilities served. Congress has never appropriated funding at the authorized level.

**IDEA Part B Funding History (Recent Years)**

| Fiscal Year | Appropriation | APPE | Full Funding (40%) | Federal Share Achieved | Gap |
|-------------|---------------|------|---------------------|------------------------|-----|
| FY 2020 | $12.9 billion | $13,600 | $31.2 billion | 13.8% | $18.3 billion |
| FY 2021 | $13.0 billion | $14,100 | $33.2 billion | 13.2% | $20.2 billion |
| FY 2022 | $13.3 billion | $14,800 | $35.1 billion | 12.8% | $21.8 billion |
| FY 2023 | $14.0 billion | $15,300 | $36.3 billion | 13.0% | $22.3 billion |
| FY 2024 | $14.2 billion | $15,800 | $37.6 billion | 12.7% | $23.4 billion |

*Sources: ED Budget Justifications (FY 2020-2024); NCES, Digest of Education Statistics (2024); CRS, "IDEA Part B Funding" (2024).*

**Note**: The federal share has actually *declined* as a percentage because per-pupil costs have risen faster than appropriations.

### State and Local Funding

States and school districts fill the gap between federal IDEA funding and actual costs:

| Funding Source | Share of Special Education Cost | Total (estimated) |
|----------------|--------------------------------|-------------------|
| Federal (IDEA Part B) | 12-15% | $14.2 billion |
| State funding | 45-55% | $45-55 billion |
| Local funding | 30-40% | $30-40 billion |
| **Total special education spending** | **100%** | **$90-110 billion** |

*Source: CSEF, Special Education Expenditure Project (updated 2023); New America, "Special Education Funding" (2024).*

**Impact on General Education**: Districts that devote more resources to special education under the unfunded mandate have fewer resources for general education programs, creating tension between special education and general education interests.

## Workforce Crisis

### Special Education Teacher Shortage

**National Scope**

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| States reporting critical shortage | 44 out of 50 | OSEP Personnel Center (2024) |
| Unfilled special education positions | 39,000 (estimated, 2023-2024) | CEEDAR Center (2024) |
| Special education teachers on emergency credentials | 12% nationally | Learning Policy Institute (2024) |
| Annual attrition rate (special education) | 16% (vs. 8% for general education) | National Center for Education Statistics (2024) |
| Average special education caseload | 15-25 students (recommended: 8-15) | Council for Exceptional Children (2023) |

**Contributing Factors**

| Factor | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| Compensation | Special education teachers earn same or less than general education teachers despite higher demands |
| Paperwork burden | Teachers report spending 30-50% of time on IEP documentation rather than instruction |
| Emotional toll | High rates of burnout from managing complex needs, behavioral challenges, and conflict with administration |
| Insufficient preparation | Many preparation programs provide inadequate clinical experience |
| Alternative pathways | Emergency credentials and alternative certification produce teachers who leave at higher rates |

*Sources: Learning Policy Institute, "Solving the Special Education Teacher Shortage" (2024); Council for Exceptional Children, "Workforce Survey" (2023).*

## The IEP Process

### How It Works (and Fails)

**Intended Process**

1. Referral: Parent, teacher, or school identifies potential disability
2. Evaluation: Comprehensive assessment within 60 days (or state timeline)
3. Eligibility determination: Team determines if child qualifies under IDEA
4. IEP development: Team creates individualized plan with measurable goals, services, and placement
5. Implementation: School provides services specified in IEP
6. Annual review: Team reviews and revises IEP annually
7. Triennial re-evaluation: Comprehensive reassessment every 3 years

**Common Failures**

| Failure | Frequency | Impact |
|---------|-----------|--------|
| Delayed evaluations | 40% of districts report exceeding timelines | Children wait months or years for services |
| Predetermined placements | Widespread (GAO, 2023) | IEP teams decide placement before meeting |
| Inadequate parent participation | Majority of IEP meetings are district-led with limited parent input | Families feel excluded from process |
| Goals not measurable | 30-40% of IEP goals lack measurable criteria | Progress cannot be tracked |
| Services not provided | 20-30% of IEPs not fully implemented | Children do not receive promised services |
| Lack of AT consideration | AT not meaningfully considered in most IEPs | Students lack needed technology supports |

*Sources: GAO, "Special Education: IEP Implementation" (2023); National Council on Disability, "IDEA Series" (2023); OSEP monitoring data (2024).*

### Dispute Resolution

| Mechanism | Annual Volume | Resolution Rate | Average Cost to Family |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|------------------------|
| IEP meeting negotiation | Millions | Unknown | Free (but time-consuming) |
| State complaint | 12,000+ | 60-70% resolved | Free |
| Mediation | 4,500+ | 70-75% agreements | Free (federally funded) |
| Due process hearing | 18,000 filed; 2,500 adjudicated | 50-55% parent-favorable | $10,000-$50,000+ |
| Federal court | 500-700 cases/year | Varies | $50,000-$200,000+ |

*Sources: CADRE, Annual Dispute Resolution Report (2023); COPAA, "Cost of Due Process" survey (2023).*

**Access Disparities**

| Factor | Effect on Dispute Resolution Access |
|--------|--------------------------------------|
| Income | Families earning under $40,000 are 5x less likely to file due process than families earning over $100,000 |
| Race | White families file due process complaints at 2x the rate of Black families |
| Language | Non-English-speaking families file at less than half the rate of English-speaking families |
| Geography | Rural families face longer travel distances and fewer available attorneys |

*Source: COPAA, "Access to Justice in Special Education" (2024).*

## Recent Developments

### Post-COVID Recovery (2023-2025)

**Impact of COVID-19 on Students with Disabilities**

- 90% of students with IEPs experienced service disruptions during school closures (NCES, 2022)
- Compensatory education claims increased 300% (2021-2023)
- Learning loss was disproportionately severe for students with disabilities
- Behavioral and mental health needs increased significantly

**Recovery Efforts**

| Initiative | Funding | Status |
|------------|---------|--------|
| ARP IDEA funding | $3 billion (one-time) | Fully disbursed; expired September 2024 |
| Compensatory education orders | District-funded | Ongoing; backlog in most states |
| Extended school year expansion | State/local funded | Expanded in some states |
| Mental health integration | Various federal/state sources | Growing but insufficient |

### Congressional Activity (119th Congress, 2025-2026)

| Bill | Purpose | Status | Outlook |
|------|---------|--------|---------|
| IDEA Full Funding Act | Phase in 40% federal cost share over 10 years | Introduced | Bipartisan cosponsors; CBO cost concerns |
| IDEA Reauthorization | Comprehensive IDEA update (last reauthorized 2004) | Discussion stage | Complex; timing uncertain |
| Keep Kids in School Act | Limit exclusionary discipline for students with IEPs | Introduced | Democratic priority; Republican skepticism |
| Special Education Teacher Pipeline Act | Scholarships and loan forgiveness for special ed teachers | Introduced | Bipartisan support |

*Source: Congress.gov bill tracker; COPAA legislative updates (2025).*

### OSEP Regulatory Activity

| Action | Status | Impact |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Proposed rule on IDEA discipline provisions | Published 2024 | Would strengthen protections against exclusionary discipline |
| Guidance on AI and AT in IEPs | Issued 2024 | Clarifies obligations for AI-powered AT |

## Key Takeaways

1. **Chronic underfunding is the defining problem**: The federal government covers approximately 15% of excess costs, not the promised 40%, leaving states and districts to fill a $23+ billion annual gap
2. **The teacher shortage is a crisis**: 44 states report critical shortages, with 16% annual attrition and excessive caseloads undermining service quality
3. **Racial disproportionality persists**: Black and Native American students are overrepresented in restrictive categories and placements while being underserved in others
4. **Inclusion is progressing slowly**: 66% of students with disabilities spend 80%+ of the day in general education, but research suggests this could and should be higher
5. **Dispute resolution is inequitable**: Due process costs $10,000-$50,000+, creating a two-tier system based on family resources
6. **COVID recovery is incomplete**: One-time federal funding has expired, but learning loss and service gaps persist
7. **Outcomes remain unacceptable**: A 15-point graduation gap and 33-point post-secondary enrollment gap reveal systemic failure
8. **IDEA has not been reauthorized since 2004**: The law is two decades out of date
