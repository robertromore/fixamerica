# Education and IDEA: Actions

## Individual Actions

### Educate Yourself

- Learn the basics of IDEA: your child's right to a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE), Individualized Education Program (IEP), and Least Restrictive Environment (LRE)
- Visit the IDEA website at <https://sites.ed.gov/idea/> for federal resources and guidance
- Contact your state's Parent Training and Information Center (PTI) for free training on special education rights (find yours at <https://www.parentcenterhub.org/>)
- Read the Wrightslaw website (<https://www.wrightslaw.com/>) for legal guidance on IDEA rights and advocacy strategies
- Understand the difference between IDEA, Section 504 plans, and ADA accommodations
- Learn your state's specific special education regulations, which may provide protections beyond federal law

### Navigate the IEP Process

- Request a special education evaluation in writing if you suspect your child has a disability affecting learning
- Prepare for IEP meetings by reviewing your child's current IEP, progress reports, and assessment data
- Bring a support person or advocate to IEP meetings (you have the right to invite anyone you choose)
- Request that IEP goals be measurable and based on your child's present levels of performance
- Ask specific questions: "What data supports this placement?" "What supplementary aids and services were considered?" "How will progress be measured?"
- Document everything in writing: requests, concerns, and school responses
- Follow up IEP meetings with a written summary of what was agreed upon
- Request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense if you disagree with the school's evaluation

### Exercise Your Rights

- File a state complaint with your state education agency if the school district is not following IDEA requirements (free; 60-day resolution timeline)
- Request mediation through your state's dispute resolution system (free; voluntary; confidential)
- Contact your state's Protection and Advocacy organization for legal assistance (find yours at <https://www.ndrn.org/about/ndrn-member-agencies/>)
- Request compensatory education if your child was denied FAPE, especially for COVID-era service disruptions
- Know the timelines: schools must complete evaluations within 60 days (or your state's timeline) and hold annual IEP reviews

## Community Actions

### Local Organizing

- Join or start a Special Education Parent Advisory Council (SEPAC) in your school district
- Attend school board meetings to advocate for special education funding and staffing
- Partner with your local Parent Training and Information Center for advocacy training
- Build coalitions with other parent groups, disability organizations, and civil rights organizations
- Organize community forums on special education issues to educate families about their rights
- Connect with local independent living centers for advocacy support

### Awareness Campaigns

- Share information about IDEA rights in multiple languages and accessible formats in your community
- Organize events during National Special Education Day (December 2) and Disability Awareness Month (October)
- Create resource guides for families new to the special education process
- Engage local media in covering special education funding gaps and teacher shortages
- Use social media to share experiences and build community (#IDEAMatters, #SpecialEdAdvocacy, #FullFundingIDEA)
- Invite special education teachers to share their perspectives on workload and staffing challenges

### Support Families

- Volunteer as a parent mentor for families navigating the IEP process for the first time
- Translate IDEA materials and advocacy resources for non-English-speaking families
- Organize childcare for IEP meeting preparation sessions
- Create peer support groups for families of children with disabilities
- Train community members on filing state complaints and requesting mediation
- Partner with legal aid organizations to provide free special education legal clinics

## Political Actions

### Contact Your Representatives

**Call your members of Congress about IDEA reform:**

- Support the IDEA Full Funding Act to close the $23+ billion annual funding gap
- Support the Special Education Teacher Pipeline Act for scholarships and loan forgiveness
- Support IDEA reauthorization with strengthened inclusion, transition, and equity provisions
- Oppose any proposals to convert IDEA to block grants, which would eliminate the individual right to FAPE

### Template Email

```text
Subject: Fully Fund IDEA - Keep Congress's Promise to Students with Disabilities

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

I am writing as a constituent to urge you to support full funding of
the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).

When Congress enacted IDEA in 1975, it promised to cover 40% of the
excess cost of educating students with disabilities. Fifty years later,
the federal government covers only about 15%, forcing states and local
school districts to absorb a $23 billion annual gap. This chronic
underfunding produces teacher shortages, excessive caseloads,
inadequate services, and outcomes that fail our children.

I ask you to:

1. Co-sponsor and vote for the IDEA Full Funding Act, which would
   phase in the promised 40% federal cost share over 10 years
2. Support the Special Education Teacher Pipeline Act to address
   critical workforce shortages in 44 states
3. Support IDEA reauthorization that strengthens inclusion,
   transition services, and protections against racial
   disproportionality
4. Oppose any proposal to convert IDEA to a block grant program,
   which would eliminate the individual right to a free appropriate
   public education

Approximately 7.5 million children with disabilities rely on IDEA.
They deserve the education Congress promised them. Full funding is
not new spending -- it is fulfillment of a 50-year-old obligation.

Thank you for your attention to this important issue.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your Address]
```

## Talking Points

### For General Audiences

- Congress promised to cover 40% of special education costs in 1975 but has never provided more than about 15%, leaving a $23 billion annual gap
- 7.5 million children with disabilities depend on IDEA for their education, representing 15% of all public school students
- 44 states report critical shortages of special education teachers, with 39,000 unfilled positions nationwide
- Students with IEPs graduate at 72% compared to 87% for all students; only 33% enroll in post-secondary education within two years
- Due process hearings cost families $10,000-$50,000, creating a two-tier system where only affluent families can challenge inadequate services
- IDEA has not been reauthorized since 2004; the law is two decades out of date

### For Education Audiences

- Special education consumes 20-25% of district budgets while IDEA provides only 12-15% of the cost, forcing trade-offs with general education
- Special education teachers experience 16% annual attrition, double the rate for general education teachers
- 30-50% of special education teacher time goes to paperwork rather than instruction
- Research consistently shows that inclusive education benefits both students with disabilities and their nondisabled peers
- 34% of students with disabilities still spend less than 80% of the school day in general education classrooms
- The teacher shortage means 12% of special education teachers nationally work on emergency credentials

### For Policymaker Audiences

- IDEA full funding is a bipartisan issue: the IDEA Full Funding Act has co-sponsors from both parties
- Investing in special education produces long-term savings: every dollar spent on transition services returns $5-10 through increased employment and reduced SSI/SSDI dependence
- Black students are 2.3 times more likely to be placed in segregated settings, a civil rights issue that demands federal attention
- COVID-19 recovery for students with disabilities is incomplete: one-time ARP IDEA funding has expired but learning loss persists
- Phased full funding over 10 years costs approximately $2.3 billion per year in additional appropriations, a modest investment relative to the federal budget
- State and local governments would save billions as the federal share increases, freeing resources for other priorities

## Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Reality |
|--------------|---------|
| "IDEA is fully funded" | The federal government provides approximately 15% of excess costs, not the promised 40% |
| "Special education students cannot learn in general education classrooms" | Research overwhelmingly shows that most students with disabilities benefit from inclusion when adequate supports are provided |
| "IEP meetings are collaborative" | The power imbalance between districts and families means many IEP meetings are district-controlled; families often lack meaningful participation |
| "Parents can always challenge inadequate services" | Due process costs $10,000-$50,000+, making challenges unaffordable for most families |
| "All children with disabilities are identified" | Many children, particularly those from minority and low-income backgrounds, are underidentified or identified late |
| "Special education is too expensive" | The costs of not educating children with disabilities, including reduced employment, increased SSI/SSDI, and higher criminal justice involvement, far exceed the costs of adequate services |
| "Teacher shortages cannot be solved" | Research-proven strategies (scholarships, loan forgiveness, improved working conditions, competitive pay) can rebuild the pipeline |
| "IDEA requires inclusion for all students" | IDEA requires education in the least restrictive environment appropriate for each individual student; some students need and benefit from more specialized settings |
| "Racial disproportionality reflects actual disability rates" | Overrepresentation of Black students in subjective categories (emotional disturbance) but not objective categories (orthopedic impairment) indicates bias, not prevalence differences |
| "IDEA is a recent law" | IDEA was originally enacted in 1975 as the Education for All Handicapped Children Act; it has been law for 50 years |
