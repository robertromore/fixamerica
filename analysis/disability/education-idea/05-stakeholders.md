# Education and IDEA: Stakeholders

## Who Benefits from the Status Quo

### School District Administrators

**How They Benefit**

- Underfunding keeps expectations low; full funding would require demonstrating results
- Compliance-based enforcement is manageable; outcome-based accountability is threatening
- Control over IEP process allows districts to manage costs by limiting services
- Segregated placements are administratively simpler than inclusive models

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Medium-High**

- Oppose full funding mandates without full funding (want resources before requirements)
- Resist outcome-based enforcement that could expose inadequate services
- Some administrators genuinely seek reform but face resource constraints

### Teachers' Unions

**How They Benefit (Partially)**

- Current system protects teacher employment and seniority
- General education teachers may resist inclusion mandates that increase classroom demands
- Union contracts may limit special education caseload requirements

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Mixed**

- Support increased funding (benefits members)
- Resist inclusion mandates without adequate support and training
- Support higher special education teacher pay
- Oppose elimination of tenure or seniority protections in reform proposals

### Segregated Placement Providers

**Who**: Private special education schools, residential treatment facilities, alternative education programs

**How They Benefit**

- Receive per-pupil funding for students placed outside public schools
- Some private placements cost $50,000-$200,000+ per student per year
- Inclusion reforms would reduce their student populations and revenue

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **High** (to inclusion reforms specifically)

### Due Process Attorneys (Some)

**Who**: Attorneys who represent parents in special education disputes

**How They Benefit**

- Adversarial system generates demand for legal representation
- IDEA's attorney fee provision creates financial incentive
- Complex procedures require legal expertise that justifies high fees

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Low-Medium**

- Many parent attorneys are genuine advocates who support systemic reform
- Some benefit from a system that generates disputes

## Who Is Harmed

### Students with Disabilities

**7.5 million children and young adults receiving IDEA services**

**How They Are Harmed**

- Underfunded services do not meet their educational needs
- Segregated placements limit academic and social opportunities
- Excessive caseloads mean less individualized attention
- Poorly prepared teachers deliver lower-quality instruction
- Transition services fail to connect them to post-school opportunities
- Disproportionality subjects students of color to more restrictive and punitive treatment

**Disproportionate Impact**

| Population | Specific Harm |
|------------|---------------|
| Black students | Overidentified for emotional disturbance and intellectual disability; more restrictive placements; more exclusionary discipline |
| Latino students | Underidentified in some categories; language barriers to family participation |
| Native American students | 2x overrepresentation; cultural mismatch in assessment and services |
| Low-income students | Families cannot exercise procedural rights; schools in high-poverty areas have worse special education programs |
| Rural students | Limited access to specialists, therapists, and AT |
| Students with complex needs | Most likely to be placed in segregated settings regardless of potential for inclusion |

### Families

**How They Are Harmed**

- Navigate a complex system designed by and for institutional convenience
- Bear financial burden of due process when schools fail
- Experience stress, burnout, and adversarial relationships with schools
- Low-income and non-English-speaking families are systematically disadvantaged
- Must advocate constantly to ensure schools implement IEPs

### General Education Students (Indirect)

- Unfunded mandate diverts resources from general education programs
- Teacher shortages in special education increase class sizes in general education
- Poorly implemented inclusion can disrupt general education classrooms (though well-implemented inclusion benefits all students)

### Special Education Teachers

**How They Are Harmed**

- Excessive caseloads (15-25 students vs. recommended 8-15)
- 30-50% of time spent on paperwork rather than instruction
- Inadequate preparation programs
- Same or lower pay than general education teachers despite greater demands
- High burnout and 16% annual attrition rate
- Emotional toll of working in an under-resourced system

## Government Stakeholders

### Federal Agencies

| Agency | Role | Stance on Reform |
|--------|------|------------------|
| OSEP (Office of Special Education Programs) | IDEA administration, monitoring, enforcement | Supportive of results-driven accountability; constrained by political leadership |
| OSERS (Office of Special Education and Rehabilitative Services) | Parent agency of OSEP | Supportive within administration priorities |
| OCR (Office for Civil Rights) | Discrimination complaints in education | Active enforcement of disability rights |
| GAO | Oversight and evaluation | Has documented IDEA implementation failures repeatedly |
| CRS | Congressional research | Provides nonpartisan IDEA analysis |

### State Education Agencies

**Role**: Administer IDEA at state level; distribute federal funds; monitor local districts; develop state special education regulations

**Key Concerns**

- Underfunding forces states to absorb costs
- Federal monitoring creates administrative burden
- Workforce shortages affect every state
- State variation in regulations creates confusion

## Power Analysis

### Who Has Power to Change the System

| Actor | Type of Power | Likelihood of Action |
|-------|---------------|----------------------|
| Congress | Legislation: IDEA reauthorization, funding | Low-Medium (competing priorities) |
| OSEP | Regulatory: enforcement approach, guidance | Medium (within administration priorities) |
| Federal courts | Judicial: FAPE standard, LRE interpretation | Medium (through individual cases) |
| State legislatures | Legislation: state special education law, funding | Medium (varies by state) |
| School boards | Policy: local implementation decisions | Medium (responsive to parent pressure) |
| Teachers' unions | Labor: working conditions, caseloads, professional development | Medium-High (significant political influence) |

### Coalition Potential

**Strongest Coalition**: Parent organizations + disability rights organizations + CEC + teachers' unions (on funding) + bipartisan congressional champions

**Key Bridge Issues**

- **Full funding**: Unites nearly all stakeholders; the question is political will
- **Workforce development**: Teachers' unions, parent organizations, and disability rights organizations all support
- **Dispute resolution reform**: COPAA and CADRE have developed consensus proposals
- **Transition services**: Employment-focused reforms attract business community support

**Challenging Coalitions**

- **Inclusion vs. specialized services**: Divides parent community; some families want full inclusion while others want specialized settings
- **Accountability reform**: Teachers' unions resist outcome-based accountability; parent organizations support it
- **Disproportionality reform**: Requires confronting racial bias; politically sensitive
