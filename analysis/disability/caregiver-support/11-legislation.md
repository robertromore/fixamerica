# Caregiver Support: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

This document presents draft legislation addressing the caregiver support crisis through federal bills, state model legislation, and regulatory reforms. The legislative approach targets four core problems: inadequate HCBS access, poverty-level caregiver wages, insufficient family caregiver support, and fragmented program administration. Each proposal includes draft text, explanation, challenges, and an analysis of potential loopholes and shortcomings.

---

## Federal Legislation

### The Home and Community-Based Services Access Act

**Purpose**: Eliminate Medicaid HCBS waiting lists by converting HCBS from an optional to a mandatory Medicaid benefit, with enhanced federal matching to support state implementation.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Home and Community-Based Services
Access Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) Over 800,000 Americans are on waiting lists for Medicaid
    home and community-based services.
(2) The Supreme Court's decision in Olmstead v. L.C., 527 U.S.
    581 (1999), requires states to serve people with disabilities
    in the most integrated setting appropriate to their needs.
(3) Medicaid's historical structure creates an institutional bias
    by making nursing home care an entitlement while community-based
    services remain optional.
(4) Home and community-based services cost an average of $42,000
    per person annually, compared to $96,000 for institutional care.

SEC. 3. MANDATORY HOME AND COMMUNITY-BASED SERVICES.

(a) IN GENERAL.--Section 1915 of the Social Security Act (42 U.S.C.
1396n) is amended by adding the following new subsection:

"(l) MANDATORY HOME AND COMMUNITY-BASED SERVICES.--

"(1) REQUIREMENT.--Beginning on the date that is 3 years after
    the date of enactment of this subsection, each State plan under
    this title shall provide home and community-based services to
    all individuals who--
    "(A) are eligible for medical assistance under the State plan;
    "(B) require a level of care that would otherwise be provided
        in an institutional setting; and
    "(C) can be appropriately served in a home or community-based
        setting, as determined by a person-centered assessment.

"(2) MINIMUM SERVICE PACKAGE.--Home and community-based services
    provided under paragraph (1) shall include, at a minimum--
    "(A) personal care services;
    "(B) homemaker and chore services;
    "(C) respite care;
    "(D) assistive technology and environmental modifications;
    "(E) supported employment services;
    "(F) self-directed service options; and
    "(G) care coordination.

"(3) SELF-DIRECTION OPTION.--Each State shall offer a self-directed
    service option under which the individual may hire, train, and
    supervise the individual's own caregivers, including family
    members.

"(4) WAITING LIST ELIMINATION.--
    "(A) No State may maintain a waiting list for services required
        under this subsection after the date that is 5 years after
        the date of enactment of this subsection.
    "(B) Each State shall submit to the Secretary an annual plan
        for the reduction of any existing waiting list, with interim
        benchmarks."

SEC. 4. ENHANCED FEDERAL MATCHING.

(a) HCBS FMAP INCREASE.--Section 1905(b) of the Social Security Act
(42 U.S.C. 1396d(b)) is amended by adding at the end the following:

"For expenditures under section 1915(l) for home and community-based
services, the Federal medical assistance percentage shall be
increased by 10 percentage points, except that in no case shall the
Federal medical assistance percentage exceed 95 percent."

(b) INSTITUTIONAL REBALANCING.--For any State in which expenditures
for institutional long-term services and supports exceed 40 percent
of total long-term services and supports expenditures under this
title as of the date that is 5 years after the date of enactment of
this Act, the Federal medical assistance percentage for institutional
services shall be reduced by 2 percentage points per year until such
expenditures are at or below 40 percent.

SEC. 5. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated such sums as may be necessary
to carry out this Act.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill converts HCBS from an optional Medicaid benefit to a mandatory one, ensuring that all eligible individuals can access community-based services. The enhanced FMAP provides fiscal incentive for states, while the institutional rebalancing provision creates a financial disincentive for continued overreliance on nursing homes. The 3-year phase-in allows states to build capacity.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Cost: CBO would likely score this at $150-200 billion over 10 years
- State resistance to new mandates, even with enhanced matching
- Nursing home industry lobbying against institutional FMAP reduction
- Workforce capacity may lag behind service expansion

**Refinements**: Phase-in period could be extended to 5 years; institutional FMAP reduction could be made less aggressive; additional workforce development funding could be coupled with the mandate.

### The Caregiver Workforce Compensation Act

**Purpose**: Establish minimum compensation standards for Medicaid-funded direct care workers to address the workforce crisis.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Caregiver Workforce Compensation Act".

SEC. 2. MINIMUM COMPENSATION FOR DIRECT CARE WORKERS.

(a) DEFINITION.--For purposes of this Act, the term "direct care
worker" means any individual employed to provide personal care
services, home health aide services, or direct support services
to individuals receiving medical assistance under a State plan under
title XIX of the Social Security Act, including personal care aides,
home health aides, and direct support professionals.

(b) MINIMUM WAGE REQUIREMENT.--Section 1902(a) of the Social Security
Act (42 U.S.C. 1396a(a)) is amended by inserting after paragraph
(86) the following:

"(87) provide that any direct care worker providing services funded
under this title shall be compensated at a rate not less than the
greater of--
    "(A) 150 percent of the applicable minimum wage under section
        6 of the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 (29 U.S.C. 206);
        or
    "(B) 150 percent of the applicable State or local minimum wage;
and that not less than 80 percent of Medicaid payments for home
and community-based services shall be expended on direct care worker
compensation, including wages and benefits."

(c) BENEFIT REQUIREMENTS.--Each State plan shall require that
providers of home and community-based services funded under this
title offer direct care workers who work 30 or more hours per week--
    (1) employer-sponsored health insurance or an equivalent benefit;
    (2) paid sick leave of not fewer than 5 days per year;
    (3) paid family and medical leave consistent with the Family
        and Medical Leave Act of 1993; and
    (4) access to employer-sponsored retirement savings plans.

(d) RATE ADEQUACY.--The Secretary shall ensure that Federal financial
participation is available at the enhanced rate under section
1905(b) for the additional costs incurred by States to comply with
this section.

SEC. 3. WORKFORCE DEVELOPMENT.

(a) DIRECT CARE WORKFORCE FUND.--There is established in the
Treasury the Direct Care Workforce Fund, to be administered by
the Secretary of Health and Human Services, with an annual
appropriation of $1,000,000,000 for fiscal years 2027 through
2036, to support--
    (1) registered apprenticeship programs for direct care occupations;
    (2) a national portable credential for direct care workers;
    (3) student loan forgiveness for direct care workers who complete
        3 or more years of service;
    (4) career ladder programs linking direct care to nursing and
        other healthcare professions; and
    (5) recruitment initiatives targeting underrepresented communities.

SEC. 4. EFFECTIVE DATE.

The compensation requirements under section 2 shall take effect
3 years after the date of enactment of this Act.
```

**Explanation**: The 150% minimum wage floor ensures that direct care workers earn a livable wage. The 80% pass-through requirement prevents provider agencies from absorbing wage increases as profit. Benefit requirements address the lack of health insurance and paid leave that drives turnover. The Workforce Fund invests in long-term pipeline development.

**Potential Challenges**:

- State cost concerns even with enhanced FMAP
- Provider agency opposition to pass-through requirements
- Implementation complexity across 50 state Medicaid programs
- 150% minimum wage floor may be insufficient in high-cost areas

**Refinements**: Consider regional wage adjustments; allow a longer phase-in for smaller providers; include exceptions for family caregivers in self-directed programs who may prefer flexible arrangements.

### The Family Caregiver Economic Security Act

**Purpose**: Provide financial support and workplace protections for unpaid family caregivers.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Family Caregiver Economic Security Act".

SEC. 2. REFUNDABLE CAREGIVER TAX CREDIT.

(a) IN GENERAL.--Subpart C of part IV of subchapter A of chapter 1
of the Internal Revenue Code of 1986 is amended by adding the
following new section:

"SEC. 36C. FAMILY CAREGIVER CREDIT.

"(a) ALLOWANCE OF CREDIT.--In the case of an eligible caregiver,
there shall be allowed as a credit against the tax imposed by this
subtitle an amount equal to the lesser of--
    "(1) $5,000; or
    "(2) the qualified caregiving expenses paid by the eligible
        caregiver during the taxable year.

"(b) ELIGIBLE CAREGIVER.--For purposes of this section, the term
'eligible caregiver' means an individual who--
    "(1) provides at least 80 hours per month of caregiving
        assistance to a qualifying care recipient;
    "(2) is not compensated for such caregiving by any government
        program or private employer; and
    "(3) is a family member or designated caregiver of the
        qualifying care recipient.

"(c) QUALIFYING CARE RECIPIENT.--A qualifying care recipient is
an individual who--
    "(1) has been certified by a licensed healthcare provider as
        requiring assistance with 2 or more activities of daily
        living; or
    "(2) requires substantial supervision due to a cognitive or
        developmental disability.

"(d) REFUNDABILITY.--The credit under this section shall be treated
as a refundable credit."

SEC. 3. SOCIAL SECURITY CAREGIVER CREDITS.

(a) Section 215 of the Social Security Act (42 U.S.C. 415) is
amended to provide that, for any calendar year in which an
individual provides at least 80 hours per month of caregiving
to a qualifying care recipient, such individual shall be credited
with deemed wages equal to 50 percent of the national average
wage index for purposes of computing primary insurance amounts.

SEC. 4. CAREGIVER DISCRIMINATION PROTECTION.

(a) It shall be an unlawful employment practice for an employer
to discharge, refuse to hire, or otherwise discriminate against
any individual because such individual is a caregiver, as defined
in this Act.

(b) This section shall be enforced in accordance with the procedures
set forth in title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964.

SEC. 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect for taxable years beginning after
December 31 of the year of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: The tax credit provides direct financial relief to family caregivers. Social Security credits address the retirement security crisis caused by reduced employment. Caregiver discrimination protection ensures caregivers cannot be fired or penalized at work for their caregiving responsibilities.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Revenue cost of the tax credit (estimated $15-20 billion over 10 years)
- Social Security trust fund impact of deemed wage credits
- Defining and verifying caregiver status for tax purposes
- Employer opposition to caregiver discrimination protections

**Refinements**: Phase in Social Security credits; partner with states for verification; include safe harbor provisions for small employers.

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Direct Care Worker Wage Act

**Purpose**: Establish state-level minimum wage for Medicaid-funded direct care workers.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act shall be known as the "[State] Direct Care Worker Fair
Compensation Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Direct care worker" means any individual employed to provide
personal care, home health aide, or direct support services to
persons receiving services funded by the State Medicaid program.

(b) "Minimum direct care wage" means a rate of not less than
150 percent of the State minimum wage.

SECTION 3. MINIMUM COMPENSATION.

(a) No provider of Medicaid-funded home and community-based services
shall compensate a direct care worker at a rate less than the
minimum direct care wage.

(b) The State Medicaid agency shall set reimbursement rates for
home and community-based services at levels sufficient to support
compliance with this section.

(c) Not less than 80 percent of Medicaid reimbursement for home
and community-based services shall be expended on direct care
worker wages and benefits.

SECTION 4. REPORTING.

Providers shall report annually to the State Medicaid agency on
direct care worker compensation, including wages, benefits, turnover
rates, and vacancy rates.

SECTION 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on [date], with full compliance required
within 2 years.
```

**Adaptations**: States with higher costs of living may set the floor at 200% of minimum wage. States with existing living wage laws may reference those standards. Rural states may include geographic differentials.

## Regulatory Framework

### CMS HCBS Access and Adequacy Rule

**Existing Authority**: Section 1902(a)(30)(A) of the Social Security Act requires states to ensure Medicaid payments are "sufficient to enlist enough providers" for services.

**Draft Regulation**:

CMS should finalize a rule requiring states to demonstrate that HCBS payment rates are sufficient to:

1. Maintain direct care worker wages at 150% of the applicable minimum wage
2. Achieve and maintain direct care worker vacancy rates below 10%
3. Ensure that individuals authorized for HCBS can access services within 30 days
4. Maintain provider networks with geographic access standards

States failing to meet these standards would be required to submit corrective action plans with benchmarked rate increases.

**Explanation**: This regulation uses existing Medicaid authority to establish enforceable access standards without requiring new legislation. CMS proposed elements of this approach in its 2023 HCBS access rule; the regulation would codify and strengthen those requirements.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Spending Clause**: Federal conditions on Medicaid funding must be clear and unambiguous; HCBS mandate and wage requirements satisfy this standard
- **Tenth Amendment**: Medicaid conditions do not compel state action; states may decline Medicaid participation (though none has done so)
- **Equal Protection**: HCBS mandate promotes equal access for people with disabilities; aligns with ADA and *Olmstead*

### Preemption Questions

- Federal wage floor for Medicaid-funded workers would preempt lower state wages for those workers but not affect general state minimum wage laws
- State self-direction programs may need accommodation for family caregivers who prefer different compensation arrangements

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- CMS enforcement through Medicaid plan approval and federal matching withholding
- DOJ enforcement of *Olmstead* obligations
- Private right of action for individuals denied HCBS services
- IRS enforcement of tax credit eligibility
- DOL enforcement of FLSA for direct care workers

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Wage floor should be indexed to inflation and reviewed every 3 years
- HCBS waiting list elimination progress should be reported to Congress annually
- FMAP enhancement should be evaluated after 5 years for fiscal sustainability
- Workforce Fund appropriation should be reauthorized after 10 years

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Home and Community-Based Services Access Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Assessment manipulation | States could use restrictive person-centered assessments to limit eligibility for HCBS entitlement | Critical |
| Service package minimums | States could meet minimum package requirements with inadequate service hours | High |
| Institutional FMAP reduction avoidance | States could reclassify institutional spending to avoid threshold triggers | High |
| Self-direction limitation | States could technically offer self-direction while making it administratively inaccessible | Medium |
| Provider network inadequacy | States could authorize services but fail to build adequate provider networks | Critical |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No federal assessment standard | Inconsistent eligibility determinations across states | Federalism and state discretion |
| No service hour minimums | Authorized services may be insufficient to meet actual needs | Cost containment pressure |
| Phase-in allows continued waiting | 5-year waiting list elimination allows continued harm during transition | Political compromise |
| Does not address workforce supply | Mandating services without workforce does not ensure access | Requires separate legislation |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Establish federal minimum standards for person-centered assessments with independent review process
2. Set minimum service hour standards based on assessed need levels (e.g., minimum 20 hours/week for individuals requiring assistance with 3+ ADLs)
3. Require independent auditing of LTSS spending classifications to prevent institutional spending reclassification
4. Establish self-direction participation benchmarks and require states to report barriers
5. Couple HCBS mandate with workforce investment through the Caregiver Workforce Compensation Act

### Caregiver Workforce Compensation Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Worker classification avoidance | Providers could classify workers as independent contractors to avoid wage and benefit requirements | Critical |
| Part-time scheduling | Providers could limit hours below 30/week to avoid benefit requirements | High |
| Pass-through accounting manipulation | Providers could inflate non-wage costs to appear compliant while suppressing wages | Medium |
| Geographic arbitrage | Providers in border areas could shift operations to lower-wage jurisdictions | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| 150% floor may be insufficient in high-cost areas | Workers in expensive metro areas still struggle | National standard cannot address all local costs |
| Does not cover workers outside Medicaid | Private-pay and non-Medicaid workers not covered | Limited to Medicaid authority |
| Benefit requirements add complexity | Small providers may struggle with administration | Administrative burden |
| No overtime protection enhancement | Existing FLSA applies but enforcement is weak | Outside scope of Medicaid legislation |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Include anti-misclassification provisions with penalties for providers who improperly classify workers as independent contractors
2. Prorate benefit requirements for part-time workers (health insurance for 20+ hours; paid leave for all workers)
3. Require independent audits of pass-through compliance with penalties for noncompliance
4. Include cost-of-living adjustments for high-cost areas (e.g., 175% floor in metro areas above national cost median)
5. Strengthen DOL enforcement of FLSA for home care workers through dedicated enforcement funding

### Family Caregiver Economic Security Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Fraudulent claims | Individuals could claim caregiver credit without providing actual care | High |
| Definition gaming | Overly narrow definitions could exclude caregivers who provide substantial care | Medium |
| Employer retaliation | Employers could find pretextual reasons to terminate caregivers despite discrimination protection | High |
| Social Security credit impact | Deemed wages could interact unpredictably with disability and survivor benefits | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| $5,000 cap is modest relative to costs | Average caregiver expenses exceed $7,000/year | Fiscal constraints |
| No paid leave component | Caregivers still lack wage replacement during caregiving periods | Separate legislative need |
| Verification burden | Proving caregiving hours is difficult | Nature of informal caregiving |
| Does not address all caregivers | 80 hours/month threshold excludes some caregivers with significant but lower-hour commitments | Targeting trade-offs |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Establish clear verification procedures using healthcare provider certification and self-attestation with audit sampling
2. Raise credit cap to $7,500 and index to inflation
3. Lower hour threshold to 60 hours/month to capture more caregivers while maintaining targeting
4. Strengthen anti-retaliation provisions with shifting burden of proof after caregiver status disclosure
5. Commission SSA study on deemed wage interactions before implementation

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| State implementation variation | Federal minimum standards with CMS enforcement authority |
| Coordination across programs | Interagency caregiving policy council with enforcement powers |
| Data collection gaps | Standardized reporting requirements for all Medicaid HCBS programs |
| Rural access disparities | Dedicated rural access funding and telehealth integration |
| Immigration workforce barriers | Coordinate with immigration reform for caregiver visa pathways |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- All compensation standards indexed to inflation with triennial review
- HCBS entitlement effectiveness reviewed by GAO at 3 and 7 years
- Workforce Fund reauthorization required at 10 years with outcome reporting
- Tax credit impact assessed by Treasury at 5 years
- CMS required to publish annual HCBS access and quality report card
