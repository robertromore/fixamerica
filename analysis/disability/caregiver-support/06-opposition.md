# Caregiver Support: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to caregiver support reform comes from multiple sources with distinct motivations. Fiscal conservatives oppose new spending commitments, the institutional care industry resists funding rebalancing, some business interests oppose labor protections for care workers, and ideological opponents resist expanding the role of government in what they view as private family matters. Understanding these opposition dynamics is essential for designing reforms that can overcome resistance and build sustainable coalitions.

---

## Fiscal Opposition

### Cost Concerns

The most common objection to caregiver support reform centers on cost. Proposals like the Better Care Better Jobs Act ($150 billion over 10 years) face resistance from lawmakers and groups committed to fiscal restraint.

**Key Arguments**

- Federal spending is already unsustainable; adding new entitlements worsens the fiscal outlook
- Medicaid is already one of the largest items in federal and state budgets
- Making HCBS an entitlement would create open-ended spending obligations
- States cannot absorb their share of expanded HCBS costs even with enhanced FMAP
- Tax increases to fund caregiving expansion would harm economic growth

**Who Makes These Arguments**

| Actor | Mechanism |
|-------|-----------|
| Congressional Republicans (most) | Vote against HCBS expansion bills; propose cuts to Medicaid |
| Heritage Foundation | Policy papers opposing Medicaid expansion |
| Cato Institute | Research arguing against government caregiving programs |
| National Taxpayers Union | Lobbying against new federal spending |
| Americans for Prosperity | Grassroots mobilization against entitlement expansion |
| Some Democratic moderates | Concern about deficit impact; oppose unfunded mandates |

**Strength of This Opposition**: Very high. Fiscal arguments have defeated every major HCBS expansion proposal at the federal level, including the $400 billion Build Back Better HCBS provision (2022).

**Counterarguments**

- HCBS is cheaper per person than institutional care ($42,000 vs. $96,000 annually)
- Caregiver support reduces downstream healthcare costs, nursing home admissions, and emergency services use
- Family caregiver economic losses ($600 billion/year in unpaid labor; $324,000 lifetime earnings loss per caregiver) represent enormous unaccounted costs
- The current system is not "free"; costs are borne by individuals and families rather than shared publicly

## Institutional Care Industry Opposition

### Nursing Home Lobby

The nursing home industry is one of the most powerful opponents of HCBS expansion, viewing community-based services as a direct threat to institutional census and revenue.

**Industry Profile**

- Approximately 15,000 nursing homes nationwide
- Revenue: approximately $170 billion annually (majority from Medicaid and Medicare)
- Increasingly owned by private equity and large corporate chains
- Primary lobby: American Health Care Association (AHCA), representing 14,000 facilities

**Opposition Tactics**

| Tactic | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Lobbying | AHCA spends $5-8 million annually on federal lobbying |
| Campaign contributions | Industry PACs contribute to key committee members in both parties |
| State-level influence | State nursing home associations influence Medicaid rate-setting and waiver design |
| Messaging | Frame institutional care as "essential safety net"; argue HCBS cannot serve all populations |
| Regulatory capture | Industry representatives serve on state advisory bodies and rate-setting commissions |
| Grassroots opposition | Mobilize nursing home employees concerned about job losses from rebalancing |

**Key Arguments**

- Some individuals genuinely need 24/7 skilled nursing care that cannot be safely provided at home
- Rapid deinstitutionalization risks harm if community alternatives are inadequate
- Nursing homes serve as employers and economic anchors in many communities
- HCBS quality oversight is less rigorous than institutional oversight

**Effectiveness**: Moderate to high. The nursing home lobby has successfully slowed HCBS rebalancing in many states and at the federal level. However, declining institutional census and growing HCBS demand are weakening its position over time.

## Employer and Business Opposition

### Opposition to Worker Protections

Some business interests oppose labor protections for direct care workers, including minimum wage increases, overtime requirements, and unionization rights.

**Key Actors**

| Actor | Position |
|-------|----------|
| National Federation of Independent Business | Opposes mandated wage increases |
| U.S. Chamber of Commerce | Opposes expanded FLSA coverage and paid leave mandates |
| Home care agency trade associations | Resist self-direction programs that bypass agency model |
| Small home care agencies | Argue higher wages will force closures |

**Key Arguments**

- Minimum wage mandates will reduce jobs available for care workers
- Overtime regulations make scheduling 24/7 care more expensive and complex
- Unionization drives up costs that Medicaid rates cannot absorb
- Small agencies will close, reducing service options

**Counterarguments**

- Current wages drive turnover that is itself enormously costly
- Research shows moderate wage increases in caregiving do not reduce employment (monopsony effects)
- Union representation has been associated with higher wages, lower turnover, and better care quality
- The concern about agency closures is real but can be addressed through rate increases rather than wage suppression

## Ideological Opposition

### Government Role Objections

Some opposition to caregiver support reform is rooted in philosophical objections to expanding government responsibility for caregiving.

**Key Arguments**

- Caregiving is primarily a family and community responsibility, not a government function
- Government programs crowd out private, charitable, and faith-based caregiving
- Families should be free to make their own care decisions without government involvement
- Expanding Medicaid HCBS creates dependency on government programs

**Who Makes These Arguments**

- Social conservatives who view family caregiving as a moral obligation
- Libertarians who oppose government involvement in personal and family decisions
- Some religious organizations that provide faith-based caregiving services
- Think tanks including Heritage Foundation, American Enterprise Institute (on some issues)

**Strength of This Opposition**: Moderate. While these arguments resonate with some constituencies, they are weakened by the practical reality that families cannot sustain caregiving without support, and the growing recognition (including among conservatives) that family caregivers need help.

### States' Rights and Federalism Objections

Some opponents frame their resistance in terms of federalism:

- Federal HCBS mandates impose unfunded obligations on states
- States should have flexibility to design their own caregiving systems
- Federal wage mandates override state labor policy decisions
- *Olmstead* enforcement represents federal overreach into state Medicaid programs

## Opposition Within the Disability Community

### Internal Tensions

Not all opposition comes from outside the disability community. Some internal disagreements include:

**Self-Direction vs. Worker Protections**

- Some disability rights advocates worry that worker protection mandates (training requirements, overtime rules, background check requirements) will limit the pool of available caregivers and reduce consumer choice
- Concern that unionization of self-directed workers creates tensions with the employer-employee relationship between person with disability and their caregiver

**Family Payment Concerns**

- Some advocates worry that paying family members as caregivers creates conflicts of interest or reduces quality
- Concerns about exploitation of family payment programs
- Tension between recognizing family caregiving labor and maintaining professional caregiving standards

**Institutional Transition Concerns**

- Some families of people with significant disabilities argue that institutional settings provide needed safety and medical supervision
- Parents of adults with intellectual disabilities in some states have organized to preserve institutional options
- "Voice of the Retarded" (now "VOR") advocates for preserving institutional choice

## Opposition Strategies and How They Succeed

### Blocking Strategies

| Strategy | Example | Effectiveness |
|----------|---------|---------------|
| Filibuster / legislative procedure | HCBS provisions stripped from reconciliation bills | Very high |
| Cost scoring | CBO scores used to argue proposals are unaffordable | High |
| Incremental delay | "Study first, act later" approach to major reforms | High |
| Divide and conquer | Pit disability vs. aging vs. labor constituencies against each other | Moderate |
| Messaging | Frame reform as "entitlement expansion" rather than "caregiver support" | Moderate |
| State resistance | Refuse to expand HCBS despite federal incentives | Moderate-High |

### Why Opposition Often Prevails

1. **Asymmetric intensity**: Opponents (especially nursing home industry) are highly organized and focused; supporters are dispersed across multiple constituencies
2. **Status quo bias**: The existing system, however dysfunctional, is familiar; change creates uncertainty
3. **Cost visibility**: The cost of reform is visible and quantifiable; the cost of inaction is distributed, hidden, and borne by individuals
4. **Complexity**: Caregiving policy is technically complex, making it difficult to mobilize public support
5. **Political cycles**: Long-term investments in caregiving infrastructure do not produce visible results within election cycles

## Responding to Opposition

### Most Promising Counterstrategies

| Opposition Argument | Counter-Strategy |
|---------------------|------------------|
| "Too expensive" | Demonstrate cost of inaction; compare HCBS to institutional costs; frame as investment |
| "Government overreach" | Emphasize self-direction and individual choice; frame as supporting families |
| "Nursing homes are necessary" | Acknowledge need for some institutional care while demonstrating *Olmstead* mandate |
| "Wage mandates kill jobs" | Present evidence on monopsony, turnover costs, and quality improvements |
| "States should decide" | Offer flexibility within federal framework; use enhanced FMAP incentives |

### Building an Overcoming Coalition

The most effective coalition for overcoming opposition would unite:

- Disability rights organizations (moral authority, legal framework)
- AARP (political power, public credibility)
- Labor unions (organizing capacity, political resources)
- Business allies (employers who benefit from employees not having to leave to provide care)
- Veterans organizations (VA caregiver programs demonstrate model)
- Faith-based organizations (caregiving as moral imperative)
