# Caregiver Support: Overview

## Executive Summary

The caregiving crisis in America is one of the most significant yet underaddressed policy challenges facing people with disabilities. An estimated 53 million Americans serve as unpaid family caregivers, with approximately 16.8 million providing care specifically to adults with disabilities. These caregivers provide an economic contribution valued at over $600 billion annually in uncompensated labor, yet they receive minimal public support, face severe financial penalties, and experience physical and mental health consequences that compound over time. Simultaneously, the paid caregiving workforce, including personal care attendants, home health aides, and direct support professionals, faces poverty-level wages, inadequate benefits, and turnover rates exceeding 40% annually, creating a workforce crisis that threatens the ability of people with disabilities to live independently.

The caregiver support system in the United States is fragmented across federal, state, and local programs with inconsistent eligibility criteria, inadequate funding, and significant gaps in coverage. The National Family Caregiver Support Program, funded at approximately $200 million annually, reaches fewer than 700,000 caregivers out of tens of millions who need assistance. Medicaid home and community-based services (HCBS) waiver programs, the primary public funding mechanism for paid caregiving, maintain waiting lists totaling over 800,000 people nationwide. The direct care workforce shortage is projected to reach 4.7 million unfilled positions by 2028, threatening the entire infrastructure of community-based disability services.

These failures have concrete consequences. People with disabilities are forced into institutional settings not because they need institutional care, but because community-based caregiving is unavailable or unaffordable. Family caregivers leave the workforce, depleting their own retirement savings and economic security. Paid caregivers, disproportionately women of color, work in conditions that perpetuate poverty and racial inequity. Reforming caregiver support is essential to fulfilling the promise of the ADA, the *Olmstead* decision, and the broader commitment to community integration for Americans with disabilities.

## Scope

### What This Topic Covers

- **Family caregiving**: Unpaid care provided by family members and friends to people with disabilities
- **Paid caregiving workforce**: Personal care attendants, home health aides, direct support professionals, and other paid caregivers
- **Respite services**: Temporary relief for family caregivers
- **Caregiver compensation**: Wages, benefits, and economic conditions for paid caregivers; financial support for family caregivers
- **Workforce development**: Recruitment, training, retention, and career pathways for the direct care workforce
- **Support infrastructure**: Programs, policies, and systems that support caregivers, including the National Family Caregiver Support Program, Medicaid HCBS waivers, and state caregiver programs
- **Self-direction**: Programs that allow people with disabilities to hire, train, and manage their own caregivers

## Core Tensions

### Individual Responsibility vs. Social Infrastructure

A fundamental tension exists between the view that caregiving is primarily a family responsibility and the recognition that caregiving is a public good requiring social infrastructure. Conservatives emphasize family obligation and voluntary community support, while progressives argue that the scale and complexity of modern caregiving demands public investment. This tension shapes debates over public funding levels, program design, and the role of government in supporting caregivers.

### Consumer Direction vs. Workforce Standards

The disability rights movement strongly supports self-directed services, which allow people with disabilities to hire, train, and manage their own caregivers, including family members. However, self-direction can conflict with workforce standards when it allows exemptions from minimum wage, overtime, or training requirements. Balancing the autonomy of people with disabilities with the labor rights of caregivers requires careful policy design.

### Community Care vs. Institutional Bias

Despite the Supreme Court's *Olmstead v. L.C.* (1999) decision requiring states to serve people with disabilities in the most integrated setting appropriate, Medicaid's institutional bias persists. Federal matching rates historically favor institutional care over HCBS, and waiting lists for community services force people into institutions. Rebalancing spending from institutional to community-based care faces resistance from the nursing home industry and from states reluctant to expand HCBS entitlements.

### Cost Containment vs. Adequate Compensation

Medicaid reimbursement rates for HCBS are set by states and are often too low to support competitive wages for direct care workers. Raising wages increases costs to already-strained state budgets. Yet low wages drive turnover, vacancy, and quality problems that ultimately cost more. Breaking this cycle requires sustained investment that policymakers have been reluctant to commit.

## Vision of Success

A functioning caregiver support system would ensure that every American with a disability who needs personal assistance can access qualified, fairly compensated caregivers in their home and community. Family caregivers would receive meaningful financial support, respite services, and protections against economic hardship. The direct care workforce would be recognized as essential professionals with living wages, benefits, career pathways, and training that reflects the skill and importance of their work. Medicaid HCBS waiting lists would be eliminated, and community-based services would be available as an entitlement rather than a rationed resource. Self-directed services would be the norm, empowering people with disabilities to control their own care while ensuring that all caregivers, paid and unpaid, are supported. The racial and gender inequities embedded in the current caregiving system would be addressed through intentional policy design. Caregiving would be understood not as a private burden but as a shared social responsibility and a cornerstone of disability rights.
