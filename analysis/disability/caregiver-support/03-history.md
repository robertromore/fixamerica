# Caregiver Support: History

## The Institutional Era (Pre-1960s)

### Warehousing and Family Burden

For most of American history, people with significant disabilities faced two options: institutional confinement or total dependence on family care without any public support. State-run institutions for people with intellectual disabilities, mental illness, and physical disabilities proliferated in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, housing over 500,000 Americans by 1960. Those who remained at home relied entirely on family members, overwhelmingly women, who received no financial assistance, respite, or recognition.

**Key Developments**

- 1800s-1900s: State institutions expand across the country; families pressured to institutionalize disabled family members
- 1935: Social Security Act creates Old-Age Insurance but excludes most people with disabilities and provides nothing for caregivers
- 1950: Social Security Amendments add Aid to the Permanently and Totally Disabled, providing some income but no caregiver support
- 1956: Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI) established for workers who become disabled
- 1960: Over 500,000 Americans confined in state institutions

### The Hidden Workforce

Throughout this era, the formal caregiving workforce consisted primarily of institutional attendants, overwhelmingly poorly paid and untrained. Home-based paid caregiving was virtually nonexistent as a public service. Family caregivers, particularly mothers of children with disabilities and wives caring for disabled veterans, performed intensive care work without acknowledgment or support.

## Deinstitutionalization and the Rise of Community Care (1960s-1980s)

### The Deinstitutionalization Movement

Exposes of institutional abuse, the civil rights movement, and new treatment approaches catalyzed a shift toward community-based care beginning in the 1960s.

| Year | Development | Significance |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| 1963 | Community Mental Health Centers Act | Funded community alternatives to state psychiatric hospitals |
| 1965 | Medicaid established | Created federal-state healthcare program; initially focused on institutional care |
| 1967 | Burton Blatt's "Christmas in Purgatory" | Photographic expose of institutional abuse galvanized reform |
| 1971 | Intermediate Care Facilities for Individuals with Intellectual Disabilities (ICF/IID) | Medicaid coverage for institutional care; created institutional bias |
| 1972 | Geraldo Rivera's Willowbrook expose | Television documentary of abuse at Staten Island institution shocked nation |
| 1975 | *Wyatt v. Stickney* consent decree | Established right to adequate treatment in institutions |
| 1975 | Developmental Disabilities Assistance and Bill of Rights Act | Federal support for community services |

### The Birth of Independent Living

The independent living movement, emerging from disability activism in the 1960s and 1970s, fundamentally reframed caregiving. Rather than viewing people with disabilities as patients needing medical management, the movement demanded that people with disabilities control their own lives, including choosing and directing their own attendant care.

**Key Milestones**

- 1962: Ed Roberts enrolls at UC Berkeley, pioneers attendant care model
- 1972: Center for Independent Living established in Berkeley
- 1978: Amendments to Rehabilitation Act (Title VII) fund Centers for Independent Living
- 1970s-1980s: Self-directed attendant care programs emerge in California, Massachusetts, and other states

### Medicaid HCBS Waivers: A Crucial Innovation

**Section 1915(c) Waivers (1981)**

- Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1981 created HCBS waiver authority
- Allowed states to waive certain Medicaid rules to provide community-based services
- Services could include personal care, respite, supported employment, and environmental modifications
- Initially limited in scope; states adopted waivers gradually
- Critical flaw: waivers are optional for states, creating geographic inequity
- Institutional care remained an entitlement while HCBS remained discretionary

## Building the Caregiver Support Infrastructure (1990s-2000s)

### The ADA and *Olmstead* (1990-1999)

**ADA (1990)**

The Americans with Disabilities Act did not directly address caregiving, but its Title II prohibition on discrimination by state and local governments laid the groundwork for challenging institutional bias in service delivery.

**Olmstead v. L.C. (1999)**

The Supreme Court's landmark decision in *Olmstead v. L.C.*, 527 U.S. 581 (1999), held that unjustified institutionalization of people with disabilities violates the ADA's integration mandate. The decision required states to serve people with disabilities in the most integrated setting appropriate to their needs when:

- Community placement is appropriate
- The individual does not oppose community placement
- Community placement can be reasonably accommodated

*Olmstead* transformed the legal landscape for community-based care but did not mandate specific funding levels or workforce investments.

### National Family Caregiver Support Program (2000)

**Older Americans Act Amendments of 2000**

Congress created the National Family Caregiver Support Program (NFCSP) under Title III-E of the Older Americans Act. This was the first federal program specifically designed to support family caregivers.

**Original Design**

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Authorized funding | $125 million (FY 2001) |
| Services | Information, assistance, counseling, respite, supplemental services |
| Eligibility | Caregivers of persons age 60+ and grandparents raising grandchildren |
| Administration | Through state and area agencies on aging |

**Limitations from the Start**

- Underfunded relative to need (reaching fewer than 5% of eligible caregivers)
- Tied to aging services infrastructure; disability community had limited input
- No individual entitlement; services allocated through block grants
- Respite services capped at modest levels

### *Olmstead* Implementation and HCBS Expansion

Following *Olmstead*, states faced increasing legal and political pressure to expand HCBS:

| Year | Development | Impact |
|------|-------------|--------|
| 2001 | President Bush's New Freedom Initiative | Federal push for community integration; limited funding |
| 2001 | DOJ begins *Olmstead* enforcement | Settlement agreements require HCBS expansion |
| 2005 | Deficit Reduction Act creates Money Follows the Person (MFP) | Federal grants to transition people from institutions to community |
| 2006 | CMS HCBS Quality Framework | First federal quality standards for waiver programs |

## The Caregiving Crisis Emerges (2000s-2010s)

### Recognition of the Crisis

The 2000s saw growing recognition that the caregiving system was fundamentally broken:

- 2006: AARP launches "Valuing the Invaluable" report series documenting caregiver economic contributions
- 2008: National Alliance for Caregiving documents health impacts on family caregivers
- 2009: PHI (formerly Paraprofessional Healthcare Institute) begins systematic workforce data collection
- 2010: Community Living Assistance Services and Supports (CLASS) Act included in ACA but never implemented due to actuarial concerns

### Affordable Care Act Changes (2010)

The ACA included several provisions affecting caregiving:

| Provision | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| Community First Choice Option (Section 1915(k)) | 6% enhanced FMAP for states offering HCBS personal attendant services as state plan option |
| Money Follows the Person extended | Continued federal support for institutional transitions |
| CLASS Act | Would have created national LTSS insurance; suspended in 2011, repealed in 2013 |
| Balancing Incentive Program | Grants to states with less than 50% HCBS spending |

### Direct Care Worker Organizing

The 2000s and 2010s saw significant organizing among direct care workers:

| Year | Development | Impact |
|------|-------------|--------|
| 2003 | California IHSS workers unionize through SEIU | 74,000 workers gain collective bargaining; wages increase |
| 2007 | Illinois home care workers vote to unionize | 20,000+ workers join SEIU |
| 2013 | DOL extends FLSA protections to home care workers | Minimum wage and overtime apply; effective January 2015 |
| 2014 | *Harris v. Quinn*, 573 U.S. 616 | Supreme Court limits public-sector union fees for home care workers |
| 2015 | FLSA Companionship Services Rule takes effect | Home care workers guaranteed minimum wage and overtime |
| 2018 | *Janus v. AFSCME*, 585 U.S. 878 | Eliminates agency fees for public-sector unions; affects home care worker unions |

### The RAISE Act (2018)

The Recognize, Assist, Include, Support, and Engage (RAISE) Family Caregivers Act, signed into law in January 2018, directed the Secretary of HHS to establish an advisory council to develop a national strategy to support family caregivers.

- Bipartisan legislation (passed unanimously in Senate)
- Advisory council issued initial strategy report in September 2022
- Recommendations included federal coordination, financial security, respite, and workplace flexibility
- No binding requirements or funding attached

## Recent History (2020-Present)

### COVID-19 and the Caregiving Catastrophe

The COVID-19 pandemic exposed and exacerbated every weakness in the caregiving system:

**Impact on Direct Care Workers**

- Home care and nursing home workers faced among the highest COVID death rates of any occupation
- PPE shortages were severe in home care settings
- Workers lacked paid sick leave and faced pressure to work while symptomatic
- Turnover spiked to 65% in 2021

**Impact on Family Caregivers**

- Formal services suspended or reduced, increasing family care burden
- Day programs, respite centers, and adult day services closed
- Caregivers reported 30-40% increases in care hours
- Mental health impacts intensified

**Impact on People with Disabilities**

- Community-based services disrupted nationwide
- Worker shortages left people without essential personal care
- Some individuals forced into institutional settings due to lack of community support
- Mortality rates for people with intellectual disabilities in institutions exceeded general population

### American Rescue Plan Act (2021)

ARPA included the most significant federal investment in HCBS in history:

- 10 percentage point increase in FMAP for HCBS (Section 9817)
- Generated approximately $37 billion in additional HCBS spending
- States required to supplement, not supplant, existing HCBS spending
- Funds available through March 2025
- CMS approved state spending plans for workforce, infrastructure, and service expansion

### Build Back Better / Inflation Reduction Act (2021-2022)

President Biden's Build Back Better proposal included $400 billion for HCBS expansion, but this provision was excluded from the final Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 due to cost concerns and opposition from Senator Joe Manchin.

### Executive Order on Care (April 2023)

President Biden signed an executive order directing over 50 actions across federal agencies to improve care access, including:

- CMS guidance on HCBS workforce investments
- Head Start and child care workforce improvements
- Federal employee caregiver leave expansion
- Research on caregiving costs and benefits

### Current Status (2025)

As of early 2025, the caregiving landscape faces several critical developments:

- ARPA HCBS enhanced FMAP expired in March 2025
- States facing fiscal pressure to roll back wage increases and service expansions
- Better Care Better Jobs Act reintroduced but faces uncertain prospects
- Direct care workforce shortage continues; some stabilization from ARPA investments
- CMS pursuing HCBS access rule requiring states to ensure adequate provider networks
- Administration transition creating uncertainty about federal enforcement and investment priorities

## Key Turning Points

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|--------------|
| 1965 | Medicaid created | Established federal role in disability care funding; institutional bias built in |
| 1981 | HCBS waiver authority | First federal mechanism for community-based care funding |
| 1990 | ADA enacted | Civil rights framework for disability; foundation for integration mandate |
| 1999 | *Olmstead v. L.C.* | Supreme Court requires community integration |
| 2000 | NFCSP created | First federal program for family caregivers |
| 2010 | ACA Community First Choice | Enhanced FMAP for HCBS; CLASS Act attempt fails |
| 2015 | FLSA applies to home care | Direct care workers gain minimum wage and overtime protections |
| 2021 | ARPA HCBS investment | Largest-ever federal HCBS investment ($37 billion) |
| 2025 | ARPA HCBS funds expire | Risk of reversing gains in wages and services |

## Lessons from History

1. **Institutional bias is deeply embedded**: Medicaid's structure has favored institutions since 1965; reversing this requires sustained policy effort
2. **Crisis drives action but not sustainability**: Deinstitutionalization, *Olmstead*, and COVID each prompted investment, but gains erode without permanent structural reform
3. **The workforce was always invisible**: Caregiving labor, both paid and unpaid, has been historically undervalued and excluded from labor protections
4. **Disability rights framing is essential**: The shift from charitable to civil rights framing transformed the legal and political landscape for community care
5. **Temporary investments produce temporary results**: ARPA demonstrated what investment can achieve, but the lack of permanent funding structure threatens every gain
