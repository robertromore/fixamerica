# Caregiver Support: Current State

## Federal Programs Landscape

### National Family Caregiver Support Program (NFCSP)

The NFCSP, authorized under Title III-E of the Older Americans Act, is the primary federal program dedicated to supporting family caregivers. Despite its name, eligibility extends to caregivers of adults with disabilities as well as older adults.

**Program Parameters (FY 2024)**

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Annual funding | $200 million (FY 2024 appropriation) |
| Caregivers served | Approximately 650,000-700,000 annually |
| Administered by | Administration for Community Living (ACL), through state aging agencies |
| Eligible caregivers | Family or informal caregivers of persons age 60+ or adults with disabilities |
| Services provided | Information, assistance, counseling, respite, supplemental services, training |

**Funding Trends**

| Fiscal Year | Appropriation | Change |
|-------------|---------------|--------|
| FY 2019 | $178 million | Baseline |
| FY 2020 | $182 million | +2.2% |
| FY 2021 | $186 million | +2.2% |
| FY 2022 | $194 million | +4.3% |
| FY 2023 | $197 million | +1.5% |
| FY 2024 | $200 million | +1.5% |
| FY 2025 (requested) | $210 million | +5.0% |

*Source: Administration for Community Living, Budget Justifications (FY 2019-2025).*

**Limitations**

- Funding per caregiver served averages less than $300 per year
- Many state programs have waiting lists or cap services
- Respite services (the most requested benefit) average only 6-10 hours per month per caregiver
- Rural caregivers face severe access barriers due to provider shortages
- No individual entitlement; services depend on state allocation and capacity

### Medicaid Home and Community-Based Services (HCBS)

Medicaid HCBS waiver programs (Section 1915(c)) and state plan options are the primary public funding mechanism for paid caregiving services for people with disabilities.

**HCBS Spending and Enrollment (FY 2023)**

| Metric | Amount |
|--------|--------|
| Total Medicaid LTSS spending | $218 billion |
| HCBS share | $119 billion (55%) |
| Institutional care share | $99 billion (45%) |
| HCBS waiver participants | 3.6 million |
| HCBS waiver waiting list | 800,000+ |
| Number of active Section 1915(c) waivers | Approximately 290 across states |

*Source: Kaiser Family Foundation, Medicaid LTSS Spending (2024); HCBS Advocacy Coalition Waiting List Report (2024).*

**Rebalancing Progress**

The share of Medicaid long-term services and supports (LTSS) spending on HCBS versus institutional care has shifted significantly over two decades, but institutional bias persists:

| Year | HCBS Share | Institutional Share |
|------|------------|---------------------|
| 2000 | 25% | 75% |
| 2010 | 43% | 57% |
| 2015 | 51% | 49% |
| 2020 | 55% | 45% |
| 2023 | 55% | 45% |

*Source: Kaiser Family Foundation, Medicaid LTSS Spending Trends (2024).*

**Waiting Lists by State (Selected)**

| State | Waiting List Size | Average Wait Time |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Texas | 130,000+ | 10-15 years |
| Florida | 22,000+ | 5-8 years |
| Ohio | 50,000+ | 3-7 years |
| Louisiana | 13,000+ | 5-10 years |
| Pennsylvania | 15,000+ | 3-5 years |
| New York | Minimal | Under 1 year |
| Oregon | Minimal | Under 6 months |

*Source: Kaiser Family Foundation, Medicaid HCBS Waiver Waiting Lists (2024).*

### American Rescue Plan Act HCBS Enhancement

The American Rescue Plan Act (ARPA) of 2021 provided a temporary 10 percentage point increase in the federal Medicaid matching rate (FMAP) for HCBS, generating approximately $37 billion in additional HCBS investment through March 2025.

**Impact**

| Category | Investment | Status |
|----------|------------|--------|
| Workforce compensation increases | $7.2 billion across states | Temporary; many states ending increases |
| Waiting list reductions | Variable by state | Some states reduced lists; many reverting |
| New HCBS service capacity | $5.8 billion | Infrastructure improvements ongoing |
| Technology and innovation | $3.1 billion | Telehealth, remote monitoring expansion |

*Source: Kaiser Family Foundation, "How Are States Using ARPA HCBS Funds?" (2024).*

**Sustainability Concern**: As ARPA enhanced FMAP expired in March 2025, many states are reducing or eliminating workforce wage increases and service expansions funded through the temporary enhancement. The Congressional Budget Office estimated that maintaining ARPA-level HCBS investment would cost approximately $150 billion over ten years.

### Other Federal Programs

## The Direct Care Workforce

### Workforce Profile (2024)

The direct care workforce includes personal care aides, home health aides, nursing assistants, and direct support professionals who provide hands-on care to people with disabilities and older adults.

**Workforce Size and Demographics**

| Characteristic | Detail |
|---------------|--------|
| Total workforce | 4.8 million workers |
| Personal care aides | 2.6 million |
| Home health aides | 900,000 |
| Nursing assistants | 1.3 million |
| Women | 86% |
| People of color | 61% (27% Black, 22% Hispanic, 7% Asian, 5% other) |
| Foreign-born | 28% |
| Average age | 39 years |
| Part-time workers | 44% |

*Source: PHI National, "Direct Care Workers in the United States: Key Facts" (2024); BLS Occupational Employment Statistics (2024).*

### Compensation

| Metric | Personal Care Aides | Home Health Aides | Nursing Assistants |
|--------|--------------------|--------------------|-------------------|
| Median hourly wage | $15.40 | $15.60 | $16.80 |
| Median annual wage | $32,000 | $32,400 | $34,900 |
| Workers below poverty line | 17% | 15% | 12% |
| Workers receiving public benefits | 43% | 38% | 30% |
| Workers with employer health insurance | 30% | 35% | 52% |
| Workers with paid sick leave | 32% | 38% | 55% |

*Sources: BLS Occupational Employment Statistics (2024); PHI National (2024).*

**Wage Comparison**

| Occupation | Median Hourly Wage |
|------------|-------------------|
| Personal care aide | $15.40 |
| Retail salesperson | $16.20 |
| Fast food worker | $14.00 |
| Janitor/cleaner | $16.10 |
| Childcare worker | $14.60 |
| Registered nurse | $43.50 |

*Source: BLS (2024).*

### Workforce Crisis Indicators

| Indicator | 2019 | 2021 | 2023 | 2024 |
|-----------|------|------|------|------|
| Annual turnover rate | 45% | 65% | 50% | 43% |
| Vacancy rate | 12% | 23% | 18% | 15% |
| Job openings (monthly avg.) | 380,000 | 560,000 | 480,000 | 420,000 |
| Projected annual demand growth | 3.2% | 4.1% | 3.8% | 3.5% |

*Source: PHI National, "Workforce Data Center" (2024); BLS projections.*

The COVID-19 pandemic exacerbated existing workforce shortages, driving turnover to record levels in 2021. While conditions have partially stabilized, structural problems in wages and working conditions persist.

**Projected Shortage**

PHI National projects that the United States will need to fill 9.3 million direct care job openings between 2020 and 2030, accounting for both new positions and turnover replacement. At current trajectory, 4.7 million positions will go unfilled by 2028.

*Source: PHI National, "Direct Care Workers in the United States" (2023).*

### Self-Directed Services

Self-directed services programs allow people with disabilities to manage their own care budgets, hiring, training, and supervising their own caregivers, who may include family members.

**Participation (2024)**

| Metric | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| States offering self-direction | 50 states and DC (in some form) |
| Medicaid participants using self-direction | Approximately 1.3 million |
| Share of HCBS spending on self-direction | 20-25% |
| States allowing family member payment | 48 states (with restrictions) |
| States allowing spouse payment | 15 states |

*Source: National Resource Center for Participant-Directed Services (2024); Applied Self-Direction (2024).*

**Family Caregiver Payment**

States vary widely in whether and how Medicaid programs pay family caregivers:

| Policy | States |
|--------|--------|
| Pay family members (non-spouse) as attendants | 48 states |
| Pay spouses as attendants | 15 states |
| Pay parents of minor children | 35 states |
| Pay parents of adult children | 45 states |
| Require worker training for family caregivers | 30 states |
| Set wages below standard for family caregivers | 12 states |

*Source: HCBS Advocacy Coalition, "Family Caregiver Payment Policies" (2024).*

## Family Caregiver Impact

### Financial Consequences

| Impact | Scale | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Average annual out-of-pocket caregiving costs | $7,242 | AARP/National Alliance for Caregiving (2020) |
| Caregivers who reduced work hours | 21% | AARP (2020) |
| Caregivers who left workforce entirely | 10% | AARP (2020) |
| Average lifetime earnings loss for women caregivers | $324,000 | MetLife Mature Market Institute (updated 2023) |
| Caregivers who depleted personal savings | 23% | National Alliance for Caregiving (2020) |
| Caregiver Social Security benefit reduction (avg.) | $131,000 over lifetime | Social Security Administration analysis (2022) |
| Caregivers with no retirement savings | 28% of those age 50+ | Transamerica Center for Retirement Studies (2023) |

### Health Consequences

| Impact | Prevalence | Source |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Caregivers reporting fair or poor health | 21% (vs. 14% non-caregivers) | CDC BRFSS (2023) |
| Caregivers with depression symptoms | 40-70% (varies by care intensity) | Family Caregiver Alliance (2023) |
| Caregivers reporting high emotional stress | 36% | AARP (2020) |
| Caregivers with chronic conditions attributable to caregiving | 17% | National Academy of Medicine (2016, updated) |
| Higher mortality risk for strained caregivers | 63% higher than non-caregivers | *JAMA* (Schulz & Beach, 1999; confirmed in subsequent studies) |

### Disparities

| Factor | Finding | Source |
|--------|---------|--------|
| Race/ethnicity | Black and Hispanic caregivers provide more intensive care with fewer formal supports | AARP (2020) |
| Gender | Women provide 60% more care hours than male caregivers | National Alliance for Caregiving (2020) |
| Income | Low-income caregivers spend 50% more of their income on caregiving costs | AARP (2020) |
| Rural vs. urban | Rural caregivers travel 2x farther for services; fewer respite options | National Rural Health Association (2023) |
| LGBTQ+ caregivers | More likely to provide care without formal support; face discrimination in services | SAGE/MAP (2023) |

## State-Level Variation

### Innovative State Programs

| State | Program/Policy | Description |
|-------|---------------|-------------|
| Washington | Long-Term Services and Supports Trust (WA Cares) | First state-run LTSS insurance program; 0.58% payroll tax funds benefits up to $36,500 |
| California | In-Home Supportive Services (IHSS) | Largest self-directed HCBS program; 600,000+ participants |
| Oregon | K Plan (Community First Choice) | Expanded HCBS entitlement with enhanced FMAP; minimal waiting list |
| Hawaii | Kupuna Caregivers Act | Employer-supported caregiver benefit program |
| Minnesota | PCA Choice program | Self-directed personal care; comprehensive training requirements |
| Vermont | Choices for Care | Entitlement-based HCBS; no waiting list for highest-need individuals |

### States with Greatest Challenges

| State | Key Issue | Detail |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| Texas | Massive waiting lists | 130,000+ on HCBS waiver waiting lists |
| Mississippi | Low Medicaid wages | Attendant wages below $10/hour in some programs |
| Georgia | Institutional bias | Over 55% of LTSS spending on institutional care |
| Alabama | Limited services | Few HCBS waiver options; large unserved population |
| South Carolina | Provider shortages | Rural areas lack adequate home care providers |

## Current Reform Landscape

### Federal Initiatives

**Better Care Better Jobs Act**

- Reintroduced in the 119th Congress (2025)
- Would invest $150 billion over 10 years in Medicaid HCBS
- Mandatory $15/hour minimum wage for direct care workers
- Eliminate HCBS waiting lists
- Enhanced FMAP for HCBS permanently
- Bipartisan support uncertain; cost is primary obstacle

**Executive Actions**

- April 2023: President Biden signed Executive Order on Increasing Access to High-Quality Care and Supporting Caregivers
- Directed federal agencies to improve caregiver support across programs
- CMS issued guidance on HCBS workforce investments
- Implementation varies; limited enforcement mechanism

### Advocacy Priorities (2025)

| Organization | Priority |
|--------------|----------|
| PHI National | $15/hour minimum wage for direct care workers; career pathways |
| AARP | RAISE Act implementation; family caregiver tax credits; respite expansion |
| National Council on Independent Living | Self-directed services expansion; HCBS entitlement |
| SEIU | Direct care worker unionization; living wages; benefits |
| Arc of the United States | HCBS waiting list elimination; DSP wage increases |
| Disability Rights Education and Defense Fund | *Olmstead* enforcement; HCBS access as civil right |

## Key Takeaways

1. **Massive unmet need**: Over 800,000 people remain on Medicaid HCBS waiting lists, and millions more lack access to adequate caregiving support
2. **Workforce crisis is structural**: Direct care worker wages are poverty-level, driving turnover exceeding 40% and vacancy rates that leave people without care
3. **Family caregivers bear enormous costs**: The average caregiver faces $7,242 in annual out-of-pocket costs and $324,000 in lifetime earnings losses
4. **Racial and gender inequity is embedded**: The caregiving system relies disproportionately on women of color for paid care and on women for unpaid family care
5. **ARPA gains are eroding**: Temporary HCBS investments are expiring, and many states are rolling back workforce wage increases
6. **State variation is extreme**: Some states have minimal waiting lists and strong programs; others have decade-long waits and poverty-level caregiver wages
7. **Federal investment is inadequate**: The NFCSP provides less than $300 per year per caregiver served
8. **Self-direction is expanding but uneven**: All states offer some self-direction, but policies on family payment and worker standards vary widely
