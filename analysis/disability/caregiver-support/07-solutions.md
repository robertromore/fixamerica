# Caregiver Support: Solutions

## Overview

Addressing the caregiver crisis requires a comprehensive approach that simultaneously expands access to services, strengthens the caregiving workforce, supports family caregivers, and restructures the financing of long-term services and supports. The solutions presented here range from immediate, achievable reforms to transformative structural changes, organized by domain and feasibility.

---

## Solution 1: Eliminate Medicaid HCBS Waiting Lists

### Description

Make Medicaid home and community-based services an entitlement on par with institutional care, eliminating the current two-tier system where nursing home placement is guaranteed but community-based services are rationed.

### Mechanism

- Amend Section 1915(c) of the Social Security Act to require states to serve all eligible individuals in HCBS programs
- Alternatively, expand the Community First Choice Option (Section 1915(k)) with enhanced federal matching to incentivize universal HCBS access
- Establish federal minimum service packages that states must offer
- Require states to eliminate waiting lists within 5 years

### Evidence

- States with no waiting lists (Oregon, Vermont) demonstrate feasibility
- HCBS is less expensive per person than institutional care ($42,000 vs. $96,000 annually)
- *Olmstead* compliance requires community placement when appropriate; waiting lists violate this principle
- ARPA demonstrated that investment reduces waiting lists and expands access

### Estimated Cost

$150 billion over 10 years (CBO estimate for Better Care Better Jobs Act provisions)

### Feasibility

- **Political**: Moderate difficulty; cost is primary obstacle
- **Administrative**: High complexity; requires state plan amendments and provider network expansion
- **Legal**: Strong legal foundation in ADA and *Olmstead*

## Solution 2: Establish a Direct Care Workforce Compensation Floor

### Description

Set a federal minimum compensation standard for Medicaid-funded direct care workers, including wages, benefits, and career advancement opportunities.

### Mechanism

- Require Medicaid HCBS reimbursement rates sufficient to support at least 150% of the local minimum wage for direct care workers
- Mandate that a minimum percentage (75-80%) of Medicaid HCBS payments pass through to worker wages and benefits
- Require employer-sponsored health insurance or equivalent benefits for full-time direct care workers
- Fund workforce training, credentialing, and career ladder programs through dedicated federal grants

### Evidence

- States that increased direct care wages saw reduced turnover and improved care quality (PHI National, 2024)
- ARPA wage increases in several states reduced vacancy rates by 20-30%
- Research demonstrates that caregiver wages are suppressed by Medicaid monopsony, not market forces
- International comparisons show that countries with higher caregiver wages have more stable workforces (Denmark, Germany, Japan)

### Estimated Cost

$75-100 billion over 10 years for enhanced Medicaid rates; offset partially by reduced turnover costs and institutional diversions

### Feasibility

- **Political**: Moderate difficulty; bipartisan support exists for the concept, though funding mechanism is contested
- **Administrative**: Moderate complexity; requires federal rate-setting guidance and state compliance
- **Legal**: Within existing Medicaid authority with statutory amendment

## Solution 3: Create a Universal Family Caregiver Support Program

### Description

Replace the underfunded National Family Caregiver Support Program with a comprehensive family caregiver support system that provides meaningful financial assistance, respite, and services.

### Mechanism

- Expand and rename the NFCSP with funding at $2 billion annually (10x current level)
- Create a refundable Family Caregiver Tax Credit of up to $5,000 per year for caregivers who provide at least 20 hours per week of care
- Establish Social Security caregiver credits for periods of reduced employment due to caregiving
- Fund a national respite care guarantee of at least 40 hours per month for caregivers of individuals with high-intensity needs
- Extend FMLA to cover ongoing caregiving needs (not just initial onset) with partial wage replacement

### Evidence

- NFCSP at current funding reaches fewer than 2% of eligible caregivers
- Average caregiver out-of-pocket costs ($7,242/year) and lifetime earnings losses ($324,000) demonstrate enormous unmet need
- Respite services are the most requested and least available caregiver support
- International models (UK Carer's Allowance, Germany's Pflegegeld, Sweden's caregiver benefits) demonstrate feasibility

### Estimated Cost

$20-30 billion over 10 years (tax credits, NFCSP expansion, respite guarantee)

### Feasibility

- **Political**: High feasibility for tax credits (bipartisan appeal); moderate for respite expansion; difficult for Social Security credits
- **Administrative**: Moderate complexity; builds on existing infrastructure
- **Legal**: Within existing congressional authority

## Solution 4: Expand and Strengthen Self-Directed Services

### Description

Make self-directed services the default option in all Medicaid HCBS programs, with robust infrastructure to support consumer-employers and their workers.

### Mechanism

- Require all states to offer self-direction as an option in every HCBS waiver program
- Establish federal standards for fiscal intermediary services (payroll, benefits administration, tax compliance)
- Allow all states to pay family members (including spouses and parents of minor children) as self-directed caregivers
- Fund peer support and independent living center capacity to help people with disabilities manage self-directed services
- Protect both consumer-employers and self-directed workers through clear labor standards

### Evidence

- Self-directed services are associated with higher satisfaction, greater community participation, and equivalent or better health outcomes
- 1.3 million Medicaid participants currently use some form of self-direction
- Disability rights organizations consistently prioritize self-direction as a core advocacy goal
- Administrative costs for self-direction (through fiscal intermediaries) are typically lower than agency-directed services

### Estimated Cost

$5-10 billion over 10 years (infrastructure, fiscal intermediary capacity, peer support)

### Feasibility

- **Political**: High feasibility; appeals to both choice/freedom values and disability rights framework
- **Administrative**: Moderate complexity; requires fiscal intermediary infrastructure development
- **Legal**: Within existing Medicaid authority with regulatory guidance

## Solution 5: Establish a National Long-Term Services and Supports Insurance Program

### Description

Create a social insurance program to fund long-term services and supports, spreading the cost of caregiving across the working population rather than concentrating it on individuals and families who need care.

### Mechanism

- Establish a national LTSS insurance program funded through a modest payroll contribution (0.5-1.0%)
- Provide cash benefits that can be used for HCBS, institutional care, family caregiver compensation, or assistive technology
- Design benefits to supplement (not replace) Medicaid HCBS
- Model on successful programs: Washington State's WA Cares Fund, Germany's Pflegeversicherung, Japan's Long-Term Care Insurance

### Evidence

- Washington State implemented the first state-level LTSS insurance program (WA Cares Fund, 2019; benefits begin 2026)
- Germany's LTSS insurance (1995) successfully created a universal benefit that supports family and paid caregiving
- Japan's LTCI (2000) dramatically expanded home care access
- The failed CLASS Act (2010) demonstrated challenges but also public interest in social insurance for LTSS

### Estimated Cost

Self-funding through payroll contributions; initial estimates suggest 0.58% payroll tax (Washington model) would fund modest benefits

### Feasibility

- **Political**: Difficult; new payroll taxes face strong opposition; CLASS Act failure created political wariness
- **Administrative**: High complexity; requires new federal program design and administration
- **Legal**: Within congressional authority under taxing and spending power

## Solution 6: Reform Medicaid Financing to Eliminate Institutional Bias

### Description

Restructure Medicaid federal matching to actively incentivize HCBS over institutional care, reversing the historical institutional bias.

### Mechanism

- Permanently enhance the FMAP for HCBS by 10 percentage points (as ARPA did temporarily)
- Reduce FMAP for institutional care for states that fail to meet rebalancing targets
- Require states spending more than 40% of LTSS on institutional care to submit rebalancing plans
- Fund Money Follows the Person transitions at $500 million annually
- Establish federal HCBS quality standards with enforcement authority

### Evidence

- ARPA's temporary 10-point FMAP increase generated $37 billion in HCBS investment
- States with higher HCBS spending ratios have lower per-person LTSS costs
- Money Follows the Person has transitioned over 100,000 people from institutions since 2008
- Community First Choice (6% enhanced FMAP) has been adopted by 10+ states with positive results

### Estimated Cost

$100-125 billion over 10 years (enhanced FMAP; partially offset by institutional spending reductions)

### Feasibility

- **Political**: Moderate difficulty; nursing home lobby will oppose strongly
- **Administrative**: Moderate complexity; builds on existing FMAP mechanisms
- **Legal**: Within existing Medicaid authority with statutory amendment

## Solution 7: Strengthen Caregiver Workforce Pipeline

### Description

Create a comprehensive workforce development strategy for the direct care sector, addressing recruitment, training, retention, and career advancement.

### Mechanism

- Establish a Direct Care Workforce Fund ($1 billion annually) for state workforce development grants
- Create a national direct care worker credential recognized across states
- Fund registered apprenticeship programs for direct care occupations
- Provide student loan forgiveness for direct care workers who commit to 3+ years of service
- Invest in technology (telehealth, remote monitoring, scheduling tools) that supports workers
- Address immigration barriers for foreign-born care workers through visa reform

### Evidence

- PHI estimates 9.3 million direct care job openings between 2020 and 2030
- Credential programs in Oregon, Minnesota, and Washington have improved retention
- Registered apprenticeships in healthcare have shown 90%+ completion rates
- Technology investments can reduce administrative burden and improve job satisfaction

### Estimated Cost

$10-15 billion over 10 years

### Feasibility

- **Political**: High feasibility for most components; immigration reform is more contentious
- **Administrative**: Moderate complexity; builds on existing DOL and HHS workforce programs
- **Legal**: Within existing authority for most components

## International Models

### Germany: Pflegeversicherung (Long-Term Care Insurance)

- Universal social insurance since 1995
- 3.4% payroll contribution (shared employer/employee)
- Cash benefits that can be used for family or professional care
- Five care levels based on assessed need
- Family caregivers receive pension credits
- Result: stable caregiving system with universal access

### Sweden: Universal Care Services

- Tax-funded public home care system
- Municipal responsibility for service delivery
- Caregiver wages comparable to other public employees
- Family caregiver compensation through attendance allowances
- Result: high quality, low family burden, but high tax cost

### Japan: Long-Term Care Insurance (Kaigo Hoken)

- Established 2000; mandatory for all residents age 40+
- Care management system with standardized needs assessment
- Mix of public and private service providers
- Has dramatically expanded home and community-based care
- Challenges: workforce shortages, rising costs, rural access

### Lessons for the United States

1. Social insurance models can successfully fund LTSS
2. Universal programs are more politically durable than means-tested programs
3. Family caregiver compensation is feasible and effective
4. Workforce investment is essential regardless of financing model
5. No country has fully solved the caregiving challenge; all continue to refine approaches
