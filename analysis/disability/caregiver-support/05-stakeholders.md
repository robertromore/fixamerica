# Caregiver Support: Stakeholders

## Overview

The caregiving system involves a complex web of stakeholders with overlapping and sometimes conflicting interests. People with disabilities who receive care, the family members who provide it, the paid workers who deliver it, the agencies that employ them, the governments that fund them, and the advocates who fight for reform all occupy distinct positions within this system. Understanding these stakeholder dynamics is essential to designing effective policy reform.

---

## Primary Stakeholders

### People with Disabilities Who Receive Care

**Profile**

- Approximately 7 million Americans receive some form of long-term services and supports
- Ages range from early childhood through advanced age; needs and preferences vary enormously
- Include people with physical, intellectual, developmental, sensory, and psychiatric disabilities
- Many prefer self-directed care arrangements where they control hiring and service delivery

**Interests**

- Access to adequate, reliable caregiving services in community settings
- Choice and control over who provides care and how it is delivered
- Avoidance of unnecessary institutionalization
- Quality of care that respects dignity, privacy, and autonomy
- Elimination of HCBS waiting lists
- Services that are culturally and linguistically appropriate

**Power and Influence**

- Represented by strong national organizations (NCIL, ADAPT, The Arc, ASAN)
- *Olmstead* decision provides legal framework for advocacy
- Growing political engagement through disability voting rights efforts
- Limited individual political power due to health, economic, and accessibility barriers
- Power is greatest when organized through cross-disability coalitions

### Family Caregivers

**Profile**

- 53 million Americans provide unpaid care to adults or children with disabilities and chronic conditions
- 60% are women; average age 49
- 61% also work outside the home (many reducing hours)
- Average duration of caregiving: 4.5 years (24% have been caregiving for 5+ years)
- Average 23.7 hours per week of caregiving (for those caring for adults with disabilities)

**Interests**

- Respite services to prevent burnout
- Financial support (direct payments, tax credits, Social Security credits)
- Workplace flexibility and legal protections
- Healthcare coverage and mental health support
- Training and information resources
- Recognition of caregiving as valuable work

**Power and Influence**

- Numerically large but politically diffuse
- AARP is the most powerful advocacy voice (though focused primarily on aging)
- National Alliance for Caregiving provides research and visibility
- Family caregivers are underrepresented in policy discussions relative to their numbers
- Growing political salience post-COVID

### Direct Care Workers (Paid Caregivers)

**Profile**

- 4.8 million workers: personal care aides, home health aides, direct support professionals, nursing assistants
- 86% women; 61% people of color; 28% foreign-born
- Median wage: $15.40-$16.80/hour depending on role
- 43% of personal care aides rely on public benefits
- High turnover (40-60% annually)

**Interests**

- Living wages and benefits (health insurance, paid leave, retirement)
- Safe working conditions
- Training and career advancement opportunities
- Respect and recognition as essential professionals
- Union representation and collective bargaining rights
- Immigration status protections for foreign-born workers

**Power and Influence**

- Growing union representation through SEIU, AFSCME, and other unions
- Limited individual bargaining power due to market structure
- Increasing political visibility through advocacy organizations (PHI, National Domestic Workers Alliance)
- *Janus* decision (2018) weakened public-sector union capacity
- COVID-19 temporarily elevated public recognition

## Secondary Stakeholders

### Home Care Agencies

**Profile**

- Over 33,000 home health agencies and 12,000 personal care agencies nationwide
- Range from large national chains to small local providers
- For-profit agencies comprise approximately 80% of the market
- Serve as intermediaries between Medicaid/payers and direct care workers

**Interests**

- Adequate Medicaid reimbursement rates
- Regulatory stability and manageable compliance requirements
- Maintaining role as employer of record (resist self-direction models that bypass agencies)
- Workforce recruitment and retention
- Protection from liability

**Power and Influence**

- National Association for Home Care and Hospice (NAHC) is primary lobby
- State home care associations have significant legislative influence
- Industry consolidation increasing market power of large providers
- Financial interest in maintaining agency-directed model over self-direction

### Nursing Home and Institutional Care Industry

**Profile**

- Approximately 15,000 nursing homes serving 1.2 million residents
- Increasingly owned by private equity firms and large chains
- Revenue heavily dependent on Medicaid and Medicare
- Average annual cost per resident: $94,000-$108,000

**Interests**

- Maintain institutional Medicaid funding levels
- Resist rebalancing efforts that shift funding to HCBS
- Regulatory flexibility
- Workforce recruitment in competition with home care

**Power and Influence**

- American Health Care Association (AHCA) is primary lobby; one of largest healthcare lobbying organizations
- Significant campaign contributions to state and federal legislators
- Influence over state Medicaid policy through regulatory processes
- Declining census creates urgency to protect funding streams

### State Medicaid Agencies

**Profile**

- All 50 states and DC administer Medicaid HCBS programs
- Agencies responsible for waiver design, rate-setting, quality oversight, and provider enrollment
- Budgets under constant pressure from competing state spending priorities
- Capacity and commitment to HCBS vary widely by state

**Interests**

- Cost containment and fiscal predictability
- Federal funding stability (FMAP)
- Administrative efficiency
- Compliance with federal mandates including *Olmstead*
- Flexibility in program design

**Power and Influence**

- National Association of Medicaid Directors influences federal policy
- State agencies have enormous discretion in HCBS program design
- Rate-setting authority directly determines caregiver wages
- Governor and legislative priorities drive agency direction

### Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS)

**Profile**

- Federal agency administering Medicare and Medicaid
- Oversees HCBS waiver approvals, state plan amendments, and quality standards
- Issues regulations and guidance on HCBS access, person-centeredness, and provider requirements

**Interests**

- Program integrity and fiscal stewardship
- Quality of care and health outcomes
- *Olmstead* compliance across states
- HCBS access and adequacy

**Power and Influence**

- Approval authority over all HCBS waivers and state plan amendments
- Can require states to meet conditions for federal matching funds
- Regulatory authority over HCBS quality, access, and spending
- Subject to administration priorities and political direction

## Stakeholder Tensions

### People with Disabilities vs. Worker Advocates

Self-directed services empower people with disabilities to control their care but can create tension with worker advocates who seek standardized wages, benefits, and working conditions. Key tension points:

- Family member payment rates (some self-direction programs pay below market)
- Employer responsibility (who is the employer: the person with a disability, the state, or a fiscal intermediary?)
- Training requirements (workers want credentials; some people with disabilities view requirements as barriers to hiring)
- Scheduling flexibility (24/7 care needs may conflict with overtime regulations)

### Family Caregivers vs. Paid Workers

- Family caregivers seek recognition and compensation for their work
- Paid workers worry that family payment programs will depress professional wages
- Quality concerns when family members provide care without training
- Blurred boundaries between family relationship and employment relationship

### Home Care Agencies vs. Self-Direction Advocates

- Agencies seek to maintain their intermediary role and revenue
- Self-direction advocates view agencies as unnecessary overhead that reduces funds reaching workers and consumers
- Administrative support functions (payroll, benefits, training) that agencies provide can be valuable
- Self-direction requires fiscal intermediary infrastructure that may or may not be adequate

### HCBS Advocates vs. Nursing Home Industry

- Direct competition for Medicaid LTSS funding
- HCBS expansion threatens institutional census and revenue
- Nursing home industry argues institutional care remains necessary for high-need individuals
- HCBS advocates argue institutional bias violates *Olmstead* and wastes resources
