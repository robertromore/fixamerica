# Caregiver Support: Root Causes

## Overview

The caregiving crisis in the United States is not the result of a single policy failure but of deeply embedded structural, economic, political, and cultural forces that interact to produce systemic underinvestment in caregiving. Understanding these root causes is essential to designing reforms that address the problem at its foundations rather than its symptoms.

---

## Structural Causes

### Medicaid's Institutional Bias

The single most important structural cause of the caregiving crisis is Medicaid's historical and ongoing bias toward institutional care.

**How the Bias Works**

- Medicaid institutional care (nursing homes, ICF/IID) is a federal entitlement: states must cover eligible individuals
- Medicaid HCBS is optional: states may offer waiver programs but are not required to serve all eligible individuals
- Result: people entitled to institutional placement may wait years for community-based alternatives
- Federal matching rates historically did not distinguish between institutional and HCBS spending (with limited exceptions like Community First Choice)

**Consequences**

| Effect | Scale |
|--------|-------|
| HCBS waiting lists | 800,000+ people nationwide |
| Unnecessary institutionalization | Estimated 200,000+ people in institutions who could live in community with adequate support |
| Cost inefficiency | Average annual cost per person: institutional $96,000 vs. HCBS $42,000 |
| *Olmstead* violations | DOJ enforcement actions in dozens of states for failure to provide community services |

*Sources: Kaiser Family Foundation (2024); HCBS Advocacy Coalition (2024).*

### Fragmented Federal Authority

No single federal agency or program is responsible for caregiving policy. Authority is scattered across:

| Agency/Program | Scope | Limitation |
|----------------|-------|-----------|
| CMS (Medicaid) | HCBS waivers; institutional care | Optional programs; state discretion |
| ACL | NFCSP; aging services | Underfunded; aging focus |
| DOL | Wage and hour for direct care workers | Limited enforcement capacity |
| VA | Veteran caregiver programs | Veteran-specific only |
| HHS (broadly) | RAISE Act advisory; coordination | No binding authority |
| SSA | SSDI/SSI benefits | Does not address caregiving directly |

This fragmentation means no entity has the authority, resources, or incentive to address the caregiving system comprehensively.

### Waiver-Based Program Design

The reliance on Medicaid waiver authority (Section 1915(c)) for community-based services creates inherent limitations:

- **State-by-state variation**: Each state designs its own waiver programs with different services, eligibility criteria, and provider requirements
- **Enrollment caps**: Waivers may cap the number of participants, creating waiting lists
- **Renewal uncertainty**: Waivers must be renewed periodically (typically every 3-5 years)
- **Administrative complexity**: Multiple waivers per state create bureaucratic barriers for both participants and providers
- **No portability**: Moving to a different state may mean starting on a new waiting list

## Economic Causes

### The Undervaluation of Care Work

Caregiving is among the most undervalued forms of labor in the American economy. This undervaluation has deep roots:

**Historical Exclusion from Labor Protections**

- 1938: Fair Labor Standards Act excluded domestic workers (including home care workers) from minimum wage and overtime
- 1974: FLSA amendments created "companionship services" exemption, explicitly excluding home care workers
- 2015: DOL final rule extending FLSA to home care workers took effect (after decades of advocacy and litigation)
- Even with FLSA coverage, enforcement in home care settings is minimal

**Why Wages Stay Low**

| Factor | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| Monopsony power | Medicaid is the dominant purchaser of home care; sets prices administratively |
| Gender devaluation | Care work performed predominantly by women is systematically undercompensated |
| Racial devaluation | Workforce is disproportionately women of color; historical legacy of domestic service exclusions |
| Low unionization | Only 10-15% of direct care workers are union members |
| Medicaid rate-setting | State Medicaid programs set reimbursement rates too low to support competitive wages |
| Worker classification | Some workers misclassified as independent contractors, losing benefits |

### State Budget Constraints

States bear a significant share of Medicaid HCBS costs (typically 40-50% after federal matching), creating fiscal incentives to limit community-based services:

- HCBS expansion increases state Medicaid spending
- State legislatures are reluctant to create new Medicaid entitlements
- Budget cycles create uncertainty for multi-year workforce investments
- Economic downturns reduce state revenue precisely when demand for services increases
- Political opposition to "entitlement expansion" limits HCBS growth

### Market Failure in Caregiving

The caregiving market exhibits fundamental market failures that prevent efficient allocation:

| Market Failure | Description |
|----------------|-------------|
| Information asymmetry | Care recipients cannot easily assess quality; workers cannot easily evaluate employers |
| Externalities | Benefits of caregiving (reduced institutionalization, family workforce participation) are not captured in market prices |
| Public goods characteristics | Caregiving infrastructure benefits society broadly but is not profitable to provide privately |
| Principal-agent problems | Multiple parties (person with disability, family, state, provider agency) have divergent interests |
| Moral hazard | Insurance-like structures create incentive problems in service utilization |

## Political Causes

### Caregivers Lack Political Power

Despite their numbers, caregivers are poorly organized politically:

- **Dispersed and isolated**: Family caregivers are often too exhausted to engage in advocacy
- **No unified identity**: Caregivers span all demographics and do not see themselves as a political constituency
- **Competing priorities**: Disability, aging, and labor advocacy communities sometimes have conflicting interests
- **Industry opposition**: Nursing home industry, home care agency industry, and fiscal conservatives resist reforms that increase costs
- **Low voter salience**: Caregiving has historically not been a high-priority issue in elections

### Concentrated Opposition

Opponents of caregiver support reform are well-organized and politically connected:

| Opponent | Interest | Mechanism |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| Nursing home industry | Maintain institutional funding levels | Lobbying; campaign contributions; state association power |
| Home care agencies | Resist wage mandates; maintain intermediary role | Industry associations; state regulatory influence |
| Fiscal conservatives | Oppose Medicaid expansion and new entitlements | Legislative blocks on HCBS expansion |
| Anti-union interests | Prevent direct care worker organizing | Legislative restrictions; legal challenges |
| Insurance industry | Oppose public LTSS insurance | Lobbying against CLASS Act and similar proposals |

### Partisan Dynamics

Caregiver support has become increasingly partisan despite its potential for bipartisan appeal:

- **Democratic position**: Generally supports expanded HCBS funding, higher caregiver wages, and new family caregiver benefits
- **Republican position**: Generally opposes Medicaid expansion and new federal spending programs; supports family caregiving through faith-based and community initiatives
- **Bipartisan exceptions**: RAISE Act (2018), Lifespan Respite Care Act, and some tax credit proposals have drawn cross-party support

## Cultural Causes

### Caregiving as Private Responsibility

American political culture emphasizes individual and family responsibility for care, rather than viewing caregiving as a collective social obligation:

- **Family obligation norm**: Expectation that families will provide care without public support
- **Gendered expectations**: Women are expected to provide care; women who cannot or choose not to face stigma
- **Independence ideology**: American emphasis on self-reliance creates discomfort with public acknowledgment of dependency and interdependence
- **Disability stigma**: Care for people with disabilities is often viewed as tragic rather than as a normal part of human experience

### Invisibility of Caregiving Labor

Caregiving work is systematically invisible in economic accounting and public discourse:

- Unpaid caregiving is not counted in GDP
- Paid home care occurs in private spaces, out of public view
- Direct care workers rarely appear in media representations of essential workers (despite COVID-19 briefly changing this)
- Policy discussions focus on healthcare delivery rather than personal support services
- The economic contributions of caregivers are not reflected in compensation or social recognition

### Disability as "Other"

Cultural attitudes toward disability contribute to the caregiving crisis:

- People with disabilities are often viewed as objects of charity rather than as rights-bearing citizens
- Caregiving is framed as a burden rather than as a valued social function
- Policy discussions about disability care often exclude the voices of people with disabilities
- The medical model of disability (viewing disability as an individual deficit to be fixed) competes with the social model (viewing disability as created by environmental and social barriers)

## Intersectional Causes

### Race, Gender, and Caregiving

The caregiving system is shaped by interlocking racial and gender inequities:

**Historical Roots**

- Domestic care work exclusions from New Deal labor protections were intentionally designed to exclude Black workers
- The "companionship services" exemption perpetuated this exclusion for decades
- Women of color have always performed a disproportionate share of paid care work
- Family caregiving expectations fall disproportionately on women, particularly women of color who have fewer resources and less access to formal services

**Current Manifestations**

| Dimension | Finding | Source |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| Workforce composition | 61% of direct care workers are people of color | PHI National (2024) |
| Wage gap within caregiving | Black and Hispanic direct care workers earn 5-10% less than white counterparts | Economic Policy Institute (2023) |
| Family caregiver burden | Black caregivers provide 30+ hours/week at higher rates than white caregivers | AARP (2020) |
| Service access | Communities of color have fewer HCBS providers | National Health Law Program (2023) |
| Immigration status | 28% of direct care workers are foreign-born; many face visa-related work restrictions | Migration Policy Institute (2023) |

### Rural-Urban Divide

Rural communities face compounded caregiving challenges:

| Factor | Rural Impact |
|--------|-------------|
| Provider availability | Fewer home care agencies; longer travel distances |
| Wages | Even lower than urban areas due to lower Medicaid rates |
| Family caregiver isolation | Greater geographic distance from support networks |
| Broadband access | Limits telehealth and remote monitoring options |
| Transportation | Lack of accessible public transit limits service delivery |

## Feedback Loops

Several reinforcing cycles perpetuate the caregiving crisis:

### The Low Wage-High Turnover Cycle

1. Medicaid sets low reimbursement rates
2. Provider agencies pay low wages
3. Workers leave for better-paying jobs
4. Turnover disrupts care for people with disabilities
5. Worker shortages reduce quality and access
6. Policymakers view problems as inherent to workforce rather than caused by wages
7. Rates remain low

### The Institutional Bias Cycle

1. HCBS waiver programs have waiting lists
2. People needing immediate care are placed in institutions
3. Institutional census remains high
4. Nursing home industry lobbies to maintain institutional funding
5. Policymakers resist HCBS expansion due to cost
6. Waiting lists persist

### The Caregiver Depletion Cycle

1. Family caregivers provide unpaid care due to lack of services
2. Caregivers reduce work hours or leave employment
3. Caregiver household income declines
4. Caregivers cannot afford to purchase supplemental care
5. Caregiving burden increases
6. Caregiver health declines
7. Both caregiver and care recipient need more support

## Root Cause Summary

| Root Cause | Category | Severity | Addressability |
|------------|----------|----------|----------------|
| Medicaid institutional bias | Structural | Critical | Moderate (requires federal legislation) |
| Fragmented federal authority | Structural | High | Moderate (requires reorganization) |
| Systematic undervaluation of care work | Economic | Critical | Moderate (requires wage mandates and cultural shift) |
| State budget constraints | Economic | High | Moderate (requires enhanced federal matching) |
| Caregiver political disorganization | Political | High | Moderate (growing advocacy capacity) |
| Concentrated industry opposition | Political | High | Difficult (entrenched interests) |
| Caregiving as private responsibility | Cultural | Critical | Difficult (requires deep cultural change) |
| Racial and gender inequity | Intersectional | Critical | Moderate (requires intentional policy design) |
