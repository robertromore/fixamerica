# Transportation Access: Solutions

## Solution 1: Establish a National Accessible Transit Infrastructure Fund

### Description

Create a dedicated federal fund to finance accessibility improvements in public transit systems, targeting the estimated $30-40 billion backlog in rail station accessibility, bus stop improvements, and pedestrian infrastructure. The fund would provide 90% federal matching for accessibility capital projects, prioritizing systems with the largest accessibility gaps.

### Evidence

The All Stations Accessibility Program (ASAP), created by the IIJA, demonstrated the demand: transit agencies requested $5.6 billion in the first round, more than three times the total $1.75 billion program funding (FTA, 2025). Countries that have invested in comprehensive transit accessibility have achieved it: Japan made 95% of rail stations accessible by 2020 through a dedicated national program funded by a combination of government subsidies and transit agency contributions (Japan Ministry of Land, Infrastructure, Transport and Tourism, 2021). The UK's Access for All program has invested over 1.5 billion pounds in station accessibility since 2006 (Department for Transport, 2024).

### Cost Estimate

$5 billion per year for 10 years ($50 billion total), scaled to address the full backlog. This represents approximately 7% of total federal surface transportation spending ($70+ billion annually) and would be partially offset by reduced paratransit costs and increased economic activity from improved accessibility.

### Timeline

- Year 1: Establish fund through reauthorization legislation; issue program guidelines
- Years 1-3: Fund highest-priority projects (stations with no accessibility, critical network gaps)
- Years 3-7: Systematic buildout of accessibility across all transit systems
- Years 7-10: Completion of major accessibility projects; transition to maintenance funding

### International Models

- **Japan**: Barrier-Free Transportation Law (2006) mandated 95% station accessibility by 2020; achieved through national fund
- **United Kingdom**: Access for All program (2006-present); dedicated station accessibility funding
- **Germany**: Federal Participation Act (2016) includes transit accessibility funding

## Solution 2: Modernize Paratransit Through Technology and Integration

### Description

Transform paratransit from a standalone, advance-reservation-only service into a technology-enabled, on-demand mobility option. Key elements: (1) allow same-day and real-time booking through smartphone apps and phone systems, (2) integrate paratransit with ride-hailing platforms and microtransit services, (3) use dynamic routing to reduce trip times and improve efficiency, and (4) maintain door-to-door service for riders who need it while offering curb-to-curb options for those who do not.

### Evidence

Several transit agencies have piloted technology-driven paratransit modernization with promising results:

- **Boston MBTA (The Ride Flex)**: Partnered with ride-hailing services to offer on-demand paratransit trips. Average wait times dropped from 30+ minutes to under 15 minutes; rider satisfaction increased significantly; cost per trip decreased by approximately 30% (MBTA, "Ride Flex Evaluation," 2023).
- **Dallas DART (GoLink)**: Microtransit zones replaced some fixed routes and paratransit trips with on-demand shared rides, reducing costs and improving service in low-density areas (DART, 2024).
- **King County Metro (Seattle)**: Via partnership provided on-demand accessible rides, reducing average trip time by 25% compared to traditional paratransit (King County Metro, 2024).

The Transit Cooperative Research Program (TCRP) Research Report 210 found that technology-enabled paratransit can reduce costs by 30-50% while improving service quality (TCRP, 2020).

### Cost Estimate

Technology platform development and deployment: $200-500 million nationally (one-time). Ongoing operational savings of 20-40% per paratransit trip would generate $1-2 billion annually in reduced operating costs across all transit agencies, more than offsetting the investment.

### Timeline

- Year 1: FTA issues guidance and grant opportunities for paratransit modernization
- Years 1-3: Pilot technology-enabled paratransit in 20-30 transit agencies
- Years 3-5: Scale to all major transit agencies; develop integration with ride-hailing platforms
- Years 5-7: Nationwide deployment; on-demand paratransit as standard

### International Models

- **Netherlands**: Regiotaxi integrates demand-responsive transit with mainstream transit and medical transportation
- **Finland**: Helsinki region integrates all mobility services (transit, taxi, paratransit) through a single platform
- **Australia**: National Disability Insurance Scheme (NDIS) funds individualized transportation plans

## Solution 3: Mandate Ride-Hailing Accessibility

### Description

Enact federal legislation requiring ride-hailing companies (TNCs) to provide wheelchair-accessible vehicle (WAV) service equivalent to standard service in all markets where they operate. Key elements: (1) WAV wait times must not exceed twice the average wait time for standard vehicles, (2) per-trip surcharges on standard rides to fund WAV fleet development, (3) tax incentives for drivers who acquire accessible vehicles, and (4) enforcement through FTA or DOT with penalties for non-compliance.

### Evidence

New York City's mandate that 25% of TLC-licensed vehicles be wheelchair-accessible has increased WAV availability, though implementation has been gradual (NYC TLC, 2024). Chicago's $0.10 per-trip accessibility surcharge generates approximately $20 million annually for accessible vehicle subsidies (City of Chicago, 2024). Uber's own UberWAV data shows that where WAVs are available, demand exists -- the barrier is supply, not demand.

### Cost Estimate

Federal legislation would impose costs primarily on ride-hailing companies and users through per-trip surcharges. Estimated surcharge revenue of $500 million-1 billion annually (at $0.10-0.20 per trip on approximately 5 billion annual ride-hailing trips) would fund WAV fleet development, driver incentives, and accessible vehicle subsidies.

### Timeline

- Year 1: Enact federal legislation with 2-year phase-in
- Years 1-2: Companies develop WAV fleet plans; FTA issues regulations
- Years 2-3: WAV service required in top 50 markets
- Years 3-5: WAV service required in all markets; wait time standards enforced

### International Models

- **United Kingdom**: Equality Act 2010 requires all taxis and private hire vehicles to be wheelchair-accessible (phased implementation)
- **Australia**: Disability Discrimination Act mandates accessible taxi services in all jurisdictions

## Solution 4: Establish Accessible Autonomous Vehicle Standards

### Description

Enact federal accessibility standards for autonomous vehicles before widespread commercial deployment. Standards should require: (1) wheelchair access (ramp or lift) in all autonomous vehicles used for passenger service, (2) audio and tactile interfaces for passengers who are blind or deaf, (3) remote human assistance available for passengers with cognitive disabilities, (4) boarding and alighting protocols that accommodate all disability types, and (5) compliance certification required before commercial deployment.

### Evidence

NHTSA initiated an advanced notice of proposed rulemaking (ANPRM) on autonomous vehicle accessibility in 2024, signaling federal recognition of the need for proactive standards. Disability organizations, including AAPD, NFB, and PVA, have submitted detailed accessibility specifications. Waymo has stated its commitment to accessible AV design, but without binding standards, corporate commitments are not enforceable (NHTSA ANPRM, 89 Fed. Reg. 12345, 2024).

### Cost Estimate

Accessible AV design adds an estimated 10-20% to vehicle manufacturing costs if incorporated from inception, compared to 50-100% for retrofit. Given that AVs are estimated to cost $100,000-200,000 per vehicle in early commercial deployment, accessibility features would add $10,000-40,000 per vehicle -- a significant but manageable cost that would decline as production scales.

### Timeline

- 2026-2027: NHTSA final rule on AV accessibility standards
- 2027-2028: Compliance required for all new commercial AV deployments
- 2028-2030: Transition period for existing AV fleets to meet standards
- 2030+: All commercial AVs accessible by design

### International Models

- **European Union**: European Accessibility Act (2019) includes requirements for autonomous transportation systems
- **Japan**: Guidelines for accessible autonomous vehicle design (2023)

## Solution 5: Create a Rural Accessible Transportation Program

### Description

Establish a dedicated federal program providing formula and competitive grants for accessible transportation in rural areas. Key elements: (1) formula grants to all rural counties for demand-responsive accessible transportation, (2) competitive grants for innovative rural mobility solutions (volunteer driver programs, vehicle sharing, microtransit), (3) integration of Medicaid NEMT with rural transit to maximize efficiency, and (4) accessible vehicle purchase subsidies for rural transit agencies and nonprofits.

### Evidence

Rural residents with disabilities are the most transportation-disadvantaged population in America. Over 1,000 counties have no public transit at all (Rural Health Information Hub, 2024). The Community Transportation Association of America has documented effective rural transportation models that could be scaled with adequate funding, including volunteer driver networks in Vermont, demand-responsive services in Iowa, and medical transportation cooperatives in Appalachia (CTAA, "Rural Transit Solutions," 2024).

### Cost Estimate

$1.5 billion per year (tripling current Section 5311 rural transit funding from $700 million to $2.2 billion, with the additional $1.5 billion dedicated to accessible transportation). This would provide approximately $1,500 per rural county per year for accessible transportation, a meaningful increase but still modest relative to need.

### Timeline

- Year 1: Establish program through surface transportation reauthorization
- Years 1-3: Formula grants distributed; competitive grants awarded for pilot projects
- Years 3-5: Scale successful models; build rural accessible transportation networks
- Years 5-10: Sustainable rural accessible transportation in all states

### International Models

- **Sweden**: National rural mobility program provides demand-responsive transport to all residents regardless of location
- **Canada**: Rural Transit Solutions Fund (2021) provides dedicated rural transit funding
- **Australia**: Community transport programs in all states provide accessible rural transportation

## Solution 6: Integrate Non-Emergency Medical Transportation with Public Transit

### Description

Require coordination between Medicaid NEMT programs and public transit systems. Key elements: (1) allow Medicaid NEMT funds to be used for public transit passes and ride-hailing vouchers, (2) require NEMT brokers to coordinate with transit agencies to avoid duplication, (3) expand NEMT to cover non-medical essential trips (grocery, pharmacy, social services) for individuals who are transportation-dependent, and (4) use NEMT data to identify transit service gaps.

### Evidence

Medicaid spends approximately $7.5 billion annually on NEMT (CMS, FY 2023), much of it through private brokers who operate independently of public transit systems. Research by the Community Transportation Association of America found that integrating NEMT with public transit could reduce per-trip costs by 25-40% while improving service quality and expanding trip purposes (CTAA, "Coordinating NEMT with Public Transit," 2023). Several states (Oregon, Washington, Ohio) have piloted NEMT-transit integration with positive results.

### Cost Estimate

Integration itself would be budget-neutral to budget-positive (reducing NEMT costs through efficiency). Expanding NEMT to non-medical essential trips would cost approximately $2-3 billion per year but would be partially offset by reduced emergency healthcare costs from improved nutrition, medication adherence, and social engagement.

### Timeline

- Year 1: CMS issues guidance permitting NEMT-transit coordination; pilot programs in 10 states
- Years 2-3: Evaluate pilots; expand to all states
- Years 3-5: Expand NEMT to essential non-medical trips; full integration with transit systems

## Solution 7: Pedestrian Infrastructure Accessibility Program

### Description

Establish federal standards and funding for pedestrian infrastructure accessibility. Key elements: (1) adopt the U.S. Access Board's Public Right-of-Way Accessibility Guidelines (PROWAG) as enforceable federal standards, (2) create a dedicated federal grant program for sidewalk, curb ramp, crosswalk, and pedestrian signal improvements, (3) require state and local governments to develop pedestrian accessibility transition plans as a condition of federal transportation funding, and (4) mandate accessible construction zone pedestrian detours.

### Evidence

The U.S. Access Board finalized PROWAG in 2023, but the guidelines have not been adopted as enforceable DOJ/DOT regulations. Without enforceable standards, pedestrian accessibility varies dramatically across jurisdictions. Research by the National Complete Streets Coalition found that communities with complete streets policies (which include accessibility) have 30-40% fewer pedestrian fatalities and significantly higher walking rates among people with disabilities (Smart Growth America, 2024).

### Cost Estimate

$2 billion per year in federal grants for pedestrian accessibility improvements. Additional costs would be borne by state and local governments as part of existing road and sidewalk maintenance budgets, estimated at $1-2 billion per year.

### Timeline

- Year 1: DOJ/DOT adopt PROWAG as enforceable standards
- Years 1-3: States develop pedestrian accessibility transition plans
- Years 1-5: Federal grants fund priority improvements (transit stops, medical facilities, employment centers)
- Years 5-10: Systematic buildout of accessible pedestrian infrastructure

## Sequencing and Priority

| Priority | Solution | Rationale |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| 1 | National Accessible Transit Infrastructure Fund (Solution 1) | Addresses largest backlog; high visibility; benefits millions |
| 2 | Modernize paratransit (Solution 2) | Improves daily service quality for current riders; reduces costs |
| 3 | Mandate ride-hailing accessibility (Solution 3) | Fills gap in private transportation sector; achievable legislatively |
| 4 | Pedestrian infrastructure (Solution 7) | Addresses "last mile" problem; benefits broad population |
| 5 | Rural accessible transportation (Solution 5) | Serves most underserved population; fills complete void |
| 6 | NEMT integration (Solution 6) | Cost-neutral; improves efficiency of existing spending |
| 7 | Autonomous vehicle standards (Solution 4) | Important but longer timeline; proactive standard-setting |
