# Transportation Access: Root Causes

## Systemic Analysis

Transportation inaccessibility for people with disabilities is not a single problem with a single cause. It is the product of interconnected structural failures in funding, governance, design philosophy, and enforcement that reinforce each other and resist incremental reform. Understanding these root causes is essential for designing solutions that address the system rather than merely treating symptoms.

### Root Cause 1: Chronic Underfunding of Accessible Transit

**The Problem**: Federal, state, and local funding for transit accessibility has never matched the ADA's mandates. The ADA required accessible transit but did not appropriate the funds to achieve it. Section 5310 (Enhanced Mobility for Seniors and Individuals with Disabilities) provides approximately $300 million annually -- a fraction of what is needed to make the nation's transit systems fully accessible. The All Stations Accessibility Program, created by the IIJA, received $1.75 billion, but demand exceeded $5.6 billion in the first round alone (FTA, 2025).

**Why It Persists**: Transit funding generally is insufficient in the United States compared to peer nations, and accessibility within transit competes for limited dollars against maintenance, expansion, and operations. People with disabilities who depend on transit are a politically weak constituency with limited lobbying power compared to highway users, transit unions, or suburban commuters. Federal transit formulas allocate funds based on population and ridership, not accessibility need, disadvantaging the communities where accessibility gaps are greatest.

**Consequences**: Transit agencies face impossible choices between maintaining existing service, expanding coverage, and making accessibility improvements. Accessibility investments are routinely deferred in favor of more politically visible projects. The backlog of unfunded accessibility improvements grows larger each year.

### Root Cause 2: Separation of Transportation, Housing, and Employment Planning

**The Problem**: In most American communities, transportation, housing, and employment are planned by separate agencies with separate budgets, separate constituencies, and separate accountability structures. A housing agency may approve accessible housing in a location with no accessible transit. An economic development agency may recruit employers to a site inaccessible by public transit. A transit agency may plan routes without considering where people with disabilities live or work.

**Why It Persists**: Governmental fragmentation is deeply embedded in American federalism. Metropolitan planning organizations (MPOs), transit agencies, housing authorities, and workforce development boards operate under different federal statutes, different funding streams, and different performance metrics. Coordinating across these silos requires political will and institutional capacity that are rarely present.

**Consequences**: People with disabilities may live in accessible housing but cannot reach employment. Or they may have a job but cannot access medical care because the transit system does not connect their workplace to their healthcare provider. The fragmentation of planning means that no single entity is responsible for ensuring that people with disabilities can actually navigate the full range of daily activities.

### Root Cause 3: Accessibility as Compliance Rather Than Design Principle

**The Problem**: Most transit agencies and transportation planners treat accessibility as a regulatory compliance obligation -- something to be satisfied at minimum cost -- rather than as a fundamental design principle that benefits all users. This mindset produces minimum-viable accessibility: wheelchair ramps that meet ADA specifications but are deployed reluctantly, audible announcements that are technically present but poorly implemented, paratransit that meets legal minimums but fails to provide genuine mobility.

**Why It Persists**: Compliance-driven accessibility reflects how the ADA is structured and enforced. The law establishes minimum standards and enforcement occurs through complaints and litigation. Agencies that meet minimums face little incentive to exceed them. Universal design -- which creates systems that work well for everyone regardless of ability -- requires upfront investment and a design philosophy that most agencies have not adopted.

**Consequences**: The gap between legal compliance and genuine accessibility is vast. A bus may technically be accessible (it has a ramp) but functionally inaccessible (the ramp is broken, the driver does not deploy it, or the bus stop has no sidewalk connection). A rail station may have an elevator but place it in a distant, poorly lit corner that makes it dangerous to use. Compliance without commitment produces half-measures.

### Root Cause 4: The Paratransit Cost Trap

**The Problem**: ADA paratransit is extremely expensive per trip ($40-50 average, compared to $4-5 for fixed-route) because it provides door-to-door service using dedicated vehicles for small numbers of riders. This high cost creates a self-reinforcing cycle: paratransit consumes a growing share of transit budgets, leaving fewer resources for fixed-route accessibility improvements that could reduce paratransit demand.

**Why It Persists**: The ADA mandates paratransit for individuals unable to use fixed-route transit. Transit agencies cannot legally reduce paratransit eligibility or service levels below ADA minimums. But they can defer fixed-route accessibility improvements, which keeps more people dependent on paratransit, which increases costs. The financial incentive to break this cycle is long-term, but budget pressures are immediate.

**Consequences**: Paratransit quality deteriorates as demand outstrips budget. Agencies impose the maximum allowable restrictions (advance reservation, fare limits, service area limits) to manage costs. Riders experience poor service quality that undermines independence and employment. Meanwhile, fixed-route systems remain only partially accessible, ensuring continued high paratransit demand.

### Root Cause 5: Rural Transportation Neglect

**The Problem**: Rural and suburban America, where approximately 60 million people live (Census Bureau, 2020), has minimal public transit infrastructure. Federal transit funding formulas heavily favor urbanized areas. Rural transit agencies, where they exist, operate with tiny budgets and serve vast geographic areas. For rural residents with disabilities who cannot drive, transportation options are essentially nonexistent.

**Why It Persists**: Rural transit is expensive per trip due to low population density and long distances. Political support for rural transit is limited because most rural communities have never had transit and do not expect it. The political power of rural areas is concentrated in road and highway funding, not transit. The ADA's paratransit mandate applies only where fixed-route transit exists, meaning that areas without fixed-route service have no ADA obligation to provide paratransit.

**Consequences**: Rural residents with disabilities face the starkest transportation access gaps. They are more likely to be homebound, unemployed, and socially isolated than their urban counterparts. Non-emergency medical transportation (NEMT) through Medicaid provides a partial safety net for medical trips, but only for Medicaid-eligible individuals and only for medical purposes.

### Root Cause 6: Technology Governance Gaps

**The Problem**: New transportation technologies -- ride-hailing, autonomous vehicles, microtransit -- emerge faster than accessibility regulations can be developed. Companies develop and deploy technologies designed for the broadest market segment (non-disabled users) and treat accessibility as an afterthought, if they consider it at all.

**Why It Persists**: Technology companies face market incentives to launch quickly and broadly, not to serve niche populations. Federal regulatory agencies (NHTSA, FTA, FHWA) operate on rulemaking timelines of 3-7 years, while technology products iterate on cycles of months. Disability rights advocacy organizations have limited capacity to monitor and influence the rapid pace of technology development.

**Consequences**: Each generation of transportation technology initially excludes people with disabilities. Ride-hailing launched without accessible vehicles. Autonomous vehicle prototypes were designed without wheelchair access. Bike-share and e-scooter systems created sidewalk obstacles for wheelchair users and people with visual impairments. The pattern is consistent: technology first, accessibility later -- if at all.

## Perverse Incentives

### The Paratransit Budget Squeeze

Transit agencies that invest in fixed-route accessibility may see immediate budget increases (capital costs of elevators, ramps, vehicles) but delayed savings (reduced paratransit demand). Budget cycles are annual; accessibility investments pay off over decades. The rational short-term decision is to defer accessibility improvements and absorb growing paratransit costs -- even though this is irrational over the long term.

### The Accessible Vehicle Market Failure

Wheelchair-accessible vehicles cost 50-100% more than standard vehicles. Manufacturers produce them in small quantities, keeping costs high. Ride-hailing companies avoid investing in WAV fleets because the small user population does not justify the cost under profit-maximizing logic. This market failure ensures that accessible vehicles remain scarce and expensive, reinforcing transportation inaccessibility.

### The NEMT Silo

Medicaid's Non-Emergency Medical Transportation benefit ($7.5 billion annually) operates as a completely separate system from public transit. NEMT brokers contract with private transportation providers to deliver medical trips. These trips could be integrated with public transit to improve efficiency and expand accessible transportation options, but programmatic silos, different funding streams, and different oversight structures prevent coordination.

## Structural Barriers to Reform

### Fragmented Governance

No single federal agency is responsible for all aspects of transportation accessibility. FTA oversees public transit, FHWA oversees highways and pedestrian infrastructure, NHTSA oversees vehicle safety and autonomous vehicles, CMS oversees Medicaid NEMT, and DOJ enforces ADA transportation provisions. This fragmentation means that no entity has comprehensive authority or accountability for accessible transportation.

### Local Control of Land Use

Transportation accessibility is profoundly shaped by land use decisions -- where housing, employment, healthcare, and services are located relative to transit. Land use is controlled by local governments (zoning, subdivision, building codes) that typically have no accessibility expertise and face no federal accountability for the transportation implications of their land use decisions.

### Political Weakness of the Disability Constituency

People with disabilities represent 26% of the adult population but have disproportionately low political power due to poverty, social isolation, communication barriers, and inaccessible political processes. Transit advocacy organizations are dominated by environmental and urbanist groups that prioritize overall ridership growth over disability access. Disability organizations are spread across many issue areas and often lack the specialized transportation policy expertise needed to influence complex funding and planning decisions.
