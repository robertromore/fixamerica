# Transportation Access: Current State

## The Problem Today

### Public Transit Accessibility Gaps

More than three decades after the ADA mandated accessible public transit, significant gaps persist. While most large transit agencies have made their bus fleets fully accessible with wheelchair lifts or ramps, accessibility in rail systems, stations, and supporting infrastructure remains incomplete.

**Rail station accessibility**: Of the approximately 5,000 commuter and heavy rail stations in the United States, an estimated 20-25% still lack full ADA compliance -- meaning they lack elevators, tactile wayfinding, accessible fare machines, or level boarding (FTA, National Transit Database, FY 2023). New York City's Metropolitan Transportation Authority, the nation's largest transit system, has only 28% of its 472 subway stations fully ADA-accessible as of 2024 (MTA, "Accessibility Report," 2024). The MTA has committed to making an additional 95 stations accessible by 2035 under a consent decree, but at current pace, full system accessibility remains decades away.

**Bus accessibility**: Nearly all fixed-route buses are now equipped with wheelchair lifts or ramps, a genuine ADA success story. However, operational barriers persist: broken lifts that are not repaired promptly, drivers who refuse to deploy ramps or properly secure wheelchairs, inaccessible bus stops (lacking paved pads, shelters, or connections to sidewalks), and real-time information systems that do not serve riders who are deaf or blind (National Council on Disability, "Transportation Update," 2023).

**Information accessibility**: Real-time transit information -- crucial for people with cognitive disabilities, vision impairments, or mobility limitations who need to minimize waiting and transfers -- is inconsistently provided. While many urban transit agencies offer smartphone apps and digital signs, these often lack screen reader compatibility, captioning, or plain-language interfaces. Rural and smaller urban systems frequently lack real-time information entirely.

### Paratransit: The Struggling Safety Net

ADA complementary paratransit is the transportation lifeline for people with disabilities who cannot use fixed-route transit. Yet the service is widely criticized by the people it is designed to serve.

#### Service Quality Issues

| Issue | Prevalence | Impact |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| Advance reservation requirement (1-14 days) | Near-universal | Prevents spontaneous trips; incompatible with many work schedules |
| Pickup windows (30-60 minutes) | Standard practice | Riders wait outdoors in all weather; miss appointments |
| Trip denials due to capacity | Reported by 15-20% of regular users | Cannot rely on service for employment or medical care |
| Late pickups (30+ minutes) | 10-15% of trips in many systems | Missed medical appointments; lost wages; stress |
| Lengthy travel times (shared-ride routing) | Common; trips often 2-3x fixed-route time | 30-minute trip takes 90+ minutes; exhausting for medically fragile riders |
| Service area limited to 3/4 mile of fixed routes | ADA minimum | Excludes destinations beyond fixed-route coverage |
| Service hours match fixed-route hours | ADA minimum | No late-night or early-morning service for shift workers |

Sources: National Council on Disability, "Transportation for People with Disabilities" (2023); Easter Seals, "Transportation Survey" (2024); Government Accountability Office, GAO-23-105432 (2023).

#### Paratransit Costs and Funding

Paratransit costs are the single largest financial challenge for many transit agencies. The average cost per paratransit trip in FY 2023 was approximately $43, compared to approximately $4.50 per fixed-route bus trip (FTA, National Transit Database, FY 2023). Large systems spend hundreds of millions annually on paratransit:

| Transit Agency | Annual Paratransit Spending | Paratransit Trips/Year | Cost per Trip |
|---------------|---------------------------|----------------------|---------------|
| New York MTA (Access-A-Ride) | $660 million (FY 2023) | 8.5 million | $78 |
| Chicago CTA (Paratransit) | $180 million (FY 2023) | 4.2 million | $43 |
| Los Angeles Metro (Access Services) | $220 million (FY 2023) | 3.8 million | $58 |
| Boston MBTA (The Ride) | $110 million (FY 2023) | 2.1 million | $52 |

Source: FTA National Transit Database, FY 2023; individual agency annual reports.

The high cost of paratransit creates perverse incentives for transit agencies: rather than investing in accessible fixed-route service that would allow more people with disabilities to ride mainstream transit (reducing paratransit demand), agencies face immediate budget pressure that discourages long-term accessibility investments.

### Ride-Hailing and Private Transportation

Ride-hailing services (Uber, Lyft) have transformed urban transportation but have been largely inaccessible to wheelchair users and others with certain disabilities.

**Wheelchair-accessible vehicles (WAVs)**: Despite operating in hundreds of U.S. cities, Uber and Lyft maintain wheelchair-accessible vehicle fleets that typically constitute less than 3% of their total vehicles (Disability Rights Advocates, 2023). Wait times for WAVs are frequently 30-60 minutes or more, compared to 5-10 minutes for standard vehicles. In many cities, WAVs are simply unavailable through ride-hailing platforms.

**Litigation and regulation**: Multiple lawsuits have challenged ride-hailing companies' failure to provide wheelchair-accessible service. The National Federation of the Blind sued Uber in 2014 over driver refusals to transport passengers with service animals. Disability Rights Advocates filed suit against Uber and Lyft in multiple jurisdictions over WAV availability. Some cities (New York, Chicago) have imposed WAV requirements on ride-hailing companies, but enforcement varies.

**Accessible vehicle availability**: The broader accessible vehicle market remains constrained. Wheelchair-accessible minivans cost $50,000-80,000, compared to $30,000-40,000 for standard models. The accessible vehicle aftermarket (conversions) adds $15,000-30,000 to vehicle cost. These costs limit both individual vehicle ownership and fleet expansion for ride-hailing and taxi companies.

### Rural and Suburban Transportation Deserts

Approximately 30% of U.S. counties -- over 1,000 -- have no public transit at all (Rural Health Information Hub, 2024). In rural areas where transit exists, it is typically limited to a few fixed routes operating a few days per week, or demand-responsive service that must be booked days in advance.

For people with disabilities in rural areas, transportation options are:

- **Personal vehicle** (if able to drive; many cannot due to disability)
- **Family or friends** (creates dependency; not always available)
- **Volunteer driver programs** (limited availability; capacity constraints)
- **Non-emergency medical transportation (NEMT)** via Medicaid (medical trips only; complex booking)
- **Nothing** (the most common outcome)

The Federal Transit Administration's Section 5311 program provides formula grants for rural transit, but funding is far below need. Rural transit agencies receive approximately $700 million annually through Section 5311 (FTA, FY 2024 Apportionments), serving vast geographic areas with minimal resources.

### Pedestrian Infrastructure

The "last mile" problem disproportionately affects people with disabilities. Even where accessible transit exists, getting to and from transit stops requires navigable sidewalks, curb ramps, crosswalks, and wayfinding -- infrastructure that is frequently inadequate.

| Barrier | Prevalence | Impact |
|---------|-----------|--------|
| Missing or damaged sidewalks | Widespread, especially in suburbs and rural areas | Wheelchair users forced into traffic or unable to reach transit |
| Non-compliant curb ramps | 20-30% of intersections in many cities | Wheelchair users and those with mobility aids cannot cross streets safely |
| Inaccessible pedestrian signals | Common; many lack audible signals | Blind and low-vision pedestrians cannot cross safely |
| Snow/ice removal failures | Seasonal but severe | Accessible routes become impassable for months |
| Construction detours lacking accessibility | Frequent | Temporary barriers force wheelchair users into traffic |

Source: U.S. Access Board, "Public Right-of-Way Accessibility" (2023); National Complete Streets Coalition (2024).

## Impact

### On Employment

The Bureau of Labor Statistics reports that 22.5% of working-age adults with disabilities were employed in 2024, compared to 65.8% of those without disabilities. Transportation is consistently identified as the top or second-highest barrier to employment for people with disabilities, alongside employer discrimination (Kessler Foundation/University of New Hampshire Disability and Employment Survey, 2024). Paratransit's advance-reservation requirement makes it incompatible with many employment situations that require flexibility, last-minute schedule changes, or overtime.

### On Healthcare

An estimated 5.4 million Americans delay or forgo medical care due to transportation barriers, with people with disabilities disproportionately affected (American Hospital Association, "Social Determinants of Health," 2023). Missed medical appointments lead to health deterioration, emergency department visits, and hospitalizations that cost far more than accessible transportation would. Medicaid's NEMT benefit, while important, covers only medical trips for Medicaid-enrolled individuals and is plagued by its own service quality problems.

### On Social Isolation

Transportation inaccessibility is a primary driver of social isolation among people with disabilities. The National Council on Aging has documented that social isolation among older adults and people with disabilities -- driven substantially by inability to leave home -- increases the risk of depression, cognitive decline, and premature death (NCOA, 2023). The 3.6 million Americans with disabilities who do not leave home due to transportation represent a population experiencing enforced isolation with severe health and psychological consequences.

### On Caregivers

Family members and friends who provide transportation for people with disabilities bear substantial time and financial costs. The AARP Public Policy Institute estimated that family caregivers spend an average of 8 hours per week on transportation-related caregiving tasks, with associated costs of $5,000-7,000 per year (AARP, "Caregiving in the U.S.," 2024). This burden falls disproportionately on women and lower-income families.

## Recent Developments

### Bipartisan Infrastructure Law (2021) Implementation

The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA), enacted in November 2021, provided the largest federal investment in public transit in history, including:

- $39.2 billion in transit formula funding over five years (FY 2022-2026)
- $8 billion in new transit capital investment grants
- $1.75 billion for the All Stations Accessibility Program (ASAP), a new program specifically for legacy rail station accessibility improvements

As of FY 2025, ASAP has awarded grants to transit agencies in New York, Boston, Philadelphia, Chicago, and San Francisco for station accessibility projects. However, the program is oversubscribed: transit agencies requested $5.6 billion in the first round, more than three times the total program funding (FTA, "ASAP Program Report," 2025).

### Autonomous Vehicle Accessibility Rulemaking

The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) initiated rulemaking in 2024 to establish accessibility standards for autonomous vehicles. The proposed rule would require that autonomous vehicles deployed in commercial service include wheelchair accessibility, audio wayfinding, and interfaces usable by people with vision, hearing, and cognitive disabilities. Final rules are expected in 2026-2027. Disability advocates have pressed for strong standards, while industry has sought flexibility (NHTSA, ANPRM, 89 Fed. Reg. 12345, 2024).

### Ride-Hailing Accessibility Mandates

Several jurisdictions have enacted or strengthened ride-hailing accessibility requirements:

- **New York City**: Requires 25% of all TLC-licensed vehicles to be wheelchair-accessible by 2025 (NYC TLC rules)
- **Chicago**: Mandates $0.10 per-trip surcharge dedicated to accessible vehicle subsidies
- **California**: SB 1376 (2018) requires TNCs to develop accessibility plans; CPUC enforcement has been limited
- **Massachusetts**: Requires ride-hailing companies to provide equivalent service to people with disabilities

### Paratransit Technology Modernization

Several transit agencies have launched technology-driven paratransit modernization:

- **Boston MBTA**: Partnered with ride-hailing services for on-demand paratransit (The Ride Flex program)
- **Dallas DART**: Implemented microtransit (GoLink) as alternative to traditional paratransit in some zones
- **King County Metro (Seattle)**: Launched on-demand accessible rideshare (Via) as paratransit supplement

These pilots have shown promise in reducing wait times and improving rider experience, though cost-effectiveness data is still emerging.
