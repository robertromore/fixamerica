# Transportation Access: Stakeholders

## Primary Stakeholders

### People Directly Affected

| Stakeholder Group | Size | Interest | Current Impact |
|-------------------|------|----------|---------------|
| People with mobility disabilities | ~14 million adults | Accessible vehicles, stations, sidewalks, paratransit | 3.6 million homebound due to transportation; low employment rates |
| People with vision disabilities | ~7 million adults | Audio wayfinding, tactile surfaces, accessible apps | Cannot navigate many transit systems independently |
| People with cognitive disabilities | ~13 million adults | Clear signage, simplified interfaces, reliable service | Difficulty navigating complex transit systems |
| People who are deaf or hard of hearing | ~11 million adults | Visual announcements, captioned information, vibration alerts | Miss audio announcements; safety concerns |
| Older adults with mobility limitations | ~20 million (65+) | Accessible transit for medical, social, shopping trips | Increasing isolation as driving ability declines |
| Paratransit riders | ~2 million active users | Reliable, timely, flexible paratransit service | Poor service quality limits independence and employment |
| Rural residents with disabilities | ~7 million | Any accessible transportation option | Often completely without transportation options |

### Caregivers and Families

Family members who provide transportation for people with disabilities spend an average of 8 hours per week on transportation-related tasks (AARP, 2024). This burden affects caregiver employment, health, and financial security. Improved accessible transportation would directly benefit an estimated 15-20 million family caregivers by reducing their transportation burden.

## Decision Makers

### Federal Government

| Decision Maker | Role | Disposition Toward Reform |
|---------------|------|--------------------------|
| Federal Transit Administration (FTA) | Oversees transit funding, accessibility standards, paratransit oversight | Generally supportive of accessibility; constrained by appropriations |
| Federal Highway Administration (FHWA) | Pedestrian infrastructure, sidewalks, curb ramps | Supports ADA compliance; slow rulemaking |
| National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) | Vehicle safety standards, autonomous vehicle regulation | Initiated AV accessibility rulemaking; industry-influenced |
| Department of Justice (DOJ) | ADA enforcement, including transit provisions | Complaint-driven enforcement; limited proactive capacity |
| U.S. Access Board | Accessibility guidelines and standards development | Strong advocate; advisory role limits direct authority |
| Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) | Medicaid NEMT oversight | Focused on cost containment; limited coordination with transit |
| Congress (Transportation Committees) | Authorization and funding legislation | Bipartisan support for transit investment; accessibility is secondary priority |
| Congress (Appropriations) | Annual transit funding levels | Historically underfunds transit relative to highways |

### State and Local Government

| Decision Maker | Role | Disposition |
|---------------|------|------------|
| State departments of transportation | Highway and transit funding distribution | Highway-focused; transit and accessibility are secondary |
| Metropolitan planning organizations (MPOs) | Regional transportation planning | Vary widely; some include disability representation, most do not |
| Transit agencies (boards and management) | Service planning, operations, capital investment | Supportive in principle; constrained by budgets |
| City councils and county commissions | Land use, sidewalks, local transit funding | Accessibility competes with many priorities |
| Public works departments | Sidewalk maintenance, curb ramps, pedestrian signals | Often underfunded; accessibility not primary mission |

## Coalition Dynamics

### Natural Alliance: Disability + Aging + Transit Equity

People with disabilities, older adults, and low-income transit riders share interests in accessible, affordable, reliable public transportation. This coalition represents a substantial voting bloc (disability: 61 million; over 65: 56 million; low-income transit-dependent: estimated 15-20 million, with significant overlap) and has the potential to influence transportation funding and planning decisions at every level of government.

### Potential Alliance: Disability + Technology

Technology companies developing autonomous vehicles and mobility-as-a-service platforms have a potential interest in disability accessibility -- the disability market represents a large, underserved customer base. Disability organizations can work with technology companies to ensure accessibility from inception rather than through retrofit, creating better products and avoiding litigation.

### Tension: Disability Advocates vs. Transit Agencies

Disability advocates push for expanded services, better quality, and full accessibility. Transit agencies face budget constraints and resist unfunded mandates. This tension is real but bridgeable through adequate federal funding and collaborative planning that includes disability representation.

### Tension: Disability Advocates vs. Ride-Hailing Companies

Ride-hailing companies have resisted accessibility requirements, arguing that their driver-partner model does not permit them to mandate vehicle types. Disability advocates have pursued litigation and regulation. Recent settlements and regulatory actions have produced incremental progress, but the fundamental tension between profit-maximizing platforms and universal accessibility remains.

## Power Analysis

### Who Has Power

| Actor | Power Source | Level of Influence |
|-------|------------|-------------------|
| Congressional transportation committees | Authorization and funding authority | Very High |
| State DOTs | Highway and transit funding distribution | High |
| Transit agency boards | Service and investment decisions | High |
| FTA | Grant conditions, compliance oversight | Moderate-High |
| Ride-hailing companies | Market power, lobbying, technology control | Moderate-High |
| AV developers | Technology design decisions, lobbying | Moderate |
| APTA (transit industry) | Industry lobbying, policy expertise | Moderate |
| MPOs | Regional planning authority | Moderate |
| Disability rights organizations | Legal advocacy, moral authority | Moderate |

### Who Lacks Power

| Actor | Barrier to Influence | Consequence |
|-------|---------------------|-------------|
| Paratransit riders | Poverty, disability, dependence on service they are complaining about | Service quality deteriorates without effective advocacy |
| Rural residents with disabilities | Geographic isolation, no organized constituency | Transportation needs are invisible in policy process |
| Pedestrians with disabilities | Sidewalk issues are hyper-local; no organized national voice | Pedestrian infrastructure neglected |
| Family caregivers providing transportation | Invisible in transportation policy | Their burden is not counted in policy cost-benefit analyses |
| Future technology users with disabilities | Cannot advocate for products not yet designed | Accessibility excluded from initial designs |
