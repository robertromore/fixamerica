# Transportation Access: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Transportation access reform for people with disabilities requires action at multiple levels: federal legislation to establish dedicated accessibility funding and mandate ride-hailing accessibility, regulatory action to adopt pedestrian infrastructure standards and autonomous vehicle accessibility requirements, state legislation to complement federal action, and coordination reforms to integrate fragmented transportation programs. This document provides draft legal text for the key reforms identified in [Solutions](07-solutions.md).

## Federal Legislation

### Accessible Transit Infrastructure Act

**Purpose**: Establish a dedicated federal fund to finance accessibility improvements in public transit systems, targeting the estimated $30-40 billion backlog in rail station accessibility, bus stop improvements, and pedestrian infrastructure.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Accessible Transit Infrastructure
Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that—
    (1) more than 30 years after the Americans with
        Disabilities Act mandated accessible public transit,
        an estimated 20-25 percent of rail stations in the
        United States remain inaccessible;
    (2) approximately 3.6 million Americans with disabilities
        do not leave their homes due to transportation
        difficulties;
    (3) the All Stations Accessibility Program, created by
        the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act, received
        applications totaling $5.6 billion against available
        funding of $1.75 billion, demonstrating acute unmet
        demand;
    (4) inaccessible transportation costs the United States
        economy an estimated $30 billion or more annually in
        lost productivity, increased healthcare costs, and
        social service expenditures; and
    (5) countries including Japan, the United Kingdom, and
        Germany have achieved higher levels of transit
        accessibility through dedicated national funding
        programs.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to establish a
dedicated federal fund to eliminate the transit accessibility
backlog and ensure that all Americans with disabilities can
use public transportation.

SEC. 3. NATIONAL ACCESSIBLE TRANSIT FUND.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established in the Treasury of
the United States the National Accessible Transit Fund.

(b) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There is authorized
to be appropriated to the Fund $5,000,000,000 for each of
fiscal years 2027 through 2036.

(c) USE OF FUNDS.—Amounts in the Fund shall be available
to the Secretary of Transportation, acting through the
Administrator of the Federal Transit Administration, for
grants to State and local transit agencies for—
    (1) rail station accessibility improvements, including
        elevators, escalators, ramps, tactile wayfinding,
        level boarding, accessible fare equipment, and
        signage;
    (2) bus stop accessibility improvements, including paved
        pads, shelters, curb ramp connections, and real-time
        information displays;
    (3) vehicle accessibility improvements, including
        wheelchair ramps, lifts, securement systems, and
        audible and visual announcement systems;
    (4) accessible real-time transit information systems,
        including screen reader-compatible applications,
        audio announcements, and plain-language interfaces;
        and
    (5) pedestrian infrastructure connecting transit stops
        to surrounding areas, including sidewalks, curb
        ramps, and accessible pedestrian signals within
        one-quarter mile of transit stops.

(d) FEDERAL SHARE.—The Federal share of any project funded
under this section shall be 90 percent.

(e) PRIORITIZATION.—The Administrator shall prioritize
grants to transit agencies that—
    (1) have the largest proportion of inaccessible stations
        or stops;
    (2) serve areas with the highest concentrations of
        people with disabilities;
    (3) demonstrate integration of accessibility planning
        with housing and employment access; and
    (4) include people with disabilities in project planning
        and design.

(f) ACCESSIBILITY TRANSITION PLANS.—As a condition of
receiving grants under this section, transit agencies shall
submit accessibility transition plans specifying—
    (1) the timeline for achieving full system accessibility;
    (2) interim milestones for each year;
    (3) the estimated cost of full accessibility; and
    (4) the agency's commitment to maintain accessibility
        once achieved.

SEC. 4. REPORTING.

(a) ANNUAL REPORT.—The Administrator shall submit an annual
report to Congress on the status of grants awarded, projects
completed, and progress toward eliminating the national
transit accessibility backlog.

(b) GAO EVALUATION.—The Comptroller General shall conduct
a comprehensive evaluation of the Fund's effectiveness 5
years after the initial appropriation.

SEC. 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on October 1, 2027, or the first
day of the fiscal year following enactment, whichever is later.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3(a)-(b)**: Creates a dedicated fund authorized at $5 billion per year for 10 years, addressing the full estimated backlog
- **Section 3(c)**: Defines eligible uses broadly to cover stations, stops, vehicles, information, and connecting pedestrian infrastructure
- **Section 3(d)**: Sets 90% federal share to minimize local cost burden and accelerate implementation
- **Section 3(e)**: Prioritizes grants to areas with the greatest accessibility gaps and highest disability populations
- **Section 3(f)**: Requires transition plans to ensure systematic, time-bound progress

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| CBO cost score ($50 billion over 10 years) | Frame as investment with $30+ billion annual return in economic productivity; offset through reduced Medicaid NEMT and social services costs |
| Competition with highway funding | Separate fund insulates from highway-transit competition; bipartisan infrastructure precedent |
| Transit agency capacity to execute | FTA technical assistance; phased deployment; allow multi-year project timelines |
| Political viability of $5 billion/year | ASAP program proved demand; scale down to $2 billion/year as fallback |

**Refinements**:

- **Fallback position**: Expand and permanently authorize ASAP at $1 billion per year
- **Stronger version**: Include enforceable deadlines for full system accessibility (10-year mandate)
- **Phase-in option**: $2 billion in Years 1-3, $4 billion in Years 4-6, $5 billion in Years 7-10

### Ride-Hailing Accessibility Act

**Purpose**: Require ride-hailing companies (transportation network companies, or TNCs) to provide wheelchair-accessible vehicle service equivalent to standard service in all markets where they operate.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Ride-Hailing Accessibility Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that—
    (1) ride-hailing services have transformed urban
        transportation but remain largely inaccessible to
        wheelchair users and others with certain disabilities;
    (2) wheelchair-accessible vehicles constitute less than
        3 percent of ride-hailing fleets in most markets;
    (3) wait times for wheelchair-accessible ride-hailing
        vehicles frequently exceed 30-60 minutes, compared
        to 5-10 minutes for standard vehicles;
    (4) the Americans with Disabilities Act prohibits
        disability discrimination in public accommodations
        and transportation services; and
    (5) jurisdictions including New York City and Chicago
        have successfully mandated ride-hailing accessibility,
        demonstrating operational feasibility.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to ensure that
people with disabilities have equal access to ride-hailing
transportation services.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

(a) TRANSPORTATION NETWORK COMPANY.—An entity that uses a
digital network or software application to connect riders
with drivers for the purpose of providing prearranged
transportation services for compensation.

(b) WHEELCHAIR-ACCESSIBLE VEHICLE (WAV).—A vehicle that
is designed or modified to transport individuals who use
wheelchairs, including vehicles with ramps or lifts and
adequate interior space for wheelchair securement.

(c) EQUIVALENT SERVICE.—Service that provides comparable
response times, geographic coverage, hours of operation,
and pricing to standard service.

SEC. 4. ACCESSIBILITY REQUIREMENTS.

(a) WAV SERVICE MANDATE.—Every transportation network
company that provides service in a market shall, within
the timelines specified in subsection (c), provide
wheelchair-accessible vehicle service in that market.

(b) SERVICE STANDARDS.—WAV service shall meet the
following standards:
    (1) RESPONSE TIME.—Average WAV response time shall
        not exceed 2 times the average response time for
        standard vehicles in the same market.
    (2) GEOGRAPHIC COVERAGE.—WAV service shall be
        available throughout the geographic area served
        by the TNC.
    (3) HOURS.—WAV service shall be available during all
        hours when standard service is available.
    (4) PRICING.—Fares for WAV trips shall not exceed
        fares for comparable standard trips.
    (5) SERVICE ANIMALS.—TNCs shall enforce policies
        prohibiting driver refusal to transport passengers
        with service animals.

(c) IMPLEMENTATION TIMELINE.—
    (1) Within 1 year of enactment, TNCs shall provide
        WAV service in the 25 largest metropolitan
        statistical areas.
    (2) Within 2 years of enactment, TNCs shall provide
        WAV service in all metropolitan statistical areas
        with population exceeding 500,000.
    (3) Within 3 years of enactment, TNCs shall provide
        WAV service in all markets in which they operate.

SEC. 5. ACCESSIBILITY SURCHARGE.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—TNCs may impose an accessibility
surcharge of not more than $0.20 per trip on all trips
to fund WAV fleet development, driver incentives, and
accessible vehicle acquisition.

(b) DEDICATED USE.—Surcharge revenue shall be used
exclusively for—
    (1) subsidies for drivers who acquire or lease
        wheelchair-accessible vehicles;
    (2) operation of company-owned or leased WAV fleets;
    (3) partnerships with accessible vehicle fleet
        operators; and
    (4) technology development for WAV dispatch and
        service optimization.

(c) REPORTING.—TNCs shall report surcharge revenue and
expenditures to the Secretary of Transportation annually.

SEC. 6. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) FEDERAL ENFORCEMENT.—The Secretary of Transportation
shall enforce this Act. Violations are subject to civil
penalties of—
    (1) up to $50,000 per day for failure to provide WAV
        service in required markets; and
    (2) up to $10,000 per incident for failure to meet
        service standards.

(b) STATE AND LOCAL ENFORCEMENT.—This Act does not
preempt State or local laws imposing additional or
more stringent accessibility requirements on TNCs.

(c) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—Any person aggrieved by a
violation of this Act may bring a civil action for
injunctive relief, compensatory damages, and reasonable
attorney fees.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 180 days after the date of
enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 4**: Mandates WAV service with specific, enforceable standards for response time, coverage, hours, and pricing
- **Section 4(c)**: Phased implementation starting with largest markets to allow fleet development
- **Section 5**: Authorizes per-trip surcharge to fund WAV fleet development, placing costs on all riders rather than on people with disabilities
- **Section 6**: Multiple enforcement pathways including federal penalties, state/local authority, and private right of action

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Ride-hailing company opposition and lobbying | Build on precedent in NYC and Chicago; extensive disability community and consumer support |
| Independent contractor model complicates vehicle mandates | Bill allows company-owned WAV pools, partnerships with fleet operators, and driver subsidies |
| WAV vehicle supply constraints | Phase-in timeline allows supply development; manufacturing incentives in companion legislation |
| Industry argument that ADA does not apply to TNCs | Bill establishes statutory requirements independent of ADA interpretation |

**Refinements**:

- **Fallback position**: Federal guidance under existing ADA authority plus incentives (no new mandate)
- **Stronger version**: Require WAV fleet percentages (25% of all vehicles) rather than service equivalency standards
- **Phase-in option**: Start with top 10 markets in Year 1 instead of top 25

### Accessible Autonomous Vehicle Act

**Purpose**: Establish federal accessibility standards for autonomous vehicles before widespread commercial deployment.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Accessible Autonomous Vehicle
Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
    (1) autonomous vehicle technology has the potential to
        provide unprecedented transportation independence for
        people with disabilities who cannot drive;
    (2) without mandatory accessibility standards, autonomous
        vehicles risk replicating the inaccessibility of
        conventional transportation;
    (3) NHTSA initiated accessibility rulemaking in 2024,
        but binding standards have not yet been finalized;
        and
    (4) accessibility designed into vehicles from inception
        costs 10-20 percent of vehicle price, compared to
        50-100 percent for retrofit.

SEC. 3. ACCESSIBILITY STANDARDS FOR AUTONOMOUS VEHICLES.

(a) RULEMAKING.—Not later than 18 months after the date
of enactment, the Secretary of Transportation, acting
through NHTSA, shall issue final rules establishing
accessibility standards for autonomous vehicles used
in commercial passenger service.

(b) MINIMUM REQUIREMENTS.—Standards shall require that
autonomous vehicles used in commercial passenger service
include—
    (1) wheelchair access through a ramp or lift meeting
        ADA vehicle accessibility standards;
    (2) interior space sufficient for at least one occupied
        wheelchair with securement system;
    (3) audio interfaces for route information, fare
        payment, and emergency communication usable by
        passengers who are blind or have low vision;
    (4) visual displays and tactile controls for passengers
        who are deaf or hard of hearing;
    (5) plain-language interfaces and simplified controls
        for passengers with cognitive disabilities;
    (6) remote human assistance accessible through in-vehicle
        communication for any passenger requesting aid;
    (7) exterior audio signals to alert pedestrians who are
        blind or have low vision to vehicle presence and
        movement; and
    (8) boarding and alighting protocols that accommodate
        passengers with diverse disability types, including
        adequate time for boarding.

(c) FLEET REQUIREMENTS.—For any entity deploying 10 or
more autonomous vehicles in commercial passenger service,
not less than 25 percent of deployed vehicles shall be
wheelchair-accessible.

(d) COMPLIANCE CERTIFICATION.—No entity may deploy an
autonomous vehicle in commercial passenger service
without a certification of accessibility compliance
issued by NHTSA.

SEC. 4. RESEARCH PROGRAM.

(a) The Secretary shall establish a research program on
accessible autonomous vehicle technology, authorized at
$50,000,000 per year for 5 years.

(b) Research priorities shall include—
    (1) wheelchair-accessible AV design optimization;
    (2) interfaces for passengers with cognitive disabilities;
    (3) safety systems for vulnerable road users with
        disabilities, including pedestrians who are blind;
        and
    (4) rural and suburban accessible AV deployment models.

SEC. 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

(a) This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
(b) Accessibility standards under Section 3 shall apply
to all autonomous vehicles deployed in commercial passenger
service beginning 2 years after issuance of final rules.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3(a)-(b)**: Mandates specific accessibility features covering mobility, vision, hearing, and cognitive disabilities
- **Section 3(c)**: Requires wheelchair-accessible vehicles as a percentage of commercial AV fleets
- **Section 3(d)**: Prevents deployment without accessibility certification, ensuring proactive compliance
- **Section 4**: Funds research to ensure standards are technically feasible and continuously improved

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| AV industry resistance to prescriptive standards | Performance-based standards where possible; consultation with industry during rulemaking |
| Concern about delaying AV deployment | 18-month rulemaking plus 2-year compliance timeline provides adequate lead time |
| Technical feasibility of some requirements | Research program (Section 4) supports ongoing development; standards can be updated |
| Cost increases for AV manufacturers | Accessibility from inception costs 10-20% vs. 50-100% for retrofit; early standards save money |

### Rural Accessible Transportation Act

**Purpose**: Establish a dedicated federal program providing formula and competitive grants for accessible transportation in rural areas.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Rural Accessible
Transportation Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
    (1) over 1,000 counties in the United States, comprising
        approximately 30 percent of all counties, have no
        public transit;
    (2) approximately 7 million people with disabilities
        live in rural areas with limited or no accessible
        transportation;
    (3) the ADA's paratransit mandate applies only where
        fixed-route transit exists, leaving rural areas
        with no federal accessible transportation
        obligation; and
    (4) rural residents with disabilities have the highest
        rates of social isolation, unemployment, and
        unmet transportation need of any demographic
        group.

SEC. 3. RURAL ACCESSIBLE TRANSPORTATION PROGRAM.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary of Transportation, acting
through the Federal Transit Administration, shall
establish the Rural Accessible Transportation Program.

(b) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There is authorized
to be appropriated—
    (1) for formula grants under subsection (c),
        $1,000,000,000 per year for fiscal years 2027
        through 2036; and
    (2) for competitive grants under subsection (d),
        $500,000,000 per year for fiscal years 2027
        through 2036.

(c) FORMULA GRANTS.—The Administrator shall distribute
formula grants to States based on—
    (1) rural population;
    (2) number of rural residents with disabilities;
    (3) geographic area; and
    (4) absence of existing public transit service.

(d) COMPETITIVE GRANTS.—The Administrator shall award
competitive grants for—
    (1) innovative rural accessible transportation models,
        including demand-responsive service, volunteer
        driver programs, vehicle-sharing cooperatives, and
        microtransit;
    (2) integration of Medicaid NEMT with rural transit
        services;
    (3) accessible vehicle acquisition for rural transit
        agencies and nonprofit transportation providers;
        and
    (4) technology platforms for rural transportation
        coordination and booking.

(e) USE OF FUNDS.—Funds may be used for capital expenses,
operating expenses, and planning and coordination
activities.

(f) FEDERAL SHARE.—The Federal share for grants under
this section shall be 90 percent for capital expenses
and 80 percent for operating expenses.

SEC. 4. NEMT COORDINATION.

(a) The Secretary of Health and Human Services, in
coordination with the Secretary of Transportation, shall
issue guidance permitting States to—
    (1) use Medicaid NEMT funds for public transit passes
        and ride-hailing vouchers;
    (2) require NEMT brokers to coordinate with rural
        transit agencies; and
    (3) expand NEMT to cover essential non-medical trips
        for Medicaid beneficiaries who are transportation-
        dependent, including trips to grocery stores,
        pharmacies, and social services.

SEC. 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on October 1, 2027, or the
first day of the fiscal year following enactment,
whichever is later.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3(b)-(c)**: Formula grants ensure every state receives rural transportation funding based on population, disability prevalence, geography, and existing service gaps
- **Section 3(d)**: Competitive grants support innovative models suited to rural conditions
- **Section 4**: Integrates Medicaid NEMT with rural transit to maximize efficiency and expand trip purposes

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Ride-Hailing Accessibility Act

**Purpose**: State-level requirement for ride-hailing companies to provide wheelchair-accessible vehicle service.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Ride-Hailing
Accessibility Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Transportation network company" means an entity that
uses a digital network to connect riders with drivers for
prearranged transportation services for compensation.

(b) "Wheelchair-accessible vehicle" means a vehicle
designed or modified to transport individuals who use
wheelchairs, including vehicles with ramps or lifts and
adequate interior space for wheelchair securement.

SECTION 3. ACCESSIBILITY REQUIREMENTS.

(a) Every transportation network company operating in
this State shall provide wheelchair-accessible vehicle
service throughout its service area.

(b) WAV response time shall not exceed 2 times the
average response time for standard vehicles.

(c) Fares for WAV trips shall not exceed fares for
comparable standard trips.

(d) TNCs shall enforce policies prohibiting driver
refusal to transport passengers with service animals.

SECTION 4. ACCESSIBILITY SURCHARGE.

(a) TNCs are authorized to impose a surcharge of not
more than [$0.15 / $0.20] per trip to fund WAV fleet
development.

(b) Surcharge revenue shall be used exclusively for
WAV-related expenses and reported annually to the
[State Public Utilities Commission / Transportation
Department].

SECTION 5. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) The [State Public Utilities Commission / Attorney
General] shall enforce this Act.

(b) Violations are subject to civil penalties of up to
$25,000 per day for failure to provide WAV service.

(c) Private right of action for injunctive relief and
reasonable attorney fees.

SECTION 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect [1 year / 18 months] after
enactment.
```

**Adaptations**: States may adjust surcharge amounts, enforcement agencies, penalty levels, and implementation timelines. States with existing TNC regulatory frameworks should integrate accessibility requirements into those structures.

### Model State Pedestrian Accessibility Act

**Purpose**: Require state and local governments to develop and implement pedestrian infrastructure accessibility plans.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Pedestrian
Accessibility Act".

SECTION 2. PEDESTRIAN ACCESSIBILITY STANDARDS.

(a) The [State DOT / State Access Board] shall adopt
pedestrian accessibility standards for public rights-
of-way consistent with the U.S. Access Board's Public
Right-of-Way Accessibility Guidelines (PROWAG).

(b) Standards shall apply to all construction,
reconstruction, and alteration of sidewalks, curb
ramps, crosswalks, pedestrian signals, and other
pedestrian facilities on public rights-of-way.

SECTION 3. TRANSITION PLANS.

(a) Each municipality with a population exceeding
[5,000 / 10,000] shall develop and submit to the
[State DOT] a pedestrian accessibility transition
plan within 2 years of the effective date.

(b) Transition plans shall include—
    (1) an inventory of all non-compliant pedestrian
        infrastructure;
    (2) a prioritized schedule for achieving compliance;
    (3) estimated costs; and
    (4) a process for public input with accessible
        participation opportunities for people with
        disabilities.

SECTION 4. SNOW AND ICE REMOVAL.

Property owners and municipal agencies responsible for
sidewalk maintenance shall remove snow and ice from
sidewalks and curb ramps within [24 / 48] hours of the
end of a snow event. Accessible pedestrian routes to
transit stops shall be prioritized.

SECTION 5. CONSTRUCTION DETOURS.

All pedestrian detours required by construction activities
on public rights-of-way shall be accessible to persons
with disabilities, including persons using wheelchairs,
persons who are blind, and persons with other mobility
or sensory disabilities.

SECTION 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect [180 days / 1 year] after
enactment.
```

## Regulatory Framework

### DOJ/DOT: Adopt PROWAG as Enforceable Standards

**Existing Authority**: 42 U.S.C. Section 12134 (ADA Title II rulemaking authority); 28 C.F.R. Part 35 (DOJ ADA regulations)

**Recommended Regulatory Actions**

| Action | Authority | Timeline |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| Adopt PROWAG as binding accessibility standards for public rights-of-way | DOJ/DOT joint rulemaking under ADA Title II | 12-18 months |
| Require pedestrian accessibility transition plans from all entities receiving federal transportation funds | DOT administrative guidance | 6-12 months |
| Mandate accessible pedestrian detours during construction | DOT/FHWA guidance under MUTCD | 6 months |

### NHTSA: Finalize Autonomous Vehicle Accessibility Standards

**Existing Authority**: 49 U.S.C. Section 30111 (Federal Motor Vehicle Safety Standards); NHTSA ANPRM, 89 Fed. Reg. 12345 (2024)

**Recommended Actions**

- Issue NPRM on AV accessibility standards within 6 months
- Finalize rule within 18 months of ANPRM
- Require compliance certification before commercial AV deployment
- Establish ongoing research program on AV accessibility innovation

### FTA: Paratransit Modernization Guidance

**Existing Authority**: 49 U.S.C. Section 5307 (transit grants); 49 C.F.R. Part 37 (ADA paratransit requirements)

**Recommended Actions**

| Action | Authority | Timeline |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| Issue guidance permitting same-day paratransit booking as supplement to next-day requirement | FTA interpretation of 49 C.F.R. Section 37.131 | 6 months |
| Fund paratransit technology modernization pilots through Section 5310 | Existing grant authority | 12 months |
| Establish minimum paratransit service quality metrics (on-time performance, trip completion) | FTA administrative guidance | 12-18 months |
| Require real-time information for paratransit riders (estimated pickup time, vehicle location) | FTA guidance | 18 months |

### CMS: NEMT Coordination Guidance

**Existing Authority**: 42 U.S.C. Section 1396a(a)(4) (Medicaid NEMT); 42 C.F.R. Part 431 (Medicaid administration)

**Recommended Actions**

- Issue State Medicaid Director letter permitting NEMT-transit coordination
- Allow Medicaid NEMT funds for public transit passes and ride-hailing vouchers
- Pilot expansion of NEMT to essential non-medical trips in 10 states
- Require NEMT brokers to report coordination with public transit agencies

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

**Commerce Clause**: Federal ride-hailing accessibility mandates rest on well-established Commerce Clause authority. Ride-hailing companies operate interstate digital platforms; regulation of their service standards falls squarely within federal commerce power. See *Heart of Atlanta Motel v. United States*, 379 U.S. 241 (1964) (upholding civil rights mandates on businesses in interstate commerce).

**Spending Clause**: The Accessible Transit Infrastructure Act conditions new federal grants on accessibility transition plans. This is a permissible exercise of Spending Clause authority under *South Dakota v. Dole*, 483 U.S. 203 (1987), as conditions are (a) related to the federal interest in accessible transportation, (b) unambiguous, and (c) not coercive under *NFIB v. Sebelius*, 567 U.S. 519 (2012), because grants are new rather than threatening existing funding.

**Tenth Amendment**: Federal accessibility standards for autonomous vehicles and ride-hailing services regulate private companies, not state governments, and therefore do not implicate anti-commandeering principles. The Rural Accessible Transportation Act provides grants to states on a voluntary basis.

**ADA Preemption**: Federal ride-hailing accessibility legislation would supplement, not displace, existing ADA requirements. The non-preemption provision (Section 6(b) of the Ride-Hailing Accessibility Act) preserves state and local authority to impose additional requirements.

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Application |
|-----------|-------------|
| FTA grant conditions | Transit agencies must meet accessibility requirements to receive federal funds |
| DOT civil penalties | Ride-hailing companies and AV operators face penalties for non-compliance |
| DOJ ADA enforcement | Complaint-driven and pattern-or-practice enforcement of transit accessibility |
| NHTSA compliance certification | AVs must be certified as accessible before commercial deployment |
| State AG enforcement | State-level ride-hailing and pedestrian accessibility mandates |
| Private right of action | Individual and class action suits under federal and state laws |
| Medicaid program integrity | NEMT coordination requirements enforceable through Medicaid compliance |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Accessible Transit Infrastructure Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Transition plan gaming | Transit agencies could submit ambitious transition plans to receive grants but fail to meet milestones, with no automatic consequence | High |
| Definition of "fully accessible" | Agencies could claim compliance while stations remain functionally inaccessible (elevator in distant location, broken equipment) | High |
| Maintenance deferral | After achieving accessibility, agencies could defer maintenance, allowing lifts and elevators to deteriorate | Medium |
| 90% federal share encourages gold-plating | High federal match may incentivize unnecessarily expensive solutions rather than cost-effective accessibility | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not mandate a completion deadline | Agencies could stretch accessibility improvements indefinitely | Political resistance to enforceable deadlines |
| Does not address operating cost of accessibility | Capital improvements funded, but ongoing operating costs (elevator maintenance, accessible vehicle operation) are not | Operating funding is a separate appropriations challenge |
| Does not directly address paratransit quality | Fund focuses on fixed-route accessibility; paratransit improvements are addressed in separate solutions | Structural separation of capital and operating concerns |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Add enforceable milestone requirements: transit agencies receiving grants must demonstrate annual progress or face grant suspension
2. Define "fully accessible" to include functional accessibility standards (equipment working 95%+ of the time, staff trained, information systems operational)
3. Require maintenance funding plans as condition of capital grants
4. Include cost-effectiveness review in grant evaluation to prevent gold-plating
5. Link fund to paratransit modernization grants to address both capital and service quality

### Ride-Hailing Accessibility Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| "Equivalent service" gaming | TNCs could technically provide WAV service but with systemically longer wait times that remain within the 2x standard | High |
| Surcharge diversion | TNCs could classify general operational expenses as "WAV-related" to absorb surcharge revenue | Medium |
| Geographic manipulation | TNCs could narrow their service area definition to exclude low-density areas where WAV service is most difficult to provide | Medium |
| Driver incentive insufficiency | Surcharge-funded driver incentives may be too low to attract sufficient WAV drivers, resulting in compliant plans but inadequate service | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address accessible vehicle supply | WAV vehicle manufacturing remains constrained; mandate may exceed supply capacity | Vehicle manufacturing market separate from ride-hailing market |
| $0.20 surcharge cap may be insufficient | Surcharge revenue may not fund adequate WAV fleet development in all markets | Political constraint on surcharge level |
| Does not address non-wheelchair accessibility | Focuses on WAV; does not fully address needs of passengers who are deaf, blind, or have cognitive disabilities | Wheelchair accessibility is most visible and costly barrier |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Define "equivalent service" with specific, measurable benchmarks reviewed quarterly (median wait time, 90th percentile wait time, trip completion rate)
2. Require independent auditing of surcharge expenditures
3. Include companion federal manufacturing incentive for wheelchair-accessible vehicles
4. Expand accessibility requirements to include app accessibility (screen reader, simplified interface) and driver training on serving passengers with all disability types
5. Authorize surcharge increase above $0.20 if DOT determines funding is insufficient for compliance

### Accessible Autonomous Vehicle Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| 25% WAV fleet minimum may be insufficient | If demand for accessible AVs exceeds 25% of fleet, wait times for accessible vehicles will be excessive | Medium |
| "Commercial passenger service" definition | Companies could structure deployments as pilot programs or non-commercial testing to avoid requirements | High |
| Remote human assistance quality | Requirement for remote assistance does not specify response time or expertise standards | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| 18-month rulemaking timeline may slip | NHTSA rulemakings frequently take longer than authorized | Agency capacity and procedural requirements |
| Does not address non-commercial AVs | Personal autonomous vehicles not covered by commercial service requirements | Constitutional limits on regulating personal vehicle features |
| Rural AV deployment uncertain | Standards assume urban deployment; rural deployment timelines are unknown | Technology development uncertainty |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Include escalation clause: WAV fleet percentage increases if accessible AV wait times exceed 1.5x standard AV wait times
2. Define "commercial passenger service" broadly to include any compensated transportation using autonomous vehicles
3. Specify remote human assistance response time (under 60 seconds) and minimum operator training standards
4. Include congressional notification requirement if rulemaking timeline is not met
5. Fund rural AV accessibility research separately with annual progress reports

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Appropriations may fall short of authorization | Phase implementation to match available funding; build bipartisan appropriations coalition |
| Fragmented federal oversight (FTA, NHTSA, DOJ, CMS) | Establish interagency transportation accessibility coordinating committee with OSTP or Domestic Policy Council leadership |
| Technology evolves faster than rulemaking | Build technology-neutral language; require regulatory updates every 3 years |
| Enforcement capacity insufficient | Dedicated DOT accessibility enforcement unit; supplement with state AG and private enforcement |
| Disability community not adequately consulted | Mandate disability representation on all advisory committees; require accessible public comment processes |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Accessible Transit Infrastructure Act: 10-year authorization with 5-year GAO evaluation and potential reauthorization
- Ride-Hailing Accessibility Act: 3-year review of service quality data; adjust service standards if needed
- Accessible AV Act: Regulatory standards updated every 3 years as technology evolves
- Rural Accessible Transportation Act: 10-year authorization with annual performance reporting and 5-year comprehensive evaluation
- NEMT coordination: 3-year pilot evaluation before nationwide implementation
