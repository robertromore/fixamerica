# Transportation Access: History

## Exclusion by Design: Pre-ADA Transportation (Pre-1970)

For most of American history, public transportation was designed without any consideration of disability access. Buses had high steps, subway stations had only stairs, and rail platforms had gaps that made boarding impossible for wheelchair users. People with disabilities who could not walk, climb stairs, or navigate without assistance were effectively barred from public transit -- and by extension from employment, education, and community life that transit made possible for everyone else.

The private automobile, which became dominant in American transportation after World War II, offered independence to some people with disabilities (those who could drive with adaptations), but the car-centric development pattern it created -- sprawling suburbs with minimal sidewalks, separated land uses, and no public transit -- made mobility even more difficult for those who could not drive. The built environment that postwar America constructed was inhospitable to disability by default.

During this era, people with disabilities who could not drive or use inaccessible transit relied on:

- Family members (creating dependency and limiting independence)
- Institutional care (where "transportation" was unnecessary because residents did not leave)
- Charitable organizations (limited, inconsistent, stigmatizing)
- Exclusion (simply not participating in activities that required travel)

## The Disability Rights Movement and Early Transit Advocacy (1970-1990)

### Section 504 and the First Transit Mandates

The Rehabilitation Act of 1973, specifically Section 504, prohibited discrimination against people with disabilities in federally funded programs. Because most public transit agencies received federal funding, Section 504 created the first legal basis for accessible transit. However, the Department of Transportation (DOT) and the Department of Health, Education, and Welfare (HEW) were slow to issue implementing regulations.

In 1977, disability activists staged sit-ins at HEW offices across the country, most notably the 25-day occupation of the San Francisco regional office, to demand implementation of Section 504 regulations. The regulations, issued in 1977, required transit agencies to make their services accessible.

### The APTA v. Lewis Setback

The transit industry fought back. The American Public Transit Association (APTA) sued to block the accessibility regulations, arguing they were too expensive. In *APTA v. Lewis* (1981), a federal appeals court struck down DOT regulations requiring all new buses to be wheelchair-accessible, finding that DOT had not adequately considered costs. This decision set back transit accessibility by years and forced advocates to pursue legislative solutions.

### State and Local Pioneers

Despite federal setbacks, some cities and states moved forward:

| Jurisdiction | Year | Action |
|-------------|------|--------|
| Washington, D.C. (WMATA) | 1976 | Opened Metro system with all stations accessible (elevators, tactile edges) |
| San Francisco (BART) | 1972 | Designed system with accessibility from inception |
| New York City | 1984 | Began adding elevators to subway stations (very slowly) |
| Denver | 1980s | Early adoption of low-floor buses |
| Berkeley, CA | 1970s | Pioneered curb ramp installations citywide |

### Turning Points in the 1980s

| Year | Event | Impact |
|------|-------|--------|
| 1981 | *APTA v. Lewis* | Set back federal transit accessibility requirements |
| 1983 | DOT issues weakened regulations | Required "special efforts" but not full accessibility |
| 1986 | Air Carrier Access Act | Established accessible air travel (separate from ground transit) |
| 1989 | ADA introduced in Congress | Comprehensive disability rights bill including transit provisions |

## The ADA Era: Mandates and Implementation (1990-2010)

### The Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990

The ADA's transportation provisions (Title II, Subtitle B, and Title III) established comprehensive accessibility requirements:

**Fixed-route transit (public agencies)**:
- All new buses must be accessible (wheelchair lifts or ramps)
- All new rail vehicles must be accessible
- Existing rail stations must be made accessible through "key station" plans
- Complementary paratransit must be provided for individuals unable to use fixed-route transit

**Paratransit requirements**:
- Service area must cover at least 3/4 mile on each side of fixed routes
- Service hours must match fixed-route hours
- Fares cannot exceed twice the fixed-route fare
- Response time: next-day service at minimum
- No trip purpose restrictions
- No capacity constraints (agencies cannot deny trips due to capacity)

**Private transportation (Title III)**:
- Private transit companies (intercity buses, shuttles) must provide accessible service
- Demand-responsive systems must be accessible or provide equivalent service

### Implementation Challenges (1990s)

Transit agencies faced enormous implementation costs and logistical challenges:

- **Bus fleet conversion**: Most agencies needed 10-15 years to cycle through their bus fleets, replacing inaccessible vehicles with accessible ones. The ADA required that all new buses be accessible but did not mandate immediate retrofit of existing fleets.
- **Rail station accessibility**: "Key station" plans required accessibility at major stations but allowed timelines extending to 2020 for some stations. Many agencies fell behind even these extended timelines.
- **Paratransit startup**: Many agencies had to create paratransit services from scratch. The mandated service levels were expensive, and agencies struggled to balance paratransit costs against fixed-route operations.

### The Paratransit Paradox

A perverse dynamic emerged in the 1990s and 2000s: as paratransit costs grew, some transit agencies had less incentive to invest in fixed-route accessibility. If making fixed-route transit fully accessible would reduce paratransit eligibility and demand, it would save money in the long run -- but the upfront cost of accessibility improvements competed for the same limited capital budgets. The result was a self-reinforcing cycle: inadequate fixed-route accessibility drove more people to paratransit, increasing costs, which reduced funds available for fixed-route accessibility improvements.

## Modern Challenges and Emerging Technologies (2010-Present)

### The Rise of Ride-Hailing (2010-2020)

Uber launched in 2010 and Lyft in 2012, rapidly transforming urban transportation. For many Americans, ride-hailing provided unprecedented mobility: point-to-point service, available on demand, at competitive prices. For most people with disabilities, however, ride-hailing was initially inaccessible:

- Standard ride-hailing vehicles could not accommodate wheelchairs
- Drivers frequently refused to transport passengers with service animals
- Apps were not fully compatible with screen readers
- No option to request an accessible vehicle in most markets

Uber launched UberWAV (wheelchair-accessible vehicles) in select markets beginning in 2015, but availability remained extremely limited. Disability rights organizations filed lawsuits in multiple jurisdictions, leading to settlements and regulatory requirements in some cities.

### Bipartisan Infrastructure Law (2021)

The IIJA represented the largest federal transit investment in history, including the first dedicated funding stream for legacy rail station accessibility (ASAP). Key provisions:

- $1.75 billion for the All Stations Accessibility Program
- Increased Section 5310 funding for enhanced mobility programs
- Research funding for accessible autonomous vehicles
- Requirements for accessibility in transit capital projects

### Autonomous Vehicle Development

Self-driving vehicles began commercial testing in the 2020s, raising profound questions about disability access:

- **Promise**: Autonomous vehicles could provide unprecedented independence for people who cannot drive due to disability
- **Risk**: Without accessibility standards, autonomous vehicles could repeat the inaccessibility of ride-hailing
- **Regulatory gap**: No federal accessibility standards existed for autonomous vehicles until NHTSA's 2024 rulemaking

### COVID-19 Impact

The pandemic severely disrupted transportation services for people with disabilities:

- Fixed-route transit reduced service dramatically (2020-2021)
- Paratransit services were curtailed or suspended in many areas
- Telehealth reduced some medical transportation needs but highlighted digital access gaps
- Transit workforce shortages persisted post-pandemic, affecting service restoration

## Lessons from History

1. **Legal mandates without adequate funding produce incomplete implementation**: The ADA required accessible transit but did not fully fund it. Three decades later, many systems remain partially accessible.

2. **Universal design from inception is far cheaper than retrofit**: Systems designed for accessibility from the start (BART, WMATA) achieved it far more efficiently than systems that had to retrofit (NYC subway, Chicago CTA).

3. **New technologies replicate old exclusions without intervention**: Ride-hailing repeated the inaccessibility pattern of traditional transit until litigation and regulation forced change. Autonomous vehicles risk the same pattern.

4. **Specialized services are necessary but insufficient**: Paratransit fills a critical gap but cannot substitute for accessible mainstream transit. The quality and cost problems of paratransit demonstrate the limits of separate-but-equal transportation.

5. **Disability activists drive change**: Every major advance in transportation accessibility resulted from organized advocacy, litigation, or direct action by people with disabilities. Policy does not change without pressure.

6. **Cost arguments are often overstated**: Transit agencies consistently overestimated the costs of accessibility compliance. The actual costs, while significant, have been far lower than initial projections and have benefited populations beyond people with disabilities (elderly, parents with strollers, travelers with luggage).
