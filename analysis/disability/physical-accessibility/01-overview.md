# Physical Accessibility: Overview

## Executive Summary

Physical accessibility -- the ability of people with disabilities to navigate, enter, and use the built environment -- remains one of the most visible and persistent challenges in American disability policy. Despite more than three decades of the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) mandating accessible design in new construction and removal of barriers in existing buildings, millions of Americans with mobility, sensory, and other physical disabilities encounter inaccessible buildings, sidewalks, parks, healthcare facilities, and workplaces on a daily basis. An estimated 40 to 60 percent of pre-ADA commercial buildings still contain significant access barriers, and even new construction frequently fails to meet ADA Standards for Accessible Design.

The consequences of physical inaccessibility extend far beyond inconvenience. Inaccessible environments prevent people with disabilities from working, obtaining healthcare, accessing education, participating in civic life, and engaging in routine activities that most Americans take for granted. The economic cost is staggering: people with disabilities represent a market of over 61 million consumers with $1.8 trillion in aggregate disposable income, yet physical barriers systematically exclude them from commercial participation.

Physical accessibility failures reflect a convergence of inadequate enforcement, insufficient funding for retrofits, fragmented regulatory authority, cost-centered design cultures, and persistent societal attitudes that treat accessibility as an afterthought rather than a fundamental design requirement. Addressing these failures requires coordinated action across federal, state, and local governments, the construction and design industries, and civil society.

## Scope

### What This Topic Covers

- **Built environment accessibility**: Commercial buildings, government facilities, healthcare settings, educational institutions, religious facilities, and recreational spaces
- **Pedestrian infrastructure**: Sidewalks, curb cuts, crosswalks, pedestrian signals, and public rights-of-way
- **ADA design standards**: ADA Standards for Accessible Design, Architectural Barriers Act (ABA) standards, and state accessibility codes
- **Barrier removal**: Obligations to remove barriers in existing buildings ("readily achievable" standard)
- **New construction and renovation**: Accessibility requirements for new buildings and substantial renovations
- **Universal design**: Design approaches that create environments usable by the widest range of people
- **Accessibility in housing**: Physical accessibility requirements under Fair Housing Act and ADA (overlap with Housing Accessibility)

## Core Tensions

### Retrofit Cost vs. Civil Rights Obligation

Building owners and small businesses argue that making existing structures accessible can be prohibitively expensive, particularly for older buildings not designed with accessibility in mind. Disability advocates counter that accessibility is a civil right, not an optional amenity, and that the "readily achievable" standard already accounts for cost considerations. The tension is amplified by the absence of federal funding to assist with retrofits.

### Prescriptive Standards vs. Design Flexibility

ADA Standards for Accessible Design set specific measurements (e.g., minimum door width, maximum ramp slope, turning radius for wheelchairs). Architects and developers sometimes argue these standards are rigid and limit design innovation. Accessibility advocates note that prescriptive standards are necessary because vague requirements ("make it accessible") produce inconsistent and often inadequate results. The emerging universal design movement attempts to bridge this gap.

### New Construction vs. Existing Stock

ADA requirements are substantially stronger for new construction than for existing buildings. The "readily achievable" standard for existing buildings allows significant barriers to persist if removal is deemed too costly or difficult. With much of America's commercial building stock predating the ADA, this distinction means that the majority of physical spaces Americans with disabilities encounter are held to a lower standard.

### Historic Preservation vs. Accessibility

Historic buildings often receive reduced accessibility requirements under both the ADA and state historic preservation laws. While preservation of the built heritage is a legitimate interest, the practical effect is that many of America's most significant public buildings -- courthouses, libraries, museums, government offices -- remain inaccessible to people with disabilities.

## Vision of Success

A fully accessible built environment would mean that people with disabilities can enter any building, navigate any sidewalk, use any public facility, and participate in any activity without encountering physical barriers. New construction would be designed from the outset to be usable by the widest range of people, incorporating universal design principles as standard practice rather than an afterthought. Existing buildings would be systematically upgraded through well-funded retrofit programs, with enforcement mechanisms that make noncompliance more costly than compliance. Architecture and construction professionals would be trained in accessible design as a core competency, not an elective specialization. The built environment would reflect the reality that disability is a natural part of human variation and that accessible design benefits everyone -- parents with strollers, delivery workers with carts, older adults with declining mobility, and people recovering from injuries.
