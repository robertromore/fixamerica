# Physical Accessibility: History

## The Era of Exclusion (Pre-1960s)

### Architectural Barriers as Social Policy

For most of American history, the built environment was designed without any consideration for people with disabilities. This was not merely an oversight; it reflected dominant social attitudes that viewed disability as a personal tragedy requiring institutional segregation rather than environmental adaptation.

**Key Features of the Exclusionary Era**

- "Ugly laws" (1860s-1970s): Municipal ordinances in cities including Chicago, Columbus, and San Francisco prohibited people with visible disabilities from appearing in public spaces
- Institutional confinement: By 1960, over 500,000 Americans lived in state institutions, largely removed from community life
- Architectural barriers were so pervasive they were invisible to nondisabled people
- No legal concept of environmental accessibility as a right

### Early Exceptions

- 1940s-1950s: Veterans Administration hospitals and rehabilitation centers incorporated some accessibility features for returning disabled veterans
- 1961: American National Standards Institute (ANSI) published A117.1, the first voluntary accessibility standard for buildings and facilities
- ANSI A117.1 covered ramps, door widths, elevator buttons, restroom design, and parking
- Adoption was entirely voluntary; few jurisdictions or builders used it

## The Federal Response Begins (1968-1980)

### Architectural Barriers Act of 1968

**The First Federal Accessibility Law**

The Architectural Barriers Act (ABA) was the first federal law to require accessible design in buildings. Signed by President Lyndon B. Johnson on August 12, 1968, it applied to buildings designed, built, or altered with federal funds.

| Provision | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Coverage | Buildings designed, built, or altered with federal funds |
| Standards | Required compliance with accessibility standards |
| Enforcement | Initially weak; no designated enforcement agency |
| Scope | Federal buildings, federally funded buildings, post offices, VA facilities |

**Limitations**

- Applied only to federal and federally funded buildings
- No enforcement mechanism until 1973
- Did not apply to private buildings or state/local government buildings (unless federally funded)
- Standards were initially based on voluntary ANSI A117.1

### Section 502 of the Rehabilitation Act (1973)

Created the Architectural and Transportation Barriers Compliance Board (later renamed the U.S. Access Board) to enforce the ABA and develop accessibility standards.

**U.S. Access Board Functions**

- Develop and maintain accessibility guidelines
- Enforce the Architectural Barriers Act
- Provide technical assistance on accessibility
- Conduct research on accessible design

### State Building Codes (1960s-1980s)

Several states adopted their own accessibility requirements during this period:

| State | Year | Scope |
|-------|------|-------|
| North Carolina | 1973 | First state building code with accessibility requirements |
| Massachusetts | 1970 | Architectural Access Board established |
| California | 1970s | Title 24 accessibility standards |
| Texas | 1969 | Texas Accessibility Standards |
| Illinois | 1978 | Environmental Barriers Act |

These state codes varied widely in scope, technical requirements, and enforcement, creating a patchwork of standards that complicated design for national builders.

## The ADA Revolution (1990-2000)

### Passage of the Americans with Disabilities Act

The ADA, signed by President George H.W. Bush on July 26, 1990, fundamentally changed the legal framework for physical accessibility in the United States.

**Physical Accessibility Provisions**

| ADA Title | Physical Accessibility Requirement | Effective Date |
|-----------|-----------------------------------|----------------|
| Title II | State and local government programs and facilities must be accessible | January 26, 1992 |
| Title III | New commercial construction must be accessible; existing buildings must remove barriers where "readily achievable" | January 26, 1992 (new construction: January 26, 1993) |
| Title I | Reasonable accommodation in workplace, including physical modifications | July 26, 1992 |

**ADA Standards for Accessible Design (1991)**

The DOJ published the original ADA Standards for Accessible Design, based on guidelines developed by the U.S. Access Board (known as the ADA Accessibility Guidelines, or ADAAG). These standards translated broad accessibility mandates into specific, measurable design requirements.

### "Readily Achievable" Standard

For existing buildings, Title III required removal of barriers where doing so was "readily achievable" -- defined as "easily accomplishable and able to be carried out without much difficulty or expense." This standard was intentionally lower than the full accessibility requirements for new construction.

**Factors in Determining "Readily Achievable"**

1. Nature and cost of the action
2. Overall financial resources of the facility
3. Overall financial resources of the parent entity
4. Type of operation of the entity

**Practical Effect**

- Many building owners interpreted the standard as requiring minimal or no action
- DOJ provided examples of readily achievable modifications (installing ramps, widening doors, rearranging furniture) but could not mandate specific actions for each building
- Enforcement relied on complaints rather than systematic audits
- The standard created a permanent two-tier system: full accessibility for new construction, partial accessibility for existing buildings

### Early Implementation Challenges

**New Construction**

- Architects and builders had limited training in accessibility standards
- Building inspectors often did not check for ADA compliance
- Developers treated accessibility as a cost to be minimized
- Design errors were common and often not discovered until occupancy

**Existing Buildings**

- Most businesses conducted no barrier assessment
- "Readily achievable" was interpreted to require minimal investment
- DOJ enforcement focused on large chains and public entities, not individual buildings
- Small businesses often claimed exemption or ignorance

## Standards Evolution (2000-2015)

### Updated ADA Standards (2010)

The DOJ published updated ADA Standards for Accessible Design in 2010, effective March 15, 2012. The 2010 Standards were based on the Access Board's updated ADAAG (2004) and incorporated significant improvements.

**Key Changes from 1991 to 2010 Standards**

| Feature | 1991 Standard | 2010 Standard |
|---------|--------------|---------------|
| Accessible parking van spaces | 96-inch wide space, 96-inch aisle | 132-inch wide (space + aisle combined) |
| Elevator door reopening device | 20-second minimum | 20-second minimum; new communication features |
| Assembly areas | Dispersed wheelchair locations | Lines of sight over standing spectators |
| Recreational facilities | Not covered | Swimming pools, play areas, golf courses, sports facilities |
| Judicial facilities | General standards | Specific courtroom accessibility requirements |
| Detention facilities | General standards | Specific cell accessibility requirements |
| Residential dwelling units | Limited coverage | Expanded requirements for transient lodging and social service facilities |

### Safe Harbor Provision

The 2010 Standards included a "safe harbor" provision: elements in existing buildings that complied with the 1991 Standards were not required to be upgraded to the 2010 Standards unless the element was altered. This meant many older accessible features could remain at the lower 1991 standard indefinitely, even as knowledge and technology advanced.

### International Building Code Integration

The International Code Council (ICC) developed the ICC/ANSI A117.1 standard, which was incorporated into the International Building Code (IBC) adopted by most states. This created a parallel set of accessibility requirements enforced through the building permit and inspection process, supplementing ADA enforcement.

## Recent Developments (2015-Present)

### Public Rights-of-Way

Accessibility of sidewalks, crosswalks, and other pedestrian infrastructure has been a major focus of recent policy development.

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 2011 | Access Board publishes proposed PROWAG (Public Right-of-Way Accessibility Guidelines) |
| 2013 | DOJ and DOT issue joint technical assistance on ADA and sidewalks |
| 2017 | Access Board revises proposed PROWAG |
| 2023 | Access Board publishes updated proposed PROWAG for public comment |
| 2024-2025 | Final PROWAG pending; status uncertain under new administration |

### DOJ Enforcement Actions

DOJ has continued to pursue physical accessibility cases through settlement agreements and consent decrees:

| Year | Target | Issue | Outcome |
|------|--------|-------|---------|
| 2018 | Various hotel chains | Accessible rooms, pools, entrances | Multi-million dollar settlements |
| 2019 | Medical provider systems | Exam table and equipment accessibility | Policy changes, equipment purchases |
| 2021 | Kroger/grocery chains | Self-checkout accessibility | Design modifications |
| 2022 | State court systems | Courthouse accessibility | Comprehensive renovation plans |
| 2023 | National park concessionaires | Food service and lodging accessibility | Facility upgrades |
| 2024 | Hospital systems | Medical diagnostic equipment | Equipment replacement schedules |

### Universal Design Movement

The universal design movement has gained significant traction, promoting design that works for the widest range of users without requiring adaptation or specialized design.

**Seven Principles of Universal Design** (Center for Universal Design, NC State University)

1. Equitable use
2. Flexibility in use
3. Simple and intuitive use
4. Perceptible information
5. Tolerance for error
6. Low physical effort
7. Size and space for approach and use

Universal design has been adopted most extensively in:

- Residential construction (visitability ordinances)
- Product design (OXO Good Grips, Apple products)
- Urban planning (complete streets policies)
- Some commercial construction (particularly hospitality industry)

### Visitability Ordinances

Several jurisdictions have adopted "visitability" requirements for new residential construction, requiring basic accessibility features in all new homes:

| Jurisdiction | Year | Requirements |
|-------------|------|--------------|
| Atlanta, GA | 1992 | First visitability ordinance; zero-step entrance, accessible bathroom on main floor |
| Bolingbrook, IL | 2003 | Visitability in all new single-family homes |
| Pima County, AZ | 2002 | At least one accessible entrance and bathroom |
| State of Texas | 2005 | Visitability in state-funded housing |
| Vermont | 2015 | Visitability in new state-funded residential construction |

## Key Turning Points

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|--------------|
| 1961 | ANSI A117.1 published | First accessibility standard (voluntary) |
| 1968 | Architectural Barriers Act | First federal accessibility law (federal buildings) |
| 1973 | Rehabilitation Act Section 502 | Access Board created for enforcement |
| 1990 | ADA signed | Comprehensive accessibility mandates for public and commercial buildings |
| 1991 | ADA Standards for Accessible Design published | First enforceable national accessibility specifications |
| 1999 | *Olmstead v. L.C.* | Right to community integration (indirectly supports physical accessibility) |
| 2010 | Updated ADA Standards | Expanded coverage to recreation, detention, judicial facilities |
| 2023 | PROWAG proposed | First comprehensive federal pedestrian infrastructure accessibility standards |

## Lessons from History

1. **Voluntary standards do not produce accessibility**: From 1961 to 1990, voluntary accessibility guidelines produced minimal compliance; legal mandates were necessary
2. **Legal mandates require enforcement**: Even after the ADA, compliance depended on enforcement capacity, which has always been inadequate
3. **Standards must evolve**: The shift from 1991 to 2010 Standards shows that accessibility knowledge advances and standards must keep pace
4. **New construction is the leverage point**: It is far more cost-effective and technically simpler to build accessibly from the start than to retrofit later
5. **The existing building stock is the enduring challenge**: Most of America's built environment predates the ADA, and the "readily achievable" standard has proven insufficient to drive comprehensive retrofits
6. **Universal design offers a path forward**: Moving beyond minimum compliance toward design that inherently works for everyone has the potential to transform accessibility from a regulatory burden into a design value
