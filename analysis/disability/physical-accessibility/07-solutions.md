# Physical Accessibility: Solutions

## Overview

Addressing the persistent physical accessibility gaps in the American built environment requires a multi-pronged strategy combining stronger enforcement, better incentives, professional education reform, design culture change, and systematic investment in retrofitting existing buildings. The solutions below are organized by approach, with attention to feasibility, cost, and political viability.

## Enforcement Reform

### 1. Strengthen DOJ Enforcement Capacity

**Description**: Significantly increase funding and staffing for the DOJ Disability Rights Section to enable proactive compliance reviews of physical accessibility.

**Key Components**

- Triple the Disability Rights Section's budget allocation within the Civil Rights Division
- Establish a dedicated Physical Accessibility Enforcement Unit
- Hire accessibility specialists (architects, engineers) as DOJ staff to conduct compliance reviews
- Set targets for proactive compliance reviews (e.g., 500 facility reviews per year minimum)
- Publish annual enforcement reports with jurisdiction-level compliance data

**Estimated Cost**: $50-75 million annually (incremental federal spending)

**Expected Impact**: High; DOJ enforcement actions are the most effective driver of systemic compliance

**Feasibility**: Moderate; requires congressional appropriation and executive priority

### 2. Expand ADA Title III Remedies

**Description**: Amend the ADA to allow compensatory damages for Title III violations, making private enforcement a more effective deterrent.

**Key Components**

- Allow compensatory damages for individuals who encounter accessibility barriers
- Maintain injunctive relief as the primary remedy
- Include reasonable limits on damages to address business concerns (e.g., caps scaled to business size)
- Preserve attorney fee provisions for prevailing plaintiffs

**Expected Impact**: Very high; the absence of damages is the single greatest weakness in ADA physical accessibility enforcement

**Feasibility**: Low in current political environment; strong business opposition

### 3. Mandatory Accessibility Certification

**Description**: Require commercial buildings and government facilities to obtain periodic accessibility certifications, similar to fire safety inspections.

**Key Components**

- Establish a tiered certification system: certified accessible, partially accessible, not certified
- Require certification renewal every 5 years for commercial buildings
- Make certification a condition of commercial insurance or business licensing
- Create a publicly searchable database of building accessibility status
- Provide technical assistance and compliance timelines for buildings that fail certification

**Expected Impact**: High; systematic certification would identify and remediate barriers proactively

**Feasibility**: Moderate; could be implemented at state or local level

## Financial Incentives and Funding

### 4. Expand Federal Tax Incentives

**Description**: Significantly increase the federal tax credit and deduction for accessibility improvements.

**Key Components**

- Increase the Disabled Access Credit (IRC Section 44) from $5,000 to $25,000 annually
- Expand eligibility beyond businesses with under $1 million in revenue
- Increase the Barrier Removal Deduction (IRC Section 190) from $15,000 to $50,000 annually
- Create an additional credit for achieving full ADA compliance (not just individual improvements)
- Establish a carryforward provision for unused credits

**Estimated Cost**: $2-5 billion annually in foregone revenue

**Expected Impact**: Moderate to high; removes the primary stated barrier to compliance (cost)

**Feasibility**: High; bipartisan support for tax incentives

### 5. Establish a National Accessibility Retrofit Fund

**Description**: Create a federal grant program to fund accessibility retrofits for small businesses, nonprofits, and local government facilities.

**Key Components**

- $5 billion over 10 years in competitive grants
- Priority for small businesses (under 50 employees), nonprofits, and rural communities
- Maximum grant of $100,000 per project
- Require applicants to complete comprehensive accessibility assessment
- Grants conditional on achieving full compliance, not just partial improvements

**Expected Impact**: High for recipient communities; addresses the cost barrier directly

**Feasibility**: Moderate; requires congressional appropriation

### 6. Accessibility Impact Bonds

**Description**: Develop social impact bond mechanisms where private investors fund accessibility retrofits, repaid through government savings from reduced ADA enforcement costs and increased economic participation by people with disabilities.

**Key Components**

- Pilot in 5-10 metropolitan areas
- Measure outcomes: barrier removal, employment access, healthcare access, reduced enforcement costs
- Repayment triggered by measurable increases in accessibility and economic participation
- Partner with Community Development Financial Institutions (CDFIs) for implementation

**Expected Impact**: Moderate; innovative financing mechanism that reduces government upfront costs

**Feasibility**: Moderate; requires initial government commitment and outcome measurement infrastructure

## Standards and Design Reform

### 7. Adopt Universal Design Building Codes

**Description**: Amend model building codes to incorporate universal design principles that go beyond minimum ADA compliance.

**Key Components**

- Work with ICC to develop universal design provisions for the International Building Code
- Require at least one zero-step entrance in all new commercial construction
- Require accessible restrooms on every floor (not just minimum required by ADA)
- Require wider corridors and doorways than current ADA minimums
- Include comfort features: automatic doors, lever handles, adjustable-height counters

**Expected Impact**: High for new construction; would ensure that new buildings are genuinely usable, not merely minimally compliant

**Feasibility**: Moderate; building code adoption occurs at state and local level

### 8. Require Accessibility Plans for Existing Buildings

**Description**: Mandate that commercial building owners conduct accessibility assessments and develop compliance plans on a defined timeline.

**Key Components**

- Require all commercial buildings to complete an accessibility assessment within 5 years
- Require development of a prioritized barrier removal plan
- Set 10-year compliance deadlines for critical barriers (entrances, restrooms, primary circulation)
- Set 15-year compliance deadlines for all remaining barriers
- Provide technical assistance and funding support for compliance

**Expected Impact**: Very high; would systematically address the existing building stock problem

**Feasibility**: Low to moderate; significant business opposition expected

### 9. Finalize Public Rights-of-Way Standards (PROWAG)

**Description**: Complete and adopt federal accessibility standards for sidewalks, crosswalks, curb ramps, and other pedestrian infrastructure.

**Key Components**

- Finalize PROWAG through the Access Board rulemaking process
- Adopt as enforceable standards under ADA Title II and DOT regulations
- Establish compliance timelines for existing infrastructure (e.g., 20-year plan)
- Link federal transportation funding to PROWAG compliance
- Provide technical assistance for state and local governments

**Expected Impact**: High; would address one of the most pervasive categories of physical accessibility barriers

**Feasibility**: Moderate to high; PROWAG has been in development for over a decade and has broad support

## Professional Education

### 10. Mandate Accessibility Training in Professional Licensing

**Description**: Require architects, engineers, contractors, and building inspectors to complete accessibility-specific training as a condition of professional licensing.

**Key Components**

- Require dedicated accessibility coursework in accredited architecture programs (minimum 3 credit hours)
- Include accessibility in professional licensing exams (ARE for architects; PE for engineers)
- Require continuing education in accessibility for license renewal (minimum 4 hours per cycle)
- Develop standardized accessibility training for building inspectors
- Create contractor certification programs for accessibility construction

**Expected Impact**: Moderate to high; would address the knowledge gap that causes new construction errors

**Feasibility**: High; can be implemented through professional licensing boards at state level

### 11. Accessibility Inspection Integration

**Description**: Integrate comprehensive accessibility inspection into the building permit and certificate of occupancy process.

**Key Components**

- Train all building inspectors in ADA Standards for Accessible Design
- Add accessibility checkpoints to standard building inspection checklists
- Require accessibility sign-off for certificate of occupancy
- Create a dedicated accessibility inspector role in larger jurisdictions
- Establish a complaint mechanism for post-occupancy accessibility violations

**Expected Impact**: High; would catch construction errors before buildings are occupied

**Feasibility**: High; can be implemented through local building departments

## Technology Solutions

### 12. Accessibility Mapping and Information

**Description**: Develop comprehensive, publicly available accessibility information for buildings and public spaces.

**Key Components**

- Federal funding for a national accessibility database
- Require commercial buildings to report basic accessibility features (entrance type, restroom accessibility, elevator availability)
- Partner with mapping platforms (Google Maps, Apple Maps) to display accessibility information
- Support community-based accessibility mapping initiatives (e.g., Wheelmap, AccessNow)
- Develop standardized accessibility rating system

**Expected Impact**: Moderate; improves trip planning for people with disabilities and creates market pressure for accessibility

**Feasibility**: High; technology infrastructure exists; requires data collection

### 13. Remote Monitoring and Smart Building Technology

**Description**: Use technology to monitor and maintain accessibility features in real time.

**Key Components**

- Sensors to detect when elevators, automatic doors, or other accessibility features malfunction
- Automated alerts to building management and public reporting systems
- Accessible wayfinding technology (indoor navigation for people with visual impairments)
- Digital compliance tracking for building managers

**Expected Impact**: Moderate; addresses the maintenance problem (accessible features installed but broken)

**Feasibility**: High; technology is available and costs are declining
