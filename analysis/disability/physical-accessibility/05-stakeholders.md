# Physical Accessibility: Stakeholders

## Overview

Physical accessibility policy involves a broad range of stakeholders with varying levels of power, interest, and alignment. The most directly affected populations -- people with disabilities -- are also among the least powerful in policy debates, while those with the greatest power over built environment decisions -- developers, building owners, and local governments -- often have economic incentives that discourage accessibility investment.

## Directly Affected Populations

### People with Mobility Disabilities

- **Population**: 13.7 million U.S. adults with serious mobility difficulty (CDC, 2023)
- **Wheelchair users**: 3.6 million Americans (Census Bureau, 2021)
- **Power level**: Low to moderate; organized advocacy is effective but individual voices are marginalized
- **Primary concerns**: Building entrances, restrooms, interior circulation, parking, sidewalks, public transit stops
- **Key organizations**: United Spinal Association, Paralyzed Veterans of America, National Spinal Cord Injury Association

### People with Visual Impairments

- **Population**: 7.6 million Americans with significant vision loss (National Federation of the Blind, 2023)
- **Power level**: Moderate; strong advocacy organizations
- **Primary concerns**: Detectable warning surfaces, accessible pedestrian signals, tactile signage, protruding object hazards, consistent wayfinding
- **Key organizations**: National Federation of the Blind (NFB), American Council of the Blind (ACB), American Foundation for the Blind

### People with Hearing Impairments

- **Population**: 48 million Americans with some degree of hearing loss (Hearing Loss Association of America, 2023)
- **Power level**: Moderate; established advocacy infrastructure
- **Primary concerns**: Visual alarms, visual notification systems, induction loops, captioning in public venues, acoustics
- **Key organizations**: National Association of the Deaf (NAD), Hearing Loss Association of America (HLAA)

### Older Adults

- **Population**: 56 million Americans aged 65+ (Census Bureau, 2023); mobility limitations increase sharply with age
- **Power level**: High as voters; moderate in policy influence
- **Primary concerns**: Stairs, uneven surfaces, heavy doors, inadequate seating, bathroom grab bars, lighting
- **Key organizations**: AARP, National Council on Aging, Eldercare Locator

### People with Temporary Disabilities

- **Population**: Millions of Americans annually experience temporary mobility limitations from injuries, surgeries, pregnancy, or illness
- **Power level**: Low (transient constituency; rarely self-identify as stakeholders)
- **Primary concerns**: Same physical barriers as people with permanent disabilities, but experienced temporarily
- **Significance**: Demonstrates that accessibility benefits a far larger population than commonly recognized

## Institutional Stakeholders

### Federal Government

**Department of Justice (DOJ)**

- **Role**: Primary ADA enforcement agency for Titles II and III
- **Power level**: High in theory; limited by resources in practice
- **Interests**: Civil rights enforcement, but balanced against other priorities within Civil Rights Division
- **Key tensions**: Resource allocation between disability rights and other civil rights areas

**U.S. Access Board**

- **Role**: Develops accessibility standards; enforces Architectural Barriers Act
- **Power level**: Moderate; advisory/standard-setting rather than enforcement-oriented
- **Interests**: Technical excellence in accessibility standards
- **Limitations**: Small agency (approximately 30 staff); limited enforcement authority

**General Services Administration (GSA)**

- **Role**: Manages federal building stock; implements ABA standards
- **Power level**: High within federal building portfolio
- **Interests**: Balancing accessibility, cost, historic preservation, and operational needs

**Department of Transportation (DOT)**

- **Role**: Pedestrian infrastructure accessibility; transit accessibility
- **Power level**: High for federally funded infrastructure
- **Interests**: Safe, accessible transportation infrastructure

### State and Local Governments

- **Role**: Building code adoption and enforcement, sidewalk maintenance, government facility accessibility
- **Power level**: High within jurisdictions; primary regulators of construction
- **Interests**: Economic development, cost management, legal compliance
- **Key tensions**: Balancing development incentives against accessibility requirements; limited budgets for infrastructure
- **Variation**: Enormous differences between jurisdictions in commitment, capacity, and enforcement

### Courts

- **Role**: Interpret ADA requirements, adjudicate private enforcement actions
- **Power level**: High; judicial interpretation shapes the practical meaning of accessibility requirements
- **Key decisions**: Standing requirements, "readily achievable" interpretations, mootness doctrine
- **Trend**: Federal courts have become somewhat more receptive to accessibility claims since the ADA Amendments Act, but doctrinal limitations persist

## Industry Stakeholders

### Commercial Real Estate

- **Organizations**: BOMA International (Building Owners and Managers Association), National Association of Realtors, International Council of Shopping Centers
- **Power level**: Very high; significant lobbying presence
- **Position**: Generally supports accessibility in principle but opposes mandates they consider costly or inflexible
- **Key concerns**: Retrofit costs for existing buildings, liability exposure, design constraints
- **Influence**: Active lobbying on ADA enforcement legislation; support for pre-suit notice requirements

### Construction Industry

- **Organizations**: Associated General Contractors of America, National Association of Home Builders
- **Power level**: High; major political donors and lobbyists
- **Position**: Follows building codes as adopted; generally does not advocate for stronger accessibility requirements
- **Key concerns**: Construction costs, code complexity, liability for design errors
- **Role**: Quality of construction determines whether accessible designs are properly executed

### Architecture and Design

- **Organizations**: American Institute of Architects (AIA), American Society of Interior Designers, International Interior Design Association
- **Power level**: Moderate; professional standards influence but do not control practice
- **Position**: Increasingly supportive of universal design; professional liability creates incentive for compliance
- **Key concerns**: Design flexibility, aesthetic integration of accessibility features, professional liability
- **Trend**: Growing interest in universal design as a professional value, not just a code requirement

### Hospitality Industry

- **Organizations**: American Hotel and Lodging Association, National Restaurant Association
- **Power level**: High; major employer and political donor
- **Position**: Mixed; major chains generally comply, but oppose expansion of requirements
- **Key concerns**: Accessible room requirements, pool lifts (strongly opposed), restaurant seating and service counter requirements

### Small Business

- **Organizations**: National Federation of Independent Business (NFIB), U.S. Chamber of Commerce (small business division)
- **Power level**: Moderate; sympathetic political narrative
- **Position**: Frequently claims that accessibility requirements are burdensome and costly
- **Key concerns**: Retrofit costs for older buildings, ADA lawsuit exposure, lack of technical knowledge
- **Reality**: Average cost of small business accessibility improvements is often modest ($2,000-$15,000), but perception of cost exceeds reality

## Power Analysis

### High Power, Low Accessibility Interest

- **Commercial real estate industry**: Primarily concerned with cost minimization; lobbies against stronger mandates
- **Small business lobby**: Frames accessibility as regulatory burden; supports pre-suit notice requirements
- **Many local governments**: Prioritize economic development over accessibility enforcement

### High Power, Mixed Accessibility Interest

- **Federal government (DOJ, Access Board)**: Statutory mandate to enforce but chronically under-resourced
- **Architecture profession**: Professional standards support accessibility but market pressures push toward cost minimization
- **Major corporations**: Generally comply but do not advocate for stronger requirements

### Low Power, High Accessibility Interest

- **People with disabilities**: Most directly affected; limited political power relative to other constituencies
- **Disability rights organizations**: Effective advocates but operate with limited resources
- **Independent living centers**: Community-based; high commitment but limited reach

### Emerging Stakeholders

- **Technology companies**: Developing accessibility mapping, indoor navigation, and smart building technologies
- **Aging-in-place advocates**: Growing alignment between disability and aging communities on physical accessibility
- **Climate resilience planners**: Recognizing need to incorporate accessibility into climate adaptation infrastructure
- **Insurance industry**: Beginning to recognize accessibility as a risk management factor
