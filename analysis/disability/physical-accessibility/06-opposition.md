# Physical Accessibility: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to physical accessibility requirements comes from several directions, ranging from principled objections about government regulation and property rights to pragmatic concerns about cost and implementation. Understanding these opposition arguments is essential for crafting reforms that can overcome resistance while maintaining the civil rights foundation of accessibility law.

## Primary Opposition Groups

### Small Business Community

**Organizations**: National Federation of Independent Business (NFIB), local chambers of commerce, small business alliances

**Arguments Against Current Accessibility Requirements**

- **Cost burden**: Small businesses argue that accessibility retrofits impose disproportionate costs on businesses with thin margins. NFIB cites average retrofit costs of $20,000-$50,000 for comprehensive compliance, which can represent a significant percentage of annual revenue for small businesses.
- **Lawsuit abuse**: Small businesses are frequent targets of ADA Title III lawsuits, which they characterize as predatory "drive-by" litigation. They argue that a small number of plaintiffs and law firms file hundreds of lawsuits targeting minor technical violations for settlement fees rather than genuine access.
- **Complexity**: Small business owners often lack the technical knowledge to understand ADA requirements and cannot afford accessibility consultants. They argue the standards are too complex for non-specialists.
- **Unfair timeline**: Businesses that have operated for decades without complaints argue it is unfair to be suddenly sued for conditions that have existed since before the ADA.

**Policy Positions**

- Support pre-suit notice and cure requirements (ADA Education and Reform Act)
- Support increased federal tax incentives for accessibility improvements
- Oppose expansion of ADA damages or penalties
- Support technical assistance programs over enforcement

**Counter-Arguments**

- The ADA has been in effect since 1992; businesses have had over three decades to comply
- Average accessibility improvements are far less expensive than commonly claimed (Job Accommodation Network data shows median cost under $500 for many accommodations)
- Private litigation fills the gap left by inadequate federal enforcement
- The "readily achievable" standard already accounts for cost and business size

### Commercial Real Estate Industry

**Organizations**: BOMA International, National Association of Realtors, International Council of Shopping Centers, National Apartment Association

**Arguments**

- **Property rights**: Building owners argue that government-mandated modifications to private property exceed regulatory authority, particularly for existing structures
- **Return on investment**: Accessibility improvements do not generate measurable revenue increases and may reduce usable space
- **Historic properties**: Owners of older and historic buildings argue that accessibility modifications can damage the character and value of significant structures
- **Tenant vs. landlord obligations**: Disputes over whether landlords or tenants bear responsibility for accessibility improvements in leased spaces

**Policy Positions**

- Support safe harbor provisions that protect buildings meeting older standards
- Oppose mandatory accessibility audits for existing buildings
- Support limits on retroactive application of new standards
- Oppose expansion of the ABA to private buildings receiving federal tax benefits

### Construction and Developer Lobby

**Arguments**

- **Construction cost increases**: Developers argue that accessibility requirements add 1-5% to construction costs, though studies show the actual cost of incorporating accessibility into new design is typically 0.5-1% (NCD, 2023)
- **Design constraints**: Accessibility requirements limit design flexibility and architectural creativity
- **Code complexity**: Multiple overlapping accessibility codes (ADA Standards, ICC/ANSI A117.1, state codes, Fair Housing requirements) create confusion and increase compliance risk
- **Enforcement uncertainty**: Lack of a clear "safe harbor" determination during the design phase creates liability risk even for good-faith compliance efforts

### Hospitality Industry

**Organizations**: American Hotel and Lodging Association, National Restaurant Association

**Specific Objections**

- **Pool lift mandate**: Hotels strongly opposed the 2010 Standards requirement for accessible means of entry to swimming pools, arguing the cost was excessive and the liability risk for pool lift injuries was unacceptable
- **Accessible room ratios**: Hotels argue that the required percentage of accessible rooms exceeds actual demand, resulting in underutilized inventory
- **Restaurant seating**: Restaurants argue that accessible table spacing requirements reduce seating capacity and revenue
- **Reservation systems**: ADA requirements to allow specific accessible room requests create operational complexity for hotels

### Historic Preservation Community

**Organizations**: National Trust for Historic Preservation, State Historic Preservation Offices, local historic districts

**Arguments**

- Historic buildings represent irreplaceable cultural resources that may be damaged by accessibility modifications
- The character-defining features of historic buildings (stairs, narrow doorways, original materials) are precisely the elements that create accessibility barriers
- Alternative methods of access (program relocation, virtual tours) should satisfy ADA requirements without physical modification
- The ADA already provides exceptions for historic properties, and these exceptions should be maintained or expanded

**Counter-Arguments**

- Accessibility and historic preservation are not inherently incompatible; creative design solutions exist
- Excluding people with disabilities from historic sites perpetuates their historical invisibility
- The ADA's historic property exception requires "maximum feasible" access, not complete exemption
- Many successful historic preservation projects have incorporated accessibility without compromising historic character

## Ideological Opposition

### Free Market and Libertarian Arguments

- The market, not government, should determine building accessibility; businesses that exclude disabled customers will lose market share
- Private property rights should include the right to design and maintain buildings as the owner sees fit
- ADA accessibility mandates constitute a regulatory taking by requiring property owners to spend money on modifications that serve public purposes
- Voluntary accessibility standards and consumer information (reviews, ratings) would be more efficient than legal mandates

**Counter-Arguments**

- The market failed to produce accessibility for over a century before the ADA; voluntary standards produced minimal compliance
- Civil rights cannot depend on market incentives; the right to access public accommodations is fundamental
- The Supreme Court has consistently held that ADA accessibility requirements are a valid exercise of congressional power, not a taking

### Fiscal Conservative Arguments

- Federal enforcement of accessibility requirements is expensive and should be reduced
- Tax incentives for accessibility represent lost revenue
- Federal accessibility standards impose unfunded mandates on state and local governments
- Private litigation provides a more cost-effective enforcement mechanism than federal bureaucracy

## Legislative Opposition

### ADA Education and Reform Act

The most significant legislative threat to physical accessibility enforcement. This bill, which passed the House in 2018 (H.R. 620, 225-192) and has been reintroduced in subsequent Congresses:

**Provisions**

- Requires plaintiffs to provide written notice of ADA barriers before filing suit
- Gives defendants 60 days to acknowledge the barrier and 120 days to make "substantial progress" toward removal
- Allows defendants to raise "good faith" compliance efforts as a defense

**Supporters**: U.S. Chamber of Commerce, NFIB, American Hotel and Lodging Association, many Republican members of Congress

**Opponents**: Disability Rights Education and Defense Fund, National Disability Rights Network, American Association of People with Disabilities, Leadership Conference on Civil and Human Rights, most Democratic members of Congress

**Analysis**

- Proponents frame it as reasonable reform to prevent litigation abuse
- Opponents argue it guts enforcement by allowing indefinite delay and creating procedural barriers that most people with disabilities cannot navigate
- No comparable notice requirement exists for any other civil rights law
- The 120-day cure period effectively allows buildings to remain inaccessible until someone complains, then grants additional months of noncompliance

## Opposition Effectiveness

### Successful Opposition Tactics

| Tactic | Example | Effect |
|--------|---------|--------|
| "Serial litigant" narrative | Media coverage of high-volume ADA plaintiffs | Shifts public sympathy from disability rights to business protection |
| Small business framing | Highlighting costs for small, sympathetic businesses | Obscures that most ADA violations are by larger entities |
| Pre-suit notice legislation | ADA Education and Reform Act | Passed House once; continues to be reintroduced |
| State-level restrictions | Arizona, Florida, Texas pre-suit notice laws | Reduces private enforcement in those states |
| Delay and deferral | Opposing deadlines for existing building compliance | Maintains the "readily achievable" status quo indefinitely |
| Cost exaggeration | Citing high-end retrofit costs as typical | Creates public perception that accessibility is prohibitively expensive |

### Where Opposition Has Been Less Effective

- **New construction standards**: Once enacted, accessibility requirements for new buildings have been broadly accepted
- **Federal buildings**: Architectural Barriers Act compliance is generally high
- **Major corporations**: Large companies have largely accepted accessibility as a business practice
- **Public opinion**: Polls consistently show strong support for ADA and accessibility rights (82% support for ADA, National Organization on Disability, 2024)
