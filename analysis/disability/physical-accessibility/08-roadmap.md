# Physical Accessibility: Implementation Roadmap

## Overview

This roadmap outlines a phased strategy for achieving meaningful improvement in physical accessibility across the American built environment. The phases are designed to build on achievable early victories, expand capacity over time, and ultimately achieve systemic transformation. Total implementation spans 15 years, with measurable milestones at each phase.

## Phase 1: Foundation Building (Years 1-3)

### Objectives

- Strengthen enforcement capacity
- Finalize pending standards
- Expand financial incentives
- Build professional training infrastructure

### Key Actions

| Action | Lead Entity | Timeline | Milestone |
|--------|-------------|----------|-----------|
| Increase DOJ Disability Rights Section budget by 50% | Congress / DOJ | Year 1 | Budget enacted |
| Finalize PROWAG as enforceable standard | U.S. Access Board / DOJ | Year 1-2 | Final rule published |
| Expand Disabled Access Credit to $25,000 | Congress / IRS | Year 1 | Tax code amendment enacted |
| Expand Barrier Removal Deduction to $50,000 | Congress / IRS | Year 1 | Tax code amendment enacted |
| Develop standardized accessibility inspector training | Access Board / ICC | Year 1-2 | Curriculum published |
| Launch pilot accessibility certification program in 5 cities | DOJ / local governments | Year 2-3 | Pilot cities selected and launched |
| Establish accessibility continuing education requirements | State licensing boards | Year 2-3 | At least 10 states adopt requirements |
| Create national accessibility information database (pilot) | Access Board / GSA | Year 2-3 | Database launched with 5 major cities |
| Issue DOJ guidance on "readily achievable" barrier removal priorities | DOJ | Year 1 | Guidance published |

### Success Metrics (End of Year 3)

- DOJ physical accessibility enforcement actions increase by 100%
- PROWAG adopted as enforceable federal standard
- 10,000+ businesses use expanded tax credits for accessibility improvements
- 5 pilot cities operating accessibility certification programs
- 10+ states require accessibility continuing education for architects

## Phase 2: Accelerated Compliance (Years 4-7)

### Objectives

- Scale enforcement and certification programs
- Launch National Accessibility Retrofit Fund
- Mandate accessibility assessments for existing buildings
- Integrate accessibility inspection into building codes

### Key Actions

| Action | Lead Entity | Timeline | Milestone |
|--------|-------------|----------|-----------|
| Establish National Accessibility Retrofit Fund ($5B/10 years) | Congress / HUD | Year 4 | Fund authorized and first grants awarded |
| Expand accessibility certification to 50 cities | DOJ / local governments | Year 4-6 | Certification operational in 50 cities |
| Require accessibility assessments for commercial buildings (phased by size) | Congress / DOJ | Year 4-5 | Legislation enacted |
| Integrate accessibility checkpoints into IBC inspection process | ICC / state code agencies | Year 4-6 | Model code updated; 20+ states adopt |
| Link federal transportation funding to PROWAG compliance | DOT / Congress | Year 5 | Compliance conditions in place |
| Publish national accessibility survey (baseline data) | Access Board / Census Bureau | Year 5 | Baseline data published |
| Develop universal design provisions for ICC model codes | ICC / Access Board | Year 5-7 | Provisions drafted and approved |
| Launch accessibility impact bond pilot programs | HUD / Treasury | Year 6-7 | 5 pilot programs operational |
| Expand national accessibility database to all states | Access Board / GSA | Year 6-7 | Database operational nationwide |

### Success Metrics (End of Year 7)

- 50% of commercial buildings in pilot cities have completed accessibility assessments
- 25% of pre-ADA buildings have completed at least one barrier removal project
- 20+ states have integrated accessibility inspection into building permit process
- National Accessibility Retrofit Fund has awarded $2 billion in grants
- Pedestrian infrastructure compliance with PROWAG increases by 30% in urban areas

## Phase 3: Systemic Transformation (Years 8-15)

### Objectives

- Achieve comprehensive compliance in new construction
- Complete barrier removal in critical-access existing buildings
- Embed universal design in professional practice
- Establish accessibility as a cultural norm in design and construction

### Key Actions

| Action | Lead Entity | Timeline | Milestone |
|--------|-------------|----------|-----------|
| Mandate universal design features in all new commercial construction | Congress / ICC | Year 8-10 | Model code adopted in 30+ states |
| Complete barrier removal in all government buildings | Federal agencies / state-local governments | Year 8-12 | 95%+ compliance in government facilities |
| Achieve 90% compliance in healthcare facility accessibility | HHS / DOJ | Year 10-12 | Compliance verified through survey |
| Complete pedestrian infrastructure accessibility in all urban areas | DOT / local governments | Year 10-15 | PROWAG compliance in 90%+ of urban intersections |
| Establish mandatory accessibility curricula in all accredited architecture programs | NAAB | Year 8-10 | All accredited programs compliant |
| Achieve 75% barrier removal in all pre-ADA commercial buildings | Market forces + enforcement + funding | Year 12-15 | Verified through national survey |
| Expand ADA Title III to include compensatory damages | Congress | Year 10+ | Legislation enacted |
| Universal accessibility mapping coverage | Tech partners / Access Board | Year 10-15 | 90%+ of commercial buildings mapped |

### Success Metrics (End of Year 15)

- New construction noncompliance rate below 5%
- 75% of pre-ADA commercial buildings have completed comprehensive barrier removal
- 95% of government buildings fully accessible
- 90% of healthcare facilities meet MDE and physical accessibility standards
- 90% of urban intersections have compliant curb ramps and pedestrian signals
- Universal design is standard practice in architecture and construction

## Critical Dependencies

| Dependency | Risk if Not Met | Mitigation |
|------------|-----------------|------------|
| Congressional funding for enforcement and retrofit fund | Entire strategy slowed | State and local alternatives; private funding mechanisms |
| PROWAG finalization | Pedestrian infrastructure improvements lack enforceable standards | Continue enforcement under existing ADA Title II framework |
| State adoption of building code changes | Standards not uniformly applied | Federal incentives for adoption; condition federal funding on compliance |
| Professional licensing reform | Training gaps persist in new generation of professionals | Private sector training; university-level advocacy |
| Business community cooperation | Political opposition delays or blocks reforms | Frame reforms as market opportunity; highlight business case for accessibility |
| Technology development | Mapping and monitoring tools remain incomplete | Public-private partnerships; open-source solutions |

## Political Strategy

### Building the Coalition

**Year 1-3 Priorities**

- Frame accessibility as economic opportunity, not regulatory burden
- Build aging population alliance (AARP + disability organizations)
- Engage veteran organizations (high rates of mobility disability)
- Partner with construction and architecture professionals supportive of universal design
- Highlight bipartisan history of ADA support

**Year 4-7 Priorities**

- Demonstrate success of pilot programs to build support for scaling
- Publish economic data on return on investment from accessibility improvements
- Build business allies among companies that have benefited from accessibility
- Counter "serial litigant" narrative with enforcement data and individual stories

**Year 8-15 Priorities**

- Leverage demographic trends (aging population) to build political support
- Position universal design as innovation, not regulation
- Integrate accessibility into broader infrastructure investment agenda
- Build international comparisons showing U.S. lag behind peer nations
