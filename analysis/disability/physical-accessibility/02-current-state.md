# Physical Accessibility: Current State

## Built Environment Accessibility

### Commercial Buildings

The commercial building landscape in the United States presents a divided picture. Buildings constructed after the ADA's effective date (January 26, 1993 for most provisions) generally incorporate basic accessibility features, though compliance is far from universal. Buildings constructed before the ADA remain the primary source of physical barriers.

**Compliance Landscape (2024)**

| Building Category | Estimated Compliance Rate | Primary Barriers | Source |
|-------------------|--------------------------|------------------|--------|
| Post-ADA new construction | 75-85% | Design errors, poor maintenance, alteration failures | U.S. Access Board (2022) |
| Pre-ADA buildings (unaltered) | 30-40% | Entrances, restrooms, interior routes, parking | National Council on Disability (2023) |
| Pre-ADA buildings (partially retrofitted) | 50-65% | Restrooms, second-floor access, dressing rooms | DOJ settlement data (2023) |
| Government buildings (federal) | 80-90% | Older courthouses, post offices, historic buildings | GSA Accessibility Report (2023) |
| Government buildings (state/local) | 55-70% | Wide variation by jurisdiction; rural areas worst | NCD (2023) |
| Religious facilities | 20-40% | Exempt from ADA Title III but not from state laws | Disability Rights Education and Defense Fund (2024) |

**Most Common Physical Barriers**

| Barrier Type | Prevalence | Impact |
|-------------|-----------|--------|
| Inaccessible entrances (stairs only, narrow doors) | 35% of pre-ADA buildings | Complete exclusion from facility |
| Inaccessible restrooms | 45% of pre-ADA buildings | Limits duration of visit; health consequences |
| Lack of elevator or lift | 30% of multi-story commercial buildings | Excludes wheelchair users from upper floors |
| Inadequate parking (no accessible spaces or improper marking) | 25% of commercial lots | Prevents arrival at building |
| Interior navigation barriers (narrow aisles, high counters) | 40% of retail establishments | Limits ability to shop, conduct business |
| Inaccessible dressing/fitting rooms | 50% of retail clothing stores | Excludes from trying on merchandise |
| Protruding objects and inadequate signage | 30% of buildings | Hazards for people with visual impairments |

*Sources: U.S. Access Board (2022); DOJ compliance review data (2023); Disability Rights Advocates surveys (2024).*

### ADA Standards for Accessible Design

**Current Standards (2010 Standards)**

The 2010 ADA Standards for Accessible Design, effective March 15, 2012, govern new construction and alterations. Key requirements include:

| Feature | Standard | Citation |
|---------|----------|----------|
| Minimum clear door width | 32 inches | Section 404.2.3 |
| Maximum ramp slope | 1:12 (8.33%) | Section 405.2 |
| Wheelchair turning space | 60-inch diameter circle or T-shaped space | Section 304 |
| Accessible parking spaces | 1 per 25 total spaces (minimum) | Section 208.2 |
| Van-accessible spaces | 1 per 6 accessible spaces | Section 208.2.4 |
| Accessible route width | 36 inches minimum | Section 403.5.1 |
| Counter heights | 36 inches maximum (portion) | Section 904 |
| Elevator requirements | Required in buildings 3+ stories or 3,000+ sq ft per story | Section 206.2.3 |

**Safe Harbor Provision**

Elements that comply with the 1991 Standards are not required to be updated to 2010 Standards unless the element is altered. This "safe harbor" provision means many buildings remain at the lower 1991 standard indefinitely.

## Pedestrian Infrastructure

### Sidewalks and Curb Ramps

Accessible pedestrian infrastructure is critical for people with mobility and visual disabilities to navigate between buildings. The current state of pedestrian infrastructure varies dramatically by jurisdiction.

**National Statistics**

| Metric | Data | Source |
|--------|------|--------|
| Intersections lacking curb ramps | 25% nationally | FHWA Pedestrian Infrastructure Report (2022) |
| Sidewalk coverage in urban areas | 80% of streets have sidewalks on at least one side | National Complete Streets Coalition (2023) |
| Sidewalk coverage in suburban areas | 45% of streets | Smart Growth America (2023) |
| Sidewalk coverage in rural areas | Less than 10% of roads | FHWA (2022) |
| Detectable warning surfaces at curb ramps | 60% compliance where ramps exist | U.S. Access Board survey (2022) |
| Accessible pedestrian signals | 20% of signalized intersections | National Federation of the Blind (2024) |

**Condition Issues**

Even where sidewalks and curb ramps exist, condition problems create barriers:

- Cracked and uneven surfaces that impede wheelchair users
- Overgrown vegetation blocking paths
- Temporary construction obstructions without accessible detour routes
- Snow and ice removal failures (no federal mandate for clearing accessible routes)
- Utility poles, signs, and street furniture blocking the accessible path
- Cross-slopes exceeding the 2% maximum

### Public Rights-of-Way

The U.S. Access Board published proposed guidelines for accessible public rights-of-way (PROWAG) in 2023, but these have not been adopted as enforceable standards. Current enforcement relies on ADA Title II requirements and DOJ settlement agreements.

**PROWAG Key Provisions (Proposed)**

| Element | Proposed Standard |
|---------|-------------------|
| Pedestrian access route width | 48 inches minimum (4 feet) |
| Maximum cross-slope | 2% |
| Maximum running slope | 5% (consistent with general grade) |
| Curb ramp requirements | Required at all pedestrian street crossings |
| Detectable warning surfaces | Required at all curb ramps and transit platform edges |
| Accessible pedestrian signals | Required at all new and altered signalized intersections |
| Shared-use paths | 60-inch width minimum |

*Source: U.S. Access Board, Proposed Accessibility Guidelines for Pedestrian Facilities in the Public Right-of-Way (2023).*

## Healthcare Facility Accessibility

### Medical Facilities

Physical accessibility of healthcare facilities is a critical concern because inaccessible facilities directly threaten the health and lives of people with disabilities.

**Current Conditions**

| Facility Type | Accessibility Issue | Prevalence | Source |
|---------------|-------------------|-----------|--------|
| Primary care offices | No wheelchair-accessible exam room | 22% | NCD (2023) |
| Primary care offices | No height-adjustable exam table | 40% | Disability Rights Advocates (2024) |
| Hospitals | Inaccessible inpatient rooms | 15-20% | CMS survey data (2023) |
| Dental offices | Cannot accommodate wheelchair users | 30% | National Institute of Dental and Craniofacial Research (2023) |
| Imaging centers | MRI/CT equipment inaccessible to many patients | 35% | Radiology accessibility study (2022) |
| Pharmacies | High counters, inaccessible aisles | 25% | DOJ settlement data (2023) |

**Medical Diagnostic Equipment**

The U.S. Access Board issued standards for accessible medical diagnostic equipment (MDE) in January 2017, including transfer surfaces, leg supports, and height adjustability. However, these standards have not been adopted by HHS as enforceable regulations. The equipment covered includes:

- Examination tables and chairs
- Weight scales
- Radiological and imaging equipment
- Mammography equipment
- Other diagnostic equipment used in patient positioning

HHS issued an Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (ANPRM) in 2023 requesting comment on adopting MDE standards under Section 504 and the ACA. As of early 2026, no proposed rule has been issued.

## Government Facilities

### Federal Buildings

The General Services Administration (GSA) manages most federal buildings and applies the Architectural Barriers Act (ABA) standards, which predate and are generally consistent with ADA standards.

| Category | Status | Notes |
|----------|--------|-------|
| Post-1968 federal buildings | 85-90% compliant | ABA has applied since 1968 |
| Pre-1968 federal buildings | 60-75% compliant | Ongoing renovation program |
| Historic federal buildings | Varies widely | Preservation vs. accessibility tensions |
| Leased federal space | 70-80% compliant | Lease requirements include accessibility |

*Source: GSA Federal Buildings Fund Annual Report (FY 2024).*

### State and Local Government Buildings

ADA Title II requires that state and local government programs be accessible, including physical facilities. Compliance varies enormously.

**Self-Evaluation and Transition Plans**

ADA Title II requires state and local governments to conduct self-evaluations and develop transition plans for making facilities accessible. Compliance with this requirement is poor:

- Only 52% of local governments have completed ADA self-evaluations (NCD, 2023)
- Only 38% have current transition plans
- Rural municipalities are least likely to have either
- Many transition plans are decades old and have not been updated or implemented

## Parks and Recreation

### National Parks

The National Park Service (NPS) manages 429 sites, many with significant accessibility challenges due to natural terrain and historic structures.

| Category | Status | Source |
|----------|--------|--------|
| Visitor centers with accessible entrances | 90% | NPS Accessibility Assessment (2023) |
| Accessible trails (at least one per park) | 65% of parks | NPS (2023) |
| Accessible camping sites | 70% of campgrounds have accessible sites | NPS (2023) |
| Accessible restrooms at trailheads | 55% | NPS (2023) |
| Accessible interpretive programs | 50% offer accessible alternatives | NPS (2023) |

### State and Local Parks

Accessibility in state and local parks varies widely, with many parks, playgrounds, and recreational facilities lacking basic accessibility features such as accessible paths to play equipment, accessible picnic areas, and accessible restrooms.

- Only 40% of public playgrounds meet current accessibility standards (National Recreation and Park Association, 2023)
- Many sports facilities (ballfields, courts, pools) lack accessible seating, accessible routes, and accessible locker rooms

## Education Facilities

### K-12 Schools

School buildings are covered by both the ADA (as places of public accommodation and government facilities) and Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act.

| Issue | Prevalence | Source |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| Schools with accessibility deficiencies | 30% of public school buildings | Government Accountability Office (2023) |
| Schools lacking accessible science labs | 20% | National Science Teachers Association (2022) |
| Schools with inaccessible stages/auditoriums | 35% | DOJ compliance data (2023) |
| Temporary classroom buildings lacking accessibility | 45% | DOE Office for Civil Rights (2023) |

### Higher Education

Colleges and universities face particular challenges because of sprawling campuses with buildings spanning decades of construction standards.

- Historic campus buildings frequently lack elevators, accessible restrooms, and accessible classrooms
- Campus paths and outdoor spaces may lack accessible routes, particularly in hilly terrain
- Student housing accessibility remains a persistent concern, with many older dormitories lacking accessible rooms

## Emerging Issues

### Electric Vehicle Charging Stations

As electric vehicle infrastructure expands, accessibility of EV charging stations has emerged as a concern. The DOT and U.S. Access Board issued proposed accessibility standards for EV charging stations in 2023.

### Autonomous Vehicles

Self-driving vehicle technology raises questions about physical accessibility of vehicles and pick-up/drop-off infrastructure for people with mobility disabilities.

### Post-Pandemic Changes

COVID-19 accelerated adoption of curbside pickup, outdoor dining, and remote services. Some of these changes improved access (curbside pickup benefits people with mobility disabilities), while others created new barriers (temporary outdoor structures often lack accessibility features).

### Climate Adaptation

Climate-driven infrastructure changes (seawalls, elevated structures, emergency shelters) must incorporate accessibility or risk creating new barriers. Emergency shelters during extreme weather events frequently fail to accommodate people with disabilities.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Pre-ADA buildings are the primary problem**: The vast majority of physical accessibility barriers exist in buildings constructed before 1993 that have never been comprehensively retrofitted
2. **New construction compliance is imperfect**: Even buildings subject to full ADA requirements frequently contain violations due to design errors, contractor ignorance, and lack of inspection
3. **Pedestrian infrastructure lags significantly**: Sidewalks, curb ramps, and pedestrian signals remain inaccessible in many communities, particularly suburban and rural areas
4. **Healthcare accessibility is a life-threatening gap**: Inaccessible medical facilities and equipment directly harm the health of people with disabilities
5. **Government compliance is mixed**: Federal buildings generally perform well, but state and local government facilities and programs show wide variation
6. **Enforcement drives compliance**: Jurisdictions with active enforcement (through DOJ settlements, private litigation, or state agencies) have significantly better compliance rates
7. **Standards development is slow**: Critical standards for public rights-of-way and medical equipment remain in proposed or advisory status years after development
