# Physical Accessibility: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative framework for physical accessibility in the United States rests primarily on the Americans with Disabilities Act (1990), the Architectural Barriers Act (1968), Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act (1973), and state building codes. Despite this legal foundation, significant gaps remain in coverage, enforcement, and remedies. The proposals below address the most critical legislative shortcomings: inadequate remedies for accessibility violations, the absence of enforceable pedestrian infrastructure standards, insufficient funding for retrofits, and the need for stronger building code enforcement.

## Federal Legislation

### Physical Accessibility Enhancement Act

**Purpose**: Strengthen ADA Title III enforcement for physical accessibility by expanding remedies and establishing proactive compliance mechanisms.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Physical Accessibility Enhancement Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(a) More than 35 years after enactment of the Americans with Disabilities
Act, an estimated 40 to 60 percent of pre-ADA commercial buildings
contain significant physical accessibility barriers.
(b) The absence of compensatory damages under ADA Title III
limits the deterrent effect of private enforcement.
(c) People with disabilities continue to encounter inaccessible
buildings, sidewalks, and public facilities on a daily basis.
(d) Proactive compliance mechanisms are necessary to supplement
the current complaint-driven enforcement model.

SEC. 3. COMPENSATORY DAMAGES UNDER TITLE III.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 308(a) of the Americans with Disabilities
Act of 1990 (42 U.S.C. 12188(a)) is amended to provide that any
person who is subjected to discrimination on the basis of disability
in violation of title III may recover compensatory damages.
(b) LIMITATIONS.—Compensatory damages awarded under this section
shall not exceed—
    (1) $25,000 for businesses with fewer than 15 employees;
    (2) $50,000 for businesses with 15 to 100 employees;
    (3) $100,000 for businesses with 101 to 500 employees;
    (4) $200,000 for businesses with more than 500 employees.
(c) GOOD FAITH DEFENSE.—A defendant that demonstrates it has
conducted a comprehensive accessibility assessment and is implementing
a barrier removal plan in compliance with DOJ guidance may present
such efforts as a mitigating factor in determining damages.

SEC. 4. MANDATORY ACCESSIBILITY ASSESSMENTS.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Not later than 5 years after the date of enactment
of this Act, the owner or operator of any place of public
accommodation shall conduct a comprehensive accessibility assessment
of the facility using criteria established by the Attorney General.
(b) ASSESSMENT CONTENTS.—Each assessment shall identify all
physical accessibility barriers and develop a prioritized plan for
barrier removal.
(c) FILING REQUIREMENT.—Assessment results and barrier removal
plans shall be filed with the Attorney General and made available
to the public through a searchable database.
(d) UPDATES.—Assessments shall be updated not less frequently
than every 5 years or upon any substantial alteration of the facility.

SEC. 5. ENHANCED FEDERAL ENFORCEMENT.

(a) STAFFING.—The Attorney General shall increase staffing of the
Disability Rights Section by not fewer than 50 additional attorneys
and 25 accessibility specialists (architects, engineers, or
certified access specialists) within 3 years of enactment.
(b) PROACTIVE REVIEWS.—The Attorney General shall conduct not
fewer than 500 physical accessibility compliance reviews per year.
(c) ANNUAL REPORT.—The Attorney General shall publish an annual
report on physical accessibility enforcement actions, compliance
rates, and barrier removal progress.

SEC. 6. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated $100,000,000 for each of
fiscal years 2027 through 2036 to carry out this Act.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses two fundamental weaknesses in current law: the absence of compensatory damages under Title III, which limits the deterrent effect of private enforcement, and the absence of proactive compliance mechanisms that would identify barriers before individuals are harmed. The mandatory assessment requirement creates accountability and transparency while allowing reasonable timelines for compliance.

**Potential Challenges**: Strong business opposition to compensatory damages; concerns about assessment costs for small businesses; questions about federal authority to mandate assessments of private property.

**Refinements**: Phase assessment requirements by business size (largest businesses first); provide technical assistance and streamlined assessment tools for small businesses; consider safe harbor from damages for businesses that complete assessments and implement plans.

### National Accessibility Retrofit Investment Act

**Purpose**: Establish a federal grant program to fund physical accessibility retrofits for small businesses, nonprofits, and local government facilities.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "National Accessibility Retrofit
Investment Act".

SEC. 2. ESTABLISHMENT OF FUND.

(a) IN GENERAL.—There is established in the Treasury of the United
States a fund to be known as the "National Accessibility Retrofit
Fund" (referred to in this Act as the "Fund").
(b) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated to the
Fund $500,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2036.

SEC. 3. ELIGIBLE RECIPIENTS.

Grants from the Fund may be awarded to—
(a) small businesses (as defined in section 3 of the Small
Business Act) for physical accessibility improvements to existing
facilities;
(b) nonprofit organizations exempt from taxation under section
501(c)(3) of the Internal Revenue Code;
(c) state and local government entities for accessibility
improvements to public facilities;
(d) houses of worship and religious organizations for
accessibility improvements.

SEC. 4. GRANT CONDITIONS.

(a) MAXIMUM GRANT.—No single grant shall exceed $100,000.
(b) MATCHING REQUIREMENT.—Grant recipients shall provide matching
funds of not less than 25 percent of the total project cost, except
that the Secretary may waive the matching requirement for entities
with annual revenues below $500,000.
(c) COMPLIANCE.—All improvements funded under this Act shall meet
or exceed the ADA Standards for Accessible Design in effect at the
time of construction.
(d) PRIORITY.—Priority shall be given to projects that—
    (1) serve communities with high disability prevalence;
    (2) address barriers in healthcare facilities;
    (3) are located in rural or underserved areas;
    (4) achieve comprehensive accessibility rather than partial
    improvements.

SEC. 5. ADMINISTRATION.

The Secretary of Housing and Urban Development shall administer
the Fund in coordination with the U.S. Access Board and the
Attorney General.
```

**Explanation**: The absence of federal funding for accessibility retrofits is a major reason barriers persist in existing buildings. This fund addresses the cost argument directly while prioritizing the most impactful improvements.

**Potential Challenges**: Securing appropriations in a constrained budget environment; ensuring efficient administration; preventing fraud.

**Refinements**: Could be structured as a revolving loan fund rather than grant program to increase sustainability; could incorporate accessibility impact bonds for private sector participation.

### Accessible Pedestrian Infrastructure Act

**Purpose**: Codify PROWAG as enforceable federal standards and link federal transportation funding to pedestrian accessibility compliance.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Accessible Pedestrian Infrastructure Act".

SEC. 2. ADOPTION OF PROWAG.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Not later than 1 year after the date of enactment
of this Act, the Architectural and Transportation Barriers
Compliance Board shall issue final accessibility guidelines for
pedestrian facilities in the public right-of-way.
(b) ADOPTION BY DOJ.—Not later than 180 days after issuance of
the guidelines under subsection (a), the Attorney General shall
adopt such guidelines as enforceable standards under title II of
the Americans with Disabilities Act.

SEC. 3. FEDERAL FUNDING CONDITIONS.

(a) COMPLIANCE PLANS.—As a condition of receiving federal
transportation funding under title 23 or title 49 of the United
States Code, each state and local government shall submit to the
Secretary of Transportation a pedestrian accessibility compliance
plan that—
    (1) identifies all intersections and pedestrian facilities
    that do not meet the standards adopted under section 2;
    (2) establishes a timeline for achieving compliance; and
    (3) prioritizes improvements based on pedestrian traffic
    volume, proximity to transit stops, and proximity to
    facilities serving people with disabilities.
(b) COMPLIANCE DEADLINE.—All intersections and pedestrian
facilities shall be brought into compliance not later than 20
years after the date of enactment of this Act.

SEC. 4. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated to the Secretary of
Transportation $200,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through
2046 to assist state and local governments in achieving compliance.
```

**Explanation**: Pedestrian infrastructure accessibility has lacked enforceable federal standards for decades despite the development of PROWAG. This bill would complete the rulemaking process and create enforceable standards with clear timelines and federal funding support.

**Potential Challenges**: Cost to state and local governments; 20-year timeline may be considered too ambitious for some jurisdictions; political resistance to new federal mandates on local infrastructure.

**Refinements**: Include flexibility provisions for rural areas and communities with limited resources; provide additional technical assistance funding; allow extensions for demonstrated hardship.

## State Model Legislation

### Model Accessibility Certification Act

**Purpose**: Require periodic accessibility certification for commercial buildings at the state level.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Accessibility Certification Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Accessibility certification" means a determination by a
certified access specialist that a commercial facility meets the
accessibility requirements of the Americans with Disabilities Act
and applicable state accessibility standards.
(b) "Certified access specialist" means an individual who has
completed training approved by the [State] Division of Building
Standards and demonstrated competency in accessibility standards.
(c) "Commercial facility" means any facility that is a place of
public accommodation as defined in 42 U.S.C. 12181(7).

SECTION 3. CERTIFICATION REQUIREMENTS.

(a) All commercial facilities shall obtain an accessibility
certification within 5 years of the effective date of this Act.
(b) Certifications shall be renewed every 5 years.
(c) New construction shall obtain initial certification before
issuance of a certificate of occupancy.

SECTION 4. INCENTIVES.

(a) A commercial facility holding a current accessibility
certification shall receive a 10 percent reduction in commercial
property tax for the certified period.
(b) An accessibility certification shall constitute a rebuttable
presumption of compliance in any action brought under state
disability rights law.

SECTION 5. PUBLIC INFORMATION.

The [State agency] shall maintain a publicly accessible database
of all certified and non-certified commercial facilities.
```

**Explanation**: State-level certification programs create systematic accountability while offering meaningful incentives (tax reduction, litigation protection) for compliance. California's CASp program provides a partial model.

**Adaptations**: States with existing accessibility inspection programs can integrate certification; property tax incentive percentage can be adjusted based on state revenue considerations; exemptions may be needed for very small businesses.

### Model Visitability Act

**Purpose**: Require basic accessibility features in all new residential construction.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Visitability Act".

SECTION 2. REQUIREMENTS.

All new single-family homes and townhouses constructed in [State]
after the effective date of this Act shall include:
(a) At least one entrance at grade (zero-step entrance) on an
accessible route from a driveway or public sidewalk;
(b) All interior doorways on the main floor with a minimum
clear width of 32 inches;
(c) At least one bathroom on the main floor usable by a person
using a wheelchair, with reinforced walls for future installation
of grab bars.

SECTION 3. EXEMPTIONS.

The requirements of Section 2 shall not apply to—
(a) homes constructed on lots where topography makes a zero-step
entrance infeasible, as certified by a licensed engineer;
(b) additions to existing homes where compliance is structurally
infeasible.

SECTION 4. ENFORCEMENT.

Compliance with this Act shall be verified as part of the building
permit and inspection process.
```

**Explanation**: Visitability requirements ensure that new homes are accessible for visiting by people with disabilities and provide a foundation for aging in place. The cost of incorporating these features into new construction is minimal ($100-$600 per home according to Concrete Change, the national visitability advocacy organization).

## Regulatory Framework

### DOJ Readily Achievable Barrier Removal Guidance

**Existing Authority**: ADA Title III, 42 U.S.C. Section 12182(b)(2)(A)(iv)

**Proposed Regulation**: Updated DOJ guidance specifying priority categories and timelines for readily achievable barrier removal in existing buildings.

**Key Provisions**:

- Priority 1 (within 2 years): Accessible entrances, accessible routes to goods and services
- Priority 2 (within 4 years): Accessible restrooms
- Priority 3 (within 6 years): All remaining barriers including signage, counters, and secondary routes
- Annual spending floor: Businesses shall spend not less than 1% of gross revenue on barrier removal until full compliance is achieved, up to a maximum of $50,000 per year

**Explanation**: Current DOJ guidance on readily achievable barrier removal provides priority categories but no timelines or spending expectations. This updated guidance would create clear, enforceable expectations.

### HHS Medical Diagnostic Equipment Standards

**Existing Authority**: Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act; Section 1557 of the Affordable Care Act

**Proposed Regulation**: HHS adoption of Access Board MDE standards as enforceable requirements for healthcare facilities receiving federal funds.

**Key Provisions**:

- All exam tables must be height-adjustable to accommodate wheelchair transfers
- Accessible weight scales required in all primary care and specialty care offices
- Accessible mammography, MRI, and CT equipment required in all imaging facilities
- Compliance deadline: 5 years for existing facilities; immediate for new equipment purchases

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Commerce Clause authority**: ADA Title III rests on congressional commerce power; physical accessibility requirements for private businesses are well established
- **Spending power**: Linking accessibility to federal funding (transportation, healthcare) is constitutionally permissible under *South Dakota v. Dole* (1987), provided conditions are clear and related to the federal interest
- **Takings**: Accessibility requirements for existing buildings have not been held to constitute takings, but mandatory assessment requirements could face challenge

### Preemption Questions

- ADA sets a floor, not a ceiling; state and local accessibility requirements can exceed federal standards
- State building codes may differ from ADA Standards; DOJ has certified some state codes as equivalent
- Visitability ordinances are state/local initiatives that go beyond ADA requirements

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- Private right of action under ADA Title III (injunctive relief; compensatory damages if enacted)
- DOJ enforcement through complaint investigation, compliance reviews, and litigation
- State enforcement through building code inspection and state disability rights laws
- Certification programs create market-based incentives for compliance

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Physical Accessibility Enhancement Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Assessment gaming | Building owners may conduct superficial assessments that miss barriers | High |
| Good faith defense abuse | Defendants may claim good faith based on minimal efforts | Medium |
| Damages caps too low | Caps may be insufficient to deter large businesses | Medium |
| Assessment filing backlog | DOJ may lack capacity to review filed assessments | High |
| Subcontractor liability gap | Building owners may argue contractors are responsible for design failures | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address religious facilities | 20-40% of religious buildings remain inaccessible | First Amendment concerns about regulating religious properties |
| No private assessment standard | Assessment quality will vary without certification requirements | Lack of existing federal certification infrastructure |
| 5-year assessment timeline may be too long | Barriers persist during compliance period | Political compromise to gain support |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Establish federal certification standards for accessibility assessors (require CASp-equivalent credentials)
2. Create random audit program to verify assessment quality (DOJ to audit 10% of filed assessments annually)
3. Narrow the good faith defense to require specific, measurable progress benchmarks
4. Index damages caps to inflation and review every 5 years
5. Clarify that building owners bear ultimate responsibility for accessibility regardless of subcontractor arrangements

### National Accessibility Retrofit Investment Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Partial compliance projects | Grant recipients may address only the least costly barriers | Medium |
| Matching fund waivers | Excessive waivers could deplete fund more quickly | Low |
| Maintenance failure | Facilities may become inaccessible again after retrofit | High |
| Geographic concentration | Grants may flow disproportionately to areas with grant-writing capacity | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| $500 million per year may be insufficient | Cannot address all 40-60% noncompliant pre-ADA buildings | Scale of the problem exceeds proposed funding |
| No enforcement of continued compliance | Retrofitted facilities may deteriorate | Fund is designed for capital improvements, not ongoing maintenance |
| Houses of worship eligibility raises Establishment Clause issues | Legal challenges possible | Tension between accessibility and church-state separation |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Require 10-year maintenance commitments as a condition of grant receipt
2. Establish post-grant compliance monitoring (site visits at 2, 5, and 10 years)
3. Prioritize comprehensive projects over partial improvements in grant scoring
4. Reserve 25% of funds for underserved communities with technical assistance support
5. Structure religious facility grants as secular accessibility improvements consistent with *Trinity Lutheran Church v. Comer* (2017)

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|-------------------|
| Multiple overlapping standards (ADA, state codes, Fair Housing) | DOJ/Access Board to publish unified compliance guide |
| Lack of qualified accessibility inspectors | Federal funding for inspector training programs |
| Building owner ignorance of requirements | National accessibility awareness campaign with free self-assessment tools |
| Insufficient coordination between federal agencies | Interagency accessibility task force (DOJ, HHS, DOT, HUD, Access Board) |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- All legislation should include 10-year review requirements with GAO assessment of effectiveness
- Damages caps should be indexed to inflation with automatic adjustment
- Grant program effectiveness should be evaluated at the 5-year mark with report to Congress
- Assessment requirements should be reviewed after initial 5-year cycle to determine whether timelines and standards need adjustment
