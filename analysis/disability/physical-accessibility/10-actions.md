# Physical Accessibility: What Citizens Can Do

## Overview

Every citizen can contribute to improving physical accessibility in their community. The actions below are organized by level of commitment, from simple steps anyone can take to sustained advocacy that can drive systemic change.

## Individual Actions

### Immediate (Today)

- **Report accessibility barriers**: File complaints with the DOJ at <https://www.ada.gov/file-a-complaint/> or call (800) 514-0301 for ADA violations you encounter
- **Use accessibility mapping apps**: Contribute to Wheelmap, AccessNow, or Google Maps accessibility reviews to help other people with disabilities plan trips
- **Stop using accessible parking without a permit**: Accessible parking abuse is one of the most common accessibility barriers; do not park in accessible spaces or access aisles
- **Move obstructions**: If you see objects blocking accessible routes (sidewalk signs, shopping carts, delivery boxes), move them when safe to do so
- **Ask businesses about accessibility**: Inquire whether businesses you patronize are accessible; customer interest signals market demand
- **Share this information**: Help others understand why physical accessibility matters and how pervasive barriers remain

### Short-Term (This Month)

- **Audit your workplace**: Walk through your workplace using the ADA Checklist for Existing Facilities (available from the New England ADA Center at <https://www.adachecklist.org/>) and report barriers to management
- **Audit your community**: Survey sidewalks, curb ramps, and intersections in your neighborhood for accessibility barriers and report them to your local public works department
- **Contact your elected officials**: Call or write your federal, state, and local representatives to express support for accessibility funding and enforcement. Key messages:
    - Support increased DOJ Disability Rights Section funding
    - Oppose the ADA Education and Reform Act
    - Support expanded tax incentives for accessibility retrofits
    - Support finalization of PROWAG
- **Join a disability rights organization**: Become a member of ADAPT, AAPD, or your state's protection and advocacy organization

### Ongoing (This Year)

- **Attend local government meetings**: Advocate for accessibility in local planning, zoning, and building code decisions. Request that your city or county:
    - Complete an ADA self-evaluation and transition plan (required by law but often incomplete)
    - Allocate funds for sidewalk and curb ramp improvements
    - Adopt a complete streets policy that includes accessibility
    - Integrate accessibility review into the building permit process
- **Patronize accessible businesses**: Support businesses that invest in accessibility. Leave positive reviews noting accessibility features.
- **Volunteer with disability organizations**: Contribute your time and skills to organizations working on accessibility advocacy

## Community Actions

### Neighborhood Level

- **Form an accessibility task force**: Organize neighbors to survey and advocate for accessible sidewalks, crosswalks, parks, and public spaces in your community
- **Advocate for accessible playgrounds**: Work with your parks department to ensure that new and renovated playgrounds meet accessibility standards
- **Monitor construction projects**: When new construction or renovation occurs in your area, observe whether accessibility features are being properly installed and report concerns to the building department
- **Host awareness events**: Organize wheelchair accessibility tours, disability simulation exercises (with guidance from disability organizations), or ADA anniversary celebrations

### Business Community

- **Start an accessibility business alliance**: Bring together local businesses to share resources, reduce costs (group purchasing of accessibility products), and promote accessible business districts
- **Encourage accessibility as a competitive advantage**: Help businesses understand the disability market ($1.8 trillion in disposable income) and the broader customer base served by accessible design (aging population, families with strollers, travelers)
- **Nominate businesses for accessibility awards**: Many communities have business award programs; advocate for accessibility as a recognized category

### Faith Community

- **Assess your house of worship**: Religious facilities are exempt from ADA Title III but should still be welcoming. Conduct an accessibility assessment and develop a plan for improvements
- **Advocate for accessibility in denominational policies**: Work within your religious denomination to establish accessibility standards and funding for facility improvements
- **Offer accessibility improvements as a ministry**: Frame accessibility work as a justice and inclusion mission within your faith tradition

## Advocacy and Political Action

### Federal Level

**Priority Legislation to Support**

| Bill | Purpose | Action |
|------|---------|--------|
| ADA Restoration Act | Strengthen ADA enforcement and remedies | Contact senators and representatives in support |
| PROWAG Adoption | Finalize federal pedestrian accessibility standards | Comment during rulemaking; contact Access Board |
| Disability Access Tax Credit Expansion | Increase tax incentives for accessibility | Contact Ways and Means / Finance Committee members |
| National Accessibility Investment Act (proposed) | Federal funding for accessibility retrofits | Build congressional support |

**Priority Legislation to Oppose**

| Bill | Risk | Action |
|------|------|--------|
| ADA Education and Reform Act | Guts private enforcement through pre-suit notice requirements | Contact representatives to oppose; support disability organization campaigns |

### State Level

- **Push for state accessibility enforcement agencies**: Advocate for dedicated state agencies or commissions with authority to enforce accessibility requirements
- **Support state-level tax incentives**: Many states offer additional tax incentives for accessibility; advocate for expansion
- **Advocate for visitability ordinances**: Push for requirements that all new residential construction include basic accessibility features (zero-step entrance, accessible bathroom on main floor, wide doorways)
- **Oppose state pre-suit notice laws**: Several states have adopted notice requirements that weaken ADA enforcement; organize to repeal or prevent them

### Local Level

- **Serve on disability advisory committees**: Many cities have disability advisory committees or commissions; volunteer to serve
- **Run for office**: People with disabilities and accessibility advocates are underrepresented in elected office. Consider running for school board, city council, or other local positions
- **Engage in the budget process**: Advocate for local government budget allocations for accessibility improvements in public facilities, sidewalks, and parks
- **Participate in comprehensive planning**: Local comprehensive plans shape development patterns for decades. Advocate for accessibility as a core planning principle

## Professional Actions

### Architects and Designers

- Pursue advanced training in universal design
- Advocate within your firm for accessibility as a design value, not just a code requirement
- Mentor younger architects in accessibility design
- Volunteer for pro bono accessibility assessments for nonprofits and small businesses
- Participate in ADA National Network technical assistance programs

### Contractors and Builders

- Invest in accessibility construction training for your crews
- Develop expertise in accessibility retrofits as a business specialty
- Check your work against ADA Standards, not just local building codes
- Report accessibility code violations you observe on other projects

### Building Inspectors

- Seek additional training in ADA Standards for Accessible Design
- Add accessibility checkpoints to your inspection checklists
- Develop relationships with local disability organizations for consultation
- Advocate within your jurisdiction for comprehensive accessibility inspection

### Real Estate Professionals

- Include accessibility information in property listings
- Educate clients about accessibility requirements and benefits
- Develop expertise in accessible housing for aging and disabled clients
- Advocate within your professional associations for accessibility awareness
