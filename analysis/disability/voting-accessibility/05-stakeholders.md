# Voting Accessibility: Stakeholders

## Who Benefits from the Status Quo

### Election Administrators Under Budget Pressure

**Who**: County clerks, boards of elections, local election officials in under-resourced jurisdictions

**Why They "Benefit"**:

- Status quo avoids cost of accessibility upgrades (new equipment, facility modifications, training programs)
- Current approach allows use of cheaper, less accessible polling places
- Less training required for poll workers
- Fewer accommodation requests to manage

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Low to Moderate**

- Most election administrators support accessibility in principle but lack resources
- Opposition is primarily to unfunded mandates, not to accessibility itself
- Many would welcome federal funding for accessibility improvements

### Voting Equipment Vendors

**Who**: ES&S, Hart InterCivic, Dominion Voting Systems

**Why They Benefit**:

- Current HAVA requirements are minimal (one BMD per polling place)
- Low accessibility standards reduce R&D costs
- Replacement cycles of 10-15 years lock in existing equipment
- Stronger accessibility requirements would require new product development

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Low to Moderate**

- Vendors would benefit from mandated equipment replacement (new sales)
- Oppose standards they cannot meet with current products
- Support standards they helped write (regulatory capture concern)

### States Opposing Voting Expansion

**Who**: State legislatures that have passed voting restriction laws

**Why They Benefit**:

- Restrictive voting laws that happen to burden voters with disabilities serve broader voter suppression goals
- Disability barriers are rarely the stated target but function as collateral restriction
- Maintaining polling place barriers reduces turnout among populations that lean Democratic

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Moderate to High**

- Framed as election security concerns rather than opposition to disability access
- Will oppose reforms packaged with broader voting expansion measures
- May support disability-specific accommodations if separated from other voting access issues

## Who Is Harmed by the Status Quo

### Voters with Disabilities

**70 million Americans with disabilities (27% of adults)**

Broken down by disability type and specific voting barriers:

| Disability Type | Estimated Voting-Age Population | Primary Voting Barriers |
|----------------|-------------------------------|------------------------|
| Mobility impairment | 15+ million | Polling place physical access, standing in lines |
| Vision impairment (blind/low vision) | 7+ million | Ballot reading, navigation, information access |
| Hearing impairment | 11+ million | Communication with poll workers, audio-only information |
| Cognitive/intellectual disability | 8+ million | Ballot complexity, guardianship disenfranchisement |
| Psychiatric disability | 15+ million | Guardianship, crowded environments, stigma |
| Dexterity/fine motor impairment | 5+ million | Marking ballots, operating machines, envelope procedures |
| Multiple disabilities | Overlap significant | Compounded barriers from multiple categories |

**Political Power**: **Low to Moderate**

- Growing advocacy capacity through organizations like AAPD, NFB, NAD
- Cross-disability voting coalitions have emerged (REV UP campaign)
- Underrepresented in elected office and policy-making positions
- Voting gap itself reduces political influence (circular problem)

### Disability Rights Organizations

**Key Organizations Active on Voting**:

- **American Association of People with Disabilities (AAPD)**: REV UP voting campaign; national coordination
- **National Federation of the Blind (NFB)**: Accessible ballot advocacy; litigation
- **National Association of the Deaf (NAD)**: Communication access at polls
- **The Arc of the United States**: Intellectual disability voting rights; guardianship reform
- **Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law**: Guardianship voting restrictions; competency standards
- **Disability Rights Education and Defense Fund (DREDF)**: Legal advocacy
- **National Disability Rights Network (NDRN)**: Protection & Advocacy voter assistance programs
- **Self Advocates Becoming Empowered (SABE)**: Self-advocacy for people with intellectual disabilities
- **Paralyzed Veterans of America**: Physical accessibility advocacy
- **Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN)**: Neurodiversity and voting access

**Political Power**: **Moderate**

- Effective litigation capacity (NFB, DREDF, Bazelon Center)
- Growing electoral mobilization (REV UP reached 35+ states in 2024)
- Policy expertise on voting technology and accessibility standards
- Limited lobbying resources compared to election industry

### Election Integrity Organizations

**Who**: Common Cause, League of Women Voters, Brennan Center for Justice, Campaign Legal Center

**Interest**: Support voting accessibility as component of broader voting rights agenda, but disability-specific issues sometimes receive insufficient attention within their broader platforms

**Political Power**: **Moderate to High**

- Significant lobbying and litigation capacity
- Media visibility
- Sometimes prioritize other voting rights issues over disability-specific accessibility

## Key Decision-Makers

### Federal

| Entity | Role | Current Posture |
|--------|------|-----------------|
| DOJ Civil Rights Division, Voting Section | ADA enforcement at polling places | Varies by administration |
| DOJ Civil Rights Division, Disability Rights Section | ADA Title II enforcement | Active but under-resourced |
| Election Assistance Commission (EAC) | HAVA implementation, VVSG standards, funding | Advisory; no enforcement power |
| Congress (House Administration Committee, Senate Rules Committee) | Legislative authority | Low priority for disability-specific legislation |
| Federal courts | ADA enforcement, constitutional challenges | Venue for accessibility litigation |

| Polling place facility owners | Building accessibility |

## Coalition Opportunities

### Cross-Disability Voting Coalition

The strongest coalition opportunity unites organizations representing different disability types around a shared voting accessibility agenda:

- **Shared interest**: All disability types face voting barriers, though the specific barriers differ
- **Combined political power**: The disability community's voting bloc (70 million people) is potentially the largest single demographic group
- **Existing infrastructure**: REV UP campaign, AAPD coordination, NDRN protection & advocacy

### Disability + Aging Coalition

Elderly voters face many of the same voting barriers as voters with disabilities:

- Physical access challenges (mobility, vision, dexterity)
- Technology difficulties (new voting equipment)
- Cognitive challenges (complex ballots)
- AARP + disability organizations = formidable political force

### Disability + Veterans Coalition

Veterans with service-connected disabilities face specific voting barriers:

- Physical accessibility (VA facilities as polling places are generally accessible)
- PTSD and TBI affecting cognitive function and crowded environments
- Paralyzed Veterans of America has strong lobbying capacity

### Bipartisan Accessibility Funding

Federal funding for voting accessibility improvements can attract bipartisan support:

- Republicans: Support election administration improvements and equipment upgrades
- Democrats: Support voting access expansion
- Both: Support disability rights (ADA has always been bipartisan)
- Frame as infrastructure investment, not voting expansion
