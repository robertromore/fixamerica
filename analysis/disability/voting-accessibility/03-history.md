# Voting Accessibility: History

## Origins

### Historical Exclusion

For most of American history, people with disabilities were explicitly or effectively excluded from voting:

- **Colonial era through 19th century**: Many states had "idiot" and "insane person" exclusion clauses in their constitutions, reflecting the prevailing view that people with mental disabilities lacked the capacity for civic participation
- **Physical barriers**: Before the disability rights movement, the built environment was designed without consideration for physical accessibility. Polling places in upper floors, buildings with steps, heavy doors, and no accessible restrooms made voting physically impossible for many
- **Institutionalization**: Millions of Americans with disabilities were confined to institutions (state hospitals, developmental centers, nursing homes) where voting was not facilitated and often actively prevented
- **Guardianship as total incapacitation**: Guardianship laws treated the appointment of a guardian as a declaration of total legal incapacity, automatically stripping all civil rights including voting

### The Intersection with Other Voting Rights Movements

Disability voting rights did not develop in isolation. The broader voting rights movement created frameworks that disability advocates would later use:

- **15th Amendment (1870)**: Prohibited racial disenfranchisement but was undermined by literacy tests and other devices that also excluded people with disabilities
- **19th Amendment (1920)**: Women's suffrage expanded the franchise but did not address disability barriers
- **Voting Rights Act of 1965**: Created federal enforcement mechanisms for voting rights; disability advocates would later argue for similar enforcement of accessibility requirements
- **26th Amendment (1971)**: Lowered voting age to 18; demonstrated that the franchise could be expanded

## Key Milestones

### 1970s-1980s: The Disability Rights Movement Takes Shape

- **1973**: Rehabilitation Act Section 504 prohibits disability discrimination in federally funded programs, including election administration in jurisdictions receiving federal funds. First federal legal basis for voting accessibility.
- **1975**: *O'Brien v. Skinner*, 414 U.S. 524 (1974): Supreme Court rules that states cannot deny absentee ballots to voters confined to institutions, establishing that physical inability to reach a polling place does not extinguish the right to vote
- **1981**: United Nations International Year of Disabled Persons raises global awareness of disability rights
- **1984**: **Voting Accessibility for the Elderly and Handicapped Act (Public Law 98-435)**:
    - Requires accessible polling places for federal elections
    - Mandates reasonable modifications to ensure accessibility
    - Requires provision of aids for voters who are blind or have low vision
    - Allows alternative voting methods when polling places cannot be made accessible
    - **Limitation**: Weak enforcement mechanism; no private right of action; relies on DOJ enforcement

### 1990s: The ADA Era

- **1990**: **Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)**:
    - Title II prohibits disability discrimination by state and local governments, including election administration
    - Applies to all aspects of voting: registration, polling places, voting equipment, voter information
    - Stronger enforcement than 1984 Act: private right of action, DOJ enforcement, damages available
    - **Impact**: Provides the legal foundation for all subsequent voting accessibility litigation and policy
- **1993**: **National Voter Registration Act (NVRA, "Motor Voter")**:
    - Requires voter registration at disability service agencies (designated as "voter registration agencies")
    - Mandates accessible registration forms
    - **Limitation**: Compliance by disability agencies is inconsistent; many do not provide registration as required
- **1999**: *Olmstead v. L.C.*, 527 U.S. 581 (1999): Supreme Court rules that unnecessary institutionalization is discrimination under the ADA. While not a voting case, *Olmstead* supports the principle that people with disabilities belong in the community and should have access to community activities including voting.

### 2000s: HAVA and Technology

- **2000**: Florida recount debacle highlights problems with voting technology broadly, including butterfly ballots that were particularly confusing for voters with cognitive and visual disabilities
- **2002**: **Help America Vote Act (HAVA)**:
    - Requires at least one accessible voting system per polling place
    - Establishes Election Assistance Commission (EAC) to develop voting technology standards
    - Provides $3.86 billion in federal funding for election administration, including accessibility improvements
    - Establishes Voluntary Voting System Guidelines (VVSG) with accessibility standards
    - **Limitation**: "At least one" device per polling place is a minimal requirement; VVSG is voluntary; funding was front-loaded and has not been sustained
- **2006**: *National Federation of the Blind v. Volusia County*, landmark settlement requiring accessible voting equipment in Florida

### 2010s: Enforcement and Backlash

- **2010-2016**: DOJ Voting Section under Obama administration conducts aggressive polling place accessibility surveys and enforcement actions; settles with over 30 jurisdictions
- **2012**: **Presidential Commission on Election Administration** (Bauer-Ginsberg Commission) recommends improvements to voting accessibility, including expanded online voter registration and improved poll worker training
- **2013**: *Shelby County v. Holder*, 570 U.S. 529 (2013): Supreme Court strikes down Section 5 preclearance of the Voting Rights Act. While not a disability case, the loss of preclearance reduces one tool for monitoring voting changes that could affect voters with disabilities
- **2015**: Revised VVSG standards include enhanced accessibility requirements for voting systems
- **2016**: Several states begin adopting remote accessible vote-by-mail (RAVBM) systems for voters with disabilities

### 2020s: Pandemic, Expansion, and Backlash

- **2020**: COVID-19 pandemic forces rapid expansion of mail voting, curbside voting, and early voting. Many accommodations long sought by disability advocates become available to all voters:
    - Expanded mail voting access
    - Curbside voting at many polling places
    - Extended early voting periods
    - Reduced crowding through scheduling
- **2021-2023**: Post-2020 backlash produces wave of voting restriction laws in multiple states. Many provisions disproportionately affect voters with disabilities:
    - Georgia (SB 202): Restricts ballot assistance, limits drop boxes
    - Texas (SB 1): Restricts mail voting eligibility, adds ID requirements
    - Multiple states: Limit curbside voting, reduce early voting hours, add signature match requirements
- **2022**: ABLE Age Adjustment Act signed into law (relevant for SSI recipients who are also voters)
- **2023**: *National Federation of the Blind v. Lamone* (Maryland): Court orders accessible ballot delivery system for blind voters
- **2024**: Multiple states adopt or expand RAVBM systems; DOJ enforcement activity varies by administration priorities
- **2025**: Ongoing litigation challenging voting restrictions that disproportionately burden voters with disabilities

## Lessons from History

### What Has Worked

- **ADA private right of action**: The ability of individual voters and advocacy organizations to sue jurisdictions for inaccessibility has been the most effective enforcement mechanism
- **DOJ enforcement**: Active periods of DOJ enforcement (particularly 2010-2016) produced measurable improvements in polling place accessibility in targeted jurisdictions
- **Technology standards**: HAVA's requirement for accessible voting equipment, despite implementation problems, established the principle that accessible voting is a federal requirement
- **NVRA disability agency registration**: Where implemented effectively, registration at disability service agencies reaches populations who might not otherwise register

### What Has Failed

- **Voluntary compliance**: Absent enforcement, most jurisdictions do not achieve and maintain accessibility
- **One-time funding**: HAVA's front-loaded funding model did not create sustainable infrastructure for ongoing accessibility
- **Weak enforcement mechanisms**: The 1984 Act's lack of a private right of action made it largely unenforceable
- **Piecemeal approach**: Separate laws for registration (NVRA), equipment (HAVA), physical access (ADA), and information (ADA, HAVA) create gaps and confusion

### Recurring Patterns

- **Crisis-driven improvement**: Major improvements in voting accessibility have consistently followed crises (2000 election, COVID-19 pandemic) rather than proactive policy
- **Implementation gap**: Strong legal requirements exist on paper but are poorly implemented at the local level due to insufficient funding, training, and oversight
- **Backlash after expansion**: Periods of expanded voting access are followed by restrictive legislation, with disability access often collateral damage in broader voting access battles
- **Technology as double-edged sword**: Each generation of voting technology creates new accessibility opportunities and new accessibility barriers
