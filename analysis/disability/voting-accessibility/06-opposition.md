# Voting Accessibility: Opposition Analysis

## Primary Opponents

### Election Security Hardliners

**Who**: Segments of the election security community, some cybersecurity experts, organizations like Verified Voting (on electronic ballot delivery specifically)

**Arguments Against Accessibility Reforms**:

- Electronic ballot delivery (RAVBM) creates security vulnerabilities
- Remote voting of any kind cannot be fully secured
- Ballot marking devices create a "barcode problem" (voters cannot verify machine-marked ballots)
- Accessibility accommodations create potential for fraud or manipulation

**Underlying Concerns**:

- Genuine security concerns about electronic ballot transmission are legitimate, though they apply narrowly to RAVBM systems and not to most accessibility reforms
- "Security vs. accessibility" framing often masks broader opposition to expanding voting access
- Technical security concerns are sometimes overstated relative to the risk of disenfranchising millions of voters

**Opposition Intensity**: **Moderate**

- Strongest on electronic ballot delivery and online voting
- Weaker on physical accessibility, poll worker training, and other non-technology reforms
- Some security experts actively support accessible voting when implemented with appropriate safeguards

**Counterarguments**:

- The alternative to RAVBM for blind voters is no private, independent ballot at all
- Physical accessibility improvements have no security implications
- Poll worker training has no security implications
- The risk of disenfranchising millions of voters is a greater threat to election integrity than the marginal security risk of accessible technology

### State Legislatures Restricting Voting Access

**Who**: State legislators who have passed voting restriction laws since 2020 (primarily in Republican-led states)

**Arguments Against Accessibility Reforms**:

- Mail voting restrictions are necessary for election security
- Ballot assistance limitations prevent ballot harvesting and coercion
- Signature match requirements verify voter identity
- Standardizing voting procedures prevents fraud

**Underlying Concerns**:

- Voter suppression strategy: restricting access for populations that tend to vote for the opposing party
- Disability is rarely the stated target, but disability communities bear disproportionate burden
- Political calculation: the disability voting bloc is not perceived as large enough or organized enough to impose electoral costs for inaccessibility

**Opposition Intensity**: **Moderate (indirect)**

- Few legislators openly oppose disability voting access
- Opposition is channeled through general voting restriction laws that have disparate impact
- Will resist packaging disability accessibility with broader voting expansion bills

**Counterarguments**:

- The ADA requires that voting be accessible regardless of state election law preferences
- Disability-specific accommodations (curbside voting, RAVBM for blind voters) can be separated from broader voting access debates
- Restricting voting access for people with disabilities is legally and morally indefensible

### Guardianship System Defenders

**Who**: Some guardians, guardian advocacy organizations, probate judges, professional guardian associations

**Arguments Against Guardianship Voting Reform**:

- People under guardianship lack the capacity to make informed voting decisions
- Removing voting restrictions risks manipulation of vulnerable voters
- Courts should determine who can vote based on capacity assessments
- Protecting incapacitated individuals from exploitation includes protecting them from undue political influence

**Underlying Concerns**:

- Professional interest in maintaining the scope of guardianship authority
- Paternalistic assumptions about the capacities of people with intellectual disabilities and dementia
- Concern about liability if a guardian "allows" a ward to vote and the vote is challenged
- Institutional inertia: guardianship systems have operated this way for centuries

**Opposition Intensity**: **Low to Moderate**

- Guardianship reform movement (supported by ABA, NCD, and bipartisan groups) has weakened organized opposition
- Most probate judges who have examined the issue support preserving voting rights
- Opposition is passive (maintaining status quo) rather than active (campaigning against reform)

**Counterarguments**:

- No capacity test is applied to non-disabled voters, regardless of education, knowledge, or cognitive ability
- The risk of voter manipulation is speculative; documented cases are extremely rare
- International consensus (UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities) supports the right to vote regardless of guardianship status
- Research shows that most people under guardianship can express political preferences when given appropriate support

## Secondary Opponents

### Fiscal Conservatives (on Funding)

**Who**: Budget hawks who oppose increased federal spending on election administration

**Arguments**:

- Election administration should be funded by state and local governments
- Federal mandates without federal funding are inappropriate
- HAVA already provided significant funding (though most was spent years ago)

**Opposition Intensity**: **Low**

- Few oppose accessibility in principle
- Opposition is to the cost, not the goal
- Can be addressed by including funding in the reform package

### Technology Purists

**Who**: Election security researchers who favor hand-marked paper ballots exclusively

**Arguments**:

- All ballot marking devices (not just BMDs for voters with disabilities) should be eliminated
- Hand-marked paper ballots are the most secure and auditable voting method
- BMDs create a "barcode" that voters cannot verify
- Technology introduces points of failure that do not exist with paper

**Opposition Intensity**: **Low to Moderate (on BMDs specifically)**

- Acknowledge the accessibility problem but argue it should be solved through non-technological means (human assistance rather than machines)
- Most accept that some voters with disabilities need BMDs even in a hand-marked paper ballot system

**Counterarguments**:

- Human assistance eliminates ballot secrecy for voters with disabilities
- The choice between security and independence is a false dilemma; BMDs with voter-verifiable paper records address both
- Excluding BMDs from polling places eliminates the only independent voting method for many voters with disabilities

## Opposition Arguments and Responses

| Opposition Argument | Response |
|---------------------|----------|
| "Accessible voting equipment is too expensive" | The cost of democracy includes serving all citizens; HAVA-funded equipment is aging regardless; replacement cycles create opportunity for accessibility upgrades |
| "RAVBM creates security risks" | Paper ballot return mitigates electronic security concerns; the alternative (no accessible mail ballot) is worse than the marginal security risk |
| "People under guardianship can't understand elections" | No capacity test is applied to any other voter; the standard for voting is expressing a preference, not passing an exam |
| "Ballot assistance rules prevent fraud" | Restricting assistance prevents voting by people who need it; fraud through ballot assistance is vanishingly rare and can be addressed through targeted safeguards |
| "Physical accessibility is too expensive for small jurisdictions" | Federal funding can offset costs; alternative voting sites can be used; the ADA has been law for 35 years; cost is not a defense to civil rights violations |
| "Accessible voting is a niche issue" | 70 million Americans have disabilities; accessibility also benefits elderly voters, voters with temporary injuries, and voters who are not fluent in English |

## Strategic Vulnerability of Opposition

The opposition to voting accessibility reforms is politically weak:

- **No one openly opposes disability voting rights**: Opposition must be packaged in neutral terms (security, cost, federalism), which limits its political potency
- **Bipartisan ADA legacy**: The ADA was signed by a Republican president and has always had bipartisan support; framing accessibility as an ADA compliance issue invokes this legacy
- **Growing disability electorate**: As the population ages, the share of voters with age-related disabilities increases, making accessibility a majoritarian concern
- **Powerful moral narrative**: Stories of voters turned away from polling places, denied accessible equipment, or disenfranchised by guardianship are politically compelling and difficult to argue against
- **Legal vulnerability**: Jurisdictions that fail to provide accessible voting face ADA litigation they are likely to lose
