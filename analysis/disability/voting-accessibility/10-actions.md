# Voting Accessibility: Actions

## Individual Actions

### Educate Yourself

- Learn your rights as a voter with a disability under the ADA, HAVA, and your state's voting laws
- Visit <https://www.ada.gov/topics/voting/> for DOJ resources on voting accessibility
- Check your state's election website for accessible voting options (early voting, curbside voting, RAVBM)
- Understand your state's guardianship laws and how they affect voting rights
- Review the DOJ polling place accessibility checklist to know what to expect at your polling place
- Follow disability voting organizations (AAPD REV UP, NFB, The Arc) for current developments

### Report Barriers

- Document inaccessible polling places with photos, descriptions, dates, and locations
- File ADA complaints about polling place inaccessibility with the DOJ at <https://www.ada.gov/complaint/>
- Contact your state's Protection and Advocacy organization for voter assistance (find yours at <https://www.ndrn.org/about/ndrn-member-agencies/>)
- Report polling place problems to the Election Protection hotline: 1-866-OUR-VOTE (866-687-8683) or 1-888-VE-Y-VOTA (Spanish)
- Contact your county clerk or board of elections to report equipment failures, untrained poll workers, or missing accommodations
- If you are turned away from voting or denied an accommodation, request a provisional ballot and file a complaint

### Exercise Your Right to Vote

- Register to vote; contact your state's disability service agency, which is required to offer registration under the NVRA
- Request accommodations in advance: contact your election office before Election Day to confirm accessible equipment, curbside voting, or other accommodations are available
- Request an accessible mail ballot if your state offers RAVBM (remote accessible vote-by-mail)
- Bring someone to assist you if needed; federal law guarantees the right to ballot assistance from a person of your choosing (except your employer or union representative)
- Use early voting if available to avoid Election Day crowds and longer lines
- If your polling place is inaccessible, demand curbside voting; this is required as a reasonable modification under the ADA

## Community Actions

### Local Organizing

- Join the AAPD REV UP campaign to register and mobilize voters with disabilities in your community
- Organize voter registration drives at disability service agencies, independent living centers, and group homes
- Conduct community polling place accessibility audits using the DOJ checklist before elections
- Partner with your local independent living center (ILC) on voter education and transportation to polls
- Build coalitions with local chapters of the League of Women Voters, Common Cause, and NAACP for joint accessibility monitoring

### Awareness Campaigns

- Organize community events during National Disability Voter Registration Week (third week of July)
- Create and distribute accessible voter guides in plain language, large print, audio, and Easy Read formats
- Use social media to document and publicize polling place barriers (#CripTheVote, #DisabilityVote, #REVUP)
- Engage local media in covering disability voting barriers in your community
- Invite voters with disabilities to share their experiences at community forums and town halls
- Host candidate forums in accessible venues and ensure candidates address disability voting policy

### Voter Assistance

- Volunteer with your state's Protection and Advocacy (P&A) agency on Election Day to monitor polling places
- Help voters with disabilities navigate voter registration and ballot request processes
- Provide or coordinate transportation to accessible polling places or early voting sites
- Train volunteers on disability awareness and accessible voter assistance techniques
- Create a community resource list of accessible voting options in your jurisdiction

## Political Actions

### Contact Your Representatives

**Call your members of Congress about voting accessibility:**

- Support the Accessible Voting Act (polling place standards, equipment upgrades, training)
- Support federal funding for voting accessibility improvements
- Support guardianship voting rights reform (Guardianship Voting Rights Restoration Act)
- Support RAVBM expansion so every state offers accessible electronic ballot delivery
- Oppose voting restriction laws that disproportionately burden voters with disabilities

### Template Email

```text
Subject: Protect Voting Rights for Americans with Disabilities

Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name],

I am writing as a constituent to urge you to support stronger
voting accessibility for Americans with disabilities.

Despite the Americans with Disabilities Act, the Help America
Vote Act, and the Voting Accessibility for the Elderly and
Handicapped Act, the disability voting gap remains approximately
6 percentage points. An estimated 2-3 million Americans with
disabilities are effectively prevented from voting by physical
barriers at polling places, inaccessible voting equipment,
inaccessible mail voting procedures, and outdated guardianship
laws that strip voting rights.

I ask you to:

1. Support federal legislation requiring polling place
   accessibility standards with funding for compliance
2. Support funding for voting equipment that meets modern
   accessibility standards
3. Support the expansion of remote accessible vote-by-mail
   (RAVBM) to all states
4. Support legislation protecting the voting rights of
   people under guardianship
5. Oppose any voting restriction legislation that would
   disproportionately burden voters with disabilities

The right to vote is the most fundamental right of citizenship.
Seventy million Americans with disabilities deserve equal access
to the ballot box.

Thank you for your attention to this important issue.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your Address]
```

### Vote and Engage

- Research candidates' positions on disability rights and voting accessibility
- Participate in the AAPD REV UP campaign (Register, Educate, Vote, Use your Power)

- Volunteer for campaigns of candidates who support voting accessibility

## Talking Points

### For General Audiences

- Seventy million Americans have disabilities; the disability voting gap is approximately 6 percentage points, representing 2-3 million effectively suppressed votes
- Sixty to eighty percent of polling places have physical barriers that impede voters with disabilities
- The ADA, HAVA, and other federal laws require accessible voting, but enforcement has been grossly inadequate
- Voting accessibility benefits everyone: the elderly, people with temporary injuries, parents with strollers, and voters with limited English proficiency
- No American should have to choose between their dignity and their vote

### For Election Officials

- Federal law requires accessible polling places, functioning accessible voting equipment, and reasonable modifications for voters with disabilities
- The DOJ polling place accessibility checklist provides a clear, actionable standard for compliance
- Poll worker training on disability accommodations reduces complaints, litigation risk, and voter frustration
- Accessible voting options (curbside voting, RAVBM, early voting) reduce Election Day crowding and problems for all voters
- Federal funding is available (and proposed to increase) for accessibility improvements

### For Policymaker Audiences

- The ADA, HAVA, and the Voting Accessibility Act collectively establish a federal right to accessible voting, but implementation has lagged for 35 years
- The disability voting gap has barely changed in two decades despite these laws
- Thirty-nine states still restrict voting based on guardianship status, disenfranchising hundreds of thousands of Americans without any individualized assessment of voting capacity
- International comparisons show that universal design in voting dramatically reduces the disability voting gap (Sweden: less than 1 point; Australia: 2 points; U.S.: 6 points)
- Federal investment in voting accessibility is a modest cost relative to the democratic participation gains

## Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Reality |
|--------------|---------|
| "Polling places are already accessible" | GAO found 60% have at least one barrier; disability organizations report the figure is higher |
| "People with disabilities can just vote by mail" | Standard mail ballots are inaccessible for blind, low-vision, and dexterity-impaired voters; many states lack accessible electronic alternatives |
| "Accessible voting equipment is always available" | Equipment is frequently broken, missing components, or staffed by workers who cannot operate it |
| "People under guardianship cannot understand elections" | Many can and do express political preferences; no capacity test is applied to other voters |
| "Voting accessibility is too expensive" | Federal funding proposals are modest ($300-400 million/year); the cost of democracy includes serving all citizens |
| "Accessible voting creates security risks" | Physical accessibility has no security implications; RAVBM returns paper ballots; the security risk is far smaller than the disenfranchisement risk |
| "This is a small issue affecting few people" | Seventy million Americans have disabilities; accessibility also benefits elderly and temporarily injured voters |
| "Poll workers already know how to help" | Only 35-40% can correctly operate accessible equipment; disability-specific training is minimal |
| "The disability vote doesn't matter politically" | The disability voting bloc is potentially the largest demographic group; closing the gap could shift election outcomes |
| "Curbside voting solves everything" | Curbside voting eliminates privacy, singles out voters with disabilities, and is often unavailable or poorly implemented |
