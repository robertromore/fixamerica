# Voting Accessibility: Solutions

## Solution 1: Universal Polling Place Accessibility Standards

### Description

Establish and enforce mandatory, specific physical accessibility standards for all polling places, with federal funding for compliance and a clear timeline for achieving universal accessibility.

### How It Works

- **Mandatory accessibility surveys**: Every polling place surveyed for ADA compliance before each federal election using standardized DOJ/EAC checklist
- **Remediation timeline**: Barriers identified must be remediated within one election cycle; temporary modifications (portable ramps, signage, lighting) required for the next election
- **Alternative site requirement**: If a polling place cannot be made accessible, the jurisdiction must relocate to an accessible facility or provide alternative voting (curbside, early voting center)
- **Federal funding**: Grants to jurisdictions for accessibility upgrades, prioritizing under-resourced communities

### Evidence

- DOJ polling place accessibility program demonstrated that systematic surveys plus enforcement produce measurable improvement (50+ settlements since 2000)
- GAO-18-4 documented specific barrier types and recommended systematic surveys
- Jurisdictions that conduct regular accessibility surveys (e.g., under DOJ consent decrees) maintain significantly higher accessibility rates

### Estimated Cost

- **Accessibility surveys**: $50-100 million per federal election cycle (contracted survey teams)
- **Remediation grants**: $200-500 million over 10 years (physical improvements to polling places)
- **Alternative site costs**: Minimal marginal cost (most jurisdictions already maintain backup polling locations)

### Challenges

- 10,000+ local jurisdictions makes comprehensive enforcement difficult
- Building owners (churches, schools) may resist modifications to their property
- Temporary modifications (portable ramps) are less effective than permanent modifications
- Rural areas may have very limited accessible facility options

### Implementation

- EAC develops standardized accessibility survey tool (Year 1)
- States conduct initial surveys of all polling places (Year 1-2)
- Federal grants distributed for remediation (Year 2-4)
- Full compliance required by third federal election after enactment
- Ongoing monitoring through random audits and complaint investigation

## Solution 2: Next-Generation Accessible Voting Equipment

### Description

Update VVSG accessibility standards, fund replacement of aging equipment, and require meaningful usability testing with voters with disabilities as a condition of certification.

### How It Works

- **Updated VVSG accessibility standards**: Require audio ballot completion within 25 minutes (current systems often take 45-60 minutes), sip-and-puff compatibility, screen reader integration, adjustable font size and contrast, multilingual audio, and tactile navigation
- **Usability testing requirement**: No voting system may be certified without usability testing by voters representing at least 5 disability types (mobility, vision, hearing, cognitive, dexterity)
- **Equipment replacement grants**: Federal funding for jurisdictions to replace pre-2015 voting equipment with systems meeting updated standards
- **Poll worker competency requirement**: Poll workers must demonstrate ability to set up and operate accessible equipment before serving

### Evidence

- VVSG 2.0 includes enhanced accessibility requirements but adoption is voluntary and slow
- Usability testing with disabled voters has been recommended by NCD, AAPD, and EAC advisory committees
- Research demonstrates that well-designed BMDs can reduce audio ballot time by 50-60% (MIT Election Lab studies)

### Estimated Cost

- **Equipment replacement**: $1-2 billion over 10 years (shared federal/state/local funding)
- **Standards development**: $5-10 million (EAC and NIST)
- **Usability testing**: $2-5 million per certification cycle
- **Poll worker training**: $50-100 million per election cycle

### Challenges

- Voting equipment market is concentrated; vendors may resist stringent standards
- Equipment procurement cycles are long (3-5 years from RFP to deployment)
- Poll worker training is a recurring cost that must be sustained each election
- Some jurisdictions resist technology change

### Implementation

- Updated VVSG standards (Year 1-2)
- Vendor compliance period (Year 2-4)
- Federal grant program for equipment replacement (Year 2-8)
- Poll worker training program development (Year 1-2)
- Full implementation within 3 federal election cycles

## Solution 3: Universal Accessible Vote-by-Mail

### Description

Require all states that offer mail voting to provide an accessible electronic ballot delivery and marking system for voters with disabilities, with federal standards and funding.

### How It Works

- **Accessible ballot delivery**: All jurisdictions must offer electronic ballot delivery (accessible PDF or web-based ballot marking tool) to any voter with a disability who requests it
- **Accessible ballot marking**: The electronic system must be compatible with screen readers, support keyboard-only navigation, meet WCAG 2.1 AA standards, and allow voters to mark their choices electronically before printing for return
- **Flexible return options**: Voters may return accessible ballots by mail, drop box, in person, or (with appropriate security measures) by electronic transmission
- **Signature alternative**: Voters whose disabilities prevent consistent handwriting signatures may use electronic signatures, marks, or other verification methods

### Evidence

- California's RAVBM system serves as a model for accessible electronic ballot delivery
- Multiple federal courts have ordered accessible ballot delivery for blind voters
- *National Federation of the Blind v. Lamone* (Maryland, 2023) found right to accessible ballot delivery under ADA

### Estimated Cost

- **System development and deployment**: $50-100 million (scalable across jurisdictions)
- **Ongoing maintenance**: $10-20 million per year
- **Security audits**: $5-10 million per election cycle

### Challenges

- Election security concerns about electronic ballot delivery (addressed by paper return requirement)
- State law conflicts (some states prohibit electronic ballot transmission)
- Cost for small jurisdictions
- Technology access gap (some voters with disabilities lack internet access or technology)

### Implementation

- Federal standards for accessible ballot delivery systems (Year 1)
- EAC certification of approved systems (Year 1-2)
- State implementation with federal grants (Year 2-4)
- Full availability required by third federal election after enactment

## Solution 4: Guardianship Voting Rights Protection

### Description

Prohibit disenfranchisement based solely on guardianship status; require specific court finding of lack of voting capacity before voting rights can be restricted.

### How It Works

- **Presumption of voting capacity**: All persons, including those under guardianship, are presumed to have the capacity to vote
- **Prohibition on automatic disenfranchisement**: Guardianship alone does not affect voting rights
- **Specific capacity finding required**: Voting rights may only be restricted by a court order that specifically addresses voting capacity, made after a hearing at which the person has legal representation and the opportunity to demonstrate capacity
- **Capacity standard**: The capacity to vote requires only the ability to express a preference for a candidate or issue; it does not require understanding of policy or political knowledge
- **Restoration process**: Any person whose voting rights have been restricted may petition for restoration at any time, with the burden on the state to demonstrate continued incapacity

### Evidence

- NCD has recommended that guardianship never automatically affect voting rights (NCD, *Beyond Guardianship*, 2018)
- American Bar Association supports the presumption of voting capacity for people under guardianship (ABA Resolution 113, 2007)
- UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (Article 29) requires equal political participation
- 11 states + DC already impose no voting restriction based on guardianship, with no documented problems

### Estimated Cost

- Minimal direct cost (statutory change)
- Modest cost for court hearings (offset by reduced guardianship litigation)
- Voter registration assistance cost: $5-10 million nationally

### Challenges

- Requires state legislation in 39 states (or federal legislation preempting state guardianship voting restrictions)
- Resistance from guardianship system stakeholders who view voting restriction as protective
- Political sensitivity around "incompetent" voters
- Implementation requires voter registration systems to remove guardianship-based restrictions

### Implementation

- Model state legislation (Year 1)
- Federal legislation if state-by-state approach is insufficient (Year 2-3)
- Voter registration database cleanup (Year 1-2 after enactment)
- Voter education for newly restored voters (ongoing)

## Solution 5: Comprehensive Poll Worker Training Program

### Description

Develop and mandate a national poll worker disability training curriculum, with competency requirements and accountability mechanisms.

### How It Works

- **National curriculum**: EAC develops standardized disability training module (2-3 hours) covering: disability types, legal requirements, equipment operation, communication strategies, attitude and dignity, accommodation procedures
- **Competency testing**: Poll workers must pass a practical test demonstrating ability to set up accessible equipment and provide accommodations
- **Disability community trainers**: People with disabilities serve as trainers and evaluators
- **Accountability**: Jurisdictions must report accommodation requests and outcomes; patterns of failure trigger remedial training

### Evidence

- Research shows that poll worker training is the strongest predictor of voter experience for voters with disabilities (Schur and Adya, Rutgers, 2020)
- Jurisdictions with comprehensive disability training programs (e.g., those under DOJ consent decrees) show significantly higher voter satisfaction among voters with disabilities
- "Secret shopper" programs using voters with disabilities to test polling place accessibility have proven effective

### Estimated Cost

- **Curriculum development**: $5-10 million (one-time)
- **Training delivery**: $50-100 million per federal election cycle (integrated into existing poll worker training)
- **Competency testing**: Integrated into training; minimal additional cost
- **Monitoring**: $10-20 million per election cycle (secret shopper programs)

### Challenges

- Poll worker recruitment is already difficult; adding training requirements may reduce volunteer pool
- Jurisdictions resist federally mandated training
- Training must be repeated each election due to poll worker turnover
- Competency testing may discourage volunteers

### Implementation

- Curriculum development with disability community input (Year 1)
- Pilot in 10 states (Year 2)
- National rollout (Year 3)
- Ongoing refinement based on voter feedback

## Solution 6: Accessible Election Information

### Description

Require that all election-related information -- voter guides, candidate statements, ballot measures, election websites, voter registration portals -- meet accessibility standards.

### How It Works

- **WCAG 2.1 AA compliance**: All election websites and digital tools must meet Web Content Accessibility Guidelines
- **Plain language voter guides**: All ballot measures and candidate information provided in plain language versions (grade 6-8 reading level)
- **Multiple formats**: Voter information available in large print, audio, Braille, Easy Read, ASL video, and digital accessible formats
- **Accessible voter registration**: Online voter registration must be fully accessible; paper registration must be available in accessible formats

### Evidence

- WebAIM research shows that 97% of government websites fail basic accessibility tests
- Plain language voter guides increase comprehension and participation among voters with cognitive disabilities and limited English proficiency
- Oregon's accessible voter pamphlet program demonstrates feasibility

### Estimated Cost

- **Website accessibility**: $50-100 million nationally (one-time remediation plus ongoing maintenance)
- **Accessible voter guides**: $20-50 million per election cycle
- **Accessible registration**: $10-20 million (integrated into existing systems)

### Challenges

- 10,000+ local election websites to remediate
- Ongoing compliance requires dedicated accessibility expertise
- Plain language translations of complex ballot measures require skilled writers
- ASL video translation of all voter information is resource-intensive

## International Comparisons

| Country | Accessible Voting Features | Disability Voting Gap |
|---------|---------------------------|----------------------|
| United States | HAVA BMDs, variable state mail voting, weak enforcement | ~6 points |
