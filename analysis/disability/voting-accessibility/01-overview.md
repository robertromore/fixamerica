# Voting Accessibility: Overview

## Executive Summary

The right to vote is the foundational act of democratic citizenship, yet millions of Americans with disabilities face persistent barriers to exercising that right. Despite landmark legislation -- the Voting Rights Act of 1965, the Voting Accessibility for the Elderly and Handicapped Act of 1984, the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990, and the Help America Vote Act of 2002 -- the gap between the voting rate of people with disabilities and the general population remains stubbornly large. In the 2024 general election, the disability voting gap was approximately 6 percentage points, representing an estimated 2-3 million votes that were effectively suppressed by physical, technological, informational, and attitudinal barriers.

The problems are structural and pervasive. Polling places remain physically inaccessible in an estimated 60-80% of locations nationwide, with barriers ranging from missing ramps and narrow doorways to inadequate parking and inaccessible paths of travel. Voting machines that comply with HAVA's accessibility requirements are frequently broken, unavailable, or used by poll workers who do not know how to operate them. Voters who are blind or have low vision face particular challenges: despite the legal requirement for accessible ballot marking devices, many jurisdictions still cannot provide a truly private and independent voting experience for blind voters. Voters with intellectual and cognitive disabilities encounter competency challenges, confusing ballot design, and poll worker attitudes that range from unhelpful to actively hostile. Voters with psychiatric disabilities are disproportionately affected by overly broad state laws linking voting rights to guardianship or mental competency determinations.

The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated adoption of vote-by-mail and curbside voting, which many disability advocates had long championed as accessibility accommodations. However, the subsequent backlash against expanded voting access has threatened these gains, with several states imposing new restrictions on mail voting, drop boxes, and ballot assistance that disproportionately burden voters with disabilities. The tension between election security and voting accessibility is largely false -- accommodations that help voters with disabilities also benefit elderly voters, voters with temporary injuries, and voters who face logistical barriers -- but it has become politically charged in ways that threaten progress.

Voting accessibility is simultaneously a civil rights issue, a democratic participation imperative, and a test of whether the United States takes seriously its commitment to equal citizenship for people with disabilities.

## Scope

### What This Topic Covers

- **Physical accessibility of polling places**: ADA compliance, architectural barriers, parking, paths of travel, interior layout
- **Voting machine accessibility**: Accessible ballot marking devices, audio interfaces, tactile controls, sip-and-puff devices
- **Mail and absentee voting accessibility**: Ballot design, envelope requirements, signature requirements, accessible electronic ballot delivery
- **Voter registration accessibility**: Online registration, accessible forms, registration assistance
- **Information accessibility**: Voter guides, candidate information, election websites, communication access
- **Poll worker training**: Disability awareness, equipment operation, accommodation provision
- **Legal framework**: Federal and state laws governing voting accessibility, enforcement mechanisms
- **Guardianship and competency**: State laws restricting voting rights based on mental capacity determinations
- **Ballot assistance**: Laws governing who can assist voters and under what circumstances
- **Digital accessibility**: Election websites, online voter tools, accessible election information

## Core Tensions

### Security vs. Accessibility

Measures designed to enhance election security -- voter ID requirements, signature matching, limitations on ballot assistance, restrictions on mail voting -- often disproportionately burden voters with disabilities. Balancing legitimate security interests with the constitutional right of people with disabilities to vote is the central tension in voting accessibility policy.

### Uniformity vs. Local Control

Elections are administered by approximately 10,000 local jurisdictions with vastly different resources, expertise, and commitment to accessibility. Federal and state accessibility mandates provide a floor, but enforcement is uneven and many jurisdictions lack the funding or knowledge to achieve compliance. Imposing uniform standards risks unfunded mandates; allowing local discretion risks perpetuating inequality.

### Technology Promises vs. Realities

Technology can dramatically improve voting accessibility -- electronic ballot marking devices, screen readers, remote accessible vote-by-mail systems -- but it also creates new barriers (inaccessible websites, equipment failures, digital divides) and new security concerns (electronic ballot transmission, online voting). The promise of technology often exceeds the reality of implementation.

### Individual Accommodation vs. Universal Design

The accommodation model requires voters with disabilities to identify themselves, request assistance, and navigate a process designed for non-disabled voters. The universal design model designs voting systems that work for everyone from the start, reducing stigma and administrative burden. The tension between these approaches shapes policy debates about voting equipment, ballot design, and voting procedures.

## Vision of Success

In an accessible democracy, every citizen with a disability can register to vote, obtain information about candidates and issues, access their polling place, mark and cast their ballot privately and independently, and verify that their vote was counted -- all without requiring accommodations that single them out or depend on the good will of individual election workers. Polling places are universally designed with automatic doors, accessible paths, adjustable-height surfaces, and clear signage. Every voting method -- in person, by mail, early voting -- is designed from the start to accommodate the full range of human abilities. Voter registration is automatic and accessible. Ballot design follows evidence-based readability standards. Guardianship no longer carries automatic disenfranchisement. Poll workers are trained, equipped, and motivated to serve all voters. The disability voting gap is eliminated.
