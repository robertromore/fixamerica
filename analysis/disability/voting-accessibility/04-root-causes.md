# Voting Accessibility: Root Causes

## Why Polling Places Remain Inaccessible

### Decentralized Election Administration

The United States has approximately 10,000 local election jurisdictions, each with its own budget, staff, polling place selection process, and level of commitment to accessibility. This fragmentation means:

- **No uniform standards enforcement**: Federal law sets requirements, but no federal agency has the capacity or authority to inspect every polling place
- **Resource inequality**: Wealthy suburban jurisdictions can afford accessible facilities and modern equipment; rural and urban jurisdictions with limited tax bases cannot
- **Knowledge gaps**: Many local election officials have never received training on ADA accessibility requirements for polling places
- **Polling place selection driven by cost and availability**: Jurisdictions select polling places based on which buildings are cheap and available, not which are accessible
- **Turnover**: Election officials and poll workers turn over frequently, losing institutional knowledge about accessibility

### Building Stock

Much of America's building stock predates the ADA (1990) and was never designed for accessibility. Polling places are typically located in:

- **Schools**: Often accessible in newer buildings; older schools may have steps, narrow doorways, or inadequate parking
- **Churches**: Frequently chosen for community familiarity and low cost; often inaccessible (steps, narrow aisles, no accessible restrooms)
- **Fire stations**: Equipment bays may be accessible; administrative areas often are not
- **Community centers**: Vary widely; older buildings are often inaccessible
- **Government buildings**: Generally more accessible due to Section 504 and ADA requirements, but not always

The ADA does not require that every building in the country be made accessible; it requires that public services (including voting) be provided in accessible locations. But when election officials cannot find accessible buildings in a precinct, they often use inaccessible ones with inadequate temporary modifications.

### Inadequate Enforcement

Federal voting accessibility laws rely on three enforcement mechanisms, all of which are insufficient:

1. **DOJ enforcement**: The Department of Justice can bring enforcement actions, but has limited staff (fewer than 50 attorneys in the Voting Section) and must prioritize among thousands of potential targets. DOJ enforcement also varies significantly by administration.
2. **Private litigation**: Individual voters and advocacy organizations can sue under the ADA, but litigation is expensive, slow, and addresses problems one jurisdiction at a time
3. **Election Assistance Commission**: EAC provides guidance and distributes HAVA funds but has no enforcement authority; it cannot compel compliance

## Why Voting Equipment Fails

### Market Failure in Voting Technology

The voting equipment market is small, concentrated, and poorly incentivized to prioritize accessibility:

- **Three major vendors** (ES&S, Hart InterCivic, Dominion) control approximately 90% of the U.S. voting equipment market
- **Low-volume market**: Approximately 200,000 voting machines nationwide; replacement cycles of 10-15 years
- **Cost-driven procurement**: Local jurisdictions purchase on price, not accessibility quality
- **Accessibility as afterthought**: BMDs are designed to meet minimum HAVA requirements, not to provide an excellent user experience
- **Lack of user testing**: Equipment manufacturers conduct limited usability testing with actual voters with disabilities; VVSG certification focuses on technical standards, not user experience

### Poll Worker Knowledge Gap

Accessible voting equipment is only useful if poll workers know how to set it up, operate it, and offer it to voters. The knowledge gap persists because:

- **Minimal training**: Most poll workers receive 2-4 hours of total training; disability-specific content is typically 10-15 minutes
- **Infrequent practice**: Poll workers may set up BMDs once or twice a year; knowledge atrophies
- **Attitudinal barriers**: Some poll workers view accessible equipment as unnecessary ("nobody ever uses it") and do not prioritize setting it up
- **Volunteer workforce**: Poll workers are overwhelmingly volunteers, typically elderly, with limited technology comfort
- **Turnover**: Many poll workers serve only one or two elections before leaving

### Technology Obsolescence

Voting equipment ages poorly. Many jurisdictions still use machines purchased with initial HAVA funding in the mid-2000s:

- Operating systems no longer receive security updates
- Hardware components fail and replacements are unavailable
- Accessibility features that were state-of-the-art in 2005 are inadequate by 2025 standards
- Software cannot be updated to meet current VVSG accessibility requirements

## Why Mail Voting Is Not Universally Accessible

### Paper Ballot Assumptions

The mail voting system was designed around the assumption that voters can read printed text, mark a ballot with a pen, fold the ballot, insert it into an envelope, and sign their name. Each of these assumptions fails for some disability populations:

- **Blind/low vision**: Cannot read printed ballot
- **Dexterity impairment**: Cannot mark bubbles, fold paper, insert into envelope
- **Cognitive disability**: Cannot navigate multi-step envelope procedures
- **Signature disability**: Cannot produce consistent handwriting signature

### Security vs. Accessibility Framing

Electronic ballot delivery (RAVBM) solves many mail voting accessibility problems but faces opposition on security grounds:

- Electronically marked ballots are perceived as more vulnerable to tampering (though they are ultimately printed and returned on paper)
- Some election security advocates oppose any electronic transmission of ballot information
- The tension between security and accessibility is often resolved in favor of security, leaving voters with disabilities without an accessible mail voting option
- Political opposition to expanded mail voting in general sweeps up disability-specific accommodations

### State Law Variation

Mail voting accessibility varies enormously by state:

- Some states (California, Oregon) offer RAVBM to all voters with disabilities
- Other states offer no electronic ballot delivery option
- Signature match procedures, envelope requirements, and witness requirements vary by state
- No federal standard exists for accessible mail voting

## Why Guardianship Laws Persist

### Conflation of Capacity Types

State laws that disenfranchise people under guardianship reflect a fundamental conflation of different types of capacity:

- **Legal capacity** (to sign contracts) is different from **medical capacity** (to make healthcare decisions) is different from **voting capacity** (to express a political preference)
- A person who lacks the capacity to manage complex finances may well have the capacity to choose between candidates
- Guardianship courts typically do not assess voting capacity specifically; they apply a general incapacity determination

### Ableist Assumptions

The assumption that people with intellectual disabilities, dementia, or mental illness cannot participate in democracy reflects deep-seated ableism:

- No literacy test or competency test is applied to non-disabled voters
- The complexity threshold for "understanding" an election is applied only to people with disabilities
- Many voters without disabilities make uninformed or irrational voting choices without their rights being questioned
- The paternalistic logic ("we're protecting them from being manipulated") has direct parallels to historical arguments used to disenfranchise women, Black citizens, and poor people

### Political Indifference

Guardianship voting restrictions persist because:

- The affected population is small, politically invisible, and lacks organized advocacy specifically on voting rights
- No legislator loses votes by maintaining the status quo
- Reform requires confronting uncomfortable questions about the nature of democratic participation
- Opposition (from those who fear "manipulated" votes) is vocal even though the concern is largely hypothetical

## Structural Factors

### Disability Is Not a Monolith

Voting accessibility solutions that work for one disability type may not work for others:

- **Ramp** solves wheelchair access but does not help a blind voter
- **Audio ballot** solves ballot access for blind voters but does not help deaf voters
- **Large print** helps some low-vision voters but not others
- **Easy-read** materials help some cognitive disabilities but may be perceived as patronizing by others
- **Electronic interfaces** help many disability types but create barriers for voters with technology anxiety or limited digital literacy

This diversity means that "accessible voting" requires multiple simultaneous accommodations, each with its own cost, training, and implementation requirements. The tendency to implement one accommodation and declare the problem solved leaves many disability populations unserved.

### Voting as Episodic Activity

Unlike other services (healthcare, transportation) that people with disabilities use regularly, voting happens at most a few times per year. This episodic nature means:

- Accessibility failures are noticed infrequently
- Investments in accessibility have low per-use returns, discouraging spending
- Poll workers practice accessible procedures rarely
- Equipment sits unused for months and may malfunction when needed
- Problems identified in one election may not be fixed before the next

### Political Polarization of Voting Access

Voting accessibility has become entangled in partisan battles over voting access generally:

- Republican-led voting restriction efforts often impose burdens that fall disproportionately on voters with disabilities, even when disability is not the target
- Democratic-led voting expansion efforts sometimes fail to include disability-specific provisions
- Disability voting access is rarely treated as its own issue; it is subsumed into broader voting rights debates where it receives insufficient attention
- The bipartisan consensus that voting should be accessible (few legislators openly oppose disability voting rights) does not translate into bipartisan action because accessibility measures are packaged with other provisions that face partisan opposition
