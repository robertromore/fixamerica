# Voting Accessibility: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Achieving full voting accessibility for Americans with disabilities requires legislative action across four domains: physical and technological accessibility at polling places, accessible mail voting options, protection of voting rights for people under guardianship, and comprehensive poll worker training. Federal legislation must build on the existing framework of the ADA, HAVA, and the Voting Accessibility for the Elderly and Handicapped Act while addressing the enforcement gaps, funding shortfalls, and legal loopholes that have allowed the disability voting gap to persist for 35 years. This document provides draft legislation addressing each domain, along with state model legislation, regulatory framework proposals, legal analysis, and loopholes assessment.

## Federal Legislation

### Bill 1: Accessible Voting Act

**Purpose**: Establish mandatory polling place accessibility standards, require next-generation accessible voting equipment, mandate poll worker disability training, and create a federal grant program for accessibility improvements.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Accessible Voting Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.
(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that—
    (1) approximately 70 million Americans live with disabilities,
    representing 27 percent of the adult population;
    (2) the disability voting gap remains approximately 6
    percentage points, representing 2 to 3 million votes
    effectively suppressed by accessibility barriers;
    (3) an estimated 60 to 80 percent of polling places have at
    least one physical barrier to voters with disabilities;
    (4) existing federal laws, including the Americans with
    Disabilities Act, the Help America Vote Act, and the Voting
    Accessibility for the Elderly and Handicapped Act, have
    failed to achieve accessible voting due to inadequate
    enforcement, insufficient funding, and lack of specific
    standards;
    (5) poll workers receive minimal training on disability
    accommodations, and accessible voting equipment is
    frequently broken, unavailable, or improperly operated; and
    (6) the right to vote privately and independently is
    fundamental to democratic citizenship and must be
    guaranteed to all Americans regardless of disability.
(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to—
    (1) establish enforceable, specific accessibility standards
    for all polling places used in federal elections;
    (2) require accessible voting equipment that meets modern
    usability standards;
    (3) mandate comprehensive poll worker training on disability
    accommodations; and
    (4) provide federal funding to achieve and maintain polling
    place accessibility.

SEC. 3. POLLING PLACE ACCESSIBILITY STANDARDS.
(a) MANDATORY SURVEYS.—
    (1) IN GENERAL.—Each State shall conduct an accessibility
    survey of every polling place used for federal elections
    not later than 180 days before each federal general
    election.
    (2) SURVEY TOOL.—The Election Assistance Commission, in
    consultation with the Department of Justice and the U.S.
    Access Board, shall develop a standardized polling place
    accessibility survey tool within 12 months of enactment.
    (3) SURVEY ELEMENTS.—The survey tool shall assess—
        (A) parking and passenger drop-off accessibility;
        (B) exterior path of travel (surfaces, slopes, curb cuts);
        (C) entrance accessibility (ramps, door width, door
        operation);
        (D) interior path of travel (aisles, surfaces, signage);
        (E) voting area accessibility (table height, chair
        availability, lighting, noise);
        (F) restroom accessibility;
        (G) signage and wayfinding in accessible formats; and
        (H) communication accessibility.
    (4) PUBLIC REPORTING.—Survey results shall be published on
    the State election website in an accessible format not
    later than 90 days before each federal general election.
(b) REMEDIATION REQUIREMENTS.—
    (1) IN GENERAL.—Any accessibility barrier identified by
    the survey required under subsection (a) shall be
    remediated before the next federal election.
    (2) TEMPORARY MODIFICATIONS.—If permanent remediation
    cannot be completed before the next election, the
    jurisdiction shall implement temporary modifications
    that comply with ADA standards, including but not limited
    to portable ramps, temporary signage, door propping,
    and adjusted table heights.
    (3) ALTERNATIVE SITE.—If a polling place cannot be made
    accessible through permanent remediation or temporary
    modifications, the jurisdiction shall relocate voting
    to an accessible facility.
(c) CURBSIDE VOTING.—
    (1) REQUIREMENT.—Every polling place used for federal
    elections shall provide curbside voting for any voter
    who cannot enter the polling place due to a disability.
    (2) STANDARDS.—Curbside voting shall—
        (A) be available without advance request;
        (B) provide a secret ballot;
        (C) be clearly advertised with signage at the polling
        place entrance and in voter information materials;
        (D) be staffed at all times during voting hours; and
        (E) not require the voter to state the nature of their
        disability.
(d) ENFORCEMENT.—
    (1) The Attorney General may bring a civil action to
    enforce the requirements of this section.
    (2) Any voter aggrieved by a violation of this section may
    bring a civil action for injunctive relief and reasonable
    attorney fees.

SEC. 4. ACCESSIBLE VOTING EQUIPMENT STANDARDS.
(a) UPDATED STANDARDS.—
    (1) IN GENERAL.—The Election Assistance Commission, in
    consultation with the National Institute of Standards
    and Technology and the U.S. Access Board, shall update
    the Voluntary Voting System Guidelines accessibility
    requirements within 18 months of enactment.
    (2) MANDATORY ADOPTION.—For purposes of federal elections,
    the updated accessibility requirements shall be mandatory,
    not voluntary.
    (3) MINIMUM REQUIREMENTS.—Updated standards shall require
    that accessible ballot marking devices—
        (A) allow completion of an audio ballot within 25
        minutes for a ballot of average length;
        (B) be compatible with sip-and-puff devices, adaptive
        switches, and other assistive technologies;
        (C) provide adjustable font size, contrast, and color
        settings;
        (D) include tactile navigation controls;
        (E) support multiple audio languages;
        (F) produce a voter-verifiable paper record; and
        (G) pass usability testing with voters representing at
        least 5 disability types (mobility, vision, hearing,
        cognitive, dexterity).
(b) EQUIPMENT DEPLOYMENT.—
    (1) Each polling place shall have a sufficient number of
    accessible ballot marking devices to prevent unreasonable
    wait times for voters using such devices.
    (2) At minimum, each polling place shall deploy at least
    one accessible ballot marking device for every 500
    registered voters assigned to that location.
(c) EQUIPMENT REPLACEMENT GRANTS.—
    (1) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
    $200,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2034
    for grants to States and local jurisdictions to replace
    voting equipment that does not meet the standards required
    by subsection (a).
    (2) PRIORITY.—Grant priority shall be given to jurisdictions
    with the oldest equipment, lowest income, and highest
    proportion of voters with disabilities.
(d) COMPLIANCE DEADLINE.—All voting equipment used in federal
elections shall meet the updated accessibility standards not
later than the third federal general election following the
issuance of updated standards under subsection (a)(1).

SEC. 5. POLL WORKER DISABILITY TRAINING.
(a) NATIONAL CURRICULUM.—
    (1) The Election Assistance Commission shall develop a
    standardized poll worker disability training curriculum
    within 12 months of enactment.
    (2) The curriculum shall be developed in consultation with
    disability rights organizations, voters with disabilities,
    election administrators, and the Department of Justice.
    (3) CONTENT.—The curriculum shall include—
        (A) disability types and associated voting barriers;
        (B) legal requirements under the ADA, HAVA, and this Act;
        (C) accessible voting equipment setup and operation;
        (D) communication strategies for interacting with voters
        with different disability types;
        (E) curbside voting procedures;
        (F) attitudinal awareness (dignity, respect, independence);
        and
        (G) accommodation request procedures.
    (4) DURATION.—The disability-specific training component
    shall be not less than 2 hours.
(b) MANDATORY TRAINING.—All poll workers serving at federal
elections shall complete the disability training curriculum
before serving.
(c) COMPETENCY REQUIREMENT.—Poll workers shall demonstrate the
ability to set up and operate accessible voting equipment as
a condition of serving.
(d) TRAINING GRANTS.—There is authorized to be appropriated
$15,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2034 for
grants to States to implement the training program.
(e) DISABILITY COMMUNITY TRAINERS.—Not less than 25 percent of
training sessions shall be led or co-led by persons with
disabilities.

SEC. 6. VOTING ACCESSIBILITY IMPROVEMENT GRANTS.
(a) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
$100,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2034 for
grants to States and local jurisdictions for voting
accessibility improvements.
(b) ELIGIBLE USES.—Grant funds may be used for—
    (1) permanent physical accessibility modifications to
    polling places;
    (2) temporary accessibility modifications;
    (3) purchase or lease of accessible voting equipment;
    (4) poll worker training;
    (5) accessible voter information and outreach;
    (6) accessible voter registration systems;
    (7) curbside voting infrastructure; and
    (8) accessibility survey and monitoring.
(c) MATCHING.—States shall provide a 20 percent match for
grants received under this section.
(d) REPORTING.—Each grant recipient shall submit an annual
report to the EAC on the use of funds and accessibility
outcomes achieved.

SEC. 7. ACCESSIBILITY MONITORING.
(a) ELECTION DAY MONITORING.—The Attorney General shall
establish an Election Day accessibility monitoring program
to assess compliance with this Act and the ADA at polling
places during federal elections.
(b) SECRET SHOPPER PROGRAM.—The program shall include
deployment of voters with disabilities as testers at a
statistically significant sample of polling places in each
State.
(c) ANNUAL REPORT.—The Attorney General shall submit an
annual report to Congress on voting accessibility compliance
findings.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Transforms polling place accessibility from an under-enforced mandate to a surveyed, reported, and enforceable standard with clear remediation requirements
- **Section 4**: Updates HAVA's minimal "one device per polling place" requirement to modern usability standards with mandatory adoption and usability testing
- **Section 5**: Addresses the poll worker knowledge gap identified in GAO-18-4 with a comprehensive, standardized training requirement
- **Section 6**: Provides the federal funding that has been absent since initial HAVA appropriations were spent
- **Section 7**: Creates ongoing accountability through monitoring and reporting

**Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| 10,000+ jurisdictions to survey | Phased implementation; grant funding for survey costs |
| Equipment vendors may not meet standards | 3-election-cycle compliance deadline provides development time |
| Poll worker training may reduce volunteers | Compensate for training time; integrate with existing training |
| States may resist new mandates | Federal funding offsets compliance costs; framed as ADA compliance |
| Cost concerns | $315 million/year is modest relative to election administration budgets |

**Refinements**:

- **Stronger version**: Make VVSG accessibility standards apply retroactively to all existing equipment within 2 election cycles
- **Compromise version**: Make training recommended rather than mandatory; extend equipment compliance to 4 election cycles
- **Fallback version**: Grant program only (lowest political resistance)

### Bill 2: Guardianship Voting Rights Restoration Act

**Purpose**: Prohibit automatic disenfranchisement based on guardianship status; require specific judicial finding on voting capacity before voting rights can be restricted; establish a presumption of voting capacity.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Guardianship Voting Rights
Restoration Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.
(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that—
    (1) 39 States restrict the voting rights of persons under
    guardianship, with 8 States imposing automatic
    disenfranchisement upon any guardianship appointment;
    (2) approximately 1.3 million Americans are subject to
    guardianship, many of whom have the capacity and desire
    to vote;
    (3) guardianship-based voting restrictions
    disproportionately affect persons with intellectual
    disabilities, dementia, traumatic brain injuries, and
    severe mental illness;
    (4) no competency or capacity test is applied to any other
    class of voters;
    (5) the American Bar Association, the National Council on
    Disability, and the United Nations Convention on the Rights
    of Persons with Disabilities support the right to vote
    regardless of guardianship status;
    (6) 11 States and the District of Columbia currently impose
    no voting restriction based on guardianship, with no
    documented adverse consequences; and
    (7) guardianship-based disenfranchisement violates the
    equal protection principles of the Fourteenth Amendment
    and the antidiscrimination mandate of the Americans with
    Disabilities Act.
(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to protect the
fundamental right to vote for all Americans, including those
under guardianship, by prohibiting automatic disenfranchisement
and establishing a presumption of voting capacity.

SEC. 3. PROHIBITION ON GUARDIANSHIP-BASED DISENFRANCHISEMENT.
(a) IN GENERAL.—No State or political subdivision may deny or
abridge the right of any citizen of the United States to
register to vote or to vote in any federal election on the
basis that such citizen is subject to guardianship,
conservatorship, or any similar legal arrangement.
(b) PRESUMPTION OF CAPACITY.—Every citizen, including those
subject to guardianship, conservatorship, or similar legal
arrangement, shall be presumed to have the capacity to vote.
(c) SPECIFIC JUDICIAL FINDING REQUIRED.—
    (1) IN GENERAL.—The right to vote may only be restricted
    upon a specific judicial finding, made after a hearing
    at which the person has legal representation, that the
    person lacks the capacity to express a preference for a
    candidate or ballot measure.
    (2) STANDARD.—The capacity to vote requires only the
    ability to communicate, with or without support, a
    preference for a candidate or on a ballot question. It
    does not require understanding of political issues,
    knowledge of candidates' platforms, or any level of
    cognitive function beyond the ability to indicate a
    choice.
    (3) BURDEN OF PROOF.—The burden of proof shall be on the
    party seeking to restrict voting rights, by clear and
    convincing evidence.
    (4) SEPARATE PROCEEDING.—A determination regarding voting
    capacity shall be made in a proceeding separate from the
    guardianship proceeding itself.
    (5) PERIODIC REVIEW.—Any restriction on voting rights
    shall be reviewed not less frequently than every 2 years,
    with the burden on the State to demonstrate continued
    incapacity.
(d) RESTORATION OF RIGHTS.—
    (1) Any person whose voting rights have been restricted
    may petition for restoration at any time.
    (2) The burden shall be on the State to demonstrate
    continued incapacity by clear and convincing evidence.
    (3) The court shall appoint counsel for any person
    petitioning for restoration who cannot afford
    representation.

SEC. 4. VOTER REGISTRATION REQUIREMENTS.
(a) REMOVAL PROHIBITION.—No State shall remove any person from
the voter registration rolls solely on the basis of
guardianship status.
(b) DATABASE AUDIT.—Within 12 months of enactment, each State
shall audit its voter registration database to identify and
restore any persons who were removed or prevented from
registering based solely on guardianship status.
(c) REGISTRATION ASSISTANCE.—States shall provide voter
registration assistance at guardianship proceedings and at
facilities serving persons under guardianship.

SEC. 5. ENFORCEMENT.
(a) ATTORNEY GENERAL.—The Attorney General may bring a civil
action to enforce the provisions of this Act.
(b) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—Any person aggrieved by a
violation of this Act may bring a civil action for declaratory
and injunctive relief, compensatory damages, and reasonable
attorney fees.
(c) JURISDICTION.—The district courts of the United States
shall have jurisdiction of proceedings instituted under this
Act.

SEC. 6. PREEMPTION.
This Act preempts any State or local law that is inconsistent
with the protections provided by this Act. States may provide
greater protections for the voting rights of persons under
guardianship.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect 180 days after the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3(a)-(b)**: Establishes the core principle that guardianship alone does not affect voting rights, consistent with the approach in 11 states and DC
- **Section 3(c)**: Provides a narrow, carefully defined process for the rare cases where voting capacity may genuinely be absent, with robust procedural protections
- **Section 4**: Addresses the practical problem of voter registration databases that automatically flag or remove people under guardianship
- **Section 5**: Creates enforcement mechanisms including a private right of action
- **Section 6**: Preempts the 39 state laws that currently restrict voting based on guardianship

**Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| States' rights objections | Congress has clear authority under 14th and 15th Amendments to protect voting rights |
| Fear of "manipulated" votes | Risk is speculative; documented cases are extremely rare; existing fraud laws apply |
| Court capacity for hearings | Expected volume is small; most guardianship cases will not involve voting challenges |
| Resistance from guardianship system | ABA and NCD support; growing reform movement |

### Bill 3: Remote Accessible Vote-by-Mail Expansion Act

**Purpose**: Require all states that offer mail voting to provide an accessible electronic ballot delivery and marking system for voters with disabilities.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Remote Accessible Vote-by-Mail
Expansion Act" or the "RAVBM Expansion Act".

SEC. 2. ACCESSIBLE ELECTRONIC BALLOT DELIVERY.
(a) IN GENERAL.—Any State that offers voting by mail for
federal elections shall provide an accessible electronic
ballot delivery and marking system for any voter with a
disability who requests such accommodation.
(b) SYSTEM REQUIREMENTS.—An accessible electronic ballot
delivery and marking system shall—
    (1) be compatible with commonly used screen readers and
    assistive technologies;
    (2) support keyboard-only navigation;
    (3) conform to the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines
    (WCAG) version 2.1 Level AA or successor standard;
    (4) allow the voter to mark ballot selections electronically;
    (5) produce a printed paper ballot for return by mail, drop
    box, or in person;
    (6) not require the voter to disclose the nature of their
    disability; and
    (7) undergo security certification by the EAC.
(c) SIGNATURE ALTERNATIVES.—
    (1) IN GENERAL.—A State shall accept an alternative to a
    handwritten signature on a mail ballot from any voter
    whose disability prevents the production of a consistent
    handwritten signature.
    (2) ACCEPTABLE ALTERNATIVES.—Acceptable alternatives
    include electronic signatures, marks, stamps, or
    declaration under penalty of perjury in lieu of signature.
    (3) PROHIBITION ON REJECTION.—No mail ballot shall be
    rejected solely because the signature does not match a
    previously recorded signature if the voter has a
    disability affecting handwriting and has complied with
    the alternative signature provisions of this section.

SEC. 3. GRANTS.
(a) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
$25,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2034 for
grants to States to develop, procure, and deploy accessible
electronic ballot delivery systems.
(b) SECURITY CERTIFICATION.—Grant funds may be used for
security audits and certification of accessible ballot
delivery systems.

SEC. 4. COMPLIANCE DEADLINE.
States shall comply with the requirements of section 2 not
later than the second federal general election following
the date of enactment.

SEC. 5. ENFORCEMENT.
(a) ATTORNEY GENERAL.—The Attorney General may bring a civil
action to enforce this Act.
(b) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—Any voter with a disability
aggrieved by a violation of this Act may bring a civil action
for injunctive relief and reasonable attorney fees.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 2(a)-(b)**: Builds on the California RAVBM model to require accessible electronic ballot delivery in all states with mail voting, with specific technical requirements
- **Section 2(c)**: Addresses the signature match problem that disproportionately disqualifies ballots from voters with disabilities affecting handwriting
- **Section 3**: Provides federal funding so cost is not a barrier to implementation
- **Section 4**: Two-election-cycle compliance timeline balances urgency with implementation reality

**Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Election security concerns | Paper return requirement; EAC security certification |
| State law conflicts | Federal preemption under Elections Clause and 14th Amendment |
| Cost for small jurisdictions | Federal grants offset; systems scalable across jurisdictions |
| Technology access gap | Maintains in-person accessible voting as alternative; does not replace physical access |

**Refinements**:

- **Stronger version**: Require electronic ballot return option with audit trail
- **Compromise version**: Make RAVBM an option states can adopt voluntarily with federal incentive grants
- **Fallback version**: Require signature alternatives only (narrowest scope)

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Accessible Voting Act

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "[State] Accessible Voting Act".

SECTION 2. POLLING PLACE ACCESSIBILITY.
(a) The [Secretary of State/State Election Board] shall conduct
an accessibility survey of every polling place in the State
not later than 120 days before each general election.
(b) Any accessibility barrier identified shall be remediated
before the next election or the polling place shall be
relocated to an accessible facility.
(c) Every polling place shall provide curbside voting for any
voter who cannot access the polling place due to disability,
without requiring advance notice or disclosure of the nature
of the disability.

SECTION 3. ACCESSIBLE VOTING EQUIPMENT.
(a) Every polling place shall deploy at least one accessible
ballot marking device that meets the accessibility requirements
of the [State] Voting System Standards.
(b) Poll workers shall set up and test accessible voting
equipment before the opening of polls.
(c) The [Secretary of State] shall certify that voting systems
meet accessibility requirements as a condition of use in
[State] elections.

SECTION 4. POLL WORKER TRAINING.
(a) All poll workers shall complete disability awareness and
accommodation training before serving.
(b) Training shall include operation of accessible voting
equipment, communication with voters with disabilities,
and curbside voting procedures.
(c) The [State Election Board] shall develop the training
curriculum in consultation with disability rights organizations
and voters with disabilities.

SECTION 5. ACCESSIBLE VOTE-BY-MAIL.
(a) The [Secretary of State] shall provide an accessible
electronic ballot delivery and marking system for any voter
with a disability who requests such accommodation.
(b) The State shall accept an alternative to a handwritten
signature from voters whose disabilities affect their ability
to produce a consistent signature.

SECTION 6. GUARDIANSHIP AND VOTING RIGHTS.
(a) No person shall be denied the right to register to vote or
to vote solely on the basis of guardianship, conservatorship,
or similar legal status.
(b) Voting rights may be restricted only upon a specific court
order, following a hearing with legal representation, finding
that the individual lacks the capacity to express a preference
for a candidate or ballot measure.

SECTION 7. ENFORCEMENT.
(a) Any voter aggrieved by a violation of this Act may bring
a civil action for injunctive relief, damages of not less than
$[1,000] and not more than $[10,000] per violation, and
reasonable attorney fees.
(b) The [Attorney General] may bring an action to enforce
the provisions of this Act.

SECTION 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect on [date].
```

## Regulatory Framework

### EAC VVSG Accessibility Standards Update

| Element | Current Standard | Proposed Update |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Audio ballot completion time | No time standard | 25 minutes maximum for average-length ballot |
| Assistive technology compatibility | General requirement | Specific compatibility with screen readers, sip-and-puff, adaptive switches |
| Usability testing | Not required | Mandatory testing with voters representing 5+ disability types |
| Font and contrast | General requirement | Adjustable size (14-28pt), high contrast option, color-blind modes |
| Tactile controls | General requirement | Standardized tactile interface with Braille labels |
| Audio languages | English required | All languages on ballot must be available in audio |

| Area | Current Status | Recommended Action |
|------|---------------|-------------------|

| Curbside voting standards | No specific guidance | Detailed standards for curbside voting implementation |

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Authority

- **Fourteenth Amendment, Section 5**: Congress has authority to enforce equal protection through voting accessibility legislation (*Tennessee v. Lane*, 2004, upheld Title II damages for inaccessible courthouses; voting is at least as fundamental as courthouse access)
- **Elections Clause (Article I, Section 4)**: Congress has authority to regulate the "Time, Places, and Manner" of federal elections, including accessibility requirements for polling places
- **Fifteenth Amendment**: Provides additional authority where disability-based voting restrictions intersect with racial discrimination
- **Commerce Clause**: Supports regulation of voting equipment vendors and electronic ballot delivery systems

### Preemption

- Federal polling place accessibility standards preempt less protective state standards
- Guardianship Voting Rights Restoration Act preempts state guardianship-based disenfranchisement
- RAVBM Expansion Act preempts state laws prohibiting electronic ballot delivery for voters with disabilities
- States remain free to provide greater protections

### Sovereign Immunity

- *Tennessee v. Lane* (2004) establishes that Congress may abrogate state sovereign immunity under Title II of the ADA for fundamental rights including access to courts; voting is arguably more fundamental
- The Voting Rights Act precedent provides additional basis for congressional enforcement against states
- Grant program conditions provide alternative mechanism to require state compliance

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Availability | Effectiveness |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| DOJ enforcement (Voting Section + DRS) | Available; both sections have jurisdiction | High when deployed; limited by staffing |
| Private litigation (ADA Title II) | Available; private right of action | Moderate; addresses individual jurisdictions |
| Private litigation (new statutory claims) | Proposed in all three bills | High; creates specific voting accessibility causes of action |
| EAC oversight | Advisory only | Low; no enforcement authority |
| State enforcement (under model legislation) | Depends on state adoption | Variable; effective in adopting states |
| Election Protection monitoring | Volunteer-based | Moderate; identifies problems for follow-up |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Survey-only compliance | Jurisdictions conduct surveys but fail to remediate identified barriers | High |
| Temporary modification inadequacy | Temporary solutions (portable ramps, propped doors) fail to provide genuine accessibility | High |
| Equipment deployment without functionality | BMDs deployed but not set up, powered on, or loaded with correct ballot | High |
| Training without competency | Poll workers complete training requirements without achieving actual competency | Medium |
| RAVBM opt-out by eliminating mail voting | States could theoretically eliminate all mail voting to avoid RAVBM requirement | Medium |
| Guardianship capacity hearings as disenfranchisement tool | States use capacity hearing process to challenge voting rights of people under guardianship at scale | Medium |
| Curbside voting as excuse for inaccessible polling places | Jurisdictions provide curbside voting instead of making polling places accessible | Medium |
| Signature alternative abuse | Bad actors use signature alternative provisions to cast fraudulent ballots | Low |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|-----------|
| No federal enforcement of equipment usability | Vendors meet technical standards without genuine usability | VVSG testing is technical, not user-centered |
| Grant matching requirement burdens poorest jurisdictions | Under-resourced jurisdictions cannot afford 20% match | Political necessity of matching requirement |
| Phased equipment compliance delays accessibility | Voters wait 3+ election cycles for accessible equipment | Equipment procurement reality |
| No mandate for universal accessible ballot design | Ballot complexity continues to create cognitive barriers | Ballot design is state/local prerogative |
| Guardianship reform requires state implementation | 39 states must change laws or face preemption litigation | Federalism structure |
| Poll worker turnover negates training | Training must be repeated every election cycle | Volunteer workforce reality |
| Monitoring program dependent on continued funding | Secret shopper program requires sustained appropriation | Annual appropriations process |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Survey-only compliance**: Require jurisdictions to submit remediation plans with specific timelines; authorize DOJ to seek injunctive relief for failure to remediate within one election cycle; condition grant funding on demonstrated remediation progress
2. **Temporary modification inadequacy**: Establish minimum standards for temporary modifications (ramp slope, width, stability); require that temporary modifications be tested before Election Day; fund permanent accessibility improvements to reduce reliance on temporary solutions
3. **Equipment deployment failures**: Require pre-election equipment testing with accessibility checklist; mandate that accessible equipment be set up and tested before polls open; include equipment functionality in Election Day monitoring program
4. **Training without competency**: Require practical demonstration of equipment operation as condition of serving; develop standardized competency assessment; allow jurisdictions to compensate poll workers for additional training time
5. **RAVBM opt-out through mail voting elimination**: Extend RAVBM requirement to any state that offers absentee voting for any reason; separately mandate RAVBM for voters with disabilities who cannot access polling places
6. **Guardianship capacity hearing abuse**: Place burden of proof on party seeking to restrict rights; require clear and convincing evidence standard; mandate appointed counsel for individual; allow DOJ intervention in capacity proceedings
7. **Curbside as excuse for inaccessibility**: Require curbside voting as supplement to, not substitute for, physical accessibility; maintain remediation requirements regardless of curbside availability; prohibit use of curbside voting statistics to justify continued inaccessibility
8. **Signature alternative abuse**: Require documentation of disability (not nature of disability) for signature alternative; maintain existing fraud penalties; track signature alternative usage for anomaly detection

### General Implementation Concerns

- **Judicial interpretation**: Courts may interpret accessibility standards narrowly; legislative history should clearly express congressional intent for broad, functional accessibility
- **Political durability**: Voting accessibility reforms must be embedded in statute to survive administration changes; executive orders are insufficient
- **Vendor market concentration**: Three vendors control 90% of the market; new accessibility standards must be achievable but should also encourage new market entrants
- **Rural jurisdiction challenges**: Rural areas may have very limited accessible facility options; grant funding should include rural-specific allocations
- **Technology evolution**: Standards must be technology-neutral to accommodate future innovations; performance-based standards preferred over prescriptive requirements
- **Disability community diversity**: Solutions must address the full range of disability types; advisory committees should include cross-disability representation
