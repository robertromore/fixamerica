# Voting Accessibility: Current State

## The Problem Today

### Physical Barriers at Polling Places

Despite 35 years of ADA mandates, physical accessibility at polling places remains grossly inadequate. The most comprehensive federal study, conducted by GAO in 2017 (GAO-18-4), found that 60% of polling places had at least one potential impediment for voters with disabilities. Subsequent surveys by disability organizations suggest the actual rate is higher:

| Barrier Type | Prevalence | Impact |
|--------------|-----------|--------|
| Inaccessible parking (no van-accessible spaces, improper slope, too far from entrance) | 40-50% of polling places | Wheelchair users, mobility impairment |
| Inaccessible exterior paths (uneven surfaces, no curb cuts, gravel pathways) | 30-40% | Wheelchair, walker, cane users |
| Entrance barriers (steps without ramp, heavy doors, narrow doorways) | 25-35% | Wheelchair users, mobility impairment |
| Interior barriers (narrow aisles, high counters, inaccessible restrooms) | 20-30% | Wheelchair users, short stature |
| Inadequate signage (no directional signs, no accessible format) | 40-50% | Blind, low vision, cognitive disabilities |
| Lighting and noise issues | 20-30% | Low vision, sensory processing, autism |

The problem is not merely that buildings are old. Many jurisdictions use schools, churches, fire stations, and community centers as polling places, selecting them based on availability and cost rather than accessibility. When accessibility surveys identify problems, remediation is often cosmetic (temporary ramps that are too steep, "accessible" signs that lead to locked doors) rather than substantive.

### Voting Equipment Failures

The Help America Vote Act (HAVA) of 2002 required every polling place to have at least one accessible voting device that allows voters with disabilities to cast a ballot privately and independently. Twenty-three years later, compliance remains uneven:

- **Equipment availability**: Many jurisdictions have accessible ballot marking devices (BMDs) but deploy them to polling places without ensuring they are set up, powered on, and functioning on Election Day
- **Equipment functionality**: BMDs are frequently broken, missing components (headphones, sip-and-puff devices, tactile keypads), or loaded with incorrect ballot styles
- **Poll worker competence**: GAO found that only 35-40% of poll workers could correctly set up and operate accessible voting equipment (GAO-18-4). Many poll workers have never received training on accessibility features
- **Usability**: Even functional BMDs present usability challenges. Audio interfaces are slow (a full audio ballot can take 30-60 minutes), screen reader compatibility is inconsistent, and physical controls may not accommodate all disability types
- **Privacy concerns**: In some jurisdictions, BMD use is so rare that using the accessible device effectively identifies the voter as having a disability, compromising ballot secrecy

### Vote-by-Mail Accessibility

Mail voting can be either a powerful accessibility tool or a significant barrier, depending on implementation:

**Barriers in Mail Voting**:

- **Paper ballot inaccessibility**: Standard paper ballots cannot be read by blind voters, marked by voters with fine motor impairments, or folded/inserted into envelopes by voters with limited dexterity
- **Signature requirements**: Voters with disabilities that affect handwriting (tremor, paralysis, amputation) may produce signatures that do not match their registration signature, leading to ballot rejection
- **Envelope manipulation**: Multi-step envelope procedures (secrecy sleeves, oath envelopes, outer envelopes) are difficult for voters with dexterity impairments
- **Witness/notary requirements**: Some states require witnesses or notaries for absentee ballots, creating barriers for voters who are homebound or institutionalized
- **Deadline complexity**: Different deadlines for requesting, receiving, and returning ballots create cognitive barriers

**Accessible Electronic Ballot Delivery**:

Some jurisdictions have implemented systems that allow voters with disabilities to receive and mark ballots electronically, then print and return them. These systems (such as the RAVBM -- Remote Accessible Vote-by-Mail -- used in California and several other states) represent a significant accessibility advance but face opposition on security grounds and are not universally available.

### Guardianship and Competency Barriers

Thirty-nine states have laws that restrict voting rights based on mental incapacity, incompetency, or guardianship status. These laws vary widely:

| Category | Number of States | Standard |
|----------|-----------------|----------|
| No restriction based on guardianship | 11 states + DC | No mental capacity test for voting |
| Restriction only by court order specific to voting | 12 states | Court must specifically adjudicate voting capacity |
| Automatic disenfranchisement upon guardianship | 8 states | Any guardianship triggers loss of voting rights |
| "Incapacitated" or "incompetent" standard | 19 states | Varying definitions; often vague |

The Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law has documented that these laws disenfranchise hundreds of thousands of Americans, many of whom have the capacity and desire to vote. The laws fall disproportionately on people with intellectual disabilities, dementia, traumatic brain injuries, and severe mental illness. Guardianship determinations are often made without any consideration of voting capacity specifically, yet they trigger automatic loss of voting rights.

### Poll Worker Attitudes and Training

Even where physical and technological barriers are addressed, attitudinal barriers persist:

- Poll workers who question voters' right to vote based on apparent disability
- Refusal to provide required accommodations (reading assistance, curbside voting)
- Insistence that voters use the "regular" machines rather than accessible devices
- Directing voters with disabilities to separate, less desirable voting locations
- Impatience with voters who take longer to vote
- Lack of awareness that intellectual disability does not disqualify a person from voting

Most jurisdictions provide minimal disability-specific training to poll workers, often limited to a brief mention in general training materials. Few jurisdictions have disability-specific training modules, and almost none require poll workers to demonstrate competence in operating accessible equipment.

## Impact

### On Voters with Disabilities

- **Suppressed turnout**: The 6-point voting gap represents millions of voters who do not vote because they cannot, not because they choose not to
- **Loss of independence**: Voters forced to rely on poll worker assistance lose ballot secrecy and independence
- **Dignity harm**: Experiences of inaccessibility, poll worker incompetence, and questioning of voting competency cause humiliation and discouragement
- **Disproportionate burden**: Voters with disabilities must expend significantly more time, effort, and emotional energy to exercise the same right non-disabled voters take for granted
- **Self-suppression**: Many voters with disabilities, having experienced barriers in past elections, stop trying to vote

### On Democracy

- **Representational distortion**: When voters with disabilities vote at lower rates, their policy priorities receive less attention from elected officials
- **Policy feedback loop**: Lower disability turnout means less political pressure for accessibility improvements, perpetuating the cycle
- **Democratic legitimacy**: Elections that systematically exclude millions of citizens cannot claim full democratic legitimacy
- **Precedent erosion**: Acceptance of disability voting barriers normalizes voter exclusion more broadly

### Intersectional Impact

Voters with disabilities who are also members of other marginalized groups face compounded barriers:

- **Race + disability**: Black and Latino voters with disabilities face both racial voter suppression and disability barriers
- **Poverty + disability**: Cannot afford transportation to accessible polling places; cannot take time off work to deal with longer voting times
- **Age + disability**: Elderly voters with age-related disabilities face particular challenges with new technology and changed procedures
- **Rural + disability**: Fewer accessible polling places, longer travel distances, less public transportation

## Recent Developments

### Post-2020 Voting Restriction Laws

The wave of voting restriction legislation following the 2020 election has disproportionately affected voters with disabilities:

- **Mail voting restrictions**: Limits on who can request mail ballots, shortened request deadlines, and new ID requirements reduce access for voters who depend on mail voting due to physical barriers at polling places
- **Drop box elimination or reduction**: Voters with disabilities who cannot access postal services or wait in long lines lose a critical return method
- **Ballot assistance restrictions**: Laws limiting who can provide ballot assistance (such as Georgia's SB 202) may prevent disability service providers from helping clients vote
- **Signature match requirements**: Stricter signature verification disproportionately disqualifies ballots from voters with disabilities affecting handwriting

### Positive Developments

- **Electronic ballot delivery expansion**: Several states have expanded or adopted remote accessible vote-by-mail systems
- **Curbside voting formalization**: Some states have codified curbside voting as a permanent accommodation
- **Guardianship reform movement**: Several states have reformed guardianship laws to protect voting rights, including removing automatic disenfranchisement provisions
- **Accessible voting technology**: New BMD designs with improved usability, faster audio interfaces, and better physical controls are entering the market
- **DOJ enforcement**: The Department of Justice has continued to pursue polling place accessibility cases, though the pace varies by administration

### Litigation

- Ongoing litigation challenging state guardianship-based disenfranchisement under the ADA and 14th Amendment
- Challenges to signature match requirements as applied to voters with disabilities
- ADA enforcement actions against jurisdictions with persistently inaccessible polling places
- Challenges to ballot assistance restrictions that affect disability service providers
