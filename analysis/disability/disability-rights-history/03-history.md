# Disability Rights History: History

## Prehistory: Disability in America Before the Movement (Pre-1960)

### Colonial and Early Republic Era

People with disabilities in early America were largely treated as subjects of local charity, family responsibility, or institutional confinement. Towns provided "outdoor relief" (assistance in the community) or placed people with disabilities in almshouses.

- 1752: Pennsylvania Hospital (Philadelphia), first hospital in the colonies, includes wards for "lunatics"
- 1817: American School for the Deaf founded in Hartford, Connecticut, by Thomas Hopkins Gallaudet and Laurent Clerc
- 1829: New England Asylum for the Blind (later Perkins School) founded
- 1848: First state-funded institution for "idiotic and feeble-minded" persons opens in Massachusetts under Samuel Gridley Howe

### The Institutional Era (1850s-1950s)

State institutions for people with intellectual, psychiatric, and physical disabilities proliferated throughout the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. What began as small educational facilities became massive custodial warehouses.

| Period | Development | Scale |
|--------|-------------|-------|
| 1850-1890 | Training school model | Small institutions (50-200 residents); educational mission |
| 1890-1920 | Custodial shift | Growing populations; permanent confinement replaces training |
| 1907-1937 | Eugenics laws | 32 states enact forced sterilization statutes |
| 1927 | *Buck v. Bell* | Supreme Court upholds forced sterilization (never overruled) |
| 1930s-1940s | Institutional overcrowding | Many institutions at 150-200% of capacity |
| 1940s-1950s | Conscientious objector exposures | WWII COs assigned to state hospitals publicize abhorrent conditions |

**Forced Sterilization**

Between 1907 and the 1970s, an estimated 60,000-70,000 Americans were forcibly sterilized under state eugenics laws. People with intellectual disabilities, psychiatric conditions, epilepsy, and those deemed "unfit" were targeted. Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr.'s opinion in *Buck v. Bell* (1927) declaring "Three generations of imbeciles are enough" remains one of the most notorious passages in Supreme Court history.

### The Rehabilitation Model (1920s-1960s)

Parallel to institutionalization, a vocational rehabilitation model developed focused primarily on veterans and people with physical disabilities.

- 1920: Smith-Fess Act creates civilian vocational rehabilitation program
- 1935: Social Security Act includes some disability provisions (but excludes most people with disabilities from benefits)
- 1943: Barden-LaFollette Act expands VR to include people with intellectual and psychiatric disabilities
- 1945-1960: WWII and Korean War disabled veterans drive expansion of rehabilitation services
- 1954: Vocational Rehabilitation Act Amendments expand federal VR program significantly

## The Birth of the Movement (1960s-1970s)

### Intellectual Foundations

The 1960s saw the emergence of new ideas that would become the intellectual foundation of disability rights.

**Key Developments**

- 1962: Ed Roberts, a polio survivor using an iron lung, enrolls at UC Berkeley, beginning the independent living movement
- 1963: President Kennedy's Panel on Mental Retardation calls for deinstitutionalization
- 1963: Community Mental Health Centers Act begins shift from institutional to community-based mental health care
- 1966: Burton Blatt publishes *Christmas in Purgatory*, photographing conditions in state institutions
- 1968: Architectural Barriers Act requires accessibility in federally funded buildings

### The Independent Living Movement

Ed Roberts and other disabled students at UC Berkeley created the first Center for Independent Living (CIL) in 1972, establishing principles that would transform disability policy:

- People with disabilities are the best experts on their own needs
- People with disabilities deserve equal access to all aspects of society
- People with disabilities should direct their own services and lives
- Disability is a social and political condition, not merely a medical one

### Section 504 and the Sit-Ins (1973-1977)

**The Rehabilitation Act of 1973**

Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act was the first federal civil rights protection for people with disabilities, prohibiting discrimination by any entity receiving federal financial assistance.

**The Fight for Implementation**

- Section 504 was signed into law in 1973 but implementing regulations were not issued
- The Department of Health, Education, and Welfare (HEW) delayed regulations for four years under two administrations
- April 5, 1977: Disability activists occupied HEW offices in ten cities simultaneously
- The San Francisco sit-in lasted 25 days, the longest federal building occupation in U.S. history
- Led by Judy Heumann, Kitty Cone, and others, supported by the Black Panther Party (which provided food), Glide Memorial Church, and other community organizations
- April 28, 1977: HEW Secretary Joseph Califano signed the regulations

**Significance of the 504 Sit-Ins**

| Factor | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| Cross-disability coalition | First major action uniting people across disability types |
| Alliance with other movements | Black Panthers, labor, LGBTQ groups provided support |
| Direct action model | Demonstrated that disabled people could engage in militant protest |
| Policy impact | Forced implementation of the first disability civil rights law |
| Movement identity | Created a shared identity and sense of collective power |

### Other 1970s Landmarks

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|--------------|
| 1970 | Developmental Disabilities Act | Created state DD Councils; defined developmental disabilities |
| 1972 | *Wyatt v. Stickney* | Constitutional right to adequate conditions in state institutions |
| 1973 | Federal-Aid Highway Act | Required accessible pedestrian features in federally funded roads |
| 1975 | Education for All Handicapped Children Act (P.L. 94-142) | Right to free appropriate public education for all children with disabilities |
| 1975 | Developmental Disabilities P&A system created | State-level legal advocacy for people with DD |
| 1978 | Amendments to Rehabilitation Act | Established National Council on Disability; expanded independent living |

## Building Toward the ADA (1980s)

### The Reagan Era: Setbacks and Organizing

The Reagan administration attempted to weaken disability rights protections, which galvanized the movement.

**Setbacks**

- Proposed weakening of Section 504 regulations
- Attempted cuts to Social Security disability benefits (resulting in 200,000+ benefit terminations later reversed)
- Deregulatory philosophy threatened accessibility requirements
- *Grove City College v. Bell* (1984) narrowed Section 504 coverage to specific programs, not institutions

**Movement Response**

- ADAPT (originally American Disabled for Accessible Public Transit) launches direct action campaign for accessible buses (1983)
- Disability community organizes to defeat Section 504 weakening
- Congress passes Civil Rights Restoration Act (1988) overriding *Grove City*
- National Council on Disability publishes *Toward Independence* (1986), the blueprint for the ADA

### Key 1980s Legislation

| Year | Law | Significance |
|------|-----|--------------|
| 1980 | Civil Rights of Institutionalized Persons Act (CRIPA) | DOJ authority to investigate institutional conditions |
| 1984 | Voting Accessibility for the Elderly and Handicapped Act | Required accessible polling places |
| 1986 | Air Carrier Access Act | Prohibited disability discrimination in air travel |
| 1986 | Protection and Advocacy for Individuals with Mental Illness Act | Expanded P&A to mental health |
| 1988 | Fair Housing Amendments Act | Extended Fair Housing Act to cover disability |
| 1988 | Technology-Related Assistance Act | Assistive technology programs |

### The Road to the ADA

- 1986: National Council on Disability publishes *Toward Independence*, recommending a comprehensive disability civil rights law
- 1988: First ADA bill introduced by Senator Lowell Weicker (R-CT) and Representative Tony Coelho (D-CA)
- 1988: NCD publishes draft ADA legislation
- 1989: Revised ADA bill introduced by Senator Tom Harkin (D-IA) and Representative Steny Hoyer (D-MD)
- 1989: Justin Dart Jr. conducts "road tour" of all 50 states, gathering disability rights testimony

## The Americans with Disabilities Act (1990)

### Passage

The ADA was the product of decades of advocacy, bipartisan legislative strategy, and unprecedented coalition building.

**Key Figures**

| Person | Role |
|--------|------|
| Justin Dart Jr. | "Father of the ADA"; organized grassroots campaign |
| Judy Heumann | Movement leader; shaped ADA strategy |
| Tom Harkin (D-IA) | Lead Senate sponsor; brother is deaf |
| Steny Hoyer (D-MD) | Lead House sponsor |
| Bob Dole (R-KS) | Key Republican supporter; disabled WWII veteran |
| Ted Kennedy (D-MA) | Senate champion |
| Tony Coelho (D-CA) | Original House sponsor; has epilepsy |
| Evan Kemp Jr. | EEOC chair; Republican disability advocate |
| Patrisha Wright | DREDF; coordinated lobbying campaign |
| Marca Bristo | Access Living; grassroots organizing |

**Legislative History**

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| May 9, 1989 | ADA introduced in Senate |
| September 7, 1989 | Senate passes ADA, 76-8 |
| May 22, 1990 | House passes ADA, 403-20 |
| July 13, 1990 | Conference agreement reached |
| July 26, 1990 | President George H.W. Bush signs ADA into law |

**The Capitol Crawl (March 12, 1990)**

Over 1,000 disability activists gathered at the U.S. Capitol to demand passage of the ADA. In a dramatic protest, approximately 60 activists abandoned their wheelchairs and crawled up the 78 Capitol steps. Eight-year-old Jennifer Keelan crawled the steps, saying "I'll take all night if I have to." The Capitol Crawl became one of the most iconic images of the disability rights movement.

### ADA Structure

| Title | Coverage | Enforcement |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Title I | Employment (15+ employees) | EEOC |
| Title II | State and local government services | DOJ |
| Title III | Public accommodations (private businesses) | DOJ; private right of action (injunctive only) |
| Title IV | Telecommunications (relay services) | FCC |
| Title V | Miscellaneous provisions | Various |

## Post-ADA: Implementation and Backlash (1990-2008)

### Early Implementation (1990-1999)

- DOJ Disability Rights Section established
- ADA Standards for Accessible Design issued (1994)
- Curb cuts, accessible transit, and public accommodation changes implemented
- Private litigation becomes primary enforcement mechanism
- *Olmstead v. L.C.* (1999): Supreme Court rules unnecessary institutionalization is ADA discrimination

### Judicial Backlash (1999-2008)

The Supreme Court dramatically narrowed the ADA's coverage through a series of decisions.

| Case | Year | Effect |
|------|------|--------|
| *Sutton v. United Air Lines* | 1999 | Disability determined with mitigating measures |
| *Murphy v. United Parcel Service* | 1999 | Same principle applied to hypertension |
| *Albertsons v. Kirkingburg* | 1999 | Applied to monocular vision |
| *Board of Trustees v. Garrett* | 2001 | Sovereign immunity bars Title I damages against states |
| *Toyota v. Williams* | 2002 | Narrowed "substantially limits" to exclude many impairments |
| *Chevron v. Echazabal* | 2002 | "Direct threat to self" defense in employment |

**Impact**: Between 1999 and 2008, ADA defendants prevailed at summary judgment approximately 95% of the time. The Court's decisions effectively removed millions of people with manageable disabilities from ADA protection.

### The ADA Amendments Act (2008)

Congress responded to the judicial narrowing with overwhelming bipartisan support.

- Passed Senate 93-1; House by voice vote
- Signed by President George W. Bush, September 25, 2008
- Restored broad coverage by rejecting the Court's narrow interpretations
- Prohibited consideration of mitigating measures in determining disability
- Expanded the definition of "major life activities" to include major bodily functions
- Broadened "regarded as" coverage

## Contemporary History (2008-Present)

### Obama Administration (2009-2017)

- Increased ADA enforcement activity
- First DOJ enforcement actions on website accessibility
- Updated ADA Standards for Accessible Design (2010)
- *Olmstead* enforcement expansion
- Appointed first deaf Assistant Secretary of Education for OSERS

### Trump Administration (2017-2021)

- Withdrew pending web accessibility rulemaking
- Reduced ADA enforcement activity
- ADA Education and Reform Act passed House in 2018 (did not advance in Senate)
- Reduced Civil Rights Division staffing and enforcement

### Biden Administration (2021-2025)

- April 2024: DOJ issued final rule requiring web accessibility for state and local governments (WCAG 2.1 AA)
- Increased *Olmstead* enforcement
- $12.7 billion in ARPA funds for HCBS
- Long COVID recognized as potential ADA disability
- Executive Order on Care Economy (April 2023)

### Emerging Issues

| Issue | Status | Significance |
|-------|--------|--------------|
| AI and algorithmic discrimination | Emerging litigation and policy | ADA application to algorithmic bias |
| Autonomous vehicles accessibility | DOT rulemaking in progress | Transportation access for next generation |
| Telehealth accessibility | Post-COVID expansion | Healthcare access and digital divide |
| Climate change and disability | Emerging policy area | Disproportionate impact on people with disabilities |
| Genetic discrimination | GINA gaps; prenatal testing concerns | Intersection of technology and disability rights |

## Lessons from History

1. **Nothing was given; everything was fought for**: Every major disability rights advance required sustained advocacy, litigation, and often direct action
2. **Bipartisan support is achievable**: The ADA, ADAAA, and other landmark laws passed with overwhelming bipartisan support, demonstrating that disability rights can transcend partisan divisions when properly framed
3. **Implementation requires constant vigilance**: Laws without enforcement are aspirational; the Supreme Court nearly destroyed the ADA between 1999 and 2008
4. **Cross-disability coalition is power**: The movement's greatest successes came when people with diverse disabilities united around shared goals
5. **Alliances matter**: The 504 sit-ins succeeded partly because the Black Panthers, labor unions, and LGBTQ organizations provided support
6. **Nothing about us without us**: The most effective policies are those shaped by people with disabilities themselves
7. **Legal rights are necessary but insufficient**: The ADA has transformed American life but has not eliminated ableism or achieved full inclusion
