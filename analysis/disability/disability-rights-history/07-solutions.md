# Disability Rights History: Solutions

## Overview

Addressing the marginalization of disability rights history requires coordinated reforms across education, cultural institutions, government recognition, archival preservation, and international engagement. These solutions aim to ensure that disability rights history is preserved, taught, and understood as a central element of American civil rights history, and that the movement's ongoing vitality is supported by a historically informed public.

## Solution 1: Disability History in K-12 Education Standards

### Description

Enact federal incentives and state mandates requiring disability rights history to be included in K-12 social studies and history curricula, integrated into existing civil rights and American history units rather than as a standalone requirement.

### Evidence Base

- Disability history is absent from most state K-12 curriculum standards and from standard U.S. history textbooks (NCD, 2023)
- New Jersey (2019) and Illinois (2021) enacted disability history education requirements, demonstrating legislative feasibility
- Studies of civil rights education show that when movements are included in curricula, student understanding of and support for those rights increases (Southern Poverty Law Center, "Teaching the Movement," 2014)
- Disability studies scholars have developed age-appropriate curricular resources (Disability History Museum online resources; DREDF educational materials)

### Cost Estimate

- **Curriculum development**: $5-$15 million nationally for creating and distributing disability history educational materials
- **Teacher training**: $10-$20 million for professional development programs across states
- **Textbook revision**: Minimal marginal cost when integrated into regular textbook revision cycles (every 5-7 years)
- **Total**: $15-$35 million over 5 years

### Timeline

- Year 1: Federal incentive legislation enacted; model curriculum standards published
- Years 1-3: Early-adopter states enact disability history requirements
- Years 3-5: Teacher professional development programs deployed
- Years 5-7: Textbook revision cycles incorporate disability history content
- Year 10: Majority of states include disability history in standards

### International Models

- **United Kingdom**: The Equality Act 2010 and the Public Sector Equality Duty have encouraged inclusion of disability history in school curricula. The Disability History Month (UK) has been recognized since 2010.
- **Australia**: The National Disability Strategy includes provisions for disability awareness in education, including historical context.

## Solution 2: National Disability Rights History Museum

### Description

Establish a national museum dedicated to disability rights history, either as a new Smithsonian museum or as a federally chartered national museum, with a phased approach beginning with a Smithsonian exhibition, expanding to a digital museum, and culminating in a permanent physical institution.

### Evidence Base

- No dedicated national museum of disability history exists in the United States. The Museum of disABILITY History in Buffalo, NY, is a small private institution.
- The National Museum of African American History and Culture (opened 2016) demonstrated that dedicated museums transform public understanding of civil rights history
- The Smithsonian Institution Act authorizes creation of new museums through congressional action
- NCD (2023) recommended a national disability museum as a priority for public education

### Cost Estimate

- **Phase 1 (Smithsonian exhibition)**: $10-$20 million for a multi-year rotating exhibition
- **Phase 2 (digital museum)**: $15-$30 million for interactive online platform with digitized collections, oral histories, and educational resources
- **Phase 3 (permanent museum)**: $300-$600 million for construction and initial endowment (comparable to other Smithsonian museums)
- **Annual operations**: $30-$50 million/year once operational

### Timeline

- Years 1-2: Congressional authorization for feasibility study and Smithsonian exhibition
- Years 2-5: Smithsonian exhibition opens; digital museum platform launched
- Years 5-8: Site selection, design competition, fundraising for permanent museum
- Years 8-15: Construction and opening of permanent museum

### International Models

- **United Kingdom**: Disability History Month exhibitions at national museums (Victoria and Albert Museum, Science Museum) provide integrated disability history content.
- **Germany**: The Memorial to the Murdered Disabled (Tiergartenstrasse 4, Berlin) commemorates T4 program victims and serves as a permanent reminder of disability persecution.

## Solution 3: Oral History Preservation and Digitization

### Description

Launch a federally funded emergency oral history preservation initiative to capture the stories of aging disability rights movement leaders and participants before they are lost. Digitize existing oral history collections and make them freely available online.

### Evidence Base

- Key movement leaders continue to pass away (Judy Heumann, 2023; Fred Fay, 2011; Paul Longmore, 2010), taking irreplaceable firsthand accounts with them
- The ADA Legacy Project has collected oral histories but lacks funding for comprehensive coverage
- The Disability Visibility Project has demonstrated the power of disability oral histories to reach broad audiences
- The Library of Congress Veterans History Project and StoryCorps provide models for national oral history initiatives

### Cost Estimate

- **Oral history collection**: $5-$10 million over 3 years for 500-1,000 interviews nationwide
- **Digitization of existing collections**: $3-$5 million for university and organizational archives
- **Online platform and accessibility**: $2-$4 million for accessible digital platform (captioning, ASL translation, audio description, plain language versions)
- **Total**: $10-$19 million over 3-5 years

### Timeline

- Year 1: Federal funding appropriated; institutional partnerships established with Library of Congress, Smithsonian, and university archives
- Years 1-3: Emergency interviews with movement veterans aged 70+; simultaneous digitization of existing collections
- Years 3-5: Online platform launched; educational resources developed from oral history content
- Ongoing: Continued collection as new interviews become possible

### International Models

- **Australia**: The National Library of Australia's oral history program includes disability rights activists and has been digitized for public access.
- **United Kingdom**: The British Library's oral history of disability movement is an ongoing collection preserving movement history.

## Solution 4: Historic Site Designation and Memorialization

### Description

Designate key disability rights history sites as National Historic Landmarks or National Historic Sites, and establish new memorials recognizing the disability rights movement's contributions to American history.

### Evidence Base

- The San Francisco Federal Building (site of the 504 sit-in) has been nominated for the National Register of Historic Places but has not been designated
- No National Historic Landmark is specifically associated with the disability rights movement
- The FDR Memorial's wheelchair statue (added 2001) is the only major national monument addressing disability, and it was controversial precisely because FDR concealed his disability
- The Ed Roberts Campus (Berkeley) is the most significant disability rights memorial structure but is not a National Historic Site

### Cost Estimate

- **National Historic Landmark designations**: $500,000-$1 million for documentation and review (NPS administrative costs)
- **National memorial**: $20-$50 million for design, construction, and dedication of a national disability rights memorial on the National Mall or in a prominent Washington, DC location
- **State and local markers**: $2-$5 million for a national program placing historical markers at disability rights sites across the country

### Timeline

- Year 1-2: Expedite National Register and National Historic Landmark nominations for 504 sit-in site, Willowbrook State School site, and other key locations
- Years 2-5: Congressional authorization for national disability rights memorial; design competition
- Years 5-10: Memorial construction and dedication; ongoing historic marker program
- Ongoing: Additional site designations as new sites are identified

### International Models

- **Germany**: Tiergartenstrasse 4 memorial in Berlin commemorates victims of the Nazi T4 program, which targeted people with disabilities.
- **Japan**: Tsukui Yamayuri En memorial for the 2016 mass killing of people with disabilities serves as a reminder of ongoing violence against disabled people.

## Solution 5: Disability Rights History in Higher Education

### Description

Expand disability studies programs at American universities, with particular emphasis on disability history as a field of scholarly inquiry. Establish endowed chairs, graduate fellowships, and research centers dedicated to disability history.

### Evidence Base

- Approximately 40 disability studies programs exist at U.S. universities (Society for Disability Studies, 2024)
- Few history departments include disability history as a recognized subfield
- Disability history scholarship has grown significantly but remains marginalized within both history and disability studies
- The Paul K. Longmore Institute on Disability (San Francisco State) demonstrates the impact of a dedicated disability history research center

### Cost Estimate

- **Endowed chairs**: $2-$5 million per chair; 10-20 chairs nationally = $20-$100 million (private and public funding)
- **Graduate fellowships**: $30,000-$50,000 per fellowship/year; 50 fellowships = $1.5-$2.5 million/year
- **Research center support**: $1-$3 million/year for 5-10 centers
- **Total**: $25-$50 million/year (including endowment draws)

### Timeline

- Years 1-3: Federal research funding (NEH, NIDILRR) for disability history projects
- Years 2-5: University partnerships establish endowed positions and fellowships
- Years 5-10: Critical mass of disability historians producing scholarship that transforms the field
- Ongoing: Integration of disability history into mainstream history curricula at the university level

### International Models

- **United Kingdom**: University of Leeds Centre for Disability Studies is a leading global center for disability scholarship, including historical research.
- **Canada**: University of Manitoba's disability studies program includes significant historical research components.

## Solution 6: UN CRPD Ratification

### Description

Pursue ratification of the United Nations Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD), which the United States signed in 2009 but has not ratified. Ratification would place the United States within the global disability rights framework and signal commitment to the principles the disability rights movement has championed.

### Evidence Base

- 190 countries have ratified the CRPD as of 2025; the United States is among the few major nations that have not
- The Senate vote in 2012 failed 61-38 (short of the 67-vote threshold), with opposition centered on sovereignty concerns and homeschooling provisions
- The CRPD was modeled on the ADA; ratification would not require changes to existing U.S. law
- Non-ratification limits U.S. credibility in international disability rights advocacy and denies the United States a seat on the CRPD monitoring body

### Cost Estimate

- **Direct cost**: Negligible (treaty ratification does not require appropriations)
- **Implementation cost**: None (existing U.S. law meets or exceeds CRPD requirements)
- **Reporting obligations**: $500,000-$1 million/year for periodic reporting to UN CRPD Committee

### Timeline

- Years 1-2: Build bipartisan Senate support through veteran and disability organization advocacy
- Year 2-3: Senate Foreign Relations Committee hearing and vote
- Year 3-4: Senate floor vote (requires 67 votes)

### International Models

- All other G7 nations, the European Union, Australia, China, Russia, and 184 other nations have ratified the CRPD.

## Solution 7: Strengthened ADA Enforcement to Fulfill the Movement's Promise

### Description

Increase funding and authority for federal enforcement of existing disability rights laws, ensuring that the legal achievements won by the movement are implemented in practice. The gap between law and lived experience is itself a historical injustice that ongoing enforcement can address.

### Evidence Base

- ADA employment discrimination cases: defendants prevailed at summary judgment approximately 95% of the time between 1999 and 2008 (pre-ADAAA)
- HHS OCR budget ($39 million, FY 2024) is inadequate for comprehensive disability civil rights enforcement
- DOJ Civil Rights Division disability staffing has fluctuated significantly across administrations
- NCD has repeatedly recommended increased enforcement resources as essential for fulfilling the ADA's promise

### Cost Estimate

- **HHS OCR disability enforcement**: $50-$75 million/year increase
- **DOJ Civil Rights Division disability section**: $25-$40 million/year increase
- **EEOC disability enforcement**: $20-$30 million/year increase
- **Total**: $95-$145 million/year

### Timeline

- Year 1: Appropriations increases requested and enacted
- Years 1-3: Enforcement staff hired and trained; case backlog addressed
- Years 3-5: Proactive compliance review programs established
- Year 5+: Measurable improvements in ADA compliance rates

### International Models

- **United Kingdom**: Equality and Human Rights Commission has dedicated disability discrimination enforcement authority and budget.
- **Australia**: Disability Discrimination Commissioner within the Australian Human Rights Commission provides focused enforcement.

## Solution 8: Disability History Month Recognition and Public Engagement

### Description

Elevate National Disability History Awareness Month (October) from a largely invisible presidential proclamation to a nationally recognized observance with public programming, institutional participation, and media engagement comparable to Black History Month or Women's History Month.

### Evidence Base

- National Disability Employment Awareness Month (October) has been proclaimed since 1988 but has minimal public visibility
- Black History Month and Women's History Month receive significant institutional and media attention, demonstrating that designated months can meaningfully increase public awareness
- Disability History Awareness Month was recognized by presidential proclamation starting in 2014 but lacks the institutional infrastructure of other heritage months

### Cost Estimate

- **Federal coordination**: $2-$5 million/year for an interagency Disability History Month coordination effort
- **Public programming grants**: $5-$10 million/year for community events, museum exhibitions, and educational programming
- **Media campaign**: $3-$5 million/year for public service announcements and media partnerships
- **Total**: $10-$20 million/year

### Timeline

- Year 1: Congressional resolution establishing Disability Rights History Month with institutional support requirements
- Year 2: Federal agencies develop Disability History Month programming
- Years 2-5: Annual public engagement grows through media partnerships and institutional participation
- Year 5+: Disability History Month achieves visibility comparable to other heritage months

## Sequencing and Priority

| Priority | Solution | Rationale |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| 1 | Oral history preservation | Most time-sensitive; movement veterans are dying |
| 2 | K-12 education standards | Greatest long-term impact on public understanding |
| 3 | Historic site designation | Concrete, achievable, politically feasible |
| 4 | Disability History Month elevation | Builds public engagement and political support |
| 5 | National museum | Major initiative requiring sustained advocacy and funding |
| 6 | Higher education expansion | Builds scholarly infrastructure for other solutions |
| 7 | ADA enforcement strengthening | Fulfills the movement's promise in practice |
| 8 | CRPD ratification | Important but faces significant political obstacles |
