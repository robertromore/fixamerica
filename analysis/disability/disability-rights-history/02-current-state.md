# Disability Rights History: Current State

## The Movement Today

### Organizational Landscape

The disability rights movement in 2025 is organizationally diverse, spanning national advocacy organizations, legal defense funds, self-advocacy groups, grassroots collectives, and academic centers. The movement has matured from its protest-era origins into a sophisticated policy, legal, and cultural force while maintaining grassroots direct action capacity.

**Major National Organizations (2025)**

| Organization | Founded | Focus | Approach |
|-------------|---------|-------|----------|
| ADAPT | 1978 | Community integration, HCBS | Direct action, civil disobedience |
| National Council on Independent Living (NCIL) | 1982 | Independent living philosophy | Policy advocacy, peer support |
| Disability Rights Education and Defense Fund (DREDF) | 1979 | Legal advocacy, policy | Litigation, policy analysis |
| National Disability Rights Network (NDRN) | 1980 | Protection and advocacy coordination | Legal services, systemic advocacy |
| Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN) | 2006 | Autistic-led policy advocacy | Policy research, self-advocacy |
| National Federation of the Blind (NFB) | 1940 | Blindness-specific rights | Advocacy, training, litigation |
| National Association of the Deaf (NAD) | 1880 | Deaf rights and culture | Advocacy, litigation, cultural preservation |
| TASH | 1975 | Inclusion of people with significant disabilities | Advocacy, research |
| American Association of People with Disabilities (AAPD) | 1995 | Cross-disability political organizing | Voter engagement, policy advocacy |
| Disability Rights Advocates (DRA) | 1993 | Impact litigation | Class action, structural reform |
| The Arc | 1950 | Intellectual and developmental disabilities | Family advocacy, policy |
| Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law | 1972 | Mental health and disability law | Litigation, policy advocacy |

### Current Movement Priorities

**Top Legislative Priorities (2025)**

| Priority | Lead Organizations | Status |
|----------|-------------------|--------|
| HCBS Access Act (Medicaid HCBS mandatory benefit) | ADAPT, NCIL, The Arc | Introduced; committee stage |
| Oppose ADA Education and Reform Act | DREDF, NDRN, AAPD | Defensive; reintroduced in 119th Congress |
| Section 14(c) subminimum wage elimination | ASAN, SABE, NCD | DOL rulemaking + legislation |
| Web accessibility legislation | NFB, DRA, DREDF | Online Accessibility Act introduced |
| CRPD ratification | AAPD, NCD, USICD | No current Senate action |
| SSI/SSDI reform (benefits cliff) | The Arc, NCIL | Multiple bills introduced |
| Guardianship reform | NCD, ASAN, SDM coalitions | State-by-state advocacy |
| Disability integration in AI governance | DREDF, CDT, ASAN | Emerging priority |

### Internal Movement Dynamics

**Generational Shifts**

- Founding generation (1960s-1980s activists) aging; many key leaders have died (Judy Heumann, 2023; Ed Roberts, 1995)
- New generation of disability activists is more diverse, more digitally connected, and more intersectional
- Younger advocates emphasize disability justice (not just disability rights), centering race, class, gender, and sexuality alongside disability
- Social media has enabled rapid organizing and broader public engagement
- Tension between established organizations and newer, more radical movements

**Cross-Disability Dynamics**

| Dynamic | Current Status |
|---------|---------------|
| Physical vs. cognitive disability priorities | Historically dominated by physical disability; growing intellectual/developmental and psychiatric disability voice |
| Deaf culture vs. disability rights | Deaf community maintains distinct cultural identity; some Deaf people do not identify as disabled |
| Autism community divisions | Autistic self-advocates (ASAN) vs. parent-led organizations (Autism Speaks) differ significantly on priorities and framing |
| Chronic illness inclusion | Growing recognition of chronic illness within disability movement; COVID-19 accelerated this |
| Disability justice framework | Originated in communities of color; emphasizes intersectionality beyond traditional disability rights |

## Legal and Legislative Landscape

### Active Federal Litigation (2025)

| Case/Issue | Status | Significance |
|------------|--------|--------------|
| *Olmstead* enforcement actions | Multiple DOJ cases in various states | Community integration mandate |
| Website accessibility (Title III scope) | Circuit split unresolved | Whether websites are "places of public accommodation" |
| AI and algorithmic discrimination | Emerging litigation | Whether ADA covers algorithmic bias |
| Long COVID as ADA disability | Multiple district court cases | Scope of ADA coverage for new conditions |
| Medical rationing and disability | Post-COVID litigation | Disability discrimination in healthcare crisis standards |

### Key Supreme Court Developments (Recent)

| Case | Year | Issue | Impact |
|------|------|-------|--------|
| *Acheson Hotels v. Laufer* | 2023 | ADA tester standing | Vacated on mootness; left standing question unresolved |
| *Cummings v. Premier Rehab Keller* | 2022 | Emotional distress damages under Section 504/ADA | No emotional distress damages under federal funding statutes |
| *Perez v. Sturgis Public Schools* | 2023 | IDEA exhaustion and ADA damages | ADA damages claims not subject to IDEA exhaustion |

| Online Accessibility Act | Codify web accessibility standards | Supported | Bipartisan; committee |

### State-Level Activity

**Positive Developments (2024-2025)**

- 17 states + DC have eliminated subminimum wages
- 20+ states have enacted or introduced supported decision-making legislation
- Multiple states increased DSP wages using ARPA HCBS funds
- Several states enacted web accessibility requirements

**Concerning Developments**

- Some states imposed pre-suit ADA notice requirements (Arizona, Florida, Texas)
- Medicaid budget pressures in several states threaten HCBS waiver capacity
- Some states weakened disability civil rights protections in education

## Cultural Recognition

### Disability History in Public Consciousness

| Indicator | Status | Assessment |
|-----------|--------|------------|
| Disability history in K-12 curricula | Minimal; not required in most states | Very inadequate |
| National disability history museum | No dedicated national museum exists | Major gap |
| Disability History Month | October; recognized by presidential proclamation since 2014 | Low public awareness |
| ADA anniversary recognition | Annual (July 26); varies in visibility | Moderate awareness |
| Disability in media representation | Improving but still limited and often stereotypical | Slowly improving |
| Disability studies programs | Growing; approximately 40 programs at U.S. universities | Academic foundation established |

### Key Cultural Developments

**Media and Representation**

- *Crip Camp* (2020 Netflix documentary) brought disability rights history to mainstream audience; Academy Award nominated
- Increase in disability representation in film and television, though often still played by non-disabled actors
- Social media has amplified disabled voices: disability activists have significant followings on multiple platforms
- "Nothing About Us Without Us" principle increasingly recognized in media and policy

**Academic Development**

- Disability studies established as interdisciplinary academic field
- Society for Disability Studies annual conference (founded 1986)
- *Disability Studies Quarterly* and multiple academic journals
- Growing integration of disability perspectives in other fields (history, sociology, law, public health)

## Movement Challenges

### Persistent Ableism

Despite legal protections, ableism continues to pervade American culture, policy, and institutions.

| Domain | Manifestation | Evidence |
|--------|--------------|----------|
| Employment | 65%+ unemployment rate for people with disabilities | BLS (2024) |
| Poverty | 25% poverty rate (vs. 11% for non-disabled) | Census ACS (2024) |
| Political representation | Very few elected officials with visible disabilities | Ruderman Foundation (2024) |
| Healthcare | Discrimination in medical treatment and rationing | NCD (2023) |
| Education | Segregation in special education; discipline disparities | DOEd OCR (2024) |
| Criminal justice | Overrepresentation in jails and prisons | BJS (2023) |

### Movement Fragmentation

- Disability community is not monolithic; different disability types have different priorities
- Race, class, gender, and sexuality create different experiences of disability
- Organizations compete for limited funding and political attention
- National organizations sometimes dominate at the expense of grassroots voices
- Professional advocacy organizations may not reflect the priorities of people with disabilities themselves

### Political Vulnerability

- Disability rights laws depend on enforcement by administrations that may not prioritize them
- Congressional threats (ADA Education and Reform Act) could weaken existing protections
- Budget cuts to enforcement agencies reduce the practical impact of legal protections
- Medicaid and other safety net programs face ongoing fiscal pressure
- The disability community's relatively small political footprint limits leverage

## International Context

### UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD)

| Fact | Detail |
|------|--------|
| Adopted | December 13, 2006 |
| Entered into force | May 3, 2008 |
| Ratifications | 190 countries (as of 2025) |
| U.S. signature | July 30, 2009 |
| U.S. ratification | Not ratified (Senate vote failed 61-38 in December 2012; needed 67) |
| Opposition | Sovereignty concerns; homeschooling advocates; Religious Right |

**Impact of Non-Ratification**

- United States cannot participate in CRPD monitoring or enforcement mechanisms
- Limits U.S. credibility in international disability rights advocacy
- Does not prevent U.S. from implementing CRPD principles domestically
- Reflects broader U.S. reluctance to ratify international human rights treaties

### Comparative Context

| Country | Key Feature | Relevance to U.S. |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| United Kingdom | Equality Act 2010; strong disability anti-discrimination | Model for comprehensive anti-discrimination framework |
| Australia | National Disability Insurance Scheme (NDIS) | Individualized funding model for disability services |
| Sweden | Personal assistance rights; strong public services | Universal access model |
| Canada | Accessible Canada Act (2019) | Proactive accessibility approach |
| Germany | Social code disability provisions; quotas for employment | Employment quota model |

## Key Takeaways

1. **The movement is alive but challenged**: The disability rights movement remains active and effective but faces generational transition, political vulnerability, and internal tensions
2. **Legal gains are incomplete and under threat**: Major laws exist but enforcement is inadequate and legislative threats persist
3. **Cultural recognition lags behind legal recognition**: Disability history is largely absent from public education and consciousness
4. **The disability justice framework is reshaping the movement**: Younger activists are centering intersectionality and structural analysis
5. **International isolation**: U.S. failure to ratify the CRPD separates it from the global disability rights framework
6. **Technology creates both opportunities and threats**: Digital accessibility, AI bias, and assistive technology are emerging frontiers
7. **Ableism remains pervasive**: Despite 35 years of the ADA, discrimination in employment, healthcare, education, and public life persists at significant levels
