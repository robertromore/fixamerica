# Disability Rights History: Overview

## Executive Summary

The disability rights movement is one of the most significant civil rights movements in American history, yet it remains among the least understood. From the mid-twentieth century through the present, people with disabilities have organized, litigated, legislated, and protested to transform American society from one that institutionalized, sterilized, and excluded them into one that, at least in law, guarantees their equal participation. The movement's achievements are enormous: Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act (1973), the Education for All Handicapped Children Act (1975), the Fair Housing Amendments Act (1988), the Americans with Disabilities Act (1990), the ADA Amendments Act (2008), and the *Olmstead v. L.C.* Supreme Court decision (1999), among many others. These legal victories have reshaped the built environment, workplaces, schools, transportation systems, and public services across the nation.

Yet the history of disability rights is also a history of incomplete implementation, backlash, and ongoing struggle. Laws that promise equality on paper often fail in practice due to inadequate enforcement, hostile judicial interpretation, and persistent cultural attitudes that view disability as individual deficit rather than social exclusion. The gap between the promise and reality of disability rights is one of the central problems in American civil rights today. Understanding how we arrived at the current moment, what the movement has achieved, and what forces have constrained its progress is essential for crafting effective reform strategies.

This subtopic examines the disability rights movement as a whole: its intellectual foundations, key events, landmark legislation, court decisions, organizations, leaders, and ongoing evolution. It situates disability rights within the broader history of American civil rights and analyzes the factors that have shaped the movement's trajectory.

## Core Tensions

### Medical Model vs. Social Model

The foundational intellectual tension in disability rights is between the medical model, which frames disability as an individual deficiency to be cured or managed, and the social model, which frames disability as the result of social barriers that exclude people with impairments from full participation. The medical model dominated American policy until the 1970s and continues to influence policy design, professional training, and public attitudes. The social model, while ascendant in advocacy and scholarship, has not fully transformed the systems that serve people with disabilities.

### Rights vs. Charity

The disability rights movement explicitly rejected the charity model that had previously dominated attitudes toward disability. The charity model frames people with disabilities as objects of pity deserving of benevolent assistance, while the rights model frames them as citizens entitled to equal protection and participation. This tension continues to shape fundraising practices, media representation, public attitudes, and policy framing.

### Universal Design vs. Accommodation

Should society be designed from the outset to be accessible to all (universal design), or should accommodations be provided on a case-by-case basis when requested? Universal design is more effective and dignified but requires upfront investment. The ADA primarily uses an accommodation model, which places the burden on individuals to request and justify accommodations. This tension affects architecture, technology, education, and employment.

### Integration vs. Disability Culture

While the disability rights movement has fought for integration into mainstream society, it has also cultivated a distinctive disability culture and community. Some Deaf people and other disability communities value their separate cultural identity and resist assimilation. The tension between integration and cultural preservation reflects broader debates in civil rights movements about assimilation versus pluralism.

### The Problem

- Why is the disability rights movement less recognized than other civil rights movements despite comparable achievements?
- What accounts for the persistent gap between disability rights law and lived experience?
- How have backlash and retrenchment limited the movement's gains?
- Why has the United States failed to ratify the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities?

- What historical forces created the conditions that the disability rights movement arose to challenge?

## Vision of Success

A society that fully honors disability rights history would treat disability rights as fundamental civil rights, equivalent in importance and legal force to protections based on race, sex, and other protected characteristics. Disability history would be taught in schools, recognized in public monuments and museums, and understood as a central thread in the American civil rights story. The laws won by the disability rights movement would be fully and vigorously enforced. The social model of disability would inform policy design, professional training, and public consciousness. People with disabilities would hold positions of political power, cultural influence, and economic participation proportionate to their share of the population. The United States would ratify the UN CRPD and take a leadership role in global disability rights. And the movement itself would be recognized not just for its past achievements but as an ongoing, vital force for justice.
