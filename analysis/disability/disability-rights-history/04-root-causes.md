# Disability Rights History: Root Causes

## Overview

Understanding why the disability rights movement remains necessary more than three decades after the ADA requires examining the forces that created the conditions of exclusion in the first place and that continue to limit progress. The root causes of disability oppression and the ongoing challenges facing the disability rights movement are interconnected, spanning cultural attitudes, institutional structures, economic incentives, political dynamics, and historical legacies.

---

## Cultural Root Causes

### 1. Ableism as Foundational Cultural Norm

Ableism, the systematic devaluation of bodies and minds that differ from culturally constructed norms of ability, is perhaps the most fundamental root cause. Unlike racism or sexism, ableism is rarely recognized as a system of oppression by the general public.

**How Ableism Operates**

- **Individual ableism**: Attitudes of pity, fear, disgust, or assumed incompetence toward people with disabilities
- **Institutional ableism**: Systems designed exclusively for non-disabled people (education, employment, transportation, housing, healthcare)
- **Cultural ableism**: Media representations, language patterns, and social norms that marginalize disability
- **Internalized ableism**: Disabled people absorbing and acting on negative messages about disability

**Why Ableism Persists**

- Disability is perceived as "natural" misfortune rather than social construction, making ableism invisible as a system of oppression
- Virtually everyone either has or will have a disability, yet this universality paradoxically reduces identification with disability as a political category
- The medical profession continues to train practitioners in deficit-based models of disability
- Media representation reinforces "inspiration porn" (disability as inspirational struggle) and "tragic victim" narratives

### 2. The Medical Model's Dominance

The medical model frames disability as an individual biological deficit requiring treatment, cure, or management. While medical intervention is valuable, the medical model's dominance in policy and culture has profound negative consequences.

**How the Medical Model Creates Harm**

| Mechanism | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| Focus on "fixing" individuals | Ignores social barriers that create disability |
| Professional authority over disabled lives | Reduces self-determination; empowers doctors, therapists, and administrators |
| Eligibility gatekeeping | Access to services depends on medical diagnosis and professional assessment, not self-identified need |
| Research priorities | Funding prioritizes cure-oriented research over quality-of-life improvements |
| Policy design | Programs designed around medical categories rather than functional needs |

**Why the Medical Model Persists**

- Medical profession has enormous cultural authority and institutional power
- Insurance and benefit systems require diagnostic categories
- Pharmaceutical and medical device industries have financial interest in medical framing
- Cure narratives are emotionally appealing to non-disabled public and some families
- The social model requires uncomfortable acknowledgment of systemic failure

### 3. Disability as Invisible Identity

Unlike race and gender, disability is not always visually apparent, and it crosses all other demographic categories. This creates distinctive challenges for movement building.

- Many people with disabilities do not identify as disabled or as part of a disability community
- Disability can be acquired at any age, meaning most disabled people were not socialized into disability identity
- Invisible disabilities (chronic pain, mental health conditions, learning disabilities) are subject to skepticism and disbelief
- Disability identity is often stigmatized, discouraging public identification
- The disability community lacks the residential concentration or cultural institutions that other minority groups use for organizing

## Structural Root Causes

### 4. Historical Legacy of Segregation

The institutional model that confined hundreds of thousands of Americans to custodial facilities for over a century created physical, social, and psychological segregation that has not been fully undone.

**Ongoing Legacy**

- Approximately 22,000 people still reside in large state-operated facilities
- Congregate settings (group homes, sheltered workshops, day programs) continue the logic of segregation in smaller forms
- Residential segregation limits community integration even for people living independently
- Many communities lack accessible infrastructure because they were built during the era of institutionalization when disabled people were not present

### 5. Legal Framework Limitations

The ADA and other disability rights laws, while transformative, contain structural features that limit their effectiveness.

| Limitation | Effect |
|------------|--------|
| No disparate impact standard under Title I | Unlike Title VII, ADA employment discrimination requires proof of intentional discrimination or failure to accommodate |
| Title III: injunctive relief only | No compensatory damages for private enforcement of public accommodation access; reduces deterrent effect |
| Reasonable accommodation framework | Places burden on individuals to request accommodations; does not require proactive accessibility |
| Undue hardship/fundamental alteration defenses | Allow entities to avoid compliance based on cost or inconvenience |
| No affirmative action requirement | ADA does not require employers to hire people with disabilities; only prohibits discrimination |
| Enforcement dependence | ADA enforcement depends on DOJ priorities, which shift with administrations |

### 6. Fragmented Disability Policy Architecture

Disability policy in the United States is fragmented across dozens of programs, agencies, and statutes that were enacted at different times for different purposes and do not form a coherent system.

**Federal Fragmentation**

| Issue | Programs Involved | Problem |
|-------|------------------|---------|
| Income support | SSI, SSDI, state programs | Different eligibility, benefits cliffs |
| Healthcare | Medicaid, Medicare, private insurance | Coverage gaps, enrollment transitions |
| Employment | VR, WIOA, ADA, Section 503 | Uncoordinated; competing eligibility |
| Education | IDEA, Section 504, ADA | Different standards and enforcement |
| Housing | Section 811, FHA, ADA | Separate programs; long waiting lists |
| Transportation | ADA, Section 504, state laws | Inconsistent implementation |

This fragmentation means that no single agency is responsible for ensuring that people with disabilities can live, work, learn, and participate fully. Individuals and families must navigate multiple disconnected systems, each with its own eligibility rules, application processes, and waiting lists.

## Political Root Causes

### 7. Limited Political Power

Despite comprising 26% of the adult population, people with disabilities exercise political power disproportionately below their numbers.

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Low voter turnout | Inaccessible polling places, lack of accessible information, transportation barriers |
| Underrepresentation in office | Very few elected officials have visible disabilities |
| Organizational fragmentation | Disability organizations divided by type; limited unified political action |
| Poverty | Disproportionate poverty limits political contributions and participation |
| Dependence on government programs | Fear of retaliation discourages political engagement |
| Media invisibility | Limited media coverage of disability policy issues |

### 8. Backlash Dynamics

Every major disability rights advance has been followed by organized backlash.

| Advance | Backlash |
|---------|----------|
| Section 504 (1973) | Four-year delay in implementing regulations |
| ADA (1990) | Business community lobbied for weakening; judicial narrowing (1999-2008) |
| *Olmstead* (1999) | States used "fundamental alteration" defense to maintain waiting lists |
| ADAAA (2008) | ADA Education and Reform Act introduced to add pre-suit barriers |
| Web accessibility expansion | Business lobbying against Title III web accessibility rules |

**Patterns of Backlash**

- Framing disability rights as "special rights" or "litigation abuse"
- Emphasizing cost of compliance while ignoring cost of exclusion
- Proposing "reform" legislation that weakens enforcement
- Judicial narrowing through hostile interpretation
- Administrative defunding of enforcement agencies

## Economic Root Causes

### 9. The Cost Framing

Disability access is consistently framed as a cost rather than an investment, despite evidence that inclusion generates economic benefits.

| Frame | Reality |
|-------|---------|
| "ADA compliance is expensive" | Most accommodations cost under $500 (JAN data); accessible design from the outset costs 1-5% more |
| "People with disabilities can't work" | 80%+ of working-age people with disabilities can work with appropriate supports and accommodations |
| "HCBS is unaffordable" | HCBS typically costs 30-50% less per person than institutional care |
| "Accessibility regulations hurt business" | The disability market represents $490 billion in disposable income (AIR, 2023) |

### 10. Care Work Devaluation

The work of supporting people with disabilities, whether performed by family members or paid workers, is systematically undervalued.

- Direct support professionals earn poverty-level wages
- Family caregivers provide uncompensated labor valued at hundreds of billions of dollars annually
- Care work is feminized and racialized, reflecting broader patterns of economic devaluation
- Medicaid reimbursement rates, not market forces, determine DSP wages, and rate-setting is dominated by budget politics rather than labor market analysis

## Intellectual Root Causes

### 11. Eugenics Legacy

The eugenics movement's influence extends beyond the historical period of forced sterilization. Eugenics-era thinking continues to shape:

- Prenatal genetic testing and counseling (often biased toward termination of pregnancies involving disability)
- Assumptions about the "quality of life" of people with disabilities
- Debates about physician-assisted suicide and disability
- Resource allocation decisions that undervalue lives with disability
- Medical training that emphasizes disability as deficit

### 12. Charity Model Persistence

Despite the rights-based framework established by the ADA, the charity model continues to dominate public attitudes and much of the nonprofit sector.

- Telethons and pity-based fundraising remain common
- Disability charities often focus on cure rather than civil rights
- "Helping" disabled people is framed as benevolence rather than justice
- Organizations serving disabled people are often led by non-disabled people
- The charity model positions disabled people as recipients rather than agents

## Summary: Interconnected Root Causes

| Root Cause | Category | Primary Effect |
|------------|----------|---------------|
| Ableism | Cultural | Invisible system of oppression; social exclusion |
| Medical model dominance | Cultural/Institutional | Individual deficit framing; professional control |
| Invisible identity | Cultural | Limited movement cohesion; identity stigma |
| Segregation legacy | Structural | Continued physical and social separation |
| Legal framework gaps | Structural | Enforcement limitations; burden on individuals |
| Policy fragmentation | Structural | Disjointed services; gaps in coverage |
| Limited political power | Political | Underrepresentation; limited leverage |
| Backlash dynamics | Political | Systematic weakening of gains |
| Cost framing | Economic | Resistance to investment in access |
| Care work devaluation | Economic | Workforce crisis; family burden |
| Eugenics legacy | Intellectual | Devaluation of disabled lives |
| Charity model | Intellectual | Paternalism; lack of agency |

These root causes interact in reinforcing cycles. Ableism produces the medical model, which produces institutional segregation, which reduces political power, which allows backlash, which weakens legal protections, which perpetuates exclusion, which reinforces ableism.
