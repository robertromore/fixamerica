# Disability Rights History: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative landscape for disability rights history recognition rests on existing authorities for cultural preservation, education, and civil rights enforcement, but lacks dedicated legislation ensuring that disability rights history is preserved, taught, and memorialized. This document proposes legislation to close those gaps: a federal disability history education act, a national museum authorization, an oral history preservation initiative, a historic site designation program, and CRPD ratification. It also addresses strengthening the enforcement of existing disability rights laws as a means of fulfilling the movement's historic promise.

## Constitutional Amendments

No constitutional amendment is necessary for the reforms proposed in this document. Existing congressional authority under the Spending Clause (Art. I, Section 8, Cl. 1) supports education funding conditions and museum authorization. The Commerce Clause (Art. I, Section 8, Cl. 3) supports ADA enforcement measures. The Treaty Clause (Art. II, Section 2, Cl. 2) governs CRPD ratification. Congressional authority to establish museums and cultural institutions is well-established through the Smithsonian Institution Act and subsequent museum authorization legislation.

## Federal Legislation

### The Disability Rights History Education Act

**Purpose**: Establish a federal grant program incentivizing states to include disability rights history in K-12 social studies and history curricula, and fund the development of model curriculum standards, teacher training, and educational materials.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Disability Rights History Education Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
(a) the disability rights movement is one of the most significant
    civil rights movements in American history, resulting in landmark
    legislation including the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990
    (Public Law 101-336);
(b) 61 million adults in the United States, 26 percent of the adult
    population, live with disabilities;
(c) disability rights history is largely absent from K-12 curricula,
    standard U.S. history textbooks, and state education standards;
(d) fewer than 5 states require any instruction in disability rights
    history;
(e) the disability rights movement was a bipartisan achievement,
    supported by Republican and Democratic leaders, and represents
    a shared national accomplishment worthy of study by all
    Americans; and
(f) understanding the history of the disability rights movement is
    essential for informed civic participation and for the
    continued protection of disability rights.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act—
(a) DISABILITY RIGHTS HISTORY.—The term "disability rights history"
    means the history of the disability rights movement in the
    United States, including—
    (1) the eugenics era and the history of institutionalization;
    (2) the independent living movement;
    (3) the enactment and implementation of the Rehabilitation Act
        of 1973, including the Section 504 sit-ins;
    (4) the enactment and implementation of the Education for All
        Handicapped Children Act of 1975;
    (5) the enactment and implementation of the Americans with
        Disabilities Act of 1990, including the Capitol Crawl;
    (6) key court decisions including Olmstead v. L.C.;
    (7) key leaders and organizations of the disability rights
        movement;
    (8) the disability justice movement and its intersectional
        framework; and
    (9) ongoing challenges in disability rights implementation.
(b) SECRETARY.—The term "Secretary" means the Secretary of Education.
(c) STATE EDUCATIONAL AGENCY.—The term "State educational agency"
    has the meaning given in section 8101 of the Elementary and
    Secondary Education Act of 1965 (20 U.S.C. 7801).

SEC. 4. GRANT PROGRAM.

(a) IN GENERAL.—The Secretary shall award competitive grants to State
    educational agencies to support the inclusion of disability
    rights history in K-12 social studies and history curricula.
(b) USE OF FUNDS.—A State educational agency receiving a grant under
    this section shall use the funds to—
    (1) develop or adopt curriculum standards that include
        disability rights history as a component of United States
        history and civil rights education;
    (2) develop or acquire age-appropriate instructional materials
        on disability rights history;
    (3) provide professional development for teachers on
        disability rights history content and pedagogy; and
    (4) partner with disability rights organizations, disability
        studies programs, and people with disabilities to ensure
        that curriculum development reflects the perspectives and
        experiences of the disability community.
(c) PRIORITY.—The Secretary shall give priority to applications
    that—
    (1) integrate disability rights history into existing civil
        rights and American history units rather than treating it
        as a separate or supplementary topic;
    (2) include partnerships with disability-led organizations;
    (3) include assessment of student learning outcomes; and
    (4) propose sustainable implementation beyond the grant period.
(d) MATCHING REQUIREMENT.—A State educational agency receiving a
    grant shall provide matching funds equal to 25 percent of the
    grant amount.
(e) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
    $25,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2032 to
    carry out this section.

SEC. 5. MODEL CURRICULUM STANDARDS.

(a) The Secretary, in consultation with the National Council on
    Disability, the Smithsonian Institution, and nationally
    recognized disability rights organizations, shall develop and
    publish model curriculum standards for the teaching of
    disability rights history in grades K-12.
(b) The model standards shall be published not later than 1 year
    after the date of enactment of this Act.
(c) The model standards shall be advisory and shall not preempt
    State or local curriculum decisions.

SEC. 6. REPORT.

Not later than 3 years after the date of enactment, the Secretary
shall submit to Congress a report on the implementation of this Act,
including the number of States that have adopted disability rights
history curriculum standards and recommendations for further action.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill uses the federal grant incentive model (rather than a mandate) to encourage state adoption of disability rights history curricula. The model curriculum standards provide a resource for states without imposing federal requirements. The inclusion of disability community partnership requirements ensures that the curriculum is developed with, not just about, people with disabilities.

**Potential Challenges**: Education policy is primarily a state and local function; federal curriculum incentives may face resistance from states opposing federal education mandates. Appropriations may be inadequate. Textbook revision cycles are slow.

**Refinements**: Could include a dedicated NEH disability history research program. Could establish a national Disability History Education Advisory Board. Could incentivize publisher adoption of disability history content through federal textbook review standards.

### The National Disability Rights Museum Act

**Purpose**: Authorize the establishment of a national museum dedicated to disability rights history within the Smithsonian Institution.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "National Disability Rights Museum Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
(a) the disability rights movement is a defining chapter in the
    American civil rights story;
(b) no national museum dedicated to disability rights history
    exists in the United States;
(c) a national museum would preserve the movement's history,
    educate the public, and honor the contributions of disability
    rights leaders and advocates;
(d) the Smithsonian Institution is the appropriate entity to
    establish and administer such a museum; and
(e) similar national museums have been authorized and constructed
    for other civil rights communities, including the National
    Museum of African American History and Culture (Public Law
    108-184).

SEC. 3. ESTABLISHMENT.

(a) There is established within the Smithsonian Institution a museum
    to be known as the "National Museum of Disability Rights
    History" (in this Act referred to as the "Museum").
(b) The Museum shall—
    (1) collect, preserve, and interpret artifacts, documents,
        photographs, and other materials related to disability
        rights history;
    (2) develop exhibitions and educational programs on disability
        rights history for the public;
    (3) serve as a national repository for disability rights
        movement archives and oral histories;
    (4) conduct and support scholarly research on disability
        rights history; and
    (5) ensure full physical, digital, and programmatic
        accessibility for people with all types of disabilities.

SEC. 4. MUSEUM STUDY COMMISSION.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established a Commission to Study the
    Potential Creation of a National Museum of Disability Rights
    History (in this section referred to as the "Commission").
(b) MEMBERSHIP.—The Commission shall consist of 23 members
    appointed as follows—
    (1) 7 members appointed by the Board of Regents of the
        Smithsonian Institution, at least 4 of whom shall be
        people with disabilities;
    (2) 4 members appointed by the Speaker of the House;
    (3) 4 members appointed by the Minority Leader of the House;
    (4) 4 members appointed by the Majority Leader of the Senate;
    (5) 4 members appointed by the Minority Leader of the Senate.
(c) DUTIES.—The Commission shall—
    (1) conduct a study on the feasibility and cost of
        establishing the Museum;
    (2) evaluate potential sites;
    (3) develop recommendations for governance, collections, and
        programming; and
    (4) submit a report to Congress not later than 18 months
        after the date of the Commission's establishment.
(d) MAJORITY DISABILITY REPRESENTATION.—At least 12 of the 23
    members of the Commission shall be people with disabilities.
(e) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
    $3,000,000 to carry out this section.

SEC. 5. CONSTRUCTION AND FUNDING.

(a) The Board of Regents of the Smithsonian Institution is
    authorized to design and construct the Museum following the
    Commission's recommendations.
(b) There is authorized to be appropriated such sums as may be
    necessary for the design, construction, and operation of the
    Museum.
(c) The Smithsonian Institution may solicit and accept private
    donations for the Museum.

SEC. 6. ACCESSIBILITY.

The Museum shall meet or exceed all applicable accessibility
standards under the Americans with Disabilities Act, Section 504 of
the Rehabilitation Act, and the Architectural Barriers Act, and
shall serve as a model of universal design and accessibility for
all Smithsonian facilities.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill follows the model of the National Museum of African American History and Culture Act (2003), beginning with a study commission and progressing to authorization of construction. The majority disability representation requirement on the commission ensures that the museum is developed by the community whose history it tells.

**Potential Challenges**: Museum construction requires significant appropriations ($300-600 million). Smithsonian site availability in Washington, DC, is limited. Political will for new museum construction fluctuates with budget priorities.

**Refinements**: Could authorize an interim digital museum platform during the commission and construction phases. Could mandate that the museum include a research center supporting disability history scholarship. Could require partnerships with existing disability archives and collections.

### The Disability Rights Oral History Preservation Act

**Purpose**: Authorize and fund an emergency national initiative to preserve the oral histories of disability rights movement participants.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Disability Rights Oral History
Preservation Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
(a) the founding generation of the disability rights movement is
    aging, and key leaders have died in recent years, including
    Judy Heumann (2023), Justin Dart Jr. (2002), Ed Roberts
    (1995), and Paul Longmore (2010);
(b) surviving movement participants hold irreplaceable firsthand
    accounts of the events that produced the most significant
    disability civil rights laws in history;
(c) existing oral history collections, including the ADA Legacy
    Project and the Disability Visibility Project, are valuable
    but insufficiently funded and incomplete;
(d) the Library of Congress Veterans History Project and StoryCorps
    provide successful models for national oral history
    preservation initiatives; and
(e) the preservation of disability rights oral histories is a
    time-sensitive emergency that requires immediate action.

SEC. 3. ORAL HISTORY INITIATIVE.

(a) IN GENERAL.—The Librarian of Congress, in coordination with
    the Smithsonian Institution and the National Endowment for
    the Humanities, shall establish a Disability Rights Oral
    History Initiative (in this Act referred to as the
    "Initiative").
(b) PURPOSES.—The Initiative shall—
    (1) conduct oral history interviews with surviving
        participants in the disability rights movement,
        prioritizing individuals aged 70 and older;
    (2) digitize and preserve existing disability rights oral
        history collections held by universities, organizations,
        and individuals;
    (3) make all oral histories freely available to the public
        through an accessible online platform that meets WCAG 2.2
        AA standards, including captioning, audio description,
        ASL translation, and plain language summaries;
    (4) develop educational resources based on oral history
        content for use in K-12 and higher education settings; and
    (5) coordinate with disability rights organizations to
        identify and reach potential interview subjects.
(c) ACCESSIBILITY.—All oral history recordings, transcripts, and
    associated materials shall be produced in accessible formats
    including captioned video, audio, Braille-ready text, and
    plain language versions.
(d) COMMUNITY PARTNERSHIP.—The Initiative shall partner with
    disability-led organizations, Centers for Independent Living,
    and disability studies programs to ensure that the oral
    history collection process is accessible, culturally
    appropriate, and guided by the disability community.
(e) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated—
    (1) $5,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
    (2) $5,000,000 for fiscal year 2028;
    (3) $3,000,000 for fiscal year 2029;
    (4) $3,000,000 for fiscal year 2030; and
    (5) $3,000,000 for fiscal year 2031.

SEC. 4. REPORT.

Not later than 2 years after the date of enactment, the Librarian
of Congress shall submit to Congress a report on the Initiative's
progress, including the number of interviews conducted, collections
digitized, and public access provided.

SEC. 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the most time-sensitive need: preserving the firsthand accounts of movement participants before they are lost. The declining authorization amounts reflect the urgency of the initial collection phase. The accessibility requirements ensure that the oral histories themselves model the values of the movement they document.

**Potential Challenges**: Relatively modest appropriations request but may face competition for NEH and Library of Congress funding. Identifying and reaching movement participants requires extensive community partnerships. Ensuring comprehensive geographic and demographic representation requires intentional outreach.

**Refinements**: Could include a matching grant component for universities and organizations that contribute existing collections. Could establish a fellowship program for disability community members to serve as oral history collectors. Could mandate annual public programming events using oral history content.

## State Model Legislation

### Model Disability Rights History Education Act

**Purpose**: Provide a template for state-level requirements to include disability rights history in K-12 curricula.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Disability Rights History
Education Act".

SECTION 2. FINDINGS.

The [State] Legislature finds that—
(a) the disability rights movement is a significant civil rights
    movement in American history;
(b) disability rights history is currently underrepresented in
    [State]'s K-12 curricula;
(c) 26 percent of adults in [State] have a disability; and
(d) educating students about disability rights history promotes
    civic understanding, reduces prejudice, and prepares students
    for participation in a diverse society.

SECTION 3. CURRICULUM REQUIREMENTS.

(a) The [State Board of Education / State Department of Education]
    shall incorporate disability rights history into the State's
    social studies and United States history curriculum standards,
    including—
    (1) the history of disability in America, including the
        eugenics era and institutionalization;
    (2) the disability rights movement and its key events,
        leaders, and organizations;
    (3) landmark disability rights legislation, including the
        Americans with Disabilities Act; and
    (4) ongoing disability rights issues and advocacy.
(b) Disability rights history shall be integrated into existing
    civil rights and American history units, not taught as a
    separate or supplementary topic.

SECTION 4. PROFESSIONAL DEVELOPMENT.

The [State Department of Education] shall provide professional
development opportunities for teachers on disability rights history
content and pedagogy, in partnership with disability rights
organizations and disability studies programs at [State] colleges
and universities.

SECTION 5. INSTRUCTIONAL MATERIALS.

The [State Board of Education] shall evaluate and recommend
instructional materials on disability rights history, giving
preference to materials developed with the involvement of people
with disabilities.

SECTION 6. IMPLEMENTATION TIMELINE.

This Act shall be implemented not later than the beginning of the
[2028-2029] school year.

SECTION 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on [date].
```

**Explanation**: This model follows the approach of New Jersey (2019) and Illinois (2021) disability history education laws. It mandates integration rather than a standalone requirement, reducing curricular disruption. Professional development provisions address the fact that most teachers have not been trained in disability history.

**Adaptations**: States may adjust the implementation timeline, specify grade levels, and tailor partnerships to local disability organizations and university programs.

## Regulatory Framework

### Department of Education Guidance on Disability History in Civil Rights Education

**Existing Authority**: The Department of Education has authority to issue guidance to State educational agencies on the implementation of federally funded education programs, including social studies and history standards.

**Draft Guidance**: The Department of Education should issue guidance to State educational agencies recommending the inclusion of disability rights history in civil rights education, providing model curriculum resources, and identifying federal grant programs available to support implementation.

**Explanation**: This administrative approach can be pursued without new legislation, leveraging existing education funding to incentivize disability history inclusion. While guidance is not binding, it signals federal priority and provides practical resources.

### National Park Service Expedited Review of Disability Rights Sites

**Existing Authority**: The National Park Service manages the National Register of Historic Places and the National Historic Landmarks program.

**Draft Directive**: The Secretary of the Interior should direct the National Park Service to prioritize the review of disability rights movement sites for National Register and National Historic Landmark designation, including the San Francisco Federal Building (504 sit-in), the Ed Roberts Campus, Gallaudet University, and other sites identified by the disability community.

**Explanation**: NPS already has the authority to designate historic sites. An administrative directive prioritizing disability rights sites would accelerate the preservation of the movement's physical heritage without new legislation.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Spending Clause**: The Disability Rights History Education Act uses the grant incentive model, which is well within Spending Clause authority. Federal education grants with conditions are constitutionally established (*South Dakota v. Dole*, 483 U.S. 203 (1987)).
- **Treaty Clause**: CRPD ratification requires a two-thirds Senate vote (Art. II, Section 2, Cl. 2). No constitutional barrier exists to ratification; the question is political will.
- **Establishment Clause**: Education legislation must ensure that disability history curricula are secular and do not endorse or denigrate religious viewpoints on disability.
- **First Amendment**: Curriculum requirements do not raise First Amendment concerns when they prescribe topics for instruction rather than mandating specific viewpoints. States have broad authority to set curriculum standards.

### Preemption Questions

- Federal education incentive legislation does not preempt state curriculum decisions. States remain free to adopt or decline the model standards.
- Federal museum authorization does not preempt state or private museum activity.

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- Grant program compliance monitored through standard Department of Education reporting requirements
- Museum commission subject to congressional oversight
- Oral history initiative subject to Library of Congress and NEH accountability structures
- Historic site designations subject to NPS administrative review

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Disability Rights History Education Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Grant incentive without mandate | States can decline grants and ignore disability history entirely | High |
| Curriculum integration without assessment | "Integration" requirement may result in token mentions without substantive coverage | Medium |
| Professional development without standards | Teacher training may be superficial without quality benchmarks | Medium |
| Advisory model standards | Model standards have no binding effect on states that do not adopt them | High |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Voluntary state participation | Many states, particularly those resistant to civil rights education, may not participate | Federal system limits education mandates |
| Textbook market inertia | Major textbook publishers may not revise content without stronger demand signals | Market-driven textbook industry; limited federal leverage |
| Disability community diversity not guaranteed | Curriculum may default to dominant movement narratives (white, physically disabled) without intentional diversification | Historical record bias; curriculum developer unfamiliarity |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Add outcome-based reporting requirements: states must demonstrate that students can identify key events and figures in disability rights history through standardized assessments
2. Condition additional education funding (beyond disability history grants) on adoption of disability history standards after a 5-year voluntary period
3. Establish an advisory board with majority disability representation to review and approve curriculum materials
4. Fund disability history textbook development directly through NEH grants to publishers
5. Require that curriculum materials include diverse disability perspectives (intellectual disability, psychiatric disability, disability of color, Deaf culture)

### National Disability Rights Museum Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Commission without construction mandate | Commission could issue report without Congress authorizing construction funding | High |
| Appropriations dependence | Museum construction depends on future appropriations that may not materialize | High |
| Governance capture | Museum governance could be dominated by non-disabled appointees | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Long timeline | Museum may take 10-15 years from authorization to opening | Museum construction inherently slow; funding uncertain |
| Physical museum limitations | A single physical museum cannot reach all Americans | Geographic concentration of cultural institutions |
| Narrative control tensions | Different disability communities may disagree on how history is told | Legitimate internal diversity within disability community |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Mandate that Congress act on construction authorization within 2 years of commission report
2. Authorize a digital museum platform that can launch within 3 years, independent of physical museum construction
3. Require majority disability representation on all museum governance bodies, with representation across disability types
4. Establish a community advisory council including representatives from all major disability communities to guide narrative development
5. Include traveling exhibition program to extend museum reach beyond Washington, DC

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Appropriations vulnerability | Embed disability history funding in larger education and cultural authorization bills; seek bipartisan sponsorship |
| Administration changes affecting NPS and NEH priorities | Codify disability history programs in statute rather than relying on administrative directives |
| Oral history collection outpacing processing capacity | Invest in AI-assisted transcription and indexing (with human review) to accelerate processing |
| Community trust and participation | Ensure disability community control over narrative; pay disability participants for their time and expertise |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Education grant program reauthorized every 5 years with GAO review of state participation and outcomes
- Museum commission report due within 18 months; congressional action within 2 years of report
- Oral history initiative progress report after 2 years; final report after 5 years
- NPS disability rights site review every 3 years to identify additional sites for designation
