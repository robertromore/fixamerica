# Disability Rights History: Actions

## Overview

This document provides concrete actions that individuals, community groups, and advocates can take to preserve, promote, and teach disability rights history. Actions range from personal education and storytelling to institutional advocacy and legislative campaigns.

## Individual Actions

### As a Person with a Disability

- **Learn your own movement's history**: Read at least one book on disability rights history (see [Resources](09-resources.md)). Watch *Crip Camp* (Netflix) and *Lives Worth Living* (PBS). Understanding the history of the movement that created your legal protections is empowering and strategically important.
- **Record your own story**: Contact the Disability Visibility Project (disabilityvisibility.com) or your local Center for Independent Living to contribute an oral history. Every disabled person's experience is part of the movement's ongoing story.
- **Share disability history on social media**: During Disability History Month (October) and ADA anniversary (July 26), share historical facts, stories, and images. Use hashtags like #DisabilityHistory, #CripTheVote, and #ADAanniversary to reach broader audiences.
- **Mentor younger advocates**: If you have movement experience, seek out opportunities to share your knowledge with younger disability activists. The generational transfer of movement knowledge is essential for continuity.
- **Attend commemorative events**: Participate in ADA anniversary events, Disability History Month programs, and movement commemorations in your community.

### As a Family Member or Caregiver

- **Learn the parent advocacy history**: If you are a parent of a child with a disability, understand that your advocacy connects to a tradition dating to the founding of The Arc (1950) and the fight for educational rights (*PARC v. Commonwealth*, 1972).
- **Support self-advocacy**: While learning history, recognize the transition from parent-led to self-advocate-led movements. Support the principle of "Nothing About Us Without Us" by centering the voices of people with disabilities in historical narratives.
- **Advocate for disability history in your child's school**: Ask teachers and school administrators whether disability history is included in the curriculum. If not, provide resources from the DREDF Teaching Disability History Toolkit and advocate for inclusion.

### As an Educator

- **Integrate disability history into existing curricula**: You do not need a separate unit on disability history. Include the 504 sit-ins when teaching the civil rights movement. Include the ADA when teaching about landmark legislation. Include the eugenics era when teaching about civil liberties.
- **Use primary sources**: Incorporate oral histories from the ADA Legacy Project and Disability Visibility Project. Use the *Christmas in Purgatory* photographs when teaching about institutional conditions. These primary sources are powerful educational tools.
- **Invite disability rights advocates as guest speakers**: Contact your local Center for Independent Living, Protection and Advocacy agency, or disability studies program to identify speakers who can share firsthand movement experience.
- **Assess your textbooks**: Check whether your U.S. history and government textbooks include disability rights history. If they do not, advocate for supplementary materials and notify the textbook publisher that disability content is needed.
- **Pursue professional development**: Seek out training in disability history through the Society for Disability Studies, the Disability History Association, or university disability studies programs.

### As a Historian or Researcher

- **Prioritize oral history collection**: If you have access to movement veterans, prioritize recording their stories. Time is limited; many movement leaders are elderly or have already died.
- **Publish disability history for general audiences**: The field needs accessible books, articles, and media that reach beyond academic audiences. Popular history of disability rights is underdeveloped compared to other civil rights movements.
- **Integrate disability into general history**: When writing about the civil rights era, the 1970s, the 1990s, or any period of American history, include disability as a category of analysis. Disability was always present; it has just been invisible in historical writing.
- **Archive your own research materials**: Ensure that your research notes, interview recordings, and other materials are deposited in accessible archives for future scholars.

### As a Journalist or Media Professional

- **Cover disability rights as civil rights**: Frame disability rights stories in the civil rights tradition. The disability rights movement is not a "human interest" story; it is a political and legal story comparable to other civil rights movements.
- **Provide historical context in disability stories**: When covering ADA enforcement, disability policy, or accessibility issues, include historical context about how current laws and policies were won.
- **Profile movement history**: Commission or produce stories about disability rights history for mainstream audiences. The success of *Crip Camp* demonstrates that disability history has broad audience appeal.

## Community Actions

### Organizing for Change

- **Start a disability history reading group**: Organize a community reading group through your local library, Center for Independent Living, or disability organization. Focus on disability rights history texts.
- **Host a disability history film screening**: Organize public screenings of *Crip Camp*, *Lives Worth Living*, or other disability history documentaries, followed by discussion with local disability advocates.
- **Conduct local disability history research**: Document the disability rights history in your own community. Who were the local movement leaders? When did your town install its first curb cuts? What was the history of local institutions? Local history projects connect the national narrative to community experience.
- **Organize commemoration events**: Plan ADA anniversary (July 26) and Disability History Month (October) events in your community. Partner with local government, libraries, schools, and disability organizations.
- **Create historical markers**: Work with your local or state historical society to place historical markers at disability rights sites in your community (sites of local protests, locations of former institutions, homes of movement leaders).

### Advocacy Campaigns

- **State legislature advocacy for disability history education**: Support legislation requiring disability history in K-12 curricula, modeled on New Jersey and Illinois laws. Contact state representatives and testify at committee hearings.
- **School board advocacy**: Attend school board meetings to advocate for including disability history in social studies and history curricula. Provide specific curriculum resources and examples from states that have enacted requirements.
- **Museum advocacy**: Contact your local, state, and national museums to advocate for disability history exhibitions and collections. Write to the Smithsonian Institution urging creation of a national disability history museum.
- **Archive preservation advocacy**: Contact university libraries and state archives to advocate for collecting and preserving disability rights movement materials. Offer to connect archivists with movement veterans who have personal collections.

## Template Communications

### Email to School Board About Disability History Curriculum

```
Subject: Request to Include Disability Rights History in District Curriculum

Dear [School Board Chair / Superintendent],

I am writing to request that [School District] include disability rights
history in its K-12 social studies and U.S. history curricula.

The disability rights movement is one of the most significant civil rights
movements in American history. The Americans with Disabilities Act passed
the Senate 76-8 and the House 403-20 with bipartisan support, and was
signed by President George H.W. Bush in 1990. Yet disability rights
history is largely absent from our schools' curricula and textbooks.

Key events that should be taught alongside other civil rights history:
- The eugenics era, when over 60,000 Americans were forcibly sterilized
- The 504 sit-in of 1977, the longest federal building occupation in
  U.S. history
- The Capitol Crawl of 1990, when disability activists crawled up the
  Capitol steps to demand passage of the ADA
- The ADA itself, one of the most important civil rights laws ever enacted

61 million Americans live with disabilities, 26% of the adult population.
Their history deserves to be taught.

States including New Jersey and Illinois have already enacted disability
history education requirements. The Disability Rights Education and
Defense Fund (DREDF) offers a Teaching Disability History Toolkit with
age-appropriate materials.

I would welcome the opportunity to discuss this further with the board
or administration.

Sincerely,
[Name]
[Contact Information]
```

```
Hello, my name is [name] and I am a constituent from [city/zip code].

I am calling to ask [Representative/Senator name] to support legislation
to preserve and promote disability rights history.

[Choose one or more]:

- I urge support for federal legislation encouraging states to include
  disability rights history in K-12 curricula. The disability rights
  movement is one of America's most important civil rights achievements,
  yet most Americans know almost nothing about it.

- I urge support for congressional authorization of a national disability
  rights history museum. 26% of Americans have a disability, yet there
  is no national museum recognizing the movement that won their civil
  rights.

- I urge support for Senate ratification of the UN Convention on the
  Rights of Persons with Disabilities. The CRPD was modeled on the ADA.
  190 countries have ratified it. The U.S. has not.

- I urge support for increased funding for the National Endowment for
  the Humanities to support disability history research and public
  programs. Movement veterans are aging and their oral histories must
  be preserved before they are lost.

This issue affects 61 million Americans with disabilities and their
families. Can I get the [Representative/Senator]'s position on this issue?

Thank you for your time.
```

### Letter to Museum Director

```
Dear [Museum Director],

I am writing to encourage [Museum Name] to develop an exhibition or
program on disability rights history.

The disability rights movement is among the most significant civil
rights movements in American history, yet it is almost entirely absent
from major museums. The Americans with Disabilities Act (1990), the
504 sit-ins (1977), the Capitol Crawl (1990), and the independent
living movement transformed American society, yet these events are
largely unknown to the general public.

The success of the documentary Crip Camp (2020, Netflix), which was
nominated for an Academy Award, demonstrates that disability rights
history has broad audience appeal. An exhibition on this topic would
attract significant public interest and fill a major gap in how
American history is presented to the public.

Resources for exhibition development include:
- The Paul K. Longmore Institute on Disability (SFSU)
- The ADA Legacy Project oral history collection
- The Museum of disABILITY History (Buffalo, NY)
- DREDF archival collections (Berkeley, CA)
- Gallaudet University Archives (Washington, DC)

I would welcome the opportunity to discuss this proposal further.

Sincerely,
[Name]
[Contact Information]
```

## Talking Points

### For Conversations with Elected Officials

- **The history is remarkable but unknown**: The 504 sit-in of 1977 was the longest occupation of a federal building in U.S. history. The Capitol Crawl of 1990 is one of the most dramatic images of any civil rights movement. Yet most Americans cannot name a single event in disability rights history.
- **The numbers are compelling**: 61 million Americans have disabilities, 26% of the adult population. This is the largest minority group in the country, and the only one whose civil rights history is not taught in schools.
- **The precedent is bipartisan**: The ADA passed with overwhelming bipartisan support. Bob Dole, George H.W. Bush, and Justin Dart Jr. (all Republicans) were champions alongside Tom Harkin and Steny Hoyer (Democrats). Disability rights history is a story of bipartisan achievement.
- **The time is running out**: Movement veterans are dying. If we do not preserve their oral histories and personal archives now, those stories will be lost forever.
- **The cost is small**: The total cost of a national oral history preservation initiative is $10-$19 million over 3-5 years, a fraction of 1% of NEH and Smithsonian budgets.

### For Conversations with Educators

- **Integration, not addition**: Disability history does not require a standalone unit. It integrates naturally into existing civil rights, American history, and government curricula.
- **Resources exist**: The DREDF Teaching Disability History Toolkit, the online Disability History Museum, and numerous books provide ready-made educational materials.
- **Students respond**: Disability history is compelling and accessible. Students find the 504 sit-ins and Capitol Crawl as powerful as any civil rights events they study.
- **It's relevant to their lives**: Every student either has a disability, knows someone with a disability, or will experience disability. Disability history is not about "other people"; it is about all of us.

### For Media and Public Communication

- **Frame as missing history**: The disability rights movement has been called "the last great civil rights movement," yet it remains almost invisible in public consciousness. This is a story of something remarkable that most people do not know about.
- **Use vivid stories**: The 504 sit-in, with the Black Panthers delivering food to disabled activists occupying a federal building for 25 days, is an extraordinary story. Eight-year-old Jennifer Keelan crawling up the Capitol steps is an iconic image. These stories deserve to be known.
- **Connect to current events**: Every time a disability policy issue arises (ADA enforcement, HCBS funding, employment discrimination), there is an opportunity to provide historical context that most audiences lack.
