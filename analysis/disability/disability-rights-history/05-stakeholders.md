# Disability Rights History: Stakeholders

## Overview

The preservation, teaching, and advancement of disability rights history involves a wide range of stakeholders whose interests, influence, and engagement shape how this history is understood, remembered, and used to inform current policy. People with disabilities themselves are the primary stakeholders, but the ecosystem extends to academic institutions, government agencies, media organizations, and the broader public. Understanding these dynamics is essential for strategies aimed at elevating disability rights history to the prominence it deserves alongside other civil rights histories.

## Primary Stakeholders: People Affected

### People with Disabilities

| Group | Population | Stake in Disability Rights History | Source |
|-------|-----------|-----------------------------------|--------|
| People with mobility disabilities | 13.7 million | Movement history rooted in physical accessibility struggles (Section 504 sit-ins, ADAPT, Capitol Crawl) | CDC DHDS (2023) |
| People who are deaf or hard of hearing | 11.5 million | Distinct cultural history; Deaf schools and organizations predating broader movement; tension between disability rights and Deaf culture identity | NIDCD (2023) |
| People with intellectual/developmental disabilities | 7.4 million | History of institutionalization, eugenics, and self-advocacy movement; parent-led organizations shaped early movement | ACL (2023) |
| People who are blind or have low vision | 7.0 million | Among the earliest organized disability groups (NFB, 1940); long history of political advocacy | AFB (2023) |
| People with psychiatric disabilities | 14.2 million | Deinstitutionalization history; psychiatric survivor movement; ongoing stigma shapes how their history is told | SAMHSA (2023) |
| People with chronic illness and invisible disabilities | Estimated 25+ million | Often excluded from traditional disability rights narratives; COVID-19 expanded awareness | Census Bureau, ACS (2023) |

### Disability Rights Movement Veterans and Leaders

The generation that built the disability rights movement from the 1960s through the 1990s is aging. Many key leaders have died, including Ed Roberts (1995), Justin Dart Jr. (2002), Paul Longmore (2010), and Judy Heumann (2023). Surviving movement veterans hold irreplaceable oral histories, personal archives, and institutional knowledge. Their stake in how history is told is deeply personal: they fought for these rights and want their struggles accurately represented.

### Younger Disability Advocates

A new generation of disability activists, often operating through the disability justice framework, has a different relationship to movement history. They seek to expand the narrative beyond the predominantly white, physically disabled leaders who have dominated the historical record, centering the contributions of disabled people of color, people with intellectual and psychiatric disabilities, and LGBTQ+ disabled people. Their stake is in ensuring that disability rights history is told in a way that reflects the full diversity of the community.

### Family Members and Caregivers

An estimated 53 million Americans serve as unpaid caregivers for people with disabilities (AARP, 2020). Families, particularly parents of children with disabilities, played a foundational role in the movement (The Arc, founded by parents in 1950; PARC v. Commonwealth, 1972). Their stake is in recognition of family advocacy contributions while respecting the self-advocacy principle that people with disabilities should lead their own movement.

| National Endowment for the Humanities (NEH) | Funds humanities research and public programs including history | Medium | Limited disability-specific funding |
| National Endowment for the Arts (NEA) | Funds arts programs including disability culture | Low-Medium | Some disability arts funding |

| Library of Congress | National archive; oral history collections | Medium | Some disability history collections; no dedicated program |
| President (proclamation authority) | Issues National Disability Employment Awareness Month (October); ADA anniversary recognitions | Low-Medium | Proclamations issued but limited public engagement |

| Actor | Role | Variation |
|-------|------|-----------|

| State historic preservation offices | Manage state historic registers and preservation programs | Disability sites largely absent from state registers |

## Coalition Dynamics

### Existing Coalitions

**Consortium for Citizens with Disabilities (CCD)**: The primary national cross-disability advocacy coalition. While focused on current policy, CCD provides institutional continuity that preserves movement history through organizational memory and shared advocacy frameworks.

**Disability History and Culture Network**: Informal network of disability studies scholars, historians, museum professionals, and community archivists working to preserve and promote disability history. Lacks formal structure but coordinates through academic conferences and online platforms.

### Potential Coalitions

| Coalition | Members | Common Interest | Obstacle |
|-----------|---------|-----------------|----------|
| Civil Rights History Coalition | Disability orgs + NAACP + HRC + labor history orgs | Integrated civil rights education | Competition for curriculum space; disability history marginalized within broader civil rights framing |
| Museum and Archive Coalition | Smithsonian + disability orgs + NPS + state archives | Dedicated disability history museum and collections | Funding; Smithsonian institutional priorities |
| Education Standards Coalition | Disability orgs + education associations + state boards | Disability history in K-12 curriculum | Curriculum overcrowding; political resistance to mandates |
| Oral History Preservation Coalition | ADA Legacy Project + Disability Visibility Project + universities + libraries | Preserving living movement history | Urgency (aging leaders); funding for transcription and digitization |
| International Disability History Coalition | U.S. disability orgs + international disability organizations + CRPD bodies | Global disability history recognition | U.S. non-ratification of CRPD limits engagement |

## Power Analysis

### Most Influential Actors

1. **State education boards**: Control whether disability history is taught in K-12 schools, which determines whether future generations understand the movement
2. **Smithsonian Institution**: Decision to create (or not create) a national disability history museum shapes public visibility
3. **Academic institutions**: Disability studies programs produce the scholarship that defines how disability history is understood
4. **Media producers**: Documentary films, journalism, and social media determine whether disability history reaches broad audiences
5. **Congressional appropriators**: Funding decisions for NEH, NEA, NPS, and Smithsonian determine available resources for preservation and education

### Least Powerful Actors with Greatest Stake

1. **Movement veterans and aging leaders**: Hold irreplaceable oral histories but lack institutional support for systematic preservation; many live on fixed incomes and cannot self-fund archival work
2. **Disabled people of color whose contributions are underrepresented**: Contributions to the movement are least documented despite significant roles (Black Panthers supporting 504 sit-ins; disability justice framework originated in communities of color)
3. **People with intellectual and developmental disabilities**: Their history of institutionalization and self-advocacy is least well-known publicly; face the greatest barriers to telling their own stories
4. **Rural disability communities**: Movement history is concentrated in urban centers (Berkeley, Washington DC, San Francisco); rural disability experience is largely undocumented
5. **Students in states without disability history requirements**: Lack exposure to disability rights history entirely; cannot advocate for what they do not know exists
