# Independent Living: Stakeholders

## Overview

Independent living policy involves a complex ecosystem of stakeholders with deeply intertwined interests. People with disabilities, their families, direct support workers, institutional providers, government agencies, and advocacy organizations all have stakes in how LTSS is financed, delivered, and regulated. Understanding these relationships -- particularly the power asymmetries between institutional providers and HCBS advocates -- is essential for designing reforms that can succeed politically and practically.

## Primary Stakeholders

### People with Disabilities

**Population**: 61 million American adults (26% of population); approximately 12 million need long-term services and supports (MACPAC, 2023)

| Subpopulation | Estimated Size | Key Independent Living Needs |
|--------------|---------------|----------------------------|
| Intellectual/developmental disabilities | 7.4 million | Supported living, day services, employment supports |
| Physical/mobility disabilities | 13.7 million with serious difficulty | Personal care, accessible housing, transportation |
| Psychiatric disabilities | 13.2 million with serious mental illness | Peer support, housing, crisis services |
| Elderly with disabilities | 20+ million age 65+ with functional limitations | Home care, assisted technology, meal services |
| Traumatic brain injury | 5.3 million living with TBI | Behavioral support, community reintegration |
| Sensory disabilities | 7.6 million vision; 37.5 million hearing | Communication supports, accessible technology |

**Interests**: Choice of where and how to live; control over services; adequate supports; community participation; employment; meaningful relationships; safety and dignity

**Power**: Significant moral authority and legal standing (*Olmstead*); growing political organization; limited economic power relative to institutional industry

### Family Members and Caregivers

**Population**: 53 million informal caregivers (AARP/NAC, 2020)

| Caregiver Type | Estimated Size | Key Concerns |
|---------------|---------------|-------------|
| Parent caregivers (children with disabilities) | 8 million | Service access, transition planning, respite |
| Parent caregivers (adult children with IDD) | 3.5 million | Aging parents, future planning, residential supports |
| Spousal caregivers | 12 million | LTSS access, financial impact, respite |
| Adult child caregivers (aging parents) | 24 million | Work-life balance, financial burden, HCBS access |
| Grandparent/other family caregivers | 5.5 million | Legal authority, service navigation, respite |

**Interests**: Adequate HCBS to reduce caregiving burden; respite services; financial support; workplace flexibility; peace of mind about quality; future planning for when they can no longer provide care

**Power**: Large voting bloc; significant advocacy organizations (AARP, National Alliance for Caregiving, The Arc); emotional narrative power

### Direct Support Professionals

**Population**: 4.6 million (PHI, 2024)

**Interests**: Living wages; health insurance; career advancement; safe working conditions; professional recognition; training and development; labor protections

**Power**: Essential to service delivery; growing union organization in some states (SEIU, AFSCME represent DSPs in several states); collective action capacity limited by fragmented employment

## Institutional Stakeholders

### Nursing Home Industry

**Key actors**: American Health Care Association/National Center for Assisted Living (AHCA/NCAL); large chains (Kindred, Sabra, Ensign Group); state nursing home associations

**Size**: 15,300 nursing facilities; 1.67 million beds; approximately $170 billion annual revenue (CMS, 2023)

**Interests**: Maintain Medicaid institutional funding; oppose mandatory HCBS benefit (which would redirect funding); resist staffing mandates; preserve private equity investment model; maintain political influence

**Power**: Concentrated lobbying power; $6.1 million federal lobbying (2023); $4.2 million political contributions (2023); major employers in rural communities; institutional knowledge of Medicaid policy (OpenSecrets, 2024)

### HCBS Provider Agencies

**Key actors**: State associations of home care agencies; individual provider agencies (typically small to mid-size); fiscal intermediary organizations

**Size**: Estimated 40,000+ agencies nationwide; mostly small businesses

**Interests**: Adequate Medicaid reimbursement rates; stable workforce; simplified administrative requirements; program expansion; manageable regulatory burden

**Power**: Fragmented; limited lobbying capacity compared to institutional providers; represented through state associations and national organizations (National Association for Home Care & Hospice)

## Government Stakeholders

### Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS)

**Role**: Federal agency administering Medicaid, including HCBS waiver programs

**Interests**: Program integrity; cost management; quality improvement; Olmstead implementation; HCBS expansion within budget constraints

**Power**: Approves state Medicaid plans and waivers; sets federal requirements; controls enhanced FMAP programs; issues guidance and regulations

**Key actions**: HCBS Settings Rule (2014); proposed HCBS access and quality rule (2023); ARPA HCBS funding administration

### State Medicaid Agencies

**Role**: Design, administer, and partially fund LTSS programs

**Interests**: Manage state budgets; comply with federal requirements; respond to constituent needs; balance institutional and community services; manage political pressures from providers, advocates, and legislators

**Power**: Control HCBS waiver design, provider rates, enrollment caps, and service definitions. Key decision-makers in independent living policy.

### Administration for Community Living (ACL)

**Role**: Federal agency administering independent living programs, including CIL funding

**Interests**: Strengthen CIL network; expand community living options; support disability rights; administer aging and disability programs

**Power**: Controls Title VII (Independent Living) funding ($109 million, FY 2024); administers Older Americans Act programs; coordinates aging and disability services

**Limitation**: Small budget relative to Medicaid LTSS spending; advisory rather than regulatory authority

### Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD)

**Role**: Administers Section 811, Housing Choice Vouchers, and fair housing enforcement

**Interests**: Expand affordable accessible housing; enforce fair housing; coordinate housing and services

**Power**: Controls housing program funding; sets fair housing regulations; provides housing assistance to 1.1 million disability voucher holders

### Department of Justice

**Role**: ADA enforcement, including Olmstead compliance

**Interests**: Civil rights enforcement; Olmstead implementation; disability discrimination remediation

**Power**: Can bring enforcement actions against states; negotiate consent decrees; issue guidance; limited resources relative to scope of violations

## Coalition Opportunities

### HCBS Expansion Coalition

The broadest coalition for independent living reform unites disability rights organizations, AARP and aging advocates, family caregiver organizations, HCBS providers, labor unions, and sympathetic state officials. This coalition shares the goal of making HCBS a mandatory Medicaid benefit and raising DSP wages. The coalition's combined constituency -- people with disabilities, elderly Americans, family caregivers, and care workers -- represents a significant majority of the population.

### Workforce-Centered Coalition

Labor unions (SEIU, AFSCME), PHI, disability rights organizations, and HCBS providers share an interest in raising DSP wages and improving working conditions. This coalition can frame workforce investment as both a workers' rights issue and a disability rights issue, potentially bridging labor-progressive and disability advocacy communities.

### Housing Integration Coalition

Disability rights organizations, HUD, fair housing advocates, housing developers, and state housing agencies share an interest in expanding accessible, integrated, affordable housing. This coalition connects independent living to broader housing affordability advocacy.

## Power Dynamics

### Key Power Asymmetry

The nursing home industry's concentrated economic and political power significantly outweighs the HCBS advocacy community's capacity. Nursing home lobbying spending exceeds disability rights organizations' combined lobbying budgets by a factor of approximately 10-to-1. This asymmetry explains why Medicaid's institutional bias has persisted for 60 years despite evidence, public preference, and legal mandates favoring community services.

### Counterbalancing Factors

- *Olmstead* provides legal leverage that does not require legislative action
- ARPA demonstrated the political feasibility of major HCBS investment
- COVID-19 nursing home deaths fundamentally damaged the institutional care narrative
- AARP's political power allies with disability advocates on HCBS expansion
- Labor unions add political weight and organizing capacity to the HCBS coalition
