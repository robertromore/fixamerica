# Independent Living: Actions

## For Individuals

### Learn First

**Understand the Problem**

- [ ] Read [Current State](02-current-state.md) to understand HCBS access barriers, waitlists, and workforce crisis
- [ ] Read [Root Causes](04-root-causes.md) to understand why Medicaid's institutional bias persists
- [ ] Review KFF's HCBS waiver waiting list data for your state (<https://www.kff.org/medicaid/>)
- [ ] Learn about your state's Medicaid HCBS waiver programs and eligibility criteria
- [ ] Understand the direct support workforce crisis and how it affects service availability

**Know Your Rights**

- [ ] Learn about *Olmstead v. L.C.* and your right to community-based services
- [ ] Contact your state Protection and Advocacy agency for assistance with institutional transition or HCBS access
- [ ] Understand your state's HCBS waiver application and appeals process
- [ ] Connect with your nearest Center for Independent Living for peer support and advocacy
- [ ] Learn about self-directed services options in your state and how to become an employer of record

### Take Action

**Contact Representatives**

- [ ] Call your members of Congress to support the HCBS Access Act (making HCBS a mandatory Medicaid benefit)
- [ ] Email your state legislators about DSP wage increases in your state's Medicaid budget
- [ ] Write to your governor urging development of a comprehensive Olmstead plan (if your state lacks one)
- [ ] Submit public comments to CMS during HCBS rulemaking comment periods
- [ ] Attend town halls and raise independent living, HCBS waitlists, and workforce issues as a constituent concern

**Join the Movement**

- [ ] Connect with ADAPT, NCIL, or other independent living advocacy organizations
- [ ] Join your state's Independent Living Council or attend its public meetings
- [ ] Participate in ADAPT's national actions and "Free Our People" campaigns
- [ ] Attend NCIL's annual conference or virtual advocacy events
- [ ] Connect with local disability advocacy groups and cross-disability coalitions

**Share Your Story**

- [ ] Document your HCBS access experiences (waitlist times, service gaps, DSP turnover, housing barriers)
- [ ] Share your story with advocacy organizations for policy campaigns
- [ ] Testify at state legislative hearings on Medicaid HCBS funding and waiver expansion
- [ ] Write letters to the editor about HCBS waitlists, institutional bias, and the workforce crisis
- [ ] Use social media to raise awareness about independent living barriers

### Vote and Support

**Direct Support**

- [ ] Donate to disability rights organizations working on HCBS expansion (ADAPT, NCIL, The Arc)
- [ ] Volunteer with organizations advocating for independent living reform
- [ ] Support candidates who endorse HCBS expansion, DSP wages, and Olmstead enforcement
- [ ] Vote YES on state ballot measures related to Medicaid expansion or disability services

**Ongoing Engagement**

- [ ] Vote in every election, including state and local (state officials control Medicaid HCBS policy)
- [ ] Track HCBS-related legislation in your state and in Congress
- [ ] Stay informed about CMS rulemaking on HCBS quality and access
- [ ] Participate in public comment periods for HCBS-related regulations
- [ ] Monitor your state's Olmstead compliance and hold officials accountable

## For Communities

### Local Independent Living Campaigns

**Assess the Landscape**

1. Identify HCBS access barriers in your community (waitlists, service gaps, provider availability, transportation)
2. Contact your nearest Center for Independent Living to understand available services and unmet needs
3. Map local HCBS providers, DSP vacancy rates, and accessible housing availability
4. Survey community members with disabilities about independent living barriers and priorities
5. Research your state's HCBS waiver programs, waitlist sizes, and DSP wage levels

**Build a Coalition**

1. Recruit disability organizations, aging services, caregiver groups, and labor unions
2. Engage Centers for Independent Living as anchor organizations
3. Connect with HCBS provider agencies who support rate increases and workforce investment
4. Partner with housing advocates, transit agencies, and accessible technology providers
5. Include people with disabilities as leaders and spokespeople in all coalition activities
6. Engage SEIU and AFSCME locals representing direct support workers

**Community Action Projects**

- Organize community forums on HCBS waitlists and independent living barriers
- Create peer mentoring programs connecting experienced self-directed services participants with new participants
- Establish community caregiving cooperatives where families and workers share resources
- Advocate for accessible community infrastructure (transportation, housing, public spaces)
- Host "Know Your Rights" workshops on Olmstead, HCBS waivers, and self-direction options

## For Organizations

### Employers of Direct Support Professionals

**Improve Workforce Conditions**

- [ ] Advocate for Medicaid rate increases sufficient to support living wages for DSPs
- [ ] Implement career ladder programs using NADSP credentialing framework
- [ ] Provide health insurance, paid leave, and retirement benefits for all DSPs working 20+ hours
- [ ] Invest in training and mentoring programs to reduce turnover
- [ ] Create respectful workplace cultures that value DSPs as professionals

**Advocate for Workforce Policy**

- [ ] Join provider coalitions supporting Medicaid rate increases
- [ ] Share workforce data (turnover, vacancies, service gaps) with state legislators and Medicaid officials
- [ ] Support federal DSP wage floor legislation
- [ ] Partner with labor unions to develop collaborative workforce solutions
- [ ] Participate in CMS rulemaking on HCBS access and workforce standards

### Centers for Independent Living

**Strengthen Independent Living Services**

- [ ] Expand nursing home transition services using Money Follows the Person partnerships
- [ ] Develop self-direction training and support programs for HCBS participants
- [ ] Build peer support networks for people transitioning to community living
- [ ] Create housing navigation services linking HCBS participants to accessible housing
- [ ] Implement technology programs helping consumers use assistive technology for independence

**Lead Policy Advocacy**

- [ ] Coordinate state-level HCBS expansion campaigns with NCIL and ADAPT
- [ ] Serve on state Independent Living Councils and Medicaid advisory committees
- [ ] Provide testimony on HCBS waitlists, Olmstead compliance, and workforce needs
- [ ] Document community integration outcomes to build the evidence base
- [ ] Participate in Olmstead planning processes and monitor state compliance

### Healthcare and Social Service Providers

**Support Community Integration**

- [ ] Screen patients and clients for HCBS eligibility and refer to community services
- [ ] Develop discharge planning protocols that prioritize community placement over institutional referral
- [ ] Partner with CILs and HCBS providers to coordinate care transitions
- [ ] Train clinical staff on *Olmstead* requirements and community-based alternatives to institutionalization
- [ ] Advocate within your organization for community-first service models

### Housing Organizations

**Expand Accessible Housing**

- [ ] Incorporate universal design and visitability features in all new construction
- [ ] Apply for Section 811 project-based rental assistance for people with disabilities
- [ ] Develop partnerships with HCBS agencies to integrate housing and services
- [ ] Advocate for zoning reform eliminating barriers to disability housing
- [ ] Create housing navigation programs linking people with disabilities to available accessible units

## For Advocates and Policymakers

### Federal Level

**Legislative Priorities**

1. HCBS Access Act: Make home and community-based services a mandatory Medicaid benefit, eliminating waitlists
2. Direct Support Professional Workforce Act: Establish a federal DSP wage floor at 150% of local living wage
3. Olmstead Enforcement Act: Strengthen DOJ enforcement tools and create private right of action
4. Affordable Accessible Housing Act: Expand Section 811 to 200,000 units; require visitability in federal construction
5. CIL Empowerment Act: Triple CIL funding to $300 million annually

**Regulatory Priorities**

1. CMS: Finalize and strengthen HCBS access, quality, and workforce rule (80% pass-through requirement)
2. CMS: Streamline HCBS waiver authorities into a single, simplified framework
3. DOJ: Launch Olmstead Enforcement Initiative with investigations in states without plans
4. HUD: Expand Section 811 and create joint CMS-HUD supportive housing pilots
5. DOL: Establish DSP apprenticeship program and career pathway standards

### State Level

**Model Legislation Priorities**

1. State DSP minimum wage legislation (at least $20/hour)
2. Olmstead comprehensive plan requirements with measurable benchmarks
3. HCBS waiver expansion and waitlist elimination timelines
4. Self-direction expansion with budget and employer authority
5. Accessible housing visitability requirements for new residential construction

**Administrative Actions**

1. Medicaid rate increases for HCBS providers sufficient to support DSP wage increases
2. State Olmstead plan development with disability community input
3. Expansion of Community First Choice (1915(k)) option
4. Self-directed services program improvements and expansion
5. Housing-HCBS coordination agreements between state housing and Medicaid agencies

### Building Effective Advocacy

**Messaging Principles**

| Do | Do Not |
|----|--------|
| Frame independent living as a civil right established by *Olmstead* | Frame as a new entitlement or benefit expansion |
| Lead with personal stories of people on waitlists or unnecessarily institutionalized | Lead with budget numbers or policy jargon |
| Emphasize cost-effectiveness of HCBS compared to institutional care | Imply that institutional care should be eliminated entirely |
| Connect independent living to workforce dignity and living wages for DSPs | Overlook the workforce dimension of independent living |
| Highlight COVID-19 nursing home failures as evidence for community alternatives | Use crisis framing without solutions |
| Build cross-disability, cross-generational coalitions (disability + aging + labor) | Frame as a niche disability concern |

**Coalition Building**

- Disability rights organizations provide moral authority, legal standing, and personal stories
- AARP provides political influence for aging-related HCBS and caregiver support
- Labor unions (SEIU, AFSCME) add political weight and organizing capacity for workforce reforms
- HCBS providers bring employer perspective and service delivery expertise
- Family caregiver organizations bring family narrative and broad public sympathy
- Housing advocates connect independent living to broader affordable housing campaigns
- State officials from leading states provide evidence of successful HCBS expansion models
