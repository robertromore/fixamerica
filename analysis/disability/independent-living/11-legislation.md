# Independent Living: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Independent living reform requires structural changes to Medicaid financing, workforce policy, housing programs, and civil rights enforcement. This document provides draft legal text for the key reforms identified in [Solutions](07-solutions.md): making home and community-based services a mandatory Medicaid benefit, establishing a federal direct support professional wage floor, strengthening Olmstead enforcement, expanding accessible housing, and empowering Centers for Independent Living. The legislative strategy addresses the foundational problem -- Medicaid's institutional bias -- while simultaneously building the workforce, housing, and service infrastructure necessary for community living.

## Federal Legislation

### HCBS Access Act

**Purpose**: Eliminate Medicaid's institutional bias by making home and community-based services a mandatory benefit for all individuals who meet institutional level of care criteria, eliminating HCBS waitlists within five years.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Home and Community-Based
Services Access Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.--Congress finds that--
    (1) the Supreme Court held in Olmstead v. L.C., 527
        U.S. 581 (1999), that unjustified institutionalization
        of people with disabilities constitutes discrimination
        under the Americans with Disabilities Act;
    (2) more than 25 years after Olmstead, over 820,000
        Americans with disabilities remain on waiting lists
        for home and community-based services;
    (3) Medicaid nursing facility services are a mandatory
        benefit, while home and community-based services
        remain optional, creating a structural institutional
        bias in federal law;
    (4) home and community-based services cost less per
        person than institutional care for the majority of
        LTSS populations;
    (5) 90 percent of people with disabilities and seniors
        prefer to receive services in their own homes and
        communities; and
    (6) the COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated the lethal
        vulnerability of institutional congregate care
        settings, with over 200,000 nursing home residents
        and staff dying from COVID-19.

(b) PURPOSE.--The purpose of this Act is to--
    (1) make home and community-based services a mandatory
        benefit under Medicaid;
    (2) eliminate HCBS waiting lists within 5 years of
        enactment;
    (3) ensure that all Medicaid-eligible individuals who
        meet institutional level of care criteria can receive
        services in their homes and communities; and
    (4) rebalance Medicaid long-term services and supports
        financing to support community integration.

SEC. 3. HCBS AS MANDATORY MEDICAID BENEFIT.

(a) MANDATORY BENEFIT.--Section 1905(a) of the Social
Security Act (42 U.S.C. 1396d(a)) is amended to include
home and community-based services as a mandatory benefit
for individuals who--
    (1) meet the institutional level of care criteria
        established by the State for nursing facility,
        intermediate care facility, or hospital services;
        and
    (2) can be appropriately served in a home or
        community-based setting as determined through a
        person-centered assessment.

(b) COVERED SERVICES.--Home and community-based services
under this section shall include, at minimum--
    (1) personal care and attendant services;
    (2) homemaker and chore services;
    (3) adult day services;
    (4) habilitation services;
    (5) respite care;
    (6) case management;
    (7) supported employment;
    (8) community transition services for individuals
        leaving institutions;
    (9) assistive technology and home modifications;
    (10) transportation to and from HCBS; and
    (11) self-directed service options, including both
         budget authority and employer authority.

(c) CONSUMER DIRECTION.--States shall offer self-directed
service options to all HCBS participants, including--
    (1) employer authority to hire, manage, and terminate
        workers; and
    (2) budget authority to determine how allocated funds
        are spent within an approved service plan.

(d) WAITLIST ELIMINATION.--
    (1) States shall serve all eligible individuals within
        90 days of eligibility determination beginning 5
        years after the date of enactment.
    (2) During the 5-year phase-in period, states shall
        reduce existing waiting lists by at least 20 percent
        per year.

SEC. 4. ENHANCED FEDERAL MEDICAL ASSISTANCE PERCENTAGE.

(a) NEW ENROLLEES.--The Federal medical assistance
percentage for individuals newly enrolled in HCBS under
this Act shall be--
    (1) 90 percent for the first 3 fiscal years following
        enactment;
    (2) 85 percent for the 4th fiscal year;
    (3) 80 percent for the 5th fiscal year; and
    (4) the standard Federal medical assistance percentage
        thereafter, except that the FMAP for HCBS shall not
        be less than 6 percentage points above the State's
        standard FMAP.

(b) EXISTING ENROLLEES.--The Federal medical assistance
percentage for HCBS provided to individuals enrolled before
enactment of this Act shall be increased by 6 percentage
points above the State's standard FMAP.

SEC. 5. HCBS QUALITY AND ACCESS STANDARDS.

(a) QUALITY STANDARDS.--The Secretary shall establish
minimum quality standards for HCBS, including--
    (1) person-centered planning requirements;
    (2) incident reporting and management systems;
    (3) community integration outcome measures;
    (4) participant satisfaction assessment; and
    (5) workforce adequacy metrics.

(b) ACCESS MONITORING.--States shall report to the
Secretary, not less than annually--
    (1) HCBS waiting list status;
    (2) average time from eligibility determination to
        service initiation;
    (3) provider capacity and workforce data;
    (4) participant outcomes, including community
        participation, employment, and health; and
    (5) complaints and incidents.

(c) 80 PERCENT PASS-THROUGH.--Not less than 80 percent of
Medicaid HCBS payments to provider agencies shall be
expended on compensation (wages, benefits, and training)
for direct care workers providing services.

SEC. 6. STREAMLINED HCBS AUTHORITIES.

(a) The Secretary shall issue regulations, not later than
2 years after enactment, establishing a unified HCBS
framework that consolidates the requirements of sections
1915(c), 1915(i), and 1915(k) of the Social Security Act
into a single, simplified program authority.

(b) States operating HCBS programs under existing waiver
or state plan authorities shall be permitted to continue
under those authorities or transition to the unified
framework at their option.

SEC. 7. INSTITUTIONAL CARE PRESERVATION.

Nothing in this Act shall be construed to--
    (1) require any individual to receive services in a
        community setting against their will;
    (2) reduce funding for institutional services for
        individuals who choose or require institutional
        care; or
    (3) close any institution, provided that individuals
        residing therein are offered and informed of
        community alternatives.

SEC. 8. MONEY FOLLOWS THE PERSON PERMANENCE.

(a) The Money Follows the Person Rebalancing Demonstration
under section 6071 of the Deficit Reduction Act of 2005
is made a permanent program.

(b) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.--There is authorized
to be appropriated $4,000,000,000 for each fiscal year
for the Money Follows the Person program.

SEC. 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on October 1 of the first fiscal
year beginning after the date of enactment, with the
5-year phase-in period commencing on that date.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Addresses the foundational cause of independent living failure by converting HCBS from optional to mandatory, paralleling the existing mandatory nursing facility benefit. Defines covered services broadly and requires consumer direction.
- **Section 4**: Enhanced FMAP addresses state fiscal concerns by providing 90% federal matching initially, declining over 5 years. Permanent 6% FMAP increase for all HCBS maintains ongoing incentive.
- **Section 5**: Quality and access standards with 80% pass-through requirement ensure that expanded funding reaches direct care workers.
- **Section 6**: Consolidates the complex web of HCBS authorities into a unified framework, reducing administrative burden on states.
- **Section 7**: Explicitly preserves institutional care for those who choose it, addressing opposition concerns.
- **Section 8**: Makes Money Follows the Person permanent to support ongoing transition from institutions.

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| CBO cost score ($25-30 billion/year net) | Phase-in with declining enhanced FMAP; offset institutional savings; budget reconciliation pathway |
| State opposition to mandate | Enhanced FMAP at 90% initially; permanent 6% HCBS increase; 5-year phase-in |
| Nursing home industry opposition | Preserve institutional funding for those who choose it; frame as choice expansion |
| NFIB v. Sebelius coercion concerns | Enhanced FMAP (not penalty) structure; relate to existing Medicaid purpose; offer transition time |
| Workforce availability during expansion | Pair with DSP Workforce Act (below); phased enrollment expansion |

**Refinements**:

- **Fallback position**: Enhanced FMAP for HCBS (without mandatory benefit) with waitlist reduction targets and accountability
- **Stronger version**: Include HCBS as entitlement with individual right of action for failure to serve within 90 days
- **Phase-in option**: Mandatory for IDD population first (Year 1), aged/disabled (Year 3), all populations (Year 5)

### Direct Support Professional Workforce Act

**Purpose**: Establish a federal wage floor, career pathways, and labor protections for direct support professionals to stabilize the workforce that makes independent living possible.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Direct Support Professional
Workforce Investment Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.--Congress finds that--
    (1) 4.6 million direct support professionals provide
        the personal care, habilitation, and support
        services that enable people with disabilities to
        live independently in their communities;
    (2) direct support professionals earn a median wage of
        $15.50 per hour, placing many below or near the
        federal poverty line;
    (3) annual turnover among DSPs is 43.5 percent, and
        the vacancy rate is 13.8 percent, creating chronic
        service gaps;
    (4) the DSP workforce is 86 percent female and 62
        percent people of color, reflecting historical
        devaluation of care work;
    (5) DSP wage levels are determined primarily by
        Medicaid reimbursement rates set by states, which
        have been persistently inadequate; and
    (6) the workforce crisis is the single greatest barrier
        to independent living for people with disabilities.

(b) PURPOSE.--The purpose of this Act is to stabilize and
professionalize the direct support workforce through
adequate compensation, career pathways, and labor
protections.

SEC. 3. FEDERAL DSP WAGE FLOOR.

(a) MINIMUM WAGE.--No Medicaid-funded direct support
professional shall be compensated at a rate less than the
greater of--
    (1) 150 percent of the applicable local living wage
        as determined by the Secretary using Bureau of
        Labor Statistics data; or
    (2) $20 per hour, adjusted annually for inflation
        using the Consumer Price Index.

(b) IMPLEMENTATION.--
    (1) The wage floor under this section shall be fully
        implemented not later than 3 years after enactment.
    (2) During the phase-in period, the minimum DSP wage
        shall be not less than--
        (A) $18 per hour in the first year;
        (B) $19 per hour in the second year; and
        (C) the full applicable rate in the third year.

(c) BENEFITS.--Employers of Medicaid-funded DSPs working
20 or more hours per week shall provide--
    (1) health insurance coverage, or contribution toward
        marketplace coverage, meeting minimum value
        standards;
    (2) not less than 5 days of paid sick leave per year;
    (3) not less than 5 days of paid personal leave per
        year; and
    (4) access to a retirement savings plan with employer
        contribution.

SEC. 4. ENHANCED FMAP FOR WAGE COMPLIANCE.

(a) The Federal medical assistance percentage for the
incremental cost of compliance with the DSP wage floor
under Section 3 shall be 90 percent for the first 5
fiscal years and 80 percent thereafter.

(b) States shall demonstrate that enhanced FMAP funds
under this section are used exclusively for direct care
worker compensation and benefits.

SEC. 5. NATIONAL DSP CREDENTIAL AND CAREER PATHWAY.

(a) NATIONAL CREDENTIAL.--The Secretary, in consultation
with the National Alliance for Direct Support Professionals
and disability consumer organizations, shall establish a
voluntary national DSP credential with--
    (1) standardized core competencies including
        person-centered support, health and safety,
        communication, community integration, and
        self-determination;
    (2) three certification levels--
        (A) DSP I (entry level): 75 hours of training;
        (B) DSP II (experienced): DSP I plus 1 year
            experience and 40 additional hours; and
        (C) DSP III (supervisor/specialist): DSP II plus
            2 years experience and 60 additional hours;
    (3) portability across states; and
    (4) wage differentials tied to credential level,
        incorporated into Medicaid rate-setting.

(b) DOL APPRENTICESHIP.--The Secretary of Labor shall
establish a registered apprenticeship program for direct
support professionals, with--
    (1) paid on-the-job training;
    (2) classroom instruction aligned with the national
        credential; and
    (3) completion resulting in DSP II credential.

SEC. 6. DSP LABOR PROTECTIONS.

(a) EMPLOYEE STATUS.--Direct support professionals
providing Medicaid-funded services shall be classified as
employees, not independent contractors, for purposes of
the Fair Labor Standards Act, the National Labor Relations
Act, and applicable state labor laws.

(b) COLLECTIVE BARGAINING.--States shall not prohibit
collective bargaining by direct support professionals,
including those employed by consumers in self-directed
programs, through public authority or joint employer
models that preserve consumer direction over day-to-day
service delivery.

(c) WORKPLACE SAFETY.--OSHA protections shall apply to
direct support work in home-based settings, with guidance
specific to home care environments.

(d) PAID TRAINING.--All training time required by employers
or credentialing programs shall be compensated at the
worker's regular rate of pay.

SEC. 7. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There is authorized to be appropriated such sums as
necessary to carry out this Act.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment, with
the wage floor phase-in commencing on October 1 of the
first fiscal year following enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Establishes a meaningful wage floor at 150% of local living wage (approximately $20-25/hour), with benefits, addressing the root cause of the workforce crisis
- **Section 4**: Enhanced FMAP ensures states can afford wage increases without cutting services
- **Section 5**: National credential provides career pathway and professional recognition absent in current system
- **Section 6**: Labor protections address the fragmented employment structure that prevents collective bargaining

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Estimated $15-20 billion/year cost | Enhanced FMAP covers 90% initially; workforce stability reduces institutional placement costs |
| State opposition to wage mandate | Enhanced FMAP ensures federal funding covers incremental cost |
| Self-direction tensions | Public authority model preserves consumer direction while enabling collective bargaining |
| Employer compliance burden | Phase-in period; CMS technical assistance; simplified reporting |

### Olmstead Enforcement Act

**Purpose**: Strengthen federal enforcement of the *Olmstead* community integration mandate through dedicated DOJ resources, CMS conditions, and private right of action.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Olmstead Community
Integration Enforcement Act".

SEC. 2. DOJ OLMSTEAD ENFORCEMENT INITIATIVE.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--There is established within the Civil
Rights Division of the Department of Justice an Olmstead
Enforcement Unit with dedicated staff and resources for
enforcement of the community integration mandate under
Title II of the Americans with Disabilities Act.

(b) FUNCTIONS.--The Olmstead Enforcement Unit shall--
    (1) investigate State compliance with Olmstead v. L.C.
        and the community integration mandate;
    (2) bring enforcement actions against noncompliant
        States;
    (3) negotiate consent decrees and monitor compliance;
    (4) issue public guidance on Olmstead compliance
        standards; and
    (5) publish annual reports on the state of Olmstead
        compliance nationwide.

(c) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.--There is authorized
to be appropriated $30,000,000 for each fiscal year for
the Olmstead Enforcement Unit.

SEC. 3. CMS OLMSTEAD COMPLIANCE CONDITION.

(a) PLAN REQUIREMENT.--As a condition of participation
in the Medicaid program, each State shall submit to the
Secretary a comprehensive Olmstead compliance plan that--
    (1) identifies all individuals in institutional
        settings who could be appropriately served in
        community settings;
    (2) establishes measurable annual benchmarks for
        community transition;
    (3) includes an HCBS expansion plan with enrollment
        targets and waitlist reduction timelines;
    (4) addresses workforce adequacy, housing availability,
        and service capacity necessary for community
        placement; and
    (5) provides for annual reassessment of all
        institutional residents for community transition
        eligibility.

(b) PLAN APPROVAL AND MONITORING.--
    (1) The Secretary shall review and approve State
        plans within 180 days of submission.
    (2) States shall report annually on progress toward
        plan benchmarks.
    (3) The Secretary may withhold not more than 5 percent
        of a State's Federal medical assistance for
        institutional LTSS if the State fails to meet
        plan benchmarks for 2 consecutive years.

SEC. 4. PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.

(a) INDIVIDUAL ENFORCEMENT.--Any individual with a
disability who is institutionalized or at risk of
institutionalization, and who meets the criteria for
community placement as described in Olmstead v. L.C.,
may bring a civil action in Federal district court for--
    (1) declaratory and injunctive relief requiring
        the State to provide HCBS within 180 days; and
    (2) reasonable attorney fees and costs.

(b) EXHAUSTION NOT REQUIRED.--A plaintiff under this
section need not exhaust administrative remedies before
filing a civil action.

SEC. 5. INSTITUTIONAL ASSESSMENT MANDATE.

(a) Each State shall assess all residents of nursing
facilities, intermediate care facilities, and other
institutional settings for community transition
eligibility not less than annually.

(b) For each resident determined eligible for community
transition, the State shall--
    (1) provide information about community living options;
    (2) develop a transition plan within 60 days; and
    (3) complete transition within 180 days, unless the
        resident declines community placement.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 180 days after the date of
enactment. States shall submit initial Olmstead compliance
plans within 1 year of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 2**: Dedicated DOJ unit ensures sustained enforcement rather than sporadic action dependent on administration priorities
- **Section 3**: CMS conditions on Medicaid participation create financial accountability for Olmstead noncompliance
- **Section 4**: Private right of action empowers individuals to enforce their community integration rights directly
- **Section 5**: Annual institutional assessment ensures ongoing identification of people eligible for community transition

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| State sovereignty concerns | Condition is tied to voluntary Medicaid participation; 5% withholding is proportionate |
| NFIB v. Sebelius coercion argument | Limited withholding (5%); applies only to institutional LTSS funds; relates to Medicaid purpose |
| Litigation burden | Private right of action includes fee-shifting to incentivize quality legal representation |
| Implementation capacity | Phase-in period; CMS technical assistance; $30 million DOJ unit |

### CIL Empowerment Act

**Purpose**: Strengthen the Centers for Independent Living network through increased federal funding and expanded roles in community transition and Olmstead implementation.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Centers for Independent
Living Empowerment Act".

SEC. 2. FUNDING INCREASE.

(a) Title VII, Part C of the Rehabilitation Act (29 U.S.C.
796f et seq.) is amended to authorize appropriations of--
    (1) $200,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
    (2) $250,000,000 for fiscal year 2028; and
    (3) $300,000,000 for each subsequent fiscal year.

(b) CIL INNOVATION FUND.--There is established a CIL
Innovation Fund, authorized at $50,000,000 per year, for
competitive grants to CILs for--
    (1) technology-based independent living solutions;
    (2) peer mentoring and transition support models;
    (3) housing navigation and development partnerships;
    (4) workforce recruitment and training programs; and
    (5) cross-disability community integration programs.

SEC. 3. EXPANDED CIL ROLES.

(a) OLMSTEAD TRANSITION COORDINATORS.--CILs receiving
funds under Title VII, Part C shall serve as designated
community transition coordinators, providing--
    (1) institutional outreach and information services;
    (2) transition planning assistance;
    (3) community integration support for the first year
        following transition; and
    (4) peer support from individuals who have
        successfully transitioned.

(b) SELF-DIRECTION SUPPORT.--CILs shall provide training
and support to individuals participating in self-directed
HCBS programs, including--
    (1) employer skills training;
    (2) budget management assistance;
    (3) worker recruitment support; and
    (4) ongoing peer consultation.

SEC. 4. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on October 1, 2027.
```

## State Model Legislation

### Model State DSP Wage Act

**Purpose**: Establish a state-level minimum wage for Medicaid-funded direct support professionals.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Direct Support
Professional Fair Compensation Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) DIRECT SUPPORT PROFESSIONAL.--Any individual who
provides personal care, habilitation, homemaker, companion,
attendant, or supported employment services funded in
whole or in part by Medicaid.

(b) EMPLOYER.--Any entity, including provider agencies,
fiscal intermediaries, and individual consumers employing
workers through self-directed programs, that employs
direct support professionals.

SECTION 3. MINIMUM COMPENSATION.

(a) WAGE FLOOR.--No direct support professional shall be
compensated at a rate less than [$20.00 / 150% of state
minimum wage / other formula] per hour.

(b) ANNUAL ADJUSTMENT.--The minimum DSP wage shall be
adjusted annually for inflation using the Consumer Price
Index for the [state/region].

(c) BENEFITS.--Employers of DSPs working 20 or more hours
per week shall provide or contribute toward health
insurance, paid sick leave, and paid personal leave.

SECTION 4. MEDICAID RATE ADEQUACY.

(a) The [State Medicaid Agency] shall adjust Medicaid
reimbursement rates for home and community-based services
sufficient to fund the minimum compensation required
under Section 3.

(b) Rate adjustments shall be effective concurrently with
the wage floor effective date.

SECTION 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect [12 months after enactment],
with the [State Medicaid Agency] required to submit
rate adjustment proposals within 6 months of enactment.
```

**Adaptations**: States should set the wage floor appropriate to local labor market conditions. States with existing collective bargaining agreements for DSPs should coordinate the wage floor with negotiated rates.

### Model State Olmstead Planning Act

**Purpose**: Require states to develop and implement comprehensive Olmstead compliance plans with measurable benchmarks.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Community
Integration Planning Act".

SECTION 2. OLMSTEAD COMPLIANCE PLAN.

(a) The [Governor / State Disability Services Agency]
shall develop and implement a comprehensive Olmstead
compliance plan within 18 months of enactment.

(b) PLAN CONTENTS.--The plan shall include--
    (1) assessment of all institutionalized individuals
        for community transition eligibility;
    (2) measurable annual targets for community transition;
    (3) HCBS waitlist elimination timeline;
    (4) workforce development plan with DSP wage and
        retention targets;
    (5) housing development plan for accessible,
        integrated community housing;
    (6) transportation access plan; and
    (7) funding plan identifying resources for
        implementation.

(c) DISABILITY COMMUNITY INPUT.--The plan shall be
developed with meaningful participation of people with
disabilities, including--
    (1) representation on the planning committee of not
        less than 51 percent individuals with disabilities;
    (2) public comment period of not less than 60 days;
        and
    (3) accessible public hearings in each region of the
        state.

SECTION 3. ANNUAL REPORTING.

The [State Agency] shall report annually to the legislature
and the public on progress toward plan benchmarks.

SECTION 4. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect upon enactment.
```

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

**Spending Clause**: The HCBS Access Act creates a new mandatory Medicaid benefit conditioned on federal funding. Under *South Dakota v. Dole*, 483 U.S. 203 (1987), Congress may attach conditions to federal spending if the conditions are related to the federal interest and not coercive. Under *NFIB v. Sebelius*, 567 U.S. 519 (2012), a new condition that threatens loss of all existing Medicaid funding may constitute coercion. The HCBS Access Act addresses this by (a) providing enhanced FMAP at 90% to cover new costs, (b) phasing in the requirement over 5 years, and (c) framing HCBS as an extension of existing Medicaid LTSS purpose rather than a fundamentally new program. The structure closely parallels the original Medicaid expansion in the ACA, and enhanced federal funding substantially exceeds the new cost to states.

**Commerce Clause**: Federal DSP wage standards rest on Commerce Clause authority over interstate commerce, as the healthcare and LTSS industries operate in interstate markets. The DSP wage floor applies to workers funded through a federal program (Medicaid), strengthening the federal nexus.

**ADA Authority**: The Olmstead Enforcement Act builds on existing ADA Title II authority, which is well-established under Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment. The private right of action clarifies existing enforcement mechanisms rather than creating new constitutional authority.

**Tenth Amendment**: The Olmstead compliance condition tied to Medicaid participation is permissible because Medicaid is a voluntary cooperative program. The 5% withholding for noncompliance is proportionate and related to the program's purpose of serving people with disabilities in the most appropriate setting.

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### HCBS Access Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Institutional level of care manipulation | States could raise institutional LOC thresholds to reduce the number of people qualifying for mandatory HCBS | High |
| Service plan adequacy gaming | States could approve person-centered plans with insufficient hours or services to meet actual needs | High |
| 90-day service initiation without adequacy | States could "initiate" services within 90 days with minimal hours, then delay full services indefinitely | Medium |
| Woodwork effect cost shift | If enrollment increases substantially, states may argue enhanced FMAP is insufficient and reduce service quality | Medium |
| Self-direction lip service | States could offer nominal self-direction options that lack meaningful budget or employer authority | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not directly address housing shortage | Housing remains a barrier even with mandatory HCBS | Housing requires separate legislation and funding streams |
| Transportation access not mandated | Community access limited without accessible transportation | Transportation is administered by separate agencies |
| 80% pass-through may reduce provider viability | Small agencies with high administrative costs may struggle | Administrative costs vary significantly by provider size |
| Does not address spousal impoverishment in all states | Some states' income rules still impoverish families of HCBS recipients | Medicaid eligibility rules vary by state |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Establish federal minimum institutional LOC criteria that states cannot exceed, preventing threshold manipulation
2. Require that service plans authorize sufficient hours to meet assessed needs, with independent appeal mechanism for inadequate plans
3. Define "service initiation" as delivery of services at the level specified in the person-centered plan, not merely the first service contact
4. Include anti-regression clause preventing states from reducing existing HCBS quality or quantity as enrollment expands
5. Define minimum self-direction standards including meaningful budget authority (participant controls at least 50% of allocated budget) and full employer authority

### DSP Workforce Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Independent contractor misclassification | Employers could reclassify DSPs as independent contractors to avoid wage floor and benefits | High |
| Hours reduction | Employers could reduce worker hours below 20/week to avoid benefits mandate | Medium |
| Enhanced FMAP capture | States could capture enhanced FMAP in general fund rather than passing through to workers | High |
| Credential as barrier | National credential requirements could be used to limit workforce entry rather than support development | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Self-directed employer tensions | Consumer-employers may face complex compliance requirements | Dual role of consumer as employer and service recipient |
| Rural wage adequacy | $20/hour floor may be adequate in rural areas but insufficient in high-cost urban areas | Single national floor with local adjustment mechanism |
| Transition period workforce gaps | Wage increases may not immediately resolve shortage; training pipeline needed | Time lag between compensation improvement and workforce growth |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Establish strong anti-misclassification provisions with rebuttable presumption of employee status for DSPs
2. Prohibit reduction of previously established work hours for purpose of avoiding benefits requirements
3. Require state-level reporting demonstrating that enhanced FMAP is reflected in direct care worker compensation
4. Ensure credential is voluntary with prohibition on use as employment prerequisite; credential facilitates career advancement, not gatekeeping
5. Create fiscal intermediary compliance support for consumer-employers to simplify administrative burden

### Olmstead Enforcement Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| "Fundamental alteration" defense overuse | States could claim that all community placement requests constitute fundamental alteration of their programs | High |
| Assessment manipulation | States could assess institutional residents as "not appropriate" for community placement to avoid transition obligations | Medium |
| Compliance plan without implementation | States could submit plans that satisfy paperwork requirements without achievable benchmarks | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| 5% withholding may be insufficient deterrent | States may absorb small FMAP reduction rather than comply | Proportionality required to survive Spending Clause challenge |
| Private litigation is slow | Individual lawsuits may take years to resolve | Court system capacity and procedural requirements |
| No mandate for community crisis services | People in mental health crisis may still default to institutional placement | Crisis services require separate funding and infrastructure |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Define "fundamental alteration" defense narrowly in regulations: states may not invoke the defense when community placement would cost less than institutional care, or when waitlists exist for HCBS
2. Require independent assessment of institutional residents by evaluators not employed by the institution
3. CMS approval of Olmstead plans must include verification that benchmarks are achievable and measurable, with automatic triggers for enhanced oversight if benchmarks are missed for 2 consecutive years
4. Add community crisis services as a required component of Olmstead plans

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Multiple federal agencies involved (CMS, DOJ, HUD, DOL, ACL) | Establish White House Office on Community Living to coordinate federal agencies |
| Congressional appropriations may fall below authorization | Budget reconciliation pathway for HCBS mandatory benefit; bipartisan defense of appropriations |
| State implementation capacity varies | CMS technical assistance; peer learning networks among states; enhanced FMAP tied to implementation benchmarks |
| Political administration changes may weaken enforcement | Statutory mandates (not executive orders) for enforcement; congressional oversight requirements |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- HCBS mandatory benefit: No sunset (permanent Medicaid amendment); GAO evaluation at 5 and 10 years
- Enhanced FMAP: Automatic step-down schedule built into statute; review at 5 years
- DSP wage floor: Annual CPI adjustment built in; GAO evaluation at 3 and 5 years
- Olmstead Enforcement Unit: Permanent authorization; annual reporting to Congress
- CIL funding: 10-year reauthorization cycle; annual performance reporting
