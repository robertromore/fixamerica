# Independent Living: Overview

## Executive Summary

Independent living -- the right and practical ability of people with disabilities to live in their own homes and communities with the supports they choose, rather than in institutions or under the control of others -- is a foundational principle of the disability rights movement and a matter of federal civil rights law. The Supreme Court's *Olmstead v. L.C.* decision (1999) established that unjustified institutionalization of people with disabilities violates the Americans with Disabilities Act. Yet more than 25 years after *Olmstead*, hundreds of thousands of Americans with disabilities remain unnecessarily institutionalized, over 820,000 people are on waitlists for home and community-based services (HCBS), and the direct support workforce that makes independent living possible is in perpetual crisis.

The independent living movement, led by people with disabilities since the 1960s, produced a national network of Centers for Independent Living (CILs), federal legislation mandating consumer-directed services, and the *Olmstead* mandate for community integration. These achievements are real but incomplete. Medicaid -- the primary funder of long-term services and supports (LTSS) -- still contains a structural bias toward institutional care: nursing facility services are a mandatory Medicaid benefit, while home and community-based services remain optional in most states. This institutional bias costs more per person, produces worse outcomes, and contradicts the expressed preferences of the overwhelming majority of people with disabilities, 90% of whom prefer to live in their own homes and communities (AARP, 2023).

Independent living failures are not merely a disability policy problem. They intersect with housing shortages, transportation gaps, workforce shortages, healthcare access, and the economic security of both disabled individuals and their families. Approximately 53 million Americans serve as unpaid family caregivers, many of whom reduce or abandon employment to provide supports that publicly funded HCBS programs fail to deliver. Comprehensive reform requires rebalancing Medicaid financing, strengthening the direct support workforce, expanding accessible housing, ensuring community integration through transportation and technology, and empowering people with disabilities to direct their own services.

### What This Topic Covers

- **Home and community-based services (HCBS)**: Medicaid waivers, state plan options, 1915(c), 1915(i), 1915(k), and Section 1115 programs

- **Consumer direction**: Self-directed services, fiscal intermediaries, participant authority over supports

- **Deinstitutionalization**: Transition from nursing facilities and institutions to community settings

## Core Tensions

### Institutional Bias vs. Community Integration

Medicaid's structural design creates a built-in preference for institutional care. Nursing facility services are a mandatory benefit that states must provide; HCBS is optional and typically capped through waiver programs. This means that a person who needs long-term supports has a federal entitlement to a nursing home bed but no corresponding right to receive services at home. Despite *Olmstead*, rebalancing has been slow because institutional providers have significant political and economic power.

### Consumer Direction vs. Quality Assurance

The independent living philosophy insists that people with disabilities should control their own services, including hiring, training, and directing support workers. Government programs, however, require quality assurance, background checks, training standards, and oversight mechanisms that can conflict with consumer autonomy. Finding the right balance between empowerment and protection is a persistent challenge, particularly for people with cognitive disabilities.

### Workforce Crisis vs. Budget Constraints

Direct support professionals provide the hands-on assistance that makes independent living possible, yet they are among the lowest-paid workers in the economy. Raising wages to competitive levels would cost tens of billions in additional Medicaid spending. States face difficult choices between expanding HCBS coverage and increasing wages for existing workers, and the federal government has not established minimum wage or benefit standards for the direct support workforce.

### Individual Choice vs. Cost Efficiency

HCBS is generally less expensive per person than institutional care, but individual costs vary widely. Some individuals with very high support needs may cost more to serve in the community than in an institution. Policymakers must decide whether the right to community living is absolute (as *Olmstead* suggests when not a fundamental alteration of a state's program) or subject to cost-effectiveness analysis.

## Vision of Success

A fully realized independent living system would guarantee every person with a disability the right to live in their own home or a setting of their choice with the supports needed to participate in community life. Medicaid HCBS would be a mandatory benefit, eliminating waitlists and institutional bias. Direct support professionals would earn a living wage with benefits, creating a stable and skilled workforce. Accessible, affordable, integrated housing would be available in every community. Transportation systems, digital services, and community infrastructure would be universally accessible. Centers for Independent Living would be fully funded to provide peer support, advocacy, and transition services. People with disabilities would direct their own services, hire their own workers, and make their own decisions about where and how they live. Institutional care would remain available for those who choose it, but no one would be placed in an institution because community supports were unavailable.
