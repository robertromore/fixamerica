# Independent Living: Solutions

## Overview

Achieving independent living for all Americans with disabilities requires structural reform across Medicaid financing, workforce development, housing policy, and service delivery. The solutions below are organized from the most impactful systemic reforms to targeted interventions. Each builds on the others: HCBS cannot expand without a workforce; the workforce cannot stabilize without adequate wages; adequate wages cannot be funded without Medicaid restructuring; and community living cannot be achieved without accessible, affordable housing.

## Medicaid Reform

### 1. Make HCBS a Mandatory Medicaid Benefit

**Problem addressed**: Nursing facility services are mandatory; HCBS is optional, creating structural institutional bias and 820,000+ waitlists.

**Proposal**: Amend Section 1905(a) of the Social Security Act to include home and community-based services as a mandatory Medicaid benefit for all individuals who meet institutional level of care criteria.

**Key elements**:
- Define HCBS to include personal care, case management, homemaker services, adult day services, habilitation, respite, and supported employment
- Require states to serve all eligible individuals within 90 days of eligibility determination (eliminating waitlists)
- Maintain institutional care as available option (no forced deinstitutionalization)
- Phase implementation over 5 years with enhanced FMAP (90% federal match for new HCBS enrollees in years 1-3, declining to standard FMAP by year 5)
- Include cost-neutrality safeguard: total LTSS spending per capita should not exceed institutional alternative cost
- Consumer direction requirement: states must offer self-directed options for all HCBS participants

**Evidence**: ARPA HCBS investment ($37 billion) demonstrated that federal investment produces measurable waitlist reductions and service improvements. Oregon, Vermont, and other leading states show that HCBS-first systems are sustainable and effective.

**Estimated cost**: $40-50 billion per year in new federal spending (gross), offset by $15-20 billion in institutional care savings, net approximately $25-30 billion per year (MACPAC modeling, 2024).

### 2. Eliminate Institutional Bias in Medicaid Financing

**Problem addressed**: Even with mandatory HCBS, financial incentives may continue to favor institutional placement.

**Proposal**: Reform Medicaid LTSS financing to create neutral or community-preferring incentives.

**Key elements**:
- Money Follows the Person: make MFP permanent with $4 billion annual funding
- Enhanced FMAP for HCBS: provide a permanent 6% enhanced FMAP for all HCBS spending (expanding the Community First Choice incentive)
- Institutional spending accountability: require states to demonstrate that institutional placements are made only when community alternatives are unavailable or the individual chooses institutional care
- Streamline HCBS authorities: consolidate 1915(c), 1915(i), 1915(k), and related authorities into a single, simplified HCBS framework

### 3. Strengthen CMS HCBS Quality and Access Standards

**Problem addressed**: HCBS quality, access, and workforce conditions vary widely across states with limited federal oversight.

**Proposal**: Finalize and strengthen the CMS proposed rule on HCBS access, quality, and workforce.

**Key elements**:
- 80% pass-through requirement: at least 80% of Medicaid HCBS payments must go to direct care worker compensation (wages, benefits, training)
- HCBS access monitoring: states must track and report wait times, service utilization, and unmet need
- Incident management: standardized reporting and response for abuse, neglect, and exploitation in HCBS settings
- Person-centered planning enforcement: ensure individual authority over service plans, worker selection, and daily schedules
- Quality measurement: standardized outcome measures (community participation, employment, health, satisfaction) reported publicly

## Workforce Solutions

### 4. Establish a Federal Direct Support Professional Wage Floor

**Problem addressed**: DSP median wage of $15.50/hour produces 43.5% annual turnover and chronic workforce shortages.

**Proposal**: Establish a federal minimum wage for Medicaid-funded direct support work, with associated Medicaid funding.

**Key elements**:
- Set minimum DSP wage at 150% of the local living wage (approximately $20-25/hour in most markets)
- Require health insurance, paid leave, and retirement benefits for DSPs working 20+ hours per week
- Fund wage increases through enhanced FMAP (90% federal match for wage-related spending increases)
- Phase implementation over 3 years
- Index wages to inflation and cost-of-living adjustments annually

**Evidence**: States that have raised DSP wages see measurable turnover reduction. Connecticut's $19/hour minimum for DSPs reduced vacancy rates by 30% within 18 months (CT DDS, 2024). ARPA-funded wage increases produced similar results but are expiring.

**Estimated cost**: $15-20 billion per year in additional Medicaid spending for wage increases nationally.

### 5. Create Direct Support Professional Career Pathways

**Problem addressed**: DSPs have minimal training requirements, no credentialing, and limited career advancement opportunities.

**Proposal**: Establish a national DSP credential and career ladder.

**Key elements**:
- National DSP credential with standardized core competencies (CMS-approved curriculum)
- Three-tier career pathway: DSP I (entry), DSP II (experienced), DSP III (supervisor/specialist)
- Wage differentials tied to credential levels
- DOL apprenticeship program for DSP training
- Employer incentives (enhanced Medicaid reimbursement) for training and credentialing programs
- Portable credentials recognized across states

### 6. Protect DSP Labor Rights

**Problem addressed**: Fragmented employment structure limits DSP collective bargaining and labor protections.

**Proposal**: Extend full labor protections to all direct support professionals, including those employed by consumers in self-directed programs.

**Key elements**:
- Clarify DSP employee status (not independent contractors)
- Ensure collective bargaining rights for all DSPs, including consumer-employed workers (through public authority or joint employer models)
- Extend OSHA workplace safety protections to home-based work settings
- Protect DSP access to workers' compensation for workplace injuries
- Require paid training time and mileage reimbursement for travel between clients

## Housing Solutions

### 7. Expand Accessible, Integrated, Affordable Housing

**Problem addressed**: Only 3.5% of housing stock has accessibility features; 52% of renters with disabilities are cost-burdened.

**Proposal**: Major expansion of accessible housing through federal investment, regulatory reform, and incentives.

**Key elements**:
- Expand Section 811 Supportive Housing from 34,000 to 200,000 units over 10 years ($2 billion annual authorization)
- Create Housing Choice Voucher preference for people transitioning from institutions
- Require visitability features (zero-step entrance, accessible bathroom, wider doorways) in all federally funded new construction
- LIHTC incentive: additional tax credits for accessible units exceeding ADA minimums
- Integrate housing and services funding: create joint CMS-HUD pilot programs for supportive housing
- Zoning reform incentives: condition certain federal housing funds on states/localities eliminating exclusionary zoning for disability housing

### 8. Reform Housing-Services Integration

**Problem addressed**: Housing and HCBS programs are administered by separate agencies with no coordination.

**Proposal**: Require coordination between HUD housing programs and CMS HCBS programs.

**Key elements**:
- Joint CMS-HUD planning requirements for states receiving both Medicaid and HUD funds
- Single-point-of-contact systems for individuals seeking both housing and HCBS
- Data sharing between housing authorities and Medicaid agencies
- "Housing first" approaches for people with disabilities transitioning from institutions or experiencing homelessness
- State LTSS agencies must include housing plans in Olmstead plans

## Service Delivery Solutions

### 9. Strengthen Centers for Independent Living

**Problem addressed**: CILs are underfunded ($109 million federal funding for 403 centers) relative to their role as the primary community infrastructure for independent living.

**Proposal**: Increase CIL funding and expand their role in Olmstead implementation and community transition.

**Key elements**:
- Increase Title VII, Part C funding from $109 million to $300 million annually
- Create a CIL Innovation Fund ($50 million annually) for technology, peer mentoring, and systems change projects
- Designate CILs as official Olmstead transition coordinators in their service areas
- Fund CIL community integration specialists for nursing home transition
- Require state LTSS plans to include CIL roles and funding

### 10. Expand and Improve Self-Direction

**Problem addressed**: Self-directed services produce better outcomes but are available in only 48 states, with significant variation in consumer authority.

**Proposal**: Require all states to offer comprehensive self-directed options with both budget and employer authority.

**Key elements**:
- Require all states to offer self-direction for all HCBS waiver programs
- Mandate both budget authority (participant controls spending) and employer authority (participant hires and manages workers)
- Standardize fiscal intermediary requirements and accountability
- Provide self-direction training and support for participants and families
- Allow participants to pay family members as workers (where states currently prohibit this)
- Include technology and home modification in self-directed budgets

### 11. Technology for Independent Living

**Problem addressed**: Assistive technology, remote supports, and smart home technology could expand independent living options but are inadequately funded and poorly integrated into HCBS.

**Proposal**: Integrate technology supports into HCBS programs as a core service category.

**Key elements**:
- Include assistive technology assessments and devices as covered HCBS benefits
- Fund remote support technology (sensors, cameras, communication systems) as HCBS alternatives to in-person supports
- Smart home technology (automated lighting, door locks, medication dispensers) as HCBS benefit
- Digital literacy training for HCBS participants
- Accessible technology requirements for all HCBS-related platforms and communication

## Enforcement Solutions

### 12. Strengthen Olmstead Enforcement

**Problem addressed**: 25+ years after *Olmstead*, 15 states have no formal plan and hundreds of thousands remain unnecessarily institutionalized.

**Proposal**: Strengthen federal enforcement through DOJ action, CMS conditions, and private right of action.

**Key elements**:
- DOJ Olmstead Enforcement Initiative: dedicated unit with $30 million annual budget
- CMS Olmstead compliance condition: require states to have CMS-approved Olmstead plans as condition of Medicaid participation
- Private right of action: clarify that individuals can bring Olmstead claims without exhausting administrative remedies
- Institutional assessment mandate: require states to assess all institutional residents for community transition eligibility annually
- Transition timeline requirements: states must transition eligible individuals within 180 days of determination
