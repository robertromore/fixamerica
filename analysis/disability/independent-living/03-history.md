# Independent Living: History

## Overview

The independent living movement is among the most transformative social movements in American history, yet its history remains largely unknown to the general public. Beginning in the 1960s, people with disabilities challenged the assumption that disability inevitably meant dependency, institutional placement, and exclusion from community life. Through direct action, legislative advocacy, and the creation of alternative service models, the movement established the principle that people with disabilities have the right to live independently, direct their own services, and participate fully in their communities. This history traces the evolution from institutional warehousing to the current -- still incomplete -- system of community-based supports.

## Timeline

### Pre-Movement Era (Before 1960)

**Colonial era to 1900** - People with disabilities in colonial America are either cared for by families or placed in almshouses and poorhouses alongside other marginalized populations. The first purpose-built disability institutions emerge in the early 1800s.

**1848** - First state-funded institution for people with intellectual disabilities opens in Massachusetts (the Walter E. Fernald State School, later known as a site of abuse and neglect).

**1850-1920** - The institutional era expands dramatically. By 1920, every state operates institutions for people with intellectual disabilities, mental illness, or both. The eugenics movement provides ideological justification for institutional segregation. State institutions house over 200,000 people with intellectual disabilities and hundreds of thousands more with mental illness.

**1935** - Social Security Act enacted. Creates Old-Age Insurance and Aid to the Blind but excludes most people with disabilities from the social safety net. Institutionalized individuals are explicitly excluded from receiving Social Security benefits.

**1956** - Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI) created for workers aged 50-65 who become disabled. Expanded to all ages in 1960.

**1962** - President Kennedy's Panel on Mental Retardation recommends community-based alternatives to institutions, influenced by the Kennedy family's own experience with intellectual disability. Community Mental Health Centers Act (1963) follows, beginning the long process of deinstitutionalization for mental illness.

### Movement Origins (1960s-1970s)

**1962** - Ed Roberts, a polio survivor who uses a wheelchair and iron lung, enrolls at the University of California, Berkeley, after fighting the university's refusal to admit him. He becomes the founding figure of the independent living movement.

**1965** - Medicaid enacted (Title XIX, Social Security Act). Creates an open-ended federal entitlement for nursing facility services, establishing the institutional bias that persists today. Nursing home care is a mandatory Medicaid benefit; home and community-based services are not covered.

**1967** - Rolling Quads formed at UC Berkeley. Roberts and other wheelchair-using students create a peer support network and begin advocating for accessible campus environments and community services.

**1970** - Developmental Disabilities Services and Facilities Construction Act (P.L. 91-517) creates state protection and advocacy systems and developmental disabilities councils, establishing the first federal disability rights infrastructure.

**1972** - Center for Independent Living (CIL) founded in Berkeley, California. The first CIL is controlled and directed by people with disabilities, providing peer counseling, housing referral, attendant referral, and advocacy. This model of consumer-directed, community-based services becomes the template for independent living centers nationwide.

**1973** - Rehabilitation Act (P.L. 93-112) enacted. Section 504 prohibits disability discrimination in federally funded programs, establishing the first federal disability civil rights mandate. Implementation regulations are delayed for four years due to HEW resistance.

**1975** - Developmentally Disabled Assistance and Bill of Rights Act establishes federal protection for people with developmental disabilities and articulates the right to appropriate treatment and services in the least restrictive environment.

**1977** - Section 504 sit-in. After four years of federal delay in issuing Section 504 regulations, disability activists occupy the San Francisco HEW regional office for 25 days -- the longest occupation of a federal building in U.S. history. Secretary Joseph Califano signs the regulations on April 28, 1977. The sit-in is a pivotal moment in disability rights history, demonstrating the movement's capacity for sustained direct action.

**1978** - Rehabilitation Act amendments (P.L. 95-602) create Title VII, establishing federal funding for independent living services and Centers for Independent Living. Congress mandates that CILs be consumer-controlled, cross-disability, and community-based.

### Federal Framework Development (1980s)

**1981** - First Medicaid HCBS waiver program (Section 1915(c)) authorized by the Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act (P.L. 97-35). States can now request federal waivers to provide home and community-based services to people who would otherwise require institutional care. This is a breakthrough, but HCBS remains optional and capped, while institutional care remains mandatory and uncapped.

**1983** - Ed Roberts becomes Director of the California Department of Rehabilitation, the same agency that once denied him services. His appointment symbolizes the movement's transition from protest to governance.

**1984** - Katie Beckett waiver established. After President Reagan learns about Katie Beckett, a child with complex medical needs institutionalized solely because Medicaid would not fund home care, CMS creates waivers allowing states to provide HCBS to children who would otherwise be institutionalized.

**1986** - Independent living movement grows to over 200 CILs nationwide. Congress increases Title VII funding and strengthens consumer control requirements.

**1988** - Technology-Related Assistance for Individuals with Disabilities Act (Tech Act, P.L. 100-407) funds state assistive technology programs, expanding the tools available for independent living.

### ADA and Olmstead Era (1990s)

**1990** - Americans with Disabilities Act (P.L. 101-336) enacted. While the ADA does not specifically address HCBS or independent living services, its prohibition on disability discrimination and mandate for community integration provide the legal foundation for what becomes the *Olmstead* decision.

**1990** - Medicaid Community Supported Living Arrangements program created, funding models for supporting people with significant disabilities in their own homes. Program is discontinued in 1995 due to limited state participation.

**1995** - ADAPT (American Disabled for Attendant Programs Today) shifts its focus from transportation to HCBS, launching the "Free Our People" campaign demanding Medicaid rebalancing. ADAPT activists occupy government offices, block intersections, and chain themselves to buildings to demand community services.

**1997** - *Olmstead v. L.C.* reaches the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit. Lois Curtis and Elaine Wilson, two women with mental illness and intellectual disabilities in Georgia, challenge their continued institutional placement after treatment professionals determined they were ready for community living.

**1998** - Ticket to Work and Work Incentives Improvement Act (P.L. 106-170) allows states to provide Medicaid coverage to workers with disabilities, removing a major barrier to employment and community participation.

**1999** - *Olmstead v. L.C.*, 527 U.S. 581. The Supreme Court holds that unjustified institutional isolation of people with disabilities is a form of discrimination under the ADA. The decision establishes that states must provide community-based services to people with disabilities when: (1) treatment professionals have determined community placement is appropriate, (2) the individual does not oppose community living, and (3) community placement can be reasonably accommodated. The decision does not require immediate deinstitutionalization but mandates that states have "effectively working" plans to move qualified individuals to community settings.

### Post-Olmstead Implementation (2000s)

**2001** - President George W. Bush issues Executive Order 13217, directing federal agencies to assist states in implementing the *Olmstead* decision. A "New Freedom Initiative" is launched but inadequately funded.

**2003** - National Council on Disability publishes "Olmstead: Reclaiming Institutionalized Lives," documenting the slow pace of Olmstead implementation and persistent institutional bias. Money Follows the Person (MFP) demonstration begins with limited state participation.

**2005** - Deficit Reduction Act (P.L. 109-171) creates new HCBS state plan options, including Section 1915(i), making it easier for states to offer HCBS without waivers. Real Choice Systems Change grants provide funding for states to rebalance their LTSS systems.

**2006** - Money Follows the Person (MFP) reauthorized and expanded (P.L. 109-171). MFP provides enhanced federal funding to help states transition individuals from institutions to community settings. By 2023, over 107,000 people have transitioned through MFP.

**2007** - Community First Choice Option (CFC, Section 1915(k)) enacted as part of the Affordable Care Act discussions, though not finalized until 2010. Provides a 6% enhanced FMAP for states that offer attendant services as a state plan benefit.

### ACA and Modern Era (2010-Present)

**2010** - Affordable Care Act (P.L. 111-148) includes significant HCBS provisions:
- Community First Choice Option (Section 1915(k)) with 6% FMAP increase
- State Balancing Incentive Program offering 2-5% FMAP increase for rebalancing
- Extension and improvement of Money Follows the Person
- Prevention of unnecessary institutionalization provisions
- Community Living Assistance Services and Supports (CLASS) Act (later repealed as fiscally unsound)

**2011** - CMS issues final rule on Person-Centered Planning requirements for HCBS waiver programs, requiring that services be directed by the individual receiving them.

**2014** - CMS HCBS Settings Rule published. Requires that HCBS be delivered in settings that are integrated in and support full access to the broader community. Settings must ensure individual rights, autonomy, and dignity. Compliance deadline initially set for 2019, later extended to March 2023, then to March 2025.

**2018** - RAISE Family Caregivers Act (P.L. 115-119) and Supporting Grandparents Raising Grandchildren Act (P.L. 115-196) address family caregiver support, acknowledging the intersection of paid and unpaid supports for independent living.

**2020** - COVID-19 pandemic devastates nursing homes. Over 200,000 nursing home residents and staff die from COVID-19 by 2023. The crisis exposes institutional care's vulnerability and accelerates demand for HCBS.

**2021** - American Rescue Plan Act (P.L. 117-2) provides $37 billion in enhanced HCBS funding through a temporary 10 percentage point FMAP increase. States invest in workforce wages, service expansion, and technology. President Biden issues Executive Order on "Increasing Access to High-Quality Care and Supporting Caregivers."

**2023** - CMS proposes rule strengthening Medicaid HCBS access, quality, and workforce standards. Proposed provisions include HCBS access monitoring, incident reporting, and an 80% direct care spending requirement for Medicaid HCBS payments. Final rule pending.

**2024** - CMS finalizes nursing home minimum staffing rule, requiring registered nurses 24/7 and minimum staffing ratios. ARPA HCBS enhanced funding begins to expire in many states, creating sustainability concerns.

**2025** - HCBS Settings Rule compliance deadline arrives (March 2025). Debate continues over making HCBS a mandatory Medicaid benefit through the HCBS Access Act and related legislative proposals.

## Key Patterns

### Incremental Gains Through Optional Programs

Every major federal HCBS expansion since 1981 has been structured as optional for states: Section 1915(c) waivers, 1915(i) state plan options, 1915(k) Community First Choice, and Section 1115 demonstrations. While this approach has allowed innovation and gradual rebalancing, it has also produced enormous state-by-state variation and sustained institutional bias because institutional care remains mandatory while community services remain optional.

### Crisis as Catalyst

Major advances in independent living policy have consistently followed crises that exposed institutional failures: the Willowbrook State School exposé (1972), the Section 504 sit-in (1977), the *Olmstead* decision responding to unjustified institutionalization (1999), and the COVID-19 nursing home catastrophe (2020). Sustained reform in the absence of crisis has been slow.

### Disability Community as Driver

Every significant independent living policy advance has been driven by the disability rights movement. Government action has consistently followed disability community advocacy, litigation, and direct action. The movement's core principle -- "Nothing About Us Without Us" -- has shaped not only policy goals but the structure of service delivery through consumer-directed models.

### Workforce Neglect

Throughout the history of independent living, the direct support workforce has been treated as an afterthought. Low wages, minimal training, and lack of career pathways have been persistent features since the first HCBS waivers in 1981, despite universal recognition that the workforce is the foundation of independent living.
