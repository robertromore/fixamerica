# Independent Living: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to independent living policy reforms comes from three primary sources: the institutional care industry seeking to protect revenue, fiscal conservatives concerned about Medicaid spending expansion, and paternalistic interests that question the capacity of people with significant disabilities to live independently. Unlike many policy areas, opposition to independent living is rarely expressed as opposition to the concept itself -- almost no political actor openly argues that people with disabilities should be institutionalized against their will. Instead, opposition manifests as resistance to specific reforms, particularly making HCBS a mandatory Medicaid benefit, raising direct support wages, and enforcing *Olmstead* compliance.

## Primary Opposition Groups

### Nursing Home Industry

#### American Health Care Association / National Center for Assisted Living (AHCA/NCAL)

**Position**: Opposes making HCBS a mandatory Medicaid benefit. Frames nursing homes as essential components of the LTSS continuum. Supports increased Medicaid reimbursement for institutional care. Opposes mandatory staffing ratios.

**Arguments**:
- HCBS expansion should not come at the expense of institutional funding
- Many residents have needs too complex for community-based care
- Nursing homes provide 24/7 skilled nursing that HCBS cannot replicate
- Mandatory HCBS benefit would create unsustainable Medicaid costs
- Quality improvement should come through voluntary programs, not mandates

**Funding/Influence**: $6.1 million federal lobbying (2023); $4.2 million political contributions (2023); active in all 50 state legislatures; major employer in rural communities (OpenSecrets, 2024)

**Counter-evidence**: Medicaid LTSS spending data shows that HCBS costs less per person than institutional care for most populations. Over 107,000 people have successfully transitioned from institutions to communities through the Money Follows the Person program (CMS, 2024). Nursing home quality data from CMS shows persistent deficiencies: 82% of nursing homes had at least one deficiency in their most recent inspection (CMS Nursing Home Compare, 2024). *Olmstead* established that unjustified institutionalization is discrimination.

#### Private Equity-Owned Nursing Homes

**Position**: Resist any regulation that reduces profitability, including staffing mandates, transparency requirements, and Medicaid rebalancing.

**Arguments**:
- Private investment is necessary to maintain aging infrastructure
- Excessive regulation drives operators out of the market
- Quality should be measured by outcomes, not inputs (staffing levels, spending ratios)

**Counter-evidence**: Research consistently shows that private equity ownership is associated with worse care quality, higher mortality, lower staffing, and higher Medicare billing (Gupta et al., *Management Science*, 2023). Private equity firms extract value through real estate transactions, management fee arrangements, and cost reduction rather than quality improvement.

### Fiscal Conservative Organizations

#### National Governors Association (Budget-Focused Members)

**Position**: Opposes unfunded federal mandates that would require states to expand HCBS without full federal funding. Supports state flexibility to design LTSS programs.

**Arguments**:
- Making HCBS mandatory would impose billions in new costs on state budgets
- States need flexibility to design programs that fit their populations and budgets
- Woodwork effect: mandatory HCBS will draw people who currently manage without services, increasing total costs
- Federal government should fully fund any new mandate

**Counter-evidence**: ARPA HCBS investment demonstrated that states can expand HCBS when federal funding is available. HCBS is less expensive per person than institutional care. Woodwork effect concerns have been studied and found to be modest: most research estimates that HCBS expansion increases total enrollment by 5-15% while reducing per-person costs sufficiently to produce modest net savings or cost neutrality (Mathematica Policy Research, 2020).

#### Heritage Foundation

**Position**: Opposes Medicaid expansion generally. Favors block grants or per capita caps that would limit federal LTSS spending.

**Arguments**:
- Medicaid is already too expensive and growing unsustainably
- Federal mandates limit state innovation
- Block grants would give states flexibility to allocate LTSS funding efficiently
- Private insurance and family responsibility should be primary for LTSS

**Counter-evidence**: Block grants or per capita caps would almost certainly reduce HCBS spending, which is the marginal Medicaid expenditure that states would cut first. States have consistently demonstrated that, without federal mandates, they underinvest in HCBS relative to institutional care. Private long-term care insurance covers fewer than 7% of Americans over 65 (LIMRA, 2023).

### Paternalistic Interests

#### Some Family Organizations and Guardians

**Position**: A subset of family members and guardians -- particularly parents of people with significant intellectual disabilities -- express concern about full community placement without adequate safeguards. They do not oppose independent living in principle but worry about safety, exploitation, and service quality in community settings.

**Arguments**:
- Some individuals with severe disabilities need more structured environments than independent living provides
- Community settings lack the 24/7 oversight available in institutions or group homes
- Consumer direction is inappropriate for people who cannot make informed decisions about their care
- Abuse and neglect occur in community settings as well as institutions

**Counter-evidence**: Research shows that people with even the most significant disabilities can live in community settings with appropriate supports. Abuse rates in institutional settings are higher than in community settings (NCI data, 2023). Person-centered planning and supported decision-making (rather than guardianship) can ensure that individuals' preferences are respected even when they need assistance making decisions. The right to community living is not contingent on cognitive capacity; *Olmstead* applies to all people with disabilities.

#### Some Professional Associations

**Position**: Some healthcare and social work professional associations emphasize clinical assessment and professional judgment over consumer direction, potentially limiting consumer authority.

**Arguments**:
- Professional assessment should determine appropriate service levels and settings
- Consumer direction without professional oversight creates safety risks
- Quality standards require professional qualifications that consumer-directed workers may lack

**Counter-evidence**: National Core Indicators data consistently shows that self-directed services produce higher satisfaction (92% vs. 76%), greater community participation, and comparable or better safety outcomes compared to agency-directed services (NCI, 2023). Professional oversight can coexist with consumer direction.

## Opposition Arguments and Rebuttals

### "HCBS Is Not Appropriate for Everyone"

**Argument**: Some individuals have medical or behavioral needs too complex for community-based services. Forcing deinstitutionalization endangers vulnerable people.

**Rebuttal**: No serious HCBS advocate proposes forcing anyone into the community against their will. *Olmstead* explicitly requires that community placement be appropriate as determined by treatment professionals and desired by the individual. The issue is not whether some people need institutional-level care but whether hundreds of thousands are institutionalized or waitlisted when community services could meet their needs. The "not appropriate for everyone" argument is used to resist system-wide rebalancing, not to protect the small number of individuals who genuinely require institutional settings.

### "We Cannot Afford HCBS as a Mandatory Benefit"

**Argument**: Making HCBS mandatory would cost tens of billions in new Medicaid spending, potentially $40-80 billion per year when waitlists are eliminated and new recipients enroll.

**Rebuttal**: This argument ignores offsetting savings from reduced institutional care, reduced emergency room utilization, reduced caregiver productivity losses, and improved health outcomes. CBO and MACPAC analyses of various HCBS expansion proposals have found net costs significantly lower than gross costs. ARPA demonstrated that federal investment in HCBS produces measurable improvements in service delivery and workforce stability. The question is not whether the nation can afford HCBS; it is whether it can afford the current system's higher per-person institutional costs, worse outcomes, and massive unmet need.

### "The Workforce Crisis Makes HCBS Expansion Unrealistic"

**Argument**: Even if HCBS were mandatory, there are not enough workers to provide community services. Expanding HCBS without solving the workforce crisis will create paper entitlements without actual services.

**Rebuttal**: This argument inverts cause and effect. The workforce crisis exists because wages are too low, benefits are inadequate, and career pathways are absent. These conditions are a direct consequence of inadequate Medicaid reimbursement for HCBS. Making HCBS mandatory with adequate federal funding would enable the wage increases necessary to recruit and retain workers. States that have invested in DSP wages (California, Connecticut, Massachusetts) have seen workforce improvements.

### "Consumer Direction Creates Safety Risks"

**Argument**: Allowing people with disabilities to hire, fire, and direct their own workers without agency oversight creates risks of abuse, exploitation, and poor care quality.

**Rebuttal**: Data consistently shows that consumer-directed services produce outcomes equal to or better than agency-directed services across quality, safety, and satisfaction measures (NCI, 2023; Mathematica, 2020). Consumer direction does not mean absence of safeguards; fiscal intermediaries, background checks, and training requirements exist within self-directed programs. The argument conflates consumer authority with the absence of any oversight.

### "States Should Decide Their Own LTSS Policies"

**Argument**: LTSS policy should be left to states, which understand their populations and can design programs accordingly. Federal mandates are one-size-fits-all approaches inappropriate for diverse state contexts.

**Rebuttal**: *Olmstead* established that community integration is a federal civil rights obligation, not a matter of state discretion. The current state-by-state approach has produced a system where a person with the same disability and the same needs may receive full HCBS in Oregon but wait eight years for services in Texas. Civil rights should not vary by geography. Federal minimum standards can still allow state flexibility in program design.

## Internal Divisions

Opposition to independent living reform is not monolithic:

- **Within the nursing home industry**: Nonprofit nursing homes (represented by LeadingAge) are more supportive of rebalancing than for-profit chains, as many nonprofits also operate HCBS programs
- **Among fiscal conservatives**: Some recognize that HCBS is more cost-effective than institutional care and support rebalancing as a budget measure
- **Within family organizations**: Many families strongly support independent living and consumer direction; paternalistic opposition represents a vocal minority rather than the majority of family caregivers
- **Among state officials**: Progressive and moderate governors have led HCBS expansion even without federal mandates; opposition is concentrated in states with strong institutional provider influence and conservative fiscal ideology
