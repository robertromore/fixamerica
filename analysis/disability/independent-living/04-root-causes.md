# Independent Living: Root Causes

## Overview

The failure to fully realize independent living for people with disabilities in the United States is not a matter of insufficient knowledge or unproven models. Community-based services are evidence-based, cost-effective, and preferred by the overwhelming majority of people with disabilities and their families. The persistence of institutional bias, HCBS waitlists, workforce crises, and housing shortages results from identifiable structural, economic, political, and cultural causes that reinforce one another in a self-perpetuating system.

## Medicaid's Institutional Bias

### Mandatory vs. Optional Benefits

The single most important structural cause of independent living failures is Medicaid's benefit design. When Medicaid was created in 1965, nursing facility services were included as a mandatory benefit that every state must provide to eligible individuals. Home and community-based services were not included at all; they were first authorized as optional waiver programs in 1981 and remain optional today. This creates a fundamental asymmetry:

| Service Type | Medicaid Status | Federal Entitlement | State Obligation |
|-------------|----------------|--------------------|-----------------|
| Nursing facility | Mandatory benefit | Yes | Must provide |
| HCBS (1915(c) waivers) | Optional | No | May provide; may cap enrollment |
| HCBS (1915(i) state plan) | Optional | No | May provide; may limit eligibility |
| Community First Choice (1915(k)) | Optional | No | 6% FMAP incentive, but optional |

**Consequence**: A person eligible for Medicaid LTSS has a legal right to a nursing home bed but no corresponding right to receive services at home. States can -- and do -- maintain waitlists of hundreds of thousands for HCBS while nursing home beds remain available.

### Perverse Financial Incentives

Medicaid's matching formula creates additional institutional bias:

- The federal government pays 50-77% of Medicaid costs (varying by state income). This high federal match means states bear only a fraction of nursing home costs, reducing the fiscal incentive to develop cheaper HCBS alternatives.
- HCBS waiver programs have enrollment caps set by CMS, limiting how many people can receive community services. Nursing home benefits have no enrollment cap.
- States that invest in HCBS infrastructure (workforce, housing, technology) bear the upfront costs, while the savings from reduced institutionalization accrue partly to the federal government. This split incentive discourages state investment.
- Woodwork effect concerns: States fear that offering robust HCBS will attract people who are currently managing without services (family care, doing without), increasing total Medicaid costs even if per-person costs decrease.

### Federal Administrative Complexity

The HCBS system requires states to navigate multiple Medicaid authorities, each with different rules, reporting requirements, and approval processes:

- 1915(c) waivers require CMS approval, 5-year renewal cycles, and detailed cost-neutrality demonstrations
- 1915(i) state plan amendments have different eligibility and service rules
- 1915(k) Community First Choice has specific program requirements
- Section 1115 demonstrations require lengthy waiver applications
- Money Follows the Person has its own application, funding streams, and reporting

This complexity consumes state administrative capacity and creates barriers to expanding community services.

## Workforce Economics

### Poverty-Level Wages

Direct support professionals (DSPs) earn a median of $15.50/hour (BLS, May 2023), placing many below or near the poverty line. The root causes of low wages include:

- **Medicaid reimbursement rates set worker pay**: The vast majority of DSP wages are funded through Medicaid. State Medicaid programs set reimbursement rates for HCBS providers, and these rates determine what providers can pay workers. States facing budget pressures keep rates low.
- **Gendered and racialized labor devaluation**: The direct support workforce is 86% female and 62% people of color (PHI, 2024). Care work has historically been devalued as "women's work" and associated with domestic service, resulting in wages that do not reflect the skill, responsibility, or physical demands of the work.
- **No federal minimum standard**: Unlike healthcare workers in hospitals, there is no federal minimum wage, training, or benefit standard specific to direct support professionals. States set their own standards, which vary widely.
- **Fragmented employer structure**: DSPs are employed by thousands of small provider agencies, individual families, and fiscal intermediaries. This fragmentation prevents collective bargaining in most states and reduces employer capacity for training and benefits.

### Cascading Workforce Effects

Low wages produce a cascade of workforce problems:

1. **High turnover**: 43.5% annual turnover rate (NCI, 2023) means that nearly half the workforce leaves every year
2. **Chronic vacancies**: 13.8% vacancy rate means positions go unfilled for extended periods
3. **Service gaps**: People with disabilities go without scheduled services due to staffing shortages
4. **Quality erosion**: Constant turnover prevents relationship-building between workers and consumers
5. **Family burden**: When paid services fail, families absorb the caregiving burden, reducing their own employment and wellbeing
6. **Institutional placement**: When community supports collapse due to staffing shortages, people with disabilities are placed in institutions

## Political Economy of Institutional Care

### Institutional Provider Power

Nursing homes and other institutional providers wield significant political power that resists HCBS rebalancing:

- **Economic concentration**: The nursing home industry generates approximately $170 billion annually in revenue (CMS, 2023). Large chains control 54% of nursing homes, giving them concentrated lobbying power.
- **Political contributions**: The American Health Care Association (AHCA), the primary nursing home industry lobby, spent $6.1 million on federal lobbying in 2023 and contributed $4.2 million to political campaigns (OpenSecrets, 2024).
- **State-level influence**: Nursing homes are often major employers in rural areas, giving them economic and political leverage with state legislators.
- **Medicaid dependency**: Nursing homes receive approximately 60% of their revenue from Medicaid, making them highly engaged in Medicaid policy and resistant to any rebalancing that would reduce institutional funding.

### HCBS Provider Fragmentation

In contrast to concentrated institutional providers, HCBS is delivered by thousands of small agencies, individual workers, and family caregivers who lack political organization:

- No single industry association represents all HCBS providers
- Small home care agencies have minimal lobbying capacity
- Self-directed service consumers and their workers are politically dispersed
- CILs are underfunded and focused on direct service rather than political advocacy at the state level

This asymmetry means that institutional interests are consistently overrepresented in Medicaid policy debates.

## Housing Market Failures

### Accessible Housing Scarcity

Only 3.5% of the U.S. housing stock has basic accessibility features (American Housing Survey, 2023). The root causes include:

- **No federal visitability mandate**: Unlike the ADA's public accommodation requirements, private residential construction has no federal accessibility mandate (except for multifamily housing with federal funding under the Fair Housing Act)
- **Zoning barriers**: Local zoning laws in many communities restrict group homes, supportive housing, and multi-family housing that serves people with disabilities
- **Section 811 underfunding**: The primary federal program for supportive housing for people with disabilities funds only 34,000 units nationwide, less than 1% of estimated need (HUD, FY 2024)
- **Housing Choice Voucher limitations**: While 1.1 million voucher holders have disabilities, the overall waitlist is 2.5 million households, and voucher funding is not sufficient to meet demand
- **Market incentives**: Developers lack financial incentive to build accessible features because they add cost without corresponding increase in market value for the general population

### Housing-Service Integration Failure

Housing programs and HCBS programs operate through separate federal agencies (HUD vs. CMS), separate state agencies, and separate funding streams. People with disabilities must navigate both systems independently, and coordination between housing supports and service supports is rare.

## Olmstead Enforcement Gaps

### Slow Federal Action

Despite the Supreme Court's 1999 *Olmstead* decision, federal enforcement has been inconsistent:

- DOJ's Civil Rights Division has brought relatively few Olmstead enforcement actions given the scale of noncompliance
- Only 15 states are under federal consent decrees; the remaining states face limited federal accountability
- Executive Order 13217 (2001) directed federal agencies to assist with Olmstead implementation but lacked enforcement mechanisms
- CMS has used Medicaid oversight authority to support rebalancing but has not conditioned Medicaid funding on Olmstead compliance

### State Resistance

Many states have resisted or delayed Olmstead compliance:

- 15 states have no formal Olmstead plan despite the 25+ year mandate
- States have used the "fundamental alteration" defense to argue that full HCBS expansion would alter their Medicaid programs beyond what is reasonable
- Budget constraints (both real and claimed) have been used to justify continued waitlists and institutional placement
- Political opposition from institutional providers has slowed legislative action in many states

## Cultural and Systemic Factors

### Ableist Assumptions

Persistent cultural assumptions about disability undermine independent living:

- **Incompetence presumption**: People with significant disabilities, particularly intellectual and developmental disabilities, are presumed incapable of making decisions about their own lives and services
- **Safety paternalism**: Risk aversion leads to overprotective policies that restrict choice in the name of safety, particularly in group settings
- **Institutional default**: When crises occur (family breakdown, homelessness, behavioral episodes), the default response is often institutional placement rather than community crisis services
- **Professional authority**: Medical and social service professionals are trained to assess and prescribe services, not to follow consumer direction

### Fragmented Service Systems

Independent living requires coordination across multiple systems that do not communicate or coordinate effectively:

| System | Agency | Function |
|--------|--------|----------|
| HCBS | CMS / State Medicaid | Personal care, day services, respite |
| Housing | HUD / State housing agencies | Rental assistance, accessible units |
| Income | SSA | SSI/SSDI cash benefits |
| Employment | DOL / VR agencies | Job training, supported employment |
| Transportation | DOT / Local transit | Paratransit, accessible transit |
| Healthcare | CMS / State Medicaid | Medical services, equipment |
| Education | DOE / State education | Transition services, adult education |

Each system has its own eligibility rules, application processes, service definitions, and funding streams. No single entity is responsible for coordinating independent living supports across systems.
