# Assistive Technology: Stakeholders

## Who Benefits from the Status Quo

### AT Device Manufacturers

**Major Players**: Permobil, Sunrise Medical, Ottobock, Tobii Dynavox, PRC-Saltillo, Sonova, Demant

**How They Benefit**

- Concentrated markets allow premium pricing (200-2,000% markups)
- Proprietary repair ecosystems generate recurring revenue
- Limited competition protects market share
- Insurance reimbursement rates set floor prices regardless of actual costs

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **High**

- Right-to-repair laws threaten repair revenue
- Price transparency or negotiation would reduce margins
- Market entry by consumer tech companies threatens market position
- Coverage expansion could shift bargaining power to insurers

### Insurance Companies

**Who**: Major private insurers (UnitedHealth, Anthem, Cigna, Aetna, Humana), Medicare Advantage plans, Medicaid managed care organizations

**How They Benefit**

- Narrow "medical necessity" definitions limit coverage liability
- "Least costly alternative" rules minimize per-claim spending
- High denial rates reduce payouts; many beneficiaries do not appeal
- Prior authorization processes delay or prevent device delivery

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **High**

- Coverage mandates increase costs
- Broader AT definitions expand liability
- Simplified approval processes reduce insurer control over spending

### DME Suppliers and Distributors

**Who**: National and regional durable medical equipment suppliers

**How They Benefit**

- Serve as intermediaries between manufacturers and consumers
- Markup devices beyond manufacturer prices
- Control distribution channels in many markets
- Medicare supplier certification creates barriers to new entrants

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Medium-High**

- Competitive bidding has already reduced margins for some
- Direct-to-consumer models threaten intermediary role
- Right-to-repair could create competition from independent repair shops

### Some Medical Professionals

**Who**: Physicians, audiologists, and other clinicians who serve as AT gatekeepers

**How They Benefit**

- Prescribing authority gives control over device selection
- Some professionals receive manufacturer referral fees or incentives
- AT assessment generates billable services
- Professional licensing requirements limit competition

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Medium**

- OTC hearing aids threatened audiologist business model
- Expanded scope of practice for ATPs could reduce physician role
- Many medical professionals genuinely support AT access reform

## Who Is Harmed

### People with Disabilities

**61 million Americans with disabilities; 15-20 million AT users or potential users**

**How They Are Harmed**

- Cannot afford needed AT due to insurance gaps and high prices
- Face months-long delays for device approval and delivery
- Receive devices that do not match their needs due to "least costly alternative" rules
- Cannot repair devices they depend on for daily functioning
- Abandon devices at high rates due to poor assessment and training
- Live with reduced independence, employment, and social participation

**Disproportionate Impact**

| Population | Specific Harm |
|------------|---------------|
| Low-income people with disabilities | Cannot afford copays, deductibles, or uncovered devices |
| Rural residents | Limited access to AT professionals, repair services, and device trials |
| People of color with disabilities | Face compounding barriers from racial disparities in healthcare access |
| Children with disabilities | School-provided AT often inadequate; transitions to adult system lose access |
| Aging adults | Medicare hearing aid and vision aid exclusions affect tens of millions |

### Family Caregivers

**Approximately 53 million family caregivers in the U.S., many caring for people with disabilities** (AARP/NAC, National Alliance for Caregiving, 2020)

**How They Are Harmed**

- Bear physical burden when AT is unavailable (manual lifting, transferring)
- Serve as unpaid AT advocates navigating insurance denials and appeals
- Experience financial strain from out-of-pocket AT costs
- Suffer caregiver burnout exacerbated by inadequate AT support

### Employers

**How They Are Affected**

- Lose potential employees who cannot work without AT
- Face uncertainty about accommodation costs (though median cost is only $500)
- Lack guidance on AT procurement and implementation
- May avoid hiring people with disabilities due to perceived AT costs

**Level of Support for Reform**: **Medium**

- Generally support reduced AT costs and clearer accommodation guidance
- Some employers resist mandated accommodations
- Business community divided on coverage mandates that affect insurance premiums

### Healthcare System

**How It Is Affected**

- Treats preventable complications from AT deprivation (pressure injuries, falls, social isolation)
- Bears costs of institutionalization when AT could enable community living
- Spends more on emergency and acute care than AT prevention would cost

## Government Stakeholders

### Federal Agencies

| Agency | Role | Stance on AT Reform |
|--------|------|---------------------|
| ACL (Administration for Community Living) | AT Act program administration | Supportive; constrained by budget |
| CMS (Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services) | Largest AT funder through DME coverage | Cautious; focused on cost containment |
| VA (Department of Veterans Affairs) | Veterans' AT services | Generally supportive; well-funded |
| OSEP (Office of Special Education Programs) | IDEA AT requirements | Supportive; limited enforcement capacity |
| FDA | Medical device regulation | Neutral; focused on safety, not access |
| FCC | Telecommunications accessibility | Supportive within jurisdiction |
| NIDILRR | AT research | Strongly supportive; limited budget |

### State AT Program Directors

**Role**: Administer state AT Act programs; coordinate device demonstration, loan, and reuse

**Collective Voice**: Association of Assistive Technology Act Programs (ATAP)

**Key Concerns**

- Inadequate federal funding
- Growing demand exceeding program capacity
- Need for AT Act reauthorization with increased funding
- Workforce shortages in AT assessment

## Power Analysis

### Who Has Power to Change the System

| Actor | Type of Power | Likelihood of Action |
|-------|---------------|----------------------|
| Congress | Legislation: AT Act reauthorization, Medicare reform, right-to-repair | Moderate with bipartisan support |
| CMS | Regulatory: Medicare coverage decisions, competitive bidding reform | Low to moderate; cost-averse |
| FDA | Regulatory: Device classification, OTC categories | Moderate; demonstrated with hearing aids |
| State legislatures | Legislation: Medicaid coverage, right-to-repair, insurance mandates | Moderate; varies by state |
| Consumer tech companies | Market: Built-in accessibility features that compete with specialized AT | High; already occurring |
| AT manufacturers | Market: Pricing, repair policies, innovation | Low voluntary action; responsive to regulation |

### Coalition Potential

**Strongest Coalition**: Disability organizations + AARP (aging population AT needs) + consumer tech advocates (right-to-repair) + bipartisan congressional champions

**Key Bridge Issues**

- Right-to-repair: Unites disability community with broader consumer rights movement
- OTC/market competition: Appeals to both consumer advocates and fiscal conservatives
- Veterans' AT: Bipartisan support that can pull broader AT reforms forward
- Aging population: AARP's political influence could elevate AT issues
