# Assistive Technology: Opposition Analysis

## Primary Opponents

### AT Device Manufacturers

**Industry Position**: AT manufacturers oppose reforms that would reduce pricing power, open repair markets, or increase competition from consumer technology companies.

**Key Players**: Permobil (wheelchairs), Ottobock (prosthetics), Tobii Dynavox and PRC-Saltillo (AAC), Sonova and Demant (hearing aids)

**Opposition Tactics**

| Tactic | Example |
|--------|---------|
| Lobbying against right-to-repair | Industry groups argue that independent repair could compromise device safety |
| Proprietary ecosystems | Software locks, proprietary connectors, and restricted parts access |
| Patent strategies | Extend device monopolies through incremental patents |
| Regulatory capture | Participation in standards-setting bodies to maintain barriers to entry |
| Reimbursement advocacy | Lobby for higher Medicare/Medicaid reimbursement while opposing price transparency |

**Stated Justification**: Device safety, quality assurance, liability concerns, and the need for R&D investment to fund innovation.

**Underlying Motivation**: Protect profit margins in a concentrated market where competition is limited.

**Opposition Intensity**: **High**

### Insurance Industry

**Industry Position**: Insurers oppose expanded AT coverage mandates, broader definitions of medical necessity, and reduced prior authorization requirements.

**Key Players**: America's Health Insurance Plans (AHIP), UnitedHealth Group, Elevance Health (Anthem), Cigna, Aetna, Humana

**Opposition Tactics**

| Tactic | Example |
|--------|---------|
| "Medical necessity" gatekeeping | Narrow definitions that exclude functional and vocational AT needs |
| Prior authorization burden | Extensive documentation requirements that delay or prevent access |
| "Least costly alternative" policies | Cover cheapest device regardless of functional superiority |
| Denial as cost management | High initial denial rates, banking on consumers not appealing |
| Lobbying against mandates | Argue that coverage mandates raise premiums for all |

**Stated Justification**: Cost containment, evidence-based coverage, preventing fraud and abuse.

**Underlying Motivation**: Minimize claims expenditure; AT coverage is a cost center with limited revenue benefit.

**Opposition Intensity**: **High**

### Fiscal Conservatives in Congress

**Position**: Oppose increased federal spending on AT programs, expanded Medicare coverage for AT, and new federal mandates on private insurance.

**Opposition Tactics**

- Cite CBO cost scores for coverage expansion proposals
- Argue for state flexibility rather than federal mandates
- Propose block grants rather than entitlement-based coverage
- Frame AT spending as "discretionary" rather than essential

**Stated Justification**: Fiscal responsibility, limited government, market-based solutions.

**Underlying Motivation**: Generally principled fiscal conservatism, though often combined with resistance to disability-related spending specifically.

**Opposition Intensity**: **Medium-High**

## Secondary Opponents

### Some Medical Professional Associations

**Position**: Selective opposition to reforms that expand scope of practice for AT professionals or reduce physician gatekeeping roles.

**Examples**

| Association | Specific Opposition |
|-------------|---------------------|
| American Academy of Audiology | Initially opposed OTC hearing aids; sought to maintain audiologist role |
| Some state medical associations | Oppose expanded prescribing authority for ATPs and rehabilitation counselors |
| Physical therapy associations | Concern about non-PT AT assessment in mobility devices |

**Stated Justification**: Patient safety, quality of care, professional standards.

**Underlying Motivation**: Protect professional territory and revenue from AT-related services.

**Opposition Intensity**: **Medium**

### DME Suppliers

**Position**: Oppose competitive bidding reforms, direct-to-consumer models, and quality standards that increase compliance costs.

**Opposition Tactics**

- Lobby through AAHomecare (American Association for Homecare)
- Argue competitive bidding threatens access, especially in rural areas
- Oppose manufacturer direct-to-consumer sales channels
- Resist transparency requirements for pricing and quality data

**Stated Justification**: Beneficiary access, service quality, support for small businesses.

**Underlying Motivation**: Protect intermediary margins and market position.

**Opposition Intensity**: **Medium**

### Anti-Regulatory Groups

**Position**: General opposition to federal mandates, new regulations, and coverage requirements.

**Key Groups**: Heritage Foundation, Cato Institute, National Federation of Independent Business (NFIB)

**Opposition Focus**

- Oppose AT coverage mandates in private insurance as government overreach
- Resist new FDA categories or requirements for consumer technology accessibility
- Frame AT policy as individual responsibility rather than systemic obligation

**Opposition Intensity**: **Medium** (AT is not a priority issue for most anti-regulatory organizations)

## Their Arguments and Counterarguments

### "AT Coverage Mandates Raise Premiums"

**Argument**: Requiring insurers to cover more AT devices will increase premiums for all policyholders, harming businesses and individuals who do not need AT.

**Counterargument**: Actuarial analysis consistently shows that AT coverage mandates have minimal premium impact (typically 0.1-0.5% of premiums), while the economic benefits of AT-enabled employment and independence far exceed costs. The OTC hearing aid model demonstrates that market-based reforms can reduce costs rather than increase them.

*Source: National Conference of State Legislatures, "Health Insurance Mandate Cost Studies" (2023).*

### "Right-to-Repair Compromises Device Safety"

**Argument**: Power wheelchairs, prosthetics, and other AT devices are complex medical devices that require manufacturer expertise to repair safely. Independent repair could endanger users.

**Counterargument**: Users already perform basic maintenance. Right-to-repair laws can include safety provisions (e.g., requiring repair training certification) while ensuring timely access to parts, manuals, and diagnostic tools. Leaving users without functional devices for weeks while awaiting manufacturer repair is itself a safety risk.

*Source: Colorado General Assembly, HB 22-1031 Legislative Analysis (2022).*

### "Federal AT Spending Is Already Adequate"

**Argument**: Between Medicare, Medicaid, VA, and other programs, the federal government already spends billions on AT-related services.

**Counterargument**: Aggregate spending obscures the fragmentation problem. No single program provides comprehensive AT access. Millions of Americans fall through the gaps between programs, and the AT Act -- the only program specifically designed to address systemic AT barriers -- receives only $36.5 million annually.

### "The Market Will Solve AT Access Problems"

**Argument**: Consumer technology companies are already integrating accessibility features at lower prices, and market forces will eventually make specialized AT affordable.

**Counterargument**: Market forces have been operating in the specialized AT sector for decades without producing competitive pricing. The hearing aid market required *regulatory intervention* (OTC rule) to introduce price competition. Without policy action, concentrated AT markets will continue to produce monopoly pricing. Mainstream technology accessibility, while welcome, does not replace specialized devices needed by people with complex disabilities.

### "AT Abandonment Proves Devices Are Not Needed"

**Argument**: High AT abandonment rates suggest that many devices are prescribed unnecessarily, and expanding coverage would waste resources.

**Counterargument**: Abandonment reflects system failures -- poor assessment, inadequate training, and devices that do not match user needs -- not lack of need. Users who abandon devices do not stop needing AT; they simply go without. Investing in proper assessment, training, and follow-up reduces abandonment and improves outcomes.

*Source: Phillips and Zhao, "Predictors of Assistive Technology Abandonment" (updated 2023).*

## Opposition Strategy Assessment

### Where Opposition Is Strongest

- **Medicare coverage expansion**: Cost concerns and CMS institutional conservatism
- **Federal coverage mandates for private insurance**: Insurance industry lobbying power
- **AT Act funding increases**: Competing budget priorities and spending caps

### Where Opposition Is Weakest

- **Right-to-repair for AT**: Broad consumer support; bipartisan appeal; state-level momentum
- **Market competition reforms**: OTC hearing aid precedent; appeals to fiscal conservatives
- **AT research investment**: Generally bipartisan support for innovation
- **Veterans' AT improvements**: Strong bipartisan support

### Opportunities to Neutralize Opposition

| Strategy | Target | Approach |
|----------|--------|----------|
| Cost-benefit framing | Fiscal conservatives | Demonstrate ROI of AT investment ($11-$48 return per $1 spent on workplace AT) |
| OTC model expansion | Market-oriented opponents | Extend OTC hearing aid model to other AT categories |
| Safety-conscious right-to-repair | Manufacturers | Include repair certification and safety standards in legislation |
| State-level action | Federal opponents | Pursue reforms through state legislatures where opposition is weaker |
| Veterans' leadership | Bipartisan | Use veterans' AT programs as model for broader reforms |
