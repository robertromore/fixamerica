# Assistive Technology: History

## Origins

### Pre-Modern Assistive Devices

Assistive technology predates the term itself by centuries. People with disabilities have always sought tools to extend their capabilities:

- **Ancient world**: Prosthetic limbs documented in Egypt (circa 950 BCE); crude wheelchairs depicted in Chinese art (circa 525 BCE)
- **Medieval period**: Hearing trumpets, wooden prosthetics, and mobility aids in common use
- **18th century**: Benjamin Franklin invented bifocals (1784); first systematic education of deaf students using sign language (1760s, France)
- **19th century**: Louis Braille developed the braille reading system (1824); first commercial hearing aids (carbon microphone, 1890s)

### The Birth of Modern AT (1900-1945)

**World War I Impact**

- Massive numbers of disabled veterans created political pressure for rehabilitation
- Federal Board for Vocational Education (1918) began providing prosthetics
- First electric hearing aids (vacuum tube technology, 1920s)
- Motorized wheelchair invented (1916), though not commercially viable until 1950s

**World War II Impact**

- VA Prosthetics Research program established (1945)
- Penicillin and surgical advances saved more wounded soldiers, increasing disability population
- Federal investment in rehabilitation medicine expanded dramatically
- Howard Rusk established the first comprehensive rehabilitation center (1948)

## Key Turning Points

### The Rehabilitation Act of 1973

**Significance**: First federal law recognizing AT as essential to rehabilitation

- Section 504 prohibited discrimination by federal fund recipients, creating demand for accommodations including AT
- Vocational rehabilitation programs began funding AT for employment
- Established National Institute on Disability and Rehabilitation Research (NIDRR, now NIDILRR)

### The Technology-Related Assistance Act of 1988 (Tech Act)

**Background**: Senator Tom Harkin (D-IA) championed the first federal law specifically addressing AT access

**Key Provisions**

| Provision | Purpose |
|-----------|---------|
| State grants | Fund AT programs in all 50 states |
| Systems change | Address systemic barriers to AT access |
| Consumer involvement | Require disability community participation in program design |
| Protection and advocacy | Fund AT-related legal assistance |

**Impact**

- Created state AT programs in every state by 1993
- First federal recognition that AT access is a policy issue, not just a clinical matter
- Authorized at $10 million initially; grew to $30 million by mid-1990s
- Required states to develop comprehensive statewide AT programs

### The Americans with Disabilities Act (1990)

**AT Implications**

- Title I (Employment): Reasonable accommodations can include AT
- Title II (Government): Public entities must provide accessible services, often requiring AT
- Title III (Public Accommodations): Businesses must provide auxiliary aids and services
- Title IV (Telecommunications): Relay services for deaf and hard of hearing

**Limitation**: The ADA creates a right to accommodations but does not fund AT directly, leaving individuals and employers to bear costs

### The Telecommunications Act of 1996, Section 255

**Significance**: First federal mandate requiring telecommunications equipment and services to be accessible to people with disabilities

**Impact**

- Required phone manufacturers to make products accessible "if readily achievable"
- Created FCC enforcement authority for telecommunications accessibility
- Preceded broader technology accessibility mandates
- Weakened by "readily achievable" standard that allowed manufacturers broad discretion

### The AT Act of 1998 and 2004 Reauthorization

**1998 AT Act**

- Replaced the 1988 Tech Act
- Shifted from systems change to direct services (device demonstration, loan, reuse)
- Made state AT programs permanent
- Required alternative financing programs (low-interest loans for AT purchase)

**2004 Reauthorization**

| Change | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Streamlined state formula grants | Simplified administration |
| Mandated core activities | Device demonstration, loan, reuse, financing |
| Added protection and advocacy | Legal assistance for AT access |
| Authorized at $73 million | Never appropriated above $36.5 million |

### The 21st Century Communications and Video Accessibility Act (2010)

**CVAA Provisions**

- Required advanced communications services to be accessible (smartphones, tablets, internet-based communication)
- Mandated video programming accessibility (closed captioning for online video)
- Updated Section 255 for modern technology
- Gave FCC authority over internet-based communications accessibility

**Impact**: Forced Apple, Google, and other tech companies to prioritize accessibility in mobile devices and apps

### The OTC Hearing Aid Act (2017) and FDA Final Rule (2022)

**Background**

- Bipartisan legislation (Senators Warren and Grassley) to create OTC hearing aid category
- FDA issued final rule in August 2022, effective October 2022
- Allowed adults with perceived mild-to-moderate hearing loss to purchase hearing aids without prescription

**Significance**

- First major federal intervention in AT pricing through market competition
- Demonstrated that regulatory reform can reduce AT costs
- OTC hearing aids available for $200-$1,500 compared to $2,000-$8,000 for traditional
- Potential model for other AT categories

## Evolution of AT Technology

### Mobility Technology Timeline

| Era | Technology | Impact |
|-----|-----------|--------|
| Pre-1900 | Wooden wheelchairs, crutches | Basic mobility for some |
| 1900-1950 | Folding wheelchair (Everest & Jennings, 1933) | Portability; commercialization |
| 1950-1970 | Electric wheelchairs, sports wheelchairs | Expanded independence |
| 1970-1990 | Lightweight wheelchairs, power tilt/recline | Customization; pressure management |
| 1990-2010 | Power assist, standing wheelchairs | Enhanced function |
| 2010-present | Smart wheelchairs, exoskeletons, autonomous navigation | Technology convergence |

### Communication Technology Timeline

| Era | Technology | Impact |
|-----|-----------|--------|
| Pre-1960 | Picture boards, symbol systems | Basic communication for nonverbal users |
| 1960-1980 | Blissymbolics, early electronic aids | Expanded vocabulary |
| 1980-1990 | Dedicated speech-generating devices | Synthesized speech; portable communication |
| 1990-2000 | PC-based AAC, symbol-based software | Lower cost; more options |
| 2000-2010 | Touch screen AAC, eye-tracking systems | Faster communication; new access methods |
| 2010-present | iPad-based AAC apps, AI-enhanced prediction, neural AAC | Dramatic cost reduction for some; advanced input methods |

### Hearing Technology Timeline

| Era | Technology | Impact |
|-----|-----------|--------|
| Pre-1900 | Ear trumpets, speaking tubes | Limited amplification |
| 1900-1950 | Carbon, vacuum tube hearing aids | Electronic amplification |
| 1950-1980 | Transistor hearing aids, behind-the-ear (BTE) | Miniaturization |
| 1980-2000 | Digital signal processing, programmable aids | Customization; noise reduction |
| 2000-2020 | Bluetooth connectivity, rechargeable, AI noise processing | Integration with consumer tech |
| 2020-present | OTC hearing aids, AirPods/hearing features, AI-powered | Market disruption; consumer pricing |

## Policy Evolution

### Medicare DME Coverage History

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 1965 | Medicare established; DME coverage included as Part B benefit |
| 1972 | DME coverage expanded; supplier certification required |
| 1989 | Medicare fee schedule for DME established |
| 1997 | Balanced Budget Act authorized DME competitive bidding |
| 2003 | Medicare Modernization Act mandated competitive bidding implementation |
| 2011 | Competitive bidding program fully implemented; significant price reductions |
| 2016 | 21st Century Cures Act required CMS to consider beneficiary access in competitive bidding |
| 2021 | CMS suspended competitive bidding during COVID-19 public health emergency |
| 2023 | CMS expanded AAC device coverage criteria |
| 2024 | Competitive bidding program reforms under consideration |

### State AT Policy Evolution

**Early Innovators (1980s-1990s)**

- California: First state to establish comprehensive AT services program
- Massachusetts: Created AT loan program (MassMATCH)
- Wisconsin: Pioneered AT recycling and reuse programs

**Hearing Aid Mandate Wave (2000-2024)**

- 25 states now mandate private insurance coverage of hearing aids for children
- 6 states mandate coverage for adults
- Mandates vary widely in benefit amounts ($1,000-$4,000) and replacement cycles (2-5 years)

**Right-to-Repair Movement (2020-Present)**

- Colorado enacted first wheelchair-specific right-to-repair law (2022)
- California and Minnesota enacted broad right-to-repair laws including medical devices (2023)
- Federal legislation introduced but not enacted

## Lessons from History

### What Has Worked

- **Dedicated federal programs**: AT Act created infrastructure in every state, though underfunded
- **Regulatory reform for competition**: OTC hearing aid rule demonstrated market-based price reduction
- **Technology mandates**: Section 255 and CVAA forced industry to prioritize accessibility
- **Veterans' programs**: VA investment in prosthetics research produced breakthroughs that benefited civilian populations

### What Has Failed

- **Relying on insurance alone**: Medicare/Medicaid coverage has never been adequate for AT
- **Voluntary industry standards**: Without mandates, accessibility is treated as optional
- **Flat funding**: AT Act funding has never matched authorized levels, limiting program impact
- **Medical model gatekeeping**: Requiring "medical necessity" for functional tools has excluded millions from coverage

### Recurring Patterns

1. **War drives innovation**: Both World Wars, Korea, Vietnam, and post-9/11 conflicts spurred AT research
2. **Mainstream technology eventually incorporates accessibility**: Market pressure and mandates push accessibility from specialized to built-in
3. **Coverage lags technology**: Insurance and funding policies consistently trail technological capability by years or decades
4. **Cost remains the central barrier**: Despite technological progress, financial barriers to AT access persist across eras
