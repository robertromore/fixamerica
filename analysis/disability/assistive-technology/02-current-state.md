# Assistive Technology: Current State

## Federal Funding Landscape

### AT Act Programs

The Assistive Technology Act of 1998 (reauthorized 2004) funds state-level AT programs through the Administration for Community Living (ACL). Every state and territory operates an AT program providing device demonstration, short-term device loans, device reutilization (reuse), and state financing activities.

**Current Funding (FY 2024)**

| Program Component | FY 2024 Funding | Change from FY 2020 |
|-------------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| State AT programs (formula grants) | $30.5 million | +3.4% |
| National activities | $3.5 million | +2.9% |
| Protection and advocacy (AT-related) | $2.5 million | +4.2% |
| **Total AT Act** | **$36.5 million** | **+3.5%** |

*Source: HHS ACL Budget Justification (FY 2024).*

**State AT Program Activities (FY 2023)**

| Activity | People Served | Devices Involved |
|----------|---------------|------------------|
| Device demonstrations | 198,000 | 342,000 |
| Short-term device loans | 78,000 | 112,000 |
| Device reutilization (reuse) | 56,000 | 89,000 |
| State financing (loan programs) | 12,000 | 15,000 |
| Training and technical assistance | 165,000 | N/A |

*Source: ACL, National AT Program Data Report (FY 2023).*

**Funding Context**

- AT Act funding has increased only 15% in nominal terms since 2004 reauthorization
- Adjusted for inflation, real funding has *declined* approximately 25% since 2004
- Average state AT program grant is approximately $550,000, serving states with populations ranging from 500,000 to 39 million
- Programs rely heavily on state matching funds and private fundraising

### Other Federal AT Funding

| Agency/Program | AT-Related Spending (FY 2023) | Focus |
|----------------|-------------------------------|-------|

*Sources: CMS Medicare Spending Report (2024); CMS Medicaid Financial Management Report (2023); VA Budget Submission (FY 2024); RSA Annual Report (2023); OSEP Annual Report (2023); NIDILRR Budget (FY 2023); DoD Budget Justification (FY 2024).*

## Insurance Coverage

### Medicare

Medicare Part B covers durable medical equipment (DME) under 42 U.S.C. Section 1395x(n), defined as equipment that can withstand repeated use, is primarily and customarily used for a medical purpose, is appropriate for home use, and is not useful in the absence of illness or injury.

**Coverage Gaps**

| Device Category | Medicare Coverage Status | Key Limitation |
|-----------------|--------------------------|----------------|
| Manual wheelchairs | Covered | Must meet medical necessity; limited models |
| Power wheelchairs | Covered with prior authorization | Extensive documentation; frequent denials |
| Communication devices (AAC) | Covered since 2001 | Narrow coverage criteria; excludes some devices |
| Hearing aids | Not covered (except cochlear implants) | Major gap affecting millions |
| Eyeglasses | Not covered (except post-cataract) | Basic vision correction excluded |
| Prosthetic limbs | Covered | K-level functional classification limits access |
| Home modifications | Not covered | Ramps, grab bars, widened doorways excluded |
| Vehicle modifications | Not covered | Hand controls, lifts excluded |
| Smart home technology | Not covered | Environmental controls largely excluded |
| Computer access AT | Limited coverage | Only if meets DME definition |

*Source: CMS, Medicare Benefit Policy Manual, Chapter 15 (2024).*

**Competitive Bidding Program**

Medicare's DME Competitive Bidding Program, established by the Medicare Modernization Act of 2003, has significantly reduced reimbursement rates for many AT categories:

| Impact | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Price reductions | 30-50% cuts in reimbursement for many DME categories |
| Supplier consolidation | Number of DME suppliers dropped from 130,000+ to approximately 50,000 |
| Access concerns | GAO found beneficiaries in rural areas had difficulty accessing some items |
| Quality issues | Reports of lower-quality products substituted to maintain margins |

*Sources: GAO, "Medicare: CMS Should Take Actions to Continue to Improve DME Competitive Bidding" (2023); CMS Competitive Bidding Program Data (2024).*

### Medicaid

Medicaid AT coverage varies enormously by state because federal law requires coverage of only a limited set of DME for adults, while states have discretion over most AT categories.

**State Coverage Variation (2023)**

| Coverage Level | States | Examples |
|----------------|--------|----------|
| Comprehensive (broad AT definitions, few exclusions) | 12 states | California, New York, Minnesota |
| Moderate (covers major categories, some gaps) | 20 states | Pennsylvania, Ohio, Washington |
| Limited (narrow DME coverage, many exclusions) | 13 states | Texas, Georgia, Mississippi |
| Minimal (only federally mandated categories) | 5 states + DC | Various |

*Source: Kaiser Family Foundation, Medicaid Benefits Survey (2023).*

**Common Medicaid AT Barriers**

- Prior authorization required for most AT
- Dollar caps on DME spending (e.g., $2,500/year)
- Frequency limitations (e.g., one wheelchair per 5 years regardless of need)
- Narrow "medical necessity" definitions that exclude functional and vocational uses
- Long processing times (30-120 days for authorization)

### Private Insurance

**Coverage Patterns**

| AT Category | Typical Private Insurance Coverage |
|-------------|-------------------------------------|
| Hearing aids | Required in 25 states by mandate; excluded in others |
| Prosthetics | Generally covered; parity laws in 21 states |
| Wheelchairs | Usually covered as DME; limited to "least costly alternative" |
| AAC devices | Inconsistent; often denied as "educational" or "convenience" |
| Vision aids (beyond glasses) | Rarely covered |
| Home modifications | Typically excluded |
| Vehicle modifications | Typically excluded |

*Sources: ASHA, "Health Plan Coverage of AAC Devices" (2024); Amputee Coalition, "Insurance Coverage Survey" (2023); HLAA, "State Hearing Aid Mandate Map" (2024).*

**Common Denial Justifications**

- "Not medically necessary"
- "Investigational or experimental"
- "Convenience item"
- "Educational purpose, not medical"
- "Exceeds benefit maximum"
- "Least costly alternative" rule applied

## AT Market Dynamics

### Pricing and Competition

**Market Structure**

The specialized AT market is characterized by limited competition, high barriers to entry, and a captive consumer base with few alternatives.

| Market Segment | Major Manufacturers | Market Concentration |
|----------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Power wheelchairs | Permobil, Sunrise Medical, Pride Mobility, Quantum | Top 3 control approximately 80% |
| Communication devices (AAC) | PRC-Saltillo, Tobii Dynavox | Effective duopoly |
| Hearing aids (traditional) | Sonova, Demant, WS Audiology, GN Audio, Starkey | Top 5 control approximately 90% |
| Prosthetics (upper limb) | Ottobock, Ossur, Fillauer | Top 3 control approximately 75% |
| Screen readers | Freedom Scientific (JAWS), NV Access (NVDA) | Two dominant products |

*Source: Grand View Research, Assistive Technology Market Report (2024); Hearing Industries Association Market Data (2023).*

**Price Examples (2024)**

| Device | Retail Price Range | Manufacturing Cost Estimate | Markup |
|--------|--------------------|-----------------------------|--------|
| Complex power wheelchair | $25,000-$70,000 | $8,000-$20,000 | 200-350% |
| AAC speech device | $8,000-$15,000 | $1,500-$3,000 | 400-500% |
| Premium hearing aid (pair) | $4,000-$8,000 | $150-$400 | 1,000-2,000% |
| Myoelectric prosthetic hand | $20,000-$100,000 | $5,000-$25,000 | 300-400% |
| Braille display (40-cell) | $3,000-$6,000 | $800-$1,500 | 300-400% |

*Sources: RESNA Cost Survey (2023); WHO Assistive Products List pricing data (2022); manufacturer financial disclosures (2023).*

### Right-to-Repair Issues

People who use AT face particular challenges with device repair and maintenance:

- Manufacturers restrict access to repair manuals, diagnostic software, and replacement parts
- Proprietary software locks prevent third-party repair
- Warranty voiding for unauthorized repairs
- Long wait times for manufacturer-authorized repairs (2-8 weeks for wheelchairs)
- Users may be left without essential mobility or communication devices during repairs

**State Activity**

| State | AT Right-to-Repair Status (2025) |
|-------|-----------------------------------|
| Colorado | Enacted wheelchair right-to-repair law (2022) |
| California | Broad right-to-repair law (2023) includes some AT |
| Minnesota | Right-to-repair law (2023) includes medical devices |
| Massachusetts | Pending AT-specific right-to-repair bill |
| Federal | No federal AT right-to-repair legislation enacted |

*Source: PIRG, "Right to Repair" state legislation tracker (2025).*

## Technology Landscape

### Mainstream Technology Accessibility

Major consumer technology companies have significantly expanded built-in accessibility features:

| Platform | Key Accessibility Features | Year Introduced/Expanded |
|----------|----------------------------|--------------------------|
| Apple (iOS/macOS) | VoiceOver, Switch Control, Live Captions, Personal Voice, Assistive Touch | Ongoing since 2009; major expansion 2023-2024 |
| Google (Android) | TalkBack, Switch Access, Live Transcribe, Sound Amplifier, Lookout | Ongoing since 2011; major expansion 2022-2024 |
| Microsoft (Windows) | Narrator, Eye Control, Voice Access, Live Captions | Ongoing since 2015; major expansion 2022-2024 |
| Amazon (Alexa/Echo) | Voice control, Show and Tell, Adaptive Listening | Ongoing since 2018 |

*Source: Company accessibility documentation and press releases (2024).*

### Emerging AT Technologies

| Technology | Development Stage | Potential Impact | Barrier to Adoption |
|------------|-------------------|------------------|---------------------|
| AI-powered communication (eye-tracking AAC) | Early commercial | Enhanced communication speed for nonverbal users | Cost ($10,000-$25,000) |
| Brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) | Clinical trials | Direct neural device control for severe paralysis | Experimental; surgical risk |
| Exoskeletons for mobility | Early commercial | Walking capability for some wheelchair users | Cost ($80,000-$150,000); limited insurance coverage |
| AI-based image description | Widely available | Real-time visual information for blind users | Accuracy limitations |
| Smart home integration | Commercial | Environmental control for people with limited mobility | Setup cost; interoperability gaps |
| 3D-printed prosthetics | Growing availability | Dramatically lower cost prosthetics ($50-$500) | Durability; limited functionality vs. traditional |

*Sources: Rehabilitation Engineering and Assistive Technology Society of North America (RESNA) conference proceedings (2024); WHO Global Report on Assistive Technology (2022).*

## Recent Developments

### OTC Hearing Aids (2022-Present)

The FDA's final rule on over-the-counter (OTC) hearing aids took effect in October 2022, allowing adults with perceived mild-to-moderate hearing loss to purchase hearing aids without a prescription, medical exam, or audiologist fitting.

| Metric | Status (2024) |
|--------|---------------|
| OTC products on market | 100+ from 40+ manufacturers |
| Price range | $200-$1,500 per pair |
| Market share (of total hearing aid sales) | 5-8% (growing) |
| Traditional hearing aid price change | 5-10% decline in some segments |
| User satisfaction (OTC) | Mixed; lower than traditional for moderate loss |

*Sources: FDA OTC Hearing Aid Guidance (2022); Hearing Industries Association Market Report (2024); Consumer Reports OTC Hearing Aid Survey (2024).*

### Medicare AAC Coverage Expansion (2023-2024)

CMS expanded Medicare coverage for augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) devices in 2023-2024, including:

- Coverage of speech-generating devices with eye-tracking capability
- Expanded trial rental period from 3 to 6 months
- Simplified documentation requirements for renewal
- Coverage of AAC device accessories (mounting systems, cases)

*Source: CMS, "Medicare Coverage of AAC Devices: Final Decision Memo" (2023).*

## Key Takeaways

1. **Federal AT funding is stagnant**: AT Act funding has declined in real terms over two decades, while demand has grown
2. **Insurance coverage is fragmented and inadequate**: Medicare excludes hearing aids and many AT categories; Medicaid varies widely by state; private insurance frequently denies coverage
3. **Market failures keep prices high**: Concentrated markets, captive consumers, and limited price pressure produce markups of 200-2,000%
4. **Mainstream technology is outpacing specialized AT**: Consumer tech companies offer free accessibility features that rival specialized devices costing thousands
5. **Right-to-repair is an emerging AT issue**: People who depend on devices for daily functioning cannot wait weeks for manufacturer-authorized repairs
6. **AT abandonment remains high**: 30-50% abandonment rates reflect poor assessment, inadequate training, and devices that do not match user needs
7. **OTC hearing aids are a promising model**: Regulatory reform to enable competition has already reduced prices and increased access
8. **Research investment is insufficient**: Federal AT research funding is a fraction of the need, and commercialization pathways are weak
