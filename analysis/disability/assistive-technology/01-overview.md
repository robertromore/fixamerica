# Assistive Technology: Overview

## Executive Summary

Assistive technology (AT) encompasses the devices, software, equipment, and systems that enable people with disabilities to perform tasks that would otherwise be difficult or impossible. From powered wheelchairs and screen readers to hearing aids and communication devices, AT is the practical bridge between disability and participation. For the estimated 61 million Americans with disabilities, access to appropriate assistive technology can mean the difference between employment and unemployment, independence and institutionalization, social engagement and isolation.

Despite the transformative potential of AT, the United States lacks a coherent national policy for ensuring that people with disabilities can obtain, maintain, and replace the technology they need. Federal funding for AT is fragmented across more than a dozen agencies and programs, each with its own eligibility requirements, coverage limitations, and bureaucratic processes. Medicare, the largest single payer for durable medical equipment, uses coverage criteria developed decades ago that fail to account for modern technology. Medicaid coverage varies dramatically by state. Private insurance routinely denies coverage for devices it classifies as "convenience" rather than "medical necessity." The result is a system in which access to AT depends more on a person's income, insurance status, geography, and persistence than on clinical need.

The AT landscape is further complicated by rapid technological change. Consumer technology companies are integrating accessibility features into mainstream devices at an unprecedented pace, yet specialized AT remains extraordinarily expensive, with markups that reflect a small, captive market rather than actual production costs. People with disabilities frequently cannot access emerging technologies -- from smartphones to smart home systems to autonomous vehicles -- because accessibility is treated as an afterthought rather than a design requirement. Meanwhile, the federal AT Act program, which funds state-level device demonstration, loan, and reuse programs, operates on a budget that has remained essentially flat for two decades.

Strengthening the AT ecosystem requires reforms across multiple fronts: insurance coverage mandates, federal funding increases, market competition, accessibility standards for emerging technologies, and robust repair and reuse programs. The goal is a system in which every American with a disability has timely access to the technology needed for full participation in work, education, and community life.

## Core Tensions

### Medical Necessity vs. Functional Need

Insurance coverage for AT typically requires demonstration of "medical necessity," a standard that fails to capture the broader functional, social, and vocational benefits of technology. A device that enables a person to work, attend school, or participate in community life may be denied because it does not meet a narrow clinical definition. This tension pits a medical model of disability, which views AT as treatment for a condition, against a social model that views AT as a tool for participation.

### Innovation vs. Affordability

Rapid technological advancement produces increasingly capable assistive devices, but market dynamics keep prices high. The specialized AT market has few competitors, captive consumers, and limited price pressure from insurers who can deny coverage. Meanwhile, mainstream technology companies incorporate accessibility features at mass-market prices, creating a growing gap between what consumer technology offers for free and what specialized AT charges thousands of dollars to provide.

### Standardization vs. Customization

AT must be customized to individual needs, which makes mass production and standardization difficult. However, the absence of interoperability standards means that AT devices often cannot communicate with each other or with mainstream technology, creating fragmented and expensive ecosystems. Balancing individualization with interoperability is a persistent design challenge.

### Federal Consistency vs. State Flexibility

AT policy operates across a patchwork of federal and state programs, each with different rules. Federal mandates could ensure minimum coverage standards, but states argue they need flexibility to design programs that fit their populations and budgets. The result is dramatic geographic inequality in AT access.

## Vision of Success

A well-functioning AT system would ensure that every American with a disability has timely access to appropriate, affordable, and well-maintained assistive technology. Insurance coverage would be based on functional need rather than narrow medical necessity, and would include assessment, training, maintenance, and replacement. The AT market would feature genuine competition, interoperability standards, and right-to-repair protections that keep prices reasonable and devices repairable. Federal investment in AT research would drive innovation while ensuring that new technologies are accessible from the start. State AT Act programs would be funded at levels sufficient to serve all who need device demonstrations, loans, and reuse services. People with disabilities would be active participants in AT design and policy, and AT abandonment rates would decline as devices better match user needs and come with adequate training and support. The result would be a society in which technology enables rather than excludes, and disability is not a barrier to full participation.
