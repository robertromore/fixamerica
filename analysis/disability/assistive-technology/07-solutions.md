# Assistive Technology: Solutions

## Guiding Principles

### Criteria for Evaluating AT Reforms

| Criterion | Definition | Why It Matters |
|-----------|------------|----------------|
| **Universality** | All people with disabilities can access needed AT | Eliminates coverage gaps |
| **Timeliness** | AT is delivered when needed, not after months of bureaucracy | Prevents functional decline |
| **Appropriateness** | AT matches individual user needs, not "least costly alternative" | Reduces abandonment |
| **Sustainability** | AT includes maintenance, repair, training, and replacement | Ensures long-term functionality |
| **Affordability** | AT costs reflect competitive markets, not monopoly pricing | Expands access |
| **Innovation** | Policy promotes AT research and development | Improves future options |

## Policy Proposals

### Tier 1: Insurance Coverage Reform

#### Medicare AT Coverage Expansion

**Problem**: Medicare excludes hearing aids, vision aids, home modifications, vehicle modifications, and many other AT categories; covers DME under narrow "medical necessity" criteria.

**Proposal**

- Add hearing aids to Medicare Part B benefit
- Expand DME definition to include functional and vocational AT needs
- Eliminate "least costly alternative" rule; require coverage of clinically recommended devices
- Add home modification benefit (up to $10,000 per year)
- Cover AT assessment, training, maintenance, and repair as separate benefit categories
- Expand AAC device coverage to include tablet-based communication applications

**Estimated Cost**: $3-5 billion per year for hearing aid coverage; $1-2 billion for expanded DME definitions (CBO-style estimate based on coverage population and average device costs)

**Evidence Base**: VA hearing aid program demonstrates that universal coverage improves outcomes and reduces secondary healthcare costs. Job Accommodation Network data shows AT investment returns $11-$48 per dollar spent in employment settings.

#### Medicaid AT Floor

**Problem**: Medicaid AT coverage varies enormously by state, with some states providing minimal coverage.

**Proposal**

- Establish federal minimum AT coverage standard for Medicaid
- Require all states to cover: wheelchairs (manual and power), hearing aids, AAC devices, prosthetics, orthotics, and home modifications
- Prohibit arbitrary dollar caps and frequency limitations
- Require AT assessment and training as covered services
- Provide enhanced federal matching rate (90%) for AT expenditures to incentivize state compliance

**Estimated Cost**: $2-4 billion per year in additional federal Medicaid spending

#### Private Insurance AT Parity

**Problem**: Private insurance frequently excludes AT or covers only "least costly alternative" devices.

**Proposal**

- Federal AT coverage mandate for employer-sponsored and ACA marketplace plans
- Require coverage of hearing aids, prosthetics, AAC devices, and mobility equipment
- Prohibit "medical necessity" denials for devices recommended by qualified AT professionals
- Require coverage of AT assessment, training, and maintenance
- Establish AT parity with other medical device coverage (no higher copays or deductibles for AT than for other DME)

### Tier 2: Market Competition and Pricing Reform

#### Expand OTC Model to Other AT Categories

**Problem**: The OTC hearing aid model demonstrates that regulatory reform can reduce prices, but no similar reforms exist for other AT categories.

**Proposal**

- Direct FDA to create OTC categories for lower-complexity AT devices: basic mobility aids, low-vision magnifiers, simple communication devices, basic hearing amplification
- Establish safety standards for OTC AT that protect consumers without creating unnecessary barriers
- Fund consumer education on OTC AT options
- Monitor OTC AT quality and safety through post-market surveillance

#### AT Right-to-Repair

**Problem**: People who depend on AT devices for daily functioning cannot repair devices themselves or use independent repair services due to manufacturer restrictions.

**Proposal**

- Federal AT right-to-repair law requiring manufacturers to provide:
    - Repair manuals and diagnostic software to device owners and independent repair shops
    - Replacement parts at reasonable prices
    - Firmware and software updates without requiring authorized service
- Prohibit warranty voiding for independent repairs that meet safety standards
- Require manufacturers to support devices for minimum 7 years after purchase
- Establish AT repair training and certification program

**Evidence Base**: Colorado's wheelchair right-to-repair law (2022) has not produced safety incidents while reducing average repair wait times from 4-6 weeks to 1-2 weeks.

#### AT Price Transparency

**Problem**: AT pricing is opaque, with wide variation between manufacturers, suppliers, and payers for identical devices.

**Proposal**

- Require AT manufacturers to publish wholesale prices
- Require DME suppliers to disclose markup percentages
- Require Medicare and Medicaid to publish reimbursement rates by device category
- Create public AT price comparison database maintained by ACL
- Require insurance companies to provide AT price information to consumers before purchase

### Tier 3: Federal Investment and Infrastructure

#### AT Act Reauthorization and Funding Increase

**Problem**: AT Act funding has declined in real terms over 20 years, leaving state programs unable to meet demand.

**Proposal**

- Reauthorize the AT Act with increased authorized funding: $100 million annually (current: $36.5 million)
- Appropriate at authorized level over 5-year phase-in
- Add new authorized activities: AT recycling/refurbishment centers, telehealth AT assessment, AT workforce training
- Strengthen data collection requirements for AT Act programs
- Establish national AT device exchange registry

#### AT Research and Development Investment

**Problem**: Federal AT research funding is insufficient to drive innovation in a market where private R&D investment is limited by small market size.

**Proposal**

- Increase NIDILRR AT research funding to $100 million annually (current: $35 million)
- Create dedicated AT innovation program at NSF ($50 million annually)
- Establish AT commercialization grants to bridge the gap between research and market availability
- Fund accessibility research within AI, robotics, and HCI programs
- Require federal research grants in relevant fields to include accessibility considerations

#### AT Workforce Development

**Problem**: Critical shortage of AT professionals, particularly in rural areas.

**Proposal**

- Create federal AT workforce scholarship program (similar to NHSC for health professionals)
- Fund 20 additional AT graduate training programs at universities
- Establish AT professional residency program in underserved areas
- Increase Medicare/Medicaid reimbursement rates for AT assessment and training services
- Develop telehealth AT assessment capabilities to extend workforce reach

---

### Tier 4: Technology Standards and Accessibility

#### Universal AT Interoperability Standard

**Problem**: AT devices operate in isolation, unable to communicate with each other or with mainstream technology.

**Proposal**

- Direct NIST to develop AT interoperability standards in collaboration with industry and disability community
- Require AT manufacturers to comply with interoperability standards as condition of Medicare/Medicaid reimbursement
- Integrate AT connectivity requirements into smart home and IoT accessibility standards
- Fund development of open-source AT software platforms

#### Emerging Technology Accessibility

**Problem**: New technologies (AI, autonomous vehicles, smart cities) are developed without accessibility as a design requirement.

**Proposal**

- Require accessibility impact assessments for federally funded technology development
- Update Section 508 standards to cover AI, autonomous vehicles, and smart city technologies
- Establish accessibility testing requirements for new technology categories
- Create tax incentives for companies that incorporate universal design in product development

## Implementation Considerations

### Political Feasibility

| Reform | Feasibility | Path |
|--------|------------|------|
| OTC model expansion | High | FDA regulatory action; bipartisan support |
| AT right-to-repair | High | State-level momentum; bipartisan federal support |
| AT Act reauthorization | Medium-High | Bipartisan tradition; funding level contested |
| AT price transparency | Medium | Industry opposition; consumer support |
| Medicare hearing aid coverage | Medium | Strong advocacy; CBO cost concerns |
| Medicaid AT floor | Medium-Low | Cost to states; Medicaid politics |
| Federal private insurance mandate | Low-Medium | Insurance industry opposition; ACA precedent |
| AT interoperability standards | Medium | Technical complexity; industry resistance |

### Cost-Benefit Summary

| Reform | Estimated Annual Cost | Estimated Annual Benefit | ROI |
|--------|----------------------|--------------------------|-----|
| Medicare hearing aid coverage | $3-5 billion | $5-8 billion (reduced falls, cognitive decline, social isolation) | 1.3-2.0x |
| AT Act funding increase | $63.5 million (additional) | $200-400 million (employment, independence) | 3-6x |
| Right-to-repair | Minimal federal cost | $500 million-$1 billion (reduced repair costs, reduced wait times) | Very high |
| AT workforce development | $50-100 million | $500 million-$1 billion (reduced abandonment, improved outcomes) | 5-10x |

*Estimates based on extrapolation from JAN ROI data (2023), VA hearing aid program outcomes, and state right-to-repair experience.*
