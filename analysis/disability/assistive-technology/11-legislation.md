# Assistive Technology: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Assistive technology policy reform requires action at multiple levels: federal legislation to expand insurance coverage and strengthen AT programs, state legislation to mandate coverage and enable right-to-repair, and regulatory action to expand market competition and establish interoperability standards. This document provides draft legal text for the key reforms identified in [Solutions](07-solutions.md).

## Federal Legislation

### Assistive Technology Access Act

**Purpose**: Expand Medicare and Medicaid coverage of assistive technology, reform the DME benefit, and establish federal AT coverage standards for private insurance.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Assistive Technology Access Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that—
    (1) approximately 61 million Americans live with disabilities;
    (2) assistive technology is essential for independence,
        employment, education, and community participation;
    (3) existing insurance coverage for assistive technology
        is fragmented, inconsistent, and based on narrow
        medical necessity criteria that fail to account for
        functional and vocational needs;
    (4) the assistive technology market is characterized by
        limited competition and prices that exceed competitive
        levels; and
    (5) investment in assistive technology produces returns
        of $11 to $48 per dollar spent in employment settings.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to ensure that all
Americans with disabilities have timely access to appropriate,
affordable assistive technology.

SEC. 3. MEDICARE ASSISTIVE TECHNOLOGY COVERAGE EXPANSION.

(a) HEARING AIDS.—Section 1862(a) of the Social Security Act
(42 U.S.C. 1395y(a)) is amended to remove the exclusion of
hearing aids and related examinations, and to add hearing
aids as a covered benefit under Part B with coverage of
one pair of hearing aids per ear every 3 years.

(b) EXPANDED DME DEFINITION.—Section 1861(n) of the Social
Security Act (42 U.S.C. 1395x(n)) is amended to include
devices that are—
    (1) necessary for the functional independence of the
        beneficiary, including employment, education, and
        community participation; or
    (2) necessary to prevent institutionalization.

(c) HOME MODIFICATION BENEFIT.—A new benefit is established
under Part B covering home modifications necessary for
accessibility, including ramps, grab bars, widened doorways,
and accessible bathroom modifications, up to $10,000 per
beneficiary per year.

(d) AT ASSESSMENT AND TRAINING.—Medicare shall cover assistive
technology assessment by certified assistive technology
professionals, device training, and follow-up services as
Part B benefits, reimbursed separately from device costs.

(e) ELIMINATION OF LEAST COSTLY ALTERNATIVE.—The Secretary
shall not apply a least costly alternative standard to
assistive technology devices when a qualified assistive
technology professional documents that a specific device
is necessary to meet the beneficiary's functional needs.

SEC. 4. MEDICAID ASSISTIVE TECHNOLOGY FLOOR.

(a) MINIMUM COVERAGE.—States participating in the Medicaid
program shall provide coverage for—
    (1) wheelchairs (manual and power);
    (2) hearing aids;
    (3) augmentative and alternative communication devices;
    (4) prosthetics and orthotics;
    (5) home modifications necessary for accessibility;
    (6) assistive technology assessment, training, and
        maintenance; and
    (7) vehicle modifications necessary for independent
        transportation.

(b) PROHIBITION ON ARBITRARY LIMITATIONS.—States shall not
impose dollar caps, frequency limitations, or prior
authorization requirements that are more restrictive than
those applied to other durable medical equipment categories.

(c) ENHANCED FEDERAL MATCHING.—The Federal medical assistance
percentage for assistive technology expenditures under this
section shall be 90 percent for the first 5 years following
enactment, and 80 percent thereafter.

SEC. 5. PRIVATE INSURANCE COVERAGE.

(a) GROUP HEALTH PLANS.—The Employee Retirement Income
Security Act of 1974 is amended to require group health
plans to cover assistive technology devices, assessment,
training, and maintenance on the same terms as other
durable medical equipment.

(b) INDIVIDUAL MARKET.—Section 1302 of the Patient Protection
and Affordable Care Act is amended to include assistive
technology as an essential health benefit.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 18 months after the date of
enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Addresses the largest single gap in AT coverage -- Medicare -- by adding hearing aids, expanding the DME definition, covering home modifications, and requiring coverage of assessment and training
- **Section 4**: Establishes a federal floor for Medicaid AT coverage, eliminating the worst geographic disparities, with enhanced federal matching to ease state fiscal impact
- **Section 5**: Extends AT coverage requirements to private insurance using existing ERISA and ACA frameworks
- **Section 6**: Provides 18-month implementation period for insurers and providers

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| CBO cost score | Phase in coverage; demonstrate ROI through reduced institutionalization and healthcare costs |
| State opposition to Medicaid mandate | Enhanced federal matching rate (90% initial, 80% ongoing) |
| Insurance industry opposition | Build on ACA essential health benefit framework |
| ERISA preemption concerns | Federal mandate applies directly to ERISA plans |

**Refinements**:

- **Fallback position**: Medicare hearing aid coverage alone (most politically feasible component)
- **Stronger version**: Include dental and vision AT; eliminate all prior authorization for AT
- **Phase-in option**: Implement hearing aid coverage first (Year 1), expanded DME definition (Year 2), home modifications (Year 3)

### Assistive Technology Right-to-Repair Act

**Purpose**: Ensure that people with disabilities and independent repair shops can repair AT devices without manufacturer restrictions.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Assistive Technology
Right-to-Repair Act".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) ASSISTIVE TECHNOLOGY DEVICE.—Any item, piece of
equipment, or product system used to increase, maintain,
or improve the functional capabilities of an individual
with a disability, including but not limited to powered
wheelchairs, communication devices, hearing aids,
prosthetics, and computer access equipment.

(b) MANUFACTURER.—Any entity that manufactures, assembles,
or sells assistive technology devices.

(c) INDEPENDENT REPAIR PROVIDER.—Any person or entity,
other than the manufacturer, that provides repair
services for assistive technology devices.

SEC. 3. MANUFACTURER OBLIGATIONS.

(a) REPAIR DOCUMENTATION.—Manufacturers shall make
available to device owners and independent repair
providers, on fair and reasonable terms—
    (1) service manuals, technical bulletins, and repair
        instructions;
    (2) diagnostic software and tools;
    (3) firmware and software updates; and
    (4) replacement parts.

(b) PARTS AVAILABILITY.—Manufacturers shall make
replacement parts available for purchase at reasonable
prices for a minimum of 7 years after the last date of
manufacture of the device model.

(c) SOFTWARE LOCKS.—Manufacturers shall not use software
locks, digital rights management, or other technical
measures to prevent device owners or independent repair
providers from performing repairs.

(d) WARRANTY PROTECTION.—A manufacturer may not void or
limit the warranty on an assistive technology device
solely because the device was repaired by the owner or
an independent repair provider, provided that the repair
did not cause the defect or damage.

SEC. 4. SAFETY PROVISIONS.

(a) REPAIR STANDARDS.—The Secretary of Health and Human
Services, in consultation with the Access Board and
RESNA, shall establish voluntary repair certification
standards for independent AT repair providers.

(b) SAFETY REPORTING.—Independent repair providers shall
report safety incidents to the manufacturer and FDA
under the same requirements applicable to manufacturer-
authorized repair.

SEC. 5. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) FTC ENFORCEMENT.—A violation of this Act shall be
treated as an unfair or deceptive act or practice under
Section 5 of the Federal Trade Commission Act.

(b) STATE ATTORNEY GENERAL.—State attorneys general may
bring civil actions to enforce this Act.

(c) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—Any person aggrieved by a
violation of this Act may bring a civil action for
injunctive relief, damages, and reasonable attorney fees.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 1 year after the date of
enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Core right-to-repair provisions modeled on Colorado HB 22-1031 but applied federally
- **Section 4**: Addresses manufacturer safety concerns through voluntary repair certification
- **Section 5**: Multiple enforcement pathways ensure effective implementation
- **Section 6**: One-year implementation allows manufacturers to prepare

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Manufacturer safety arguments | Safety provisions in Section 4; no reported safety incidents in Colorado |
| FDA medical device preemption | Repair is maintenance, not modification; FDA has stated repair is not within device regulatory scope |
| Industry lobbying | Broad consumer coalition (PIRG, iFixit) and bipartisan support |

### AT Act Reauthorization and Enhancement Act

**Purpose**: Reauthorize the Assistive Technology Act with increased funding and expanded program activities.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Assistive Technology Act
Reauthorization and Enhancement Act".

SEC. 2. REAUTHORIZATION.

(a) The Assistive Technology Act of 1998 (29 U.S.C. 3001
et seq.) is reauthorized through fiscal year 2034.

(b) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—
    (1) STATE GRANTS.—$80,000,000 for each of fiscal years
        2026 through 2034.
    (2) NATIONAL ACTIVITIES.—$12,000,000 for each of fiscal
        years 2026 through 2034.
    (3) PROTECTION AND ADVOCACY.—$8,000,000 for each of
        fiscal years 2026 through 2034.

SEC. 3. NEW PROGRAM ACTIVITIES.

(a) AT REUSE AND REFURBISHMENT.—State AT programs shall
establish or expand device reuse and refurbishment programs,
including collection, sanitization, repair, and
redistribution of used AT devices.

(b) TELEHEALTH AT ASSESSMENT.—State AT programs shall
develop telehealth capabilities for remote AT assessment,
device demonstration, and training.

(c) AT WORKFORCE TRAINING.—State AT programs shall support
training and professional development for AT professionals,
including scholarships, internships, and continuing education.

(d) DATA COLLECTION.—State AT programs shall collect and
report standardized data on AT needs, services provided,
outcomes, and unmet demand.

SEC. 4. NATIONAL AT DEVICE REGISTRY.

The Secretary shall establish a national registry of
available AT devices for reuse, exchange, and loan,
accessible through a public online platform.

SEC. 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on October 1, 2026.
```

**Explanation**:

- Increases authorized funding from $36.5 million to $100 million over 10 years
- Adds new program activities reflecting current needs (reuse, telehealth, workforce)
- Creates national infrastructure for AT device exchange

## State Model Legislation

### Model State AT Right-to-Repair Act

**Purpose**: State-level right-to-repair for assistive technology devices, for states seeking to act before federal legislation.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Assistive Technology
Right-to-Repair Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

[Same definitions as federal bill, adapted for state law]

SECTION 3. MANUFACTURER OBLIGATIONS.

[Same obligations as federal bill, adapted for state
enforcement mechanisms]

SECTION 4. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) The [State Attorney General / State Consumer Protection
Agency] shall enforce this Act.

(b) Violations are subject to civil penalties of up to
$5,000 per violation.

(c) Private right of action for injunctive relief, actual
damages, and reasonable attorney fees.

SECTION 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect [180 days / 1 year] after
enactment.
```

**Adaptations**: States may adjust civil penalty amounts, enforcement agencies, and effective dates. States with existing consumer protection frameworks should integrate AT right-to-repair into those structures.

### Model State Hearing Aid Coverage Mandate

**Purpose**: Require private insurers to cover hearing aids for adults and children.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Hearing Aid
Coverage Act".

SECTION 2. COVERAGE REQUIRED.

(a) Every health insurance policy delivered or issued for
delivery in this state shall provide coverage for hearing
aids for insureds of all ages.

(b) BENEFIT MINIMUM.—Coverage shall include—
    (1) one hearing aid per ear every 3 years;
    (2) fitting and adjustment services;
    (3) batteries or charging accessories; and
    (4) a minimum benefit of $3,000 per ear per
        replacement cycle.

(c) NO DISCRIMINATION.—Cost-sharing requirements for
hearing aids shall not be greater than those applied to
other prosthetic devices.

SECTION 3. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act applies to policies issued or renewed on or after
[date].
```

## Regulatory Framework

### CMS: Medicare DME Coverage Modernization

**Existing Authority**: 42 U.S.C. Section 1395x(n) (DME definition); 42 U.S.C. Section 1395y (coverage exclusions)

**Recommended Regulatory Actions**

| Action | Authority | Timeline |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| Expand AAC coverage criteria to include tablet-based devices | CMS coverage determination | 12-18 months |
| Eliminate face-to-face encounter requirement for AT renewal | CMS rulemaking | 6-12 months |
| Reform competitive bidding to include quality metrics | Medicare Modernization Act Section 1847 | 18-24 months |
| Establish AT assessment as separately billable service | Medicare physician fee schedule | 12 months |

### FDA: OTC AT Category Expansion

**Existing Authority**: Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act Section 520(q) (OTC hearing aid authority); potential extension to other low-risk AT categories

**Recommended Actions**

- Issue guidance on OTC AT device safety standards for mobility aids, low-vision magnifiers, and simple communication devices
- Establish streamlined 510(k) pathway for low-risk AT innovations
- Develop AT-specific quality system requirements

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

**Commerce Clause**: Federal AT coverage mandates and right-to-repair legislation rest on well-established Commerce Clause authority over interstate commerce, including insurance and medical device markets.

**Spending Clause**: Medicaid AT floor requirements are permissible conditions on federal funding under South Dakota v. Dole (1987), provided they are related to the federal interest in Medicaid and do not constitute coercion under NFIB v. Sebelius (2012). The enhanced federal matching rate addresses the coercion concern.

**ERISA Preemption**: The AT Access Act directly amends ERISA to add AT coverage requirements, avoiding preemption issues that would affect state-only mandates.

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Application |
|-----------|-------------|
| CMS enforcement | Medicare and Medicaid AT coverage compliance |
| FTC enforcement | AT right-to-repair violations |
| State AG enforcement | State right-to-repair and coverage mandates |
| Private right of action | AT right-to-repair; insurance coverage denials |
| Medicare appeals process | Individual coverage denial challenges |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Assistive Technology Access Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| "Functional independence" interpretation | Insurers could narrowly interpret "functional independence" to exclude social and recreational AT | High |
| Home modification cap gaming | $10,000 annual cap could be circumvented by artificial project splitting or exploited by requiring multiple years of modifications | Medium |
| Enhanced matching sunset | 90% federal matching drops to 80% after 5 years; states may reduce coverage when matching decreases | Medium |
| Prior authorization workarounds | Prohibition on "more restrictive" PA could be circumvented by applying general medical necessity standards | High |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address AT pricing directly | High prices persist even with expanded coverage | Market structure not reformed |
| No AT workforce mandate | Coverage expansion without providers to deliver services | Workforce shortage |
| ERISA coverage mandate may face legal challenge | Coverage expansion delayed by litigation | Employer opposition to mandates |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Define "functional independence" explicitly in regulations to include employment, education, community participation, and social engagement
2. Add AT price negotiation authority for CMS (similar to drug price negotiation under Inflation Reduction Act)
3. Link AT workforce development funding to coverage expansion timeline
4. Establish expedited judicial review for ERISA challenges

### AT Right-to-Repair Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| "Reasonable terms" undefined | Manufacturers could charge excessive prices for parts and manuals while claiming terms are "reasonable" | High |
| Software update bundling | Manufacturers could bundle accessibility features with software updates that require authorized service | Medium |
| Voluntary certification as barrier | Repair certification could be used to create de facto licensing requirements | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No AT-specific repair training infrastructure | Independent repair providers may lack AT-specific expertise | Workforce development gap |
| Does not address AT design for repairability | Future devices may be designed to be harder to repair | No design requirements |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Define "reasonable terms" with specific pricing benchmarks (e.g., parts at no more than 100% of manufacturing cost)
2. Prohibit bundling of essential functionality with maintenance-only software updates
3. Make repair certification truly voluntary with explicit statutory prohibition on its use as gatekeeping
4. Add design-for-repairability requirements in future legislation

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Funding appropriations may fall short of authorization | Build bipartisan appropriations coalition; link to veterans' programs |
| State implementation variation | Strong federal regulations; technical assistance from ACL |
| Technology evolves faster than legislation | Build technology-neutral language; regular regulatory updates |
| Manufacturer compliance monitoring | Dedicated FTC enforcement unit; industry reporting requirements |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Coverage expansion: 5-year review with GAO evaluation of access outcomes
- Right-to-repair: 3-year review of safety data and repair access improvements
- AT Act programs: 10-year reauthorization with annual performance reporting
- OTC AT categories: Annual FDA safety review; biennial market analysis
