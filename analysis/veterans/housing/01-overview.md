# Veteran Housing: Overview

## Executive Summary

Veteran housing encompasses one of the most consequential policy areas in the veterans' services landscape, affecting everything from homelessness prevention to wealth building and community reintegration. The United States has made significant progress in reducing veteran homelessness--from an estimated 74,770 veterans experiencing homelessness on a single night in 2010 to approximately 35,574 in 2024 (HUD Annual Homeless Assessment Report)--yet veterans remain dramatically overrepresented among the homeless population, comprising roughly 7% of all homeless adults while constituting about 6% of the adult population. The gap is especially stark among certain subpopulations: Black veterans are five times more likely to experience homelessness than white veterans, and women veteran homelessness has increased even as overall veteran homelessness declined.

Beyond homelessness, veteran housing policy encompasses the VA Home Loan Guaranty program--which has helped over 28 million veterans and service members purchase homes since its creation in 1944 (VA, FY 2024)--as well as the HUD-VASH program (combining Housing Choice Vouchers with VA case management), the Supportive Services for Veteran Families (SSVF) program, and the Grant and Per Diem (GPD) transitional housing program. These programs represent a combined federal investment exceeding $8 billion annually.

Despite this infrastructure, systemic challenges persist: the VA Home Loan's effectiveness is eroded by housing affordability crises in many markets, transitional housing supply is insufficient, rural veterans lack access to housing services, and the transition from military housing (guaranteed, subsidized) to civilian housing (market-rate, unassisted) remains abrupt and poorly supported. Addressing veteran housing requires coordinating VA, HUD, DOD, and community partners while tackling both emergency homelessness services and long-term housing stability.

## Core Tensions

### Housing First vs. Treatment First

| Position | Argument |
|----------|----------|
| **Housing First** | Stable housing is a prerequisite for addressing other issues; no preconditions for housing |
| **Treatment First** | Substance abuse and mental health must be addressed before housing can succeed |
| **Evidence** | Housing First consistently produces better housing retention and equivalent or better treatment outcomes (RAND, 2023) |

### Emergency Response vs. Prevention

| Position | Argument |
|----------|----------|
| **Emergency focus** | Must house currently homeless veterans immediately |
| **Prevention focus** | Preventing homelessness is more cost-effective than responding to it |
| **Integrated approach** | Both are necessary; prevention reduces emergency demand |

### VA Home Loan Affordability vs. Market Reality

| Position | Argument |
|----------|----------|
| **Program expansion** | Increase loan limits and reduce fees to improve access |
| **Market-based** | Housing affordability is a market problem, not a VA program problem |
| **Supply-side** | Increase housing supply rather than expanding demand-side subsidies |

## Vision of Success

A well-functioning veteran housing ecosystem would feature:

- **Functional zero homelessness**: Every community has the capacity to rapidly house any veteran who becomes homeless, with return to homelessness rare
- **Effective homeownership pathway**: VA Home Loan accessible and effective in all housing markets, with financial literacy and sustained support
- **Seamless transition**: Military-to-civilian housing transition planned and supported, with no gap in housing stability
- **Equitable outcomes**: No racial, gender, or geographic disparities in veteran housing outcomes
- **Prevention priority**: Investment focused on preventing housing instability before it becomes homelessness
- **Aging in place**: Older veterans and disabled veterans supported with accessible housing and home modifications

|--------|-------|
| **Veteran homeownership rate** | 75% (vs. 65% general population, Census 2024) |

| Outcome | Housing Connection |
|---------|-------------------|
