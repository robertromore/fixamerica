# Veteran Housing: Root Causes

## Overview

Veteran housing instability and homelessness result from the interaction of veteran-specific risk factors (service-connected conditions, transition disruption, military culture) with broader housing system failures (affordability crisis, insufficient supply, fragmented services). The root causes operate at individual, institutional, and systemic levels, and effective solutions must address all three.

---

## Veteran-Specific Risk Factors

### Service-Connected Conditions

| Condition | Prevalence Among Homeless Veterans | Housing Impact |
|-----------|-----------------------------------|----------------|
| **PTSD** | 50% | Social isolation, relationship breakdown, employment difficulty |
| **Traumatic brain injury (TBI)** | 20-25% | Cognitive impairment affecting housing navigation and stability |
| **Substance use disorder** | 45% | Income loss, eviction, program noncompliance |
| **Physical disability** | 40%+ | Accessibility needs, reduced employment capacity |
| **Military sexual trauma** | 25% (40% among women) | Gender-specific housing needs, distrust of mixed-gender programs |
| **Chronic pain** | 30%+ | Employment limitations, substance use risk |

### Transition Disruption

The military provides guaranteed housing (barracks, on-base family housing, Basic Allowance for Housing) that ends abruptly at separation. This creates a "housing cliff" that is particularly dangerous for veterans without civilian housing experience, family support, or financial reserves.

| Factor | Military Reality | Civilian Reality | Gap |
|--------|-----------------|-----------------|-----|
| **Housing guarantee** | Housing always provided or subsidized | Must compete in market | No bridging support |
| **Housing cost** | BAH covers costs or housing is provided | Must pay from earnings | Income may decrease |
| **Housing search** | Assigned or limited choices | Must navigate market | No civilian experience |
| **Lease/mortgage knowledge** | Not needed on base | Essential | Financial literacy gap |
| **Security deposits** | Not needed | Often 1-2 months rent | Savings often insufficient |
| **Credit history** | May be limited | Required for housing | Limited civilian credit profile |

### Social Isolation

| Factor | Mechanism | Impact |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| **Loss of unit cohesion** | Military social bonds severed at separation | Reduced social support network |
| **Geographic dislocation** | Veterans may separate far from family/community | Isolation in unfamiliar areas |
| **Cultural divide** | Military-civilian cultural gap creates alienation | Difficulty building civilian social networks |
| **Stigma** | Veterans may avoid seeking help due to military culture | Late intervention when housing instability becomes crisis |
| **Family breakdown** | Deployment stress leads to higher divorce rates | Loss of household income and housing stability |

## Systemic Housing Market Failures

### Affordability Crisis

| Factor | Impact on Veterans |
|--------|-------------------|
| **National housing shortage** | Estimated 4-7 million unit deficit (2024); veterans compete with all households |
| **Rising rents** | Median rent increased 30%+ since 2019; BAH-to-civilian income gap widens |
| **Wage stagnation** | Veteran earnings may not keep pace with housing costs, especially for younger and disabled veterans |
| **High-cost markets** | VA Home Loan less effective where median prices exceed $600,000 |
| **Landlord preferences** | Some landlords avoid VA loans (appraisal delays) and vouchers (administrative burden) |

### Housing Supply Constraints

| Constraint | Impact |
|------------|--------|
| **Zoning restrictions** | Limit affordable housing construction in many communities |
| **NIMBYism** | Community opposition to supportive housing and affordable development |
| **Construction costs** | Building materials and labor costs make affordable housing financially challenging |
| **Rural housing stock** | Limited rental inventory in areas near military installations |
| **Accessible housing shortage** | Insufficient wheelchair-accessible and ADA-compliant units |

## Institutional Failures

### Program Fragmentation

| Agency | Programs | Limitation |
|--------|----------|------------|
| **VA** | HUD-VASH (case management), SSVF, GPD, SAH | Healthcare-focused; limited housing development capacity |
| **HUD** | HUD-VASH (vouchers), public housing, CDBG | General housing focus; veteran identification inconsistent |
| **DOD** | BAH, on-base housing | Responsibility ends at separation; no transition housing |
| **USDA** | Rural housing programs | Limited veteran targeting |
| **State agencies** | Homeless services, housing finance | Varies dramatically by state; veteran identification uneven |
| **Local CoCs** | Coordinated entry, shelter, rapid re-housing | Veteran prioritization depends on local implementation |

### VA Home Loan Program Design Issues

| Issue | Impact |
|-------|--------|
| **Funding fee** | Adds 1.25-3.3% to loan cost; effectively a tax on veteran homeownership |
| **Seller reluctance** | VA appraisal requirements and timelines make some sellers prefer conventional offers |
| **Financial literacy gap** | Veterans may not understand VA loan benefits or homeownership responsibilities |
| **Refinancing risks** | IRRRL and cash-out refinance products have been used to extract veteran equity |
| **Geographic mismatch** | Veterans may use VA loans in base-adjacent areas with limited appreciation potential |

### HUD-VASH Utilization Gaps

| Gap | Cause | Impact |
|-----|-------|--------|
| **24% of vouchers unused** | Administrative delays, landlord reluctance, client barriers | Wasted resources; unhoused veterans |
| **Lease-up delays** | 90-120 days average; tight housing markets | Veterans remain homeless during search |
| **Landlord participation declining** | Fair Market Rent below actual market; administrative burden | Fewer available units |
| **Case management capacity** | VA staffing constraints; high caseloads | Insufficient support for housing stability |
| **Rural applicability** | Vouchers useless without available rental units | Rural veterans cannot utilize benefit |

## Who Benefits from the Status Quo

| Beneficiary | How They Benefit | Incentive to Maintain |
|-------------|------------------|-----------------------|
| **Landlords in tight markets** | Avoid voucher holders; charge market rates without subsidy compliance | Administrative freedom; higher-income tenants |
| **For-profit lending** | VA loan refinancing generates fees and commissions | Revenue from veteran loan products |
| **Local NIMBY interests** | Block affordable/supportive housing to protect property values | Property value maintenance; community control |
| **Some transitional housing providers** | GPD funding model incentivizes bed-filling over rapid placement | Per diem payments continue while beds are occupied |
| **Defense housing contractors** | Military housing privatization contracts; no post-separation obligation | Continued military housing contracts |
| **Real estate industry** | VA loan funding fees reduce market share concern from zero-down loans | Level playing field perception |

---

## Perverse Incentives

| Perverse Incentive | Mechanism | Consequence |
|--------------------|-----------|-------------|
| **Per diem funding model (GPD)** | Programs paid per bed-night occupied | Incentivizes longer stays rather than rapid permanent placement |
| **HUD-VASH voucher structure** | Funding based on vouchers allocated, not used | No penalty for underutilization; 24% vacancy |
| **VA Home Loan funding fee** | Fee revenue funds VA programs | VA has financial incentive to maintain fee; reduces veteran purchasing power |
| **Disability-housing interaction** | Higher disability rating = more income but also less employability | Complex benefits calculus discourages some housing-positive behaviors |
| **Emergency services focus** | Crisis response more politically visible than prevention | Prevention underfunded relative to emergency shelter |
| **Annual PIT count methodology** | Single-night count misses transitional homelessness | Understates true scope; reduces urgency |

## Causal Chain

### Primary Pathway: Veteran Homelessness

```
Service-connected conditions (PTSD, TBI, SUD)
    |
    v
Employment instability (underemployment, job loss)
    |
    v
Income insufficient for housing costs
    |
    v
Housing instability (missed rent, overcrowding, doubling up)
    |
    v
Social support network erosion (isolation, family breakdown)
    |
    v
Homelessness (sheltered or unsheltered)
    |
    v
Cycle of emergency services (shelter, ER, incarceration)
    |
    v
Chronic homelessness without intervention
```

### Secondary Pathway: Housing Transition Failure

```
Military housing ends at separation
    |
    v
BAH income stops; civilian income may be lower
    |
    v
No civilian housing experience (lease, mortgage, market navigation)
    |
    v
Poor housing decisions (unaffordable, inappropriate, unstable)
    |
    v
Rapid housing instability (within 12 months of separation)
    |
    v
Cascading crises (employment loss, family stress, mental health decline)
```

### Tertiary Pathway: Homeownership Barriers

```
VA Home Loan available but veteran lacks financial literacy
    |
    v
Funding fee increases effective cost; sellers prefer conventional offers
    |
    v
Veteran purchases in less competitive market (may be less desirable area)
    |
    v
Limited appreciation; equity growth slower than peers
    |
    v
Wealth gap between veteran and civilian homeowners in some markets
    |
    v
OR: Veteran cannot purchase at all in high-cost market
```

## Racial Disparities in Veteran Housing

### Historical Roots

The racial disparities in veteran housing--Black veterans five times more likely to experience homelessness--have deep historical roots in the discriminatory implementation of the original GI Bill, redlining, and systemic racism in housing markets.

| Factor | Historical Impact | Current Manifestation |
|--------|-------------------|----------------------|
| **GI Bill exclusion** | Black WWII veterans denied VA loans by racist lenders and developers | Intergenerational wealth gap in homeownership |
| **Redlining** | FHA and VA maps marked Black neighborhoods as "hazardous" | Persistent neighborhood disinvestment |
| **Restrictive covenants** | Legal barriers to Black homeownership in suburban developments | Residential segregation patterns |
| **Predatory lending** | Targeting of minority veterans for subprime products | Higher foreclosure rates; wealth extraction |
| **Employment discrimination** | Black veterans face dual discrimination (race + veteran status) | Higher unemployment leading to housing instability |
