# Veteran Housing: What Citizens Can Do

## Overview

Improving housing outcomes for veterans--from ending homelessness to expanding homeownership to supporting aging in place--requires action at every level, from individual citizens and community organizations to state governments and federal agencies. This document provides concrete, actionable steps organized by effort level and target audience.

## For All Citizens

### Learn and Share

1. **Understand the scope of veteran housing challenges**: Read the HUD Annual Homeless Assessment Report and VA homeless program data. Key facts to share: approximately 35,574 veterans experience homelessness on any given night, Black veterans are 5x more likely to be homeless, and women veteran homelessness is growing even as overall numbers have declined.

2. **Contact your representatives**: Call or write your U.S. Senators and Representative to:
    - Support increased HUD-VASH voucher funding and utilization reforms
    - Advocate for VA Home Loan funding fee reduction for first-time veteran homebuyers
    - Support source-of-income discrimination protections for voucher holders
    - Request annual reporting on veteran housing disparities by race, gender, and geography

3. **Challenge NIMBY opposition**: When supportive housing for veterans is proposed in your community, attend public hearings and speak in support. Evidence shows that well-managed supportive housing does not reduce neighboring property values (Urban Institute, 2023). Veterans who served their country deserve housing in safe communities.

4. **Volunteer**: Contact your local VA Medical Center, SSVF grantee, or VSO about opportunities to support veteran housing, including:
    - Stand Down events (community resource fairs for homeless veterans)
    - Habitat for Humanity veteran builds
    - Furniture and household goods donations for newly housed veterans
    - Move-in assistance for veterans transitioning from shelters to permanent housing

### Donate and Fund

5. **Support organizations serving veteran housing needs**: Directed donations to organizations with demonstrated housing programs, including:
    - National Coalition for Homeless Veterans (NCHV)
    - U.S. VETS (housing and services for homeless veterans)
    - Volunteers of America (veteran housing and SSVF programs)
    - Swords to Plowshares (Bay Area veteran housing and legal services)
    - Community Solutions (Built for Zero initiative)
    - Habitat for Humanity (veteran homeownership programs)
    - Fisher House Foundation (temporary housing for veteran families near VA facilities)

## For Veterans and Military Families

### Access Available Resources

6. **Know your VA Home Loan benefit**: The VA Home Loan offers zero-down-payment, no-PMI home financing. Even if you have used the benefit before, you may have remaining entitlement. Contact VA at 1-877-827-3702 or visit benefits.va.gov/homeloans to check eligibility.

7. **Seek pre-purchase homeownership counseling**: Free HUD-certified counseling is available through NeighborWorks America (1-888-995-4673) and local HUD-approved housing counseling agencies. Counseling reduces foreclosure risk by 30-40% for first-time buyers.

8. **Access housing assistance if at risk**: If you are facing housing instability--missed rent, eviction notice, utility shutoff, or doubling up with others--contact:
    - **SSVF**: Call the National Call Center for Homeless Veterans at 1-877-424-3838
    - **HUD-VASH**: Ask your VA provider for a HUD-VASH referral
    - **211**: Dial 211 for local housing assistance resources
    - **Veterans Crisis Line**: If housing stress is causing a crisis, call 988 (press 1)

9. **File for Specially Adapted Housing if eligible**: Veterans with qualifying service-connected disabilities may be eligible for SAH grants (up to $109,986) or SHA grants (up to $22,036) for home modifications. Apply through VA at benefits.va.gov/homeloans/adaptedhousing.asp.

10. **Use SCRA protections**: Active duty service members are protected against foreclosure and eviction under the Servicemembers Civil Relief Act. If you are being foreclosed upon or evicted during or shortly after active duty, contact your installation legal assistance office or the VA Foreclosure Prevention team.

### Plan Your Housing Transition

11. **Start housing planning 12 months before separation**: Do not wait until the last month. Key steps:
    - Assess your credit score and address any issues
    - Research housing costs in your planned civilian location
    - Explore VA Home Loan pre-approval
    - Calculate your civilian housing budget (your income may change)
    - Build an emergency fund covering at least 3 months of housing costs

12. **Connect with veteran housing navigators**: Many SSVF grantees and Vet Centers offer free housing navigation services. They can help with housing search, application assistance, and connecting to resources in your new community.

### Advocate

13. **Share your experience**: Contact the VA Homeless Programs Office or your Congressional representatives to share your housing challenges. Personal stories from veterans are the most effective advocacy tool for policy change.

14. **Join or support a VSO**: Organizations like the American Legion, VFW, DAV, and IAVA advocate for veteran housing policy at the federal and state level. Your membership strengthens their voice.

## For Community Organizations

### Build Local Support Infrastructure

15. **Participate in Built for Zero**: If your community is not already participating in the Built for Zero initiative, advocate for your local Continuum of Care to join. Contact Community Solutions at community.solutions for information on enrollment and methodology.

16. **Become an SSVF grantee**: Nonprofit organizations with housing services capacity can apply for SSVF grants to provide rapid re-housing and prevention services to veterans. Application information is available at va.gov/homeless/ssvf.

17. **Create landlord engagement programs**: Partner with local housing authorities and landlords to increase acceptance of HUD-VASH vouchers. Offer landlord incentives, damage mitigation funds, and dedicated points of contact for landlord concerns.

18. **Organize Stand Down events**: Stand Down events bring together housing, healthcare, employment, legal, and social services for homeless and at-risk veterans in a single-day or multi-day community event. The National Coalition for Homeless Veterans provides planning guides at nchv.org.

19. **Support women veteran housing**: Develop or expand gender-specific housing programs for women veterans, including family-friendly units and trauma-informed services for MST survivors. The VA Women's Health Program can provide guidance on gender-responsive program design.

### Partner with VA and HUD

20. **Apply for GPD grants**: Community organizations can apply to operate transitional housing programs for homeless veterans through the Grant and Per Diem program. Application information is available at va.gov/homeless/gpd.asp.

21. **Host housing resource fairs**: Partner with your local VAMC and housing authority to organize housing resource events connecting veterans with VA loans, rental assistance, legal services, and homeownership counseling.

## For Housing Professionals

### Serve Veteran Homebuyers

22. **Become a VA-approved lender**: Lenders can apply to originate VA-guaranteed home loans. The VA loan market represents approximately 10% of all mortgage origination and growing. Information available at benefits.va.gov/homeloans/lenders.asp.

23. **Earn Military Relocation Professional certification**: Real estate agents can complete the MRP certification through the National Association of Realtors to better serve military and veteran homebuyers.

24. **Accept HUD-VASH vouchers**: Landlords and property managers can accept HUD-VASH vouchers, which provide guaranteed monthly rental payments and VA case management support for tenants. Contact your local housing authority for information.

25. **Understand VA loan processes**: VA loans do not require down payments or private mortgage insurance. Appraisal requirements protect the veteran buyer. Closing timelines are comparable to conventional loans when properly managed. Educate yourself and your clients on the facts.

## For State and Local Government

### Policy and Funding

26. **Enact source-of-income discrimination protections**: Only 17 states and DC prohibit landlord discrimination against voucher holders. State legislation protecting veteran voucher holders significantly increases HUD-VASH utilization.

27. **Create veteran preferences in state housing programs**: State housing finance agencies can create veteran set-asides or preferences in Low-Income Housing Tax Credit developments, state affordable housing trust funds, and public housing waitlists.

28. **Fund veteran housing counseling**: States can supplement federal funding for homeownership counseling specifically targeting veteran populations, including minority veterans and first-generation homebuyers.

29. **Reform zoning for supportive housing**: Local governments can reduce barriers to supportive housing development by allowing by-right siting of supportive housing in residential zones and streamlining approval processes.

30. **Support aging-in-place programs**: State and local governments can fund home modification programs for aging and disabled veterans, leveraging USDA Rural Development, CDBG, and HOME funds.

## For Employers

31. **Support employee housing stability**: Employers can provide housing-related benefits including relocation assistance, emergency financial assistance for housing crises, and flexible scheduling for veterans dealing with housing transitions.

32. **Partner with VA employment programs**: Stable employment is the strongest predictor of housing stability. Contact your local VA Medical Center's Compensated Work Therapy program or Veteran Readiness and Employment office for veteran hiring partnerships.

33. **Provide financial wellness programs**: Employers can offer financial literacy education, homeownership counseling, and credit-building programs that particularly benefit veteran employees transitioning to civilian careers.
