# Veteran Housing: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative landscape for veteran housing spans veterans affairs law, housing and urban development policy, civil rights protections, and defense transition programs. Current law provides a substantial but incomplete framework: the VA Home Loan Guaranty (38 U.S.C. Chapter 37), HUD-VASH (appropriations-based), SSVF (38 U.S.C. 2044), GPD (38 U.S.C. 2011-2013), and SAH (38 U.S.C. Chapter 21) form the core programs, while the SCRA (50 U.S.C. 3901 et seq.) provides legal protections for service members. Significant statutory gaps remain in homelessness prevention mandates, Housing First codification, funding fee reform, military-to-civilian transition housing support, source-of-income discrimination protection, and equity requirements. This document proposes federal legislation, state model legislation, and regulatory reforms to address these gaps.

## Federal Legislation

### Veteran Housing Stability Act

**Purpose**: Comprehensive legislation addressing veteran homelessness prevention, HUD-VASH reform, Housing First codification, and housing supply development for veteran populations.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Veteran Housing Stability Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(1) approximately 35,574 veterans experience homelessness on any
    given night, despite a 52 percent reduction since 2010;
(2) progress on reducing veteran homelessness has stalled since
    2022, requiring renewed and targeted investment;
(3) 24 percent of HUD-VASH vouchers are not in active use due
    to landlord reluctance, administrative barriers, and
    insufficient case management;
(4) Housing First approaches produce 80-85 percent housing
    retention at 12 months, compared with 50-60 percent for
    treatment-first models;
(5) prevention of homelessness costs approximately $2,500 per
    household, compared with $40,000 per veteran per year in
    emergency services;
(6) Black veterans are five times more likely to experience
    homelessness than white veterans; and
(7) women veteran homelessness is increasing even as overall
    veteran homelessness has declined.

SEC. 3. HOUSING FIRST REQUIREMENT.

(a) IN GENERAL.--All programs funded under sections 2011 through
    2044 of title 38, United States Code, and the HUD-Veterans
    Affairs Supportive Housing program shall adhere to Housing
    First principles.
(b) HOUSING FIRST DEFINED.--For purposes of this section, "Housing
    First" means an approach to serving homeless veterans that--
    (1) provides immediate access to permanent housing without
        preconditions of sobriety, treatment compliance, or
        program participation;
    (2) offers voluntary, accessible, and integrated supportive
        services;
    (3) uses a harm reduction approach to substance use; and
    (4) emphasizes client choice in housing and service options.
(c) FIDELITY STANDARDS.--The Secretary of Veterans Affairs, in
    consultation with the Secretary of Housing and Urban
    Development, shall establish Housing First fidelity standards
    and require compliance by all grantees within 18 months of
    enactment.
(d) EXCEPTIONS.--Nothing in this section shall prohibit programs
    from maintaining reasonable lease requirements, including
    adherence to building rules and timely rent payment.

SEC. 4. HUD-VASH REFORM.

(a) LANDLORD INCENTIVE PROGRAM.--The Secretary of Housing and Urban
    Development shall establish a HUD-VASH Landlord Incentive
    Program providing--
    (1) signing bonuses of not less than $2,000 and not more than
        $5,000 per unit for landlords who lease to HUD-VASH
        voucher holders;
    (2) a damage mitigation fund covering up to $5,000 in damages
        beyond normal wear and tear; and
    (3) streamlined payment processing guaranteeing landlord
        payment within 5 business days of the first of each month.
(b) FAIR MARKET RENT REFORM.--The Secretary of Housing and Urban
    Development shall--
    (1) permit HUD-VASH administering agencies to use Small Area
        Fair Market Rents; and
    (2) authorize exception payment standards of up to 120 percent
        of the applicable Fair Market Rent for HUD-VASH vouchers
        in markets where voucher utilization falls below 80
        percent.
(c) HOUSING NAVIGATORS.--The Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall
    hire not fewer than 500 housing navigators to assist veterans
    in identifying available housing, completing applications,
    and achieving lease-up within 60 days of voucher issuance.
(d) CASE MANAGEMENT STAFFING.--The Secretary of Veterans Affairs
    shall ensure that HUD-VASH case management caseloads do not
    exceed 25 veterans per case manager.
(e) UTILIZATION TARGETS.--Each public housing authority
    administering HUD-VASH vouchers shall achieve a utilization
    rate of not less than 90 percent within 12 months of receiving
    voucher allocation. The Secretary of Housing and Urban
    Development may reallocate underutilized vouchers to higher-
    performing agencies after 18 months of noncompliance.

SEC. 5. PREVENTION-FIRST INVESTMENT.

(a) SSVF PREVENTION ALLOCATION.--Section 2044 of title 38, United
    States Code, is amended to require that not less than 55
    percent of SSVF program funds be allocated to homelessness
    prevention services.
(b) HOUSING STABILITY SCREENING.--The Secretary of Veterans Affairs
    shall integrate a standardized Housing Stability Screening
    tool into all VA primary care and mental health encounters,
    to identify veterans at risk of housing instability before
    crisis occurs.
(c) EMERGENCY FINANCIAL ASSISTANCE.--The Secretary of Veterans
    Affairs shall establish an Emergency Housing Assistance Fund
    providing up to $5,000 per eligible veteran household per
    year for rent arrears, utility payments, and security
    deposits.
(d) LEGAL SERVICES.--SSVF grantees shall provide or arrange for
    legal services to prevent eviction, including representation
    in eviction proceedings, lease dispute resolution, and fair
    housing complaints.

SEC. 6. VETERAN HOUSING EQUITY.

(a) OFFICE OF HOUSING EQUITY.--There is established within the
    Department of Veterans Affairs an Office of Housing Equity,
    which shall--
    (1) monitor racial, gender, and geographic disparities in all
        veteran housing programs;
    (2) publish annual disparity reports;
    (3) develop targeted interventions to reduce disparities; and
    (4) conduct racial equity impact assessments for all proposed
        changes to veteran housing policy.
(b) WOMEN VETERAN HOUSING.--The Secretary of Veterans Affairs
    shall--
    (1) fund not fewer than 5,000 gender-specific permanent
        supportive housing units over 5 years;
    (2) require all HUD-VASH and SSVF grantees to provide
        gender-responsive services; and
    (3) establish childcare-compatible housing options for women
        veterans with dependent children.
(c) TARGETED OUTREACH.--The Secretary shall fund community-based
    organizations in historically underserved communities to
    provide housing navigation and program enrollment assistance
    to minority veterans.

SEC. 7. NATIONAL FUNCTIONAL ZERO INITIATIVE.

(a) INITIATIVE.--The Secretary of Veterans Affairs, in coordination
    with the Secretary of Housing and Urban Development and the
    Executive Director of the United States Interagency Council on
    Homelessness, shall establish a National Veteran Functional
    Zero Initiative with the goal of achieving functional zero
    veteran homelessness in every community by 2033.
(b) FUNCTIONAL ZERO DEFINED.--For purposes of this section,
    "functional zero" means a community has the capacity to rapidly
    house any veteran who becomes homeless, the number of veterans
    experiencing homelessness at any point is fewer than the
    average monthly housing placement rate, and returns to
    homelessness are rare.
(c) COMMUNITY GRANTS.--The Secretary shall award competitive grants
    to Continuums of Care to support adoption of by-name lists,
    coordinated entry, and quality improvement methodologies.
(d) DATA INFRASTRUCTURE.--The Secretary shall build a real-time
    national veteran homelessness dashboard integrating VA, HUD,
    and Continuum of Care data systems.

SEC. 8. SOURCE-OF-INCOME DISCRIMINATION PROTECTION.

(a) PROHIBITION.--It shall be unlawful to refuse to sell, rent, or
    negotiate for the sale or rental of a dwelling, or to
    discriminate in the terms, conditions, or privileges of sale
    or rental, because a prospective buyer or tenant intends to
    use a federal housing voucher or other lawful source of
    income, including HUD-VASH vouchers, Housing Choice Vouchers,
    and VA-funded rental assistance.
(b) ENFORCEMENT.--Violations of this section shall be enforceable
    under title VIII of the Civil Rights Act of 1968 (Fair Housing
    Act), as administered by the Secretary of Housing and Urban
    Development.
(c) PENALTIES.--Civil penalties for violations shall be not less
    than $10,000 for a first offense and not less than $25,000
    for subsequent offenses.

SEC. 9. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There is authorized to be appropriated to carry out this Act--
(1) $2,000,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2031;
    and
(2) such sums as may be necessary for fiscal years 2032 through
    2036.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3** codifies Housing First as the required approach for all federally funded veteran housing programs, replacing the current policy-only status with statutory mandate. The fidelity standards ensure consistent implementation while the lease-requirement exception addresses legitimate housing management concerns.
- **Section 4** attacks the 24% HUD-VASH underutilization rate through landlord incentives, FMR reform, dedicated navigators, and utilization accountability. The reallocation provision creates consequences for persistent underperformance.
- **Section 5** shifts the funding balance toward prevention, which costs a fraction of crisis response, while creating early warning systems through clinical screening.
- **Section 6** addresses the stark racial and gender disparities in veteran housing through institutional structures, targeted programs, and accountability mechanisms.
- **Section 7** establishes the national infrastructure for achieving functional zero, building on the proven Built for Zero model.
- **Section 8** addresses a primary barrier to voucher utilization--landlord refusal to accept vouchers--through federal fair housing protection.

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Cost ($2B/year) | Represents cost savings: housing veterans costs $15-20K/yr vs. $40K/yr for homelessness |
| Source-of-income protection opposition from property owners | Landlord incentives (Sec. 4) sweeten the deal; protections mirror 17 existing state laws |
| Housing First resistance from treatment-first advocates | 20+ years of evidence; exception clause preserves reasonable lease standards |
| Fair housing enforcement capacity | Phased implementation; leverage existing HUD enforcement infrastructure |

**Refinements**:

- **Fallback position**: If comprehensive bill is infeasible, pursue HUD-VASH reform (Sec. 4) and prevention investment (Sec. 5) as standalone bills with the broadest bipartisan support
- **Pilot approach**: Source-of-income protections (Sec. 8) could begin with a 3-year pilot in 10 high-need metropolitan areas before national expansion
- **Sunset provision**: 10-year authorization with mandatory GAO evaluation at year 5

### Veteran Homeownership Improvement Act

**Purpose**: Reform the VA Home Loan Guaranty program to reduce costs for veteran homebuyers, improve competitiveness of VA-backed offers, and expand homeownership counseling.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Veteran Homeownership Improvement Act".

SEC. 2. FUNDING FEE REDUCTION.

(a) FIRST-TIME USERS.--Section 3729(b)(2) of title 38, United
    States Code, is amended to reduce the funding fee for first-
    time users of the VA Home Loan Guaranty with no down payment
    from 2.15 percent to 1.0 percent of the total loan amount.
(b) SUBSEQUENT USERS.--The funding fee for subsequent-use purchase
    loans with no down payment is reduced from 3.3 percent to 1.5
    percent.
(c) DOWN PAYMENT INCENTIVE.--The funding fee for users making a
    down payment of 5 percent or more is reduced to 0.5 percent.
(d) CASH-OUT REFINANCE.--The funding fee for cash-out refinance
    loans shall remain at the current rate of 3.3 percent for
    first-time users and 3.3 percent for subsequent users.
(e) REVENUE OFFSET.--The reduction in funding fee revenue shall be
    offset by general appropriations, recognizing that the VA Home
    Loan benefit is earned through military service and should not
    be self-funded at the expense of veteran purchasing power.
(f) EFFECTIVE DATE.--This section shall take effect 180 days after
    enactment.

SEC. 3. VA APPRAISAL MODERNIZATION.

(a) TIMELINE.--The Secretary shall ensure that VA appraisals are
    completed within 7 business days of order placement.
(b) AUTOMATED VALUATION.--For properties meeting criteria
    established by the Secretary (including prior recent sale,
    standard construction, and low-risk characteristics), the
    Secretary may accept automated valuation models in lieu of
    full appraisals.
(c) APPRAISER PANEL EXPANSION.--The Secretary shall expand the VA
    appraiser panel by 25 percent within 12 months of enactment,
    with priority recruitment in high-cost and high-volume markets.
(d) RAPID CLOSE PROGRAM.--The Secretary shall establish a VA Rapid
    Close program for pre-approved borrowers, targeting 21
    calendar days from contract to closing.

SEC. 4. HOMEOWNERSHIP COUNSELING.

(a) PRE-PURCHASE COUNSELING.--Each veteran applying for a VA-
    guaranteed home loan shall complete a homeownership counseling
    session provided by a HUD-approved counseling agency at no
    cost to the veteran, prior to loan closing.
(b) POST-PURCHASE SUPPORT.--The Secretary shall establish a post-
    purchase counseling program providing financial guidance to
    veteran homeowners for the first 3 years of homeownership.
(c) FORECLOSURE PREVENTION.--The Secretary shall establish a VA
    Foreclosure Prevention program providing:
    (1) a dedicated hotline for veterans facing foreclosure risk;
    (2) legal assistance for loan modification, forbearance, and
        foreclosure defense; and
    (3) emergency financial assistance of up to $10,000 for
        mortgage arrears.
(d) FINANCIAL LITERACY.--The Secretary, in coordination with the
    Secretary of Defense, shall integrate housing financial
    literacy into the Transition Assistance Program not later than
    12 months before projected separation date.

SEC. 5. ANTI-DISCRIMINATION.

(a) PROHIBITION.--It shall be unlawful for a seller of residential
    real estate to refuse to consider, or to impose additional
    conditions upon, a purchase offer solely because the offer
    involves financing guaranteed by the Department of Veterans
    Affairs.
(b) ENFORCEMENT.--Violations shall be enforceable through the
    Department of Housing and Urban Development and applicable
    state real estate licensing authorities.

SEC. 6. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There is authorized to be appropriated to carry out this Act
$1,500,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2036,
primarily to offset funding fee revenue reduction.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 2** reduces the effective tax on veteran homeownership. The funding fee was originally intended to make the program self-sustaining, but it now extracts billions from veteran homebuyers. The distinction between purchase and cash-out refinance reflects the different risk profiles.
- **Section 3** addresses seller reluctance by making VA loans competitive on timeline. The automated valuation provision reduces unnecessary delays for straightforward transactions.
- **Section 4** addresses the financial literacy gap that contributes to foreclosure risk and predatory refinancing. Pre-purchase counseling is proven to reduce foreclosure by 30-40%.
- **Section 5** prohibits the growing practice of sellers refusing VA-backed offers outright.

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Revenue offset ($1.0-1.2B annually) | VA loan benefit is earned through service; general fund offset is appropriate |
| Self-funding status loss | Program never truly cost taxpayers; framing as self-funded artificially constrained benefit |
| Seller anti-discrimination enforcement | Model on existing fair housing enforcement mechanisms |
| Mandatory counseling delays closings | Counseling can be completed during pre-approval process |

### Veteran Housing Transition Act

**Purpose**: Create a structured housing transition from military to civilian life, including a Transitional Housing Allowance and housing planning integrated into military separation.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Veteran Housing Transition Act".

SEC. 2. TRANSITIONAL HOUSING ALLOWANCE.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--The Secretary of Defense, in coordination with
    the Secretary of Veterans Affairs, shall establish a
    Transitional Housing Allowance (THA) for eligible separating
    service members.
(b) ELIGIBILITY.--A service member is eligible for THA if--
    (1) the member is separating from active duty with an
        honorable or general discharge;
    (2) the member has fewer than 3 months of housing expenses in
        liquid savings as certified by the member; and
    (3) the member has participated in housing transition planning
        under Section 3 of this Act.
(c) AMOUNT AND DURATION.--
    (1) For the first 3 months after separation, THA shall equal
        80 percent of the Basic Allowance for Housing rate for
        the member's planned civilian location, based on the
        member's pay grade at separation.
    (2) For months 4 through 6 after separation, THA shall equal
        50 percent of the applicable BAH rate.
(d) PERMISSIBLE USES.--THA may be used for rent payments, mortgage
    payments, security deposits, and utility deposits.
(e) RECOUPMENT.--THA payments shall not be subject to recoupment
    except in cases of fraud.

SEC. 3. HOUSING TRANSITION PLANNING.

(a) INTEGRATION INTO TAP.--The Secretary of Defense shall integrate
    a comprehensive housing transition module into the Transition
    Assistance Program, beginning not later than 12 months before
    projected separation.
(b) COMPONENTS.--The housing transition module shall include--
    (1) credit assessment and improvement guidance;
    (2) civilian housing market orientation for the member's
        planned location;
    (3) VA Home Loan education and pre-approval assistance;
    (4) rental market navigation, including tenant rights education;
    (5) budgeting for civilian housing costs; and
    (6) connection to VA housing programs (SSVF, HUD-VASH) for
        at-risk members.
(c) POST-SEPARATION CHECK-INS.--The Secretary of Veterans Affairs
    shall conduct housing stability check-ins at 3, 6, and 12
    months after separation for all veterans who participated in
    housing transition planning.

SEC. 4. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There is authorized to be appropriated $1,000,000,000 for each of
fiscal years 2027 through 2036 to carry out this Act.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 2** addresses the most preventable cause of early veteran housing instability: the abrupt loss of BAH at separation with no bridging support. The means test and planning requirement ensure fiscal responsibility while targeting assistance to those who need it.
- **Section 3** ensures that housing planning is not an afterthought but a core element of military transition, beginning a full year before separation.

## State Model Legislation

### Veteran Housing Protection Act (Model)

**Purpose**: State-level legislation to protect veteran housing voucher holders, support aging veterans, and facilitate supportive housing development.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Veteran Housing Protection
Act".

SECTION 2. SOURCE-OF-INCOME PROTECTION.

(a) It shall be unlawful for any person to refuse to sell, rent, or
    lease, or to otherwise deny or withhold any housing
    accommodation from any person because such person's source of
    income includes a federal, state, or local housing voucher,
    including HUD-VASH, Housing Choice Vouchers, or any other
    lawful rental assistance.
(b) A violation of this section shall constitute an unfair housing
    practice under [State Fair Housing Act citation].
(c) Remedies shall include actual damages, injunctive relief, and
    civil penalties of not less than $5,000 per violation.

SECTION 3. VETERAN PREFERENCE IN STATE HOUSING PROGRAMS.

(a) The [State Housing Finance Agency] shall establish a veteran
    preference of not less than 10 percent of units in any
    affordable housing development receiving state Low-Income
    Housing Tax Credits, state housing trust fund grants, or
    other state housing subsidies.
(b) The preference shall apply to veterans as defined in 38 U.S.C.
    Section 101(2) and their immediate family members.

SECTION 4. AGING VETERAN HOME MODIFICATION GRANTS.

(a) The [State Department of Veterans Affairs] shall establish a
    grant program providing up to $15,000 per eligible veteran
    for home accessibility modifications, including wheelchair
    ramps, grab bars, bathroom modifications, and doorway
    widening.
(b) Eligible veterans are those who are age 65 or older or who have
    a service-connected disability rated at 50 percent or higher.
(c) The state shall appropriate not less than $[amount] annually
    for this program.

SECTION 5. SUPPORTIVE HOUSING SITING.

(a) No municipality shall deny a permit for the construction or
    operation of supportive housing for veterans solely on the
    basis of the population to be served or the provision of
    supportive services.
(b) Supportive housing for veterans shall be permitted by right in
    any residential zone that permits multifamily housing.
(c) Any municipality that denies a permit for veteran supportive
    housing shall state in writing the specific, nondiscriminatory
    basis for the denial.
```

**Adaptations**: States with existing source-of-income protections (17 states + DC) may focus on veteran preference and supportive housing provisions. States with strong zoning home-rule traditions may need to modify Section 5 to provide incentives rather than mandates.

## Regulatory Framework

### HUD-VASH Administration Reform

**Existing Authority**: Annual HUD-VA appropriations and HUD PIH authority over Housing Choice Voucher administration.

**Proposed Regulation**:

HUD should promulgate a rule amending 24 C.F.R. Part 982 to:

1. **Require Small Area FMR usage for HUD-VASH**: Where available, Small Area FMRs should replace metro-wide FMRs for HUD-VASH to better reflect neighborhood-level rental markets
2. **Authorize exception payment standards**: Allow up to 120% of applicable FMR for HUD-VASH vouchers in markets where utilization falls below 80%
3. **Establish utilization benchmarks**: Housing authorities must achieve 85% utilization within 12 months and 90% within 24 months or face voucher reallocation
4. **Landlord payment timeline**: Mandate landlord payment within 5 business days of the first of each month for HUD-VASH units

### VA Home Loan Appraisal Standards

**Existing Authority**: 38 U.S.C. 3731 authorizes the Secretary to establish appraisal requirements.

**Proposed Administrative Action**:

The Secretary should:

1. Establish a 7-business-day maximum appraisal completion timeline
2. Expand automated valuation model acceptance for properties meeting low-risk criteria
3. Increase appraiser panel size by 25% through targeted recruitment
4. Establish a VA Rapid Close pilot program targeting 21-day closings

### GPD Program Realignment

**Existing Authority**: 38 U.S.C. 2011-2013 authorizes the Secretary to administer the Grant and Per Diem program.

**Proposed Administrative Action**:

The Secretary should:

1. Convert per diem payments from bed-night to outcome-based funding (housing placement rate)
2. Require all GPD programs to adopt Housing First principles within 18 months
3. Allow GPD funds to support permanent supportive housing operations where transitional housing is no longer needed
4. Prioritize GPD renewal funding for programs with highest permanent housing placement rates

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Source-of-income protection (Fair Housing Act expansion)**: Federal authority to prohibit source-of-income discrimination rests on the Commerce Clause, as housing rental and sale are commercial activities affecting interstate commerce. The Supreme Court has upheld expansive fair housing protections under this authority.
- **Funding fee reduction**: Congress has plenary authority over the VA Home Loan program under Article I and the Fourteenth Amendment. Revenue offset through general appropriations is a straightforward spending power exercise.
- **Transitional Housing Allowance**: Congress's authority to provide for military personnel (Article I, Section 8) and to spend for the general welfare supports a transition benefit.
- **Seller anti-discrimination**: Prohibiting discrimination against VA-backed offers raises novel questions, as the financing type is not a protected class under current fair housing law. Enforcement through real estate licensing boards may be more viable than Fair Housing Act integration.

### Preemption Questions

- **Source-of-income protections**: Federal law would preempt state laws that permit landlord discrimination against voucher holders. States with existing protections would not be affected. States without protections would need to comply.
- **Supportive housing siting (state model)**: State legislation preempting local zoning decisions on supportive housing may face challenges under state constitutional home-rule provisions. Incentive-based approaches may be more politically viable in strong home-rule states.

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- **HUD-VASH utilization**: Voucher reallocation for underperforming housing authorities creates direct financial consequences
- **Source-of-income protection**: Fair Housing Act enforcement infrastructure (HUD complaints, DOJ litigation, private right of action)
- **VA Home Loan reforms**: VA administrative authority; Congressional oversight through annual reporting
- **Housing First fidelity**: Grant renewal conditioned on fidelity assessment; technical assistance before termination

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Veteran Housing Stability Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Housing First exception abuse | Sec. 3(d) allows "reasonable lease requirements," which programs could interpret broadly to impose de facto treatment preconditions | High |
| Landlord incentive gaming | Landlords could accept signing bonuses, lease to veterans, then terminate leases after minimum period | Medium |
| FMR exception standard cap | 120% of FMR may still be insufficient in the highest-cost markets (San Francisco, New York, Boston) | Medium |
| Voucher reallocation timing | 18 months of noncompliance before reallocation gives underperforming agencies too long | Medium |
| Women veteran housing unit counting | VA could count mixed-gender units as "gender-specific" if they serve any women veterans | Medium |
| Functional zero definition gaming | Communities could manipulate by-name lists or housing placement counts to claim functional zero prematurely | Low-Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No housing supply mandate | Vouchers and subsidies are ineffective without available units; the bill does not directly increase housing supply | Federal government cannot mandate construction; supply is a state/local issue |
| Rural voucher limitations | HUD-VASH vouchers are useless in rural areas with no rental stock | Structural limitation of voucher-based programs |
| Case management workforce pipeline | Hiring 1,000+ case managers requires trained workforce that may not exist | Healthcare and social work workforce shortages |
| Voluntary Built for Zero participation | Communities can decline to participate in functional zero initiative | Federal government cannot mandate local government participation |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Housing First exception clause**: Add regulatory language defining "reasonable lease requirements" narrowly (rent payment, property maintenance, compliance with building codes) and explicitly prohibiting sobriety, treatment attendance, or curfew as lease conditions
2. **Landlord incentive protections**: Require minimum 12-month lease terms for incentive eligibility; prorate signing bonus with clawback for early termination by landlord
3. **FMR cap flexibility**: Authorize exception payment standards up to 150% of FMR in markets designated as "high-cost" by HUD (top decile by rent-to-income ratio)
4. **Reallocation timeline**: Reduce noncompliance period to 12 months; add interim corrective action requirements at 6 months
5. **Women veteran housing definition**: Define "gender-specific" as units with separate entrances, bathrooms, and common areas exclusively for women veterans and their children
6. **Functional zero verification**: Require independent third-party verification of functional zero claims using standardized methodology

### Veteran Homeownership Improvement Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Counseling requirement waiver | Veterans could seek waivers or minimal-compliance counseling, reducing effectiveness | Medium |
| Automated valuation abuse | Expansion of automated valuations could reduce appraisal accuracy and veteran protection | Medium |
| Seller anti-discrimination enforcement gap | Proving a seller refused an offer "solely because" of VA financing is difficult | High |
| Rapid Close pressure | 21-day close target could pressure veterans into inadequate due diligence | Low-Medium |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Counseling standards**: Require minimum counseling duration (3 hours) covering specific topics; prohibit online-only delivery for first-time buyers
2. **Automated valuation safeguards**: Limit automated valuations to properties with a recent comparable sale within 6 months and 5 miles; require appraiser review of all automated valuations above $500,000
3. **Seller discrimination**: Shift burden of proof in patterns-or-practice cases; require seller disclosure of all rejected offers in Fair Housing Act complaints
4. **Rapid Close protections**: Mandate a 3-day pre-closing review period regardless of timeline; require veterans to confirm understanding of all loan terms

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| VA-HUD coordination historically weak | Codify Joint Housing Office with statutory reporting requirements |
| Housing market conditions change rapidly | Require annual reassessment of FMR standards, funding levels, and program targets |
| Political cycle disruption | 10-year authorization provides continuity; key provisions should be mandatory spending |
| Data lag prevents real-time management | National dashboard (Sec. 7(d)) with real-time CoC integration |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- **Year 3**: GAO interim evaluation of HUD-VASH reforms and landlord incentive effectiveness
- **Year 5**: Comprehensive GAO evaluation of all programs; Secretary reports to Congress with recommendations for modification
- **Year 7**: Functional zero progress assessment; Congressional hearing on housing equity metrics
- **Year 10**: Authorization expires unless reauthorized; all programs subject to evaluation
