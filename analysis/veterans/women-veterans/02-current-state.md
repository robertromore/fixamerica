# Women Veterans: Current State

## Demographics

### Population and Growth

## Healthcare Access

### VA Enrollment and Utilization

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Women veterans enrolled in VA healthcare | ~700,000 | VA Office of Women's Health, 2024 |
| Share of eligible women enrolled | ~40% | VA, 2024 |
| Women using VA as primary care source | ~30% | VA, 2024 |
| Annual VA outpatient visits by women | 19.3 million | VA Office of Women's Health, FY2023 |
| Growth in women VA users (2010-2024) | +63% | VA Women's Health Services, 2024 |

**The utilization gap is significant.** While approximately 40% of eligible women veterans are enrolled in VA healthcare, many rely on private insurance, Medicaid, or go without care entirely. By comparison, roughly 50% of eligible male veterans are enrolled.

### Gender-Specific Care Availability

| Service | Availability at VA Facilities | Source |
|---------|-------------------------------|--------|
| Designated Women's Health Provider | 100% of VA medical centers | VA, 2024 |
| Comprehensive gynecological care | ~80% of VA medical centers | GAO, 2024 |
| Maternity care coordination | All VA medical centers (community referral) | VA, 2024 |
| Infertility treatment (IVF) | Limited; expanded under PACT Act | VA, 2024 |
| Breast cancer screening | All VA medical centers | VA, 2024 |
| Cervical cancer screening | All VA medical centers | VA, 2024 |
| Mental health with MST specialty | ~90% of VA facilities | VA, 2024 |

### Persistent Gaps

Despite improvements, critical gaps remain:

- **Privacy concerns**: Many VA facilities still lack private examination rooms, separate waiting areas, or women's restrooms near clinical areas
- **Provider shortages**: Gynecology and obstetrics specialists remain scarce in rural VA facilities
- **Community care coordination**: When VA refers women to community providers, coordination is often fragmented
- **Pharmacy limitations**: Some VA pharmacies do not stock the full range of contraceptive options
- **Prosthetics**: Prosthetic devices and durable medical equipment are often designed for male bodies

## Military Sexual Trauma

### Prevalence

MST remains a pervasive issue affecting women who serve:

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Women veterans screening positive for MST at VA | 1 in 3 (~33%) | VA MST Screening Data, 2024 |
| Men veterans screening positive for MST at VA | 1 in 50 (~2%) | VA MST Screening Data, 2024 |
| Estimated sexual assaults in military (FY2023) | ~29,000 | DoD Annual Report on Sexual Assault, 2024 |
| Reporting rate for sexual assaults | ~20% | DoD, 2024 |
| Reports resulting in prosecution | ~8% of reported cases | DoD, 2024 |
| Convictions from prosecuted cases | ~50% of prosecuted | DoD, 2024 |

### Treatment Access

- **Universal MST screening**: VA screens all veterans for MST; positive screens trigger referral
- **Free MST-related care**: No copay or enrollment requirement for MST-related treatment
- **Treatment types available**: Individual therapy, group therapy, PTSD residential programs, medication management
- **Wait times**: MST-related mental health appointments average 15-25 days (VA access data, 2024)
- **Specialty programs**: 17 MST-specific residential treatment programs nationwide

### Ongoing Challenges

- **Underreporting persists**: Cultural stigma, fear of retaliation, and distrust of chain of command
- **Prosecution reforms incomplete**: The Military Justice Improvement and Increasing Prevention Act (2021) moved prosecution decisions outside the chain of command for sexual offenses, but implementation has been gradual
- **Secondary effects**: MST is linked to higher rates of PTSD, depression, substance use, homelessness, and suicide
- **Male survivors underserved**: Men who experience MST face additional stigma and fewer targeted resources

## Homelessness

### Scope

Women veteran homelessness has distinct characteristics:

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Women veterans experiencing homelessness (2024) | ~5,700 (point-in-time) | HUD Annual Homeless Assessment Report, 2024 |
| Share of all homeless veterans who are women | ~10% | HUD, 2024 |
| Growth in women veteran homelessness (2020-2024) | +17% | HUD, 2024 |
| Women veterans with dependent children experiencing homelessness | ~60% of homeless women veterans | National Alliance to End Homelessness, 2024 |
| MST as contributing factor | ~50% of homeless women veterans report MST | VA National Center on Homelessness Among Veterans, 2023 |

### Risk Factors Specific to Women

- **Military Sexual Trauma**: MST-related PTSD and mental health conditions increase homelessness risk
- **Childcare responsibilities**: Women veterans are more likely to be single parents, complicating shelter access
- **Intimate Partner Violence**: IPV affects women veterans at rates 2-3 times higher than civilian women (Iverson et al., 2017)
- **Lower service-connected disability ratings**: Women receive lower average disability ratings, reducing income
- **Fewer women-specific shelters**: Most veteran shelters were designed for men; many lack accommodations for women and children

## Employment and Economic Security

### Employment Outcomes

| Metric | Women Veterans | Male Veterans | Source |
|--------|----------------|---------------|--------|
| Unemployment rate (2024) | 3.8% | 3.2% | Bureau of Labor Statistics, 2024 |
| Underemployment rate | 12.5% | 9.1% | BLS/Census, 2024 |
| Median earnings | $52,000 | $58,000 | American Community Survey, 2023 |
| Poverty rate | 9.4% | 6.2% | Census Bureau, 2023 |
| Using GI Bill education benefits | 48% | 45% | VA Education Service, 2024 |

### Contributing Factors

- **Military Occupational Specialty (MOS) transferability**: Women have historically been concentrated in support roles with less direct civilian equivalence
- **Credential gaps**: Military training and certifications often do not translate to civilian credentials
- **Employer bias**: Some employers associate "veteran" with male combat roles, failing to recognize women's service
- **Childcare gaps**: Transition programs rarely address childcare needs during job training
- **Networking disadvantages**: Veteran networking organizations remain predominantly male

## Childcare and Family Services

### Current State

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Women veterans with children under 18 | ~40% | Census/ACS, 2023 |
| Single-parent women veterans | ~25% of women veterans with children | Census, 2023 |
| VA facilities offering on-site childcare | ~50 medical centers (pilot program) | VA, 2024 |
| Childcare hours available during VA appointments | 2-hour maximum at participating sites | VA, 2024 |

### Gaps

- **Limited pilot scope**: On-site childcare is available at only a fraction of VA facilities
- **Hour restrictions**: Two-hour limits are insufficient for complex appointments or multiple services
- **No coverage during residential treatment**: Women needing inpatient MST or substance abuse treatment often cannot access care because of childcare responsibilities
- **Transition programs lack childcare**: TAP workshops and job training programs rarely offer childcare

## Recent Developments (2024-2025)

### Legislative and Policy Advances

- **PACT Act implementation**: Expanded healthcare eligibility for toxic-exposed veterans includes women who served near burn pits; IVF services expanded
- **Military Justice reforms**: Independent prosecution of sexual offenses continuing implementation under Office of Special Trial Counsel
- **VA Women's Health expansion**: VA announced goal of 100% designated Women's Health Providers at all primary care clinics by 2026
- **Childcare pilot expansion**: VA expanded on-site childcare from 29 to approximately 50 sites

### Ongoing Challenges

- **Facility upgrades**: Many older VA buildings still lack privacy accommodations for women
- **Rural access**: Women veterans in rural areas face the longest travel distances for gender-specific care
- **Data gaps**: VA still lacks comprehensive longitudinal data on women veterans' health outcomes
- **Culture change**: Institutional culture at VA and VSOs continues to default to male veteran norms

## The Gap Between Need and Response

| Need | Current Response | Gap |
|------|------------------|-----|
| Gender-specific primary care | Designated providers at all medical centers | Quality and availability vary widely |
| MST treatment | Universal screening, free care | Wait times, rural access, residential program capacity |
| Maternity care | Community referral model | Fragmented coordination, inconsistent quality |
| Homelessness prevention | Some women-specific programs | Insufficient capacity, especially for families |
| Childcare during care | Pilot program at ~50 sites | Covers fraction of facilities; limited hours |
| Employment support | General transition programs | Lack gender-specific components |
