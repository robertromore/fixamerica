# Women Veterans: History

## Origins

### Women's Military Service in Early America

Women have served in every American conflict, though their contributions were often unrecognized:

- **Revolutionary War (1775-1783)**: Women served as nurses, cooks, laundresses, and in rare cases disguised as male soldiers. Deborah Sampson served under the name Robert Shurtleff and was later granted a military pension.
- **Civil War (1861-1865)**: An estimated 400+ women disguised themselves as men to fight. Clara Barton and Dorothea Dix organized nursing corps. Mary Edwards Walker became the only woman to receive the Medal of Honor.
- **Spanish-American War (1898)**: Congress authorized the Army Nurse Corps in 1901 and the Navy Nurse Corps in 1908, the first formal military roles for women.

**Key limitation**: These women received no veteran status, no pensions (with rare exceptions), and no formal recognition for decades or centuries.

### World War I and Formalization

World War I marked the first large-scale formal enlistment of women:

- **33,000+ women served**: Nurses, telephone operators ("Hello Girls"), clerks, translators
- **Yeomanettes and Marinettes**: Navy and Marine Corps enlisted women for the first time
- **Army Nurse Corps**: ~21,500 nurses served, including overseas deployments
- **No veteran status**: Most women who served in WWI did not receive veteran benefits until decades later

**The "Hello Girls" case**: Army telephone operators in France were not recognized as veterans until 1979 -- more than 60 years after their service -- when Congress finally granted them veteran status. Most had already died.

### World War II: The Watershed

World War II transformed women's military participation:

| Organization | Established | Peak Strength | Notes |
|--------------|-------------|---------------|-------|
| Women's Army Auxiliary Corps (WAAC) | 1942 | 150,000 | Later became WAC with full military status |
| Women Accepted for Volunteer Emergency Service (WAVES) | 1942 | 100,000 | Navy women |
| Marine Corps Women's Reserve | 1943 | 23,000 | |
| Coast Guard SPARS | 1942 | 11,000 | |
| Women Airforce Service Pilots (WASP) | 1942 | 1,074 | Denied veteran status until 1977 |
| Army/Navy Nurse Corps | Expanded | 76,000+ | Served in combat zones |

**Total**: Over 350,000 women served in WWII.

**Critical milestone**: The Women's Armed Services Integration Act of 1948 (Pub. L. 80-625) gave women permanent status in the military, but imposed severe restrictions:
- Capped women at 2% of total force
- Barred women from combat
- Limited ranks (no general officers)
- Restricted specialties

### Korea and Vietnam: Incremental Change

**Korean War (1950-1953)**

- ~120,000 women served, primarily as nurses
- First women integrated into regular military units (not separate corps)
- Army Nurse Corps members served in field hospitals near combat

**Vietnam War (1961-1975)**

- ~11,000 women served in Vietnam; ~265,000 served during the Vietnam era
- ~7,500 women served as military nurses in Vietnam
- 8 military women killed in Vietnam, all nurses
- Women in Vietnam experienced combat exposure despite "non-combat" designations
- Vietnam Women's Memorial dedicated in 1993 -- 11 years after the men's memorial

**Post-Vietnam challenges**: Women Vietnam veterans faced unique struggles:
- PTSD unrecognized for nurses who treated catastrophic injuries
- "You weren't really in combat" dismissiveness
- VA system entirely oriented toward male infantry veterans
- Agent Orange exposure unrecognized for women

### The All-Volunteer Force and Expansion (1973-2000)

The end of the draft in 1973 fundamentally changed women's military participation:

**Key milestones**:

| Year | Milestone |
|------|-----------|
| 1973 | All-Volunteer Force begins; military needs women to fill ranks |
| 1976 | Service academies open to women (West Point, Annapolis, Air Force Academy) |
| 1978 | Women's Army Corps dissolved; women integrated into regular Army |
| 1988 | Department of Defense Risk Rule replaces combat exclusion, still restricting women |
| 1991 | 40,000+ women deploy to Persian Gulf War; combat exclusion debated |
| 1993 | Combat aircraft and combat ships opened to women |
| 1994 | Direct Ground Combat Definition and Assignment Rule: women excluded from units whose primary mission is direct ground combat |
| 2000 | USS Cole attack: women sailors killed and injured in combat conditions |

**The 1991 Gulf War effect**: The deployment of 40,000 women -- and the capture of Army Specialist Melissa Rathbun-Nealy as a POW -- shattered the fiction that women could be kept out of combat. Fifteen women died in the Gulf War.

### Post-9/11: Combat and Recognition (2001-Present)

The wars in Iraq and Afghanistan eliminated the practical distinction between "combat" and "non-combat" roles:

**Key developments**:

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2001-2021 | 300,000+ women deploy to Iraq and Afghanistan |
| 2005 | Sergeant Leigh Ann Hester becomes first woman to receive Silver Star for direct combat since WWII |
| 2010 | Female Engagement Teams (FETs) created for operations in Afghanistan |
| 2011 | Lioness teams and Cultural Support Teams in direct combat operations |
| 2013 | DoD lifts combat exclusion policy (Panetta directive) |
| 2015 | All combat positions officially open to women, including infantry, armor, and special operations |
| 2016 | First women graduate Army Ranger School |
| 2017 | First women complete Marine infantry training |
| 2020 | First woman completes Army Special Forces qualification |

**Casualties**: Over 170 women have been killed in post-9/11 combat operations; over 1,000 have been wounded in action (DoD Casualty Report, 2024).

## Policy Evolution

### VA Healthcare for Women

The VA's adaptation to women veterans has been slow and reactive:

| Year | Policy Change |
|------|---------------|
| 1992 | Veterans Health Care Act (Pub. L. 102-585) mandated sexual trauma counseling and gender-specific healthcare |
| 1994 | VA established Women Veterans Health Program |
| 2008 | VA required Designated Women's Health Providers at each medical center |
| 2010 | Caregivers and Veterans Omnibus Health Services Act expanded maternity care and newborn coverage |
| 2015 | VA launched comprehensive women's health redesign |
| 2022 | PACT Act expanded healthcare; IVF services added |
| 2024 | VA expanded reproductive health services following Executive Order |

### Military Sexual Trauma Policy

MST policy has evolved through sustained advocacy:

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 1991 | Tailhook scandal: 83 women assaulted at Navy convention; widespread coverup exposed |
| 1992 | Congress mandated VA provide MST counseling |
| 1994 | VA began universal MST screening |
| 2004 | DoD established Sexual Assault Prevention and Response Office (SAPRO) |
| 2005 | Restricted reporting option created (allows treatment without triggering investigation) |
| 2013 | Military Justice Improvement Act introduced (would remove prosecution from chain of command) |
| 2014 | Reforms to Uniform Code of Military Justice: Article 120 strengthened |
| 2021 | Independent Review Commission on Sexual Assault recommends removing prosecution from chain of command |
| 2021 | Military Justice Improvement and Increasing Prevention Act passed as part of NDAA |
| 2023 | Office of Special Trial Counsel established to independently prosecute sexual offenses |

### Women Veteran Homelessness Policy

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 2009 | HEARTH Act broadened homeless assistance |
| 2010 | Supportive Services for Veteran Families (SSVF) program launched |
| 2011 | VA began tracking women veteran homelessness separately |
| 2016 | VA expanded Grant and Per Diem program to include women-specific grants |
| 2020 | COVID-19 eviction moratorium temporarily reduced women veteran homelessness |
| 2023 | VA announced women-specific homeless prevention initiatives |

## Cultural and Recognition Milestones

### Overcoming Invisibility

Women veterans have fought not only in wars but for recognition of their service:

- **1997**: Women in Military Service for America Memorial dedicated at Arlington National Cemetery
- **2017**: VA changed its motto from "To care for him who shall have borne the battle" to "To fulfill President Lincoln's promise to care for those who have served in our nation's military and for their families, caregivers, and survivors" after sustained advocacy
- **2021**: VA updated clinical spaces to include imagery reflecting women veterans
- **2023**: VA launched campaign to encourage women veterans to self-identify and claim benefits

### Organizational Advocacy

Key advocacy organizations have driven policy change:

| Organization | Founded | Focus |
|--------------|---------|-------|
| Service Women's Action Network (SWAN) | 2007 | MST reform, policy advocacy |
| Women Veterans Interactive | 2009 | Transition, empowerment, community |
| Vietnam Women's Memorial Foundation | 1984 | Recognition, memorial |
| Disabled American Veterans Women Veterans Program | 2014 | Benefits, healthcare access |
| American Women Veterans | 2012 | Community, services, advocacy |

## Lessons from History

### Recurring Patterns

1. **Retroactive recognition**: Women's contributions are consistently recognized only decades after their service (WASP waited 35 years; Hello Girls waited 62 years)
2. **Policy lag**: VA and DoD policy changes trail actual women's military participation by years or decades
3. **Combat reality vs. policy fiction**: Women have been in combat in every war; formal policy only caught up in 2015
4. **Institutional resistance**: Every expansion of women's military roles faced institutional and cultural resistance
5. **Advocacy required**: Change has never been voluntary; it has required sustained advocacy, litigation, and legislative action

### What Changed and Why

| Change Driver | Examples |
|---------------|----------|
| Operational necessity | All-Volunteer Force needed women; asymmetric warfare put all troops in combat |
| Advocacy and litigation | Lawsuits challenging combat exclusion; SWAN's MST campaigns |
| Scandal and media pressure | Tailhook, Aberdeen, Vanessa Guillen case |
| Legislative action | Women's Armed Services Integration Act, MST counseling mandates |
| Demographic inevitability | Growing numbers made old systems unsustainable |
