# Women Veterans: Stakeholders

## Who Benefits from the Status Quo

### VA Institutional Inertia

**The VA Bureaucracy**

- ~400,000 employees accustomed to serving a predominantly male population
- Existing clinical protocols, facility designs, and workflows built around male patients
- Change requires retraining, facility renovation, and reorganization -- all costly and disruptive

**Specific Benefits of Status Quo**

- No costly facility renovations needed
- Existing clinical training and staffing models remain unchanged
- Performance metrics do not need revision
- Budgets do not need to account for gender-specific services

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Low-Moderate**

- VA leadership officially supports women's health expansion
- Resistance is passive (bureaucratic inertia) rather than active opposition
- Individual VA employees and facilities vary widely in their commitment to change

### Traditional Veterans Service Organizations

**American Legion, VFW, DAV**

- Historically male-dominated membership and leadership
- Post cultures can be unwelcoming to women
- Advocacy priorities historically focused on combat veterans (male-coded)
- Membership models built around social club traditions

**Specific Benefits of Status Quo**

- Existing membership and power structures remain unchanged
- Advocacy expertise is concentrated in traditional (male-oriented) issues
- Social culture of posts need not change

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Low**

- Most VSOs have officially embraced women's inclusion
- Resistance is cultural and local rather than policy-level
- DAV has been a leader in women veterans advocacy

## Who Is Harmed by the Status Quo

### Women Veterans (2.2 million)

**How They Are Harmed**

- Inadequate gender-specific healthcare
- Barriers to MST treatment and reporting
- Lower disability ratings and benefits utilization
- Cultural alienation from VA and veteran communities
- Higher rates of homelessness, poverty, and suicide relative to civilian women
- Childcare barriers to accessing services

**Demographic Profile**

| Characteristic | Detail |
|----------------|--------|
| Population | ~2.2 million (2024) |
| Age distribution | Bimodal: large cohorts of post-9/11 (20s-40s) and Vietnam/Cold War era (60s-80s) |
| Racial diversity | 44% non-white (more diverse than male veterans) |
| Geographic distribution | Concentrated in South, West, and near military bases |
| Family status | ~40% have children under 18; ~25% single parents |

**Level of Engagement**: **Growing**

- Younger women veterans are more likely to self-identify and seek benefits
- Advocacy organizations are expanding reach
- Social media has created new community spaces

### Women Currently Serving (~350,000 active duty)

**How They Are Harmed**

- MST risk during service
- Knowledge that VA may not meet their post-service needs
- Limited gender-specific care during active duty
- Career limitations despite end of combat exclusion (cultural barriers persist)
- Awareness of past failures affects willingness to serve

**Level of Engagement**: **Moderate**

- Active duty members have limited ability to publicly advocate
- Many are unaware of VA system deficiencies until transition

### Children of Women Veterans

**How They Are Harmed**

- When mothers cannot access mental health treatment, children suffer secondary trauma
- Homelessness among women veterans directly affects their children (~60% of homeless women veterans have dependent children)
- Childcare barriers prevent mothers from accessing VA care, leading to worse maternal health
- Children of MST survivors may experience intergenerational trauma effects

**Level of Engagement**: **None** (dependent population)

### Male MST Survivors

**How They Are Harmed**

- Discussion of MST is disproportionately framed as a women's issue
- Male MST survivors face additional stigma that discourages help-seeking
- Resources and programming targeted at MST often focus on women, underserving men
- ~38% of VA MST-positive screens are male (due to larger male population), but male-specific programming is limited

**Level of Engagement**: **Low** (stigma prevents visibility)

## Key Institutional Stakeholders

### Government Agencies

**Department of Veterans Affairs**

| Division | Role | Influence |
|----------|------|-----------|
| VA Office of Women's Health | Coordinates women-specific healthcare across VHA | Moderate (advisory role, limited budget authority) |
| Veterans Health Administration (VHA) | Delivers healthcare to 9+ million veterans | High |
| Veterans Benefits Administration (VBA) | Processes disability and benefit claims | High |
| VA Center for Women Veterans | Advocacy and awareness within VA | Low-Moderate (limited staff and budget) |
| VA National Center on Homelessness | Programs for homeless veterans | Moderate |

**Department of Defense**

| Division | Role | Influence |
|----------|------|-----------|
| Office of Special Trial Counsel | Independent prosecution of sexual offenses | High (new authority as of 2023) |
| SAPRO | Sexual assault prevention and response | Moderate |
| Transition Assistance Program (TAP) | Military-to-civilian transition | Moderate |
| Defense Advisory Committee on Women in the Services (DACOWITS) | Advises SecDef on women's military issues | Low-Moderate (advisory only) |

**Congress**

| Entity | Role | Influence |
|--------|------|-----------|
| Senate Veterans' Affairs Committee | Oversight, legislation | High |
| House Veterans' Affairs Committee | Oversight, legislation | High |
| Congressional Caucus for Women's Issues | Cross-cutting women's policy | Moderate |
| Individual champions (e.g., Senators Hirono, Ernst) | Legislation, hearings | Moderate |

### Advocacy Organizations

**Women-Veteran-Specific Organizations**

| Organization | Focus | Influence |
|--------------|-------|-----------|
| Service Women's Action Network (SWAN) | MST reform, policy advocacy | High (drove military justice reform) |
| Women Veterans Interactive | Transition, community, empowerment | Moderate |
| Final Salute Inc. | Housing for women veterans and their children | Moderate |
| American Women Veterans | Community, services | Low-Moderate |
| Grace After Fire | Mental health and peer support | Low-Moderate |

**Traditional VSOs with Women's Programs**

| Organization | Women's Initiative | Influence |
|--------------|-------------------|-----------|
| Disabled American Veterans (DAV) | Women Veterans Program (since 2014) | High |
| American Legion | Women Veterans Task Force | High |
| Veterans of Foreign Wars (VFW) | Women veterans outreach | High |
| Iraq and Afghanistan Veterans of America (IAVA) | She Who Borne the Battle campaign | Moderate |

**Research and Policy Organizations**

| Organization | Focus | Influence |
|--------------|-------|-----------|
| RAND Corporation | Military personnel research | Moderate-High |
| VA Women's Health Research Network | Clinical research | Moderate |
| National Center for PTSD | MST and trauma research | Moderate |
| Brookings Institution | Veteran policy | Moderate |

### Private Sector Stakeholders

**Healthcare Providers**

- Community healthcare providers who receive VA referrals for maternity, gynecology, and other gender-specific care
- Telehealth companies expanding access to rural women veterans
- Mental health practitioners specializing in trauma

**Employers**

- Companies with veteran hiring programs (often male-focused)
- Federal contractors required to hire veterans
- Small businesses owned by women veterans

**Technology Companies**

- Firms developing women-specific health apps for veterans
- Telehealth platforms targeting underserved veteran populations

## Power Analysis

### Who Has Power to Change the System

| Stakeholder | Type of Power | Likelihood of Using It |
|-------------|---------------|------------------------|
| Congress | Legislative authority | Moderate (bipartisan support exists) |
| VA Secretary | Administrative authority | Moderate (depends on priorities) |
| VA Office of Women's Health | Program authority within VHA | High (mission-aligned) |
| Large VSOs (DAV, AL, VFW) | Political influence, lobbying | Moderate (increasingly supportive) |
| SWAN and women's advocacy orgs | Public pressure, media attention | High (mission-driven) |
| Women veterans themselves | Voting, organizing, storytelling | Growing |
| DoD leadership | Policy authority for active duty | Moderate |

### Coalition Dynamics

**Pro-Reform Coalition**

- Women veteran advocacy organizations
- DAV Women Veterans Program
- IAVA
- Congressional champions (bipartisan)
- VA Office of Women's Health
- Academic researchers

**Ambivalent Stakeholders**

- VA bureaucracy (supports reform in principle, resists implementation costs)
- Large VSOs (officially supportive, culturally slow to change)
- DoD (supports reforms but prioritizes readiness)

**Potential Opposition**

- Budget hawks who oppose increased VA spending
- Social conservatives who oppose expanded reproductive health services
- Military traditionalists who resist gender integration
- VA employees resistant to practice changes

## Stakeholder Engagement Strategy

### Priority Engagement

| Stakeholder | Why Priority | Engagement Approach |
|-------------|-------------|---------------------|
| Congressional VA committees | Legislative authority | Testimony from women veterans, data briefings |
| VA Office of Women's Health | Implementation authority | Support expansion of authority and budget |
| DAV, IAVA | Political influence | Align advocacy priorities, joint campaigns |
| Women veteran communities | Authentic voice | Peer support, community organizing |

### Secondary Engagement

| Stakeholder | Why Important | Engagement Approach |
|-------------|---------------|---------------------|
| Local VA medical center directors | Facility-level implementation | Women's health advisory councils |
| Community healthcare providers | Deliver referred care | Training on military culture and MST |
| Employers | Employment outcomes | Women veteran hiring best practices |
| Media | Public awareness | Storytelling, data-driven reporting |
