# Women Veterans: Root Causes

## Structural Causes

### A System Designed for Men

The VA was established in 1930 and expanded dramatically after World War II to serve a veteran population that was 99%+ male. This foundational design persists:

**Infrastructure**

- VA medical facilities were built without women's restrooms near clinical areas, private examination rooms, or maternity wards
- Prosthetic and rehabilitation equipment was designed for male body types
- Residential treatment programs were designed for male-only cohorts
- Building renovations are slow and expensive -- many VA buildings date to the 1940s-1960s

**Clinical Practice**

- Provider training historically focused on conditions prevalent in male veterans (combat PTSD, Agent Orange exposure, traumatic brain injury)
- Gender-specific conditions (reproductive health, MST, postpartum depression) were afterthoughts
- Clinical guidelines were developed using predominantly male patient populations
- Research funding for women veterans' health has historically been minimal (Yano et al., 2011)

**Organizational Culture**

- VA staff accustomed to serving male patients may unconsciously signal that women do not belong
- Veterans Service Organizations (VSOs) like the American Legion and VFW were founded as male-only organizations; some posts still have unwelcoming cultures
- "Thank you for your service" is often directed at male companions rather than the woman veteran herself

### The Combat Exclusion Legacy

Although formally ended in 2013-2015, the legacy of combat exclusion policies continues to shape how women veterans are perceived and served:

**Disability Ratings**

- Women who served in positions officially classified as "non-combat" face higher evidentiary burdens for service-connected disability claims, even when they experienced combat conditions
- Average disability rating for women veterans (38%) is lower than for men (44%), partly because adjudicators undervalue non-combat-coded service (VA Annual Benefits Report, FY2023)
- MST-related PTSD claims require extensive documentation that combat PTSD claims do not

**Benefits Eligibility**

- Some benefits tied to "combat veteran" status may exclude women who served in combat zones but in technically non-combat MOSs prior to 2015
- Transition programs historically focused on combat arms career translation, poorly serving support MOS personnel

**Cultural Recognition**

- Persistent public perception that women "don't really serve in combat" despite 20+ years of evidence to the contrary
- Media portrayal of veterans remains overwhelmingly male and combat-focused
- Women veterans frequently report being asked "Were you really in the military?" or "Did you see combat?"

### Military Sexual Trauma as Systemic Failure

MST is not merely a collection of individual crimes. It is a systemic failure with structural roots:

**Chain of Command Conflict**

- Prior to 2021 reforms, sexual assault cases were investigated and prosecuted within the victim's chain of command
- Commanders had inherent conflicts of interest: prosecuting a subordinate could affect unit readiness, their career, and unit reputation
- Result: chronic underreporting and under-prosecution

**Culture of Impunity**

- Military culture values unit cohesion, obedience, and toughness -- values that can be weaponized against MST survivors
- Reporting is perceived as "breaking unit cohesion" or "not being tough enough"
- Retaliation against reporters is well-documented: 64% of women who reported sexual assault experienced retaliation (DoD SAPRO, 2023)
- Perpetrators often outrank victims, creating power imbalances

**Institutional Incentives**

- Commanders were historically evaluated partly on sexual assault statistics in their units, creating incentives to suppress reporting
- DoD's prevention programs have been criticized as performative "check the box" training (Independent Review Commission, 2021)
- Career consequences typically fall on the reporter, not the perpetrator

### Fragmented Healthcare Delivery

Women veterans' healthcare suffers from a delivery model that fragments their care:

**The Dual-System Problem**

- VA provides some services directly but refers others (maternity, complex gynecology) to community providers
- Community providers often lack understanding of military culture, MST, or combat-related conditions
- Coordination between VA and community providers is inconsistent
- Women must navigate two bureaucracies to receive comprehensive care

**Specialty Scarcity**

- VA has historically struggled to recruit gynecologists, obstetricians, and reproductive endocrinologists
- Rural VA facilities are least likely to have gender-specific specialists on staff
- Telehealth has expanded access but cannot replace all in-person care (pelvic exams, procedures)

**The Primary Care Gap**

- VA's Patient Aligned Care Team (PACT) model assigns women to designated Women's Health Providers, but these providers often have inadequate training in the full scope of women's health
- Many providers are generalists with limited women's health experience, designated by default
- The quality of gender-specific care varies enormously across facilities

## Cultural and Social Causes

### Veteran Identity Gatekeeping

Women veterans face a pervasive cultural problem: their veteran status is routinely questioned or denied:

**External Gatekeeping**

- Civilians assume "veteran" means male; women are asked to prove their service
- Retail and restaurant "military discounts" are sometimes denied to women
- VA staff and VSO personnel sometimes assume women in VA facilities are spouses, not veterans
- Social media veteran communities can be hostile or dismissive toward women

**Internal Identity Conflict**

- Some women who served do not identify as "veterans," particularly if they did not serve in combat
- The narrow cultural definition of "veteran" (male, infantry, combat) excludes many women's experiences
- This non-identification leads to not seeking earned benefits

**Institutional Reinforcement**

- VA imagery, messaging, and marketing historically centered male veterans
- VA's original motto ("To care for him who shall have borne the battle") explicitly excluded women
- VSO post cultures often remain male-dominated social clubs

### Intersectional Disadvantages

Women veterans face compounding disadvantages at the intersection of gender with other identities:

**Race and Ethnicity**

- Black women veterans face both racial and gender barriers at VA facilities
- Hispanic/Latina veterans may face language barriers on top of gender barriers
- Women of color veterans experience higher rates of MST, homelessness, and economic hardship (Lehavot et al., 2017)

**Sexual Orientation and Gender Identity**

- LGBTQ+ women veterans were forced to hide their identities under Don't Ask, Don't Tell (1993-2011) and prior outright bans
- "Bad paper" discharges for homosexuality denied benefits to many women
- Transgender women veterans face additional barriers in both military and VA contexts

**Disability**

- Women veterans with service-connected disabilities face the combined stigma of disability, female gender, and veteran status
- Disability claims for conditions associated with women (e.g., pelvic floor dysfunction from military training) are poorly understood by adjudicators

### Childcare as Structural Barrier

The absence of adequate childcare creates a structural barrier to accessing all other services:

- VA appointments, especially mental health sessions, require childcare arrangements
- Women veterans are disproportionately single parents (~25% of women veterans with children)
- Residential treatment programs historically did not accommodate patients with children
- Transition programs (TAP) do not provide childcare during multi-day workshops
- The VA childcare pilot (expanded to ~50 sites) covers only a fraction of need and limits sessions to 2 hours

## Economic Causes

### Lower Disability Ratings

Women veterans receive lower average service-connected disability ratings than men:

| Factor | Impact on Ratings |
|--------|-------------------|
| Combat exclusion legacy | Non-combat MOS reduces presumed exposure to hazards |
| MST documentation burden | Higher evidentiary standard than combat PTSD |
| Gender bias in adjudication | Studies show women receive lower ratings for same conditions (Murdoch et al., 2018) |
| Underreporting of injuries | Military culture discourages reporting; women fear being seen as weak |
| Conditions not recognized | Military-related conditions disproportionately affecting women (pelvic floor, reproductive) are under-recognized |

### Employment and Credential Gaps

- Military occupational training often does not translate to civilian credentials, and this gap is wider for MOSs where women were historically concentrated
- Employer perceptions of "veteran" often do not match women's military experience
- Resume gaps during deployments affect women's careers differently in female-dominated civilian fields
- Networking in veteran-specific employment programs often defaults to male-oriented industries (construction, law enforcement, logistics)

## Political and Institutional Causes

### Advocacy Fragmentation

- Women veterans' advocacy organizations are smaller and less politically powerful than traditional VSOs
- Large VSOs (American Legion, VFW, DAV) have only recently begun dedicated women veterans programming
- Congressional advocacy for women veterans, while bipartisan, is often a secondary priority within broader veteran legislation
- Media attention is episodic (triggered by scandals like Tailhook or the Vanessa Guillen case) rather than sustained

### Data Deficiencies

- The VA did not begin tracking women veterans as a distinct population in many data systems until the 2000s
- Longitudinal health outcomes data for women veterans remains thin
- Employment data often does not separate women veterans from civilian women
- Homelessness data for women veterans has only been disaggregated since 2011
- These data gaps make it harder to build evidence-based policy and secure funding

### Slow Institutional Change

- VA is one of the largest bureaucracies in the federal government (~400,000 employees)
- Culture change in large organizations takes years or decades
- Facility renovations to accommodate women require capital investment that competes with other VA priorities
- Provider recruitment and training pipelines take years to fill
- Leadership turnover at VA disrupts multi-year reform initiatives

## Reinforcing Feedback Loops

The root causes interact in self-reinforcing cycles:

```
Inadequate services --> Women avoid VA --> Low utilization data
--> Appears to justify limited investment --> Services remain inadequate
```

```
MST experience --> Distrust of institutions --> Avoidance of VA
--> Untreated conditions --> Worse health outcomes
--> Reinforces perception that VA "doesn't work" for women
```

```
No veteran identity --> No benefit claims --> No data on need
--> No policy response --> Women remain invisible as veterans
```
