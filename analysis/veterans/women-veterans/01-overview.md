# Women Veterans: Overview

## Executive Summary

Women are the fastest-growing segment of the U.S. veteran population, yet the Department of Veterans Affairs (VA) and the broader veterans support ecosystem were designed primarily for men. As of 2024, approximately 2.2 million women veterans comprise roughly 10% of the total veteran population, a share projected to reach 18% by 2040 (National Center for Veterans Analysis and Statistics, 2023). Despite their increasing numbers and expanding roles in the military, women veterans face persistent gaps in healthcare access, gender-specific services, recognition, and institutional responsiveness.

The challenges women veterans confront are both unique and compounding. Military Sexual Trauma (MST) affects an estimated 1 in 3 women who serve, creating lifelong health and psychological consequences. VA medical facilities often lack adequate gynecological and obstetric services. Homelessness among women veterans has grown even as overall veteran homelessness has declined. And cultural barriers -- from being questioned about their service to navigating male-dominated VA waiting rooms -- discourage many women from seeking the benefits they have earned.

Addressing these issues requires systemic reform: redesigning VA healthcare delivery, expanding gender-specific programming, improving data collection, strengthening accountability for military sexual trauma, and changing institutional culture to fully recognize women's military service.

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Military Sexual Trauma (MST)** | Sexual assault or repeated, threatening sexual harassment experienced during military service, as defined by 38 U.S.C. SS 1720D |
| **Women's Health Program** | VA program providing gender-specific primary care through designated Women's Health Providers |
| **PACT Act** | Promise to Address Comprehensive Toxics Act (2022), expanding healthcare and benefits for veterans exposed to toxic substances |
| **Women Veterans Call Center** | VA resource (1-855-VA-WOMEN) providing referrals and support for women veterans |
| **Maternity Care Coordination** | VA program coordinating prenatal and postpartum care through community partnerships |
| **Intimate Partner Violence (IPV)** | Physical, sexual, or psychological harm by a current or former partner, affecting women veterans at elevated rates |

## Key Questions

1. **Why do women veterans underutilize VA benefits?** What institutional, cultural, and practical barriers prevent women from accessing earned services?

2. **How adequate is VA healthcare for women?** Do facilities, providers, and programs meet the gender-specific health needs of women veterans?

3. **What is the scope of Military Sexual Trauma?** How prevalent is MST, how effectively is it addressed, and what reforms are needed?

4. **Why is women veteran homelessness rising?** What unique risk factors drive housing instability among women who have served?

5. **How can the VA evolve to serve a changing veteran population?** What structural, cultural, and programmatic changes are necessary as women become a larger share of veterans?

## Why This Matters

The failure to adequately serve women veterans represents both a moral obligation unmet and a practical crisis growing worse:

| Current Shortfall | Consequence |
|-------------------|-------------|
| Inadequate gender-specific healthcare | Women avoid or delay necessary medical care |
| MST treatment gaps | Lasting psychological harm goes unaddressed |
| Rising homelessness | Women veterans, often with children, face housing instability |
| Cultural alienation | Women feel unwelcome at VA facilities and VSOs |
| Benefits underutilization | Earned benefits go unclaimed |
| Employment challenges | Higher unemployment and underemployment than male peers |

**The demographic shift is irreversible.** Women's roles in the military have expanded dramatically since the end of combat exclusion policies in 2013 and 2015. The VA must adapt now or face a growing population it cannot adequately serve.

## The Opportunity

Reform is achievable because:

- **Bipartisan support exists**: Women veterans' issues attract cross-party legislative action
- **VA leadership has acknowledged the problem**: The VA has designated women's health as a priority area
- **Models exist**: Some VA medical centers have implemented exemplary women's health programs
- **Data is improving**: Better tracking enables evidence-based solutions
- **Advocacy is growing**: Organizations like the Service Women's Action Network (SWAN) and Women Veterans Interactive are raising visibility
