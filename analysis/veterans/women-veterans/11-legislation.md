# Women Veterans: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Legislative reform for women veterans spans healthcare delivery, military justice, benefits equity, and housing. The legislative landscape has evolved significantly since 2020, with major reforms to military sexual assault prosecution (2021 NDAA), expanded healthcare under the PACT Act (2022), and growing momentum for women-specific VA improvements. This document provides draft legal text for key reforms that address persistent gaps in the system.

## Constitutional Amendments

No constitutional amendments are required for women veteran reforms. The necessary changes can be accomplished through federal legislation, VA administrative action, and regulatory updates within existing constitutional authority under the Spending Clause (Art. I, SS 8, cl. 1) and the power to "raise and support Armies" (Art. I, SS 8, cl. 12).

## Federal Legislation

### Women Veterans Healthcare Improvement Act

**Purpose**: Mandate comprehensive gender-specific healthcare at all VA medical facilities and establish enforceable standards for women's health services across the Veterans Health Administration.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Women Veterans Healthcare
Improvement Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) Women veterans constitute approximately 10 percent of the
    total veteran population and are the fastest-growing
    demographic group among veterans.
(2) The Department of Veterans Affairs healthcare system was
    designed primarily for male patients and has not adequately
    adapted to serve women veterans.
(3) Women veterans underutilize VA healthcare services due to
    inadequate gender-specific care, privacy concerns, and
    cultural barriers.
(4) Military Sexual Trauma affects approximately 1 in 3 women
    veterans, creating lifelong health consequences requiring
    specialized treatment.

SEC. 3. COMPREHENSIVE WOMEN'S HEALTH SERVICES.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Not later than 3 years after the date of
    enactment, each VA medical center and community-based
    outpatient clinic shall provide, either directly or through
    formal community care agreements meeting quality standards
    established under subsection (c)—
    (1) gynecological care, including preventive screening;
    (2) maternity care coordination;
    (3) breast and cervical cancer screening;
    (4) contraceptive services and counseling;
    (5) mental health services with expertise in military
        sexual trauma;
    (6) pelvic floor and musculoskeletal care; and
    (7) menopause management.

(b) DESIGNATED WOMEN'S HEALTH PROVIDERS.—
    (1) Each VA medical center shall employ at least one
        Designated Women's Health Provider who has completed
        training requirements established by the Under Secretary
        for Health.
    (2) Training requirements shall include not fewer than
        80 hours of gender-specific clinical education and
        annual continuing education.
    (3) Designated Women's Health Providers shall not be
        assigned the designation solely by virtue of their
        primary care appointment.

(c) QUALITY STANDARDS.—The Under Secretary for Health shall
    establish quality standards for women's health services,
    including—
    (1) clinical outcome metrics disaggregated by gender;
    (2) patient experience measures specific to women veterans;
    (3) access standards for gender-specific appointments; and
    (4) community care provider qualifications for referred
        services.

SEC. 4. FACILITY PRIVACY AND INFRASTRUCTURE.

(a) ASSESSMENT.—Not later than 1 year after the date of
    enactment, the Secretary shall complete a privacy and
    infrastructure assessment of each VA medical facility
    to identify deficiencies in—
    (1) private examination rooms suitable for sensitive
        health services;
    (2) women's restroom facilities in clinical areas;
    (3) lactation rooms;
    (4) secure waiting areas; and
    (5) residential treatment accommodations for women.

(b) REMEDIATION.—Not later than 5 years after the date of
    enactment, the Secretary shall remediate all deficiencies
    identified under subsection (a).

(c) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There are authorized to
    be appropriated $2,000,000,000 for fiscal years 2027
    through 2031 for facility improvements under this section.

SEC. 5. MATERNITY CARE COORDINATION.

(a) COORDINATION PROGRAM.—The Secretary shall establish a
    standardized maternity care coordination program ensuring
    that women veterans receiving maternity care through
    community providers receive—
    (1) a designated VA maternity care coordinator;
    (2) integrated prenatal, delivery, and postpartum care;
    (3) mental health screening during and after pregnancy;
    (4) newborn care for not fewer than 7 days following
        delivery; and
    (5) postpartum follow-up for not fewer than 12 months.

(b) QUALITY REPORTING.—The Secretary shall report annually to
    Congress on maternity care outcomes for women veterans,
    including maternal morbidity and mortality rates.

SEC. 6. REPORTING.

Not later than 1 year after the date of enactment, and annually
thereafter, the Secretary shall submit to Congress a report on—
(a) women veterans' healthcare utilization rates;
(b) gender-disaggregated clinical outcomes;
(c) facility remediation progress; and
(d) patient satisfaction among women veterans.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Mandates specific services rather than aspirational goals; includes training standards for providers
- **Section 4**: Addresses the physical infrastructure gap that deters women from VA care; $2 billion authorization covers estimated renovation costs
- **Section 5**: Standardizes maternity care coordination, currently inconsistent across facilities
- **Section 6**: Creates accountability through mandatory reporting

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Cost ($2 billion) | Investment serves fastest-growing VA population; prevents higher downstream costs |
| Provider recruitment | VA loan repayment programs can be expanded; telehealth extends specialist reach |
| Community care coordination | Standardized program with quality metrics addresses current fragmentation |
| Implementation timeline | 3-5 year phased approach allows orderly implementation |

**Refinements**:

- **Stronger version**: Mandate in-house OB/GYN at all medical centers (not just community referral)
- **Fallback version**: Apply to 50 highest-volume VAMCs first; expand later
- **Technical addition**: Include telehealth platform requirements for rural access

### Veterans Childcare Act

**Purpose**: Provide free childcare for all veterans accessing VA services, removing the primary barrier that prevents women veterans from attending appointments.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Veterans Childcare Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) Approximately 25 percent of women veterans with children
    are single parents.
(2) Childcare barriers are the primary reason women veterans
    miss or delay VA healthcare appointments.
(3) The VA childcare pilot program has demonstrated that
    providing childcare increases appointment attendance by
    35 percent.

SEC. 3. CHILDCARE SERVICES.

(a) IN GENERAL.—The Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall provide
    childcare services to any veteran who is the primary
    caretaker of a child while the veteran is—
    (1) receiving healthcare services at a VA medical facility;
    (2) attending a mental health or substance abuse treatment
        appointment, including group therapy;
    (3) receiving residential or inpatient treatment;
    (4) attending a benefits claims appointment at a VA
        Regional Office;
    (5) participating in a Transition Assistance Program
        workshop; or
    (6) participating in a VA-sponsored employment training
        or education program.

(b) DURATION.—Childcare services shall be available for the
    full duration of the veteran's appointment or program,
    without time limitation.

(c) RESIDENTIAL TREATMENT.—For veterans receiving residential
    or inpatient treatment, the Secretary shall arrange
    childcare or dependent care for a period not to exceed
    90 days.

(d) METHODS OF PROVISION.—The Secretary may provide childcare
    services through—
    (1) on-site childcare facilities at VA medical centers
        and regional offices;
    (2) contracts with licensed childcare providers in the
        community; or
    (3) stipends to veterans for childcare expenses incurred
        while accessing VA services.

SEC. 4. STAFFING AND STANDARDS.

(a) STANDARDS.—Childcare provided under this section shall
    comply with applicable State and local licensing
    requirements.
(b) BACKGROUND CHECKS.—All childcare providers shall undergo
    background checks consistent with the requirements of the
    Child Care and Development Block Grant Act of 1990.

SEC. 5. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated $400,000,000 for each
of fiscal years 2027 through 2031 to carry out this Act.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Covers all VA service contexts, not just healthcare -- a significant expansion beyond the current pilot
- **Section 3(b)**: Eliminates the 2-hour limit in current pilot
- **Section 3(c)**: Addresses the critical gap for residential treatment -- women often cannot enter treatment because of childcare
- **Section 3(d)**: Flexible delivery methods allow adaptation to local conditions

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Annual cost ($400M) | Less than 0.15% of VA budget; demonstrated ROI through increased utilization |
| Liability | State licensing compliance and background checks mitigate risk |
| Facility space | Community provider contracts and stipends provide alternatives to on-site |

### Women Veterans Equity in Benefits Act

**Purpose**: Address gender disparities in disability claims adjudication and establish presumptive service connection for MST-related conditions.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Women Veterans Equity in
Benefits Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. PRESUMPTIVE SERVICE CONNECTION FOR MST-RELATED
         CONDITIONS.

(a) IN GENERAL.—For purposes of section 1110 of title 38,
    United States Code, post-traumatic stress disorder,
    depression, anxiety, and substance use disorder shall be
    considered to be service-connected in any case where—
    (1) the veteran has a current diagnosis of such condition;
    (2) the veteran has screened positive for military sexual
        trauma under section 1720D of title 38; and
    (3) a qualified mental health professional has determined
        that the condition is at least as likely as not related
        to the military sexual trauma.

(b) DOCUMENTATION.—No additional documentation beyond the
    requirements of subsection (a) shall be required to
    establish service connection under this section. The
    veteran's testimony regarding the occurrence of military
    sexual trauma shall be sufficient absent affirmative
    evidence to the contrary.

SEC. 3. GENDER EQUITY IN CLAIMS ADJUDICATION.

(a) TRAINING.—Not later than 180 days after the date of
    enactment, the Secretary shall implement mandatory
    training for all Veterans Benefits Administration
    adjudicators on—
    (1) gender-specific conditions related to military service,
        including reproductive health effects, pelvic floor
        dysfunction, and hormonal disruption;
    (2) the effects of military sexual trauma on long-term
        health; and
    (3) implicit bias in claims adjudication.

(b) AUDIT.—Not later than 1 year after the date of enactment,
    and annually thereafter, the Secretary shall conduct an
    audit of disability rating decisions to identify gender
    disparities in—
    (1) average disability ratings for comparable conditions;
    (2) denial rates for initial claims;
    (3) processing times; and
    (4) appeal outcomes.

(c) CORRECTIVE ACTION.—If the audit under subsection (b)
    identifies statistically significant gender disparities,
    the Secretary shall—
    (1) report findings to Congress within 30 days;
    (2) implement corrective measures within 180 days; and
    (3) re-audit within 1 year of corrective action.

SEC. 4. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 180 days after the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 2**: Establishes presumptive service connection, eliminating the burdensome stressor verification that disproportionately affects MST survivors
- **Section 2(b)**: Codifies that veteran testimony is sufficient, addressing the documentation gap unique to MST
- **Section 3**: Creates systematic accountability for gender equity in claims

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Fraudulent claims concern | Clinical diagnosis and MST screening provide medical gatekeeping |
| Cost of additional benefits | Correctly rating service-connected conditions is legally required regardless of cost |
| Adjudicator resistance | Mandatory training with accountability metrics |

## State Model Legislation

### Women Veteran Services Act (Model)

**Purpose**: Establish state-level coordination and services for women veterans, filling gaps not covered by federal programs.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Women Veteran
Services Act".

SECTION 2. WOMEN VETERAN COORDINATOR.

(a) The [State Department of Veterans Affairs/Military Affairs]
    shall appoint a Women Veteran Coordinator responsible for—
    (1) identifying and addressing the needs of women veterans
        in the State;
    (2) coordinating State services for women veterans;
    (3) serving as liaison to federal VA women's health programs;
    (4) conducting outreach to women veterans who may not
        self-identify or access services; and
    (5) submitting an annual report to the Governor and
        Legislature on the status of women veterans in the State.

SECTION 3. STATE WOMEN VETERAN SERVICES.

(a) The [State agency] shall provide or coordinate—
    (1) housing assistance for women veterans, including
        emergency shelter with capacity for dependent children;
    (2) employment and career counseling;
    (3) legal assistance for family law matters, benefits
        appeals, and protective orders; and
    (4) peer support networks.

SECTION 4. WOMEN VETERAN ADVISORY COUNCIL.

(a) There is established a Women Veteran Advisory Council
    consisting of not fewer than 9 members, including—
    (1) women veterans representing diverse service eras,
        branches, and backgrounds;
    (2) representatives of women veteran organizations; and
    (3) representatives of relevant State agencies.
(b) The Council shall advise the [State agency] on policies
    and programs affecting women veterans and shall meet not
    fewer than 4 times per year.

SECTION 5. APPROPRIATION.

There is appropriated from the general fund the sum of
$[amount] for fiscal year [year] to carry out this Act.
```

**Adaptations**:

| State Situation | Modification |
|-----------------|--------------|
| State with existing veteran services | Integrate women's coordinator into existing structure |
| State with limited veteran population | Combine with broader veteran services coordinator |
| State with strong university system | Add research partnership provision |
| State with rural challenges | Add mobile services and telehealth requirements |

## Regulatory Framework

### VA Women's Health Standards

**Existing Authority**: Under Secretary for Health has authority to establish clinical standards across VHA.

**Draft Regulation**:

```text
PART 17—WOMEN VETERANS HEALTH SERVICES

Sec. 17.400 Purpose.

This part establishes minimum standards for the delivery of
gender-specific healthcare services to women veterans across
the Veterans Health Administration.

Sec. 17.401 Definitions.

(a) "Comprehensive women's health services" means the full
    range of gender-specific healthcare, including but not
    limited to gynecology, maternity care coordination,
    breast and cervical cancer screening, contraceptive
    services, mental health with MST expertise, and
    menopause management.
(b) "Designated Women's Health Provider" means a primary care
    provider who has completed not fewer than 80 hours of
    women's health-specific training and maintains annual
    continuing education in women's health.

Sec. 17.402 Service requirements.

(a) Each VA medical center shall provide comprehensive
    women's health services either directly or through
    community care agreements.
(b) Each VA medical center shall employ at least one
    Designated Women's Health Provider per 500 enrolled
    women veterans.

Sec. 17.403 Privacy standards.

(a) Each VA medical facility shall provide—
    (1) private examination rooms for sensitive health services;
    (2) women's restroom facilities accessible from clinical
        areas; and
    (3) secure waiting areas for women's health appointments
        where feasible.

Sec. 17.404 Quality metrics.

(a) The Under Secretary for Health shall establish and monitor
    gender-disaggregated quality metrics, including—
    (1) appointment wait times for gender-specific services;
    (2) patient satisfaction among women veterans;
    (3) clinical outcomes by gender; and
    (4) community care coordination quality.
```

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Spending Clause**: Congress has broad authority to condition VA funding on compliance with women's health standards
- **Equal Protection**: Gender-specific healthcare addresses medical differences, not impermissible classifications; satisfies intermediate scrutiny
- **Due Process**: Presumptive service connection for MST-related conditions is consistent with existing presumptive frameworks (e.g., Agent Orange, Gulf War illness)

### Preemption Questions

| State Law | Federal Impact |
|-----------|----------------|
| State women veteran programs | Federal law supplements; no preemption |
| State childcare licensing | VA childcare must comply with state standards |
| State medical licensing | Community care providers must hold state licenses |

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Provision | Enforcement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Women Veterans Healthcare Improvement Act | Congressional oversight, GAO audits, annual reporting |
| Veterans Childcare Act | VA Inspector General, facility-level compliance reviews |
| Women Veterans Equity in Benefits Act | VBA audit requirements, Congressional reporting |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Women Veterans Healthcare Improvement Act

**Potential Loopholes**:

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| "Community care" escape valve | VA could meet requirements by referring all women's care externally, fragmenting service | High |
| Training quality variation | 80-hour training could be low quality or narrowly focused | Medium |
| Slow facility remediation | 5-year timeline could be exploited through intentional delay | High |
| Quality metrics gaming | Facilities could game metrics without improving actual care | Medium |

**Shortcomings**:

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address provider pay | Cannot recruit specialists if VA salaries uncompetitive | Separate pay reform needed |
| No enforcement penalty | Reporting requirement without consequences for non-compliance | Political difficulty of penalizing VA |
| Rural coverage gaps | Community care may not exist in rural areas for referral | Structural healthcare desert problem |

**Rectification Procedures**:

1. Add requirement that at least 50% of gender-specific services be provided directly (not referred)
2. Establish certification standards for the 80-hour training with independent accreditation
3. Add interim milestones with automatic escalation to Congressional oversight committees
4. Include patient-reported outcome measures that resist gaming
5. Create VA women's health specialist pay scale competitive with private sector
6. Mandate telehealth platform for gender-specific services to address rural gaps

### Veterans Childcare Act

**Potential Loopholes**:

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Capacity limitations | VA could technically comply by offering childcare with insufficient slots | High |
| Geographic limitations | Community providers may not exist near VA facilities in rural areas | Medium |
| Stipend inadequacy | Stipend amounts could be set too low to cover actual childcare costs | Medium |

**Shortcomings**:

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Only covers VA services | Veterans accessing community care referrals not covered | Scope limitation |
| No evening/weekend hours | Mental health crises do not follow business hours | Staffing constraints |
| 90-day residential limit | Some treatment programs exceed 90 days | Arbitrary cap |

**Rectification Procedures**:

1. Require childcare capacity sufficient for projected daily demand (not arbitrary slot numbers)
2. Include childcare for community care referral appointments
3. Tie stipend amounts to local market rates using CCDF rate surveys
4. Extend residential childcare to match treatment duration (remove 90-day cap)

### Women Veterans Equity in Benefits Act

**Potential Loopholes**:

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Audit without consequences | Identifying disparities without mandatory corrective action timelines | Medium |
| Training as check-the-box | Adjudicator training completed but not applied | Medium |
| "Affirmative evidence to the contrary" | Could be interpreted broadly to challenge veteran testimony | High |

**Shortcomings**:

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Limited to specific conditions | Only covers PTSD, depression, anxiety, SUD; not all MST-related conditions | Conservative drafting |
| Does not address appeals backlog | Women with denied claims still face long appeal timelines | Separate systemic issue |
| No retroactive review | Past underrated claims not automatically corrected | Administrative complexity |

**Rectification Procedures**:

1. Expand presumptive conditions list to include all mental health diagnoses related to MST
2. Define "affirmative evidence to the contrary" narrowly (e.g., service records showing the veteran was not present during claimed period)
3. Add automatic retrospective review for previously denied MST-related claims
4. Tie corrective action to specific timelines with escalation provisions

### General Implementation Concerns

- **Funding sustainability**: Authorizations expire; annual appropriations may fall short
- **VA capacity**: Implementing multiple reforms simultaneously may strain VA administrative capacity
- **Interagency coordination**: Women veteran homelessness requires HUD-VA-DOL coordination that is often weak
- **Data systems**: VA legacy IT systems may struggle with new gender-disaggregated reporting requirements
- **Culture change**: Legislation can mandate processes but cannot mandate genuine culture change
