# Prosthetics and Rehabilitation: Solutions

## Guiding Principles

### Criteria for Effective Reform

| Principle | Definition | Application |
|-----------|------------|-------------|
| **Technology access** | Veterans receive the most clinically appropriate prosthetic device regardless of cost | Formulary reform, expedited technology adoption |
| **Timeliness** | Prosthetic device delivery timelines approach commercial-sector standards | Procurement streamlining, workforce expansion |
| **Geographic equity** | Specialized rehabilitation is accessible regardless of veteran location | Telerehabilitation, hub-and-spoke models, community partnerships |
| **Lifetime commitment** | Funding and care models accommodate decades of prosthetic needs | Long-term obligation accounting, device replacement guarantees |
| **Innovation pipeline** | Research advances reach veteran patients within years, not decades | Streamlined formulary adoption, parallel VA-FDA review |
| **Seamless transition** | DOD-to-VA rehabilitation handoff involves no gap in care or device access | Joint transition programs, device portability |

### The Prosthetic Care Continuum

Effective veteran prosthetic policy must address the full continuum:

1. **Acute care**: Initial amputation or injury stabilization, surgical optimization for prosthetic fitting
2. **Rehabilitation**: Intensive inpatient or outpatient rehabilitation, prosthetic fitting and training
3. **Community reintegration**: Adaptive sports, vocational rehabilitation, home and vehicle modifications
4. **Lifetime maintenance**: Device replacement, component upgrades, ongoing rehabilitation as needs evolve
5. **Research and innovation**: Continuous improvement in prosthetic technology and rehabilitation techniques

## Policy Proposals

### Tier 1: Immediate Priorities

#### Procurement Streamlining

**What**: Reduce VA prosthetic device delivery timelines from the current 52-day average to 30 days or less for new prosthetic limbs, with an ultimate goal of matching commercial-sector timelines (14-28 days).

**How**:

- Establish a "prosthetic fast track" procurement pathway for FDA-approved devices already on the VA formulary, eliminating redundant funding authorization steps
- Authorize direct prosthetist ordering for formulary devices within established K-level criteria, without requiring separate administrative approval
- Pre-authorize funding for prosthetic device replacement at defined intervals (every 3-5 years for limb prosthetics) to eliminate re-authorization delays
- Implement electronic procurement workflows that reduce paper-based processing
- Create emergency procurement authority for prosthetic repairs, allowing same-day or next-day repair service

**Evidence**: Commercial prosthetic providers deliver new devices in 14-28 days using the same manufacturers and components; the difference is entirely attributable to VA administrative processes (VA PSAS Performance Data, 2024; GAO-23-105208, 2023).

**Cost**: Minimal net cost; procurement reform reallocates existing administrative resources. Potential savings from reduced temporary device costs and fewer emergency room visits for prosthetic failure.

**Barriers**: Federal Acquisition Regulation constraints; institutional resistance from procurement staff; risk aversion in authorizing prosthetist-level ordering

#### Formulary Modernization

**What**: Reduce the time from FDA approval to VA formulary availability from the current 1-3 years to 6 months or less.

**How**:

- Establish a "parallel review" process where VA Technology Assessment Program evaluation begins during the FDA review, not after approval
- Accept FDA approval as sufficient evidence of safety and efficacy for interim formulary listing, with VA-specific evaluation occurring post-listing
- Create a "provisional formulary" category allowing VA providers to prescribe newly FDA-approved devices at designated centers while full formulary review proceeds
- Hire additional Technology Assessment Program evaluators (from 12 to 30 FTEs) to reduce review backlogs
- Coordinate with DOD to accept joint technology evaluations, eliminating duplicative review

**Evidence**: The 1-3 year gap between FDA approval and VA formulary availability means veterans wait years for devices their civilian counterparts can access immediately. Osseointegration (FDA approved 2023) did not become available at VA centers until 2024; the DEKA/LUKE Arm (FDA approved 2014) took years to reach VA patients (VA Technology Assessment Program Reports, 2023-2024).

**Cost**: Estimated $15-20 million annually for expanded Technology Assessment Program staffing and parallel review infrastructure

**Barriers**: Institutional culture favoring conservative evaluation; concern about approving devices without VA-specific evidence; potential industry resistance to provisional formulary terms

#### Workforce Recruitment and Retention

**What**: Close the 18% prosthetist vacancy rate and reduce vacancy rates across rehabilitation disciplines to below 10%.

**How**:

- Increase VA certified prosthetist/orthotist (CPO) salaries to match or exceed the private-sector average ($105,000), closing the current 12% pay gap
- Establish a VA Prosthetics Scholarship Program covering tuition and living expenses for students in accredited prosthetics/orthotics programs in exchange for 4-year VA service commitments
- Create a Prosthetics Education Debt Reduction Program providing up to $100,000 in student loan repayment for VA-employed CPOs
- Fund 5 new VA-affiliated prosthetics residency programs at VA Regional Amputation Centers
- Expand rehabilitation specialist (physiatrist, recreation therapist, rehabilitation engineer) recruitment with targeted bonuses for hard-to-fill positions
- Establish a VA Prosthetics Technician career pathway for veterans, providing accelerated training for veterans with relevant military experience

**Evidence**: The national prosthetist shortage (8,200 professionals, 450 new certifications annually, 17% projected demand growth) cannot be solved by VA alone, but VA must compete effectively for its share. VA salaries 12% below market are the primary barrier to recruitment (BLS, 2024; ABC, 2024).

**Cost**: Estimated $80-120 million annually for salary increases, scholarships, and recruitment programs

**Barriers**: Federal pay scale constraints; competition with private sector; limited training program capacity nationally

### Tier 2: Medium-Term Reforms

#### Hub-and-Spoke Rehabilitation Network

**What**: Extend specialized prosthetic and rehabilitation expertise from the 5 Polytrauma Rehabilitation Centers and 7 Regional Amputation Centers to every veteran through a structured hub-and-spoke model.

**How**:

- Designate each PRC and RAC as a "hub" with responsibility for a defined geographic region
- Establish satellite "spoke" clinics at 50 additional VA Medical Centers, staffed by hub-trained providers on rotating assignments
- Deploy telerehabilitation platforms connecting spoke-site veterans with hub-site specialists for prosthetic adjustment, rehabilitation guidance, and follow-up care
- Create a Prosthetic Outreach Team program, sending mobile prosthetic teams from hubs to underserved VA facilities on scheduled rotations
- Fund hub-to-spoke provider training rotations ensuring all spoke-site prosthetists maintain specialized competency

**Evidence**: Geographic concentration of specialized care means most veterans live hours from the nearest PRC or RAC. Hub-and-spoke models have demonstrated effectiveness in other VA specialty care areas, including spinal cord injury and transplant surgery (VA SCI/D System of Care model; VA National Surgery Office).

**Cost**: Estimated $200-300 million annually for satellite clinics, telerehabilitation infrastructure, mobile teams, and provider rotations

**Barriers**: Capital costs for satellite clinic equipment; prosthetist staffing requires resolving workforce shortages first; telerehabilitation cannot fully replace in-person fitting and adjustment

#### 3D Printing and Digital Fabrication Expansion

**What**: Scale the current VA 3D-printed prosthetic socket pilot program from 5 centers to all Regional Amputation Centers and Polytrauma Rehabilitation Centers, with a goal of system-wide deployment.

**How**:

- Expand 3D printing capacity at all 7 RACs and 5 PRCs within 2 years
- Establish VA-wide digital scanning and CAD/CAM protocols for prosthetic socket design
- Train 200 VA prosthetists in digital socket design and 3D printing fabrication
- Partner with VA Innovation Ecosystem (VHA IE) to develop proprietary 3D-printed socket designs optimized for veteran populations
- Create a "virtual fitting" capability combining 3D scanning, telerehabilitation, and shipped printed sockets for veterans in remote locations

**Evidence**: VA's 5-center pilot has demonstrated that 3D-printed sockets reduce fabrication time from weeks to days and improve socket fit through digital precision. Commercial 3D prosthetic fabrication is growing rapidly (Zuniga et al., *PLOS One*, 2015; VA 3D Printing Network pilot data, 2024).

**Cost**: Estimated $50-80 million for expansion (equipment, training, facility modification)

**Barriers**: Regulatory uncertainty about 3D-printed medical devices; limited evidence for long-term durability; resistance from traditional prosthetic fabrication providers

#### DOD-VA Transition Improvement

**What**: Eliminate the care gap experienced by 21% of veterans transitioning from DOD to VA rehabilitation, and reduce average transition time from 11 months to 6 months or less.

**How**:

- Establish mandatory joint DOD-VA rehabilitation planning beginning 12 months before medical separation
- Create device portability agreements ensuring that prosthetic devices fitted at DOD facilities are maintained and replaced by VA without refitting
- Assign VA Prosthetic Liaison Officers at Walter Reed and SAMMC (the two primary military amputee rehabilitation centers) to manage transition in real time
- Implement a shared electronic prosthetic record accessible to both DOD and VA providers
- Reduce Federal Recovery Coordination Program caseloads from 35 to 25 per coordinator, as recommended by GAO

**Evidence**: 21% of transitioning wounded warriors report care gaps, and transition satisfaction is only 68%. Federal Recovery Coordinator caseloads exceed recommended levels (GAO-23-105208, 2023; VA Wounded Warrior Survey, 2024).

**Cost**: Estimated $60-80 million annually for liaison officers, shared records, and coordinator hiring

**Barriers**: DOD-VA institutional boundaries; different electronic health record systems; budget disputes over which department funds transition services

### Tier 3: Systemic Reforms

#### Lifetime Prosthetic Care Obligation Model

**What**: Replace annual budget-cycle prosthetics funding with a long-term obligation model that actuarially accounts for the lifetime prosthetic care costs of each veteran.

**How**:

- Establish a VA Prosthetic Care Trust Fund, similar to military retirement trust funds, that receives annual appropriations based on actuarial projections of lifetime prosthetic costs
- For each newly enrolled veteran amputee, calculate the projected lifetime prosthetic cost and reserve funds accordingly
- Allow the Trust Fund to carry balances across fiscal years, eliminating the "use it or lose it" dynamic that distorts annual purchasing decisions
- Require annual actuarial reports projecting future prosthetic obligations, submitted to Congress with the VA budget request
- Guarantee that veterans' prosthetic care is not subject to annual appropriations volatility

**Evidence**: A 25-year-old bilateral above-knee amputee has estimated lifetime prosthetic costs of $4.8 million. Current annual appropriations create uncertainty about whether future Congresses will fund these obligations. The military retirement trust fund model provides a proven template for long-term obligation management (VA Health Economics Resource Center, 2023; DOD Actuarial Reports).

**Cost**: Initial capitalization of $5-10 billion; subsequent annual contributions of $1-2 billion based on actuarial projections. Net present value may be similar to current spending trajectory but provides certainty and removes annual budget risk.

**Barriers**: Requires significant initial appropriation; competes with other federal priorities; Congress may resist binding future Congresses to specific spending levels; accounting complexity

#### Research-to-Care Pipeline Acceleration

**What**: Reduce the total time from prosthetic research breakthrough to veteran patient availability from the current 7-14 years to 3-5 years.

**How**:

- Establish a VA Prosthetics Breakthrough Therapy designation (modeled on FDA's Breakthrough Therapy pathway) that provides accelerated formulary review for devices that demonstrate substantial improvement over existing alternatives
- Create a VA Prosthetics Innovation Center that operates at the intersection of research and clinical care, with authority to deploy experimental devices in clinical settings under expanded access protocols
- Fund VA participation in FDA clinical trials for prosthetic devices, allowing VA researchers to generate the evidence needed for both FDA approval and VA formulary simultaneously
- Establish a technology transfer office specifically for VA prosthetic research, managing intellectual property and industry partnerships
- Co-fund research programs with DARPA and NIH that include VA clinical deployment milestones as grant conditions

**Evidence**: The current 7-14 year pipeline means that technologies developed to serve post-9/11 wounded warriors may not reach VA patients until the 2030s. The FDA's Breakthrough Therapy pathway has demonstrated that accelerated review can maintain safety while dramatically reducing approval timelines (FDA Breakthrough Therapy Designations, 2013-2024; VA RR&D Program Data).

**Cost**: Estimated $50-100 million annually for accelerated review infrastructure, clinical trial participation, and technology transfer operations

**Barriers**: FDA regulatory requirements are external to VA; research-to-clinical cultural divide within VA; intellectual property complexities; risk of deploying inadequately tested devices

#### Adaptive Sports and Recreation Expansion

**What**: Ensure every veteran with a physical disability has access to adaptive sports and recreation programs within 90 minutes of travel.

**How**:

- Expand VA Adaptive Sports Program funding from $18 million to $50 million annually
- Establish community-based adaptive sports partnerships in 200 additional communities beyond current VA program reach
- Create a VA Adaptive Equipment Bank that loans specialized sports equipment (handcycles, sit-skis, racing wheelchairs) to veterans
- Fund Paralympic sport pipeline development in partnership with the U.S. Olympic and Paralympic Committee
- Integrate adaptive recreation prescriptions into rehabilitation treatment plans as a standard of care

**Evidence**: Adaptive sports demonstrate significant benefits for physical rehabilitation, mental health, and social integration. Veterans participating in adaptive sports programs report 40% lower depression scores and 35% higher community reintegration compared to non-participants (Sporner et al., *Disability and Rehabilitation*, 2009; Hawkins et al., *Therapeutic Recreation Journal*, 2014).

**Cost**: Estimated $30-50 million annually for expanded programs and community partnerships

**Barriers**: Geographic access in rural areas; limited adaptive sports infrastructure nationally; volunteer coaching capacity
