# Prosthetics and Rehabilitation: Overview

## Executive Summary

The Department of Veterans Affairs operates the largest prosthetic and rehabilitative care program in the United States, serving approximately 3.2 million veterans who receive prosthetic devices, sensory aids, or rehabilitative services annually at a cost of $4.8 billion (FY 2024) (VA Prosthetic and Sensory Aids Service, 2024). The program encompasses far more than limb prosthetics--it includes wheelchairs, hearing aids, visual aids, orthotics, home modifications, assistive technology, and comprehensive rehabilitation services for polytrauma, traumatic brain injury (TBI), spinal cord injury (SCI), and other conditions.

Two decades of post-9/11 conflicts have transformed the landscape of veteran prosthetic needs. Advances in body armor and battlefield medicine have dramatically improved survival rates from injuries that would have been fatal in prior conflicts, creating a population of severely wounded veterans with complex, lifelong rehabilitation needs. Approximately 1,650 post-9/11 service members have experienced major limb amputations (Department of Defense Trauma Registry, 2024), and tens of thousands more live with polytrauma combinations of TBI, burns, blast injuries, and musculoskeletal damage.

The VA prosthetics program has been a pioneer in many areas--its research has driven advances in microprocessor-controlled knees, myoelectric upper-limb prostheses, and osseointegration techniques. However, the system faces mounting challenges: procurement bureaucracy that delays device delivery by months, geographic disparities in access to specialized rehabilitation centers, a shortage of trained prosthetists and rehabilitation specialists, an aging Vietnam-era veteran population with growing limb loss from vascular disease and diabetes, and a technology landscape where the most advanced prosthetic devices cost $100,000-$200,000 each and require ongoing maintenance and component replacement.

## Scope

### What This Subtopic Covers

- **Prosthetic devices**: Limb prosthetics (upper and lower extremity), orthotic devices, microprocessor and myoelectric components
- **Sensory aids**: Hearing aids, cochlear implants, visual aids, blind rehabilitation
- **Mobility devices**: Wheelchairs (manual and power), scooters, adaptive equipment
- **Rehabilitation services**: Physical therapy, occupational therapy, speech-language pathology, recreation therapy
- **Polytrauma care**: Multi-system injury rehabilitation, TBI treatment, burn care
- **Spinal cord injury**: SCI/D (spinal cord injury and disorders) care system
- **Adaptive sports and recreation**: Therapeutic recreation, Paralympic programs, adaptive equipment
- **Assistive technology**: Home modifications, vehicle modifications, computer access aids
- **Research and innovation**: VA prosthetic research programs, technology transfer

## Core Tensions

- **Technology access vs. cost**: The most advanced prosthetic devices offer dramatically better function and quality of life but cost 5-10x more than basic alternatives, creating formulary and budget pressure
- **Centralized expertise vs. geographic access**: The best prosthetic and rehabilitation care is concentrated at a few specialized centers; most veterans live far from these centers
- **Lifetime care obligation vs. budget cycles**: Prosthetic devices require replacement every 3-5 years, component maintenance, and evolving care needs over decades, but funding is appropriated annually
- **VA procurement vs. commercial innovation**: VA's procurement process can take months to approve new devices that are available commercially in weeks
- **Standardization vs. individualization**: Prosthetic care is inherently individualized, but VA systems favor standardized formularies and procurement processes
- **Military-to-VA transition**: The handoff from DOD military treatment facilities to VA rehabilitation care creates gaps in continuity

## Key Questions

1. **Is the VA providing veterans with the most advanced prosthetic technology available?** What barriers prevent access to cutting-edge devices?
2. **How does geographic access to specialized rehabilitation affect outcomes?** Can telerehabilitation and community partnerships close the gap?
3. **What is the lifetime cost of prosthetic and rehabilitation care for severely wounded veterans?** Is the current funding model adequate?
4. **How should the VA balance its role as a direct provider of prosthetic care with community care referrals?** Where does VA expertise add the most value?
5. **What research investments would most improve veteran quality of life?** Brain-computer interfaces, advanced myoelectrics, regenerative medicine, or osseointegration?

## Vision of Success

A successful veteran prosthetics and rehabilitation system would provide every eligible veteran with the most appropriate prosthetic device--including the most technologically advanced options when clinically indicated--within 30 days of prescription. Specialized rehabilitation services would be accessible within 90 minutes of travel or via high-quality telerehabilitation. Every veteran amputee would have access to a certified prosthetist with military and veteran experience. The VA would lead the world in prosthetic research and innovation, with technology transfer pathways that rapidly move laboratory advances to patient care. Adaptive sports and recreation programs would be available to every veteran with a physical disability. The transition from DOD to VA rehabilitation would be seamless, with no gap in care or device access. The VA prosthetics budget would be structured to accommodate lifetime care obligations rather than annual budget cycles.
