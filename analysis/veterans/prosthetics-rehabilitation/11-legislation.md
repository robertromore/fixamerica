# Prosthetics and Rehabilitation: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Legislative reform for veteran prosthetics and rehabilitation requires action at three levels: federal legislation to modernize VA procurement, accelerate technology adoption, and fund workforce development; state legislation to ensure prosthetic parity in insurance coverage and support adaptive recreation; and regulatory changes to streamline the VA formulary process and improve community care quality oversight. This document provides draft legal text for key reforms identified in [Solutions](07-solutions.md), organized by level of government and likelihood of enactment.

## Federal Legislation

### Veterans Prosthetic Care Modernization Act

**Purpose**: Streamline VA prosthetic device procurement, accelerate technology adoption through formulary modernization, and establish delivery timelines that approach commercial-sector standards.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Veterans Prosthetic Care
Modernization Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) The Department of Veterans Affairs serves approximately
3,200,000 veterans with prosthetic devices and services
annually at a cost of $4,800,000,000.
(2) The average time from prosthetic limb prescription to
delivery within the VA system is 52 days, compared to 14 to
28 days in the commercial sector.
(3) New prosthetic technologies approved by the Food and Drug
Administration take an additional 1 to 3 years to become
available through the VA National Prosthetics Formulary.
(4) The VA certified prosthetist vacancy rate stands at
18 percent, with VA salaries approximately 12 percent below
private-sector equivalents.
(5) Advanced prosthetic devices, including microprocessor-
controlled knees and myoelectric upper-limb prostheses,
provide documented clinical benefits including reduced falls,
improved mobility, and greater community participation.

SEC. 3. PROSTHETIC DEVICE DELIVERY TIMELINES.

(a) IN GENERAL.--The Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall
ensure that prosthetic devices prescribed to eligible veterans
are delivered within the following timelines:

(1) PROSTHETIC LIMBS.--Not later than 30 calendar days
after the date of prescription for a new prosthetic limb.

(2) WHEELCHAIRS AND MOBILITY DEVICES.--Not later than
21 calendar days after the date of prescription for a
wheelchair or powered mobility device.

(3) HEARING AIDS.--Not later than 14 calendar days after
the date of prescription for hearing aids.

(4) PROSTHETIC REPAIRS.--Not later than 48 hours after
the request for emergency prosthetic device repair.

(5) HOME MODIFICATIONS.--Not later than 90 calendar days
after the date of approval for home modification projects.

(b) REPORTING.--The Secretary shall report to the Committees
on Veterans' Affairs of the Senate and House of Representatives,
on a quarterly basis, compliance rates with the timelines
established under subsection (a), disaggregated by Veterans
Integrated Service Network and facility.

(c) WAIVER.--The timelines under subsection (a) may be
extended by not more than 15 calendar days if the veteran's
clinical needs require custom fabrication or fitting that
cannot be completed within the standard timeline, provided
that the veteran is notified of the extension and the reason
therefor.

SEC. 4. FORMULARY MODERNIZATION.

(a) PARALLEL REVIEW.--The Secretary shall establish a
parallel review process under which the VA Technology
Assessment Program begins evaluation of prosthetic devices
during the device's review by the Food and Drug Administration,
rather than after such approval.

(b) PROVISIONAL FORMULARY LISTING.--Not later than 180 days
after the date on which a prosthetic device receives approval
or clearance from the Food and Drug Administration, the
Secretary shall--

(1) add such device to the National Prosthetics Formulary
on a provisional basis, available at designated VA centers
of excellence; or

(2) issue a written determination, made publicly available,
explaining the clinical or safety basis for declining
provisional listing.

(c) BREAKTHROUGH DESIGNATION.--The Secretary shall establish
a VA Prosthetics Breakthrough Therapy designation for devices
that demonstrate substantial improvement over existing
alternatives, providing--

(1) accelerated Technology Assessment Program review
completed within 90 days of FDA approval;
(2) immediate provisional formulary listing at all
Regional Amputation Centers and Polytrauma Rehabilitation
Centers; and
(3) expedited system-wide formulary listing within
12 months.

(d) TECHNOLOGY ASSESSMENT PROGRAM STAFFING.--The Secretary
shall increase the number of full-time evaluators in the VA
Technology Assessment Program from 12 to not fewer than 30
within 2 years of enactment.

SEC. 5. PROCUREMENT STREAMLINING.

(a) DIRECT ORDERING AUTHORITY.--VA certified prosthetists
and orthotists shall have authority to order prosthetic
devices listed on the National Prosthetics Formulary for
veterans within their care, without requiring separate
administrative funding authorization, provided that the
device is consistent with the veteran's clinical
classification and treatment plan.

(b) PRE-AUTHORIZED REPLACEMENT.--The Secretary shall
establish a pre-authorized replacement program under which
prosthetic devices are approved for replacement at clinically
appropriate intervals without requiring new funding
authorization for each replacement cycle. Standard intervals
shall be--

(1) 3 to 5 years for prosthetic limbs;
(2) 5 to 7 years for wheelchairs and power mobility
devices; and
(3) 3 to 5 years for hearing aids.

(c) ELECTRONIC PROCUREMENT.--Not later than 18 months after
the date of enactment, the Secretary shall implement
electronic procurement workflows for all prosthetic device
ordering, eliminating paper-based processing.

SEC. 6. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated to carry out this
Act $200,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2031.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Creates enforceable delivery timelines that hold VA accountable to standards approaching commercial-sector performance. The 30-day prosthetic limb target is ambitious but achievable given that the commercial sector delivers in 14-28 days using the same manufacturers
- **Section 4**: Addresses the 1-3 year gap between FDA approval and VA formulary availability by requiring parallel review and provisional listing. The Breakthrough Designation creates a fast track for transformative technologies
- **Section 5**: Eliminates the administrative funding authorization bottleneck that adds weeks to prosthetic delivery without clinical benefit. Pre-authorized replacement removes the need to re-justify devices at each replacement cycle

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Cost ($200M/year) | Procurement streamlining generates savings; advanced devices reduce downstream healthcare costs (falls, hospitalizations) |
| Federal Acquisition Regulation constraints | Section 5 creates prosthetics-specific authority within existing FAR framework |
| Quality concerns about accelerated formulary | FDA approval already ensures safety; VA review focuses on comparative effectiveness |
| Technology Assessment Program resistance | Section 4(d) provides staffing to handle increased workload |

**Refinements**:

- **Stronger version**: Mandate 21-day delivery timelines and 90-day formulary adoption for all FDA-approved devices
- **Fallback version**: Apply 30-day standard to Regional Amputation Centers and Polytrauma Rehabilitation Centers first, with system-wide implementation in 3 years
- **Addition**: Include prosthetics-specific Other Transaction Authority for rapid technology acquisition, modeled on DOD procurement flexibility

### Veterans Rehabilitation Workforce and Access Act

**Purpose**: Address the prosthetist workforce shortage, fund hub-and-spoke rehabilitation networks, and improve DOD-to-VA rehabilitation transitions.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Veterans Rehabilitation
Workforce and Access Act".

SEC. 2. PROSTHETIST COMPENSATION REFORM.

(a) SPECIAL SALARY RATE.--The Secretary of Veterans Affairs
shall establish a special salary rate for certified
prosthetists and orthotists employed by the Department that
is not less than the 75th percentile of compensation for
equivalent positions in the private sector, as determined
by the Bureau of Labor Statistics.

(b) RECRUITMENT AND RETENTION BONUSES.--The Secretary is
authorized to offer recruitment bonuses of up to $50,000
and retention bonuses of up to $25,000 annually for
certified prosthetists, rehabilitation engineers, and
physiatrists at facilities with vacancy rates exceeding
15 percent.

SEC. 3. PROSTHETICS EDUCATION PIPELINE.

(a) SCHOLARSHIP PROGRAM.--The Secretary shall establish a
VA Prosthetics Scholarship Program providing full tuition,
fees, and a monthly stipend of not less than $2,000 to
students enrolled in accredited prosthetics and orthotics
programs, in exchange for a service obligation of not less
than 4 years of employment with the Department.

(b) EDUCATION DEBT REDUCTION.--The Secretary shall establish
a Prosthetics Education Debt Reduction Program providing up
to $120,000 in student loan repayment for certified
prosthetists, orthotists, physiatrists, and rehabilitation
engineers employed by the Department.

(c) RESIDENCY PROGRAMS.--The Secretary shall establish not
fewer than 5 new VA-affiliated prosthetics and orthotics
residency programs at Regional Amputation Centers within
3 years of enactment.

(d) VETERAN TECHNICIAN PATHWAY.--The Secretary shall
establish an accelerated prosthetics technician training
program for veterans with relevant military occupational
experience, providing a career pathway to full prosthetics
certification.

SEC. 4. HUB-AND-SPOKE REHABILITATION NETWORK.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--The Secretary shall establish a
hub-and-spoke prosthetic and rehabilitation network in
which each Polytrauma Rehabilitation Center and Regional
Amputation Center serves as a hub with responsibility for
a defined geographic region.

(b) SATELLITE CLINICS.--The Secretary shall establish
satellite prosthetic and rehabilitation clinics at not
fewer than 50 additional VA Medical Centers within 5 years
of enactment, staffed by hub-trained providers.

(c) TELEREHABILITATION.--The Secretary shall deploy
telerehabilitation platforms at all VA Medical Centers
and community-based outpatient clinics, enabling remote
prosthetic consultation, adjustment guidance, and
rehabilitation follow-up with hub-site specialists.

(d) MOBILE TEAMS.--The Secretary shall establish Prosthetic
Outreach Teams that travel from hub sites to underserved
VA facilities on scheduled rotations to provide specialized
prosthetic fitting, adjustment, and rehabilitation services.

SEC. 5. DOD-VA TRANSITION IMPROVEMENT.

(a) JOINT REHABILITATION PLANNING.--The Secretary of
Veterans Affairs and the Secretary of Defense shall jointly
establish rehabilitation transition planning for each
service member expected to separate due to combat or
training injury, beginning not later than 12 months before
projected separation.

(b) DEVICE PORTABILITY.--Prosthetic devices fitted at
Department of Defense military treatment facilities shall
be maintained, repaired, and replaced by the Department
of Veterans Affairs without requiring refitting or new
clinical justification, provided the device remains
clinically appropriate.

(c) ELECTRONIC RECORDS.--The Secretary of Veterans Affairs
and the Secretary of Defense shall implement a shared
electronic prosthetic record accessible to providers at
both departments, enabling continuity of prosthetic care
during transition.

(d) COORDINATOR CASELOADS.--The Secretary of Veterans
Affairs shall ensure that Federal Recovery Coordinators
assigned to severely wounded service members maintain
caseloads of not more than 25 veterans each.

SEC. 6. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated to carry out this
Act $350,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2031.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 2**: Directly addresses the primary recruitment barrier (12% salary gap) and provides targeted bonuses for hard-to-fill positions at understaffed facilities
- **Section 3**: Builds the long-term workforce pipeline through scholarships, loan repayment, residencies, and a veteran-specific career pathway
- **Section 4**: Extends specialized rehabilitation expertise beyond the current 5 PRCs and 7 RACs to reach veterans wherever they live
- **Section 5**: Eliminates the DOD-VA transition gap that 21% of wounded warriors report experiencing

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Cost ($350M/year) | Workforce investment reduces community care referral costs and improves outcomes that reduce long-term healthcare spending |
| National prosthetist shortage limits hiring | Scholarship and residency programs address the pipeline; salary reform makes VA competitive for existing professionals |
| DOD resistance to joint transition planning | Modeled on existing VA-DOD Joint Executive Committee authorities; builds on established wounded warrior programs |
| Satellite clinic capital costs | Can leverage existing VA Medical Center space; telerehabilitation reduces need for full-service satellite facilities |

### Veterans Prosthetic Care Trust Fund Act

**Purpose**: Establish a long-term obligation funding model for veteran prosthetic care, replacing annual budget-cycle funding with actuarially projected lifetime care guarantees.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Veterans Prosthetic Care
Trust Fund Act".

SEC. 2. ESTABLISHMENT OF TRUST FUND.

(a) IN GENERAL.--There is established in the Treasury of
the United States a fund to be known as the "Veterans
Prosthetic Care Trust Fund" (referred to in this section
as the "Fund").

(b) PURPOSE.--The Fund shall be available, without fiscal
year limitation, for the provision of prosthetic devices,
sensory aids, rehabilitative services, and related care to
eligible veterans.

(c) DEPOSITS.--There shall be deposited into the Fund--
(1) amounts appropriated to the Fund; and
(2) any interest earned on investments of the Fund.

SEC. 3. ACTUARIAL VALUATION.

(a) ANNUAL VALUATION.--The Secretary of Veterans Affairs
shall conduct an annual actuarial valuation of the
projected lifetime prosthetic care costs for all enrolled
veteran beneficiaries, based on--

(1) the number and demographic characteristics of
enrolled veteran amputees, SCI/D patients, and other
prosthetic beneficiaries;
(2) projected device costs, replacement cycles, and
technology trends;
(3) projected rehabilitation service needs; and
(4) mortality and morbidity projections.

(b) REPORT.--The Secretary shall submit the annual
actuarial valuation to the Committees on Veterans' Affairs
and Appropriations of the Senate and House of
Representatives not later than 60 days after the
submission of the President's annual budget request.

SEC. 4. FUNDING.

(a) INITIAL CAPITALIZATION.--Congress shall appropriate
to the Fund such sums as are necessary to fully fund
the actuarial liability identified in the first valuation
under section 3, to be deposited in not more than 5
annual installments.

(b) ANNUAL CONTRIBUTIONS.--For each fiscal year following
full capitalization under subsection (a), Congress shall
appropriate to the Fund the annual normal cost of
prosthetic care for newly enrolled beneficiaries plus
any amortization of unfunded liability.

SEC. 5. GUARANTEE.

The provision of prosthetic devices and rehabilitative
services to eligible veterans shall not be subject to
reduction or delay due to annual appropriations shortfalls,
provided that the Fund maintains sufficient assets to
cover projected obligations.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 2**: Creates a trust fund structure that carries balances across fiscal years, eliminating the "use it or lose it" dynamic of annual appropriations
- **Section 3**: Requires actuarial projections that quantify the government's lifetime prosthetic care obligation, making the true cost of wounded warrior care visible to Congress
- **Section 5**: Guarantees that prosthetic care is not subject to annual budget volatility--the core reform that addresses the fundamental mismatch between lifetime care obligations and annual appropriations

## State Model Legislation

### Model Veterans Prosthetic Parity Act

**Purpose**: Provide a template for state-level legislation requiring insurance parity for prosthetic devices, ensuring that veterans with private insurance and state-funded coverage receive advanced prosthetics when clinically indicated.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Veterans Prosthetic
Parity Act".

SECTION 2. PROSTHETIC DEVICE PARITY.

(a) Every health insurance policy issued or renewed in this
state that provides coverage for medical and surgical
benefits shall provide coverage for prosthetic devices,
including but not limited to:
(1) upper and lower extremity prosthetic limbs;
(2) microprocessor-controlled prosthetic components;
(3) myoelectric prosthetic components;
(4) orthotic devices;
(5) prosthetic repair, replacement, and maintenance; and
(6) physical and occupational therapy related to
prosthetic fitting and use.

(b) Coverage under subsection (a) shall not be subject to--
(1) annual or lifetime dollar limits that do not apply
to other medical and surgical benefits;
(2) higher copayments, coinsurance, or deductibles
than apply to comparable medical and surgical benefits; or
(3) restrictions on the number of prosthetic devices or
components that may be provided, except as determined by
clinical need.

(c) Coverage shall include the most clinically appropriate
prosthetic device as determined by the prescribing physician
and certified prosthetist, including advanced technology
components when the patient's functional classification
and activity level indicate clinical benefit.

SECTION 3. ADAPTIVE SPORTS AND RECREATION.

The [State Department of Veterans Affairs] shall establish
a grant program, subject to appropriations, to fund adaptive
sports and recreation programs for veterans with physical
disabilities, with priority given to communities without
existing VA adaptive sports programming.

SECTION 4. PROSTHETIST WORKFORCE.

(a) The [State Higher Education Authority] shall designate
prosthetics and orthotics as a workforce shortage area
eligible for state tuition assistance and loan repayment
programs.

(b) State universities with health science programs are
encouraged to establish or expand prosthetics and orthotics
training programs to address the documented national
workforce shortage.
```

**Adaptations**:

- States with existing prosthetic parity laws can focus on strengthening enforcement and adding advanced technology coverage
- States without Medicaid expansion may need alternative funding mechanisms for Section 3
- Workforce provisions can be adjusted based on existing state tuition assistance programs
- States with strong veterans affairs departments can integrate Section 3 into existing veteran service programs

## Regulatory Framework

### VA Prosthetics Procurement Reform Regulation

**Existing Authority**: 38 U.S.C. Section 8123 (VA procurement authority); Secretary's discretion over prosthetic services delivery

**Draft Regulation**:

```text
DEPARTMENT OF VETERANS AFFAIRS

38 CFR Part 17 -- Medical

Subpart -- Prosthetic Device Procurement and Delivery

Sec. 17.XX1 Purpose.
This subpart establishes streamlined procurement procedures
for prosthetic devices to ensure timely delivery to eligible
veterans.

Sec. 17.XX2 Direct Ordering Authority.
(a) VA certified prosthetists and orthotists are authorized
to order prosthetic devices listed on the National Prosthetics
Formulary for veterans within their care without requiring
separate administrative funding authorization, provided that:
(1) the device is consistent with the veteran's clinical
functional classification;
(2) the order is documented in the veteran's electronic
health record; and
(3) the total device cost does not exceed the threshold
established by the Prosthetic and Sensory Aids Service.

Sec. 17.XX3 Pre-Authorized Replacement.
(a) Prosthetic devices shall be approved for replacement
at the following intervals without requiring new clinical
justification or funding authorization:
(1) Prosthetic limbs: every 3 to 5 years based on
component wear and clinical assessment;
(2) Wheelchairs and power mobility devices: every 5 to
7 years;
(3) Hearing aids: every 3 to 5 years;
(4) Orthotic devices: as clinically indicated.

Sec. 17.XX4 Emergency Repair.
All VA medical centers and community-based outpatient
clinics with prosthetic services shall maintain the
capacity to initiate prosthetic device repair within
24 hours of a veteran's request.

Sec. 17.XX5 Performance Metrics.
The Prosthetic and Sensory Aids Service shall track
and report monthly:
(a) average days from prescription to delivery by
device category;
(b) percentage of devices delivered within established
timelines;
(c) emergency repair response time; and
(d) veteran satisfaction with prosthetic services.
```

**Explanation**: Codifies procurement streamlining into binding regulation, ensuring consistency across VA facilities and protection against future bureaucratic drift. Direct ordering authority and pre-authorized replacement eliminate the administrative steps that account for most VA-specific delivery delays.

### VA Community Care Prosthetics Quality Regulation

**Existing Authority**: 38 U.S.C. Section 1703 (VA Community Care Program); VA MISSION Act implementation authority

**Draft Regulation**:

```text
DEPARTMENT OF VETERANS AFFAIRS

38 CFR Part 17 -- Medical

Subpart -- Community Care Prosthetic Services Quality

Sec. 17.YY1 Purpose.
This subpart establishes quality standards for prosthetic
services delivered to veterans through community care
arrangements.

Sec. 17.YY2 Provider Credentialing.
Community prosthetic providers receiving VA referrals shall:
(a) employ certified prosthetists and orthotists
credentialed by a nationally recognized certifying body;
(b) maintain accreditation from a recognized prosthetic
facility accreditation organization; and
(c) demonstrate compliance with VA clinical practice
guidelines for prosthetic care.

Sec. 17.YY3 Veteran-Specific Competency.
Community prosthetic providers serving more than 10 VA-
referred veterans annually shall ensure that at least one
practitioner at each facility has completed VA-approved
military cultural competency training.

Sec. 17.YY4 Outcomes Reporting.
Community prosthetic providers shall report to the
Department, on a quarterly basis:
(a) device delivery timelines;
(b) patient satisfaction scores;
(c) device-related adverse events; and
(d) prosthetic outcomes using validated instruments
specified by the Prosthetic and Sensory Aids Service.

Sec. 17.YY5 Quality Review.
The Department shall conduct annual quality reviews of
community prosthetic providers, including comparison of
outcomes with VA-delivered prosthetic care, and shall
discontinue referrals to providers that fail to meet
established quality standards.
```

**Explanation**: Addresses the quality oversight gap for the growing number of prosthetics delivered through community care referrals (24,000 in FY 2024, up from 18,000 in FY 2022). Currently, community providers are not required to meet VA credentialing standards or report outcomes.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Spending Clause**: Federal prosthetics legislation operates through VA appropriations and the Trust Fund mechanism--no constitutional barrier. Congress has broad authority to appropriate funds for veteran healthcare under Article I, Section 8
- **Commerce Clause**: Federal regulation of prosthetic device manufacturers and insurers is well-established under *Gonzales v. Raich*, 545 U.S. 1 (2005), and existing medical device regulation
- **Takings Clause**: Prosthetic parity mandates on private insurers do not constitute a taking; they regulate the terms of insurance contracts, which is a permissible exercise of state police power under *Lingle v. Chevron U.S.A.*, 544 U.S. 528 (2005)
- **Due process**: Procurement reform and formulary changes expand veteran access and do not restrict rights

### Preemption Questions

- Federal prosthetics standards do not preempt state prosthetic parity laws; states may impose additional requirements on insurers
- ERISA preemption may limit state prosthetic parity mandates for self-insured employer plans; federal legislation would be needed to close this gap
- VA community care quality standards preempt conflicting state regulations only within the scope of VA-funded care
- FDA prosthetic device regulations preempt conflicting state device safety requirements

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- VA Inspector General oversight of procurement timeline compliance and formulary modernization implementation
- Congressional reporting requirements with facility-level data on delivery timelines and technology adoption
- Government Accountability Office audits of Trust Fund actuarial assumptions and financial management
- Veteran advocacy organizations as informal enforcement through public accountability
- Community care provider quality reviews with referral discontinuation authority

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Veterans Prosthetic Care Modernization Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Community care escape valve | VA could meet 30-day timeline by referring to community providers with inadequate quality oversight | High |
| Waiver abuse | The 15-day waiver for custom fabrication could become a routine extension | Medium |
| Provisional formulary limitations | "Designated centers of excellence" for provisional listing could be limited to 2-3 sites, effectively restricting access | High |
| Electronic procurement without workflow redesign | Digitizing a broken process does not fix it; electronic procurement could replicate paper-based delays | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No pricing reform | Advanced device costs continue to rise without negotiation authority | Industry lobbying against price controls |
| 5-year authorization | Programs may lose funding after the authorization period | Standard legislative practice |
| No community care quality linkage | Procurement reform addresses VA-delivered care but not the growing community care prosthetics volume | Separate regulatory authority needed |
| No veteran input mechanism | Bill does not require veteran advisory input on formulary decisions | Oversight in legislative design |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Add explicit language requiring community care prosthetic referrals to meet the same delivery timelines as VA-delivered prosthetics
2. Limit waiver usage: require facility-level reporting of waiver frequency and Secretary certification that waivers are not being used routinely
3. Define "designated centers of excellence" as all Regional Amputation Centers and Polytrauma Rehabilitation Centers (minimum 12 sites) with expansion to all VA Medical Centers within 3 years
4. Require workflow redesign as a prerequisite for electronic procurement implementation, with external process engineering consultation
5. Establish a Veterans Prosthetics Advisory Committee with veteran amputee and rehabilitation patient representation

### Veterans Rehabilitation Workforce and Access Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Salary parity erosion | 75th percentile compensation could fall below market if private-sector salaries rise faster than VA adjustments | Medium |
| Scholarship service obligation enforcement | VA has historically struggled to enforce service obligations from scholarship recipients | Medium |
| Hub-and-spoke without hub quality | Satellite clinics trained by hubs are only as good as the hub's expertise and the training methodology | Medium |
| Telerehabilitation substitution | VA could use telerehabilitation to replace rather than supplement in-person specialized care | High |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Require annual salary benchmarking against BLS data with automatic adjustment to maintain the 75th percentile standard
2. Establish enforceable service obligation repayment provisions consistent with existing VA health professions scholarship models
3. Require hub-site certification by an independent rehabilitation accreditation body before satellite clinics can be established
4. Define telerehabilitation as supplemental to, not a replacement for, in-person prosthetic fitting, adjustment, and intensive rehabilitation

### Veterans Prosthetic Care Trust Fund Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Underfunding initial capitalization | Congress could appropriate less than the actuarial liability, creating an underfunded trust | High |
| Actuarial assumption manipulation | Conservative or optimistic assumptions could distort projections | Medium |
| Trust Fund diversion | Future Congresses could redirect Trust Fund assets to other VA purposes | High |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Require initial capitalization schedule in statute with penalties (automatic continuing resolution authority) if missed
2. Mandate independent actuarial review by a firm not employed by VA, with publicly available methodology
3. Include a "lockbox" provision prohibiting the use of Trust Fund assets for any purpose other than prosthetic care and rehabilitation

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| VA facility-level variability in implementation | Tie facility director performance evaluations to prosthetic delivery timeline metrics |
| Provider resistance to direct ordering authority | Phase implementation with training and liability protections for ordering prosthetists |
| EHR transition disruption | Require prosthetics-specific module testing and certification before EHR deployment at any facility |
| Interagency coordination (DOD-VA) | Establish a permanent joint prosthetics transition working group with Congressional reporting requirements |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- All programs should include 5-year reauthorization requirements with mandatory GAO evaluation
- VA should report annually to Congress on delivery timelines, formulary adoption times, workforce metrics, and transition outcomes
- An independent advisory committee of veterans, clinicians, researchers, and industry representatives should review program effectiveness every 3 years
- The Trust Fund actuarial assumptions should be reviewed by the Congressional Budget Office at the midpoint of each authorization period
