# Prosthetics and Rehabilitation: Root Causes

## Overview

The challenges facing veteran prosthetic and rehabilitation care are rooted in structural tensions between technological advancement and institutional bureaucracy, between centralized expertise and distributed access, and between lifetime care obligations and annual budget cycles. These root causes explain why veterans can wait months for devices available commercially in weeks, why geographic location determines access to cutting-edge rehabilitation, and why the system struggles to fulfill its obligation to provide lifelong prosthetic care.

## Procurement and Bureaucratic Root Causes

### 1. VA Procurement System Designed for Commodities, Not Custom Devices

The VA procurement system was designed to purchase standardized medical supplies at scale. Prosthetic devices are inherently custom--each socket must be individually fabricated, each device must be configured for the user's anatomy, activity level, and lifestyle. The collision between standardized procurement and customized care creates systemic delay.

**How this manifests:**

| Process Step | VA Timeline | Commercial Timeline | Source |
|-------------|------------|-------------------|--------|
| Physician prescription | 1-3 days | 1 day | VA PSAS, 2024 |
| Prosthetics formulary review | 5-15 days | N/A (not required) | VA PSAS, 2024 |
| Funding authorization | 7-21 days | N/A (insurance pre-auth: 3-5 days) | VA PSAS, 2024 |
| Device ordering/fabrication | 14-30 days | 7-14 days | Industry average |
| Fitting and adjustment | 7-14 days | 5-10 days | Industry average |
| **Total** | **34-83 days** | **13-30 days** | -- |

The formulary review and funding authorization stages--which have no equivalent in the private sector--account for most of the VA-specific delay.

### 2. National Prosthetics Formulary Rigidity

The VA National Prosthetics Formulary standardizes which devices are available system-wide. While standardization ensures quality and cost control, it also:

- **Delays new technology adoption**: Devices must pass VA Technology Assessment Program review (1-3 years) even after FDA approval
- **Limits individualization**: Veterans may be prescribed formulary devices that are not optimal for their specific needs
- **Creates regional variation**: Some VISN-level exceptions allow advanced devices; others do not, creating geographic inequity
- **Discourages innovation**: Manufacturers have less incentive to develop VA-specific solutions when formulary approval is uncertain and slow

### 3. Technology Assessment Bottleneck

The VA Technology Assessment Program evaluates new prosthetic devices for clinical effectiveness, safety, and cost-effectiveness before formulary inclusion. While this function is important, the process is:

- Chronically understaffed (12 full-time evaluators for the entire VA system)
- Reliant on published evidence that may not exist for cutting-edge technologies
- Designed for pharmaceutical-style assessment, which fits poorly with medical devices that iterate rapidly
- Not coordinated with DOD evaluation processes, leading to duplicative review

## Workforce Root Causes

### 4. National Prosthetist Shortage

The United States produces approximately 450 new certified prosthetists/orthotists annually against a workforce of 8,200 (American Board for Certification, 2024). With 17% projected demand growth through 2032, the shortage will worsen. Root factors include:

- **Limited training programs**: Only 13 accredited master's programs in prosthetics/orthotics exist nationally
- **Long training pipeline**: 6-7 years (4 years undergraduate + 2 years master's + 1 year residency) from entry to certification
- **Below-market VA compensation**: VA CPO salaries average $92,000 vs. $105,000 private sector (BLS, 2024)
- **Geographic concentration**: Most prosthetists practice in urban areas; rural VA facilities are particularly difficult to staff
- **Career awareness**: Prosthetics/orthotics is not widely known as a career option, limiting the applicant pipeline

### 5. Rehabilitation Specialist Pipeline Gaps

Beyond prosthetists, the VA faces shortages across rehabilitation disciplines:

| Discipline | Training Programs Nationally | Annual Graduates | VA Vacancy Rate | Root Factor |
|-----------|------------------------------|-----------------|----------------|-------------|
| Physiatry (PM&R) | 82 residency programs | 400 | 19% | Specialty not well-known to medical students |
| Recreation therapy | ~75 programs | 600 | 20% | Low salaries across the profession |
| Rehabilitation engineering | ~20 programs | 100 | 22% | Highly specialized; few training options |
| Speech-language pathology | ~280 programs | 8,000 | 16% | VA competes with schools, hospitals |

*Sources: ACGME, BLS, VA Workforce Management, 2024.*

## Geographic and Access Root Causes

### 6. Centralization of Specialized Care

The VA's tiered system of care--5 Polytrauma Rehabilitation Centers, 7 Regional Amputation Centers, 25 SCI/D Centers--concentrates the highest-quality rehabilitation expertise at a small number of locations. This design reflects clinical reality (complex rehabilitation requires multidisciplinary teams and specialized equipment) but creates access barriers:

- Most veterans live hours from the nearest specialized center
- Travel for inpatient rehabilitation separates veterans from family support systems
- Follow-up care after discharge is fragmented between specialized centers and local VA facilities
- Telerehabilitation can supplement but not fully replace in-person specialized care
- Community care referrals send veterans to private providers who lack veteran-specific expertise

### 7. Disconnect Between Specialized and General VA Care

When veterans receive initial rehabilitation at a Polytrauma Rehabilitation Center or Regional Amputation Center and then return to their local VA Medical Center or CBOC for ongoing care, the transition frequently involves:

- Loss of specialized expertise (local providers may have limited prosthetic experience)
- Inconsistent access to advanced components and maintenance
- Reduced rehabilitation intensity
- Communication gaps between specialized and general care teams
- Veteran frustration and disengagement from care

## Funding and Economic Root Causes

### 8. Annual Budget Cycles vs. Lifetime Care Obligation

Prosthetic and rehabilitation care for a severely wounded veteran spans decades. A 25-year-old bilateral amputee may need prosthetic care for 50+ years, at an estimated lifetime cost of $4.8 million (VA Health Economics Resource Center, 2023). But VA prosthetics is funded through annual appropriations:

- **No long-term obligation accounting**: The VA does not maintain an actuarial reserve for future prosthetic costs
- **Budget volatility**: Annual appropriations can fluctuate, threatening care continuity
- **Delayed technology adoption**: Multi-year budget uncertainty discourages investment in new technology programs
- **Component replacement tension**: Budget pressure may lead to extending device life beyond optimal replacement cycles

### 9. Cost of Advanced Technology

The gap between basic and advanced prosthetic technology has widened dramatically:

| Component | Basic/Mechanical | Advanced/Microprocessor | Cost Ratio |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------------|------------|
| Prosthetic knee | $2,000-$5,000 | $50,000-$100,000 | 10-20x |
| Prosthetic hand | $1,500-$3,000 (hook) | $50,000-$150,000 (myoelectric) | 30-50x |
| Prosthetic foot | $500-$2,000 | $15,000-$30,000 | 10-15x |
| Wheelchair | $2,000 (manual) | $25,000-$60,000 (power) | 10-30x |

*Source: VA National Prosthetics Formulary; manufacturer pricing, 2024.*

Advanced devices provide dramatically better function, but their cost creates formulary tension: providing the best technology to all who would benefit is financially challenging; restricting access to advanced technology based on cost raises equity and clinical concerns.

## Systemic Root Causes

### 10. DOD-VA Transition Gap

The transition from military treatment facilities (where active-duty wounded warriors receive initial care) to the VA system creates a structural vulnerability:

- **Different systems**: DOD and VA use different electronic health records, procurement systems, and clinical protocols
- **Different cultures**: Military rehabilitation emphasizes return to duty; VA rehabilitation emphasizes community reintegration
- **Timing pressure**: DOD medical boards often move faster than VA intake processes
- **Geographic disruption**: Service members may be rehabilitated at Walter Reed (Bethesda) or SAMMC (San Antonio) and then transferred to a VA facility far from their intended home
- **Device continuity**: Prosthetic devices fitted at DOD facilities may not be on the VA formulary, requiring refitting

### 11. Research-to-Care Pipeline Dysfunction

VA and DOD jointly fund world-class prosthetic research, but moving innovations from lab to veteran is structurally slow:

| Barrier | Description | Effect |
|---------|-------------|--------|
| Regulatory pathway | FDA approval required for new devices | 5-10 years for novel technologies |
| VA formulary review | Separate review after FDA approval | Additional 1-3 years |
| Training requirement | VA providers must be trained on new devices | Additional 6-18 months |
| Infrastructure | Some technologies require facility upgrades | Capital budget delays |
| Procurement | Contracts must be established with manufacturers | Additional months |
| **Cumulative delay** | -- | **7-14 years from lab to veteran** |

Commercial prosthetic innovations (developed outside VA/DOD research) face the same VA-specific delays, meaning veterans may wait years for devices their private-sector counterparts can access immediately.

### 12. Quality Oversight for Community Care Prosthetics

As more prosthetic services are delivered through community care referrals (24,000 in FY 2024, up from 18,000 in FY 2022), quality oversight gaps have emerged:

- Community prosthetic providers are not required to meet VA credentialing standards
- No systematic outcomes tracking for prosthetics delivered through community care
- Community providers may lack experience with combat-related amputations, polytrauma, or veteran-specific needs
- VA clinical oversight of community-provided prosthetics is inconsistent across VISNs

## Root Cause Interaction

The root causes create reinforcing cycles:

1. **Technology-Bureaucracy Spiral**: Advanced technology increases cost, which increases procurement scrutiny, which delays delivery, which means veterans receive older technology, which generates demand for faster technology adoption, which the bureaucracy cannot accommodate
2. **Workforce-Access Spiral**: Prosthetist shortages lead to longer wait times, which drive veterans to community care, which drains VA prosthetic referral volume, which reduces VA justification for additional prosthetist positions
3. **Centralization-Equity Tension**: Concentrating expertise improves quality but reduces access, which drives community care referrals to less-expert providers, which reduces overall quality for veterans far from specialized centers
