# Prosthetics and Rehabilitation: Current State

## Prosthetic Device Provision

### Scale of VA Prosthetic Services

The VA Prosthetic and Sensory Aids Service (PSAS) is the largest provider of prosthetic devices and services in the United States, supporting approximately 3.2 million unique veterans annually across a range of devices far broader than limb prosthetics.

| Device Category | Annual Volume (FY 2024) | Budget Share | Source |
|----------------|------------------------|-------------|--------|
| Hearing aids | 1,100,000 devices | 22% | VA Audiology Service, FY 2024 |
| Wheelchairs/mobility devices | 215,000 | 18% | VA PSAS, FY 2024 |
| Orthotics (braces, supports) | 380,000 | 12% | VA PSAS, FY 2024 |
| Limb prosthetics (lower extremity) | 42,000 | 16% | VA PSAS, FY 2024 |
| Limb prosthetics (upper extremity) | 8,500 | 8% | VA PSAS, FY 2024 |
| Visual aids and blind rehab devices | 95,000 | 4% | VA PSAS, FY 2024 |
| Home modifications | 28,000 projects | 6% | VA PSAS, FY 2024 |
| Vehicle modifications | 4,200 | 3% | VA PSAS, FY 2024 |
| Other (oxygen, CPAP, surgical implants) | 850,000+ | 11% | VA PSAS, FY 2024 |

*Source: VA Prosthetic and Sensory Aids Service Annual Report, FY 2024.*

### Limb Prosthetic Population

| Era/Cause | Estimated Amputees Receiving VA Care | Primary Amputation Type | Source |
|-----------|--------------------------------------|------------------------|--------|
| Post-9/11 combat | 1,350 | Lower extremity (bilateral common) | DOD Trauma Registry, 2024 |
| Vietnam era (combat) | 5,200 | Lower extremity | VA Patient Care Services, 2024 |
| All eras (dysvascular/diabetes) | 48,000 | Lower extremity (below-knee most common) | VA PSAS, 2024 |
| All eras (trauma, non-combat) | 8,200 | Mixed | VA PSAS, 2024 |
| Upper extremity (all causes) | 5,400 | Transradial most common | VA PSAS, 2024 |
| **Total receiving VA prosthetic limbs** | **~68,000** | -- | VA PSAS, 2024 |

The veteran amputee population is dominated by dysvascular (diabetes and vascular disease) amputations, which outnumber combat-related limb loss by approximately 10:1. This distinction is important because the two populations have different needs: combat amputees are generally younger, more active, and require high-performance prosthetic components; dysvascular amputees are typically older, less active, and may face additional comorbidities.

### Prosthetic Technology Levels

VA categorizes prosthetic limb components by functional level (K-level):

| K-Level | Description | Typical Components | Approximate Cost |
|---------|------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| K0 | Non-ambulatory | Cosmetic prosthesis only | $2,000-$5,000 |
| K1 | Limited household ambulation | Basic mechanical knee, SACH foot | $5,000-$15,000 |
| K2 | Community ambulation (limited) | Single-axis knee, dynamic foot | $10,000-$25,000 |
| K3 | Community ambulation (varied speeds) | Microprocessor knee, energy-storing foot | $40,000-$80,000 |
| K4 | Active/athletic | Advanced microprocessor, running components | $60,000-$150,000 |

*Source: VA National Prosthetics Formulary; Medicare HCPCS coding system.*

Wait times have improved but remain significantly longer than commercial prosthetic providers, where typical delivery is 2-4 weeks for a new prosthetic limb. VA procurement processes, supply chain issues, and prosthetist shortages contribute to delays.

## Technology and Innovation

### Current Prosthetic Technology Landscape

| Technology | Status | VA Availability | Key Limitations |
|-----------|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| Microprocessor-controlled knees (e.g., C-Leg, Genium) | Mature | Available through formulary | Cost ($50K-$100K); requires battery; maintenance |
| Myoelectric upper-limb (e.g., i-Limb, bebionic) | Mature | Available through formulary | Limited dexterity; training intensive; cost ($50K-$150K) |
| Osseointegration (bone-anchored prosthetics) | Emerging | Available at select VA centers (FDA approved 2023) | Infection risk; surgical; limited provider expertise |
| Powered exoskeletons (e.g., ReWalk, Ekso) | Limited | Available at 7 SCI/D centers | Not yet approved for home use by VA; cost ($100K+) |
| Targeted muscle reinnervation (TMR) | Growing | Available at PRCs and RACs | Surgical; limited surgeons trained |
| Pattern recognition myoelectric control | Growing | Available at select centers | Training intensive; performance varies |
| Brain-computer interface prosthetics | Research | Not clinically available | Experimental; implant durability concerns |
| 3D-printed prosthetics | Emerging | Pilot programs at 5 VA centers | Limited to specific applications; regulatory uncertainty |
| Regenerative medicine (limb regrowth) | Early research | Not available | Decades from clinical application |

*Source: VA Rehabilitation Research and Development Service, 2025; FDA Medical Device Approvals, 2023-2024.*

### Technology Transfer Challenges

Despite world-class research, VA faces significant delays in moving innovations from laboratory to patient care:

| Stage | Average Duration | Bottleneck |
|-------|-----------------|------------|
| Research to FDA approval | 5-10 years | Regulatory pathway complexity |
| FDA approval to VA formulary | 1-3 years | VA Technology Assessment Program review |
| VA formulary to field availability | 6-18 months | Procurement, training, infrastructure |
| **Total: lab to veteran** | **7-14 years** | Cumulative bureaucratic and regulatory delay |

*Source: VA Technology Assessment Program Reports, 2023-2024; GAO-23-105208, 2023.*

The DOD-VA transition remains problematic despite improvement. The Federal Recovery Coordination Program (FRCP) assigns coordinators to severely wounded service members to manage the transition, but coordinator caseloads average 35 veterans each, above the recommended 25 (GAO-23-105208, 2023).

## Recent Developments (2024-2025)

### Policy and Program Changes

- **Osseointegration approval (2023)**: FDA approved the OPRA Implant System for bone-anchored prosthetics; VA began offering the procedure at 4 centers in 2024
- **3D printing pilot (2024)**: VA launched prosthetic socket printing pilot at 5 centers, reducing socket fabrication time from weeks to days
- **Hearing aid over-the-counter impact**: The 2022 OTC hearing aid rule created consumer alternatives, but VA continues to provide prescription-grade devices at no cost to veterans
- **PACT Act enrollment**: Expanded eligibility brought approximately 180,000 additional veterans into prosthetic services eligibility through FY 2024
- **Powered exoskeleton home use**: VA established a clinical protocol for prescribing powered exoskeletons for home use by SCI/D veterans, with 12 prescriptions completed in FY 2024

### Ongoing Challenges

- Prosthetic procurement reform remains stalled despite years of GAO recommendations
- Prosthetist recruitment continues to lag demand
- Community care prosthetic referrals are growing but quality oversight is limited
- Aging infrastructure at some SCI/D centers requires capital investment
- Research-to-care pipeline remains slow relative to the pace of commercial innovation

## The Gap Between Problem and Action

| The Problem | Current Response | The Gap |
|-------------|-----------------|---------|
| 52-day average wait for prosthetic limb | Procurement streamlining efforts | Commercial sector delivers in 14-28 days |
| 18% prosthetist vacancy rate | Recruitment bonuses, loan repayment | Salaries 12% below private sector |
| Advanced technology delayed by formulary | VA Technology Assessment Program | 1-3 years from FDA approval to VA availability |
| Geographic concentration of specialized care | 5 PRCs, 7 RACs nationally | Most veterans live hours from nearest center |
| Lifetime care costs inadequately budgeted | Annual appropriations | No long-term obligation model for lifetime care |
| 21% experience care gap during DOD-VA transition | Federal Recovery Coordination Program | Coordinator caseloads above recommended levels |
