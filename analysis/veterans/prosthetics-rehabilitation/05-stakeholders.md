# Prosthetics and Rehabilitation: Stakeholders

## Overview

Veteran prosthetics and rehabilitation involves a uniquely complex stakeholder landscape that spans federal agencies, military institutions, healthcare providers, technology companies, research institutions, and advocacy organizations. The field is characterized by deep technical expertise, significant commercial interests, and a strong sense of moral obligation to wounded warriors that transcends typical policy debates.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Veterans with Prosthetic and Rehabilitation Needs

**Population**: Approximately 3.2 million veterans receive some form of prosthetic or rehabilitative service annually; approximately 68,000 use prosthetic limbs; approximately 28,000 receive SCI/D care.

**Subgroups with distinct needs:**

| Subgroup | Population (est.) | Distinct Needs |
|----------|------------------|---------------|
| Post-9/11 combat amputees | ~1,350 receiving VA care | High-activity prosthetics, polytrauma rehabilitation, young-family support |
| Vietnam-era amputees | ~5,200 | Aging-related complications, prosthetic replacement, chronic pain |
| Dysvascular amputees | ~48,000 | Diabetes/vascular management, lower-activity prosthetics, comorbidity care |
| SCI/D veterans | ~28,000 | Power wheelchairs, home modifications, attendant care, lifetime follow-up |
| TBI/polytrauma veterans | ~55,000 screened/year | Cognitive rehabilitation, assistive technology, multi-system care |
| Blind/visually impaired | ~130,000 receiving services | Visual aids, orientation/mobility training, assistive technology |
| Hearing-impaired veterans | ~1.1M receiving hearing aids | Hearing aids, cochlear implants, audiology services |

**Power and influence**: Wounded veterans have high public sympathy and significant media attention. Organizations like the Wounded Warrior Project and Fisher House amplify individual stories. However, the diversity of needs within this population can diffuse advocacy focus.

| Ossur (Iceland) | Rheo Knee, i-Limb | Major VA formulary supplier |

| Permobil (Sweden) | Power wheelchairs, seating | Major VA wheelchair supplier |
| Cochlear (Australia) | Cochlear implants | VA audiology supplier |

**Interests**: VA contracts (VA is the largest single purchaser of prosthetics in the US), formulary inclusion, research partnerships, technology demonstration

**Tensions**: Manufacturers want faster formulary approval and higher-volume contracts; VA wants cost control and evidence-based adoption

### Prosthetic Fabrication and Fitting Providers

Both VA-employed prosthetists and community-based prosthetic practices provide fitting and fabrication services. Community prosthetic providers include:

- Large national chains (Hanger Clinic, with 700+ locations)
- Regional prosthetic practices
- Individual certified prosthetists in private practice

**Interests**: Community care referral volume, adequate reimbursement, clinical autonomy

## Coordination Challenges

1. **DOD-VA divide**: Different systems, cultures, and priorities create transition friction for wounded warriors
2. **Research-clinical gap**: Researchers and clinicians operate in different institutional contexts with poor communication
3. **Industry-government tension**: Manufacturers want market access; VA wants cost control and evidence
4. **Advocacy fragmentation**: Multiple organizations serve overlapping populations with limited coordination
5. **Federal-community care split**: VA and community prosthetic providers compete rather than collaborate on expertise
