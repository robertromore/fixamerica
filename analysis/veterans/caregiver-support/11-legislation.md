# Caregiver Support: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative framework for veteran caregiver support rests primarily on two statutes: the Caregivers and Veterans Omnibus Health Services Act of 2010 (P.L. 111-163), which established the PCAFC and PGCSS, and the VA MISSION Act of 2018 (P.L. 115-182, Section 161), which mandated expansion to all eras of service. Despite this foundation, significant statutory gaps remain in stipend adequacy, Social Security crediting, workplace protections, respite care guarantees, eligibility consistency, and aging caregiver support. This document proposes federal legislation, state model legislation, and regulatory reforms to close those gaps.

## Federal Legislation

### Veteran Caregiver Empowerment and Fair Compensation Act

**Purpose**: Comprehensive legislation addressing stipend reform, Social Security credits, eligibility simplification, reassessment protections, respite care guarantees, and aging caregiver support.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Veteran Caregiver Empowerment and Fair
Compensation Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(1) approximately 5,500,000 Americans serve as caregivers to
    veterans, with 1,100,000 providing intensive care of more than
    20 hours per week;
(2) the Program of Comprehensive Assistance for Family Caregivers
    enrolls only 37,000 caregivers, a fraction of those eligible;
(3) PCAFC stipends represent 50 to 68 percent of the market rate
    for comparable professional care;
(4) 52 percent of veteran caregivers screen positive for depression,
    42 percent report burnout, and 9 percent report suicidal ideation;
(5) caregivers who leave employment lose an average of $304,000 in
    lifetime wages and Social Security benefits;
(6) approximately 7,500 caregivers were terminated from the PCAFC
    through reassessments in 2022 and 2023 under criteria that the
    VA Office of Inspector General found were inconsistently applied;
(7) an estimated 400,000 caregivers over age 65 care for veterans
    from the Vietnam, Korea, and World War II eras with no
    specialized support; and
(8) family caregiver labor saves the Department of Veterans Affairs
    an estimated $14,000,000,000 annually compared to the cost of
    equivalent institutional care.

SEC. 3. PCAFC STIPEND REFORM.

(a) BENCHMARK.--Section 1720G(a)(3)(C) of title 38, United States
    Code, is amended--
    (1) by striking "the amount of compensation a home health
        agency would pay" and inserting "the amount of compensation
        a licensed practical nurse or comparable skilled care
        professional would receive"; and
    (2) by adding at the end the following: "The Secretary shall
        ensure that stipend rates reflect the specialized skills
        required to manage traumatic brain injury, post-traumatic
        stress disorder, spinal cord injury, and other complex
        service-connected conditions.".

(b) SKILL-BASED ADJUSTMENTS.--The Secretary shall establish
    supplemental stipend adjustments of not less than $200 and not
    more than $600 per month for caregivers who--
    (1) manage ventilator or tracheostomy equipment;
    (2) provide behavioral health crisis intervention for veterans
        with severe PTSD or traumatic brain injury;
    (3) manage complex wound care or medical devices; or
    (4) provide care requiring specialized clinical training as
        determined by the Secretary.

(c) ANNUAL ADJUSTMENT.--The Secretary shall adjust PCAFC stipend
    rates annually based on a caregiver-specific cost index that
    accounts for changes in healthcare costs, housing costs, and
    transportation costs in the caregiver's geographic area, in
    lieu of the general Bureau of Labor Statistics wage index.

(d) IMPLEMENTATION.--The stipend reforms under this section shall
    take effect not later than 180 days after the date of enactment.

SEC. 4. SOCIAL SECURITY CREDITS FOR CAREGIVERS.

(a) DEEMED WAGES.--For purposes of title II of the Social Security
    Act (42 U.S.C. 401 et seq.), amounts received by an individual
    as a PCAFC stipend under section 1720G of title 38, United
    States Code, shall be treated as wages paid to such individual
    for employment.

(b) EMPLOYER CONTRIBUTION.--The Department of Veterans Affairs shall
    pay the employer-equivalent Federal Insurance Contributions Act
    tax with respect to deemed wages under subsection (a).

(c) CAREGIVER ELECTION.--A PCAFC-enrolled caregiver may elect to--
    (1) pay the employee-equivalent Federal Insurance Contributions
        Act tax from the stipend amount; or
    (2) have the Department of Veterans Affairs pay the employee-
        equivalent tax on behalf of the caregiver, in which case the
        stipend amount shall be reduced accordingly.

(d) RETROACTIVE CREDITS.--A caregiver who was enrolled in the PCAFC
    for any period before the date of enactment of this section may
    elect to receive deemed wage credits for such prior enrollment
    period, subject to the payment of applicable taxes for such
    period.

(e) IMPLEMENTATION.--The Commissioner of Social Security, in
    coordination with the Secretary of Veterans Affairs, shall
    promulgate regulations to implement this section not later than
    18 months after the date of enactment.

SEC. 5. ELIGIBILITY SIMPLIFICATION.

(a) STANDARDIZED ASSESSMENT.--Not later than 1 year after the date
    of enactment, the Secretary shall implement a standardized,
    objective functional assessment instrument for PCAFC eligibility
    determinations that--
    (1) uses validated measures of activities of daily living and
        instrumental activities of daily living;
    (2) defines numeric score thresholds corresponding to each
        PCAFC tier;
    (3) minimizes subjective clinical judgment in eligibility
        determinations; and
    (4) is applied uniformly across all Department medical centers.

(b) PRESUMPTIVE ELIGIBILITY.--A veteran shall be presumptively
    eligible for PCAFC enrollment at the tier corresponding to the
    veteran's functional needs if the veteran has a service-connected
    disability rated at 70 percent or higher and has been diagnosed
    with any of the following:
    (1) Traumatic brain injury with functional impairment.
    (2) Spinal cord injury.
    (3) Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS).
    (4) Severe post-traumatic stress disorder with documented
        behavioral health incidents requiring caregiver intervention.
    (5) Polytrauma requiring assistance with 3 or more activities
        of daily living.

(c) PROCESSING TIME.--The Secretary shall process each PCAFC
    application within 45 days of receipt. If the Secretary fails
    to process an application within 45 days--
    (1) the applicant shall be granted provisional enrollment at
        Tier 1 pending completion of the eligibility determination;
        and
    (2) provisional enrollment shall continue until a final
        determination is made.

(d) CONSISTENCY AUDITING.--The Inspector General of the Department
    shall conduct annual audits of PCAFC eligibility determinations
    and report to Congress on--
    (1) approval rates by Department medical center;
    (2) tier assignment distribution by facility;
    (3) facilities with approval rates deviating more than 10
        percentage points from the national average; and
    (4) corrective actions taken.

SEC. 6. REASSESSMENT PROTECTIONS.

(a) GRANDFATHER PROVISION.--A caregiver enrolled in the PCAFC
    before the date of enactment of this Act shall not be
    involuntarily terminated or reduced in tier unless the
    Secretary demonstrates by clear and convincing evidence that
    the veteran's functional status has materially improved.

(b) ANNUAL REVIEW.--The Secretary shall replace comprehensive
    reassessments with annual wellness reviews focused on--
    (1) caregiver support needs and service utilization;
    (2) caregiver health and wellness indicators;
    (3) veteran functional status changes; and
    (4) opportunities for enhanced services.

(c) TRANSITION PROTECTION.--A caregiver involuntarily terminated
    from the PCAFC shall receive--
    (1) not less than 12 months of continued stipend payments at
        75 percent of the caregiver's current stipend level;
    (2) continued CHAMPVA healthcare coverage for 24 months; and
    (3) referral to the Program of General Caregiver Support
        Services and community resources.

(d) INDEPENDENT APPEAL.--A caregiver contesting a termination or
    tier reduction may appeal to an independent review board
    composed of clinicians not affiliated with the enrolling
    Department medical center. The review board shall issue a
    decision within 60 days.

(e) REINSTATEMENT.--Not later than 90 days after the date of
    enactment, the Secretary shall offer reinstatement to every
    caregiver terminated from the PCAFC through reassessment
    between January 1, 2022, and the date of enactment, pending
    individual review under the standardized assessment instrument
    established under section 5.

SEC. 7. RESPITE CARE GUARANTEE.

(a) ANNUAL ENTITLEMENT.--Each caregiver enrolled in the PCAFC
    shall be entitled to not fewer than 60 days of respite care
    per year, available as--
    (1) in-home respite provided by trained respite workers;
    (2) facility-based institutional respite;
    (3) adult day health care; or
    (4) a direct respite stipend of $200 per day, allowing the
        caregiver to arrange respite care independently.

(b) ACCESS STANDARDS.--The Secretary shall ensure that--
    (1) routine respite care is available within 7 days of request;
    (2) emergency respite care is available within 48 hours of
        request; and
    (3) geographic access to respite care providers meets the same
        standards as community care access under section 1703 of
        title 38, United States Code.

(c) AGING CAREGIVERS.--Caregivers over age 60 enrolled in the
    PCAFC shall be entitled to 90 days of respite care per year.

SEC. 8. AGING CAREGIVER PROGRAM.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--The Secretary shall establish within the
    Caregiver Support Program an Aging Caregiver Program to provide
    specialized support for caregivers over age 60.

(b) SERVICES.--The Aging Caregiver Program shall provide--
    (1) comprehensive health and capacity assessments for aging
        caregivers, conducted annually;
    (2) supplemental in-home support (10 to 20 hours per week of
        home health aide assistance) to assist aging caregivers;
    (3) succession planning services for transition of caregiving
        responsibilities when the primary caregiver can no longer
        provide care;
    (4) dual-care coordination integrating the health needs of
        both the veteran and the aging caregiver; and
    (5) enhanced respite care as provided in section 7(c).

(c) ELIGIBILITY.--A caregiver is eligible for the Aging Caregiver
    Program if the caregiver--
    (1) is enrolled in the PCAFC or PGCSS;
    (2) is age 60 or older; and
    (3) provides care to a veteran with a service-connected
        disability.

SEC. 9. CAREGIVER MENTAL HEALTH INTEGRATION.

(a) AUTOMATIC ELIGIBILITY.--Every caregiver enrolled in the PCAFC
    shall be automatically eligible for Department mental health
    services, including individual therapy, group therapy, and
    peer support.

(b) CARE PLAN INTEGRATION.--The Secretary shall require that the
    treatment plan for every veteran with an enrolled family
    caregiver include the caregiver's mental health needs as a
    component of the veteran's care plan.

(c) EVIDENCE-BASED PROGRAMS.--The Secretary shall ensure that
    evidence-based caregiver interventions, including REACH VA
    and Caregiver Skill Building, are available at every Department
    medical center.

(d) PEER SUPPORT.--The Secretary shall establish a national
    caregiver peer support network of not fewer than 5,000 trained
    peer mentors, with peer support available to all enrolled
    caregivers.

SEC. 10. CAREGIVER CREDENTIAL AND CAREER PATHWAY.

(a) CREDENTIAL.--The Secretary shall establish a VA Caregiver
    Skills Certification recognizing training and experience in--
    (1) wound care and medication management;
    (2) behavioral health support and crisis intervention;
    (3) traumatic brain injury care;
    (4) medical device operation; and
    (5) care coordination and system navigation.

(b) ACADEMIC CREDIT.--The Secretary shall enter into agreements
    with accredited nursing programs and allied health programs
    to grant academic credit for VA Caregiver Skills Certification
    and documented caregiving experience.

(c) CAREER SERVICES.--Former PCAFC caregivers transitioning out
    of the caregiving role shall be eligible for career counseling,
    training, and job placement services comparable to those
    provided under chapter 31 of title 38, United States Code.

(d) VA HIRING PREFERENCE.--Former PCAFC caregivers with relevant
    skills and certification shall receive preference in Department
    hiring, as determined by the Secretary.

SEC. 11. CARMA IT SYSTEM REPLACEMENT.

The Secretary shall, not later than 3 years after the date of
enactment, replace the Caregiver Record Management Application
(CARMA) with a modern digital platform that--
(1) provides online application, status tracking, and stipend
    management for caregivers;
(2) includes a mobile application for communication with the
    Department, appointment scheduling, and resource access;
(3) integrates with Department electronic health record systems
    and the Veterans Benefits Management System; and
(4) automates eligibility pre-screening and document verification.

SEC. 12. INTERAGENCY CAREGIVER COORDINATION.

(a) COUNCIL.--The President shall establish a Federal Caregiver
    Coordination Council, co-chaired by the Secretary of Veterans
    Affairs and the Secretary of Health and Human Services.

(b) FUNCTIONS.--The Council shall--
    (1) coordinate Department caregiver programs with Medicaid
        home and community-based services waiver programs;
    (2) align Department of Labor workplace protection enforcement
        with military caregiver status;
    (3) coordinate Social Security Administration crediting with
        Department enrollment data;
    (4) integrate state caregiver support programs with federal
        benefits; and
    (5) submit an annual report to Congress on the veteran caregiver
        support ecosystem.

SEC. 13. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There is authorized to be appropriated to carry out this Act--
(1) $500,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
(2) $2,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2028;
(3) $3,500,000,000 for fiscal year 2029;
(4) $5,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2030; and
(5) such sums as may be necessary for each fiscal year thereafter.

These amounts are in addition to amounts otherwise authorized for
the Program of Comprehensive Assistance for Family Caregivers.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3** addresses the most immediate financial inequity by rebenchmarking stipends to skilled care wages rather than home health aide wages, adding skill-based supplements, and creating a caregiver-specific cost index.
- **Section 4** corrects the structural penalty on caregivers' retirement security by providing Social Security credits for PCAFC stipends, including a retroactive option.
- **Section 5** eliminates the eligibility lottery by replacing subjective clinical assessments with standardized, objective tools and creating presumptive eligibility for clearly qualifying conditions.
- **Section 6** rebuilds trust by protecting enrolled caregivers from arbitrary reassessment termination, providing transition protections, and offering reinstatement to those terminated in 2022-2023.
- **Section 7** transforms respite care from a rationed benefit to a guaranteed entitlement with on-demand access standards.
- **Section 8** creates the first specialized program for aging caregivers, addressing the unique needs of elderly caregivers of Vietnam and Korea-era veterans.
- **Section 9** integrates caregiver mental health into the veteran's care plan rather than treating it as a separate, peripheral service.
- **Section 10** recognizes caregiver skills formally and creates career pathways to prevent the economic dead end that caregiving can become.
- **Sections 11-12** address systemic infrastructure (IT replacement, interagency coordination).

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Total cost ($5B+/year at scale) | Represents a fraction of the $14B annual value of caregiver labor and a fraction of institutional care alternatives ($25-35B) |
| Social Security precedent | Limited to PCAFC caregivers (37,000-150,000); multiple OECD countries provide caregiver pension credits |
| SSA implementation complexity | 18-month implementation timeline provides adequate lead time; similar deemed wage systems exist for military service |
| Presumptive eligibility scope | Limited to conditions with unambiguous caregiving needs; preserves clinical assessment for ambiguous cases |
| Reinstatement of terminated caregivers | Morally necessary; individual review under new standards prevents blanket reinstatement without assessment |

**Refinements**:

- **Fallback position**: If comprehensive bill is infeasible, pursue sections individually: stipend reform (Section 3), reassessment protections (Section 6), and respite guarantee (Section 7) as highest-priority standalone bills
- **Pilot approach**: Aging Caregiver Program (Section 8) and caregiver credential (Section 10) could be authorized as 5-year pilots before national rollout
- **Phased stipend increase**: If immediate LPN-benchmark shift is fiscally difficult, phase the increase over 3 years (Year 1: +15%, Year 2: +15%, Year 3: remaining gap)

### Military Caregiver Employment Protection Act

**Purpose**: Federal workplace protections for military and veteran caregivers.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Military Caregiver Employment
Protection Act".

SEC. 2. PROHIBITION ON CAREGIVER DISCRIMINATION.

(a) UNLAWFUL EMPLOYMENT PRACTICES.--It shall be an unlawful
    employment practice for an employer to--
    (1) fail or refuse to hire, or to discharge, any individual
        because of such individual's status as a military or
        veteran caregiver;
    (2) discriminate against any individual with respect to
        compensation, terms, conditions, or privileges of
        employment because of such status; or
    (3) limit, segregate, or classify employees in any way that
        would deprive or tend to deprive any individual of
        employment opportunities because of such status.

(b) DEFINITION.--The term "military or veteran caregiver" means
    an individual who--
    (1) is enrolled in the Program of Comprehensive Assistance
        for Family Caregivers under section 1720G of title 38,
        United States Code; or
    (2) provides regular and substantial care (not less than 20
        hours per week) to a veteran with a service-connected
        disability rated at 50 percent or higher.

SEC. 3. REASONABLE ACCOMMODATION.

(a) DUTY.--An employer shall make reasonable accommodations to the
    known caregiving needs of a qualified military or veteran
    caregiver, unless the employer can demonstrate that the
    accommodation would impose an undue hardship on the operation
    of the business.

(b) ACCOMMODATIONS.--Reasonable accommodations may include--
    (1) modified work schedules or flexible hours;
    (2) remote work arrangements;
    (3) permission to take intermittent leave for medical
        appointments and care emergencies; and
    (4) reassignment to a vacant position.

(c) INTERACTIVE PROCESS.--An employer and caregiver shall engage
    in an interactive process to determine effective reasonable
    accommodations.

SEC. 4. SMALL EMPLOYER SUPPORT.

(a) TAX CREDIT.--An employer with fewer than 50 employees may
    claim a tax credit of up to $5,000 per taxable year for
    expenses incurred in providing reasonable accommodations
    under section 3.

(b) TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE.--The Department of Labor shall provide
    technical assistance to small employers on compliance with
    this Act.

SEC. 5. ENFORCEMENT.

This Act shall be enforced in the same manner and with the same
remedies as the Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment
Rights Act (38 U.S.C. 4301 et seq.).

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 180 days after the date of enactment.
```

## State Model Legislation

### Military Caregiver Support Act (Model)

**Purpose**: State-level legislation supplementing federal caregiver programs with state tax credits, respite funding, and workforce protections.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Military Caregiver Support Act".

SECTION 2. CAREGIVER TAX CREDIT.

(a) A resident of this state who provides care to a veteran with a
    service-connected disability for not less than 20 hours per week
    may claim a refundable income tax credit of up to $3,000 per
    taxable year for unreimbursed caregiving expenses, including
    home modifications, medical supplies, and transportation costs.
(b) The credit shall be reduced by $1 for every $3 of household
    income exceeding $100,000.

SECTION 3. STATE CAREGIVER RESPITE PROGRAM.

(a) The [State Department of Veterans Affairs] shall establish a
    State Caregiver Respite Program to supplement federal respite
    care for military and veteran caregivers.
(b) The program shall provide up to 15 additional days of respite
    care per year, funded through state appropriations, to caregivers
    who have exhausted federal respite entitlements.
(c) Respite care may be delivered through licensed home health
    agencies, adult day care programs, or qualified individual
    respite providers.

SECTION 4. CAREGIVER EMPLOYMENT PROTECTIONS.

(a) It shall be an unlawful employment practice in this state to
    discriminate against an individual on the basis of the
    individual's status as a military or veteran caregiver, as
    defined in Section 5.
(b) A military or veteran caregiver employed in this state shall
    have the right to request flexible work arrangements, and the
    employer shall not deny such request without demonstrating
    that it would cause undue hardship.

SECTION 5. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Military or veteran caregiver" means an individual who is
    enrolled in a federal caregiver support program or who provides
    regular care of not less than 20 hours per week to a veteran
    with a service-connected disability.
(b) "Unreimbursed caregiving expenses" means expenses directly
    related to the care of a disabled veteran that are not
    reimbursed by any federal, state, or private program.

SECTION 6. APPROPRIATION.

There is appropriated from the general fund $[amount] for the
State Caregiver Respite Program established under Section 3.
```

**Adaptations**: States with existing robust veteran programs (Texas, California, Minnesota) may focus on tax credit and respite provisions. States with minimal caregiver infrastructure should adopt all sections.

## Regulatory Framework

### PCAFC Eligibility Standards Revision

**Existing Authority**: 38 U.S.C. Section 1720G authorizes the Secretary to establish eligibility criteria and clinical assessment processes for the PCAFC.

**Proposed Regulation**:

The Secretary should promulgate a rule amending 38 C.F.R. Part 71 to:

1. **Adopt standardized functional assessment**: Replace the current subjective clinical assessment with a validated, scoring-based instrument (modified Katz ADL scale or equivalent) with defined score thresholds for each PCAFC tier
2. **Establish presumptive eligibility categories**: Create a list of diagnosed conditions with associated disability ratings that presumptively qualify veterans for PCAFC enrollment
3. **Define geographic consistency requirements**: Require that PCAFC approval rates at any VA medical center not deviate by more than 10 percentage points from the national average, with mandatory corrective action for outlier facilities
4. **Impose processing time standards**: Require application processing within 45 days, with provisional enrollment triggered by VA failure to meet the deadline

### Respite Care Access Standards

**Existing Authority**: 38 U.S.C. Section 1720G(a)(3)(A)(ii) provides for respite care services for PCAFC caregivers.

**Proposed Regulation**:

The Secretary should promulgate a rule establishing:

1. **Minimum annual respite entitlement**: 60 days for all PCAFC caregivers; 90 days for caregivers over age 60
2. **Access time standards**: Routine respite within 7 calendar days of request; emergency respite within 48 hours
3. **Flexible delivery models**: In-home, facility-based, adult day care, or direct stipend options
4. **Geographic access**: Respite care available within the same access standards as community care under 38 C.F.R. Section 17.4040

### Caregiver Stipend Rate Methodology

**Existing Authority**: 38 U.S.C. Section 1720G(a)(3)(C) directs the Secretary to set stipend rates.

**Proposed Administrative Action**: The Secretary should modify the stipend rate methodology to:

1. Benchmark to licensed practical nurse wages (BLS SOC 29-2061) rather than home health aide wages (BLS SOC 31-1120)
2. Apply local geographic adjustments using GSA locality pay areas rather than broad BLS regional averages
3. Include skill-based supplemental adjustments for caregivers managing complex conditions
4. Adjust annually using a caregiver-specific cost index incorporating healthcare, housing, and transportation costs

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Spending power**: All proposed programs are authorized expenditures under Congress's spending power. The federal government's obligation to provide veteran benefits is well-established under Article I, Section 8 and the Fourteenth Amendment.
- **Social Security crediting**: Congress has authority to define covered wages under the Social Security Act. Deemed wage provisions have precedent in military service credits (38 U.S.C. Section 3101 et seq.) and gratuitous wage credits for WWII-era service.
- **Employment discrimination**: Federal authority to prohibit employment discrimination is well-established under the Commerce Clause. The proposed Military Caregiver Employment Protection Act is modeled on USERRA, which has been upheld as valid exercise of federal power.
- **Tenth Amendment**: The proposed legislation does not mandate state action. State model legislation operates through state sovereign authority.

### Preemption Questions

- **State caregiver programs**: Federal legislation does not preempt more generous state caregiver programs. The proposed federal law establishes a floor, not a ceiling.
- **FMLA interaction**: The Military Caregiver Employment Protection Act supplements but does not replace existing FMLA Military Caregiver Leave provisions.
- **ERISA**: Employer tax credits and accommodation requirements must be structured to avoid ERISA preemption issues for employer benefit plans.

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- **PCAFC standards**: VA OIG annual auditing with Congressional reporting creates transparency
- **Processing time mandate**: Provisional enrollment triggered by VA failure creates self-enforcing incentive
- **Employment protections**: USERRA-equivalent enforcement through DOL Veterans' Employment and Training Service (VETS)
- **Reassessment protections**: Independent appeal board provides due process; clear and convincing evidence standard raises termination threshold

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Veteran Caregiver Empowerment and Fair Compensation Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Presumptive eligibility circumvention | VA could interpret "functional impairment" or "documented behavioral health incidents" narrowly, effectively nullifying presumptive categories | High |
| Stipend benchmark manipulation | VA could select the lowest-paying LPN wage data available rather than median or prevailing rates | Medium |
| Provisional enrollment gaming | VA could process applications at 44 days to avoid provisional enrollment while still causing unreasonable delays | Medium |
| Transition stipend cliff | 12 months at 75% still creates a cliff; terminated caregivers face financial shock at month 13 | Medium |
| Aging caregiver program underfunding | Authorization without sufficient appropriation renders the program nominal | High |
| CARMA replacement delay | IT procurement timelines frequently slip; 3-year mandate may prove optimistic | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address non-PCAFC caregivers directly | 99% of veteran caregivers remain outside the stipend program | PCAFC eligibility structure limits scope; full universalization exceeds current fiscal framework |
| Social Security credits limited to PCAFC enrollees | Caregivers not in PCAFC (the vast majority) receive no retirement protection | Scoring costs of broader crediting are prohibitive |
| No mandate for caregiver training standards | Training quality varies across VA medical centers | Training is administrative, not legislative; difficult to standardize in statute |
| Cultural barriers unaddressed by legislation | Stigma and veteran resistance reduce program utilization | Cultural change requires community engagement, not statutes |
| Does not reform CHAMPVA adequacy | CHAMPVA coverage gaps (dental, vision) remain for non-PCAFC caregivers | CHAMPVA reform is a separate legislative vehicle |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Presumptive eligibility specification**: Add regulatory guidance requiring the Secretary to define "functional impairment" and "documented behavioral health incidents" through notice-and-comment rulemaking within 12 months, with stakeholder input from caregiver organizations
2. **Stipend benchmark precision**: Amend Section 3 to specify the use of the Bureau of Labor Statistics' 50th percentile (median) wage for Licensed Practical Nurses (SOC 29-2061) in the veteran's metropolitan or nonmetropolitan statistical area
3. **Processing time precision**: Amend Section 5(c) to specify that the 45-day clock begins on the date the VA acknowledges receipt of the application, not the date of a "complete" application (which the VA could delay by requesting additional documentation)
4. **Transition period extension**: Amend Section 6(c) to provide a graduated transition: 100% of stipend for months 1-6, 75% for months 7-12, 50% for months 13-18, with continued CHAMPVA for 36 months
5. **Aging caregiver program mandatory funding**: Reclassify the Aging Caregiver Program as mandatory spending (not discretionary) to prevent appropriations shortfalls
6. **IT procurement safeguards**: Require quarterly progress reports to Congress on CARMA replacement, with automatic extension of the existing system's operational support if the replacement is not certified ready

### Military Caregiver Employment Protection Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| "Undue hardship" overuse | Employers could claim undue hardship for nearly any accommodation request | Medium |
| 20-hour threshold gaming | Employers could require caregivers to document 20 hours weekly, creating privacy and verification burdens | Low |
| Contractor exclusion | Independent contractors are not covered; caregivers in gig work receive no protection | Medium |
| Retaliation through performance management | Employers could use performance standards to constructively terminate caregivers | Medium |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Undue hardship definition**: Add regulatory guidance specifying that undue hardship requires documented evidence of significant difficulty or expense, not merely inconvenience, using the same standards as ADA undue hardship analysis
2. **Self-certification**: Allow caregivers to self-certify their status through PCAFC enrollment documentation or a signed statement, without requiring ongoing hour-by-hour verification
3. **Anti-retaliation provision**: Add explicit anti-retaliation language modeled on USERRA Section 4311, prohibiting adverse action within 12 months of an accommodation request absent documented, pre-existing performance concerns

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| VA institutional culture resists caregiver integration | Mandate caregiver inclusion in treatment planning through Section 9; create caregiver ombudsman positions |
| Interagency coordination historically weak | Codify Federal Caregiver Coordination Council with statutory reporting requirements (Section 12) |
| IT procurement delays are endemic | Phased CARMA replacement with interim manual processing surge |
| Budget scoring disadvantages caregiver expansion | Require CBO to score avoided institutional care costs as offsets in caregiver program estimates |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- **Year 3**: GAO interim evaluation of eligibility simplification, reassessment protections, and stipend reform implementation
- **Year 5**: Comprehensive GAO evaluation of all programs; Secretary reports to Congress with recommendations
- **Year 7**: Congressional reauthorization hearing with updated cost-benefit analysis comparing caregiver program costs to institutional care alternatives
- **Year 10**: Full reauthorization with evidence-based adjustments
