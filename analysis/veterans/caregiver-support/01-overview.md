# Caregiver Support: Overview

## Executive Summary

Family caregivers of disabled and injured veterans provide an essential but largely invisible service that saves the federal government billions of dollars annually while bearing enormous personal costs in lost income, physical health deterioration, and psychological distress. An estimated 5.5 million Americans serve as caregivers to veterans, with approximately 1.1 million providing intensive care (more than 20 hours per week) to veterans with serious injuries or illnesses related to military service (RAND Corporation, *Hidden Heroes*, 2023 update). These caregivers--predominantly spouses, parents, and adult children--manage complex medical conditions including traumatic brain injury, spinal cord injury, post-traumatic stress disorder, and the effects of toxic exposures, often with inadequate training, financial support, and respite.

The federal government's primary caregiver support program, the Program of Comprehensive Assistance for Family Caregivers (PCAFC), was originally limited to post-9/11 veterans under the Caregivers and Veterans Omnibus Health Services Act of 2010. The VA MISSION Act of 2018 mandated expansion to all eras of service, but implementation has been marked by delays, IT system failures, controversial eligibility reassessments that terminated thousands of caregivers, and a persistent gap between the program's promise and its delivery. As of FY 2024, approximately 37,000 caregivers are enrolled in the PCAFC, a fraction of those who qualify (VA Caregiver Support Program, 2024).

Beyond the PCAFC, the broader ecosystem of caregiver support--including respite care, mental health services, financial assistance, and workplace protections--remains fragmented, underfunded, and poorly coordinated. Caregiver burnout rates exceed 40%, with associated increases in depression, anxiety, and physical illness that ultimately degrade the quality of care veterans receive (Elizabeth Dole Foundation, *Caregiving in the U.S.*, 2023).

## Core Tensions

- **Institutional care vs. home-based care**: The VA system is designed around institutional service delivery, but most veteran care occurs at home through family caregivers who receive a fraction of what institutional care would cost
- **Post-9/11 vs. all-era equity**: The original limitation of PCAFC to post-9/11 veterans created a two-tier caregiver system; expansion has been administratively troubled
- **Stipend adequacy vs. fiscal constraint**: Current stipends do not compensate caregivers at rates comparable to professional care; increasing stipends raises significant budget concerns
- **Eligibility rigor vs. access**: Tightening eligibility criteria reduces costs and prevents abuse but excludes caregivers who provide essential services
- **Caregiver autonomy vs. VA oversight**: Caregivers operate independently in the home environment, creating tension between VA accountability for care quality and caregiver independence

## Key Questions

1. **Why has PCAFC expansion been so troubled?** What administrative, technological, and policy factors have undermined the MISSION Act's mandate?
2. **How should caregiver stipends be structured?** What level of compensation reflects the true value of caregiver labor while remaining fiscally sustainable?
3. **What support do caregivers need beyond stipends?** How effective are respite care, mental health services, and training programs?
4. **How should aging caregivers be supported?** What happens when the caregiver is elderly and becoming disabled themselves?
5. **What workplace protections do caregivers need?** How can employment law be reformed to prevent caregiver discrimination?

## Vision of Success

A successful veteran caregiver support system would be characterized by: PCAFC enrollment that reflects the actual population of eligible caregivers across all eras of service; stipends that approximate the market value of comparable professional care; comprehensive respite care available on demand rather than by rationing; mental health services specifically designed for caregivers integrated into VA care plans; workplace protections that prevent employment discrimination based on caregiver status; Social Security credits that prevent caregivers from being penalized in retirement; a smooth eligibility process that does not traumatize caregivers through arbitrary reassessments and terminations; and a cultural shift within the VA that recognizes caregivers as essential partners in veteran healthcare rather than ancillary beneficiaries.
