# Caregiver Support: What Citizens Can Do

## Overview

Improving support for the 5.5 million Americans who care for disabled and injured veterans requires action at every level--from individual citizens and caregivers themselves to community organizations, employers, and state governments. This document provides concrete, actionable steps organized by audience and effort level.

## For All Citizens

### Learn and Share

1. **Understand the caregiver crisis**: The basic facts are not widely known. Only 37,000 of an estimated 1.1 million intensive veteran caregivers receive PCAFC stipends. Caregiver stipends cover 50-68% of what the VA would pay for equivalent professional care. More than half of veteran caregivers screen positive for depression. Share these facts and the Elizabeth Dole Foundation's *Hidden Heroes* materials (hiddenheroes.org) with your community.

2. **Contact your representatives**: Call or write your U.S. Senators and Representative to:
    - Support the Caregiver COLA Act (caregiver-specific cost-of-living adjustments)
    - Support the Military and Veteran Caregiver Services Improvement Act (Social Security credits)
    - Advocate for PCAFC stipend increases benchmarked to skilled nursing wages
    - Request oversight of PCAFC reassessment practices and eligibility consistency
    - Support workplace protections for military and veteran caregivers

3. **Support caregiver organizations**: Directed donations to organizations with demonstrated caregiver programs, including:
    - Elizabeth Dole Foundation (hiddenheroes.org)--caregiver fellowships, research, advocacy
    - Wounded Warrior Project (woundedwarriorproject.org)--caregiver direct services
    - Hope For The Warriors (hopeforthewarriors.org)--caregiver respite and wellness
    - Give an Hour (giveanhour.org)--free mental health services for military families
    - Fisher House Foundation (fisherhouse.org)--free lodging near VA medical centers

4. **Nominate a caregiver for recognition**: The Elizabeth Dole Foundation's Dole Caregiver Fellow program identifies and supports caregiver leaders. Nominate someone you know at hiddenheroes.org.

## For Veteran Caregivers

### Access Available Resources

5. **Apply for the PCAFC**: If you provide care for a veteran with a service-connected disability and are not enrolled in the PCAFC, apply through your VA medical center's Caregiver Support Coordinator or call the VA Caregiver Support Line at 1-855-260-3274. Enrollment provides monthly stipends, CHAMPVA healthcare coverage, mental health services, and respite care.

6. **Use the VA Caregiver Support Line**: Call 1-855-260-3274 for 24/7 support, information about available programs, and crisis assistance. The support line serves all veteran caregivers, not just PCAFC enrollees.

7. **Request respite care**: PCAFC caregivers are entitled to up to 30 days of respite care annually. Contact your VA medical center to schedule in-home, facility-based, or adult day care respite. If wait times exceed 14 days, escalate to the facility's Patient Advocate.

8. **Access mental health services**: Veteran caregivers enrolled in the PCAFC are eligible for VA mental health services including individual therapy, group therapy, and peer support. Ask your Caregiver Support Coordinator about REACH VA, an evidence-based caregiver intervention program available at many VA medical centers.

9. **File for all applicable benefits**: Beyond the PCAFC, ensure the veteran has applied for:
    - Aid and Attendance benefit (additional disability compensation for veterans needing caregiver assistance)
    - Special Monthly Compensation (for severe disabilities)
    - Housebound benefit (if applicable)
    - PACT Act toxic exposure presumptions (may increase disability rating and PCAFC eligibility)

10. **Know your FMLA rights**: If you are employed, you may be eligible for up to 26 weeks of unpaid leave under the Military Caregiver Leave provision of the FMLA. Contact your employer's HR department or the Department of Labor at 1-866-487-9243.

### Connect and Advocate

11. **Join a caregiver peer support group**: Connect with other caregivers through:
    - VA Peer Support Mentoring Program (through your VA medical center)
    - Elizabeth Dole Foundation peer support network (hiddenheroes.org)
    - Wounded Warrior Project Peer Support program
    - Blue Star Families community events

12. **Share your experience with Congress**: Caregiver testimony is the most powerful advocacy tool. Contact your Senators' and Representative's offices to share your story. Ask to meet with the staff member handling veterans' affairs. The Elizabeth Dole Foundation can help connect caregivers with congressional advocacy opportunities.

13. **Participate in VA feedback mechanisms**: Complete VA SHEP (Survey of Healthcare Experiences of Patients) surveys when offered. Contact the VA Patient Advocate at your medical center to report concerns about PCAFC administration. File formal complaints if you experience inconsistent eligibility determinations or unreasonable processing delays.

14. **Appeal if terminated or denied**: If you are denied PCAFC enrollment or terminated through reassessment, you have the right to appeal. Request a Clinical Appeal Review within 90 days of the decision. Contact a Veterans Service Organization (DAV, PVA, or VFW) for free assistance with the appeals process.

15. **Document your caregiving**: Keep detailed records of caregiving hours, expenses, medical appointments, and the veteran's functional needs. These records are essential for PCAFC applications, appeals, and congressional testimony.

## For Community Organizations

### Build Caregiver Support Infrastructure

16. **Establish caregiver support groups**: Community organizations, houses of worship, and VSO posts can host monthly caregiver support groups. The VA's Peer Support Toolkit provides guidance, and the Elizabeth Dole Foundation offers training for peer support facilitators.

17. **Provide respite volunteering**: Organize trained volunteer respite caregivers who can provide temporary relief for veteran caregivers. The Caregiver Action Network (caregiveraction.org) provides respite volunteer training resources.

18. **Host caregiver resource fairs**: Partner with your local VA medical center's Caregiver Support Coordinator to organize community events where caregivers can learn about available programs, meet other caregivers, and access services.

19. **Apply for caregiver support grants**: The Elizabeth Dole Foundation, Bob Woodruff Foundation, and other organizations provide grants to community-based caregiver support programs. The Staff Sergeant Parker Gordon Fox Suicide Prevention Grant Program also funds caregiver-adjacent services.

20. **Create emergency caregiver plans**: Help caregivers develop backup care plans for emergencies--if the caregiver is hospitalized or otherwise unable to provide care, who steps in? Community organizations can maintain registries of trained backup caregivers.

## For Employers

### Support Caregiver Employees

21. **Adopt flexible scheduling policies**: Allow veteran caregivers to adjust work hours around VA appointments, care emergencies, and respite schedules. Flexible scheduling is the single most impactful employer accommodation and costs nothing beyond managerial willingness.

22. **Provide caregiver-specific leave**: Beyond FMLA requirements, consider paid caregiver leave policies. Companies that offer 5-10 days of paid caregiver leave report higher retention and lower recruitment costs for affected employees (SHRM, 2024).

23. **Train managers on caregiver awareness**: Ensure supervisors understand that caregiver employees are not less committed but face unique scheduling constraints. The Elizabeth Dole Foundation offers employer training resources.

24. **Participate in the Hidden Heroes Cities initiative**: Cities and companies can join the Elizabeth Dole Foundation's Hidden Heroes initiative to demonstrate commitment to supporting military and veteran caregivers.

25. **Hire former caregivers**: Recognize that caregivers who spent years managing complex medical conditions, navigating bureaucratic systems, and coordinating care have developed transferable skills. Consider caregiver experience as relevant work experience in healthcare, social services, and administrative roles.

## For Healthcare Providers

### Serve Caregiver Patients

26. **Screen for caregiver burden**: If you treat patients who are also veteran caregivers, screen for caregiver burden, depression, and anxiety. The Zarit Burden Interview and the PHQ-9 are validated instruments. Refer to VA caregiver mental health services or community mental health providers.

27. **Coordinate with VA**: If your patient is a PCAFC caregiver, coordinate with the veteran's VA care team. Caregiver health directly affects veteran outcomes. Contact the VA medical center's Caregiver Support Coordinator.

28. **Learn about caregiver-specific conditions**: Secondary traumatic stress, compassion fatigue, and caregiver burnout are distinct clinical entities with evidence-based treatments. The Rosalynn Carter Institute for Caregivers (rosalynncarter.org) provides clinical training resources.

## For State and Local Government

### Policy and Funding

29. **Establish state caregiver tax credits**: States can supplement federal support with state-level tax credits for veteran caregivers. Maryland, Minnesota, and Oregon provide models of state caregiver tax provisions.

30. **Fund state caregiver respite programs**: State-funded respite care programs can supplement VA respite, reducing wait times and expanding options. The ARCH National Respite Network (archrespite.org) provides state program models.

31. **Include caregivers in workforce development**: State workforce agencies can create pathways for veteran caregivers to obtain healthcare credentials based on their caregiving experience, facilitating career transitions.

32. **Support caregiver-friendly employer recognition**: State programs recognizing employers who adopt caregiver-supportive policies (flexible scheduling, caregiver leave) can encourage private-sector accommodation without mandates.

33. **Include caregiver needs in state veterans' strategic plans**: State Departments of Veterans Affairs should incorporate caregiver support metrics and programs into their strategic plans, with dedicated staff and funding.
