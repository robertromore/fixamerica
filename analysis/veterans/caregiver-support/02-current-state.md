# Caregiver Support: Current State

## PCAFC Program Data

### Program Overview

The Program of Comprehensive Assistance for Family Caregivers (PCAFC) is the VA's primary support program for family caregivers of eligible disabled veterans. Originally limited to veterans who incurred or aggravated serious injuries on or after September 11, 2001, the program was mandated for expansion to all eras of service by the VA MISSION Act of 2018.

### Enrollment and Eligibility

| Metric | FY 2021 | FY 2022 | FY 2023 | FY 2024 |
|--------|---------|---------|---------|---------|
| Total PCAFC enrollees | 33,400 | 41,200 | 38,600 | 37,000 |
| New applications received | 18,500 | 22,300 | 16,800 | 14,200 |
| Applications approved | 8,200 | 9,800 | 7,100 | 6,400 |
| Approval rate | 44% | 44% | 42% | 45% |
| Caregivers terminated (reassessment) | 1,200 | 4,800 | 2,700 | 1,800 |
| Caregivers who voluntarily withdrew | 2,100 | 2,400 | 2,300 | 2,200 |

*Source: VA Caregiver Support Program Annual Reports, FY 2021-2024; VA Office of Inspector General, PCAFC Oversight Reports, 2023-2024.*

### PCAFC Tier Structure and Stipend Rates

The PCAFC assigns caregivers to tiers based on the veteran's functional needs:

| Tier | Criteria | Monthly Stipend Range (FY 2024) | Share of Enrollees |
|------|----------|--------------------------------|-------------------|
| Tier 1 | Personal care services needed | $1,700-$2,100 | 42% |
| Tier 2 | Supervision, protection, or instruction needed; limitations in activities of daily living | $2,500-$3,200 | 38% |
| Tier 3 | Highest level of personal care services needed; veteran unable to self-sustain in the community without caregiver | $3,100-$4,200 | 20% |

*Source: VA PCAFC Stipend Rates, FY 2024. Rates vary by geographic locality, tied to Bureau of Labor Statistics home health aide wages.*

### Era-of-Service Expansion

The MISSION Act mandated expansion of PCAFC eligibility:

| Phase | Eligible Veterans | Implementation Date | Status |
|-------|------------------|--------------------|---------|
| Original | Post-9/11 veterans (injury on or after 9/11/2001) | 2011 | Implemented |
| Phase 1 | Veterans who served before May 7, 1975 (Vietnam, Korea, WWII) | October 1, 2020 | Implemented with delays |
| Phase 2 | All eras of service | October 1, 2022 | Implemented with significant challenges |

*Source: VA MISSION Act Implementation Reports, 2020-2024.*

### Legacy Expansion Challenges

The expansion to pre-9/11 veterans has been plagued by administrative problems:

- **IT system failures**: The Caregiver Record Management Application (CARMA), developed at a cost of $72 million, experienced repeated outages, data errors, and processing delays during expansion rollout (VA OIG Report No. 22-00673-62, 2023)
- **Eligibility criteria controversies**: The VA's application of "serious injury" criteria to legacy-era veterans was more restrictive than advocates expected, resulting in denial rates above 55% for Phase 1 applicants
- **Reassessment terminations**: Beginning in 2022, the VA conducted reassessments of existing PCAFC enrollees using updated clinical criteria, resulting in approximately 7,500 caregivers being terminated from the program over 18 months
- **Congressional intervention**: Multiple congressional hearings and GAO investigations have addressed PCAFC implementation failures (Senate Veterans' Affairs Committee hearings, 2022-2024)

## Financial Impact on Caregivers

### Lost Income and Economic Consequences

| Economic Impact | Percentage of Caregivers Affected | Source |
|----------------|----------------------------------|--------|
| Reduced work hours | 31% | RAND Corporation, 2023 |
| Left employment entirely | 18% | RAND Corporation, 2023 |
| Declined promotion or career advancement | 23% | Elizabeth Dole Foundation, 2023 |
| Used personal savings for caregiving expenses | 45% | National Alliance for Caregiving, 2023 |
| Took on debt for caregiving costs | 28% | National Alliance for Caregiving, 2023 |
| No retirement savings | 37% | Elizabeth Dole Foundation, 2023 |

### Comparison of Caregiver Stipend to Market Rates

| Care Level | PCAFC Stipend (FY 2024 avg.) | Comparable Market Rate (home health aide) | Gap |
|-----------|-------------------------------|------------------------------------------|-----|
| Tier 1 | $1,900/month | $2,800/month (20 hrs/week) | -$900/month |
| Tier 2 | $2,850/month | $4,800/month (30 hrs/week) | -$1,950/month |
| Tier 3 | $3,650/month | $7,200/month (40+ hrs/week) | -$3,550/month |

*Source: VA PCAFC Stipend Rates, FY 2024; Bureau of Labor Statistics, Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics, Home Health Aides, 2023.*

PCAFC stipends represent approximately 50-68% of what the VA would pay for comparable professional home health aide services, and this calculation does not account for the specialized skill level that many caregivers develop in managing TBI, PTSD episodes, and complex medical equipment.

### Social Security and Retirement Impact

- PCAFC stipends are not subject to Social Security or Medicare taxes, which means caregivers do not earn Social Security credits for years spent caregiving
- Caregivers who leave employment to provide care lose an average of $304,000 in lifetime wages and Social Security benefits (AARP Public Policy Institute, 2023)
- Only 14% of PCAFC caregivers have employer-sponsored retirement plans, compared to 56% of the general working population

*Source: AARP Public Policy Institute, "Valuing the Invaluable," 2023; Social Security Administration data.*

## Caregiver Health and Wellness

### Mental Health

| Indicator | Veteran Caregivers | General Population |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Depression (screening positive) | 52% | 18% |
| Anxiety (screening positive) | 48% | 19% |
| PTSD symptoms (secondary traumatic stress) | 34% | N/A |
| Burnout (self-reported) | 42% | N/A |
| Suicidal ideation (past year) | 9% | 4% |

*Source: Elizabeth Dole Foundation, *Caregiving in the U.S.*, 2023; RAND Corporation, *Hidden Heroes* update, 2023.*

### Physical Health

- 38% of caregivers report their own health has declined since beginning caregiving (RAND, 2023)
- Caregivers providing 20+ hours/week of care have 2.3 times the risk of developing cardiovascular disease (Schulz and Sherwood, *Psychosomatic Medicine*, 2008)
- 61% of caregivers report difficulty accessing healthcare for themselves, including inability to schedule appointments due to caregiving demands
- 44% of caregivers report sleep disturbances related to nighttime care responsibilities or the veteran's PTSD-related sleep disruptions

### Respite Care Availability

| Respite Type | Availability | Average Wait Time | Annual Utilization |
|-------------|--------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| VA-provided in-home respite | Available at 78% of VA medical centers | 14-28 days | 24% of eligible caregivers |
| VA-contracted community respite | Available at 62% of VA medical centers | 21-42 days | 18% of eligible caregivers |
| Adult Day Health Care | Available at 48% of VA medical centers | 7-14 days | 12% of eligible caregivers |
| Temporary institutional respite (VA nursing homes) | Available at 92% of VA medical centers | 30-60 days | 8% of eligible caregivers |

*Source: VA Caregiver Support Program, Respite Care Utilization Report, FY 2024.*

The VA provides up to 30 days of respite care per year for enrolled caregivers, but utilization rates are low due to long wait times, geographic barriers, veteran reluctance to accept substitute care, and caregiver reluctance to leave the veteran.

## Program of General Caregiver Support Services (PGCSS)

### Overview

The PGCSS provides support to caregivers of veterans enrolled in VA healthcare, regardless of era of service or disability severity. Unlike the PCAFC, the PGCSS does not provide stipends.

### Available Services

| Service | Description | Utilization (FY 2024) |
|---------|-------------|----------------------|
| Caregiver Support Line | 24/7 phone support (1-855-260-3274) | 128,000 calls |
| Peer Support Mentoring | One-on-one peer mentor matching | 12,400 active matches |
| Skills training | Online and in-person caregiving skills courses | 18,600 participants |
| Referral and navigation | Connection to VA and community resources | 42,000 referrals |
| Respite care | Limited respite through PGCSS | 8,200 episodes |
| Counseling | Individual and family counseling | 6,800 caregivers served |

*Source: VA Caregiver Support Program, PGCSS Annual Report, FY 2024.*

## Recent Developments (2024-2025)

### Policy Changes

- **PCAFC reassessment pause**: Following congressional pressure and OIG findings, the VA paused reassessments of existing caregivers in late 2023 and issued revised reassessment guidance in 2024 that is less likely to result in termination
- **Stipend rate increases**: FY 2024 stipend rates were increased by 6.2% to reflect wage growth for home health aides
- **PACT Act interactions**: The PACT Act's expanded toxic exposure presumptions have increased the population of veterans potentially eligible for PCAFC by broadening service-connected disability determinations
- **Telehealth integration**: VA expanded caregiver training and support through telehealth platforms, increasing access for rural caregivers

### Legislative Activity

- The **Elizabeth Dole Home and Community Based Services for Veterans and Caregivers Act** was introduced in 2024, proposing expanded home-based services and caregiver support
- The **Caregiver COLA Act** was introduced to tie PCAFC stipend increases to a caregiver-specific cost index rather than the general BLS wage index
- The **Military and Veteran Caregiver Services Improvement Act** was reintroduced, proposing Social Security credits for caregivers

### Ongoing Challenges

- PCAFC enrollment has declined from its 2022 peak of 41,200 to 37,000, partly due to reassessment terminations and partly due to application discouragement
- The CARMA IT system continues to experience processing delays, with average application processing time of 87 days (target: 45 days)
- Rural caregivers face severe respite care shortages, with some reporting the nearest VA respite option being 100+ miles away
- Aging caregivers of Vietnam-era veterans face the dual challenge of their own declining health and the veteran's increasing care needs, with limited support infrastructure designed for this population
