# Caregiver Support: Stakeholders

## Overview

Veteran caregiver policy involves stakeholders ranging from the caregivers themselves--who bear the greatest burden with the least institutional power--to the federal agencies, congressional committees, advocacy organizations, and healthcare industries whose decisions shape the support system. Understanding each stakeholder's interests, influence, and constraints is essential to building effective reform coalitions.

---

## Directly Affected Populations

### Family Caregivers of Disabled Veterans

**Population**: Approximately 5.5 million, with 1.1 million providing intensive care (RAND, 2023)

**Demographics**: 85-91% female; average age 42 (PCAFC) to 52 (all veteran caregivers); 72% are spouses/partners

**Interests**:

- Adequate financial compensation for caregiving labor
- Social Security and retirement protections
- Accessible, reliable respite care
- Mental health services addressing caregiver burnout, depression, and secondary traumatic stress
- Stable PCAFC enrollment without arbitrary reassessment terminations
- Simplified application and eligibility processes
- Training in medical care, behavioral health management, and VA system navigation
- Workplace protections for those who maintain employment

**Power and Constraints**:

- Growing political visibility through the Elizabeth Dole Foundation, Dole Caregiver Fellows, and media attention
- Limited organizational capacity: caregivers' time constraints prevent sustained political engagement
- Emotional power of personal testimony before Congress
- Fragmented by service era, condition type, and geography
- PCAFC enrollees have more voice than the millions of non-enrolled caregivers

### Veterans Receiving Family Care

**Population**: Approximately 3.5 million veterans receiving some level of family caregiving

**Interests**:

- Continued home-based care rather than institutionalization
- Preservation of autonomy and dignity
- Caregiver wellness (a healthy caregiver provides better care)
- Access to VA healthcare and disability compensation

**Power and Constraints**:

- Veterans retain significant political influence as a class
- Many veterans with severe disabilities have limited ability to self-advocate
- Veteran identity may conflict with the "care recipient" identity, creating resistance to caregiver programs
- Veterans' advocates (VSOs) may not prioritize caregiver issues relative to direct veteran benefits

### Aging Caregivers

**Population**: An estimated 400,000+ caregivers over age 65 caring for Vietnam, Korea, and WWII-era veterans

**Interests**:

- Support for their own declining health needs while continuing to provide care
- Transition planning for when they can no longer provide care
- Financial security after decades of reduced or forgone earnings
- Recognition of lifetimes of caregiving service

**Power and Constraints**:

- Largely invisible in policy discussions focused on younger post-9/11 caregivers
- Limited organizational representation
- Physical limitations restrict political engagement
- Concentrated advocacy through Vietnam Veterans of America and aging-focused organizations

## Government Institutions

### Department of Veterans Affairs

**Key Subentities**:

| Entity | Role | Key Personnel |
|--------|------|---------------|
| VA Caregiver Support Program | PCAFC and PGCSS administration | ~1,200 staff nationwide |
| Veterans Health Administration | Clinical eligibility determinations | Clinicians at 170+ medical centers |
| Office of Information Technology | CARMA IT system management | Contractor-dependent |
| VA Office of Inspector General | PCAFC oversight and auditing | Independent oversight |
| Caregiver Support Coordinators | Local program coordination | One per VA medical center |

**Interests**: Program effectiveness, budget management, congressional confidence, veteran and caregiver satisfaction

**Constraints**: Budget competition with other VA programs, IT system deficiencies, workforce capacity for caregiver assessments, institutional culture oriented toward facility-based care

### Congressional Committees

**Key Committees**:

- Senate Veterans' Affairs Committee
- House Veterans' Affairs Committee
- Senate Appropriations Subcommittee on Military Construction, Veterans Affairs
- House Appropriations Subcommittee on Military Construction, Veterans Affairs

**Interests**: Constituent service, oversight effectiveness, bipartisan veteran support

**Key Champions**: The caregiver issue has generated bipartisan congressional champions, particularly Senators and Representatives with strong veteran constituencies and military family connections

### Office of Management and Budget

**Role**: Budget scoring, spending caps, cost-effectiveness analysis

**Interests**: Fiscal discipline, accurate cost projections

**Constraints**: Budget scoring rules that treat caregiver stipends as new spending rather than as cost-avoidance relative to institutional care

### Social Security Administration

**Role**: Manages Social Security credits and benefit calculations affected by caregiver work gaps

**Interests**: System integrity, actuarial soundness

**Constraints**: Any change to Social Security crediting rules requires legislative action and SSA administrative capacity

|-------------|---------|----------------|

| Organization | Position on Caregiver Support |
|-------------|-------------------------------|
| Disabled American Veterans (DAV) | Strong advocate for caregiver programs; priority legislative issue |
| Paralyzed Veterans of America (PVA) | Active on caregiver support due to spinal cord injury population |

## Industry Stakeholders

### Home Health Industry

**Who**: Home health agencies, professional caregiver workforce, industry associations (National Association for Home Care & Hospice)

**Interests**: Expansion of VA-funded home health services, workforce development, reimbursement rates

**Relationship to Family Caregivers**: Complex. The home health industry provides professional alternatives to family caregiving but also competes for the same policy attention and funding. Family caregiver stipends at below-market rates could be seen as either complementary (reducing demand on the professional workforce) or competitive (substituting unpaid for paid labor).

### Health Insurance Industry

**Who**: CHAMPVA administrators, private insurance companies

**Interests**: Risk management, cost containment, network adequacy

**Relationship to Caregivers**: PCAFC provides CHAMPVA coverage to enrolled caregivers. Expansion of caregiver health coverage affects insurance risk pools and costs.

### IT Contractors

**Who**: Companies developing and maintaining the CARMA system and VA caregiver support IT infrastructure

**Interests**: Contract revenue, system performance, contract renewal

**Constraints**: CARMA's troubled development and deployment has drawn congressional scrutiny and OIG investigation

## Stakeholder Power Analysis

| Stakeholder | Power Level | Type of Power | Alignment with Reform |
|-------------|-------------|---------------|----------------------|
| Congress (committees) | High | Legislative, appropriations | Strong bipartisan support |
| VA Caregiver Support Program | Moderate | Administrative, programmatic | Moderate (implementation capacity constraints) |
| Elizabeth Dole Foundation | Moderate-High | Advocacy, media, research | Strong |
| DAV/PVA | Moderate | Political influence, lobbying | Strong |
| OMB | High | Budget scoring, fiscal policy | Constraining |
| RAND/research organizations | Moderate | Evidence, expertise | Strong |
| Family caregivers | Low-Moderate | Moral authority, testimony | Strong |
| Aging caregivers | Low | Limited visibility | Strong but unheard |
| Home health industry | Moderate | Economic interest, lobbying | Mixed |
| IT contractors | Low-Moderate | Technical dependency | Neutral |

### Key Dynamic

The caregiver support issue benefits from unusually strong bipartisan congressional support and powerful advocacy organizations (particularly the Elizabeth Dole Foundation), but is constrained by VA administrative capacity, OMB fiscal resistance, and the inherent difficulty of organizing a caregiver population that lacks the time and energy for sustained political engagement.
