# Caregiver Support: History

## Overview

The history of veteran caregiver support in the United States is a story of delayed recognition, piecemeal policy development, and the persistent gap between the rhetorical celebration of military families and the material support provided to those who shoulder the burden of caring for injured and disabled veterans. For most of American history, family caregiving was simply assumed--an unpaid obligation that fell disproportionately on women, was invisible to policymakers, and received no institutional support. Only in the 21st century, driven by the visible casualties of the Iraq and Afghanistan wars and sustained advocacy by caregiver organizations, has federal policy begun to formally recognize and support family caregivers of veterans.

---

## Pre-Modern Era: Invisible Labor (Before 1980)

### Early American Conflicts

- Family caregiving for disabled veterans has existed as long as military conflict, but was entirely unrecognized by law or policy
- After the Civil War, the National Home for Disabled Volunteer Soldiers (established 1865) provided institutional care, but families who kept disabled veterans at home received nothing
- Widows' pensions existed from the Revolutionary War onward, but support for caregivers of living disabled veterans was not considered
- The cultural assumption was that wives, mothers, and daughters would provide care as a natural duty, requiring no compensation or support

### World War I and World War II

- The scale of casualties from both World Wars created large populations of disabled veterans requiring long-term care
- The VA hospital system expanded dramatically, but the expectation remained that family members would provide home-based care between or instead of VA hospitalizations
- The GI Bill (1944) provided education and economic benefits to veterans but nothing to the family members who cared for disabled veterans
- War Risk Insurance and later VA disability compensation provided income to disabled veterans, but no recognition that caregiving labor reduced or eliminated the caregiver's own earning capacity

### Korean and Vietnam Wars

- Agent Orange and combat injuries from Vietnam created complex, chronic care needs that families were expected to manage
- PTSD, first formally diagnosed in 1980, produced behavioral health challenges that fell primarily on family members to manage, often without training or support
- The VA's Community Residential Care program (begun in the 1950s) provided some institutional alternatives but was limited in scope
- No federal policy recognized family caregivers as a distinct population with specific needs

## Emerging Recognition (1980-2000)

### Respite Care Beginnings

- The VA Respite Care Program was first authorized in 1986 as part of a broader veterans' healthcare reform, providing temporary institutional care to give family caregivers a break
- Initial implementation was limited to a small number of VA medical centers, and utilization was low due to lack of awareness and veteran reluctance to enter institutional settings
- The Family Medical Leave Act of 1993 (FMLA) provided unpaid leave for family caregiving but did not cover military-specific caregiving needs and was limited to employers with 50+ employees

### Research and Awareness

- Academic research on caregiver burden began appearing in gerontology literature in the 1980s, but veteran-specific caregiving research was virtually nonexistent until the 2000s
- The National Alliance for Caregiving, founded in 1996, began documenting the scope and impact of family caregiving nationally but did not distinguish veteran caregivers as a subpopulation
- VA-funded research focused on clinical treatment of veterans rather than the impact of care on family members

## Post-9/11 Crisis and Policy Response (2001-2010)

### The Visible Casualties

- The wars in Iraq and Afghanistan produced a new generation of severely injured veterans with conditions--traumatic brain injury, blast injuries, polytrauma, severe PTSD--that required intensive, long-term home-based care
- Advances in battlefield medicine meant that service members survived injuries that would have been fatal in earlier conflicts, creating a population of young, severely disabled veterans requiring decades of care
- Media coverage of families caring for catastrophically injured veterans, particularly the Walter Reed Army Medical Center scandal (2007), brought national attention to caregiver struggles
- Organizations like the Wounded Warrior Project (founded 2003) and the Fisher House Foundation began providing caregiver-adjacent support

### Advocacy Movement

- Military and veteran family advocacy organizations, particularly the Elizabeth Dole Foundation (established 2012 but with precursor advocacy), began specifically advocating for caregiver recognition and support
- Congressional hearings in 2008-2009 heard testimony from caregivers detailing the financial devastation, health consequences, and lack of institutional support they experienced
- Rosalynn Carter (former First Lady and longtime caregiver advocate) and Elizabeth Dole (former Senator and wife of a disabled WWII veteran) provided high-profile advocacy

### Caregivers and Veterans Omnibus Health Services Act of 2010

**Public Law 111-163** was the first comprehensive federal legislation recognizing and supporting family caregivers of veterans:

**Key provisions:**

- Established the Program of Comprehensive Assistance for Family Caregivers (PCAFC) for caregivers of veterans with serious injuries incurred or aggravated in the line of duty on or after September 11, 2001
- PCAFC benefits included monthly stipends, healthcare coverage through CHAMPVA, mental health services, and respite care
- Established the Program of General Caregiver Support Services (PGCSS) for caregivers of all veterans enrolled in VA healthcare
- PGCSS benefits included education and training, caregiver support line, peer support, and counseling
- Required VA to designate a Caregiver Support Coordinator at each VA medical center

**Limitations:**

- PCAFC eligibility was limited to post-9/11 veterans, excluding caregivers of Vietnam, Korea, and WWII veterans with equally severe injuries
- The era-of-service limitation was immediately controversial and criticized as creating a two-tier caregiver system
- No Social Security credits for caregiver labor
- Stipend rates were set below market rates for comparable professional care
- The VA was slow to implement the law, with full program rollout not occurring until 2012

## Expansion Struggle (2010-2020)

### Two-Tier System Criticism

- Advocacy organizations, particularly Vietnam Veterans of America and the National Alliance for Caregiving, repeatedly called for PCAFC expansion to all eras of service
- Congressional hearings highlighted the inequity: a caregiver of a Vietnam veteran with TBI from combat received no PCAFC benefits, while a caregiver of a post-9/11 veteran with identical TBI received stipends, healthcare, and respite care
- The VA estimated that expansion to all eras would cost $5-10 billion over 10 years, creating budget resistance

### PCAFC Program Challenges (2012-2018)

- PCAFC enrollment grew from 5,500 in FY 2012 to over 25,000 by FY 2018
- The program experienced inconsistent eligibility determinations across VA medical centers, with approval rates varying from 28% to 62% depending on location (VA OIG, 2018)
- Clinical eligibility criteria were ambiguous, leading to disputes about what constituted a "serious injury" and what level of functional impairment qualified for each tier
- Caregiver stipend rates were periodically adjusted but remained below market rates for professional home health aide services

### VA MISSION Act of 2018

**Public Law 115-182, Section 161** mandated expansion of PCAFC eligibility:

**Key provisions:**

- Phase 1 (October 1, 2020): Expansion to veterans who served before May 7, 1975 (Vietnam, Korea, WWII eras)
- Phase 2 (October 1, 2022): Expansion to all eras of service
- Required implementation of a new IT system (CARMA) for program management
- Required development of uniform eligibility criteria
- Directed the VA to establish a new clinical assessment process for PCAFC eligibility

**Implementation problems:**

- The VA delayed Phase 1 implementation, citing IT system readiness concerns
- The CARMA IT system, developed by a private contractor at a cost of $72 million, failed repeatedly during rollout
- Eligibility criteria developed for expansion were more restrictive than the criteria used for the original post-9/11 population, resulting in high denial rates for legacy applicants
- Congressional oversight hearings in 2020-2021 documented widespread frustration among caregivers attempting to navigate the expansion process

## Reassessment Crisis and Current Challenges (2020-Present)

### The Reassessment Controversy

Beginning in 2022, the VA conducted reassessments of existing PCAFC enrollees using the new clinical criteria developed for MISSION Act expansion:

- Approximately 7,500 caregivers were terminated from the PCAFC between 2022 and 2023 after reassessment found they no longer met eligibility criteria under the new standards
- Caregiver advocacy organizations argued that the reassessments applied more restrictive criteria retroactively, penalizing caregivers who had relied on PCAFC support for years
- Multiple caregivers testified before Congress that termination from the program occurred without adequate notice, appeal opportunity, or transition support
- VA OIG Report No. 22-00673-62 (2023) found "significant inconsistencies" in reassessment determinations across VA medical centers
- Congressional pressure led to a reassessment pause in late 2023 and revised guidance in 2024

### Elizabeth Dole Foundation Impact

- The Elizabeth Dole Foundation's *Hidden Heroes* campaign (launched 2014) transformed public awareness of military and veteran caregivers
- The Foundation's partnership with RAND Corporation produced the landmark *Hidden Heroes: America's Military Caregivers* study (2014, updated 2023), providing the first comprehensive data on the caregiver population
- Senator Elizabeth Dole's personal advocacy--as the wife of the late Senator Bob Dole, a severely disabled WWII veteran--gave the issue bipartisan credibility
- The Foundation has supported caregiver fellowship programs, peer support networks, and research that have shaped policy discussions

## Lessons from History

### Recurring Patterns

1. **Invisible labor made visible only by crisis**: Caregiver support became a policy issue only when the visible casualties of Iraq and Afghanistan created a narrative that media and Congress could not ignore. Caregivers of Vietnam and Korean War veterans provided the same labor for decades without recognition.
2. **Legislation followed by inadequate implementation**: Both the 2010 Act and the 2018 MISSION Act established strong mandates but were undermined by administrative delays, IT failures, and restrictive interpretation of eligibility criteria.
3. **Era-of-service inequity**: The political dynamic that limited initial PCAFC eligibility to post-9/11 veterans created a persistent two-tier system that has only partially been resolved.
4. **Gender blindness**: The caregiver population is 85-91% female, but policy debates rarely acknowledge the gendered nature of caregiving labor or its intersection with women's economic inequality.
5. **Budget constraints vs. moral obligation**: Every caregiver expansion has been contested on budget grounds, despite the fact that family caregivers save the VA $14 billion annually compared to the cost of equivalent institutional care.

### Implications for Current Policy

- Implementation capacity matters as much as legislative mandate; the MISSION Act experience demonstrates that ambitious laws fail without adequate administrative infrastructure
- Eligibility criteria must be designed with transparency and consistency; subjective clinical assessments produce arbitrary outcomes
- Reassessment processes must include adequate notice, appeal rights, and transition support; abrupt termination destroys trust
- The caregiver workforce is aging alongside the veteran population; policies designed for young post-9/11 caregivers may not serve elderly Vietnam-era caregivers
