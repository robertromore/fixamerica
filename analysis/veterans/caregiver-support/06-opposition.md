# Caregiver Support: Opposition to Reform

## Overview

Unlike many policy areas where opposition is primarily ideological, veteran caregiver reform faces barriers that are more often structural, institutional, and fiscal than partisan. Few elected officials openly oppose supporting military caregivers--the issue carries bipartisan moral weight. Instead, reform is impeded by budget competition within the VA, institutional resistance to redefining caregivers as partners in care delivery, industry concerns about market disruption, and philosophical disagreements about the appropriate scope and structure of government support. Understanding these obstacles requires examining both explicit opposition and the implicit resistance embedded in bureaucratic processes and budget politics.

## Explicit Opposition

### Fiscal Hawks and Budget Scorekeepers

**Position**: Expanding caregiver stipends, Social Security credits, and support services represents unsustainable mandatory spending growth.

| Argument | Evidence Used | Counterargument |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| PCAFC costs have grown rapidly ($2.4B in FY 2022 to $3.1B in FY 2024) | VA budget submissions | Growth reflects overdue expansion to all eras of service, not program bloat |
| Raising stipends to market rate would cost $8-12B annually | CBO projections | Current stipends save the VA $10-14B annually compared to institutional care |
| Social Security credits for caregivers set an expensive precedent | SSA actuarial analysis | Caregiver credits correct a structural penalty on unpaid labor that disproportionately harms women |
| PCAFC enrollment could grow to 200,000+ with liberalized criteria | VA estimates | Actual take-up has been far below projections; 37,000 enrolled vs. 1.1 million intensive caregivers |

**Key actors**: Office of Management and Budget, Congressional Budget Office (scoring role), Heritage Foundation, some fiscal conservative members of Congress on Appropriations committees

**Severity**: High. Budget scoring is the most consistent obstacle to caregiver reform. CBO scores that treat stipend increases as pure cost growth--without crediting the institutional care costs avoided--systematically bias against expansion.

### VA Institutional Resistance

**Position**: Not outright opposition, but bureaucratic inertia that resists the fundamental reorientation from facility-based to home-based care delivery.

**Manifestations:**

| Resistance Type | Example | Effect |
|----------------|---------|--------|
| Clinical gatekeeping | Eligibility assessments use subjective criteria applied inconsistently | Approval rates range from 28% to 62% across VA medical centers |
| Reassessment as cost control | 7,500 caregivers terminated in 2022-2023 reassessments | Enrollment reduced; trust destroyed |
| IT system as bottleneck | CARMA system failures add months to application processing | Eligible caregivers give up or never apply |
| Performance metrics | VA medical center directors evaluated on facility metrics, not home-based care | Caregiver support treated as peripheral to core mission |
| Budget allocation | Caregiver stipends classified as benefits, not healthcare | Separated bureaucratically from veteran treatment planning |

**Severity**: Very High. Institutional resistance is the most persistent barrier because it operates continuously through thousands of daily administrative decisions, is not subject to political override through a single legislative act, and is deeply embedded in VA organizational culture.

### VA Privatization Advocates

**Position**: Replace the PCAFC with veteran choice vouchers allowing families to purchase care on the private market.

**Arguments:**

- Government-run caregiver programs are inherently bureaucratic and inefficient
- Veterans and families should choose their own care arrangements
- Private-sector home health agencies can deliver care more effectively
- Competition would improve quality and reduce costs

**Why this would harm caregivers:**

- The home health aide market does not serve the specialized conditions (TBI, severe PTSD, polytrauma) that veteran caregivers manage
- Private home health agencies have 60%+ annual turnover, compared to family caregivers who provide continuous, relationship-based care
- Voucher systems create administrative burden for families navigating private markets
- Rural areas where many veterans live have severe home health aide shortages; a voucher would have no providers to purchase from
- The VA's veteran-specific expertise in TBI, PTSD, and military sexual trauma is not replicated in the private market

**Key actors**: Concerned Veterans for America (Koch-affiliated), some libertarian policy organizations

**Severity**: Medium. Privatization rhetoric can divert political attention from strengthening the PCAFC toward market alternatives that do not address caregiver needs, but outright privatization of caregiver support lacks broad congressional support.

### Employer and Business Opposition to Workplace Protections

**Position**: Mandating workplace accommodations and anti-discrimination protections for caregivers creates undue regulatory burden on employers.

**Arguments:**

| Argument | Counterargument |
|----------|-----------------|
| Caregiver status is a personal choice, not an immutable characteristic | Caregiving is a response to military service-connected injury; it is not optional |
| Expanding FMLA creates compliance costs | Military caregiver FMLA leave already exists under the National Defense Authorization Act; proposed reforms extend protections beyond leave to include scheduling and discrimination |
| Small businesses cannot absorb additional mandates | Thresholds can exempt small employers; tax credits can offset costs |
| Flexible scheduling mandates reduce managerial discretion | Rigid schedules are the primary reason caregivers leave the workforce entirely |

**Key actors**: U.S. Chamber of Commerce, National Federation of Independent Business, Society for Human Resource Management

**Severity**: Medium. Employer opposition to workplace protections is a familiar dynamic that can be managed through exemptions, phase-ins, and tax incentives, but it has historically delayed or weakened caregiver employment legislation.

## Implicit Opposition and Structural Barriers

### Gendered Devaluation of Care Work

The most powerful form of opposition is not articulated as opposition at all. The systematic undervaluation of caregiving labor--because it is predominantly performed by women and culturally coded as a natural family obligation rather than skilled work--means that proposals to compensate caregivers at market rates face an implicit baseline assumption that caregiving should be free or cheap.

**How this operates:**

- PCAFC stipends are benchmarked to home health aide wages ($15.40/hour median), one of the lowest-paid occupations in the economy, rather than to the actual skill level caregivers develop
- Proposals for Social Security credits for caregivers face the objection that "other unpaid workers" would demand similar treatment, ignoring that caregiving replaces a government service obligation
- The emotional framing of caregiving as "loving sacrifice" undermines economic arguments for fair compensation
- Caregiver advocacy is constrained by caregivers' own time poverty--the people who would benefit most from reform have the least capacity for political engagement

### Competition Within the VA Budget

Caregiver support competes with other VA priorities that have their own powerful constituencies:

| Competing Priority | Budget Impact | Political Constituency |
|-------------------|---------------|----------------------|
| VA healthcare facility construction | Capital budget competition | Local construction interests, AFGE |
| Disability compensation increases | Mandatory spending growth | All VSOs, disabled veteran population |
| PACT Act toxic exposure implementation | $280B over 10 years | Burn pit veteran advocates, Jon Stewart-led coalition |
| VA electronic health record modernization | $16B+ program | Technology contractors, Congressional mandates |
| VA staffing and retention (system-wide) | Growing personnel costs | AFGE, all VA employees |

Caregiver support lacks a constituency as politically powerful as the disability compensation or toxic exposure coalitions. While the Elizabeth Dole Foundation has significantly elevated caregiver visibility, caregivers are not a voting bloc comparable to the broader veteran population.

### SSA and Retirement System Conservatism

Proposals to provide Social Security credits for caregiver labor face resistance from the Social Security Administration and retirement policy establishment:

- Any change to Social Security crediting rules requires legislative action and creates precedent concerns
- SSA actuarial projections are designed around employment-based contributions; non-employment credits are viewed as structural risks to the trust fund
- Retirement policy experts across the political spectrum are cautious about expanding Social Security credits beyond traditional employment, even for sympathetic populations
- The estimated cost of caregiver credits ($2-4B annually if applied to all veteran caregivers providing 20+ hours/week) faces the same budget scoring challenges as stipend increases

### AFGE Union Concerns

The American Federation of Government Employees supports strong VA caregiver programs in principle but has specific concerns about implementation that can slow reform:

- **Scope of practice**: AFGE has raised concerns about expanding the role of non-clinical caregivers in ways that could overlap with VA employee functions
- **Staffing models**: Union concerns about whether expanded home-based care reduces demand for facility-based VA staff
- **Training standards**: AFGE advocates for rigorous caregiver training requirements that, while well-intentioned, can create additional barriers to PCAFC enrollment
- **Contracting**: Union opposition to outsourcing caregiver support services to contractors or nonprofits

**Severity**: Low-Medium. Union concerns are manageable through negotiation but can delay implementation.

## Opposition Durability Assessment

| Opposition Source | Durability | Negotiability | Strategy to Address |
|------------------|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| Budget scoring bias | Very High | Medium | Require CBO to score avoided institutional care costs as offsets |
| VA institutional inertia | Very High | Medium | Legislate caregiver inclusion in treatment planning; reform performance metrics |
| Gendered devaluation | Very High | Low | Cultural change requires long-term advocacy; benchmark stipends to skilled nursing, not home health aide |
| OMB fiscal resistance | High | Medium | Demonstrate cost-effectiveness through pilot data |
| Employer opposition to workplace protections | Medium | High | Exemptions for small employers; tax credits; phase-in periods |
| Privatization advocacy | Medium | Low | Document failure of market alternatives for complex veteran care |
| SSA crediting resistance | High | Medium | Limit initial proposal to PCAFC caregivers (37,000) to reduce cost and precedent concerns |
| AFGE union concerns | Medium | High | Negotiate role clarity; frame caregivers as complementary to VA staff |

## Key Insight

The most significant opposition to veteran caregiver reform is not ideological but structural. The budget scoring framework, the VA's institutional design, and the cultural devaluation of care work create a system where even bipartisan goodwill toward caregivers is insufficient to produce adequate support. Overcoming these barriers requires not just political coalition-building but fundamental changes in how the government accounts for caregiving costs and values caregiving labor.
