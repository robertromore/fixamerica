# Aging Veterans: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes aging veteran care policy through nine distinct political perspectives, evaluating current state assessments, root cause analyses, proposed solutions, and legislation. Aging veteran care is among the more bipartisan veteran issues, with broad agreement that the nation owes its aging veterans comprehensive care, but significant disagreement about the role of government versus the private sector, the appropriate level of spending, the scope of caregiver support, and the balance between institutional and home-based care. Each perspective receives engagement consistency ratings and position scores, culminating in compromise proposals that bridge multiple viewpoints.

## Perspective Definitions

| Perspective | Core Values | Policy Orientation |
|-------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Tradition, limited government, individual responsibility | Incremental change, fiscal restraint, market solutions |
| Liberal | Progress, equality, social responsibility | Government as problem-solver, regulation, safety nets |
| Progressive | Systemic change, economic justice, structural reform | Transformative reform, wealth redistribution, worker power |
| Libertarian | Individual liberty, minimal state, free markets | Deregulation, privatization, voluntary association |
| Constitutionalist | Original intent, enumerated powers, federalism | Strict constitutional limits, states' rights |
| Populist | Anti-elite, pro-worker, economic nationalism | Trade protection, anti-monopoly, immigration restriction |
| Centrist | Pragmatism, compromise, incremental progress | Bipartisan solutions, evidence-based policy |
| Religious Right | Faith-based values, traditional morality | Social conservatism, religious liberty |
| Democratic Socialist | Worker ownership, public goods, democratic control | Public investment, labor rights, universal programs |

## Perspective Analyses

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Genuine concern for veterans as a national obligation; fiscal restraint is sincerely held |
| Principle consistency | Applies limited-government principles consistently but makes principled exceptions for veteran care |
| Goalpost stability | Positions on veteran care are stable; cost thresholds may shift based on budget environment |
| Zero-sum behavior | Generally not zero-sum on aging veteran issues; willing to negotiate on spending levels |

**Justification:** Conservatives have a strong tradition of supporting veteran care as a moral obligation of national defense. Their fiscal concerns are genuine, not pretextual, and they will support significant spending when framed as fulfilling an existing commitment rather than creating new entitlements. They are among the most reliable coalition partners on aging veteran issues because the issue aligns with their patriotic values.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Republican leadership has consistently supported VA budget increases for geriatric care, even during austerity periods (FY 2024-2025 budget debates)
- Heritage Foundation supports VA geriatric reform framed as efficiency improvement and veteran choice (Heritage, 2024)
- Rural Republican members are strong advocates for State Veterans Home funding and caregiver support
- Conservative senators co-sponsored PCAFC expansion under the VA MISSION Act

Conservatives would strengthen community care provisions for aging veterans, expand State Veterans Home capacity through block grants rather than federal mandates, pursue caregiver support through tax credits rather than direct stipends, and emphasize veteran choice in selecting care settings. They would prefer a more modest spending trajectory with pilot programs before national rollout.

- **Unlikely partners:** Democratic Socialist (on spending levels and government role)

## Compromise Proposals

### Compromise 1: Home and Community Care Package

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Centrist + Populist + Religious Right + Progressive

**Core Agreement:** Nearly all perspectives agree that aging veterans should be able to age in place with adequate support, and that HBPC is among the VA's most effective programs.

**Policy Description:**

Triple HBPC capacity from 37,000 to 100,000+ veterans over 5 years. Expand Veteran-Directed Care to 50,000 participants. Add 25 new ADHC sites. Expand respite care availability for all caregivers. Total annual cost: approximately $3.4B at scale.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Conservative | Cost-effective alternative to expensive institutional care; proven program |
| Liberal | Expands VA-delivered home-based care; serves most vulnerable veterans |
| Centrist | Evidence-based; demonstrated 24% hospitalization reduction; strong ROI |
| Populist | Tangible, visible service to veterans in their homes and communities |
| Religious Right | Family-centered care model; enables families to care for veterans at home |
| Progressive | Expands VA direct care capacity; creates jobs in home health |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Accepting significant new federal spending on VA programs |
| Libertarian | Accepting government-delivered home care rather than vouchers |
| Progressive | Accepting phased rollout rather than immediate universal coverage |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 7/9 perspectives (all except Libertarian and Constitutionalist full support)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 5/7 coalition members rated 4+ consistency
- **Durability:** Strong--HBPC expansion creates institutional capacity that is unlikely to be reversed
- **Implementation Path:** Appropriations increase plus Elizabeth Dole Act; no constitutional barriers

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The Home and Community Care Package (Compromise 1) has the broadest support, the strongest evidence base, and the most proven program model (HBPC). It should be the first legislative priority. The Caregiver Recognition and Protection Package (Compromise 2) has similarly broad support and strong bipartisan appeal through the Elizabeth Dole Act framework.

### Key Obstacles

The primary obstacle across all compromise proposals is cost. At full implementation, the three compromise packages total approximately $8.5B annually--a 67% increase over current VA geriatric care spending. Conservatives and Libertarians will resist this level of spending, while Progressives and Democratic Socialists will argue it is insufficient. The centrist argument--that investing now prevents more expensive institutional care later--is the most effective bridge.

The secondary obstacle is the VA direct care vs. community care divide. Conservatives and Libertarians want aging veterans to access private-sector geriatric care through choice and voucher models. Progressives and Democratic Socialists view this as privatization that undermines VA capacity. The Veteran-Directed Care model offers a compromise: veterans receive a government-funded budget but direct their own care, including from private providers.

### Low Consistency Partners

Populists and the Religious Right (both rated 3/5) provide strong rhetorical support for aging veteran care but may be less reliable on specific policy mechanics. Engagement strategies should emphasize moral obligation, family values, and protecting vulnerable elderly veterans--framing that resonates across these perspectives.

### Recommended Approach

Pursue all three compromise proposals, with sequencing based on political feasibility:

1. **Immediate**: Caregiver Recognition and Protection Package (Compromise 2)--broadest emotional appeal, bipartisan sponsors through Elizabeth Dole Act, A&A reform is low-cost quick win
2. **Year 1-2**: Home and Community Care Package (Compromise 1)--builds on proven HBPC model, strong evidence base, CBO-supported cost-effectiveness
3. **Year 2-3**: Cognitive Health and Elderly Veteran Safety Net (Compromise 3)--leverages expanded care infrastructure from Compromises 1 and 2

This sequencing allows each reform to demonstrate results before pursuing the next, building political support through evidence of success while addressing the most urgent needs first.
