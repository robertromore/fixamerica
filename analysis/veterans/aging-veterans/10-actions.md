# Aging Veterans: What Citizens Can Do

## Overview

Improving care for aging veterans requires action at every level--from individual citizens and family members to community organizations, healthcare providers, and state and local governments. The aging veteran crisis is driven by demographics that are well understood but insufficiently addressed. Public awareness, advocacy, direct support, and political pressure are all necessary to close the gap between what aging veterans need and what they receive.

## For All Citizens

### Learn and Share

1. **Understand the aging veteran crisis**: Nearly half of all living veterans are age 65 or older. The Vietnam-era cohort--5.9 million veterans with a median age of 77--is driving unprecedented demand for geriatric care, cognitive health services, and caregiver support that the VA is not prepared to meet.

2. **Contact your representatives**: Call or write your U.S. Senators and Representative to:
    - Support the Elizabeth Dole Home and Community-Based Services Act
    - Advocate for increased VA geriatric care funding in annual appropriations
    - Support PCAFC eligibility expansion and application reform
    - Request legislation prohibiting predatory Aid and Attendance financial services
    - Support VA geriatric workforce investment

3. **Share aging veteran statistics**: Key facts to communicate:
    - 7.8 million veterans are age 65+ (2024)
    - HBPC serves only 18% of eligible homebound veterans
    - Only 1.4% of family caregivers of aging veterans receive PCAFC support
    - Elderly veteran suicide rates (75+) are 45.2 per 100,000--far exceeding civilian rates
    - An estimated 500,000-800,000 veterans are eligible for Aid and Attendance but not receiving it

4. **Check on aging veterans you know**: Social isolation is one of the most significant drivers of poor outcomes for aging veterans. Regular contact--a phone call, a visit, a shared meal--can be life-changing for an isolated elderly veteran.

### Donate and Support

5. **Support organizations serving aging veterans**: Direct donations to organizations with demonstrated programs:
    - Elizabeth Dole Foundation (caregiver support and advocacy)
    - DAV Transportation Network (rides to VA appointments)
    - Alzheimer's Association (dementia support and research)
    - American Legion and VFW (local aging veteran programs)
    - Give an Hour (free mental health services for veterans)

## For Veterans and Military Families

### Access Available Resources

6. **Enroll in VA healthcare**: Even if you do not currently need geriatric services, enrollment establishes eligibility and ensures you are in the system when needs arise. Call 1-877-222-8387 or visit va.gov/health-care/apply.

7. **Apply for Aid and Attendance**: If you are age 65+ and need help with daily activities (bathing, dressing, medication management), you may be eligible for the Aid and Attendance enhanced pension of up to $2,229/month (single) or $2,643/month (married). Apply through a VSO--never pay a private firm for application assistance. Contact a VSO or call VA at 1-800-827-1000.

8. **Request Home-Based Primary Care**: If you are homebound or have difficulty traveling to VA appointments, ask your VA primary care provider about Home-Based Primary Care. HBPC teams come to your home for comprehensive care.

9. **Explore Veteran-Directed Care**: If you prefer to manage your own care, the Veteran-Directed Care program provides a budget you control to hire caregivers, purchase services, and arrange your own support.

10. **Know your caregiver support options**: If a family member provides your care:
    - Apply for the PCAFC through your VA Medical Center's Caregiver Support Coordinator
    - Call the Caregiver Support Line: 1-855-260-3274
    - Access free online caregiver training at caregiver.va.gov
    - Request respite care to give your caregiver a break

11. **Get cognitive screening**: If you are 65+ and concerned about memory, thinking, or cognitive changes, request a cognitive screening at your next VA primary care appointment. Early detection allows early intervention.

12. **Access the Veterans Crisis Line**: Call 988 (press 1), text 838255, or chat at veteranscrisisline.net. Service is available 24/7 regardless of VA enrollment status or age.

### For Family Caregivers

13. **Apply for the PCAFC**: If you provide care for an aging veteran, apply through the VA Caregiver Support Program. Even if you have been denied previously, reapply--eligibility criteria have evolved and your veteran's needs may have changed.

14. **Use available support**: Even if not enrolled in PCAFC:
    - Call the Caregiver Support Line (1-855-260-3274) for guidance and referrals
    - Access free training on medication management, fall prevention, dementia care
    - Request respite care through your VA Medical Center
    - Join VA caregiver support groups (in-person and virtual)

15. **Protect your own health**: Caregiver burnout affects 40% of veteran caregivers. Prioritize:
    - Your own medical appointments and preventive care
    - Regular respite breaks (request respite through VA)
    - Mental health support (free counseling available through VA Caregiver Support)
    - Connection with other caregivers through support groups

16. **Document and advocate**: Keep records of the care you provide, the veteran's condition changes, and any VA service gaps you encounter. This documentation supports benefits claims and congressional advocacy.

## For Community Organizations

### Build Local Support

17. **Create aging veteran social programs**: American Legion posts, VFW halls, churches, community centers, and senior centers can organize regular social events for aging veterans. Monthly gatherings, shared meals, card games, skill-sharing workshops, and community service projects combat the isolation that drives depression, cognitive decline, and suicide.

18. **Establish caregiver respite networks**: Community organizations can create volunteer respite networks where trained volunteers spend a few hours with an aging veteran so the family caregiver can rest, run errands, or attend to their own health.

19. **Host Aid and Attendance clinics**: Partner with local VSOs to hold free A&A application assistance events at senior centers, community centers, and houses of worship. These events can identify eligible veterans who are not receiving benefits.

20. **Organize veteran transportation**: Coordinate volunteer driver programs to transport aging veterans to VA appointments, social programs, and community events. The DAV Transportation Network provides a model and may provide supplemental resources.

### Partner with VA

21. **Apply for suicide prevention grants**: The Staff Sergeant Parker Gordon Fox Suicide Prevention Grant Program provides funding to community organizations for veteran suicide prevention, including programs targeting elderly veterans.

22. **Become a VA community partner**: Contact your local VA Medical Center's Voluntary Service office to explore partnership opportunities, including providing space for veteran programs, organizing volunteer activities, and hosting VA outreach events.

23. **Support VA Adult Day Health Care**: Community adult day programs can explore contracts with VA to serve aging veterans, expanding ADHC capacity without requiring new VA facilities.

## For Healthcare Providers

### Serve Aging Veterans

24. **Learn veteran-specific geriatric care**: Military service creates unique aging patterns--toxic exposure diseases, TBI-related cognitive decline, late-life PTSD re-emergence, chronic pain from service injuries. Free training is available through:
    - VA's Geriatric Scholars Program
    - SAMHSA's Military Family Resources
    - VA's Center for Integrated Healthcare

25. **Screen for military service**: Ask elderly patients about military service history. Many aging veterans do not self-identify or may not realize their health conditions are service-related. A simple screening question can connect them to VA benefits and services.

26. **Coordinate with VA**: If you treat veterans who also receive VA care, contact the veteran's VA primary care team to coordinate medications, treatments, and care plans. The VA-Medicare coordination gap is a significant source of preventable complications.

27. **Consider VA employment**: The VA offers competitive salary, federal benefits, student loan repayment, and public service loan forgiveness eligibility. VA geriatric medicine positions are available at VA Medical Centers and through HBPC programs.

28. **Specialize in geriatric medicine**: The United States faces a shortage of 26,000 geriatricians (American Geriatrics Society, 2024). Geriatric medicine fellowship programs exist at VA GRECCs and academic medical centers. The VA's Geriatric Scholars Program provides additional training for existing providers.

## For State and Local Government

### Policy and Funding

29. **Fund State Veterans Homes**: Advocate for state investment in State Veterans Home construction, renovation, and staffing. With 28 states reporting waitlists, additional capacity is urgently needed.

30. **Strengthen Adult Protective Services**: Ensure state Adult Protective Services has the resources and training to identify and protect aging veterans from financial exploitation, including predatory Aid and Attendance services.

31. **Support aging-in-place infrastructure**: Fund home modification programs, accessibility improvements, and age-friendly community design that enables aging veterans to remain in their homes safely.

32. **Expand Medicaid home and community-based services**: In states that have not expanded Medicaid HCBS waiver programs, aging veterans who do not qualify for VA extended care may have no long-term care options other than spending down to Medicaid institutional care.

33. **Integrate veteran data into aging services planning**: State aging agencies and Area Agencies on Aging should incorporate veteran population data into service planning, ensuring that Older Americans Act programs reach aging veterans.

## For Employers

34. **Support employee caregivers**: An estimated 3.8 million Americans provide unpaid care to aging veterans. Employer policies that accommodate caregiving--flexible scheduling, remote work options, paid family leave, caregiver support programs--benefit both the caregiver and the veteran.

35. **Hire aging veteran caregivers returning to work**: When caregiving ends (due to the veteran's death or transition to institutional care), former caregivers often need to re-enter the workforce after years of absence. Employers can partner with workforce development programs to recruit and support these experienced workers.
