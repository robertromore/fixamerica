# Aging Veterans: Current State

## Demographic Profile

### Population Overview

The veteran population is aging rapidly, with a disproportionate share of the total veteran population now in the 65+ age bracket.

| Age Group | Number of Veterans (2024) | Share of Total | Projected 2030 | Source |
|-----------|--------------------------|----------------|----------------|--------|
| Under 35 | 2.1 million | 13.0% | 1.9 million | VA NCVAS, 2024 |
| 35-54 | 3.2 million | 19.8% | 3.0 million | VA NCVAS, 2024 |
| 55-64 | 3.1 million | 19.1% | 2.5 million | VA NCVAS, 2024 |
| 65-74 | 4.1 million | 25.3% | 3.2 million | VA NCVAS, 2024 |
| 75-84 | 2.7 million | 16.7% | 3.4 million | VA NCVAS, 2024 |
| 85+ | 1.0 million | 6.2% | 1.4 million | VA NCVAS, 2024 |
| **Total** | **16.2 million** | **100%** | **15.4 million** | VA NCVAS, 2024 |

*Source: VA National Center for Veterans Analysis and Statistics (NCVAS), Veteran Population Projection Model, 2024.*

The total veteran population is declining as older cohorts pass and military service rates remain low, but the 75+ cohort is growing rapidly as Vietnam-era veterans advance into their late 70s and 80s. By 2030, an estimated 4.8 million veterans will be age 75 or older.

### Service Era Cohorts

| Era | Approximate Veterans (2024) | Median Age (2025) | Key Aging Issues |
|-----|----------------------------|-------------------|------------------|
| WWII | 119,000 | 99 | End-of-life care, nursing home needs |
| Korean War | 584,000 | 92 | Advanced geriatric care, isolation |
| Vietnam Era | 5.9 million | 77 | Agent Orange diseases, dementia, chronic pain |
| Gulf War Era (1990-2001) | 3.8 million | 55 | Early onset chronic conditions, toxic exposures |
| Post-9/11 | 4.4 million | 39 | TBI long-term effects, early aging from combat stress |

*Source: VA NCVAS, 2024; American Community Survey, 2023.*

The Vietnam-era cohort is the primary driver of the current aging veteran surge. These 5.9 million veterans are entering the period of highest healthcare utilization, with many experiencing the convergence of age-related decline and service-connected conditions that have worsened over decades.

Sun Belt states and retirement destinations have disproportionate aging veteran populations, often exceeding local VA capacity. Rural areas, where many Vietnam-era veterans settled, face particular challenges due to provider shortages and distance from VA facilities.

Rural aging veterans face compounding disadvantages: greater distance from VA facilities, fewer civilian geriatric providers, limited public transportation, reduced access to home health aides, and higher rates of social isolation.

## Health Conditions and Needs

### Age-Related Conditions in Veterans

Aging veterans experience the same conditions as aging civilians but at higher rates and earlier onset due to service-related factors:

|-----------|---------------------------|-------------------------------|------------|--------|
| Chronic pain | 58% | 42% | 1.38 | VA Pain Management, 2024 |
| Hearing loss | 52% | 33% | 1.58 | VA Audiology, 2024 |
| Cardiovascular disease | 48% | 38% | 1.26 | VA Cardiology, 2024 |
| Diabetes | 34% | 27% | 1.26 | VA Endocrinology, 2024 |
| Depression | 22% | 13% | 1.69 | VA Mental Health, 2024 |
| PTSD (active symptoms) | 14% | N/A | N/A | VA National PTSD Center, 2024 |

### Service-Connected Accelerated Aging

Military service creates specific pathways to accelerated aging:

- **Toxic exposures**: Agent Orange (Vietnam), depleted uranium (Gulf War), burn pits (Iraq/Afghanistan), contaminated water (Camp Lejeune) cause cancers, respiratory disease, and metabolic disorders decades after exposure
- **Musculoskeletal wear**: Decades of carrying heavy loads, parachute jumps, vehicle jolts, and training injuries create joint degeneration, spinal disease, and chronic pain that compounds natural aging
- **Traumatic brain injury**: TBI from blast exposure or combat impacts accelerates cognitive decline and increases dementia risk by 2-4x (Barnes et al., *Neurology*, 2014)
- **PTSD and allostatic load**: Chronic stress from untreated or undertreated PTSD accelerates cellular aging through allostatic load--the cumulative physiological wear from sustained stress response activation (McEwen, *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences*, 2006)

### Cognitive Health

Dementia and cognitive impairment represent one of the most significant challenges for aging veterans:

| Cognitive Condition | Veterans in VA Care (FY 2024) | Annual Growth Rate | Source |
|--------------------|-------------------------------|-------------------|--------|
| Dementia (all types) | 430,000 | 5.8% | VA Mental Health Services, 2024 |
| Mild cognitive impairment | 280,000 | 8.2% | VA Mental Health Services, 2024 |
| TBI-related cognitive decline | 85,000 | 6.1% | Defense and Veterans Brain Injury Center, 2024 |

*The interaction between TBI and dementia is particularly concerning for post-9/11 veterans, who experienced unprecedented rates of blast-related TBI and will begin entering their 60s and 70s in the 2040s-2060s.*

### Late-Life Mental Health

| Condition | Prevalence in Veterans 65+ | Key Risk Factors | Source |
|-----------|---------------------------|-------------------|--------|
| Depression | 22% | Social isolation, chronic pain, bereavement, functional decline | VA Mental Health, 2024 |
| PTSD (persistent or re-emergent) | 14% | Retirement triggers, loss of structure, mortality confrontation | VA National PTSD Center, 2024 |
| Anxiety disorders | 18% | Healthcare burden, financial stress, caregiver strain | VA Mental Health, 2024 |
| Suicide (rate per 100,000) | 45.2 | Social isolation, chronic pain, firearms access, loss of purpose | VA National Suicide Data Report, 2023 |

Late-life re-emergence of PTSD is a well-documented phenomenon. Veterans who managed PTSD symptoms through work, activity, and social engagement during their careers may experience symptom resurgence upon retirement, when structure and distraction diminish (Davison et al., *Clinical Gerontologist*, 2006).

## VA Geriatric Programs

### Institutional Care

#### Community Living Centers (CLCs)

VA Community Living Centers (formerly VA nursing homes) are the primary institutional care option for eligible veterans.

| Metric | FY 2021 | FY 2022 | FY 2023 | FY 2024 | Source |
|--------|---------|---------|---------|---------|--------|
| Average daily census | 38,200 | 39,800 | 41,100 | 42,000 | VA Geriatrics and Extended Care, 2024 |
| Number of CLC facilities | 134 | 133 | 134 | 134 | VA Geriatrics and Extended Care, 2024 |
| Average length of stay (days) | 72 | 68 | 65 | 63 | VA Geriatrics and Extended Care, 2024 |
| Staff vacancy rate | 18% | 16% | 14% | 13% | VA Workforce Management, 2024 |
| CMS 5-star quality rating (avg) | 3.2 | 3.3 | 3.4 | 3.5 | CMS Nursing Home Compare, 2024 |

*Source: VA Geriatrics and Extended Care Annual Report, 2024.*

CLC quality has improved but remains uneven. Staff vacancy rates, while declining, continue to affect care quality, particularly for nursing assistants and direct care workers. The 134 CLC facilities are insufficient for the projected demand as the Vietnam-era cohort ages further.

### Home and Community-Based Services

The VA has invested heavily in home and community-based services (HCBS) as alternatives to institutional care, consistent with the overwhelming preference of aging veterans to remain at home.

| Program | Description | Veterans Served (FY 2024) | Source |
|---------|-------------|--------------------------|--------|
| Home-Based Primary Care (HBPC) | Primary care teams visit homebound veterans | 37,000 | VA GEC, 2024 |
| Veteran-Directed Care | Veterans manage their own care budget | 4,200 | VA GEC, 2024 |
| Homemaker/Home Health Aide | Personal care and housekeeping assistance | 78,000 | VA GEC, 2024 |
| Adult Day Health Care (ADHC) | Daytime supervision, activities, healthcare | 8,500 | VA GEC, 2024 |
| Respite Care | Temporary relief for family caregivers | 28,000 episodes | VA GEC, 2024 |
| Medical Foster Home | Veteran lives with trained caregiver family | 1,800 | VA GEC, 2024 |
| Hospice and Palliative Care | End-of-life care | 52,000 | VA GEC, 2024 |

*Source: VA Geriatrics and Extended Care (GEC) Annual Report, 2024.*

Home-Based Primary Care has been one of the VA's most successful programs, reducing hospitalizations by 24% and nursing home admissions by 36% among participants (VA HBPC Outcomes Report, 2023). However, it serves only 37,000 of an estimated 200,000+ homebound veterans who could benefit.

## Caregiver Support

### Program of Comprehensive Assistance for Family Caregivers (PCAFC)

The PCAFC, authorized by the Caregivers and Veterans Omnibus Health Services Act of 2010 and expanded by the VA MISSION Act of 2018, provides stipends, training, and support to family caregivers of eligible veterans.

| Metric | FY 2021 | FY 2022 | FY 2023 | FY 2024 | Source |
|--------|---------|---------|---------|---------|--------|
| Enrolled caregivers | 33,000 | 38,000 | 45,000 | 53,000 | VA Caregiver Support, 2024 |
| Applications received | 52,000 | 61,000 | 68,000 | 74,000 | VA Caregiver Support, 2024 |
| Application approval rate | 48% | 51% | 53% | 55% | VA Caregiver Support, 2024 |
| Monthly stipend range | $1,800-$3,100 | $1,900-$3,200 | $2,000-$3,400 | $2,100-$3,500 | VA Caregiver Support, 2024 |
| Total program spending | $1.9B | $2.3B | $2.8B | $3.2B | VA Budget Submissions |

*Source: VA Caregiver Support Program Annual Report, 2024.*

The PCAFC has expanded significantly since the VA MISSION Act required its extension to pre-9/11 veterans (phased in from October 2020). However, the 55% approval rate indicates that nearly half of applications are denied, often due to stringent eligibility criteria that some advocates argue exclude veterans with genuine care needs.

| Dependency and Indemnity Compensation | 412,000 survivors | $1,612/month | VA Benefits Administration, 2024 |

The Aid and Attendance benefit--which provides enhanced pension for veterans who need help with daily activities--is widely underutilized. Estimates suggest that 500,000-800,000 additional veterans may be eligible but are not receiving the benefit, often due to lack of awareness, application complexity, or predatory financial planning services that exploit elderly veterans (Government Accountability Office, 2023).

## Recent Developments (2024-2025)

### Policy Changes

- **PACT Act Implementation (2022-present)**: The PACT Act's expansion of toxic exposure presumptions has significantly increased the number of aging veterans eligible for service-connected healthcare and disability compensation, particularly Vietnam-era veterans with Agent Orange-related conditions
- **PCAFC Expansion**: The VA MISSION Act-mandated expansion of the caregiver support program to pre-9/11 veterans was fully implemented in October 2020, with enrollment growing 60% through FY 2024
- **Geriatric Scholars Program**: The VA has expanded its geriatric training pipeline, though workforce shortages persist
- **Telehealth for Homebound Veterans**: VA expanded tablet distribution and telehealth infrastructure for homebound aging veterans, reaching 120,000 veterans through FY 2024

### Legislative Activity

- **Elizabeth Dole Home and Community-Based Services for Veterans and Caregivers Act**: Introduced to expand HCBS and caregiver support; pending in 119th Congress
- **VA Employee Fairness Act**: Addresses VA staffing shortages that disproportionately affect geriatric care facilities
- **FY 2025 appropriations**: Congress maintained growth trajectory for geriatric care but below VA request levels

### Ongoing Challenges

- CLC staffing vacancies remain persistent, particularly for nursing assistants and certified nursing aides
- Home-Based Primary Care serves only an estimated 18% of eligible veterans
- PCAFC application denial rate of 45% generates frustration and congressional complaints
- Rural aging veterans remain underserved due to geographic barriers to both VA and civilian geriatric providers
- Predatory financial planning targeting elderly veterans for Aid and Attendance applications continues despite GAO recommendations

## The Gap Between Need and Capacity

| Need | Current Capacity | Gap |
|------|-----------------|-----|
| 7.8 million veterans age 65+ | VA geriatric care reaches ~340,000 | ~96% of aging veterans rely primarily on non-VA systems |
| 200,000+ homebound veterans eligible for HBPC | 37,000 served | 81% unserved |
| 3.8 million family caregivers of aging veterans | 53,000 in PCAFC | 99% without formal VA caregiver support |
| 430,000 veterans with dementia in VA care | Limited specialized dementia units | Most dementia care in general medical settings |
| 305,000 Aid and Attendance recipients | 500,000-800,000 additional eligible | 40-70% of eligible veterans not receiving benefit |
