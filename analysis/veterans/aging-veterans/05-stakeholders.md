# Aging Veterans: Stakeholders

## Who Is Affected

### Aging Veterans (65+)

**Population**: 7.8 million veterans age 65 or older (2024)

**Most Affected Subgroups**:

- **Vietnam-era veterans (median age 77)**: The largest aging cohort, facing the convergence of Agent Orange-related diseases, decades of chronic pain from service injuries, and late-life PTSD re-emergence
- **Veterans age 85+**: Approximately 1.0 million veterans with the most intensive care needs, including high rates of dementia, frailty, and end-of-life care requirements
- **Rural aging veterans**: 1.3 million veterans age 65+ living in rural areas with limited access to VA and civilian geriatric care
- **Veterans with service-connected cognitive decline**: 85,000+ veterans with TBI-related cognitive deterioration compounded by age-related dementia risk
- **Low-income aging veterans**: Veterans in Priority Groups 5-8 who face copayments and limited eligibility for VA extended care services
- **Aging female veterans**: A growing population (approximately 450,000 age 65+) whose needs are often overlooked in geriatric programs designed for the overwhelmingly male veteran population

**What They Need**:

- Accessible, comprehensive geriatric healthcare including preventive, chronic, and acute care
- Long-term services and supports that enable aging in place
- Cognitive health screening and dementia care
- Social connection programs to combat isolation
- Financial security including pension, Aid and Attendance, and disability compensation

### Family Caregivers

**Population**: Approximately 3.8 million family caregivers of veterans age 65+ (National Alliance for Caregiving, 2023)

**Profile**:

- Average age of caregivers: 60 years (many are aging themselves)
- 65% are spouses; 25% are adult children
- Average hours of unpaid care: 24 hours per week
- 40% report high caregiver burden (Elizabeth Dole Foundation, 2024)
- Economic value of unpaid care: $24 billion annually (RAND, 2023)

**Impact**:

- Physical and mental health deterioration from sustained caregiving demands
- Employment loss or reduction averaging $25,000 in annual lost income per caregiver
- Social isolation as caregiving consumes time and energy
- Financial strain from out-of-pocket caregiving expenses
- Only 53,000 caregivers (1.4% of the total) are enrolled in the VA's PCAFC

**What They Need**:

- Financial compensation for caregiving through expanded PCAFC or equivalent programs
- Respite care to prevent burnout
- Training in geriatric care, medication management, and condition-specific skills
- Health insurance coverage (for caregivers not otherwise covered)
- Mental health support and peer connection with other caregivers

### Surviving Spouses and Dependents

**Population**: Approximately 412,000 survivors receiving Dependency and Indemnity Compensation (VA, FY 2024)

**Impact**:

- Loss of veteran's income and benefits upon death
- Transition from caregiver to bereaved survivor, often after years of intensive caregiving
- Reduced Social Security benefits, pension, and healthcare access
- Many surviving spouses are themselves elderly and in need of care

## Who Has Power

### Department of Veterans Affairs

**Role**: Primary provider and policymaker for aging veteran care

**Key Decision-Makers**:

- VA Secretary and Under Secretary for Health
- VA Geriatrics and Extended Care (GEC) leadership
- VA Caregiver Support Program Office
- Individual VA Medical Center directors (control local resource allocation)
- VA Office of Rural Health

**Leverage Points**:

- Controls $12.8 billion in geriatric care spending (FY 2024)
- Sets eligibility criteria, program policies, and clinical standards
- Determines PCAFC enrollment criteria and stipend levels
- Decides CLC capacity and HBPC expansion
- Controls community care referral policies for geriatric services

**Current Posture**: Committed to HCBS expansion and caregiver support in principle, but constrained by workforce shortages, budget limitations, and institutional inertia

### Congress

**Role**: Authorizes and funds VA geriatric programs; sets legislative mandates

**Key Committees**:

| Committee | Role |
|-----------|------|
| Senate Veterans' Affairs | Authorization and oversight of VA geriatric programs |
| House Veterans' Affairs | Authorization and oversight |
| Senate Appropriations (MILCON-VA) | VA funding levels |
| House Appropriations (MILCON-VA) | VA funding levels |
| Senate Finance / House Ways and Means | Medicare policy affecting dual-eligible veterans |
| Senate HELP / House Education and Labor | Caregiver workforce policy |

**Leverage Points**:

- Sets VA appropriations including geriatric care funding
- Passes legislation on caregiver support, long-term care, and benefits eligibility
- Conducts oversight hearings that shape VA priorities
- Confirms VA leadership

**Current Posture**: Bipartisan support for veteran geriatric care in principle; partisan disagreements on spending levels and program design

### Veterans Service Organizations

**Major Organizations and Aging Veteran Focus**:

| Organization | Aging Veteran Priorities |
|--------------|------------------------|
| American Legion | CLC quality, caregiver support, pension protections |
| VFW | Geriatric care access, Aid and Attendance reform |
| DAV | Service-connected disability in aging, PCAFC expansion |
| Vietnam Veterans of America | Agent Orange presumptives, Vietnam-era aging issues |
| Paralyzed Veterans of America | Accessible aging-in-place, spinal cord injury aging |
| Elizabeth Dole Foundation | Caregiver support (primary mission) |

**Leverage Points**:

- Significant congressional lobbying influence
- Shape public awareness of aging veteran issues
- Operate direct service programs (transportation, benefits counseling)
- Provide testimony and data for legislative hearings

### Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS)

**Role**: Administers Medicare and Medicaid, which provide significant healthcare and long-term care for aging veterans

**Leverage Points**:

- Medicare covers 65+ veterans for non-VA care
- Medicaid funds long-term care for low-income veterans
- CMS quality standards affect State Veterans Homes
- VA-CMS coordination (or lack thereof) shapes dual-eligible veteran care

### State Governments

**Role**: Operate State Veterans Homes, administer Medicaid, and provide state-level aging services

**Key Functions**:

- 158 State Veterans Homes with 30,100 beds
- State Medicaid programs covering long-term care for low-income veterans
- State aging agencies providing Area Agency on Aging services
- State veteran affairs departments coordinating state-level benefits and services

## Who Is Neutral or Uninvolved

### General Public

Most Americans support caring for aging veterans but have limited awareness of the specific challenges. Aging veteran issues receive less media attention than active combat, suicide prevention, or disability benefits. Public support is broad but shallow.

### Private Long-Term Care Industry

The private nursing home and home health industry has a complex relationship with aging veteran care:

- Provides care to veterans through VA community care contracts and State Veterans Home partnerships
- Benefits from VA referrals and per diem payments
- May resist policies that expand VA direct care at the expense of private contracting
- Quality varies widely; VA has sometimes contracted with facilities with poor quality records

### Academic and Research Community

Geriatric researchers at VA GRECCs and universities produce evidence that informs policy but have limited direct influence over resource allocation. Key research areas include dementia-TBI interaction, PTSD and aging, toxic exposure late effects, and caregiver interventions.
