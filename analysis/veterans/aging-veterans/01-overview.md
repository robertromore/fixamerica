# Aging Veterans: Overview

## Executive Summary

The United States is experiencing an unprecedented demographic shift in its veteran population. Of the approximately 16.2 million living veterans as of 2024, nearly half--roughly 7.8 million--are age 65 or older, and this aging cohort is placing escalating demands on the Department of Veterans Affairs, Medicare, Medicaid, and community-based care systems (VA National Center for Veterans Analysis and Statistics, 2024). By 2030, the Vietnam-era generation will be in their late 70s and 80s, driving a surge in demand for geriatric care, long-term services and supports, cognitive health treatment, and end-of-life care that the current system is not prepared to meet.

Aging veterans face a convergence of challenges that distinguish them from both younger veterans and aging civilians. Service-related injuries and exposures--from Agent Orange in Vietnam to burn pits in Iraq and Afghanistan--accelerate physical decline and create unique disease profiles. Combat-related PTSD can re-emerge or intensify in late life as veterans retire, lose social connections, and confront mortality. Chronic pain from decades-old musculoskeletal injuries compounds age-related disability. Social isolation, particularly among male veterans who relied on military and employment networks for social connection, is a potent risk factor for cognitive decline, depression, and suicide.

The VA operates the largest integrated healthcare system in the United States and is one of the few systems with significant geriatric care capacity, including Community Living Centers (nursing homes), Home-Based Primary Care, and the Program of Comprehensive Assistance for Family Caregivers. Yet these programs serve only a fraction of eligible veterans, face persistent staffing shortages, and are geographically concentrated in ways that leave rural aging veterans underserved. The broader American elder care system--fragmented, expensive, and increasingly strained--provides the backdrop against which veteran-specific challenges play out.

## Scope

### What This Subtopic Covers

- **Demographics of aging veterans**: Population trends, cohort characteristics, geographic distribution
- **Geriatric healthcare needs**: Age-related conditions compounded by service-related injuries and exposures
- **VA geriatric programs**: Community Living Centers, Home-Based Primary Care, adult day health care, hospice, and palliative care
- **Caregiver support**: The Program of Comprehensive Assistance for Family Caregivers (PCAFC) and informal caregiving
- **Cognitive health**: Dementia, Alzheimer's disease, and the interaction with traumatic brain injury and PTSD
- **Long-term services and supports**: Institutional and community-based options for aging veterans
- **Social isolation and mental health**: Late-life depression, suicide risk in elderly veterans, and social connection
- **Financial security**: Pension, Aid and Attendance, and the economic challenges of aging with service-connected disabilities

## Core Tensions

- **Institutional vs. home-based care**: Most aging veterans prefer to remain at home, but home-based care requires robust caregiver support and community services that are underdeveloped; institutional care (nursing homes) is expensive and often inadequate
- **VA capacity vs. demographic surge**: The VA's geriatric infrastructure was built for a smaller, younger veteran population; the Vietnam-era aging wave is straining capacity
- **Caregiver burden vs. system support**: Family caregivers provide the majority of long-term care for aging veterans, often at enormous personal cost; the VA's caregiver support program, while expanding, reaches only a fraction of those in need
- **Service-connected vs. non-service-connected aging**: The VA's eligibility and priority system creates disparities between veterans with service-connected conditions and those whose aging-related needs are not formally linked to service
- **Cost of comprehensive geriatric care vs. fiscal constraints**: Providing the level of care aging veterans need would require substantial investment at a time of competing budget priorities

## Key Questions

1. **Is the VA prepared for the Vietnam-era aging surge?** What are the specific capacity gaps in geriatric care, long-term services, and cognitive health treatment?
2. **How can home-based care be scaled?** What would it take to enable the majority of aging veterans to age in place rather than entering institutional settings?
3. **What is the impact of service-related exposures on aging?** How do Agent Orange, burn pits, and other military exposures accelerate or modify the aging process?
4. **How should caregiver support be expanded?** What level of financial, respite, and training support do family caregivers of aging veterans need?
5. **Why is elderly veteran suicide so high?** What interventions are specific to the drivers of late-life veteran suicide--isolation, chronic pain, loss of purpose?

## Vision of Success

A successful system for aging veterans would feature: sufficient VA geriatric care capacity to serve all eligible veterans without waitlists; robust home-based primary care and community-based services enabling veterans to age in place; a caregiver support program that provides meaningful financial compensation, respite care, and training to all family caregivers of aging veterans; proactive cognitive health screening and early intervention for dementia and Alzheimer's disease; integrated palliative and hospice care that honors veterans' service and respects their end-of-life wishes; a sustained reduction in elderly veteran suicide through social connection programs and mental health outreach; and recognition that the costs of caring for aging veterans are a long-term consequence of military service that must be budgeted and planned for, not treated as an unexpected crisis.
