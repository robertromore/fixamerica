# Rural Veterans: Overview

## Executive Summary

Approximately 4.7 million veterans--nearly one in four of the nation's 18.6 million living veterans--reside in rural or highly rural areas (VA Office of Rural Health, 2024). These veterans face compounding barriers to healthcare, benefits, and social services that urban veterans rarely encounter: long distances to VA medical centers, severe provider shortages, limited broadband connectivity that undermines telehealth solutions, and geographic isolation that exacerbates mental health challenges and suicide risk. Rural veterans experience higher rates of suicide, substance use disorders, and chronic disease, while simultaneously having fewer resources to address these conditions.

The VA has acknowledged the rural access crisis through dedicated programs--Community-Based Outpatient Clinics (CBOCs), the MISSION Act's community care provisions, telehealth expansion, and the Office of Rural Health (ORH)--but these efforts have not closed the gap. Rural veterans remain 2.5 times more likely to travel over 60 minutes for VA primary care than urban veterans (VA Office of Rural Health, 2023), and rural VA facilities face provider vacancy rates 30-50% higher than their urban counterparts (GAO-24-106285, 2024). The MISSION Act's community care network, designed to supplement VA capacity by partnering with private providers, has had limited success in areas where private providers are equally scarce.

Addressing rural veteran access requires a fundamentally different model than urban-centric VA service delivery: mobile health units, robust broadband investment, sustainable telehealth infrastructure, community health worker programs embedded in rural communities, enhanced travel reimbursement, and recruitment pipelines that bring healthcare professionals to underserved regions. These solutions overlap substantially with broader rural health policy, creating opportunities for cross-sector collaboration but also risks of veterans being deprioritized within general rural health initiatives.

## Core Tensions

- **Centralized VA system vs. dispersed rural population**: The VA was designed around large medical centers in population centers; rural veterans need decentralized care
- **Community care promise vs. rural provider scarcity**: The MISSION Act allows veterans to see private providers, but many rural areas have no private providers either
- **Telehealth as solution vs. broadband as prerequisite**: Virtual care could transform rural veteran access, but connectivity infrastructure lags
- **Workforce recruitment vs. rural desirability**: Competitive salaries alone cannot overcome the professional and personal preferences that draw providers to urban areas
- **Federal standardization vs. local adaptation**: VA policies designed nationally may not account for the vast differences between rural Alaska, rural Appalachia, and rural Great Plains
- **Cost per veteran vs. obligation per veteran**: Serving rural veterans costs substantially more per capita, creating budget pressure that urban-focused metrics obscure

## Key Questions

1. **Why has two decades of rural veteran health initiatives failed to close the urban-rural access gap?** What structural barriers persist?
2. **Can telehealth meaningfully substitute for in-person care for rural veterans?** What are its limitations and prerequisites?
3. **How should the VA balance investment in its own rural facilities versus community care partnerships?** Which model better serves veterans?
4. **What workforce strategies can realistically attract providers to rural VA facilities?** Are current incentives sufficient?
5. **How do rural veteran needs differ by region?** Does a Montana veteran face the same barriers as an Appalachian veteran or a Native veteran on tribal lands?

## Vision of Success

A successful rural veteran healthcare system would ensure that no veteran must travel more than 60 minutes for primary care or 90 minutes for specialty care, either through VA facilities, community care partners, or high-quality telehealth with reliable broadband. Rural VA facilities would maintain provider vacancy rates within 5 percentage points of urban facilities through competitive compensation, student loan repayment, and community integration programs. Every rural veteran would have access to same-day mental health services via telehealth, with in-person behavioral health available within one week. Mobile health units would regularly serve the most remote communities. The rural veteran suicide rate would be reduced to within 10% of the urban veteran rate within a decade. Rural veteran service organizations would be funded to provide benefits navigation, peer support, and social connection in every rural county with more than 500 veteran residents.
