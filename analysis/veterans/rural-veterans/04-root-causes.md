# Rural Veterans: Root Causes

## Overview

The persistent gap in healthcare access and outcomes between rural and urban veterans is not the result of any single policy failure but rather the product of deeply embedded structural, geographic, economic, and cultural factors. These root causes interact and reinforce each other, creating a system of compounding disadvantage that incremental reforms have been unable to dismantle.

## Structural Root Causes

### 1. Urban-Centric VA System Design

The VA healthcare system was built around large medical centers affiliated with academic medical schools, nearly all located in metropolitan areas. This design choice--made under General Omar Bradley in the late 1940s--produced world-class clinical care and research but embedded an urban geographic bias into the system's DNA.

**How this manifests:**

- 170 VA Medical Centers, with the majority located in or near cities of 250,000+ population (VA Capital Asset Inventory, 2024)
- Specialty care concentrated at tertiary medical centers, with no pathway for rural delivery
- VA budgeting formulas historically weighted toward facility-based care, disadvantaging rural outpatient models
- Capital investment decisions favor high-volume urban centers with better utilization metrics

### 2. Provider Shortage Feedback Loop

Rural areas face a self-reinforcing cycle that makes provider recruitment and retention structurally difficult:

| Stage | Dynamic | Effect |
|-------|---------|--------|
| Training | Medical schools and residency programs located in urban areas | Physicians trained in urban settings develop urban practice preferences |
| Recruitment | Rural facilities offer fewer peers, less technology, fewer career advancement options | Top candidates choose urban postings |
| Retention | Rural providers face burnout from high patient loads, isolation | Average tenure 4.2 years vs. 6.8 urban (VA Workforce Management, 2024) |
| Cascade | Departing providers increase burden on remaining staff | Further burnout and departures |
| Result | Chronic vacancies become normalized | Rural facilities operate permanently understaffed |

### 3. MISSION Act Community Care Design Flaw

The MISSION Act's community care framework assumed that private-sector providers could fill gaps where VA facilities were absent. This assumption fails in rural areas:

- **Provider scarcity is area-wide**: If no VA psychiatrist exists within 60 miles, there is typically no private psychiatrist either
- **Network adequacy is measured inadequately**: Third-party administrators (Optum, TriWest) meet contractual network adequacy standards by counting distant providers as "available"
- **Reimbursement rates discourage participation**: VA community care reimbursement rates based on Medicare plus adjustments may be insufficient to attract rural providers who can fill their panels with better-paying patients
- **Administrative burden**: Community care billing and authorization processes deter small rural practices from participating

### 4. Infrastructure Deficits

Rural veteran access depends on infrastructure that is chronically underfunded:

| Infrastructure | Rural Deficit | Impact on Veterans |
|---------------|--------------|-------------------|
| Broadband | 22% of rural veterans lack reliable service (FCC, 2024) | Excludes from telehealth, online benefits filing |
| Roads/highways | Rural road conditions deteriorating (ASCE, 2025) | Longer, more dangerous travel to facilities |
| Public transit | 89% of rural counties lack public transportation (NACo, 2023) | Veterans without vehicles cannot reach care |
| Cellular service | 14% of rural areas have no reliable signal (FCC, 2024) | Crisis line inaccessible in dead zones |

## Geographic Root Causes

### 5. Distance as a Determinant of Health

The fundamental geographic challenge cannot be engineered away without either moving veterans or moving facilities:

- Average rural veteran lives 81 miles from a VA Medical Center (VA Geographic Access Analysis, 2024)
- At 60 mph, this represents a 2.7-hour round trip for a single appointment
- For veterans with disabilities, elderly veterans, or those without reliable transportation, even 30-mile trips to a CBOC may be prohibitive
- Weather conditions in northern and mountain states can make winter travel dangerous or impossible

### 6. Regional Variation

"Rural" encompasses vastly different environments, each with distinct challenges:

| Region | Characteristics | Unique Barriers |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| Great Plains (MT, WY, ND, SD) | Extremely low population density, harsh climate | Distances of 200+ miles to nearest VA, winter isolation |
| Appalachia (WV, KY, TN) | Mountainous terrain, economic distress, opioid crisis | Road infrastructure, provider shortage, substance abuse |
| Rural South (MS, AL, AR) | Racial health disparities, Medicaid non-expansion | Minority veterans face compounded barriers |
| Alaska | Frontier conditions, indigenous populations | Air-only access to many communities, cultural barriers |
| Pacific Northwest (OR, WA rural) | Dispersed population, forest/mountain terrain | Limited broadband, seasonal access challenges |
| Southwest (AZ, NM tribal areas) | Tribal sovereignty, Indian Health Service overlap | Jurisdictional complexity, cultural competency |

## Economic Root Causes

### 7. Rural Economic Decline

Rural communities where many veterans live have experienced decades of economic contraction:

- Rural poverty rate 15.4% vs. 11.2% urban (Census Bureau, 2023)
- 64% of rural counties lost population between 2010 and 2020 (USDA ERS, 2023)
- Rural hospital closures: 140+ since 2010, with 600+ more at risk (Chartis Center for Rural Health, 2024)
- Loss of rural employers reduces veteran employment opportunities, increasing financial stress and VA dependence

**Veteran-specific economic effects:**

- Rural veterans more likely to be self-employed or in agriculture, with irregular income and limited employer health benefits
- Higher out-of-pocket transportation costs for VA care
- Less access to vocational rehabilitation and employment assistance programs
- Lower property values reduce wealth accumulation

### 8. VA Budget Model Disadvantages Rural Care

The Veterans Equitable Resource Allocation (VERA) model distributes funding based on workload and enrollment. This structurally disadvantages rural care:

- Higher per-encounter costs in rural settings (provider travel, lower volume) reduce efficiency metrics
- Rural facilities generate less revenue from community care billing
- Capital investment formulas favor high-volume facilities
- ORH supplemental funding ($134 per rural enrollee, FY 2024) partially offsets but does not eliminate the disparity

## Cultural Root Causes

### 9. Self-Reliance Culture and Help-Seeking Stigma

Rural communities and military culture share overlapping values of self-reliance, stoicism, and skepticism of institutional help:

- Rural veterans are significantly less likely to seek mental health care, even when symptomatic (Stecker et al., *Military Medicine*, 2013)
- Cultural norms in rural communities frame healthcare use--particularly mental healthcare--as weakness
- Smaller communities offer less anonymity; veterans may avoid local clinics where they might be recognized
- Faith-based coping may substitute for professional treatment, sometimes effectively but sometimes delaying necessary care

### 10. Distrust of Federal Government

Rural communities disproportionately express distrust of federal institutions, including the VA:

- VA scandals (wait time manipulation, claims backlog) reinforce pre-existing skepticism
- Political culture in many rural areas frames government programs as inefficient or intrusive
- Some eligible veterans decline VA enrollment on principle, despite need
- This distrust coexists with strong support for military service, creating a paradox where veterans resist using the benefits they earned

### 11. Gun Culture and Lethal Means

Rural veteran communities have deeply embedded firearms culture that intersects with the highest-risk suicide factor:

- Rural firearm ownership rates exceed 60% in many veteran-heavy states (RAND, 2023)
- Cultural resistance to any form of firearm restriction, including voluntary safe storage programs
- Lethal means safety messaging perceived as gun control rather than suicide prevention
- Rural veterans who die by suicide use firearms at rates of 78%, compared to 63% for urban veterans (VA National Suicide Data Report, 2023)

## Systemic Root Causes

### 12. Fragmented Federal Response

Rural veteran needs cross multiple federal agencies with poor coordination:

| Agency | Rural Veteran Responsibility | Coordination Gap |
|--------|----------------------------|-----------------|
| VA | Healthcare, benefits, burial | Limited integration with non-VA rural health |
| USDA | Rural development, broadband | Programs not targeted to veteran needs |
| HHS/HRSA | Community health centers, workforce | FQHCs not designed for veteran populations |
| FCC | Broadband deployment | No veteran-specific connectivity mandate |
| DOT | Rural transportation | No veteran transit programs |
| DOD | Transition assistance | Programs end at separation; no rural handoff |

### 13. Data and Measurement Gaps

The VA and Congress lack the data needed to fully understand and address rural veteran challenges:

- VA access data does not consistently differentiate rural from urban outcomes
- Community care utilization data does not capture care quality in rural settings
- Suicide and mental health data lag 2-3 years, obscuring current rural trends
- Rural veteran satisfaction surveys have lower response rates, potentially biasing results
- No standardized measurement of rural telehealth effectiveness versus in-person care

## Root Cause Interaction Map

The root causes described above do not operate in isolation. They create feedback loops:

1. **Access-Health Spiral**: Distance from care leads to delayed treatment, which leads to worse health outcomes, which increases care needs and costs, which strains rural VA budgets, which reduces services, which increases distance to care
2. **Workforce-Quality Spiral**: Provider shortages increase workload, which increases burnout, which drives departures, which worsens shortages, which reduces care quality, which reduces veteran satisfaction, which reduces political support for rural VA investment
3. **Technology-Equity Paradox**: Telehealth could solve distance barriers, but broadband investment bypasses the most rural areas, leaving the most isolated veterans excluded from the primary solution being deployed
4. **Culture-Access Interaction**: Cultural resistance to help-seeking means that even when access improves, utilization may not follow without parallel cultural engagement efforts
