# Rural Veterans: Historical Context and Evolution

## Overview

The challenge of serving rural veterans is as old as the Veterans Administration itself, rooted in the fundamental tension between a centralized medical system designed around large urban hospitals and a veteran population that has always included substantial rural and frontier communities. From the post-Civil War era through the current MISSION Act framework, the history of rural veteran healthcare reflects recurring cycles of problem recognition, programmatic response, and persistent structural inadequacy.

## Timeline of Key Developments

### Early Federal Veterans Care (1811-1930)

- **1811**: Congress authorized the first federal veterans facility (Naval Home, Philadelphia), establishing the pattern of institutional, centralized care
- **1865-1870**: National Home for Disabled Volunteer Soldiers established at 11 locations, some in rural areas, but designed as residential institutions rather than outpatient care
- **1917-1921**: World War I generated 204,000 disabled veterans. The Public Health Service, Bureau of War Risk Insurance, and Federal Board for Vocational Education divided responsibility chaotically, with rural veterans particularly underserved
- **1921**: Veterans Bureau established under Charles Forbes, consolidating services but maintaining a hospital-centric model
- **1930**: Veterans Administration created, inheriting 54 hospitals. Rural access was not a distinct policy concern

### VA Hospital System Expansion (1930-1970)

- **1930s**: VA built hospitals near population centers and military installations, creating the geographic distribution pattern that persists today
- **1945-1950**: Post-WWII expansion added 80+ hospitals. General Omar Bradley, as VA Administrator, prioritized affiliating VA hospitals with medical schools--almost exclusively located in urban areas. This partnership transformed VA medicine but cemented urban concentration
- **1946**: VA established 5,000+ outpatient clinics, but most were co-located with hospitals rather than distributed to rural areas
- **1950s-1960s**: Korean and Vietnam War-era veterans swelled demand. Rural veterans frequently drove hours for care. Congress periodically authorized "satellite clinics" but funding was minimal

### Recognition of the Rural Problem (1970-2000)

- **1973**: VA eligibility reform (P.L. 93-82) expanded outpatient care eligibility, increasing demand from rural veterans who previously were limited to hospital-based care
- **1978**: VA began systematic study of geographic access; first reports documented multi-hour travel times for rural veterans in western states
- **1985**: VA Inspector General report found that veterans in remote areas of Montana, Wyoming, and Alaska received "substantially inferior access" to care compared to urban veterans
- **1989**: Department of Veterans Affairs created (elevated to Cabinet level). Rural access was not a stated priority of the reorganization
- **1993**: VA established the first Community-Based Outpatient Clinics (CBOCs) under a pilot program (P.L. 103-210). Initial clinics opened in McAlester, Oklahoma; Galesburg, Illinois; and other rural locations
- **1995-2000**: CBOC expansion accelerated under VA Secretary Jesse Brown and Kenneth Kizer's Veterans Health Administration restructuring. By 2000, approximately 300 CBOCs operated, roughly one-third in rural areas

### Office of Rural Health and CBOC Growth (2000-2015)

- **2001**: VA established the Rural Health Resource Center within the Office of Policy and Planning, the first dedicated organizational focus on rural veterans
- **2006**: Congress mandated creation of the VA Office of Rural Health (ORH) through Section 212 of P.L. 109-461 (Veterans Benefits, Health Care, and Information Technology Act). ORH received $20 million in initial funding
- **2007**: ORH formally established with a mission to improve healthcare access for rural veterans through telehealth, outreach, and facility planning
- **2009**: VA expanded ORH budget to $85 million under the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act, funding 38 rural health pilot projects
- **2010**: Veterans Rural Health Access Initiative launched, placing mobile health clinics in 12 states
- **2011**: VA's Project ARCH (Access Received Closer to Home) piloted community care referrals in five highly rural areas (Maine, Montana, Kansas, Arizona, South Carolina)--an early precursor to the MISSION Act
- **2012**: ORH budget reached $250 million. CBOC count exceeded 800
- **2013**: VA Strategic Plan for Rural Veterans Health included targets for telehealth expansion, broadband access, and CBOC staffing
- **2014**: VA Access Crisis and the wait time scandal. Phoenix VA Medical Center and other facilities were found to have falsified wait time data. While the scandal centered on urban facilities, it exposed systemic access failures that were even more severe in rural areas

### Choice Act, MISSION Act, and Modern Era (2014-Present)

- **2014**: Veterans Access, Choice, and Accountability Act (Choice Act, P.L. 113-146). Authorized veterans to seek private-sector care if they lived more than 40 miles from a VA facility or faced wait times exceeding 30 days. The 40-mile rule was significant for rural veterans but implementation was chaotic
- **2015-2017**: Choice Act implementation revealed that 40-mile calculation (driving distance vs. straight-line distance) created arbitrary eligibility differences. Rural provider networks were inadequate. Administrative burden discouraged participation
- **2017**: MISSION Act Commission recommended replacing Choice Act with a permanent community care framework
- **2018**: MISSION Act (P.L. 115-182) signed into law. Replaced Choice Act with access standards based on drive time (30 minutes for primary care/mental health, 60 minutes for specialty care) and wait times (20 days for primary care/mental health, 28 days for specialty care). Established Community Care Network contracts with third-party administrators
- **2019**: MISSION Act implementation began June 6, 2019. Community Care Networks organized into six regional contracts
- **2020**: COVID-19 pandemic forced emergency telehealth expansion. VA issued temporary waivers allowing video and phone appointments across state lines, dramatically expanding rural veterans' access to VA providers
- **2020**: COMPACT Act (P.L. 116-214) authorized emergency suicidal crisis care at non-VA facilities, particularly important for rural veterans far from VA emergency departments
- **2021**: VA ATLAS (Accessing Telehealth through Local Area Stations) program expanded to 50+ community locations (Walmart, American Legion posts, VFW halls)
- **2022**: PACT Act (P.L. 117-168) expanded toxic exposure eligibility, bringing an estimated 280,000 previously unenrolled rural veterans into VA care by FY 2024
- **2023**: VA proposed revising MISSION Act access standards to tighten drive time thresholds, potentially expanding community care eligibility for more rural veterans
- **2024**: ORH budget reached $327 million. CBOC count reached 1,074 (502 rural). VA ATLAS expanded to 148 community telehealth sites

### Telehealth Evolution

| Era | Technology | Reach | Limitation |
|-----|-----------|-------|------------|
| 1998-2005 | Store-and-forward imaging | ~5,000 rural veterans | Narrow clinical applications |
| 2006-2012 | Clinical video telehealth (hub-spoke) | ~50,000 rural veterans | Required travel to CBOC |
| 2013-2019 | VA Video Connect (home-based) | ~180,000 rural veterans | Broadband dependency |
| 2020-2022 | Pandemic expansion (all modalities) | ~890,000 rural veterans | Temporary authorities |
| 2023-present | Permanent telehealth framework | ~1.2M rural veterans | Broadband gap persists |

*Source: VA Office of Connected Care; VA Office of Rural Health.*

### Community Care Models

| Program | Years | Mechanism | Rural Impact | Outcome |
|---------|-------|-----------|-------------|---------|
| Fee-basis care | 1945-2014 | Individual provider agreements | Inconsistent, provider-dependent | Replaced by Choice |
| Project ARCH | 2011-2014 | Pilot community referrals (5 areas) | Demonstrated viability | Informed Choice Act |
| Choice Act | 2014-2019 | 40-mile/30-day eligibility triggers | Expanded access in theory | Poor rural provider networks |
| MISSION Act | 2019-present | Drive-time/wait-time standards | More rational eligibility | Network adequacy remains problematic |

## Lessons from History

### What Has Worked

- **CBOC expansion**: The most significant structural improvement in rural veteran access since the VA system was created, placing care closer to where veterans live
- **Telehealth during COVID**: Demonstrated that regulatory barriers, not technological ones, were the primary obstacle to virtual care delivery
- **ORH as dedicated advocate**: Having an office specifically responsible for rural veterans has ensured that rural needs remain visible in VA planning
- **PACT Act outreach**: Targeted rural enrollment efforts have been more successful than general outreach

### What Has Failed

- **Community care in provider deserts**: Every iteration of community care--fee-basis, Choice Act, MISSION Act--has struggled with the same fundamental problem: private providers are equally scarce in rural areas
- **Workforce incentives**: Decades of recruitment bonuses and loan repayment have not resolved rural provider shortages
- **One-size-fits-all policy**: National VA policies repeatedly fail to account for the enormous variation in rural geography, infrastructure, and population density
- **Funding adequacy**: ORH budgets, while growing, remain insufficient relative to the scope of rural access challenges

### Recurring Patterns

1. **Problem recognition without structural change**: Congress and the VA repeatedly acknowledge rural access gaps without fundamentally redesigning the urban-centric delivery model
2. **Technology as substitute for investment**: Telehealth, mobile clinics, and digital tools are deployed as alternatives to building sustainable rural healthcare infrastructure
3. **Community care as backstop**: Private-sector referrals are positioned as the solution when the VA cannot provide care, but rural private-sector capacity is equally limited
4. **Pilots that do not scale**: Successful rural health pilot programs (Project ARCH, mobile clinics, ATLAS sites) demonstrate viability but receive insufficient funding for national deployment
