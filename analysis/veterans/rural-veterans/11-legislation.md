# Rural Veterans: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative landscape for rural veteran healthcare sits at the intersection of veterans affairs law, telecommunications policy, healthcare workforce policy, and rural development. Current law--principally the MISSION Act (2018), PACT Act (2022), and COMPACT Act (2020)--provides a framework for community care and expanded eligibility, but significant statutory gaps remain in telehealth licensing, broadband access, workforce incentives, and transportation reimbursement. This document proposes federal legislation, state model legislation, and regulatory reforms to close the rural veteran access gap.

## Federal Legislation

### Rural Veterans Healthcare Access Act

**Purpose**: Comprehensive legislation addressing telehealth licensing, travel reimbursement, workforce incentives, and facility enhancement for rural veterans.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Rural Veterans Healthcare Access Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
(1) approximately 4,700,000 veterans reside in rural or highly
    rural areas;
(2) rural veterans face travel distances, provider shortages, and
    broadband barriers that significantly impede access to healthcare;
(3) the suicide rate among rural veterans is 67 percent higher than
    among urban veterans;
(4) existing community care networks are inadequate in rural areas
    where private providers are equally scarce; and
(5) a comprehensive approach addressing telehealth, workforce,
    transportation, and facilities is required.

SEC. 3. CROSS-STATE TELEHEALTH AUTHORITY.

(a) IN GENERAL.--Notwithstanding any provision of State law, a
    covered healthcare professional may provide covered telehealth
    services to a veteran located in any State, regardless of where
    the professional is licensed.
(b) COVERED HEALTHCARE PROFESSIONAL.--The term "covered healthcare
    professional" means--
    (1) an employee of the Department of Veterans Affairs who is
        authorized to provide clinical care; or
    (2) a healthcare professional providing services to veterans
        pursuant to a community care contract under section 1703 of
        title 38, United States Code.
(c) COVERED TELEHEALTH SERVICES.--The term "covered telehealth
    services" means healthcare services delivered through real-time
    audio-video communication, asynchronous store-and-forward
    technology, or remote patient monitoring.
(d) MALPRACTICE.--A covered healthcare professional providing
    covered telehealth services under this section shall be treated
    as an employee of the Federal Government for purposes of chapter
    171 of title 28, United States Code (relating to tort claims).
(e) STATE NOTIFICATION.--The Secretary shall notify the licensing
    board of each State in which a covered healthcare professional
    provides telehealth services under this section.

SEC. 4. BENEFICIARY TRAVEL REFORM.

(a) MILEAGE RATE.--Section 111(g) of title 38, United States Code,
    is amended by striking "41.5 cents per mile" and inserting "the
    standard mileage rate established by the Internal Revenue Service
    for medical purposes, adjusted annually".
(b) ADVANCE PAYMENT.--The Secretary shall establish a program to
    provide advance travel payments to eligible veterans who--
    (1) reside more than 50 miles from the nearest Department
        facility providing the needed service; and
    (2) demonstrate financial hardship as determined by the Secretary.
(c) LODGING.--The Secretary shall reimburse reasonable lodging
    expenses for veterans who must travel more than 100 miles one way
    for Department care, at rates not to exceed the Federal per diem
    rate.
(d) AUTOMATED CLAIMS.--Not later than 18 months after enactment,
    the Secretary shall implement an automated travel reimbursement
    system using GPS-verified appointment attendance, eliminating the
    requirement for paper claim submission.

SEC. 5. RURAL WORKFORCE INCENTIVES.

(a) RURAL DIFFERENTIAL PAY.--Each employee of the Department
    assigned to a facility located in a rural or highly rural area
    (as defined by Rural-Urban Commuting Area codes) shall receive
    a pay differential of not less than 10 percent above the
    applicable rate of basic pay.
(b) ENHANCED RECRUITMENT BONUSES.--The Secretary may offer
    recruitment bonuses of up to $100,000 for physicians and $50,000
    for other clinical professionals recruited to rural Department
    facilities, with a minimum 3-year service obligation.
(c) LOAN REPAYMENT.--The Secretary shall establish a Rural VA Loan
    Repayment Program providing up to $250,000 in student loan
    repayment for clinical professionals who complete a 4-year
    service obligation at a rural Department facility.
(d) RURAL RESIDENCY POSITIONS.--The Secretary shall fund not fewer
    than 200 graduate medical education residency positions at rural
    Department facilities, with priority given to primary care,
    psychiatry, and general surgery.
(e) HOUSING ASSISTANCE.--The Secretary may provide housing assistance,
    including government quarters or housing allowances, to clinical
    professionals assigned to rural facilities in areas with
    demonstrated housing scarcity.

SEC. 6. ENHANCED COMMUNITY-BASED OUTPATIENT CLINICS.

(a) DESIGNATION.--The Secretary shall designate not fewer than 100
    community-based outpatient clinics in rural areas as "Enhanced
    CBOCs" providing expanded services including--
    (1) mental health and substance use disorder treatment;
    (2) dental care;
    (3) basic diagnostic imaging;
    (4) physical therapy and rehabilitation; and
    (5) women's health services.
(b) STAFFING.--Each Enhanced CBOC shall be staffed by not fewer than
    one physician or advanced practice provider, one mental health
    professional, one dental provider, and appropriate support staff.
(c) AUTHORIZATION.--There is authorized to be appropriated
    $500,000,000 for capital improvements to establish Enhanced CBOCs
    and $300,000,000 annually for operating costs.

SEC. 7. MOBILE HEALTH UNITS.

(a) FLEET.--The Secretary shall procure and deploy not fewer than
    100 mobile health units to serve veterans in highly rural areas.
(b) SERVICES.--Each mobile health unit shall provide primary care,
    basic diagnostics, mental health counseling, telehealth
    connectivity, and pharmacy services.
(c) SCHEDULE.--Each mobile health unit shall maintain a regular
    circuit serving not fewer than 5 communities on a recurring
    schedule of not less than biweekly visits.

SEC. 8. RURAL VETERAN BROADBAND ACCESS.

(a) PROGRAM.--The Secretary, in coordination with the Federal
    Communications Commission and the Secretary of Agriculture,
    shall establish a Rural Veteran Broadband Access Program to
    ensure telehealth-capable internet connectivity for all enrolled
    rural veterans.
(b) COMPONENTS.--The program shall include--
    (1) coordination with Broadband Equity, Access, and Deployment
        (BEAD) program to prioritize veteran-dense rural areas;
    (2) expansion of the Digital Divide Consult program;
    (3) subsidies for satellite internet service for veterans in
        areas without terrestrial broadband; and
    (4) installation of community telehealth access points at not
        fewer than 500 locations nationwide.

SEC. 9. VA-FQHC PARTNERSHIP AUTHORITY.

(a) PARTNERSHIPS.--The Secretary shall enter into formal partnership
    agreements with not fewer than 500 Federally Qualified Health
    Centers in rural areas for the delivery of healthcare services
    to enrolled veterans.
(b) CREDENTIALING.--The Secretary shall establish streamlined
    credentialing processes for FQHC providers delivering services
    to veterans under partnership agreements.
(c) REIMBURSEMENT.--FQHC providers shall be reimbursed at rates
    not less than Medicare rates plus 40 percent for services
    delivered to veterans under partnership agreements.
(d) INTEROPERABILITY.--The Secretary shall ensure electronic health
    record interoperability between Department systems and FQHC
    systems within 3 years of enactment.

SEC. 10. RURAL VETERAN ACCESS METRICS.

(a) REPORTING.--The Secretary shall submit to Congress an annual
    report on rural veteran access that includes--
    (1) rural-specific wait times, drive times, and satisfaction data;
    (2) rural provider vacancy rates by facility and position;
    (3) telehealth utilization and outcomes (rural vs. urban);
    (4) community care network adequacy in rural areas; and
    (5) rural veteran suicide rates.
(b) PERFORMANCE STANDARDS.--The Secretary shall establish
    rural-specific performance standards that account for geographic
    and demographic differences between rural and urban facilities.

SEC. 11. FEDERAL RURAL VETERAN COORDINATION.

The President shall establish a Federal Rural Veteran Coordination
Council, co-chaired by the Secretary of Veterans Affairs and the
Secretary of Agriculture, to coordinate rural veteran services
across Federal agencies.

SEC. 12. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There is authorized to be appropriated to carry out this Act
$3,000,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2036.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3** resolves the most immediate barrier to rural telehealth by establishing federal preemption of state licensing requirements. The FTCA malpractice coverage eliminates liability ambiguity.
- **Section 4** modernizes Beneficiary Travel to reflect actual costs and eliminates administrative barriers through automation.
- **Section 5** creates a competitive compensation framework that addresses the primary reasons providers decline or leave rural VA positions.
- **Sections 6-7** build physical healthcare infrastructure in rural areas through both fixed facility enhancement and mobile service delivery.
- **Section 8** addresses the broadband prerequisite for telehealth expansion.
- **Section 9** leverages existing FQHC infrastructure rather than building parallel systems.
- **Sections 10-11** create accountability and coordination mechanisms.

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Cost ($3B/year) | Represents <3% of total VA budget; offset by reduced emergency care and hospitalization costs |
| State licensing preemption | Necessary for telehealth; modeled on existing VA authority; preserves notification |
| Union concerns about differential pay | Differential applies to all rural employees, not just new hires |
| FQHC capacity concerns | Partnerships voluntary; reimbursement rate designed to be attractive |

**Refinements**:

- **Fallback position**: If comprehensive bill is infeasible, pursue individual sections as standalone bills (telehealth licensing, travel reform, workforce incentives)
- **Pilot approach**: Authorize Enhanced CBOCs and Mobile Health Units as 5-year pilots in 20 states before national deployment
- **Sunset provision**: Include 10-year authorization with mandatory GAO evaluation at year 5

## State Model Legislation

### Rural Veteran Telehealth Access Act (Model)

**Purpose**: State-level legislation to facilitate VA telehealth delivery and supplement federal programs.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Rural Veteran Telehealth
Access Act".

SECTION 2. TELEHEALTH LICENSING RECIPROCITY.

(a) A healthcare professional licensed in another state who provides
    telehealth services to a veteran located in this state pursuant
    to a Department of Veterans Affairs program or community care
    contract shall be deemed to hold a valid license in this state
    for the purposes of such services.
(b) This section shall not apply to prescribing of controlled
    substances unless the professional holds a valid DEA registration.

SECTION 3. BROADBAND PRIORITY AREAS.

The [State Broadband Authority] shall designate census tracts with
veteran populations exceeding 5 percent of the total population as
priority deployment areas under the state broadband plan.

SECTION 4. COUNTY VETERANS SERVICE OFFICERS.

(a) Each county with 500 or more veteran residents shall employ at
    least one full-time County Veterans Service Officer.
(b) The state shall provide matching funds of up to $50,000 per
    county for CVSO salaries and operations.
(c) CVSOs shall receive annual training on VA telehealth access,
    community care eligibility, and rural-specific resources.

SECTION 5. VETERAN TRANSPORTATION.

(a) The [State Department of Transportation] shall include veteran
    healthcare access as a criterion in rural transit planning.
(b) The state shall provide a tax credit of $0.30 per mile for
    volunteer drivers who transport veterans to VA appointments.
```

**Adaptations**: States with existing robust veteran services (Texas, California, Minnesota) may focus on broadband and telehealth provisions; states with minimal infrastructure may need to adopt all sections.

## Regulatory Framework

### VA Community Care Network Adequacy Standards

**Existing Authority**: 38 U.S.C. Section 1703(h) authorizes the Secretary to establish access standards and network adequacy requirements.

**Proposed Regulation**:

The Secretary should promulgate a rule amending 38 C.F.R. Part 17 to:

1. **Define rural network adequacy separately from urban standards**: Require community care network administrators to demonstrate that network providers are available within 45 minutes (primary care) and 90 minutes (specialty care) for veterans in rural areas, using actual drive time rather than straight-line distance
2. **Prohibit distant provider counting**: Network adequacy calculations shall not count providers located more than 90 minutes' drive time from the veteran population being served
3. **Require telehealth alternatives**: Where no in-person provider is available within time standards, network administrators must provide telehealth access within 7 calendar days
4. **Impose penalties for unfulfilled authorizations**: Network administrators shall be financially penalized for community care authorizations that remain unfulfilled for more than 30 days due to provider unavailability

### VA VERA Model Rural Adjustment

**Existing Authority**: 38 U.S.C. Section 8110 authorizes the Secretary to establish resource allocation methodologies.

**Proposed Administrative Action**: The Secretary should modify the VERA allocation model to include a rural cost adjustment factor of 15-25%, reflecting the higher per-encounter costs of serving dispersed rural veteran populations.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Federal preemption of state licensing**: The Supremacy Clause supports federal authority to regulate VA healthcare delivery. The VA already exercises limited preemption under existing authority; the proposed legislation makes this explicit for telehealth.
- **Spending power**: All proposed programs are authorized expenditures under Congress's spending power and the government's obligation to provide veteran benefits.
- **No Second Amendment implications**: This legislation does not address firearm access or lethal means safety.

### Preemption Questions

- **State medical licensing boards**: Explicit preemption of state licensing requirements for VA telehealth may face challenges from state medical boards. Historical precedent supports federal authority for VA providers (see 38 U.S.C. Section 7301).
- **Controlled substance prescribing**: Cross-state telehealth prescribing of controlled substances remains subject to DEA registration requirements. The PRESCRIBE Act would need coordination with DEA regulatory authority.

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- **Rural access metrics**: Annual Congressional reporting creates transparency and accountability
- **Network adequacy enforcement**: Financial penalties for unfulfilled authorizations create contractor incentives
- **GAO evaluation**: Biennial independent evaluation ensures objective assessment
- **VA OIG oversight**: Inspector General retains authority to audit rural programs

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Rural Veterans Healthcare Access Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Enhanced CBOC designation gaming | VA could designate facilities that barely qualify as "rural" to meet the 100-site requirement while avoiding the most remote areas | High |
| Mileage rate lag | Tying reimbursement to IRS medical rate still understates actual costs (IRS rate is $0.22/mile vs. actual costs of $0.50+) | Medium |
| Loan repayment without retention | Providers could complete 4-year obligation and immediately depart | Medium |
| FQHC partnership limitations | FQHCs in financial distress may be unable to absorb veteran patients regardless of reimbursement | Medium |
| Mobile health unit coverage gaps | 100 units for 740,000 highly rural veterans leaves significant populations unserved | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No mandate for broadband deployment | VA cannot compel ISPs to build infrastructure | Broadband is FCC/USDA jurisdiction; VA can only coordinate |
| Workforce pipeline takes years | Provider shortages persist during pipeline development | Medical education timeline is structurally long |
| Cultural barriers unaddressed by legislation | Stigma and distrust reduce utilization even when access improves | Cultural change requires community engagement, not statutes |
| Community care network design unchanged | Network administrators retain perverse incentives | Contractual reform may be more effective than statutory |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **CBOC designation formula**: Require Enhanced CBOC designation to prioritize facilities in the bottom quintile of rural access (highest drive times, lowest provider availability), not merely facilities classified as "rural"
2. **Mileage rate adjustment**: Amend Section 4 to use the IRS standard business mileage rate ($0.67/mile in 2024) rather than the medical rate, or create a VA-specific rural travel rate based on actual cost studies
3. **Retention incentives**: Add graduated retention bonuses (25% at year 5, 50% at year 8, 100% at year 10) to supplement loan repayment
4. **FQHC capacity assessment**: Require VA to assess FQHC capacity before establishing partnerships; provide supplemental funding for FQHC capacity expansion where needed
5. **Mobile unit scaling**: Include authorization for expansion beyond 100 units based on coverage assessments at years 3 and 5

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Interagency coordination historically weak | Codify Rural Veteran Coordination Council with statutory reporting requirements |
| VA bureaucratic delays | Require implementation timelines with Congressional notification for missed deadlines |
| Data lag prevents real-time management | Invest in real-time rural access dashboards (funded in Section 10) |
| Election-cycle disruption | 10-year authorization provides continuity beyond political cycles |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- **Year 3**: GAO interim evaluation of Enhanced CBOC and Mobile Health Unit pilots
- **Year 5**: Comprehensive GAO evaluation of all programs; Secretary reports to Congress with recommendations for modification
- **Year 8**: Congressional reauthorization hearing with updated cost-benefit analysis
- **Year 10**: Authorization expires unless reauthorized; all programs subject to evaluation
