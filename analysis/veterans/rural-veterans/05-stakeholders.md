# Rural Veterans: Stakeholders

## Overview

Rural veteran healthcare involves a complex web of stakeholders spanning federal and state government, healthcare providers, veteran service organizations, community groups, and the private sector. The fragmented nature of rural service delivery means that effective reform requires coordination across actors who often have competing priorities and limited channels for collaboration.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Rural Veterans and Their Families

**Population**: 4.7 million rural veterans; an estimated 7-9 million family members

**Needs and interests:**

- Accessible healthcare within reasonable travel distance
- Mental health and substance use disorder treatment without stigma
- Reliable telehealth options with broadband connectivity
- Adequate travel reimbursement for VA facility visits
- Employment opportunities and economic stability
- Social connection and community integration
- Benefits navigation assistance

**Subgroups with distinct needs:**

| Subgroup | Population (est.) | Distinct Challenges |
|----------|------------------|-------------------|
| Elderly rural veterans (65+) | 2.1 million | Mobility limitations, chronic disease, isolation |
| Post-9/11 rural veterans | 850,000 | Transition difficulties, PTSD, TBI, young families |
| Native American rural veterans | 140,000 | Tribal sovereignty, IHS overlap, cultural barriers |
| Women rural veterans | 280,000 | MST services, reproductive healthcare, childcare |
| Rural veterans with disabilities | 1.4 million | Accessibility of facilities and transportation |

**Power and influence**: Low individually but significant collectively. Rural veterans vote at high rates and are disproportionately represented in Congress through rural-state overrepresentation in the Senate.

### Department of Veterans Affairs

**Key units:**

| Unit | Role | Budget (FY 2024) |
|------|------|-----------------|
| Office of Rural Health (ORH) | Coordinates rural programs, pilots, funding | $327M |
| Veterans Health Administration (VHA) | Operates medical centers and CBOCs | $105B (total) |
| Office of Connected Care | Telehealth, digital health, VA Video Connect | $1.2B |
| Office of Community Care | MISSION Act, community provider networks | $24B |
| Beneficiary Travel Program | Travel reimbursement for VA appointments | $1.2B |

**Interests**: Fulfilling its mission to serve all veterans; managing budget constraints; demonstrating access improvements; avoiding Congressional criticism

**Tensions**: ORH advocates for rural investment while VHA central office prioritizes system-wide efficiency metrics that favor urban high-volume care

### Congress

**Key committees:**

| Committee | Rural Veteran Relevance |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Senate Veterans' Affairs Committee | Authorization, oversight of VA rural programs |
| House Veterans' Affairs Committee | Authorization, oversight, constituent casework |
| Senate/House Appropriations (MilCon-VA) | Funding for ORH, CBOCs, telehealth |
| Senate Commerce Committee | Broadband deployment affecting rural telehealth |
| House Rural Caucus | Cross-cutting rural infrastructure and services |

**Key members (as of 2025)**:

- Senators from high-rural-veteran states (Montana, West Virginia, Maine, Mississippi) have outsized influence on rural VA policy
- The Senate VA Committee has historically been bipartisan on rural access issues
- House members from rural districts constitute active caseworkers for VA access complaints

**Interests**: Responding to constituent complaints; maintaining VA facilities in their districts; demonstrating veteran support; managing federal spending

### State Governments

**Role in rural veteran services:**

- Administer state veteran benefits (property tax exemptions, education grants, employment preferences)
- Operate state veterans homes (primarily long-term care)
- Run Governor's Challenge suicide prevention programs
- License telehealth providers (state licensing requirements can restrict cross-state VA telehealth)
- Manage Medicaid programs that serve some low-income veterans
- Deploy broadband infrastructure through state BEAD programs

**Variation**: State-level veteran services vary enormously. Texas, California, and New York invest hundreds of millions; some smaller states invest single-digit millions.

## Healthcare Delivery Stakeholders

### Community Health Centers (FQHCs)

**Relevance**: 1,400+ Federally Qualified Health Centers operate in rural areas, many serving veteran populations. Potential partners for VA community care but not currently well-integrated.

**Interests**: Maintaining funding, expanding patient base, avoiding administrative burden of VA billing

### Rural Hospitals and Clinics

**Status**: 140+ rural hospitals have closed since 2010 (Chartis Center for Rural Health, 2024), with 600+ more at risk of closure. Surviving rural hospitals are potential VA community care partners but face their own financial precarity.

**Interests**: Financial sustainability, maintaining patient volume, avoiding unfunded mandates

### Community Care Network Administrators

**Current contractors**: Optum (most regions) and TriWest (some western regions) manage VA community care referral networks under MISSION Act contracts.

**Interests**: Contract compliance, network adequacy metrics, profit margins

**Criticism**: Network adequacy standards that count distant providers as "available" mask actual rural access gaps

### Private Rural Providers

**Role**: Independent physicians, nurse practitioners, and specialists in rural areas who participate (or decline to participate) in VA community care networks.

**Interests**: Adequate reimbursement, manageable administrative burden, clinical autonomy

**Challenge**: Many rural private providers are at capacity and cannot absorb additional VA patients even if willing

## Veteran Service Organizations (VSOs)

### National Organizations

| Organization | Rural Focus | Key Activities |
|-------------|------------|---------------|
| American Legion | 12,500+ posts, majority in small towns and rural areas | Benefits assistance, community support, advocacy |
| Veterans of Foreign Wars (VFW) | 6,000+ posts, significant rural presence | Legislative advocacy, community engagement |
| Disabled American Veterans (DAV) | Transportation program (650+ vans) | Free rides to VA facilities for rural veterans |
| Iraq and Afghanistan Veterans of America (IAVA) | Policy advocacy, some rural outreach | Transition support, mental health awareness |
| Paralyzed Veterans of America (PVA) | Specialized focus | Accessibility, rehabilitation, spinal cord injury |

**Power**: VSOs have significant Congressional influence, testifying regularly before the VA committees and mobilizing grassroots advocacy. The American Legion and VFW, with their deep rural presence, are particularly influential on rural veteran issues.

### State and Local VSOs

- County Veterans Service Officers (CVSOs) serve as frontline benefits navigators in rural areas. Approximately 2,500 CVSOs operate nationally, but many rural counties share a CVSO or have none
- State veterans commissions coordinate state-level services
- Local volunteer organizations (Rotary, Lions clubs, faith communities) provide informal support

## Technology and Infrastructure Stakeholders

### Telehealth and Health Technology Companies

**Role**: Develop and provide telehealth platforms, remote patient monitoring, and digital health tools used by the VA

**Key players**: VA Video Connect (VA-developed), Cerner/Oracle (VA electronic health record), remote monitoring device manufacturers

**Interests**: VA contracts, technology adoption, regulatory environment

### Broadband Providers and Programs

**Key programs:**

| Program | Funding | Rural Veteran Relevance |
|---------|---------|----------------------|
| BEAD (Broadband Equity, Access, and Deployment) | $42.5B | Primary federal broadband deployment mechanism |
| ACP (Affordable Connectivity Program) | Expired 2024 | Subsidized rural veteran internet access |
| USDA ReConnect | $3.1B | Rural broadband loans and grants |
| VA Digital Divide Consult | ~$50M annually | Tablets and hotspots for veterans |

**Tension**: Broadband deployment timelines extend 3-5 years; rural veterans need connectivity now

## Opposition and Skeptic Stakeholders

### Fiscal Conservative Organizations

Organizations such as the Heritage Foundation and Cato Institute raise cost concerns about expanding rural VA programs, advocating instead for privatization of VA care and market-based solutions.

### VA Privatization Advocates

Some policy groups and political figures advocate for replacing VA direct care with voucher systems. While framed as expanding veteran choice, privatization would likely worsen rural access because private-sector healthcare is equally or more scarce in rural areas.

### VA Employee Unions

AFGE (American Federation of Government Employees) represents approximately 270,000 VA employees. The union supports VA direct care and opposes community care expansion but may resist workforce restructuring needed for rural service models (e.g., mobile health teams, expanded scope of practice for non-physician providers).

## Coordination Gaps

The most significant stakeholder challenge is coordination failure:

1. **VA-USDA disconnect**: Rural health and rural broadband programs operate in separate silos despite overlapping beneficiaries
2. **VA-IHS overlap**: Native American veterans may be eligible for both VA and Indian Health Service care, but the systems coordinate poorly
3. **State-federal fragmentation**: State veteran services and VA programs often duplicate or conflict rather than complement
4. **VSO-VA tension**: VSOs advocate for veterans but sometimes resist reforms that would change established service delivery patterns
5. **Community care coordination**: Third-party administrators, VA, community providers, and veterans navigate a complex system with multiple handoff points where information is lost
