# Rural Veterans: What Citizens Can Do

## Overview

Improving healthcare access and outcomes for rural veterans requires action at every level--from individual citizens and community organizations to state governments and federal agencies. This document provides concrete, actionable steps organized by effort level and target audience.

## For All Citizens

### Learn and Share

1. **Understand the rural veteran access gap**: Read the VA Office of Rural Health annual reports and share key statistics with your community. The basic facts--4.7 million rural veterans, 41% exceeding drive time standards, 67% higher suicide rates--are not widely known outside veteran policy circles.

2. **Contact your representatives**: Call or write your U.S. Senators and Representative to:
    - Support the PRESCRIBE Act (cross-state VA telehealth licensing)
    - Advocate for increased Beneficiary Travel reimbursement rates
    - Request rural-specific reporting on VA access metrics
    - Support broadband infrastructure investment in veteran-heavy rural areas

3. **Support broadband equity**: Advocate for your state's BEAD (Broadband Equity, Access, and Deployment) plan to prioritize areas with high veteran populations

4. **Volunteer**: Contact your local VA Medical Center volunteer coordinator or VSO post about opportunities to support rural veterans, including:
    - DAV transportation volunteer driver programs
    - American Legion or VFW community outreach events
    - Veteran peer support training programs

### Donate and Fund

5. **Support organizations serving rural veterans**: Directed donations to organizations with demonstrated rural programs, including:
    - DAV Transportation Network
    - American Legion Rural Veteran Outreach
    - Give an Hour (free mental health services for veterans)
    - Bob Woodruff Foundation (veteran and family support, including rural programs)

## For Veterans and Military Families

### Access Available Resources

6. **Enroll in VA healthcare**: Even if you do not currently need care, enrollment establishes eligibility and access. Call 1-877-222-8387 or visit va.gov/health-care/apply

7. **Know your MISSION Act rights**: If you live more than 30 minutes from VA primary care or 60 minutes from specialty care, you may be eligible for community care. Contact your VA facility's Community Care office.

8. **Use VA telehealth**: Request VA Video Connect appointments for routine care. If you lack broadband, ask about the Digital Divide Consult program for a free tablet and internet service.

9. **File for Beneficiary Travel reimbursement**: Submit travel claims for every VA appointment--mileage reimbursement at $0.415/mile for qualifying veterans. Available through VA.gov or at facility travel offices.

10. **Access the Veterans Crisis Line**: Call 988 (press 1), text 838255, or chat at veteranscrisisline.net. Service is available 24/7 regardless of VA enrollment status.

### Connect and Advocate

11. **Join or start a veteran peer support group**: Contact your nearest Vet Center or VA facility about peer support programs. If none exist locally, the VA's Peer Support Toolkit provides guidance on establishing groups.

12. **Connect with your County Veterans Service Officer (CVSO)**: CVSOs provide free benefits navigation. Find yours through your county government website or the National Association of County Veterans Service Officers (NACVSO).

13. **Share your experience**: Contact the VA Office of Rural Health (ruralhealth.va.gov) to provide feedback on rural access challenges. Participate in VA SHEP surveys when offered.

14. **Testify**: If asked, provide testimony to Congressional committees, state legislatures, or VA advisory bodies about your experiences accessing care in rural areas. Personal stories are the most effective advocacy tool.

## For Community Organizations

### Build Local Support Infrastructure

15. **Host VA telehealth access points**: Libraries, community centers, American Legion posts, VFW halls, and churches can host VA ATLAS telehealth stations. Contact the VA Office of Connected Care for equipment and connectivity support.

16. **Create veteran transportation networks**: Organize volunteer driver programs to transport rural veterans to VA appointments. The DAV Transportation Network provides a model and may provide supplemental vehicles.

17. **Sponsor community health worker positions**: Community organizations can apply for VA grants to employ Community Health Workers who serve rural veterans with benefits navigation, health monitoring, and social connection.

18. **Organize veteran community events**: Regular social events reduce isolation, the most significant non-clinical driver of rural veteran mental health deterioration. Monthly gatherings, skill-sharing workshops, and community service projects are effective models.

### Partner with VA

19. **Apply for VA community partnership grants**: The Staff Sergeant Parker Gordon Fox Suicide Prevention Grant Program provides funding to community organizations for veteran suicide prevention, including rural outreach.

20. **Participate in the Governor's Challenge**: Every state has a Governor's Challenge team for veteran suicide prevention. Community organizations can join state teams to contribute rural-specific strategies.

## For Healthcare Providers

### Serve Rural Veterans

21. **Join VA community care networks**: Rural providers can enroll with Optum or TriWest to see veterans under MISSION Act community care referrals. Contact the Community Care Network administrator for your region.

22. **Pursue VA telehealth credentialing**: Providers interested in serving veterans via telehealth can explore VA clinical positions or community care telehealth agreements.

23. **Learn veteran-specific care**: Free training on military culture, PTSD, TBI, military sexual trauma, and veteran suicide risk factors is available through the VA's Center for Integrated Healthcare and SAMHSA's Military Family Resources.

24. **Consider rural VA employment**: VA offers competitive salary, federal benefits, student loan repayment (up to $200K), recruitment bonuses (up to $75K for hard-to-fill positions), and a public service loan forgiveness-qualifying employer status.

## For State and Local Government

### Policy and Funding

25. **Fund County Veterans Service Officers**: Ensure every rural county has a dedicated CVSO with adequate resources. Colorado, Minnesota, and Wisconsin provide models of strong state CVSO funding.

26. **Expand Medicaid**: In states that have not expanded Medicaid, low-income veterans who do not qualify for VA care or cannot access it due to distance may have no healthcare coverage. Medicaid expansion provides a safety net.

27. **Prioritize veteran areas in broadband deployment**: State BEAD plans should weight veteran population density in deployment prioritization formulas.

28. **Remove state-level telehealth barriers**: States can unilaterally adopt telehealth licensing reciprocity through the Interstate Medical Licensure Compact, even before federal action.

29. **Support rural veteran housing programs**: State housing finance agencies can create veteran-specific programs for rural housing rehabilitation, energy efficiency improvements, and accessibility modifications.

## For Employers

30. **Hire rural veterans**: Many rural veterans have skills directly transferable to agriculture, manufacturing, construction, and technical fields. The VA's Veteran Readiness and Employment program provides employer incentives.

31. **Accommodate veteran healthcare needs**: Flexible scheduling policies that allow veterans to attend distant VA appointments without losing income are a low-cost, high-impact support.

32. **Partner with VA employment programs**: Contact your local VA Medical Center's Compensated Work Therapy program or Veteran Readiness and Employment office for veteran hiring partnerships.
