# Rural Veterans: Solutions

## Overview

Closing the rural veteran access gap requires a multi-pronged strategy that addresses geographic barriers, workforce shortages, technology infrastructure, and cultural factors simultaneously. No single intervention can solve the problem. The solutions presented here are organized from most immediately implementable to most structurally ambitious, with evidence and cost estimates where available.

## Solution Category 1: Telehealth and Digital Health Expansion

### 1A. Universal Veteran Broadband Access

**Problem addressed**: 22% of rural veterans lack broadband, excluding them from telehealth

**Proposal**: Establish a VA Broadband Access Program that guarantees telehealth-capable internet connectivity to every enrolled rural veteran within 3 years.

**Components:**

| Component | Description | Estimated Cost |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| VA-ISP partnerships | Negotiate bulk broadband access agreements in underserved areas | $200M annually |
| Device distribution | Expand Digital Divide Consult to all eligible veterans | $150M annually |
| Satellite internet subsidy | Subsidize Starlink or similar LEO satellite for frontier veterans | $100M annually |
| ATLAS site expansion | Increase community telehealth access points from 148 to 500 | $75M one-time + $30M annually |
| Mobile connectivity units | Deploy cellular-equipped mobile telehealth vans | $50M one-time + $20M annually |

**Evidence**: VA's Digital Divide Consult program showed that veterans who received tablets and connectivity increased telehealth utilization by 340% within 6 months (VA Office of Connected Care, 2023). Starlink has demonstrated reliable 100+ Mbps connectivity in frontier areas where terrestrial broadband is unavailable.

**Timeline**: 1-3 years for full deployment

### 1B. Cross-State Telehealth Licensing Reform

**Problem addressed**: State licensing barriers restrict VA providers from serving veterans across state lines

**Proposal**: Pass the PRESCRIBE Act or equivalent legislation establishing federal preemption for VA telehealth, allowing any VA-credentialed provider to serve any veteran regardless of location.

**Components:**

- Federal preemption of state licensing requirements for VA telehealth
- Uniform malpractice framework for VA telehealth encounters
- Interstate compact for community care telehealth providers
- Reciprocal licensing for VA-affiliated community providers

**Evidence**: During the COVID-19 emergency, VA telehealth waivers allowed cross-state practice, resulting in a 1,500% increase in rural telehealth encounters with no documented increase in adverse outcomes (VA Office of Connected Care, 2022).

**Timeline**: Legislation needed; 1-2 years for implementation after passage

### 1C. Specialty Telehealth Hub Network

**Problem addressed**: Rural veterans cannot access specialty care without traveling to VA Medical Centers

**Proposal**: Create 20 regional Specialty Telehealth Hubs, each staffed with full-time specialists who provide virtual consultations to rural CBOCs and veterans' homes.

**Specialties prioritized:**

| Specialty | Current Rural Gap | Telehealth Suitability |
|-----------|------------------|----------------------|
| Psychiatry | 62% of rural counties: zero VA or community psychiatrists | High |
| Dermatology | 48% of rural counties: zero network providers | High (store-and-forward) |
| Neurology | 55% of rural counties: zero network providers | Medium-High |
| Cardiology | 38% of rural counties: zero network providers | Medium |
| Endocrinology | 52% of rural counties: zero network providers | High |
| Rheumatology | 58% of rural counties: zero network providers | Medium-High |

**Estimated cost**: $250M annually for 20 hubs (staffing, technology, coordination)

**Evidence**: VA's TeleMental Health program demonstrated that tele-psychiatry outcomes are equivalent to in-person care for PTSD, depression, and anxiety (Morland et al., *Journal of Clinical Psychology*, 2020).

## Solution Category 2: Workforce

### 2A. Rural VA Provider Pipeline

**Problem addressed**: 30-50% vacancy rates at rural VA facilities

**Proposal**: Create a comprehensive rural VA workforce pipeline from education through retirement.

**Components:**

| Stage | Intervention | Estimated Cost |
|-------|-------------|---------------|
| Education | 500 annual scholarships for rural-origin students entering health professions, with 4-year rural VA service obligation | $100M annually |
| Residency | Fund 200 rural VA residency positions annually across disciplines | $150M annually |
| Recruitment | Triple recruitment bonuses for rural VA positions (up to $75K for physicians, $40K for NPs/PAs) | $80M annually |
| Retention | Automatic 10% rural differential pay for all rural VA positions | $350M annually |
| Loan repayment | Full student loan repayment ($250K cap) for 4-year rural VA commitment | $200M annually |
| Housing | VA-provided or subsidized housing in high-cost or housing-scarce rural areas | $50M annually |
| Spouse employment | Partner career assistance program (remote work matching, local employer partnerships) | $20M annually |

**Evidence**: The National Health Service Corps shows that loan repayment programs retain 55% of participants in underserved areas beyond their service obligation (HRSA, 2023). VA's existing recruitment incentives are too small to compete with private-sector offers in many specialties.

**Timeline**: 3-5 years for pipeline effects; immediate impact from enhanced incentives

### 2B. Community Health Worker Program

**Problem addressed**: Rural veterans need navigation, social support, and basic health monitoring between clinical visits

**Proposal**: Deploy 2,000 Community Health Workers (CHWs) in rural areas, each serving 100-200 veterans.

**Role**: Home visits, benefits navigation, medication management support, social isolation assessment, crisis identification, connection to VA and community services

**Model**: Based on successful VA Whole Health Peer Support and HRSA Community Health Worker programs

**Estimated cost**: $120M annually (salary, training, supervision, travel)

**Evidence**: CHW programs in rural settings have demonstrated 22% reduction in emergency department use, 18% improvement in chronic disease management, and significant improvements in patient-reported social connection (National Academy for State Health Policy, 2023).

### 2C. Expanded Scope of Practice

**Problem addressed**: Physician shortages leave rural CBOCs understaffed even when nurse practitioners and physician assistants are available

**Proposal**: Authorize full practice authority for nurse practitioners at all VA rural facilities, with physician oversight via telehealth rather than on-site.

**Current situation**: VA granted full practice authority to NPs in 2016 (38 C.F.R. Part 17), but implementation has been uneven, and some rural facilities still operate under restrictive supervision models.

**Estimated cost**: Minimal direct cost; potential savings through reduced physician dependency

**Evidence**: Studies consistently show NPs provide comparable-quality primary care to physicians, with higher patient satisfaction in some measures (Stanik-Hutt et al., *Medical Care Research and Review*, 2013).

## Solution Category 3: Facility and Transportation

### 3A. Mobile Health Unit Fleet

**Problem addressed**: Veterans in highly rural areas cannot travel to any fixed facility

**Proposal**: Deploy 100 mobile health units (MHUs) nationally, each equipped for primary care, basic diagnostics, mental health counseling, and telehealth.

**Operating model:**

- Each MHU serves a rotating circuit of 5-8 rural communities on a weekly or biweekly schedule
- Staffed by NP/PA, mental health professional, and community health worker
- Connected to VA Medical Center via satellite telehealth for physician consultation
- Pharmacist available via telehealth for medication management
- Basic lab draw and imaging capability; samples transported to VA labs

**Estimated cost**: $200M one-time (vehicles, equipment) + $150M annually (staffing, operations, maintenance)

**Evidence**: VA's Veterans Rural Health Access Initiative piloted 12 mobile health units (2010-2015) and demonstrated 34% increase in preventive care utilization among served veterans (VA Office of Rural Health, 2016).

### 3B. Beneficiary Travel Reform

**Problem addressed**: Current travel reimbursement ($0.415/mile) does not cover actual costs; administrative burden deters claims

**Proposal:**

- Increase mileage rate to IRS standard ($0.67/mile for 2024) or actual cost, whichever is greater
- Provide advance travel funding rather than reimbursement-only
- Automate travel claims through GPS-verified appointment attendance
- Add lodging coverage for veterans requiring multi-day travel
- Establish volunteer driver networks with mileage reimbursement

**Estimated cost**: $400M annually (incremental over current $1.19B program)

**Evidence**: DAV's volunteer transportation network provides 750,000 free rides annually, demonstrating unmet demand (DAV Transportation Annual Report, 2024). Veterans who receive adequate travel support show 28% higher appointment adherence (VA Health Services Research, 2022).

### 3C. Strategic CBOC Enhancement

**Problem addressed**: Rural CBOCs offer only primary care, forcing referrals for routine services

**Proposal**: Upgrade 100 highest-volume rural CBOCs to "Enhanced CBOCs" with expanded services:

- Mental health and substance use disorder treatment (on-site + telehealth)
- Basic radiology (X-ray, ultrasound)
- Dental care (preventive + basic restorative)
- Physical therapy and rehabilitation
- Women's health services
- Pharmacy dispensing

**Estimated cost**: $500M one-time (facility upgrades, equipment) + $300M annually (additional staffing)

**Evidence**: Enhanced CBOCs piloted in Montana and Mississippi showed 40% reduction in VA Medical Center referrals for enrolled veterans (VA ORH Evaluation, 2022).

## Solution Category 4: Community Integration

### 4A. VA-FQHC Partnership Network

**Problem addressed**: VA and Federally Qualified Health Centers both serve rural populations but operate in separate silos

**Proposal**: Establish formal partnerships between VA and 500 rural FQHCs to deliver VA-standard care at community health center sites.

**Components:**

- VA credentialing of FQHC providers for veteran care
- Shared electronic health records (or interoperable exchange)
- VA specialist telehealth consultation available to FQHC providers treating veterans
- Joint funding model (VA reimburses FQHC at community care rates, FQHC provides space and infrastructure)

**Estimated cost**: $200M annually (reimbursement, EHR interoperability, training)

**Evidence**: VA-FQHC partnerships piloted in 10 states (2018-2022) showed comparable care quality with 45% reduction in veteran travel burden (VA-HRSA Collaboration Report, 2023).

### 4B. Rural Veteran Social Connection Program

**Problem addressed**: Social isolation drives mental health deterioration and suicide risk among rural veterans

**Proposal**: Fund structured social connection programs in every rural county with 500+ veteran residents.

**Components:**

- Peer support groups (weekly, facilitated by trained veteran peers)
- Community service projects (veterans helping their communities)
- Skill-sharing programs (veterans teaching and learning from each other)
- Family integration activities
- Virtual connection options for the most isolated veterans

**Estimated cost**: $75M annually (peer specialist salaries, program materials, facility rental)

**Evidence**: Peer support programs reduce veteran suicide risk by 20-30% and improve treatment engagement by 35% (Chinman et al., *Psychiatric Services*, 2014).

## Solution Category 5: Systemic Reform

### 5A. Rural Veteran Access Standards

**Problem addressed**: VA performance metrics do not adequately capture rural access challenges

**Proposal**: Establish rural-specific access standards separate from urban metrics:

| Metric | Current Standard | Proposed Rural Standard |
|--------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Primary care drive time | 30 minutes | 45 minutes (with telehealth alternative within 24 hours) |
| Mental health drive time | 30 minutes | 45 minutes (with telehealth same-day) |
| Specialty care drive time | 60 minutes | 90 minutes (with telehealth alternative within 7 days) |
| Appointment wait (primary) | 20 days | 20 days (same, but telehealth counts) |
| Emergency care | N/A | 60 minutes (including community care, mobile units) |

### 5B. VA Budget Model Reform

**Problem addressed**: VERA funding model disadvantages rural care delivery

**Proposal**: Reform VERA to include a rural cost adjustment factor that accounts for:

- Higher per-encounter costs in low-volume facilities
- Transportation and travel reimbursement costs
- Telehealth infrastructure investment
- Workforce recruitment premium
- Mobile health unit operations

**Estimated adjustment**: 15-25% rural supplement applied to VERA base allocations

### 5C. Federal Rural Veteran Coordination Office

**Problem addressed**: Rural veteran needs span multiple federal agencies (VA, USDA, FCC, HHS, DOT) with no coordination mechanism

**Proposal**: Establish a Federal Rural Veteran Coordination Office, co-chaired by VA and USDA, to align rural veteran programs across agencies.

**Functions:**

- Coordinate broadband deployment priorities with veteran population data
- Align USDA rural health programs with VA outreach
- Integrate HHS workforce programs with VA recruitment
- Connect DOT rural transit funding with veteran transportation needs
- Report annually to Congress on rural veteran access metrics

## Prioritization

**Highest impact, most feasible (pursue immediately):**

1. Cross-state telehealth licensing (1B)--legislative, minimal cost
2. Beneficiary Travel reform (3B)--administrative + legislative
3. Community Health Workers (2B)--programmatic, scalable
4. Enhanced recruitment incentives (portion of 2A)--administrative

**High impact, medium feasibility (pursue within 2 years):**

5. Broadband access program (1A)--requires coordination with BEAD
6. CBOC Enhancement (3C)--requires capital investment
7. VA-FQHC Partnerships (4A)--requires institutional negotiation
8. Specialty Telehealth Hubs (1C)--requires staffing pipeline

**Transformational, longer timeline:**

9. Rural Provider Pipeline (2A full)--5+ years for results
10. Mobile Health Unit Fleet (3A)--procurement and deployment
11. Budget Model Reform (5B)--requires VA and OMB alignment
12. Federal Coordination Office (5C)--requires interagency agreement
