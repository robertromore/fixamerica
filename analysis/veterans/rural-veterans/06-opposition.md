# Rural Veterans: Opposition to Reform

## Overview

Unlike many policy areas where opposition is ideologically driven, rural veteran healthcare reform faces obstacles that are primarily structural, institutional, and economic rather than partisan. Few political actors openly oppose improving rural veteran access. Instead, reform is impeded by competing priorities, institutional inertia, cost concerns, and philosophical disagreements about how to deliver care. Understanding these barriers requires examining both explicit opposition and the implicit resistance that prevents action.

## Explicit Opposition

### Fiscal Hawks and Budget Constraints

**Position**: The cost of closing the rural access gap is prohibitive and unsustainable.

| Argument | Evidence Used | Counterargument |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Rural per-capita costs are too high | Serving 4.7M dispersed veterans costs more per enrollee than concentrated urban populations | The obligation to serve all veterans exists regardless of where they live |
| ORH budget has tripled with no outcomes improvement | $327M (FY 2024) vs. $107M (FY 2015) without proportional access gains | Funding increases offset inflation and expanded eligibility, not program growth |
| Telehealth is more cost-effective than facilities | Virtual care costs fraction of brick-and-mortar | Telehealth cannot substitute for all care and requires broadband investment |

**Key actors**: Heritage Foundation, Cato Institute, some fiscal conservative members of Congress, Office of Management and Budget (in constraining VA budget requests)

**Severity**: Medium. These actors do not oppose rural veteran care in principle but consistently resist funding increases and new programs.

### VA Privatization Advocates

**Position**: Replace VA direct care with a veteran choice/voucher system allowing all veterans to seek private-sector care.

**Arguments:**

- Market competition will improve quality and access
- Veterans should have the same choices as other Americans
- Government-run healthcare is inherently inefficient

**Why this would worsen rural access:**

- Private providers are equally or more scarce in rural areas than VA providers
- The MISSION Act already allows private-sector care; rural veterans cannot find providers
- VA telehealth infrastructure has no private-sector equivalent at scale
- VA specialization in veteran-specific conditions (PTSD, TBI, military sexual trauma) would be lost
- Small rural hospitals accepting VA vouchers may lack the clinical expertise for complex veteran conditions

**Key actors**: Concerned Veterans for America (Koch-affiliated), some libertarian policy organizations, occasionally echoed by members of Congress

**Severity**: Medium-High. Privatization rhetoric can divert resources and political attention from rural VA capacity-building toward market-based alternatives that do not function in provider-scarce areas.

### VA Institutional Resistance

**Position**: Not opposition per se, but institutional inertia that resists the fundamental restructuring needed for effective rural care delivery.

**Manifestations:**

| Resistance Type | Example | Effect |
|----------------|---------|--------|
| Centralized decision-making | Rural CBOC staffing decisions made at VISN level, not locally | Local needs overridden by regional priorities |
| Efficiency metrics | Performance measured by volume and throughput | Rural low-volume facilities score poorly despite meeting community needs |
| Facility-centric culture | Career advancement tied to medical center leadership | Best administrators gravitate to large facilities |
| Change fatigue | Multiple recent reforms (Choice Act, MISSION Act, PACT Act, EHR transition) | Staff bandwidth consumed by compliance, not innovation |
| Risk aversion | Fear of negative OIG findings or Congressional scrutiny | Rural pilot programs proceed cautiously, slowly |

**Severity**: High. This is perhaps the most significant barrier because it operates continuously and is not subject to political override.

### VA Employee Union Concerns

**Position**: AFGE supports strong VA direct care but resists some restructuring that could improve rural access.

**Specific tensions:**

- **Scope of practice expansion**: AFGE has expressed concerns about nurse practitioners and physician assistants operating independently in rural settings, preferring physician supervision even when physicians are unavailable
- **Mobile health teams**: Union concerns about travel requirements, variable work locations, and compensation for mobile assignments
- **Telehealth staffing models**: Questions about whether telehealth encounters generate the same workload credit as in-person visits
- **Community care expansion**: Union opposes diverting VA resources to community care providers, viewing it as outsourcing VA jobs

**Severity**: Medium. Union concerns are legitimate but can delay implementation of rural-adapted care models.

## Implicit Opposition and Structural Barriers

### Urban-Majority VA Priorities

Most VA-enrolled veterans live in urban areas. Resource allocation decisions that maximize benefit for the largest number of veterans will systematically deprioritize rural investments:

- A new specialty clinic in a city of 500,000 serves more veterans per dollar than a mobile clinic serving 200 veterans across 10,000 square miles
- VISN directors managing both urban and rural facilities face pressure to address urban wait times that generate more media coverage and Congressional complaints
- VA central office initiatives (EHR modernization, facility upgrades, hiring surges) are designed for large facilities

### State Licensing Barriers to Telehealth

Many states require healthcare providers to be licensed in the state where the patient is located. While the VA has some exemptions under federal supremacy, these exemptions are not universal and face opposition from:

- **State medical boards**: Concerned about losing regulatory authority over providers serving their residents
- **State medical associations**: Fear competition from out-of-state VA providers reducing demand for local physicians
- **Malpractice liability concerns**: Unclear liability frameworks for cross-state telehealth encounters

The PRESCRIBE Act (proposed) would allow VA providers to practice via telehealth in any state, but has faced opposition from state licensing interests.

### Community Care Network Administrator Incentives

The third-party administrators managing VA community care networks (Optum, TriWest) face financial incentives that can work against rural veterans:

- **Network adequacy gaming**: Counting providers 90+ miles away as "available" to meet contractual network adequacy requirements
- **Urban provider preference**: Easier to build robust networks in urban areas; rural network development is costly
- **Administrative complexity**: Referral and authorization processes designed for high-volume urban settings create disproportionate burden for small rural practices
- **Cost management**: Administrators incentivized to minimize per-referral costs, which may mean routing veterans to distant but cheaper providers rather than closer but more expensive ones

### Broadband Industry Resistance

Telecommunications companies have historically underinvested in rural broadband due to low return on investment:

- Cost per connection in rural areas can be 5-10x urban deployment costs
- Regulatory frameworks have allowed ISPs to designate areas as "served" based on inadequate speed thresholds
- BEAD program funding is significant ($42.5B) but deployment timelines extend to 2028-2030, leaving rural veterans without telehealth-capable connectivity for years

### Political Dynamics

While rural veteran healthcare enjoys bipartisan rhetorical support, political dynamics can impede action:

| Dynamic | Effect |
|---------|--------|
| VA as political football | Healthcare access debates become proxy fights over government-run vs. private healthcare |
| Election-year posturing | Promises of "fixing the VA" substitute for sustained policy development |
| Rural-urban political divide | Rural areas are politically conservative; some advocates frame VA as government overreach even while demanding better VA services |
| Committee jurisdiction fragmentation | Rural veteran issues span VA, Commerce, Agriculture, and Appropriations committees, diluting legislative focus |

## Opposition Durability Assessment

| Opposition Source | Durability | Negotiability | Strategy to Address |
|------------------|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| Fiscal constraints | High | Medium | Demonstrate cost-effectiveness of prevention vs. crisis care |
| Privatization ideology | High | Low | Show empirical failure of market solutions in provider deserts |
| VA institutional inertia | Very High | Medium | Require rural-specific metrics in VA performance evaluation |
| Union resistance | Medium | High | Negotiate rural-specific workforce models with enhanced compensation |
| State licensing barriers | Medium | High | Federal preemption for VA telehealth (PRESCRIBE Act) |
| Broadband underinvestment | Medium | Medium | Leverage BEAD; VA-specific connectivity mandates |
| Community care network gaming | Medium | High | Reform network adequacy standards with rural-specific requirements |
