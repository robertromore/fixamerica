# VA Healthcare: Solutions

## Reform Framework

### Principles

- **Strengthen VA direct care**: Invest in capacity rather than outsourcing
- **Competitive workforce**: Pay and hiring reforms to close the 46,000-position gap
- **Modern infrastructure**: Replace aging facilities and adopt 21st-century care models
- **Smart community care**: Supplement, not supplant, VA direct care
- **Accountability with support**: Hold leaders accountable while providing resources for success
- **Evidence-based**: Ground reforms in VA's demonstrated quality advantages

### Strategic Approach

| Principle | Application |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Capacity first** | Invest in VA workforce and infrastructure before expanding community care |
| **Fix hiring, not mission** | Address process barriers rather than outsourcing clinical care |
| **Targeted community care** | Use private sector for geographic gaps and routine services |
| **Preserve specialization** | Maintain VA's unique capabilities in military-specific conditions |
| **Data-driven management** | Use transparent metrics for facility-level improvement |

## Proposed Solutions

### Solution 1: VA Workforce Competitiveness Act

**Description**: Overhaul VA hiring authorities, compensation, and recruitment to close the workforce gap within five years.

**Key Components**:

| Component | Specification |
|-----------|---------------|
| Pay parity | VA physician pay at 95th percentile of federal + 90% of local private sector |
| Streamlined hiring | Reduce time-to-hire to 45 days for clinical positions |
| Title 38 expansion | Extend flexible hiring to all clinical and IT roles |
| Loan repayment | $200,000 loan repayment for 4-year service commitment |
| Residency pipeline | Expand VA residency positions by 2,000 annually |
| Retention bonuses | Targeted bonuses for shortage specialties and rural facilities |

**Evidence**:

- VA facilities that implemented locality pay adjustments reduced vacancy rates by 30-40%[^1]
- Education debt repayment programs have 85% retention rate through commitment period[^2]
- Private-sector healthcare systems recruit physicians in 30-45 days; VA averages 94 days[^1]

**Implementation**:

| Phase | Timeline | Action |
|-------|----------|--------|
| 1 | Year 1 | Emergency hiring authority for critical shortage occupations |
| 2 | Year 1-2 | Pay parity implementation, loan repayment expansion |
| 3 | Year 2-3 | Hiring process reengineering, Title 38 expansion |
| 4 | Year 3-5 | Residency pipeline expansion, retention program maturity |

**Estimated Cost**: $8-12 billion over 5 years (offset by reduced community care referrals and turnover costs)

**Challenges**: Federal pay scale politics, OMB resistance, union negotiations on classification changes

**Impact Metrics**: Vacancy rate reduction, time-to-hire, turnover rate, community care referral volume

### Solution 2: Infrastructure Modernization and Realignment

**Description**: Create a mechanism to close, consolidate, and replace outdated VA facilities while building modern outpatient and telehealth infrastructure.

**Key Components**:

| Component | Specification |
|-----------|---------------|
| Facility Modernization Commission | Independent commission with binding recommendations (BRAC model) |
| $50 billion capital plan | 10-year facility replacement and renovation program |
| Modern outpatient model | Expand CBOCs, same-day clinics, and mobile units |
| Seismic remediation | Prioritize 57 high-risk facilities |
| Deferred maintenance | Address $35B backlog through dedicated funding stream |
| Joint VA-DoD facilities | Co-locate where both populations are served |

**Evidence**:

- New VA facilities achieve 20-30% higher patient satisfaction[^3]
- Outpatient-focused models reduce costs by 15-25% compared to inpatient-heavy facilities[^3]
- BRAC process successfully realigned military installations over multiple rounds (1988-2005)[^4]

**Implementation**:

| Phase | Timeline | Action |
|-------|----------|--------|
| 1 | Year 1-2 | Establish commission, conduct facility assessments |
| 2 | Year 2-3 | Commission recommendations, Congressional vote |
| 3 | Year 3-10 | Execute realignment plan |
| 4 | Ongoing | Annual deferred maintenance reduction targets |

**Estimated Cost**: $50 billion over 10 years ($5B/year)

**Challenges**: Political resistance to facility closures, community economic impacts, construction timelines

**Impact Metrics**: Facility condition index, deferred maintenance backlog, patient satisfaction by facility age

### Solution 3: Community Care Reform

**Description**: Restructure community care to function as a genuine supplement to VA direct care, with quality standards, cost controls, and care coordination requirements.

**Key Components**:

| Component | Specification |
|-----------|---------------|
| Revised access standards | Raise wait-time threshold to 28/42 days; maintain drive-time standards |
| Quality requirements | Community providers must meet VA quality benchmarks |
| Care coordination mandate | Shared records, VA-managed care plans for referred veterans |
| Rate negotiation | VA negotiates rates rather than using Medicare-plus schedules |
| Performance-based referrals | Preferred networks of high-quality community providers |
| VA capacity investment trigger | Community care savings redirected to VA capacity |

**Evidence**:

- Community care costs ~50% more per encounter than VA direct care ($6,200 vs. $4,100)[^5]
- RAND found community care coordination is poor, with gaps in follow-up and record sharing[^6]
- Tricare Prime managed care model demonstrates successful network management for military populations[^6]

**Implementation**:

| Phase | Timeline | Action |
|-------|----------|--------|
| 1 | Year 1 | Revise access standard regulations |
| 2 | Year 1-2 | Establish quality standards for community providers |
| 3 | Year 2-3 | Build preferred provider networks |
| 4 | Year 3-5 | Implement care coordination IT systems |

**Estimated Cost**: Budget-neutral to net savings (reduces community care spending growth)

**Challenges**: Industry opposition to tighter standards, Congressional resistance, implementation complexity

**Impact Metrics**: Community care spending growth rate, care coordination completeness, quality parity

### Solution 4: Telehealth and Digital Health Expansion

**Description**: Scale VA's telehealth infrastructure to provide comprehensive virtual care, particularly for rural veterans and routine follow-up appointments.

**Key Components**:

| Component | Specification |
|-----------|---------------|
| Universal telehealth access | VA-issued tablets for all enrolled veterans without broadband |
| Hub-and-spoke model | Specialty teleconsults from VAMCs to CBOCs and home |
| Remote patient monitoring | Chronic disease management through connected devices |
| Mental health integration | Telemental health as default for follow-up appointments |
| Broadband partnerships | Partner with USDA/FCC for rural broadband expansion |
| Licensing flexibility | Permanent COMPACT Act implementation for cross-state practice |

**Evidence**:

- VA telehealth satisfaction rates comparable to in-person care (87% vs. 89%)[^7]
- VA telemental health reduces no-show rates by 25-30%[^7]
- Remote patient monitoring reduces hospitalizations by 20% for enrolled patients[^7]
- COVID-era telehealth expansion proved feasibility at scale (17.4 million encounters in FY2024)[^7]

**Implementation**:

| Phase | Timeline | Action |
|-------|----------|--------|
| 1 | Year 1 | Device distribution, broadband partnerships |
| 2 | Year 1-2 | Specialty teleconsult network buildout |
| 3 | Year 2-3 | Remote monitoring expansion |
| 4 | Year 3-5 | Full integration with in-person care model |

**Estimated Cost**: $3-5 billion over 5 years

**Challenges**: Broadband gaps in rural areas, digital literacy, clinician adoption, technology maintenance

**Impact Metrics**: Telehealth utilization rate, rural access improvement, no-show rate, patient satisfaction

### Solution 5: EHR Modernization Reset

**Description**: Pause the Oracle Health deployment, conduct an independent assessment, and develop a revised strategy that may include a hybrid approach using VistA modernization alongside commercial components.

**Key Components**:

| Component | Specification |
|-----------|---------------|
| Independent technical review | Non-conflicted assessment of options |
| VistA modernization option | Evaluate open-source VistA enhancement as alternative |
| Interoperability focus | Prioritize VA-DoD data exchange regardless of platform |
| Phased deployment | No further sites until safety standards met |
| Clinician input | Frontline clinicians on governance board |
| Contract accountability | Performance-based payments with clawback provisions |

**Evidence**:

- VistA was rated superior to commercial systems by clinicians and independent assessments[^8]
- Oracle Health deployment produced 148+ patient safety events at 6 sites[^8]
- Open-source VistA communities have continued to develop the platform[^8]
- VA-DoD interoperability can be achieved through standards-based APIs without a common platform

**Implementation**:

| Phase | Timeline | Action |
|-------|----------|--------|
| 1 | Year 1 | Independent review, deployment freeze |
| 2 | Year 1-2 | Strategy development based on review findings |
| 3 | Year 2-5 | Revised deployment or VistA modernization |
| 4 | Year 3-5 | Interoperability implementation |

**Estimated Cost**: $5-8 billion savings if VistA modernization replaces full Oracle deployment

**Challenges**: Oracle contract obligations, sunk costs, Congressional pressure, technical complexity

**Impact Metrics**: Patient safety events, clinician satisfaction, system uptime, interoperability metrics

### Solution 6: PACT Act Implementation Acceleration

**Description**: Provide dedicated funding and staffing to fully implement PACT Act eligibility expansion without degrading existing care capacity.

**Key Components**:

| Component | Specification |
|-----------|---------------|
| Dedicated hiring surge | 15,000 additional clinical positions for PACT Act patients |
| Toxic exposure centers of excellence | Regional specialty clinics for burn pit, Agent Orange, radiation |
| Claims processing modernization | AI-assisted claims adjudication to reduce backlog |
| Outreach and enrollment | Targeted outreach to newly eligible veterans |
| Research investment | Expand toxic exposure health effects research |

**Evidence**:

- PACT Act added 739,000+ new enrollees through FY2024 but hiring lagged targets[^9]
- Toxic exposure conditions require specialized medical expertise concentrated at few VA sites
- Claims backlog grew with new filings, delaying access to care for eligible veterans

**Implementation**:

| Phase | Timeline | Action |
|-------|----------|--------|
| 1 | Year 1 | Emergency hiring for toxic exposure specialties |
| 2 | Year 1-2 | Centers of excellence established |
| 3 | Year 2-3 | Claims processing modernization |
| 4 | Year 3-5 | Full implementation and ongoing research |

**Estimated Cost**: $15-20 billion over 5 years (within PACT Act authorized levels)

**Challenges**: Workforce availability, specialty expertise development, claims volume

**Impact Metrics**: PACT Act enrollment completion rate, toxic exposure screening rate, claims processing time

### Solution 7: VA Governance Reform

**Description**: Restructure VA governance to improve accountability, reduce political interference, and enable long-term strategic planning.

**Key Components**:

| Component | Specification |
|-----------|---------------|
| VHA CEO model | Appointed healthcare executive with 5-year term |
| Performance-based management | VISN directors with clear authority and accountability |
| Transparent metrics | Real-time public access to facility-level quality and access data |
| Inspector General independence | Enhanced OIG authority and resources |
| Congressional oversight consolidation | Single committee of jurisdiction (aspirational) |
| Veteran advisory boards | Elected veteran representatives at each VISN |

**Evidence**:

- Average VA Secretary tenure of ~2 years prevents sustained reform[^10]
- The Kizer transformation (1994-1999) demonstrated that sustained leadership produces results
- Kaiser Permanente, Cleveland Clinic, and other integrated systems use CEO models with long tenures
- Transparent quality reporting drives improvement in Medicare and private-sector settings

**Implementation**:

| Phase | Timeline | Action |
|-------|----------|--------|
| 1 | Year 1 | Legislation establishing VHA CEO with term protection |
| 2 | Year 1-2 | Performance management system redesign |
| 3 | Year 2-3 | Transparency platform deployment |
| 4 | Year 3-5 | Veteran advisory board establishment |

**Estimated Cost**: Minimal direct cost; efficiency gains from improved management

**Challenges**: Congressional resistance to reducing oversight authority, political appointment traditions

**Impact Metrics**: Leadership tenure, facility-level quality improvement rate, veteran satisfaction trends

## International Models

| Country | Model | Relevant Lesson |
|---------|-------|-----------------|
| **Australia** | Department of Veterans' Affairs contracts private providers | Care coordination challenges mirror VA community care |
| **United Kingdom** | NHS provides most veteran care with priority treatment | Integration can work but requires military-specific pathways |
| **Canada** | Veterans Affairs Canada contracts provincial healthcare | Mixed outcomes; specialty care gaps |
| **Israel** | Ministry of Defense healthcare for active/veterans | Strong integration between military and civilian care |

## Solution Sequencing

### Quick Wins (Year 1-2)

- Emergency hiring authority for critical shortage occupations
- Telehealth device distribution to rural veterans
- Community care quality standards rulemaking
- EHR modernization independent review
- PACT Act outreach acceleration

### Medium-Term (Year 2-5)

- Pay parity implementation
- Infrastructure Modernization Commission establishment
- Community care preferred provider networks
- Residency pipeline expansion
- Toxic exposure centers of excellence

### Long-Term (Year 5-10)

- Facility realignment execution
- Full EHR modernization/replacement
- Deferred maintenance backlog elimination
- Governance reform maturation
- Workforce vacancy rate below 5%
