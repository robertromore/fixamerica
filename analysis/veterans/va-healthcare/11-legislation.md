# VA Healthcare: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Reforming VA healthcare requires action at federal legislative, administrative, and regulatory levels. The legislative approach includes:

1. **Federal legislation** to reform workforce authorities, infrastructure modernization, and community care
2. **Regulatory action** through VA rulemaking on access standards, quality requirements, and hiring
3. **Appropriations** to fund workforce expansion, facility modernization, and PACT Act implementation
4. **Governance reform** to stabilize VHA leadership and improve accountability

## Constitutional Amendments

No constitutional amendments required. VA healthcare delivery falls squarely within federal authority under the Spending Clause and the power to raise and support armies. Title 38 provides the statutory framework for VA healthcare.

### VA Workforce Competitiveness Act

**Purpose**: Close the VA clinical workforce gap by making VA compensation competitive, streamlining hiring, and expanding the training pipeline.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "VA Workforce Competitiveness Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.
Congress finds the following:
(1) The Veterans Health Administration has over 46,000 unfilled
clinical positions as of fiscal year 2024.
(2) VA physician compensation averages 10-12 percent below
private-sector levels in comparable specialties and markets.
(3) The average time to hire a VA physician is 94 days, more
than double the private-sector average.
(4) Workforce shortages directly contribute to wait times,
reduced access, and increased reliance on community care
that costs 50 percent more per encounter.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.
In this Act:
(1) COVERED POSITION.—The term "covered position" means any
position within the Veterans Health Administration that
provides direct patient care or direct clinical support,
including—
    (A) physicians, dentists, and podiatrists;
    (B) registered nurses and licensed practical nurses;
    (C) psychologists and social workers;
    (D) physician assistants and nurse practitioners;
    (E) pharmacists;
    (F) such other positions as the Secretary may designate.

SEC. 4. COMPETITIVE PAY.
(a) PAY PARITY.—Not later than 1 year after the date of
enactment, the Secretary shall adjust compensation for covered
positions to not less than the 50th percentile of compensation
for comparable positions in the relevant local labor market, as
determined by Bureau of Labor Statistics data.
(b) CRITICAL SHORTAGE PREMIUM.—For occupations designated as
critical shortage by the Secretary, compensation shall be not
less than the 75th percentile of the local labor market.
(c) ANNUAL ADJUSTMENT.—Compensation under this section shall
be adjusted annually based on the Employment Cost Index for
healthcare workers.

SEC. 5. HIRING REFORM.
(a) TIME-TO-HIRE STANDARD.—The Secretary shall establish a
standard of not more than 45 days from position posting to
offer letter for all covered positions.
(b) DIRECT HIRE AUTHORITY.—The Secretary may exercise direct
hire authority under section 3304 of title 5, United States
Code, for all covered positions without further delegation.
(c) CREDENTIALING STREAMLINING.—The Secretary shall establish
a streamlined credentialing process that accepts state medical
board verification and prior VA or DoD credentialing.
(d) ONBOARDING REFORM.—The Secretary shall reduce the average
onboarding period to not more than 14 days from acceptance
of offer to first day of clinical duty.

SEC. 6. EDUCATION DEBT REDUCTION.
(a) ENHANCED PROGRAM.—The Secretary shall increase the maximum
education debt reduction payment to $200,000 for participants
who commit to 4 years of service in covered positions.
(b) RURAL PREMIUM.—For service at facilities designated as
rural or highly rural, the maximum shall be $250,000.
(c) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated
$2,000,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2026 through 2030
to carry out this section.

SEC. 7. RESIDENCY EXPANSION.
(a) The Secretary shall increase the number of VA-sponsored
residency positions by not fewer than 2,000 per year for
5 years, with priority given to primary care, psychiatry,
and other shortage specialties.
(b) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated
$500,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2026 through 2030
to carry out this section.

SEC. 8. RETENTION INCENTIVES.
(a) The Secretary shall establish retention bonuses of not
less than 25 percent of base pay for clinicians in critical
shortage occupations who commit to 3-year service extensions.
(b) The Secretary shall establish a career ladder program for
nursing and allied health professionals within VHA.
```

**Explanation**: The bill addresses the three primary workforce barriers: compensation that lags the private sector, hiring processes that take twice as long as the private sector, and insufficient pipeline investment. The critical shortage premium targets the most acute vacancies.

**Potential Challenges**: OMB scoring of pay increases; federal employee pay equity concerns from non-VA agencies; Congressional resistance to expanded direct hire authority.

**Refinements**: Could include a sunset provision on critical shortage premiums to address fiscal concerns; could phase pay parity over 3 years.

### VA Infrastructure Modernization Act

**Purpose**: Establish a mechanism to close, consolidate, and replace outdated VA facilities through an independent commission with binding recommendations.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "VA Infrastructure Modernization Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.
Congress finds the following:
(1) More than 60 percent of VA healthcare facilities are over
50 years old.
(2) The deferred maintenance backlog exceeds $35,000,000,000.
(3) Fifty-seven VA facilities are at high seismic risk.
(4) The VA Asset and Infrastructure Review Commission
established by the MISSION Act was canceled without executing
any facility realignment.

SEC. 3. VA FACILITY MODERNIZATION COMMISSION.
(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established the VA Facility
Modernization Commission (the "Commission").
(b) MEMBERSHIP.—The Commission shall consist of 9 members
appointed as follows:
    (1) 3 members appointed by the President;
    (2) 2 members appointed by the Speaker of the House;
    (3) 2 members appointed by the Senate Majority Leader;
    (4) 1 member appointed by the House Minority Leader;
    (5) 1 member appointed by the Senate Minority Leader.
(c) QUALIFICATIONS.—Members shall have expertise in healthcare
delivery, facility management, veterans affairs, or public
administration. Not fewer than 3 members shall be veterans.

SEC. 4. COMMISSION DUTIES.
(a) The Commission shall—
    (1) assess the condition and utilization of all VHA facilities;
    (2) identify facilities for closure, consolidation, renovation,
    or replacement;
    (3) develop a 10-year capital plan prioritized by patient
    safety, access impact, and cost-effectiveness;
    (4) recommend new facility construction based on veteran
    population projections and care delivery models.
(b) TIMELINE.—The Commission shall submit recommendations to the
President not later than 2 years after the date of enactment.

SEC. 5. CONGRESSIONAL REVIEW.
(a) The President shall forward Commission recommendations to
Congress within 30 days of receipt.
(b) Congress shall have 45 days to pass a joint resolution of
disapproval; absent such resolution, recommendations take
effect.

SEC. 6. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.
There are authorized to be appropriated $50,000,000,000 for fiscal
years 2027 through 2036 to carry out the recommendations of the
Commission, including construction, renovation, and transition costs.

SEC. 7. COMMUNITY IMPACT MITIGATION.
(a) The Commission shall include community impact assessments
for facility closures.
(b) The Secretary shall provide transition assistance to affected
communities and employees.
(c) Closed facilities shall be offered for reuse by federal,
state, or local agencies for healthcare, housing, or other
veteran-serving purposes.
```

**Explanation**: Modeled on the successful BRAC process, this creates an independent commission with binding authority to overcome the political resistance that killed the AIR Commission. The 10-year authorization provides stable funding.

**Potential Challenges**: Intense political opposition to facility closures; communities dependent on VA facility employment; concerns about reduced access during transition.

**Refinements**: Could require that no facility close until replacement capacity is operational; could include expedited environmental review.

### Community Care Accountability Act

**Purpose**: Reform the MISSION Act community care program to ensure quality, cost control, and care coordination.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Community Care Accountability Act".

SEC. 2. REVISED ACCESS STANDARDS.
(a) WAIT TIME STANDARD.—Section 1703(a) of title 38, United
States Code, is amended by striking "20 days" and "28 days"
and inserting "28 days" and "42 days" respectively.
(b) DRIVE TIME STANDARD.—Drive time standards of 30 minutes
(primary care) and 60 minutes (specialty care) shall remain
unchanged.
(c) VA CAPACITY CONSIDERATION.—Before authorizing community
care, the relevant VA facility shall certify that it cannot
provide the requested service within the applicable standard
and that no other VA facility within the drive time standard
can provide the service.

SEC. 3. QUALITY STANDARDS FOR COMMUNITY PROVIDERS.
(a) The Secretary shall establish quality standards that
community care providers must meet, including—
    (1) accreditation by a CMS-recognized accrediting body;
    (2) reporting of quality metrics comparable to VA SAIL
    measures;
    (3) minimum patient satisfaction scores;
    (4) compliance with VA clinical practice guidelines for
    service-connected conditions.
(b) REMOVAL.—The Secretary may remove providers from the
community care network for failure to meet quality standards.

SEC. 4. CARE COORDINATION REQUIREMENTS.
(a) Community care providers shall—
    (1) submit clinical notes to the veteran's VA electronic
    health record within 5 business days of each encounter;
    (2) obtain VA authorization before ordering additional
    procedures beyond the original referral;
    (3) coordinate with the veteran's VA primary care team.
(b) PENALTIES.—Providers who fail to comply with coordination
requirements shall be subject to payment reduction and
potential network removal.

SEC. 5. COST CONTROLS.
(a) RATE NEGOTIATION.—The Secretary shall negotiate rates with
community providers rather than using Medicare-based fee
schedules.
(b) BUDGET NEUTRALITY.—Community care spending growth shall
not exceed the growth rate of VA medical services spending.
(c) REINVESTMENT.—Any savings from community care reform
shall be redirected to VA direct care capacity.

SEC. 6. TRANSPARENCY AND REPORTING.
(a) The Secretary shall publish annual reports comparing—
    (1) cost per encounter in VA versus community care;
    (2) quality outcomes in VA versus community care;
    (3) care coordination completion rates;
    (4) veteran satisfaction in VA versus community care.
```

**Explanation**: The current access standards create automatic referrals that undermine VA capacity. This bill tightens triggers, adds quality and coordination requirements, and ensures community care actually supplements VA rather than replacing it.

**Potential Challenges**: Strong opposition from private healthcare industry; Congressional allies of community care expansion; framing as "restricting veteran choice."

**Refinements**: Could grandfather existing community care users; could exempt rural areas with no VA capacity; could phase in quality standards.

### VA Electronic Health Record Accountability Act

**Purpose**: Establish accountability for the EHR modernization program and authorize alternatives.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "VA Electronic Health Record
Accountability Act".

SEC. 2. INDEPENDENT REVIEW.
(a) The Secretary shall commission an independent review of the
EHR modernization program by an entity with no financial
relationship to Oracle Health or its subsidiaries.
(b) The review shall assess—
    (1) patient safety events at deployed sites;
    (2) cost overruns and projected total program cost;
    (3) clinician satisfaction and workflow impact;
    (4) interoperability with DoD and community providers;
    (5) feasibility of alternative approaches, including VistA
    modernization.
(c) REPORT.—The review shall be completed within 180 days of
enactment and made publicly available.

SEC. 3. PERFORMANCE STANDARDS.
(a) No further site deployment until—
    (1) patient safety event rate at deployed sites is at or
    below the rate at non-deployed VA sites;
    (2) clinician satisfaction scores exceed 60 percent approval;
    (3) system uptime exceeds 99.5 percent.
(b) CLAWBACK.—The Secretary may recover payments from the
contractor for periods when performance standards are not met.

SEC. 4. ALTERNATIVE AUTHORIZATION.
(a) The Secretary is authorized to pursue VistA modernization
or other alternatives if the independent review finds that
the current program cannot meet performance standards within
2 years.
(b) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated such
sums as necessary to pursue alternative approaches.
```

**Explanation**: Creates accountability for a $16+ billion program that has produced serious patient safety events, while preserving the option to change course based on evidence.

**Potential Challenges**: Oracle lobbying; sunk cost arguments; DoD interoperability concerns.

**Refinements**: Could include a joint VA-DoD interoperability requirement regardless of platform choice.

### VHA Governance Stability Act

**Purpose**: Establish term-protected VHA leadership to enable sustained reform.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "VHA Governance Stability Act".

SEC. 2. VHA CHIEF EXECUTIVE OFFICER.
(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established within the Department of
Veterans Affairs the position of Chief Executive Officer of the
Veterans Health Administration.
(b) APPOINTMENT.—The CEO shall be appointed by the President,
by and with the advice and consent of the Senate.
(c) TERM.—The CEO shall serve a term of 5 years and may be
reappointed for one additional term.
(d) REMOVAL.—The CEO may be removed only for cause, including—
    (1) malfeasance or neglect of duty;
    (2) felony conviction;
    (3) incapacity.
(e) QUALIFICATIONS.—The CEO shall have not fewer than 10 years
of experience in healthcare administration or delivery.

SEC. 3. AUTHORITIES.
(a) The CEO shall have authority over—
    (1) VHA clinical operations and quality;
    (2) VISN director selection and performance evaluation;
    (3) workforce management within VHA;
    (4) capital planning recommendations.
(b) The CEO shall report to the Secretary of Veterans Affairs
on administrative matters and to Congress on clinical
performance through annual reports.

SEC. 4. TRANSPARENCY.
(a) The CEO shall publish quarterly reports on—
    (1) facility-level quality metrics;
    (2) access and wait time data;
    (3) workforce vacancy rates;
    (4) community care utilization and costs.
(b) All reports shall be publicly accessible.
```

**Explanation**: Modeled on term-protected positions like the FBI Director and Federal Reserve Chair, this insulates VHA clinical leadership from political turnover while maintaining accountability through reporting and cause-based removal.

**Potential Challenges**: Executive branch resistance to limiting appointment authority; concerns about accountability reduction.

**Refinements**: Could include a Congressional veto mechanism for cause determination.

## State Model Legislation

### State VA Healthcare Licensing Compact

**Purpose**: Enable VA clinicians to practice across state lines without additional licensing.

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "[State] VA Healthcare
Licensing Compact Act".

SECTION 2. RECOGNITION OF VA CREDENTIALS.
(a) A healthcare professional credentialed by the Veterans
Health Administration who holds an active, unrestricted
license in any state shall be authorized to provide care
to veterans within this state through VA facilities or
VA-authorized telehealth.
(b) This authorization shall apply to—
    (1) in-person care at VA facilities within the state;
    (2) telehealth services delivered to veterans in the state;
    (3) community care coordinated by VA within the state.
```

**Adaptations**: States may limit to telehealth only; may require notification to state board; may exclude controlled substance prescribing.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| Federal authority | Spending Clause and military powers provide clear authority |
| State preemption | Federal licensing compact may preempt state licensing for VA care |
| Due process | Employee removal provisions must satisfy minimal due process |
| Takings | Facility closure and land disposition must follow federal procedures |

### Preemption

VA healthcare operates under federal supremacy in most areas. State licensing requirements do not apply to care delivered at VA facilities under the Supremacy Clause. The COMPACT Act of 2020 extended this to telehealth.

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Pay parity gaming | Facilities could classify positions at lower levels to avoid pay increases | Medium |
| Community care workaround | Providers could meet minimum quality standards while providing inferior care | High |
| Commission politicization | Political appointees could stack the Modernization Commission | High |
| EHR contractor delays | Oracle could delay assessment participation to run out clock | Medium |
| Hiring authority misuse | Direct hire could bypass merit system protections | Low |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|-----------|
| No mandatory appropriation | Funding subject to annual politics | Budget process |
| Industry lobbying unaddressed | Private sector will continue to push community care expansion | First Amendment limits |
| Rural access gap remains | Infrastructure and telehealth insufficient for most remote veterans | Geographic reality |
| Leadership term may be circumvented | Future Congress could amend term protections | Legislative prerogative |
| State compact voluntary | States may not adopt licensing compact | Federalism |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Pay parity audit**: Require OIG annual audits of position classification and actual compensation relative to market data
2. **Community care quality enforcement**: Establish mandatory minimum outcome metrics with automatic network removal for non-compliance
3. **Commission independence safeguards**: Require bipartisan appointment, financial disclosure, conflict-of-interest prohibition
4. **EHR contractor accountability**: Include liquidated damages for assessment non-cooperation in contract modifications
5. **Hiring oversight**: Maintain merit system principles reporting to OPM for direct hire usage
6. **Appropriations floor**: Advocate for advance appropriations (as currently exists for some VA medical care) to protect against government shutdowns

### General Implementation Concerns

- Community care reform will face intense lobbying from the $28B/year private healthcare revenue stream
- Infrastructure modernization will encounter fierce local opposition to facility closures
- EHR reform depends on contractual obligations and potential litigation with Oracle
- Governance reform requires unusual bipartisan agreement to limit executive appointment authority
- Workforce reform must navigate federal employee union negotiations and OPM coordination
