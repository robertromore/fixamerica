# Veteran Substance Abuse: Stakeholders

## Who Is Affected

### Veterans with Substance Use Disorders

**Population**: Approximately 1.1 million veterans with a past-year SUD (SAMHSA NSDUH, 2024)

**Most Affected Subgroups**:

- **Post-9/11 veterans with combat exposure**: Highest rates of co-occurring PTSD and SUD, polysubstance use, and service-related chronic pain
- **Recently separated veterans**: The transition period (first 1-3 years after separation) is peak risk for substance abuse escalation
- **Rural veterans**: Greater geographic barriers to treatment, higher rates of alcohol use disorder, less access to MAT providers
- **Veterans with other-than-honorable discharges**: Restricted VA eligibility creates a population of high-need, low-access individuals; estimated 500,000 veterans (Veteran Legal Services, 2024)
- **Female veterans**: Rising rates of alcohol use disorder and unique barriers to treatment including childcare, military sexual trauma history, and treatment programs designed for male populations
- **American Indian/Alaska Native veterans**: Highest SUD prevalence rates (17.4%) with compounding systemic disadvantages

**What They Need**:

- Immediate access to evidence-based treatment without waitlists or bureaucratic barriers
- Integrated treatment for co-occurring conditions (PTSD, chronic pain, TBI)
- Peer support from veterans with lived recovery experience
- Economic stability (employment, housing, disability benefits) as a foundation for recovery
- Reduced stigma within their communities and institutions

### Veteran Families

**Population**: Approximately 5 million family members of veterans with SUDs

**Impact**:

- Spouses and partners bear the burden of caregiving, financial management during disability, and navigating a complex benefits system
- Children of veterans with SUDs face elevated ACE scores, behavioral problems, and intergenerational transmission of trauma
- Divorce rates among veteran couples where one partner has an SUD are approximately 3x the general veteran population (National Military Family Association, 2023)
- Family members often lack access to VA services and must navigate separate civilian systems for their own support needs

**What They Need**:

- Family therapy integrated into veteran SUD treatment programs
- Direct access to support services regardless of their own veteran status
- Respite care and childcare during treatment periods
- Financial counseling and benefits navigation assistance

### Veterans in the Criminal Justice System

**Population**: Approximately 107,000 veterans in state prisons; 58% with SUD history (Bureau of Justice Statistics, 2023)

**Impact**:

- Incarceration rather than treatment for substance-related offenses
- Loss of VA benefits, voting rights, housing eligibility, and employment prospects
- Inadequate treatment within correctional facilities
- Post-release barriers to reentry compounded by criminal record

**What They Need**:

- Diversion from incarceration through veteran treatment courts
- Continuity of VA healthcare during and after incarceration
- Reentry programs specifically addressing co-occurring SUD and justice involvement
- Expungement pathways for substance-related offenses

### Homeless Veterans

**Population**: 35,574 veterans experiencing homelessness on a single night in 2024 (HUD Annual Homeless Assessment, 2024)

**Connection to Substance Abuse**:

- Approximately 70% of homeless veterans have a substance use disorder (National Coalition for Homeless Veterans, 2024)
- SUD is both a driver and consequence of homelessness: substance use leads to job loss and relationship breakdown; homelessness exacerbates substance use through environmental exposure and coping
- Housing First programs have demonstrated that providing stable housing first, without sobriety requirements, improves both housing retention and treatment engagement

## Who Has Power

### Department of Veterans Affairs

**Role**: Primary provider of SUD treatment for veterans through 1,298 healthcare facilities

**Key Decision-Makers**:

- VA Secretary and Deputy Secretary
- Under Secretary for Health (Veterans Health Administration)
- VA Office of Mental Health and Suicide Prevention (oversees SUD programs)
- VA Pharmacy Benefits Management (controls formulary and MAT access)
- Individual VA Medical Center directors (significant local discretion)

**Leverage Points**:

- Controls $2.1 billion in SUD treatment spending (FY 2024)
- Sets prescribing policies, treatment protocols, and facility priorities
- Determines which evidence-based practices are adopted systemwide
- Can expand or restrict community care referrals for SUD treatment

**Current Posture**: Supportive of MAT expansion and harm reduction but constrained by budget, provider shortages, and institutional inertia at the facility level

### Department of Defense

**Role**: Shapes the military culture and policies that create substance use risk; manages transition programs

**Key Decision-Makers**:

- Secretary of Defense and service secretaries
- Defense Health Agency
- Military service surgeons general
- Installation commanders (set local alcohol policies)

**Leverage Points**:

- Controls alcohol availability and pricing on military installations
- Sets drug testing policies and consequences
- Manages the Transition Assistance Program (TAP)
- Determines discharge characterization for substance-related misconduct

**Current Posture**: Focused on readiness; alcohol culture reform is slow; zero-tolerance drug policy remains firm

### Congress

**Role**: Authorizes and funds veteran SUD programs; sets policy through legislation

**Key Committees**:

| Committee | Role |
|-----------|------|
| Senate Veterans' Affairs | Authorization and oversight of VA programs |
| House Veterans' Affairs | Authorization and oversight of VA programs |
| Senate Appropriations (Military Construction, VA) | VA funding levels |
| House Appropriations (Military Construction, VA) | VA funding levels |
| Senate HELP Committee | Broader drug policy affecting veterans |
| House Energy and Commerce | Broader drug policy, DEA scheduling |

**Leverage Points**:

- Sets VA budget levels, including SUD treatment funding
- Passes legislation expanding or restricting treatment eligibility
- Conducts oversight hearings that influence VA priorities
- Confirms VA leadership nominations

**Current Posture**: Bipartisan support for veteran SUD treatment in principle; disagreements over funding levels, harm reduction approaches, and cannabis policy

### Veterans Service Organizations (VSOs)

**Major Organizations**:

| Organization | Membership | SUD Policy Position |
|--------------|------------|---------------------|
| American Legion | 1.6 million | Supports MAT expansion; cautious on harm reduction |
| Veterans of Foreign Wars (VFW) | 1.5 million | Supports increased SUD treatment funding |
| Disabled American Veterans (DAV) | 1.0 million | Advocates for integrated PTSD-SUD treatment |
| Iraq and Afghanistan Veterans of America (IAVA) | 425,000 | Supports cannabis research, comprehensive SUD reform |
| Vietnam Veterans of America (VVA) | 86,000 | Advocates for Agent Orange-related SUD treatment |
| Wounded Warrior Project | 230,000+ registered | Funds peer support, treatment access programs |

**Leverage Points**:

- Significant lobbying influence on Congress
- Shape veteran community attitudes toward treatment-seeking
- Operate direct-service programs (housing, employment, peer support)
- Provide benefit claims assistance that intersects with SUD disclosure

**Current Posture**: Universally support expanded SUD treatment; vary on harm reduction and cannabis policy

### Pharmaceutical and Treatment Industries

**Key Actors**:

- **Buprenorphine manufacturers** (Indivior, others): Profit from MAT expansion; lobby for favorable prescribing policies
- **Naloxone manufacturers** (Emergent BioSolutions, Hikma): Benefit from naloxone distribution programs
- **Residential treatment providers**: Private facilities that contract with VA and accept veteran insurance
- **Telehealth SUD platforms**: Growing role in delivering MAT and counseling remotely

**Leverage Points**:

- Influence drug pricing and formulary decisions
- Shape treatment protocols through marketing and clinical education
- Lobby for and against regulatory changes

**Current Posture**: Generally supportive of expanded treatment access (aligned with business interests); resist price controls

### State and Local Governments

**Role**: Operate Medicaid programs, state veteran agencies, and drug courts that serve veterans

**Key Functions**:

- State Medicaid programs provide SUD treatment for low-income veterans not enrolled in VA
- State veteran affairs departments coordinate community resources
- State drug courts and veteran treatment courts divert veterans from incarceration
- State licensing boards regulate civilian SUD treatment providers

**Leverage Points**:

- Control Medicaid coverage decisions affecting non-VA veterans
- Set state policies on naloxone access, harm reduction, and cannabis
- Fund and operate veteran treatment courts

## Who Is Neutral or Uninvolved

### General Public

Most Americans express sympathy for veterans with substance abuse issues but rank it low among policy priorities. Public awareness of the veteran-specific dimensions of the substance abuse crisis is limited, often conflated with the broader opioid epidemic narrative.

### Academic and Research Community

Researchers in addiction medicine, public health, and military studies produce evidence that informs policy but have limited direct influence over implementation. Key research institutions include:

- VA Health Services Research and Development
- RAND Corporation National Defense Research Institute
- National Institute on Drug Abuse (NIDA)
- Uniformed Services University of the Health Sciences

### Civilian Healthcare Providers

The majority of civilian healthcare providers lack military cultural competency and do not routinely screen for military service or service-related risk factors. Approximately 60% of veterans receive some or all of their healthcare outside the VA (VA Enrollment Data, 2024), but civilian providers are often unprepared to address the military-specific dimensions of substance abuse.
