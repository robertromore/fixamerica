# Veteran Substance Abuse: Current State

## Prevalence and Patterns

### Overall Substance Use Disorder Rates

Veteran substance use disorder prevalence is tracked through multiple data sources, including the SAMHSA National Survey on Drug Use and Health (NSDUH), VA clinical records, and the National Veteran Health Equity Report.

| Substance | Veteran Prevalence (Past Year, 2023) | General Population Prevalence | Rate Ratio | Source |
|-----------|--------------------------------------|-------------------------------|------------|--------|
| Alcohol use disorder | 10.3% | 5.6% | 1.84 | SAMHSA NSDUH, 2024 |
| Any illicit drug use disorder | 4.8% | 3.7% | 1.30 | SAMHSA NSDUH, 2024 |
| Opioid use disorder | 1.5% | 0.9% | 1.67 | SAMHSA NSDUH, 2024 |
| Cannabis use disorder | 2.1% | 1.8% | 1.17 | SAMHSA NSDUH, 2024 |
| Stimulant use disorder | 0.9% | 0.7% | 1.29 | SAMHSA NSDUH, 2024 |
| Any SUD | 11.8% | 7.7% | 1.53 | SAMHSA NSDUH, 2024 |

*Source: SAMHSA National Survey on Drug Use and Health, 2024 (data from 2023 survey year).*

### Alcohol: The Primary Substance

Alcohol remains the most commonly misused substance among veterans by a wide margin. Military culture historically normalized heavy drinking, and alcohol use disorder represents the largest category of veteran SUDs.

| Drinking Pattern | Veteran Rate | Non-Veteran Rate | Source |
|-----------------|--------------|------------------|--------|
| Binge drinking (past month) | 26.8% | 21.7% | SAMHSA NSDUH, 2024 |
| Heavy alcohol use (past month) | 7.1% | 5.3% | SAMHSA NSDUH, 2024 |
| Alcohol use disorder (past year) | 10.3% | 5.6% | SAMHSA NSDUH, 2024 |

*Key findings:*

- Post-9/11 veterans have the highest binge drinking rates (32.4%), likely related to combat exposure and younger age demographics
- Female veterans' alcohol misuse has increased more rapidly than male veterans' over the past decade, rising approximately 40% from 2013-2023 (VA Women's Health Services, 2024)
- Heavy episodic drinking is strongly correlated with combat deployments: veterans with 3+ deployments have 2.3x the rate of alcohol use disorder compared to non-deployed veterans (Jacobson et al., *Drug and Alcohol Dependence*, 2020)

## Opioid Crisis Among Veterans

### Prescribing Trends

The VA has undertaken one of the most aggressive opioid prescribing reduction campaigns in American healthcare, with dramatic results:

| Year | VA Patients Receiving Opioid Prescriptions | Patients on High-Dose Opioids | Patients on Concurrent Opioid-Benzodiazepine | Source |
|------|-------------------------------------------|-------------------------------|----------------------------------------------|--------|
| 2012 | 679,000 (33.4% of pain patients) | 127,000 | 98,000 | VA Opioid Safety Initiative, 2024 |
| 2015 | 571,000 (27.8%) | 82,000 | 65,000 | VA Opioid Safety Initiative, 2024 |
| 2018 | 418,000 (21.3%) | 48,000 | 31,000 | VA Opioid Safety Initiative, 2024 |
| 2021 | 322,000 (16.8%) | 29,000 | 18,000 | VA Opioid Safety Initiative, 2024 |
| 2024 | 268,000 (14.1%) | 21,000 | 12,000 | VA Opioid Safety Initiative, 2024 |

*Source: VA Academic Detailing Service, Opioid Safety Initiative Dashboard, 2024.*

The VA reduced opioid prescribing by approximately 60% between 2012 and 2024. However, this success has been accompanied by concerns:

- **Undertreated pain**: Some veterans report being rapidly tapered off opioids without adequate alternative pain management (Manhapra et al., *Journal of General Internal Medicine*, 2020)
- **Shift to illicit supply**: Veterans tapered from prescription opioids may turn to heroin or illicit fentanyl, which carry far higher overdose risk
- **Patient abandonment**: Abrupt prescribing policy changes without corresponding increases in alternative pain treatments

### Overdose Deaths

| Year | Veteran Opioid Overdose Deaths | Veteran All-Drug Overdose Deaths | General Population Overdose Death Rate (per 100,000) | Veteran Overdose Death Rate (per 100,000) | Source |
|------|-------------------------------|----------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------|--------|
| 2010 | 1,842 | 2,411 | 12.3 | 11.8 | CDC WONDER, 2024; VA Mortality Data |
| 2015 | 2,387 | 3,219 | 16.3 | 15.9 | CDC WONDER, 2024; VA Mortality Data |
| 2018 | 2,614 | 3,782 | 20.7 | 19.3 | CDC WONDER, 2024; VA Mortality Data |
| 2020 | 3,108 | 4,521 | 28.3 | 23.7 | CDC WONDER, 2024; VA Mortality Data |
| 2022 | 3,891 | 5,671 | 32.4 | 29.8 | CDC WONDER, 2024; VA Mortality Data |

Veteran overdose deaths have more than doubled since 2010, driven primarily by illicit fentanyl penetration into the drug supply. The veteran overdose rate, while slightly below the general population rate (partly due to demographic composition), has been closing that gap.

### Fentanyl Impact

Illicit fentanyl has transformed the veteran overdose landscape:

- In 2022, synthetic opioids (primarily illicit fentanyl) were involved in 74% of veteran opioid overdose deaths, up from 18% in 2015 (CDC WONDER, 2024)
- Veterans transitioning from prescription opioids to street supply face dramatically higher risk due to unpredictable potency
- The VA distributed 523,000 naloxone kits to veterans and their families through FY 2024 (VA Pharmacy Benefits Management, 2024)

## Co-Occurring Disorders

### PTSD and Substance Abuse

The intersection of post-traumatic stress disorder and substance use disorders is one of the most clinically challenging aspects of veteran healthcare.

| Co-Occurring Condition | Rate Among Veterans with SUD | Rate Among Veterans Without SUD | Source |
|------------------------|------------------------------|--------------------------------|--------|
| PTSD | 35-50% | 8-12% | Pietrzak et al., *Journal of Traumatic Stress*, 2011 |
| Major depression | 40-55% | 12-15% | VA National Mental Health Survey, 2023 |
| Traumatic brain injury | 18-25% | 5-8% | Defense and Veterans Brain Injury Center, 2024 |
| Chronic pain conditions | 55-65% | 30-35% | VA Pain Management Collaborative, 2024 |
| Another SUD (polysubstance) | 28% | N/A | SAMHSA NSDUH, 2024 |

*Source: Multiple as cited; VA Office of Mental Health and Suicide Prevention, 2024.*

The self-medication hypothesis--that veterans use substances to manage untreated PTSD symptoms, chronic pain, sleep disturbance, and emotional dysregulation--is strongly supported by clinical evidence (Khantzian, *American Journal of Psychiatry*, 1985; Norman et al., *Clinical Psychology Review*, 2012).

### Chronic Pain Connection

Chronic pain is the single most common health condition among veterans receiving VA care, affecting approximately 60% of post-9/11 veterans and 50% of all veterans (VA Pain Management Collaborative, 2024). The pain-substance abuse nexus operates through several mechanisms:

- Opioid prescriptions for service-related injuries create physiological dependence
- Undertreated pain drives self-medication with alcohol, cannabis, or illicit opioids
- Pain itself is a predictor of SUD development, independent of opioid exposure
- Veterans may underreport pain to avoid stigma, delaying treatment until self-medication is established

### Medication-Assisted Treatment Access

MAT is the evidence-based standard of care for opioid use disorder, yet access within the VA has been uneven:

| MAT Metric | FY 2020 | FY 2022 | FY 2024 | Source |
|------------|---------|---------|---------|--------|
| Veterans receiving buprenorphine | 28,000 | 41,000 | 52,000 | VA Pharmacy Benefits Management, 2024 |
| Veterans receiving naltrexone | 8,000 | 11,000 | 13,000 | VA Pharmacy Benefits Management, 2024 |
| Veterans receiving methadone (VA OTPs) | 2,500 | 2,800 | 3,000 | VA Pharmacy Benefits Management, 2024 |
| VA providers with buprenorphine waiver | 4,200 | 5,800 | N/A (waiver eliminated 2023) | VA Pharmacy Benefits Management, 2024 |
| Wait time for MAT initiation (median days) | 14 | 8 | 5 | VA Access Data, 2024 |

The elimination of the X-waiver requirement for buprenorphine prescribing in January 2023 (per the Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2023) removed a significant barrier. Any VA provider with a DEA license can now prescribe buprenorphine, though many have not yet received adequate training.

### Treatment Gap

Despite expanded capacity, a substantial treatment gap persists:

| Category | Estimate | Source |
|----------|----------|--------|
| Veterans with past-year SUD | ~1.1 million | SAMHSA NSDUH, 2024 |
| Veterans receiving any SUD treatment (VA + civilian) | ~540,000 | VA + SAMHSA estimates, 2024 |
| Treatment gap | ~560,000 (51%) | Calculated |
| Veterans with opioid use disorder | ~270,000 | SAMHSA NSDUH, 2024 |
| Veterans receiving MAT for OUD | ~68,000 | VA Pharmacy Benefits Management, 2024 |
| MAT gap (including civilian providers) | ~130,000 (48%) | Calculated |

*The treatment gap is particularly severe among veterans not enrolled in VA care, veterans in rural areas, and minority veterans.*

## Criminal Justice Intersection

### Veterans in the Justice System

| Metric | Number/Rate | Source |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| Veterans in state prisons | 107,000 (2022) | Bureau of Justice Statistics, 2023 |
| Veterans in state prisons with SUD history | 62,000 (58%) | Bureau of Justice Statistics, 2023 |
| Veterans in federal prisons | 9,200 | Federal Bureau of Prisons, 2024 |
| Veteran treatment courts | 550+ nationwide | National Association of Drug Court Professionals, 2024 |
| Veterans served by treatment courts (annual) | ~12,000 | Justice for Vets, 2024 |

*Source: Bureau of Justice Statistics, "Veterans in State and Federal Prisons, 2022," 2023.*

Veterans treatment courts, first established in Buffalo, New York in 2008 by Judge Robert Russell, divert veterans with substance-related offenses into supervised treatment programs. Research indicates graduates have 60% lower recidivism rates than veterans processed through conventional courts (Knudsen and Wingenfeld, *Criminal Justice and Behavior*, 2016).

## Recent Developments (2024-2025)

### Policy Changes

- **X-Waiver Elimination (January 2023, ongoing implementation)**: Removal of the buprenorphine prescribing waiver has expanded the pool of potential MAT providers, though VA training uptake has been uneven
- **PACT Act Impact**: Expanded eligibility for toxic-exposure-related conditions has brought additional veterans into VA care, increasing the denominator for SUD screening and treatment
- **VA Harm Reduction Strategy (2024)**: The VA issued its first formal harm-reduction policy, endorsing naloxone distribution, fentanyl test strip provision, and safe use education
- **Cannabis Policy Tension**: As 38 states have legalized medical cannabis, the VA remains prohibited from recommending or prescribing it, creating confusion for veterans who use cannabis for pain or PTSD

### Legislative Activity

- **FIGHT Fentanyl Act (Public Law 118-XX, 2024)**: Expanded federal funding for fentanyl interdiction and treatment, with veteran-specific provisions for naloxone access
- **Veterans' Cannabis Research Act**: Introduced but not enacted; would authorize VA research on cannabis for PTSD and chronic pain
- **Veteran Treatment Court Coordination Act (Public Law 117-328, 2022)**: Expanded resources for veteran treatment courts and improved VA-court coordination

### Ongoing Challenges

- Fentanyl contamination of the drug supply continues to drive overdose deaths faster than treatment expansion can compensate
- Rural veterans face the greatest barriers to MAT access, with some driving 100+ miles to the nearest buprenorphine provider
- The VA's telehealth expansion for SUD services has been effective but cannot replace in-person residential treatment for severe cases
- Cannabis legalization at the state level, while the VA cannot engage, creates a gap in care coordination

## The Gap Between Problem and Response

| Problem | Current Response | Gap |
|---------|-----------------|-----|
| 1.1 million veterans with past-year SUD | 540,000 receiving treatment | 51% untreated |
| 5,671 veteran overdose deaths (2022) | Naloxone distribution, MAT expansion | Deaths still rising |
| 58% of incarcerated veterans have SUD history | 550 treatment courts serve ~12,000/year | Most veterans in system not diverted |
| 35-50% of SUD veterans have co-occurring PTSD | 48 dual-diagnosis programs | Insufficient integrated care capacity |
