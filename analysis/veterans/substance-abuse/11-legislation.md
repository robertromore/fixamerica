# Veteran Substance Abuse: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Legislative reform for veteran substance abuse requires action at three levels: federal legislation to expand VA treatment capacity and eliminate eligibility barriers, state legislation to establish treatment courts and ensure community-based access, and regulatory changes to modernize prescribing and treatment delivery. This document provides draft legal text for key reforms identified in [Solutions](07-solutions.md), organized by level of government and likelihood of enactment.

## Federal Legislation

### Veterans Comprehensive Substance Use Treatment Act

**Purpose**: Mandate universal access to medication-assisted treatment within the VA system, eliminate prior authorization barriers, and ensure integrated treatment for co-occurring conditions.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Veterans Comprehensive Substance
Use Treatment Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) Approximately 1,100,000 veterans meet diagnostic criteria
for a substance use disorder in any given year.
(2) Fewer than half of veterans with substance use disorders
receive any form of treatment.
(3) Medication-assisted treatment reduces opioid overdose
mortality by 50 to 75 percent.
(4) Veterans with co-occurring post-traumatic stress disorder
and substance use disorders achieve better outcomes through
integrated treatment than through sequential or parallel
treatment.

SEC. 3. UNIVERSAL MEDICATION-ASSISTED TREATMENT ACCESS.

(a) IN GENERAL.--The Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall ensure
that medication-assisted treatment for opioid use disorder,
including buprenorphine, naltrexone, and methadone, is available
at every medical center of the Department of Veterans Affairs
and through community care arrangements under the Veterans
Community Care Program.

(b) TIMELINESS.--Treatment under subsection (a) shall be
initiated not later than 72 hours after a veteran requests
such treatment.

(c) PRIOR AUTHORIZATION.--The Secretary shall not require
prior authorization for the prescribing of medications approved
by the Food and Drug Administration for the treatment of
substance use disorders.

(d) EMERGENCY DEPARTMENT INITIATION.--Each emergency department
of a medical center of the Department shall have the capacity
to initiate medication-assisted treatment and provide bridge
prescriptions for continuation of such treatment.

SEC. 4. INTEGRATED TREATMENT FOR CO-OCCURRING DISORDERS.

(a) IN GENERAL.--The Secretary shall ensure that each medical
center of the Department offers integrated treatment for veterans
diagnosed with both a substance use disorder and post-traumatic
stress disorder, traumatic brain injury, chronic pain, or
depression.

(b) TREATMENT MODELS.--Integrated treatment under subsection (a)
shall include evidence-based models such as Concurrent Treatment
of PTSD and Substance Use Disorders Using Prolonged Exposure
(COPE), Seeking Safety, or Integrated Cognitive Behavioral
Therapy.

(c) PROHIBITION ON SOBRIETY PREREQUISITES.--The Secretary
shall not require a veteran to achieve sobriety as a condition
of receiving treatment for post-traumatic stress disorder or
other mental health conditions.

SEC. 5. NALOXONE CO-PRESCRIBING.

(a) IN GENERAL.--The Secretary shall ensure that each veteran
who is prescribed an opioid medication by the Department is
offered a naloxone kit at the time of prescribing.

(b) STANDING ORDERS.--Each medical center and community-based
outpatient clinic of the Department shall maintain a standing
order for the dispensing of naloxone to any veteran or veteran
family member who requests it.

SEC. 6. PEER SUPPORT EXPANSION.

(a) IN GENERAL.--The Secretary shall hire not fewer than
2,500 peer support specialists with lived experience in
substance use disorder recovery to serve in the Department
by not later than 2 years after the date of enactment.

(b) TRAINING.--The Secretary shall establish a national
certification program for veteran peer recovery support
specialists.

SEC. 7. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated to carry out this
Act $450,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2031.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Eliminates the primary access barrier--wait times and facility-level variability in MAT availability. The 72-hour standard creates accountability without being unrealistically immediate
- **Section 4**: Mandates the integration of SUD and mental health treatment that evidence shows produces superior outcomes. The sobriety prerequisite prohibition addresses the common VA practice of requiring veterans to be sober before treating their PTSD--a clinically counterproductive policy
- **Section 5**: Turns naloxone from an opt-in program to a default co-prescribing practice, consistent with harm-reduction best practices
- **Section 6**: Recognizes that peer support specialists are among the most effective treatment tools and addresses the current shortage

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Cost ($450M/year) | Untreated veteran SUD costs $20+ billion annually in healthcare, criminal justice, and lost productivity |
| Provider capacity | Peer specialists and telehealth can partially substitute for physician shortages |
| MAT ideology resistance | Bill mandates availability, not that every veteran must accept MAT |
| VA implementation capacity | Phased 5-year authorization allows gradual rollout |

**Refinements**:

- **Stronger version**: Mandate MAT initiation within 24 hours rather than 72 hours
- **Fallback version**: Apply 72-hour standard to urban VA facilities initially, with 7-day standard for rural
- **Addition**: Include cannabis research authorization (politically controversial but clinically important)

### Veteran Discharge Fairness and Treatment Access Act

**Purpose**: Eliminate the other-than-honorable discharge barrier to VA SUD treatment eligibility and create an expedited discharge upgrade process for substance-related discharges.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Veteran Discharge Fairness
and Treatment Access Act".

SEC. 2. ELIGIBILITY FOR SUBSTANCE USE DISORDER TREATMENT.

(a) IN GENERAL.--Section 1720I of title 38, United States
Code, is amended by adding at the end the following:

"(e) ELIGIBILITY FOR FORMER MEMBERS WITH OTHER THAN
HONORABLE DISCHARGES.--A former member of the Armed Forces
who was discharged or released under conditions other than
honorable shall be eligible for substance use disorder
treatment under this section if--

"(1) the former member's discharge or release was related
to substance use or a mental health condition; or

"(2) the former member has a substance use disorder that
the Secretary determines is connected to the former member's
military service.".

(b) PRESUMPTION.--In determining eligibility under subsection
(a), the Secretary shall apply a presumption that substance
use and mental health conditions that manifested during or
within 5 years after military service are connected to such
service.

SEC. 3. EXPEDITED DISCHARGE REVIEW.

(a) IN GENERAL.--The Secretary of Defense, in coordination
with the Secretary of Veterans Affairs, shall establish an
expedited review process for former members of the Armed
Forces whose discharge characterization was based in whole
or in part on conduct related to substance use, post-traumatic
stress disorder, traumatic brain injury, or other mental health
conditions.

(b) STANDARD OF REVIEW.--In conducting reviews under
subsection (a), the applicable Discharge Review Board shall
apply liberal consideration to evidence that the conduct
underlying the discharge was connected to a service-related
mental health condition or substance use disorder, consistent
with the guidance issued by the Under Secretary of Defense
for Personnel and Readiness on September 3, 2014, and
supplemental guidance issued on August 25, 2017.

(c) TIMELINE.--Reviews under subsection (a) shall be
completed not later than 180 days after receipt of an
application.

SEC. 4. OUTREACH.

The Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall conduct outreach
specifically targeting former members of the Armed Forces
with other-than-honorable discharges to inform them of their
eligibility for substance use disorder treatment under
section 1720I(e) of title 38, United States Code.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 2**: Directly addresses the perverse cycle where substance-related misconduct leads to an OTH discharge, which bars the veteran from the treatment they need for the condition that caused the misconduct
- **Section 3**: Leverages existing DOD guidance on liberal consideration for mental health-related discharges but creates binding timelines and standards
- **Section 4**: Recognizes that eligibility expansion is meaningless without outreach, as many OTH veterans believe they have no VA access

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| DOD resistance to discharge reclassification | Bill focuses on treatment eligibility, not automatic upgrade |
| Moral hazard (incentivizing misconduct) | Applies only to substance/mental health-related conduct, not all misconduct |
| Cost of serving additional population | Preventive treatment is far cheaper than crisis care, incarceration, and homelessness |

### Veteran Treatment Court Standardization Act

**Purpose**: Establish veteran treatment courts in every federal judicial district and provide resources for state adoption.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Veteran Treatment Court
Standardization Act".

SEC. 2. FEDERAL VETERAN TREATMENT COURTS.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--Not later than 3 years after the date
of enactment, each United States District Court shall
establish a veteran treatment court program, or equivalent
diversion program, for veterans charged with nonviolent
offenses related to substance use disorders, mental health
conditions, or both.

(b) ELEMENTS.--Each program under subsection (a) shall
include--
(1) substance use disorder screening and assessment;
(2) individualized treatment plans incorporating
medication-assisted treatment where clinically appropriate;
(3) judicial supervision with regular status hearings;
(4) connection to Veterans Affairs healthcare services;
(5) peer mentor assignment from a veteran with lived
recovery experience;
(6) housing and employment support; and
(7) charges dismissed upon successful completion.

(c) VA LIAISON.--The Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall
assign a liaison officer to each veteran treatment court
established under this section to coordinate treatment
services and benefits enrollment.

SEC. 3. STATE GRANTS.

(a) IN GENERAL.--The Attorney General, in consultation
with the Secretary of Veterans Affairs, shall award grants
to states and localities for the establishment, expansion,
or improvement of veteran treatment court programs.

(b) AUTHORIZATION.--There are authorized to be appropriated
$150,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2031
for grants under this section.

SEC. 4. DATA COLLECTION AND REPORTING.

The Attorney General shall collect and publish annual data
on veteran treatment court outcomes, including completion
rates, recidivism rates, substance use outcomes, and
employment and housing stability at 12 and 24 months
post-completion.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 2**: Creates a national floor for veteran diversion from incarceration, ensuring geographic consistency
- **Section 2(b)(7)**: Charge dismissal upon completion is critical for removing the criminal record barrier to reentry
- **Section 3**: Federal grants incentivize state adoption without mandating it (respecting state sovereignty over criminal justice)

## State Model Legislation

### Model Veterans Substance Use Treatment and Diversion Act

**Purpose**: Provide a template for state-level legislation establishing veteran treatment courts, ensuring Medicaid coverage for SUD treatment, and implementing Good Samaritan protections.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Veterans Substance Use
Treatment and Diversion Act".

SECTION 2. VETERAN TREATMENT COURT PROGRAM.

(a) Each judicial district in this state shall, subject to
appropriations, establish a veteran treatment court program
for eligible veterans charged with nonviolent offenses
related to substance use or mental health conditions.

(b) A veteran shall be eligible for diversion to a veteran
treatment court if:
(1) the veteran is charged with a nonviolent offense;
(2) the offense is related to a substance use disorder
or mental health condition; and
(3) the veteran agrees to participate in a treatment plan
supervised by the court.

(c) Upon successful completion of the treatment court
program, charges against the veteran shall be dismissed
and the record sealed.

SECTION 3. MEDICAID COVERAGE FOR VETERAN SUD TREATMENT.

The [State Medicaid Agency] shall ensure that substance use
disorder treatment, including medication-assisted treatment,
is covered under the state Medicaid program for all eligible
veterans, including those not enrolled in Department of
Veterans Affairs healthcare.

SECTION 4. GOOD SAMARITAN PROTECTIONS.

(a) A person, including a veteran, who in good faith seeks
medical assistance for another individual experiencing a
drug overdose shall not be charged or prosecuted for
possession of a controlled substance if the evidence for
the charge was obtained as a result of the person's seeking
medical assistance.

(b) A person who experiences a drug overdose and is in
need of medical assistance shall not be charged or
prosecuted for possession of a controlled substance if
the evidence for the charge was obtained as a result of
the overdose and the need for medical assistance.

SECTION 5. NALOXONE ACCESS.

Any person may purchase naloxone from a licensed pharmacy
without an individual prescription pursuant to a statewide
standing order issued by the State Health Officer.
```

**Adaptations**:

- States with existing treatment courts can adopt the veteran-specific provisions only
- Non-Medicaid-expansion states may need alternative funding mechanisms for Section 3
- States with existing Good Samaritan laws can incorporate veteran-specific outreach requirements
- Naloxone access provisions can be adjusted based on existing state pharmacy law

## Regulatory Framework

### VA Harm Reduction Regulation

**Existing Authority**: 38 U.S.C. Section 1710 (VA healthcare services); Secretary's discretion over treatment modalities

**Draft Regulation**:

```text
DEPARTMENT OF VETERANS AFFAIRS

38 CFR Part 17 -- Medical

Subpart -- Harm Reduction Services for Substance Use Disorders

Sec. 17.XX1 Purpose.
This subpart establishes harm reduction services as a
component of the Department's substance use disorder
treatment continuum.

Sec. 17.XX2 Definitions.
(a) "Harm reduction" means evidence-based strategies that
reduce the negative consequences of substance use without
requiring abstinence as a precondition for services.
(b) "Naloxone distribution" means the provision of naloxone
rescue kits to veterans, veteran family members, and
caregivers.
(c) "Fentanyl test strips" means immunoassay-based test
strips used to detect the presence of fentanyl and fentanyl
analogs in drug supplies.

Sec. 17.XX3 Required Services.
Each VA medical center shall make available the following
harm reduction services:
(a) Naloxone rescue kits, dispensed without limit to
veterans and their family members;
(b) Fentanyl test strips, upon request;
(c) Safe use education, including overdose prevention
counseling;
(d) Referral to substance use disorder treatment at
every harm reduction contact.

Sec. 17.XX4 Training.
All VA clinical staff in emergency departments, primary
care, and mental health shall complete training in harm
reduction principles and naloxone administration not later
than 12 months after the effective date of this regulation.
```

**Explanation**: Codifies the VA's 2024 harm-reduction policy into binding regulation, ensuring consistency across facilities and protection against future policy reversals.

### DEA Scheduling and Prescribing Reforms

**Existing Authority**: 21 U.S.C. Section 823 (DEA registration); Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2023 (X-waiver elimination)

**Recommended Regulatory Actions**:

- DEA should issue guidance confirming that all VA providers with active DEA registrations may prescribe buprenorphine for opioid use disorder without additional certification
- DEA should expedite approval for VA mobile MAT units, allowing treatment delivery in community settings
- DEA should clarify that VA telehealth MAT prescribing (initiated via telephone during COVID-19) may continue permanently

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Spending Clause**: Federal legislation operates through VA appropriations--no constitutional barrier to expanded treatment eligibility
- **Commerce Clause**: Federal regulation of controlled substances (MAT medications) is well-established under *Gonzales v. Raich*, 545 U.S. 1 (2005)
- **Due process**: Discharge status reform raises no due process concerns; it expands rather than restricts eligibility
- **Equal protection**: Veteran-specific treatment courts and diversion programs are constitutionally permissible as rational classifications based on veteran status

### Preemption Questions

- Federal SUD treatment standards do not preempt state treatment laws; states may impose additional requirements
- DEA scheduling and prescribing rules preempt conflicting state regulations
- State cannabis laws remain in tension with federal prohibition; VA providers cannot recommend cannabis regardless of state law until federal law changes

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- VA Inspector General oversight of treatment access compliance
- Congressional reporting requirements with facility-level data
- Government Accountability Office audits of program effectiveness
- Veteran advocacy organizations as informal enforcement through public accountability

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Veterans Comprehensive Substance Use Treatment Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Community care escape valve | VA could satisfy 72-hour MAT requirement by referring to unavailable community providers | High |
| "Availability" vs. "access" | Bill mandates availability, but staffing and capacity may not match demand | High |
| Peer specialist qualifications | Without clear standards, peer specialist positions could be filled with underqualified candidates | Medium |
| Integrated treatment definition | Facilities could claim compliance with minimal integrated programming | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No cannabis research authorization | Veterans continue to lack access to a potentially effective treatment modality | Political controversy makes inclusion difficult |
| 5-year authorization | Programs may lose funding after the authorization period | Standard legislative practice, but creates uncertainty |
| No private insurance mandate | Veterans with private insurance may not receive equivalent SUD treatment | Limited federal authority over private insurance |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Add explicit language defining "available" to include staffing capacity sufficient to meet demand within 72 hours
2. Require VA to report community care referral outcomes, including whether veterans successfully accessed treatment
3. Establish minimum training and certification standards for peer support specialists
4. Define integrated treatment as requiring co-located providers trained in both SUD and mental health treatment modalities
5. Include cannabis research authorization in a separate title to avoid jeopardizing the broader bill

### Veteran Discharge Fairness and Treatment Access Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Narrow "related to" definition | DOD could interpret "related to substance use" narrowly to exclude many discharges | High |
| Outreach funding | Section 4 mandates outreach but does not appropriate specific funding | Medium |
| 180-day review timeline | No enforcement mechanism if Discharge Review Boards miss deadlines | Medium |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Define "related to" to include any discharge where substance use or mental health conditions were a contributing factor, not solely the basis
2. Appropriate dedicated funding for OTH veteran outreach
3. Establish a presumption of upgrade if the 180-day timeline is not met

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| VA facility-level variability in implementation | Tie facility director performance evaluations to SUD treatment access metrics |
| Provider training gaps | Fund mandatory continuing education in MAT and trauma-informed care |
| Data lag in outcomes measurement | Require real-time electronic reporting of SUD treatment metrics |
| Interagency coordination (DOD-VA-DOJ) | Establish a permanent interagency coordinating committee on veteran substance abuse |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- All programs should include 5-year reauthorization requirements with mandatory GAO evaluation
- VA should report annually to Congress on treatment access, wait times, overdose deaths, and treatment outcomes
- An independent advisory committee of veterans, clinicians, and researchers should review program effectiveness every 3 years
