# Veteran Substance Abuse: Solutions

## Guiding Principles

### Criteria for Effective Reform

| Principle | Definition | Application |
|-----------|------------|-------------|
| **Evidence-based** | Reforms grounded in clinical evidence and outcomes research | MAT as standard of care, integrated treatment models |
| **Trauma-informed** | Recognizes the role of military trauma in substance abuse | All treatment should address underlying PTSD, TBI, pain |
| **Accessible** | Treatment available to all veterans regardless of enrollment, geography, or discharge status | Community-based care, telehealth, eligibility reform |
| **Culturally competent** | Designed for military and veteran populations | Peer support, military-informed clinical approaches |
| **Harm reduction** | Prioritizes keeping veterans alive and reducing damage | Naloxone, fentanyl test strips, managed approaches |
| **Integrated** | Treats the whole person, not isolated conditions | Co-occurring disorder treatment, wraparound services |

### The Treatment Continuum

Effective veteran SUD policy must address the full continuum:

1. **Prevention**: Reducing risk before substance abuse develops
2. **Early intervention**: Screening and brief intervention for emerging problems
3. **Treatment**: Evidence-based clinical care for diagnosed SUDs
4. **Recovery support**: Long-term support for sustaining recovery
5. **Harm reduction**: Keeping people alive and reducing damage while in active use

## Policy Proposals

### Tier 1: Immediate Priorities

#### Universal MAT Access

**What**: Ensure every veteran with opioid use disorder can access medication-assisted treatment within 72 hours of request, at any VA facility or through community care.

**How**:

- Train all VA primary care providers to prescribe buprenorphine (post-X-waiver elimination)
- Establish same-day MAT initiation at all VA emergency departments
- Expand VA Opioid Treatment Programs (methadone clinics) from current limited capacity
- Fund community care agreements with civilian MAT providers for veterans in areas without VA access
- Eliminate prior authorization requirements for MAT medications within VA

**Evidence**: MAT reduces opioid overdose death by 50-75%, reduces illicit opioid use, improves social functioning, and is endorsed by every major medical organization worldwide (National Academies, 2019; WHO, 2009).

**Cost**: Estimated $150-200 million annually above current spending for full-access MAT

**Barriers**: Provider training gaps, institutional culture favoring abstinence in some facilities, methadone clinic siting opposition

#### Comprehensive Naloxone Distribution

**What**: Provide naloxone to every veteran prescribed opioids, every veteran in SUD treatment, every veteran's family member, and through community distribution.

**How**:

- Automatic naloxone co-prescribing with all VA opioid prescriptions
- Free naloxone available at all VA pharmacies, Vet Centers, and community outreach events
- Training for veteran family members and peers in naloxone administration
- Standing orders allowing any VA provider to dispense naloxone without individual prescriptions
- Funding for community organizations to distribute naloxone in veteran-dense areas

**Evidence**: Naloxone distribution programs are associated with 27-46% reductions in opioid overdose deaths in communities where implemented (Walley et al., *BMJ*, 2013).

**Cost**: Estimated $60-80 million annually for universal distribution

**Barriers**: Cost of naloxone formulations (though declining); some opposition from abstinence-oriented groups

#### Integrated PTSD-SUD Treatment

**What**: Mandate integrated treatment for co-occurring PTSD and substance use disorders at all VA medical centers, replacing the current model of sequential or parallel treatment.

**How**:

- Adopt evidence-based integrated protocols: COPE (Concurrent Treatment of PTSD and SUD Using Prolonged Exposure), Seeking Safety, Integrated Cognitive Behavioral Therapy
- Train all VA mental health and SUD providers in at least one integrated treatment model
- Establish dual-diagnosis treatment teams at every VA medical center
- Eliminate policies requiring sobriety before PTSD treatment--evidence shows concurrent treatment produces better outcomes for both conditions

**Evidence**: Integrated treatment for PTSD-SUD produces significantly better outcomes than sequential treatment (treating SUD first, then PTSD) or parallel treatment (treating both in separate clinical tracks). Veterans receiving integrated treatment show 40-60% greater improvement on both PTSD and SUD measures (Norman et al., *Clinical Psychology Review*, 2012; Back et al., *Journal of Substance Abuse Treatment*, 2014).

**Cost**: Primarily a reallocation of existing resources rather than new spending; estimated $50 million in training and implementation

**Barriers**: Provider resistance to treating active substance users with exposure-based PTSD therapy; capacity constraints

### Tier 2: Medium-Term Reforms

#### Discharge Status Reform

**What**: Eliminate the other-than-honorable discharge barrier to VA SUD treatment.

**How**:

- Expand the existing authority for VA to provide SUD treatment to veterans with OTH discharges who have service-connected conditions
- Establish a presumption that substance-related misconduct during service is service-connected
- Create an expedited discharge upgrade process for veterans whose OTH discharge was related to substance use or mental health conditions
- Fund VA outreach specifically targeting OTH-discharge veterans for SUD treatment enrollment

**Evidence**: Veterans with OTH discharges have 2-3x higher rates of homelessness, suicide, and substance abuse compared to honorably discharged veterans; denying them treatment is both clinically counterproductive and ethically questionable (Veteran Legal Services, 2024).

**Cost**: Estimated $200-300 million annually for expanded treatment eligibility

**Barriers**: DOD resistance to reclassifying discharge characterizations; concerns about undermining military discipline

#### Veteran Treatment Court Expansion

**What**: Establish veteran treatment courts in every federal judicial district and incentivize state adoption.

**How**:

- Federal legislation mandating veteran treatment court options in all 94 federal judicial districts
- Federal grants to states for establishing and operating veteran treatment courts
- Standardize treatment court protocols based on evidence from existing programs
- Require VA liaison officers in every treatment court to connect participants to VA services
- Fund research on treatment court outcomes to build the evidence base

**Evidence**: Graduates of veteran treatment courts have recidivism rates 58-65% lower than veterans processed through conventional courts; participants show significant improvements in substance use, employment, and housing stability (Knudsen and Wingenfeld, *Criminal Justice and Behavior*, 2016; National Association of Drug Court Professionals, 2024).

**Cost**: Estimated $150 million annually for federal courts; $200 million in state grants

**Barriers**: Judicial system resistance in some jurisdictions; concerns about equal treatment under law; veteran self-identification challenges

#### Pain Management Modernization

**What**: Replace the opioid-centric pain management paradigm with comprehensive, multimodal approaches.

**How**:

- Expand VA Whole Health programs integrating complementary approaches: acupuncture, chiropractic care, yoga, tai chi, massage therapy, and mindfulness-based stress reduction
- Fund research on non-opioid pain management specifically for veteran populations
- Establish pain management fellowship programs within the VA to train specialized providers
- Implement stepped-care pain models that reserve opioids for cases where non-opioid approaches have been tried
- Authorize VA research on cannabis for chronic pain (requires legislative action)

**Evidence**: The VA's Whole Health program, piloted at 18 flagship facilities, showed 20% reduction in opioid prescriptions and improved patient-reported pain outcomes (VA Whole Health Evaluation, 2023). Multimodal pain management reduces long-term opioid dependence without worsening pain control (Kerns et al., *Pain Medicine*, 2018).

**Cost**: Estimated $300 million annually for expanded Whole Health and complementary medicine capacity

**Barriers**: Provider training gaps in complementary approaches; veteran expectations for pharmaceutical solutions; cannabis research requires Congressional authorization

### Tier 3: Systemic Reforms

#### Community-Based Prevention Networks

**What**: Build comprehensive community-based veteran substance abuse prevention infrastructure in every state.

**How**:

- Expand the COMPACT Act grant program from $80 million to $300 million annually
- Fund community organizations--veteran service organizations, faith-based groups, peer recovery centers--to provide prevention, early intervention, and recovery support
- Require state veteran suicide prevention plans (Governor's Challenge) to include substance abuse components
- Establish community health worker positions specifically for veteran substance abuse outreach
- Create a national veteran peer recovery support certification program

**Evidence**: Community-based prevention approaches reach veterans who are not enrolled in VA care--the population that accounts for the majority of veteran overdose deaths. Peer recovery support specialists improve treatment engagement, reduce relapse, and are cost-effective (Reif et al., *Journal of Substance Abuse Treatment*, 2014).

**Cost**: Estimated $250-350 million annually for expanded community infrastructure

**Barriers**: Coordination challenges across multiple organizations; quality control for community programs; sustainability of grant-funded programs

#### Military Culture Reform

**What**: Address the military drinking culture and substance use norms that create risk upstream of veteran status.

**How**:

- Eliminate alcohol sales below market price on military installations (currently subsidized at commissaries and exchanges)
- Implement evidence-based alcohol screening and brief intervention (SBIRT) at every military healthcare visit
- Revise military training curricula to address substance use risk and healthy coping strategies
- Establish unit-level alcohol use monitoring with interventions triggered by unit-level indicators rather than individual punishment
- Develop leadership training that addresses alcohol-related safety risks without stigmatizing help-seeking

**Evidence**: Military installations that implemented alcohol pricing reforms saw 10-15% reductions in alcohol-related incidents (Ames and Cunradi, *Military Medicine*, 2004). Screening and brief intervention reduces hazardous drinking by 20-30% in military populations (Cucciare et al., *Drug and Alcohol Dependence*, 2015).

**Cost**: Minimal direct cost; potential loss of commissary/exchange revenue offset by reduced alcohol-related healthcare and disciplinary costs

**Barriers**: Strong cultural resistance within the military; potential morale impact; political sensitivity of changing military traditions

#### Seamless Transition Care

**What**: Eliminate the gap in substance abuse services during the military-to-civilian transition.

**How**:

- Begin SUD screening and treatment planning 12 months before military separation
- Require joint DOD-VA treatment teams for all transitioning service members with identified SUD risk
- Extend TRICARE coverage for 12 months post-separation to bridge the insurance gap
- Mandate warm handoff (direct provider-to-provider communication) for all transitioning veterans in SUD treatment
- Integrate the Solid Start program with SUD screening and referral

**Evidence**: The transition period is the highest-risk window for substance abuse escalation and overdose death. Veterans in the first 12 months post-separation have 2.5x the overdose risk compared to longer-separated veterans (Finlay et al., *Drug and Alcohol Dependence*, 2017).

**Cost**: Estimated $200 million annually for extended coverage and transition care teams

**Barriers**: DOD-VA coordination challenges; privacy concerns about sharing substance use information; funding disputes between DOD and VA budgets
