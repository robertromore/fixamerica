# Veteran Substance Abuse: Roadmap

## Phase 1: Immediate Actions (0-12 Months)

### Administrative Actions (No Legislation Required)

**VA Internal Policy Changes**:

- Issue directive requiring same-day MAT initiation at all VA emergency departments
- Eliminate prior authorization for buprenorphine and naltrexone prescriptions
- Mandate automatic naloxone co-prescribing with all opioid prescriptions
- Expand telehealth MAT to all VA medical centers
- Implement SBIRT (Screening, Brief Intervention, and Referral to Treatment) at all VA primary care visits

**DOD Coordination**:

- Establish joint DOD-VA transition SUD screening protocols
- Begin SUD risk assessment during the Transition Assistance Program (TAP)
- Pilot warm-handoff programs at 10 military installations with highest veteran SUD rates

**Training and Workforce**:

- Launch emergency training initiative for VA primary care providers on buprenorphine prescribing
- Recruit 500 additional peer support specialists with SUD recovery experience
- Develop military-cultural competency training module for civilian SUD treatment providers

### Quick Wins

| Action | Timeline | Impact | Authority |
|--------|----------|--------|-----------|
| Same-day MAT at VA EDs | 3 months | High | VA Secretary directive |
| Naloxone co-prescribing mandate | 3 months | High | VA pharmacy policy |
| Telehealth MAT expansion | 6 months | Medium-High | Existing VA authority |
| SBIRT at primary care | 6 months | Medium | VA clinical policy |
| Peer specialist hiring surge | 6-12 months | Medium | VA budget allocation |

## Phase 2: Short-Term Goals (1-2 Years)

### Legislative Priorities

**Veterans Substance Use Treatment Improvement Act** (proposed):

- Mandate MAT availability at all VA medical centers
- Expand COMPACT Act funding for community SUD prevention grants from $80M to $200M annually
- Authorize VA cannabis research for pain management and PTSD
- Extend TRICARE coverage for 12 months post-military separation

**Veteran Treatment Court Expansion Act** (proposed):

- Require veteran treatment court options in all 94 federal judicial districts
- Provide $100M in annual grants for state veteran treatment courts
- Mandate VA liaison officers in all veteran treatment courts
- Fund national evaluation of treatment court outcomes

### VA System Implementation

| Priority | Target Metric | Current Baseline | 2-Year Goal |
|----------|---------------|------------------|-------------|
| MAT access (72-hour initiation) | % of requests met | ~65% | 95% |
| Integrated PTSD-SUD treatment | VA facilities offering | 48 | 100 |
| Peer support specialists | Total employed | 1,800 | 2,500 |
| Naloxone kits distributed annually | Annual distribution | 130,000 | 250,000 |
| Veteran treatment courts | Number nationwide | 550 | 700 |

### Coalition Building

**Target Allies**:

- Veterans Service Organizations: American Legion, VFW, DAV, IAVA for legislative lobbying
- Medical professional organizations: American Medical Association, American Society of Addiction Medicine for clinical standards advocacy
- Bipartisan congressional champions: Leverage the historically bipartisan nature of veteran policy
- State governors: Expand Governor's Challenge to include substance abuse components

**Messaging Strategy**:

- Frame SUD treatment as fulfilling the nation's obligation to those who served
- Emphasize cost-effectiveness: treatment is cheaper than incarceration, emergency room visits, and lost productivity
- Use veteran peer advocates as spokespeople to reduce stigma
- Avoid partisan framing; maintain bipartisan appeal

## Phase 3: Medium-Term Reforms (2-5 Years)

### Structural Changes

**Discharge Status Reform**:

- Pass legislation granting full VA SUD treatment eligibility to veterans with OTH discharges where the discharge was related to substance use or mental health conditions
- Establish expedited discharge upgrade review boards with presumption of upgrade for substance/mental health-related cases
- Target: 50,000+ veterans reclassified or granted treatment eligibility within 3 years

**Pain Management Transformation**:

- Expand VA Whole Health programs from pilot facilities to all VA medical centers
- Integrate complementary and integrative health approaches (acupuncture, chiropractic, yoga, mindfulness) into standard pain management
- Implement stepped-care pain management protocols at all facilities
- Reduce opioid prescribing rates by an additional 25% while maintaining or improving patient-reported pain outcomes

**Community Prevention Infrastructure**:

- Scale COMPACT Act community grants to $300M annually
- Establish veteran peer recovery centers in the 100 communities with highest veteran overdose rates
- Fund community health worker positions specifically for veteran SUD outreach in underserved areas
- Create national veteran peer recovery support specialist certification program

## Phase 4: Long-Term Systemic Change (5-10 Years)

### Military Culture Reform

- Implement evidence-based alcohol pricing on military installations (eliminate below-market subsidies)
- Integrate substance use resilience training into military professional development at all career stages
- Develop unit-level substance use monitoring that triggers supportive interventions rather than punitive action
- Reduce military binge drinking rates by 30% within 10 years

### Healthcare System Integration

- Establish seamless data sharing between DOD, VA, and civilian healthcare systems for SUD treatment continuity
- Require military cultural competency certification for all civilian SUD treatment providers who receive federal funding
- Integrate veteran-specific screening questions into civilian emergency department protocols nationwide
- Create a national veteran health record that follows veterans across all treatment settings

### Research Priorities

| Research Area | Objective | Timeline |
|---------------|-----------|----------|
| Cannabis for veteran pain/PTSD | Controlled clinical trials within VA | 3-5 years |
| Psychedelic-assisted therapy | MDMA and psilocybin for PTSD-SUD | 3-7 years |
| Predictive analytics for SUD risk | Machine learning models using VA/DOD data | 2-5 years |
| Long-acting MAT formulations | Extended-release buprenorphine and naltrexone | 2-4 years |
| Telehealth treatment effectiveness | Comparative outcomes for in-person vs. telehealth SUD treatment | 2-3 years |

### Vision for 2035

A fully realized veteran substance abuse system would include:

- Zero veteran overdose deaths that are preventable through existing interventions
- Same-day treatment access for any veteran, at any location, regardless of enrollment status
- Military culture that does not normalize heavy drinking or punish help-seeking
- Community-based prevention networks in every state reaching veterans outside the VA
- Full integration of SUD treatment with mental health, pain management, and social services
- Research-driven adoption of emerging therapies including psychedelic-assisted treatment
- Veteran overdose death rate at or below the age-adjusted civilian rate

## Implementation Risks

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|--------|------------|
| Congressional budget cuts to VA SUD programs | Medium | High | Build bipartisan coalition; demonstrate cost-effectiveness |
| Provider workforce shortage limits expansion | High | High | Accelerate training; expand peer support and telehealth roles |
| Fentanyl supply intensifies faster than treatment capacity | High | Very High | Rapid naloxone scale-up; community prevention investment |
| Cultural resistance within VA slows implementation | Medium | Medium | Performance metrics tied to facility leadership evaluation |
| Political backlash against harm reduction | Medium | Medium | Frame as life-saving, not condoning use; veteran spokespeople |
| Data privacy concerns limit DOD-VA-civilian integration | Medium | Medium | Develop consent-based sharing frameworks with robust safeguards |
