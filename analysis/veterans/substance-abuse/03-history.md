# Veteran Substance Abuse: History

## The Military Drinking Tradition

### Colonial Era Through the Civil War

Alcohol has been intertwined with American military service since the nation's founding.

- **Revolutionary War**: Continental Army soldiers received daily rum rations of approximately 4 ounces, following British military tradition. George Washington himself operated a whiskey distillery at Mount Vernon
- **1790s-1830s**: The Army formalized daily whiskey rations at half a gill (approximately 2 ounces). Alcohol was considered essential for morale, as medicine, and as a safe alternative to potentially contaminated water
- **1832**: Congress officially ended the daily liquor ration, though the practice continued informally for decades
- **Civil War (1861-1865)**: Widespread alcohol and opium use. Surgeons administered morphine liberally for battlefield wounds, creating what was called "the soldier's disease"--opiate dependence among returning veterans. An estimated 400,000 Civil War veterans developed morphine dependence (Courtwright, *Dark Paradise: A History of Opiate Addiction in America*, 2001)

### World War I Through World War II

- **Prohibition Era (1920-1933)**: Military installations were officially dry, but enforcement was uneven. The Veterans Bureau (precursor to the VA) largely ignored substance abuse as a treatment need
- **World War II (1941-1945)**: Alcohol was a central feature of military culture. Beer was classified as a morale item and shipped to overseas theaters. The military provided amphetamines to combat fatigue among pilots and soldiers--approximately 200 million Benzedrine tablets were issued to U.S. military personnel during the war (Rasmussen, *On Speed*, 2008)
- **Post-WWII**: The newly created VA (1930) and its expanded postwar system treated alcoholism as a disciplinary problem rather than a medical condition, consistent with prevailing attitudes

### The Vietnam Era: A Turning Point

The Vietnam War (1964-1975) fundamentally transformed the relationship between military service and substance abuse.

**Heroin Use in Vietnam**

- Cheap, potent heroin was widely available in Southeast Asia. Estimates indicate that 10-15% of enlisted personnel in Vietnam used heroin regularly by 1971 (Robins et al., *Archives of General Psychiatry*, 1974)
- Marijuana use was even more widespread, with surveys indicating 50-60% of troops had used it in-theater
- The combination of combat stress, boredom, geographic availability, and the breakdown of unit cohesion in the later war years fueled widespread use

**Operation Golden Flow (1971-1972)**

- In response to public alarm, the Nixon administration implemented mandatory urinalysis testing for all military personnel returning from Vietnam
- Soldiers who tested positive were detained for treatment before being allowed to return to the United States
- The program identified approximately 5% of departing personnel as heroin-positive

**Robins Study: A Landmark Finding**

Lee Robins' 1974 study of returning Vietnam veterans produced one of the most important findings in addiction research:

- 20% of Vietnam veterans reported heroin addiction during service
- Only 5% remained addicted one year after returning home
- The finding demonstrated that environment, not just pharmacology, drives addiction--a conclusion that challenged the prevailing model of addiction as irreversible
- The study influenced subsequent understanding of addiction as context-dependent, though its implications were not fully incorporated into VA treatment for decades

*Source: Robins, L.N., et al. "Drug Use by U.S. Army Enlisted Men in Vietnam: A Follow-Up on Their Return Home." American Journal of Epidemiology 99, no. 4 (1974): 235-249.*

### Post-Vietnam Reforms (1975-1990)

- **1970**: Congress established the Alcohol and Drug Abuse Education Act, the first major federal investment in substance abuse education
- **1971**: President Nixon declared drug abuse "America's public enemy number one" and created the Special Action Office for Drug Abuse Prevention
- **1972**: The VA established its first dedicated alcohol treatment programs--prior to this, alcoholism was explicitly excluded from VA treatment eligibility in many facilities
- **1980**: The VA began developing specialized PTSD programs, but treatment for co-occurring PTSD and substance abuse remained fragmented
- **1986**: The military implemented comprehensive drug testing through the Military Drug and Alcohol Testing Program. Illicit drug use rates among active duty dropped dramatically, from an estimated 27% in 1980 to 5% by 1988 (DOD Survey of Health-Related Behaviors, 1988)
- **Zero-tolerance policies**: The military adopted strict zero-tolerance approaches to illicit drugs while largely tolerating heavy alcohol use, creating a split culture around substance use that persists today

## The Modern Era

### The War on Drugs and Veterans (1990-2001)

- VA substance abuse treatment remained underfunded throughout the 1990s, with bed capacity declining and outpatient programs limited
- The VA's policy framework continued to emphasize abstinence-based treatment, resisting the emerging evidence base for harm reduction and medication-assisted treatment
- Methadone maintenance, the gold standard for opioid addiction treatment since the 1960s, was available at very few VA facilities
- Gulf War veterans returning with undiagnosed PTSD and chronic pain received limited substance abuse screening

### The Opioid Epidemic and the VA (2001-2015)

The convergence of the post-9/11 wars and the national opioid epidemic created the current crisis.

**VA Opioid Prescribing Expansion**

- **2001-2010**: The VA, like civilian healthcare, dramatically increased opioid prescribing for chronic pain, responding to the "pain as the fifth vital sign" campaign promoted by the American Pain Society (1996) and later adopted by the Joint Commission
- VA opioid prescriptions rose from approximately 17% of pain patients in 2001 to 33% by 2012 (VA Academic Detailing Service, 2024)
- Veterans returning from Iraq and Afghanistan with combat injuries, chronic pain, and traumatic brain injuries were particularly likely to receive opioid prescriptions
- A 2011 VA Inspector General report found that one VA pharmacy alone dispensed more than 5 million oxycodone tablets in a single year

**The Reckoning (2012-2015)**

- **2012**: VA Secretary Eric Shinseki launched the Opioid Safety Initiative (OSI), the VA's comprehensive effort to reduce risky opioid prescribing
- **2013**: The VA began publishing facility-level opioid prescribing data for the first time, creating transparency and accountability
- **2014**: The VA implemented Academic Detailing--sending clinical pharmacists to educate VA prescribers about opioid risks and alternatives
- **2014**: Congress passed the Veterans Access to Care through Choice, Accountability, and Transparency (VACAAT) Act, partly in response to the wait-time scandal but also including provisions for opioid safety

### VA Reform and the MAT Revolution (2015-Present)

**Medication-Assisted Treatment Expansion**

- **2015**: VA leadership formally endorsed MAT as the standard of care for opioid use disorder, a significant cultural shift from abstinence-only approaches
- **2016**: The VA launched stepped-care models integrating MAT into primary care rather than requiring referral to specialty addiction programs
- **2017**: VA began training primary care providers to prescribe buprenorphine, dramatically expanding access
- **2018**: The VA MISSION Act consolidated community care authorities and allowed veterans to access SUD treatment from civilian providers when VA capacity was insufficient
- **2020**: COVID-19 pandemic prompted emergency regulations allowing telephone-initiated buprenorphine prescribing, which the VA subsequently made permanent

**Key Legislation**

| Year | Law | Impact on Veteran Substance Abuse |
|------|-----|-----------------------------------|
| 2016 | Comprehensive Addiction and Recovery Act (CARA) | Expanded VA MAT programs, naloxone distribution |
| 2017 | VA MISSION Act (precursor provisions) | Improved community care access for SUD treatment |
| 2018 | SUPPORT for Patients and Communities Act | Required VA to prescribe MAT for OUD, expanded peer support |
| 2020 | COMPACT Act | Authorized emergency SUD treatment at non-VA facilities |
| 2022 | Consolidated Appropriations Act (X-waiver elimination) | Removed buprenorphine prescribing barrier |

### Cultural Shifts

The past decade has seen meaningful but incomplete cultural change within both the VA and the broader veteran community:

- **From punishment to treatment**: The VA's formal posture has shifted from treating substance abuse as a disciplinary issue to treating it as a medical condition, though implementation varies by facility
- **From abstinence-only to harm reduction**: The VA's 2024 harm-reduction policy--endorsing naloxone, fentanyl test strips, and safe-use education--marked a significant departure from decades of abstinence-only ideology
- **From siloed to integrated care**: Treatment for co-occurring PTSD-SUD has expanded from a handful of specialized programs to broader adoption of integrated protocols such as Seeking Safety and COPE (Concurrent Treatment of PTSD and Substance Use Disorders Using Prolonged Exposure)
- **From shame to peer support**: The VA's employment of over 1,800 peer support specialists--veterans in recovery who provide mentorship and navigation--represents a growing recognition that lived experience is a clinical asset

### Unresolved Legacy Issues

Several historical patterns continue to shape the current crisis:

- **Drinking culture**: The military's normalization of heavy alcohol use has not been fundamentally addressed; alcohol remains the most misused substance among veterans
- **Pain management paradigm**: The pendulum has swung from over-prescribing to aggressive reduction, without a clear replacement strategy for managing the genuine chronic pain that affects millions of veterans
- **Delayed recognition**: The VA was slow to recognize substance abuse as a legitimate treatment need (alcohol not until the 1970s, opioid use disorder not until the 2010s), and this delay created generations of undertreated veterans
- **Zero tolerance paradox**: The military's zero-tolerance drug policy may deter active-duty use but also creates a culture where acknowledging substance problems is career-ending, a dynamic that follows veterans into civilian life
