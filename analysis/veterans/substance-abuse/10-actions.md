# Veteran Substance Abuse: Actions

## For Individuals

### Learn First

**Understand the Problem**

- [ ] Read [Current State](02-current-state.md) to understand the scope of veteran substance abuse
- [ ] Read [Root Causes](04-root-causes.md) to understand why veterans face elevated risk
- [ ] Learn about medication-assisted treatment and why it is the evidence-based standard of care
- [ ] Understand the difference between physical dependence, tolerance, and addiction

**Know the Signs**

- [ ] Learn to recognize signs of substance use disorders in veterans you know
- [ ] Understand the relationship between PTSD, chronic pain, and substance use
- [ ] Know that substance abuse in veterans often looks different than stereotypes suggest--it may present as isolation, irritability, sleep problems, or declining functioning rather than obvious intoxication

**Know the Resources**

- [ ] Save the Veterans Crisis Line number: 988 (press 1)
- [ ] Locate your nearest VA medical center and Vet Center (see [Resources](09-resources.md))
- [ ] Learn about SAMHSA's National Helpline: 1-800-662-4357
- [ ] Find out if your area has a veteran treatment court

### Take Action

**If You Are a Veteran**

- [ ] If you are struggling with substance use, know that SUDs are medical conditions, not moral failures
- [ ] Contact VA SUD treatment at your nearest VA medical center or call 1-800-698-2411
- [ ] Ask your VA primary care provider about screening for substance use disorders
- [ ] If you have opioid use disorder, ask about medication-assisted treatment (buprenorphine, naltrexone)
- [ ] Request a naloxone kit from your VA pharmacy--it could save your life or a fellow veteran's
- [ ] Connect with peer support specialists who have lived experience with recovery
- [ ] If you have an other-than-honorable discharge, you may still be eligible for SUD treatment--contact VA eligibility at 1-877-222-8387

**If You Are a Family Member**

- [ ] Learn about Al-Anon and family support resources for substance use disorders
- [ ] Encourage your veteran family member to seek help without ultimatums or judgment
- [ ] Learn to administer naloxone if your family member uses opioids
- [ ] Seek your own support through VA caregiver support (1-855-260-3274) or civilian counseling
- [ ] Understand that enabling and supporting are different: helping someone avoid consequences is different from helping them access treatment
- [ ] Contact the VA Caregiver Support Line for resources specific to veteran families

**Contact Representatives**

- [ ] Call your U.S. senators and representative to support expanded VA SUD treatment funding
- [ ] Urge support for veteran treatment court expansion legislation
- [ ] Advocate for discharge status reform to extend VA eligibility to veterans with OTH discharges
- [ ] Support legislation authorizing VA cannabis research for pain and PTSD
- [ ] Attend town halls and ask about veteran substance abuse policy

**Join the Movement**

- [ ] Join a Veterans Service Organization (American Legion, VFW, DAV, IAVA) that advocates for SUD treatment
- [ ] Volunteer with veteran peer support organizations
- [ ] Support The Phoenix or similar sober active community organizations
- [ ] Follow organizations like Shatterproof, Faces and Voices of Recovery, or Justice for Vets
- [ ] Share educational content about veteran substance abuse to reduce stigma

### Reduce Stigma

**In Your Community**

- [ ] Use person-first language: say "veteran with a substance use disorder," not "addicted veteran"
- [ ] Challenge the idea that substance abuse is a choice or moral failing
- [ ] Share veteran recovery stories (with permission) to normalize help-seeking
- [ ] Speak up when you hear myths about MAT ("replacing one addiction with another")
- [ ] Recognize that recovery is not one-size-fits-all; abstinence is one valid path among several

## For Communities

### Build Local Support

**Establish or Support Veteran Peer Recovery Programs**

- [ ] Partner with VA to create community-based veteran recovery centers
- [ ] Apply for COMPACT Act grants for veteran substance abuse prevention
- [ ] Train community members in naloxone administration through local health departments
- [ ] Create veteran-friendly spaces at community recovery events and meetings
- [ ] Develop veteran-specific support groups at existing community recovery organizations

**Employer Actions**

- [ ] Adopt Employee Assistance Programs (EAPs) that include SUD treatment coverage
- [ ] Train managers to recognize signs of substance abuse and respond with support rather than punishment
- [ ] Offer flexible scheduling for employees in recovery who need treatment appointments
- [ ] Partner with VA vocational rehabilitation for veteran employee support
- [ ] Adopt policies that do not penalize employees for seeking SUD treatment

**Faith-Based Communities**

- [ ] Offer meeting space for veteran support groups and 12-step programs
- [ ] Train clergy in recognizing veteran substance abuse and appropriate referral
- [ ] Partner with VA chaplaincy services
- [ ] Provide nonjudgmental support that treats SUDs as health conditions, not spiritual failings
- [ ] Host naloxone training events for congregation members

### Local Government Actions

- [ ] Establish or expand veteran treatment courts in your jurisdiction
- [ ] Fund community health workers specifically for veteran outreach
- [ ] Ensure local Medicaid programs cover SUD treatment for veterans not enrolled in VA
- [ ] Include veteran substance abuse in community health needs assessments
- [ ] Train first responders (police, EMS, fire) to recognize veterans in crisis and connect them to VA resources

## For Organizations

### Veterans Service Organizations

- [ ] Integrate SUD screening and referral into benefits counseling services
- [ ] Train post and chapter leadership on substance abuse recognition and destigmatization
- [ ] Partner with VA for naloxone distribution at VSO events
- [ ] Include substance abuse recovery stories in organizational publications
- [ ] Advocate legislatively for expanded SUD treatment funding and eligibility

### Healthcare Organizations

- [ ] Screen all patients for military service history using the "Have you ever served in the military?" question
- [ ] Train providers in military cultural competency through PsychArmor Institute or similar programs
- [ ] Establish referral pathways to VA SUD treatment for veteran patients
- [ ] Participate in VA Community Care Network to provide SUD treatment to referred veterans
- [ ] Train emergency department staff to offer naloxone and MAT initiation to veterans presenting with opioid-related emergencies

### Legal and Justice Organizations

- [ ] Support establishment of veteran treatment courts in your jurisdiction
- [ ] Provide pro bono legal services for veterans seeking discharge upgrades
- [ ] Advocate for alternatives to incarceration for veterans with substance-related offenses
- [ ] Train public defenders and prosecutors on veteran-specific diversion options
- [ ] Connect incarcerated veterans with VA healthcare enrollment and benefits counseling before release

## For Policymakers

### Federal Level

- [ ] Fund VA SUD treatment at levels necessary to close the 51% treatment gap
- [ ] Expand COMPACT Act grants to $300 million annually for community-based prevention
- [ ] Pass legislation mandating veteran treatment courts in all federal judicial districts
- [ ] Reform discharge characterization policy to extend VA eligibility for substance-related OTH discharges
- [ ] Authorize and fund VA cannabis research for pain management and PTSD
- [ ] Extend TRICARE coverage for 12 months post-military separation to bridge the insurance gap
- [ ] Mandate integrated PTSD-SUD treatment at all VA medical centers

### State Level

- [ ] Ensure state Medicaid programs cover SUD treatment for veterans not enrolled in VA
- [ ] Establish or expand state veteran treatment courts with dedicated funding
- [ ] Include veteran substance abuse in state veteran suicide prevention plans (Governor's Challenge)
- [ ] Fund state veteran peer recovery programs
- [ ] Adopt Good Samaritan laws protecting individuals (including veterans) who call for help during overdoses
- [ ] Ensure naloxone is available without prescription at state pharmacies

### Local Level

- [ ] Fund community-based veteran recovery support services
- [ ] Train local law enforcement in veteran-specific crisis intervention techniques
- [ ] Include veteran-specific resources in community overdose response plans
- [ ] Support housing programs that accept veterans in recovery (Housing First model)
- [ ] Ensure local zoning does not prohibit SUD treatment facilities in residential areas

## Measuring Progress

### Individual Impact

- Track whether veterans you know are connected to appropriate treatment
- Monitor local veteran treatment court activity and outcomes
- Observe whether stigma around veteran substance abuse is decreasing in your community

### Community Impact

| Metric | How to Measure |
|--------|---------------|
| Local veteran overdose deaths | Request data from county coroner/medical examiner |
| Veteran treatment court enrollment | Contact local court administration |
| Naloxone distribution | Track through local health department |
| VA SUD treatment wait times | Check accesstocare.va.gov |

### National Impact

| Metric | Source | Current | Target |
|--------|--------|---------|--------|
| Veteran overdose deaths | CDC WONDER | 5,671 (2022) | <3,500 by 2030 |
| Treatment gap | SAMHSA/VA | 51% | <25% by 2030 |
| MAT enrollment | VA Pharmacy | 68,000 | 140,000 by 2030 |
| Treatment courts | NADCP | 550 | 1,000 by 2030 |
