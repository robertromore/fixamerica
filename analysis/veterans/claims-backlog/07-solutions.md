# VA Claims Backlog: Solutions

## Overview

Solving the VA claims backlog requires addressing multiple root causes simultaneously. No single reform will eliminate the backlog; a comprehensive approach combining process redesign, technology modernization, workforce investment, and structural changes is necessary. The solutions below are organized by category and assessed for impact, feasibility, and evidence base.

---

## Process Reforms

### 1. Mandatory Capacity Planning for Eligibility Expansions

**Description**: Require all legislation expanding VA benefits eligibility to include a capacity impact assessment and mandatory appropriations for processing.

**How It Works**:

- Congressional Budget Office (CBO) would be required to estimate claims volume impact for any eligibility expansion
- Appropriations for processing capacity would be mandatory (not discretionary) and linked to the authorization
- The VA would be required to submit a staffing and technology plan within 90 days of enactment
- Funding would be available immediately upon enactment, not subject to annual appropriations delays

**Evidence**: The PACT Act's claims surge was predictable; CBO estimated 3.5 million potential new claims, but processing capacity funding was not secured until 18 months later (CBO Score, PACT Act, 2022; CRS, 2024).

**Impact**: High -- would prevent future eligibility-driven backlog surges
**Feasibility**: Moderate -- requires changing Congressional budgeting practices
**Timeline**: Could be implemented for future legislation immediately

### 2. Presumptive Approval for Straightforward Claims

**Description**: Automatically approve claims that meet clearly defined criteria without requiring individualized medical examination.

**How It Works**:

- Define categories of claims where service connection is well-established (e.g., hearing loss for infantry, PTSD for combat veterans with documented deployments)
- Claims meeting defined criteria receive automatic presumptive approval at a baseline rating
- Veterans can request full evaluation if they believe their condition warrants a higher rating
- Quality audits sample approved claims to verify the presumption was correctly applied

**Evidence**: The VA's Decision Ready Claims (DRC) program, which processes fully developed claims on an expedited basis, demonstrated that pre-developed claims can be processed 40% faster with comparable accuracy (VA DRC Program Evaluation, 2023).

**Impact**: High -- could remove 20-30% of claims from the standard processing pipeline
**Feasibility**: High -- builds on existing presumptive framework
**Timeline**: 12-18 months for regulatory implementation

### 3. Reduce Rework Through "Right First Time" Initiative

**Description**: Redesign the initial claims process to maximize the likelihood of a correct decision on the first attempt.

**How It Works**:

- Enhanced intake process that identifies evidence gaps before claims enter the processing queue
- Pre-submission evidence development assistance (currently offered but underutilized)
- Standardized evidence checklists for common condition types
- Quality review at the intake stage rather than only at the decision stage
- Feedback loops connecting appeals outcomes to initial rating guidance

**Evidence**: The VA OIG estimated rework accounts for 45% of total claims workload; reducing the rework rate by 50% would be equivalent to hiring 6,000 additional claims processors (VA OIG Report 23-01847-189, 2024).

**Impact**: Very high -- reducing rework is the single highest-leverage intervention
**Feasibility**: Moderate -- requires process redesign and cultural change
**Timeline**: 2-3 years for full implementation

### 4. Streamlined Medical Examination Process

**Description**: Reduce the time and complexity of Compensation and Pension examinations.

**How It Works**:

- Accept existing medical evidence (VA treatment records, private physician statements) in lieu of C&P examinations where sufficient
- Implement telehealth C&P examinations for conditions that can be assessed remotely (mental health, many internal conditions)
- Consolidate multiple examination requests into single appointments
- Create performance-based contracts for examination vendors that tie payment to quality and timeliness
- Establish maximum turnaround times (30 days) with penalties for delays

**Evidence**: The VA estimated that 35% of C&P examinations could have been decided based on existing medical evidence (VA Benefits Administration Medical Evidence Analysis, 2023). Telehealth C&P exams piloted during COVID-19 showed comparable accuracy for mental health conditions (VA Health Services Research, 2022).

**Impact**: High -- medical examination is the largest single processing bottleneck
**Feasibility**: High -- builds on COVID-19 telehealth experience
**Timeline**: 12-18 months

## Technology Solutions

### 5. AI-Assisted Claims Triage and Decision Support

**Description**: Deploy artificial intelligence to sort claims by complexity, suggest evidence development needs, and recommend ratings for straightforward conditions.

**How It Works**:

- Machine learning models trained on historical claims data identify claim complexity at intake
- Simple claims (e.g., hearing loss increase with recent audiogram) are routed for expedited processing
- Complex claims (e.g., multiple conditions, toxic exposure, mental health) receive enhanced development resources
- AI recommends ratings for review by human adjudicators, not replacing human decision-making
- Algorithmic decisions are transparent, auditable, and subject to veteran challenge

**Evidence**: The VA's Automated Decision Support pilot demonstrated 30-40% time savings for hypertension and hearing loss claims (VA OIT, Automated Decision Support Evaluation, 2024). The Social Security Administration's use of AI for disability screening provides a comparable federal model.

**Impact**: High -- could reduce per-claim processing time by 25-35% for suitable claim types
**Feasibility**: Moderate -- requires careful validation, bias testing, and transparency safeguards
**Timeline**: 2-3 years for full deployment

### 6. Integrated Evidence Platform

**Description**: Create a single platform that automatically aggregates all relevant evidence for a claim from multiple sources.

**How It Works**:

- Direct digital integration with DOD personnel records, VA health records, and Social Security Administration
- Automated records requests to private medical providers through health information exchanges
- Single evidence package assembled before the claim reaches an adjudicator
- Status tracking visible to the veteran, their representative, and the adjudicator

**Evidence**: Evidence development accounts for approximately 60% of total processing time (VA Benefits Administration process analysis, 2023). Automated evidence aggregation could reduce this by 40-50%.

**Impact**: Very high -- addresses the single largest time component of claims processing
**Feasibility**: Moderate -- requires interagency data sharing agreements and technology investment
**Timeline**: 3-5 years for full implementation

### 7. Modern Claims Processing Platform

**Description**: Replace or significantly modernize VBMS with a platform built for current and projected workload.

**How It Works**:

- Cloud-based architecture capable of scaling with demand
- Built-in decision support tools and automated workflow management
- Real-time analytics for workload management and quality monitoring
- Mobile-accessible interface for veterans and representatives
- API-based integration with external evidence sources

**Evidence**: VBMS was designed in the early 2010s and has been retrofitted repeatedly; it was not built for the volume or complexity of post-PACT Act claims processing (VA OIT, 2024).

**Impact**: High -- infrastructure enabling multiple other improvements
**Feasibility**: Low-moderate -- major IT projects have a poor track record in federal government
**Timeline**: 5-7 years

## Workforce Solutions

### 8. Sustainable Workforce Model

**Description**: Build a claims processing workforce sized for sustained demand rather than crisis-driven surges.

**How It Works**:

- Establish a permanent workforce floor based on projected claims volume plus a 20% surge buffer
- Create a reserve corps of trained adjudicators who can be activated during surges (similar to military reserve model)
- Implement multi-year hiring plans not subject to annual appropriations uncertainty
- Reduce the training timeline through improved curriculum, simulation-based training, and mentoring programs
- Competitive compensation to reduce turnover (currently 13-14%)

**Evidence**: The VA's hiring surge in FY 2023-2024 added net only 400 employees after accounting for departures, demonstrating that reactive hiring cannot keep pace with attrition (VA OIG Workforce Analysis, 2024).

**Impact**: High -- addresses the fundamental workforce pipeline limitation
**Feasibility**: Moderate -- requires sustained funding commitment
**Timeline**: 3-5 years for full implementation

### 9. Specialized Claims Processing Teams

**Description**: Create dedicated teams specializing in specific claim types rather than expecting all adjudicators to master all 800+ diagnostic codes.

**How It Works**:

- Organize processing teams by body system (musculoskeletal, mental health, respiratory, cardiovascular, etc.)
- Adjudicators develop deep expertise in a subset of conditions rather than superficial knowledge of all conditions
- Route claims to the appropriate specialized team based on condition type
- Cross-train team leads to handle multi-condition claims or establish coordination protocols

**Evidence**: Specialization models have improved accuracy and speed in other adjudication contexts, including Social Security disability and workers' compensation (National Academy of Social Insurance, 2022).

**Impact**: Moderate-high -- would improve both accuracy and speed for specialized conditions
**Feasibility**: High -- can be implemented within existing organizational structure
**Timeline**: 12-24 months

## Structural Reforms

### 10. Independent Claims Processing Quality Agency

**Description**: Create an independent entity responsible for monitoring and enforcing claims processing quality, separate from the organization responsible for processing volume.

**How It Works**:

- Independent quality review board (not part of VBA management) conducts regular accuracy audits
- Review sample size increased from 3% to 10%+ of claims
- Regional offices and individual adjudicators receive specific feedback on errors
- Systemic error patterns trigger mandatory corrective action
- Annual public reporting on accuracy by office, condition type, and adjudicator experience level

**Evidence**: The current STAR accuracy review program samples too few claims to detect patterns and is administered by the same organization it evaluates, creating conflicts of interest (VA OIG Report 22-02122-153, 2023).

**Impact**: Moderate -- would improve accuracy and reduce rework over time
**Feasibility**: Moderate -- requires organizational change and possibly legislation
**Timeline**: 2-3 years

### 11. Mandatory Processing Timelines with Accountability

**Description**: Establish legally binding maximum processing times with consequences for failure.

**How It Works**:

- Statutory maximum of 90 days for initial claims, 45 days for supplemental claims, and 180 days for Board appeals
- If the VA fails to decide within the timeline, automatic provisional approval at the claimed rating level, subject to later verification
- VA Secretary required to report to Congress quarterly on compliance rates
- Individual employees protected from accountability for systemic capacity failures; accountability directed at organizational leadership

**Evidence**: Mandatory timelines with consequences have been effective in other contexts (e.g., the Speedy Trial Act for federal criminal cases; IDEA timelines for special education evaluations).

**Impact**: Very high -- creates structural incentive for capacity investment
**Feasibility**: Low -- strong VA institutional opposition; provisional approval is controversial
**Timeline**: Requires legislation; 2-3 year implementation

### 12. Rating Schedule Modernization

**Description**: Simplify and modernize the VA Schedule for Rating Disabilities (VASRD) to reduce adjudication complexity.

**How It Works**:

- Replace granular percentage ratings with broader severity bands (e.g., mild/moderate/severe) for appropriate conditions
- Update diagnostic criteria to align with current medical knowledge and ICD-11 codes
- Eliminate outdated conditions and add codes for conditions common in post-9/11 veterans
- Reduce the number of subjective judgment calls through clearer, more objective criteria
- Maintain veteran-favorable interpretations in ambiguous cases

**Evidence**: The VA began a comprehensive VASRD update in 2018 and has revised several body systems, but the process is slow and incomplete (38 CFR Part 4 revision history, 2018-2024).

**Impact**: High long-term -- simplification reduces errors, training time, and processing time
**Feasibility**: Low -- politically sensitive; any change creates winners and losers
**Timeline**: 5-10 years for comprehensive revision
