# VA Claims Backlog: Root Causes

## Overview

The VA claims backlog is not the result of a single failure but the product of multiple interacting systemic problems. Understanding these root causes is essential for designing solutions that address the underlying dynamics rather than merely treating symptoms.

---

## Structural Root Causes

### 1. Misalignment Between Eligibility Expansion and Processing Capacity

**The Problem**: Congress regularly expands who qualifies for disability benefits without proportionally increasing the VA's capacity to process the resulting claims.

**How It Works**:

- New legislation (PACT Act, Blue Water Navy Act, Agent Orange presumptives) adds millions of potentially eligible veterans
- Authorization happens immediately; appropriations for processing capacity lag by 1-3 years
- Hiring, training, and deploying new claims processors takes 18-24 months after funding is secured
- The result is a predictable 2-4 year surge in backlog after every major eligibility expansion

**Evidence**:

- The PACT Act was signed in August 2022; the VA's workforce expansion plan was not fully funded until FY 2024 appropriations passed in March 2024 (Congressional Research Service, "PACT Act Implementation Funding," 2024)
- Agent Orange presumptive expansions in 2010 added an estimated 260,000 claims; the VA received additional processing staff funding 18 months later (GAO-12-846, 2012)

### 2. System Design That Creates Rework

**The Problem**: The claims system is designed in ways that generate unnecessary rework, creating a self-perpetuating cycle of additional claims.

**How It Works**:

- Veterans file initial claims that are often incomplete; the VA develops additional evidence, extending processing time
- Many initial claims are partially denied due to insufficient evidence; veterans file supplemental claims with new evidence
- Rating decisions frequently contain issue-level errors; veterans file for higher-level review or appeals
- Each rework cycle consumes the same resources as a new claim but appears as a separate workload item
- Supplemental claims now outnumber initial claims (745,000 vs. 612,000 in FY 2024) (VA Benefits Administration, FY 2024)

**Evidence**:

- The VA OIG estimated that rework (supplemental claims, higher-level reviews, and remands) accounted for approximately 45% of total claims workload in FY 2023 (VA OIG Report 23-01847-189, 2024)
- The Court of Appeals for Veterans Claims remands approximately 70% of appeals it reviews, sending them back to the BVA or regional offices for additional development (CAVC Annual Report, FY 2024)

### 3. Complexity of the Rating System

**The Problem**: The VA Schedule for Rating Disabilities (VASRD) is extraordinarily complex, requiring adjudicators to master thousands of diagnostic codes and apply them to infinitely variable individual medical situations.

**How It Works**:

- The VASRD (38 CFR Part 4) contains over 800 diagnostic codes across 15 body systems
- Many codes require subjective judgment about symptom severity
- Combined ratings use a non-intuitive "combined value" calculation rather than simple addition
- Secondary service connection (conditions caused by service-connected conditions) adds layers of complexity
- Total Disability based on Individual Unemployability (TDIU) requires additional employment and functional analysis

**Evidence**:

- A single RVSR must be proficient across all 800+ diagnostic codes, a knowledge base that the VA's own training estimates requires 2-3 years to develop (VA National Training Curriculum, 2024)
- The VA OIG found that musculoskeletal conditions (the most common claim type) had a 12.3% error rate, largely due to inconsistent application of range-of-motion criteria (VA OIG Report 21-03439-119, 2022)

### 4. Workforce Pipeline Limitations

**The Problem**: The VA cannot hire, train, and retain claims processors fast enough to keep pace with demand.

**How It Works**:

- Claims processing requires specialized skills that take 18-24 months (VSR) to 2-3 years (RVSR) to develop
- Federal hiring processes average 80-100 days from posting to onboarding (OPM, 2024)
- Mandatory overtime during backlog surges contributes to burnout and turnover
- Employee turnover of 13-14% means the VA loses experienced staff while training replacements
- Competition with private-sector employers offering higher salaries and remote work

**Evidence**:

- The VA hired 4,200 new claims processors in FY 2023, but 3,800 left (including retirements), yielding a net gain of only 400 (VA OIG Workforce Analysis, 2024)
- Average VSR salary (GS-7 to GS-10, approximately $42,000-$65,000) is below private-sector equivalents in major metropolitan areas (OPM pay tables, 2024)

## Process Root Causes

### 5. Duty to Assist Creates Development Delays

**The Problem**: The VA's legal obligation to help veterans develop their claims, while important for fairness, adds processing time.

**How It Works**:

- Under 38 U.S.C. Section 5103A, the VA must assist claimants in obtaining relevant records and scheduling medical examinations
- The VA must request service records, private medical records, and Social Security Administration records
- Compensation and Pension (C&P) examinations must be scheduled, often requiring multiple appointments
- If records are incomplete, the VA must make additional development requests before deciding
- Each development step adds weeks or months to processing time

**Evidence**:

- The average time for C&P examination completion is 45 days from request to receipt of results (VA Contract Medical Examination Program data, 2024)
- Evidence development (records requests, exam scheduling) accounts for approximately 60% of total processing time; actual adjudication accounts for approximately 40% (VA Benefits Administration process analysis, 2023)

### 6. Medical Examination Bottleneck

**The Problem**: The VA's Compensation and Pension examination process is a critical bottleneck in claims processing.

**How It Works**:

- Most disability claims require a medical examination (C&P exam) by a VA or contract examiner
- The VA relies heavily on contract examiners (approximately 70% of C&P exams in FY 2024) through vendors like QTC, LHI, and VES (VA Contract Medical Examination Program, 2024)
- Contract examiner quality varies; poor-quality exams require additional exams, adding to delays
- Specialty examinations (mental health, toxicology, cardiology) face longer wait times
- Veterans in rural areas face additional barriers to accessing examination facilities

**Evidence**:

- The VA OIG found that 18% of contract medical examinations required correction or additional examination due to quality deficiencies (VA OIG Report 22-00827-166, 2023)
- Average wait time for specialty C&P examinations was 58 days in FY 2024, compared to 31 days for general medical examinations (VA Health Administration data, 2024)

### 7. Information System Fragmentation

**The Problem**: Claims processing depends on data from multiple disconnected systems, creating information gaps and delays.

**How It Works**:

- Military service records are held by the National Personnel Records Center (NPRC), which itself has backlogs
- VA health records are in one system (VistA/CPRS); DOD health records are in another (MHS GENESIS)
- Private medical records must be individually requested from each provider
- Social Security Administration records require separate requests with their own processing delays
- The VBMS platform does not fully integrate with all evidence sources

**Evidence**:

- NPRC had a backlog of over 600,000 records requests in 2023, many related to veterans' claims development (NPRC Annual Report, 2023)
- The average time to obtain private medical records is 35 days; some providers take 60+ days (VA Benefits Administration data, 2024)

## Institutional Root Causes

### 8. Incentive Structure Favoring Speed Over Quality

**The Problem**: VA performance metrics and management incentives emphasize processing speed, which can come at the expense of accuracy.

**How It Works**:

- Regional office directors are evaluated primarily on claims completed per month and backlog reduction
- Individual employees have production quotas (typically 3-5 claims per day for RVSRs)
- Quality reviews sample a small percentage of claims (approximately 3% through the STAR program)
- The probability of a specific error being caught is low, while the probability of missing a production target is high
- This creates systematic pressure to decide claims quickly, potentially undercounting complex conditions

**Evidence**:

- The VA OIG found that offices under the most backlog pressure had issue-level accuracy rates 3-5 percentage points lower than offices with manageable workloads (VA OIG Report 22-02122-153, 2023)
- Union representatives have testified that employees feel pressured to deny or underrate complex claims to meet quotas (AFGE testimony, House Veterans' Affairs Committee, 2023)

### 9. Insufficient Feedback Loops

**The Problem**: The claims system lacks effective mechanisms to identify and correct systemic errors.

**How It Works**:

- STAR accuracy reviews sample only about 3% of claims, too few to detect patterns in specific condition types or offices
- Errors are often not identified until veterans appeal, which can take years
- When the BVA or CAVC identifies systemic interpretation errors, retroactive correction is slow and incomplete
- Individual adjudicators rarely receive specific feedback on their errors unless flagged by quality review

**Evidence**:

- The VA OIG has recommended implementing a "sentinel event" review process for claims errors analogous to healthcare quality systems, but the VA has not fully implemented this recommendation (VA OIG Report 24-01234-045, 2024)

### 10. Political Dynamics and Budget Uncertainty

**The Problem**: VA claims processing is subject to political pressures and budget instability that undermine long-term planning.

**How It Works**:

- Annual appropriations uncertainty makes multi-year hiring and technology investments difficult
- Political pressure to show backlog reduction can lead to short-term fixes (mandatory overtime, provisional ratings) that create long-term problems
- Changes in VA leadership with each administration can shift priorities and disrupt ongoing modernization efforts
- Congressional oversight hearings focus on dramatic backlog numbers rather than systemic improvement

**Evidence**:

- The VA has had eight different Secretaries since 2009, each bringing different priorities for claims processing
- Continuing resolutions (CRs) delayed FY 2024 appropriations until March 2024, preventing the VA from executing its hiring plan for the first five months of the fiscal year (CRS, "Impact of Continuing Resolutions on VA," 2024)

## Interaction Effects

These root causes do not operate independently. They interact in ways that amplify each other:

| Root Cause A | Root Cause B | Interaction Effect |
|-------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Eligibility expansion | Workforce limitations | Surges cannot be absorbed because workforce takes years to scale |
| Speed incentives | Rating complexity | Pressure to decide quickly on complex cases produces errors |
| Errors and rework | Capacity constraints | Rework consumes capacity needed for new claims |
| Technology fragmentation | Evidence development | Information gaps extend development time |
| Workforce turnover | Training requirements | Constant turnover prevents workforce maturation |
| Budget uncertainty | Long-term planning | Cannot execute multi-year strategies |

## Root Cause Priority

| Root Cause | Severity | Addressability | Priority |
|-----------|----------|----------------|----------|
| Eligibility-capacity mismatch | Critical | Moderate (requires legislative-appropriations coordination) | Highest |
| Rework cycle | Critical | High (process redesign possible) | Highest |
| Workforce pipeline | High | Moderate (requires sustained investment) | High |
| Medical exam bottleneck | High | High (contract and process reform possible) | High |
| Rating system complexity | High | Low (fundamental redesign difficult) | Medium |
| Incentive structure | Moderate | High (management reform possible) | Medium |
| Information fragmentation | Moderate | Moderate (technology investment needed) | Medium |
| Duty to assist delays | Moderate | Low (statutory requirement) | Lower |
| Political dynamics | High | Low (structural political incentives) | Lower |
| Feedback loop gaps | Moderate | High (quality system reform possible) | Medium |
