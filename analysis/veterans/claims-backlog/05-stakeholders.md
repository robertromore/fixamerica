# VA Claims Backlog: Stakeholders

## Overview

The VA claims backlog affects a broad ecosystem of individuals, organizations, and institutions. Understanding stakeholder interests, power, and relationships is essential for identifying viable reform coalitions and anticipating resistance.

---

## Primary Stakeholders

### Veterans and Their Families

**Who They Are**: Approximately 16.5 million living veterans (Census Bureau, 2024) and their dependents. Of these, approximately 6.2 million receive disability compensation, and hundreds of thousands have claims pending at any time.

**Interests**:

- Timely processing of disability claims (financial stability depends on compensation)
- Accurate rating decisions that reflect true severity of service-connected conditions
- Transparent status updates during the claims process
- Fair and consistent outcomes regardless of regional office or examiner
- Access to healthcare and other benefits that depend on disability rating

**Power and Influence**:

- Individually, veterans have limited influence over the claims system
- Collectively, veterans are a politically powerful constituency; "supporting the troops" is one of the few truly bipartisan positions
- Veterans who are vocal about claims problems receive significant media attention
- However, many veterans, especially older and rural veterans, are not organized or connected to advocacy networks

**Impact of the Backlog**:

- Financial hardship during waiting periods, especially for veterans who left the military recently
- Delayed access to VA healthcare (some benefits require a disability rating)
- Mental health deterioration from uncertainty and frustration
- The VA has documented cases of veterans who died before their claims were resolved (VA OIG, 2023)
- Family members bear secondary impacts: caregiving burden, financial stress, relationship strain

### Veterans Service Organizations (VSOs)

**Who They Are**: Congressionally chartered organizations that assist veterans with claims filing and advocacy. Major VSOs include:

| Organization | Membership | Claims Assistance Volume (FY 2024) |
|-------------|------------|--------------------------------------|
| American Legion | 1.6 million | ~350,000 claims filed |
| Veterans of Foreign Wars (VFW) | 1.5 million | ~310,000 claims filed |
| Disabled American Veterans (DAV) | 1.1 million | ~275,000 claims filed |
| AMVETS | 250,000 | ~85,000 claims filed |
| Vietnam Veterans of America (VVA) | 85,000 | ~45,000 claims filed |
| Paralyzed Veterans of America (PVA) | 18,000 | ~22,000 claims filed |

*Source: VSO annual reports and VA Office of General Counsel accreditation data, FY 2024.*

**Interests**:

- Maintaining their role as veterans' primary claims representatives
- Ensuring the VA adequately staffs and funds claims processing
- Preserving veterans' due process rights in the claims system
- Organizational relevance and membership recruitment (claims assistance is a key member benefit)
- Influencing VA policy through their advisory committee roles

**Power and Influence**:

- VSOs have significant lobbying power on Capitol Hill; their support or opposition can determine legislative outcomes
- VSO representatives are accredited by the VA and have access to claims systems and regional offices
- VSOs testify regularly before Congressional committees and are consulted on VA policy changes
- They serve on the VA's Advisory Committee on Disability Compensation

### VA Benefits Administration (VBA) Workforce

**Who They Are**: Approximately 28,600 claims processing employees, including Veterans Service Representatives (VSRs), Rating Veterans Service Representatives (RVSRs), and support staff across 56 regional offices.

**Interests**:

- Manageable workloads and reasonable production expectations
- Adequate training and professional development
- Competitive compensation and benefits
- Job security and career advancement opportunities
- Being able to do quality work without being pressured to rush

**Power and Influence**:

- Represented by the American Federation of Government Employees (AFGE), which negotiates working conditions
- Frontline workers can affect processing speed and quality through their daily decisions
- High turnover rates give workers leverage in tight labor markets
- AFGE advocacy brings internal VBA concerns to Congressional attention

**Impact of the Backlog**:

- Mandatory overtime is common during backlog surges, contributing to burnout
- Pressure to meet production quotas can compromise quality
- New employees face steep learning curves with inadequate mentoring due to experienced staff being too busy
- Morale issues when the workforce is blamed for backlogs caused by systemic factors

## Secondary Stakeholders

### Congress

**Who They Are**: The House and Senate Veterans' Affairs Committees, as well as the Appropriations subcommittees that fund the VA.

**Interests**:

- Demonstrating responsiveness to veteran constituents
- Passing legislation that expands veteran benefits (politically popular)
- Holding the VA accountable for performance
- Managing the federal budget impact of expanding benefits
- Conducting oversight hearings that generate media coverage

**Power and Influence**:

- Controls VBA's annual appropriations (currently approximately $3.8 billion)
- Can pass legislation expanding or restricting eligibility and processing requirements
- Oversight hearings can pressure VA leadership to prioritize backlog reduction
- Confirmation authority over VA Secretary and key appointees

**Contradictions**:

- Congress frequently expands eligibility without adequately funding processing capacity
- Oversight hearings criticize the VA for backlogs created by Congress's own legislation
- Budget hawks resist the appropriations needed to hire and train additional staff
- Members want credit for expanding benefits but not responsibility for the resulting backlog

### VA Leadership (Secretary and Under Secretary for Benefits)

**Who They Are**: The Secretary of Veterans Affairs and the Under Secretary for Benefits, who lead the Department and VBA respectively.

**Interests**:

- Meeting performance targets and reducing the backlog
- Implementing legislative mandates (PACT Act, etc.)
- Managing public perception and Congressional relationships
- Modernizing technology and processes
- Balancing competing priorities across the VA's three major administrations (Health, Benefits, Cemetery)

**Power and Influence**:

- Direct authority over VBA operations, staffing, and priorities
- Budget formulation authority for annual budget requests
- Can implement process changes and technology investments
- Subject to Presidential appointment and Congressional confirmation
- Limited by Civil Service protections, union agreements, and appropriations

### Contract Medical Examination Providers

**Who They Are**: Private companies that conduct Compensation and Pension (C&P) examinations under contract with the VA. Major contractors include QTC Management (Leidos), Logistics Health Incorporated (LHI), and Veterans Evaluation Services (VES).

**Interests**:

- Maintaining and expanding government contracts (estimated $1.5 billion annually)
- Meeting volume and quality metrics
- Recruiting and retaining qualified medical examiners
- Minimizing contract penalties and performance deductions

**Power and Influence**:

- Conduct approximately 70% of C&P examinations (VA Contract Medical Examination Program, 2024)
- Quality of examinations directly affects claims processing speed and accuracy
- Lobbying for contract renewals and favorable terms
- Can create bottlenecks if capacity is insufficient

## Institutional Stakeholders

### Board of Veterans' Appeals (BVA)

**Role**: Adjudicates appeals of VBA rating decisions. The BVA has approximately 118 Veterans Law Judges and decides approximately 104,000 appeals annually.

**Interests**: Reducing the appeals backlog; maintaining legal accuracy; ensuring due process; adequate judicial staffing.

**Power**: BVA decisions set precedent for how VBA interprets disability law; remand decisions create additional work for regional offices.

### Court of Appeals for Veterans Claims (CAVC)

**Role**: The Article I federal court that reviews BVA decisions. Hears approximately 7,000 cases annually.

**Interests**: Consistent application of veterans' benefits law; protecting veteran rights; maintaining judicial independence.

**Power**: CAVC decisions are binding on the VA and can require systemic changes to how claims are processed. The court has driven many of the procedural requirements that add complexity to claims processing.

### VA Office of Inspector General (OIG)

**Role**: Independent oversight of VA operations, including claims processing accuracy and efficiency.

**Interests**: Identifying waste, fraud, and abuse; improving VA performance; maintaining institutional credibility.

**Power**: OIG reports carry significant weight with Congress and the media. Recommendations, while not binding, create pressure for VA reforms.

### Government Accountability Office (GAO)

**Role**: Congressional watchdog that audits VA programs and maintains the "High-Risk List" that includes VA disability claims.

**Interests**: Improving government performance; informing Congressional decision-making; tracking implementation of recommendations.

**Power**: GAO reports are widely cited in Congressional hearings and media coverage. High-risk designation keeps sustained attention on the claims backlog.

## External Stakeholders

### Veterans' Attorneys and Claims Agents

**Who They Are**: Private attorneys and accredited claims agents who represent veterans in claims and appeals. The number of accredited attorneys has grown significantly since fee agreements were authorized in 2006.

**Interests**:

- Earning fees from successful claims (typically 20-33% of retroactive benefits)
- Maintaining access to VA records and systems
- Ensuring robust due process rights (their business depends on complex claims)
- Expanding the scope of representable claims

**Power and Influence**:

- File a growing share of appeals and complex claims
- Lobby for procedural protections that can add processing complexity
- File class action litigation that forces systemic VA changes
- Some veterans' advocates argue that attorney involvement improves outcomes; others argue it adds unnecessary complexity

### Academic Researchers and Think Tanks

**Key Players**: RAND Corporation, Institute for Defense Analyses (IDA), National Academy of Public Administration (NAPA), various university VA research centers.

**Interests**: Producing research on VA performance; influencing policy; securing research funding.

**Power**: Research findings inform Congressional and VA decision-making. RAND and IDA frequently receive contracts to evaluate VA programs.

### Media

**Role**: Coverage of VA claims backlog issues influences public opinion and political pressure.

**Key Dynamics**: VA backlog stories receive periodic intense coverage (especially during crises) followed by long periods of minimal attention. Investigative journalism has been critical in exposing processing failures and veteran hardship.

## Stakeholder Relationships

### Alliance Map

| Alliance | Members | Shared Interest |
|----------|---------|-----------------|
| Veterans' advocacy coalition | VSOs + veterans' attorneys + veteran activists | Expanding access, protecting due process |
| Congressional oversight alliance | HVAC/SVAC + GAO + VA OIG | Accountability, reform pressure |
| VA institutional alliance | VA leadership + VBA workforce + contract examiners | Operational capacity, resources |
| Reform coalition | Think tanks + tech modernizers + centrist legislators | Efficiency, modernization |

### Tension Points

| Tension | Parties | Nature of Conflict |
|---------|---------|-------------------|
| Speed vs. due process | VA leadership vs. VSOs/attorneys | VA wants faster processing; advocates want thorough development |
| Workforce vs. management | AFGE vs. VA leadership | Overtime mandates, production quotas, telework policies |
| Expansion vs. capacity | Congress vs. VA | Congress expands eligibility; VA lacks processing capacity |
| Automation vs. individual review | Tech modernizers vs. VSOs | AI-assisted decisions raise fairness and transparency concerns |
| Quality vs. cost | VA vs. contract examiners | VA wants better exams; contractors want to control costs |
