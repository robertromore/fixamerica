# VA Claims Backlog: Opposition to Reform

## Overview

Unlike many policy areas, opposition to reducing the VA claims backlog is not primarily ideological. No political perspective openly advocates for slow claims processing. Instead, resistance to effective reform comes from structural interests, institutional inertia, competing priorities, and disagreements about *how* to fix the problem rather than *whether* to fix it. This document examines the sources of resistance and the dynamics that perpetuate the backlog despite universal rhetorical support for timely claims processing.

---

## Sources of Resistance

### 1. Fiscal Conservatives and Budget Hawks

**Position**: Support veterans in principle but resist the appropriations needed to solve the backlog.

**How They Create Obstacles**:

- Oppose multi-year mandatory funding for VBA staffing, arguing it reduces Congressional budget flexibility
- Support eligibility expansions (politically popular) while voting against corresponding appropriations (fiscally uncomfortable)
- Frame backlog reduction as an efficiency problem rather than a resource problem, deflecting from the need for additional spending
- Use deficit concerns to resist investments in technology modernization and workforce expansion

**Evidence**:

- The PACT Act's Toxic Exposure Fund was initially structured as mandatory spending, but some fiscal conservatives attempted to reclassify it as discretionary, which would have subjected it to annual appropriations caps (Senate floor debate, July-August 2022)
- VBA's budget has grown from $3.2 billion (FY 2022) to $3.8 billion (FY 2024), but GAO has estimated that a sustained backlog reduction would require approximately $4.5-5.0 billion annually (GAO-23-106059, 2023)

### 2. VA Institutional Resistance to Process Change

**Position**: The VA bureaucracy resists fundamental process redesign, preferring incremental improvements to existing systems.

**How It Manifests**:

- Middle management resistance to automation that could reduce the need for certain positions
- Institutional attachment to the regional office structure even when it creates inefficiencies
- Reluctance to adopt private-sector processing methods (e.g., presumptive approval for straightforward claims)
- Slow adoption of new technology due to procurement complexity and risk aversion
- "Not invented here" rejection of external recommendations from GAO, OIG, and academic researchers

**Evidence**:

- GAO has made 47 recommendations related to claims processing since 2015; as of 2024, only 29 have been fully implemented (GAO High-Risk Progress Update, 2024)
- The VA's Automated Decision Support pilot has been in testing for over three years without full deployment, citing "risk management" concerns (VA CIO testimony, 2024)

### 3. Due Process Advocates and Legal Community

**Position**: Prioritize thorough claims development and veteran protections over processing speed.

**How They Create Obstacles**:

- Oppose streamlining measures that might reduce procedural protections
- Advocate for more detailed notice requirements, additional development steps, and expanded hearing rights
- Challenge automated or AI-assisted claims decisions as violating due process
- File litigation that creates new procedural requirements, adding processing complexity
- Argue that accuracy, not speed, should be the primary metric

**Evidence**:

- The National Veterans Legal Services Program (NVLSP) and other legal organizations have opposed proposals for "auto-adjudication" of simple claims, arguing they could produce systematic underrating (NVLSP position statement, 2024)
- Court of Appeals for Veterans Claims decisions have added procedural steps that individually protect veterans but collectively extend processing time (e.g., *Dingess v. Nicholson*, 19 Vet. App. 473 (2006); *Vazquez-Flores v. Peake*, 22 Vet. App. 37 (2008))

### 4. Contract Examination Industry

**Position**: Support the current system that generates substantial contract revenue.

**How They Create Obstacles**:

- Lobby for continued and expanded reliance on contract examinations rather than VA in-house capacity
- Resist quality accountability measures that could reduce contract volume
- Insufficient investment in examiner quality control creates poor-quality examinations that require rework
- Push back against proposals to reduce the number of examinations required per claim

**Evidence**:

- Major C&P examination contractors (QTC/Leidos, LHI, VES) spent a combined $2.8 million on federal lobbying in FY 2023 (OpenSecrets.org, 2024)
- The VA OIG found that 18% of contract examinations contained deficiencies requiring correction or re-examination (VA OIG Report 22-00827-166, 2023)

### 5. Union Resistance to Productivity Reforms

**Position**: Protect workers from unreasonable production demands and work condition changes.

**How It Manifests**:

- Resistance to changes in production quotas, arguing they sacrifice quality
- Opposition to mandatory overtime beyond negotiated limits
- Challenges to telework policy changes and office reorganizations
- Resistance to performance-based accountability that links outcomes to individual employees
- Skepticism about automation as a workforce reduction tool

**Evidence**:

- AFGE Local 520 filed a formal grievance in 2023 against increased production targets at the Houston Regional Office, arguing the targets were "arbitrary and harmful to claims quality" (AFGE press release, 2023)
- Negotiations over telework policy consumed significant VA-AFGE bargaining time in 2023-2024, diverting management attention from processing reforms

**Important Context**: Union concerns about production pressure are often legitimate. The tension between worker protection and processing efficiency is genuine, and the solution is likely adequate staffing rather than pressuring existing staff to work harder.

### 6. Status Quo Congressional Dynamics

**Position**: Congress benefits politically from the backlog crisis cycle.

**How It Manifests**:

- Members of Congress gain political credit by passing eligibility expansions without bearing responsibility for resulting backlogs
- Oversight hearings generate media coverage and constituent engagement without requiring members to make difficult budget trade-offs
- The Veterans' Affairs Committees are not the same committees that control appropriations, creating a disconnect between authorization and funding
- Institutional incentives favor high-profile legislation over unglamorous operational funding

**Evidence**:

- The PACT Act passed with 86 Senate votes and 342 House votes but did not include specific additional appropriations for claims processing staff (Public Law 117-168, 2022)
- FY 2024 VA appropriations were delayed by a continuing resolution for nearly six months, preventing the VA from executing its hiring plan on schedule

## Arguments Against Specific Reforms

### Against Automated Claims Processing

| Argument | Who Makes It | Validity Assessment |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| AI decisions lack transparency and due process | Legal advocates, VSOs | Partially valid; algorithmic accountability is a legitimate concern |
| Automation will create systematic bias against complex conditions | Veterans' attorneys | Valid concern requiring careful system design and oversight |
| Veterans deserve human review of their claims | VSOs, some members of Congress | Emotionally compelling but may not be necessary for straightforward claims |
| Technology will displace workers | AFGE | Partially valid; workforce transition planning is needed |

### Against Presumptive Processing Expansion

| Argument | Who Makes It | Validity Assessment |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Presumptive conditions without medical evidence invite fraud | Fiscal conservatives | Minimal evidence of fraud in existing presumptive programs; fraud rate below 1% |
| Too costly to presume service connection broadly | Budget hawks | Valid fiscal concern but addressable through targeted eligibility criteria |
| Undermines the evidence-based disability system | Some VA medical professionals | Partially valid; presumptions should be based on strong epidemiological evidence |

### Against Private-Sector Outsourcing

| Argument | Who Makes It | Validity Assessment |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Private contractors lack accountability | VSOs, AFGE | Valid; contract oversight has been inadequate |
| Outsourcing is more expensive per claim | AFGE, some analysts | Mixed evidence; depends on contract structure |
| Veterans' benefits should be administered by government employees | AFGE, some progressive advocates | Philosophical position; effectiveness should be the primary criterion |

### Against Simplified Rating Schedule

| Argument | Who Makes It | Validity Assessment |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Current system is medically precise and individualized | VA medical professionals | Partially valid but precision is illusory given error rates |
| Simplification would create winners and losers | VSOs | Valid; any change would affect some veterans' ratings |
| The schedule has been refined over decades | VA institutional defenders | True but "refined" does not mean "optimal" |

## Political Dynamics

### Why the Backlog Persists Despite Universal Support for Solving It

1. **Diffuse costs, concentrated benefits**: The cost of solving the backlog is spread across all taxpayers; the benefits accrue to specific veterans. This makes expansion more politically attractive than operational improvement.

2. **Attention cycles**: Media and Congressional attention to the backlog is episodic. Intense focus during crises (2013, 2023) fades between surges, removing pressure for sustained reform.

3. **Authorization-appropriations disconnect**: The committees that authorize benefits expansion are different from those that fund processing capacity. Neither takes full responsibility.

4. **Moral hazard of crisis**: Each backlog crisis generates additional resources that are withdrawn once the crisis recedes, preventing sustained capacity building.

5. **Complexity as shield**: The technical complexity of claims processing deters media scrutiny and public engagement between crises.

## Addressing Opposition

### Strategies for Building Reform Support

| Opposition Source | Strategy | Mechanism |
|------------------|----------|-----------|
| Fiscal conservatives | Demonstrate return on investment | Show that faster processing reduces appeals costs and rework |
| VA institutional resistance | Engage frontline workers in design | Bottom-up process improvement with management support |
| Due process advocates | Build transparency into automation | Open-source algorithms; human review of AI-flagged cases |
| Contract exam industry | Performance-based contracts | Tie payment to examination quality, not volume |
| Union resistance | Involve AFGE in modernization planning | Retraining programs; job transition guarantees |
| Congressional dynamics | Link expansion to mandatory processing funding | Require appropriations in authorization bills |
