# VA Claims Backlog: History

## Origins of the System

### Revolutionary War Through Civil War (1776-1865)

The United States has provided disability compensation to veterans since the nation's founding. The Continental Congress passed the first pension law in 1776, offering half-pay to soldiers disabled in service. After the Civil War, the Pension Bureau administered benefits to hundreds of thousands of Union veterans, creating the first large-scale federal benefits adjudication system.

- **1776**: Continental Congress authorizes disability pensions for Continental Army soldiers
- **1818**: Service Pension Act extends pensions beyond disability to all Revolutionary War veterans meeting service requirements
- **1862**: General Law Pension System established for Union Civil War veterans (Act of July 14, 1862, 12 Stat. 566)
- **1890**: Dependent Pension Act dramatically expands eligibility, creating a near-universal pension system for Union veterans
- **1890s peak**: The Pension Bureau processed over 500,000 pending claims, representing the first recorded claims backlog crisis (Skocpol, *Protecting Soldiers and Mothers*, 1992)

### World War I and the Birth of the VA (1917-1930)

World War I created both a massive new veteran population and a bureaucratic mess of overlapping agencies.

- **1917-1919**: Three separate agencies handle veteran benefits: Bureau of War Risk Insurance, Federal Board for Vocational Education, and U.S. Public Health Service
- **1921**: Veterans Bureau created to consolidate benefits administration (Public Law 67-47)
- **1924**: World War Adjusted Compensation Act ("Bonus Act") creates additional claims burden
- **1930**: President Hoover signs Executive Order 5398, consolidating all veteran agencies into the **Veterans Administration**

### World War II and Mass Adjudication (1941-1955)

The 16 million World War II veterans created an unprecedented claims challenge.

- **1944**: Servicemen's Readjustment Act (GI Bill) signed; creates massive benefits administration workload
- **1945-1947**: VA processes over 5 million initial claims in three years--an achievement not matched until the 2020s
- **1946**: VA employment peaks at 200,000, many of them veterans themselves (VA History Office, 2020)
- **1950s**: Claims volume stabilizes as World War II cohort is processed; processing times average 60-90 days

### Vietnam Era and Growing Complexity (1960-1990)

The Vietnam War introduced new types of claims and new controversies.

- **1962-1975**: Vietnam War generates 2.7 million veterans with service-connected disability potential
- **1970s**: Agent Orange exposure becomes a contested disability claim issue; VA initially denies nearly all claims
- **1979**: *Nehmer v. United States Veterans Administration* class action lawsuit filed, eventually forcing VA to recognize Agent Orange presumptive conditions
- **1980**: Agent Orange claims begin flooding regional offices; VA lacks scientific framework for adjudication
- **1988**: Department of Veterans Affairs Act (Public Law 100-527) elevates the VA to Cabinet-level department
- **1989**: Agent Orange Act of 1991 (passed 1991) eventually establishes presumptive service connection for conditions linked to herbicide exposure

## The Modern Claims Crisis

### The 1990s: Growing Backlogs and Court Oversight (1990-2000)

- **1988**: Veterans' Judicial Review Act (Public Law 100-687) creates the Court of Veterans Appeals (later renamed Court of Appeals for Veterans Claims), giving veterans the right to appeal VA decisions to an independent court for the first time
- **1991**: Gulf War veterans begin filing claims for unexplained multi-symptom illness
- **1994**: VA backlog exceeds 500,000 pending claims for the first time in the modern era
- **1996**: Veterans Claims Assistance Act attempts to streamline processing but creates new procedural requirements
- **Late 1990s**: Court of Veterans Appeals decisions repeatedly reverse VA denials, forcing remands that add to backlog

### Post-9/11 Surge (2001-2012)

The wars in Afghanistan and Iraq created a sustained surge in claims that the VA was not structured to absorb.

| Year | Total Pending Claims | Key Development |
|------|---------------------|-----------------|
| 2001 | 325,000 | Pre-9/11 baseline |
| 2005 | 455,000 | Iraq/Afghanistan claims accelerating |
| 2008 | 625,000 | Financial crisis compounds veteran hardship |
| 2010 | 564,000 | Agent Orange Blue Water Navy expansion |
| 2011 | 735,000 | VA adds new Agent Orange presumptives |
| 2012 | 883,000 | IOM report adds ischemic heart disease |
| 2013 (March) | 900,000+ | All-time pending record |
| 2013 (September) | 836,000 (611,000 backlogged) | Backlog rate: 73.1% |

*Source: VA Monday Morning Workload Reports, 2001-2013; Congressional Research Service, "Veterans' Benefits: Claims Backlog," 2014.*

**Key drivers of the 2009-2013 crisis:**

- Three Agent Orange presumptive condition additions in 2010 (ischemic heart disease, Parkinson's disease, B-cell leukemia) added an estimated 260,000 claims (VA estimate, 2010)
- Post-9/11 veteran claims accelerated as troops returned from Iraq
- Transition from paper-based to electronic claims processing (VBA Transformation Plan) initially slowed operations
- Court decisions requiring more thorough development of claims increased per-claim processing time

### The 2013-2015 Backlog Reduction Campaign

Secretary Eric Shinseki and later Secretary Robert McDonald launched an aggressive backlog reduction effort.

- **2013**: Secretary Shinseki pledges to eliminate the backlog by end of 2015
- **2013**: VBA Transformation Plan launched, including mandatory overtime, overtime funding increases, and "Strategic Plan to Eliminate the Claims Backlog"
- **2013-2014**: Fully Developed Claims (FDC) program expanded, incentivizing veterans to submit complete evidence packages
- **2014**: Veterans Benefits Management System (VBMS) deployed to all regional offices, replacing paper-based processing
- **2014**: VA wait time scandal erupts (healthcare, not claims), but draws attention to all VA backlogs
- **2015 (September)**: Backlog reduced to approximately 75,000--a 88% reduction from the 2013 peak

**Methods used to reduce the backlog:**

- Mandatory overtime for all claims processors (controversial; contributed to burnout)
- Provisional ratings for clear-cut conditions, with final ratings to follow
- National Work Queue distributing claims to offices with capacity
- Simplified decision letters and rating worksheets
- Increased use of Disability Benefits Questionnaires (DBQs) for medical evidence

### Post-Reduction Drift (2016-2021)

After the 2015 reduction, the backlog slowly crept back upward.

- **2016-2017**: Backlog stabilizes around 70,000-80,000 but never reaches zero
- **2017**: Appeals Modernization Act (Public Law 115-55) signed, creating three review lanes to replace the broken legacy appeals system
- **2018**: Blue Water Navy Vietnam Veterans Act negotiations begin (eventually signed in 2019)
- **2019**: Blue Water Navy Act (Public Law 116-23) extends Agent Orange presumption to veterans who served offshore Vietnam; adds estimated 90,000 new claims
- **2020**: COVID-19 pandemic disrupts claims processing as regional offices reduce in-person operations
- **2020**: Backlog rises to 118,000 as pandemic slows medical exams and evidence gathering
- **2021**: Appeals Modernization Act implementation completed; legacy appeals backlog eliminated but new-system appeals growing

### The PACT Act Era (2022-Present)

The PACT Act created the most dramatic claims surge in modern VA history.

**Timeline:**

- **June 2022**: Senate initially fails to advance PACT Act amid procedural dispute; veteran advocates stage dramatic Capitol Hill protest led by Jon Stewart
- **August 10, 2022**: President Biden signs the PACT Act (Public Law 117-168)
- **August-December 2022**: Initial PACT Act claims surge begins; 380,000 claims filed in four months
- **2023**: VA processes record 1.84 million claims but receives 2.65 million; backlog surges to 305,000
- **2024**: VA processes record 1.98 million claims but continues to fall behind incoming volume
- **January 2025**: Total pending exceeds 907,000; VA projects continued elevated volume through FY 2026

## Technology Evolution

### Paper to Digital (1990s-2015)

| Era | System | Impact |
|-----|--------|--------|
| Pre-1998 | Paper claims folders ("C-files") | Physical files shipped between offices; lost/damaged files common |
| 1998 | Benefits Delivery Network (BDN) | First electronic tracking; paper files still primary |
| 2002 | Rating Board Automation 2000 (RBA2000) | Automated some rating calculations; limited adoption |
| 2010-2014 | Veterans Benefits Management System (VBMS) | Full electronic processing; scanned paper files |
| 2013 | eBenefits portal | Veterans can file claims online for the first time |
| 2018 | VA.gov claims portal | Modernized online filing and tracking |
| 2020-2024 | Automated Decision Support tools | AI-assisted rating suggestions in pilot |

*Source: VA Office of Information and Technology historical documentation; Congressional Research Service, "VBA IT Modernization," 2023.*

### National Work Queue (2016-Present)

The National Work Queue (NWQ) was a transformative change that allowed claims to be processed by any regional office rather than the veteran's home office.

- Reduced regional processing time disparities from an 180-day spread to an 80-day spread
- Enabled the VA to shift workload to offices with excess capacity
- Created challenges: adjudicators less familiar with local medical providers and veteran communities
- By FY 2024, approximately 40% of claims were processed outside the veteran's home regional office

## Lessons from History

### Recurring Patterns

1. **Eligibility expansion without capacity expansion**: Every major expansion of presumptive conditions or eligibility criteria has created a backlog surge because Congress authorizes new benefits without proportionally funding processing capacity
2. **Crisis-driven reform, drift between crises**: The VA makes dramatic improvements during backlog crises (2013-2015) but lacks the institutional discipline to sustain gains during quieter periods
3. **Technology as partial solution**: Each technological modernization has improved efficiency but never solved the fundamental mismatch between demand and capacity
4. **Workforce whiplash**: Rapid hiring during surges followed by attrition creates a perpetually undertrained workforce
5. **Court-driven complexity**: Judicial review has improved veteran protections but added procedural requirements that slow processing

### Structural Constants

- The VA has never sustained a backlog below 50,000 claims for more than 18 months
- Every war and every presumptive condition expansion has produced a claims surge
- Processing accuracy declines during periods of high-pressure backlog reduction
- The appeals system always develops its own backlog parallel to the initial claims backlog
