# Survivors Benefits: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to survivors benefit reform is rarely expressed as opposition to survivors themselves. Rather, it takes the form of fiscal objections, competing priorities, ideological resistance to entitlement expansion, and institutional inertia. Understanding these dynamics is essential for designing strategies that can overcome or circumvent them.

---

## Fiscal Opposition

### Congressional Budget Hawks

**Position**: Survivor benefit expansions must be offset by spending reductions elsewhere or revenue increases, per the Congressional Budget Act.

**Key Arguments**:

| Argument | Counterargument |
|----------|-----------------|
| DIC increases are unfunded entitlement expansion | DIC spending is a fraction of VA budget and military spending |
| Remarriage penalty elimination sets precedent | Social Security already allows remarriage without benefit loss after age 60 |
| COLA adjustments already maintain benefit value | COLA maintains a starting point that was never benchmarked to adequacy |
| Resources should go to living veterans first | Survivors' needs are a direct consequence of the service that created veteran benefits |

**Tactics**:

- Refuse to bring bills to floor without offsets
- Use CBO scoring to present reforms as more expensive than they are in context
- Bundle survivor provisions with larger bills where they can be traded away
- Defer action to future Congresses

**Influence Level**: High. Budget rules give fiscal hawks procedural leverage to block reforms that have majority support.

### Office of Management and Budget

- OMB reviews all executive branch budget proposals and typically resists increases beyond COLA
- Presidential budget requests for VA rarely include DIC rate increases above COLA
- OMB's role is institutional, not ideological--it applies fiscal discipline across all agencies
- Can influence the administration's position on survivor legislation

## Ideological Opposition

### Entitlement Expansion Resistance

Some policymakers oppose expanding survivor benefits as part of a broader resistance to growing entitlement spending:

**Arguments**:

- The federal government already spends over $300 billion annually on veteran-related programs (VA FY 2025 Budget Submission)
- Expanding DIC or eliminating remarriage penalties creates irreversible spending commitments
- Need-based programs (Survivors Pension) should target the most vulnerable rather than expanding universal benefits (DIC)
- Private sector solutions (life insurance, financial planning) should supplement government benefits

**Weakness of These Arguments**:

- Military families are required to make sacrifices that private-sector families are not; government benefits compensate for imposed disadvantages
- The "entitlement" framing ignores that DIC is compensation for a death caused by government service
- Military families cannot purchase private insurance that replaces the career disruption, geographic instability, and emotional toll of military service

### Means-Testing Advocates

Some argue that survivor benefits should be more heavily means-tested to concentrate resources on those in greatest need:

| Approach | Proponent Argument | Opponent Argument |
|----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Income-test DIC | Wealthy survivors don't need DIC | DIC is earned compensation, not welfare; means-testing creates administrative burden and stigma |
| Tighten pension eligibility | Prevent abuse of Aid & Attendance | 2018 net worth rule already addressed this; further tightening harms vulnerable elderly |
| Cap education benefits | Expensive degrees don't need full funding | Survivors should have the same educational opportunities as the veteran would have had |

## Institutional Barriers

### VA Administrative Complexity

The VA does not actively oppose reform, but its institutional structure creates friction:

**Sources of Inertia**:

| Factor | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| IT system limitations | Benefits processing systems are built around current rules; changes require expensive reprogramming |
| Staff training requirements | Each legislative change requires training 28,000+ VBA employees |
| Regulatory implementation lag | New legislation requires rulemaking, which takes 12-24 months |
| Interagency coordination | SBP-DIC interactions require VA-DoD coordination, adding complexity |

**Example**: The SBP-DIC offset elimination (enacted December 2019) required three years of phased implementation because VA and DoD systems could not execute the change simultaneously (P.L. 116-92 implementation timeline).

### DoD Institutional Interests

The Department of Defense has institutional interests in the Survivor Benefit Plan that can create tension with reform:

- SBP is partially funded by retiree premiums; changing benefit structures affects the program's actuarial balance
- DoD argues that SBP should be treated as an insurance product, distinct from VA entitments
- Full delinking of SBP from DIC (beyond the offset elimination) could shift costs from DoD to VA

### Inter-Committee Jurisdiction

Survivor benefits fall under the jurisdiction of multiple committees:

| Jurisdiction | Committee | Issue |
|-------------|-----------|-------|
| VA programs (DIC, pension, CHAMPVA) | Veterans' Affairs committees | Must coordinate with... |
| SBP | Armed Services committees | ...which have different membership and priorities |
| Social Security interactions | Finance/Ways and Means | ...which rarely focus on survivor-specific issues |
| Tax provisions | Finance/Ways and Means | Tax treatment of survivor benefits |

This fragmented jurisdiction means no single committee "owns" survivors benefits comprehensively, making holistic reform difficult.

## Industry Opposition

### Insurance Industry

The private insurance industry does not actively lobby against survivor benefit improvements, but benefits indirectly from their inadequacy:

- Supplemental life insurance marketed to military families fills gaps left by inadequate government benefits
- Industry publications cite government benefit limitations as a reason to purchase additional coverage
- Estimated $8-12 billion in annual life insurance premiums paid by active-duty and veteran families (American Council of Life Insurers, 2023)
- Any significant increase in government survivor benefits could reduce demand for supplemental products

### Predatory Financial Services

While the worst abuses (pension poaching) have been curtailed by VA regulation, a segment of the financial services industry continues to profit from survivor benefit complexity:

- Fee-based "veteran benefit consultants" who charge for help navigating free government programs
- Companies marketing high-fee financial products to recently bereaved survivors
- Reverse mortgage companies targeting elderly survivor homeowners

## Political Dynamics

### Bipartisan Support, Procedural Obstacles

Survivor benefit reform enjoys unusual bipartisan support in principle:

| Legislation | Senate Cosponsors | House Cosponsors | Status | Obstacle |
|-------------|------------------:|------------------:|--------|----------|
| SBP-DIC offset elimination (multiple Congresses) | 70+ | 350+ | Eventually passed in FY 2020 NDAA | Budget offset required 14 years of advocacy |
| Remarriage age reduction (multiple Congresses) | 20-40 | 100-200 | Not passed as of 2025 | Lack of floor time, budget scoring |
| DIC rate increase (various proposals) | 10-20 | 50-100 | Not passed | Cost, competing priorities |

The pattern is clear: reforms that have majority support in both chambers fail to advance because of procedural rules (budget scoring, committee jurisdiction, floor time allocation) rather than substantive opposition.

### Electoral Irrelevance

Surviving spouses number approximately 367,000--a small population compared to the 18+ million living veterans. In most congressional districts, the survivor population is too small to constitute a decisive voting bloc. This limits the electoral incentive for members to champion survivor issues over other veterans' concerns with larger constituencies.

## Opposition Arguments and Responses

### "Benefits Are Already Generous"

**Argument**: The total package of DIC, CHAMPVA, education benefits, and home loan guarantees provides substantial support.

**Response**: The total package value depends on individual circumstances:

| Survivor Profile | Annual Benefit Value (est.) | Annual Expenses (est.) | Gap |
|-----------------|---------------------------:|----------------------:|----:|
| Spouse, age 35, 2 children, urban | $29,000 (DIC + child add-ons) | $55,000+ | -$26,000 |
| Spouse, age 70, alone, rural | $19,350 (DIC) | $28,000+ | -$8,650 |
| Spouse, age 45, remarried at 50 | $0 (benefits terminated) | $40,000+ | -$40,000 |

### "Remarriage Is a Personal Choice"

**Argument**: Survivors choose to remarry knowing the benefit consequences; the government is not responsible for subsidizing second marriages.

**Response**: No other federal survivor benefit--Social Security, Federal Employees' Retirement--imposes a permanent benefit loss for remarriage. The policy effectively punishes survivors for a personal decision that has no bearing on the sacrifice that earned the benefit. It disproportionately affects younger survivors, particularly those widowed by combat.

### "We Can't Afford It"

**Argument**: Expanding survivor benefits would cost hundreds of millions or billions of dollars annually.

**Response**: The most commonly proposed reforms are relatively modest in context:

| Reform | Annual Cost | As % of VA Budget ($350B+) | As % of Defense Budget ($886B, FY 2024) |
|--------|------------|---------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| Eliminate remarriage penalty | $280M | 0.08% | 0.03% |
| Increase DIC by 10% | $950M | 0.27% | 0.11% |
| Improve CHAMPVA access | $300M | 0.09% | 0.03% |

## Strategies That Have Overcome Opposition

### SBP-DIC Offset: A Case Study

The 14-year campaign to eliminate the SBP-DIC offset succeeded through:

1. **Sustained advocacy**: TAPS, Gold Star Wives, and VSOs maintained pressure across multiple Congresses
2. **Branding**: The "widows tax" label was effective at communicating the unfairness
3. **Cosponsor strategy**: Building supermajority support made leadership's refusal to act politically costly
4. **NDAA attachment**: Attaching the provision to the must-pass defense authorization bill bypassed budget scoring obstacles
5. **Incremental approach**: The phased elimination (2021-2023) reduced the immediate budget impact

This model provides a template for future survivor benefit reforms.
