# Survivors Benefits: Stakeholders

## Those Directly Affected

### Surviving Spouses

**Population**: Approximately 367,000 surviving spouses receive DIC (VA Annual Benefits Report, FY 2024). An additional unknown number are eligible but unenrolled, or were denied service-connection for the veteran's death.

**Profile**:

| Characteristic | Detail | Source |
|----------------|--------|--------|
| Gender | 87% female, 13% male | VA Annual Benefits Report, FY 2024 |
| Median age | 72 | VA demographic data, FY 2024 |
| Under age 45 | ~15% | VA demographic data, FY 2024 |
| With dependent children | ~22% | VA demographic data, FY 2024 |
| Rural residence | ~31% | VA geographic data, FY 2024 |

**Key Concerns**:

- Adequacy of DIC relative to household costs
- Remarriage penalty forcing choice between relationships and financial security
- CHAMPVA provider access, particularly in rural areas
- Navigating multiple agency applications during acute grief
- Loss of community and identity after the military connection ends

**Organizing**: Gold Star Wives of America, Tragedy Assistance Program for Survivors (TAPS), American Gold Star Mothers

### Surviving Children

**Population**: Approximately 64,000 children receive DIC directly; additional children benefit through increased DIC to the surviving parent (VA Annual Benefits Report, FY 2024).

**Key Concerns**:

- Educational access and continuity after parent's death
- Mental health and developmental impacts of parental loss
- Age limits on education benefits (Fry Scholarship eligibility ends at 33; DEA at 26)
- Transition to adulthood without parental guidance
- Social stigma and isolation

**Unique Vulnerabilities**:

| Age Group | Primary Concern | Benefit Gap |
|-----------|----------------|-------------|
| Under 6 | Childcare costs for surviving parent | No childcare benefit provided |
| 6-17 | Educational disruption, mental health | Limited counseling through VA |
| 18-26 | College affordability, career start | DEA benefit is significantly less than Fry Scholarship |
| 26-33 | Fry Scholarship window closing | No extension provisions |

### Surviving Parents

**Population**: Approximately 9,000 parents receive DIC for the death of a child in service (VA Annual Benefits Report, FY 2024).

**Key Concerns**:

- Stringent income limits ($14,480/year for sole surviving parent, 2025)
- Emotional burden of outliving a child who died in service
- The income threshold has not been meaningfully updated
- Small population means limited political advocacy

### Gold Star Families (Non-Monetary Concerns)

Beyond financial benefits, Gold Star families experience:

- Loss of military community and base access
- Need for long-term grief support that exceeds short-term casualty assistance
- Desire for recognition and remembrance of their service member's sacrifice
- Frustration with bureaucratic processes during periods of acute grief

## Government Stakeholders

### Department of Veterans Affairs

**Role**: Administers DIC, Survivors Pension, CHAMPVA, education benefits, home loan guarantees, and burial benefits.

**Key Offices**:

| Office | Function |
|--------|----------|
| Veterans Benefits Administration (VBA) | DIC claims adjudication, pension processing |
| Veterans Health Administration (VHA) | CHAMPVA administration |
| National Cemetery Administration | Burial and memorial services |
| Board of Veterans' Appeals (BVA) | Adjudicates DIC appeals |

**Institutional Interests**:

- Manage claims volume within existing resources
- Demonstrate responsiveness to congressional oversight
- Implement PACT Act provisions without processing collapse
- Maintain quality while managing the growing DIC caseload

### Department of Defense

**Role**: Administers the Survivor Benefit Plan (SBP), provides casualty assistance, manages TRICARE eligibility for some survivors.

**Key Offices**:

| Office | Function |
|--------|----------|
| Defense Finance and Accounting Service (DFAS) | SBP annuity payments |
| Each service branch Casualty Office | Notification, short-term support |
| TRICARE Management Activity | Healthcare coverage for eligible survivors |
| DoD Actuary | SBP financial management |

**Institutional Interests**:

- SBP financial solvency (the program is partially funded by retiree premiums, partially by DoD appropriations)
- Successful casualty assistance during the acute post-death period
- Maintaining recruitment and retention by demonstrating survivor support

### Congressional Committees

| Committee | Role |
|-----------|------|
| Senate Veterans' Affairs Committee | Authorizes VA survivor benefit programs |
| House Veterans' Affairs Committee | Authorizes VA survivor benefit programs |
| Senate Armed Services Committee | Authorizes SBP and DoD survivor programs |
| House Armed Services Committee | Authorizes SBP and DoD survivor programs |
| Senate/House Appropriations Subcommittees on Military Construction, Veterans Affairs | Funds VA survivor programs |

**Key Dynamics**:

- Veterans' committees generally supportive of survivor benefit expansion
- Budget committees and leadership often block reforms due to cost
- Bipartisan support for survivor issues in principle; division over funding mechanisms
- Individual members champion survivor causes (often those with military districts)

### Social Security Administration

**Role**: Administers Social Security survivor benefits, which most military survivors are also eligible for based on the veteran's earnings record.

**Interaction**: Social Security survivor benefits are independent of VA benefits and are not offset against DIC. However, the two systems do not coordinate or share information, so survivors must apply separately.

## Those Who Benefit from the Status Quo

### Budget Hawks and Fiscal Conservatives

Not opposed to survivors in principle, but prioritize deficit reduction and resist expanding entitlement spending without offsets. The relatively small cost of survivor benefit reforms ($280 million/year for remarriage penalty elimination) is viewed through the lens of precedent-setting for larger entitlement expansions.

### Insurance and Financial Services Industry

- Private life insurance companies benefit when military families perceive government survivor benefits as inadequate and purchase supplemental coverage
- Financial advisors market products to military families based on benefit gaps
- The pension-poaching industry (largely addressed by 2018 regulations) had previously profited from steering survivors into Aid and Attendance applications

### VA Bureaucracy (Institutional Inertia)

While individual VA employees are dedicated to serving survivors, the institution has interests that can conflict with reform:

- System complexity creates a need for specialized staff and infrastructure
- Benefit simplification could reduce headcount and budget authority
- Existing processes resist reorganization even when they create barriers for survivors

## Intermediaries and Service Providers

### Accredited Claims Agents and Attorneys

| Category | Role | Regulation |
|----------|------|------------|
| VSO-accredited representatives | Free claims assistance | VA-accredited, supervised by VSOs |
| Claims agents | Fee-based claims filing | VA-accredited, limited to 20% of past-due benefits |
| Attorneys | Fee-based representation, especially on appeals | VA-accredited, fee agreements filed with VA |

Accredited representatives play a crucial role in helping survivors navigate the claims process. However:

- Availability varies geographically
- Quality varies significantly
- Unaccredited individuals sometimes charge fees for services, which is prohibited by law (38 U.S.C. 5905)

### State and County Veterans Service Officers

- Approximately 3,500 County Veterans Service Officers (CVSOs) nationwide provide free assistance to veterans and survivors (National Association of County Veterans Service Officers, 2024)
- Quality and availability vary dramatically by jurisdiction
- Some states fund robust CVSO programs; others provide minimal support
- CVSOs are often the first point of contact for survivors who are not connected to VSOs

### Congressional Caseworkers

- Members of Congress employ caseworkers who assist constituents with federal agency issues, including VA survivor benefit claims
- Congressional inquiries can expedite stalled claims
- This creates a system in which survivors with political connections or knowledge of the casework process receive faster service

## Key Power Dynamics

| Actor | Power Source | Interest |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| TAPS and Gold Star organizations | Moral authority, media attention | Expand and simplify benefits |
| Major VSOs | Membership, political infrastructure | Support survivor reform but prioritize living veteran issues |
| Congressional veterans' committees | Authorizing legislation | Generally sympathetic but constrained by budget rules |
| CBO | Budget scoring authority | Neutral scorer, but cost estimates shape legislative viability |
| VA leadership | Administrative discretion, rulemaking | Balance service improvement with resource constraints |
| Budget leadership (House/Senate) | Control of floor votes and offsets | Fiscal restraint |
