# Survivors Benefits: History

## Overview

The history of survivors benefits for American military families reflects shifting national attitudes toward the obligations owed to those who serve and their families. From ad hoc pensions granted by colonial legislatures to the comprehensive--but still inadequate--system in place today, survivors benefits have consistently lagged behind the needs they are intended to address. Each major conflict has expanded the beneficiary population, but the structural design of benefits has evolved slowly, often requiring decades of advocacy to correct inequities that were apparent from the start.

---

## Colonial and Revolutionary Era (1636-1789)

### Earliest Provisions

- Plymouth Colony enacted the first military survivor benefit in American history in 1636, providing that if a soldier "be sent forthe and slaine in warres" the colony would "maintain his wife and children competently" (Plymouth Colony Records, 1636)
- During the Revolutionary War, the Continental Congress passed resolutions providing half-pay for seven years to widows and orphans of officers killed in service (Continental Congress Resolution, August 24, 1780)
- Enlisted survivors received no comparable benefit, establishing an officer-enlisted disparity that would persist for over a century
- Benefits were paid irregularly due to the Continental Congress's lack of taxing authority

### Post-Revolution

- The first federal pension act (1789) continued Revolutionary War officer-widow provisions under the new Constitution
- Benefits were viewed as charitable, not as earned entitlements
- No systematic enrollment or claims process existed

---

## 19th Century: Pensions as Patronage (1789-1900)

### War of 1812 and Mexican-American War

- Congress extended survivor pensions to these conflicts on a war-by-war, case-by-case basis
- Widows typically received benefits only if the death occurred in battle or from wounds sustained in combat
- Disease-related deaths--which accounted for the majority of wartime mortality--were generally excluded
- Administration was handled by the War Department with minimal infrastructure

### Civil War Transformation

- The Civil War created the first mass survivor benefit system, with Congress passing the General Law Pension Act of 1862 providing pensions to widows, children, and dependent mothers of Union soldiers killed in service
- By 1893, the Pension Bureau was the largest federal agency, processing over 876,000 pension claims (Skocpol, *Protecting Soldiers and Mothers*, 1992)
- Survivor pensions became a major instrument of political patronage, with Republican Party machines assisting Union widows in filing claims in exchange for political support
- Confederate survivors received no federal benefits; some Southern states established their own pension systems
- The Dependent Pension Act of 1890 expanded eligibility beyond service-connected deaths, establishing a precedent for need-based survivor support

### Key Limitations

| Limitation | Detail | Period |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| Remarriage penalty | Widows lost pensions upon remarriage from the beginning | 1862 onward |
| Race exclusion | Black widows faced systematic barriers to proving marriage and filing claims | 1862-1900 |
| Service connection | Disease-related deaths excluded until later expansions | 1862-1890 |
| Enlisted disparity | Officers' widows received higher rates | 1780-1900s |

## Early 20th Century: Modernization (1900-1945)

### World War I

- The War Risk Insurance Act of 1914, amended in 1917, created a government life insurance program (War Risk Insurance) and established a new framework for survivor benefits administered by the Bureau of War Risk Insurance
- For the first time, survivor benefits were structured as insurance rather than pensions, reflecting Progressive Era influence
- Monthly indemnity payments to widows and children were set at rates reflecting enlisted pay
- The Bureau of War Risk Insurance was notoriously inefficient, processing claims slowly and inconsistently (Keene, *Doughboys, the Great War, and the Remaking of America*, 2001)

### Interwar Period

- The Veterans' Bureau (created 1921) and then the Veterans Administration (created 1930) consolidated survivor benefit administration
- The Economy Act of 1933 dramatically cut veteran and survivor pensions during the Great Depression, affecting over 400,000 beneficiaries (Congressional Record, 1933)
- Benefits were partially restored in 1934 after massive protests by veterans' organizations

### World War II

- The Servicemen's Readjustment Act of 1944 (GI Bill) focused on living veterans, with relatively little attention to survivors
- The National Service Life Insurance (NSLI) program provided low-cost life insurance that served as a de facto survivor benefit
- Approximately 16 million served; wartime mortality created hundreds of thousands of surviving families
- Survivor pension rates remained modest, and the system continued to distinguish between service-connected and non-service-connected deaths

## Post-War and Cold War Era (1945-1990)

### Korean War

- Korean War survivors received benefits under the existing WWII framework
- No significant expansion of survivor programs occurred
- The growing Cold War military establishment created a standing force whose families had ongoing benefit needs, not just wartime survivors

### Dependency and Indemnity Compensation Created (1956)

- The Servicemen's and Veterans' Survivor Benefits Act of 1956 (P.L. 84-881) created Dependency and Indemnity Compensation (DIC) as a replacement for the old death compensation system
- DIC was tied to the veteran's pay grade, with higher-ranking veterans' survivors receiving more--a structure that persisted until 1993
- DIC applied to deaths caused by service-connected conditions, while the pension program continued for non-service-connected deaths of wartime veterans

### Survivor Benefit Plan Created (1972)

- The Uniformed Services Survivor Benefit Plan (SBP) was established by P.L. 92-425 in 1972 as a contributory annuity program for military retiree survivors
- Retirees paid premiums (initially 2.5%, rising to 6.5%) for coverage providing 55% of retired pay to surviving spouses
- The SBP-DIC offset was built into the program from the start: if a survivor was eligible for both SBP and DIC, the DIC amount was subtracted from SBP
- This was justified at the time as preventing "double-dipping" from two federal programs for the same loss

### Vietnam Era and Aftermath

- Vietnam War deaths created a new generation of young survivors, many with small children
- Veterans' organizations began advocating for remarriage penalty reform, noting that young war widows were forced to choose between companionship and financial security
- The Agent Orange controversy raised questions about service-connected deaths that would not be fully addressed until the 2020s

## Modern Reform Era (1990-2010)

### DIC Rate Restructuring (1993)

- P.L. 102-568 (1992, effective 1993) replaced the pay grade-based DIC system with a flat rate for all survivors, regardless of the veteran's rank
- This eliminated the officer-enlisted disparity that had existed since 1780
- The flat rate was initially set at $750/month, well below what many officer-widow beneficiaries had been receiving, so transitional provisions protected existing beneficiaries

### Remarriage Age Threshold (2003)

- P.L. 108-183 (Veterans Benefits Act of 2003) allowed DIC to continue if the surviving spouse remarried at age 57 or older
- This was a compromise; advocates had pushed for full elimination of the remarriage penalty
- The age 57 threshold was chosen to roughly align with Social Security's treatment of survivor benefits (which continue after remarriage at age 60)
- Survivors Pension remained subject to termination upon remarriage at any age

### Post-9/11 Era

| Development | Year | Law | Significance |
|-------------|------|-----|--------------|
| Fry Scholarship created | 2009 | P.L. 111-32 | GI Bill benefits for survivors of post-9/11 combat deaths |
| DIC kicker for 8+ year service-connection | 2009 | P.L. 111-37 | Additional DIC for long service-connected disability |
| Fry Scholarship expanded to spouses | 2013 | P.L. 113-146 | Extended education benefits beyond children |
| VA home loan expanded to survivors | 2010 | P.L. 111-275 | Surviving spouses eligible for VA home loans |

## Recent History (2010-Present)

### SBP-DIC Offset Elimination Campaign

The campaign to eliminate the SBP-DIC offset ("widows tax") represents one of the longest sustained advocacy efforts in military family policy:

| Year | Action | Result |
|------|--------|--------|
| 2005 | First major legislative push (S. 185, 109th Congress) | Did not advance |
| 2008 | Special Survivors Indemnity Allowance (SSIA) created | Partial offset ($310/month initially) |
| 2013-2018 | Annual legislation introduced; majority cosponsors | Blocked by budget concerns |
| 2019 | National Defense Authorization Act for FY 2020 (P.L. 116-92) | Phased elimination enacted |
| 2021 | 1/3 offset eliminated | Implementation began |
| 2022 | 2/3 offset eliminated | Phase 2 |
| 2023 | Full elimination | Implementation complete |

### PACT Act and Toxic Exposure (2022)

- The Sergeant First Class Heath Robinson Honoring our Promise to Address Comprehensive Toxics (PACT) Act (P.L. 117-168, August 2022) dramatically expanded presumptive service-connection for toxic exposure
- For survivors, this meant that deaths previously denied service-connection--particularly from cancers linked to burn pit exposure, Agent Orange, and contaminated water at Camp Lejeune--could now qualify for DIC
- The VA reported a 34% increase in DIC claims filed in FY 2024 compared to FY 2022 (VA Benefits Administration data, FY 2024)
- Many survivors who had been denied DIC for decades were able to refile under the expanded presumptive conditions

### Ongoing Remarriage Penalty Advocacy

- The Survivor Benefits Improvement Act has been introduced in multiple Congresses to lower or eliminate the remarriage age threshold
- As of 2025, the age 57 threshold remains in place for DIC, and no age exception exists for Survivors Pension
- The Tragedy Assistance Program for Survivors (TAPS) has made remarriage penalty reform a top legislative priority

## Historical Patterns

Several recurring patterns emerge from this history:

1. **Reactive expansion**: Survivor benefits are expanded after conflicts, not in anticipation of need
2. **Slow correction**: Known inequities (remarriage penalties, SBP-DIC offset, rank-based disparities) persist for decades before legislative correction
3. **Advocacy-driven reform**: Virtually every improvement has required sustained, multi-year campaigns by veterans' service organizations and survivor advocacy groups
4. **Budget as obstacle**: Fiscal concerns have been the primary stated reason for delaying reforms, even when reforms have broad bipartisan support
5. **Two-tier systems**: The system consistently creates disparities--between officer and enlisted, service-connected and non-service-connected, pre- and post-9/11, wartime and peacetime
6. **Remarriage as moral judgment**: The persistence of remarriage penalties reflects an outdated view of survivor benefits as conditioned on continued fidelity to the deceased, rather than as earned compensation for sacrifice
