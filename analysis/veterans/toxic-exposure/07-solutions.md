# Toxic Exposure: Solutions

## Overview

Addressing veteran toxic exposure requires solutions across multiple domains: closing gaps in the PACT Act, accelerating research on emerging hazards, reforming the claims process, preventing future exposures, and ensuring that family members and underserved populations receive adequate care. The solutions below build on the historic achievement of the PACT Act while addressing its implementation challenges and coverage gaps.

## Solution 1: Expand Presumptive Conditions for Emerging Hazards

### Description

Establish presumptive service connections for conditions linked to exposures not fully covered by the PACT Act, including depleted uranium (beyond kidney disease), PFAS/PFOS, jet fuel (JP-8), Karshi-Khanabad (K2) base contamination, and Fort McClellan chemical weapons agent exposure.

### Evidence Base

- DOD studies document elevated cancer rates among depleted uranium-exposed Gulf War veterans (DOD Depleted Uranium Follow-Up Program, 2023)
- EPA identifies PFAS as linked to kidney cancer, testicular cancer, thyroid disease, and immune system effects (EPA PFAS Action Plan, 2024)
- K2 veterans show elevated rates of cancers and respiratory disease (DOD K2 Health Assessment, 2023)
- National Academies *Gulf War and Health* series documents multiple exposure-disease associations

### Implementation

- **Step 1**: Direct the VA Secretary to initiate rulemaking for new presumptive conditions based on existing evidence
- **Step 2**: Use the PACT Act's "concessions" authority (Section 804) to add conditions without new legislation
- **Step 3**: Fund targeted epidemiological studies to fill specific evidence gaps
- **Step 4**: Establish a standing Toxic Exposure Science Advisory Board to recommend new presumptives on a rolling basis

### Cost Estimate

- $15-40 billion over 10 years depending on the number of conditions and eligible populations (based on CBO scoring methodology for PACT Act presumptives)

### Timeline

- Rulemaking initiation: 6 months
- First new presumptive conditions: 12-18 months
- Ongoing review cycle: Annual

### International Comparison

- **Australia**: Uses a "reasonable hypothesis" standard for veteran toxic exposure claims, placing less burden on veterans than the U.S. system (Australian Repatriation Medical Authority)
- **Canada**: Veterans Affairs Canada applies a "balance of probabilities" standard and has recognized burn pit exposure conditions since 2019

## Solution 2: Accelerate Claims Processing for Toxic Exposure

### Description

Implement specialized processing tracks, automated decision support, and dedicated staffing to reduce PACT Act claims processing times from 155 days to under 90 days.

### Evidence Base

- VA has demonstrated that specialized processing lanes reduce average decision time by 30-40% (VA Benefits Administration Annual Report, FY 2024)
- Automated decision support tools for presumptive conditions can reduce per-claim processing time by 50% when conditions and service documentation are clear (VA IT Modernization Report, 2024)

### Implementation

- **Step 1**: Create dedicated toxic exposure claims processing centers with specialized training
- **Step 2**: Deploy automated decision support for straightforward presumptive claims (confirmed service location + confirmed diagnosis = automatic grant)
- **Step 3**: Hire additional 1,500 claims examiners specifically for toxic exposure claims
- **Step 4**: Establish quality review processes to ensure consistency across regional offices
- **Step 5**: Implement proactive outreach to toxic-exposed veterans who have not filed claims

### Cost Estimate

- $1.2 billion over 5 years for staffing, technology, and training (based on VA staffing cost models)

### Timeline

- Specialized centers operational: 6-12 months
- Automated decision support deployed: 12-18 months
- 90-day processing target achieved: 24-36 months

## Solution 3: Fund Comprehensive Longitudinal Research

### Description

Establish a $500 million annual Toxic Exposure Research Program that tracks exposed populations over decades, identifies emerging health conditions, and builds the evidence base for future presumptive conditions before veterans have to fight for them.

### Evidence Base

- The DOD Millennium Cohort Study has provided critical data on deployment health but was not designed specifically for toxic exposure tracking (Millennium Cohort Study, 2024)
- National Academies reviews are limited by available epidemiological data; investing in research now will prevent decades of delay for future conditions
- The VA Airborne Hazards Center of Excellence has demonstrated the value of specialized toxic exposure research

### Implementation

- **Step 1**: Consolidate toxic exposure research under a single joint DOD-VA research directorate
- **Step 2**: Establish 10-year cohort studies for burn pit, PFAS, depleted uranium, and K2 exposed populations
- **Step 3**: Fund biorepository programs to collect and store biological samples from exposed veterans
- **Step 4**: Create a Toxic Exposure Research Registry linking exposure records, medical records, and claims data
- **Step 5**: Require annual reporting to Congress on research findings and presumptive condition recommendations

### Cost Estimate

- $500 million annually ($5 billion over 10 years); partially offset by reduced future claims processing costs

### Timeline

- Research directorate established: 12 months
- Cohort studies initiated: 18-24 months
- First actionable findings: 3-5 years
- Ongoing monitoring: Indefinite

## Solution 4: Reform the Burden of Proof

### Description

Shift the burden of proof for toxic exposure claims from the veteran to the government. When a veteran documents service in a location and time period associated with known toxic exposures, the government should bear the burden of demonstrating that the veteran's condition is *not* related to the exposure, rather than requiring the veteran to prove causation.

### Evidence Base

- The Australian "reasonable hypothesis" standard has resulted in faster and more equitable claims processing without significant increases in fraud (Australian Department of Veterans' Affairs Annual Report, 2024)
- The current U.S. system requires veterans to prove causation for non-presumptive conditions, which is inherently unfair when the government controlled the exposure conditions and failed to document them

### Implementation

- **Step 1**: Amend 38 U.S.C. Section 1110 and 1131 to establish a rebuttable presumption of service connection for conditions arising in veterans with documented toxic exposure service
- **Step 2**: Define "documented toxic exposure service" to include service in burn pit zones, Agent Orange spray areas, contaminated installations, radiation exposure sites, and other identified locations
- **Step 3**: Require the VA to rebut the presumption with clear and convincing evidence that the condition is unrelated to service
- **Step 4**: Apply the new standard to pending and future claims

### Cost Estimate

- $30-60 billion over 10 years (significant but addresses the fundamental inequity)

### Timeline

- Legislation: 12-18 months
- Rulemaking: 6-12 months after enactment
- Implementation: 2-3 years

## Solution 5: Address Military Family Exposure

### Description

Extend healthcare eligibility and, where appropriate, compensation to military family members who were exposed to toxic substances on military installations, including Camp Lejeune family members, PFAS-contaminated base residents, and dependents exposed to Agent Orange.

### Evidence Base

- Camp Lejeune water contamination affected family members at equal rates to service members; the Honoring America's Veterans Act (2012) provided healthcare but not compensation for most family members
- Studies document elevated rates of birth defects, childhood cancers, and developmental disorders in children of Agent Orange-exposed veterans (National Academies, 2018)
- PFAS contamination affects groundwater and drinking water at over 700 military installations, exposing current and former residents (DOD PFAS Task Force, 2024)

### Implementation

- **Step 1**: Expand VA healthcare eligibility to all family members with documented exposure at contaminated installations
- **Step 2**: Fund research on intergenerational effects of military toxic exposures
- **Step 3**: Establish a compensation framework for family members with exposure-related conditions
- **Step 4**: Create a Military Family Toxic Exposure Registry

### Cost Estimate

- $5-15 billion over 10 years for healthcare; compensation costs depend on framework design

### Timeline

- Healthcare eligibility expansion: 12 months
- Family exposure registry: 18 months
- Compensation framework: 24-36 months

## Solution 6: Prevent Future Military Toxic Exposures

### Description

Mandate DOD environmental health standards that prevent toxic exposures to service members and require real-time exposure monitoring, documentation, and health surveillance during all military operations.

### Evidence Base

- The military's repeated use of hazardous substances (Agent Orange, burn pits, PFAS firefighting foam) demonstrates that voluntary environmental compliance is insufficient
- DOD Instruction 4715.05 (Environmental Compliance) exists but lacks enforcement mechanisms tied to commander accountability
- Real-time exposure monitoring technology exists and is used in civilian industrial settings

### Implementation

- **Step 1**: Amend 10 U.S.C. to require occupational exposure monitoring for all deployed service members
- **Step 2**: Require DOD to develop and use alternatives to burn pits and hazardous waste disposal methods
- **Step 3**: Phase out military use of PFAS-containing firefighting foam (aqueous film-forming foam)
- **Step 4**: Establish commander accountability for environmental health violations
- **Step 5**: Create an independent DOD Environmental Health Inspector General

### Cost Estimate

- $3-8 billion over 10 years for monitoring equipment, alternative waste disposal, and PFAS foam replacement

### Timeline

- Legislative mandate: 12-18 months
- Monitoring deployment: 2-3 years
- PFAS foam phase-out: 5 years
- Full compliance: 7-10 years

## Solution 7: Accelerate Camp Lejeune Justice Act Resolution

### Description

Reform the Camp Lejeune Justice Act litigation process to resolve claims within 2 years, reduce attorney fee disputes, and ensure that aging and dying claimants receive compensation before it is too late.

### Evidence Base

- Over 200,000 claims filed; fewer than 5,000 resolved as of mid-2025 (Eastern District of North Carolina, 2025)
- Average claimant age is over 65; many are terminally ill with exposure-related cancers
- DOJ's slow processing has drawn bipartisan congressional criticism

### Implementation

- **Step 1**: Require DOJ to process all administrative claims within 180 days
- **Step 2**: Establish a structured settlement framework with tiered payments based on condition severity and exposure duration
- **Step 3**: Create an expedited track for claimants with terminal diagnoses
- **Step 4**: Increase DOJ staffing dedicated to CLJA claims
- **Step 5**: Cap government litigation costs per claim to prevent strategic delay

### Cost Estimate

- $21 billion total estimated liability (CBO, 2022); acceleration does not change total cost but changes timing

### Timeline

- Administrative reform: 6 months
- Settlement framework: 12 months
- Expedited terminal track: 3 months
- Majority of claims resolved: 3 years

## Solution 8: Proactive Outreach and Enrollment

### Description

Launch a national outreach campaign to identify and enroll the millions of toxic-exposed veterans who are eligible for PACT Act benefits but have not filed claims, with particular focus on veterans not enrolled in VA healthcare.

### Evidence Base

- VA estimates that millions of eligible veterans have not applied for PACT Act benefits (VA PACT Act Outreach Report, 2024)
- Approximately 60% of veteran suicides involve veterans not enrolled in VA healthcare; toxic exposure screening at enrollment could identify at-risk individuals (VA National Suicide Data Report, 2023)
- Community-based outreach through VSOs and state veterans affairs offices has proven more effective than VA-direct outreach (RAND Corporation, 2023)

### Implementation

- **Step 1**: Partner with VSOs, state veterans affairs offices, and community organizations for outreach
- **Step 2**: Use DOD deployment records to identify and directly contact potentially eligible veterans
- **Step 3**: Simplify the PACT Act enrollment process with online self-certification for eligible service periods
- **Step 4**: Deploy mobile claims processing units to rural areas and veteran-dense communities
- **Step 5**: Fund VSO claims assistance capacity expansion

### Cost Estimate

- $500 million over 5 years for outreach; additional benefits costs depend on enrollment numbers

### Timeline

- National campaign launch: 3 months
- Direct contact with identified veterans: 6-12 months
- Mobile processing units deployed: 12 months
- Ongoing: Continuous

## Solution Sequencing

| Priority | Solution | Rationale |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| Immediate (0-12 months) | Proactive outreach and enrollment | Low cost; immediate impact on unserved veterans |
| Immediate (0-12 months) | Accelerate Camp Lejeune resolution | Claimants are aging and dying |
| Short-term (1-2 years) | Accelerate claims processing | Reduces backlog and wait times |
| Short-term (1-2 years) | Expand presumptive conditions | Addresses known coverage gaps |
| Medium-term (2-3 years) | Reform burden of proof | Systemic change requiring legislation |
| Medium-term (2-3 years) | Address military family exposure | Requires new framework development |
| Long-term (3-10 years) | Fund comprehensive research | Prevents future delay but requires sustained commitment |
| Long-term (3-10 years) | Prevent future exposures | Requires cultural and operational change within DOD |
