# Toxic Exposure: History

## Overview

The history of military toxic exposure is a story of repeated patterns: the government exposes service members to hazardous substances during wartime or military operations, denies or minimizes the health consequences for years or decades, and eventually--under sustained pressure from veterans, advocates, and scientific evidence--acknowledges responsibility and extends benefits. This pattern has played out with Agent Orange, nuclear radiation, Gulf War illness, burn pits, and contaminated water, each time with devastating human cost during the period of denial.

---

## Era 1: Nuclear Testing and Early Cold War (1945-1962)

### Context

Between 1945 and 1962, approximately 400,000 service members participated in atmospheric nuclear weapons tests or occupied irradiated areas in Japan. These "atomic veterans" were exposed to ionizing radiation with little or no protective equipment, often deliberately placed near detonation sites to study the effects on military operations.

### Key Events

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1945 | Occupation of Hiroshima and Nagasaki | Troops exposed to residual radiation |
| 1946-1958 | Pacific Proving Grounds tests (Bikini, Enewetak) | 67 nuclear detonations; Navy personnel exposed on ships and during cleanup |
| 1951-1962 | Nevada Test Site atmospheric tests | 100 above-ground detonations; troops positioned at varying distances |
| 1957 | Operation Plumbbob | 29 detonations in 5 months; 18,000 DOD personnel participated |
| 1977-1980 | Enewetak Atoll cleanup | 6,000 service members cleaned radioactive debris without adequate protection |

### Government Response

- **Secrecy**: Classified exposure records and prohibited veterans from discussing participation
- **Denial**: Claimed radiation doses were within "safe limits" despite inadequate monitoring
- **Limited recognition**: Radiation Exposure Compensation Act (1990) provided modest payments but only to specific cohorts
- **Gradual expansion**: Presumptive conditions for atomic veterans expanded through regulatory action over several decades

### Lasting Impact

The atomic veterans experience established the template for government denial of military toxic exposure. Many died before receiving recognition. Surviving atomic veterans remain among the oldest and most underserved veteran cohorts.

## Era 2: Agent Orange and the Vietnam War (1962-1991)

### Context

Between 1962 and 1971, the U.S. military sprayed approximately 20 million gallons of herbicides--primarily Agent Orange containing the highly toxic dioxin TCDD--over Vietnam, Laos, and Cambodia as part of Operation Ranch Hand. An estimated 2.6 million U.S. service members served in-country during the spraying period.

### Key Events

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1962 | Operation Ranch Hand begins | First large-scale tactical herbicide use |
| 1970 | TCDD identified as contaminant | Scientific evidence of Agent Orange toxicity emerges |
| 1971 | Spraying halted | Military stops herbicide use after scientific concerns |
| 1978 | VA denies Agent Orange claims | VA refuses to recognize herbicide-related conditions |
| 1979 | *Reutershahn v. VA* class action filed | First major Agent Orange lawsuit |
| 1984 | Agent Orange class action settlement | $180 million settlement with chemical companies (Dow, Monsanto) |
| 1991 | Agent Orange Act | Congress mandates National Academy of Sciences review of herbicide health effects |
| 1994 | First NAS report (*Veterans and Agent Orange*) | Establishes "sufficient evidence" and "limited/suggestive evidence" categories |
| 2010 | VA adds 3 conditions | Ischemic heart disease, Parkinson's disease, B-cell leukemia added as presumptive |

### Government Response

- **Systemic denial (1971-1991)**: VA rejected virtually all Agent Orange claims for 20 years
- **Congressional intervention**: Agent Orange Act of 1991 required independent scientific review
- **Incremental recognition**: Conditions added one at a time over 30+ years as NAS reviews accumulated evidence
- **Presumptive framework established**: The Agent Orange experience created the model for presumptive service connection
- **Geographic expansion**: Blue Water Navy veterans (offshore) finally recognized in 2019; Thailand veterans recognized in later rulings

### Lasting Impact

Agent Orange remains the defining case of military toxic exposure. The 20-year delay in recognition caused immeasurable suffering. The National Academies review process, while scientifically rigorous, was slow and allowed political considerations to delay action even when evidence was strong.

## Era 3: Gulf War and New Hazards (1990-2001)

### Context

The 1990-1991 Gulf War exposed approximately 700,000 U.S. service members to a complex mixture of hazards including oil well fire smoke, depleted uranium ammunition, chemical warfare agent releases (Khamisiyah demolition), pesticides, and experimental vaccines.

### Key Events

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1991 | Gulf War ends; veterans report unexplained symptoms | "Gulf War Syndrome" enters public discourse |
| 1993 | NIH workshop on Gulf War health | Initial government acknowledgment of health concerns |
| 1994 | VA Gulf War Registry established | First exposure registry for this conflict |
| 1996 | DOD admits Khamisiyah chemical release | Acknowledged 100,000+ troops may have been exposed to nerve agents |
| 1998 | Presidential Advisory Committee report | Found "stress" not a sufficient explanation; called for more research |
| 2000 | Research Advisory Committee on Gulf War Veterans' Illnesses established | Independent scientific body to guide research |
| 2008 | RAC report: *Gulf War Illness and the Health of Gulf War Veterans* | Concluded Gulf War Illness is a distinct condition affecting 175,000-250,000 veterans |

### Government Response

- **Initial dismissal**: DOD and VA initially attributed symptoms to stress and psychological factors
- **Reluctant research**: Funding for Gulf War Illness research was slow and often misdirected toward psychological explanations
- **Limited presumptives**: Undiagnosed illnesses in Gulf War veterans received presumptive status, but specific conditions remained contested
- **Depleted uranium**: Acknowledged exposure but denied most health effects beyond kidney toxicity

### Lasting Impact

Gulf War Illness demonstrated that toxic exposure could manifest as complex, multi-system conditions difficult to diagnose with conventional medicine. The government's initial "stress" framing damaged trust and delayed treatment for hundreds of thousands of veterans.

## Era 4: Burn Pits and Post-9/11 Operations (2001-2022)

### Context

Throughout operations in Iraq, Afghanistan, and other post-9/11 theaters, the U.S. military used open-air burn pits to dispose of waste including plastics, medical waste, batteries, electronics, paint, solvents, human waste, and other materials. The largest pit, at Joint Base Balad in Iraq, burned 200+ tons of waste daily in an area adjacent to living quarters for 25,000 troops.

### Key Events

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 2001-2021 | Widespread burn pit use | Open burning standard waste disposal method across theaters |
| 2009 | DOD directive to reduce burn pit use | Instruction to transition to incinerators; compliance uneven |
| 2013 | VA Airborne Hazards and Open Burn Pit Registry created | First systematic tracking of burn pit exposures |
| 2014 | Burn Pits 360 founded by veteran advocates | Grassroots organization catalyzes national awareness |
| 2018 | Burn pit legislation introduced in Congress | Multiple bills proposed; none pass |
| 2020 | Biden campaign: personal connection to burn pits | Candidate Biden attributes son Beau Biden's brain cancer to burn pit exposure |
| 2021 | Comprehensive toxic exposure bills gain momentum | Bipartisan support grows; CBO scoring debate begins |
| 2022 | PACT Act passes Senate (86-11) and House (342-88) | Largest toxic exposure legislation in U.S. history |
| 2022 | President Biden signs PACT Act (August 10) | Historic expansion of VA healthcare and benefits |

### Government Response

- **Operational necessity argument**: DOD justified burn pits as the only viable waste disposal option
- **Delayed registry**: Airborne hazards registry not established until 12 years after 9/11 operations began
- **Slow science**: Longitudinal studies (Millennium Cohort Study) ongoing but results lagged behind veteran illness
- **PACT Act breakthrough**: Bipartisan passage after sustained advocacy, public pressure, and political championship

### Lasting Impact

The PACT Act represented a deliberate effort to avoid repeating the Agent Orange delays. The legislation included "concessions" provisions allowing future presumptive conditions to be added without new legislation. However, implementation challenges demonstrate that passing a law is only the beginning.

## Era 5: Camp Lejeune and Installation Contamination (1953-Present)

### Context

Marine Corps Base Camp Lejeune in North Carolina provided contaminated drinking water to as many as 1 million people between 1953 and 1987. The water contained trichloroethylene (TCE), perchloroethylene (PCE), benzene, and vinyl chloride at levels up to 3,400 times permitted levels.

### Key Events

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1953-1987 | Contaminated water supplied at Camp Lejeune | Volatile organic compounds from dry cleaning, fuel leaks, industrial waste |
| 1982 | Contamination first detected | USMC testing finds VOCs in water; wells closed 1984-1985 |
| 1991 | Camp Lejeune added to Superfund list | EPA designates as contaminated site |
| 1997 | ATSDR begins health surveys | Federal health investigation formally launched |
| 2012 | Honoring America's Veterans Act (Camp Lejeune healthcare) | Provided VA healthcare to exposed veterans and family members |
| 2017 | VA establishes Camp Lejeune presumptive conditions | 8 diseases designated presumptive |
| 2022 | Camp Lejeune Justice Act (Title VIII of PACT Act) | Allowed tort claims against federal government; waived sovereign immunity |
| 2023-2025 | Massive litigation filing | Over 200,000 claims filed in Eastern District of North Carolina |

### Government Response

- **30-year cover-up**: Marine Corps failed to notify residents of contamination for decades
- **Slow healthcare provision**: VA healthcare for exposed individuals not established until 2012--25 years after contamination ended
- **Justice Act**: Historic waiver of sovereign immunity allowing tort claims, but implementation overwhelmed courts and DOJ

### Lasting Impact

Camp Lejeune represents the most extensive contamination of a U.S. military installation. It demonstrates that toxic exposure is not limited to combat zones--military families on home soil were poisoned for decades. The litigation backlog threatens to delay justice for aging and dying claimants.

## Turning Points

| Year | Event | Why It Mattered |
|------|-------|-----------------|
| 1991 | Agent Orange Act | Established independent scientific review as basis for presumptive conditions |
| 1994 | First NAS *Veterans and Agent Orange* report | Created evidence-based framework for linking exposures to diseases |
| 2008 | RAC Gulf War Illness report | Definitively established Gulf War Illness as a real, physiological condition |
| 2010 | VA adds 3 Agent Orange presumptives | Demonstrated that scientific evidence could overcome institutional resistance |
| 2019 | Blue Water Navy Vietnam Veterans Act | Extended Agent Orange presumptive coverage to offshore veterans |
| 2022 | PACT Act passage | Largest expansion of toxic exposure benefits; attempted to preempt future delay |
| 2022 | Camp Lejeune Justice Act | First waiver of sovereign immunity for military toxic exposure tort claims |

## Lessons from History

1. **Pattern of denial**: Every major military toxic exposure has followed the same pattern--exposure, denial, advocacy, grudging recognition, belated benefits--at enormous human cost during the decades of delay
2. **Burden of proof as weapon**: Requiring veterans to prove individual causation for mass exposures has been the primary mechanism for denying or delaying benefits
3. **Political leadership matters**: The PACT Act passed because of specific political champions (Jon Stewart, veterans advocates, sympathetic legislators) and a president with personal connection to the issue
4. **Scientific review can delay or enable**: The National Academies process provided credibility but also allowed political actors to demand higher evidence thresholds than the science warranted
5. **Laws are necessary but not sufficient**: The Camp Lejeune Justice Act and PACT Act demonstrate that passage of legislation does not guarantee timely implementation
6. **Each generation repeats the mistake**: Despite the Agent Orange experience, the military continued practices (burn pits) known to be hazardous, suggesting that operational convenience consistently overrides health considerations
