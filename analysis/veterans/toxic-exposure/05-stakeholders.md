# Toxic Exposure: Stakeholders

## Overview

The toxic exposure policy landscape involves a complex network of stakeholders ranging from affected veterans and their families to government agencies, advocacy organizations, scientific institutions, and private industry. Understanding who has power, who is affected, and who influences policy is essential to advancing reform and ensuring effective implementation of existing legislation.

## Affected Populations

### Primary Affected Groups

| Group | Estimated Size | Primary Exposures | Key Concerns |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|--------------|
| Vietnam-era veterans | 5.9 million living (2024); 2.6 million Agent Orange exposed | Agent Orange, tactical herbicides | Aging population; long-latency cancers; declining numbers |
| Post-9/11 veterans | 4.1 million; 3.5 million burn pit exposed | Burn pits, depleted uranium, airborne hazards | Respiratory disease; cancers emerging; PACT Act implementation |
| Gulf War veterans | 700,000 deployed | Oil well fires, depleted uranium, chemical agents | Gulf War Illness; unresolved causation debates |
| Camp Lejeune residents | Up to 1 million (military + families) | Contaminated water (TCE, PCE, benzene) | Litigation delays; aging population; cancer clusters |
| Atomic veterans | 400,000 (few surviving) | Ionizing radiation | Extreme age; historical injustice; declining cohort |
| Military families | Millions (installations worldwide) | PFAS, contaminated water, soil contamination | Children's health; reproductive effects; limited benefits eligibility |
| National Guard and Reserve | 800,000+ deployed post-9/11 | Same as active duty deployments | Access barriers; part-time status complicates claims |

### Disproportionately Affected Subgroups

| Subgroup | Why Disproportionately Affected |
|----------|-------------------------------|
| Enlisted personnel | More likely to live and work near burn pits and hazard sources than officers |
| Rural veterans | Farther from VA medical centers; less access to specialty care |
| Women veterans | Reproductive health effects underresearched; underrepresented in exposure studies |
| Minority veterans | Higher rates of certain exposure-linked conditions; less likely to file claims |
| Veterans not enrolled in VA | Lack screening and outreach; account for majority of unrecognized cases |

## Decision Makers

### Government Officials

| Decision Maker | Authority | Current Stance |
|----------------|-----------|----------------|
| VA Secretary | Administers PACT Act implementation; sets claims processing priorities | Committed to PACT Act implementation; facing capacity challenges |
| Under Secretary for Benefits (VBA) | Manages disability claims processing | Processing PACT claims as priority; struggling with volume |
| Under Secretary for Health (VHA) | Oversees VA healthcare delivery | Expanding toxic exposure screening and treatment capacity |
| Secretary of Defense | DOD environmental and health policies | Limited engagement with veteran toxic exposure issues |
| Attorney General | Manages Camp Lejeune Justice Act litigation | DOJ criticized for slow claims processing |
| Congressional Veterans Affairs Committees | Authorization and oversight of VA programs | Bipartisan support for PACT Act; oversight of implementation |
| Congressional Appropriations Committees | Funding levels for VA and PACT Act programs | Control annual appropriations for Cost of War Toxic Exposures Fund |

## Advocacy Organizations

### Major Veterans Service Organizations

| Organization | Focus | Influence |
|-------------|-------|-----------|
| Burn Pits 360 | Burn pit exposure advocacy and support | Founded by Le Roy and Rosie Torres; catalyzed national burn pit awareness |
| Wounded Warrior Project | Post-9/11 veteran services including toxic exposure | Major lobbying presence; supports PACT Act implementation |
| Veterans of Foreign Wars (VFW) | Broad veteran advocacy including toxic exposure | 1.5 million members; significant congressional influence |
| American Legion | Comprehensive veteran policy advocacy | 1.6 million members; long history of Agent Orange advocacy |
| Disabled American Veterans (DAV) | Disability benefits and toxic exposure claims | Free claims assistance for toxic-exposed veterans |
| Vietnam Veterans of America (VVA) | Vietnam-era veteran advocacy | Pioneered Agent Orange advocacy; aging membership |
| Iraq and Afghanistan Veterans of America (IAVA) | Post-9/11 veteran advocacy | Strong media presence; burn pit awareness campaigns |
| National Association of Atomic Veterans | Atomic veteran recognition and benefits | Small but historically important; most members deceased |

### Legal and Scientific Organizations

| Organization | Role | Impact |
|-------------|------|--------|
| National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine | Independent scientific review of exposure-disease links | Authoritative evidence reviews guide presumptive decisions |
| Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry (ATSDR) | Federal health assessments for toxic exposures | Camp Lejeune health studies; site assessments |
| DOD Millennium Cohort Study | Longitudinal health study of service members | Key data source for burn pit and deployment health research |
| VA Airborne Hazards Center of Excellence | Specialized research on airborne exposures | Clinical and research expertise for burn pit conditions |
| Environmental Working Group | Environmental health advocacy | PFAS contamination tracking at military installations |

## Opposition and Resistance

### Stakeholders Opposing Reform

| Stakeholder | Opposition Type | Motivation |
|-------------|----------------|------------|
| Fiscal conservatives in Congress | Budget-driven opposition | Concern over $280 billion PACT Act cost |
| DOD environmental compliance offices | Institutional resistance | Accountability for creating exposure conditions |
| Defense contractors | Liability avoidance | Potential tort claims for toxic products |
| DOJ Civil Division | Litigation management | Seeking to minimize Camp Lejeune payouts |
| OMB budget analysts | Spending control | Concern about mandatory spending growth |

### Stakeholders with Mixed Positions

| Stakeholder | Position | Complexity |
|-------------|----------|------------|
| VA claims adjudicators | Support veterans but constrained by regulations | Overwhelmed by volume; inconsistent application of presumptives |
| Military leadership | Acknowledge exposure harm but resist operational constraints | Support benefits for veterans but oppose environmental mandates on operations |
| Private healthcare providers | Serve toxic-exposed veterans but lack expertise | Community Care Network expanding but toxic exposure training limited |
| State veterans affairs offices | Support outreach but lack resources | Vary widely in capacity and engagement |

## Coalition Dynamics

### Effective Advocacy Coalitions

| Coalition | Members | Success |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| PACT Act coalition | Burn Pits 360, IAVA, VFW, American Legion, DAV, WWP, Jon Stewart | Passed the most comprehensive toxic exposure legislation in U.S. history |
| Camp Lejeune advocacy | Camp Lejeune veterans and families, legal organizations, NC congressional delegation | Achieved waiver of sovereign immunity for tort claims |
| Agent Orange coalition | VVA, American Legion, scientific researchers | Expanded presumptive conditions over 30 years through sustained advocacy |

### Potential Future Coalitions

| Coalition Opportunity | Potential Members | Objective |
|----------------------|-------------------|-----------|
| PFAS military exposure | Environmental groups, veteran organizations, state AGs | Presumptive conditions for PFAS-related diseases |
| K2 veterans | Specific deployment cohort, congressional champions | Recognition of Karshi-Khanabad base contamination |
| Depleted uranium advocacy | Gulf War and post-9/11 veteran groups, researchers | Expanded presumptive conditions beyond kidney disease |
| Military family environmental health | Military family organizations, environmental groups | Benefits for family members exposed on installations |

## Power Analysis

### Who Has the Most Influence

| Actor | Power Source | Leverage Points |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| Congressional Veterans Affairs Committees | Authorization and oversight | Hearings, legislation, funding |
| VA Secretary | Implementation authority | Rulemaking, resource allocation, presumptive decisions |
| Veteran advocacy organizations (unified) | Public pressure, media, constituent contact | Bipartisan moral authority |
| National Academies | Scientific credibility | Evidence reviews that guide presumptive decisions |
| Jon Stewart and celebrity advocates | Media amplification | Public awareness and political pressure |
| Congressional Appropriations Committees | Funding control | Annual spending levels for PACT Act programs |

### Who Has the Least Power

| Actor | Why Powerless | Consequence |
|-------|--------------|-------------|
| Individual toxic-exposed veterans | Navigating complex claims system alone | Denied benefits; delayed care; financial hardship |
| Family members of deceased veterans | Limited survivor benefit eligibility | Many ineligible for DIC or healthcare |
| Military children exposed on installations | No benefits framework; limited research | Health effects unacknowledged and untreated |
| Foreign nationals exposed by U.S. operations | No standing in U.S. benefits system | Vietnamese Agent Orange victims have received minimal U.S. assistance |
