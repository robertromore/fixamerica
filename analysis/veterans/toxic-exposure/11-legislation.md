# Toxic Exposure: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

This document presents legislative proposals to address remaining gaps in veteran toxic exposure policy after the PACT Act. While the PACT Act (Public Law 117-168) represented historic progress, significant gaps remain in burden of proof standards, coverage for emerging hazards, military family protections, DOD prevention requirements, and Camp Lejeune litigation resolution. The proposals below build on the PACT Act framework and address its implementation challenges.

## Federal Legislation

### The Toxic Exposure Burden of Proof Reform Act

**Purpose**: Shift the burden of proof for toxic exposure disability claims from the veteran to the government, establishing a rebuttable presumption of service connection for conditions arising in veterans with documented toxic exposure service.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Toxic Exposure Burden of Proof
Reform Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(a) Millions of veterans have been exposed to toxic substances
during military service, including Agent Orange, burn pit emissions,
contaminated water, ionizing radiation, depleted uranium, and
per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances.
(b) The Department of Defense has historically failed to monitor,
document, and record service member exposures to toxic substances.
(c) Requiring individual veterans to prove that their specific
condition was caused by a specific toxic exposure is inherently
unfair when the government controlled the exposure conditions and
failed to document them.
(d) The current burden of proof has resulted in decades of delay
in recognition of exposure-related conditions.

SEC. 3. REBUTTABLE PRESUMPTION OF SERVICE CONNECTION.

(a) In General.--Chapter 11 of title 38, United States Code, is
amended by adding a new section as follows:

"Sec. 1120. Rebuttable presumption for toxic exposure conditions.

"(a) PRESUMPTION.--For purposes of sections 1110 and 1131 of
this title, a condition or disability arising in a veteran who has
documented toxic exposure service shall be presumed to be service-
connected unless the Secretary demonstrates by clear and convincing
evidence that the condition or disability is not related to the
veteran's toxic exposure service.

"(b) DOCUMENTED TOXIC EXPOSURE SERVICE.--For purposes of this
section, a veteran has documented toxic exposure service if the
veteran served--
"(1) in a location and during a period identified by the
Secretary as involving exposure to toxic substances, including
but not limited to--
"(A) burn pit zones as defined in section 1119 of this
title;
"(B) areas of Agent Orange or tactical herbicide use;
"(C) Camp Lejeune, North Carolina, between 1953 and
1987;
"(D) locations of atmospheric nuclear weapons testing
between 1945 and 1962;
"(E) locations identified by the Department of Defense
as contaminated with per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances;
or
"(F) such other locations and periods as the Secretary
shall designate by regulation; or
"(2) in any capacity in which the veteran was exposed to
toxic substances as documented by the Department of Defense
Individual Longitudinal Exposure Record or other official
military records.

"(c) SCOPE.--The presumption under subsection (a) applies to
all conditions and disabilities, whether or not included in
any list of presumptive conditions established under this
chapter or chapter 13 of this title.

"(d) REGULATIONS.--The Secretary shall promulgate regulations
to implement this section not later than 180 days after the
date of the enactment of this section."

SEC. 4. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall apply to all claims pending on or filed after the
date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill fundamentally restructures the evidentiary framework for toxic exposure claims. Instead of requiring veterans to prove causation--often impossible given DOD's failure to document exposures--the bill establishes that conditions arising in toxic-exposed veterans are presumed service-connected unless the VA can demonstrate otherwise by clear and convincing evidence. This reflects the reality that the government created the exposure and should bear the burden of proving it was not harmful.

**Potential Challenges**: The VA may argue this creates an unmanageable volume of claims. Fiscal conservatives will cite cost concerns. The "clear and convincing evidence" standard is deliberately high, meaning the VA will rarely be able to rebut the presumption.

**Refinements**: Could include a sunset provision requiring congressional reauthorization after 10 years; could limit the presumption to conditions that have at least "limited/suggestive" evidence of association in scientific literature.

### The Military Family Toxic Exposure Protection Act

**Purpose**: Extend healthcare eligibility and establish a compensation framework for military family members exposed to toxic substances on or near military installations.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Military Family Toxic Exposure
Protection Act".

SEC. 2. HEALTHCARE ELIGIBILITY FOR EXPOSED FAMILY MEMBERS.

(a) In General.--Chapter 17 of title 38, United States Code, is
amended to provide that a family member of a veteran or service
member who resided on or near a military installation during a
period in which the installation was contaminated with toxic
substances shall be eligible for hospital care, medical services,
and nursing home care through the Department of Veterans Affairs
for any condition associated with the toxic exposure.

(b) COVERED INSTALLATIONS.--The Secretary of Defense, in
consultation with the Administrator of the Environmental Protection
Agency and the Director of the Agency for Toxic Substances and
Disease Registry, shall maintain a public list of military
installations and periods of contamination covered by this section.

(c) COVERED CONDITIONS.--The Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall
establish by regulation a list of conditions associated with each
type of installation contamination. Until such regulations are
promulgated, any condition for which a presumptive service
connection exists for veterans exposed to the same toxic substance
shall be covered.

SEC. 3. MILITARY FAMILY TOXIC EXPOSURE COMPENSATION.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--The Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall
establish a Military Family Toxic Exposure Compensation Program
to provide monetary compensation to family members who develop
conditions associated with exposure to toxic substances on or
near military installations.

(b) COMPENSATION AMOUNTS.--Compensation under this section
shall be determined based on--
(1) the severity of the condition;
(2) the duration of exposure;
(3) the age of the family member at the time of exposure;
and
(4) such other factors as the Secretary determines
appropriate.

(c) MAXIMUM AMOUNTS.--The Secretary shall establish maximum
compensation amounts by regulation, which shall not be less
than the amounts provided to veterans under chapter 11 of
this title for comparable conditions.

SEC. 4. MILITARY FAMILY TOXIC EXPOSURE REGISTRY.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--The Secretary of Veterans Affairs, in
coordination with the Secretary of Defense, shall establish
a Military Family Toxic Exposure Registry to track and monitor
the health of family members who resided on or near contaminated
military installations.

(b) REGISTRATION.--Registration shall be voluntary and shall
be available to current and former family members.

(c) RESEARCH.--Data from the Registry shall be made available,
in de-identified form, to researchers studying the health effects
of military installation contamination.

SEC. 5. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There is authorized to be appropriated $2,000,000,000 for each
of fiscal years 2027 through 2036 to carry out this Act.
```

**Explanation**: Military families did not volunteer for toxic exposure, yet they have historically been excluded from benefits available to veterans. This bill addresses that gap by providing healthcare and compensation to family members exposed on installations, while establishing a registry for long-term health monitoring.

**Potential Challenges**: Defining "on or near" an installation requires regulatory precision. Cost estimates will be significant. Some may argue this expands VA's mission beyond its traditional veteran-serving role.

**Refinements**: Could phase in coverage by installation contamination severity; could leverage existing Tricare infrastructure for family member healthcare rather than building new VA capacity.

### The Military Toxic Exposure Prevention Act

**Purpose**: Mandate DOD environmental health standards that prevent toxic exposures and require real-time exposure monitoring for all service members.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Military Toxic Exposure Prevention
Act".

SEC. 2. OCCUPATIONAL EXPOSURE MONITORING.

(a) REQUIREMENT.--Chapter 55 of title 10, United States Code, is
amended to require the Secretary of Defense to implement
occupational exposure monitoring for all members of the Armed
Forces, including--
(1) baseline health assessments before deployment to any
location where toxic exposure risks are identified;
(2) continuous or periodic environmental monitoring during
deployment;
(3) post-deployment health assessments within 90 days of
return; and
(4) longitudinal health monitoring at intervals of not
less than 5 years.

(b) INDIVIDUAL EXPOSURE RECORDS.--All exposure monitoring data
shall be recorded in each service member's Individual Longitudinal
Exposure Record and made available to the Department of Veterans
Affairs for claims processing purposes.

SEC. 3. BAN ON OPEN BURN PITS.

(a) PROHIBITION.--Except as provided in subsection (b), the
Secretary of Defense shall prohibit the use of open-air burn
pits for waste disposal at any military installation or
operational location.

(b) EXCEPTION.--The prohibition in subsection (a) may be
waived by the Secretary of Defense upon written determination
that--
(1) no feasible alternative exists;
(2) the waiver is necessary for military operations; and
(3) the duration of the waiver does not exceed 30 days.

(c) REPORTING.--Any waiver under subsection (b) shall be
reported to the congressional defense committees within 7
days, including the justification, affected personnel, and
health mitigation measures implemented.

SEC. 4. PFAS PHASE-OUT.

(a) TIMELINE.--The Secretary of Defense shall phase out the
use of per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances in firefighting
foam and all other non-essential applications not later than
5 years after the date of enactment of this Act.

(b) ALTERNATIVES.--The Secretary shall develop and deploy
fluorine-free firefighting foam alternatives that meet
military performance specifications.

(c) REMEDIATION.--The Secretary shall remediate PFAS
contamination at all military installations where
contamination exceeds EPA health advisory levels.

SEC. 5. COMMANDER ACCOUNTABILITY.

(a) PERFORMANCE EVALUATIONS.--Compliance with environmental
health standards established under this Act shall be included
as an element of commander performance evaluations.

(b) ENVIRONMENTAL HEALTH INSPECTOR GENERAL.--The Secretary
of Defense shall establish an Environmental Health Inspector
General to oversee compliance with this Act and investigate
reports of toxic exposure resulting from military operations
or installation contamination.

SEC. 6. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There is authorized to be appropriated $1,500,000,000 for each
of fiscal years 2027 through 2036 to carry out this Act.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the prevention side of toxic exposure by mandating exposure monitoring, banning burn pits (with narrow exceptions), phasing out PFAS, and holding commanders accountable for environmental health compliance. Prevention is ultimately less expensive than treatment and compensation.

**Potential Challenges**: DOD will argue operational constraints. PFAS phase-out requires viable alternatives. Commander accountability provisions may face resistance from military leadership. Cost of remediation at hundreds of installations will be substantial.

**Refinements**: Could provide longer phase-out timelines for PFAS in critical military applications; could begin with pilot monitoring programs before full deployment.

### The Camp Lejeune Claims Acceleration Act

**Purpose**: Reform the Camp Lejeune Justice Act litigation process to resolve claims within 2 years.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Camp Lejeune Claims Acceleration Act".

SEC. 2. ADMINISTRATIVE CLAIMS TIMELINE.

(a) REQUIREMENT.--The Attorney General shall issue a decision on
all administrative claims filed under section 804 of Public Law
117-168 within 180 days of receipt.

(b) DEEMED DENIAL.--Any administrative claim not acted upon within
180 days shall be deemed denied, and the claimant may proceed to
file a civil action in the United States District Court for the
Eastern District of North Carolina.

SEC. 3. STRUCTURED SETTLEMENT FRAMEWORK.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--The Attorney General shall establish a
structured settlement framework with tiered compensation based
on--
(1) the diagnosed condition and its severity;
(2) the duration of residence or service at Camp Lejeune
during the contamination period; and
(3) the claimant's age and current health status.

(b) MINIMUM AMOUNTS.--The Attorney General shall establish
minimum settlement amounts for each tier, which shall be
published in the Federal Register within 90 days of enactment.

(c) EXPEDITED TRACK.--Claimants with terminal diagnoses shall
be offered expedited settlement within 60 days of diagnosis
documentation.

SEC. 4. ADDITIONAL RESOURCES.

(a) STAFFING.--The Attorney General shall hire or reassign
not fewer than 500 additional attorneys, paralegals, and
claims examiners to process Camp Lejeune claims.

(b) AUTHORIZATION.--There is authorized to be appropriated
$500,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 and 2028 to carry
out this Act.
```

**Explanation**: With over 200,000 claims filed and fewer than 5,000 resolved, the current pace of Camp Lejeune claims resolution is unacceptable. Many claimants are elderly and terminally ill. This bill mandates timelines, creates a structured settlement framework, and provides resources for accelerated processing.

**Potential Challenges**: DOJ will argue that each claim requires individual assessment. Structured settlements may not adequately address the range of circumstances. Fiscal impact is significant.

**Refinements**: Could allow claimants to opt into the structured settlement or pursue individual litigation; could establish a special master to oversee the process.

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Toxic Exposure Veterans Registry Act

**Purpose**: Create state-level registries of toxic-exposed veterans to facilitate outreach, healthcare coordination, and research.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Toxic Exposure Veterans
Registry Act".

SECTION 2. ESTABLISHMENT OF REGISTRY.

(a) The [State Department of Veterans Affairs/Commission] shall
establish and maintain a Toxic Exposure Veterans Registry to
identify and track veterans residing in [State] who were exposed
to toxic substances during military service.

(b) The Registry shall include, at minimum, the veteran's name,
contact information, military service dates and locations,
identified exposures, and current health conditions.

SECTION 3. OUTREACH.

(a) The [State Department of Veterans Affairs/Commission] shall
conduct outreach to identify toxic-exposed veterans who are not
enrolled in VA healthcare or receiving VA benefits.

(b) Outreach shall include partnerships with county veterans
service officers, community health centers, and veteran service
organizations.

SECTION 4. COORDINATION WITH FEDERAL AGENCIES.

The [State Department of Veterans Affairs/Commission] shall
coordinate with the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs and
the Department of Defense to share data and facilitate claims
filing for registered veterans.

SECTION 5. ANNUAL REPORT.

The [State Department of Veterans Affairs/Commission] shall
submit an annual report to the legislature on the number of
registered veterans, outreach activities, and recommendations
for state action.

SECTION 6. APPROPRIATION.

There is appropriated from the [state fund] $[amount] for each
fiscal year to carry out this Act.
```

**Explanation**: State registries complement federal efforts by providing local outreach capacity. Many toxic-exposed veterans are not connected to the VA system and are more likely to engage with state and local services.

**Adaptations**: States with larger veteran populations may need more robust funding. States with significant military installations may include installation-specific contamination tracking.

## Regulatory Framework

### VA Toxic Exposure Screening Protocol

**Existing Authority**: 38 U.S.C. Section 1710 (VA healthcare eligibility); PACT Act Section 103 (toxic exposure screening)

**Draft Regulation**: The VA shall require toxic exposure screening at every initial healthcare enrollment and at every periodic health assessment for enrolled veterans. Screening shall include deployment history review, exposure questionnaire, Individual Longitudinal Exposure Record review, and referral for condition-specific evaluation when exposure is identified.

**Explanation**: While the PACT Act mandated toxic exposure screening, implementation has been uneven. This regulation standardizes the process and ensures every veteran encounter includes exposure assessment.

### DOD Exposure Documentation Standard

**Existing Authority**: 10 U.S.C. Section 1074f (post-deployment health assessments)

**Draft Regulation**: The Secretary of Defense shall require that all environmental hazard assessments conducted at military installations and operational locations be documented in a standardized format, retained for not less than 75 years, and made available to the VA for claims processing purposes within 30 days of request.

**Explanation**: The fundamental barrier to toxic exposure claims has been the absence of exposure documentation. This regulation ensures that future exposures are documented and that records are preserved and accessible.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Spending Clause**: Congressional authority to spend for the general welfare supports toxic exposure benefits programs
- **Due Process**: Shifting the burden of proof to the government raises no due process concerns because the government created the exposure
- **Sovereign immunity**: The Camp Lejeune Justice Act already waived sovereign immunity for Camp Lejeune; extending this to other installations would require additional legislation
- **Takings Clause**: No takings issue arises from benefits programs

### Preemption Questions

- Federal toxic exposure benefits generally preempt state tort claims against the federal government (Feres doctrine)
- State environmental laws may apply to military installation contamination under CERCLA waivers
- Camp Lejeune Justice Act specifically created a federal cause of action, preempting state limitations periods

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- VA claims adjudication with appeals to the Board of Veterans' Appeals and Court of Appeals for Veterans Claims
- DOJ litigation management for Camp Lejeune claims
- DOD Inspector General for environmental health compliance
- Congressional oversight through Veterans Affairs and Armed Services committees

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Burden of proof reform should include a 10-year review requirement
- Military family compensation program should include 5-year sunset with mandatory reauthorization
- Prevention mandates should include annual compliance reporting

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Toxic Exposure Burden of Proof Reform Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| "Clear and convincing" standard interpretation | VA may interpret the rebuttal standard loosely, undermining the presumption | High |
| Location designation delays | VA could delay designating locations as toxic exposure sites | High |
| Record accessibility | ILER system may not contain comprehensive exposure data | Medium |
| Condition exclusions | VA could argue certain conditions are categorically unrelated to toxic exposure | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address pre-existing conditions | Veterans with conditions predating service may face VA rebuttal | Presumption framework requires some service connection nexus |
| Limited to documented exposure service | Veterans whose service records are incomplete may not qualify | Depends on DOD documentation quality |
| No private right of action | Veterans cannot sue for wrongful denial under the new standard | Administrative remedy remains sole option |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Include legislative history defining "clear and convincing" to match legal standard (75%+ certainty)
2. Mandate that all locations identified in PACT Act and predecessors are automatically designated
3. Require DOD to certify ILER completeness or, where incomplete, presume exposure for the relevant period
4. Establish an independent review board to hear appeals of VA rebuttal determinations

### Military Toxic Exposure Prevention Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Burn pit waiver abuse | 30-day waiver could be renewed repeatedly | Critical |
| PFAS "essential use" exception | Military may classify many PFAS uses as essential | High |
| Commander accountability enforcement gap | Performance evaluation element may be given low weight | Medium |
| Environmental Health IG independence | IG may lack independence from DOD chain of command | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address past exposures | Prevention only; no retroactive effect | Forward-looking legislation by design |
| Monitoring technology limitations | Real-time monitoring not available for all hazard types | Technology development needed |
| Cost of remediation not capped | PFAS remediation costs unknown and potentially enormous | Emerging contamination scope |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Limit burn pit waivers to 3 cumulative 30-day periods per location, requiring congressional notification for any extension
2. Define "essential use" narrowly with independent scientific review of each claimed essential application
3. Weight environmental health compliance as a critical element in commander performance evaluations
4. Establish Environmental Health IG as an independent presidential appointment, not DOD chain of command

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| VA staffing insufficient for expanded claims volume | Mandatory minimum staffing ratios tied to claims volume |
| Claims consistency across regional offices | Centralized quality review and standardized decision templates |
| DOD-VA data sharing remains inadequate | Mandate interoperable electronic systems with statutory deadlines |
| Long-latency conditions will continue emerging for decades | Standing advisory board with authority to recommend new presumptives annually |
| Political will may erode after initial reform momentum | Automatic triggers tied to veteran health outcomes data rather than discretionary action |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- All toxic exposure legislation should include 5-year mandatory congressional review
- Presumptive condition lists should be reviewed annually by the Toxic Exposure Science Advisory Board
- Prevention mandates should include biennial compliance audits by the Environmental Health IG
- Family member compensation should include actuarial review every 3 years to ensure adequate funding
