# Toxic Exposure: Overview

## Executive Summary

Millions of American veterans have been exposed to toxic substances during military service, from Agent Orange in Vietnam to burn pits in Iraq and Afghanistan to contaminated water at Camp Lejeune. For decades, the federal government denied, minimized, or delayed recognition of the health consequences of these exposures, leaving veterans to suffer from cancers, respiratory diseases, neurological disorders, and other debilitating conditions without adequate care or compensation. The passage of the Sergeant First Class Heath Robinson Honoring Our Promise to Address Comprehensive Toxics (PACT) Act in August 2022 represented a historic breakthrough, expanding VA healthcare eligibility and establishing presumptive service connections for dozens of conditions linked to toxic exposures. However, implementation challenges, claims processing backlogs, gaps in scientific understanding, and the sheer scale of affected populations mean that the promise of the PACT Act remains only partially fulfilled.

The scope of military toxic exposure is staggering. An estimated 3.5 million post-9/11 veterans were exposed to burn pits and other airborne hazards in Iraq, Afghanistan, and Southwest Asia (VA Airborne Hazards and Open Burn Pit Registry, 2023). Approximately 2.6 million Vietnam-era veterans were exposed to Agent Orange and tactical herbicides (VA Agent Orange Registry, 2023). At Camp Lejeune alone, up to 1 million military personnel and family members drank contaminated water between 1953 and 1987 (ATSDR, 2023). Radiation exposure affected veterans of atmospheric nuclear tests, the occupation of Hiroshima and Nagasaki, and cleanup operations at sites including Enewetak Atoll.

The PACT Act added 23 burn pit and airborne hazard presumptive conditions, expanded the list of Agent Orange presumptive conditions, and provided VA healthcare eligibility to toxic-exposed combat veterans regardless of disability rating. As of September 2025, the VA had processed over 1.3 million PACT Act-related claims, granting benefits to more than 890,000 veterans and survivors (VA PACT Act Dashboard, FY 2025). Yet critical challenges remain: the claims backlog has grown, scientific research on long-latency conditions lags behind veteran illness, and entire categories of exposure--including depleted uranium, jet fuel, and per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS)--lack presumptive recognition.

Addressing toxic exposure fully requires sustained funding for research, continued expansion of presumptive conditions as evidence emerges, streamlined claims processing, proactive outreach to veterans who do not know they are eligible, and an honest reckoning with the institutional failures that allowed exposure to occur and recognition to be delayed for generations.

## Core Tensions

- **Scientific certainty vs. moral obligation**: The government has historically demanded conclusive proof of causation before granting benefits, while veterans argue that the burden of proof should reflect the government's responsibility for the exposure itself
- **Presumptive coverage vs. fiscal cost**: Expanding presumptive conditions provides faster relief to veterans but carries enormous budgetary implications that invite political opposition
- **Individual claims vs. systemic recognition**: The case-by-case adjudication model is ill-suited to mass exposure events affecting millions; systemic approaches face resistance from those concerned about fraud
- **Military readiness vs. environmental safety**: Operational demands like burn pits were justified as field necessities, creating tension between mission requirements and duty of care to service members
- **Current needs vs. future unknowns**: Latency periods for many toxic exposure conditions mean the full health burden will not be apparent for decades, requiring sustained commitment beyond typical political time horizons

## Key Questions

1. **Is the PACT Act being implemented effectively?** Are veterans receiving timely decisions on claims, and are the presumptive conditions appropriately calibrated to known exposure risks?
2. **What exposures remain unrecognized?** How should the government address gaps in presumptive coverage for depleted uranium, PFAS, jet fuel, and other hazards?
3. **Why did recognition take so long?** What institutional, political, and scientific factors caused decades of delay in acknowledging Agent Orange and other exposure hazards?
4. **How can research keep pace with emerging evidence?** What is needed to accelerate epidemiological studies on long-latency conditions?
5. **What about family members?** How should policy address the health effects on spouses, children, and community members exposed to toxic substances on or near military installations?

## Vision of Success

A successful toxic exposure policy framework would include: comprehensive presumptive coverage for all scientifically plausible exposure-condition links with the burden of proof appropriately placed on the government rather than the veteran; a fully funded and adequately staffed VA claims system capable of processing toxic exposure claims within 90 days; robust longitudinal research programs tracking exposed populations and their descendants; proactive outreach that identifies and enrolls eligible veterans before they become symptomatic; accountability mechanisms that prevent future exposures through improved environmental practices on military installations; and a cultural shift within the Department of Defense that treats the long-term health of service members as a core mission responsibility rather than an afterthought.
