# Education Benefits: Current State

## Program Overview

### GI Bill Programs

The VA administers multiple education benefit programs, with the Post-9/11 GI Bill (Chapter 33) as the primary vehicle:

| Program | Eligibility | Benefits | Recipients (FY2023) |
|---------|------------|----------|---------------------|
| Post-9/11 GI Bill (Ch. 33) | 90+ days post-9/11 active duty service | Tuition, housing, books | ~700,000 |
| Montgomery GI Bill (Ch. 30) | Pre-9/11 service; $1,200 contribution | Monthly stipend ($2,185/mo for full-time) | ~45,000 |
| Survivors' and Dependents' (Ch. 35) | Dependents of disabled/deceased veterans | Monthly stipend | ~90,000 |
| Vocational Rehabilitation (Ch. 31) | Service-connected disability 20%+ | Tuition, supplies, stipend | ~125,000 |
| Reserve Education (Ch. 1606/1607) | Guard/Reserve service | Monthly stipend | ~55,000 |

*Source: VA Education Service Annual Report, FY2023*

### Post-9/11 GI Bill Benefits Structure

| Component | Maximum Benefit (AY 2024-2025) | Notes |
|-----------|-------------------------------|-------|
| Tuition and fees | $28,937.94 (public school cap) | Covers in-state tuition at public schools; private/foreign school cap applies separately |
| Monthly housing allowance | Varies by ZIP code (avg ~$2,000/mo) | Based on E-5 with dependents BAH rate; paid only during enrollment |
| Book and supplies stipend | $1,000/year | Paid proportionally each term |
| Yellow Ribbon supplement | Varies by school | School and VA each cover a portion of tuition exceeding the cap |
| Tutorial assistance | $100/month (max $1,200) | For students needing tutoring |
| Licensing and certification | Up to $2,000 | One-time payment for exam fees |

*Source: VA Education Service, AY2024-2025 rates*

### Eligibility Tiers

| Service Duration | Benefit Level |
|-----------------|---------------|
| 36+ months | 100% of maximum benefits |
| 30 months | 90% |
| 24 months | 80% |
| 18 months | 70% |

*Note: Purple Heart recipients receive 100% regardless of service length (effective 2018)*

## For-Profit College Issues

### The Problem

For-profit colleges have systematically targeted veterans and their GI Bill benefits, producing poor educational outcomes while extracting maximum federal dollars:

**Scale of the Problem (FY2023)**

| Metric | For-Profit | Public | Private Non-Profit | Source |
|--------|-----------|--------|---------------------|--------|
| Share of GI Bill students | 18% | 62% | 20% | VA, 2024 |
| Share of GI Bill spending | 25% | 50% | 25% | VA, 2024 |
| Graduation rate | 23% | 59% | 66% | NCES, 2024 |
| Student loan default rate (3-year) | 15.6% | 9.6% | 6.8% | Dept. of Education, 2024 |
| Share of VA complaint cases | 65%+ | ~20% | ~15% | VA GI Bill Feedback System, 2024 |

### The 90/10 Loophole

The 90/10 rule requires for-profit colleges to derive no more than 90% of revenue from federal student aid. However, until 2023, GI Bill and DoD Tuition Assistance funds were *excluded* from the federal revenue calculation, making veterans the "10%" schools needed to access billions in other federal student aid.

**Timeline of reform:**

- **Pre-2023**: GI Bill funds counted as "private" revenue under 90/10 rule
- **2022**: American Rescue Plan Act included provision to count GI Bill funds as federal revenue (effective 2023)
- **2023**: Rule change took effect; some for-profit schools began losing eligibility
- **2024-2025**: Enforcement continues but compliance monitoring lags

**Current status**: The loophole has been substantially closed, but enforcement capacity remains limited and some schools restructure to avoid the rule (e.g., converting to non-profit status while maintaining for-profit practices).

### Aggressive Recruitment Tactics

For-profit schools use targeted veteran recruitment strategies:

- Military-themed advertising and campus branding
- Recruiters stationed near military bases
- Misleading job placement claims
- Exaggerated transfer credit promises
- High-pressure enrollment tactics during transition periods
- Promising military credential recognition that does not materialize

### State Authorization and Oversight Gaps

| Issue | Description | Impact |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| SAA funding | State Approving Agencies that oversee GI Bill-eligible schools are underfunded | Inadequate inspections and compliance reviews |
| Multi-state operations | Online for-profit schools operate across state lines, complicating oversight | Regulatory arbitrage |
| Closure protections | When schools close mid-term, veterans lose benefits and credits | Students left without degrees or remaining entitlement |

## Housing Allowance Adequacy

### The Problem

The GI Bill's Monthly Housing Allowance (MHA) is based on E-5 with dependents Basic Allowance for Housing (BAH) rates, but these rates often fall short of actual housing costs:

| Market | MHA Rate (2024) | Avg. Rent (1BR) | Gap | Source |
|--------|-----------------|------------------|-----|--------|
| San Francisco, CA | $4,878 | $3,200 | +$1,678 | VA/Zillow, 2024 |
| New York, NY | $4,167 | $3,500 | +$667 | VA/Zillow, 2024 |
| Washington, DC | $3,222 | $2,400 | +$822 | VA/Zillow, 2024 |
| Austin, TX | $2,265 | $1,600 | +$665 | VA/Zillow, 2024 |
| Rural Midwest | $1,100-1,400 | $700-900 | +$300-500 | VA/Zillow, 2024 |

**Note**: While BAH rates in high-cost areas may appear adequate or generous, veterans who are full-time students cannot work full-time, and the MHA is often their primary income source. Additionally, MHA is prorated for part-time students and eliminated entirely for exclusively online students (who receive half the national average instead).

### Online Learning Penalty

Veterans enrolled exclusively online receive a reduced housing allowance equal to 50% of the national average BAH rate (~$1,000/month in 2024) regardless of their actual living costs. This penalty:

- Discourages flexible learning arrangements
- Penalizes veterans who cannot physically attend classes (disabled, rural, caregiving)
- Does not reflect that online students still pay rent
- Was partially addressed during COVID-19 (temporary parity) but reverted

## Benefit Gaps and Structural Issues

### Non-Degree Training

GI Bill benefits were designed primarily for four-year college programs but increasingly need to serve non-traditional education:

| Training Type | GI Bill Coverage | Gap |
|---------------|-----------------|-----|
| Apprenticeships | Covered (reduced stipend) | Stipend decreases annually during apprenticeship |
| On-the-job training | Covered (reduced stipend) | Same declining stipend problem |
| Short-term certifications | Limited coverage | Many high-value certifications take <1 semester; benefits poorly suited |
| Coding bootcamps | Limited (only VA-approved programs) | Many quality programs not approved |
| Microcredentials | Not covered | Industry-recognized credentials often excluded |

### Delimiting Date Issues

Veterans must use GI Bill benefits within a set period after separation:

- **Forever GI Bill (2017)**: Eliminated delimiting date for veterans who separated after January 1, 2013
- **Pre-2013 separations**: Still subject to 15-year delimiting date
- **Impact**: Older veterans who delayed education may lose benefits
- **Inconsistency**: Purple Heart recipients have no delimiting date regardless of separation date

### Part-Time Enrollment

- Housing allowance is prorated for less than full-time enrollment
- Part-time enrollment consumes entitlement months at a reduced rate
- Many student veterans work or have family obligations requiring part-time study
- The penalty structure discourages flexible enrollment patterns

### Economic Return on Investment

The GI Bill produces substantial economic returns:

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Original GI Bill ROI (1944-1956) | $7 returned for every $1 invested | Bound & Turner, 2002 |
| Post-9/11 GI Bill estimated ROI | $3-5 for every $1 invested | RAND, 2021 |
| Lifetime earnings premium (bachelor's degree) | $800,000-$1.2 million | Georgetown CEW, 2023 |
| Tax revenue increase from educated veterans | Estimated $2.4 billion annually | SVA analysis, 2023 |

### Disparities

- **For-profit school outcomes**: Veterans at for-profit schools graduate at less than half the rate of those at public schools and have 60% higher default rates
- **Women veterans**: Slightly higher GI Bill utilization rate but more likely to be part-time and receive reduced benefits
- **Minority veterans**: Black veterans are disproportionately enrolled at for-profit schools (29% vs. 18% overall) (NCES, 2024)
- **Rural veterans**: Fewer school choices; may rely on online programs that carry housing allowance penalties

## Recent Developments (2024-2025)

### Legislative

- **90/10 rule enforcement**: First enforcement actions under reformed rule beginning to affect for-profit school eligibility
- **GI Bill housing parity debates**: Proposals to equalize online student housing allowances
- **VET-TEC expansion**: VA's technology education program expanded to additional bootcamp providers
- **Delimiting date proposals**: Legislation introduced to eliminate pre-2013 delimiting dates

### Administrative

- **VA IT modernization**: New claims processing system reducing initial processing times
- **School compliance reviews**: Increased inspections of for-profit schools receiving GI Bill funds
- **Risk-based oversight**: VA implementing risk-based model to focus oversight on highest-risk schools
- **STEM scholarship expansion**: Additional funding for veterans pursuing STEM degrees beyond 36 months

### Challenges

- **Cost increases**: Tuition inflation continues to outpace GI Bill cap increases
- **Housing market**: Rising rents in many markets erode housing allowance value
- **For-profit adaptations**: Some schools restructuring to avoid 90/10 enforcement while maintaining predatory practices
- **Benefit exhaustion**: 36-month cap is insufficient for many graduate and professional programs

## The Gap Between Promise and Performance

| Promise | Performance | Gap |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| GI Bill covers college costs | Tuition cap does not cover many private schools; housing allowance inadequate | Cost burden shifts to veterans |
| Benefits protect veterans from bad schools | For-profit schools still enroll 18% of GI Bill students with poor outcomes | Insufficient oversight and enforcement |
| Benefits serve modern learners | Online, part-time, and non-degree students are penalized | Benefit structure is outdated |
| Payments arrive on time | Processing errors create financial hardship | IT and staffing inadequacies |
| Service leads to opportunity | Outcomes vary dramatically by school type | Promise depends on school choice |
