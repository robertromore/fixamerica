# Education Benefits: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Modernizing the GI Bill requires legislative action to protect veterans from predatory schools, update benefit structures for modern learners, and improve the delivery of education benefits. This document provides draft legal text for key reforms identified in [Solutions](07-solutions.md), addressing the three core challenges: for-profit school exploitation, outdated benefit design, and inadequate oversight infrastructure.

## Federal Legislation

### GI Bill Modernization Act

**Purpose**: Update GI Bill benefit structures to reflect the realities of modern education, including online learning, non-degree training, and flexible enrollment patterns.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "GI Bill Modernization Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) The Post-9/11 GI Bill (chapter 33 of title 38, United
    States Code) was designed primarily for full-time,
    in-person, four-year degree programs, yet a growing
    share of veterans pursue education through online
    programs, part-time enrollment, vocational training,
    and short-term credential programs.
(2) Veterans enrolled exclusively in online programs receive
    a housing allowance equal to 50 percent of the national
    average, regardless of their actual housing costs, creating
    a penalty that discourages flexible learning arrangements
    and disproportionately harms disabled veterans, rural
    veterans, and veterans with caregiving responsibilities.
(3) The labor market increasingly values industry-recognized
    credentials, apprenticeships, and short-term training
    programs, yet GI Bill coverage for non-degree programs
    remains limited and poorly structured.
(4) Apprenticeship stipends decline annually during the
    apprenticeship period, creating a financial disincentive
    for veterans to pursue apprenticeship programs that
    produce strong employment outcomes.
(5) Veterans who separated from service before January 1,
    2013, remain subject to a 15-year delimiting date that
    may cause them to lose earned education benefits.

SEC. 3. HOUSING ALLOWANCE PARITY FOR ONLINE STUDENTS.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 3313(c) of title 38, United States
    Code, is amended to provide that an individual enrolled
    in a program of education pursued exclusively through
    distance learning shall receive a monthly housing
    allowance equal to—
    (1) the monthly amount of the basic allowance for
        housing payable under section 403 of title 37 for
        a member with dependents in pay grade E-5 residing
        in the area in which the individual resides; or
    (2) if the individual's residence cannot be verified,
        the national average of such basic allowance for
        housing.

(b) PRORATION.—The housing allowance under subsection (a)
    shall be prorated for individuals enrolled on a less
    than full-time basis in the same manner as housing
    allowances for individuals attending in-person programs.

(c) EFFECTIVE DATE.—This section shall take effect on August
    1 following the date of enactment of this Act.

SEC. 4. APPRENTICESHIP AND ON-THE-JOB TRAINING REFORM.

(a) FLAT-RATE STIPEND.—Section 3313(g) of title 38, United
    States Code, is amended to provide that the monthly
    housing stipend for an eligible individual enrolled in
    an apprenticeship or on-the-job training program shall
    be the full monthly housing allowance applicable under
    section 3313(c) for the duration of the program,
    without annual reduction.

(b) ENTITLEMENT CONSUMPTION.—Entitlement months consumed
    during apprenticeship or on-the-job training shall be
    calculated at a rate of 0.75 months of entitlement
    for each month of training, recognizing the employment
    component of such programs.

SEC. 5. NON-DEGREE TRAINING EXPANSION.

(a) SHORT-TERM PROGRAMS.—Chapter 33 of title 38, United
    States Code, is amended to authorize coverage for
    programs of education as short as 8 weeks in duration
    if—
    (1) the program leads to an industry-recognized
        credential on the Employer-Validated Credential
        List maintained under subsection (c); and
    (2) the program is approved by the State Approving
        Agency of the State in which the institution is
        located.

(b) MICROCREDENTIALS.—The Secretary of Veterans Affairs
    shall develop regulations for coverage of stackable
    microcredential programs that, when combined, lead
    to a recognized credential or are accepted for
    academic credit at a degree-granting institution.

(c) EMPLOYER-VALIDATED CREDENTIAL LIST.—
    (1) The Secretary, in coordination with the Secretary
        of Labor, shall establish and maintain an
        Employer-Validated Credential List identifying
        industry-recognized credentials with demonstrated
        employment value.
    (2) The list shall be updated not less than annually
        based on labor market data, employer surveys, and
        credential outcome analysis.
    (3) Programs leading to credentials on the list shall
        be eligible for GI Bill coverage under this section.

(d) VET-TEC PERMANENCE.—The Veteran Employment Through
    Technology Education Courses program under section
    3702 of title 38, United States Code, is hereby made
    a permanent program with annual authorization of
    appropriations.

SEC. 6. ENTITLEMENT FLEXIBILITY.

(a) PARTIAL-MONTH USAGE.—The Secretary shall develop
    regulations allowing entitlement consumption in
    increments of less than one month for short-term
    programs under section 5, with a minimum increment
    of one week.

(b) PROGRAM SWITCHING.—Section 3322 of title 38, United
    States Code, is amended to allow an eligible individual
    who has elected to receive educational assistance under
    chapter 33 to revoke such election and receive
    assistance under chapter 30 if—
    (1) the individual has remaining entitlement under
        chapter 30; and
    (2) the Secretary determines that assistance under
        chapter 30 would be more advantageous to the
        individual.

(c) DELIMITING DATE ELIMINATION.—Section 3321 of title 38,
    United States Code, is amended to eliminate the
    delimiting date for all eligible individuals regardless
    of date of separation from active duty.

SEC. 7. STEM AND HEALTHCARE EXTENSION.

(a) EXPANDED ELIGIBILITY.—Section 3320 of title 38, United
    States Code, is amended to extend the additional 9
    months of entitlement available under the Edith Nourse
    Rogers STEM Scholarship to individuals enrolled in—
    (1) programs designated as STEM programs by the
        National Science Foundation;
    (2) nursing and allied health programs; and
    (3) teaching credential programs in STEM fields.

(b) INCREASED MAXIMUM.—The maximum additional entitlement
    under subsection (a) is increased from 9 months to
    12 months.

SEC. 8. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

(a) There are authorized to be appropriated such sums as
    may be necessary to carry out this Act.

(b) The Congressional Budget Office shall provide a cost
    estimate within 60 days of introduction of this Act.

SEC. 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

Except as otherwise provided, this Act shall take effect on
the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Eliminates the online learning penalty by basing housing on the veteran's residence ZIP code rather than providing a flat reduced rate. COVID-19 temporary parity demonstrated this is feasible.
- **Section 4**: Fixes the declining apprenticeship stipend that discourages veterans from completing programs with 94% success rates when adequately supported.
- **Section 5**: Brings the GI Bill into alignment with the modern labor market by covering short-term credentials, microcredentials, and technology training.
- **Section 6**: Provides flexibility for veterans to use benefits across programs without irrevocable elections or whole-month minimums.
- **Section 7**: Extends STEM scholarship support to address the 36-month cap problem for longer programs.

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Cost of housing parity (estimated $500-800M annually) | ROI analysis shows increased completion rates; COVID-19 parity showed no fraud increase |
| Non-degree training quality control | Employer-Validated Credential List provides quality filter; SAA approval required |
| Moral hazard in apprenticeship flat rate | Apprenticeship completers earn $72,000 median; strong employment outcomes justify investment |
| Complexity of partial-month entitlement | IT modernization required; phased implementation recommended |

**Refinements**:

- **Stronger version**: Increase the 36-month cap to 48 months for all programs; index tuition cap to actual tuition inflation
- **Fallback version**: Implement housing parity and apprenticeship reform first; phase in non-degree expansion
- **Pilot option**: Pilot non-degree credential coverage at select institutions before national rollout

### Veteran Education Protection Act

**Purpose**: Establish outcome-based eligibility standards for schools receiving GI Bill funds, strengthen oversight capacity, and protect veterans from predatory institutions.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Veteran Education Protection Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) For-profit colleges enroll 18 percent of GI Bill students
    but receive 25 percent of GI Bill spending, while
    producing graduation rates below 25 percent and student
    loan default rates more than 60 percent higher than
    public institutions.
(2) Veterans at for-profit schools face disproportionately
    poor outcomes, including higher debt, lower graduation
    rates, and worse employment prospects.
(3) Black veterans are disproportionately enrolled at for-
    profit institutions (29 percent compared to 18 percent
    overall), perpetuating historical inequities in GI Bill
    access.
(4) State Approving Agencies, the primary oversight mechanism
    for GI Bill-eligible schools, are funded at approximately
    $25 million annually, insufficient to conduct adequate
    compliance reviews.
(5) The closure of GI Bill-eligible schools mid-term leaves
    veterans without degrees, without remaining entitlement,
    and often with student debt.

SEC. 3. OUTCOME-BASED ELIGIBILITY STANDARDS.

(a) MINIMUM STANDARDS.—No institution of higher learning
    shall be eligible to receive educational assistance
    payments under chapters 30, 33, or 35 of title 38,
    United States Code, unless the institution meets the
    following minimum standards for its GI Bill recipient
    population:
    (1) COMPLETION RATE.—A completion rate of not less
        than 30 percent, calculated as the share of GI Bill
        recipients who complete a degree or credential
        within 150 percent of the normal program length.
    (2) EMPLOYMENT RATE.—An employment rate of not less
        than 50 percent, calculated as the share of
        graduates employed in a field related to their
        program of study within 12 months of completion.
    (3) DEBT-TO-EARNINGS RATIO.—A median debt-to-annual-
        earnings ratio of not more than 1.5 to 1 for
        graduates, calculated using the first three years
        of earnings following completion.
    (4) COHORT DEFAULT RATE.—A three-year cohort default
        rate of not more than 15 percent.

(b) DATA COLLECTION.—
    (1) The Secretary of Veterans Affairs, in coordination
        with the Secretary of Education and the Commissioner
        of the Bureau of Labor Statistics, shall collect
        and publish data necessary to calculate the
        standards under subsection (a).
    (2) Institutions receiving educational assistance
        payments under title 38 shall provide such data
        as the Secretary may require.

(c) PROBATIONARY STATUS.—
    (1) An institution that fails to meet one or more
        standards under subsection (a) shall be placed on
        probationary status for a period of not more than
        2 academic years.
    (2) During probationary status, the institution shall—
        (A) develop and submit a corrective action plan
            to the Secretary;
        (B) provide enhanced disclosures to prospective
            GI Bill students, including a statement that
            the institution is on probation and the
            specific standards not met; and
        (C) be subject to increased oversight by the
            applicable State Approving Agency.

(d) LOSS OF ELIGIBILITY.—An institution that fails to meet
    the minimum standards under subsection (a) at the
    conclusion of the probationary period shall lose
    eligibility to receive educational assistance payments
    under title 38.

(e) APPEALS.—An institution subject to loss of eligibility
    may appeal to the Secretary within 60 days. The
    Secretary shall render a decision within 90 days.

(f) SMALL POPULATION EXCEPTION.—Institutions with fewer than
    25 GI Bill recipients in any measurement cohort shall
    be evaluated using a 3-year rolling average to ensure
    statistical reliability.

(g) PHASE-IN.—
    (1) Data collection under subsection (b) shall begin
        not later than 1 year after the date of enactment.
    (2) Probationary status under subsection (c) shall
        first be applied not earlier than 3 years after
        the date of enactment.
    (3) Loss of eligibility under subsection (d) shall
        first be applied not earlier than 5 years after
        the date of enactment.

SEC. 4. STATE APPROVING AGENCY STRENGTHENING.

(a) FUNDING.—Section 3674 of title 38, United States Code,
    is amended to authorize appropriations for State
    Approving Agencies of not less than $75,000,000
    annually.

(b) INSPECTION REQUIREMENTS.—Each State Approving Agency
    shall conduct a compliance inspection of—
    (1) each for-profit institution receiving educational
        assistance payments under title 38 not less
        frequently than annually; and
    (2) each other institution receiving such payments
        not less frequently than once every 3 years.

(c) ENFORCEMENT AUTHORITY.—State Approving Agencies are
    authorized to—
    (1) issue binding compliance orders to institutions
        found to be in violation of approval standards;
    (2) impose conditional approval status requiring
        corrective action within 90 days; and
    (3) suspend approval of new GI Bill enrollments
        pending correction of serious violations.

(d) TRAINING AND STANDARDS.—The Secretary shall establish—
    (1) standardized training requirements for SAA
        compliance personnel;
    (2) a national certification program for SAA
        inspectors; and
    (3) uniform inspection protocols and reporting
        standards.

(e) INTERAGENCY DATA SHARING.—The Secretary of Veterans
    Affairs and the Secretary of Education shall establish
    data sharing agreements to ensure SAAs have access to
    all relevant institutional data, including Federal
    Student Aid compliance data, accreditation actions,
    and complaint histories.

SEC. 5. MANDATORY PRE-ENROLLMENT COUNSELING.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Any individual seeking to use educational
    assistance under chapters 30, 33, or 35 of title 38
    to enroll at an institution on probationary status
    under section 3(c) or on the VA education risk
    watchlist shall complete a pre-enrollment counseling
    session before enrollment certification is processed.

(b) CONTENTS.—A pre-enrollment counseling session shall
    include—
    (1) school outcome data, including graduation rates,
        employment rates, and complaint history;
    (2) comparison with at least 3 alternative
        institutions offering similar programs in the
        same geographic area or online;
    (3) financial impact analysis, including estimated
        out-of-pocket costs and post-graduation earnings;
        and
    (4) information on the individual's rights as a GI
        Bill student.

(c) DELIVERY.—Counseling under this section may be provided
    in person, by telephone, or through a synchronous
    virtual session. Asynchronous online modules alone
    shall not satisfy the requirement.

(d) TIMING.—Counseling shall not delay enrollment by more
    than 10 business days.

(e) CHOICE PRESERVED.—Completion of counseling shall not
    restrict an individual's choice of institution. The
    individual may enroll at any eligible institution
    after receiving counseling.

SEC. 6. SCHOOL CLOSURE PROTECTIONS.

(a) ENTITLEMENT RESTORATION.—When an institution ceases
    operations or loses GI Bill eligibility while a
    veteran is enrolled, the Secretary shall—
    (1) restore all entitlement months consumed at that
        institution during the final term of enrollment;
        and
    (2) provide housing allowance continuation for up to
        4 months while the veteran transitions to a new
        institution.

(b) CREDIT RECOVERY.—The Secretary, in coordination with the
    Secretary of Education, shall assist affected veterans
    in identifying institutions that accept transfer
    credits from the closed institution.

(c) EARLY WARNING SYSTEM.—The Secretary shall establish an
    early warning system using financial, enrollment, and
    compliance data to identify institutions at risk of
    closure, and shall provide notice to enrolled GI Bill
    students not less than 6 months before projected
    closure when feasible.

SEC. 7. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

(a) IN GENERAL.—There are authorized to be appropriated—
    (1) $75,000,000 for each fiscal year for State
        Approving Agency operations under section 4;
    (2) $25,000,000 for each fiscal year for data
        collection and outcome monitoring under section 3;
    (3) $15,000,000 for each fiscal year for pre-
        enrollment counseling under section 5; and
    (4) $10,000,000 for each fiscal year for school
        closure protections under section 6.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

Except as otherwise provided, this Act shall take effect on
the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Creates minimum outcome standards that would primarily affect the worst-performing schools. The 30% graduation threshold is deliberately set below the national average to target only the most egregious performers, with a generous phase-in period.
- **Section 4**: Triples SAA funding and provides enforcement tools that SAAs currently lack.
- **Section 5**: Preserves veteran choice while ensuring informed decision-making at high-risk schools.
- **Section 6**: Addresses the recurring problem of school closures leaving veterans without degrees or remaining benefits.

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| For-profit industry lobbying against outcome standards | Standards target only worst performers; bipartisan framing as fiscal responsibility |
| School choice objections | Section 5 preserves choice; counseling does not restrict enrollment |
| Data collection burden on institutions | Build on existing IPEDS and College Scorecard infrastructure |
| Small school statistical reliability | Section 3(f) provides rolling average for small populations |
| Risk of schools gaming metrics | Multiple overlapping metrics make gaming difficult; SAA inspections verify data |

**Refinements**:

- **Stronger version**: Lower graduation threshold to 25%; add ban on high-pressure recruitment tactics; require schools to reimburse veterans for failed programs
- **Fallback version**: Implement counseling and SAA strengthening without outcome-based eligibility; pursue eligibility standards through regulation
- **Pilot option**: Apply outcome-based standards to for-profit schools first before extending to all institutions

### Veteran Credential Translation Act

**Purpose**: Create a systematic national framework for translating military training and experience into civilian academic credit and professional credentials.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Veteran Credential Translation
Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) Service members receive extensive technical training in
    fields including electronics, medicine, logistics, and
    engineering, yet much of this training does not
    translate to civilian academic credit or professional
    credentials.
(2) Veterans often must retake courses in subjects they
    mastered during military service, consuming GI Bill
    entitlement months unnecessarily and delaying their
    entry into the civilian workforce.
(3) The American Council on Education (ACE) provides credit
    recommendations for military training, but acceptance
    of these recommendations by institutions is voluntary
    and inconsistent.
(4) Many civilian licensing bodies do not recognize military
    training or experience as qualifying for licensure,
    requiring veterans to complete redundant education.

SEC. 3. NATIONAL CREDENTIAL TRANSLATION DATABASE.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary of Defense, in coordination
    with the Secretary of Veterans Affairs and the Secretary
    of Education, shall establish and maintain a National
    Military Credential Translation Database.

(b) CONTENTS.—The database shall include, for each military
    occupational specialty and rating—
    (1) ACE credit recommendations;
    (2) equivalent civilian credentials and certifications;
    (3) gap analysis identifying additional training
        required to achieve civilian credentials;
    (4) recommended bridge programs to fill identified
        gaps; and
    (5) a list of institutions that accept the credit
        recommendations.

(c) ACCESSIBILITY.—The database shall be—
    (1) publicly accessible online;
    (2) integrated into the VA GI Bill Comparison Tool;
        and
    (3) available through the Transition Assistance
        Program.

SEC. 4. CREDIT ACCEPTANCE REQUIREMENTS.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Any institution receiving educational
    assistance payments under title 38, United States Code,
    shall—
    (1) evaluate ACE credit recommendations for all
        enrolled GI Bill students within 30 days of
        enrollment;
    (2) accept credit recommendations that are relevant
        to the student's program of study, unless the
        institution provides a written, academically
        justified reason for denial; and
    (3) publish its credit acceptance rates for military
        training on the VA GI Bill Comparison Tool.

(b) REPORTING.—Institutions shall report annually to the
    Secretary of Veterans Affairs on the number of ACE
    credits requested by GI Bill students, the number
    accepted, and the reasons for any denials.

SEC. 5. BRIDGE PROGRAMS.

(a) GRANTS.—The Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall award
    competitive grants to institutions of higher learning
    and training providers to develop bridge programs that—
    (1) fill specific gaps between military training and
        civilian credential requirements;
    (2) target the 25 military occupational specialties
        with the highest volume of separating service
        members; and
    (3) lead to industry-recognized civilian credentials
        within 6 months or fewer.

(b) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
    $50,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through
    2031 for grants under this section.

SEC. 6. LICENSING BODY PARTNERSHIPS.

(a) The Secretary of Defense shall engage with civilian
    licensing bodies to establish military experience
    pathways to professional licensure in fields with
    high military-to-civilian transition volume,
    including—
    (1) healthcare (nursing, emergency medicine,
        radiology);
    (2) transportation (commercial driving, aviation
        maintenance);
    (3) information technology (network administration,
        cybersecurity);
    (4) skilled trades (electrical, plumbing, HVAC); and
    (5) law enforcement and security.

(b) ANNUAL REPORT.—The Secretary of Defense shall report
    annually to Congress on the progress of licensing
    body partnerships, including the number of pathways
    established and the number of veterans utilizing
    such pathways.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Centralizes credential translation information that currently exists in scattered, inconsistent formats.
- **Section 4**: Shifts the burden from veterans to institutions by requiring schools to evaluate and accept relevant military credits.
- **Section 5**: Funds targeted programs that fill the specific gaps between military training and civilian credentials.
- **Section 6**: Addresses the licensing barrier that forces veterans to repeat training for professional certifications.

## State Model Legislation

### Veterans Education Counseling and Protection Act (State Model)

**Purpose**: Establish state-level programs to assist veterans in making informed education decisions and provide additional oversight of schools recruiting veterans.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Veterans Education
Counseling and Protection Act".

SECTION 2. VETERANS EDUCATION COUNSELING PROGRAM.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The [State Department of Veterans Affairs]
    shall establish a Veterans Education Counseling Program
    to help veterans of this State make informed education
    decisions when using federal education benefits.

(b) SERVICES.—The program shall provide—
    (1) individualized education counseling, including
        school comparison and program selection assistance;
    (2) information on school outcome data, including
        graduation rates, employment outcomes, and student
        debt levels;
    (3) referral to federal GI Bill counseling resources;
    (4) assistance with military credential translation
        and credit evaluation; and
    (5) guidance on state-specific education benefits
        and tuition waivers available to veterans.

SECTION 3. STATE APPROVING AGENCY ENHANCEMENT.

(a) The [State Approving Agency] shall—
    (1) maintain a publicly accessible list of schools
        in this State on probationary or cautionary
        status with the VA or the Department of Education;
    (2) publish an annual report on the outcomes of
        veterans at schools in this State, disaggregated
        by institution type; and
    (3) refer complaints received from veterans to
        the appropriate federal oversight agency within
        10 business days.

SECTION 4. PROHIBITION ON DECEPTIVE RECRUITMENT.

(a) No institution of higher learning in this State shall—
    (1) use military-themed branding or imagery to
        imply an affiliation with the United States
        Armed Forces that does not exist;
    (2) make false or misleading claims about job
        placement rates, salary outcomes, or credit
        transferability to prospective veteran students;
    (3) station recruiters on or adjacent to military
        installations without written authorization
        from the installation commander; or
    (4) enroll a veteran using federal education benefits
        through high-pressure tactics, including same-day
        enrollment requirements.

(b) ENFORCEMENT.—Violations of this section shall be subject
    to enforcement by the [State Attorney General] under
    the State consumer protection act.

(c) PENALTIES.—An institution found in violation of this
    section shall be subject to—
    (1) civil penalties of not less than $10,000 per
        violation; and
    (2) referral to the State Approving Agency for review
        of GI Bill eligibility approval.

SECTION 5. APPROPRIATION.

There is appropriated from the general fund the sum of
$[amount] for the fiscal year ending [date] for the
purposes of this Act.
```

## Regulatory Framework

### VA Education Benefit Payment Standards

**Existing Authority**: 38 U.S.C. Section 501 gives the Secretary broad authority to prescribe regulations for VA operations.

**Draft Regulation**:

```text
PART 21—ADJUDICATION: EDUCATION BENEFIT PAYMENT STANDARDS

Sec. 21.9000 Purpose.

This part establishes standards for the timely and accurate
delivery of educational assistance payments under chapters
30, 33, and 35 of title 38, United States Code.

Sec. 21.9001 Processing Timelines.

(a) The Veterans Benefits Administration shall process
    initial education benefit claims within 21 days of
    receipt of a complete enrollment certification.

(b) Supplemental claims and enrollment changes shall be
    processed within 14 days of receipt.

(c) The first housing allowance payment for a new
    enrollment period shall be issued within 14 days of
    the enrollment start date.

Sec. 21.9002 Advance Payment.

(a) An eligible individual enrolling for the first time
    or beginning a new enrollment period may request an
    advance housing allowance payment.

(b) The advance payment shall equal one month of the
    applicable housing allowance and shall be issued not
    later than 7 days before the enrollment start date.

(c) The advance payment shall be recovered through pro-rata
    reduction of subsequent housing allowance payments over
    the enrollment period.

Sec. 21.9003 Error Correction.

(a) When an overpayment results from VA processing error
    rather than veteran misrepresentation, the Secretary
    shall waive collection of the overpayment.

(b) When an underpayment is identified, the Secretary shall
    issue a corrective payment within 14 days.

Sec. 21.9004 Automated Enrollment Verification.

(a) The Secretary shall establish automated enrollment
    verification agreements with institutions receiving
    educational assistance payments.

(b) Participating institutions shall transmit enrollment
    data electronically in a format prescribed by the
    Secretary.

(c) Automated verification shall replace manual certification
    at participating institutions, reducing processing
    time and errors.
```

### VA Education Program Risk-Based Oversight Standards

**Draft Regulation**:

```text
PART 21—ADJUDICATION: RISK-BASED OVERSIGHT

Sec. 21.9100 Purpose.

This part establishes a risk-based oversight framework for
institutions receiving educational assistance payments under
title 38.

Sec. 21.9101 Risk Assessment.

(a) The Secretary shall maintain a risk assessment model
    that evaluates each institution receiving educational
    assistance payments based on—
    (1) graduation and completion rates for GI Bill
        recipients;
    (2) student loan default rates;
    (3) complaint volume through the GI Bill Feedback
        System;
    (4) ratio of marketing expenditure to instructional
        expenditure;
    (5) SAA inspection findings;
    (6) accreditation status and actions; and
    (7) financial stability indicators.

(b) Institutions shall be classified as low risk, moderate
    risk, or high risk based on the model in subsection (a).

Sec. 21.9102 Oversight Frequency.

(a) High-risk institutions shall be subject to compliance
    review not less than annually.

(b) Moderate-risk institutions shall be subject to
    compliance review not less than every 2 years.

(c) Low-risk institutions shall be subject to compliance
    review not less than every 4 years.

Sec. 21.9103 Education Risk Watchlist.

(a) The Secretary shall maintain and publish an Education
    Risk Watchlist identifying institutions classified
    as high risk under section 21.9101.

(b) Prospective GI Bill students seeking to enroll at a
    watchlist institution shall receive notice of the
    institution's risk status.

(c) The watchlist shall be updated quarterly and published
    on the VA GI Bill Comparison Tool.
```

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

**Spending Clause (Article I, Section 8)**:

- Congress has broad authority to condition federal spending on meeting quality standards
- Outcome-based eligibility standards are a permissible exercise of the spending power, analogous to conditions on other federal education funding
- Schools voluntarily participate in GI Bill programs; standards apply to participation, not to the right to operate

**Due Process (Fifth Amendment)**:

- Schools facing loss of eligibility have property interest in continued program participation
- The phase-in period (Section 3(g) of the Veteran Education Protection Act) provides adequate notice
- Appeals process (Section 3(e)) satisfies procedural due process requirements
- Pre-enrollment counseling preserves veteran choice and does not restrict access

**First Amendment**:

- Restrictions on deceptive recruitment (state model legislation) are permissible regulation of commercial speech under *Central Hudson Gas & Electric v. Public Service Commission*, 447 U.S. 557 (1980)
- Mandatory disclosures are permissible under *Zauderer v. Office of Disciplinary Counsel*, 471 U.S. 626 (1985)

### Preemption Questions

| State Action | Federal Impact |
|-------------|----------------|
| State education counseling programs | Supplement federal system; no preemption issue |
| State deceptive recruitment prohibitions | Consumer protection enforcement; likely not preempted |
| State SAA enhanced reporting | Within SAA authority; supported by federal framework |
| State-specific tuition waivers for veterans | Complement GI Bill; no conflict |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### GI Bill Modernization Act

**Potential Loopholes**:

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Housing allowance gaming | Veterans could claim residence in high-cost areas while living elsewhere to maximize housing allowance | Medium |
| Credential list capture | Industry groups could lobby to include low-value credentials on the Employer-Validated Credential List | Medium |
| Short-term program churn | Schools could create a series of 8-week programs to maximize tuition payments with minimal educational value | Medium |
| Apprenticeship enrollment without completion intent | Higher flat-rate stipend could attract enrollees without genuine completion intent | Low |

**Shortcomings**:

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Cost uncertainty | Housing parity cost depends on enrollment shifts that are difficult to predict | Budget projection difficulty |
| IT systems required | Partial-month entitlement, residence-based BAH, and credential tracking require significant IT investment | Legacy system limitations |
| Employer-Validated Credential List maintenance | List requires continuous updating as labor market evolves | Ongoing administrative burden |
| Does not address tuition inflation | Benefits are still indexed to tuition caps that may not keep pace with costs | Structural limitation |

**Rectification Procedures**:

1. Require residence verification through VA records, tax filings, or utility documentation to prevent housing allowance gaming
2. Establish an independent credential review board with employer, veteran, and academic representation to maintain list integrity
3. Require short-term programs to demonstrate minimum 50% employment placement rates to maintain eligibility
4. Cap entitlement consumption for apprenticeship programs to prevent enrollment without completion intent
5. Authorize annual tuition cap adjustment based on Higher Education Price Index rather than general CPI

### Veteran Education Protection Act

**Potential Loopholes**:

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Metric gaming | Schools could manipulate graduation rates by encouraging low-performing students to withdraw before being counted | High |
| Non-profit conversion | For-profit schools could convert to non-profit status to avoid heightened scrutiny while maintaining predatory practices | High |
| Data manipulation | Schools could report inflated employment data or selectively survey successful graduates | Medium |
| Probation cycling | Schools could improve marginally during probation, regain full status, then decline again | Medium |
| SAA capture | State Approving Agencies could be influenced by local economic pressure to approve noncompliant schools | Medium |

**Shortcomings**:

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Phase-in delay | 5-year timeline before loss of eligibility means veterans at failing schools continue to be harmed during transition | Political necessity of gradual implementation |
| Data lag | Outcome data is inherently retrospective; current students may face different conditions than measured cohorts | Measurement limitation |
| Does not address all school types | Outcome standards apply to all schools but worst outcomes concentrated at for-profit institutions; broad application may catch some legitimate programs | Overinclusion risk |
| Counseling capacity | Mandatory pre-enrollment counseling requires trained counselors that VA may not have in sufficient numbers | Workforce constraint |

**Rectification Procedures**:

1. Define graduation rate calculation precisely in regulations to prevent withdrawal manipulation; use IPEDS methodology
2. Apply heightened scrutiny to recently converted non-profit institutions for a minimum of 5 years after conversion
3. Require third-party verification of employment outcomes data; cross-reference with state unemployment insurance records
4. Establish a minimum 3-year sustained compliance period before removal from probation
5. Implement conflict-of-interest rules for SAA personnel; require annual disclosures; tie SAA funding to compliance performance

### Cross-Cutting Issues

**General Implementation Concerns**:

- All three federal bills require sustained Congressional appropriations commitment, which is historically unreliable for oversight and administrative functions
- IT modernization for credential tracking, partial-month entitlement, and residence-based housing calculations requires significant systems investment
- Interagency coordination between VA, DoE, DoD, and DoL has historically been slow and incomplete
- For-profit industry lobbying will intensify against outcome-based standards; early bipartisan coalition building is essential
- Credential translation requires buy-in from thousands of educational institutions and licensing bodies, which cannot be mandated but must be incentivized

**Ongoing Monitoring Requirements**:

| Requirement | Frequency | Responsible Entity |
|-------------|-----------|---------------------|
| School outcome data reporting | Annually | VA Education Service, NCES |
| SAA inspection completion rates | Quarterly | VA oversight of SAAs |
| Housing allowance adequacy review | Annually | VA Education Service |
| Credential translation database updates | Continuously | DoD-VA interagency working group |
| For-profit school compliance monitoring | Quarterly | SAAs, VA, DoE |
| Pre-enrollment counseling completion rates | Quarterly | VA Education Service |
| Payment processing accuracy and timeliness | Monthly | VA OIG |
