# Education Benefits: Root Causes

## Structural Causes

### Benefit Design Anchored in a 20th-Century Model

The Post-9/11 GI Bill was designed around the traditional four-year, full-time, on-campus college experience. This model does not reflect how a growing share of veterans actually pursue education:

**Assumptions baked into the system**:

| Design Assumption | Modern Reality |
|-------------------|----------------|
| Students attend full-time, in person | Many veterans attend part-time or online due to work, family, disability |
| Bachelor's degrees are the primary goal | Many veterans seek vocational training, certifications, or short-term programs |
| Housing costs track military BAH rates | Civilian housing markets differ from military installation areas |
| Students enroll in a single program at one school | Many students transfer, combine programs, or attend multiple institutions |
| 36 months is sufficient | Graduate programs, professional degrees, and career changes may require more |

**Result**: The benefit structure rewards traditional students and penalizes non-traditional ones -- precisely the veterans who may need the most support.

### The Online Learning Penalty

The decision to provide only 50% of the national average housing allowance for exclusively online students reflects an outdated assumption that online students have lower costs:

- Online students still pay rent, food, and living expenses
- Many online students are disabled veterans who cannot attend in person
- Many online students are in rural areas without nearby schools
- COVID-19 temporarily equalized online housing rates, proving it was feasible
- After COVID-19, rates reverted to the penalty structure

**The perverse incentive**: Some veterans enroll in one in-person class they do not need just to qualify for the full housing allowance -- wasting both their entitlement months and the government's money.

### For-Profit Industry Structure

The for-profit education industry's business model creates structural misalignment with veteran interests:

**The Revenue Model**

- For-profit schools derive revenue primarily from federal financial aid
- Shareholder pressure demands profit maximization
- Marketing and recruitment budgets often exceed instructional spending
- Student outcomes (graduation, employment) are not revenue drivers -- enrollment is

**Why Veterans Are Targeted**

1. **Generous benefits**: GI Bill benefits are among the highest per-student federal education payments
2. **90/10 loophole (pre-2023)**: GI Bill funds counted as "private" revenue, enabling schools to access additional federal aid
3. **Motivated population**: Veterans are eager to improve their civilian prospects
4. **Institutional trust**: Veterans accustomed to institutional authority may trust official-sounding schools
5. **Transition vulnerability**: The transition period creates urgency to enroll quickly

**The fundamental problem**: For-profit schools' fiduciary duty is to shareholders, not students. This structural conflict of interest cannot be resolved through regulation alone -- it must be addressed through oversight, accountability, and benefit design that rewards outcomes.

### Tuition Inflation and Benefit Erosion

GI Bill benefits are indexed to in-state tuition at public schools, but tuition has increased faster than inflation:

| Period | Average Public University Tuition Growth | General Inflation | Source |
|--------|------------------------------------------|-------------------|--------|
| 2008-2024 | ~3.5% per year | ~2.5% per year | NCES/BLS |

**The erosion effect**: Each year, the GI Bill covers a slightly smaller share of the total cost of attendance. While tuition is capped, fees, materials, and living costs are not fully covered.

**Contributing factors**:

- State disinvestment in public higher education shifts costs to students
- Ancillary fees (technology, lab, activity) increase faster than tuition
- Textbook and materials costs add $1,200+/year beyond the $1,000 stipend
- Schools may increase fees for veterans knowing benefits will cover them

## Regulatory and Oversight Causes

### Weak State Approving Agency System

State Approving Agencies (SAAs) are the primary gatekeepers for GI Bill-eligible schools, but the system is structurally weak:

- SAAs are state agencies funded by federal VA dollars
- Funding has not kept pace with the number of schools and programs to oversee
- SAA staff are often part-time or undertrained
- Conflict of interest: state economic interest in keeping schools open
- Inspection cycles are infrequent (some schools go years without review)
- SAAs lack enforcement authority beyond recommending withdrawal of VA approval

### Delayed Enforcement Cycle

When problems are identified at schools, the enforcement response is slow:

1. **Complaints accumulate** over months or years
2. **Investigation** by SAA, VA, or GAO takes 6-18 months
3. **Warning letters** give schools time to respond (or restructure)
4. **Caution/suspension** may take years to implement
5. **School closes** -- veterans lose credits and remaining entitlement
6. **Students bear the cost** of the entire enforcement delay

**The recurring pattern**: By the time enforcement action occurs, hundreds or thousands of veterans have already been harmed.

### Information Asymmetry

Veterans making education decisions face significant information disadvantages:

- **School marketing vs. reality**: Schools' marketing materials emphasize success stories; outcome data is hard to find
- **GI Bill Comparison Tool limitations**: VA's tool provides some school data but lacks comprehensive outcomes information
- **Credential value opacity**: Veterans cannot easily assess whether a degree or certificate will be valued by employers
- **Transfer credit unpredictability**: Veterans enrolling at one school cannot know whether credits will transfer

## Economic and Market Causes

### Housing Market Disconnect

The GI Bill's housing allowance (BAH-based) does not accurately reflect student veteran housing markets because:

- **BAH is designed for military families**: It covers average housing costs near military installations, not college towns
- **College towns have distinct markets**: High demand from student populations inflates rents near campuses
- **Market timing**: BAH rates are set annually based on prior-year data; rapidly changing markets are underserved
- **Shared housing assumption**: BAH rates for E-5 with dependents assume family housing, not the studio apartments many single student veterans need

### Credential Mismatch

The disconnect between military training and civilian credentials creates unnecessary education spending:

- Military members receive extensive technical training (electronics, medicine, logistics, engineering)
- Much of this training does not translate to civilian credentials or academic credit
- Veterans must retake courses in subjects they already know
- GI Bill entitlement months are consumed by redundant education
- Licensing exams may require education that duplicates military training

**Root cause**: The military and civilian education systems do not communicate effectively. Credential translation efforts (e.g., ACE credit recommendations) are voluntary and inconsistent.

## Political and Institutional Causes

### Veterans as Political Symbol vs. Policy Priority

The GI Bill enjoys near-universal political support in rhetoric, but translating that support into adequate funding and reform is difficult:

- **Bipartisan agreement on the principle**: Everyone supports "the troops" and education benefits
- **Disagreement on the details**: How much to spend, how to regulate for-profit schools, whether to expand benefits
- **For-profit lobbying**: The for-profit college industry spends tens of millions annually on lobbying and campaign contributions
- **VA institutional culture**: VA's education service has historically been underfunded relative to healthcare and disability
- **Competing priorities**: Within the VA budget, education competes with healthcare, disability, homelessness, and other programs

### VA's Dual Role Conflict

The VA simultaneously administers education benefits and oversees the schools that receive them:

- As benefit administrator, VA wants to maximize veteran access and choice
- As regulator, VA should restrict access to poor-quality schools
- These roles create tension: restricting access to schools means restricting veteran choice
- VA has historically prioritized access over quality oversight

### Legislative Complexity

The accumulation of GI Bill programs creates complexity that harms veterans:

- Multiple overlapping programs (Chapter 30, 31, 33, 35, 1606, 1607) with different rules
- Irrevocable election requirements (once you choose a program, you cannot switch back)
- Complex eligibility determination based on service dates, duration, and character of discharge
- Benefit calculation rules that even VA staff struggle to apply correctly

## Reinforcing Feedback Loops

The root causes interact in self-reinforcing cycles:

```
For-profit school lobbying --> Weak oversight rules
--> Veterans enroll in poor programs --> Bad outcomes
--> Veterans exhaust benefits without degrees --> Cannot advocate for themselves
--> Low completion data obscured --> No political pressure for reform
```

```
Benefit structure penalizes non-traditional students
--> Veterans choose traditional enrollment to maximize benefits
--> Non-traditional programs receive less veteran enrollment
--> VA does not invest in non-traditional benefit pathways
--> Penalty structure persists
```

```
Credential mismatch --> Veterans must take redundant courses
--> Entitlement months consumed faster --> Benefits run out sooner
--> Veterans leave without completing degrees --> Poor outcomes data
--> Reinforces perception that veteran students underperform
```
