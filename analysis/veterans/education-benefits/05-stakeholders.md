# Education Benefits: Stakeholders

## Who Benefits from the Status Quo

### For-Profit College Industry

**Revenue at Stake**

- For-profit colleges receive ~$3.3 billion annually in GI Bill funds (25% of total GI Bill spending, FY2023)
- Some large chains derive 10-30% of total revenue from veteran education benefits
- Veterans are among the most profitable students for for-profit schools: high benefit levels, less financial aid counseling, strong enrollment motivation

**Specific Benefits of Status Quo**

- Continued access to veteran enrollment revenue
- Limited oversight and enforcement capacity
- School choice framing protects market access
- High veteran recruitment enables compliance with 90/10 rule (even under reformed calculation)

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **High**

- Well-funded lobbying (industry spent $20+ million in the 2024 election cycle on lobbying and campaign contributions)
- Organized trade associations (Career Education Colleges and Universities)
- Significant Congressional allies in both parties
- Legal challenges to new oversight rules

### VA Education Service

**Institutional Interest**

- Prioritizes processing speed and access over quality oversight
- Dual role as benefit administrator and regulator creates structural tension
- Culture values customer service (to veterans) over regulatory enforcement
- Historical understaffing in oversight positions

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Low-Moderate**

- Not ideologically opposed but resistant to mission expansion
- Bureaucratic inertia favors existing processes
- Regulatory enforcement requires resources VA would prefer to spend elsewhere

## Who Is Harmed by the Status Quo

### Student Veterans (~900,000 annually)

**How They Are Harmed**

- Veterans at for-profit schools face graduation rates below 25% and high debt
- Housing allowance inadequacy forces many to work excessive hours or take on debt
- Online learning penalty discourages flexible education paths
- Payment processing errors create financial crises
- Complex program rules cause veterans to make suboptimal benefit elections
- Exhausted benefits before degree completion

**Demographic Profile**

| Characteristic | Detail |
|----------------|--------|
| Population | ~900,000 using benefits annually |
| Average age | 33 (older than traditional students) |
| Family status | ~47% married; ~35% with dependent children |
| Disability | ~30% have service-connected disability |
| First-generation college | ~60% |
| Full-time enrollment | ~55% |

**Level of Engagement**: **Growing**

- Student Veterans of America (SVA) has chapters at 1,500+ schools
- Veteran student organizations are increasingly vocal advocates
- Social media amplifies individual experiences

### Veterans Who Exhausted Benefits Without Completing Degrees

**How They Are Harmed**

- Used all 36 months of entitlement without earning a degree
- May carry student debt from costs not covered by GI Bill
- Lost economic mobility that education was supposed to provide
- Particularly concentrated among for-profit school enrollees
- Cannot re-earn education benefits

**Level of Engagement**: **Low** (often disengaged from veteran advocacy)

### Minority Veterans

**How They Are Harmed**

- Black veterans are disproportionately enrolled at for-profit schools (29% vs. 18% overall, NCES, 2024)
- Historical echoes of original GI Bill racial discrimination
- Less access to school choice information and guidance
- Targeted recruitment by for-profit schools in minority communities

**Level of Engagement**: **Moderate** (growing through organizations like Minority Veterans of America)

### Taxpayers

**How They Are Harmed**

- ~$3.3 billion annually flows to for-profit schools with poor outcomes
- Overpayments and processing errors cost ~$400 million annually
- Poor education outcomes reduce the ROI of GI Bill investment
- Veterans who do not complete degrees may require more VA services long-term

## Key Institutional Stakeholders

### Government Agencies

**Department of Veterans Affairs**

| Division | Role | Influence |
|----------|------|-----------|
| VA Education Service | Administers GI Bill benefits | High |
| VA Office of Inspector General | Audits and investigations | Moderate |
| State Approving Agencies (SAAs) | Approve schools for GI Bill eligibility | Moderate (underfunded) |

**Department of Education**

| Division | Role | Influence |
|----------|------|-----------|
| Federal Student Aid | Administers non-GI Bill federal aid | High (90/10 rule enforcement) |
| Office of Postsecondary Education | Accreditation and institutional oversight | Moderate |
| National Center for Education Statistics | Data collection and analysis | Low (informational) |

**Department of Defense**

| Division | Role | Influence |
|----------|------|-----------|
| Transition Assistance Program | Education counseling during transition | Moderate |
| Tuition Assistance Program | Pre-separation education benefits | Moderate |
| Credentialing and licensing offices | Military-to-civilian credential translation | Low-Moderate |

**Congress**

| Entity | Role | Influence |
|--------|------|-----------|
| Senate Veterans' Affairs Committee | Oversight, legislation | High |
| House Veterans' Affairs Committee | Oversight, legislation | High |
| Senate HELP Committee | Higher education oversight | Moderate |
| House Education Committee | Higher education oversight | Moderate |

### Advocacy Organizations

**Veteran-Focused**

| Organization | Focus | Influence |

| Veterans Education Success (VES) | Protecting veterans from predatory schools | High |
| Iraq and Afghanistan Veterans of America (IAVA) | Broad veteran advocacy including education | Moderate-High |
| American Legion | Traditional VSO with education advocacy | High |
| VFW | Traditional VSO with education advocacy | High |

**Higher Education and Consumer**

| Organization | Focus | Influence |
|--------------|-------|-----------|

### Educational Institutions

**Public Colleges and Universities**

- Enroll 62% of GI Bill students
- Generally supportive of strong GI Bill benefits (revenue source)
- Often have dedicated veteran student services offices
- Vary widely in quality of veteran support

**Private Non-Profit Colleges**

- Enroll 20% of GI Bill students
- Yellow Ribbon Program participants cover tuition above the cap
- Generally aligned with reform (not threatened by oversight)

**For-Profit Colleges**

- Enroll 18% of GI Bill students; receive 25% of GI Bill spending
- Organized opposition to increased oversight and enforcement
- Trade association (CECU) lobbies actively
- Some individual schools have reformed; industry-wide problems persist

## Power Analysis

### Who Has Power to Change the System

| Stakeholder | Type of Power | Likelihood of Using It |
|-------------|---------------|------------------------|
| Congress | Legislative authority | Moderate (bipartisan support exists) |
| VA Secretary | Administrative authority | Moderate (competing priorities) |
| Department of Education | Regulatory authority (90/10, accreditation) | Moderate |
| SVA and VES | Advocacy, media, research | High (mission-driven) |
| Large VSOs (AL, VFW, DAV) | Political lobbying | Moderate |
| State Approving Agencies | School approval authority | Low (underfunded) |
| Accrediting agencies | School accreditation | Low (historically weak) |

### Coalition Dynamics

**Pro-Reform Coalition**

- Student Veterans of America
- Veterans Education Success
- IAVA
- Consumer advocacy organizations
- Many Congressional Democrats and some Republicans
- Department of Education (under reform-oriented administrations)

**Status Quo / Anti-Reform Coalition**

- For-profit college industry and trade associations
- Congressional allies of for-profit industry (both parties)
- Some VA officials who prioritize access over oversight
- Libertarian-leaning organizations opposing regulation

**Swing Stakeholders**

- VA Education Service (reform-supportive but resource-constrained)
- Large VSOs (supportive of veterans but cautious about restricting school choice)
- State governments (SAAs want more funding but resist federal mandates)

### Priority Engagement

|-------------|-------------|---------------------|
| Congressional VA committees | Legislative authority | Data on for-profit outcomes, veteran testimony |
| SVA and VES | Lead advocacy organizations | Align reform priorities, coordinate campaigns |

| Department of Education | 90/10 enforcement | Interagency coordination |

| Stakeholder | Why Important | Engagement Approach |

| Public colleges | Majority of veteran enrollment | Veteran support best practices |
| Employers | Ultimate validation of education quality | Credential value feedback |
| Military leadership | Transition counseling influence | Education counseling improvements |
