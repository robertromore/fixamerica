# Minority Veterans: History

## Overview

The history of minority veterans in the United States is inseparable from the history of racial discrimination in American military service and society. From the Revolutionary War through the present, minority service members have fought in every American conflict while simultaneously fighting for recognition, equal treatment, and the benefits promised to all who serve. This history reveals a persistent pattern: minority Americans serve in disproportionate numbers during wartime, face systemic discrimination within the military, and upon return encounter barriers to the benefits that transform military service into civilian opportunity.

Understanding this history is not merely an exercise in documenting past injustices. The legacies of discriminatory discharges, segregated service, unequal GI Bill access, and institutional racism in the VA continue to shape minority veteran outcomes today. Many current disparities are not new problems but the compounded effects of generations of differential treatment.

## Revolutionary War Through the Civil War (1775-1865)

### African Americans in Early Military Service

- An estimated 5,000 Black soldiers served in the Continental Army during the Revolutionary War, though initial policy barred their enlistment (Washington reversed this in 1778 due to manpower needs)
- Black soldiers served in racially integrated units in some cases during the Revolution, a practice that would not be replicated for nearly two centuries
- The War of 1812 saw approximately 2,500 Black soldiers, including the famous Battalion of Free Men of Color at the Battle of New Orleans under Andrew Jackson
- Despite service, Black veterans received no pensions and no land grants provided to white veterans under early benefit programs

### The Civil War

- Approximately 180,000 African Americans served in the Union Army (United States Colored Troops) and 19,000 in the Navy
- Black soldiers were initially paid $10 per month compared to $13 for white soldiers, a disparity corrected by Congress in 1864 after sustained protest
- 16 Black soldiers received the Medal of Honor during the Civil War
- After the war, Black veterans were systematically excluded from the veterans' benefits that helped build the white middle class; the Freedmen's Bureau, while not exclusively for veterans, was chronically underfunded and dismantled by 1872

### Native Americans and Other Minorities

- Native Americans served in various conflicts, often as scouts, but received no veteran status or benefits
- Hispanic Americans served in significant numbers in the Mexican-American War and the Civil War, with limited historical documentation of their contributions
- The concept of "veteran" as a legally defined status with attendant benefits did not fully develop until the 20th century, meaning early minority service generated no formal claims to post-service support

## Segregated Service: World War I Through World War II (1917-1945)

### World War I

- Approximately 380,000 African Americans served in World War I, primarily in segregated units assigned to labor and support roles
- The 369th Infantry Regiment ("Harlem Hellfighters") served 191 continuous days in combat, the longest of any American unit, and received the French Croix de Guerre; the unit received no comparable American recognition until decades later
- Black veterans returning from World War I faced violent racial hostility; the "Red Summer" of 1919 saw white mobs attack Black veterans in over 25 cities, specifically targeting men in uniform
- Native Americans who served in World War I were not U.S. citizens and were ineligible for benefits; the Indian Citizenship Act was not passed until 1924

### World War II: Service

- Approximately 1.2 million African Americans served in World War II in fully segregated units
- The 332nd Fighter Group (Tuskegee Airmen), 761st Tank Battalion ("Black Panthers"), and the 92nd Infantry Division were among the distinguished Black units
- Approximately 500,000 Hispanic Americans served; the 65th Infantry Regiment (Borinqueneers) from Puerto Rico saw extensive combat
- Approximately 44,000 Native Americans served, including the famous Navajo Code Talkers who developed an unbreakable communications code in the Pacific theater
- Over 33,000 Japanese Americans served, many from families held in internment camps; the 442nd Regimental Combat Team became the most decorated unit in U.S. Army history relative to size and length of service
- Filipino Americans served in large numbers, with the First and Second Filipino Infantry Regiments

### World War II: The GI Bill Betrayal

The Servicemen's Readjustment Act of 1944 (GI Bill) was the most transformative veterans' benefit in American history, providing education, home loans, and business loans that built the white middle class. For minority veterans, the GI Bill functioned very differently:

**African Americans:**

- Southern states administered GI Bill benefits through local VA offices that enforced racial segregation
- Black veterans were steered to historically Black colleges (HBCUs), which could not accommodate the demand; by 1946, 20,000 Black veterans were denied enrollment due to HBCU capacity constraints (Katznelson, *When Affirmative Action Was White*, 2005)
- VA home loan guarantees were effectively worthless for Black veterans because banks would not lend and communities enforced racial covenants; of 67,000 VA-backed home mortgages issued in Mississippi and New York in 1947, fewer than 100 went to non-white veterans (Herbold, *Journal of Blacks in Higher Education*, 1994)
- The Federal Housing Administration's discriminatory lending policies (redlining) compounded GI Bill exclusion
- Black veterans who attempted to use GI Bill benefits faced violence and intimidation, particularly in the South

**Other Minority Groups:**

- Hispanic veterans faced language barriers and discrimination in benefit access, though regional variation was significant
- Native American veterans on reservations could not use VA home loans because tribal trust land could not serve as collateral
- Filipino veterans who served under U.S. command were stripped of benefits by the Rescission Act of 1946, which classified their service as not constituting "active duty" for benefit purposes; this affected approximately 260,000 veterans
- Japanese American veterans faced housing discrimination and employment barriers upon return, despite their distinguished combat record

## Korea, Vietnam, and the Civil Rights Era (1950-1975)

### Korean War

- Integration of the armed forces began with Executive Order 9981 (Truman, 1948), but full implementation took until the Korean War when manpower needs accelerated desegregation
- The 65th Infantry Regiment (Puerto Rico) served with distinction in Korea; its members later faced mass courts-martial in 1954 that are now widely viewed as racially motivated (pardoned collectively in 2023 by the Army)
- Korean War-era minority veterans were the first generation to serve in nominally integrated units, though racial discrimination within the military persisted

### Vietnam War

- African Americans comprised approximately 11% of the U.S. population but 12.6% of Vietnam-era service members and an estimated 23% of combat troops in the war's early years (before reforms reduced the disparity) (Westheider, *Fighting on Two Fronts*, 2005)
- The draft system, which allowed college deferments, disproportionately sent poor and minority Americans to Vietnam
- Racial tension within the military escalated dramatically during the Vietnam era, with documented incidents of racial violence, nonjudicial punishment disparities, and discriminatory military justice
- Black service members received non-judicial punishment at twice the rate of whites and were more likely to receive less-than-honorable discharges (DOD Task Force on the Administration of Military Justice, 1972)
- The DOD Task Force on the Administration of Military Justice (1972) documented systemic racial bias in courts-martial, confinement, and discharge characterization
- Hispanic service members, many of whom were drafted from agricultural communities, faced similar disparities with less documentation
- Native American Vietnam veterans, particularly from the Navajo, Lakota, and other nations, served at rates exceeding their population proportion and returned to reservations with virtually no access to VA services

### Post-Vietnam Discharge Disparities

The Vietnam era produced a legacy of discriminatory discharges that continue to deny benefits to minority veterans:

- An estimated 300,000 veterans received less-than-honorable discharges during the Vietnam era
- Black veterans received "bad paper" discharges at rates 2-3 times higher than white veterans
- Many of these discharges reflected racial bias in military justice rather than genuine misconduct
- Congress created the Special Discharge Review Program in 1977, which upgraded some discharges, but the program ended in 1982 with most cases unreviewed

## All-Volunteer Force and Modern Era (1973-Present)

### The Volunteer Military and Minority Representation

- The transition to the All-Volunteer Force (AVF) in 1973 dramatically increased minority representation, as the military offered economic opportunity and social mobility
- By 1980, African Americans comprised 33% of the Army, far exceeding their 12% share of the population
- Hispanic representation grew steadily, reaching 17.8% of active duty by 2023
- Women of color entered military service in growing numbers, creating a population facing intersecting racial and gender disparities

### Gulf War and Post-9/11 Conflicts

- Minority service members constituted approximately 30% of deployed forces in the Gulf War, Iraq, and Afghanistan
- The growth of the minority veteran population from these conflicts is reshaping the demographics of the veteran population
- Post-9/11 combat exposure among minority service members has contributed to PTSD, TBI, and suicide risk, with emerging evidence of racial disparities in diagnosis and treatment
- The PACT Act (2022), which expanded toxic exposure presumptions, has disproportionately benefited minority veterans who served in burn pit zones

### Reform Efforts

**Legislative milestones:**

- Public Law 103-446 (1994): Established the VA Center for Minority Veterans and the Advisory Committee on Minority Veterans
- Public Law 106-117 (1999): Required VA to collect and report data on minority veteran healthcare utilization
- Executive Order 13625 (2012): Directed VA to improve outreach to minority veterans
- VA Equity Action Plan (2022): Comprehensive equity framework, though without enforcement mechanisms

**Institutional changes:**

- VA Office of Health Equity established (2012)
- Minority Veteran Program Coordinators designated at each VA medical center
- VA began systematic disaggregated data reporting by race/ethnicity (2022)
- DOD expanded discharge upgrade review criteria (2017, expanded 2024)

### Deported Veterans

An issue that emerged into public attention in the 2010s involves veterans, primarily Hispanic, who served honorably but were subsequently deported due to immigration violations:

- The Deported Veterans Support House (known as "The Bunker") in Tijuana, Mexico, serves an estimated 300+ deported U.S. military veterans
- Many deported veterans served during periods when military service was understood as a pathway to citizenship, but administrative failures or subsequent criminal convictions (including minor drug offenses) led to deportation
- Executive action in 2022 established a process for case-by-case review, but no legislation guarantees returning deported veterans to the U.S.

## Historical Recognition and Memorialization

### Belated Recognition

The historical contributions of minority veterans have received increasing recognition, though significant gaps remain:

| Recognition Event | Year | Details |
|-------------------|------|---------|
| Tuskegee Airmen Congressional Gold Medal | 2007 | Awarded to approximately 300 surviving members |
| Navajo Code Talkers Congressional Gold Medal | 2000 | Awarded to the original 29 Code Talkers |
| 442nd RCT Congressional Gold Medal | 2011 | Awarded to Japanese American WWII veterans |
| 65th Infantry Regiment Congressional Gold Medal | 2014 | Awarded to the Borinqueneers |
| Filipino Veterans Congressional Gold Medal | 2016 | Awarded to Filipino WWII veterans |
| Emmett Till Antilynching Act acknowledgment of veteran victims | 2022 | Recognized that many lynching victims were Black veterans |
| Army pardon of 65th Infantry Regiment courts-martial | 2023 | Corrected racially biased Korean War-era convictions |

### Gaps in Historical Record

- The military's historical records for minority service members are incomplete, particularly for pre-WWII eras
- The National Personnel Records Center fire of 1973 destroyed approximately 16-18 million records, disproportionately affecting minority veterans whose records were stored in the affected sections
- Many minority veterans' contributions remain undocumented, particularly Hispanic, Native American, and Asian American service in pre-Vietnam conflicts

## Lessons from History

### Recurring Patterns

1. **Service without equal benefit**: In every era, minority veterans have served at or above their population share while receiving fewer benefits
2. **Benefit exclusion through administration**: Formally race-neutral benefit programs have been administered in racially discriminatory ways (GI Bill, disability compensation, discharge characterization)
3. **Delayed recognition**: Military contributions of minority groups are recognized decades after service, often when most participants have died
4. **Reform through crisis**: Significant reforms occur only when disparities become politically untenable, not through proactive equity measures
5. **Legacy compounding**: Each generation's discrimination creates cumulative disadvantage that shapes the next generation's outcomes

### Implications for Current Policy

- Discharge upgrade reviews must account for the historical context of racial bias in military justice
- Retroactive benefit analysis should examine how GI Bill exclusion affected wealth accumulation across generations
- Data disaggregation is essential to prevent the invisibility that allowed disparities to persist for decades
- Cultural competency cannot be achieved without understanding the historical reasons for minority veteran distrust of military and VA institutions
