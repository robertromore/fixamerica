# Transition Assistance: Stakeholders

## Overview

The military-to-civilian transition involves a wide array of stakeholders, from the approximately 200,000 service members who separate each year and their families to the government agencies, employers, educational institutions, and community organizations that shape their post-service outcomes. Understanding these stakeholders' interests, power, and alignment is essential to designing effective reform.

## Affected Populations

### Primary Affected Groups

| Group | Estimated Size | Key Transition Challenges | Most Critical Need |
|-------|---------------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| Separating enlisted (E-1 to E-4) | ~80,000/year | Low civilian credentials; income decline; young age | Credential translation; career counseling; financial planning |
| Separating enlisted (E-5 to E-9) | ~50,000/year | Leadership skills undervalued; identity loss; family stability | Career matching; identity transition support; family services |
| Separating officers (O-1 to O-6) | ~25,000/year | Overqualified for some roles; culture shock; entrepreneurship interest | Professional networking; executive transition; entrepreneurship support |
| Retiring service members (20+ years) | ~35,000/year | Major identity transition; financial adjustment; health concerns | Identity support; healthcare navigation; second career planning |
| Medically separated | ~15,000/year | Disability; limited work capacity; complex benefits | Integrated medical-vocational planning; disability benefits |
| Involuntarily separated | ~10,000/year | Unexpected transition; possible discipline issues; stigma | Rapid employment; benefits awareness; mental health support |
| National Guard and Reserve | ~25,000/year | Part-time identity; employer relations; geographically dispersed | Civilian employer coordination; benefits navigation |
| Military spouses | ~130,000 per year (estimated) | Career disruption; geographic instability; child care | Employment assistance; credential portability; support services |
| Military children | ~250,000 affected per year | School changes; social disruption; parent adjustment | Educational continuity; counseling; community integration |

### Disproportionately Affected Subgroups

| Subgroup | Why Disproportionately Affected |
|----------|-------------------------------|
| Women veterans | Face gender-specific transition challenges; VA system historically designed for men; employment discrimination |
| Minority veterans | Higher unemployment rates post-separation; less access to professional networks |
| Rural-destined veterans | Fewer transition services; limited job markets; greater distance from VA facilities |
| Veterans with combat injuries/PTSD | Medical-vocational transition requires integrated planning not available through standard TAP |
| First-generation service members | No family experience to guide transition; fewer civilian professional networks |
| Junior enlisted without degrees | Limited civilian credentials; most significant income decline; least career counseling |

## Decision Makers

### Government Officials

| Decision Maker | Authority | Current Stance |
|----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Secretary of Defense | Overall DOD policy on transition; TAP management | Supports TAP reform in principle; operational readiness remains priority |
| Under Secretary of Defense for Personnel and Readiness | Direct authority over TVPO and TAP policy | Managing TVPO; incremental reform approach |
| Service Chiefs (Army, Navy, Air Force, Marines, Space Force) | Service-specific transition implementation | Vary; some services more supportive of SkillBridge and transition time |
| VA Secretary | VA-side transition programs; Solid Start; benefits delivery | Committed to improving transition support; limited pre-separation authority |
| Secretary of Labor | DOL Employment Workshop; VETS program | Manages DOL components; supports employer engagement |
| Congressional Veterans Affairs Committees | Authorization and oversight | Bipartisan support for TAP reform; multiple reform bills introduced |
| Congressional Armed Services Committees | DOD policy authorization | Must authorize any changes to military personnel policy |
| Installation commanders | Control local TAP delivery and service member access | Key gatekeepers; decisions vary widely by commander |

### Military Chain of Command

| Level | Role in Transition | Issue |
|-------|-------------------|-------|
| Service Secretary | Sets service-level transition policy | Policy exists but enforcement varies |
| Major command leaders | Allocate resources for transition programs at installations | Resource allocation competes with operational needs |
| Installation commanders | Manage TAP delivery; control SkillBridge approval | Enormous variability in support for transition activities |
| Unit commanders | Determine when service members can attend TAP | Single most important gatekeepers; often delay access |
| First sergeants/senior enlisted | Direct interface with separating enlisted | Can be either strong advocates or barriers |

## Opposition and Resistance

### Stakeholders Resisting Reform

| Stakeholder | Resistance Type | Motivation |
|-------------|----------------|------------|
| Unit commanders (some) | Operational resistance | Service members in TAP are not available for duty |
| DOD budget offices | Resource resistance | Extended transition programs cost more |
| Some military traditionalists | Cultural resistance | View transition support as "coddling"; veterans should be tough enough to figure it out |
| For-profit education sector | Reform resistance | Aggressive marketing to veterans could be curtailed by better counseling |
| Transition program contractors | Status quo preservation | Current contracts based on existing model; reforms could displace incumbents |

### Stakeholders with Mixed Positions

| Stakeholder | Position | Complexity |
|-------------|----------|------------|
| Service branch leaders | Support transition in principle; resist operational impact | Balance readiness with transition needs |
| VA healthcare system | Wants early enrollment; constrained by DOD data sharing | Benefits from pre-separation enrollment but cannot mandate it |
| Private employers | Want to hire veterans; struggle to translate military credentials | Support reform but may not invest in their own capacity |
| State governments | Manage veteran employment and licensing; varying capacity | Some states lead on credentialing reform; others lag |

## Coalition Dynamics

### Existing Effective Coalitions

| Coalition | Members | Achievement |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| SkillBridge expansion advocates | Hiring Our Heroes, DOD, participating employers, VSOs | Grew program from pilot to 25,000 annual participants |
| Credential recognition coalition | SHRM, state legislators, DOD, DOL | 48 states with some military credential recognition |
| Transition research community | IVMF, RAND, VA, academic researchers | Produced evidence base documenting TAP's limitations |
| Military family transition advocates | Blue Star Families, MOAA, NMFA | Elevated family transition needs in policy discussions |

### Potential Future Coalitions

| Coalition Opportunity | Potential Members | Objective |
|----------------------|-------------------|-----------|
| Extended transition continuum | IVMF, VA, DOD TVPO, VSOs, employers | 12-month pre to 24-month post support system |
| Identity transition initiative | Mental health organizations, peer support networks, researchers | Programs addressing psychological transition |
| SkillBridge universalization | Employers, congressional champions, veteran advocates | Make SkillBridge accessible to all separating members |
| Veteran-ready communities | Local governments, civic organizations, employers, VSOs | Community-level integration infrastructure |

## Power Analysis

### Who Has the Most Influence

| Actor | Power Source | Leverage Points |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| Unit commanders | Direct authority over service member access to TAP | Can accelerate or block participation |
| Congressional Armed Services Committees | Authorize DOD policy changes | NDAA provisions; hearing oversight |
| Secretary of Defense | Sets DOD-wide policy | Directives, instructions, budget priorities |
| Employer coalitions (Veteran Jobs Mission, Hiring Our Heroes) | Market demand for veteran talent | Hiring commitments; SkillBridge partnerships |
| IVMF/RAND research | Evidence base for reform | Studies cited in legislation and policy debates |

### Who Has the Least Power

| Actor | Why Powerless | Consequence |
|-------|--------------|-------------|
| Individual separating service members | Subject to chain of command; no collective voice | Must accept whatever transition support is offered, whenever offered |
| Military spouses | Secondary beneficiary; no direct standing | Transition needs often overlooked |
| Military children | Dependent; no voice in policy | School transitions, social disruption not systematically addressed |
| Junior enlisted separating involuntarily | Least rank; least agency; highest risk | Most likely to experience poor transition outcomes |
