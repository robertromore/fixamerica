# Transition Assistance: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative landscape for military-to-civilian transition spans the Transition Assistance Program (10 U.S.C. 1142-1144), SkillBridge fellowships (10 U.S.C. 1143), VA benefits delivery (38 U.S.C. Chapters 30-36), and workforce development (29 U.S.C. 3101 et seq.). Despite multiple legislative reforms since 1990, the transition system remains structurally inadequate: TAP is compressed into the final days of service, post-separation support is nearly nonexistent, the DOD-VA handoff creates a gap where no agency is accountable, and identity transition is entirely unaddressed. This document proposes federal legislation, state model legislation, and regulatory reforms to establish a comprehensive 12/24 Transition Continuum, universalize SkillBridge access, create an identity transition program, unify DOD-VA data systems, and mandate outcome-based program evaluation.

---

## Federal Legislation

### Bill 1: Comprehensive Transition Continuum Act

**Purpose**: Replace the current compressed TAP model with a 12/24 Transition Continuum providing structured support from 12 months before separation through 24 months after, with individualized planning, assigned transition coordinators, and mandatory outcome tracking.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Comprehensive Transition Continuum
Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that—
    (1) approximately 200,000 service members separate from the Armed
        Forces annually;
    (2) only 55 percent of Transition Assistance Program participants
        rate the program as helpful, and only 31 percent report that it
        addressed their individual needs;
    (3) the first year after military separation carries the highest
        suicide risk of any period in a veteran's life, with recently
        separated veterans 1.5 times more likely to die by suicide
        than comparable longer-separated veterans;
    (4) 43 percent of veterans are underemployed within 2 years of
        separation, and 44 percent report difficulty adjusting to
        civilian life;
    (5) the existing Transition Assistance Program compresses career
        counseling, benefits orientation, financial planning, and life
        skills into approximately 5 days of classroom instruction
        delivered in the final weeks of service;
    (6) no federal program provides sustained support during the
        12-to-24-month post-separation period when most transition
        difficulties arise; and
    (7) allied nations including the United Kingdom, Australia, and
        Canada provide 12 to 24 months of transition support with
        measurably better outcomes.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to—
    (1) establish a 12/24 Transition Continuum providing structured
        support from 12 months before separation through 24 months
        after;
    (2) assign each separating service member an individualized
        Transition Coordinator;
    (3) create protected transition time that cannot be overridden
        by unit commanders without senior officer justification;
    (4) establish a post-separation support system including career
        coaching, peer mentoring, benefits navigation, and community
        integration; and
    (5) mandate outcome-based program evaluation.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
    (1) TRANSITION CONTINUUM.—The term "Transition Continuum" means
        the period beginning 12 months before a member's anticipated
        date of separation and ending 24 months after the date of
        separation.
    (2) TRANSITION COORDINATOR.—The term "Transition Coordinator"
        means a jointly appointed DOD-VA professional assigned to
        manage a separating member's transition from pre-separation
        planning through post-separation stabilization.
    (3) INDIVIDUALIZED TRANSITION PLAN.—The term "Individualized
        Transition Plan" means a comprehensive, personalized plan
        developed in consultation with the member that addresses
        career goals, credential translation, financial planning,
        benefits enrollment, family needs, identity transition, and
        community integration.
    (4) PROTECTED TRANSITION TIME.—The term "Protected Transition
        Time" means periods during which a member is authorized to
        participate in transition activities and may not be recalled
        for unit duties except in cases of deployment, imminent
        deployment, or emergency.

SEC. 4. TRANSITION CONTINUUM ESTABLISHMENT.

(a) AMENDMENT TO TITLE 10.—Section 1142 of title 10, United States
    Code, is amended—
    (1) in'by striking "not later than 90 days" each place it appears
        and inserting "not later than 365 days"; and
    (2) by adding at the end the following new subsections:
    "(g) PRE-SEPARATION PHASE.—
        (1) TRANSITION READINESS ASSESSMENT.—Not later than 12 months
            before the anticipated date of separation, each member
            shall receive a Transition Readiness Assessment
            identifying individual needs across the following
            domains:
            (A) career goals and civilian credential requirements;
            (B) education plans and GI Bill utilization strategy;
            (C) financial readiness and post-separation budget;
            (D) VA benefits enrollment and disability claims;
            (E) housing and relocation planning;
            (F) family transition needs including spousal employment
                and children's education;
            (G) identity and psychological adjustment; and
            (H) community integration and social connection.
        (2) INDIVIDUALIZED TRANSITION PLAN.—Not later than 10 months
            before the anticipated date of separation, each member
            shall have a completed Individualized Transition Plan
            developed in consultation with the member's assigned
            Transition Coordinator.
        (3) MILESTONE SCHEDULE.—The Individualized Transition Plan
            shall include milestones at the 12-month, 9-month,
            6-month, 3-month, and 1-month marks before separation,
            with specific activities and assessments at each point.
    "(h) POST-SEPARATION PHASE.—
        (1) SUSTAINED SUPPORT.—The Secretary of Veterans Affairs,
            in coordination with the Secretary of Labor, shall
            provide each separated member with continued transition
            support for not fewer than 24 months following the date
            of separation, including—
            (A) career coaching and job placement assistance;
            (B) peer mentoring from trained veteran mentors;
            (C) benefits navigation and claims assistance;
            (D) housing stability support;
            (E) identity transition and community integration
                programming; and
            (F) proactive wellness check-ins at 1, 3, 6, 9, 12,
                18, and 24 months post-separation.
        (2) WARM HANDOFF.—The member's Transition Coordinator shall
            maintain responsibility for the member's case for the
            first 6 months following separation, after which
            responsibility shall transfer to a VA-assigned case
            manager through a documented handoff process.
    "(i) PROTECTED TRANSITION TIME.—
        (1) MINIMUM TIME.—Each separating member shall be authorized
            not fewer than 40 days of Protected Transition Time
            distributed across the 12-month pre-separation period
            for transition activities, including but not limited to
            TAP sessions, credential acquisition, employer
            engagement, financial planning, and SkillBridge
            participation.
        (2) COMMANDER LIMITATIONS.—A unit commander may not deny or
            delay a member's access to Protected Transition Time
            except in cases of—
            (A) deployment or imminent deployment;
            (B) a declared national emergency; or
            (C) a specific operational necessity documented in
                writing and approved by the first general or flag
                officer in the member's chain of command.
        (3) ACCOUNTABILITY.—Each installation commander shall report
            quarterly to the service Secretary on the utilization
            of Protected Transition Time and any denials under
            paragraph (2), and such reports shall be included in
            the annual report required under section 8."

SEC. 5. TRANSITION COORDINATOR PROGRAM.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary of Defense and the Secretary of
    Veterans Affairs shall jointly establish a Transition Coordinator
    Program staffed by professionals trained in career counseling,
    benefits navigation, military culture, and psychosocial
    transition support.

(b) STAFFING.—The program shall maintain a ratio of not more than
    1 Transition Coordinator per 125 separating service members per
    year at each installation.

(c) QUALIFICATIONS.—Each Transition Coordinator shall—
    (1) hold a bachelor's degree or equivalent experience in
        counseling, social work, human resources, or a related field;
    (2) complete a certification program in military transition
        support developed under this section;
    (3) demonstrate competency in military culture and veteran
        identity transition; and
    (4) be subject to annual performance evaluation based on
        participant outcomes.

(d) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
    $350,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2031 to
    carry out this section.

SEC. 6. POST-SEPARATION PEER MENTORING PROGRAM.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall establish
    a Veteran Peer Mentoring Program pairing each recently separated
    veteran with a trained veteran mentor for the first 12 months
    following separation.

(b) MENTOR TRAINING.—The Secretary shall develop a standardized
    training curriculum for peer mentors addressing—
    (1) active listening and motivational interviewing;
    (2) benefits navigation and resource referral;
    (3) crisis recognition and Veterans Crisis Line protocols;
    (4) identity transition and purpose development; and
    (5) community integration strategies.

(c) MENTOR COMPENSATION.—Peer mentors shall receive a stipend of
    not less than $500 per month per mentee for their service.

(d) PARTNERSHIPS.—The Secretary shall partner with veteran service
    organizations, including but not limited to Team Red White & Blue,
    The Mission Continues, and Team Rubicon, to recruit and support
    peer mentors.

(e) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
    $200,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2031 to
    carry out this section.

SEC. 7. FAMILY TRANSITION SUPPORT.

(a) SPOUSAL SERVICES.—The Transition Continuum shall include, for
    each military spouse—
    (1) career counseling and job placement assistance beginning
        at the 12-month pre-separation mark;
    (2) access to SkillBridge-equivalent employer partnerships;
    (3) credential portability assistance; and
    (4) resume and interview preparation.

(b) CHILDREN'S SERVICES.—The Secretary of Defense shall provide—
    (1) educational transition planning for each military child,
        including school selection, transcript transfer, and
        counseling referral;
    (2) access to Military Child Education Coalition resources;
        and
    (3) continuation of military family support services for 12
        months following separation.

(c) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
    $250,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2031 to
    carry out this section.

SEC. 8. OUTCOME TRACKING AND REPORTING.

(a) DATA SYSTEM.—The Secretary of Defense and the Secretary of
    Veterans Affairs shall jointly establish a Transition Outcomes
    Data System that tracks, for each separating service member—
    (1) employment status at 6, 12, and 24 months post-separation;
    (2) earnings relative to pre-separation military pay;
    (3) education enrollment and completion;
    (4) VA healthcare enrollment and utilization;
    (5) housing stability;
    (6) disability claim status; and
    (7) self-reported well-being and adjustment measures.

(b) PRIVACY PROTECTIONS.—Data collected under this section shall
    be subject to the Privacy Act of 1974 (5 U.S.C. 552a) and
    shall not be disclosed for purposes other than program
    evaluation and improvement without the veteran's written
    consent.

(c) ANNUAL REPORT.—Not later than 1 year after the date of
    enactment of this Act, and annually thereafter, the Secretary
    of Defense and the Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall jointly
    submit to Congress a Transition Outcomes Report disaggregated
    by service branch, rank, military occupational specialty,
    gender, race, disability status, and geographic location.

(d) PERFORMANCE-BASED CONTRACTING.—Beginning not later than 3
    years after the date of enactment of this Act, not less than
    20 percent of TAP contractor compensation shall be tied to
    demonstrated participant outcomes as measured by the Transition
    Outcomes Data System.

(e) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
    $150,000,000 for development of the data system, and
    $50,000,000 annually for each of fiscal years 2028 through
    2032 for system operation and reporting.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the most fundamental structural failure of the current transition system: its compression into the final days of service with no post-separation support. The 12/24 Transition Continuum creates a sustained support framework matching the approaches of allied nations. Protected Transition Time addresses the commander discretion problem that has undermined every previous timeline mandate. The Transition Coordinator program provides individualized case management across the DOD-VA boundary. The peer mentoring program leverages veteran lived experience while creating veteran employment. Outcome tracking is essential for accountability and continuous improvement.

**Potential Challenges**:

- DOD resistance to 40 days of Protected Transition Time over 12 months
- Cost of staffing Transition Coordinators at every installation ($350 million/year)
- Interagency coordination between DOD and VA for joint Coordinator positions
- Privacy concerns regarding comprehensive outcome tracking
- Commander culture change regarding Protected Transition Time

**Refinements**:

- Phase Protected Transition Time from 20 days in Year 1 to 40 days by Year 3
- Allow flexible Transition Coordinator staffing models including nonprofit partnerships
- Create a joint DOD-VA Transition Office to manage coordination
- Implement opt-in consent model for outcome tracking with strong privacy protections
- Include transition outcomes in commander performance evaluations to incentivize compliance

### Bill 2: SkillBridge Universalization Act

**Purpose**: Make SkillBridge participation available to all separating service members as a right, remove the commander approval requirement, expand employer partnerships, and create virtual SkillBridge options for remote and overseas locations.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "SkillBridge Universalization Act
of 2026".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that—
    (1) the Department of Defense SkillBridge program has a 78
        percent job placement rate, the highest of any federal
        transition program;
    (2) employer satisfaction with SkillBridge participants exceeds
        90 percent;
    (3) service members who complete SkillBridge experience 40
        percent lower unemployment rates in the first year after
        separation compared to non-participants;
    (4) only 12 percent of eligible service members participate in
        SkillBridge, primarily due to commander approval requirements
        and information gaps;
    (5) the current commander approval requirement creates inequitable
        access based on unit assignment, commanding officer attitude,
        and service branch culture rather than individual merit or
        need; and
    (6) SkillBridge participants are in their final 180 days of
        service, a period of declining operational productivity
        regardless of SkillBridge participation.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to—
    (1) establish SkillBridge participation as a right for all
        separating service members;
    (2) replace the commander approval requirement with a
        notification requirement;
    (3) expand employer partnerships to 10,000 or more; and
    (4) create virtual SkillBridge options for service members at
        remote or overseas locations.

SEC. 3. SKILLBRIDGE AS A RIGHT.

(a) AMENDMENT TO TITLE 10.—Section 1143 of title 10, United States
    Code, is amended by adding the following:
    "(f) SKILLBRIDGE PARTICIPATION RIGHT.—
        (1) IN GENERAL.—Each member of the Armed Forces who is
            within 180 days of the anticipated date of separation
            or retirement shall have the right to participate in
            a SkillBridge fellowship with an approved employer
            partner.
        (2) NOTIFICATION REQUIREMENT.—A member seeking to
            participate in SkillBridge shall provide written
            notification to the member's unit commander not later
            than 60 days before the planned start date of the
            fellowship.
        (3) LIMITATION ON DENIAL.—A unit commander may not deny a
            member's SkillBridge participation except when the
            member is—
            (A) deployed or on orders for imminent deployment; or
            (B) subject to a declared national emergency requiring
                retention of the member's specific skills.
        (4) DENIAL REVIEW.—Any denial under paragraph (3) shall be—
            (A) documented in writing with specific justification;
            (B) reviewed by the first general or flag officer in
                the chain of command within 15 business days; and
            (C) reported to the service Secretary and the Transition
                to Veterans Program Office.
        (5) EXTENDED FELLOWSHIP.—For service members in occupational
            specialties with civilian unemployment rates exceeding
            the national average, SkillBridge fellowships may be
            extended to 270 days."

SEC. 4. EMPLOYER PARTNERSHIP EXPANSION.

(a) TARGET.—The Secretary of Defense shall ensure that not fewer
    than 10,000 employers participate in the SkillBridge program by
    the end of fiscal year 2029.

(b) SECTOR ALIGNMENT.—The Secretary shall prioritize employer
    partnerships in—
    (1) healthcare and allied health professions;
    (2) information technology and cybersecurity;
    (3) skilled trades and advanced manufacturing;
    (4) transportation and logistics;
    (5) energy and utilities;
    (6) financial services; and
    (7) public administration and government.

(c) SMALL BUSINESS PARTNERSHIPS.—The Secretary shall create a
    small business SkillBridge track with simplified application
    procedures for employers with fewer than 500 employees.

(d) EMPLOYER TAX INCENTIVE.—Section 51 of the Internal Revenue
    Code of 1986 is amended to provide a tax credit of $2,500
    to any employer that hires a SkillBridge participant within
    90 days of the participant's date of separation for a position
    at or above the participant's SkillBridge role level.

SEC. 5. VIRTUAL SKILLBRIDGE.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary of Defense shall establish a
    Virtual SkillBridge Program for service members stationed at
    installations that lack local employer partners, including—
    (1) remote domestic installations;
    (2) overseas installations; and
    (3) installations with limited local labor markets.

(b) VIRTUAL FELLOWSHIP STANDARDS.—Virtual SkillBridge fellowships
    shall include—
    (1) remote work with an approved employer partner;
    (2) structured training and mentorship;
    (3) regular performance evaluation; and
    (4) a clear pathway to post-separation employment.

SEC. 6. INSTALLATION SKILLBRIDGE COORDINATORS.

(a) ASSIGNMENT.—The Secretary of Defense shall assign at least one
    SkillBridge Coordinator at each installation with 500 or more
    active-duty service members.

(b) DUTIES.—Each SkillBridge Coordinator shall—
    (1) promote SkillBridge opportunities to service members
        beginning at the 12-month pre-separation mark;
    (2) assist service members in identifying appropriate employer
        partners;
    (3) manage employer partnerships at the installation level; and
    (4) track participation and outcomes data.

SEC. 7. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There is authorized to be appropriated $500,000,000 for each of
fiscal years 2027 through 2031 to carry out this Act.
```

**Explanation**: SkillBridge is the most effective transition program in the federal portfolio, yet only 12% of eligible service members can access it. This bill transforms SkillBridge from a privilege subject to commander discretion into a right for all separating members. The notification-only requirement removes the most significant barrier to participation while the deployment exception preserves genuine operational flexibility. Virtual SkillBridge addresses geographic access gaps. The employer tax incentive encourages post-fellowship hiring, and small business participation expands the range of available opportunities.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Strong DOD resistance to eliminating commander approval authority
- Service branch culture differences in SkillBridge support
- Scaling from 4,000 to 10,000 employer partners requires significant outreach investment
- Virtual SkillBridge quality assurance across remote settings
- Revenue cost of the employer tax incentive

**Refinements**:

- Phase implementation: convert to notification-only for retirement-eligible members first, then expand
- Create a SkillBridge Advisory Board including employer and veteran representatives
- Establish quality standards and employer performance metrics for virtual fellowships
- Include SkillBridge participation rates and outcomes in commander performance evaluations
- Sunset the employer tax incentive at 5 years with renewal contingent on program evaluation

### Bill 3: Military Identity Transition Support Act

**Purpose**: Establish a structured program addressing the psychological, social, and identity dimensions of military-to-civilian transition, integrated into the Transition Continuum and delivered through trained veteran peer facilitators.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Military Identity Transition Support
Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that—
    (1) 52 percent of veterans report loss of purpose after military
        separation;
    (2) 44 percent of veterans report difficulty adjusting to
        civilian life;
    (3) 38 percent of veterans report social isolation within the
        first year after separation;
    (4) research demonstrates that identity loss is a stronger
        predictor of transition difficulties than employment or
        financial status;
    (5) no existing federal program addresses the psychological and
        identity dimensions of military-to-civilian transition;
    (6) military basic training invests substantial time and resources
        in transforming civilian identity into military identity, but
        no comparable investment is made in the reverse transition; and
    (7) peer-led programs operated by organizations such as Team Red
        White & Blue, The Mission Continues, and Team Rubicon
        demonstrate the effectiveness of purpose-based community
        engagement for veteran wellbeing.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to—
    (1) establish a Military Identity Transition Program integrated
        into the Transition Continuum;
    (2) train veteran peer facilitators to deliver identity
        transition programming;
    (3) address the psychological, social, and identity dimensions
        of transition alongside practical concerns; and
    (4) connect transitioning members to community-based veteran
        organizations for sustained purpose and belonging.

SEC. 3. MILITARY IDENTITY TRANSITION PROGRAM.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary of Defense and the Secretary of
    Veterans Affairs shall jointly establish a Military Identity
    Transition Program as a required component of the Transition
    Continuum.

(b) CURRICULUM DEVELOPMENT.—The Secretary of Defense shall, in
    partnership with military psychologists, veteran peer mentors,
    identity transition researchers, and veteran service
    organizations, develop an evidence-based Identity Transition
    Curriculum addressing—
    (1) understanding military identity formation and its effects
        on civilian reintegration;
    (2) developing a post-military sense of purpose and meaning;
    (3) building civilian social networks and community belonging;
    (4) navigating civilian workplace and social culture;
    (5) managing the loss of rank-based status and military tribal
        identity;
    (6) strengthening family relationships during transition; and
    (7) connecting to community-based organizations that provide
        sustained purpose and social engagement.

(c) DELIVERY.—The Identity Transition Program shall be delivered
    in two phases:
    (1) PRE-SEPARATION PHASE.—Group-based programming delivered
        during the 6-to-3-month pre-separation period, consisting
        of not fewer than 6 facilitated sessions of 2 hours each.
    (2) POST-SEPARATION PHASE.—Community-based programming during
        the first 12 months after separation, including—
        (A) monthly peer group meetings;
        (B) individual coaching sessions as needed; and
        (C) community integration activities in partnership with
            veteran service organizations.

(d) PEER FACILITATORS.—
    (1) TRAINING.—The Secretary shall develop a standardized
        facilitator training program of not fewer than 80 hours
        for veteran peer facilitators.
    (2) COMPENSATION.—Peer facilitators shall be compensated as
        federal employees or contractors at a rate commensurate
        with their training and responsibilities.
    (3) STAFFING TARGET.—The Secretary shall train not fewer than
        1,000 veteran peer facilitators within 3 years of
        enactment.

(e) FAMILY INCLUSION.—Relevant components of the Identity
    Transition Program shall be open to family members of
    separating service members.

SEC. 4. COMMUNITY ORGANIZATION PARTNERSHIPS.

(a) PARTNERSHIPS.—The Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall establish
    formal partnerships with veteran community organizations for
    the delivery of post-separation identity transition programming.

(b) GRANT PROGRAM.—The Secretary shall award grants to eligible
    veteran service organizations to support the expansion of
    purpose-based community engagement programs to serve recently
    separated veterans.

(c) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
    $50,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2031 to
    carry out the grant program under this section.

SEC. 5. RESEARCH AND EVALUATION.

(a) RESEARCH.—The Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall fund
    independent research on the effectiveness of identity
    transition programming, including randomized controlled trials
    where feasible.

(b) METRICS.—The program shall be evaluated using validated
    measures of—
    (1) sense of purpose and meaning;
    (2) social connectedness;
    (3) civilian identity development;
    (4) adjustment to civilian life; and
    (5) overall well-being and mental health.

(c) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
    $20,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2031 to
    carry out this section.

SEC. 6. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There is authorized to be appropriated $300,000,000 for each of
fiscal years 2027 through 2031 to carry out this Act, in addition
to amounts authorized under sections 4 and 5.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses what research identifies as the most powerful yet most neglected dimension of military-to-civilian transition: identity. The military invests heavily in transforming civilian identity into military identity through basic training and years of cultural immersion, but invests nothing in the reverse process. The result is that more than half of veterans report loss of purpose and nearly half report difficulty adjusting, even when employment and financial outcomes are adequate. The peer facilitator model leverages veteran lived experience while creating veteran employment. Community organization partnerships build on the proven effectiveness of organizations like Team RWB and The Mission Continues.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Cultural resistance within DOD to "soft" programming focused on identity and emotions
- Difficulty measuring identity transition outcomes with validated instruments
- Recruiting and retaining 1,000 qualified peer facilitators
- Ensuring program quality across diverse installation and community settings
- Risk that mandatory participation reduces engagement

**Refinements**:

- Frame the program as "mission transition" or "next mission preparation" to reduce cultural resistance
- Develop the curriculum with input from senior enlisted leaders and respected veteran voices
- Allow flexible delivery formats (in-person, virtual, hybrid) to accommodate different settings
- Make the pre-separation component mandatory but offer the post-separation component as opt-in with proactive outreach
- Build evaluation requirements into the program from inception to demonstrate effectiveness early

### Bill 4: DOD-VA Transition Data Integration Act

**Purpose**: Create a unified data system that seamlessly transfers service records, medical records, training documentation, and transition planning information from DOD to VA at the point of separation, eliminating the bureaucratic gap that forces veterans to re-navigate benefits systems.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "DOD-VA Transition Data Integration
Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that—
    (1) the Government Accountability Office has repeatedly
        identified DOD-VA data sharing as a critical transition
        gap;
    (2) veterans spend an average of 40 or more hours navigating
        VA enrollment, disability claims, and benefits applications
        after separation;
    (3) only 62 percent of veterans enroll in VA healthcare within
        1 year of separation, in part due to enrollment complexity;
    (4) DOD service records, medical records, and training records
        do not automatically transfer to VA in a usable format;
    (5) the United Kingdom's Career Transition Partnership operates
        a unified data system that automatically transfers service
        records to Veterans UK; and
    (6) the absence of unified data prevents outcome tracking and
        program evaluation.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to—
    (1) establish a Joint DOD-VA Transition Data Office;
    (2) create a standardized Veteran Transition Record that
        compiles all relevant service information;
    (3) enable automatic VA enrollment and pre-populated benefit
        applications at separation; and
    (4) support privacy-protected outcome tracking.

SEC. 3. JOINT DOD-VA TRANSITION DATA OFFICE.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary of Defense and the Secretary of
    Veterans Affairs shall jointly establish a Transition Data
    Office with authority over transition-related data systems in
    both departments.

(b) DIRECTOR.—The Office shall be led by a Director appointed
    jointly by the two Secretaries, who shall report to both the
    Under Secretary of Defense for Personnel and Readiness and
    the VA Under Secretary for Benefits.

(c) AUTHORITY.—The Director shall have the authority to—
    (1) establish data standards for transition-related information;
    (2) require DOD and VA components to transmit data in
        standardized formats;
    (3) manage the Veteran Transition Record system; and
    (4) coordinate with the Department of Labor, the Department
        of Education, and state agencies for outcome tracking
        purposes.

SEC. 4. VETERAN TRANSITION RECORD.

(a) CREATION.—Not later than 18 months after the date of enactment
    of this Act, the Director shall establish a Veteran Transition
    Record system that automatically compiles the following for each
    separating service member:
    (1) complete service record, including assignments, deployments,
        and awards;
    (2) military medical records, including exposure records;
    (3) training certificates and completion records;
    (4) military occupational specialty qualifications;
    (5) civilian credential equivalencies as determined under
        applicable DOD and DOL standards;
    (6) Individualized Transition Plan and progress; and
    (7) SkillBridge participation and outcomes.

(b) TRANSMISSION.—The Veteran Transition Record shall be
    transmitted to VA not later than 30 days before the member's
    date of separation.

(c) AUTOMATIC ENROLLMENT.—Upon receipt of the Veteran Transition
    Record, the VA shall—
    (1) automatically enroll the veteran in VA healthcare unless
        the veteran opts out;
    (2) generate pre-populated disability claim forms based on
        service medical records;
    (3) activate education benefit eligibility; and
    (4) provide the veteran with a summary of all available
        benefits and enrollment status.

(d) VETERAN ACCESS.—Each veteran shall have electronic access to
    their Veteran Transition Record through a secure online portal,
    with the ability to authorize data sharing with—
    (1) state veterans affairs offices;
    (2) employers for credential verification;
    (3) educational institutions for credit evaluation; and
    (4) veteran service organizations for benefits assistance.

SEC. 5. OUTCOME DATA INTEGRATION.

(a) DATA MATCHING.—The Director, in coordination with the Secretary
    of Labor and the Commissioner of the Bureau of Labor Statistics,
    shall establish a privacy-protected data matching system linking
    DOD transition records with—
    (1) DOL wage and employment records;
    (2) VA healthcare and benefits utilization data;
    (3) Department of Education enrollment and completion data; and
    (4) HUD housing assistance data.

(b) PRIVACY.—All data matching under this section shall comply
    with the Privacy Act of 1974 (5 U.S.C. 552a), and
    individually identifiable data shall not be disclosed except
    in aggregate form for program evaluation purposes or with
    the individual's written consent.

SEC. 6. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

(a) DEVELOPMENT.—There is authorized to be appropriated
    $500,000,000 for the development and deployment of the Veteran
    Transition Record system.

(b) OPERATIONS.—There is authorized to be appropriated
    $100,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2028 through 2032 for
    the operation and maintenance of the system.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the DOD-VA handoff gap that forces veterans to re-prove their service history and re-navigate bureaucracies after separation. The Veteran Transition Record creates a comprehensive, portable digital record that follows the service member from DOD to VA and beyond. Automatic VA enrollment eliminates the barrier that currently leaves 38% of veterans unenrolled in VA healthcare during their first year. The data integration provisions enable the outcome tracking essential for program evaluation. The joint office structure ensures that neither DOD nor VA can unilaterally control the system.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Legacy IT systems in both DOD and VA make data integration technically difficult
- Interagency bureaucratic resistance to shared data governance
- Privacy advocates may oppose comprehensive data matching
- Cost overrun risk for large federal IT projects
- Timeline risk given the history of DOD-VA IT initiatives

**Refinements**:

- Adopt modular, cloud-based architecture to avoid monolithic system risks
- Phase deployment starting with highest-volume installations
- Engage privacy advocacy organizations in system design from inception
- Include independent technical oversight with reporting to Congress
- Build on existing successful data sharing initiatives rather than creating entirely new systems

## State Model Legislation

### Model Veteran-Ready Community Act

**Purpose**: Establish state-level programs supporting community preparedness for veteran integration, with employer engagement, educational institution support, and civic organization involvement.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Veteran-Ready Community Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

As used in this Act:
    (a) "Veteran-Ready Community" means a municipality, county, or
        region that has been certified by the Department of Veterans
        Affairs as meeting the standards established under Section 3.
    (b) "Community transition plan" means a plan developed by a
        municipality, county, or region to support the integration
        of transitioning veterans and military families.
    (c) "Recently separated veteran" means a veteran who has been
        discharged or released from the Armed Forces within the
        preceding 24 months.

SECTION 3. VETERAN-READY COMMUNITY STANDARDS.

    (a) The [State Department of Veterans Affairs / equivalent agency]
        shall develop Veteran-Ready Community standards addressing—
        (1) employer engagement, including veteran hiring programs,
            SkillBridge partnerships, and military credential
            recognition;
        (2) healthcare provider training in military cultural
            competency;
        (3) educational institution support, including ACE credit
            acceptance, veteran student centers, and flexible
            scheduling;
        (4) civic organization involvement in veteran integration;
        (5) housing access, including coordination with VA home
            loan programs; and
        (6) peer support networks connecting recently separated
            veterans with established community members.
    (b) Standards shall be developed in consultation with veteran
        service organizations, employers, educational institutions,
        and local governments.

SECTION 4. CERTIFICATION PROCESS.

    (a) Any municipality, county, or region may apply for
        Veteran-Ready Community certification by submitting a
        community transition plan demonstrating compliance with
        the standards under Section 3.
    (b) Certification shall be valid for 3 years, subject to renewal
        upon demonstration of continued compliance.
    (c) The [State Department of Veterans Affairs] shall maintain a
        public registry of certified Veteran-Ready Communities.

SECTION 5. INCENTIVES.

    (a) Certified Veteran-Ready Communities shall receive priority
        consideration for state economic development grants.
    (b) Employers within certified communities may receive a state
        tax credit of $1,000 per veteran hired and retained for 12
        months.
    (c) The State shall promote certified communities to the
        Department of Defense as preferred destinations for
        separating service members.

SECTION 6. REPORTING.

    The [State Department of Veterans Affairs] shall report annually
    to the legislature on—
    (1) the number of certified Veteran-Ready Communities;
    (2) veteran population and transition outcomes in certified
        communities compared to non-certified communities; and
    (3) recommendations for program improvement.
```

**Explanation**: This model legislation creates a state-level framework for community preparedness to receive and integrate transitioning veterans. The certification model provides incentives without mandates, encouraging communities to invest in veteran integration infrastructure. The standards cover the full range of transition needs--employment, healthcare, education, housing, and social connection. The reporting requirement enables evidence-based improvement.

**Adaptations**: States may adjust certification standards, incentive levels, and reporting requirements based on their veteran population, economic conditions, and existing veteran service infrastructure. States with large military installations may prioritize communities adjacent to bases, while states with dispersed veteran populations may focus on regional coordination.

### Model Military Spouse Transition Employment Act

**Purpose**: Address military spouse employment barriers during the transition period through expedited credential recognition, employer engagement, and job placement support.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Military Spouse Transition
Employment Act".

SECTION 2. EXPEDITED CREDENTIAL RECOGNITION.

    (a) Any military spouse relocating to this State as a result
        of a service member's separation or retirement shall be
        eligible for expedited processing of any state-regulated
        professional license, certification, or registration.
    (b) Applications from eligible military spouses shall be
        processed within 30 days of receipt.
    (c) Where the spouse holds a valid license from another state,
        temporary authorization to practice shall be granted upon
        application, pending full license review.

SECTION 3. TRANSITION EMPLOYMENT SERVICES.

    (a) The [State Department of Labor / workforce agency] shall
        provide transition employment services to military spouses,
        including—
        (1) career counseling and assessment;
        (2) resume and interview preparation;
        (3) referral to employer partners; and
        (4) information on state workforce development programs.
    (b) Services shall be available beginning 6 months before the
        anticipated date of the service member's separation.

SECTION 4. EMPLOYER TAX CREDIT.

    Employers who hire military spouses transitioning as a result
    of a service member's separation shall be eligible for a state
    tax credit of $1,500 per spouse hired and retained for 12 months.
```

**Explanation**: Military spouses face a 21% unemployment rate during transition, and 68% must find new employment when the service member separates. This model legislation addresses the spousal employment challenge through expedited credentialing, transition-specific employment services, and employer incentives.

**Adaptations**: States may adjust the tax credit amount, credential processing timelines, and service delivery models based on local labor market conditions and existing workforce development infrastructure.

## Regulatory Framework

### DOD TAP Modernization Regulation

**Existing Authority**: 10 U.S.C. 1142-1144; VOW to Hire Heroes Act (P.L. 112-56)

**Draft Regulation**:

The Secretary of Defense shall amend DOD Instruction 1332.35 to:

1. Replace the one-size-fits-all TAP curriculum with differentiated tracks based on rank, military occupational specialty, education level, family status, and career goals
2. Require Transition Readiness Assessments at the 12-month mark with individualized planning
3. Reduce maximum TAP class size to 25 participants to enable meaningful interaction
4. Require TAP facilitators to hold relevant professional qualifications and complete military cultural competency training
5. Add a mandatory Identity Transition module addressing purpose, belonging, and civilian identity development
6. Convert at least 50% of TAP delivery from passive lecture to experiential learning, mentoring, and employer engagement
7. Establish minimum standards for SkillBridge employer partners including mentorship, training quality, and employment outcome reporting
8. Prohibit virtual-only TAP delivery where in-person delivery is available, except for service members at remote installations

**Explanation**: These regulatory changes can be implemented through DOD instruction under existing statutory authority, without requiring new legislation. They address the most immediate quality issues with TAP delivery: one-size-fits-all curriculum, passive delivery, large class sizes, and facilitator quality.

### VA Post-Separation Support Enhancement Regulation

**Existing Authority**: 38 U.S.C. Chapter 31; 38 U.S.C. 6301-6308

**Draft Regulation**:

The Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall amend 38 C.F.R. to:

1. Transform the Solid Start program from phone check-ins to comprehensive post-separation case management with assigned case managers
2. Reduce the VA Veteran Readiness and Employment (VR&E) counselor-to-veteran ratio to 1:100 maximum
3. Establish an expedited VR&E enrollment track for veterans within 24 months of separation
4. Create automatic VA healthcare enrollment for all separating service members, with opt-out rather than opt-in
5. Require VA medical centers to designate a Transition Veteran Coordinator responsible for ensuring recently separated veterans receive timely healthcare access
6. Establish community integration programming at VA medical centers in partnership with veteran service organizations

### DOD-VA Joint Transition Instruction

**Existing Authority**: 10 U.S.C. 1142; 38 U.S.C. 6301

**Draft Regulation**:

The Secretary of Defense and the Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall issue a Joint Transition Instruction establishing:

1. A Joint Transition Governance Board with representatives from DOD, VA, DOL, and Education, meeting quarterly to coordinate transition policy
2. Shared data standards for transition-related information transfer
3. A single point of accountability for each transitioning member from -12 months to +24 months
4. Standardized outcome metrics aligned across all federal transition programs
5. Joint training requirements for all personnel delivering transition services

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **War Powers and Military Personnel**: Article I, Section 8 grants Congress broad authority over the Armed Forces, including the power to "make Rules for the Government and Regulation of the land and naval Forces." This provides clear constitutional authority for the Comprehensive Transition Continuum Act and the SkillBridge Universalization Act
- **Spending Clause**: Federal grants for Veteran-Ready Communities and community organization partnerships are supported by the Spending Clause (Article I, Section 8, Clause 1). Conditions on state participation must be reasonably related to the purpose of the spending, per *South Dakota v. Dole*, 483 U.S. 203 (1987)
- **Privacy (Fourth and Fifth Amendments)**: The DOD-VA Transition Data Integration Act's comprehensive data collection raises potential Fourth Amendment concerns. The bill addresses these through Privacy Act protections, opt-in consent for third-party sharing, and prohibition on individual data disclosure without consent
- **Due Process (Fifth Amendment)**: Protected Transition Time restrictions on commanders must include adequate exceptions and review procedures to avoid due process concerns. The bill provides both

### Preemption Questions

- The Comprehensive Transition Continuum Act and SkillBridge Universalization Act operate within federal military authority and do not preempt state law
- The DOD-VA Transition Data Integration Act's automatic enrollment provisions operate within federal agency authority over VA benefits
- State model legislation operates within state sovereignty over community development, occupational licensing, and tax policy
- The Identity Transition Support Act's community partnerships operate through voluntary agreements, not preemption

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- Protected Transition Time: Commander accountability through quarterly reporting, performance evaluation inclusion, and inspector general review of denials
- SkillBridge access: Written justification for denials with general/flag officer review; annual reporting to Congress
- Outcome tracking: Mandatory annual report to Congress; performance-based contracting for TAP
- Data integration: Joint DOD-VA governance with congressional oversight
- Community certification: State-level reporting; federal grant conditions

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- All programs subject to independent evaluation at 5-year intervals beginning 3 years after enactment
- Transition Outcome Reports published annually with trend analysis
- SkillBridge employer partnerships reviewed annually for quality and employment outcomes
- Identity Transition Program evaluated using validated instruments with results published
- DOD-VA Transition Data Office subject to GAO audit every 3 years
- Performance-based contracting percentages reviewed and adjusted every 3 years

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Comprehensive Transition Continuum Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Commander override abuse | "Operational necessity" exception could be used routinely to deny Protected Transition Time | High |
| Transition Coordinator understaffing | Staffing ratio (1:125) may be difficult to maintain at smaller installations, leaving some members without meaningful support | Medium |
| Compliance without engagement | Members may attend required sessions without genuine engagement, replicating the current "check-the-box" problem | Medium |
| Post-separation dropout | Veterans may disengage from post-separation support after the first few months, when support is most needed | High |
| Contractor quality variation | TAP contractors may meet minimum requirements without providing quality services | Medium |
| Data reporting gaps | Privacy protections could be interpreted to prevent meaningful outcome tracking | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Guard and Reserve coverage | Part-time service members have different separation timelines and less access to installation-based programs | Different employment relationship; geographically dispersed population |
| Involuntary separations | Members separated for disciplinary reasons may not receive full 12-month pre-separation support | Separation timing not always predictable |
| Rural post-separation access | Veterans in rural areas may lack access to in-person post-separation services | Geographic dispersion; limited VA and community resources |
| Employer-dependent outcomes | Transition success still depends on civilian employer willingness to hire and retain veterans | Beyond federal program control |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Define "operational necessity" narrowly in implementing regulations, with mandatory inspector general review of installations where denial rates exceed 10%
2. Allow flexible Transition Coordinator staffing models including part-time, shared, and nonprofit partnership arrangements for smaller installations
3. Replace passive attendance tracking with engagement metrics including individual plan completion, milestone achievement, and satisfaction assessment
4. Implement graduated post-separation outreach with increasing intensity for veterans who disengage, including home visits and warm referrals to community organizations
5. Establish minimum quality standards for TAP contractors with annual performance reviews and contract termination for persistent underperformance
6. Create a parallel transition support pathway for Guard and Reserve members delivered through their home communities
7. Develop expedited transition programming for involuntary separations

### SkillBridge Universalization Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Deployment exception gaming | Units could place separating members on deployment orders to prevent SkillBridge participation | High |
| Employer quality variation | Employer partners may use SkillBridge as free labor without genuine training or employment pathway | Medium |
| Geographic mismatch | Service members may not find SkillBridge opportunities in their intended post-separation location | Medium |
| Small business capacity | Small employers may lack capacity to provide meaningful SkillBridge experiences | Low |
| Virtual SkillBridge quality | Remote fellowships may lack the immersive experience that drives SkillBridge's high placement rate | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Limited to final 180 days | Some career transitions require longer preparation than SkillBridge's 180-day window | DOD operational concerns about earlier release |
| Employer concentration | Majority of SkillBridge employers are large corporations; limited small business and nonprofit options | Administrative burden of partnership |
| Rank disparity | Officers and senior NCOs may access better SkillBridge opportunities than junior enlisted | Professional network and information advantages |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Require inspector general investigation of any installation where more than 5% of SkillBridge applications are denied
2. Establish employer performance standards including minimum mentorship hours, training quality metrics, and employment outcome reporting, with removal from the program for persistent underperformance
3. Create a SkillBridge matching system that connects service members to employers in their intended post-separation geographic area
4. Provide administrative support and mentorship to small business SkillBridge partners through local chambers of commerce and SBA offices
5. Establish quality standards for virtual SkillBridge with required in-person components where feasible
6. Create a SkillBridge equity review to ensure junior enlisted access is comparable to senior members

### Military Identity Transition Support Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Cultural resistance bypass | Installations could implement the program minimally to meet compliance requirements without genuine engagement | Medium |
| Facilitator quality variation | Peer facilitators may vary significantly in skill and effectiveness | Medium |
| Measurement gaming | Programs could optimize for easily measurable outcomes rather than genuine identity development | Low |
| Community organization selectivity | Grant recipients could serve only easy-to-reach veterans, neglecting those most in need | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Difficult to measure | Identity transition outcomes are harder to quantify than employment or income | Inherently qualitative dimension of human experience |
| Cultural stigma | Military culture may stigmatize participation in "soft" programming | Deep-rooted military cultural norms |
| Facilitator retention | Trained peer facilitators may leave for higher-paying positions | Competitive labor market |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Require senior military leadership endorsement and participation in Identity Transition programming to reduce cultural stigma
2. Develop validated psychometric instruments for measuring identity transition outcomes, in partnership with military psychology researchers
3. Establish peer facilitator career pathways with advancement opportunities and competitive compensation
4. Require grant recipients to serve diverse veteran populations with outreach to underserved communities
5. Include feedback mechanisms allowing participants to evaluate program quality and facilitator effectiveness

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Interagency coordination failure | Establish a White House-level Veteran Transition Coordination Office with authority over DOD, VA, DOL, and Education transition activities |
| Congressional funding instability | Seek multi-year mandatory appropriations for the Transition Continuum rather than annual discretionary funding |
| State implementation variation for model legislation | Provide implementation templates, technical assistance, and federal incentive grants for state adoption |
| Nonprofit fragmentation | Create coordination platforms (building on AmericaServes model) to connect transitioning members to the full range of available community resources |
| IT project risk | Adopt agile development methodology with phased deployment and independent technical oversight for all data systems |
| Commander culture change | Incorporate transition outcomes into officer and NCO evaluation reports; provide senior leader education on transition importance |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- All programs subject to independent evaluation at 5-year intervals with published results
- Transition Outcome Reports published annually with trend analysis
- Peer facilitator training curriculum updated every 3 years based on research and participant feedback
- SkillBridge employer partnerships reviewed annually; underperforming employers removed
- Data system privacy impact assessment conducted annually by an independent privacy officer
- Annual congressional testimony from DOD, VA, and DOL on transition outcomes
- GAO audit of the Transition Data Office every 3 years
