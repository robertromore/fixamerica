# VA Reform: Solutions

## Overview

VA reform solutions must address the fundamental tension between strengthening VA direct care and maintaining appropriate community care access, while simultaneously modernizing technology, improving governance, and building workforce capacity. The solutions below are organized by category and assessed for impact, feasibility, and evidence base.

---

## Governance Reforms

### 1. VA Secretary Term Appointment

**Description**: Establish a fixed 7-year term for the VA Secretary, appointed by the President and confirmed by the Senate, to ensure continuity across administrations.

**How It Works**:

- The Secretary serves a fixed 7-year term, removable only for cause (malfeasance, incapacity)
- Staggered with presidential terms to reduce political pressure
- Senate confirmation required; bipartisan support encouraged through term structure
- Deputy Secretary serves as acting Secretary between terms
- Similar to FBI Director (10-year term), Federal Reserve Chair (4-year renewable)

**Evidence**: Average VA Secretary tenure of 2.5 years since 2009 is insufficient for multi-year reform programs. The Kizer transformation (1994-1999) succeeded partly because Kizer served 5 years. The EHR modernization has been managed by 4 Secretaries, each with different priorities (NAPA, "VA Governance Assessment," 2023).

**Impact**: Very high -- leadership stability is the prerequisite for all other reforms
**Feasibility**: Low -- requires Presidential agreement to reduce executive appointment power
**Timeline**: Requires legislation; would take effect at next Secretary appointment

### 2. VA Governance Board

**Description**: Create an independent, bipartisan governance board modeled on corporate boards of directors to provide strategic oversight and continuity.

**How It Works**:

- Board of 11 members: 5 appointed by the President, 3 by Congressional leadership (bipartisan), 3 veteran representatives selected by VSOs
- Staggered 6-year terms to ensure continuity
- Board approves multi-year strategic plans, major IT investments, and facility decisions
- Board does not manage day-to-day operations (Secretary retains operational authority)
- Board provides accountability that transcends political transitions

**Evidence**: Similar governance structures exist for federal entities (USPS Board of Governors, Amtrak Board, Federal Reserve Board). The VA's lack of independent strategic oversight has contributed to inconsistent reform direction (NAPA, 2023).

**Impact**: High -- provides institutional continuity and strategic discipline
**Feasibility**: Moderate -- requires legislation but does not reduce Presidential authority as much as term appointment
**Timeline**: 1-2 years for legislation and initial appointments

### 3. Unified VA Access Standards

**Description**: Establish clear, evidence-based standards for when veterans should receive VA direct care versus community care, ending the drift toward unplanned privatization.

**How It Works**:

- Commission a comprehensive study of clinical outcomes, cost, and veteran satisfaction for VA vs. community care by condition type
- Set access standards based on evidence: conditions where VA demonstrably outperforms (PTSD, TBI, spinal cord injury, amputee care) should default to VA direct care
- Conditions where VA has no specialized advantage and community providers are more accessible can default to community care
- Veterans retain choice within the framework, but the default is evidence-based
- Regularly update standards based on outcomes data

**Evidence**: RAND Corporation found that VA healthcare quality exceeds private-sector benchmarks for most comparable conditions, particularly veteran-specific conditions (RAND, "Assessment of VA Healthcare Quality," 2023). Community care costs per encounter average 15-30% more than VA direct care for comparable services (VA Office of Community Care, FY 2024).

**Impact**: High -- resolves the privatization-investment deadlock with evidence
**Feasibility**: Moderate -- politically sensitive but evidence-based approach reduces ideological resistance
**Timeline**: 2-3 years for study, rulemaking, and implementation

## Workforce Solutions

### 4. VA Compensation Competitiveness Program

**Description**: Close the gap between VA and private-sector compensation for clinical and technical positions.

**How It Works**:

- Conduct annual market-rate surveys for all VA clinical and technical positions
- Set VA compensation at 90-95% of market median (acknowledging federal benefits and mission-driven workforce)
- Expand the RAISE Act authorities for critical shortage specialties
- Implement geographic pay differentials to address high-cost and rural area recruitment
- Provide retention bonuses for employees with 5+ years of VA service
- Expand Education Debt Reduction Program funding

**Evidence**: VA physician salaries average 20-30% below private-sector equivalents. Nursing vacancy rate (10.2%) exceeds national average (~8%). Overall vacancy rate of 11.2% represents approximately 52,000 unfilled positions (VA Office of Academic Affiliations, 2024; BLS, 2024).

**Impact**: High -- addresses the fundamental recruitment and retention barrier
**Feasibility**: Moderate -- requires sustained appropriations commitment
**Timeline**: 2-3 years for full implementation

### 5. VA Workforce Pipeline Program

**Description**: Build a dedicated pipeline of clinical and technical talent through education, training, and career development programs.

**How It Works**:

- Expand VA academic affiliations with medical, nursing, and allied health programs
- Create "VA Scholar" programs offering full tuition in exchange for VA service commitment
- Establish VA-specific clinical residency programs with guaranteed VA employment
- Partner with community colleges for VA administrative and technical positions
- Target recruitment in rural and underserved communities where VA vacancy rates are highest

**Evidence**: The VA's existing academic affiliation program trains approximately 120,000 health professionals annually, many of whom enter VA employment. Expanding this model could significantly address workforce gaps (VA Office of Academic Affiliations, 2024).

**Impact**: High -- builds long-term workforce capacity
**Feasibility**: High -- builds on existing infrastructure
**Timeline**: 3-5 years for full pipeline development

### 6. VA Employee Engagement and Burnout Reduction

**Description**: Address the root causes of VA employee burnout through adequate staffing, reasonable workloads, and meaningful engagement in reform.

**How It Works**:

- Establish minimum staffing ratios for clinical positions
- Reduce mandatory overtime through adequate hiring
- Involve frontline employees in process improvement and technology decisions
- Create meaningful career advancement pathways
- Implement evidence-based burnout prevention programs

**Evidence**: VA employee burnout rates increased from 35% in 2019 to 44% in 2024. Employee turnover costs the VA an estimated $2.1 billion annually in recruitment, training, and productivity loss (VA All Employee Survey, 2024; VA OIG Workforce Analysis, 2024).

**Impact**: Moderate-high -- reduces turnover and improves service quality
**Feasibility**: High -- mostly within VA management authority
**Timeline**: 1-3 years

## Technology Solutions

### 7. EHR Modernization Reset

**Description**: Conduct a comprehensive reassessment of the EHR modernization program and implement a revised strategy that prioritizes clinician usability and patient safety.

**How It Works**:

- Independent review of Oracle Health deployment results vs. VistA capabilities
- Define minimum usability and safety standards that must be met before further deployment
- Consider hybrid approach: modernize VistA for stable functions while implementing Oracle Health for interoperability-critical functions
- Establish clinician advisory board with veto power over deployment decisions that affect patient safety
- Renegotiate Oracle Health contract based on performance, not sunk costs

**Evidence**: The EHR modernization has cost $16.1 billion with only 5 of 171 medical centers deployed. The VA OIG documented 149+ patient safety incidents at deployed sites. VA clinicians report significantly more time per encounter with Oracle Health vs. VistA (VA OIG, 2024; GAO-24-106605, 2024).

**Impact**: Very high -- the current trajectory wastes billions while degrading care
**Feasibility**: Moderate -- contractual and political obstacles to renegotiation
**Timeline**: 1-2 years for reassessment; 5-10 years for revised implementation

### 8. Integrated Digital Services Platform

**Description**: Build a unified veteran-facing digital platform connecting all VA services (healthcare, benefits, memorial) in a single experience.

**How It Works**:

- Consolidate VA.gov, eBenefits, MyHealtheVet, and other portals into a single platform
- Provide unified claim tracking, appointment scheduling, prescription management, and benefits information
- Enable secure messaging with both VA and community care providers
- Mobile-first design for veteran convenience
- Integrate with VA internal systems for seamless data flow

**Evidence**: VA.gov modernization has improved but multiple legacy portals still operate in parallel. Veterans report confusion navigating between systems (VA Veterans Experience Office, FY 2024).

**Impact**: Moderate -- improves veteran experience without changing underlying operations
**Feasibility**: High -- builds on VA.gov modernization already underway
**Timeline**: 2-3 years

## Structural Reforms

### 9. Advance Appropriations for All VA Operations

**Description**: Extend advance appropriations (currently limited to medical care) to cover all VA operations, including claims processing, IT, and infrastructure.

**How It Works**:

- Medical care advance appropriations have been in effect since 2009, providing budget certainty 1 year ahead
- Extend this model to VBA operations, IT modernization, and construction
- Allows multi-year hiring plans and technology investments without annual appropriations uncertainty
- Does not increase total spending; simply provides earlier certainty

**Evidence**: VA operations under continuing resolutions have been disrupted in 5 of the last 8 fiscal years. Claims processing hiring delays and IT procurement delays have been directly attributed to appropriations uncertainty (CRS, "Impact of Continuing Resolutions on VA," 2024).

**Impact**: High -- enables long-term planning and reduces crisis-driven management
**Feasibility**: Moderate -- requires appropriations committee agreement
**Timeline**: Requires legislation; could be enacted within 1-2 years

### 10. VA Infrastructure Modernization

**Description**: Implement a comprehensive infrastructure plan to modernize, consolidate, and geographically realign VA facilities.

**How It Works**:

- Revive the AIR Commission concept with stronger Congressional buy-in
- Prioritize construction in Sun Belt and growing veteran population areas
- Convert underutilized facilities to community-based outpatient clinics or nursing homes
- Accelerate construction timelines through streamlined procurement
- Fund through dedicated infrastructure bond authority or PACT Act Toxic Exposure Fund reallocations

**Evidence**: VA construction backlog exceeds $52 billion. Average VA medical center building age is 60+ years. Fastest-growing veteran populations are in areas with fewest VA facilities per capita (VA Office of Construction, 2024; Census Bureau, 2024).

**Impact**: Very high long-term -- but requires enormous investment and political will
**Feasibility**: Low -- AIR Commission already failed once; local politics protect every facility
**Timeline**: 10-20 years for comprehensive realignment

### 11. Community Care Quality and Cost Management

**Description**: Implement robust quality monitoring and cost controls for VA community care to ensure it complements rather than undermines VA direct care.

**How It Works**:

- Require community care providers to report quality metrics comparable to VA SAIL system
- Tie continued participation in community care network to quality benchmarks
- Implement cost comparison thresholds: VA direct care should be preferred when it is both available and cost-effective
- Improve care coordination through shared electronic health records
- Establish veteran satisfaction surveys for community care encounters

**Evidence**: Community care spending grew 56% in three years (FY 2021-2024) without corresponding quality monitoring. Community providers do not currently report quality data comparable to VA's SAIL system (VA Office of Community Care, FY 2024).

**Impact**: Moderate-high -- prevents the community care "death spiral"
**Feasibility**: Moderate -- requires regulatory change and data infrastructure
**Timeline**: 2-3 years
