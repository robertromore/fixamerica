# VA Reform: History

## Foundations (1930-1988)

### Creation and Growth

- **1930**: President Hoover creates the Veterans Administration by Executive Order 5398, consolidating the Veterans Bureau, Bureau of Pensions, and National Home for Disabled Volunteer Soldiers
- **1944**: The Servicemen's Readjustment Act (GI Bill) transforms the VA from a disability agency into a comprehensive benefits provider; VA becomes one of the largest federal agencies
- **1946**: VA employment peaks at 200,000 as the agency processes millions of World War II veteran claims and builds a national hospital system
- **1946**: General Omar Bradley appointed VA Administrator; professionalizes the agency and establishes the medical school affiliation program that remains the backbone of VA healthcare
- **1950s-1960s**: VA healthcare system expands to 171 hospitals; becomes the largest integrated healthcare system in the United States
- **1970s**: Vietnam veterans challenge the VA's reluctance to acknowledge Agent Orange exposure and PTSD as service-connected conditions; VA culture clashes with younger, more skeptical veteran population

### Elevation to Cabinet Level

- **1988**: Department of Veterans Affairs Act (Public Law 100-527) elevates the VA to Cabinet-level status, effective March 15, 1989
- The elevation was intended to give the Secretary a seat at the budget table and greater political leverage
- Critics argued it would insulate the VA from reform by increasing bureaucratic prestige without changing operations
- The VA became the Department of Veterans Affairs, the 14th Cabinet department

### Veterans' Judicial Review

- **1988**: Veterans' Judicial Review Act (Public Law 100-687) creates the Court of Veterans Appeals (later renamed Court of Appeals for Veterans Claims)
- For the first time, veterans could appeal VA benefit decisions to an independent court
- The court dramatically increased procedural requirements for VA adjudications
- Judicial review improved fairness but added complexity that contributed to processing delays

## The VistA Era (1982-2010)

### Development of VistA

The Veterans Health Information Systems and Technology Architecture (VistA) was one of the most successful government IT projects in history.

- **1982**: VA clinicians begin developing the Decentralized Hospital Computer Program (DHCP)
- **1996**: DHCP renamed VistA; becomes fully integrated electronic health record
- **1997-2003**: Under VA Secretary Ken Kizer, VistA implementation transforms VA healthcare quality
- The system was developed largely by VA clinicians (not contractors), creating user buy-in and functionality
- VistA became a model studied by health systems worldwide

### The Kizer Transformation (1994-1999)

Under Secretary Kenneth Kizer, the VA underwent its most successful reform period:

| Reform | Description | Impact |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| Regional restructuring | Reorganized from 4 regions to 22 Veterans Integrated Service Networks (VISNs) | Decentralized decision-making; improved accountability |
| Performance measurement | Implemented comprehensive quality metrics | VA quality rose from below to above private-sector benchmarks |
| VistA deployment | Electronic health record to all facilities | Transformed care coordination and quality |
| Primary care emphasis | Shifted from hospital-centric to primary care model | Better preventive care; reduced hospitalizations |
| Accountability | Linked VISN director tenure to performance | Created real consequences for underperformance |

*Source: Longman, Phillip. *Best Care Anywhere: Why VA Health Care Would Work Better for Everyone*. Polipoint Press, 2012.*

The Kizer era demonstrated that the VA *can* be reformed through strong leadership, data-driven management, and organizational restructuring. However, many of his reforms eroded after his departure.

## Crisis and Response (2014-2018)

### The 2014 Wait-Time Scandal

The most significant VA crisis in modern history erupted in April 2014 when whistleblowers at the Phoenix VA Medical Center revealed that veterans were dying while waiting for healthcare appointments, and that VA officials had created secret wait lists to conceal the true extent of delays.

**Timeline:**

- **April 2014**: CNN reports on secret wait lists at Phoenix VAMC; at least 40 veterans died while waiting for care
- **May 2014**: VA OIG confirms systemic manipulation of wait-time data at Phoenix and other facilities
- **May 30, 2014**: Secretary Eric Shinseki resigns
- **June 2014**: Independent Assessment of VA Health Care (commissioned; report published 2015)
- **August 2014**: Veterans Access, Choice, and Accountability Act (Choice Act) signed, creating the Veterans Choice Program allowing private-sector care

**Key findings:**

- Wait-time manipulation was not limited to Phoenix; the VA OIG identified problems at facilities nationwide
- The scandal was ultimately a failure of organizational culture: the VA's reporting systems incentivized gaming rather than honest assessment
- The structural problem was not that veterans lacked access to care, but that the VA measured and reported access dishonestly

### Veterans Choice Program (2014-2018)

The Choice Act created the Veterans Choice Program, which for the first time gave veterans broad access to private-sector healthcare:

| Metric | FY 2015 | FY 2016 | FY 2017 | FY 2018 |
|--------|---------|---------|---------|---------|
| Choice appointments | 5.2M | 12.4M | 18.7M | 22.1M |
| Choice spending | $3.8B | $7.2B | $10.5B | $13.8B |

*Source: VA Office of Community Care, FY 2015-2018.*

**Problems with the Choice Program:**

- Third-party administrator (Health Net/TriWest) scheduling delays sometimes exceeded VA wait times
- Care coordination between VA and community providers was poor
- Payment delays to community providers caused many to stop accepting VA patients
- The program was initially funded as temporary, creating uncertainty
- Eligibility criteria (40-mile distance, 30-day wait) were rigid and poorly designed

### VA Accountability Act (2017)

The VA Accountability and Whistleblower Protection Act (Public Law 115-41, 2017) was intended to make it easier to fire underperforming VA employees.

**Key provisions:**

- Shortened appeals process for removals
- Reduced standard of evidence for adverse actions
- Created Office of Accountability and Whistleblower Protection
- Protected whistleblowers from retaliation

**Results:**

- The act was used primarily to remove lower-level employees rather than senior leaders
- The Office of Accountability and Whistleblower Protection was itself found to have retaliated against whistleblowers (VA OIG Report 19-00041-241, 2020)
- Federal courts struck down some provisions as violating due process (e.g., *Sayers v. VA*, MSPB, 2019)
- Overall impact on VA accountability was limited

## The MISSION Act Era (2018-Present)

### VA MISSION Act (2018)

The VA Maintaining Internal Systems and Strengthening Integrated Outside Networks (MISSION) Act (Public Law 115-182, 2018) replaced the Choice Program with a permanent community care framework.

**Key provisions:**

- Permanent eligibility for community care based on access standards (wait times and drive times)
- Asset and Infrastructure Review (AIR) Commission to evaluate VA facility needs
- Caregiver support program expansion
- Urgent care benefit for enrolled veterans
- Walk-in care authority

### Community Care Growth

Community care has grown rapidly under the MISSION Act, shifting an increasing share of VA healthcare spending to the private sector:

| Year | Community Care as % of VHA Spending | Total Community Care Spending |
|------|--------------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| FY 2018 | 22% | $13.8B |
| FY 2019 | 24% | $16.2B |
| FY 2020 | 25% | $17.1B |
| FY 2021 | 28% | $19.8B |
| FY 2022 | 31% | $23.5B |
| FY 2023 | 33% | $27.4B |
| FY 2024 | 35% | $30.8B |

*Source: VA Office of Community Care, FY 2018-2024; VA Budget Submissions.*

### AIR Commission (Failed)

The Asset and Infrastructure Review (AIR) Commission, modeled on the Base Realignment and Closure (BRAC) process, was intended to rationalize VA's facility footprint. Congress rejected the commission's recommendations in 2023, leaving the VA's aging infrastructure unreformed.

### EHR Modernization (2018-Present)

The decision to replace VistA with a commercial EHR (Cerner, now Oracle Health) has become the VA's most troubled modernization effort.

**Key events:**

- **2017**: VA Secretary Shulkin announces decision to adopt the same EHR as DOD (Cerner's MHS GENESIS)
- **2018**: $10 billion contract awarded to Cerner Corporation
- **2020**: First deployment at Mann-Grandstaff VAMC in Spokane, Washington
- **2021-2022**: Serious problems identified: medication errors, scheduling failures, clinician resistance
- **2022**: Cerner acquired by Oracle; contract transferred to Oracle Health
- **2023 (April)**: VA Secretary McDonough pauses deployment pending safety review
- **2024**: Deployment remains paused at most sites; cost projected at $16.1 billion+
- **2025**: New administration reviewing whether to continue, modify, or cancel the program

### PACT Act (2022)

The PACT Act was the largest expansion of VA benefits since the original GI Bill, primarily affecting healthcare eligibility and disability compensation for toxic exposure conditions. While primarily a benefits expansion, it has significant reform implications:

- Brought millions of additional veterans into the VA healthcare system
- Strained claims processing capacity (see [Claims Backlog](../claims-backlog/01-overview.md))
- Created the Toxic Exposure Fund ($280.5 billion over 10 years)
- Expanded VA healthcare eligibility regardless of disability rating for certain toxic-exposed veterans

## Lessons from Reform History

### What Has Worked

1. **Strong, empowered leadership**: The Kizer era (1994-1999) demonstrated that transformational reform is possible with the right leader given sufficient authority and time
2. **Data-driven performance measurement**: Quality metrics and transparent reporting drove genuine improvement in the late 1990s and early 2000s
3. **Clinician-led technology development**: VistA succeeded because it was built by its users, not imposed by contractors
4. **Decentralized management with accountability**: VISN structure improved responsiveness when coupled with performance expectations

### What Has Failed

1. **Crisis-driven reform**: Major reforms (Choice Act, Accountability Act) passed in crisis conditions often create new problems
2. **Contractor-led IT modernization**: The EHR replacement has cost billions more than planned with less functionality than the system it replaces
3. **Accountability without resources**: Firing employees does not solve understaffing; accountability measures without workforce investment are counterproductive
4. **Commission-based reform**: The AIR Commission (2023) and previous facility review attempts have been blocked by local political interests

### Recurring Patterns

- Every VA Secretary promises transformation; average tenure is too short to deliver it
- Technology modernization projects consistently overrun budgets and timelines
- Community care expansion grows because it is easier than fixing VA direct care
- Congress is more willing to add new programs than to fund adequate staffing for existing ones
- Veterans' trust in the VA rises and falls with media coverage of scandals, independent of actual performance changes
