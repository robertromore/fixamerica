# VA Reform: Stakeholders

## Overview

VA reform involves a complex ecosystem of stakeholders with often competing visions for the agency's future. The fundamental disagreement--whether to strengthen the VA as a direct healthcare and benefits provider or to shift toward private-sector delivery--creates alliances and conflicts that cut across traditional political lines.

---

## Primary Stakeholders

### Veterans (16.5 Million)

**Who They Are**: The veteran population is diverse, spanning from World War II-era to post-9/11, across all demographics and geographic regions.

**Interests**:

- Timely access to quality healthcare and benefits
- Choice in where and how they receive care
- Seamless coordination between VA and community providers
- Transparent and accountable VA leadership
- Preservation of veteran-specific clinical expertise (PTSD, TBI, military sexual trauma)

**Key Divisions Among Veterans**:

| Segment | Size | VA Preference |
|---------|------|--------------|
| Enrolled in VA healthcare | 9.8 million | Generally prefer VA direct care |
| Using community care | 4.0 million | Value choice; may or may not prefer VA |
| Not enrolled in VA | ~6.7 million | May be unaware of benefits or prefer private insurance |
| Rural veterans | ~4.7 million | Need community care due to distance; value VA when accessible |
| Post-9/11 veterans | ~4.1 million | More open to technology and community care options |
| Vietnam-era veterans | ~5.8 million | Strong attachment to VA; most likely to prefer VA direct care |

*Source: Census Bureau, 2024; VA enrollment data, FY 2024; VA Survey of Veteran Enrollees, 2024.*

**Power and Influence**: Veterans are a powerful political constituency. However, they are not monolithic--different generations, service eras, and geographic locations produce different preferences about VA reform. The most organized veterans (those in VSOs) tend to favor VA direct care; the least organized may prefer private-sector alternatives.

### Veterans Service Organizations (VSOs)

**Major Organizations and Positions on VA Reform**:

| Organization | Members | Reform Position | Privatization Stance |
|-------------|---------|-----------------|---------------------|
| American Legion | 1.6M | Strengthen VA; cautious reform | Opposed |
| VFW | 1.5M | Strengthen VA; pragmatic reform | Opposed |
| DAV | 1.1M | Strengthen VA; protect veteran rights | Strongly opposed |
| AMVETS | 250K | Moderate reform; open to innovation | Cautious |
| IAVA | N/A (advocacy) | Technology, transparency, accountability | Neutral on delivery model |
| CVA (Concerned Veterans) | N/A (advocacy) | Systemic overhaul; expand choice | Pro-privatization |

*Source: Organization annual reports and policy positions, 2024.*

**Key Dynamics**: The traditional "Big Six" VSOs (American Legion, VFW, DAV, AMVETS, VVA, PVA) generally oppose privatization and support VA direct care investment. The Concerned Veterans for America (CVA), funded by Koch-affiliated organizations, represents a different constituency that favors market-based reform. This split creates a tension within "veteran advocacy" that complicates the reform debate.

### VA Employees (412,000)

**Composition**:

- Approximately 370,000 in VHA (clinical and support staff)
- Approximately 28,600 in VBA (benefits processing)
- Approximately 2,400 in NCA (memorial affairs)
- Approximately 11,000 in staff offices

**Interests**:

- Job security and adequate staffing levels
- Competitive compensation and benefits
- Manageable workloads and reduced burnout
- Adequate technology and tools
- Professional respect and organizational pride

**Union Representation**:

| Union | Coverage | Key Concerns |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| AFGE | Largest federal employee union at VA; most VBA and support staff | Oppose privatization; protect jobs; negotiate working conditions |
| National Nurses United | VA registered nurses | Staffing ratios; patient safety; oppose privatization |
| National Federation of Federal Employees | Various VA employees | Compensation; working conditions |

**Power**: VA employees collectively represent a significant political force, particularly through AFGE's lobbying and campaign contributions. Employees' day-to-day decisions directly affect VA service quality.

## Secondary Stakeholders

### Congress

**Key Committees**:

| Committee | Role | Key Dynamic |
|-----------|------|------------|
| House Veterans' Affairs Committee | Authorization, oversight | Jurisdiction over VA programs |
| Senate Veterans' Affairs Committee | Authorization, oversight, confirmation | Confirms Secretary and senior leaders |
| House/Senate Appropriations subcommittees | Funding | Controls discretionary spending levels |

**Interests**: Demonstrating responsiveness to veterans; managing budget impact; oversight visibility; local facility protection.

**Key Dynamic**: The disconnect between authorization committees (which create programs) and appropriations committees (which fund them) is a structural cause of VA dysfunction.

### VA Leadership

**Who They Are**: The Secretary of Veterans Affairs, Under Secretaries for Health and Benefits, and the senior political and career leadership team.

**Interests**:

- Implementing the President's agenda for VA
- Meeting performance targets
- Managing public perception during crises
- Balancing competing stakeholder demands
- Building institutional legacy in limited tenure

**Constraints**: Short tenures, Congressional micromanagement, union bargaining requirements, complex procurement rules, and the sheer scale of the organization.

### Private Healthcare Industry

**Who They Are**: Community care contractors, hospitals, physician practices, and third-party administrators that provide care to veterans under VA community care programs.

**Interests**:

- Expanded community care referrals and spending (currently $30.8 billion annually)
- Timely VA payment for services rendered
- Reduced administrative burden of VA billing requirements
- Favorable access standards that drive more veterans to private care

**Key Players**:

| Entity | Role | Revenue from VA |
|--------|------|----------------|
| TriWest Healthcare Alliance | Community care administrator (West) | Contract-based |
| Optum (UnitedHealth Group) | Community care administrator (East/Central) | Contract-based |
| Oracle Health (formerly Cerner) | EHR modernization contractor | $16.1B contract |
| QTC Management (Leidos) | Compensation exam contractor | Contract-based |

**Power**: The healthcare industry's lobbying power and financial resources make it a significant force in VA reform debates. Companies benefiting from community care expansion have financial incentives to support further privatization.

### Think Tanks and Advocacy Organizations

| Organization | Affiliation | VA Reform Position |
|-------------|------------|-------------------|
| Concerned Veterans for America | Koch network | Pro-privatization; VA as insurer not provider |
| Veterans Healthcare Policy Institute | Independent | Pro-VA direct care; evidence-based reform |
| RAND Corporation | Nonpartisan | Evidence-based; balanced analysis |
| Heritage Foundation | Conservative | Pro-privatization; efficiency reform |
| Center for American Progress | Liberal | Pro-VA investment; oppose privatization |
| Bipartisan Policy Center | Centrist | Balanced reform; both investment and accountability |

## Institutional Stakeholders

### Department of Defense

**Role**: Source of the veteran population; operates separate health and records systems; transition partner.

**Interests**: Smooth military-to-civilian transition; interoperable health records; recruitment tool (good VA benefits attract recruits).

**Key Dynamic**: DOD-VA coordination on health records, transition programs, and data sharing is essential but historically poor. The EHR modernization was supposed to create interoperability but has not delivered.

### Office of Management and Budget (OMB)

**Role**: Reviews VA budget requests and enforces administration fiscal priorities.

**Interests**: Budget discipline; alignment with presidential priorities.

**Power**: OMB can significantly constrain VA budget requests before they reach Congress, affecting staffing, IT, and infrastructure investment.

### Government Accountability Office (GAO)

**Role**: Congressional watchdog; maintains VA disability claims on High-Risk List; audits VA programs.

**Interests**: Improving government performance; informing Congress.

**Power**: GAO reports carry significant weight and drive Congressional oversight attention.

### VA Office of Inspector General (OIG)

**Role**: Independent oversight of VA operations.

**Interests**: Identifying waste, fraud, and abuse; patient safety; program integrity.

**Power**: OIG reports on EHR modernization, claims processing, and patient safety have been particularly influential in driving reform debates.

## Stakeholder Dynamics

### The Privatization Coalition

| Member | Motivation | Contribution |
|--------|-----------|--------------|
| Concerned Veterans for America | Ideological (market solutions) | Advocacy, media, policy papers |
| Heritage Foundation/AEI | Ideological (limited government) | Research, Congressional briefings |
| Private healthcare industry | Financial (expanded VA spending) | Lobbying, campaign contributions |
| Some conservative members of Congress | Ideological and constituency | Legislation, oversight |

### The VA Investment Coalition

| Member | Motivation | Contribution |
|--------|-----------|--------------|
| Traditional VSOs (Legion, VFW, DAV) | Organizational (VA direct care access) | Lobbying, grassroots, testimony |
| AFGE and NNU | Employment (VA jobs) | Lobbying, campaign contributions, mobilization |
| Veterans Healthcare Policy Institute | Evidence-based (VA quality) | Research, media |
| Most Democratic members of Congress | Ideological (public services) | Legislation, appropriations |

### Tension Points

| Tension | Parties | Nature |
|---------|---------|--------|
| Community care growth vs. VA direct care | Privatization coalition vs. investment coalition | Zero-sum competition for budget resources |
| Employee accountability vs. worker rights | VA leadership vs. AFGE/NNU | Management authority vs. civil service protection |
| EHR modernization vs. VistA | Oracle Health vs. VA clinicians/VistA advocates | Technology choice with billion-dollar stakes |
| Facility closure/realignment vs. local politics | OMB/reformers vs. local Congressional delegations | Every VA facility has a Congressional defender |
| Centralization vs. local control | National VA leadership vs. VISN directors | Consistency vs. responsiveness |
