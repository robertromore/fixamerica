# VA Reform: Root Causes

## Overview

The VA's persistent dysfunction is not the result of one failure but the interaction of structural, organizational, political, and cultural factors. Understanding these root causes explains why decades of reform efforts have produced limited sustained improvement.

---

## Structural Root Causes

### 1. Political Leadership Instability

**The Problem**: The VA Secretary, as a political appointee, changes with each presidential administration and sometimes within administrations. Average tenure since 2009 is approximately 2.5 years.

**How It Works**:

- Each new Secretary brings different priorities and reform strategies
- Senior leadership teams are replaced, disrupting institutional continuity
- Multi-year reform plans are abandoned or deprioritized by successors
- Career civil servants learn to wait out political appointees rather than fully commit to their initiatives
- Confirmation processes leave the agency without permanent leadership for months

**Evidence**:

- Eight VA Secretaries since 2009, with an average tenure of 2.5 years
- The EHR modernization project has been managed by four different Secretaries, each with different levels of commitment to the program
- Career VA officials have described a pattern of "waiting out" political leadership changes (NAPA, "VA Governance Assessment," 2023)

### 2. Scale and Complexity

**The Problem**: The VA is asked to perform an extraordinarily diverse set of functions--hospital system, insurance company, benefits administrator, cemetery operator, housing lender, education funder--at a scale rivaling entire countries' health systems.

**How It Works**:

- VHA operates 171 medical centers, more than most private hospital chains combined
- VBA processes millions of claims using a rating system with 800+ diagnostic codes
- NCA maintains 155 national cemeteries across 50 states
- VA also administers education benefits (GI Bill), home loans, life insurance, and vocational rehabilitation
- Managing this diversity requires expertise that few organizations possess

**Evidence**:

- The VA's 412,000 employees make it the second-largest federal agency (after DOD)
- VHA is the largest integrated healthcare system in the United States
- The breadth of VA functions exceeds that of any single private-sector organization

### 3. Infrastructure Obsolescence

**The Problem**: The VA's physical infrastructure was largely built in the mid-20th century and does not match current veteran population distribution or modern healthcare delivery needs.

**How It Works**:

- Many VA medical centers were built in the 1940s-1960s in locations chosen for post-World War II veteran populations
- Veteran populations have migrated to the South and West; VA facilities remain concentrated in the Northeast and Midwest
- Aging facilities require billions in maintenance and renovation
- The federal construction process is slow and expensive, averaging 7-10 years from authorization to completion
- The AIR Commission (2022-2023), designed to address this problem, was rejected by Congress due to local political resistance

**Evidence**:

- The VA's estimated construction backlog exceeds $52 billion (VA Office of Construction and Facilities Management, 2024)
- Average age of VA medical center buildings is 60+ years
- The fastest-growing veteran populations (post-9/11) are concentrated in Sun Belt states with the fewest VA facilities per capita (Census Bureau, 2024)

### 4. Misaligned Funding Model

**The Problem**: The VA's budget is split between mandatory spending (benefits, which grow automatically) and discretionary spending (healthcare, staff, IT, which must be annually appropriated), creating structural imbalances.

**How It Works**:

- Mandatory spending on disability compensation, pensions, and education benefits grows automatically with eligible population and cost-of-living adjustments
- Discretionary spending on healthcare, claims processing, IT, and infrastructure requires annual Congressional appropriation
- Continuing resolutions delay appropriations for months, preventing the VA from executing hiring and procurement plans
- The growing mandatory spending share of the budget creates political pressure to constrain discretionary spending
- PACT Act's Toxic Exposure Fund created new mandatory spending, but most operational spending remains discretionary

**Evidence**:

- VA discretionary spending has been under continuing resolutions for 4+ months in 5 of the last 8 fiscal years (CRS, 2024)
- The VA Advance Appropriations Act (2009) provided advance appropriations for medical care, but not for claims processing, IT, or infrastructure

## Organizational Root Causes

### 5. Siloed Administration Structure

**The Problem**: VHA, VBA, and NCA operate as largely independent organizations with limited coordination, even though veterans interact with all three.

**How It Works**:

- A veteran receiving healthcare through VHA, disability compensation through VBA, and education benefits through VBA's Education Service interacts with three separate bureaucracies
- Clinical data from VHA is not seamlessly shared with VBA for claims processing
- IT systems are developed independently by each administration
- Management incentives do not reward cross-administration coordination
- Veterans must navigate separate portals, phone numbers, and processes for each service

**Evidence**:

- The VA OIG has repeatedly documented care coordination failures between VHA and VBA (multiple reports, 2020-2024)
- The inability to share clinical data between VHA and VBA contributes to the claims backlog (see [Claims Backlog: Root Causes](../claims-backlog/04-root-causes.md))

### 6. Resistance to Change in Organizational Culture

**The Problem**: The VA's organizational culture, particularly within VHA, is resistant to externally imposed change and skeptical of reform initiatives.

**How It Works**:

- Career employees have experienced multiple reform cycles that produced temporary disruption followed by reversion to the status quo
- The VA's bureaucratic culture rewards compliance and risk avoidance over innovation
- Academic medical affiliations create a physician-dominated culture that can resist administrative reforms
- Union relationships (AFGE, National Nurses United) can slow organizational changes through bargaining requirements
- The "not invented here" syndrome leads to resistance to outside solutions and best practices

**Evidence**:

- The EHR modernization's most significant resistance has come from VA clinicians who prefer the familiar VistA system (VA OIG EHR reports, 2022-2024)
- The VA All Employee Survey (2024) found that only 41% of employees believe the VA "effectively manages change"
- GAO has noted the VA's slow implementation of recommendations as a persistent concern (GAO High-Risk Progress Updates, 2019-2023)

### 7. Workforce Competitiveness Gaps

**The Problem**: The VA cannot consistently attract and retain the clinical and technical talent it needs because its compensation, hiring processes, and work environment do not compete with the private sector.

**How It Works**:

- Federal pay scales constrain VA compensation, particularly for physicians, IT professionals, and specialized technical roles
- Federal hiring processes average 80-100 days, compared to 30-45 days in the private sector (OPM, 2024)
- Rural VA facilities face extreme recruitment challenges for specialized clinical positions
- Burnout from understaffing creates a vicious cycle: departures increase workload on remaining staff, accelerating further departures
- VA-specific hiring authorities (Title 38) help for clinical positions but do not fully close the gap

**Evidence**:

- VA physician salaries average 20-30% below private-sector equivalents (VA Office of Academic Affiliations, 2024)
- VA nursing vacancy rate (10.2%) is higher than the national nursing vacancy rate (~8%) (BLS, 2024)
- The VA's overall vacancy rate (11.2%) represents approximately 52,000 unfilled positions

## Political Root Causes

### 8. The Privatization-Investment Deadlock

**The Problem**: VA reform is stuck between two competing paradigms--fix the VA from within through investment, or shift to private-sector delivery--and the political system cannot choose.

**How It Works**:

- Conservatives and libertarians push for expanded community care and privatization
- Liberals and progressives push for increased investment in VA direct care
- Each side blocks the other's preferred solution
- The result is an incoherent middle ground: VA direct care is underfunded while community care grows without clear limits
- The community care spending trajectory threatens to create a "death spiral" for VA direct care--as more resources shift to community care, VA quality declines, driving more veterans to community care

**Evidence**:

- Community care spending grew from 22% of VHA spending (FY 2018) to 35% (FY 2024) without a deliberate strategic decision to shift the balance (VA Office of Community Care, FY 2018-2024)
- The Concerned Veterans for America (Koch-affiliated) has explicitly advocated for transitioning the VA from healthcare provider to healthcare insurer; major VSOs oppose this vision

### 9. Congressional Oversight Dysfunction

**The Problem**: Congressional oversight of the VA is fragmented, episodic, and driven by crisis rather than sustained management attention.

**How It Works**:

- The House and Senate Veterans' Affairs Committees have jurisdiction over VA programs but not appropriations
- Veterans' Affairs Committees are not considered "A-list" committee assignments, attracting less experienced members
- Oversight hearings spike during crises and decline between them
- Members focus on local VA facilities rather than systemic reform
- The disconnect between authorization and appropriations committees means programs are created without guaranteed funding

**Evidence**:

- Congressional hearings on VA issues spiked in 2014 (wait-time scandal) and declined significantly by 2016-2017, only to spike again with PACT Act debates in 2022 (Congressional Record)
- Multiple former VA Secretaries have testified that Congressional requests for individual casework consume significant senior leadership time (NAPA, 2023)

### 10. Interest Group Cross-Pressures

**The Problem**: The major interest groups involved in VA policy--VSOs, employee unions, privatization advocates, and the healthcare industry--have competing visions that create policy paralysis.

**How It Works**:

- VSOs (American Legion, VFW, DAV) generally support VA direct care and oppose privatization
- AFGE and National Nurses United oppose privatization and resist management reforms they view as anti-worker
- Concerned Veterans for America and aligned organizations push for privatization
- The healthcare industry (including community care contractors) benefits from expanded VA spending on private-sector care
- These cross-pressures prevent any single reform vision from gaining sufficient support

**Evidence**:

- The MISSION Act (2018) was a negotiated compromise between VSO preferences (maintain VA care) and privatization advocates (expand community access), satisfying neither fully
- Lobbying spending on VA issues exceeded $35 million in FY 2023 (OpenSecrets.org, 2024)

## Root Cause Interactions

| Root Cause A | Root Cause B | Interaction |
|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Leadership instability | Organizational culture resistance | Short tenures mean leaders cannot overcome institutional inertia |
| Infrastructure obsolescence | Funding model | Construction backlog grows because discretionary funding is inadequate |
| Workforce gaps | Privatization debate | Unresolved privatization debate prevents commitment to VA workforce investment |
| Siloed structure | IT modernization | Each administration develops IT independently; integration fails |
| Congressional dysfunction | Interest group cross-pressures | Congress cannot choose between competing visions; produces incoherent compromises |

## Root Cause Priority

| Root Cause | Severity | Addressability | Priority |
|-----------|----------|----------------|----------|
| Leadership instability | Critical | Moderate (governance reform possible) | Highest |
| Privatization-investment deadlock | Critical | Low (requires political resolution) | Highest |
| Workforce competitiveness | High | Moderate (requires funding and authority) | High |
| Infrastructure obsolescence | High | Low (massive cost; political resistance to closures) | Medium-high |
| Funding model misalignment | High | Moderate (advance appropriations expansion possible) | High |
| Siloed structure | Moderate | High (organizational design within VA authority) | Medium |
| Cultural resistance | High | Low-moderate (cultural change takes years) | Medium |
| Congressional oversight dysfunction | Moderate | Low (structural Congressional incentives) | Lower |
| Interest group cross-pressures | Moderate | Low (reflects genuine value disagreements) | Lower |
| Scale and complexity | Moderate | Low (inherent to mission) | Lower |
