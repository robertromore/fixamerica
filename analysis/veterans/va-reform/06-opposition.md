# VA Reform: Opposition to Reform

## Overview

Opposition to VA reform is unusual because virtually everyone claims to support "reform"--the disagreements are over what reform means. The opposition landscape is best understood not as for-or-against reform but as competing visions that block each other. This document examines the sources of resistance to the specific reforms proposed in this analysis, which center on strengthening VA direct care capacity while maintaining appropriate community care access.

---

## Opposition to Strengthening VA Direct Care

### 1. Privatization Advocates

**Who**: Concerned Veterans for America (CVA), Heritage Foundation, American Enterprise Institute, some Republican members of Congress, private healthcare industry.

**Position**: The VA should transition from a direct healthcare provider to a healthcare insurer/coordinator. Veterans should receive vouchers or insurance cards to access private-sector care, with the VA serving primarily as a benefits administrator.

**Arguments**:

| Argument | Assessment |
|----------|-----------|
| "Veterans deserve the same choice as everyone else" | Appealing but ignores VA's specialized expertise in veteran-specific conditions |
| "Private sector is more efficient than government" | Mixed evidence; VA quality often exceeds private sector on comparable metrics |
| "VA is too large to manage effectively" | Scale is a challenge but other countries operate large public health systems effectively |
| "Competition would improve VA quality" | VA already competes through MISSION Act community care; full privatization would eliminate VA quality benchmarks |

**Power and Resources**:

- CVA is funded by Koch-affiliated organizations with significant financial resources
- Private healthcare industry lobbying on VA issues exceeded $15 million in FY 2023 (OpenSecrets.org, 2024)
- Strong media presence and policy research capacity
- Aligned with broader conservative policy infrastructure

**Counter-Evidence**:

- VA outperforms the private sector on most clinical quality metrics (RAND Corporation, "Assessment of VA Healthcare Quality," 2023)
- Countries with similar public veteran health systems (UK, Canada, Australia) do not consider full privatization
- Community care costs per encounter are generally higher than VA direct care (VA Office of Community Care, FY 2024)
- Private providers often lack expertise in military-specific conditions (TBI, PTSD, military sexual trauma)

### 2. Fiscal Critics

**Who**: Budget hawks in both parties, OMB, Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget.

**Position**: VA spending growth (20% in three years) is unsustainable. Reform should focus on efficiency rather than additional investment.

**Arguments**:

| Argument | Assessment |
|----------|-----------|
| "VA spending is growing faster than inflation" | True, but driven by PACT Act and rising healthcare costs, not waste |
| "Throwing money at the problem hasn't worked" | Partially true; investment must be accompanied by management reform |
| "VA should be more efficient before getting more resources" | Valid in principle but used to justify permanent underfunding |

**Impact**: Fiscal critics constrain appropriations for VA workforce expansion, IT modernization, and infrastructure--the very investments needed to reduce reliance on community care.

### 3. Some VA Employees and Unions (on Management Reform)

**Who**: AFGE, National Nurses United, career civil servants.

**Position**: Support investment in VA but resist management reforms that could increase accountability, change work rules, or allow more flexible employment practices.

**Arguments**:

| Argument | Assessment |
|----------|-----------|
| "Accountability Act is used to fire whistleblowers" | Partially valid; implementation has been problematic |
| "Production quotas sacrifice quality" | Valid concern in many cases |
| "Telework improves recruitment and retention" | Evidence supports this for many positions |
| "Union bargaining protections are essential" | True for worker rights but can slow necessary organizational change |

**Important Context**: Union opposition to management reforms is often legitimate. Many proposed "accountability" measures have been used punitively rather than constructively, and workforce concerns about unreasonable expectations are frequently justified by the evidence.

## Opposition to Specific Reforms

### Against EHR Modernization (as Currently Implemented)

| Stakeholder | Position | Underlying Concern |
|------------|----------|-------------------|
| VA clinicians | "Oracle Health is inferior to VistA" | Loss of familiar, functional system; legitimate usability concerns |
| VistA advocates | "Upgrade VistA instead of replacing it" | Sunk cost in VistA expertise; valid technical arguments |
| Fiscal critics | "The project is a billion-dollar boondoggle" | Valid concern about cost overruns |
| Patient safety advocates | "The new system is causing patient harm" | Documented patient safety incidents support this concern |

### Against Community Care Limits

| Stakeholder | Position | Underlying Concern |
|------------|----------|-------------------|
| Privatization advocates | "Tighter access standards restrict veteran choice" | Ideological preference for private-sector care |
| Rural veteran advocates | "Community care is the only practical option for rural veterans" | Legitimate access concern; VA facilities are too distant |
| Private healthcare industry | "Restricting referrals reduces revenue" | Financial interest in maintaining community care volume |
| Some veterans | "I prefer my private doctor" | Personal preference and established care relationships |

### Against Governance Reform (Longer Secretary Terms, Bipartisan Commission)

| Stakeholder | Position | Underlying Concern |
|------------|----------|-------------------|
| White House (any administration) | "The President should choose who leads the VA" | Executive authority and political control |
| Senate (confirmation role) | "We should maintain confirmation power" | Congressional oversight authority |
| VSOs | "The Secretary should be accountable to veterans through the political process" | Democratic accountability concern |

### Against Workforce Reform (Competitive Pay, Flexible Hiring)

| Stakeholder | Position | Underlying Concern |
|------------|----------|-------------------|
| Fiscal conservatives | "Government workers should not make market-rate salaries" | Opposition to government spending growth |
| OPM | "VA exceptions undermine government-wide pay system" | Bureaucratic consistency concern |
| AFGE | "Flexible hiring authorities bypass union protections" | Worker protection concern |

## Why the Status Quo Persists

### The Structural Deadlock

The VA reform debate is stuck in a structural deadlock with four dimensions:

1. **Investment vs. privatization**: Neither side has enough political power to fully implement its vision, so both proceed partially, creating an incoherent hybrid
2. **Authorization vs. appropriations**: Congress authorizes new programs and eligibility without funding the capacity to implement them
3. **Short-term crisis vs. long-term reform**: Political attention spikes during crises and fades between them, preventing sustained reform implementation
4. **Centralization vs. local control**: National reform plans are blocked by local Congressional delegations protecting specific facilities

### Political Benefits of the Status Quo

| Actor | How They Benefit from Status Quo |
|-------|--------------------------------|
| Privatization advocates | Community care grows incrementally without a decisive fight |
| VA investment advocates | VA direct care continues without being formally replaced |
| Congress | Can blame the VA for problems created by Congressional underfunding |
| Private healthcare industry | Growing revenue from community care without full privatization fight |
| VA bureaucracy | Institutional survival is not threatened |

### The Cost of Deadlock

The deadlock's cost falls on veterans:

- Increasing wait times as VA direct care is slowly defunded through community care diversion
- Fragmented care between VA and community providers
- Billions spent on a failed EHR modernization that may never deliver its promised benefits
- Persistent workforce vacancies driving burnout and departures
- Aging infrastructure that degrades veteran care experience

## Strategies for Overcoming Opposition

| Opposition Source | Strategy | Key Argument |
|------------------|----------|-------------|
| Privatization advocates | Demonstrate VA quality superiority with data; frame as "and" not "or" | VA direct care is cost-effective AND higher quality for veteran-specific conditions |
| Fiscal critics | Show long-term cost savings from VA investment | Community care costs more per encounter; prevention reduces downstream spending |
| Union resistance to management reform | Involve unions in reform design; separate legitimate concerns from institutional resistance | Worker input improves reform design; meaningful engagement reduces obstruction |
| EHR opponents | Acknowledge current failure; propose reset with clinician-centered approach | Clinician buy-in is essential; technology must serve users |
| Congressional dynamics | Link authorization to appropriation; engage appropriators early | End the practice of creating unfunded mandates |
