# Veteran Disability Compensation: Overview

## Executive Summary

The VA disability compensation system is the primary mechanism through which the United States fulfills its obligation to veterans whose health was damaged by military service. In FY 2024, the Department of Veterans Affairs paid approximately $134 billion in disability compensation to over 6.2 million veterans and survivors--making it the single largest expenditure in the VA budget and one of the largest entitlement programs in the federal government (VA FY 2024 Budget Submission; VA Annual Benefits Report, 2024). Despite this enormous investment, the system is plagued by delays, inconsistency, complexity, and a claims backlog that has persisted for decades.

The disability compensation system assigns veterans a disability rating from 0% to 100% in 10% increments, with monthly payments ranging from approximately $171 (10% rating) to $3,737 (100% rating) as of 2024. The rating is determined by evaluating service-connected medical conditions against the VA Schedule for Rating Disabilities (VASRD), a framework that has not been fundamentally updated since 1945 and that critics argue is poorly suited to the conditions of modern warfare--including traumatic brain injury, post-traumatic stress disorder, toxic exposures, and military sexual trauma.

The current system forces veterans into an adversarial relationship with the VA, where they must prove the severity of their conditions against bureaucratic resistance. Average claims processing time exceeded 150 days in FY 2024, with appeals taking 3-7 years. The PACT Act (2022) expanded eligibility for toxic-exposure-related conditions but also dramatically increased the claims workload, creating new backlogs even as old ones were partially resolved. Fundamental reform requires modernizing the rating schedule, simplifying the claims process, addressing the appeals crisis, and reconsidering whether the current system's structure--designed for World War I-era physical injuries--is appropriate for 21st-century veterans.

## Core Tensions

- **Speed vs. accuracy**: Processing claims faster risks more errors; ensuring accuracy takes time
- **Generosity vs. fiscal sustainability**: Expanding eligibility and raising compensation levels increases costs in a system already exceeding $134 billion annually
- **Individual assessment vs. presumptive conditions**: Individual evaluation is thorough but slow; presumptive conditions are faster but may over- or under-compensate
- **Adversarial vs. collaborative**: The current system requires veterans to "prove" their disabilities; a more collaborative model would accept veteran self-report more readily but risks fraud
- **Legacy rating schedule vs. modern conditions**: The VASRD was designed for physical combat injuries; modern conditions (PTSD, TBI, MST, toxic exposures) fit poorly into the framework

## Key Questions

1. **Why has the claims backlog persisted for decades despite repeated reform efforts?** What structural factors perpetuate delays?
2. **Is the VA Schedule for Rating Disabilities fit for purpose?** How should conditions like PTSD, TBI, and toxic exposures be evaluated?
3. **What does the appeals crisis reveal about the underlying system?** Why do so many claims require appeal, and what can be done?
4. **How has the PACT Act changed the landscape?** Is the VA equipped to handle the surge in toxic exposure claims?
5. **Are there alternative models?** How do other countries compensate military disability, and what can the U.S. learn?

## Vision of Success

A successful disability compensation system would: process initial claims within 30 days using simplified criteria and presumptive conditions for common service-connected disabilities; use a modernized rating schedule that accurately reflects the functional impact of 21st-century conditions including PTSD, TBI, and toxic exposures; resolve appeals within 12 months; provide emergency bridge payments to veterans awaiting decisions; maintain a collaborative (rather than adversarial) relationship with veterans; be transparent about its processes, timelines, and error rates; and ensure that no veteran's financial security or mental health is endangered by bureaucratic delay.
