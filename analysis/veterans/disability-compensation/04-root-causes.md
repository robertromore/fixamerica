# Veteran Disability Compensation: Root Causes

## Overview

The chronic dysfunction of the VA disability compensation system is not the result of any single failure but of deeply embedded structural features that make delays, inconsistency, and veteran frustration inevitable. The system was designed in the 1930s-1940s for a different era of warfare, a different veteran population, and a different understanding of disability. Its fundamental architecture--an adversarial, individual-assessment model with a World War II-era rating schedule--has been patched and expanded without fundamental redesign. Understanding the root causes requires examining the system's design, the incentive structures within VBA, the political economy that sustains dysfunction, and the cultural assumptions that shape how the system treats veterans.

## Systemic Root Causes

### 1. The Outdated Rating Schedule (VASRD)

The VA Schedule for Rating Disabilities has not been fundamentally restructured since 1945. While individual body systems have been updated piecemeal since 2017, the framework itself remains rooted in a World War II-era understanding of disability.

**Evidence**:

- The VASRD measures "average impairment of earning capacity"--not functional impairment, quality of life, or actual disability severity (38 CFR 4.1)
- Mental health conditions (PTSD, depression, anxiety) are rated on a single General Rating Formula that uses vague criteria like "occupational and social impairment with reduced reliability and productivity" (38 CFR 4.130)
- The combined rating formula (38 CFR 4.25) uses a bilateral factor that is mathematically counterintuitive and penalizes veterans with multiple moderate disabilities
- Musculoskeletal conditions--the most commonly rated body system--are still evaluated under criteria that have not been updated since the 1990s
- TBI rating criteria were only added in 2008 and remain poorly suited to the condition's complexity

**Causal mechanism**: The outdated rating schedule forces claims processors to fit modern conditions into archaic categories, increasing processing time, error rates, and appeal rates. When the criteria do not match the condition, subjective judgment fills the gap--leading to inconsistency across raters and regions.

### 2. The Adversarial Burden of Proof

The disability compensation system requires veterans to prove their conditions are "at least as likely as not" connected to military service. While the benefit-of-the-doubt rule (38 U.S.C. 5107(b)) is supposed to tip close cases in the veteran's favor, in practice the system operates adversarially.

**Evidence**:

- 28% of veterans describe the claims process as adversarial (VA Veteran Experience Office, 2024)
- The CAVC reversal/remand rate of approximately 75% suggests that initial decisions and BVA reviews systematically underweight veteran evidence (CAVC Annual Report, FY 2024)
- Veterans must obtain medical evidence, often at their own expense, to support claims--even when the VA has access to military medical records
- The C&P (Compensation and Pension) examination process is frequently adversarial in tone, with examiners perceived as seeking to minimize disability rather than accurately evaluate it (IAVA Member Survey, 2024)

**Causal mechanism**: The adversarial structure exists because the VA faces conflicting mandates: serve veterans generously but protect the public fisc. In practice, the fiscal protection mandate creates institutional incentives to deny or minimize claims, which then drives appeals, rework, and delay.

### 3. Structural Complexity of the Claims Process

The claims process involves multiple stages, each with its own requirements, timelines, and potential failure points.

**Evidence**:

- A typical initial claim requires: application (VA Form 21-526EZ), military service records (DD-214), medical evidence, lay statements, C&P examination, rating decision, and notification
- Each step introduces delay: records requests (30-60 days), C&P exam scheduling (30-60 days), exam to decision (30-60 days), and decision drafting (7-14 days)
- Complex claims involving multiple conditions, secondary service connection, or toxic exposure add additional steps and evidence requirements
- 54% of veterans find the paperwork confusing (VA Veteran Experience Office, 2024)

**Causal mechanism**: The process was designed for an era of paper records and single-condition claims. Modern claims frequently involve 5-10+ conditions, electronic and paper records from multiple sources, and medical issues that require specialized evaluation. Each layer of complexity multiplies delay.

### 4. C&P Examination Bottleneck

Compensation and Pension examinations--the medical evaluations that determine disability ratings--have become a critical bottleneck.

**Evidence**:

- The VA contracts with private companies (VES, QTC, LHI) to conduct the majority of C&P exams due to insufficient internal capacity
- Contract examiner quality varies significantly; some examiners have been found to be unqualified for the conditions they evaluate (VA OIG, "Contracted Disability Exam Quality," 2023)
- Average time from exam request to completed exam: 45-60 days (VA Performance Data, 2024)
- Scheduling backlogs in rural and high-volume areas add additional delay
- Veterans frequently report that exams feel cursory, lasting 15-30 minutes for complex conditions

**Causal mechanism**: The VA outsourced C&P exams to address internal capacity constraints but created a new problem: quality control over thousands of contract examiners across the country. The examination, which is supposed to be the most important input into the rating decision, has become a perfunctory bottleneck rather than a thorough evaluation.

### 5. Workforce Limitations and Training

Claims processors (Veterans Service Representatives and Rating Veterans Service Representatives) face enormous caseloads and complex work with limited training and institutional support.

**Evidence**:

- Average caseload per claims processor: 150-200 active claims (VBA Workforce Data, 2024)
- Training for new claims processors: 6-12 months before independent production; full competency takes 2-3 years
- Annual turnover rate for claims processors: approximately 15-18% (VBA HR Data, 2024)
- Overtime spending: $280 million in FY 2024, indicating chronic understaffing
- The PACT Act introduced 20+ new presumptive conditions requiring retraining of the entire workforce

**Causal mechanism**: Claims processing is one of the most complex jobs in the federal government--requiring knowledge of law, medicine, military service, and bureaucratic procedure. High turnover, insufficient training, and overwhelming caseloads produce errors, inconsistency, and delay.

### 6. IT System Deficiencies

The VA's IT infrastructure for claims processing has been the subject of decades of failed modernization efforts.

**Evidence**:

- VBMS (Veterans Benefits Management System), launched in 2013, was intended to be fully electronic but still relies on manual processes for many claim types
- Integration between VBMS, the VA electronic health record, and DOD military service records remains incomplete
- IT modernization spending has exceeded $10 billion over the past decade with mixed results (GAO, "VA IT Modernization," 2024)
- Automated claims processing (for simple, single-issue claims) has been piloted but covers only a small percentage of total claims

**Causal mechanism**: Without modern IT systems, claims processors spend significant time on data entry, records retrieval, and manual verification that could be automated. This reduces throughput and increases error rates.

### 7. The Appeals Spiral

The appeals system creates a self-reinforcing cycle of rework that consumes enormous resources.

**Evidence**:

- Approximately 20-25% of initial rating decisions are appealed (VBA data, FY 2024)
- The CAVC reversal/remand rate of ~75% means most appealed cases are sent back to VBA for re-adjudication
- Remanded cases re-enter the queue and compete with new claims for processing resources
- Some claims cycle through multiple rounds of appeal, remand, and re-adjudication, consuming years and thousands of processor-hours

**Causal mechanism**: High initial error rates (masked by VA's self-measured 95.8% accuracy rate, which uses a narrow definition of "error") generate appeals. Appeals consume processing capacity, reducing resources available for initial claims, which increases backlogs and pressure to process quickly, which increases error rates. The cycle is self-sustaining.

## Political Economy: Who Benefits from the Status Quo

| Actor | Benefit from Current System | Mechanism |
|-------|----------------------------|-----------|
| VA bureaucracy | Institutional control over $134B annual budget | Complexity justifies large workforce and budget |
| C&P exam contractors (VES, QTC, LHI) | Multi-billion-dollar federal contracts | More exams = more revenue; no incentive to reduce exam volume through presumptive conditions |
| Claims attorneys and agents | Represent veterans in complex system; fees | System complexity creates demand for legal representation |
| Congressional appropriators | VA budget as earmark and constituent service vehicle | VA spending is politically popular; reform is risky |
| VSO accredited representatives | Gatekeeping role in claims assistance | System complexity makes veterans dependent on VSO assistance |

## Perverse Incentives

1. **VA measures speed but not accuracy meaningfully**: The VA's self-reported accuracy rate (95.8%) uses a narrow definition that counts only specific error types, while the 75% CAVC reversal rate suggests much higher real error rates
2. **Claims processors are incentivized to close cases, not get them right**: Production quotas encourage fast decisions, not thorough ones
3. **The appeals system has no cost to the VA for incorrect initial decisions**: When a claim is remanded, the same regional office re-adjudicates it with no accountability for the original error
4. **Expanding presumptive conditions is politically easy but operationally difficult**: Politicians can claim credit for expanding eligibility while the VA absorbs the processing burden
5. **IT modernization is rewarded for spending, not outcomes**: Contractors are paid for development, not for demonstrated improvements in processing speed or accuracy

## Why Reform Has Failed

### Treating Symptoms Instead of Causes

Every backlog crisis has been addressed through surge strategies--hiring, overtime, and temporary rule changes--that reduce the immediate inventory without addressing the underlying structural complexity. When the surge ends (because it is unsustainable and expensive), the backlog returns.

### Rating Schedule Untouchable

The VASRD is the structural foundation of the entire system, yet it is the most resistant to reform. Changing the rating schedule affects every veteran's compensation and creates enormous political risk: any change that could lower any veteran's rating is politically toxic, even if the overall system would be improved.

### Institutional Resistance to Simplification

Simplifying the claims process (through expanded presumptive conditions, simplified rating criteria, or reduced evidentiary burdens) threatens the institutional power and employment of tens of thousands of claims processors, medical examiners, and supervisors whose jobs depend on the system's complexity.

### Political Incentive to Expand Without Reforming

Elected officials earn political credit by expanding eligibility (as with the PACT Act) but bear no cost from the implementation challenges. The result is a system that takes on ever-more obligations without the structural capacity to fulfill them.

### The Fraud Bogeyman

Opponents of simplification routinely invoke the specter of fraud, despite the VA's own data showing that confirmed fraud represents less than 1% of disability compensation payments (VA OIG, Annual Report, 2024). Fear of fraud perpetuates an adversarial system that punishes the 99% of honest veterans to guard against the 1%.
