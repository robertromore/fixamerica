# Veteran Disability Compensation: Stakeholders

## Overview

The VA disability compensation system involves a dense network of stakeholders with often conflicting interests. Veterans and their families need timely, accurate, and fair compensation. The VA bureaucracy needs to process claims efficiently while controlling costs. Congress needs to demonstrate support for veterans while managing a $134 billion annual program. Contractors need to maintain profitable exam and IT contracts. Attorneys and VSO representatives need the system to be complex enough to require their services. Understanding these interests--especially the tensions between them--is essential to understanding why reform is so difficult.

## Coalition Mapping

### Pro-Modernization Coalition

**Core members**: IAVA, NVLSP, Wounded Warrior Project, CAVC judges (indirectly through decisions), academic researchers, some VBA modernizers, most congressional members

**Shared agenda**: Updated VASRD, faster processing, expanded presumptive conditions, IT modernization, reduced adversarial posture

**Internal tensions**: Degree of willingness to accept risk of fraud; preferences between VA-administered and outsourced processing; attitudes toward AI-assisted decision-making

### Status Quo Coalition

**Core members**: VBA institutional leadership (some), VA employee unions (AFGE), C&P exam contractors, established claims attorneys, some traditional VSOs

**Shared interest**: Maintaining current system complexity (which sustains employment, contracts, and fee income), incremental rather than structural reform

**Internal tensions**: VA employees want more hiring; contractors want more contracts (these compete for the same budget); attorneys benefit from complexity but also want their clients to receive benefits

### Fiscal Restraint Coalition

**Core members**: OMB, congressional appropriators (some), fiscal conservatives, CBO analysts

**Shared interest**: Controlling the growth of a $134B+ program; ensuring sustainability; preventing fraud

**Internal tensions**: Genuine concern about fiscal sustainability vs. political reluctance to appear anti-veteran

## Influence Mechanisms

### Formal Channels

- **Congressional testimony**: VSOs testify before Veterans' Affairs committees multiple times per year; their positions heavily influence legislation
- **VA Advisory Committee on Disability Compensation**: Advises VA on rating schedule updates; includes veterans, medical experts, and policy analysts
- **Federal rulemaking**: VASRD changes go through notice-and-comment rulemaking, allowing public input
- **CAVC decisions**: Court decisions interpreting VA statutes and regulations shape claims processing policy

### Informal Channels

- **VSO access to VBA leadership**: Major VSOs have regular meetings with VBA leadership and participate in policy development
- **Industry lobbying**: C&P exam contractors and IT vendors lobby Congress and VA for contract continuation
- **Attorney fee incentives**: Claims attorneys earn fees from successful appeals, creating financial incentives to advocate for generous interpretation of benefit statutes
- **Veteran social media**: Online veteran communities (Reddit r/VeteransBenefits, Facebook groups) share claims strategies and create informal pressure on VA practices

### Power Asymmetries

The most significant power asymmetry is between the VA institutional bureaucracy and individual veterans. The VA controls the process, the timeline, the evidence standards, and the rating criteria. Individual veterans--especially those without VSO representation or legal counsel--face an opaque, complex system with no leverage beyond the appeals process. This asymmetry is partially mitigated by VSO representation (free to veterans) and legal representation (fee-based), but remains the fundamental structural feature that makes the system adversarial.
