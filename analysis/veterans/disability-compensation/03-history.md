# Veteran Disability Compensation: History

## Overview

The history of veteran disability compensation in the United States spans from the colonial era to the present day, reflecting evolving social attitudes toward military service, disability, and the government's obligation to those who serve. The system has grown from modest pensions for Revolutionary War soldiers to a $134 billion annual entitlement program, yet its fundamental architecture--an adversarial, bureaucratic process for proving service-connected disability--has remained remarkably constant despite the radical transformation of both warfare and medical understanding. Each major conflict has prompted expansion of the system, but the core structural problems--delays, inconsistency, and outdated evaluation criteria--have persisted across every era.

---

## Colonial and Revolutionary Era (1636-1800)

- The Plymouth Colony enacted the first American military disability provision in 1636, providing that soldiers "maimed or disabled" in the colony's defense would be maintained at public expense
- The Continental Congress established pensions for disabled Revolutionary War soldiers in 1776, with payments based on rank and degree of disability
- These early pensions were administered by individual states, creating the first precedent for federal-state friction in veteran benefits administration
- The system was rudimentary: no standardized evaluation criteria, no medical examination requirements, and rampant fraud

---

## 19th Century: The Pension System (1800-1900)

### War of 1812 and Mexican-American War

- Congress gradually expanded pension eligibility beyond combat injuries to include disease contracted during service
- Administration shifted from states to the federal government, first through the War Department and then through the newly created Bureau of Pensions (1833)

### Civil War and Its Aftermath

- The Civil War created an unprecedented pension obligation: approximately 1.9 million Union veterans (and eventually their widows and dependents) became eligible
- The Arrears Act of 1879 allowed retroactive claims, triggering a massive surge in applications
- By 1893, military pensions consumed 42% of all federal expenditures--the largest single item in the federal budget (Skocpol, *Protecting Soldiers and Mothers*, 1992)
- A cottage industry of pension attorneys and claim agents emerged, many of whom exploited veterans through excessive fees and fraudulent claims
- The pension system became deeply politicized: the Republican Party used pension expansion as a patronage tool, while reformers decried waste and fraud
- This era established patterns--backlogs, inconsistent adjudication, attorney exploitation, political manipulation--that persist to the present day

### Key Legislation

- General Law Pension Act (1862): Established disability ratings tied to military rank and degree of disability
- Arrears Act (1879): Allowed retroactive claims, creating the first major backlog
- Disability Pension Act (1890): Expanded eligibility to all Union veterans with any disability, regardless of service connection

---

## World War I and the Modern System (1917-1945)

### Creation of the Veterans Bureau

- World War I created 4.7 million veterans and revealed the inadequacy of the Bureau of Pensions
- The War Risk Insurance Act (1914, amended 1917) introduced disability insurance as an alternative to traditional pensions
- The Veterans Bureau was created in 1921 to consolidate veteran benefit administration
- The Veterans Bureau was plagued by scandal: Director Charles Forbes was convicted of corruption in 1925 after embezzling bureau funds

### VA Creation and Rating Schedule Origins

- President Hoover signed Executive Order 5398 in 1930, creating the Veterans Administration by consolidating the Veterans Bureau, the Bureau of Pensions, and the National Home for Disabled Volunteer Soldiers
- The VA Schedule for Rating Disabilities (VASRD) was first codified in 1933, establishing the framework of percentage-based disability ratings tied to average loss of earning capacity
- The 1945 revision of the VASRD established the fundamental structure that remains in use today--a structure designed primarily for physical combat injuries of World War II

### Key Design Decisions

| Decision | Rationale | Legacy Impact |
|----------|-----------|--------------|
| Earning capacity model | Compensate for lost ability to work, not for suffering | Fails to capture quality-of-life impact; penalizes veterans who work despite disability |
| Percentage-based ratings | Simple administrative framework | 10% increments are crude; combined rating formula confuses veterans |
| Service connection requirement | Limit compensation to military-caused conditions | Creates adversarial burden of proof; excludes conditions aggravated but not caused by service |
| Individual evaluation | Each veteran assessed individually | Labor-intensive; creates backlogs; subjective |

## Post-World War II Expansion (1945-1975)

### GI Bill and Benefit Expansion

- The Servicemen's Readjustment Act of 1944 (GI Bill) created education and housing benefits but did not substantially reform disability compensation
- Disability compensation remained the primary financial benefit for veterans with service-connected conditions
- The Korean War and Vietnam War added millions of new beneficiaries without fundamental system reform
- The VA became the largest independent federal agency, administering benefits for tens of millions of veterans

### Vietnam Era and Agent Orange

- Vietnam veterans faced a new challenge: toxic exposure claims that did not fit the existing injury-based framework
- Agent Orange exposure claims were initially denied systematically by the VA, which maintained that insufficient evidence linked herbicide exposure to specific conditions
- The Agent Orange Act of 1991 eventually established presumptive service connection for specific conditions, but only after decades of veteran suffering and advocacy
- This battle established the pattern that would repeat with Gulf War illness and PACT Act toxic exposures: initial denial, veteran advocacy, and eventual (delayed) recognition

---

## Modern Reform Attempts (1975-2010)

### Veterans' Benefits Improvements Act of 1994

- Created the Court of Appeals for Veterans Claims (originally the Court of Veterans Appeals), providing judicial review of VA benefit decisions for the first time
- Before 1988, veterans had no judicial recourse for VA decisions--a remarkable absence of due process for a federal benefit program
- The Veterans' Judicial Review Act (1988) and subsequent legislation gradually expanded access to courts

### IT Modernization Efforts

- The VA began computerizing claims processing in the 1990s but progress was painfully slow
- The Veterans Benefits Management System (VBMS) was launched in 2013 to replace paper-based processing
- Despite billions in IT investment, the claims process remained slow and error-prone
- The VA's IT modernization failures have been documented in dozens of GAO and IG reports

### The 2013 Backlog Crisis

- By March 2013, the disability claims backlog reached an all-time high of 611,000 claims pending more than 125 days
- Public outrage and congressional pressure led to a "war on the backlog" including:
    - Mandatory overtime for claims processors
    - Provisional ratings for certain conditions
    - Fully Developed Claims program (expedited processing for complete submissions)
    - Hiring surge of thousands of additional claims processors
- The backlog was reduced to 74,000 by 2017--but began growing again almost immediately as the reforms created unsustainable workloads and the underlying structural problems remained unaddressed

---

## Current Era: PACT Act and Ongoing Reform (2010-Present)

### Appeals Modernization Act (2017)

- Replaced the legacy appeals process (which could take 5-7 years) with three review lanes
- Intended to reduce appeals wait times and provide veterans with more options
- Implementation has been mixed: Higher-Level Review lane is relatively fast, but BVA lane remains slow
- Legacy appeals continue to clog the system years after the transition

### PACT Act (2022)

- The Sergeant First Class Heath Robinson Honoring Our Promise to Address Comprehensive Toxics Act (PACT Act) was the most significant expansion of veteran benefits since the Agent Orange Act
- Established presumptive service connection for 20+ conditions linked to burn pits, Agent Orange (Thailand and other locations), radiation, and other toxic exposures
- Expanded VA healthcare eligibility to 3.5 million+ additional veterans
- Created a massive surge in new claims that has strained the system
- CBO estimated the 10-year cost at $797 billion, including healthcare and compensation

### Ongoing Challenges

- The PACT Act's expansion, while morally necessary, has created a new backlog that the VA's hiring and modernization efforts have not yet resolved
- The VASRD modernization project remains incomplete, with the most important body systems (musculoskeletal, mental health) still pending
- IT modernization continues to lag, with the Electronic Health Record Modernization (EHRM) program experiencing significant delays and cost overruns

## Lessons from History

1. **Every war creates a new class of disability that the system is unprepared for**: From shell shock to Agent Orange to burn pits, the VA has consistently been caught flat-footed by the health consequences of military service. Proactive toxic exposure monitoring would prevent decades of delay.

2. **Backlogs are structural, not cyclical**: The claims backlog has existed in some form since the Civil War. Each "crisis" is resolved through temporary surges (hiring, overtime, rule changes) that do not address the underlying structural complexity of the system.

3. **The adversarial model persists because it serves institutional interests**: The requirement for veterans to "prove" their disabilities protects the VA from open-ended liability but imposes enormous human costs. Every attempt to shift toward a more collaborative model has been resisted by institutional actors concerned about fraud and cost.

4. **Presumptive conditions are the most effective reform tool**: When the VA establishes presumptive service connection (as with Agent Orange and PACT Act conditions), claims are processed faster and veterans receive benefits sooner. The pattern is always the same: resistance, then grudging acceptance, then recognition that presumptive conditions should have been established years earlier.

5. **IT modernization has been promised and failed repeatedly**: From the 1990s to the present, the VA has spent billions on IT systems that were late, over budget, and underwhelming. Effective reform must include realistic IT expectations and accountability.

6. **The rating schedule is the untouched foundation**: Despite decades of reform efforts targeting claims processing speed and appeals, the VASRD itself--the framework that determines what veterans are paid--has barely been modified since 1945. Modernizing the rating schedule is the single most impactful structural reform available, and the one most consistently avoided.
