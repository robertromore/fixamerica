# Veteran Suicide Prevention: History

## Overview

The history of veteran suicide prevention in the United States is a story of delayed recognition, reactive policymaking, and persistent institutional resistance to addressing the structural conditions that put veterans at risk. For most of American history, veteran suicide was invisible--uncounted, unstudied, and culturally suppressed. Only in the 21st century, under the pressure of sustained media attention and advocacy, did the federal government begin treating veteran suicide as a crisis demanding systematic response. Yet the approaches adopted have largely replicated civilian clinical models without addressing the unique military-to-civilian transition failures and cultural factors that distinguish veteran suicide from the broader public health problem.

---

## Pre-Modern Era: Invisible Suffering (Before 1970)

### Civil War Through World War I

- Veteran psychological trauma was recognized informally as "soldier's heart" (Civil War) and "shell shock" (World War I), but suicide was not tracked or studied as a distinct veteran issue
- The National Home for Disabled Volunteer Soldiers (established 1865) provided institutional care but did not address mental health
- Mental health treatment consisted primarily of institutionalization; suicide was a source of shame, not policy concern
- No federal agency tracked veteran suicide rates; deaths were recorded as civilian suicides without military service notation

### World War II and Korea

- "Combat fatigue" was recognized as a legitimate condition, but the dominant cultural expectation was that veterans would "get over it" upon returning home
- The GI Bill (1944) provided economic supports that may have inadvertently reduced suicide risk by addressing social determinants--education, housing, employment--though this was not the intent
- The VA hospital system expanded dramatically, but mental health remained a secondary priority focused on psychosis and severe conditions rather than depression and suicidality
- Korean War veterans received less public recognition and fewer transition supports, establishing a pattern of declining post-conflict investment

### Vietnam Era (1964-1975)

- The Vietnam War produced the first widespread public awareness of veteran psychological wounds, eventually codified as Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) in 1980
- VA Vet Centers were established in 1979 specifically to provide readjustment counseling to Vietnam veterans, representing the first community-based outreach model
- However, veteran suicide was not systematically studied; the primary focus was on PTSD symptoms, substance abuse, and homelessness
- Cultural hostility toward Vietnam veterans compounded isolation and help-seeking barriers

## The Invisible Crisis: Early Recognition (1970-2005)

### PTSD and the Mental Health Framework

- The inclusion of PTSD in the DSM-III (1980) provided a diagnostic framework that legitimized veteran mental health treatment
- The National Center for PTSD was established within the VA in 1989, creating a research infrastructure focused on combat-related trauma
- Suicide research in this period was largely civilian-focused; veteran suicide was assumed to be a subset of the broader problem rather than requiring distinct analysis
- The VA did not maintain a centralized suicide surveillance system; individual medical centers tracked outcomes inconsistently

### Early Warning Signs

- Scattered studies in the 1990s and early 2000s began identifying elevated suicide rates among veterans, particularly Vietnam-era veterans
- The CBS News investigation "Suicide and the Soldier" (2007) used state death records to estimate approximately 6,256 veteran suicides in 2005, generating significant public attention
- The VA initially disputed the CBS findings, claiming its own internal data showed lower numbers--a response that undermined public trust and galvanized advocacy
- Internal VA emails (later revealed through litigation) showed officials were aware of the elevated rate but concerned about the public relations impact of acknowledging it

### The Katz Emails (2007-2008)

In 2008, a lawsuit by Veterans for Common Sense revealed internal VA emails between Dr. Ira Katz, then head of VA Mental Health, and colleagues:

- One email noted 12,000 suicide attempts per year among veterans receiving VA care
- Another stated "Shh!" regarding the suicide data, fearing media attention
- The revelation severely damaged VA credibility and created political pressure for transparency and action
- The incident remains a pivotal moment in the history of veteran suicide prevention, illustrating institutional resistance to acknowledging the crisis

## The Modern Era: Crisis Acknowledgment and Response (2005-2020)

### The Joshua Omvig Veterans Suicide Prevention Act (2007)

- Named for a 22-year-old Iraq veteran who died by suicide in 2005
- Required the VA to develop a comprehensive suicide prevention program including:
    - Screening for suicide risk at all VA medical facilities
    - Mental health staff training on suicide assessment
    - A 24-hour crisis hotline
    - Research on best practices
- Represented the first federal legislation specifically targeting veteran suicide

### The Veterans Crisis Line (2007-Present)

- Launched in 2007 as the Veterans Crisis Line (1-800-273-8255, Press 1)
- Initially plagued by operational problems: long wait times, dropped calls, inadequate staffing
- A 2016 VA Inspector General report found critical deficiencies including rolled-over calls to voicemail
- Significant improvements after 2017 reorganization, including consolidation of call centers and increased staffing
- Transitioned to 988 (Press 1) in 2022 with the national suicide prevention number launch

### Executive Order 13822 (2018)

- President Trump's executive order directed a national roadmap for veteran suicide prevention
- Emphasized community-based approaches and public-private partnerships
- Led to the creation of the **PREVENTS** (President's Roadmap to Empower Veterans and End a National Tragedy of Suicide) task force
- The task force released its roadmap in 2020, recommending a shift toward upstream prevention and community engagement
- Critics noted the roadmap lacked enforcement mechanisms and dedicated funding

### VA National Suicide Data Reports (2012-Present)

- Beginning in 2012, the VA began publishing annual National Suicide Data Reports using death certificate data matched with military service records
- These reports provided the first comprehensive national picture of veteran suicide, including veterans outside the VA system
- The reports revealed that the commonly cited "22 a day" statistic (from an early analysis using limited state data) was approximately accurate, later refined to 17-18 per day
- The data lag of 2-3 years remains a significant limitation

### COMPACT Act and Commander Hannon Act (2020)

- The **COMPACT Act** (Public Law 116-214) authorized emergency stabilization care for veterans in acute suicidal crisis at non-VA facilities, regardless of enrollment status
- The **Commander John Scott Hannon Veterans Mental Health Care Improvement Act** (Public Law 116-171) expanded mental health care access, authorized community grants, and established the Governor's Challenge
- Together, these laws represented the most significant legislative investment in veteran suicide prevention since the Omvig Act
- Both were named for veterans who died by suicide, reflecting the advocacy power of families who had lost service members

## Current Period: Structural Reform Attempts (2020-Present)

### Biden Administration Priorities

- Executive Order 14072 (2021) declared veteran suicide prevention a "whole-of-government" priority
- Directed all federal agencies (not just VA) to contribute to prevention efforts
- Announced the **Reducing Military and Veteran Suicide** strategy emphasizing lethal means safety, transition support, and community integration
- Increased funding for the Staff Sergeant Parker Gordon Fox Suicide Prevention Grant Program

### PACT Act Implications (2022-Present)

- While primarily focused on toxic exposure benefits, the PACT Act (Public Law 117-168) expanded VA healthcare eligibility to millions of veterans previously excluded
- By bringing more veterans into the VA system, the PACT Act may increase the reach of VA suicide prevention programs
- The full impact on suicide rates will not be measurable for several years due to data lag

### Cultural and Community Shifts

- The "Be There" campaign and similar public awareness efforts have increased visibility of the crisis
- Firearm industry partnerships (National Shooting Sports Foundation, American Foundation for Suicide Prevention) represent a new approach to lethal means safety
- Peer support models have expanded significantly, moving beyond clinical settings into community organizations
- Despite these efforts, the fundamental challenge remains: reaching veterans who are not connected to any system of care

## Lessons from History

1. **Institutional resistance delayed action for decades**: The VA's initial suppression of suicide data (Katz emails) cost lives and trust. Transparency must be non-negotiable.

2. **Naming laws after dead veterans is not a substitute for effective prevention**: Multiple pieces of legislation named for individual veterans have been enacted, yet the rate has not declined. Symbolic action must be followed by structural reform.

3. **The GI Bill precedent matters**: The most successful "suicide prevention" program in American history was not labeled as such--it was the GI Bill, which addressed social determinants. This suggests prevention must extend beyond clinical interventions.

4. **Crisis hotlines are necessary but insufficient**: The Veterans Crisis Line represents real progress in crisis response, but it intercepts only a fraction of those at risk and only at the point of acute crisis.

5. **Community-based approaches are chronically underfunded**: Despite repeated recommendations to invest in community prevention, the vast majority of funding remains within VA clinical programs that reach only 40% of at-risk veterans.

6. **Data infrastructure matters**: The absence of real-time surveillance data means policymakers are always fighting the last war. The 2-3 year lag in the National Suicide Data Report hamstrings evidence-based decision-making.

7. **Cultural change is slower than policy change**: Legislative and administrative reforms can be enacted in months; shifting the veteran community's attitudes toward help-seeking takes generations.
