# Veteran Suicide Prevention: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative landscape for veteran suicide prevention has grown significantly since the 2007 Joshua Omvig Act, yet existing law remains insufficient to address the structural drivers of veteran suicide. Current legislation focuses primarily on VA clinical programs and crisis response, with inadequate attention to community-based prevention, lethal means safety, transition reform, and the disability claims process. The proposals below address these gaps through a combination of new federal legislation, state model legislation, regulatory reform, and reauthorization of existing programs.

## Federal Legislation

### Veteran Lethal Means Safety Act

**Purpose**: Establish a national voluntary firearm safe storage network for veterans in crisis, fund lethal means counseling training, and create liability protections for temporary storage providers.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Veteran Lethal Means Safety Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) In 2021, 6,392 veterans died by suicide in the United States.
(2) Firearms are used in 71.1 percent of veteran suicides, compared
    to 50.6 percent of non-veteran suicides.
(3) Firearm suicide attempts are fatal 85-90 percent of the time,
    compared to 1-2 percent for poisoning.
(4) 90 percent of individuals who survive a suicide attempt do not
    go on to die by suicide.
(5) Reducing access to lethal means during a suicidal crisis reduces
    overall suicide mortality without complete method substitution.
(6) Voluntary temporary firearm storage during personal crisis is
    consistent with responsible gun ownership and the Second Amendment.

SEC. 3. NATIONAL VETERAN SAFE STORAGE NETWORK.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary of Veterans Affairs, in
    coordination with the Attorney General and the Secretary of
    Defense, shall establish a National Veteran Safe Storage Network
    consisting of participating firearms retailers, shooting ranges,
    law enforcement agencies, and other entities that voluntarily
    agree to provide temporary firearm storage for veterans.

(b) PARTICIPATING ENTITIES.—
    (1) Any federally licensed firearms dealer, law enforcement
        agency, shooting range, or other entity approved by the
        Secretary may participate in the Network.
    (2) Participation shall be voluntary.
    (3) Participating entities shall agree to—
        (A) accept firearms for temporary storage from any veteran
            or veteran's family member who requests it;
        (B) return stored firearms upon request by the owner,
            subject to a minimum 24-hour waiting period;
        (C) maintain secure storage facilities meeting standards
            established by the Secretary; and
        (D) protect the privacy of individuals using the service.

(c) LIABILITY PROTECTION.—A participating entity acting in good
    faith under this section shall not be liable in any civil action
    for any injury or damage resulting from—
    (1) accepting a firearm for temporary storage;
    (2) returning a stored firearm to its owner; or
    (3) the use of a stored firearm after its return.

(d) FUNDING.—
    (1) Gun lock distribution: $25,000,000 annually for cable-style
        gun locks distributed through VA facilities, Vet Centers,
        veteran service organizations, and participating retailers.
    (2) Storage network: $50,000,000 annually for infrastructure,
        training, and operational support for participating entities.
    (3) Training: $15,000,000 annually for lethal means counseling
        training for VA clinicians, peer specialists, and community
        providers.

SEC. 4. LETHAL MEANS COUNSELING.

(a) STANDARD OF CARE.—The Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall
    establish lethal means counseling as a standard component of
    all VA mental health assessments and safety planning
    interventions.

(b) TRAINING REQUIREMENT.—All VA mental health providers, peer
    support specialists, and suicide prevention coordinators shall
    complete lethal means counseling training within 12 months of
    this Act's enactment.

(c) COMMUNITY PROVIDER TRAINING.—The Secretary shall make lethal
    means counseling training available, at no cost, to community
    mental health providers, emergency department staff, primary
    care providers, and clergy.

SEC. 5. PROHIBITION ON FIREARM REGISTRIES.

Nothing in this Act shall be construed to authorize the
establishment of a federal registry of firearms or firearm owners.
No information collected under this Act regarding firearm ownership
or storage shall be used for any purpose other than the voluntary
safe storage program established under Section 3.

SEC. 6. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There is authorized to be appropriated $90,000,000 for each of
fiscal years 2027 through 2031 to carry out this Act.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the single most impactful intervention--lethal means safety--while incorporating explicit protections against concerns about gun registries and mandatory storage. The liability protection for participating entities (Section 3(c)) removes a major barrier to retailer and law enforcement participation. The 24-hour waiting period for return balances crisis intervention with property rights. The prohibition on registries (Section 5) is designed to preempt NRA opposition.

**Potential Challenges**: Despite the voluntary framework and Second Amendment protections, the NRA and some Republican members are likely to oppose any federal involvement in firearm storage. The 24-hour waiting period may face constitutional challenges.

**Refinements**: Consider making the waiting period optional (at the owner's discretion) to reduce opposition; add a sunset clause requiring reauthorization in 5 years; include an explicit preemption clause preventing states from using this framework to impose mandatory storage.

### Veterans Transition Reform Act

**Purpose**: Replace the current 5-day Transition Assistance Program with a 12-month comprehensive transition process, extend TRICARE coverage, and establish mandatory warm handoffs to VA or community providers.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Veterans Transition Reform Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. COMPREHENSIVE TRANSITION PROCESS.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Chapter 58 of title 10, United States Code, is
    amended to require that transition assistance for members of
    the Armed Forces begin not later than 365 days before the
    anticipated date of separation.

(b) TRANSITION COORDINATOR.—
    (1) The Secretary of Defense shall assign a transition
        coordinator to each member of the Armed Forces not later
        than 365 days before separation.
    (2) The transition coordinator shall—
        (A) conduct an initial needs assessment;
        (B) develop an individualized transition plan;
        (C) coordinate with the Department of Veterans Affairs
            for healthcare continuity;
        (D) arrange a warm handoff to a VA or community mental
            health provider; and
        (E) monitor the member's transition progress through
            365 days post-separation.

(c) WARM HANDOFF REQUIREMENT.—
    (1) No member of the Armed Forces shall be separated from
        active duty without a documented warm handoff to a mental
        health provider at the Department of Veterans Affairs or
        a community provider approved by the Secretary.
    (2) A "warm handoff" means a direct, personal introduction
        between the transitioning member and the receiving
        provider, conducted in person or via telehealth.

SEC. 3. TRICARE EXTENSION.

(a) Section 1145 of title 10, United States Code, is amended to
    extend transitional health care benefits from 180 days to
    365 days following separation from active duty.

(b) Coverage under this section shall include mental health services,
    substance use treatment, and prescription medications without
    interruption from the date of separation.

SEC. 4. PEER MENTOR PROGRAM.

(a) The Secretary of Veterans Affairs, in coordination with the
    Secretary of Defense, shall establish a Veteran Peer Mentor
    Program to assign a trained veteran peer mentor to each
    separating service member.

(b) Peer mentors shall—
    (1) be veterans who have successfully transitioned to civilian
        life;
    (2) complete a standardized training program; and
    (3) maintain contact with assigned mentees for not less than
        12 months post-separation.

(c) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated
    $150,000,000 annually for the Peer Mentor Program.

SEC. 5. REPORTING.

(a) The Secretaries of Defense and Veterans Affairs shall jointly
    report to Congress annually on—
    (1) the number of members receiving transition services;
    (2) warm handoff completion rates;
    (3) healthcare coverage gap incidents;
    (4) mental health outcomes at 6 and 12 months post-separation;
        and
    (5) suicide rates among recently separated members compared to
        the general veteran population.

SEC. 6. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There is authorized to be appropriated $1,200,000,000 for each of
fiscal years 2027 through 2031 to carry out this Act.
```

**Explanation**: This legislation addresses the critical transition period that represents the highest-risk window for veteran suicide. The warm handoff requirement (Section 2(c)) ensures no veteran falls through the gap between DOD and VA systems. The TRICARE extension (Section 3) eliminates healthcare coverage gaps. The peer mentor program (Section 4) leverages the most trusted resource for veterans--other veterans.

**Potential Challenges**: DOD resistance to extending its operational responsibility; cost of TRICARE extension; difficulty recruiting 20,000+ peer mentors; IT interoperability between DOD and VA systems.

**Refinements**: Phase the warm handoff requirement beginning with high-risk populations (combat veterans, those with mental health diagnoses, those receiving other-than-honorable discharges); allow DOD flexibility in transition coordinator implementation.

### Community Veteran Suicide Prevention Act

**Purpose**: Expand the Staff Sergeant Parker Gordon Fox Suicide Prevention Grant Program and establish a veteran suicide prevention coordinator in every county.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Community Veteran Suicide Prevention
Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. EXPANSION OF FOX GRANT PROGRAM.

(a) Section 201 of the Commander John Scott Hannon Veterans Mental
    Health Care Improvement Act (Public Law 116-171) is amended by
    increasing the authorization of appropriations to $750,000,000
    for each fiscal year.

(b) COUNTY COORDINATOR GRANTS.—The Secretary shall award grants
    to establish at least one full-time veteran suicide prevention
    coordinator in each county or county-equivalent in the
    United States.

(c) COORDINATOR DUTIES.—Each coordinator shall—
    (1) maintain a directory of local veteran services;
    (2) conduct community outreach and education;
    (3) coordinate local prevention coalitions;
    (4) facilitate lethal means safety programs;
    (5) connect at-risk veterans to care; and
    (6) report quarterly to the Secretary on prevention activities
        and outcomes.

SEC. 3. OUTCOME-BASED METRICS.

(a) The Secretary shall establish outcome-based performance metrics
    for all grant recipients, including—
    (1) number of veterans contacted and connected to services;
    (2) reduction in local veteran suicide rates;
    (3) lethal means safety interventions conducted; and
    (4) community training events held.

(b) PERFORMANCE REVIEW.—Grant recipients shall be subject to annual
    performance review. Grants may be revoked for failure to meet
    established metrics.

SEC. 4. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There is authorized to be appropriated $750,000,000 for each of
fiscal years 2027 through 2031 to carry out this Act.
```

**Explanation**: The Fox Grant Program is the most promising vehicle for community-based prevention but is currently funded at only $74 million--a fraction of what is needed for national coverage. This bill provides the funding to place a coordinator in every county and requires outcome-based accountability.

**Potential Challenges**: Cost; workforce availability for 3,143+ coordinators; rural recruitment; quality control across thousands of grantees.

**Refinements**: Allow multi-county coordinator positions in rural areas; partner with existing county health departments; phase implementation beginning with highest-risk counties.

## State Model Legislation

### Model Veteran Suicide Prevention Coordination Act

**Purpose**: Establish state-level interagency veteran suicide prevention coordination with dedicated funding and accountability.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Veteran Suicide Prevention
Coordination Act".

SECTION 2. INTERAGENCY COUNCIL.

(a) There is established the [State] Interagency Council on
    Veteran Suicide Prevention, consisting of representatives
    from the departments of health, veterans affairs, behavioral
    health, and public safety, as well as representatives from
    veteran service organizations, mental health providers, and
    community organizations.

(b) The Council shall—
    (1) develop and maintain a state veteran suicide prevention plan;
    (2) coordinate prevention activities across state agencies;
    (3) administer state-funded prevention grants; and
    (4) report annually to the Governor and Legislature on
        prevention outcomes.

SECTION 3. FUNDING.

(a) There is appropriated from the general fund $[amount] annually
    for prevention activities coordinated by the Council.

(b) The Council shall seek to match state funds with federal grants
    available under the Staff Sergeant Fox Grant Program and other
    federal programs.

SECTION 4. VOLUNTARY SAFE STORAGE.

(a) Law enforcement agencies in this state are authorized to accept
    firearms for voluntary temporary storage from any individual
    who requests it.

(b) A law enforcement agency accepting a firearm under this section
    shall return it to the owner upon request, subject to standard
    background check requirements.

(c) No record of voluntary storage under this section shall be used
    as evidence in any proceeding to restrict the individual's
    right to possess firearms.
```

**Adaptations**: States may customize funding levels, council composition, and safe storage provisions based on local conditions. States with existing interagency councils (via the Governor's Challenge) can use this legislation to formalize and fund those structures.

## Regulatory Framework

### VA Regulation: Universal Safety Planning

**Existing Authority**: 38 U.S.C. ch. 17 (VA healthcare authority)

**Draft Regulation**: The Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall require that a structured safety plan, using the Stanley-Brown Safety Planning Intervention or equivalent evidence-based protocol, be completed for every veteran who screens positive for suicide risk on the Columbia-Suicide Severity Rating Scale or equivalent instrument, and that follow-up contact be made within 72 hours of safety plan creation.

**Explanation**: While the VA has encouraged safety planning, it is not universally required. This regulation would standardize the most evidence-supported clinical intervention across all VA facilities.

### VA Regulation: Claims Fast-Track for Suicidal Veterans

**Existing Authority**: 38 U.S.C. ch. 51 (VA claims processing authority)

**Draft Regulation**: The Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall establish a fast-track claims processing pathway for any veteran who presents with suicidal ideation, has a documented suicide attempt, or has been identified as high-risk by the REACH VET program. Fast-track claims shall be processed within 30 days.

**Explanation**: The claims backlog contributes to financial despair, which is a documented suicide risk factor. Prioritizing at-risk veterans' claims addresses a direct driver of suicide while the broader backlog is resolved.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Second Amendment**: All lethal means safety provisions must be voluntary and include explicit protections against mandatory storage or registry creation. The Veteran Lethal Means Safety Act includes both protections.
- **Due Process**: Any fast-track claims process must not reduce procedural protections for veterans; it must accelerate processing, not lower evidentiary standards.
- **Federalism**: State model legislation respects state sovereignty while encouraging coordination with federal programs.

### Preemption Questions

- Federal legislation on voluntary safe storage should explicitly preempt state laws that prohibit law enforcement from accepting firearms for temporary storage
- State legislation should be designed to complement, not conflict with, federal VA programs

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- Grant programs enforce through performance metrics and funding revocation
- VA regulations enforce through standard VA oversight and Inspector General authority
- Warm handoff requirements enforce through mandatory reporting to Congress

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- All federal legislation includes 5-year authorization periods requiring reauthorization
- Annual reporting requirements provide ongoing Congressional oversight
- The real-time surveillance system enables evidence-based program evaluation

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Veteran Lethal Means Safety Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Voluntary participation gap | Insufficient participating entities in rural areas leaves coverage gaps | High |
| 24-hour return window | May be too short for crisis resolution but too long for constitutional comfort | Medium |
| No coverage for non-firearms means | Act focuses on firearms; other lethal means (medication, rope) not addressed | Medium |
| Privacy circumvention | Storage records could potentially be accessed through court orders despite protections | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Cannot reach veterans who do not self-identify as in crisis | Voluntary program misses those who do not recognize their own risk | Relies on self-identification |
| No enforcement mechanism for community provider training | Training is offered but not required for non-VA providers | Respects provider autonomy but limits reach |
| Does not address illegal firearms | Veterans with unregistered or illegally obtained firearms are outside the system | Legal and practical limitations |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Require minimum geographic coverage standards (at least one participating entity per county) as a condition of network funding
2. Add a research mandate to study optimal return waiting periods based on crisis duration data
3. Expand the scope to include medication lock boxes and other means safety tools in a subsequent amendment
4. Strengthen privacy protections with criminal penalties for unauthorized disclosure of storage records

### Veterans Transition Reform Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Other-than-honorable discharge exclusion | Veterans with OTH discharges may be excluded from warm handoff requirements | Critical |
| DOD compliance without substance | DOD could assign coordinators who perform minimal functions | High |
| Telehealth warm handoff quality | Remote handoffs may be less effective than in-person introductions | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Cannot force veteran engagement | Transition services can be offered but not mandated for voluntary separations | Individual autonomy |
| IT system interoperability | DOD and VA electronic health records remain incompatible | Decades of separate IT development |
| National Guard and Reserve coverage | Part-time service members have different transition pathways | Different employment structure |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Explicitly include veterans with OTH discharges in all transition provisions (consistent with COMPACT Act and recent VA eligibility expansions)
2. Establish DOD coordinator performance standards with consequences for non-compliance
3. Require in-person warm handoffs for high-risk populations; allow telehealth for others
4. Mandate interoperability standards for DOD-VA health record exchange within 3 years
5. Create a National Guard/Reserve-specific transition pathway with state-level coordination

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Fragmented authority across VA, DOD, HHS, states | Create an interagency coordinating body with budgetary authority |
| 2-3 year data lag prevents evidence-based adjustments | Real-time surveillance system (addressed in separate legislation) |
| Workforce shortages limit implementation capacity | Phased implementation; peer specialist expansion; telehealth |
| Political vulnerability of funding to annual appropriations | Seek mandatory spending classification for core prevention programs |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

All legislation includes 5-year authorization periods. The Veteran Suicide Prevention Accountability Act (proposed separately) would require:

- Annual outcome reports to Congress comparing veteran suicide rates to funded prevention activities
- Independent program evaluations every 3 years
- GAO review of implementation effectiveness at the 5-year reauthorization point
- Mandatory sunset if no measurable reduction in veteran suicide rate within 5 years (forcing Congress to either reform or reauthorize with modifications)
