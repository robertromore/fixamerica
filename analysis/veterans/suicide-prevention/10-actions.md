# Veteran Suicide Prevention: Actions

## Overview

Every American can contribute to reducing veteran suicide. The actions below are organized by level of engagement, from individual steps anyone can take to sustained political advocacy. The most important message: you do not need to be a mental health professional to save a veteran's life. Knowing how to recognize warning signs, having a conversation, and connecting someone to resources can be the difference between life and death.

## Individual Actions

### Learn the Warning Signs

Veteran suicide warning signs include but are not limited to:

- Talking about being a burden to others
- Increasing alcohol or drug use
- Withdrawing from friends, family, and activities
- Expressing feelings of hopelessness or having no reason to live
- Giving away prized possessions
- Sleeping too little or too much
- Showing rage, desire for revenge, or reckless behavior
- Expressing feelings of being trapped
- Sudden calmness after a period of distress (may indicate decision has been made)

### Have the Conversation

If you are concerned about a veteran in your life:

1. **Ask directly**: "Are you thinking about suicide?" Direct questions do not increase risk--they reduce isolation.
2. **Listen without judgment**: Do not dismiss, minimize, or try to "fix" the problem immediately.
3. **Express concern**: "I care about you and I'm worried."
4. **Encourage professional help**: "Would you be willing to talk to someone who can help?"
5. **Stay connected**: Follow up. A text saying "thinking of you" can matter more than you know.

### Promote Safe Storage

If you are a firearm owner or know veterans who are:

- Store firearms securely (locked safe, cable lock, trigger lock) when not in use
- Request free gun locks from the VA (available at any VA medical center)
- Consider voluntary temporary off-site storage during times of personal crisis
- Talk to fellow veterans about safe storage as a responsible practice, not a political statement
- Visit the VA's KeepItSecure campaign at va.gov/reach

### Support Veteran Transition

- Hire veterans: employers are a critical part of the prevention ecosystem
- Mentor a transitioning service member through programs like American Corporate Partners or Veterati
- Welcome veterans into community organizations, faith communities, and social groups
- Understand that transition is not a one-time event but a multi-year process

## Community Actions

### Start or Join a Community Prevention Coalition

The Governor's Challenge to Prevent Veteran Suicide provides a framework for state and community coalitions. To get involved:

1. Contact your state's Governor's Challenge team (list available at va.gov/prevent-veteran-suicide)
2. Attend community meetings focused on veteran services
3. Volunteer with local veteran service organizations (VSOs)
4. Organize a community training event on veteran suicide prevention (QPR or similar)

### Become a Trained Gatekeeper

Several evidence-based training programs teach non-professionals to recognize and respond to suicide risk:

| Training | Duration | Format | Cost | Provider |
|----------|----------|--------|------|----------|
| QPR (Question, Persuade, Refer) | 1-2 hours | Online or in-person | Free-$30 | QPR Institute |
| safeTALK | 3 hours | In-person | Varies | LivingWorks |
| ASIST (Applied Suicide Intervention Skills Training) | 2 days | In-person | Varies | LivingWorks |
| Mental Health First Aid | 8 hours | In-person or virtual | Free-$75 | National Council for Mental Wellbeing |
| VA S.A.V.E. Training | 1 hour | Online | Free | VA (free for anyone) |

### Support Veterans in Your Workplace

- Advocate for veteran-friendly hiring practices
- Support Employee Resource Groups for veterans
- Ensure your workplace Employee Assistance Program (EAP) includes providers trained in military culture
- Allow flexibility for VA appointments and adjustment periods
- Create a culture where talking about mental health is normalized

### Faith Community Engagement

Faith communities serve as critical support networks, especially in rural areas:

- Invite veteran speakers to share their experiences (with appropriate support)
- Train clergy and lay leaders in suicide prevention basics
- Partner with VA chaplains and Vet Center staff
- Offer meeting space for peer support groups
- Incorporate veteran recognition into community life beyond Veterans Day

## Political Actions

### Contact Your Representatives

Veterans' issues enjoy bipartisan support, but elected officials must hear that constituents care about specific reforms--not just general "support the troops" sentiment.

**Priority legislative asks**:

1. Increase funding for the Staff Sergeant Fox Grant Program to $750 million annually
2. Mandate a 12-month transition process with no healthcare gap
3. Fund the national voluntary temporary firearm storage network
4. Establish real-time veteran suicide surveillance
5. Accelerate disability claims processing with emergency bridge payments

### Template Email

Subject: Veteran Suicide Prevention Requires Structural Reform

Dear [Representative/Senator],

I am writing to urge you to support comprehensive veteran suicide prevention reform. Despite billions in federal spending, 17+ veterans die by suicide every day--a rate that has not declined in two decades.

I respectfully ask you to support the following specific measures:

1. **Increase community-based prevention funding**: The Staff Sergeant Fox Grant Program ($74 million in FY 2024) should be expanded to $750 million to fund prevention coordinators in every county.

2. **Reform the military-to-civilian transition**: The current 5-day TAP curriculum is inadequate. A 12-month transition process with continuous healthcare coverage would address the highest-risk period for veteran suicide.

3. **Accelerate disability claims processing**: Veterans waiting months or years for disability determinations face financial despair that contributes to suicide. Emergency bridge payments and faster processing are needed.

4. **Fund voluntary lethal means safety programs**: 71% of veteran suicides involve firearms. Voluntary safe storage programs--not gun restrictions--can save lives while respecting Second Amendment rights.

We owe our veterans more than awareness campaigns. We owe them a system that actually works.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your Address]

### Template Phone Script

*"Hello, my name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [City/ZIP]. I'm calling to ask [Representative/Senator] to support increased funding for veteran suicide prevention, specifically expanding the Staff Sergeant Fox Grant Program and reforming the military-to-civilian transition process. 17 veterans die by suicide every day, and the rate hasn't declined in 20 years. ### Attend Town Halls and Public Meetings

- Prepare a concise question about veteran suicide prevention
- Bring specific data (e.g., your state's veteran suicide rate)
- Ask what specific legislation the representative supports
- Follow up with written correspondence referencing the conversation

## Talking Points

### For Conversations with Elected Officials

- "The veteran suicide rate has not declined in 20 years despite $600 million annually in prevention spending. The current approach is not working."
- "62% of veterans who die by suicide are not in the VA system. We need community-based prevention, not just VA programs."
- "71% of veteran suicides involve firearms. Voluntary safe storage programs--developed with the firearms community--can save lives without restricting rights."
- "The disability claims backlog creates financial despair. A veteran waiting 6 months for a claim decision is a veteran in crisis."
- "The military-to-civilian transition is the highest-risk period. Five days of PowerPoint presentations is not adequate preparation."

### For Conversations with Community Members

- "You don't have to be a professional to help. Asking someone directly about suicide does not make them more likely to attempt it."
- "Safe storage of firearms during a personal crisis is responsible gun ownership. It's not about taking anyone's guns."
- "Veterans are not broken. They are dealing with the consequences of service in a system that fails them during transition."
- "The most important thing you can do is stay connected. Social isolation is one of the strongest risk factors."

## Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Reality |
|--------------|---------|
| "Asking about suicide puts the idea in someone's head" | Research consistently shows that asking directly reduces risk by creating an opening for help (Dazzi et al., *Psychological Medicine*, 2014) |
| "Veterans who want to die cannot be stopped" | 90% of suicide attempt survivors do not go on to die by suicide; the crisis is temporary (Owens et al., *British Journal of Psychiatry*, 2002) |
| "22 veterans die by suicide every day" | The current estimate is 17.5 per day (2021 data); "22 a day" was based on older, less comprehensive data |
| "Only combat veterans are at risk" | Veterans who never deployed also face elevated suicide risk; transition challenges, military sexual trauma, and other factors matter |
| "The VA is the problem" | The VA provides high-quality care for veterans it reaches; the problem is that most at-risk veterans are not in the VA system |
| "More mental health providers will solve the problem" | Providers are needed, but suicide is driven by social determinants (financial stress, isolation, housing) as much as clinical conditions |
| "Safe storage means the government wants to take our guns" | VA-supported safe storage is voluntary and temporary; it is about reducing access during a crisis, not removing gun rights |
| "Awareness campaigns are helping" | Awareness has increased, but the suicide rate has not declined; awareness without accessible services is insufficient |
