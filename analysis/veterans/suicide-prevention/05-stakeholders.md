# Veteran Suicide Prevention: Stakeholders

## Overview

Veteran suicide prevention involves a complex web of stakeholders ranging from individual veterans and their families to massive federal bureaucracies, congressional committees, advocacy organizations, and the firearms industry. Understanding who holds power, who bears risk, and whose interests are served by the status quo is essential to designing effective reform strategies. Notably, the stakeholders with the most at stake--veterans in crisis and their families--have the least institutional power, while the stakeholders with the most power--federal agencies, Congress, and industry groups--face minimal personal consequences from inaction.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Those Directly Affected

| Stakeholder | Population Size | Stake | Power Level |
|-------------|----------------|-------|-------------|
| Veterans at risk of suicide | Estimated 1-2 million with elevated risk factors | Life and death | Very Low |
| Families of veterans | Estimated 5-8 million immediate family members | Loss, grief, caregiving burden | Low |
| Recently separated service members | ~200,000 annually | Highest-risk transition period | Very Low |
| Veterans in VA mental health care | ~1.8 million | Quality and accessibility of treatment | Low |
| Veterans not in VA care | ~9.5 million | Lack of access to veteran-specific prevention | Very Low |
| Rural veterans | ~4.7 million | Geographic barriers to all services | Very Low |
| Female veterans | ~2.1 million | Rising suicide rates, unique risk factors | Low |
| Minority veterans | ~4.8 million | Disparities in care and rising rates | Low |
| Veteran survivors of suicide attempts | ~60,000+ annually | Follow-up care, means restriction | Very Low |

### Service Providers

| Stakeholder | Role | Stake | Power Level |
|-------------|------|-------|-------------|
| VA mental health clinicians | Direct treatment of veterans | Caseload, burnout, moral distress | Medium |
| VA peer support specialists | Community-based veteran support | Program continuation and growth | Low |
| Veterans Crisis Line staff | Crisis intervention | Staffing, training, operational capacity | Low |
| Community mental health providers | Treat veterans outside VA system | Reimbursement, training in military culture | Low |
| Emergency department staff | First responders to suicide attempts | COMPACT Act implementation, training | Low |

## Decision Makers

| Decision Maker | Authority | Current Position | Key Constraints |
|----------------|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **VA Secretary** | Directs VA policy, budget allocation, program priorities | Supportive of expanded prevention | Bureaucratic inertia, union negotiations, budget limitations |
| **VA Under Secretary for Health** | Oversees VA healthcare including mental health | Supportive of clinical expansion | Provider shortages, facility limitations |
| **Senate Veterans' Affairs Committee** | Authorization and oversight of VA programs | Bipartisan support for prevention funding | Competing priorities, legislative gridlock |
| **House Veterans' Affairs Committee** | Authorization and oversight, appropriations input | Bipartisan support, mixed on means safety | Firearms lobby pressure on some members |
| **Senate/House Appropriations Committees** | Funding levels for all VA programs | Generally supportive of VA mental health | Overall budget constraints, earmark pressures |
| **DOD Secretary** | Transition programs, active-duty mental health | Focused on force readiness | Post-separation outcomes not in DOD metrics |
| **SAMHSA Administrator** | Community mental health grants, 988 system | Supportive of veteran-specific initiatives | Limited dedicated veteran funding |
| **State Governors** | Governor's Challenge participation, state programs | Variable by state and political orientation | State budget limitations, competing priorities |
| **President** | Executive orders, budget proposals, bully pulpit | Both parties have prioritized rhetorically | Short attention span, competing crises |

### Firearms Industry and Advocacy

| Organization | Position | Significance |
|-------------|----------|-------------|
| National Rifle Association (NRA) | Opposes any policy framed as restricting firearm access | Enormous political influence; blocks legislative means-safety measures |
| National Shooting Sports Foundation (NSSF) | Supports voluntary safe storage; partners with VA/AFSP | More pragmatic than NRA; key to industry engagement |
| Gun Owners of America (GOA) | Opposes all firearms-related prevention measures | Absolutist position; secondary influence to NRA |
| Second Amendment Foundation | Legal challenges to firearms restrictions | Litigation strategy blocks state-level measures |

## Coalition Mapping

### Pro-Reform Coalition

**Core members**: IAVA, Wounded Warrior Project, AFSP, Cohen Veterans Network, academic researchers, VA suicide prevention leadership, most Democratic lawmakers, some Republican veterans

**Shared agenda**: Increased prevention funding, expanded community programs, lethal means safety initiatives, transition reform, data transparency

**Internal tensions**: Degree of willingness to address firearms directly; VA-centric vs. community-centric approaches; funding priorities between clinical and social determinant interventions

### Status Quo Coalition

**Core members**: VA institutional leadership (distinct from suicide prevention office), VA employee unions, some traditional VSOs, NRA, some Republican lawmakers, DOD bureaucracy

**Shared interest**: Maintaining current funding flows, avoiding politically risky firearms discussions, incremental rather than structural reform

**Internal tensions**: VA leadership genuinely wants better outcomes but resists systemic reorganization; traditional VSOs support veterans but are cautious about challenging military culture; NRA blocks even voluntary programs if they set precedents

### Potential Swing Stakeholders

| Stakeholder | Current Position | What Could Move Them |
|-------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| American Legion | Cautiously supportive of reform | Evidence-based programs that respect gun culture |
| VFW | Supportive of funding, resistant on firearms | Veteran-led safe storage programs, peer models |
| Republican veterans in Congress | Support funding, oppose means safety mandates | Framing lethal means safety as military-compatible, not "gun control" |
| Rural state governors | Variable | Tailored rural prevention programs with federal funding |
| Firearms retailers | Wary but potentially engaged | NSSF-led voluntary programs, liability protection |
| DOD leadership | Disengaged post-separation | Accountability metrics that extend beyond separation date |

## Influence Mechanisms

### Formal Channels

- **Congressional testimony**: VSOs provide regular testimony before Veterans' Affairs committees; their positions heavily influence legislation
- **VA advisory committees**: The VA Mental Health and Suicide Prevention Advisory Committee includes veteran advocates, clinicians, and researchers
- **Federal rulemaking**: VA regulations implementing legislation are subject to public comment
- **Inspector General oversight**: The VA OIG investigates failures and makes recommendations that carry significant weight

### Informal Channels

- **Media advocacy**: Organizations like IAVA and Wounded Warrior Project use media attention to pressure policymakers
- **Individual veteran stories**: Congressional hearings featuring bereaved families are among the most powerful advocacy tools
- **Campaign contributions**: The firearms lobby's contributions create political pressure against means-safety measures
- **Executive branch access**: VSOs have regular meetings with VA leadership; industry groups lobby DOD and congressional staff

### Power Asymmetries

The most significant power asymmetry in veteran suicide prevention is between the firearms lobby and suicide prevention advocates. The NRA and allied organizations can mobilize millions of members and deploy significant campaign contributions to block any measure framed as restricting gun access. Suicide prevention advocates have moral authority but lack comparable political infrastructure. This asymmetry explains why the most evidence-supported intervention--lethal means safety--remains the most politically difficult to implement.
