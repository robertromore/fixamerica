# Veteran Employment: Root Causes

## Overview

Veteran employment challenges stem from structural misalignments between military and civilian systems rather than deficiencies in veterans themselves. The root causes operate at multiple levels: institutional design that treats transition as an afterthought, labor market structures that fail to recognize military competencies, cultural gaps between military and civilian workplaces, and fragmented government programs that lack coordination. Understanding these causes is essential for designing interventions that address systems rather than symptoms.

---

## Structural Misalignment

### Military-Civilian Skills Translation Gap

The fundamental root cause of veteran underemployment is that the military and civilian labor markets use entirely different systems for classifying, credentialing, and valuing human capital.

| Factor | Military System | Civilian System | Gap |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|-----|
| **Job classification** | Military Occupational Specialty (MOS) codes | Standard Occupational Classification (SOC) codes | No standardized crosswalk; different organizing principles |
| **Credentialing** | Internal military certifications | State licenses, industry certifications | Military training often unrecognized |
| **Performance evaluation** | Narrative evaluations, promotion history | Resumes, interviews, portfolios | Different formats and cultures |
| **Leadership recognition** | Rank, command experience | Titles, reporting structure | Military leadership undervalued or misunderstood |
| **Training documentation** | DD-214, military transcripts | College transcripts, certifications | Incompatible documentation systems |

### Transition Timeline Compression

The military separation process compresses a major life transition into an insufficient window, preventing adequate preparation.

| Phase | Current Duration | Recommended Duration | Gap |
|-------|------------------|---------------------|-----|
| **Pre-separation planning** | 90 days minimum (often less) | 18-24 months | 12-21 months |
| **TAP attendance** | 5 days | Ongoing engagement over 12+ months | Shift from event to process |
| **Job search** | Begins at separation or later | Should begin 12 months before | 6-12 months |
| **Credential translation** | Often post-separation | Should be completed before separation | 3-12 months |
| **Network building** | Minimal pre-separation | Should begin 18 months before | Entire timeline |

### Fragmented Program Architecture

Veteran employment programs are distributed across multiple agencies with limited coordination, creating confusion and coverage gaps.

| Agency | Programs | Limitation |
|--------|----------|------------|
| **DOD** | TAP, SkillBridge, COOL | Ends at separation; limited follow-through |
| **VA** | VR&E, education benefits | Focused on disabled veterans; long wait times |
| **DOL** | JVSG, HVRP, USERRA | Underfunded; limited employer engagement |
| **SBA** | Boots to Business, SDVOSB | Entrepreneurship-focused; narrow reach |
| **State agencies** | Workforce development | Varies dramatically by state |
| **Nonprofit sector** | Hundreds of organizations | Fragmented; uneven quality; duplication |

## Labor Market Barriers

### Credentialing and Licensing Fragmentation

State-by-state licensing requirements create a patchwork that penalizes veterans, who relocate frequently and whose training comes from a national institution.

| Occupation | Military Training | Civilian Requirement | Barrier |
|------------|-------------------|---------------------|---------|
| **Commercial driver** | Military vehicle operation (often larger than civilian) | CDL with state-specific endorsements | Duplicative testing despite superior training |
| **Emergency medical technician** | Combat medic training (68W) exceeds civilian EMT | State EMT certification | State exams test civilian-specific protocols |
| **Electrician** | Military electrical systems training | State apprenticeship + license | Military experience not counted toward apprenticeship hours |
| **Nurse** | Military nursing (highly complex trauma care) | State nursing license (NCLEX) | Exam-focused; experience not weighted |
| **Cybersecurity** | Top Secret clearance + operational experience | Industry certifications (CISSP, etc.) | Certifications require civilian-specific experience |

### Employer Knowledge Gaps

Employers frequently lack understanding of military experience, leading to both over- and under-valuation of veteran candidates.

| Misconception | Reality | Impact |
|---------------|---------|--------|
| **Veterans are rigid/authoritarian** | Military teaches adaptability and decentralized decision-making | Veterans screened out of "creative" roles |
| **All veterans have PTSD** | ~15-20% of combat veterans develop PTSD; most are treated | Employers avoid hiring due to perceived risk |
| **Military skills don't transfer** | Leadership, logistics, technology, healthcare skills are highly transferable | Veterans placed in entry-level positions |
| **Veterans are overqualified** | Veterans may need onboarding support in civilian culture | Employers fear high turnover from "overqualified" hires |
| **Veterans prefer hierarchy** | Veterans value clear expectations but are highly adaptable | Cultural fit concerns limit opportunities |

## Who Benefits from the Status Quo

| Beneficiary | How They Benefit | Incentive to Maintain |
|-------------|------------------|-----------------------|
| **State licensing boards** | Control entry to professions; maintain standards narrative | Revenue from licensing fees; protectionism for incumbent practitioners |
| **For-profit credentialing programs** | Veterans pay for training they already possess | Revenue from duplicative credential programs |
| **Defense contractors** | Hire veterans at below-market rates using security clearances | Clearance premium creates captive labor pool |
| **Federal bureaucracy** | Each agency maintains separate programs and budgets | Institutional self-preservation; avoiding consolidation |
| **Staffing agencies** | Place veterans in temporary/contract positions | Fees from placement; underemployment generates repeat business |
| **Some nonprofits** | Mission depends on veteran employment problems existing | Perpetual need justifies continued funding |

## Perverse Incentives

### Program Design Flaws

| Perverse Incentive | Mechanism | Consequence |
|--------------------|-----------|-------------|
| **Placement-rate metrics** | Programs measured by speed of placement, not quality | Veterans placed in any job rather than matched to skills |
| **TAP as compliance exercise** | DOD treats TAP as a checklist requirement | Emphasis on attendance rather than outcomes |
| **Disability compensation interaction** | Working can reduce or complicate disability benefits | Some veterans avoid employment to protect benefits |
| **WOTC design** | Tax credit goes to employer, not veteran | Employers hire veterans for credit without investing in retention |
| **VR&E counselor caseloads** | Counselors measured by throughput, not outcomes | Rushed counseling; suboptimal career planning |
| **Federal contractor VEVRAA** | Self-reported hiring benchmarks; weak enforcement | Paper compliance without meaningful hiring |

### Economic Incentives

| Perverse Incentive | Mechanism | Consequence |
|--------------------|-----------|-------------|
| **Clearance as commodity** | Security clearances have enormous private-sector value | Veterans channeled into cleared roles regardless of career interest |
| **Geographic lock-in** | VA benefits tied to geographic areas; military bases in remote locations | Veterans remain in areas with limited labor markets |
| **Education benefit timing** | GI Bill used immediately post-separation | Veterans attend school before knowing career direction |
| **Short-term employer thinking** | High training costs discourage investing in veteran onboarding | Employers prefer veterans who need no cultural adjustment |

## Causal Chain

### Primary Causal Pathway

```
Military designs training for military needs (not civilian portability)
    |
    v
Skills and credentials do not map to civilian systems
    |
    v
Transition process is compressed and compliance-driven
    |
    v
Veterans enter labor market without adequate preparation
    |
    v
Employers cannot evaluate military experience
    |
    v
Veterans accept positions below their skill level (underemployment)
    |
    v
High first-year turnover; job-hopping in early transition
    |
    v
Negative employer perceptions reinforced; hiring bias
```

### Secondary Causal Pathways

**Credentialing Pathway:**

```
50-state licensing fragmentation
    |
    v
Military training not recognized by state boards
    |
    v
Veterans must retrain and retest for credentials they already hold
    |
    v
Time and money wasted; career entry delayed 6-18 months
    |
    v
Veterans accept unlicensed work or leave the field entirely
```

**Cultural Pathway:**

```
Military culture emphasizes mission, hierarchy, collective identity
    |
    v
Civilian workplace emphasizes individual initiative, flat structures, self-promotion
    |
    v
Veterans struggle with self-advocacy, networking, interviewing
    |
    v
Qualified veterans underperform in hiring processes
    |
    v
Employer misconceptions about veteran capabilities reinforced
```

**Disability Pathway:**

```
Service-connected injuries (physical, TBI, PTSD)
    |
    v
Disability rating process is slow and adversarial
    |
    v
Veterans cannot access VR&E until rating established (months to years)
    |
    v
Meanwhile, employment without accommodations fails
    |
    v
Veterans exit labor force or accept inappropriate positions
    |
    v
Long-term unemployment/underemployment becomes entrenched
```

## Systemic Failures

### Data and Accountability

| Failure | Impact |
|---------|--------|
| **No unified outcome tracking** | Cannot measure long-term career trajectories post-separation |
| **DOD-VA-DOL data silos** | Agencies cannot identify veterans who fall through cracks |
| **Underemployment unmeasured** | Policy focuses on unemployment rate, ignoring skills mismatch |
| **Program evaluation lacking** | Few programs subject to rigorous impact evaluation |
| **Success measured at separation** | TAP measures attendance and satisfaction, not 12-month outcomes |

### Institutional Design

| Failure | Impact |
|---------|--------|
| **DOD responsibility ends at separation** | Military has no incentive to invest in post-service outcomes |
| **VA focused on benefits, not employment** | Employment treated as secondary to healthcare and compensation |
| **DOL underfunded for veteran mission** | VETS budget is fraction of veteran employment challenge |
| **No single accountable entity** | Everyone is responsible means no one is responsible |
| **Nonprofit fragmentation** | Hundreds of veteran employment nonprofits compete for funding rather than coordinating |

### Cultural and Social

| Failure | Impact |
|---------|--------|
| **Military-civilian divide** | Less than 1% of population serves; civilian employers lack exposure to veterans |
| **Veteran identity transition** | Loss of military identity affects job search behavior and career decisions |
| **Spouse employment** | Military spouse unemployment (~22%) affects family economic stability |
| **Geographic constraints** | Military installations concentrated in specific areas; veterans cluster near bases |
| **Stigma around help-seeking** | Military culture discourages asking for employment assistance |
