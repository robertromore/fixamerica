# Veteran Employment: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative landscape for veteran employment spans federal employment protections (USERRA, VEVRAA), employer incentives (WOTC), transition programs (TAP), vocational rehabilitation (VR&E), and credentialing reform. This document proposes federal legislation, state model legislation, and regulatory reforms to address the structural barriers identified in the analysis. The approach prioritizes credential reciprocity, transition timeline extension, employer engagement reform, and data-driven accountability.

---

## Federal Legislation

### Bill 1: National Military Credential Reciprocity Act

**Purpose**: Establish federal standards requiring states to recognize military training and experience for civilian occupational licensing, with competency-based bridge programs for documented skill gaps.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "National Military Credential Reciprocity
Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that—
    (1) approximately 200,000 service members separate from the Armed
        Forces annually;
    (2) military training in many occupational specialties meets or
        exceeds civilian training standards;
    (3) state-by-state licensing requirements create unnecessary barriers
        to veteran employment, delaying career entry by 6 to 18 months;
    (4) studies by the RAND Corporation and other researchers demonstrate
        that military-trained professionals perform comparably or better
        than civilian-trained counterparts; and
    (5) states that have adopted military credential reciprocity report
        no increase in public safety incidents.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to—
    (1) establish minimum federal standards for state recognition of
        military training and experience;
    (2) create competency-based assessment frameworks for military-to-
        civilian credential translation; and
    (3) fund bridge programs for documented skill gaps.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
    (1) COVERED OCCUPATION.—The term "covered occupation" means any
        occupation requiring a state license, certification, or
        registration to practice.
    (2) MILITARY TRAINING.—The term "military training" means formal
        training, education, and practical experience obtained during
        service in the Armed Forces, as documented in military service
        records, training certificates, and the DD-214.
    (3) COMPETENCY-BASED ASSESSMENT.—The term "competency-based
        assessment" means an evaluation of an individual's demonstrated
        ability to perform occupational tasks, without regard to the
        source or duration of training.
    (4) BRIDGE PROGRAM.—The term "bridge program" means a targeted
        training course addressing specific documented gaps between
        military training and civilian licensure requirements.

SEC. 4. FEDERAL CREDENTIAL RECOGNITION STANDARDS.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Not later than 2 years after the date of enactment
    of this Act, the Secretary of Defense, in consultation with the
    Secretary of Labor and relevant state licensing authorities, shall
    publish competency maps for not fewer than 50 military occupational
    specialties with direct civilian equivalents.

(b) STATE COMPLIANCE.—
    (1) REQUIREMENT.—Not later than 3 years after the date of
        publication of the competency maps under subsection (a), each
        State shall—
        (A) accept military training as meeting licensure requirements
            for any covered occupation where the competency map
            demonstrates substantial equivalence; or
        (B) offer a bridge program of not more than 90 days duration
            for any documented skill gap.
    (2) NONCOMPLIANCE.—A State that fails to comply with paragraph (1)
        shall be ineligible for Federal workforce development grants
        under the Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act (29 U.S.C.
        3101 et seq.).

(c) DIGITAL CREDENTIAL PORTFOLIO.—The Secretary of Defense shall
    establish a digital credential portfolio system providing each
    separating service member with a comprehensive, verified record
    of military training, certifications, and experience in a format
    recognized by state licensing authorities.

SEC. 5. BRIDGE PROGRAM FUNDING.

(a) GRANTS.—The Secretary of Labor shall award grants to States for
    the development and operation of bridge programs under section 4(b).

(b) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated $50,000,000
    for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2031 to carry out this section.

SEC. 6. INTERSTATE COMPACT INCENTIVES.

(a) COMPACT GRANTS.—The Secretary of Labor shall award grants to
    States participating in interstate licensing compacts that include
    military credential recognition provisions.

(b) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated $25,000,000
    for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2031 to carry out this section.

SEC. 7. REPORTS.

(a) ANNUAL REPORT.—Not later than 1 year after the date of enactment
    of this Act, and annually thereafter, the Secretary of Labor shall
    submit to Congress a report on—
    (1) the number of veterans who have obtained civilian credentials
        through military training recognition;
    (2) the average time from separation to credential issuance;
    (3) state compliance with the requirements of section 4(b); and
    (4) any public safety incidents attributable to military credential
        recognition.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the most significant structural barrier to veteran employment by establishing federal minimum standards for state credential recognition. The competency-based approach ensures public safety while eliminating unnecessary retraining. The WIOA funding sanction provides meaningful enforcement leverage. The bridge program framework acknowledges that some gaps are genuine while preventing states from using "gap" claims to maintain full re-credentialing requirements.

**Potential Challenges**:
- Tenth Amendment arguments against federal preemption of state licensing
- Resistance from state licensing boards that view this as federal overreach
- Cost of developing 50+ competency maps with industry and state input
- Political difficulty of attaching WIOA funding conditions

**Refinements**:
- Phase implementation by occupation criticality (healthcare, transportation, emergency services first)
- Offer enhanced compliance incentives rather than only punitive sanctions
- Include licensing board representation in competency map development
- Create an appeal mechanism for states with legitimate public safety concerns

### Bill 2: Veteran Transition Enhancement Act

**Purpose**: Extend the military transition timeline to 18 months, expand SkillBridge, and establish post-separation career counseling.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Veteran Transition Enhancement Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. EXTENDED TRANSITION TIMELINE.

(a) AMENDMENT TO TITLE 10.—Section 1142 of title 10, United States Code,
    is amended—
    (1) by striking "not later than 90 days" and inserting "not later
        than 18 months"; and
    (2) by adding at the end the following:
    "(g) INDIVIDUALIZED TRANSITION PLAN.—
        (1) Not later than 18 months before the anticipated date of
            separation, each member shall be assigned a transition
            counselor who shall develop, in consultation with the
            member, an individualized transition plan that includes—
            (A) career assessment and goal-setting;
            (B) credential identification and translation;
            (C) employer engagement activities;
            (D) financial planning; and
            (E) family support coordination.
        (2) The transition counselor shall meet with the member not
            fewer than once per quarter until the date of separation,
            and monthly during the final 6 months before separation."

SEC. 3. SKILLBRIDGE EXPANSION.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—Section 1143 of title 10, United States Code, is
    amended by adding the following:
    "(f) SKILLBRIDGE FELLOWSHIPS.—
        (1) The Secretary of Defense shall ensure that not fewer than
            10,000 employer partners participate in the SkillBridge
            program by fiscal year 2029.
        (2) SkillBridge fellowships may be extended to 270 days for
            service members in occupational specialties with the
            lowest civilian employment rates.
        (3) No commander may deny a SkillBridge application solely
            on the basis of unit manning or operational tempo,
            except in cases of deployment or imminent deployment."

SEC. 4. POST-SEPARATION CAREER COUNSELING.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary of Veterans Affairs, in coordination
    with the Secretary of Labor, shall establish a post-separation
    career counseling program providing—
    (1) a dedicated career counselor for each veteran for 24 months
        following separation;
    (2) quarterly career progress assessments;
    (3) referrals to employment services, training programs, and
        employer networks; and
    (4) specialized support for veterans with service-connected
        disabilities, women veterans, and rural veterans.

(b) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated $100,000,000
    for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2031 to carry out this section.

SEC. 5. TRANSITION OUTCOME TRACKING.

(a) DATA SYSTEM.—The Secretary of Defense and the Secretary of Veterans
    Affairs shall jointly establish a data system that tracks employment
    outcomes for each separating service member for not fewer than 5
    years following separation.

(b) METRICS.—The data system shall track—
    (1) employment status;
    (2) occupation and industry;
    (3) earnings relative to military pay grade;
    (4) credential utilization;
    (5) underemployment indicators; and
    (6) program utilization.

(c) ANNUAL REPORT.—The Secretary of Defense shall submit to Congress an
    annual report on transition outcomes disaggregated by service branch,
    rank, military occupational specialty, gender, race, disability
    status, and geographic location.
```

**Explanation**: This bill transforms the transition from a compressed compliance exercise into a sustained process. The 18-month timeline allows for meaningful career planning, credential acquisition, and employer engagement. The SkillBridge expansion and commander restriction address the operational tension that currently limits participation. Post-separation counseling fills the gap between DOD's responsibility ending at separation and VA/DOL programs that veterans may not access for months.

**Potential Challenges**:
- DOD resistance to releasing service members 18 months before separation
- Cost of transition counselor staffing
- Commander culture change regarding SkillBridge approvals
- Interagency coordination for post-separation counseling

**Refinements**:
- Phase the 18-month requirement by allowing 12 months initially, expanding to 18
- Allow flexible implementation that does not require full-time transition activity for 18 months
- Include commander incentives (unit readiness credit for successful transitions)
- Build counselor capacity through nonprofit partnerships rather than federal hiring alone

### Bill 3: Veteran Employment Accountability Act

**Purpose**: Reform employer incentives, strengthen VEVRAA enforcement, and create data-driven accountability for veteran employment programs.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Veteran Employment Accountability Act
of 2026".

SEC. 2. WOTC REFORM.

(a) RETENTION INCENTIVE.—Section 51 of the Internal Revenue Code of
    1986 is amended to provide that for veteran employees—
    (1) the initial hiring credit shall be $1,200 (reduced from current
        levels);
    (2) a retention credit of $2,400 shall be available for each
        veteran employee retained for 12 months; and
    (3) an additional retention credit of $2,400 shall be available
        for each veteran employee retained for 24 months.

(b) SMALL BUSINESS ENHANCEMENT.—For employers with fewer than 500
    employees, the credits under subsection (a) shall be increased
    by 50 percent.

SEC. 3. VEVRAA ENFORCEMENT.

(a) AUDIT REQUIREMENT.—The Office of Federal Contract Compliance
    Programs shall audit not fewer than 10 percent of covered federal
    contractors annually for compliance with the Vietnam Era Veterans'
    Readjustment Assistance Act (38 U.S.C. 4212).

(b) TRANSPARENCY.—Each covered federal contractor shall publish
    annually—
    (1) the number of veteran employees;
    (2) veteran hiring rates;
    (3) veteran retention rates at 12 and 24 months; and
    (4) veteran employee satisfaction scores.

(c) PENALTIES.—A contractor found in willful noncompliance with
    VEVRAA requirements shall be subject to—
    (1) debarment from federal contracting for not less than 1 year; and
    (2) civil penalties of not more than $50,000 per violation.

SEC. 4. PROGRAM EVALUATION.

(a) INDEPENDENT EVALUATION.—The Secretary of Labor shall contract
    with an independent research organization to conduct rigorous
    impact evaluations of—
    (1) the Transition Assistance Program;
    (2) Jobs for Veterans State Grants;
    (3) VA Vocational Rehabilitation and Employment; and
    (4) the Work Opportunity Tax Credit for veterans.

(b) EVALUATION STANDARDS.—Each evaluation shall use randomized
    controlled trial or quasi-experimental methods with comparison
    groups.

(c) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated $20,000,000
    for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2031 to carry out this section.
```

**Explanation**: This bill shifts the incentive structure from quantity (initial hiring) to quality (retention). The WOTC reform makes the credit meaningful for long-term employment while the small business enhancement addresses the size disparity in veteran hiring capacity. VEVRAA enforcement strengthening replaces paper compliance with meaningful accountability. The evaluation requirement ensures that programs producing poor results can be identified and reformed.

**Potential Challenges**:
- Business opposition to VEVRAA audit increases and transparency requirements
- Revenue impact of WOTC restructuring (may need to be revenue-neutral)
- Resistance to rigorous program evaluation from agencies and programs that may score poorly
- Implementation capacity at OFCCP for increased auditing

**Refinements**:
- Phase VEVRAA audit increase over 3 years
- Include employer support resources alongside enforcement increases
- Make WOTC restructuring revenue-neutral by adjusting credit levels
- Publish evaluation results with improvement recommendations, not just findings

## State Model Legislation

### Model Military Credential Reciprocity Act

**Purpose**: State-level implementation of military credential recognition with competency-based assessment.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Military Credential Reciprocity Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

As used in this Act:
    (a) "Covered license" means any license, certification, or
        registration required by the State to practice an occupation.
    (b) "Military training" means education, training, and experience
        obtained during service in the United States Armed Forces.
    (c) "Substantial equivalence" means that military training covers
        at least 80 percent of the competencies required for a covered
        license.

SECTION 3. CREDENTIAL RECOGNITION.

    (a) Each licensing board in this State shall, within 180 days of
        enactment, review military training for substantial equivalence
        to the requirements of each covered license within its
        jurisdiction.
    (b) Where substantial equivalence is found, the board shall issue
        a covered license to an applicant who provides documentation
        of military training, subject only to payment of standard
        licensing fees and completion of a background check.
    (c) Where substantial equivalence is not found, the board shall
        identify specific competency gaps and offer a bridge program
        of not more than 90 days duration.

SECTION 4. EXPEDITED PROCESSING.

    (a) Applications from veterans and military spouses shall be
        processed within 30 days of receipt of a complete application.
    (b) No additional fees beyond standard licensing fees shall be
        charged for military credential recognition.

SECTION 5. REPORTING.

    Each licensing board shall report annually to the legislature on
    the number of military credential applications received, approved,
    denied, and referred to bridge programs.
```

**Explanation**: This model legislation provides a template that states can adopt or adapt. The 80% substantial equivalence threshold balances public safety with practical credential recognition. The 90-day bridge program limit prevents boards from creating de facto re-credentialing through excessively long gap-filling requirements.

**Adaptations**: States may adjust the substantial equivalence threshold, bridge program duration, and reporting requirements based on local conditions and occupational risk profiles.

## Regulatory Framework

### DOD Transition Assistance Program Reform

**Existing Authority**: 10 U.S.C. 1142-1144; VOW to Hire Heroes Act (P.L. 112-56)

**Draft Regulation**:

The Secretary of Defense shall amend DOD Instruction 1332.35 to:

1. Require individualized transition plans beginning 18 months before anticipated separation
2. Establish career track specialization pathways co-designed with industry partners
3. Mandate quarterly progress assessments with documented career milestones
4. Prohibit commanders from denying SkillBridge applications except for deployment or imminent deployment
5. Require transition counselor assignment with maximum caseload of 1:100
6. Establish post-separation outcome tracking for 5 years following separation

**Explanation**: Administrative implementation through DOD instructions can proceed faster than legislation and does not require congressional action. This regulation operationalizes the extended transition timeline within existing statutory authority, while congressional authorization of additional funding would support full implementation.

### VA VR&E Program Enhancement

**Existing Authority**: 38 U.S.C. Chapter 31

**Draft Regulation**:

The Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall amend 38 C.F.R. Part 21 to:

1. Reduce VR&E counselor-to-veteran ratio to 1:100 maximum
2. Establish an expedited enrollment track for recently separated veterans (within 24 months)
3. Allow concurrent VR&E and GI Bill benefits for veterans with 50%+ disability ratings
4. Create employer partnership agreements enabling direct placement from VR&E into SkillBridge-style fellowships
5. Require annual publication of VR&E outcome data disaggregated by demographic characteristics

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Commerce Clause**: Federal credential reciprocity standards are defensible under the Commerce Clause as regulation of interstate commerce in labor markets
- **Tenth Amendment**: States retain authority to regulate professions; federal approach uses funding conditions rather than direct preemption, which courts have generally upheld under *South Dakota v. Dole* (1987)
- **Spending Clause**: Conditioning WIOA funds on compliance with credential reciprocity standards follows established precedent
- **Due Process**: Veterans must still meet competency standards; the reform changes how competency is demonstrated, not whether it is required

### Preemption Questions

- Federal credential standards would establish a floor, not a ceiling; states may offer more generous recognition
- USERRA preemption of state employment law is well established
- VEVRAA enforcement applies only to federal contractors under existing federal authority
- State model legislation operates within state sovereignty over licensing

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- WIOA funding sanctions for credential reciprocity noncompliance
- OFCCP auditing for VEVRAA compliance
- DOL VETS for USERRA complaints
- Private right of action for credential recognition denial (state law)
- Congressional oversight through required annual reports

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Credential reciprocity competency maps reviewed and updated every 5 years
- WOTC veteran credits subject to 7-year reauthorization
- Independent program evaluations every 5 years with published results
- SkillBridge expansion targets reviewed annually and adjusted

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### National Military Credential Reciprocity Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Bridge program abuse | States could create 90-day bridge programs for every occupation, effectively maintaining full re-credentialing | High |
| Competency map gaming | Licensing boards could challenge competency maps to delay implementation | Medium |
| Documentation gaps | Veterans with incomplete military training records may be unable to demonstrate equivalence | Medium |
| Fee inflation | States could impose excessive fees for credential recognition processing | Low |
| Occupation reclassification | States could reclassify occupations to avoid covered occupation definitions | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address occupations without state licensing | Many veteran skills (leadership, project management) are not licensed | Legislation focused on regulated occupations |
| Limited to current MOS structure | Military occupational restructuring could create gaps | Static competency maps |
| Does not address civilian employer credential preferences | Employers may prefer civilian credentials even when military is equivalent | Cultural bias beyond regulatory reach |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Establish federal oversight board to review bridge program content and duration for reasonableness
2. Require annual state reporting on bridge program completion rates and employment outcomes
3. Create appeal mechanism for veterans whose credential applications are denied
4. Cap bridge program fees at standard licensing fee levels
5. Update competency maps every 3 years rather than 5 to keep pace with MOS changes
6. Include employer education component to address civilian credential preference bias

### Veteran Transition Enhancement Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Commander override | "Operational tempo" exceptions could swallow the rule on SkillBridge denials | High |
| Counselor quality variation | No standards for transition counselor qualifications | Medium |
| Post-separation counselor access | Rural veterans may lack access to counseling services | Medium |
| Data system privacy concerns | Comprehensive tracking could deter veteran participation | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Guard/Reserve coverage gaps | Part-time members have less access to extended transition | Different employment relationship |
| Spouse transition not addressed | Military spouses face parallel employment challenges | Narrow legislative scope |
| Does not mandate employer participation | Employer engagement remains voluntary | Political feasibility |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Define "operational tempo" exceptions narrowly with inspector general review of denials
2. Establish minimum qualifications and certification for transition counselors
3. Fund virtual career counseling for rural veterans
4. Implement opt-in consent model with strong privacy protections for data system
5. Create parallel transition support pathway for Guard/Reserve and military spouses

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Interagency coordination failure | Establish White House-level veteran employment coordination office |
| Congressional funding instability | Seek multi-year mandatory appropriations rather than annual discretionary |
| State implementation variation | Provide implementation templates and technical assistance |
| Nonprofit duplication | Create coordination platform and outcome standards for funded organizations |
| Employer engagement fatigue | Simplify participation requirements; demonstrate clear ROI |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- All programs subject to independent evaluation at 5-year intervals
- Competency maps updated every 3 years with industry and state input
- WOTC veteran credits reviewed for effectiveness at each reauthorization
- Annual congressional testimony from DOD, VA, and DOL on transition outcomes
- Data system privacy impact assessment conducted annually
