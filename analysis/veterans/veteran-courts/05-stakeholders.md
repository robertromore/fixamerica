# Veteran Treatment Courts: Stakeholders

## Those Directly Affected

### Justice-Involved Veterans

**Population**: An estimated 437,000 veterans are under criminal justice supervision at any given time--approximately 262,000 incarcerated and 175,000 on probation or parole (Bureau of Justice Statistics, 2024).

**Profile of VTC-Eligible Veterans**:

| Characteristic | Percentage/Detail | Source |
|----------------|-------------------|--------|
| Male | 92% | National Drug Court Institute, 2023 |
| Female | 8% | National Drug Court Institute, 2023 |
| Post-9/11 era | 45% | National Drug Court Institute, 2023 |
| Vietnam era | 20% | National Drug Court Institute, 2023 |
| Average age | 38 | National Drug Court Institute, 2023 |
| Combat deployment | 55% of VTC participants | Justice for Vets, 2024 |
| PTSD diagnosis | 60% of VTC participants | Justice for Vets, 2024 |
| Substance use disorder | 75% of VTC participants | Justice for Vets, 2024 |
| Homeless at intake | 20% | Justice for Vets, 2024 |

**Key Concerns**:

- Access to VTC programs (geographic, eligibility barriers)
- Quality and timeliness of VA treatment
- Impact of criminal record on future employment and housing
- Discharge status affecting VA eligibility
- Stigma of both veteran status and criminal record

### Families of Justice-Involved Veterans

**Impact**: Families bear the collateral consequences of veteran justice involvement:

| Impact | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Financial hardship | Loss of income during incarceration; legal costs |
| Relationship strain | PTSD-related behavior, substance abuse, domestic conflict |
| Child welfare | Children may enter foster care if sole veteran parent is incarcerated |
| Social stigma | Shame associated with family member's arrest and criminal record |
| Caregiver burden | Managing veteran's treatment compliance, court appearances, reentry |

### Victims of Veteran-Committed Crimes

**Concerns**: Victims and victim advocacy organizations have legitimate interests in VTC processes:

| Concern | VTC Response |
|---------|-------------|
| Public safety | Judicial supervision and graduated sanctions maintain accountability |
| Accountability | VTC completion requires engagement with treatment, not just avoiding detection |
| Restitution | VTCs typically require restitution as a condition of completion |
| Voice in process | Victim input varies by VTC; best practices include victim notification |
| Special concern: domestic violence | Controversy over whether DV cases should be diverted to VTC |

## Government Stakeholders

### Department of Veterans Affairs

**Role**: Primary treatment provider for VTC participants; operates VJO and HCRV programs.

**Key Offices**:

| Office | Function |
|--------|----------|
| Veterans Justice Programs (VHA) | Operates VJO and HCRV; coordinates VA-court interface |
| Mental Health and Substance Use Services | Treatment delivery for VTC participants |
| Homeless Programs | HUD-VASH, SSVF, and GPD for housing-unstable VTC participants |
| Disability Compensation (VBA) | Benefits for service-connected conditions |

**Institutional Interests**:

- Demonstrate that VA healthcare reduces incarceration and recidivism
- Expand VJO program and justify continued funding
- Manage healthcare demands from court-mandated treatment within existing capacity
- Ensure clinical decisions are not dictated by judicial timelines

### Department of Justice

**Role**: Funds VTCs through the Bureau of Justice Assistance (BJA); provides research support through the National Institute of Justice.

**Key Programs**:

| Program | Function | Funding (FY 2024) |
|---------|----------|-------------------:|
| SJIS Grants (BJA) | Direct VTC operational support | $25 million |
| Justice and Mental Health Collaboration | Training and technical assistance | $10 million (shared) |
| National Institute of Justice | VTC research grants | ~$2 million |

### State Court Systems

VTCs are created and operated within state judicial systems, making state courts the administrative home for most VTCs:

| Stakeholder | Role |
|-------------|------|
| State Supreme Courts | Authorize VTC establishment, set standards |
| Administrative Offices of Courts | Provide operational support, data collection |
| Local/county courts | House VTCs within existing court structures |
| VTC judges | Exercise judicial leadership and discretion |

### Prosecutors

**Role**: Prosecutors control case referrals to VTCs. Their cooperation is essential.

**Perspectives**:

| Position | Rationale |
|----------|-----------|
| Supportive prosecutors | VTCs reduce recidivism and caseloads; treatment addresses root causes |
| Reluctant prosecutors | Concerned about accountability, victim rights, public safety |
| Opposed to DV inclusion | Domestic violence cases require victim safety focus that may conflict with treatment model |

### Public Defenders

**Role**: Defense attorneys identify veteran clients and advocate for VTC referral.

**Key Function**: Public defenders are often the first to identify a client as a veteran and initiate the VTC referral process. In jurisdictions without VTCs, public defenders may present military service as a mitigating factor at sentencing.

## Peer Mentors

### Veteran Peer Mentors

Peer mentoring is a defining feature of VTCs that distinguishes them from other specialty courts. Peer mentors are veterans--typically volunteers--who are matched with VTC participants to provide support, accountability, and guidance.

| Metric | Detail | Source |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Estimated volunteer mentors nationwide | 8,000-12,000 | Justice for Vets, 2024 |
| Average mentors per VTC | 15-20 | Justice for Vets, 2024 |
| Paid vs. volunteer | ~90% volunteer | Justice for Vets, 2024 |
| Training requirements | Vary by court; Justice for Vets provides standardized training | Justice for Vets, 2024 |
| Burnout/turnover rate | ~30% annually | Justice for Vets, 2024 |

**Key Concerns**:

- Burnout from emotional demands of mentoring
- Lack of compensation limits recruitment and retention
- Training quality varies; some mentors are insufficiently prepared
- Personal triggers (mentors may have their own service-connected conditions)
- Boundary management between mentoring and friendship

## Those Who May Oppose VTC Expansion

### Victims' Rights Advocates

Some victims' rights organizations express concern that VTCs prioritize offender rehabilitation over victim safety and accountability, particularly in domestic violence cases.

### Prosecutors in Conservative Jurisdictions

In some jurisdictions, prosecutorial culture views diversion programs as "soft on crime," and elected prosecutors may resist VTC referrals for political reasons.

### Fiscal Conservatives (at State Level)

State legislators may resist funding VTCs when budgets are constrained, viewing them as additional government programs rather than cost-saving alternatives to incarceration.

### Equal Justice Advocates

Some civil liberties organizations argue that creating a separate court for veterans raises equal protection concerns--why should a veteran charged with the same crime as a non-veteran receive different treatment?

## Key Power Dynamics

| Actor | Power Source | Interest |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| VTC judges | Judicial authority, case-level discretion | Effective rehabilitation, reduce recidivism |
| Prosecutors | Case referral control | Public safety, accountability |
| VA (VJO program) | Treatment access, institutional resources | Serve justice-involved veterans, justify funding |
| Justice for Vets | National standards, training, advocacy | VTC expansion and quality |
| State legislatures | Funding authority, statutory framework | Balance cost, public safety, constituent values |
| Federal funders (BJA) | Grant funding | Evidence-based criminal justice reform |
| Peer mentors | Personal connection, moral authority | Fellow veteran recovery and support |
| Veterans service organizations | Political influence, volunteer infrastructure | Support members, community service |
