# Veteran Treatment Courts: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Legislative reform for Veterans Treatment Courts requires action at three levels: federal legislation to expand funding, standardize operations, and ensure VA-court coordination; state legislation to establish VTCs, set eligibility criteria, and provide sustainable funding; and regulatory changes to improve VA treatment access for court-referred veterans. This document provides draft legal text for the key reforms identified in [Solutions](07-solutions.md), organized by level of government and priority.

## Federal Legislation

### Veterans Treatment Court Expansion and Standardization Act

**Purpose**: Establish a permanent, formula-based federal funding program for VTCs, mandate VTC access in all federal judicial districts, expand VJO staffing, and create a national VTC data system.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Veterans Treatment Court
Expansion and Standardization Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) Approximately 600 Veterans Treatment Courts operate in
49 States and the District of Columbia, serving an estimated
35,000 to 40,000 veterans annually.
(2) An estimated 437,000 veterans are under criminal justice
supervision at any given time, the majority of whom do not
have access to a Veterans Treatment Court.
(3) Veterans Treatment Courts reduce recidivism by 25 to
50 percent compared to traditional criminal processing and
produce estimated savings of $2.50 to $5.00 per dollar
invested.
(4) Geographic coverage is uneven, with 82 percent of VTCs
located in urban and suburban areas while 33 percent of
veterans reside in rural communities.
(5) Nearly half of Veterans Treatment Courts lack a
dedicated Veterans Justice Outreach specialist from the
Department of Veterans Affairs.

SEC. 3. FEDERAL VETERANS TREATMENT COURTS.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--Not later than 3 years after the date
of enactment of this Act, each United States District Court
shall establish a veterans treatment court program, or an
equivalent veteran diversion track within an existing
specialty court, for veterans charged with offenses related
to substance use disorders, mental health conditions, or
both.

(b) ELEMENTS.--Each program under subsection (a) shall
include--
(1) systematic screening of all defendants for military
service history;
(2) clinical assessment for service-connected conditions
including post-traumatic stress disorder, traumatic brain
injury, and substance use disorders;
(3) individualized treatment plans incorporating
evidence-based modalities;
(4) judicial supervision with regular status hearings not
less frequently than biweekly during the initial phase and
monthly thereafter;
(5) coordination with Department of Veterans Affairs
healthcare services through a Veterans Justice Outreach
specialist;
(6) assignment of a trained veteran peer mentor;
(7) housing and employment support services;
(8) graduated sanctions and incentives; and
(9) dismissal or reduction of charges upon successful
program completion.

(c) ELIGIBILITY.--
(1) IN GENERAL.--A veteran shall be eligible for a
program under this section regardless of discharge
characterization if--
(A) the veteran is charged with an offense;
(B) a clinical assessment determines that the veteran
has a service-connected mental health condition,
traumatic brain injury, or substance use disorder
that contributed to the conduct underlying the
offense; and
(C) the veteran agrees to participate in a court-
supervised treatment plan.
(2) RISK ASSESSMENT.--For offenses classified as
felonies, eligibility shall be determined on a case-by-
case basis using a validated risk assessment instrument
approved by the Bureau of Justice Assistance.
(3) EXCLUSIONS.--A court may exclude a veteran from
participation if the court determines, based on the risk
assessment under paragraph (2), that participation would
pose an unacceptable risk to public safety. Sex offenses
against minors shall be excluded.

SEC. 4. STATE VETERANS TREATMENT COURT GRANTS.

(a) IN GENERAL.--The Attorney General, acting through
the Director of the Bureau of Justice Assistance, shall
award grants to States and units of local government for
the establishment, expansion, or improvement of veterans
treatment court programs.

(b) FORMULA ALLOCATION.--
(1) Of funds appropriated under this section, 70 percent
shall be allocated among the States by a formula based on--
(A) the veteran population of the State;
(B) the number of justice-involved veterans in the
State; and
(C) the extent of VTC coverage gaps in the State.
(2) The remaining 30 percent shall be awarded through
competitive grants, with priority for--
(A) rural jurisdictions;
(B) jurisdictions establishing new VTCs; and
(C) VTCs implementing domestic violence track
protocols, gender-responsive programming, or
racial equity initiatives.

(c) STATE MATCH.--As a condition of receiving a grant
under this section, a State shall provide matching funds
equal to not less than 25 percent of the grant amount.

(d) CONDITIONS.--As a condition of receiving a grant
under this section, a veterans treatment court shall--
(1) accept veterans regardless of discharge
characterization, subject to clinical eligibility;
(2) conduct an annual racial and gender equity audit
of referral, acceptance, completion, and outcome data;
(3) participate in the national data system established
under section 6; and
(4) comply with certification standards developed under
section 7.

(e) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.--There are authorized
to be appropriated to carry out this section $100,000,000
for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2031.

SEC. 5. VETERANS JUSTICE OUTREACH EXPANSION.

(a) IN GENERAL.--The Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall
increase the number of Veterans Justice Outreach specialists
to not fewer than 700 by not later than 3 years after the
date of enactment.

(b) ASSIGNMENT.--The Secretary shall ensure that--
(1) each veterans treatment court receiving federal
funding under this Act has access to a dedicated Veterans
Justice Outreach specialist; and
(2) remaining specialists are assigned to county jails,
state prisons, and other justice facilities with
significant veteran populations.

(c) TELEHEALTH.--In rural areas where in-person VJO
coverage is not feasible, the Secretary shall provide
telehealth-based VJO services to supplement in-person
coverage.

(d) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.--There are authorized
to be appropriated to carry out this section $90,000,000
for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2031.

SEC. 6. NATIONAL VETERANS TREATMENT COURT DATA SYSTEM.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--The Attorney General, in consultation
with the Secretary of Veterans Affairs and the National
Drug Court Institute, shall establish a national data
system for veterans treatment courts.

(b) REQUIRED REPORTING.--Each veterans treatment court
receiving federal funding under this Act shall report to
the data system not less frequently than quarterly on--
(1) participant demographics, including age, gender,
race, ethnicity, era of service, and discharge
characterization;
(2) clinical data, including diagnoses, treatment
modalities, and treatment completion;
(3) justice data, including offense type, program
completion, termination, and recidivism at 1, 2, and
3 years;
(4) outcome data, including employment, housing
stability, VA healthcare enrollment, and mental health
status at program exit; and
(5) equity data, including comparisons of referral,
acceptance, completion, and outcome rates by race,
gender, and discharge status.

(c) PUBLIC REPORTING.--The Attorney General shall publish
an annual report summarizing national VTC data, including
aggregate outcomes, equity analyses, and cost-effectiveness
estimates.

(d) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.--There are authorized
to be appropriated to carry out this section $15,000,000
for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2031.

SEC. 7. CERTIFICATION STANDARDS.

(a) DEVELOPMENT.--Not later than 18 months after the date
of enactment, the Attorney General, in consultation with
Justice for Vets and the National Drug Court Institute,
shall develop national certification standards for
veterans treatment courts based on the Ten Key Components
of Veterans Treatment Courts.

(b) REQUIREMENTS.--Certification standards shall include--
(1) minimum program elements consistent with section 3(b);
(2) judicial training requirements in military culture,
service-connected conditions, and trauma-informed practice;
(3) peer mentor training and supervision standards;
(4) victim notification and participation protocols;
(5) domestic violence track protocols for VTCs accepting
domestic violence cases;
(6) gender-responsive programming standards; and
(7) outcome benchmarks for program completion, recidivism,
and participant well-being.

(c) CONDITION OF FUNDING.--Beginning 3 years after the
date of enactment, receipt of federal funding under this
Act shall be conditioned on certification under this section.

SEC. 8. PEER MENTOR SUPPORT.

(a) STIPEND PROGRAM.--The Secretary of Veterans Affairs
shall establish a stipend program providing compensation
of not less than $5,000 and not more than $10,000 per year
to trained veteran peer mentors serving in veterans
treatment courts.

(b) TRAINING.--The Secretary, in coordination with Justice
for Vets, shall establish a national certification program
for veteran peer mentors, including training in--
(1) military cultural competency;
(2) substance use disorder and mental health awareness;
(3) motivational interviewing and active listening;
(4) boundary management and self-care;
(5) crisis recognition and referral; and
(6) confidentiality and ethical standards.

(c) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.--There are authorized
to be appropriated to carry out this section $25,000,000
for each of fiscal years 2027 through 2031.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Establishes a federal floor for veteran diversion access, ensuring geographic consistency across all federal districts. Eligibility is based on clinical need and veteran status, not discharge characterization or blanket charge exclusions
- **Section 4**: Creates the first formula-based VTC funding program, replacing the current competitive-only model that disadvantages smaller and rural jurisdictions. The 25% state match builds sustainability
- **Section 5**: Addresses the critical VJO staffing gap, doubling the specialist workforce to ensure every VTC has dedicated VA liaison support
- **Section 6**: Creates the national data infrastructure necessary for evidence-based VTC improvement and equity monitoring
- **Section 7**: Institutionalizes quality standards so VTC effectiveness does not depend on the commitment of individual judges
- **Section 8**: Professionalizes the peer mentor role, addressing the burnout and turnover that threatens a core VTC component

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Cost ($230M/year total authorization) | VTCs save $20,000-50,000 per participant compared to incarceration; investment pays for itself |
| State resistance to matching requirement | 25% match is modest; phased implementation allows states to budget |
| Prosecutorial resistance to expanded eligibility | Risk assessment safeguards ensure public safety; case-by-case felony evaluation preserves discretion |
| VA capacity to hire 360 additional VJO specialists | Phased 3-year hiring timeline; telehealth supplements in-person coverage |
| Judicial concerns about certification mandates | Certification developed with judicial input; 3-year transition period |

**Refinements**:

- **Stronger version**: Mandate VTC access in state courts serving counties with 250 or more veterans (not just federal districts)
- **Fallback version**: Make certification voluntary with funding incentives rather than mandatory
- **Addition**: Include explicit authorization for VTC participants to access VA community care when VA wait times exceed 14 days

### Veterans Treatment Court Eligibility Reform Act

**Purpose**: Eliminate discharge-status barriers to VTC participation and mandate case-by-case eligibility assessment rather than blanket charge-type exclusions.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Veterans Treatment Court
Eligibility Reform Act".

SEC. 2. ELIGIBILITY BASED ON CLINICAL NEED.

(a) DISCHARGE STATUS.--No veterans treatment court
receiving federal funding under the Veterans Treatment
Court Expansion and Standardization Act or the Stafford
J. English Justice for Incarcerating Servicemembers Act
shall exclude a veteran from eligibility solely on the
basis of the veteran's discharge characterization.

(b) CLINICAL CRITERIA.--Eligibility for a federally
funded veterans treatment court shall be based on--
(1) confirmation of military service;
(2) a clinical assessment documenting a mental health
condition, traumatic brain injury, or substance use
disorder that contributed to the conduct underlying
the charged offense; and
(3) agreement by the veteran to participate in a
court-supervised treatment plan.

(c) CHARGE-TYPE ASSESSMENT.--
(1) No federally funded veterans treatment court shall
impose a blanket exclusion based on offense category,
except that sex offenses against minors may be excluded.
(2) For offenses classified as felonies or involving
violence, eligibility shall be determined on a case-by-
case basis using a validated risk assessment instrument.
(3) Courts shall consider the nexus between the
veteran's service-connected condition and the charged
offense in making eligibility determinations.

SEC. 3. DOMESTIC VIOLENCE TRACK.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--Each federally funded veterans
treatment court that accepts cases involving domestic
violence shall establish a specialized domestic violence
track incorporating--
(1) mandatory lethality assessment at intake and at
regular intervals;
(2) a safety plan developed by a certified domestic
violence advocate in consultation with the victim;
(3) mandatory no-contact orders during the initial
phase of the program, with modification only upon
court finding that contact does not pose a safety risk;
(4) batterer intervention programming in addition to
PTSD and substance use treatment;
(5) court-funded victim advocacy and support services;
and
(6) victim notification of all significant program
events, including entry, status changes, violations,
and graduation.

(b) VICTIM INPUT.--The court shall provide the victim
with the opportunity to provide input on the veteran's
participation in the program at intake and at each
subsequent review hearing.

SEC. 4. EQUITY REQUIREMENTS.

(a) ANNUAL AUDIT.--Each federally funded veterans
treatment court shall conduct an annual equity audit
examining--
(1) referral rates by race, gender, and discharge
characterization;
(2) acceptance rates by race, gender, and discharge
characterization;
(3) completion rates by race, gender, and discharge
characterization; and
(4) post-program outcomes (recidivism, employment,
housing) by race and gender.

(b) CORRECTIVE ACTION.--If an equity audit reveals
statistically significant disparities in referral,
acceptance, or completion rates, the court shall develop
and implement a corrective action plan within 6 months.

(c) GENDER-RESPONSIVE PROGRAMMING.--Each federally
funded veterans treatment court shall--
(1) screen all participants for military sexual trauma;
(2) provide or arrange childcare during court
appearances and treatment sessions;
(3) offer gender-specific treatment groups or access
to community-based women's treatment programs; and
(4) recruit and train female veteran peer mentors.

SEC. 5. VA TREATMENT ACCESS FOR VTC PARTICIPANTS.

(a) PRIORITY SCHEDULING.--The Secretary of Veterans
Affairs shall ensure that veterans referred by a
veterans treatment court receive an initial treatment
appointment not later than 14 days after referral.

(b) COMMUNITY CARE.--If the Department of Veterans
Affairs is unable to provide the treatment required by
a veteran's court-ordered treatment plan within the
timeline established by the court, the Secretary shall
authorize community care under section 1703 of title 38,
United States Code.

(c) OTH DISCHARGE VETERANS.--Veterans with other-than-
honorable discharges participating in a veterans
treatment court shall be eligible for mental health and
substance use disorder treatment from the Department
under section 1720I of title 38, United States Code,
regardless of whether the discharge was related to the
condition being treated.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 2**: Eliminates the two most significant VTC access barriers--discharge characterization and blanket charge-type exclusions--replacing them with clinical need-based eligibility
- **Section 3**: Addresses the domestic violence controversy by creating a specialized track with robust victim safety protections, allowing DV cases to be heard in VTCs without compromising victim safety
- **Section 4**: Mandates equity monitoring and corrective action to address documented racial, gender, and discharge-status disparities in VTC access
- **Section 5**: Ensures that VTC judicial timelines are matched by VA treatment access, using community care as a backup when VA capacity is insufficient

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Prosecutorial resistance to felony eligibility | Case-by-case risk assessment preserves public safety; court retains discretion to exclude high-risk individuals |
| Victim advocate concerns about DV track | DV track includes mandatory lethality assessment, safety planning, no-contact orders, and victim services |
| VA resistance to 14-day treatment mandate | Community care authorization provides a safety valve when VA capacity is insufficient |
| Cost of equity audits and gender-responsive programming | Modest costs compared to overall VTC operations; can be funded through existing BJA grants |

## State Model Legislation

### Model Veterans Treatment Court Act

**Purpose**: Provide a template for state-level legislation establishing VTCs with standardized eligibility, victim protections, and sustainable funding.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Veterans Treatment
Court Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

As used in this Act:
(a) "Veteran" means a person who served in the active
military, naval, or air service and was discharged or
released therefrom, regardless of the characterization
of such discharge.
(b) "Service-connected condition" means post-traumatic
stress disorder, traumatic brain injury, substance use
disorder, military sexual trauma, or other mental health
condition that originated during or was aggravated by
military service.
(c) "Veterans treatment court" means a specialized court
program that diverts eligible veterans charged with
criminal offenses into court-supervised treatment for
service-connected conditions.

SECTION 3. ESTABLISHMENT.

(a) Each judicial district in this State shall, subject
to appropriations, establish a veterans treatment court
program for eligible veterans, or establish a veteran-
specific track within an existing specialty court.

(b) In judicial districts with insufficient veteran
caseload to sustain a standalone program, two or more
districts may establish a regional veterans treatment
court serving multiple jurisdictions.

SECTION 4. ELIGIBILITY.

(a) A defendant shall be eligible for a veterans
treatment court if:
(1) the defendant is a veteran as defined in Section 2;
(2) a clinical assessment determines that the defendant
has a service-connected condition that contributed to
the conduct underlying the charged offense; and
(3) the defendant agrees to participate in a court-
supervised treatment plan.

(b) Eligibility shall not be denied solely on the basis
of discharge characterization or offense category, except
that the court may exclude a defendant based on an
individualized risk assessment finding that participation
would pose an unacceptable risk to public safety.

(c) Sex offenses against minors shall be excluded.

SECTION 5. PROGRAM ELEMENTS.

Each veterans treatment court shall include:
(a) Clinical screening and assessment by qualified
professionals;
(b) Individualized treatment plans addressing service-
connected conditions;
(c) Coordination with the Department of Veterans Affairs
or, for veterans not eligible for VA care, with community
treatment providers;
(d) Regular judicial supervision with status hearings;
(e) Assignment of a trained veteran peer mentor;
(f) Graduated sanctions and incentives;
(g) Housing and employment support; and
(h) Victim notification and, where applicable, victim
participation protocols.

SECTION 6. DOMESTIC VIOLENCE PROTOCOLS.

A veterans treatment court that accepts domestic violence
cases shall implement:
(a) Lethality assessment at intake;
(b) A safety plan developed with the victim by a
certified domestic violence advocate;
(c) Mandatory no-contact orders during the initial
program phase;
(d) Batterer intervention programming; and
(e) Victim notification of all significant program events.

SECTION 7. COMPLETION AND RECORD.

(a) Upon successful completion of the veterans treatment
court program, charges against the veteran shall be
dismissed.

(b) Upon successful completion, the court record shall
be sealed and the veteran shall be entitled to respond
to inquiries regarding the arrest and prosecution as if
the arrest and prosecution had not occurred.

SECTION 8. FUNDING.

(a) There is established a Veterans Treatment Court Fund
in the State Treasury.

(b) The Fund shall consist of:
(1) appropriations by the Legislature;
(2) federal grants received for veterans treatment
court purposes; and
(3) any other funds designated for veterans treatment
court operations.

(c) The [State Administrative Office of Courts] shall
administer the Fund and allocate resources to judicial
districts based on veteran population and caseload.

SECTION 9. DATA COLLECTION.

Each veterans treatment court shall collect and report
annually to the [State Administrative Office of Courts]:
(a) Participant demographics;
(b) Offense types;
(c) Program completion and termination rates;
(d) Recidivism rates at 1 and 2 years post-entry;
(e) Employment and housing status at program exit; and
(f) Referral, acceptance, and completion rates by race,
gender, and discharge characterization.
```

**Adaptations**:

- States with existing VTCs can adopt the eligibility reform (Section 4) and domestic violence (Section 6) provisions without restructuring existing programs
- States without existing VTC legislation should adopt the full act
- The regional VTC provision (Section 3(b)) is critical for rural states
- Record sealing provisions (Section 7(b)) may require coordination with existing state expungement laws
- Funding provisions (Section 8) should be adapted to each state's budget process and court funding structure

## Regulatory Framework

### VA Priority Scheduling for VTC Referrals

**Existing Authority**: 38 U.S.C. Section 1710 (VA healthcare services); VA Secretary's discretion over scheduling priorities

**Draft Regulation**:

```text
DEPARTMENT OF VETERANS AFFAIRS

38 CFR Part 17 -- Medical

Subpart -- Priority Scheduling for Veterans Treatment
Court Participants

Sec. 17.XX1 Purpose.
This subpart establishes priority scheduling for veterans
referred by a veterans treatment court to ensure timely
initiation of court-mandated treatment.

Sec. 17.XX2 Definitions.
(a) "Veterans treatment court" means a court-supervised
program that diverts eligible veterans into treatment for
service-connected conditions as an alternative to
traditional criminal processing.
(b) "Court referral" means a referral from a veterans
treatment court to the Department for treatment services
as part of a court-supervised treatment plan.

Sec. 17.XX3 Scheduling Standard.
(a) Each VA medical center shall schedule an initial
treatment appointment for a veteran referred by a veterans
treatment court not later than 14 calendar days after
receipt of the referral.
(b) If the medical center is unable to meet the 14-day
standard, the medical center shall authorize community
care under section 1703 of title 38, United States Code.

Sec. 17.XX4 VJO Coordination.
The Veterans Justice Outreach specialist assigned to
each veterans treatment court shall serve as the
scheduling liaison, ensuring that court referrals
are processed and appointments confirmed within the
timeline established in Sec. 17.XX3.

Sec. 17.XX5 Reporting.
Each VA medical center shall report quarterly to the
Under Secretary for Health on--
(a) the number of VTC referrals received;
(b) the percentage meeting the 14-day scheduling
standard;
(c) the number of community care authorizations issued
under this subpart; and
(d) treatment completion rates for VTC participants.
```

**Explanation**: Codifies the priority scheduling commitment into binding regulation, creating an enforceable standard and a community care safety valve when VA capacity is insufficient.

### VJO Staffing and Assignment Standards

**Existing Authority**: 38 U.S.C. Section 7401 (VA personnel authority); VA Secretary's discretion over staffing allocation

**Recommended Regulatory Actions**:

- Issue VA directive establishing a staffing standard of not fewer than one VJO specialist per VTC
- Establish VJO performance metrics including VTC participant treatment initiation rates, program completion support, and benefits enrollment
- Require VA medical center directors to report annually on VJO staffing levels relative to local VTC caseloads
- Authorize telehealth-based VJO services for rural VTCs that cannot support in-person specialist coverage

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Spending Clause**: Federal VTC grants to states operate under Congress's spending power and are constitutionally well-established; conditioning grants on eligibility standards is permissible under *South Dakota v. Dole*, 483 U.S. 203 (1987)
- **Equal Protection**: VTCs have been upheld against equal protection challenges as rational classifications based on the government's unique relationship with veterans and the VA treatment infrastructure (e.g., *State v. Harris*, 2012; *People v. Farook*, 2017)
- **Due Process**: VTC participation is voluntary; veterans retain the right to decline diversion and proceed through traditional criminal processing
- **Separation of Powers**: Federal mandates on state court structure raise potential concerns; the legislation addresses this by using grant conditions rather than direct mandates
- **10th Amendment**: State VTC mandates must respect state sovereignty over criminal justice; the federal bill uses incentive grants rather than commandeering state courts

### Preemption Questions

- Federal VTC standards for federally funded courts do not preempt state authority over VTCs that do not receive federal funding
- VA healthcare eligibility rules (38 U.S.C. 101(2)) govern VA treatment access; state VTCs may set broader eligibility for court participation while VA eligibility determines treatment provider
- State record-sealing and expungement provisions for VTC graduates operate under state law and are not preempted by federal criminal records systems

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- BJA grant conditions create contractual enforcement: noncompliance with eligibility, equity, and data requirements results in loss of federal funding
- VA Inspector General oversight of VJO operations and treatment access compliance
- Congressional reporting requirements with facility-level and court-level data
- Annual equity audits with mandatory corrective action plans create self-enforcing accountability
- Justice for Vets certification provides an external quality assurance mechanism

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Veterans Treatment Court Expansion and Standardization Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Federal districts only | Mandates VTCs in federal districts but only incentivizes state courts, leaving most veterans in state systems without guaranteed access | High |
| Formula gaming | States could inflate veteran population or justice-involvement data to increase formula allocations | Medium |
| Certification delay | 3-year transition period allows noncompliant VTCs to receive funding without meeting standards | Medium |
| Community care escape valve | VA could satisfy 14-day treatment standard by referring to unavailable community providers | Medium |
| Peer mentor qualifications | Without rigorous certification standards, stipend positions could be filled with underqualified individuals | Low |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No mandate on state courts | Majority of veterans are processed through state courts; federal mandate reaches only a fraction | 10th Amendment limits on federal authority over state courts |
| 5-year authorization | Programs may lose funding after authorization period | Standard legislative practice; creates uncertainty |
| No enforcement against prosecutorial non-referral | Prosecutors can effectively nullify VTCs by refusing to refer cases | Prosecutorial discretion is constitutionally protected |
| Limited reach for incarcerated veterans | VTCs serve pretrial diversion; does not address 262,000 already incarcerated veterans | VTC model is designed for diversion, not post-conviction |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Add a "state court incentive" provision increasing federal match to 50% for states that mandate VTC access in all judicial districts
2. Require independent verification of state data used in formula allocation, with penalties for misrepresentation
3. Shorten the certification transition period to 2 years and require interim compliance milestones
4. Define the 14-day treatment standard to include confirmed appointment with a provider who has capacity to accept the patient, not merely a referral
5. Establish minimum training hours and competency assessments for peer mentor certification
6. Add a post-conviction diversion provision allowing incarcerated veterans to petition for transfer to VTC supervision for the remainder of their sentence

### Veterans Treatment Court Eligibility Reform Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Clinical assessment gatekeeping | Restrictive clinical criteria could effectively replicate discharge-status or charge-type exclusions | High |
| DV track underfunding | Mandate for DV protocols without dedicated funding could result in inadequate victim services | High |
| Equity audit without teeth | Corrective action plans have no external enforcement mechanism if courts fail to implement them | Medium |
| OTH treatment eligibility | Section 5(c) extends VA treatment to OTH veterans in VTCs but does not address OTH veterans outside VTCs | Medium |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Establish standardized clinical assessment protocols developed by an independent body (e.g., RAND or NDCI) to prevent gatekeeping through restrictive interpretation
2. Appropriate dedicated funding for DV track implementation, including victim advocacy positions, within the BJA grant program
3. Add a provision allowing BJA to reduce or suspend grant funding if a court fails to implement its corrective action plan within the specified timeframe
4. Expand OTH treatment eligibility to all justice-involved veterans, not only those in VTCs, under the VA's existing authority (38 U.S.C. 1720I)

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Judicial champion dependency | Certification standards and institutional structure reduce reliance on individual judges |
| VA-court information sharing barriers (38 U.S.C. 7332) | Develop standardized consent forms and data-sharing agreements compliant with VA privacy rules |
| Prosecutorial non-referral | Require VTC coordinators to screen all veteran defendants independently, not rely solely on prosecutorial referral |
| Rural access gaps | Regional VTC model with telehealth; transportation assistance funding |
| Peer mentor burnout | Stipend program, training, supervision, and manageable caseloads |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- All programs should include 5-year reauthorization requirements with mandatory GAO evaluation
- BJA should report annually to Congress on VTC coverage, outcomes, equity data, and cost-effectiveness
- An independent advisory committee of veterans, judges, clinicians, and researchers should review program effectiveness every 3 years
- National data system should publish annual public reports enabling independent analysis
