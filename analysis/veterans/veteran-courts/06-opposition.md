# Veteran Treatment Courts: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to Veterans Treatment Courts comes from several distinct directions: equal justice advocates who question whether veterans deserve preferential treatment, victims' rights advocates concerned about accountability, fiscal hawks questioning the cost of specialized courts, and some within the traditional criminal justice system who view diversion as insufficiently punitive. Understanding these objections is important both for addressing legitimate concerns and for designing VTCs that can withstand political and legal challenges.

---

## Equal Justice Concerns

### "Why Veterans and Not Others?"

**Argument**: Creating a separate court system for veterans violates the principle of equal justice under law. Other populations--first responders, sexual assault survivors, poverty-affected individuals--also have trauma histories that contribute to criminal behavior. Singling out veterans for preferential treatment is discriminatory.

**Who Makes This Argument**: ACLU (in some jurisdictions), legal scholars, public defense organizations, criminal justice reform advocates.

**Specific Concerns**:

| Concern | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Equal Protection | If two people commit the same crime but one is a veteran, different outcomes may violate the 14th Amendment |
| Race and class proxy | Veterans are disproportionately white and male; VTCs may exacerbate racial disparities in the justice system |
| Expansion problem | If veterans merit a separate court, why not a court for every trauma-affected population? |
| Resource competition | VTC funding could be used for drug courts and mental health courts that serve all populations |

**Response**: The legal foundation for VTCs rests on the government's unique relationship with veterans--the federal government created the conditions that led to their service-connected conditions. Additionally, the VA provides a built-in treatment infrastructure that does not exist for other populations, making veteran-specific diversion practically feasible in ways that are not replicable for other groups. Courts have consistently upheld VTCs against equal protection challenges, finding a rational basis for the classification (e.g., *State v. Harris*, 2012; *People v. Farook*, 2017).

## Victims' Rights Concerns

### Domestic Violence Cases

The most contentious VTC eligibility question concerns domestic violence. Approximately 8% of VTC participants nationally were charged with domestic violence (National Drug Court Institute, 2023), and 45% of VTCs exclude DV cases entirely (Justice for Vets survey, 2024).

**Arguments Against DV Inclusion in VTCs**:

| Argument | Source |
|----------|--------|
| Victim safety must be the primary concern | National Coalition Against Domestic Violence |
| VTCs prioritize offender rehabilitation over victim protection | Victim advocacy organizations |
| DV is about power and control, not just trauma response | DV research literature |
| Military culture of dominance may be reinforced by veteran-centric court | Feminist legal scholars |
| Lethality assessment and safety planning require DV-specific expertise | DV service providers |

**Arguments For DV Inclusion (with Safeguards)**:

| Argument | Source |
|----------|--------|
| PTSD-related domestic violence is qualitatively different from predatory abuse | Clinical research |
| Without treatment, PTSD-driven DV will recur; punishment alone does not address root cause | RAND, 2020 |
| VTCs with DV protocols can incorporate safety planning and victim services | Justice for Vets best practices, 2024 |
| Excluding DV cases sends the message that veteran DV is less important to address | Some victim advocates |

### Victim Notification and Participation

Not all VTCs have robust victim notification and participation protocols:

| Best Practice | % of VTCs Implementing | Source |
|--------------|----------------------:|--------|
| Victim notification of VTC entry | 75% | Justice for Vets survey, 2024 |
| Victim input in treatment plan | 40% | Justice for Vets survey, 2024 |
| Victim notification of graduation | 65% | Justice for Vets survey, 2024 |
| Restitution required | 80% | Justice for Vets survey, 2024 |
| No-contact orders enforced | 90% (in DV cases) | Justice for Vets survey, 2024 |

## Prosecutorial Resistance

### "Soft on Crime" Narrative

In some jurisdictions, prosecutors--particularly elected district attorneys--resist VTC referrals because diversion is perceived as lenient.

**Dynamics**:

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Elected prosecutors | Campaign on conviction rates and "tough on crime" messaging |
| Prosecutorial discretion | DA offices can simply decline to refer cases to VTC |
| Case-by-case gatekeeping | Even in jurisdictions with VTCs, individual prosecutors may resist referrals |
| Felony resistance | Many prosecutors refuse to refer felony charges to VTC |

**Impact**: In jurisdictions where prosecutors are uncooperative, VTCs struggle to maintain adequate caseloads and may serve only the least serious offenses--precisely those where diversion adds the least value.

### Accountability Concerns

Some prosecutors argue that VTC completion--which typically results in charges being dismissed or reduced--does not hold veterans sufficiently accountable for criminal conduct:

| Concern | VTC Response |
|---------|-------------|
| Charges dismissed after completion | VTC participants must complete 12-24 months of treatment, supervision, and compliance |
| No prison time | Treatment is more effective than incarceration at preventing recidivism |
| Perceived message to victims | VTCs should incorporate victim input and restitution |
| Public safety during program | Judicial supervision, graduated sanctions, and testing maintain accountability |

## Fiscal Opposition

### Cost Arguments

**At the State Level**: State legislators may view VTCs as an additional unfunded mandate:

| Argument | Counterargument |
|----------|-----------------|
| VTCs require staff, courtroom time, and resources | VTCs save money by reducing incarceration ($35,000-45,000/year per inmate vs. $5,000-12,000/year per VTC participant) |
| Federal grants are time-limited and uncertain | Grant dependency is a real concern; sustainable state funding models are needed |
| The VA should handle veterans, not state courts | The VA provides treatment; courts provide accountability and legal authority |
| Existing drug courts and mental health courts can serve veterans | General specialty courts lack military cultural competency and VA coordination |

**At the Federal Level**: Some members of Congress question whether BJA grants to VTCs represent the best use of criminal justice funding.

## Institutional Resistance

### Traditional Court Culture

Not all judges and court administrators embrace the specialty court model:

| Resistance Point | Rationale |
|-----------------|-----------|
| Judicial purists | Believe judges should adjudicate, not manage treatment programs |
| Case management burden | VTCs require significantly more judicial time per case than traditional dockets |
| Staff resistance | Probation officers and clerks must take on new roles in VTC teams |
| Court administrator concerns | VTCs complicate scheduling, courtroom allocation, and staffing |

### VA Institutional Limitations

While the VA is a crucial VTC partner, institutional limitations create friction:

| Limitation | Impact on VTCs |
|-----------|---------------|
| Clinical autonomy | VA clinicians may resist court-ordered treatment timelines that conflict with clinical judgment |
| Wait times | Court-mandated treatment must begin promptly; VA wait times may not accommodate |
| Resource competition | VTC participants compete for the same treatment slots as all VA patients |
| Data sharing | VA privacy rules (38 U.S.C. 7332) limit information sharing with courts |
| Scope of authority | VA can provide treatment but cannot enforce court orders |

## Ideological Opposition

### From the Left: Criminal Justice Abolition Framework

Some criminal justice abolitionists argue that VTCs, while well-intentioned, legitimize the broader carceral system by creating a "good" diversion path within a fundamentally unjust system.

**Arguments**:

- VTCs reinforce the idea that the justice system can be reformed rather than replaced
- By creating a preferential path for veterans (disproportionately white and male), VTCs make the justice system appear fairer while leaving its structural inequities intact
- Resources spent on VTCs could be directed toward community-based alternatives to policing and prosecution

### From the Right: Individual Responsibility Framework

Some traditional conservatives argue that VTC participation should not excuse criminal behavior:

- Veterans are adults who made choices; mental health conditions explain but do not justify criminal acts
- Treatment should be available through the VA without requiring court involvement
- VTCs may create incentives for veterans to seek diversion rather than taking responsibility

## Discharge Status Controversy

### OTH Discharge Exclusion

The exclusion of veterans with Other-Than-Honorable discharges from many VTCs is both an opposition argument and a systemic failure:

**Arguments for OTH Exclusion**:

| Argument | Source |
|----------|--------|
| OTH discharge indicates character/conduct issues unrelated to service | Some prosecutors and court administrators |
| VA cannot provide full treatment for OTH veterans | VA eligibility rules (38 U.S.C. 101(2)) |
| Integrity of VTC as "veteran" court requires honorable service | Some VTC judges |

**Arguments Against OTH Exclusion**:

| Argument | Source |
|----------|--------|
| Many OTH discharges result from PTSD/TBI symptoms--the very conditions VTCs treat | Department of Defense Task Force on OTH Discharges, 2020 |
| P.L. 115-271 expanded VA mental health access for OTH veterans | Federal law |
| Excluding OTH veterans punishes them twice: once with the discharge, again with denial of treatment | National Veterans Legal Services Program |
| States have independent authority to establish VTC eligibility | State sovereignty over court systems |

## Addressing Opposition: Proven Strategies

| Opposition | Strategy | Evidence |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| Equal justice concerns | Emphasize government's unique obligation; cite legal precedent | Multiple state courts have upheld VTC constitutionality |
| Victim safety (DV) | Implement robust DV protocols (safety planning, victim services, no-contact orders) | Justice for Vets DV best practices guide, 2024 |
| Prosecutorial resistance | Educate DAs on outcomes; invite to observe VTC sessions; build relationships | Jurisdictions that engage prosecutors see higher referral rates |
| Fiscal concerns | Present cost-benefit data; emphasize incarceration savings | RAND cost-effectiveness analysis, 2020 |
| VA capacity | Advocate for VJO expansion; develop community treatment partnerships | MISSION Act authorizes community care referrals |
