# Veteran Treatment Courts: Actions

## For Individuals

### Learn First

**Understand the Problem**

- [ ] Read [Current State](02-current-state.md) to understand VTC coverage, outcomes, and access gaps
- [ ] Read [Root Causes](04-root-causes.md) to understand why veterans enter the justice system due to service-connected conditions
- [ ] Learn how Veterans Treatment Courts differ from traditional criminal processing and from general drug courts and mental health courts
- [ ] Understand the role of PTSD, TBI, and substance use disorders in veteran justice involvement

**Know the Signs**

- [ ] Learn to recognize signs of service-connected conditions in veterans you know--PTSD symptoms (hypervigilance, anger, isolation), TBI effects (impulsivity, memory problems), and substance use disorders
- [ ] Understand that criminal behavior in veterans is often a downstream consequence of untreated service-connected conditions, not a character flaw
- [ ] Recognize that veterans with Other-Than-Honorable discharges may have been separated for conduct caused by the very conditions they need treatment for

**Know the Resources**

- [ ] Save the Veterans Crisis Line number: 988 (press 1)
- [ ] Locate your nearest VA medical center and Vet Center (see [Resources](09-resources.md))
- [ ] Determine whether your jurisdiction has a Veterans Treatment Court (use the Justice for Vets court finder at justiceforvets.org)
- [ ] Identify local Veterans Justice Outreach (VJO) specialists through your VA medical center

### Take Action

**If You Are a Justice-Involved Veteran**

- [ ] Ask your attorney whether your jurisdiction has a Veterans Treatment Court and whether you may be eligible
- [ ] Contact the Veterans Justice Outreach specialist at your nearest VA medical center, even before court proceedings begin
- [ ] If you have an Other-Than-Honorable discharge, know that some VTCs accept OTH veterans and that the VA provides mental health treatment to OTH veterans under P.L. 115-271
- [ ] Request a VA mental health evaluation documenting any service-connected conditions (PTSD, TBI, substance use disorder) that may be relevant to your case
- [ ] If no VTC exists in your jurisdiction, ask your defense attorney to present your military service and service-connected conditions as mitigating factors at sentencing

**If You Are a Family Member**

- [ ] Learn about VTCs and whether your veteran family member may be eligible for diversion
- [ ] Contact the VA Caregiver Support Line (1-855-260-3274) for resources specific to families of justice-involved veterans
- [ ] Support your veteran's participation in VTC programming (treatment compliance, court appearances, peer mentoring) without enabling avoidance of accountability
- [ ] Seek your own support--family counseling, Al-Anon, or veterans family support groups--to manage the stress of a family member's justice involvement
- [ ] If domestic violence is involved, contact the National Domestic Violence Hotline (1-800-799-7233) for safety planning regardless of VTC participation

**If You Are a Veteran Peer Mentor (or Want to Become One)**

- [ ] Contact your local VTC or Justice for Vets to volunteer as a peer mentor
- [ ] Complete peer mentor training through Justice for Vets, the American Legion, or your VTC's training program
- [ ] Maintain your own well-being: seek supervision, set boundaries, and access support if mentoring triggers your own service-connected conditions
- [ ] Advocate for peer mentor compensation in your VTC to reduce burnout and improve recruitment

**Contact Representatives**

- [ ] Call your U.S. senators and representative to support the Veterans Treatment Court Expansion Act and increased BJA SJIS grant funding
- [ ] Urge support for expanding VTC eligibility to include veterans with Other-Than-Honorable discharges
- [ ] Advocate for increased VJO specialist funding to ensure every VTC has a dedicated VA liaison
- [ ] Contact your state legislators to support state VTC funding and eligibility reforms
- [ ] Attend town halls and ask about veteran justice diversion policy

**Join the Movement**

- [ ] Join a Veterans Service Organization (American Legion, VFW, DAV, IAVA) that advocates for VTC expansion
- [ ] Volunteer as a VTC peer mentor if you are a veteran
- [ ] Follow organizations like Justice for Vets, NADCP, and your local VTC program
- [ ] Share information about VTCs to increase awareness and reduce stigma around justice-involved veterans
- [ ] Attend VTC graduation ceremonies (most are open to the public) to show community support

### Reduce Stigma

**In Your Community**

- [ ] Challenge the assumption that veterans who commit crimes are "bad apples" rather than people whose service-connected conditions went untreated
- [ ] Use person-first language: say "veteran involved in the justice system" rather than "criminal veteran"
- [ ] Share VTC success stories (with permission) to demonstrate that treatment-based diversion works
- [ ] Speak up when you hear arguments that VTCs give veterans "special treatment"--explain that the government created the conditions that led to justice involvement and has an obligation to address them
- [ ] Recognize that VTCs are about accountability through treatment, not about letting veterans off the hook

## For Communities

### Build Local Support

**Establish or Support a Veterans Treatment Court**

- [ ] If your jurisdiction does not have a VTC, organize a coalition of judges, prosecutors, defense attorneys, VA officials, and veterans to explore establishing one
- [ ] Contact Justice for Vets for technical assistance and startup support
- [ ] Apply for BJA SJIS grants to fund VTC operations
- [ ] Partner with the local VA medical center to secure VJO support for the VTC
- [ ] Recruit veteran peer mentors from local VSO posts and veteran community organizations

**Support Existing VTCs**

- [ ] Organize peer mentor recruitment drives through local VSO chapters, churches, and community organizations
- [ ] Provide transportation assistance for VTC participants who live far from the courthouse or VA facility
- [ ] Offer employment opportunities to VTC graduates through local businesses committed to hiring veterans
- [ ] Provide meeting space for VTC peer support groups and treatment sessions
- [ ] Attend VTC sessions (most are open to observers) to understand how they work and advocate for them

**Employer Actions**

- [ ] Adopt hiring policies that do not automatically disqualify applicants with criminal records, particularly for offenses related to service-connected conditions
- [ ] Partner with local VTCs to provide employment opportunities for participants and graduates
- [ ] Offer flexible scheduling for employees participating in VTC programs who need time for treatment and court appearances
- [ ] Train managers to recognize veteran-specific challenges and respond with support rather than termination

**Faith-Based Communities**

- [ ] Offer meeting space for VTC peer support groups
- [ ] Train clergy in recognizing veteran justice involvement as a consequence of service-connected conditions and providing appropriate referrals
- [ ] Volunteer as community mentors or support providers for VTC participants
- [ ] Provide nonjudgmental support that treats justice involvement as a symptom of untreated conditions, not a moral failure

### Local Government Actions

- [ ] Establish or expand Veterans Treatment Courts in your jurisdiction with dedicated local funding
- [ ] Train local law enforcement in veteran identification during encounters and de-escalation techniques for veterans in crisis
- [ ] Include veteran justice involvement data in community health needs assessments
- [ ] Ensure that local public defender offices screen all clients for military service and VTC eligibility
- [ ] Fund community health workers specifically for veteran justice outreach

## For Organizations

### Veterans Service Organizations

- [ ] Establish or expand peer mentor programs at local VTCs
- [ ] Train post and chapter leadership on VTC operations and how to refer justice-involved veterans
- [ ] Advocate legislatively for expanded VTC funding, eligibility, and VJO staffing at both federal and state levels
- [ ] Provide emergency assistance (housing deposits, transportation, clothing) to VTC participants through post-level programs
- [ ] Include VTC success stories in organizational publications to build awareness and reduce stigma

### Bar Associations and Legal Organizations

- [ ] Develop CLE courses on VTC practice, veteran client identification, and military cultural competency for attorneys
- [ ] Train public defenders and criminal defense attorneys to screen clients for military service and initiate VTC referrals
- [ ] Provide pro bono legal services for discharge upgrades that would make veterans eligible for VTC participation and VA treatment
- [ ] Advocate for state legislation establishing or expanding VTCs

### Healthcare Organizations

- [ ] Screen all patients for military service history using the standard screening question: "Have you ever served in the military?"
- [ ] Train providers in military cultural competency through PsychArmor Institute or similar programs
- [ ] Establish referral pathways to VA SUD and mental health treatment for veteran patients who are justice-involved
- [ ] Participate in VA Community Care Network to provide timely treatment to VTC-referred veterans when VA capacity is insufficient

### Criminal Justice Organizations

- [ ] Support VTC establishment and expansion in your jurisdiction
- [ ] Train prosecutors and defense attorneys on VTC outcomes and referral procedures
- [ ] Implement veteran identification screening at booking, pretrial, and sentencing stages
- [ ] Develop prosecution office policies encouraging VTC referrals for eligible veterans
- [ ] Connect incarcerated veterans with VA VJO and HCRV specialists before release

## For Policymakers

### Federal Level

- [ ] Increase BJA SJIS grant funding from $25 million to $100 million annually with formula-based allocation
- [ ] Fund 360 additional VJO specialists to ensure every VTC has a dedicated VA liaison
- [ ] Pass legislation requiring VTC access in all 94 federal judicial districts
- [ ] Require all federally funded VTCs to accept veterans regardless of discharge characterization (when clinical criteria are met)
- [ ] Establish a national VTC data system with standardized outcome metrics
- [ ] Fund peer mentor stipend programs through VA Veterans Justice Programs
- [ ] Mandate annual racial and gender equity audits for federally funded VTCs

### State Level

- [ ] Enact state legislation authorizing and funding VTCs in every judicial district
- [ ] Provide dedicated state appropriations for VTC operations rather than relying solely on federal grants
- [ ] Expand VTC eligibility to include OTH discharge veterans and allow case-by-case felony eligibility
- [ ] Establish DV-track protocols with victim safety protections for VTCs that accept domestic violence cases
- [ ] Fund regional VTC models for rural jurisdictions that lack the caseload for standalone courts
- [ ] Require veteran identification screening at all criminal justice system entry points

### Local Level

- [ ] Fund local VTC operations through county budgets to reduce grant dependency
- [ ] Train law enforcement in veteran identification and crisis intervention
- [ ] Establish veteran reentry support programs providing housing, employment, and continuing mentorship for VTC graduates
- [ ] Ensure local public defender offices have veteran client screening protocols
- [ ] Include VTC outcome data in local criminal justice planning

## Measuring Progress

### Individual Impact

- Track whether justice-involved veterans you know are connected to VTCs or other diversion programs
- Monitor whether your local VTC exists, is adequately funded, and is serving veterans effectively
- Observe whether awareness of VTCs and justice-involved veteran issues is increasing in your community

### Community Impact

| Metric | How to Measure |
|--------|---------------|
| Local VTC existence and caseload | Contact local court administration |
| VTC completion rate | Request data from VTC coordinator |
| Veteran identification at booking | Inquire with local law enforcement |
| VJO specialist availability | Contact local VA medical center |
| Peer mentor recruitment | Contact local VTC or VSO posts |

### National Impact

| Metric | Source | Current | Target |
|--------|--------|---------|--------|
| Total VTCs nationwide | Justice for Vets | ~600 (2024) | 900 by 2030 |
| Veterans served annually | NDCI | 35,000-40,000 | 75,000 by 2030 |
| VJO specialists | VA | 340 (FY 2024) | 700 by 2030 |
| VTC recidivism rate | NDCI | 18-22% | <12% by 2030 |
| VTCs accepting OTH veterans | Justice for Vets | 45% | 95% by 2030 |
