# Veteran Treatment Courts: Solutions

## Overview

Solutions for veteran treatment courts must address five core challenges: geographic coverage gaps, eligibility restrictions that exclude veterans in need, funding sustainability, VA capacity limitations, and equity concerns. The proposals below are organized from the most immediately achievable to the most structurally ambitious.

---

## Coverage Expansion

### Mandate VTC Access in All Federal Districts

**Current Problem**: VTC coverage is concentrated in urban areas; rural veterans in jurisdictions without VTCs face traditional criminal processing.

**Proposal**: Require every federal judicial district to establish a veteran diversion track, either as a standalone VTC or as a veteran-specific track within an existing specialty court. For state courts, provide incentive grants conditioned on VTC establishment.

**Implementation**:

| Component | Federal Courts | State Courts |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| Authority | Judicial Conference of the United States directive | Incentive grants from BJA |
| Coverage target | All 94 federal districts | Counties with 500+ veterans |
| Timeline | 24 months | 36 months |
| Funding | Federal judiciary budget | BJA SJIS grants + state match |

### Regional VTC Model for Rural Areas

**Current Problem**: Rural counties lack the caseload and resources to sustain a standalone VTC.

**Proposal**: Create multi-county regional VTCs that serve rural areas through a combination of in-person sessions at rotating locations and telehealth-based treatment and monitoring.

**Design**:

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Structure | One VTC judge covers 3-5 rural counties |
| Court sessions | Monthly in-person at county seat; biweekly telehealth check-ins |
| Treatment | VA telehealth for mental health and SUD; in-person for residential |
| Peer mentoring | Veterans volunteer from all covered counties; virtual mentoring supplement |
| Transportation | Federal grant funding for participant transportation assistance |

### Telehealth Integration

**Current Problem**: COVID-era telehealth expansion improved VTC access, but its continuation is uncertain.

**Proposal**: Permanently authorize telehealth for VTC court appearances, treatment sessions, and peer mentoring contacts. VA telehealth infrastructure should be leveraged for VTC treatment delivery.

## Eligibility Reform

### Expand Eligibility to All Discharge Types

**Current Problem**: 55% of VTCs exclude veterans with Other-Than-Honorable discharges, despite evidence that many OTH discharges result from service-connected conditions (DoD Task Force on OTH Discharges, 2020).

**Proposal**: Require VTCs receiving federal funding to accept veterans regardless of discharge characterization, provided the veteran meets clinical criteria (service-connected mental health or substance use condition).

**Rationale**:

| Factor | Analysis |
|--------|----------|
| Legal authority | P.L. 115-271 already expanded VA mental health access for OTH veterans; VTCs should match |
| Clinical need | OTH veterans have equal or greater need for treatment-based diversion |
| Justice equity | Excluding OTH veterans punishes them for conduct that may itself be a symptom of service-connected conditions |
| Cost | Minimal, as OTH veterans can access VA mental health services under existing authority |

### Expand to Include Felony Offenses

**Current Problem**: 75% of VTCs exclude violent felonies and 90% exclude sex offenses. While some exclusions are appropriate, broad felony exclusion means VTCs serve primarily those with minor offenses who may not benefit as much from intensive intervention.

**Proposal**: Allow VTCs to accept felony cases on a case-by-case basis, using validated risk assessment tools (such as the Level of Service/Case Management Inventory) and clinical evaluation, rather than blanket charge-based exclusion.

**Safeguards**:

| Safeguard | Purpose |
|-----------|---------|
| Validated risk assessment | Ensure public safety is not compromised |
| Victim consultation | Victim input on diversion appropriateness |
| Enhanced judicial supervision | More frequent court contact for felony participants |
| Extended program duration | 18-24 months for felony cases (vs. 12-18 for misdemeanor) |
| Automatic disqualification | Sex offenses against minors remain excluded |

### Domestic Violence Track with Specialized Protocols

**Current Problem**: Domestic violence cases present legitimate safety concerns that make blanket inclusion or exclusion both problematic.

**Proposal**: Create a DV-specific track within VTCs that incorporates both VTC treatment principles and domestic violence best practices:

| Component | VTC Standard | DV-Track Modification |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------|
| Victim notification | Optional | Mandatory |
| Safety planning | General | DV-specific safety plan with victim advocate |
| No-contact orders | Discretionary | Mandatory during initial phase |
| Treatment focus | PTSD/SUD | PTSD/SUD + batterer intervention program |
| Victim services | Referral | Court-funded victim advocacy and support |
| Lethality assessment | Not required | Required at intake and periodically |

## Funding Sustainability

### Dedicated Federal Funding Stream

**Current Problem**: Most VTCs depend on competitive, time-limited BJA grants. When grants expire, courts may lose staff, reduce services, or close.

**Proposal**: Establish a permanent, formula-based federal funding program for VTCs, administered by BJA, that provides baseline operational support based on veteran population and caseload.

| Feature | Current (Grant-Based) | Proposed (Formula-Based) |
|---------|----------------------|-------------------------|
| Funding mechanism | Competitive grants, 3-year cycles | Formula allocation based on veteran population |
| Predictability | Uncertain renewal | Guaranteed baseline |
| Amount | $25 million (FY 2024) | $100 million/year (proposed) |
| Distribution | Application-based; favors sophisticated applicants | Automatic to qualifying VTCs |
| State match | Not required | 25% state match required |

### State Funding Mandates

**Proposal**: Encourage states to appropriate dedicated VTC funding through matching grant incentives--federal funding contingent on state funding commitment.

### Peer Mentor Compensation

**Current Problem**: Most peer mentors are unpaid volunteers, leading to high turnover (30% annually) and recruitment difficulties (Justice for Vets, 2024).

**Proposal**: Provide stipends or part-time compensation for trained peer mentors through VA Veterans Justice Programs funding.

| Current | Proposed |
|---------|----------|
| ~90% volunteer, unpaid | $5,000-10,000/year stipend per active mentor |
| High turnover, limited recruitment | Improved retention and recruitment |
| No standardized training compensation | Training hours compensated |

## VA Capacity and Coordination

### Dedicated VJO at Every VTC

**Current Problem**: Only 60% of VTCs have a dedicated VJO specialist. The remainder share VJO coverage with jails and other courts (VA Veterans Justice Programs, FY 2024).

**Proposal**: Expand the VJO program from 340 to 700 specialists, ensuring at least one dedicated VJO per VTC plus coverage for jails and reentry.

| Metric | Current | Proposed |
|--------|--------:|--------:|
| VJO specialists | 340 | 700 |
| VJO-to-VTC ratio | 0.57 | 1.0+ |
| Estimated annual cost | $45 million | $90 million |
| Coverage | 60% of VTCs have dedicated VJO | 100% |

### Priority Access for Court-Mandated Treatment

**Current Problem**: VA mental health and substance abuse treatment wait times (30-45 days for new patients) conflict with court-mandated treatment timelines.

**Proposal**: Establish a priority scheduling tier for VTC-referred veterans, ensuring first appointments within 14 days of referral. This does not require creating new slots--it uses the VA's existing priority scheduling authority.

**Alternative**: When VA capacity is insufficient, authorize community care referrals under the MISSION Act for VTC-mandated treatment, with VA coordination and oversight.

### Residential Treatment Beds

**Current Problem**: Many VA Medical Centers have limited residential treatment capacity for substance use disorders and PTSD. VTC participants who need residential treatment may face waitlists.

**Proposal**: Designate a portion of VA residential treatment beds (5%) for VTC referrals, ensuring availability for court-mandated intensive treatment.

## Standardization and Quality

### National VTC Data System

**Current Problem**: There is no standardized national data collection system for VTCs. Each court tracks its own outcomes (or does not), making cross-court comparison and national evaluation impossible.

**Proposal**: Establish a national VTC data system, administered by the National Drug Court Institute in partnership with BJA, requiring all federally funded VTCs to report standardized metrics.

**Required Metrics**:

| Category | Metrics |
|----------|---------|
| Demographics | Age, gender, race, era of service, discharge type |
| Clinical | Diagnoses, treatment modalities, treatment completion |
| Justice | Charge type, program completion, recidivism (1, 2, 3 years) |
| Outcomes | Employment, housing, VA enrollment, mental health status |
| Equity | Racial and gender outcome comparisons |

### VTC Certification Standards

**Proposal**: Develop a voluntary national certification program for VTCs, based on the Justice for Vets Ten Key Components, with peer review and outcome benchmarks. Certification would be required for federal funding eligibility.

## Equity Reforms

### Racial Equity Audit

**Current Problem**: VTCs may disproportionately serve white male veterans while underserving Black, Hispanic, and other minority veterans (National Drug Court Institute, 2023).

**Proposal**: Require annual equity audits of all federally funded VTCs, examining:

| Dimension | Analysis |
|-----------|----------|
| Referral rates | Are minority veterans referred to VTC at the same rate as white veterans? |
| Acceptance rates | Are minority veterans accepted into VTC at the same rate? |
| Completion rates | Are minority veterans completing VTC at the same rate? |
| Outcome disparities | Are recidivism, employment, and housing outcomes equitable? |
| Discharge type barriers | Are OTH exclusions disproportionately affecting minority veterans? |

### Gender-Responsive Programming

**Current Problem**: Women veterans represent 8% of VTC participants but 10% of justice-involved veterans (National Drug Court Institute, 2023; Bureau of Justice Statistics, 2024). Women veterans face distinct issues (Military Sexual Trauma, childcare, gender-specific treatment needs) that many VTCs are not equipped to address.

**Proposal**: Require VTCs to develop gender-responsive programming, including:

- Screening for Military Sexual Trauma
- Childcare support during court appearances and treatment sessions
- Women-specific treatment groups (or access to community-based women's programs)
- Female peer mentors (currently rare)
- Trauma-informed court practices that account for gender-specific trauma

## Prevention and Early Intervention

### Justice-Involvement Screening at VA

**Proposal**: Integrate criminal justice risk screening into VA primary care and mental health intake, allowing early intervention before an arrest occurs.

### Military-to-Civilian Transition Enhancement

**Proposal**: Expand Transition Assistance Program (TAP) to include criminal justice risk assessment and prevention, identifying separating service members at elevated risk and connecting them to VA services before crisis occurs.

### Law Enforcement Veteran Identification Training

**Proposal**: Provide training to law enforcement agencies on veteran identification during encounters, enabling officers to connect veterans to crisis services rather than arresting them for symptoms of service-connected conditions.

| Component | Implementation |
|-----------|---------------|
| Training curriculum | Developed by Justice for Vets with law enforcement input |
| Delivery | Integrated into police academy training and in-service training |
| Content | Veteran identification, PTSD recognition, de-escalation, VA referral |
| Funding | BJA Community Oriented Policing Services (COPS) grants |
