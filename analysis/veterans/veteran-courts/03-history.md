# Veteran Treatment Courts: History

## Overview

Veterans Treatment Courts are a 21st-century innovation, but the problem they address--veterans entangled in the criminal justice system due to service-related conditions--is as old as American warfare. The VTC movement emerged from the convergence of three historical streams: the specialty court movement that began with drug courts in 1989, the post-9/11 surge in veteran mental health and substance abuse crises, and a growing recognition that the traditional criminal justice system was failing a population that had served the country.

---

## Veterans and Criminal Justice: The Long History (Pre-2000)

### Early Patterns

- Veteran involvement in crime following military service has been documented since the Revolutionary War, though it was rarely analyzed as a systemic issue
- After the Civil War, Union veterans were disproportionately represented in state prisons and poorhouses, a pattern attributed to war injuries, alcoholism, and opium addiction from wartime medical treatment (Skocpol, *Protecting Soldiers and Mothers*, 1992)
- No distinction was made in the justice system between veterans and civilians; military service was irrelevant to criminal proceedings

### Post-World War II

- The GI Bill (1944) provided economic supports that may have reduced criminal justice involvement for WWII veterans by addressing social determinants (education, housing, employment)
- Korean War veterans received fewer supports, and studies later found elevated rates of alcohol-related offenses among Korean War combat veterans (Kulka et al., *Trauma and the Vietnam War Generation*, 1990)

### Vietnam Era

- Vietnam veterans became the first generation widely recognized as having service-connected behavioral issues that contributed to criminal justice involvement
- PTSD was not a recognized diagnosis until 1980 (DSM-III); before that, Vietnam veterans' behavioral symptoms were treated as individual failures rather than service-connected conditions
- VA Vet Centers (established 1979) provided readjustment counseling but had no formal connection to the criminal justice system
- Veteran incarceration rates peaked during the Vietnam generation's criminal justice involvement, with an estimated 250,000+ incarcerated veterans in the 1990s (Bureau of Justice Statistics, "Veterans in State and Federal Prison," 2000)

### The Gap: No Systematic Approach

Throughout the 20th century, the criminal justice system had no mechanism to:

- Identify defendants as veterans
- Assess whether criminal behavior was connected to military service
- Divert veterans into treatment for service-connected conditions
- Coordinate with the VA for treatment and support

## The Specialty Court Revolution (1989-2007)

### Drug Courts

- The first drug court was established in Miami-Dade County, Florida in 1989 by Judge Herbert Klein
- Drug courts diverted non-violent drug offenders into treatment programs under judicial supervision, rather than incarcerating them
- By 2007, over 2,100 drug courts operated nationwide, with consistent evidence of reduced recidivism (20-35% reduction) and cost savings (National Association of Drug Court Professionals, 2007)
- The drug court model established key principles that VTCs would later adopt: judicial leadership, treatment integration, graduated sanctions, peer support, and team-based case management

### Mental Health Courts

- The first mental health courts appeared in the late 1990s (Broward County, Florida, 1997; King County, Washington, 1999)
- Mental health courts diverted defendants with serious mental illness into treatment as an alternative to incarceration
- By 2007, approximately 250 mental health courts operated nationwide (Council of State Governments, 2007)

### Veteran Recognition Gap

Despite the growth of specialty courts, no court specifically addressed the intersection of military service, combat trauma, and criminal behavior. Veterans were sometimes routed into drug courts or mental health courts, but these programs did not address military-specific factors:

- Military culture and identity
- Combat-specific trauma
- VA healthcare eligibility and navigation
- Peer connection with fellow veterans
- Service-connected conditions not captured by civilian diagnostic frameworks

## The Birth of Veterans Treatment Courts (2008)

### Buffalo, New York: The First VTC

In January 2008, Judge Robert Russell of Buffalo City Court established the nation's first Veterans Treatment Court in Erie County, New York. The court emerged from Judge Russell's observation that a growing number of defendants in his drug court were veterans of Iraq and Afghanistan who had:

- Service-connected PTSD and TBI
- Substance abuse that began during or after military service
- No connection to VA healthcare or benefits
- Distinct treatment needs that civilian-oriented drug courts were not addressing

**Key Innovations**:

| Innovation | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Veteran identification | Systematic screening of all defendants for military service |
| VA integration | Partnership with the Buffalo VA Medical Center for treatment |
| Peer mentoring | Trained veteran volunteers providing one-on-one support |
| Military cultural competency | Court staff and treatment providers trained in military culture |
| Holistic approach | Addressing housing, employment, education, and benefits alongside treatment |

### Early Results

Judge Russell's Buffalo VTC demonstrated outcomes that captured national attention:

- 75% program completion rate in the first three years
- Less than 10% recidivism among graduates (compared to 35-40% for traditionally processed veterans)
- Participants connected to VA healthcare, housing, and education benefits
- Model attracted visits from judges and court administrators nationwide (Russell, "Veterans Treatment Court: A Proactive Approach," 2009)

| 2015 | 400 | Justice for Vets historical data |

### Factors Driving Expansion

1. **Post-9/11 veteran crisis**: The wars in Iraq and Afghanistan produced a generation of veterans with high rates of PTSD, TBI, and substance use disorders. As these veterans returned home, criminal justice involvement increased, creating political urgency.

2. **Bipartisan appeal**: VTCs appealed to both conservative values (supporting the military, public safety, fiscal responsibility) and liberal values (treatment over incarceration, addressing root causes, social justice).

3. **Justice for Vets**: The National Association of Drug Court Professionals established Justice for Vets in 2010 as a dedicated initiative to promote and support VTC development. Justice for Vets provided training, technical assistance, and a national framework.

4. **VA engagement**: The VA established the Veterans Justice Outreach (VJO) program in 2009, providing specialists who could serve as the link between courts and VA healthcare. This institutional investment signaled federal support.

5. **Media attention**: High-profile cases of veterans committing crimes after deployment, combined with investigative reporting on veteran incarceration, created public pressure for alternatives.

## Federal Legislative Support (2009-2022)

### Key Federal Actions

| Year | Legislation/Action | Impact |
|------|-------------------|--------|
| 2009 | VA establishes Veterans Justice Outreach (VJO) program | Federal resource for VTC-VA coordination |
| 2010 | Justice for Vets launched by NADCP | National framework and training infrastructure |
| 2013 | Bureau of Justice Assistance (BJA) creates SJIS grant program | Federal funding for VTC operations |
| 2015 | Veterans Treatment Court Improvement Act (P.L. 114-198, Sec. 1004) | Authorized BJA grants specifically for VTCs |
| 2016 | VA expands VJO to 280 specialists | Increased VA court liaison capacity |
| 2018 | MISSION Act (P.L. 115-182) includes VTC provisions | Authorized VA community care referrals for VTC participants |
| 2018 | SUPPORT Act (P.L. 115-271) | Expanded VA mental health access for OTH discharge veterans |
| 2022 | PACT Act (P.L. 117-168) includes justice provisions | Expanded toxic exposure screenings for justice-involved veterans |

### State Legislation

States have adopted VTC-enabling legislation at varying paces:

| Wave | Period | States | Key Features |
|------|--------|--------|-------------|
| Early adopters | 2008-2012 | New York, California, Texas, Ohio, Illinois | Court establishment authority, basic eligibility |
| Second wave | 2012-2016 | Florida, Pennsylvania, Michigan, Virginia, Colorado | Standardized procedures, some state funding |
| Third wave | 2016-2020 | Remaining states (except Wyoming) | Often broader eligibility, some OTH inclusion |
| Expansion reforms | 2020-2025 | Michigan, Oregon, Colorado, Minnesota | OTH discharge inclusion, felony eligibility expansion |

## Maturation and Standardization (2015-Present)

### Justice for Vets "Ten Key Components"

In 2012, Justice for Vets published the "Ten Key Components of Veterans Treatment Courts," which became the de facto national standard:

1. Integration of alcohol, drug treatment, and mental health services with justice system case processing
2. Non-adversarial approach with prosecution and defense promoting public safety and participant recovery
3. Early identification and placement of eligible participants
4. Access to a continuum of alcohol, drug, mental health, and other related treatment and rehabilitation services
5. Compliance monitored by frequent alcohol and drug testing
6. Coordinated strategy among judge, prosecution, defense, and treatment providers
7. Ongoing judicial interaction with each participant
8. Monitoring and evaluation to measure achievement of program goals
9. Continuing interdisciplinary education for VTC staff
10. Partnerships among the court, VA, public agencies, and community-based organizations, including veteran peer mentors

### Research Development

The evidence base for VTCs has grown significantly:

| Period | Research Focus | Key Findings |
|--------|---------------|-------------|
| 2008-2012 | Single-site case studies | Promising outcomes at individual courts |
| 2012-2016 | Multi-site comparisons | Consistent recidivism reduction across sites |
| 2016-2020 | RAND Corporation evaluation | 25-50% recidivism reduction; cost-effectiveness demonstrated |
| 2020-2024 | Equity and access analysis | Disparities identified in race, gender, geography |
| 2024-present | Longitudinal outcomes | Long-term follow-up studies emerging |

### Current Challenges

Despite maturation, VTCs face ongoing issues that the next phase of development must address:

- Geographic coverage gaps, particularly in rural areas
- Eligibility restrictions that exclude many justice-involved veterans
- Funding sustainability beyond grant cycles
- Racial and gender disparities in access and outcomes
- VA capacity limitations that affect treatment timelines
- Lack of standardized data collection and outcome measurement
