# Veteran Mental Health: History

## Historical Development

The recognition and treatment of combat-related psychological injury has evolved dramatically over more than a century, from the denial and institutionalization of the Civil War era to today's evidence-based treatment system. This evolution reflects both advances in clinical understanding and the political mobilization of veterans who demanded that the psychological costs of war be acknowledged and treated. Each major conflict has forced a reckoning with the mental health consequences of military service, yet lessons have often been forgotten between wars.

---

### Civil War: "Soldier's Heart" (1861-1900)

The Civil War produced the first large-scale recognition of combat's psychological effects in America:

- **"Soldier's heart"** or **"irritable heart"**: Physical symptoms attributed to cardiac strain
- **"Nostalgia"**: Homesickness diagnosed as a medical condition
- **Institutionalization**: Disturbed veterans confined to asylums
- **No psychological model**: All symptoms interpreted as physical weakness or moral failure

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 1863 | First military hospital for "insane" soldiers (Government Hospital for the Insane, later St. Elizabeths) |
| 1865 | National Home for Disabled Volunteer Soldiers established |
| 1871 | Jacob Mendes Da Costa publishes "On Irritable Heart" |
| 1880s | Term "soldier's heart" widely used for combat-related symptoms |

The dominant understanding was that affected soldiers were constitutionally weak. There was no concept of environmental or trauma-based causation.[^1]

---

### World War I: "Shell Shock" (1914-1930)

World War I forced a fundamental revision of understanding as millions of previously healthy men developed debilitating psychological symptoms:

- **"Shell shock"**: Initially attributed to blast concussion, later recognized as psychological
- **Charles Myers** coined the term in *The Lancet* (1915)
- **Treatment innovation**: Forward treatment near the front (proximity, immediacy, expectancy)
- **Scale**: Approximately 80,000 British and unknown American soldiers treated for shell shock

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 1915 | Charles Myers publishes first account of "shell shock" |
| 1917 | U.S. enters WWI; encounters large-scale combat stress |
| 1918 | Military psychiatry established as discipline |
| 1919 | Many shell shock patients institutionalized long-term |
| 1921 | Veterans Bureau created, includes some psychiatric services |

The key advance was recognizing that normal men could break down under extreme stress. However, military leadership often regarded shell shock as cowardice, and many affected soldiers received dishonorable discharges rather than treatment.[^1][^2]

---

### World War II: "Combat Fatigue" and the Birth of Military Psychiatry (1941-1955)

World War II produced psychiatric casualties on a massive scale that forced the development of organized military mental health:

- **"Combat fatigue"** or **"battle fatigue"**: Replaced "shell shock" with less stigmatizing term
- **504,000** U.S. soldiers hospitalized for psychiatric reasons during the war
- **Screening failures**: Pre-induction psychiatric screening rejected 1.75 million men but failed to predict who would develop combat stress
- **Forward treatment**: Principles from WWI rediscovered and systematized

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 1941 | Military psychiatric screening implemented (largely ineffective) |
| 1943 | Record psychiatric casualty rates at Guadalcanal, North Africa |
| 1944 | General Patton "slapping incident" highlights stigma |
| 1945 | VA begins major psychiatric treatment expansion |
| 1946 | VA Department of Medicine and Surgery established |
| 1946 | National Mental Health Act passed (P.L. 79-487) |
| 1952 | DSM-I published, includes "gross stress reaction" |

The Patton incident--where General Patton struck two hospitalized soldiers he accused of cowardice--became a public controversy that paradoxically increased awareness of combat stress as a legitimate condition. Post-war VA expansion included substantial psychiatric capacity.[^2]

---

### Korea and the "Forgotten War" (1950-1965)

The Korean War reinforced lessons from WWII but received less political attention:

- **Continued forward treatment**: Reduced chronic psychiatric casualties
- **Rotation policy**: 12-month tours limited cumulative exposure
- **POW experience**: Brainwashing fears led to research on extreme stress
- **Limited post-war services**: Korean War veterans received less attention than WWII generation

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 1950 | Korean War begins; forward psychiatry deployed |
| 1952 | DSM-I includes "gross stress reaction" category |
| 1953 | Armistice; returning POWs generate research interest |
| 1955 | "Gross stress reaction" studied but poorly defined |
| 1965 | Category dropped from DSM-II--a significant setback |

The removal of "gross stress reaction" from DSM-II in 1965 reflected the assumption that stress reactions were temporary and would resolve without long-term treatment. This decision would have devastating consequences for Vietnam veterans.[^1][^3]

---

### Vietnam: The PTSD Revolution (1965-1990)

Vietnam represented the most significant turning point in understanding and treating combat-related mental health:

- **Delayed onset**: Unlike previous wars, many symptoms emerged years after service
- **Moral injury**: Vietnam's controversial nature added guilt and moral conflict to trauma
- **Agent Orange and dual exposure**: Toxic and psychological injuries combined
- **Veteran activism**: Vietnam veterans organized to demand recognition of their psychological wounds
- **PTSD recognized**: Included in DSM-III in 1980, validating veterans' experience

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 1965 | Major U.S. troop deployments to Vietnam begin |
| 1970 | "Rap groups" organized by Vietnam Veterans Against the War |
| 1972 | Term "post-Vietnam syndrome" appears in psychiatric literature |
| 1978 | Congress authorizes readjustment counseling study |
| 1979 | Vet Center program established (P.L. 96-22) |
| 1980 | **PTSD included in DSM-III**--landmark recognition |
| 1983 | National Vietnam Veterans Readjustment Study authorized |
| 1988 | NVVRS finds ~30% lifetime PTSD prevalence among Vietnam combat veterans |

The creation of Vet Centers in 1979 was driven by Vietnam veteran advocacy and established a community-based, non-clinical counseling model that remains operational today. The inclusion of PTSD in the DSM-III gave veterans a diagnostic framework for their suffering and enabled compensation claims and treatment access.[^3][^4]

---

### Gulf War and Peacekeeping Era (1990-2001)

The Gulf War and 1990s military operations generated new research and treatment advances:

- **Gulf War Illness**: Unexplained physical and psychological symptoms in ~250,000 veterans
- **Brief combat exposure**: Short ground war but high PTSD rates among some units
- **Peacekeeping stress**: Bosnia, Somalia, and other missions created different trauma patterns
- **Research acceleration**: VA research program expanded trauma studies

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 1991 | Gulf War; rapid combat followed by persistent unexplained symptoms |
| 1993 | Somalia deployment (Black Hawk Down); peacekeeping trauma recognized |
| 1995-1999 | Bosnia/Kosovo operations |
| 1998 | VA National Center for PTSD fully established |
| 1999 | Evidence-based treatment research accelerates |
| 2000 | Cognitive Processing Therapy effectiveness demonstrated |

---

### Post-9/11: Scale, Complexity, and System Transformation (2001-Present)

The wars in Iraq and Afghanistan produced the most comprehensive military mental health response in history, while also revealing persistent gaps:

- **Scale**: ~2.8 million service members deployed; hundreds of thousands affected
- **Polytrauma**: TBI, PTSD, chronic pain, and substance use frequently co-occur
- **Multiple deployments**: 58% of post-9/11 veterans deployed two or more times
- **Military sexual trauma**: Increased recognition and screening
- **Telehealth revolution**: COVID-19 accelerated virtual mental health delivery
- **Evidence-based therapy dissemination**: VA systematically trained thousands of therapists in CPT and PE
- **Peer support**: Formalized peer specialist roles within VA

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 2001 | Afghanistan war begins |
| 2003 | Iraq war begins |
| 2004 | *New England Journal of Medicine* publishes landmark Iraq PTSD study |
| 2007 | VA begins systematic evidence-based psychotherapy dissemination |
| 2008 | RAND *Invisible Wounds of War* quantifies mental health crisis |
| 2010 | VA mandates universal mental health screening |
| 2012 | Executive Order 13625 expands veteran mental health access |
| 2014 | Clay Hunt SAV Act strengthens suicide prevention and mental health |
| 2016 | VA Whole Health initiative launches (complementary approaches) |
| 2019 | PREVENTS task force established for suicide prevention |
| 2020 | COVID-19 drives telemental health expansion |
| 2022 | COMPACT Act enables cross-state telehealth |
| 2022 | PACT Act expands mental health eligibility for toxic exposure |

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|--------------|
| 1915 | "Shell shock" described | First formal recognition of combat psychological injury |
| 1946 | VA psychiatric expansion | Federal government accepts responsibility for combat mental health |
| 1965 | "Gross stress reaction" removed from DSM-II | Set back recognition of chronic combat stress |
| 1979 | Vet Centers established | Community-based counseling model created |
| 1980 | PTSD included in DSM-III | Diagnostic legitimacy for combat trauma |
| 1988 | NVVRS results published | Documented scale of Vietnam-era PTSD |
| 2007 | VA evidence-based therapy dissemination | Systematic training in CPT and PE therapy |

| 2020 | COVID telemental health expansion | Removed geographic barriers to mental health access |

## Lessons from History

1. **Every war requires relearning**: Each conflict has forced rediscovery of combat's psychological effects, as lessons from previous wars are forgotten or dismissed.

2. **Veteran advocacy drives recognition**: From Vietnam rap groups to post-9/11 organizations, veterans themselves have been the primary force demanding that combat mental health be taken seriously.

3. **Diagnosis enables treatment**: The inclusion of PTSD in DSM-III was essential--without a diagnostic framework, veterans could not access treatment or compensation.

4. **Stigma is persistent**: Despite a century of evidence, stigma against mental health treatment remains the single largest barrier to care for military populations.

5. **Forward treatment works but is not enough**: Treating conditions quickly and close to the source improves acute outcomes but does not prevent chronic conditions.

6. **Co-occurring conditions are the norm**: From soldier's heart to polytrauma, combat rarely produces a single, isolated mental health condition.

7. **Political attention is cyclical**: Mental health funding and services expand during and immediately after wars but contract during peacetime.
