# Veteran Mental Health: Current State

## Present Conditions

The VA mental health system has expanded significantly over the past two decades yet remains unable to meet the full scope of veteran mental health needs. The system treats 1.7 million veterans annually through a combination of outpatient clinics, residential programs, Vet Centers, and telehealth, but an estimated 3 million additional veterans with mental health conditions remain outside VA care. Within the system, workforce shortages--particularly in psychiatry--create access barriers that disproportionately affect rural veterans and those seeking specialized trauma-focused treatment.

|-----------|----------------------|------------------------------|------------|
| PTSD | 11-20% (post-9/11); ~7% (all eras) | 3.6% | ~660,000 |
| Major depressive disorder | ~20% | ~8% | ~720,000 |
| Generalized anxiety disorder | ~15% | ~6% | ~540,000 |
| Substance use disorder | ~11% | ~7% | ~370,000 |
| Alcohol use disorder | ~8% | ~5.6% | ~280,000 |
| Traumatic brain injury effects | ~22% (post-9/11 combat) | N/A | ~190,000 |
| Adjustment disorders | ~8% | ~5% | ~230,000 |
| Bipolar disorder | ~3% | ~2.8% | ~95,000 |
| Schizophrenia/psychotic disorders | ~1.5% | ~1% | ~68,000 |

Veterans experience higher rates of most mental health conditions than the general population, with PTSD and depression showing the largest disparities.[^1]

### Military Sexual Trauma (2024)[^3]

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Women veterans screening positive for MST | ~25% |
| Men veterans screening positive for MST | ~1.5% |
| Total MST-positive veterans in VA care | ~150,000 |
| MST-related treatment encounters (annual) | ~2.1 million |
| VA facilities providing MST treatment | 100% (mandated) |

MST is associated with significantly elevated rates of PTSD, depression, and substance use disorders. All VA facilities are required to provide MST-related treatment regardless of service connection status or discharge characterization.[^3]

### Co-Occurring Conditions[^1]

| Combination | Prevalence Among VA Mental Health Patients |
|-------------|-------------------------------------------|
| PTSD + depression | ~52% |
| PTSD + substance use disorder | ~35% |
| Depression + anxiety | ~48% |
| TBI + PTSD | ~33% (post-9/11 combat veterans) |
| PTSD + chronic pain | ~60% |
| Three or more co-occurring conditions | ~28% |

Co-occurring conditions are the norm rather than the exception among veterans seeking mental health care, requiring integrated treatment approaches.[^1]

### Evidence-Based Treatment Availability (2024)[^5]

| Therapy | Coverage | Description |
|---------|----------|-------------|
| Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT) | 100% of VAMCs | Structured 12-session PTSD treatment |
| Prolonged Exposure (PE) | 100% of VAMCs | Graduated trauma memory processing |
| Eye Movement Desensitization (EMDR) | ~85% of VAMCs | Trauma reprocessing with bilateral stimulation |
| Motivational Enhancement Therapy | ~90% of VAMCs | Substance use disorder treatment |

| Medication management | 100% of VAMCs | Psychopharmacology |

VA has invested heavily in disseminating evidence-based psychotherapies for PTSD, but availability of specific modalities varies by facility and trained clinician availability.[^5]

### Wait Time Trends (FY2020-2024)[^7]

| Year | New Patient Wait (days) | Community Care Referrals |
|------|-------------------------|--------------------------|
| FY2020 | 25.3 | 380,000 |
| FY2021 | 28.7 | 445,000 |
| FY2022 | 29.4 | 510,000 |

Wait times for new mental health patients have increased steadily, with community care referrals growing in parallel--suggesting growing demand is outpacing combined capacity.[^7]

### Barriers to Treatment-Seeking[^8]

| Barrier | % of Veterans Citing |
|---------|---------------------|
| Stigma / concerns about career impact | 55% |
| Belief that treatment will not help | 38% |
| Distance to VA facility | 32% |
| Wait times | 28% |
| Negative prior experience with VA | 24% |
| Preference for self-management | 22% |
| Did not know services were available | 18% |
| Childcare / work schedule conflicts | 15% |

Stigma remains the most commonly cited barrier, particularly among active-duty-to-veteran transitions and among veterans who separate with other-than-honorable discharges.[^8]

## Treatment Outcomes

### PTSD Treatment Effectiveness (VA Data, 2023)[^5]

| Measure | CPT | Prolonged Exposure | Medication Only |
|---------|-----|-------------------|-----------------|
| Clinically meaningful improvement | 53% | 56% | 38% |
| Full remission | 28% | 31% | 15% |
| Treatment completion rate | 65% | 58% | 72% |
| Dropout rate | 35% | 42% | 28% |

Evidence-based psychotherapies produce better outcomes than medication alone but face significant dropout rates, particularly for Prolonged Exposure therapy which requires confronting trauma memories.[^5]

### Treatment Completion Challenges[^5]

| Factor | Impact on Completion |
|--------|---------------------|
| Session frequency disruption | Scheduling gaps reduce effectiveness |
| Travel burden (rural veterans) | Distance reduces attendance |
| Co-occurring substance use | Complicates engagement |
| Provider turnover | Breaks therapeutic relationship |
| Treatment intensity | 12-session protocols require sustained commitment |
| Avoidance symptoms | PTSD itself creates treatment resistance |

| Telemental health satisfaction | 86.4% | 83.2% |
| Vet Center satisfaction | 91.8% | N/A |
| Residential program satisfaction | 85.2% | N/A |
