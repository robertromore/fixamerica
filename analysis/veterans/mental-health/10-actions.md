# Veteran Mental Health: Citizen Actions

## What You Can Do

### If You Are a Veteran

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Seek help** | Contact VA mental health, Vet Center, or Veterans Crisis Line (988, press 1) | Your wellbeing matters |
| **Get screened** | Ask for PTSD/depression screening at your next VA visit | Early identification improves outcomes |
| **Try telemental health** | Request VA Video Connect appointment | Access from home, reduced travel |
| **Explore Vet Centers** | Walk-in counseling, no enrollment required | Confidential, community-based |
| **Connect with peers** | Peer support programs, veteran organizations | You are not alone |
| **Share your story** | If comfortable, speak at hearings, to media, or to peers | Reduce stigma, drive change |
| **Join a VSO** | IAVA, Wounded Warrior Project, DAV | Collective advocacy |
| **Know your rights** | PACT Act eligibility, MST treatment access, OTH options | Access all available services |

### If You Are a Veteran's Family Member

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Learn the signs** | Recognize PTSD, depression, and substance use symptoms | Early intervention |
| **Offer support without judgment** | Listen, encourage, avoid ultimatums | Create space for help-seeking |
| **Access family services** | VA Caregiver Support, Vet Center family counseling | Your mental health matters too |
| **Learn about VA resources** | Help navigate enrollment and appointments | Reduce barriers for your veteran |
| **Seek your own support** | NAMI family groups, caregiver organizations | Secondary trauma is real |
| **Contact legislators** | Share family impact stories | Personal advocacy |

### If You Are a Mental Health Provider

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Get military cultural competence training** | PsychArmor Institute (free online) | Better serve veteran patients |
| **Join Give an Hour** | Provide pro bono sessions to veterans | Expand access |
| **Learn evidence-based PTSD treatments** | CPT and PE training available through VA | Improve outcomes |
| **Become a VA community care provider** | Enroll in VA network | Increase veteran access |
| **Advocate for licensing reform** | Support cross-state telehealth compacts | Expand geographic access |

### General Citizen Actions

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Learn the facts** | Understand veteran mental health challenges | Counter misconceptions |
| **Contact legislators** | Advocate for VA mental health funding | Political pressure |
| **Vote** | Research candidates on veteran mental health | Electoral impact |
| **Volunteer** | Vet Centers, veteran organizations, crisis lines | Direct support |
| **Reduce stigma** | Challenge misconceptions when you hear them | Cultural change |
| **Donate** | Cohen Veterans Network, Headstrong, Give an Hour | Fund treatment access |

## Political Actions

### Contact Your Representatives

**Federal Issues**:

- VA mental health workforce funding and pay parity
- Telemental health permanent authority and expansion
- OTH discharge mental health eligibility
- Anti-stigma campaign funding
- DoD-VA transition mental health screening mandate
- Military cultural competence standards for community care

**State Issues**:

- Cross-state telehealth licensing compacts
- Veteran mental health court expansion
- State mental health funding for veteran programs
- Employer mental health accommodation laws

### Template Email: Federal Representative

> Dear [Representative/Senator name],
>
> I am writing to urge your support for expanding and improving veteran mental health services. An estimated 4.8 million veterans have mental health conditions, but only about 40% seek treatment. For those who do, the average wait for a new VA mental health appointment is over 30 days, and the VA's psychiatry vacancy rate of 22.4% is the highest of any specialty.
>
> These are not just statistics. Behind them are veterans suffering from PTSD, depression, traumatic brain injury effects, and moral injury who are not getting the care they earned through service. The consequences include elevated suicide rates, homelessness, substance abuse, and family disruption.
>
> I am asking you to:
>
> 1. Fund VA mental health workforce expansion with competitive pay for psychiatrists
> 2. Expand telemental health access through device distribution and permanent cross-state authority
> 3. Extend mental health eligibility to veterans with other-than-honorable discharges
> 4. Fund a national anti-stigma campaign designed and delivered by veterans
> 5. Mandate comprehensive mental health screening before military separation
>
> Mental health conditions are not a sign of weakness--they are the cost of service. We must honor that service with the resources and care our veterans deserve.
>
> Sincerely,
> [Your name]
> [Your address]

### Phone Script: Federal Representative

> "Hello, my name is [name] and I'm a constituent from [city/zip]. I'm calling to urge [Representative/Senator] to support expanded funding for veteran mental health services.
>
> The VA has a 22% psychiatry vacancy rate--the highest of any specialty--and veterans wait over 30 days for a new mental health appointment. Meanwhile, 60% of veterans with mental health conditions don't seek treatment at all, largely because of stigma.
>
> I'm asking [Representative/Senator] to support workforce pay parity, telemental health expansion, and eligibility for veterans with other-than-honorable discharges who were often separated because of the very mental health conditions they need treatment for.
>
> Can I count on [Representative/Senator]'s support? Thank you."

## Talking Points

### For Conversations About Veteran Mental Health

1. **The problem is access, not demand**: Only 40% of veterans with mental health conditions seek treatment, and those who do face 30+ day wait times for new appointments.[^1]

2. **Stigma is the biggest barrier**: 55% of veterans with mental health conditions cite stigma or career concerns as the reason they do not seek help.[^2]

3. **VA mental health quality is strong**: VA mental health satisfaction is 88.1%, above the national average of 82.6%.[^3]

4. **Community care is not the answer for specialty conditions**: Only 35% of private mental health providers have military cultural competency.[^4]

5. **PTSD is real and diagnosable**: PTSD prevalence rates of 11-20% among combat veterans have been consistent across decades of independent research.[^5]

6. **Treatment works but must be accessible**: Evidence-based therapies like CPT and PE produce clinically meaningful improvement in over 50% of cases.[^6]

7. **Untreated mental health costs more**: RAND estimates that early treatment saves $1.5-2.7 billion per two-year cohort in reduced downstream costs.[^7]

8. **OTH discharge veterans need care**: Many other-than-honorable discharges are caused by untreated mental health conditions; denying care perpetuates a cycle of harm.[^8]

## Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Reality |
|---------------|---------|
| "Most veterans have PTSD" | 11-20% of post-9/11 combat veterans have PTSD; not all veterans are combat veterans |
| "PTSD means violent behavior" | PTSD manifests as avoidance, hypervigilance, sleep disruption; most veterans with PTSD are not violent |
| "Veterans should just be tougher" | Mental health conditions are medical conditions caused by combat exposure, not personal weakness |
| "Treatment doesn't work for PTSD" | Evidence-based treatments produce clinically meaningful improvement in over 50% of cases |
| "VA mental health is terrible" | VA mental health satisfaction exceeds the national average |
| "Private care is always better" | Most private providers lack military cultural competency; VA has specialized expertise |
| "Veterans with OTH discharges don't deserve care" | Many OTH discharges are caused by untreated mental health conditions |
| "Mental health is just PTSD" | Veterans also face depression, substance use, TBI effects, moral injury, and MST-related conditions |
