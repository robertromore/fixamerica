# Veteran Mental Health: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Reforming veteran mental health requires coordinated federal legislation, DoD-VA coordination, regulatory action, and state-level policy changes. The legislative approach includes:

1. **Federal legislation** to expand the mental health workforce, mandate transition screening, and establish anti-stigma programs
2. **Regulatory action** through VA rulemaking on eligibility, community care standards, and cultural competence
3. **DoD-VA coordination** on transition mental health screening and warm handoff protocols
4. **State legislation** for licensing compacts, veteran treatment courts, and community mental health
5. **Appropriations** to fund workforce expansion, telemental health, and treatment programs

## Constitutional Amendments

No constitutional amendments required. VA mental health services fall within existing federal authority under Title 38 and the military powers clause.

## Federal Legislation

### Legislative Alignment

| Proposal | Primary Problem Addressed | Policy Lever |
|----------|---------------------------|--------------|
| Veteran Mental Health Workforce Act | 22.4% psychiatry vacancy; 14.9% overall mental health vacancy | Pay, hiring, pipeline |
| Transition Mental Health Bridge Act | "Deadly gap" between separation and VA engagement | DoD screening, warm handoff, eligibility |
| Veteran Anti-Stigma and Treatment Access Act | 60% of veterans with conditions don't seek care | National campaign, career protection |
| Military Cultural Competence Standards Act | 35% of community providers have cultural competency | Certification, training, requirements |
| Veteran Whole Health Act | 35-42% dropout from evidence-based therapy | Complementary approaches, multiple pathways |

### Veteran Mental Health Workforce Act

**Purpose**: Close the VA mental health workforce gap with emphasis on psychiatry, the most critically short specialty.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Veteran Mental Health
Workforce Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.
Congress finds the following:
(1) The Veterans Health Administration has a 22.4 percent
vacancy rate for psychiatrists, the highest of any clinical
specialty.
(2) Sixty-eight percent of VA medical centers report severe
psychiatry shortages.
(3) The average wait time for a new VA mental health appointment
is 30.1 days.
(4) VA psychiatrist compensation averages 88 percent of
private-sector median, contributing to recruitment difficulty.
(5) An estimated 60 percent of veterans with mental health
conditions do not seek treatment.

SEC. 3. PSYCHIATRY PAY PARITY.
(a) MINIMUM COMPENSATION.—Not later than 180 days after the
date of enactment, the Secretary shall adjust compensation for
VA psychiatrists to not less than the 75th percentile of
psychiatrist compensation in the relevant local labor market.
(b) CRITICAL SHORTAGE BONUS.—For facilities designated as
experiencing severe psychiatry shortage, the Secretary shall
provide a recruitment and retention bonus of not less than
25 percent of base pay.
(c) ANNUAL ADJUSTMENT.—Compensation under this section shall
be adjusted annually based on the Employment Cost Index for
healthcare workers.

SEC. 4. MENTAL HEALTH HIRING REFORM.
(a) DIRECT HIRE AUTHORITY.—The Secretary may exercise direct
hire authority for all mental health clinical positions,
including psychiatrists, psychologists, social workers,
licensed professional counselors, marriage and family
therapists, psychiatric nurses, and peer support specialists.
(b) TIME-TO-HIRE STANDARD.—The Secretary shall establish a
standard of not more than 60 days from position posting to
offer letter for mental health positions.
(c) INTERNATIONAL RECRUITMENT.—The Secretary shall establish
a program for recruiting mental health professionals from
allied countries, including credentialing assistance and
visa sponsorship.

SEC. 5. EDUCATION DEBT REDUCTION FOR MENTAL HEALTH.
(a) ENHANCED PROGRAM.—The Secretary shall increase the maximum
education debt reduction payment for mental health professionals
to $150,000 for participants who commit to 4 years of service.
(b) RURAL PREMIUM.—For service at rural or highly rural
facilities, the maximum shall be $200,000.
(c) PSYCHIATRY PREMIUM.—For psychiatrists, the maximum shall
be $250,000 for a 4-year commitment.

SEC. 6. PRESCRIBING PSYCHOLOGIST PROGRAM.
(a) PILOT PROGRAM.—The Secretary shall establish a 3-year
pilot program to train and credential clinical psychologists
for prescriptive authority within VHA.
(b) SCOPE.—Prescribing psychologists shall be authorized to
prescribe psychotropic medications for mental health conditions
within their scope of competence.
(c) TRAINING.—The Secretary shall develop a postdoctoral
psychopharmacology training program of not fewer than
2 years in duration.
(d) EVALUATION.—The Secretary shall report to Congress on
pilot program outcomes not later than 3 years after the
first prescribing psychologist begins practice.

SEC. 7. PEER SUPPORT EXPANSION.
(a) The Secretary shall increase the number of VA peer support
specialist positions to not fewer than 2,400.
(b) TRAINING.—The Secretary shall establish a national peer
support specialist training and certification program.
(c) INTEGRATION.—Peer support specialists shall be integrated
into mental health treatment teams at all VA medical centers.

SEC. 8. RESIDENCY EXPANSION.
(a) The Secretary shall increase VA-sponsored psychiatry
residency positions by not fewer than 200 per year for 5
years.
(b) The Secretary shall increase VA-sponsored clinical
psychology internship positions by not fewer than 150 per
year for 5 years.
(c) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated
$300,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2026 through 2030
to carry out this section.

SEC. 9. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.
There are authorized to be appropriated $1,500,000,000 for each
of fiscal years 2026 through 2030 to carry out this Act.
```

**Explanation**: The bill addresses the most acute workforce shortage (psychiatry) with the most direct interventions (pay, hiring, pipeline). The prescribing psychologist provision creates a new prescribing workforce to supplement the psychiatrist shortage.

**Potential Challenges**: Psychiatry professional organizations may oppose prescribing psychologist authority; OMB scoring concerns; federal pay equity issues with other specialties.

**Refinements**: Could limit prescribing psychologist authority to VA-only practice to reduce scope-of-practice opposition; could phase pay increases over 2 years.

### Transition Mental Health Bridge Act

**Purpose**: Eliminate the "deadly gap" between military separation and VA mental health engagement by mandating comprehensive screening, warm handoffs, and universal eligibility during the transition period.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Transition Mental Health
Bridge Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.
Congress finds the following:
(1) The average delay between military separation and first VA
mental health appointment is 1 to 3 years.
(2) The first year after separation is the highest-risk period
for veteran suicide.
(3) Brief separation screenings miss many mental health
conditions that develop or worsen after discharge.
(4) Approximately 500,000 post-9/11 veterans received
other-than-honorable discharges, many caused by untreated
mental health conditions.

SEC. 3. MANDATORY COMPREHENSIVE MENTAL HEALTH ASSESSMENT.
(a) REQUIREMENT.—Not later than 1 year after the date of
enactment, the Secretary of Defense shall require a
comprehensive mental health assessment for every service
member separating from active duty, to be conducted not
fewer than 90 days before the date of separation.
(b) CONTENT.—The assessment shall include—
    (1) structured clinical interview by a licensed mental
    health professional;
    (2) validated screening instruments for PTSD, depression,
    anxiety, substance use, and traumatic brain injury;
    (3) military sexual trauma screening;
    (4) assessment of suicidal ideation and lethal means access;
    (5) referral recommendations and warm handoff initiation.
(c) VOLUNTARY TREATMENT.—Participation in recommended treatment
shall be voluntary, but the assessment itself shall be
mandatory for separation processing.

SEC. 4. WARM HANDOFF PROTOCOL.
(a) REQUIREMENT.—The Secretary of Defense and the Secretary of
Veterans Affairs shall jointly establish a warm handoff
protocol whereby—
    (1) the separating service member's mental health provider
    directly introduces the service member to a VA mental
    health provider;
    (2) relevant clinical records are transferred to VA before
    separation;
    (3) a VA mental health appointment is scheduled before
    separation, to occur within 30 days of discharge.

SEC. 5. TRANSITION PERIOD ELIGIBILITY.
(a) UNIVERSAL COVERAGE.—All separating service members shall
be eligible for VA mental health services for a period of
not fewer than 1 year after separation, regardless of
discharge characterization.
(b) EXTENSION.—The Secretary of Veterans Affairs may extend
the transition period to 2 years for service members
identified as having mental health conditions during
the separation assessment.

SEC. 6. OTHER-THAN-HONORABLE DISCHARGE MENTAL HEALTH.
(a) PERMANENT ELIGIBILITY.—Section 1720I of title 38, United
States Code, is amended to provide that veterans with
other-than-honorable discharges shall be eligible for VA
mental health services on the same basis as veterans with
honorable discharges.
(b) OUTREACH.—The Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall conduct
targeted outreach to OTH discharge veterans to inform them
of their eligibility for mental health services.

SEC. 7. PROACTIVE OUTREACH.
(a) The Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall contact every
separating service member at 30, 90, and 180 days after
separation to assess mental health status and offer
services.
(b) Outreach shall be conducted through the veteran's
preferred communication method (phone, text, email, or
letter).
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the highest-risk period in the veteran lifecycle. The warm handoff ensures continuity rather than requiring veterans to navigate a new system independently. OTH discharge eligibility acknowledges that mental health conditions often cause the conduct that leads to separation.

**Potential Challenges**: DoD resistance to mandatory screening extending separation timelines; cost of universal 1-year eligibility; OTH discharge eligibility expansion faces political opposition from military discipline advocates.

**Refinements**: Could limit OTH eligibility to mental health conditions that existed during service; could phase in mandatory screening over 2 years.

### Veteran Anti-Stigma and Treatment Access Act

**Purpose**: Fund a sustained, veteran-led national campaign to reduce mental health stigma and increase treatment-seeking among veterans.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Veteran Anti-Stigma and
Treatment Access Act".

SEC. 2. NATIONAL ANTI-STIGMA CAMPAIGN.
(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall
establish a national campaign to reduce stigma associated
with mental health treatment-seeking among veterans and
active-duty service members.
(b) DESIGN.—The campaign shall be—
    (1) designed with input from a veteran advisory board
    composed of combat veterans from multiple eras;
    (2) delivered primarily by veteran ambassadors;
    (3) informed by evidence on effective anti-stigma
    interventions.
(c) COMPONENTS.—The campaign shall include—
    (1) media partnerships including television, radio,
    podcast, and social media content;
    (2) a peer ambassador program of not fewer than 1,000
    trained veteran ambassadors;
    (3) employer partnership programs to normalize mental
    health treatment in veteran-employing workplaces;
    (4) military leadership engagement, including participation
    by senior officers and non-commissioned officers.

SEC. 3. CAREER PROTECTION.
(a) No veteran or service member shall face adverse career,
employment, or security clearance consequences solely for
seeking or receiving mental health treatment.
(b) ENFORCEMENT.—Violations of this section shall be subject
to administrative remedies and may be brought before the
Merit Systems Protection Board or Equal Employment
Opportunity Commission as appropriate.

SEC. 4. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.
There are authorized to be appropriated $100,000,000 for each
of fiscal years 2026 through 2030 to carry out this Act.
```

**Explanation**: Stigma is the most commonly cited barrier to treatment-seeking. This bill funds a sustained campaign designed by veterans, for veterans, rather than a top-down public health messaging approach.

**Potential Challenges**: Measuring stigma reduction is difficult; maintaining campaign momentum over 5 years; some military culture resistance.

**Refinements**: Could include annual outcome measurement with Congressional reporting; could coordinate with DoD equivalent campaign.

### Military Cultural Competence Standards Act

**Purpose**: Require military cultural competence certification for all mental health providers who treat veterans through VA community care.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "Military Cultural Competence
Standards Act".

SEC. 2. CERTIFICATION REQUIREMENT.
(a) COMMUNITY CARE.—Not later than 2 years after the date of
enactment, the Secretary of Veterans Affairs shall require
that all mental health providers participating in the VA
community care network hold a military cultural competence
certification.
(b) TRAINING.—The Secretary shall make available free online
training sufficient to meet the certification requirement.
(c) CURRICULUM.—The certification curriculum shall include—
    (1) military culture, rank structure, and values;
    (2) combat stress, PTSD, and traumatic brain injury;
    (3) military sexual trauma;
    (4) transition challenges and reintegration;
    (5) substance use in military and veteran populations;
    (6) suicide risk assessment for military populations;
    (7) moral injury;
    (8) veteran communication styles and therapeutic rapport.
(d) VETERAN CO-TRAINERS.—Training shall be developed and
delivered with participation by veterans.

SEC. 3. VA STAFF REQUIREMENT.
(a) All VA mental health staff shall complete military cultural
competence training within 1 year of hire and complete
annual refresher training thereafter.

SEC. 4. CONTINUING EDUCATION.
(a) Certified providers shall complete not fewer than 4 hours
of military cultural competence continuing education annually.

SEC. 5. QUALITY ASSESSMENT.
(a) The Secretary shall include military cultural competence
questions in veteran satisfaction surveys for mental health
services.
(b) Providers receiving consistently low cultural competence
ratings shall be required to complete additional training.
```

**Explanation**: Only 35% of community care mental health providers have military cultural competency. Since VA sends 620,000 mental health referrals to community care annually, quality standards for these providers are essential.

**Potential Challenges**: Community provider resistance to additional requirements; defining and measuring competence; enforcement across large provider networks.

**Refinements**: Could phase in over 3 years; could exempt providers who see fewer than 5 veterans per year.

## State Model Legislation

### State Veteran Mental Health Court Expansion Act

**Purpose**: Expand veteran treatment courts to provide alternatives to incarceration for justice-involved veterans with mental health conditions.

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "[State] Veteran Mental Health
Court Expansion Act".

SECTION 2. ESTABLISHMENT.
(a) Each judicial district in this state shall establish or
have access to a veteran treatment court program.
(b) The program shall provide—
    (1) mental health assessment upon identification of
    veteran status;
    (2) individualized treatment plans;
    (3) coordination with VA mental health services;
    (4) peer mentor assignment;
    (5) judicial supervision with treatment-focused incentives.

SECTION 3. ELIGIBILITY.
(a) Any veteran charged with a non-violent offense whose
conduct is connected to a mental health condition, substance
use disorder, or traumatic brain injury shall be eligible
for the veteran treatment court program.
(b) Participation shall be voluntary.
```

**Adaptations**: States may expand to include violent offenses at judicial discretion; may include National Guard and Reserve members; may partner with VA Vet Centers for treatment.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| Federal authority | Clear under Title 38 and military powers clause |
| Career protection | First Amendment and due process implications for employment actions |
| OTH discharge eligibility | Federal discretion over benefit eligibility |
| Prescribing psychologist authority | Federal practice preempts state licensing within VA |
| DoD screening mandate | Congressional authority over military organization |

### Preemption

VA mental health services operate under federal supremacy. State licensing requirements do not apply to care at VA facilities. The COMPACT Act extends this to telehealth. However, community care providers remain subject to state licensing laws.

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Screening gaming | Military units could conduct perfunctory screenings to meet mandate | High |
| Cultural competence certification without practice change | Providers could pass training without changing clinical approach | Medium |
| Career protection enforcement gaps | Violations difficult to prove and enforce | Medium |
| OTH eligibility confusion | Veterans may not know about expanded eligibility | Medium |
| Prescribing psychologist scope creep resistance | Psychiatric associations may limit implementation | Medium |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|-----------|
| Stigma is cultural, not legal | Legislation cannot directly change military culture norms | Cultural inertia |
| National psychiatry shortage | VA competes for limited pool of psychiatrists nationally | Pipeline inadequacy |
| Co-occurring condition complexity | No single intervention addresses polytrauma | Clinical complexity |
| DoD cooperation uncertain | Transition screening requires DoD buy-in | Institutional interests |
| Measurement challenges | Stigma reduction and cultural competence are difficult to measure | Methodological limits |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Screening quality audit**: Require OIG audits of separation screening quality, including interviewer qualifications and duration
2. **Cultural competence outcome tracking**: Tie certification to patient-reported competence scores, not just training completion
3. **Career protection enforcement**: Establish ombudsman office for reporting violations with whistleblower protections
4. **OTH eligibility outreach**: Fund targeted outreach through VSOs and community organizations
5. **Prescribing psychologist evaluation**: Require independent evaluation of safety and effectiveness before expansion
6. **Anti-stigma campaign evaluation**: Annual independent assessment with published results and campaign adjustments

### General Implementation Concerns

- DoD may resist extending separation processing timelines for comprehensive mental health screening
- Psychiatric professional organizations may oppose prescribing psychologist authority expansion
- Community care providers may resist cultural competence requirements as an additional burden
- Anti-stigma campaign effectiveness is inherently difficult to measure in the short term
- Budget competition with other VA priorities may reduce funding below authorized levels
