# Local Journalism: History

## The Penny Press Era (1830s-1890s)

### Origins of Mass Local Journalism

Before the 1830s, newspapers were expensive, partisan, and aimed at elites. The "penny press" revolution democratized news.

| Development | Impact |
|-------------|--------|
| **New York Sun (1833)** | First successful penny daily |
| **New York Herald (1835)** | Pioneered local crime, courts coverage |
| **Steam-powered printing** | Enabled mass production |
| **Literacy expansion** | Created mass readership |
| **Advertising model** | Low price subsidized by ads |

### Characteristics of Penny Press

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Price** | 1 cent (vs. 6 cents for partisan papers) |
| **Content** | Local news, crime, human interest |
| **Politics** | Less partisan than predecessors |
| **Business model** | Advertising-supported |
| **Distribution** | Street sales, newsboys |

## The Golden Age of Newspapers (1890s-1950s)

### Expansion and Consolidation

| Era | Development |
|-----|-------------|
| **1890s-1910s** | "Yellow journalism"; Hearst vs. Pulitzer |
| **1910s-1920s** | Professionalization; journalism schools founded |
| **1920s-1930s** | Chain ownership begins (Scripps, Hearst) |
| **1940s-1950s** | Post-war prosperity; newspapers dominant |

### The One-Paper Town Emerges

| Decade | Trend |
|--------|-------|
| **1900** | Most cities had multiple competing dailies |
| **1920** | Competition declining |
| **1940** | Joint operating agreements begin |
| **1960** | Most cities down to one or two papers |
| **1980** | One-paper towns become norm |

**Causes of Consolidation**

- High fixed costs favored larger circulation
- Advertisers preferred dominant paper
- Labor costs rose
- Television competition for advertising

### Peak Newspaper Era (1950s-1980s)

| Characteristic | Description |
|----------------|-------------|
| **Profitability** | 20-30% profit margins common |
| **Employment** | Large newsrooms; specialized beats |
| **Community role** | Civic institution; agenda-setter |
| **Advertising monopoly** | Classifieds, retail, legal notices |
| **Ownership** | Mostly family or local chains |

## The Chain Era (1960s-2000s)

### Rise of Corporate Ownership

| Chain | Founded | Approach |
|-------|---------|----------|
| **Gannett** | 1906 (expanded 1960s+) | Standardization, cost control |
| **Knight Ridder** | 1974 merger | Quality journalism focus |
| **Tribune Company** | 1847 (expanded 1980s+) | Major market papers |
| **McClatchy** | 1857 (expanded 1990s+) | Regional chains |
| **MediaNews** | 1983 | Dean Singleton's acquisitions |

### Public Ownership Era

| Development | Impact |
|-------------|--------|
| **IPOs in 1960s-70s** | Newspapers become public companies |
| **Wall Street expectations** | Demand for consistent profit growth |
| **Short-term pressure** | Quarterly earnings focus |
| **Cost cutting begins** | Efficiency over journalism quality |

### Peak and Decline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1984 | 63 million daily circulation (peak) |
| 1987 | Peak newspaper ad revenue |
| 1995 | Craigslist founded |
| 2000 | Newspaper ad revenue: $48.7 billion |
| 2005 | Classified revenue begins collapse |

## The Digital Disruption (2000-2010)

### Craigslist and the Classified Collapse

| Year | Newspaper Classified Revenue |
|------|------------------------------|
| 2000 | $19.6 billion |
| 2005 | $17.3 billion |
| 2008 | $10.0 billion |
| 2010 | $6.0 billion |
| 2012 | $4.6 billion |

### Google and Facebook Rise

| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 2000 | Google AdWords launches |
| 2004 | Facebook founded |
| 2006 | Facebook opens to public |
| 2007 | Facebook News Feed launches |

### Newspaper Response Failures

| Strategy | Result |
|----------|--------|
| **Free online content** | Trained readers not to pay |
| **Late paywalls** | Difficult to reverse free habit |
| **Portal strategies** | Failed to compete with Google |
| **Digital-first mandates** | Print decline faster than digital growth |

## The Hedge Fund Era (2010-Present)

### New Ownership Model

| Development | Impact |
|-------------|--------|
| **Distressed debt investors** | Bought struggling chains cheap |
| **Cost-cutting focus** | Extract value, not build journalism |
| **Real estate sales** | Sold headquarters, printing plants |
| **Staff reductions** | 50-75% cuts typical |
| **Short-term horizon** | No investment in future |

### Alden Global Capital

| Year | Action |
|------|--------|
| 2010 | Begins acquiring MediaNews Group |
| 2012 | Gains control of Digital First Media |
| 2020 | Acquires Tribune Publishing (Chicago Tribune, etc.) |
| 2021 | Largest newspaper owner in U.S. |

**Alden's Pattern**

1. Acquire distressed newspaper company
2. Sell real estate
3. Cut staff 50-75%
4. Reduce print frequency
5. Consolidate operations
6. Extract cash flow
7. Repeat or close papers

### Other Hedge Fund Players

| **Fortress Investment** | Various | Financial engineering |
| **Chatham Asset Management** | McClatchy | Cost cutting |
| **Apollo Global** | Various investments | Turnaround focus |

## Lessons from History

### What Enabled the Golden Age

| Factor | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Advertising monopoly** | Newspapers were only game in town |
| **High barriers to entry** | Printing presses expensive |
| **Local focus** | Coverage tied to community |
| **Civic ethos** | Journalism as public service |
| **Stable employment** | Career journalists developed expertise |

### What Caused the Collapse

| Factor | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| **Digital advertising** | Platforms captured revenue |
| **Free content norm** | Established by newspapers themselves |
| **Wall Street pressure** | Short-term profit over journalism |
| **Hedge fund extraction** | No investment in future |
| **Audience fragmentation** | Readers scattered across sources |

### Historical Parallels

| Past | Present |
|------|---------|
| Penny press disrupted elite papers | Digital disrupted newspapers |
| Telegraph enabled wire services | Internet enabled aggregation |
| Radio threatened newspapers | Social media threatens newspapers |
| Chains replaced family owners | Hedge funds replaced chains |

## What History Suggests

### Patterns to Note

1. **Technology disruption is recurring** - Each new medium threatens predecessors
2. **Business models can be rebuilt** - Penny press, then advertising, then subscriptions
3. **Public interest requires investment** - Quality journalism has never been fully market-supported
4. **Consolidation precedes collapse** - Warning signs came decades before crisis
5. **New models emerge from crisis** - Nonprofit, digital-native outlets are innovation

### Questions History Raises

- Has advertising-supported journalism ever been sustainable long-term?
- What level of public investment has journalism historically required?
- How have other democracies maintained local news?
- What would it take to rebuild local news from scratch?
