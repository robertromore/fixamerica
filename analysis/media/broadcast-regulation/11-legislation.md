# Broadcast Regulation: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Broadcast regulation operates under the Communications Act of 1934, as amended, with the FCC as the primary regulator. Reform can occur through congressional legislation, FCC rulemaking, or judicial decisions. This document provides draft legal text for strengthening public interest obligations.

## Federal Legislation

### Broadcast Public Interest Enhancement Act

**Purpose**: Strengthen and codify public interest obligations for broadcast licensees.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Broadcast Public Interest Enhancement
Act of 2025".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
    (1) the electromagnetic spectrum is a public resource;
    (2) broadcast licensees receive valuable spectrum rights in
    exchange for serving the public interest;
    (3) deregulation has diminished public interest service;
    (4) local communities have lost access to local news and
    information;
    (5) public participation in broadcast licensing has declined;
    and
    (6) strengthening public interest obligations serves democracy.

SEC. 3. PUBLIC INTEREST STANDARD.

(a) DEFINITION.—Section 309 of the Communications Act of 1934 is
amended to add:

"(k) PUBLIC INTEREST DEFINED.—For purposes of this Act, the 'public
interest, convenience, and necessity' with respect to broadcast
licenses shall include—
    "(1) service to the local community;
    "(2) local news and public affairs programming;
    "(3) diverse programming reflecting the community served;
    "(4) educational programming, including children's programming;
    "(5) emergency alert and preparedness services;
    "(6) access for political candidates and issue discussion; and
    "(7) other matters as determined by the Commission."

SEC. 4. LOCAL PROGRAMMING REQUIREMENTS.

(a) MINIMUM REQUIREMENTS.—The Commission shall require broadcast
licensees to provide—
    (1) not less than 3 hours per week of locally-originated
    programming for television stations; and
    (2) not less than 2 hours per week of locally-originated
    programming for radio stations.

(b) LOCAL NEWS.—The Commission shall encourage locally-originated
news programming as part of the public interest obligation.

SEC. 5. LICENSE RENEWAL.

(a) PERFORMANCE REVIEW.—Section 309(k) is amended to require that
license renewal applications include a demonstration of—
    (1) compliance with public interest requirements;
    (2) local programming provided;
    (3) community engagement activities; and
    (4) response to community needs.

(b) PUBLIC PARTICIPATION.—The Commission shall provide meaningful
opportunity for public participation in license renewal proceedings,
including—
    (1) notice in the community served;
    (2) opportunity for public comment;
    (3) consideration of community input; and
    (4) public hearing upon petition of 100 or more community
    members.

SEC. 6. COMMUNITY NEEDS ASSESSMENT.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Broadcast licensees shall, at least once during
each license term, conduct an assessment of community needs for
programming and public service.

(b) DOCUMENTATION.—The assessment shall be documented in the public
file and shall inform programming decisions.

SEC. 7. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) PENALTIES.—The Commission may impose penalties for failure to
comply with public interest requirements, including—
    (1) fines of up to $100,000 per violation;
    (2) short-term renewal;
    (3) license revocation for repeated violations.

(b) REPORTING.—The Commission shall report annually to Congress on
compliance with public interest requirements.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 180 days after enactment.
```

### Broadcast Ownership Reform Act

**Purpose**: Strengthen ownership limits to promote diversity and localism.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Broadcast Ownership Reform Act of 2025".

SEC. 2. NATIONAL OWNERSHIP CAP.

(a) TELEVISION.—Section 202 of the Telecommunications Act of 1996
is amended to provide that no entity may own, operate, or control
television broadcast stations reaching more than 25 percent of
television households nationally.

(b) REVIEW.—The Commission shall review this cap every 4 years and
may adjust it only upon a finding that adjustment would serve the
public interest in diversity and localism.

SEC. 3. LOCAL OWNERSHIP.

(a) TELEVISION DUOPOLY.—The Commission shall not permit common
ownership of two television stations in the same market except
where—
    (1) the market contains at least 8 independently owned
    television stations; and
    (2) at least one of the commonly owned stations is not among
    the four highest-rated stations in the market.

(b) RADIO-TELEVISION.—The Commission shall not permit common
ownership of radio and television stations in the same market where
such ownership would result in undue concentration.

SEC. 4. DIVERSE OWNERSHIP.

(a) PROGRAM.—The Commission shall establish a program to promote
ownership of broadcast stations by minorities, women, and small
businesses.

(b) ELEMENTS.—The program shall include—
    (1) tax incentives for sales to diverse buyers;
    (2) incubator requirements for large station groups;
    (3) technical assistance for new entrants; and
    (4) preference in comparative proceedings.

SEC. 5. ATTRIBUTION.

(a) REVIEW.—The Commission shall review attribution rules to ensure
that all forms of control over broadcast stations are appropriately
attributed.

(b) JSA AND SSA.—Joint sales agreements, shared services agreements,
and similar arrangements shall be attributed as ownership interests
where they convey effective control.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect upon enactment, with existing owners
granted 3 years to comply with new limits.
```

### Political Advertising Transparency Act

**Purpose**: Enhance transparency in political advertising on broadcast.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Political Advertising Transparency
Act of 2025".

SEC. 2. ENHANCED DISCLOSURE.

(a) SPONSORSHIP IDENTIFICATION.—Section 317 of the Communications
Act of 1934 is amended to require that political advertisements
include—
    (1) clear identification of the sponsor;
    (2) for organizations, identification of the top 5 funders of
    the organization; and
    (3) a statement of whether the advertisement is coordinated
    with any candidate.

(b) PUBLIC FILE.—Political advertisement information in the public
file shall include—
    (1) the complete advertisement;
    (2) the sponsor and contact information;
    (3) the amount paid;
    (4) the target audience; and
    (5) the dates and times of broadcast.

SEC. 3. ISSUE ADVERTISEMENTS.

(a) DEFINITION.—"Issue advertisement" means any advertisement that
addresses a matter of public importance, whether or not it expressly
advocates for or against a candidate.

(b) REQUIREMENTS.—Issue advertisements shall be subject to the same
disclosure requirements as candidate advertisements.

SEC. 4. ONLINE EXTENSION.

The requirements of this Act shall apply to online political
advertising by broadcast licensees to the same extent as broadcast
advertising.

SEC. 5. ENFORCEMENT.

Violations of this Act shall be subject to fines of up to $50,000
per violation.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 90 days after enactment.
```

## FCC Rulemaking

### Model Public Interest Rulemaking

**Notice of Proposed Rulemaking Elements**:

```text
BEFORE THE FEDERAL COMMUNICATIONS COMMISSION

In the Matter of:
Review of Public Interest Obligations of Broadcast Licensees

NOTICE OF PROPOSED RULEMAKING

1. The Commission proposes to strengthen public interest obligations
of broadcast licensees to ensure that the public receives meaningful
benefits from the use of public spectrum.

2. The Commission seeks comment on:

    a. Whether to establish minimum local programming requirements;
    b. How to define and measure local service;
    c. Whether to restore community needs ascertainment;
    d. How to enhance public participation in license renewal;
    e. What enforcement mechanisms are appropriate;
    f. How to adapt public interest obligations for digital platforms.

3. The Commission proposes the following specific rules:

    a. Television licensees shall provide not less than 3 hours per
    week of locally-originated programming;

    b. Licensees shall conduct community needs assessments at least
    once during each license term;

    c. License renewal shall require demonstration of public interest
    compliance;

    d. Public participation in license renewal shall be enhanced
    through notice, comment, and hearing opportunities.

4. Comments are due [date]. Reply comments are due [date].
```

## Constitutional Considerations

### First Amendment

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| **Content requirements** | More scrutiny than structural rules |
| **Disclosure requirements** | Generally permissible |
| **Ownership limits** | Upheld under rational basis |
| **Public interest standard** | Long-established |

### Scarcity Doctrine

| Argument | Status |
|----------|--------|
| **Red Lion precedent** | Still good law |
| **Spectrum limitation** | Still applies |
| **Changed circumstances** | Some judicial skepticism |
| **Structural rules** | More defensible |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Definition gaming** | What counts as "local"? | Medium |
| **Minimal compliance** | Lowest-cost compliance | High |
| **Enforcement weakness** | FCC rarely acts | High |
| **Digital exclusion** | Online not covered | Medium |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| **First Amendment limits** | Content rules challenged | Constitutional |
| **Political appointments** | FCC politicized | Structural |
| **Industry resources** | Outmatched advocates | Economic |
| **Technology change** | Rules become outdated | Technical |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Clear definitions** with specific requirements
2. **Meaningful metrics** for compliance measurement
3. **Enhanced enforcement** with real consequences
4. **Public participation** in oversight
5. **Regular review** and adaptation
6. **Digital extension** as technology changes
7. **Intervenor funding** to support public participation
