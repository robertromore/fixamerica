# Force Structure: Reform Roadmap

Reforming U.S. military force structure is a generational undertaking. The services, Congress, and industry must be aligned through a phased approach that builds political support in the short term, implements structural changes in the medium term, and achieves a fully adapted force in the long term. The roadmap below sequences reforms to address the most achievable changes first, building momentum and institutional capacity for deeper structural transformation. Each phase builds on the previous one, and the sequencing is designed to create political facts on the ground that make subsequent phases more feasible.

---

## Phase 1: Foundations and Quick Wins (Years 1-2)

This phase focuses on legislative authorization, analytical groundwork, and reforms that can be implemented within existing authorities or through the annual NDAA.

| Priority | Action | Key Performance Indicator (KPI) |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Pass Force Structure Modernization Act** | Enact legislation mandating the Quadrennial Force Structure Review and establishing the "1+2" force-sizing construct. Include requirements for CBO independent cost estimates and GAO audit of the QFSR process. | Legislation signed into law; DoD begins QFSR planning within 90 days. |
| **2. Launch Recruiting Reform** | Expand Future Soldier Preparatory Course to all services. Implement 15-20% junior enlisted pay raise. Modernize medical/moral waiver standards through MEPCOM directive. Expand JROTC to 200 additional underrepresented schools. | 10% increase in qualified applicants within 12 months; all services within 5% of authorized end strength. |
| **3. Submit BRAC Request** | SecDef submits formal BRAC authorization request to Congress with updated excess capacity analysis, community transition plan, and reformed criteria addressing 2005 round criticisms. | Request submitted; Congressional hearings scheduled; bipartisan BRAC coalition of at least 20 Senators and 50 Representatives identified. |
| **4. Begin Total Force Review** | SecDef commissions comprehensive active-reserve mission suitability analysis across all services, led by the Vice Chairman of the Joint Chiefs with participation from all service secretaries and the Chief of the National Guard Bureau. | Review charter signed; initial findings within 12 months; at least 10 candidate missions for active-to-reserve transfer identified. |
| **5. Accelerate Autonomous Systems** | Issue rapid acquisition directives for CCA, unmanned surface vessels, and autonomous logistics vehicles. Designate these programs as ACAT I with streamlined milestone reviews. Fund dedicated manned-unmanned teaming exercises in each service. | First CCA squadron declared initial operational capability; two unmanned vessel prototypes in fleet exercises; Army autonomous resupply demonstrated at brigade combat team exercise. |
| **6. Address Munitions Shortfall** | Increase funding for priority munitions production (155mm, GMLRS, Standard Missile, LRASM) by $5 billion annually. Invoke Defense Production Act Title III authorities for critical munitions supply chains. | 155mm production at 50,000 rounds/month (up from 14,000 baseline); GMLRS and LRASM production doubled. |
| **7. Begin Shipyard Investment** | Allocate $5 billion in first-year funding for submarine industrial base (SIOP -- Submarine Industrial Base Optimization Plan), including workforce development, dry dock expansion, and digital shipbuilding tools at Electric Boat and Newport News. | 3,000 new shipyard workers hired; Norfolk Naval Shipyard dry dock modernization contract awarded. |

**Goal of Phase 1**: Establish the legislative and analytical framework for reform while implementing quick wins in recruiting, compensation, and munitions production that demonstrate commitment and build political support.

**Political Strategy**: Phase 1 reforms are designed to be achievable through the annual NDAA, executive authority, or narrow bipartisan legislation. Recruiting reform and compensation increases enjoy broad Congressional support. The BRAC request will face opposition but establishes the marker for Phase 2. Framing reforms as "strengthening readiness" rather than "cutting the military" is essential.

## Phase 2: Structural Reform (Years 3-5)

This phase implements the major force structure changes identified in Phase 1 analysis, including active-reserve rebalancing, BRAC execution, and force design transformation.

| Priority | Action | Key Performance Indicator (KPI) |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Execute Active-Reserve Rebalance** | Implement tiered readiness model across all reserve components. Transfer identified missions to reserve component with adequate resources. Increase training days and full-time support for Tier 1 reserve units. | 3-5 mission transfers completed; Tier 1 units meeting readiness benchmarks within 24 months; employer partnership agreements signed with 100+ major companies for USERRA-plus commitments. |
| **2. Conduct BRAC Round** | BRAC Commission completes review and submits recommendations using QFSR-aligned criteria. Congress approves or rejects (no amendments). Community transition programs funded at $50 million minimum per affected community. | Commission recommendations approved; closure/realignment implementation begins for 30-50 installations; transition assistance programs operational. |
| **3. Field New Force Design Concepts** | Marine Littoral Regiments at full operational capability. Army MDTFs expanded to five. Air Force ACE exercises become routine at wing level. Navy unmanned vessel program transitions to program of record. | All three MLRs operational and exercised in Pacific theater; two additional MDTFs stood up (total of five); ACE demonstrated at wing level in Europe and Pacific; 30+ unmanned vessels in service. |
| **4. Grow the Fleet** | Increase shipbuilding rate toward 12 ships per year through sustained industrial base investment. Prioritize Virginia-class submarine production (2.0/year), DDG-51 Flight III destroyers, Constellation-class frigates, and Light Amphibious Warships. | Shipbuilding rate at 10+ per year; submarine build rate at 2.0/year; frigate program on schedule; shipyard workforce expanded by 15,000. |
| **5. Implement PPBE Reform** | Replace legacy PPBE system with modernized process per Commission recommendations. Establish portfolio-based budgeting for major mission areas. Create innovation accounts. | Reprogramming timelines reduced by 50%; innovation accounts operational with $5+ billion in funding; first portfolio budget submitted for Indo-Pacific deterrence mission. |
| **6. Establish Space National Guard** | Stand up the Space National Guard with initial operating capability, providing a reserve component for space operations that leverages commercial-sector expertise. | Space National Guard authorized; 2,000+ Guardians transferred or recruited; 3+ units operational in satellite operations and space domain awareness. |

**Goal of Phase 2**: Execute the structural changes that produce a more efficiently sized and distributed force optimized for great power competition. This phase will generate the most political friction because it involves base closures, mission transfers, and changes to established equities.

**Political Strategy**: Link BRAC savings to readiness investments that members can claim credit for. Ensure community transition assistance is generous enough to reduce opposition from affected delegations. Frame active-reserve rebalancing as "strengthening the Guard and Reserve" rather than "cutting the active force." Celebrate early successes in autonomous systems and new force design concepts to build momentum.

## Phase 3: Adapted Force (Years 6-10)

This phase realizes the long-term vision of a force that is right-sized, technologically advanced, and strategically aligned.

| Priority | Action | Key Performance Indicator (KPI) |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Achieve Strategy-Resource Balance** | QFSR validates that force structure meets the "1+2" construct at acceptable risk levels. Budget fully funds the recommended force. | QFSR certifies strategy-force alignment; no "significant risk" categories in Chairman's Risk Assessment; budget-strategy gap closed to within 5%. |
| **2. Fleet of 350+ Ships** | Navy reaches 350+ battle force ships, including 60+ unmanned vessels. Submarine fleet stabilized at 50+ SSNs. | Fleet count at 350+; unmanned vessels completing independent operations; submarine maintenance backlog reduced by 50%. |
| **3. Human-Machine Teaming at Scale** | Autonomous systems integrated into every combat formation. CCA squadrons operational across the Air Force. Navy USVs/UUVs deployed to Indo-Pacific. Army autonomous systems at every brigade. | 200+ CCA aircraft operational; every Army BCT has organic unmanned ISR and logistics systems; 100+ Navy unmanned vessels in fleet. |
| **4. Sustainable AVF** | All services meeting authorized end strength. Retention rates at target levels. Recruiting pool expanded through preparatory programs and diversified pipelines. | Zero recruiting shortfall for 3 consecutive years; first-term retention above 55%; eligible/propensed population expanded by 25% through preparatory programs and waiver modernization. |
| **5. BRAC Savings Realized** | Closed bases remediated and repurposed. Annual savings from BRAC reinvested in readiness and modernization. Community economic recovery underway. | $3-5 billion annual savings validated by GAO; 80% of closed base communities show positive economic trajectory within 5 years. |
| **6. Munitions and Industrial Base Resilience** | Munitions production rates sufficient to sustain 30 days of high-intensity conflict consumption. Defense industrial base surge capacity validated through industrial mobilization exercises. | 155mm production at 100,000 rounds/month; key precision munitions stockpiles at 90%+ of wartime requirements; annual industrial surge exercise conducted. |

**Goal of Phase 3**: The U.S. military is sized to its strategy, organized for distributed operations, technologically advanced, industrially resilient, and sustainable within its budget.

## Risk Assessment

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Congressional refusal to authorize BRAC | High | Medium | Build bipartisan support through community transition assistance provisions; link BRAC to readiness funding. Commission independent "excess capacity" study that generates public pressure. |
| Recruiting crisis deepens despite reforms | Medium | High | Develop contingency plans including expanded preparatory programs, lateral entry from civilian sector for specialized skills, allied foreign recruiting (Australian model), and selective service modernization study. |
| Shipyard capacity insufficient for fleet growth | High | High | Begin shipyard investment immediately (Phase 1); explore allied shipyard partnerships (Japan, South Korea, Australia under AUKUS); consider expanding Austal USA (Mobile, AL) for surface combatant production. |
| Service resistance to mission transfers | High | Medium | Tie transfers to QFSR findings; ensure receiving reserve units are adequately funded. Secure CJCS endorsement. Establish independent review of transfer outcomes at 3-year mark. |
| Autonomous systems technology not mature | Medium | Medium | Maintain parallel development of manned alternatives; pursue incremental deployment rather than revolutionary transformation. Use Ukraine conflict data to validate operational concepts before scaling. |
| Geopolitical crisis accelerates timeline | Medium | High | Prioritize munitions production and readiness investments in Phase 1 to ensure the current force is combat-ready while structural reforms proceed. |
| Continued fiscal constraints (sequestration-like) | Medium | High | Advocate for bipartisan defense spending floor linked to GDP (e.g., 3.5% of GDP minimum). Build Congressional coalition around strategy-resource alignment messaging. |

## Sequencing Rationale

The three-phase approach reflects the political reality that comprehensive reform cannot be achieved in a single legislative action. Each phase builds on the previous one:

1. **Phase 1 (Foundations)** creates the analytical framework (QFSR), addresses immediate shortfalls (recruiting, munitions), and begins the industrial base investment that Phase 2 will require.
2. **Phase 2 (Structural Reform)** executes the major changes (BRAC, active-reserve rebalancing, fleet growth, force design transformation) that require the political foundation built in Phase 1.
3. **Phase 3 (Adapted Force)** realizes the long-term vision, leveraging the institutional capacity and political momentum built in Phases 1 and 2.

This sequencing also reflects the principle of "irreversibility" -- once the QFSR is established, once BRAC savings are redirected to readiness, once autonomous systems are fielded in operational units, and once the tiered readiness model is implemented, these changes become self-sustaining and resistant to rollback.
