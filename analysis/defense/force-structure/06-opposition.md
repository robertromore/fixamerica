# Force Structure: Opposition

Force structure reform -- whether it involves cutting end strength, realigning the active-reserve balance, closing bases, or canceling legacy programs -- faces determined opposition from multiple stakeholders whose institutional, economic, or political interests are threatened by change.

---

## Services Protecting End Strength

Each military service resists reductions to its end strength and core force structure because these are the primary metrics of institutional power and budget share.

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **"End strength equals readiness"** | Reducing the number of soldiers, sailors, airmen, or Marines directly degrades the military's ability to fight and win wars. Every cut increases risk and reduces the force's ability to respond to multiple contingencies. | End strength alone does not equal combat power. A hollow force with large numbers but inadequate training, equipment, and readiness is less effective than a smaller, fully resourced force. The late 1970s "hollow Army" and the 2017 Navy readiness crisis both demonstrate that prioritizing numbers over readiness creates dangerous illusions of capability. |
| **"We cannot predict future threats"** | The military must maintain a large, diverse force because the next conflict will come from an unexpected direction. Cutting force structure optimized for one scenario leaves the nation vulnerable to others. History shows that threats emerge faster than forces can be rebuilt. | While strategic uncertainty justifies maintaining a balanced force, it does not justify maintaining every legacy program. Resources spent sustaining platforms and units optimized for past conflicts are resources not invested in capabilities needed for future ones. Hedging against uncertainty requires adaptation, not preservation of the status quo. |
| **"The industrial base depends on production rates"** | Reducing procurement of ships, aircraft, or vehicles below minimum sustaining rates will cause the industrial base to atrophy. Once skilled workers and specialized facilities are lost, rebuilding them takes a decade or more -- and costs far more than maintaining them. | This is a legitimate concern but it is also self-serving. The defense industry has consolidated to the point where a small number of contractors can hold force structure decisions hostage to industrial base arguments. The solution is strategic industrial base planning, not perpetual procurement of systems the military does not need. |

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: Service Chiefs, service-aligned think tanks (Association of the United States Army, Navy League, Air & Space Forces Association), retired senior officers, and the defense media ecosystem.

## Congressional Delegations Protecting Bases

Members of Congress from both parties vigorously oppose base closures and force reductions that affect their districts, regardless of strategic rationale.

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **"BRAC devastates communities"** | Base closures eliminate thousands of jobs and can destroy local economies. The economic impact falls disproportionately on rural and lower-income communities that have limited alternative employment options. The human cost is real and cannot be dismissed as a mere "efficiency." | The economic impact of BRAC is real but often overstated. GAO and academic studies have found that most BRAC communities recover within 5-10 years, and many (like former bases in San Francisco and Denver) ultimately thrive. The $12 billion in annual savings from previous BRAC rounds funds capabilities that save lives. Maintaining excess infrastructure to preserve jobs is a defense policy driven by economic considerations, not strategic ones. |
| **"The military needs geographic distribution"** | Maintaining bases across the country ensures the military has a presence in all regions, facilitating recruiting, homeland defense, and community connection. Concentrating forces in a few locations creates strategic vulnerability and severs the military-civilian relationship. | Geographic distribution for recruiting and community relations is a valid consideration, but it does not justify maintaining installations that are 50% utilized. The military can maintain recruiting and community presence without maintaining full-scale bases. Reserve centers, ROTC programs, and National Guard armories serve these functions at a fraction of the cost. |
| **"Congress should decide, not commissions"** | The BRAC process delegates Congressional authority over federal spending to an unelected commission. Elected representatives should make decisions about which bases to close, not technocrats. | Congress has demonstrated over five decades that it is incapable of closing bases through the normal legislative process. Every member will fight to protect their own bases while voting to close others. The BRAC commission process was specifically designed to overcome this collective action problem. The alternative to BRAC is not Congressional decision-making -- it is permanent excess capacity. |

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: Bipartisan Congressional coalitions from base-heavy states (Virginia, California, Texas, North Carolina, Georgia), state and local government officials, chambers of commerce, and defense community advocacy organizations.

## Defense Industry Protecting Production Lines

Defense contractors oppose force structure changes that reduce demand for their products, using economic and strategic arguments interchangeably.

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **"Minimum sustaining rates protect the industrial base"** | Major weapons programs require minimum production rates to remain economically viable. Dropping below these rates increases per-unit costs, drives suppliers out of business, and can make it impossible to surge production in a crisis. The submarine industrial base, for example, requires two boats per year to sustain the skilled workforce. | The minimum sustaining rate argument is valid for a small number of truly strategic industrial capabilities (nuclear shipbuilding, solid rocket motors, semiconductor fabrication). But it is frequently invoked for programs that are simply profitable, not strategically essential. The solution is selective industrial base investment, not blanket protection of all production lines. |
| **"Foreign military sales sustain capacity"** | Exporting weapons to allies helps sustain production lines, reduce per-unit costs, and strengthen alliances. Cutting domestic procurement without increasing exports will undermine both the industrial base and alliance relationships. | FMS is a useful tool but cannot substitute for domestic demand. Export decisions are constrained by technology transfer policies, geopolitical considerations, and ally budgets. Using FMS to justify production rates the U.S. military does not need reverses the proper relationship between strategy and procurement. |
| **"Jobs and economic impact"** | The defense industry supports approximately 2.1 million direct jobs and millions more in the broader supply chain. Cutting programs destroys high-skill, high-wage manufacturing jobs that are difficult to replace, particularly in communities without diverse economic bases. | Defense spending is not a jobs program. Every dollar spent on an unwanted weapon system is a dollar not spent on capabilities the military actually needs, or on domestic priorities that also create jobs. The argument that defense programs must continue because they employ people is economically identical to digging holes and filling them in. |

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: Prime defense contractors and their lobbying arms, the Aerospace Industries Association, the National Defense Industrial Association, and the Congressional delegations representing defense manufacturing centers.

## Veteran and Military Advocacy Organizations

Some veteran and military advocacy organizations oppose force structure reforms that they perceive as undermining military strength or service member welfare.

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **"Smaller forces mean more deployments"** | Reducing end strength without reducing operational commitments means the remaining force deploys more frequently, with shorter dwell time between deployments. This degrades readiness, increases family stress, and drives retention problems. | This is a legitimate concern that must be addressed as part of any force reduction. The solution is to reduce commitments proportionally to reductions, not to maintain unsustainable end strength to avoid the harder task of prioritizing missions. |
| **"Don't break faith with volunteers"** | The All-Volunteer Force compact requires the nation to provide adequate resources, quality of life, and support for those who choose to serve. Cutting force structure while maintaining high operational tempo breaks this compact. | Honoring the compact with service members is essential. But this argument is sometimes deployed to block any structural reform, no matter how necessary. The compact is better served by a right-sized, fully resourced force than by an oversized, under-funded one. |

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: VFW, American Legion, Military Officers Association of America (MOAA), National Military Family Association, and enlisted advocacy organizations.
