# Force Structure: Opposition

Force structure reform -- whether it involves cutting end strength, realigning the active-reserve balance, closing bases, or canceling legacy programs -- faces determined opposition from multiple stakeholders whose institutional, economic, or political interests are threatened by change. Unlike many policy areas where opposition is primarily ideological, resistance to force structure reform is driven by material interests: jobs, institutional budgets, community survival, and corporate revenue. This makes the opposition both more intense and more bipartisan than in most policy debates.

---

## Services Protecting End Strength

Each military service resists reductions to its end strength and core force structure because these are the primary metrics of institutional power and budget share.

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **"End strength equals readiness"** | Reducing the number of soldiers, sailors, airmen, or Marines directly degrades the military's ability to fight and win wars. Every cut increases risk and reduces the force's ability to respond to multiple contingencies. | End strength alone does not equal combat power. A hollow force with large numbers but inadequate training, equipment, and readiness is less effective than a smaller, fully resourced force. The late 1970s "hollow Army" and the 2017 Navy readiness crisis both demonstrate that prioritizing numbers over readiness creates dangerous illusions of capability. Chief of Staff General Edward Meyer's 1980 testimony about the "hollow Army" was an explicit acknowledgment that the Army had prioritized end strength over combat readiness. |
| **"We cannot predict future threats"** | The military must maintain a large, diverse force because the next conflict will come from an unexpected direction. Cutting force structure optimized for one scenario leaves the nation vulnerable to others. History shows that threats emerge faster than forces can be rebuilt. | While strategic uncertainty justifies maintaining a balanced force, it does not justify maintaining every legacy program. Resources spent sustaining platforms and units optimized for past conflicts are resources not invested in capabilities needed for future ones. Hedging against uncertainty requires adaptation, not preservation of the status quo. The Army's failure to anticipate the counterinsurgency demands of Iraq and Afghanistan -- despite maintaining a large conventional force -- demonstrates that hedging through mass alone does not ensure preparedness. |
| **"The industrial base depends on production rates"** | Reducing procurement of ships, aircraft, or vehicles below minimum sustaining rates will cause the industrial base to atrophy. Once skilled workers and specialized facilities are lost, rebuilding them takes a decade or more -- and costs far more than maintaining them. The submarine industrial base requires two boats per year to sustain its workforce. | This is a legitimate concern for a small number of truly strategic industrial capabilities (nuclear shipbuilding, solid rocket motors, semiconductor fabrication). But it is frequently invoked for programs that are simply profitable, not strategically essential. The Abrams tank production line in Lima, Ohio has been kept running by Congressional adds even when the Army explicitly testified it did not need additional tanks. The solution is strategic industrial base planning, not perpetual procurement of systems the military does not need. |
| **"The lessons of Ukraine demand a larger force"** | The Russia-Ukraine war demonstrates that peer conflict is attritional, consumes munitions at rates far exceeding peacetime planning, and requires mass. The U.S. must grow, not shrink, its force structure to be prepared for similar demands. | The lessons of Ukraine are real but selective. Ukraine has also demonstrated that small, distributed forces with precision weapons can defeat much larger formations; that unmanned systems can substitute for manned platforms in many roles; and that industrial surge capacity matters more than peacetime stockpile size. The appropriate response to Ukraine is not simply "more of everything" but a more resilient, adaptable, and industrially sustainable force design. |

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: Service Chiefs and their staffs; service-aligned associations (Association of the United States Army, Navy League, Air & Space Forces Association, Marine Corps Association); retired senior officers who maintain institutional loyalty; and the defense media ecosystem (*Defense News*, *Breaking Defense*, service-specific publications) that amplifies service messaging.

**Case Study -- The A-10 Warthog**: The Air Force has attempted to retire the A-10 Thunderbolt II close air support aircraft in nearly every budget cycle since the early 2010s, arguing that the aircraft is too slow and vulnerable for contested airspace and that its missions can be performed by the F-35 and other multi-role platforms. Congress has blocked every attempt, driven by a combination of Army and Marine Corps advocacy (ground forces value the A-10's close air support capability), Congressional champions from states with A-10 basing (Arizona, Michigan, Idaho), and nostalgia for the aircraft's performance in Desert Storm and Afghanistan. The A-10 saga illustrates how a platform can become politically immortal regardless of the service's strategic assessment.

## Congressional Delegations Protecting Bases

Members of Congress from both parties vigorously oppose base closures and force reductions that affect their districts, regardless of strategic rationale.

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **"BRAC devastates communities"** | Base closures eliminate thousands of jobs and can destroy local economies. The economic impact falls disproportionately on rural and lower-income communities that have limited alternative employment options. The human cost is real and cannot be dismissed as a mere "efficiency." | The economic impact of BRAC is real but often overstated. GAO and academic studies have found that most BRAC communities recover within 5-10 years, and many (like former bases in San Francisco's Presidio, Denver's Lowry, and San Antonio's Brooks) ultimately thrive as mixed-use developments, technology parks, or educational campuses. The $12 billion in annual savings from previous BRAC rounds funds capabilities that save lives. Maintaining excess infrastructure to preserve jobs is a defense policy driven by economic considerations, not strategic ones. |
| **"The military needs geographic distribution"** | Maintaining bases across the country ensures the military has a presence in all regions, facilitating recruiting, homeland defense, and community connection. Concentrating forces in a few locations creates strategic vulnerability and severs the military-civilian relationship. | Geographic distribution for recruiting and community relations is a valid consideration, but it does not justify maintaining installations that are 50% utilized. The military can maintain recruiting and community presence without maintaining full-scale bases. Reserve centers, ROTC programs, National Guard armories, and Military Entrance Processing Stations (MEPS) serve these functions at a fraction of the cost. |
| **"Congress should decide, not commissions"** | The BRAC process delegates Congressional authority over federal spending to an unelected commission. Elected representatives should make decisions about which bases to close, not technocrats. | Congress has demonstrated over five decades that it is incapable of closing bases through the normal legislative process. Every member will fight to protect their own bases while voting to close others. The BRAC commission process was specifically designed to overcome this collective action problem. The alternative to BRAC is not Congressional decision-making -- it is permanent excess capacity that wastes $5-8 billion annually. |
| **"The 2005 round was politicized"** | The 2005 BRAC round deviated from military criteria and appeared to reflect political considerations, undermining confidence in the process. A new round would be similarly manipulated. | The 2005 round had flaws, but they can be addressed through reformed criteria, enhanced transparency, and independent oversight. Allowing a flawed past round to permanently block future rounds means accepting billions in annual waste indefinitely. |

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: Bipartisan Congressional coalitions from base-heavy states (Virginia, California, Texas, North Carolina, Georgia, Florida); state and local government officials; chambers of commerce; the Association of Defense Communities; and defense community advocacy organizations.

**Scale of the Problem**: DoD has requested BRAC authorization in nearly every budget submission since 2013. Congress has rejected every request. The estimated 20-25% excess infrastructure capacity represents approximately 200 million square feet of excess space and $5-8 billion in annual operating costs that could be redirected to readiness and modernization.

## Defense Industry Protecting Production Lines

Defense contractors oppose force structure changes that reduce demand for their products, using economic and strategic arguments interchangeably.

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **"Minimum sustaining rates protect the industrial base"** | Major weapons programs require minimum production rates to remain economically viable. Dropping below these rates increases per-unit costs, drives suppliers out of business, and can make it impossible to surge production in a crisis. The submarine industrial base, for example, requires two boats per year to sustain the skilled workforce at Electric Boat and Newport News. | The minimum sustaining rate argument is valid for a small number of truly strategic industrial capabilities (nuclear shipbuilding, solid rocket motors, semiconductor fabrication). But it is frequently invoked for programs that are simply profitable, not strategically essential. The solution is selective industrial base investment, not blanket protection of all production lines. The Pentagon should identify and protect genuinely critical industrial capabilities while allowing non-critical programs to be competed or consolidated. |
| **"Foreign military sales sustain capacity"** | Exporting weapons to allies helps sustain production lines, reduce per-unit costs, and strengthen alliances. Cutting domestic procurement without increasing exports will undermine both the industrial base and alliance relationships. | FMS is a useful tool but cannot substitute for domestic demand. Export decisions are constrained by technology transfer policies (ITAR, EAR), geopolitical considerations, ally budgets, and Congressional approval requirements. Using FMS to justify production rates the U.S. military does not need reverses the proper relationship between strategy and procurement. |
| **"Jobs and economic impact"** | The defense industry supports approximately 2.1 million direct jobs and millions more in the broader supply chain. Cutting programs destroys high-skill, high-wage manufacturing jobs that are difficult to replace, particularly in communities without diverse economic bases. | Defense spending is not a jobs program. Every dollar spent on an unwanted weapon system is a dollar not spent on capabilities the military actually needs, or on domestic priorities that also create jobs. CBO and academic research consistently find that defense spending generates fewer jobs per dollar than equivalent spending on infrastructure, education, or healthcare. The argument that defense programs must continue because they employ people is economically equivalent to subsidizing any other industry for employment purposes -- it should be evaluated on those terms, not disguised as a national security imperative. |

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: Prime defense contractors and their lobbying arms (each of the Big Five spends $10-14 million annually on lobbying), the Aerospace Industries Association, the National Defense Industrial Association, the Shipbuilders Council of America, and the Congressional delegations representing defense manufacturing centers (Connecticut for submarines, Texas for helicopters and vehicles, California for aircraft, Virginia for shipbuilding, Alabama for missiles and space).

## Veteran and Military Advocacy Organizations

Some veteran and military advocacy organizations oppose force structure reforms that they perceive as undermining military strength or service member welfare.

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **"Smaller forces mean more deployments"** | Reducing end strength without reducing operational commitments means the remaining force deploys more frequently, with shorter dwell time between deployments. This degrades readiness, increases family stress, and drives retention problems -- a vicious cycle that further reduces the force. | This is a legitimate concern that must be addressed as part of any force reduction. The solution is to reduce commitments proportionally to reductions, not to maintain unsustainable end strength to avoid the harder task of prioritizing missions. The 2018 NDS Commission explicitly recommended that if resources are insufficient for the strategy, the strategy should be revised rather than accepting a hollow force. |
| **"Don't break faith with volunteers"** | The All-Volunteer Force compact requires the nation to provide adequate resources, quality of life, and support for those who choose to serve. Cutting force structure while maintaining high operational tempo breaks this compact and will accelerate the recruiting crisis. | Honoring the compact with service members is essential. But this argument is sometimes deployed to block any structural reform, no matter how necessary. The compact is better served by a right-sized, fully resourced force than by an oversized, under-funded one. The sequestration era demonstrated that an underfunded force is more damaging to service member welfare than a smaller, properly funded force. |
| **"The Guard is essential for homeland defense"** | Reducing National Guard end strength threatens the nation's ability to respond to natural disasters, pandemics, civil emergencies, and homeland security threats. The Guard's domestic mission is at least as important as its federal warfighting mission. | The Guard's domestic mission is genuinely important, but it is sometimes invoked to protect Guard force structure that is primarily justified by federal warfighting requirements. A more honest approach would be to separately resource and evaluate the Guard's domestic and federal missions, funding each at the level their respective requirements demand rather than using one mission to justify the other. |

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: VFW, American Legion, Military Officers Association of America (MOAA), National Military Family Association (NMFA), National Guard Association of the United States (NGAUS), Enlisted Association of the National Guard of the United States (EANGUS), Reserve Officers Association (ROA), and enlisted advocacy organizations.

## The Bipartisan Nature of Opposition

Force structure opposition, like opposition to military-industrial complex reform more broadly, is genuinely bipartisan. Conservative members resist force reductions because they want a larger military; liberal members resist base closures and program cancellations because they want to protect jobs and community economic stability. Members of both parties on the Armed Services and Appropriations committees receive defense industry campaign contributions, both represent districts with defense jobs, and both face the same incentive structure. The result is that force structure reform cannot be advanced through single-party action and must instead be framed as a national security imperative that transcends partisan divisions.
