# Force Structure: Solutions

Reforming U.S. military force structure requires addressing the fundamental mismatch between strategy, resources, and force design. The solutions below are organized around six pillars: strategic force sizing, Total Force optimization, recruiting and retention reform, technology-enabled force design, base realignment, and institutional reform. Each solution addresses specific root causes identified in this analysis and is designed to be mutually reinforcing with the others. No single reform can resolve force structure dysfunction; the solutions must be pursued in concert.

---

## 1. Establish a Rigorous Force-Sizing Framework

### A. Link Force Structure to Strategy Through a Quadrennial Force Structure Review

- **Problem Addressed**: The persistent gap between the National Defense Strategy's mission assignments and the force available to execute them (Root Cause 1). The current process allows the strategy to expand without constraint while the force is sized to the budget rather than to the strategy.
- **Description**: Require the Secretary of Defense to conduct a Quadrennial Force Structure Review (QFSR) that explicitly sizes the force to the National Defense Strategy. The review would model required force structure against specific warfighting scenarios (China in the Western Pacific, Russia in Europe, regional contingencies), identify gaps, and present Congress with options and associated costs. The QFSR would replace the current ad hoc process in which each service develops its own force structure plan with minimal joint oversight.
- **Implementation**: Mandate the QFSR by statute, with CBO and GAO independent cost estimates required. The review would be submitted to Congress alongside the President's Budget Request, creating a direct link between strategy, force structure, and funding. The QFSR would include a classified annex with detailed warfighting analysis and an unclassified summary enabling public debate.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Ends the practice of sizing the force to the budget rather than the strategy. Creates transparency and accountability by forcing explicit acknowledgment of the risks accepted when the budget does not fully fund the recommended force. Provides Congress with a defensible basis for making force structure tradeoffs rather than relying on service testimony and lobbying.
- **International Comparison**: The United Kingdom's Integrated Review (2021) and Australia's Defence Strategic Review (2023) both represent recent attempts by allied nations to rigorously link force structure to strategy. The Australian review led to a fundamental reorientation of the Australian Defence Force toward maritime strike and undersea capabilities, demonstrating that such reviews can drive real change when backed by political commitment.

### B. Adopt a "1+2" Force-Sizing Construct

- **Problem Addressed**: The vague and contested force-sizing constructs of recent decades, which have failed to provide a clear, defensible basis for determining force size (Root Cause 1).
- **Description**: Replace the vague and contested force-sizing constructs with a clear "1+2" framework: the force must be able to fight and win one major conflict (e.g., against China in the Western Pacific) while simultaneously deterring a second adversary (e.g., Russia in Europe) and maintaining essential counterterrorism and presence operations globally. This construct is more realistic than the old "2MTW" standard (which the force could never actually meet) and more rigorous than the current NDS's implicit "1+" construct.
- **Implementation**: Use the QFSR to define the specific force requirements for each scenario, including pre-positioned equipment, forward-deployed forces, surge timelines, and munitions consumption rates. Publish unclassified summaries so that Congress and the public can evaluate the adequacy of the force. The "win" scenario would be modeled with realistic assumptions about attrition, industrial surge, and ally contributions -- not the optimistic assumptions that have historically inflated assessments of the force's adequacy.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Provides a transparent, defensible basis for force-sizing decisions. Prevents the services from inflating requirements for budget purposes while also preventing unrealistic assumptions about what a smaller force can accomplish. Forces honest accounting of the gap between strategy and resources.

## 2. Optimize the Total Force: Active-Reserve Rebalancing

### A. Reform the Total Force Policy

- **Problem Addressed**: The ambiguous status of the reserve component, which oscillates between "strategic reserve" and "operational reserve" without clear expectations for readiness, mobilization timelines, or mission allocation (Root Causes 2, 3).
- **Description**: Update the Total Force Policy to reflect post-9/11 realities. The Guard and Reserve should not be a "strategic reserve" held back for major war or an "operational reserve" deployed routinely, but a "tiered readiness reserve" with units at specified readiness levels matched to strategic timelines.
- **Implementation**: Establish three tiers:
    - **Tier 1 (Enhanced Readiness)**: Deployable within 30 days, at near-active readiness. Includes selected Guard BCTs, ANG fighter squadrons, and Reserve units with critical specialties. These units would receive increased training days (48+ per year versus the standard 39), additional full-time support staff, and priority equipment. Examples: 29th Infantry Division (Virginia/Maryland National Guard), 116th Air Control Wing (Georgia ANG).
    - **Tier 2 (Standard Readiness)**: Deployable within 90 days, requiring limited post-mobilization training. Includes most combat support and combat service support units. These units maintain standard drill schedules and participate in annual training exercises.
    - **Tier 3 (Strategic Reserve)**: Deployable within 180+ days, maintained at lower readiness for surge capacity. Includes units that would be needed only in a prolonged major conflict or for homeland defense missions below the threshold requiring rapid deployment.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Creates predictability for Guard and Reserve members and their employers. Reduces the cost of maintaining forces at high readiness when they are not needed for near-term contingencies. Ensures that the most critical reserve units receive adequate funding and training while acknowledging that not every unit needs to be at the same readiness level.

### B. Shift Appropriate Missions to the Reserve Component

- **Problem Addressed**: The inefficient allocation of missions between active and reserve components, driven by bureaucratic inertia rather than cost-effectiveness analysis (Root Cause 2).
- **Description**: Identify missions that do not require permanent active-duty presence and transfer them to the Guard or Reserve. Candidates include: cyber operations (where commercial-sector expertise in the reserve component often exceeds active-duty capability), civil affairs, military police, medical units, logistics, certain air sovereignty missions, and some intelligence functions.
- **Implementation**: Conduct a comprehensive "mission-suitability analysis" that evaluates each mission against criteria including response timeline, training intensity, equipment requirements, deployment frequency, and the availability of civilian-sector skills in the reserve population. Transfer missions where reserve component execution is feasible and cost-effective. Provide adequate resources (training days, equipment, full-time support) to receiving reserve units.
- **Evidence Base**: CBO estimates that transferring units from the active component to the reserve component saves approximately 40-60% of personnel costs per unit. The Army Reserve already provides over 50% of the Army's civil affairs capability and 70% of its medical capability. The Air National Guard successfully performs the air sovereignty alert mission (protecting U.S. airspace from unauthorized aircraft) at 16 sites across the country, demonstrating that even high-readiness missions can be performed by the reserve component.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Frees active-duty end strength for roles that require full-time readiness (forward presence, rapid response, high-intensity combat) while leveraging the reserve component's cost advantage for missions that allow longer mobilization timelines.

## 3. Recruiting and Retention Reform

### A. Military Compensation Modernization

- **Problem Addressed**: Military pay that is not competitive with private-sector alternatives, particularly for junior enlisted personnel and high-demand technical specialties (Root Cause 4).
- **Description**: Overhaul military compensation to be competitive with private-sector alternatives for the demographics the military needs to recruit.
- **Implementation**:
    - Increase basic pay for junior enlisted (E-1 through E-4) by 15-20% to close the gap with civilian entry-level wages. An E-1 currently earns approximately $24,000/year; a 20% increase would bring this to approximately $29,000, still modest but competitive with the federal minimum wage and many entry-level civilian positions.
    - Expand targeted bonuses for critical specialties (cyber, intelligence, maintenance, medical, nuclear) using real-time labor market data from the Bureau of Labor Statistics to set bonus levels that reflect actual civilian opportunity costs.
    - Reform the housing allowance (BAH) to reflect actual local housing costs, particularly in high-cost areas (San Diego, Northern Virginia, Hawaii) where BAH has not kept pace with housing inflation.
    - Create a "GI Bill Plus" that provides enhanced education benefits (including debt-free college during service through expanded tuition assistance) as a recruiting incentive, building on the demonstrated power of education benefits as the single most effective recruiting tool.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Addresses the most frequently cited barrier to enlistment (compensation) without fundamental changes to the AVF model. The 9th Quadrennial Review of Military Compensation (QRMC) found that pay competitiveness is the single most important factor in both recruiting and retention.

### B. Expand the Eligible Population

- **Problem Addressed**: The shrinking pool of young Americans who meet military eligibility requirements (Root Cause 4).
- **Description**: Reduce the barriers that currently exclude 77% of 17-24-year-olds from military service.
- **Implementation**:
    - Establish a "Future Soldier Preparatory Course" (the Army's pilot program) across all services, providing candidates who fail initial fitness or academic standards with a structured pathway to qualification. The Army's FSPC, launched at Fort Jackson in 2022, has successfully qualified approximately 95% of participants, adding thousands of soldiers who would otherwise have been turned away.
    - Modernize medical and moral waiver standards to reflect current science (e.g., many disqualifying conditions like mild asthma, childhood ADHD, and eczema are no longer medically relevant to military service). The Military Entrance Processing Command (MEPCOM) standards have not been comprehensively updated since the 1990s.
    - Expand ROTC and Junior ROTC presence in underrepresented communities, particularly urban areas and regions with low military propensity. Currently, JROTC programs are concentrated in the South and rural areas that already have high propensity; expanding into urban schools in the Northeast, Midwest, and Pacific Coast could reach untapped populations.
    - Create a "National Service Gateway" that partners with AmeriCorps, Peace Corps, and other national service programs to provide a pathway from civilian national service to military service, creating a broader on-ramp to the military.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Expands the recruiting pool without lowering standards for service. The Army's FSPC model demonstrates that many young Americans who initially fail eligibility screening can be brought to standard with relatively modest investment.

### C. Improve Quality of Life

- **Problem Addressed**: Non-monetary factors that drive attrition and reduce propensity to serve (Root Cause 4).
- **Description**: Address the systemic quality-of-life deficiencies that drive experienced personnel out of the military and discourage potential recruits.
- **Implementation**:
    - Invest $10 billion over 5 years in military housing, childcare, and spouse employment programs. Military housing conditions have been a persistent scandal: a Reuters investigation in 2018 documented widespread mold, lead paint, and pest infestations in privatized military housing managed by companies like Balfour Beatty and Lincoln Military Housing.
    - Implement deployment predictability reforms, including hard caps on deployment length (maximum 7 months for Navy, 9 months for Army/Marines) and guaranteed dwell time (minimum 1:3 for active, 1:5 for reserve).
    - Expand mental health resources and reduce stigma through command climate reforms. The military loses approximately 500 service members to suicide annually, and mental health stigma remains a barrier to care-seeking.
    - Modernize assignment processes to give service members more control over their career trajectories, including "talent marketplace" models (the Army's Assignment Interactive Module 2.0) that match soldiers to positions based on mutual preferences rather than purely centralized assignment.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Improves retention rates (which are more cost-effective than recruiting -- retaining an experienced NCO is far cheaper than recruiting and training a replacement), enhances family readiness, and makes military service more attractive to potential recruits.

## 4. Technology-Enabled Force Design

### A. Accelerate Autonomous Systems Integration

- **Problem Addressed**: The slow pace of integrating unmanned systems that could multiply force effectiveness without proportional increases in manpower (Root Cause 5).
- **Description**: Aggressively integrate unmanned and autonomous systems into the force structure to multiply the effectiveness of human forces and partially offset recruiting shortfalls.
- **Implementation**:
    - The Navy's plan for unmanned surface and subsurface vessels should be funded and accelerated, with a goal of 150+ unmanned vessels by 2035. The Large Unmanned Surface Vessel (LUSV) and Medium Unmanned Surface Vessel (MUSV) programs should transition from technology demonstration to acquisition programs of record.
    - The Air Force's Collaborative Combat Aircraft (CCA) program should field initial operational squadrons by 2028, pairing autonomous wingmen with manned fighters at a ratio of at least 2:1. Anduril and General Atomics were selected as CCA Increment 1 contractors in 2024.
    - The Army should field autonomous logistics vehicles (Autonomous Ground Resupply) and unmanned reconnaissance systems (Future Tactical UAS) at the brigade level by 2027, reducing the personnel footprint of logistics and reconnaissance formations.
    - All services should establish dedicated "manned-unmanned teaming" units to develop doctrine, tactics, and training for human-machine operations.
- **Evidence Base**: Ukraine's effective use of low-cost drones (both commercial quadcopters and purpose-built systems like the Bayraktar TB2) has demonstrated that unmanned systems can provide reconnaissance, strike, and electronic warfare capabilities at a fraction of the cost of manned platforms. Israel's use of autonomous systems in the Heron TP and Harop programs provides additional evidence of operational utility.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Unmanned systems can perform high-risk missions (mine clearance, ISR, forward resupply, suppression of enemy air defenses) without risking human lives. They also reduce the personnel demand of the force, partially offsetting recruiting shortfalls and allowing the military to distribute capability more widely without proportional increases in manpower.

### B. Invest in Distributed Operations

- **Problem Addressed**: The vulnerability of concentrated formations to precision strike in a great power conflict (Root Cause 5).
- **Description**: Redesign force structure for distributed, survivable operations rather than concentrated formations that are vulnerable to precision strike and area-effect weapons.
- **Implementation**: Fund the Marine Corps' Force Design 2030 vision (Marine Littoral Regiments operating within the adversary's weapons engagement zone), the Army's Multi-Domain Task Forces (integrating fires, cyber, EW, space, and information across 1,000+ kilometer operational areas), and the Air Force's Agile Combat Employment concept (dispersing aircraft from large, vulnerable main operating bases to austere forward locations). These require smaller, more mobile units that can operate independently, disperse to avoid detection, and mass effects through networked fires rather than physical concentration.
- **Evidence Base**: The Russia-Ukraine war has demonstrated that large, concentrated formations are highly vulnerable to precision strike, drone surveillance, and electronic targeting. Russian forces suffered devastating losses when they concentrated armor columns on roads during the early invasion. The lesson is that future force structure must prioritize distribution and survivability over mass concentration.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Improves survivability against peer competitors with precision strike capabilities. Reduces dependence on large, expensive, and vulnerable platforms (e.g., aircraft carriers in contested waters near Chinese A2/AD systems) while maintaining the ability to generate decisive combat power through distributed but networked forces.

## 5. Base Realignment

### A. Authorize a New BRAC Round

- **Problem Addressed**: An estimated 20-25% excess base infrastructure that wastes $5-8 billion annually in operating costs (Root Cause 3).
- **Description**: Congress should authorize a new Base Realignment and Closure round to eliminate excess infrastructure and redirect savings to readiness and modernization.
- **Implementation**: Pass BRAC authorization legislation with the following reforms to address criticisms of the 2005 round:
    - Enhanced community transition assistance: 5-year federal economic adjustment grants (minimum $50 million per affected community), job retraining programs, and priority access to federal economic development programs.
    - Transparent, strategy-driven criteria: The BRAC Commission must use publicly reviewable criteria linked to the QFSR, with no classified criteria that cannot be challenged.
    - Environmental remediation commitment: Federal commitment to remediate environmental contamination at closed installations within 10 years, removing a major community concern.
    - Independent audit: GAO audit of the BRAC process within 2 years of completion to verify savings and identify lessons learned.
- **Evidence Base**: Previous BRAC rounds save approximately $12 billion annually. A new round could save an additional $2-5 billion per year. The five previous BRAC rounds closed or realigned over 350 installations. Academic research (Hooker and Knetter, 2001) found that most BRAC communities experience short-term economic disruption but recover within 5-10 years, with many exceeding pre-closure economic performance.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Eliminates excess infrastructure that drains resources from readiness and modernization. Demonstrates fiscal discipline and provides a credible mechanism for aligning the base structure with the force it supports.

### B. Consolidate Overseas Basing

- **Problem Addressed**: A global base structure inherited from the Cold War that does not align with current strategic priorities (Root Cause 3).
- **Description**: Rationalize the overseas base network to focus on theaters of strategic priority (Indo-Pacific, Europe) while reducing legacy presence elsewhere.
- **Implementation**: Negotiate expanded access agreements in the Western Pacific (Philippines EDCA sites, Japan force posture realignment, Australia rotational presence, Palau compact of free association) while reducing permanent garrisons in locations that no longer align with strategic priorities. Shift from large permanent bases to smaller, distributed access points with pre-positioned equipment, adopting the "Places, Not Bases" concept advocated by defense strategists.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Improves posture for great power competition while reducing the cost and political complications of large permanent overseas garrisons. The Indo-Pacific reposture -- including Marine rotational deployments to Australia, EDCA expansion in the Philippines, and Guam defense buildup -- represents the most significant overseas basing shift since the end of the Cold War.

## 6. Institutional Reform

### A. Strengthen Joint Force Requirements

- **Problem Addressed**: Service parochialism in force structure planning that prioritizes institutional preferences over joint warfighting needs (Root Cause 2).
- **Description**: Give combatant commanders greater influence over force structure decisions to counterbalance service parochialism.
- **Implementation**: Require the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs to submit an annual "Joint Force Requirements Assessment" to Congress, independently evaluating whether the services' force structure plans meet combatant commander needs. Give the CJCS budget certification authority for joint priority programs -- the power to certify that specific programs are essential for joint warfighting and should receive priority funding regardless of service preferences. Reform the Joint Requirements Oversight Council (JROC) to include combatant commander representatives with voting authority, breaking the service monopoly on requirements validation.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Creates a counterweight to service-centric planning. Ensures that force structure decisions are driven by operational requirements rather than institutional preferences. Provides Congress with an independent assessment of whether the force is structured for joint warfighting or for service budget protection.

### B. Reform the Planning, Programming, Budgeting, and Execution (PPBE) System

- **Problem Addressed**: The PPBE system, designed by Secretary Robert McNamara in the 1960s, is too slow and rigid for modern force design (Root Cause 1).
- **Description**: The PPBE system takes 2-3 years from initial planning to enacted appropriation, making it impossible to respond quickly to emerging threats or opportunities. Reform it to enable more rapid reallocation of resources.
- **Implementation**: Implement recommendations from the bipartisan PPBE Commission (2024), including:
    - Streamlining budget categories to reduce the number of line items Congress must appropriate, giving DoD more flexibility to reallocate within categories.
    - Increasing reprogramming flexibility by raising the thresholds that trigger Congressional notification, allowing faster movement of funds to urgent needs.
    - Creating "innovation accounts" funded at 1-2% of the defense topline that bypass traditional programming timelines and can be allocated by the Secretary of Defense to emerging priorities.
    - Adopting portfolio-based budgeting that groups programs by mission area (e.g., "Indo-Pacific deterrence") rather than by service or appropriation category, enabling tradeoff analysis across service boundaries.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Enables faster adaptation to changing threats and opportunities. Reduces the 2-3 year lag between identifying a force structure need and funding it. Allows the military to respond to lessons from current conflicts (like Ukraine) in months rather than years.
