# Force Structure: Root Causes

## Systemic Analysis

Force structure debates are not the result of any single policy failure. They persist because of deep structural tensions built into the American defense establishment -- tensions between strategy and resources, between the services and the joint force, between political incentives and military requirements, and between the demands of current operations and the need to prepare for future wars. Each actor in the system -- the services, Congress, the defense industry, combatant commanders, and the White House -- behaves rationally within the incentive structure they face, yet the collective outcome is a force that is simultaneously too small for its stated strategy, too expensive for its budget, and too slow to adapt to new threats. Understanding these root causes is essential to designing reforms that can change outcomes rather than merely rearranging the bureaucracy.

## 1. The Strategy-Resource Mismatch

The most fundamental cause of force structure dysfunction is the persistent gap between the strategy the military is asked to execute and the resources it is given.

- **Strategy Exceeds Capacity**: Every National Defense Strategy since the end of the Cold War has assigned the military more missions than it can simultaneously execute with the force it has. The 2022 NDS requires deterring China, deterring Russia, countering terrorism, maintaining global presence, and assuring allies. The 2024 Commission on the National Defense Strategy concluded that the current force "is not sized, shaped, or postured for the security environment we face." No administration has been willing to either adequately fund the strategy or reduce the missions to match available forces, because both choices carry political costs.
- **Sizing Construct Dysfunction**: The "two-major-theater-war" construct that anchored force sizing from 1993 to 2017 was never a realistic planning tool -- it was a political compromise to justify a force that was large enough to satisfy the services but small enough to deliver a "peace dividend." The shift to "one-plus" or "one major war with the ability to deter" under recent NDSs has not resolved the fundamental mismatch. The sizing construct itself has become a political artifact rather than a strategic planning tool: the services know that the construct determines their budget share, so they shape their threat assessments and requirements to justify the force they want rather than the force the construct requires.
- **Budget-Strategy Disconnect**: The defense budget is set through the political process (annual appropriations, continuing resolutions, sequestration) rather than through a rigorous analysis of what the strategy requires. The result is that the force is sized to the budget rather than the budget being sized to the force. Continuing resolutions, which have been the norm rather than the exception for defense appropriations, prevent the DoD from starting new programs or increasing production rates, further disconnecting strategy from resources. Between FY2010 and FY2024, the DoD operated under a continuing resolution for at least part of every fiscal year.
- **The Biennial Budget Commission's Warning**: The Commission on PPBE Reform (2024) found that the Pentagon's budget process takes approximately 2-3 years from initial planning to enacted appropriation, meaning that force structure decisions are based on threat assessments and strategic assumptions that are years out of date by the time funding arrives.

## 2. Service Parochialism

Each military service has powerful institutional incentives to protect its traditional force structure, even when doing so conflicts with joint requirements or the national strategy.

- **Institutional Identity**: Each service defines itself by its core platforms and formations -- the Army by its divisions and brigades, the Navy by its carriers and submarines, the Air Force by its fighter wings, the Marines by their expeditionary forces. Proposals to reduce these core elements are resisted as existential threats. As Carl Builder demonstrated in *The Masks of War* (1989), the services have distinct institutional cultures and identities that shape force structure decisions far more powerfully than rational strategic analysis. The Army sees itself as the "indispensable service" for sustained ground combat; the Navy sees itself as the guarantor of global access and freedom of navigation; the Air Force sees itself as the provider of precision strike and information dominance; and the Marine Corps sees itself as the nation's "force in readiness."
- **Budget Competition**: The services compete for shares of the defense budget. Protecting end strength and platform counts is the primary mechanism for justifying budget share. An Army that shrinks from 31 to 24 BCTs will receive a proportionally smaller share of the top line. This creates a perverse incentive to maintain unit counts even when individual units are under-resourced, producing the "hollow force" phenomenon where formations exist on paper but lack the personnel, equipment, and training to fight.
- **Congressional Alliances**: Each service cultivates Congressional champions who protect their programs. The "Iron Triangle" of service headquarters, defense industry, and Congressional committees creates a self-reinforcing system that resists structural change. The Association of the United States Army (AUSA), the Navy League, the Air & Space Forces Association, and the Marine Corps Association function as lobbying arms for their respective services, mobilizing retired officers, defense workers, and community leaders to oppose unfavorable force structure decisions.
- **Joint vs. Service Culture**: Despite Goldwater-Nichols, the services retain dominant influence over force structure decisions because they "organize, train, and equip" the force (Title 10 responsibility). Combatant commanders, who actually employ the force in operations, have limited influence over how it is structured. The Joint Requirements Oversight Council (JROC), intended to validate requirements from a joint perspective, is chaired by the Vice Chairman of the Joint Chiefs but staffed by service representatives who advocate for their service's programs. Multiple studies, including the 2024 PPBE Reform Commission report, have found that the JROC has not been effective at prioritizing joint requirements over service preferences.

## 3. Political Constraints on Base Closures and Force Reductions

Force structure decisions have enormous political implications for communities, states, and Congressional districts.

- **BRAC Paralysis**: Congress has not authorized a BRAC round since 2005, despite DoD estimates of 20-25% excess infrastructure capacity. Members of Congress resist BRAC because base closures devastate local economies -- a single base can be the largest employer in a district. Every proposed BRAC round generates bipartisan opposition from affected delegations, and the logrolling dynamic (members trading votes to protect each other's bases) produces majorities against BRAC even when a majority would benefit from the savings. The 2005 BRAC round was criticized for political manipulation after the commission deviated from purely military criteria, and this perception has been used to block subsequent rounds.
- **Geographic Distribution of Defense Spending**: Military bases, defense industry facilities, and R&D centers are distributed across all 50 states by deliberate political design. This distribution makes every force structure decision a political decision: reducing a weapons program or closing a base affects jobs and votes in specific districts. The F-35 program, with suppliers in 45+ states, is the paradigmatic example of geographic distribution as political insurance, but the same principle applies to Army bases, Navy shipyards, and Air Force installations.
- **The "Tyranny of Legacy"**: Existing programs and bases develop political constituencies that outlive their strategic rationale. The M1 Abrams tank production line in Lima, Ohio continues to produce tanks the Army has not requested, because the Congressional delegation protects the jobs. The A-10 Warthog has survived multiple Air Force retirement attempts because its Congressional supporters block the cuts, overriding the Air Force's judgment that the aircraft is too vulnerable for contested airspace. The USS *Bonhomme Richard*, which burned in San Diego in 2020, was kept in service long past its optimal service life in part because decommissioning it would have reduced the amphibious fleet below the Marine Corps' stated requirement.
- **Community Economic Dependency**: In many cases, the relationship between a military installation and its host community creates a dependency that makes rational base decisions politically impossible. Fort Liberty (formerly Fort Bragg) is the largest employer in the Fayetteville, North Carolina metropolitan area. Naval Station Norfolk supports an economic ecosystem of over 100,000 direct and indirect jobs. These communities have limited economic diversification, making any reduction in the installation's footprint a potential economic catastrophe.

## 4. Recruiting Challenges in a Tight Labor Market

The All-Volunteer Force model depends on the military's ability to attract sufficient numbers of qualified volunteers. This assumption is under severe strain.

- **Demographic Headwinds**: The military-eligible population is shrinking due to rising obesity rates (42% of adults, per CDC), substance use, mental health conditions, and declining educational attainment. Only 23% of 17-24-year-olds meet military eligibility requirements without a waiver. The DoD's own analysis estimates that the eligible *and propensed* population -- those who are both qualified and willing -- numbers fewer than 500,000, a pool that the military, higher education, and the civilian labor market all compete for.
- **Economic Competition**: In a low-unemployment economy, the military competes directly with private employers who can offer higher pay, flexible work arrangements, and no risk of combat deployment. Military starting pay (approximately $24,000/year for an E-1) is below the federal minimum wage in several states when calculated hourly. While military compensation includes non-monetary benefits (healthcare, housing, education), these benefits are often poorly understood by potential recruits and their influencers (parents, teachers, coaches).
- **Cultural Disconnect**: The declining share of Americans with personal or familial connection to military service means fewer young people consider the military as a career option. The geographic concentration of military families in a shrinking number of states and communities reinforces this disconnect. Fewer than 1% of the population serves in uniform, and the percentage of Americans who know someone currently serving has declined steadily since the end of the draft. This creates an information vacuum that is filled by media portrayals, social media, and peer impressions -- sources that rarely provide an accurate picture of military life.
- **Propensity to Serve**: Survey data consistently show declining interest in military service among young Americans. Factors include negative perceptions of military quality of life, concerns about political use of the military, competing career opportunities in the tech and services sectors, and a generational shift toward valuing work-life balance and personal autonomy that the military's hierarchical structure struggles to accommodate.
- **Quality vs. Quantity**: The recruiting crisis creates pressure to lower standards to fill billets, but lowering standards increases attrition, disciplinary problems, and training costs. The Army experienced this dynamic in 2005-2007 when it increased the number of "moral waivers" (allowing recruits with criminal records) to sustain surge-era end strength, resulting in measurable increases in indiscipline and misconduct. The current Army leadership has resisted lowering standards, choosing to accept under-strength formations rather than compromise quality.

## 5. Technology vs. Manpower Tradeoffs

The relationship between technology and force size is a persistent source of debate.

- **The "Quality Over Quantity" Argument**: Proponents argue that advanced technology (precision weapons, autonomous systems, AI-enabled command and control) can offset numerical disadvantage. A smaller force with better technology may be more effective than a larger force with legacy equipment. The Gulf War seemed to validate this thesis: a coalition force of 540,000 defeated an Iraqi army of over 1 million with minimal casualties. This success drove the RMA (Revolution in Military Affairs) consensus that technology could substitute for mass.
- **The Attrition Problem**: Critics counter that technology cannot eliminate the need for mass. In a prolonged great power conflict, attrition will consume platforms and people faster than they can be replaced. A "perfect but small" force that cannot absorb losses will fail. The Russia-Ukraine war has provided the most compelling contemporary evidence: both sides have suffered massive equipment losses (Russia has lost over 3,000 tanks as of early 2025), and the war has settled into an attritional struggle where production capacity and manpower reserves matter as much as technological quality.
- **The Adaptation Gap**: The services are slow to adopt new technologies due to lengthy acquisition timelines (15-20 years from concept to fielding for major systems), risk-averse testing cultures, and the institutional resistance of communities whose platforms are being replaced. The Air Force's F-35 program took 25 years from concept to full-rate production. The Army's Future Combat Systems (FCS) program was canceled in 2009 after $18 billion in investment. The Pentagon's history of failed technology programs makes the services rationally cautious about betting force structure on unproven systems.
- **Human-Machine Teaming**: The emerging concept of human-machine teaming -- where autonomous systems augment rather than replace human forces -- could in theory allow a smaller human force multiplied by unmanned systems. But the technology, doctrine, and organizational structures for this are immature. The Air Force's Collaborative Combat Aircraft program and the Navy's unmanned surface vessel program are the most advanced initiatives, but neither has achieved operational deployment. The gap between concept and reality remains years or decades wide.
- **The Munitions Industrial Base**: The Russia-Ukraine war has also exposed critical weaknesses in the munitions industrial base. The U.S. has provided Ukraine with over $50 billion in military aid, including Javelin anti-tank missiles, HIMARS rocket systems, and 155mm artillery ammunition, drawing down U.S. stockpiles to levels that would be insufficient for a simultaneous major conflict. The Army's 155mm ammunition production rate has been increased from 14,000 rounds per month to a target of 100,000 rounds per month, but this surge has taken years to achieve. The lesson is that force structure is not just about platforms and people -- it is also about the industrial capacity to sustain combat operations.

## 6. Operational Tempo and Force Management

The way the military manages its force creates its own structural problems.

- **Demand Signal**: Combatant commanders consistently request more forces than the military can provide. The "global force management" process that allocates forces to commands is perpetually oversubscribed, creating a gap between demand and supply that drives overextension. In any given year, combatant commander force requests exceed available supply by 30-50%, requiring the Secretary of Defense to accept risk by denying requests from lower-priority theaters.
- **Readiness-Modernization Tradeoff**: In constrained budgets, the services face a zero-sum choice between spending on current readiness (training, maintenance, spare parts) and investing in future capabilities (R&D, new platforms). The historical pattern is to sacrifice modernization for near-term readiness during budget squeezes, creating a future force structure crisis. Alternatively, during periods of relative peace, the services may sacrifice readiness for modernization, producing a well-equipped but poorly trained force -- as occurred in the late 1990s when the Air Force prioritized F-22 and JSF development while allowing flying-hour programs to decline.
- **Deployment Burden Distribution**: A disproportionate share of deployment burden falls on specific communities -- submariners, special operations forces, intelligence analysts, cyber operators, and maintenance personnel -- creating burnout and retention crises in the most in-demand specialties. The Navy's submarine community, for example, has a deployment-to-dwell ratio significantly worse than the 1:3 target, contributing to retention rates among mid-grade officers that have fallen below 40% in some year groups.

## Causal Chain of Force Structure Dysfunction

```text
National Defense Strategy assigns missions exceeding capacity
    |
    v
Services protect institutional equities, resist restructuring
    |
    v
Congress blocks base closures and protects legacy programs
    |
    v
Budget is sized politically, not strategically
    |
    v
Recruiting shortfalls prevent even authorized end strength
    |
    v
Technology adoption too slow to substitute for manpower
    |
    v
Munitions and industrial base cannot sustain prolonged conflict
    |
    v
Result: A force that is simultaneously too small for its strategy,
        too expensive for its budget, too slow to adapt,
        and too fragile to sustain attrition
```
