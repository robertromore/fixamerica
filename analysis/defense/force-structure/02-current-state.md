# Force Structure: Current State

## Active Duty End Strength by Service

The authorized and actual end strength of the active-duty force reflects both strategic requirements and recruiting realities. As of FY2024, all services except the Space Force are at or below authorized levels, with the Army experiencing the most significant shortfall. The total active-duty force has declined by approximately 57,000 from FY2020 levels, driven primarily by recruiting challenges and deliberate end-strength reductions in the Marine Corps.

| Service | Authorized End Strength (FY2024) | Actual End Strength (est.) | Change from FY2020 | Key Platform/Unit Metric |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Army | 452,000 | ~445,000 | -37,000 | 31 Active BCTs |
| Navy | 337,800 | ~332,000 | -8,000 | 296 Battle Force Ships |
| Marine Corps | 172,300 | ~170,000 | -14,000 | 3 Marine Expeditionary Forces |
| Air Force | 325,100 | ~320,000 | -7,000 | ~40 Tactical Fighter Squadrons |
| Space Force | 9,400 | ~9,400 | +9,400 (new) | 5 Delta commands |
| **Total** | **~1,296,600** | **~1,276,400** | **~-57,000** | |

### Army Force Structure

- **Brigade Combat Teams (BCTs)**: The Army maintains 31 active-duty BCTs (11 Armored, 7 Stryker, 13 Infantry) and 27 National Guard BCTs. This is down from a peak of 45 active BCTs during the Iraq/Afghanistan surge. The Army is restructuring BCTs to add long-range precision fires and air defense capabilities for great power competition. Each BCT comprises approximately 4,000-4,700 soldiers depending on type, with armored BCTs being the largest.
- **Multi-Domain Task Forces (MDTFs)**: The Army is standing up five MDTFs designed to integrate fires, cyber, electronic warfare, space, and information operations for theater-level effects. Two are operational (I MDTF at Joint Base Lewis-McChord for the Pacific theater and the 2nd MDTF in Wiesbaden, Germany for Europe), with the 3rd MDTF forming at Fort Liberty for global contingencies. MDTFs represent the Army's primary organizational innovation for great power competition, providing capabilities that previously resided only at echelons above corps.
- **Divisions and Corps**: The Army is rebuilding division and corps-level headquarters capabilities that atrophied during two decades of brigade-centric counterinsurgency operations. The Army activated the reestablished V Corps headquarters at Fort Knox in 2020 and forward-deployed it to Poznan, Poland in 2022. The 10 active-duty division headquarters are being revitalized as warfighting commands rather than administrative organizations, a shift the Army calls "division-centric" warfighting.
- **Army Pre-Positioned Stocks (APS)**: The Army maintains pre-positioned equipment sets in Europe (APS-2, with a full armored brigade set in Belgium and the Netherlands), Korea (APS-4), and afloat (APS-3). These sets enable rapid reinforcement by allowing units to fly into theater and draw equipment rather than shipping it. Post-Ukraine invasion, the Army increased APS-2 from one to two brigade sets.
- **Aviation**: Army aviation comprises approximately 3,700 rotary-wing aircraft, including AH-64 Apache attack helicopters, UH-60 Black Hawk utility helicopters, and CH-47 Chinook heavy-lift helicopters. The Future Vertical Lift (FVL) program, which was to produce next-generation replacements, was restructured in 2024 when the Future Attack Reconnaissance Aircraft (FARA) program was canceled after an $8 billion investment, redirecting funds toward unmanned aerial systems and other modernization priorities.

### Navy Force Structure

- **Battle Force Ships**: The Navy currently fields approximately 296 battle force ships against a stated requirement of 381 ships (the Navy's 2016 Force Structure Assessment). Independent analyses (e.g., CBO, Hudson Institute) have recommended fleets ranging from 355 to over 500 ships. The gap between the current fleet and any stated requirement has widened steadily since the mid-2000s, driven by an industrial base that cannot produce ships fast enough to replace aging vessels.
- **Carrier Strike Groups**: 11 aircraft carriers (CVNs) and their associated air wings, cruisers, and destroyers. The carrier force is the backbone of global power projection. The newest carrier, USS Gerald R. Ford (CVN-78), achieved initial operational capability in 2022 after a troubled development that saw costs rise from $10.5 billion to $13.3 billion and a 4-year schedule delay. The follow-on USS John F. Kennedy (CVN-79) is expected to deliver in 2025. The Ticonderoga-class cruisers that anchor carrier strike groups are being decommissioned, with the DDG(X) next-generation destroyer not expected until the 2030s, creating a potential surface combatant gap.
- **Submarine Force**: 49 attack submarines (SSNs), 14 ballistic missile submarines (SSBNs), and 4 guided missile submarines (SSGNs). The attack submarine fleet is projected to decline to as few as 46 boats in the late 2020s before AUKUS commitments (selling 3-5 Virginia-class submarines to Australia) and new construction stabilize it. The Columbia-class SSBN program -- replacing the aging Ohio-class -- is the Navy's top acquisition priority, with the first boat expected to deliver in the late 2020s at an estimated program cost of $132+ billion for 12 submarines. Submarine maintenance backlogs at the four public shipyards (Norfolk, Portsmouth, Puget Sound, Pearl Harbor) have caused submarines to spend an average of 33 months in maintenance versus a planned 25 months, reducing operational availability.
- **Amphibious Ships**: 31 amphibious warfare ships, below the Marine Corps' stated requirement of 31 L-class ships for 2.0 Marine Expeditionary Brigades. The Commandant of the Marine Corps has identified 31 as a "minimum" and argued that the Navy's amphibious fleet decline threatens the nation's ability to project power ashore. The new Light Amphibious Warship (LAW) program is designed to support Marine Littoral Regiments with smaller, more affordable vessels, but funding has been contentious.
- **Shipbuilding Challenges**: The industrial base can produce approximately 7-9 ships per year. Sustaining a 355+ ship navy would require 12-15 ships per year, a rate not achieved since the Reagan-era 600-ship navy buildup of the 1980s. The two nuclear-capable shipyards -- Huntington Ingalls Industries' Newport News Shipbuilding and General Dynamics' Electric Boat -- face workforce shortages of approximately 10,000-15,000 skilled workers combined. The Navy's FY2024 shipbuilding budget requested 9 new ships, but the actual delivery rate has been 6-7 per year due to construction delays.

### Air Force Force Structure

- **Fighter/Attack Aircraft**: Approximately 2,000 total fighter and attack aircraft across active, Reserve, and Guard components. The average age of the fighter fleet is over 30 years -- the oldest in Air Force history. The Air Force plans to retire older F-15C/Ds and F-16C/Ds as F-35As arrive, but F-35 production rates (approximately 100-130 per year across all variants and customers) have not kept pace with the retirement of legacy airframes. The resulting "fighter gap" is projected to worsen through the late 2020s before stabilizing.
- **Next Generation Air Dominance (NGAD)**: The Air Force's planned sixth-generation fighter was restructured in 2024, with the service reconsidering whether a crewed fighter costing an estimated $300+ million per aircraft is the right approach versus a more affordable design complemented by autonomous wingmen. The program's future remains uncertain and illustrates the tension between exquisite capability and affordable mass.
- **Collaborative Combat Aircraft (CCA)**: The Air Force's autonomous wingman program, formerly known as Loyal Wingman, envisions 1,000+ unmanned aircraft that operate alongside manned fighters. Anduril Industries and General Atomics were selected as CCA Increment 1 contractors in 2024, with initial operational capability targeted for the late 2020s. CCA represents the Air Force's primary attempt to use autonomous systems to increase force capacity without proportional increases in pilot requirements.
- **Bomber Force**: 141 bombers (B-52H, B-1B, B-2A), with the B-21 Raider entering production at Northrop Grumman's Palmdale, California facility. The B-21 completed its first flight in November 2023 and is expected to reach initial operational capability in the mid-2020s. The Air Force plans to procure at least 100 B-21s. The B-1B fleet is being retired due to structural fatigue after decades of high operational tempo, with the last B-1Bs expected to leave service by the early 2030s.
- **Tanker Force**: 396 tanker aircraft (KC-135, KC-46, KC-10). The KC-46A Pegasus, built by Boeing, has experienced significant developmental delays and deficiencies, including problems with the Remote Vision System (RVS) that prevented it from refueling stealth aircraft until a redesigned RVS 2.0 was fielded. The KC-135 fleet averages over 60 years of age, and the Air Force has identified aerial refueling capacity as a critical enabler for Indo-Pacific operations.
- **Combat Air Patrols**: The Air Force has stated it needs 386 operational squadrons to meet the National Defense Strategy. It currently has approximately 312, a shortfall of approximately 74 squadrons representing one of the largest gaps between stated requirement and actual capacity in the force.

### Marine Corps Force Structure

- **Force Design 2030**: The Marine Corps is undergoing its most significant reorganization since World War II. Under Force Design 2030, the Corps is divesting heavy armor (all three active-duty tank battalions deactivated by 2023), reducing cannon artillery from 21 to 5 batteries, and investing in long-range precision fires (Navy/Marine Expeditionary Ship Interdiction System, or NMESIS, anti-ship missiles), unmanned systems, and Marine Littoral Regiments (MLRs) optimized for operations in the Western Pacific.
- **Marine Littoral Regiments**: Three MLRs are being activated (3rd MLR on Okinawa, 12th MLR in Hawaii, and a third to be determined), designed to operate as distributed, survivable forces within the weapons engagement zone of Chinese anti-access/area-denial (A2/AD) systems. Each MLR comprises approximately 1,800-2,000 Marines organized into a Littoral Combat Team, a Littoral Anti-Air Battalion, and a Littoral Logistics Battalion.
- **End Strength Reduction**: The Corps has voluntarily reduced its end strength from 186,000 to 172,000 to reallocate resources to modernization, trading manpower for capability in a deliberate "divest to invest" strategy.
- **Controversy**: Force Design 2030 is the most contested service-level transformation in a generation. A group of retired Marine generals and former senior officials -- including former Commandants and assistant commandants -- have publicly criticized the plan, arguing it makes the Marine Corps a "one-scenario force" optimized only for a China contingency while abandoning the general-purpose capability that has made the Corps useful across the full range of military operations. Defenders counter that the Corps must prioritize the pacing threat and that the legacy force was already inadequate for the Indo-Pacific environment.

### Space Force Force Structure

- **Organization**: The newest service, established by the National Defense Authorization Act for FY2020 and activated on December 20, 2019, with approximately 9,400 Guardians organized into three Field Commands (Space Operations Command at Peterson Space Force Base, Space Systems Command at Los Angeles Air Force Base, Space Training and Readiness Command at Peterson SFB) and five operational Deltas.
- **Mission**: Responsible for space domain awareness, satellite operations, missile warning and missile tracking, nuclear detonation detection, GPS operations, and satellite communications. The Space Force also operates the Space Surveillance Network, tracking over 47,000 objects in orbit.
- **Growth Challenges**: The Space Force is the smallest service and is still in the process of transferring personnel and missions from the Air Force, Army, and Navy. It faces a unique workforce challenge: its most critical skills (satellite operations, space systems engineering, orbital mechanics) are in high demand from the commercial space industry, where companies like SpaceX, Blue Origin, and Amazon's Project Kuiper offer significantly higher compensation. The Space Force has requested authority for a dedicated Space National Guard, currently blocked in Congress due to cost concerns and opposition from the Air National Guard.

## Reserve Component Strength

The Reserve component (Army Reserve, Navy Reserve, Marine Corps Reserve, Air Force Reserve, Army National Guard, Air National Guard) constitutes a significant portion of the Total Force and provides capabilities that would be prohibitively expensive to maintain on active duty.

| Component | End Strength (FY2024) | Primary Missions |
|---|---|---|
| Army National Guard | ~325,000 | 8 Division HQs, 27 BCTs, combat support, homeland defense |
| Army Reserve | ~189,000 | Logistics, medical, civil affairs, military police |
| Air National Guard | ~107,000 | Air sovereignty alert, fighter/airlift squadrons, cyber |
| Air Force Reserve | ~70,000 | Airlift, aerial refueling, rescue, weather |
| Navy Reserve | ~58,000 | Logistics, intelligence, construction (Seabees), medical |
| Marine Corps Reserve | ~36,000 | 1 Marine Division (4th MarDiv), 1 Air Wing (4th MAW) |

### Total Force Policy

The Total Force Policy, established in 1973 by Secretary of Defense James Schlesinger and codified by the Goldwater-Nichols Act (1986) and subsequent legislation, requires the Department of Defense to treat active and reserve components as an integrated force. The policy was originally conceived by Army Chief of Staff General Creighton Abrams, who deliberately placed critical combat support and combat service support capabilities in the reserve component to ensure that any future major deployment would require reserve mobilization -- and therefore Congressional and public engagement. In practice:

- **Reserve component forces provide approximately 39% of total military capability** at roughly 15% of the personnel cost per capita of active forces. CBO estimates the annual cost per active-duty soldier at approximately $130,000 versus $30,000-$50,000 for a reserve component soldier, depending on the unit's readiness tier.
- **The Army National Guard provides 32% of the Army's combat power** (27 of 58 total BCTs) and nearly 40% of its combat support and combat service support. The Guard also provides critical domestic response capabilities, deploying for natural disasters, civil unrest, and pandemic response under state authority (Title 32) or federal mobilization (Title 10).
- **The Air National Guard flies approximately 30% of all Air Force fighter sorties** and provides a significant share of airlift and aerial refueling. The ANG maintains 80+ flying squadrons and operates every major aircraft type in the Air Force inventory. ANG pilots typically have more total flying hours than their active-duty counterparts due to longer service tenure.
- **Post-mobilization readiness timelines** vary from 30 days for high-priority enhanced readiness units to 180+ days for lower-priority units, constraining their utility in rapid-onset conflicts. The Army has established Enhanced Security Cooperation Brigades and Associated Units programs to increase the readiness of selected Guard BCTs, but the fundamental timeline challenge remains.

### Reserve Component Recruiting and Retention

The reserve components face their own recruiting challenges, distinct from but related to the active-duty crisis:

- The Army National Guard missed its recruiting goal by approximately 10% in FY2023, falling to its lowest end strength since 1998.
- Prior-service recruiting -- historically the reserve components' primary source of new members -- has declined as the active-duty force shrinks and fewer veterans are available for reserve service.
- Employer support, critical for citizen-soldiers who must balance civilian careers with military obligations, has eroded as post-9/11 mobilization frequency has created employer resistance in some sectors.
- The Employer Support of the Guard and Reserve (ESGR) program advocates for service members' employment rights under the Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment Rights Act (USERRA), but enforcement is limited.

## Readiness Metrics

Readiness -- the ability of units to deploy and fight on their required timelines -- is the critical link between force structure and combat effectiveness. A force that is large on paper but poorly trained, undermanned, or lacking spare parts and ammunition is a "hollow force" that creates an illusion of capability.

### Current Readiness Assessment

| Indicator | Status | Trend | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Overall DoD Readiness | Moderate | Flat | GAO reports persistent readiness challenges across services |
| Army BCT Readiness (C-1/C-2 rated) | ~65% of active BCTs | Improving | Army's Regionally Aligned Readiness Model helping |
| Navy Ship Readiness (deployable) | ~55% of fleet | Declining | Maintenance backlogs, shipyard capacity constraints |
| Air Force Mission Capable Rate | ~72% overall | Flat | F-35 at ~55%; legacy fighters at ~75-80% |
| Marine Corps Unit Readiness | Moderate | Improving | Force Design 2030 transition causing temporary dips |
| Munitions Stockpiles | Below wartime requirements | Declining | Ukraine aid has drawn down key stocks; production surge underway |

### Readiness by Domain

- **Ground Readiness**: The Army's Regionally Aligned Readiness Model (ReARRM), replacing the previous Sustainable Readiness Model, assigns BCTs to specific readiness windows aligned with combatant command requirements. Under ReARRM, approximately two-thirds of active BCTs achieve C-1 or C-2 readiness ratings (the two highest levels) at their peak readiness window, but readiness degrades during the "reset" and "build" phases.
- **Naval Readiness**: The Navy's readiness crisis is the most acute of any service. Maintenance backlogs at the four public shipyards result in ships spending months longer in drydock than planned. The GAO found in 2024 that Navy ships lost approximately 33,000 operational days between FY2017 and FY2022 due to maintenance delays. The Optimized Fleet Response Plan (OFRP), designed to provide predictable carrier deployment cycles, has been repeatedly disrupted by extended deployments and maintenance delays.
- **Air Readiness**: The Air Force's Mission Capable (MC) rate -- the percentage of aircraft available for operations on any given day -- has been a persistent concern. The F-35A fleet averages approximately 55% MC, well below the 80% goal. Older aircraft types (F-15E, F-16C/D) achieve higher MC rates of 75-80%, but require increasing maintenance hours per flight hour as airframes age.

### Deployment Tempo

- **OPTEMPO**: The average active-duty service member deploys approximately once every 3-4 years, down significantly from the peak of the Iraq/Afghanistan era (once every 12-18 months for Army and Marines). However, certain high-demand/low-density specialties -- including special operations forces, submariners, intelligence analysts, cyber operators, and explosive ordnance disposal technicians -- continue to deploy at unsustainable rates.
- **Navy Deployment Length**: Standard carrier deployments have been extended from 6 months to 7-8 months due to fleet size constraints, with some extending to 9+ months. The USS Dwight D. Eisenhower's 2023-2024 deployment to the Red Sea/Gulf of Aden extended past 9 months amid Houthi attacks on commercial shipping. Extended deployments degrade crew morale and equipment reliability.
- **Dwell-to-Deploy Ratio**: DoD policy targets a 1:3 ratio for active forces (one deployment for every three periods at home) and 1:5 for reserves. Most services are meeting active targets but some Navy communities and special operations forces remain stressed.

## Basing and Global Posture

- **Domestic Bases**: Approximately 750 military installations across 50 states, with significant concentrations in Virginia, California, Texas, North Carolina, and Georgia. An estimated 20-25% of base capacity is excess to requirements, but Congress has not authorized a BRAC round since 2005. DoD estimates the annual cost of maintaining excess infrastructure at $5-8 billion per year -- resources that could fund readiness and modernization.
- **Overseas Presence**: Approximately 170,000 personnel stationed overseas at roughly 750 sites in 80 countries. Major concentrations in Japan (~56,000, including Marine Corps Air Station Iwakuni, Naval Station Yokosuka, Camp Schwab/Hansen/Foster on Okinawa), Germany (~35,000, including Ramstein Air Base and Grafenwohr Training Area), South Korea (~28,000, primarily at Camp Humphreys, the largest overseas U.S. military installation), and Italy (~12,000, including Naval Station Naples and Aviano Air Base).
- **Pacific Reposture**: The Indo-Pacific posture is being expanded through new Marine rotational deployments to Australia (Darwin) and Guam, Army MDTF deployment to Hawaii, and agreements with the Philippines (Enhanced Defense Cooperation Agreement, or EDCA, expanding from 5 to 9 agreed locations) and Papua New Guinea for rotational access. The Guam defense buildup includes relocation of approximately 5,000 Marines from Okinawa, construction of a new Aegis Ashore missile defense site, and expansion of Andersen Air Force Base as a bomber and tanker hub.
- **European Reinforcement**: Post-Ukraine invasion, the U.S. has increased its European presence by approximately 20,000 troops (from ~65,000 to ~85,000), reactivated V Corps headquarters in Poznan, Poland, established new rotational brigade deployments to Romania and the Baltic states, and deployed additional air defense and fighter squadrons to Eastern Europe. NATO allies have simultaneously increased their own force posture, with the alliance establishing multinational battlegroups in all Eastern European member states.

## Recruiting and Retention Challenges

The All-Volunteer Force faces its most significant recruiting crisis since its inception in 1973.

### Recruiting Shortfalls

| Service | FY2023 Recruiting Goal | FY2023 Actual | Shortfall |
|---|---|---|---|
| Army | 65,000 | ~55,000 | ~10,000 (15%) |
| Navy | 37,700 | ~30,800 | ~6,900 (18%) |
| Air Force | 26,877 | ~23,600 | ~3,300 (12%) |
| Marine Corps | 31,100 | ~31,000 | Met goal |
| Space Force | 500 | ~500 | Met goal |
| **Total** | **~161,000** | **~141,000** | **~20,000** |

### Root Causes of the Recruiting Crisis

- **Shrinking Eligible Population**: Only approximately 23% of Americans aged 17-24 are eligible for military service without a waiver (due to obesity, drug use, mental health conditions, criminal records, or academic deficiencies). The obesity rate among young adults has risen from approximately 25% in 2005 to over 35% in 2023, per CDC data. The DoD estimates that the eligible and propensed population -- those who are both qualified and interested -- numbers fewer than 500,000 nationwide.
- **Competitive Labor Market**: Unemployment at historic lows (3.5-4.0% through 2023-2024) means the military competes directly with private employers offering higher pay, signing bonuses, and remote work options that the military cannot match. Military starting pay (approximately $24,000/year for an E-1, rising to approximately $28,000 for an E-3 with one year of service) is below entry-level wages at many retailers and fast-food chains when calculated hourly.
- **Declining Propensity to Serve**: The percentage of young Americans who say they would "definitely" or "probably" consider military service has fallen from 13% in 2004 to approximately 9% in 2023, per DoD's Joint Advertising, Market Research & Studies (JAMRS) survey. The decline is particularly pronounced among demographic groups that have historically provided a disproportionate share of recruits, including Southern and rural youth.
- **Military-Civilian Divide**: Only 1% of Americans serve, and the share of youth with a parent who served has declined from 40% in 1995 to approximately 12%, reducing the familial connection to service that historically drove recruiting. The military recruiting market is increasingly concentrated in a shrinking number of states and communities -- the top 10 states by per-capita enlistment rate account for a disproportionate share of total accessions.
- **Trust Deficit**: Public confidence in the military, while still higher than most institutions, has declined from 70% in 2018 to approximately 60% in 2024, per Gallup polling. The decline has been attributed to political polarization around military topics, perceptions of a "woke military" narrative on the right, and concerns about military culture and sexual assault on the left. The reduction of the "halo effect" that supported recruiting has been accompanied by a decline in positive media portrayals of military service.
- **Service-Level Responses**: The Army's Future Soldier Preparatory Course (FSPC), launched in 2022, allows recruits who fail initial fitness or academic screening to complete a preparatory program before shipping to basic training. Approximately 95% of FSPC participants successfully qualify and enter service. The Navy has increased bonuses for critical ratings, and the Air Force has expanded its Delayed Entry Program to extend the window between commitment and shipping.

### International Comparison

The U.S. recruiting crisis is not unique. Peer democracies face similar challenges:

| Country | Military Size (Active) | Recruiting Model | Key Challenge |
|---|---|---|---|
| United Kingdom | ~148,000 | All-volunteer | Army missed recruiting targets for 7+ consecutive years; outsourced recruiting to Capita (widely criticized) |
| Australia | ~60,000 | All-volunteer | Recruiting shortfalls driving AUKUS workforce concerns; considering foreign recruits |
| Canada | ~68,000 | All-volunteer | 16,000 below authorized strength as of 2024; retention crisis in skilled trades |
| Germany | ~183,000 | All-volunteer (since 2011) | Ended conscription but struggling to recruit; considering limited return to draft |
| France | ~205,000 | All-volunteer (since 2001) | Better recruiting performance than peers; credits national service culture and higher military prestige |
