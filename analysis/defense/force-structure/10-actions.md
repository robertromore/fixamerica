# Force Structure: What You Can Do

Force structure decisions may seem like the exclusive domain of Pentagon planners and Congressional committees, but they are ultimately political decisions made by elected officials responsive to public pressure. Citizens have a meaningful role in shaping these outcomes through informed engagement, advocacy, and community action. The defense policy community is surprisingly small and responsive to informed citizen input -- a well-researched letter to a member of Congress about a specific force structure issue carries weight precisely because such communications are rare.

---

## 1. For Individuals: Educate Yourself and Engage

Understanding force structure requires engaging with defense policy beyond headlines. Individual citizens can make a difference by becoming informed advocates who bring strategic thinking to a debate often dominated by parochial interests.

### Learn the Fundamentals

- **Follow the Defense Budget**: The annual National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA) and defense appropriations bills are where force structure decisions are made. Track key provisions related to end strength, weapons programs, and base closures through CBO and CRS reports (available free online at cbo.gov and crsreports.congress.gov). The CBO's *U.S. Military's Force Structure: A Primer* is the single best entry point for understanding force structure.
- **Understand the Key Metrics**: Learn the basic force structure metrics that drive debate -- active-duty end strength by service, Navy battle force ship count, Army brigade combat team count, Air Force squadron count -- and track how they change year over year. Mark Cancian's annual CSIS *U.S. Military Forces* report provides the most accessible public summary.
- **Follow the Recruiting Crisis**: The sustainability of the All-Volunteer Force is the most consequential force structure issue of the 2020s. Track DoD recruiting data (published annually in the DoD Manpower Requirements Report), understand the shrinking eligible population, and evaluate proposed solutions.
- **Study the Lessons of Ukraine**: The Russia-Ukraine war is providing real-time lessons about modern warfare that have direct implications for U.S. force structure. Follow analysis from RUSI (Royal United Services Institute), CSIS, and RAND on attrition rates, munitions consumption, drone warfare, and the balance between offense and defense.

### Contact Your Representatives

- **Call or Write**: Call or write your Senators and Representative about defense priorities. Specific, informed constituent communications on defense topics carry significant weight because they are rare -- defense committees receive far fewer constituent contacts than committees covering healthcare, education, or taxation. Ask about:
    - Whether they support a new BRAC round to reduce the estimated 20-25% excess base infrastructure and redirect $5-8 billion annually to readiness.
    - Their position on military recruiting reform, including the Future Soldier Preparatory Course, compensation increases, and waiver modernization.
    - Whether they support the Quadrennial Force Structure Review concept to create a transparent link between strategy, force structure, and budget.
    - Their position on autonomous systems funding (CCA, unmanned vessels) and whether they believe technology can help offset recruiting shortfalls.
    - Whether they have blocked the retirement of specific weapons systems (A-10, older F-15/F-16 aircraft, specific ship classes) and what strategic rationale justifies maintaining those systems.
- **Be Specific**: General statements like "support a strong military" are less effective than specific questions about named programs, end-strength levels, or basing decisions. Reference GAO reports, CBO cost estimates, or CRS analyses by name.
- **Attend Town Halls**: Ask your elected officials specific questions about defense priorities at town halls and public events. Many members serve on Armed Services or Appropriations committees and will respond to informed constituent questions. Questions about local base infrastructure, Guard and Reserve readiness, and the recruiting crisis are particularly relevant at the constituent level.
- **Vote on Defense Policy**: Evaluate candidates' defense positions during elections. Look for specifics (not just "strong defense" rhetoric) about how they would size and resource the military. Do they support BRAC? Do they support military pay reform? Do they have a position on the Navy's fleet size target?

## 2. For Communities: Support the Military-Civilian Connection

The growing disconnect between the military and civilian society weakens democratic accountability for defense decisions and exacerbates the recruiting crisis.

### Strengthen Community-Military Ties

- **Support Local Veterans and Military Families**: Volunteer with organizations that assist military families, especially in communities near bases. Strong community support improves retention and reduces the human cost of military service. Organizations like Blue Star Families, the USO, and local military family support groups provide concrete volunteer opportunities. Military family quality of life -- housing, childcare, spouse employment -- is a force structure issue because it directly affects retention.
- **Engage with Local Guard and Reserve Units**: The National Guard and Reserve are embedded in communities across the country. Attend their community events, learn about their missions, and support employers who accommodate service members' military obligations. The Employer Support of the Guard and Reserve (ESGR) program recognizes supportive employers -- nominate deserving employers through ESGR's website.
- **Support Military Spouse Employment**: Military spouses face significant employment challenges due to frequent moves, with an unemployment rate approximately 4 times the national average. Support legislation and local programs that improve professional license portability (the Servicemembers Opportunity Colleges Consortium), create remote work opportunities, and remove barriers to spouse employment. Several states have enacted interstate license compacts for teaching, nursing, and other professions -- advocate for your state to join.
- **Support JROTC Expansion**: Junior ROTC programs in high schools are one of the most effective tools for bridging the military-civilian divide and expanding the propensity to serve. Advocate for JROTC programs in schools in your community, particularly in urban and suburban areas where they are underrepresented. JROTC participants are not obligated to serve but are significantly more likely to consider military service.

### If Your Community Hosts a Base

- **Engage Constructively with BRAC Discussions**: Support realistic transition planning rather than reflexive opposition to any change. Communities that plan proactively for base realignment tend to recover faster and can turn former military installations into economic assets. The Presidio of San Francisco, Lowry Air Force Base (Denver), and Brooks Air Force Base (San Antonio) are success stories of post-BRAC redevelopment.
- **Advocate for Community-Military Integration**: Work with local government and the installation commander to ensure that military families have access to quality schools, healthcare, and community services. The Military Child Education Coalition provides resources for school districts with military-connected students.
- **Monitor Environmental Issues**: Military installations often have significant environmental legacies (PFAS contamination, fuel spills, unexploded ordnance). Engage with your installation's Restoration Advisory Board (RAB) to monitor cleanup progress and advocate for community health protections.

## 3. For Political Engagement: Advocate for Reform

### Support Specific Reforms

- **Support BRAC Reform**: Advocate for a new BRAC round with robust community transition assistance. The current 20-25% excess base capacity wastes $5-8 billion annually that could fund readiness and modernization. Frame BRAC support as a readiness issue, not a cost-cutting exercise: every dollar spent heating empty buildings is a dollar not spent training soldiers or maintaining ships.
- **Push for Total Force Reform**: Advocate for a rational active-reserve balance based on strategic requirements rather than bureaucratic inertia. The reserve component provides essential capability at 40-60% lower cost per capita. A tiered readiness model that matches reserve unit readiness to strategic timelines would optimize this investment.
- **Demand Strategy-Budget Alignment**: Ask elected officials whether the defense budget actually funds the National Defense Strategy. If it does not, demand either more resources or a revised strategy -- not the current practice of accepting unacknowledged risk. The 2024 Commission on the National Defense Strategy found that the current budget does not fund the current strategy, a finding that demands a political response.
- **Support Recruiting Reform Without Lowering Standards**: Advocate for competitive military pay (particularly for junior enlisted), expanded preparatory programs (the Army's FSPC model), waiver modernization based on current medical science, and quality-of-life improvements (housing, childcare, spouse employment) as alternatives to lowering enlistment standards.
- **Advocate for Autonomous Systems Investment**: Support Congressional funding for the Air Force's Collaborative Combat Aircraft, the Navy's unmanned vessel programs, and the Army's autonomous logistics systems. These programs can help offset recruiting shortfalls by reducing the personnel intensity of the force while maintaining or increasing combat capability.

### Engage in the Budget Process

- **Track the NDAA**: The annual National Defense Authorization Act is the primary vehicle for force structure decisions. Monitor the HASC and SASC markups (available via committee websites and C-SPAN webcasts) to see how your representatives vote on end-strength levels, weapons programs, and base closure provisions.
- **Monitor Congressional Adds**: Track provisions in the NDAA and defense appropriations bill that add weapons or programs the Pentagon did not request. CBO and CRS publish analyses identifying these adds. Ask your representatives to justify any adds that benefit their districts but were not requested by the military.
- **Support Oversight Funding**: Advocate for full funding of the GAO, CBO, CRS, and DoD Inspector General -- the institutions that provide independent oversight of force structure decisions. These organizations are perennially underfunded relative to their mission.

## 4. For Employers: Support the Reserve Component

Employers play a critical but often overlooked role in force structure sustainability. The reserve component depends on civilian employers who accommodate service members' military obligations.

- **Provide USERRA-Plus Support**: The Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment Rights Act (USERRA) provides baseline protections, but many employers can do more. Consider offering differential pay (topping up military pay to match civilian salary during deployments), extended leave policies, and career protections beyond USERRA minimums.
- **Hire Veterans and Military Spouses**: Veterans bring leadership, discipline, and technical skills to the civilian workforce. Military spouses bring adaptability and resilience. Both populations face employment challenges that informed hiring practices can address. Organizations like Hiring Our Heroes (U.S. Chamber of Commerce Foundation) and the Military Spouse Employment Partnership (MSEP) connect employers with military-connected talent.
- **Support Guard and Reserve Employees**: If your organization employs Guard or Reserve members, work with ESGR to develop supportive policies and ensure that military service does not penalize employees' civilian careers.

## Effectiveness Tips

- **Be specific**: General complaints about "defense spending" are less effective than specific concerns about named programs, end-strength levels, basing decisions, or recruiting challenges. Reference GAO report numbers, CBO cost estimates, or specific NDAA provisions.
- **Be nonpartisan**: Force structure reform is not a left-right issue. Fiscal conservatives who want efficient defense spending and progressives who want accountable military policy can find common ground on issues like BRAC, recruiting reform, and strategy-budget alignment.
- **Use data**: Reference GAO reports, CBO analyses, CRS primers, and CSIS force structure assessments in your communications with elected officials. Data-driven advocacy is more credible than ideological arguments.
- **Be persistent**: Defense policy decisions are made continuously, not episodically. The NDAA cycle runs year-round (committee hearings in spring, markup in summer, conference in fall, enactment in winter). Sustained engagement is more effective than one-time contacts.
- **Build coalitions**: The most effective force structure reform efforts bring together fiscal conservatives concerned about waste, progressives concerned about accountability, military families concerned about quality of life, veterans concerned about readiness, and community leaders concerned about sustainable economic development. These groups may disagree on many issues but can unite around a force structure that is right-sized, well-funded, and strategically aligned.
