# Force Structure: Stakeholders

Force structure decisions affect -- and are influenced by -- a broad array of government, military, industry, and community stakeholders. Unlike many policy areas where the public is largely disengaged, force structure decisions directly impact millions of service members, their families, defense workers, and the communities that host military installations. The stakeholder landscape is characterized by a fundamental asymmetry: those who benefit from the status quo (services protecting institutional equities, defense contractors protecting production lines, communities protecting bases) are highly organized and politically powerful, while those who bear the costs of force structure dysfunction (taxpayers, deployed service members, future warfighters who may face an inadequately structured force) are diffuse and poorly represented.

## Military Service Stakeholders

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Role in Force Structure |
|---|---|---|
| **Department of the Army** | Maintaining end strength, BCT count, and modernization funding for Multi-Domain Operations. | Largest service by personnel. Advocates for ground force primacy and increased end strength. The Army argues that great power competition will ultimately require sustained ground campaigns and that the service has been disproportionately cut relative to the Navy and Air Force. |
| **Department of the Navy** | Growing the fleet toward 355+ ships, maintaining 11 carrier strike groups, and sustaining the submarine industrial base. | Controls the largest share of capital investment (shipbuilding). Advocates for maritime primacy, arguing that the Indo-Pacific theater -- the priority theater for China competition -- is fundamentally a maritime domain. The Navy's shipbuilding plan is the most capital-intensive element of U.S. force structure. |
| **Department of the Air Force** | Recapitalizing aging aircraft fleets, fielding the B-21 and NGAD/CCA, and growing the Space Force. | Manages the most technologically complex and capital-intensive force structure. The Air Force argues that air superiority is the prerequisite for all other military operations and that its aging fleet of fighters, bombers, and tankers represents the most urgent recapitalization need in the force. |
| **U.S. Marine Corps** | Executing Force Design 2030 transformation while maintaining amphibious capability and end strength. | Unique as a naval expeditionary force; currently undergoing the most radical service-level restructuring in a generation. The Commandant has staked institutional credibility on Force Design 2030, making it both a strategic initiative and a test of service-level reform. |
| **U.S. Space Force** | Establishing organizational identity, growing personnel, and securing independent budget authority. | Newest service, competing for resources and institutional legitimacy within the Air Force budget. The Space Force seeks to establish itself as a fully independent service with its own acquisition pipeline, training infrastructure, and reserve component (Space National Guard). |
| **National Guard Bureau** | Protecting Guard end strength, dual-mission (federal/state) authorities, and domestic response capabilities. | The Guard's unique federal-state status gives it powerful political protections through governors and state adjutants general. The 54 adjutants general (one for each state, territory, and the District of Columbia) form a politically influential constituency that can mobilize gubernatorial and Congressional support for Guard programs. |

**Internal Conflicts:**

- **Army vs. Navy/Air Force**: The Army advocates for larger ground forces and argues that great power competition will require sustained ground campaigns, pointing to historical precedent (every major U.S. war has required ground forces) and the lessons of Ukraine. The Navy and Air Force argue that the Indo-Pacific theater is primarily a maritime and air domain, and that ground forces are less relevant to the most likely scenarios involving China. This inter-service debate has a direct budgetary dimension: the Navy's shipbuilding and the Air Force's recapitalization plans consume resources that could alternatively fund Army end strength and modernization.
- **Active vs. Guard/Reserve**: Active component leaders prefer a larger active force for speed and readiness, arguing that the rapid-onset scenarios in the Western Pacific do not allow time for reserve mobilization. Guard and Reserve advocates argue that reserve forces provide equivalent capability at lower cost (CBO estimates 40-60% savings per capita) and that the Total Force Policy requires meaningful reserve component roles. The debate is complicated by the Guard's domestic response mission -- state governors depend on the Guard for disaster response, wildfire suppression, and civil emergency support, creating a constituency for Guard end strength that is independent of federal defense strategy.
- **USMC Institutional Debate**: Force Design 2030 is deeply controversial within the Marine Corps. A group of 18 retired Marine generals signed a letter in 2023 opposing the divestiture of tanks and reduction of artillery, arguing it narrows the Corps' utility and makes it a single-scenario force optimized only for the Pacific. Defenders, including Commandant General David Berger (who initiated Force Design) and his successor General Eric Smith, counter that the legacy force was already inadequate for the Indo-Pacific environment and that the Corps must prioritize the pacing threat. The debate has divided the Marine Corps community in a way not seen since the Vietnam era.

## Combatant Commander Stakeholders

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Force Structure Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **U.S. Indo-Pacific Command (INDOPACOM)** | Deterring China; maintaining presence and alliance credibility in the Western Pacific. | The largest consumer of naval and air forces. Drives demand for more ships, submarines, long-range bombers, forward-deployed forces, and missile defense. INDOPACOM Commander Admiral John Aquilino publicly testified in 2024 that deterrence is "eroding" due to insufficient force posture. |
| **U.S. European Command (EUCOM)** | Deterring Russia; supporting NATO defense of Eastern Europe. | Post-Ukraine invasion, increased demand for Army rotational forces, air defense, pre-positioned equipment, and intelligence capabilities. The reactivation of V Corps and rotational brigade deployments to Poland and Romania represent significant force structure commitments. |
| **U.S. Central Command (CENTCOM)** | Counterterrorism; regional stability in the Middle East. | Reduced footprint from peak Iraq/Afghanistan era but still requires significant ISR, SOF, and maritime presence. The Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping (2023-2024) demonstrated that CENTCOM still consumes significant naval and air assets. |
| **U.S. Northern Command (NORTHCOM)** | Homeland defense; support to civil authorities (disaster response). | Depends heavily on National Guard for domestic response missions. NORTHCOM's homeland defense mission -- including air sovereignty, missile defense of the homeland, and defense against emerging threats (cruise missiles, hypersonics) -- drives requirements for air defense and domain awareness capabilities. |
| **U.S. Special Operations Command (SOCOM)** | Maintaining global special operations capability across the full spectrum of conflict. | SOCOM forces (approximately 70,000 across all services) are among the most deployed in the military. The command has experienced sustained high OPTEMPO since 9/11 and faces retention challenges in its most experienced ranks. |
| **U.S. Cyber Command (CYBERCOM)** | Conducting cyberspace operations; defending DoD networks. | Drives demand for cyber operators, a specialty that competes directly with the private sector for talent. The Cyber Mission Force of 133 teams was declared fully operational in 2018, but sustaining the workforce requires constant recruiting against private-sector competition. |

**Key Tension**: Combatant commanders submit requirements that collectively exceed the available force by 30-50%. The Global Force Management process, managed by the Joint Staff J-3, allocates forces but consistently leaves commands short of their stated needs, forcing strategic risk acceptance at the SecDef level. Each combatant commander can credibly argue that their unfilled requirements represent unacceptable risk, but the total demand always exceeds supply. This structural oversubscription means that force structure decisions are ultimately about choosing which risks to accept, not about eliminating risk.

## Congressional Stakeholders

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Source of Influence |
|---|---|---|
| **House Armed Services Committee (HASC)** | Authorization of defense programs, force levels, and military policy. | Writes the annual National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA), the primary legislative vehicle for force structure decisions. The HASC has approximately 60 members and is one of the most sought-after committee assignments. |
| **Senate Armed Services Committee (SASC)** | Authorization and confirmation of senior defense officials. | Shapes force structure through NDAA provisions and influence over senior military appointments. The SASC's 27 members include some of the most influential voices on defense policy. |
| **Defense Appropriations Subcommittees** | Funding for authorized programs. | Controls the money; can fund programs below authorized levels or add unrequested items. The gap between authorization and appropriation is a persistent source of force structure instability. |
| **State Delegations** | Protecting bases, installations, and defense industry jobs in their states and districts. | Form bipartisan coalitions to block BRAC rounds and protect local defense spending. The Virginia, California, Texas, and Connecticut delegations are among the most active in protecting defense interests. |
| **Congressional Budget Office (CBO)** | Independent cost analysis and force structure options. | Provides nonpartisan analysis that often challenges DoD cost estimates. CBO force structure primers and shipbuilding analyses are among the most rigorous publicly available assessments. |

**Key Dynamic**: Congress has a structural tendency to add forces and programs (particularly those that create jobs in members' districts) while resisting force reductions, base closures, and program cancellations. This results in a force that is broader but shallower than strategic planners would prefer. The NDAA regularly includes provisions that prevent the services from retiring aircraft, decommissioning ships, or reducing end strength below specified levels, even when the services have requested these changes. In FY2024, Congress blocked the Air Force from retiring dozens of older aircraft that the service assessed as excess to requirements, forcing the Air Force to spend maintenance dollars on aircraft it does not want.

## Defense Industry Stakeholders

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Source of Power |
|---|---|---|
| **Prime Contractors** (Lockheed Martin, RTX, Boeing, Northrop Grumman, General Dynamics) | Sustaining and expanding production lines for major platforms (F-35, Virginia-class submarines, DDG-51, B-21). | Concentrated market power; employ hundreds of thousands of workers in politically important states. Defense revenues for the Big Five exceed $180 billion annually. |
| **Shipbuilding Industry** (Huntington Ingalls, General Dynamics/Bath Iron Works, Austal USA) | Maintaining shipbuilding rate and workforce. | Only two companies can build nuclear-powered warships. The industrial base is a strategic asset and a political constituency. The two nuclear shipyards employ approximately 55,000 skilled workers, and workforce shortages of 10,000-15,000 constrain production rates. |
| **Small/Mid-Tier Suppliers** | Maintaining subcontract work from prime contractors. | Distributed across hundreds of Congressional districts, creating broad political support for defense programs. The F-35 supply chain includes over 1,700 suppliers in 45+ states. |
| **New Technology Entrants** (Anduril, Shield AI, SpaceX, Palantir) | Breaking into the defense market; disrupting traditional procurement models. | Bring commercial technology and venture capital funding; advocate for procurement reform and faster acquisition pathways. Their success (or failure) will shape whether the force structure of the future is built primarily by traditional contractors or by a more diverse industrial base. |

**Key Dynamic**: The defense industrial base has consolidated from 51 major prime contractors in the 1990s to 5 today. This oligopolistic structure means that force structure decisions directly affect a small number of politically powerful companies. Canceling a major program can eliminate thousands of jobs in a single community and potentially cause a supplier to exit the defense market entirely, reducing future competition. The consolidation creates a mutual dependency: the government needs the contractors to produce weapons, and the contractors need the government as their primary (or sole) customer. This dynamic makes it exceptionally difficult to cancel underperforming programs or shift resources from legacy platforms to new ones.

## Military Families and Personnel

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Source of Influence |
|---|---|---|
| **Active-Duty Service Members** | Quality of life, deployment tempo, career stability, compensation. | Indirect influence through retention decisions; cannot lobby directly due to military regulations. When retention declines in critical specialties, it sends a powerful signal about force structure sustainability. |
| **Military Families** | Housing quality, spouse employment, child education, healthcare (TRICARE), deployment predictability. | Advocacy organizations (National Military Family Association, Blue Star Families) and Congressional testimony. Blue Star Families' annual Military Family Lifestyle Survey consistently identifies housing, childcare, and spouse employment as top concerns. |
| **Recruiters and Recruiting Command** | Meeting accession goals with qualified applicants. | Provide ground-truth data on the feasibility of end-strength targets. U.S. Army Recruiting Command (USAREC) operates approximately 1,400 recruiting stations nationwide and employs roughly 10,000 recruiters. |

## Veterans Organizations

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Source of Influence |
|---|---|---|
| **Veterans of Foreign Wars (VFW)** | Maintaining strong military, adequate VA funding. | 1.5 million members; Congressional testimony and grassroots lobbying. Historically advocates for higher defense spending and larger force structure. |
| **American Legion** | National defense, veteran benefits. | 1.6 million members; historically influential with Congress. The Legion's annual "Commanders Call" brings state-level leaders to Washington to lobby for defense priorities. |
| **Iraq and Afghanistan Veterans of America (IAVA)** | Post-9/11 veteran issues, including deployment tempo impacts. | Advocacy and media presence focused on younger veterans. IAVA has been particularly vocal on the human costs of high OPTEMPO. |
| **Military Officers Association of America (MOAA)** | Officer career issues, retirement benefits, force structure. | 350,000+ members; lobbies on officer retention, compensation, and healthcare. |

## Communities Near Military Bases

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Source of Influence |
|---|---|---|
| **Base-Adjacent Communities** | Economic viability tied to base spending (jobs, housing demand, retail). | Organize through chambers of commerce, defense community coalitions (such as the Association of Defense Communities), and their Congressional delegations. The economic impact of a major military installation on its host community can range from $500 million to $5+ billion annually. |
| **State Governors** | National Guard force structure, domestic emergency response capability, state economic impact of defense spending. | Constitutional role as commanders-in-chief of state National Guard forces; political influence on Congressional delegations. Governors from Guard-heavy states (Texas, California, Pennsylvania) are particularly active in protecting Guard end strength. |
| **Defense Community Infrastructure Program (DCIP) Recipients** | Federal funding for infrastructure supporting military installations. | Apply political pressure to maintain base viability and prevent BRAC selections. DCIP provides grants for roads, utilities, schools, and other infrastructure that supports military installations, creating an additional incentive for communities to oppose base realignment. |

## Stakeholder Power Analysis

| Stakeholder | Political Power | Economic Resources | Information Access | Organizational Capacity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Military services (collectively) | Very High | High (budget control) | Very High | Very High |
| Congress (defense committees) | Very High | N/A | High | High |
| Prime defense contractors | Very High | Very High | High | Very High |
| Combatant commanders | High | Low | Very High | Medium |
| National Guard Bureau / Governors | High | Low | Medium | High |
| Veterans organizations | Medium | Low | Low | High |
| Base-adjacent communities | Medium | Low | Low | Medium |
| Military families | Low-Medium | Low | Low | Medium |
| Reform think tanks | Low | Low | Medium | Medium |
| Taxpayers (general public) | Low (diffuse) | N/A | Low | Very Low |

The fundamental imbalance in this stakeholder landscape mirrors that of the military-industrial complex more broadly: the actors who benefit from maintaining the current force structure are highly organized, well-resourced, and concentrated, while those who would benefit from reform -- taxpayers, future warfighters, and communities that could thrive with redirected resources -- are diffuse and difficult to mobilize.
