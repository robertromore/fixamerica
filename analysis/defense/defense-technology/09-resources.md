# Defense Technology: Resources

## Key Government Documents and Reports

### Strategic and Policy Documents

- **National Defense Strategy (2022)**: Outlines the strategic imperative for innovation, the concept of "integrated deterrence," and the priority of competition with China. Available via the DoD website.
- **National Defense Industrial Strategy (2024)**: The first comprehensive industrial strategy published by the DoD, addressing supply chain resilience, workforce, and the need to modernize the defense industrial base.
- **DoD Data, Analytics, and AI Adoption Strategy (2023)**: The Pentagon's plan for becoming an AI-enabled organization, published by the CDAO.
- **DoD Directive 3000.09: Autonomy in Weapon Systems (updated 2023)**: The foundational policy governing the development and deployment of autonomous and semi-autonomous weapons systems, including requirements for human oversight.
- **Responsible AI Strategy and Implementation Pathway (2022)**: The DoD's framework for developing and fielding AI systems ethically, including principles for transparency, reliability, governability, and equitability.

### Commission and Panel Reports

- **PPBE Reform Commission Final Report (March 2024)**: Comprehensive recommendations for fixing the Planning, Programming, Budgeting, and Execution process, including portfolio budgeting, delegated reprogramming, and a software appropriation category. The single most important reform document for defense acquisition.
- **National Security Commission on Artificial Intelligence (NSCAI) Final Report (March 2021)**: The blueprint for U.S. AI strategy in national security, chaired by Eric Schmidt (former Google CEO) and Robert Work (former Deputy Secretary of Defense). Recommended $40 billion in AI investment, creation of a National AI Research Resource, and fundamental acquisition reform.
- **Defense Innovation Board Reports (2016-2024)**: A series of reports on software acquisition, AI ethics, data governance, and workforce reform. The "Software is Never Done" report (2019) was particularly influential in driving the Software Acquisition Pathway.
- **Section 809 Panel Final Report (2018-2019)**: Three volumes of recommendations for streamlining defense acquisition regulation, including 98 specific reforms to the FAR and DFARS.

### GAO and CRS Reports

- **GAO Annual Weapons Systems Assessment**: Published annually, this report tracks cost, schedule, and performance on major defense acquisition programs. Essential reading for understanding the systemic acquisition dysfunction.
- **GAO, "Defense Acquisitions: Actions Needed to Increase Competition" (GAO-24-106890, 2024)**: Documents the decline in competitive contracting and the rise of sole-source awards.
- **CRS, "Defense Primer: Research, Development, Test, and Evaluation (RDT&E)"**: Concise overview of the RDT&E budget structure and categories.
- **CRS, "Artificial Intelligence and National Security" (R45178, updated regularly)**: Comprehensive analysis of AI applications, programs, and policy issues in defense.
- **CRS, "Hypersonic Weapons: Background and Issues for Congress" (R45811)**: Overview of U.S. and adversary hypersonic programs, test results, and funding.
- **CRS, "Defense Innovation Unit (DIU)" (IF11584)**: Summary of DIU's mission, authorities, and performance.

## Books

### Essential Reading

- **"The Kill Chain: Defending America in the Future of High-Tech Warfare"** by Christian Brose (2020). The definitive critique of the current acquisition system and argument for autonomous systems, written by a former staff director of the Senate Armed Services Committee. Brose argues that the U.S. military's inability to close the "kill chain" (the sensor-to-shooter decision cycle) faster than adversaries is an existential strategic risk.
- **"Unit X: How the Pentagon and Silicon Valley Are Transforming the Future of War"** by Raj M. Shah and Christopher Kirchhoff (2024). An inside account of the Defense Innovation Unit from its co-founders. Documents the challenges and successes of bringing commercial technology into the Pentagon.
- **"Four Battlegrounds: Power in the Age of Artificial Intelligence"** by Paul Scharre (2023). Analysis of the U.S.-China AI competition across four dimensions: data, compute, talent, and institutions. Argues that institutional factors (not just technical capability) will determine the winner.
- **"Army of None: Autonomous Weapons and the Future of War"** by Paul Scharre (2018). Deep dive into the ethics, strategy, and technology of lethal autonomous weapons systems. Scharre, a former Army Ranger and DoD policy official, provides the most balanced treatment of the LAWS debate.

### Additional Reading

- **"The Bomb: Presidents, Generals, and the Secret History of Nuclear War"** by Fred Kaplan (2020). Relevant for understanding how nuclear weapons policy shaped the institutional culture of defense technology decision-making.
- **"Prediction Machines: The Simple Economics of Artificial Intelligence"** by Ajay Agrawal, Joshua Gans, and Avi Goldfarb (2018). An economic framework for understanding AI's impact, applicable to defense applications.
- **"Chip War: The Fight for the World's Most Critical Technology"** by Chris Miller (2022). Essential background on the semiconductor supply chain, TSMC's dominance, and the geopolitical stakes of microelectronics.
- **"Sandworm: A New Era of Cyberwar and the Hunt for the Kremlin's Most Dangerous Hackers"** by Andy Greenberg (2019). Detailed account of Russian cyber warfare capabilities, including the NotPetya attack.
- **"The Innovator's Dilemma"** by Clayton Christensen (1997). While not defense-specific, this classic on disruptive innovation explains the structural dynamics that cause established organizations (including the DoD) to fail at adopting transformative technologies.
- **"Boyd: The Fighter Pilot Who Changed the Art of War"** by Robert Coram (2002). Biography of John Boyd, whose OODA loop concept and advocacy for lightweight, agile fighters challenged the Pentagon bureaucracy -- a historical parallel to today's innovation reformers.
- **"Bureaucracy Does Its Thing"** by Robert Komer (1972). RAND study of why the U.S. military's institutional processes produced suboptimal outcomes in Vietnam. The structural pathologies Komer identified remain largely unchanged.

## Think Tanks and Research Organizations

### Primary Sources for Defense Technology Analysis

- **Center for Security and Emerging Technology (CSET)**: Georgetown University-based think tank providing data-driven analysis on AI, workforce, and advanced technology policy. Founded by Jason Matheny (former IARPA director). Website: [cset.georgetown.edu](https://cset.georgetown.edu)
- **Center for a New American Security (CNAS)**: Leading work on the "American Way of War," defense technology strategy, and responsible AI. Key researchers include Paul Scharre and Stacie Pettyjohn. Website: [cnas.org](https://www.cnas.org)
- **RAND Corporation**: Federally funded research and development center conducting defense analysis since 1948. Produces authoritative studies on acquisition reform, force structure, and technology policy. Website: [rand.org](https://www.rand.org)
- **Hudson Institute**: Center for Defense Concepts and Technology, led by Bryan Clark, focuses on force design and emerging technology integration. Website: [hudson.org](https://www.hudson.org)
- **Special Competitive Studies Project (SCSP)**: Founded by Eric Schmidt as the successor to the NSCAI, focused on long-term U.S. competitiveness in AI, biotech, and advanced computing. Website: [scsp.ai](https://www.scsp.ai)
- **Atlantic Council**: GeoTech Center and Scowcroft Center for Strategy and Security address technology competition and defense modernization. Website: [atlanticcouncil.org](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org)
- **Project on Government Oversight (POGO)**: Non-partisan watchdog organization that investigates defense spending waste, the revolving door, and contractor accountability. Website: [pogo.org](https://www.pogo.org)
- **Stimson Center**: Work on autonomous weapons, arms control, and defense technology governance. Website: [stimson.org](https://www.stimson.org)
- **Institute for Defense Analyses (IDA)**: FFRDC providing technical assessment and operational analysis for the DoD. Not publicly accessible but influential in program decisions.

## Congressional Testimony and Hearings

Key congressional hearings on defense technology provide insight into the policy debate:

- **SASC Hearing on PPBE Reform (2024)**: Commission members presented findings; service acquisition executives responded.
- **HASC Hearing on Replicator Initiative (2024)**: DepSecDef Hicks and DIU Director testified on autonomous systems fielding progress.
- **SASC Hearing on AI and National Security (2023)**: CDAO, DARPA, and industry witnesses on AI adoption challenges.
- **SASC Hearing on Hypersonics (2023)**: Service program managers testified on test failures and path forward.
- **House Science Committee Hearing on CHIPS Act Implementation (2024)**: Commerce Department and semiconductor industry witnesses on fab construction timelines.

Hearing transcripts are available through the Senate Armed Services Committee ([armed-services.senate.gov](https://www.armed-services.senate.gov)) and House Armed Services Committee ([armedservices.house.gov](https://armedservices.house.gov)).

## Films and Documentaries

- **"Kill Chain: The Cyber War on America's Elections" (2020)**: HBO documentary on cybersecurity vulnerabilities, relevant to understanding cyber defense challenges.
- **"The Pentagon Wars" (1998)**: HBO film dramatizing the dysfunctional development of the Bradley Fighting Vehicle -- a satirical but accurate depiction of acquisition pathologies that persist today.
- **"War Games: At the End of the Day" (various military exercise documentaries)**: Provide insight into how the military tests new concepts and technologies.

## Academic Programs and Centers

- **Stanford Hacking for Defense (H4D)**: University course pairing students with DoD problem sponsors. Now offered at 50+ universities. [h4d.stanford.edu](https://www.h4d.stanford.edu)
- **MIT Lincoln Laboratory**: Federally funded R&D center focused on defense electronics, air defense, and space systems.
- **Johns Hopkins Applied Physics Laboratory (APL)**: FFRDC conducting defense research in undersea warfare, space, and missile defense.
- **Georgia Tech Research Institute (GTRI)**: Major defense research institution focused on electronic warfare, cybersecurity, and autonomous systems.
- **Carnegie Mellon Software Engineering Institute (SEI)**: FFRDC focused on software engineering, cybersecurity, and AI for defense.
- **Naval Postgraduate School (NPS)**: Graduate education for military officers; strong programs in AI, cyber, and defense analysis.
- **Air Force Institute of Technology (AFIT)**: Air Force's graduate school; programs in systems engineering, cyber operations, and autonomous systems.
- **Sandia National Laboratories**: DOE national lab with significant defense work in nuclear weapons science, microsystems, and cybersecurity.
- **University of Dayton Research Institute (UDRI)**: Major defense research center focused on materials, sensors, and aerospace systems; located near Wright-Patterson AFB.
