# Defense Technology: Solutions

## Overview

Accelerating defense innovation requires a holistic approach that simultaneously addresses funding mechanisms, acquisition processes, workforce pipelines, cultural barriers, and industrial base structure. No single reform is sufficient; the interlocking nature of the root causes demands coordinated action across multiple fronts. The solutions presented here draw on recommendations from the PPBE Reform Commission (2024), the National Security Commission on Artificial Intelligence (2021), the Defense Innovation Board, and leading defense technology practitioners and analysts.

## 1. Bridge the "Valley of Death"

The gap between successful prototype demonstration and production contract is the single most lethal barrier to defense innovation. Bridging it requires both new funding mechanisms and structural changes to how the DoD transitions technology.

### A. Establish a Technology Transition Fund

- **Proposal**: Create a dedicated, flexible appropriation of $1-2 billion per year specifically for transitioning successful prototypes to initial production. This fund would be managed by OUSD(R&E) in coordination with DIU and the services.
- **Mechanism**: When a prototype successfully demonstrates capability through DIU, DARPA, or a service rapid prototyping effort, the Transition Fund would provide bridge funding to sustain the vendor and begin low-rate initial production while the program is incorporated into the formal budget cycle.
- **Precedent**: Israel's Ministry of Defense operates a similar mechanism through its Directorate of Defense Research and Development (DDR&D, known as MAFAT), which can move successful prototypes to production within 12-18 months.
- **Impact**: Prevents startup bankruptcy during the budget cycle gap; signals to venture capitalists that successful prototypes will lead to production contracts, encouraging private investment in defense technology.

### B. Standardize Bridge Contracts

- **Proposal**: Expand and standardize the use of Indefinite Delivery/Indefinite Quantity (IDIQ) and Basic Ordering Agreement (BOA) vehicles that allow immediate follow-on production orders upon successful prototyping.
- **Mechanism**: Pre-negotiate contract terms during the prototype phase so that a production order can be issued within 30 days of successful demonstration, rather than requiring a new competition or contract negotiation.
- **Example**: DIU's "Transition Success" metric should track not just the number of prototypes delivered but the percentage that achieve production contracts within 18 months.

### C. Venture Capital Alignment Mechanisms

- **Proposal**: Create a government-backed "Defense Innovation Fund" (modeled on In-Q-Tel but with broader scope) that co-invests alongside private VCs in defense-relevant startups, providing patient capital aligned with defense timelines.
- **Mechanism**: The fund would take equity positions (like In-Q-Tel) or provide subordinated debt that extends runway beyond typical VC horizons. Returns would be reinvested.
- **International model**: The UK's National Security Strategic Investment Fund (NSSIF) and France's Defense Innovation Agency (AID) provide analogous models for government co-investment in defense startups.

### D. Lessons from Allied Models

- **Israel's MAFAT (DDR&D)**: Israel's defense R&D organization operates with a streamlined process that can move from concept to production in 12-18 months. The key features: program managers with broad authority, tight integration between developers and operators, and a culture that accepts failure as a necessary cost of speed.
- **UK's DASA (Defence and Security Accelerator)**: DASA runs open innovation challenges that award contracts to small companies in weeks, not months. It has been more successful than DIU at scaling commercial technology adoption, partly because UK procurement regulations are less burdensome than the FAR.
- **Adoption**: The U.S. should study and selectively adopt elements of allied innovation models, while recognizing that scale differences (the U.S. defense budget is 10x Israel's) create distinct challenges.

## 2. Reform Acquisition and Budgeting

### A. Portfolio Budgeting

- **Proposal**: Implement the PPBE Reform Commission's recommendation to move from line-item budgeting (funding specific widgets) to portfolio budgeting (funding capabilities like "Unmanned Systems" or "AI-Enabled C2").
- **Mechanism**: Program Executive Officers (PEOs) would receive authority to shift funds within their portfolio to the best-performing technologies without needing individual congressional approval for every reallocation. Congress would conduct oversight at the portfolio level rather than the program level.
- **Safeguard**: Congress would set portfolio-level funding ceilings and require quarterly reporting on reallocation decisions. Major shifts (>15% of portfolio value) would require notification.
- **Impact**: Increases agility; allows the DoD to cut underperforming programs and double down on winners without a 2-year budget cycle delay.

### B. Software Appropriation Category (BA-8)

- **Proposal**: Create a single budget activity category for software (BA-8) that covers development, procurement, and sustainment within a unified appropriation.
- **Background**: The DoD has piloted this concept through the Software Acquisition Pathway (2020) and several "software factory" initiatives. The PPBE Commission recommended full implementation.
- **Impact**: Enables modern DevSecOps practices where software is continuously updated through agile sprints, rather than bought once and maintained separately under different appropriation accounts. Eliminates the absurdity of needing three different "colors of money" to develop, buy, and update the same software.

### C. Expand Rapid Acquisition Pathways

- **Proposal**: Make the Middle Tier of Acquisition (MTA, Section 804) pathway -- which allows prototyping and rapid fielding within 5 years -- the default pathway for software-intensive and autonomous systems, rather than an exception.
- **Proposal**: Raise the OTA (Other Transaction Authority) threshold so that DIU and the services can award larger prototype and production contracts without reverting to the FAR-based process.
- **Current limitation**: OTAs currently have soft caps that encourage splitting large programs into smaller transactions, adding administrative overhead. Raising the threshold to $500 million for prototype OTAs and removing the cap for production OTAs (with appropriate oversight) would enable at-scale adoption of commercial technology.

### D. Outcome-Based Contracting

- **Proposal**: Shift from specifying detailed technical requirements ("the system shall weigh no more than X pounds and operate at Y frequency") to specifying desired outcomes ("the system shall detect 95% of targets at Z range in contested environments"). Allow vendors to propose their own technical approaches.
- **Impact**: Enables innovation by letting companies solve problems creatively rather than building to government-specified designs. Reduces the ability of incumbents to shape requirements around their existing products.
- **Precedent**: The UK Ministry of Defence has experimented with "Themed Competitions" that define outcomes rather than specifications, with promising early results.

## 3. Strengthen the Innovation Ecosystem

### A. Empower the Defense Innovation Unit (DIU)

- **Proposal**: DIU has been elevated to report directly to the Secretary of Defense (2023). The next step is to significantly increase its budget authority to $2-3 billion annually (from ~$900 million) and grant it the authority to award production contracts, not just prototype contracts.
- **Impact**: Gives the "commercial-first" advocates institutional weight commensurate with the traditional acquisition bureaucracy. A DIU with production authority could bypass the Valley of Death entirely for commercial technologies that demonstrate capability.

### B. Mandatory Hedge Portfolio

- **Proposal**: Require each military service to allocate a minimum percentage (e.g., 5%) of its procurement budget to non-traditional vendors and low-cost, attritable systems. This would create a guaranteed market floor for new entrants.
- **Mechanism**: Track compliance through the annual budget process and NDAA language. Report the percentage of procurement dollars going to non-traditional firms as a key metric.
- **Impact**: Creates a structural demand signal for startups and their investors, reducing the risk that successful prototypes will die in the Valley of Death for lack of a customer.

### C. Open Architecture and Data Rights Reform

- **Proposal**: Mandate modular open systems approaches (MOSA) and government-purpose data rights for all new defense contracts. End vendor lock-in by requiring interoperable interfaces, standard data formats, and the ability to compete sustainment and upgrade contracts.
- **Mechanism**: Implement through DFARS (Defense Federal Acquisition Regulation Supplement) revisions and NDAA provisions. Require open APIs as a condition of contract award.
- **Tradeoff**: Commercial firms will resist government data rights claims. The compromise: the government should pay a premium for data rights (a "data rights buyout") rather than demanding them as a default, allowing companies to maintain their commercial IP while giving the government what it needs for competition and sustainment.

## 4. Workforce and Talent

### A. Digital Workforce Service

- **Proposal**: Expand "tour of duty" programs (Defense Digital Service, Presidential Innovation Fellows, U.S. Digital Corps) to bring 1,000+ private sector tech workers into the DoD annually for 1-2 year rotations.
- **Mechanism**: Offer competitive compensation (Special Rate Tables, recruitment bonuses) and expedited clearance processing. Ensure that rotating talent works on meaningful problems with real operational impact, not bureaucratic busywork.
- **Impact**: Injects modern tech skills, agile culture, and commercial-sector best practices into the Pentagon workforce. Creates alumni networks that improve DoD-industry relations.

### B. Lateral Entry for Technical Talent

- **Proposal**: Allow experienced civilian technical professionals to commission into the military at mid-career ranks (O-3 through O-5, i.e., Captain/Major/Lieutenant Commander) commensurate with their education and experience.
- **Background**: The Army has experimented with direct commissioning for cyber officers, and the Space Force has explored similar pathways. These experiments need to be expanded and standardized across all services.
- **International model**: The Israeli Defense Forces routinely commission technical specialists at ranks reflecting their expertise. The UK has a "Direct Entry" scheme for specialist officers.

### C. Acquisition Workforce Professionalization

- **Proposal**: Increase acquisition workforce compensation to be competitive with the private sector for technical roles (software engineers, data scientists, systems engineers). Implement Special Rate Tables and retention bonuses for critical acquisition specialties.
- **Proposal**: Create a dedicated "Technology Acquisition" career field with its own promotion track that values technical expertise rather than program management compliance. Currently, the acquisition workforce career path rewards process adherence over technical judgment.

### D. Expedite Security Clearances

- **Proposal**: Reduce the average clearance processing time from 8-12 months to 60-90 days through Continuous Vetting (CV), automation of background investigations, and reciprocal recognition of clearances across agencies.
- **Background**: The Trusted Workforce 2.0 initiative (2018) was intended to reform the clearance process. Implementation has been slow. Full implementation would remove one of the most significant barriers preventing commercial firms and their employees from engaging with classified defense work.

## 5. Infrastructure and Data

### A. Joint Simulation Environment (JSE)

- **Proposal**: Invest heavily in digital twins, synthetic training environments, and modeling and simulation infrastructure that allows rapid testing of AI, autonomous systems, and new operational concepts without expensive physical exercises.
- **Impact**: Accelerates the test-and-evaluation cycle; allows for thousands of simulated engagements to validate AI algorithms before live testing. Reduces the cost and timeline of operational testing.
- **Example**: The Air Force's Joint Simulation Environment has been used to test F-35 software and CCA concepts in high-fidelity virtual combat scenarios, compressing test timelines from months to weeks.

### B. Data Infrastructure and AI-Ready Data

- **Proposal**: Mandate open, interoperable data standards and APIs for all new defense systems. Invest in a unified data fabric (the CDAO's vision) that makes DoD data accessible across classification levels and organizational boundaries for AI training and analysis.
- **Mechanism**: Enforce through contract requirements and the CDAO's authority to set data standards. Establish "data exchanges" where operational data can be securely shared with AI developers under appropriate controls.
- **Challenge**: Bridging the classified-unclassified divide for AI development remains the hardest technical problem. Approaches include training models on synthetic data that mimics classified datasets, using federated learning to train models without moving data, and creating "cross-domain solutions" that allow secure data transfer.

### C. 5G and Advanced Networking

- **Proposal**: Accelerate the deployment of 5G and next-generation wireless networks on military bases and in operational environments. These networks provide the low-latency, high-bandwidth connectivity needed for autonomous systems, IoT sensors, and AI-enabled operations.
- **Status**: The DoD has established 5G testbeds at 12 military installations. Full operational deployment requires resolving spectrum-sharing conflicts with commercial carriers.

## 6. Supply Chain Resilience

### A. Microelectronics

- **Proposal**: Fully fund and accelerate CHIPS Act implementation to ensure domestic fabrication capacity for defense-critical semiconductors. Invest in the State of the Art Heterogeneous Integrated Packaging (SHIP) and Rapid Assured Microelectronics Prototypes (RAMP) programs to develop trusted domestic chip production.
- **Timeline**: TSMC, Samsung, and Intel fab construction in Arizona, Texas, and Ohio is underway but will not reach full production until 2027-2028. The DoD needs interim strategies to stockpile critical chips and diversify supply chains through allied-nation sources (Japan, South Korea, EU).

### B. Rare Earth Minerals

- **Proposal**: Use Defense Production Act Title III authorities and the newly established Defense Industrial Base Consortium to invest in domestic and allied-nation rare earth mining and processing. Reduce dependence on China (which controls ~60% of mining and ~90% of processing).
- **Investments**: Support MP Materials (Mountain Pass, CA), Lynas Rare Earths (U.S./Australia), and Energy Fuels (Utah) to build end-to-end supply chains outside Chinese control.

### C. Munitions Industrial Base

- **Proposal**: Invest in expanding production capacity for precision munitions (JDAM, Javelin, Stinger, GMLRS, HIMARS rounds), which Ukraine consumption has revealed to be dangerously inadequate. Current production rates cannot sustain a major conflict.
- **Status**: The Army has awarded multi-year contracts to increase Javelin production from ~2,100/year to ~4,000/year, and 155mm artillery shell production from ~24,000/month to ~100,000/month by 2028. These increases are necessary but may still fall short of wartime consumption rates.

## 7. Allied Technology Integration

### A. AUKUS Pillar II Implementation

- **Proposal**: Accelerate the implementation of AUKUS Pillar II by creating blanket ITAR exemptions for Australia and the United Kingdom in specified technology areas: AI, quantum, hypersonics, electronic warfare, undersea capabilities, and cyber.
- **Mechanism**: Pass legislation amending the Arms Export Control Act to create a "Strategic Technology Partnership" category with streamlined licensing or license-free transfer for specified technologies to designated allied nations.
- **Challenges**: The State Department's Directorate of Defense Trade Controls (DDTC) and parts of the intelligence community resist blanket exemptions, citing concerns about allied nations' export control enforcement and the risk of re-transfer to adversaries. These concerns are legitimate but must be weighed against the strategic cost of impeding allied interoperability.
- **International models**: The "Five Eyes" intelligence-sharing arrangement demonstrates that close allies can share highly sensitive information securely when appropriate frameworks are in place.

### B. Expand Allied Co-Development

- **Proposal**: Establish joint technology development programs with Japan, South Korea, and select EU nations modeled on AUKUS Pillar II. Focus on areas where allied capabilities complement U.S. strengths: Japanese robotics and manufacturing, South Korean shipbuilding and munitions, European quantum research.
- **Impact**: Expands the allied innovation base, reduces costs through burden-sharing, and creates interoperable capabilities that strengthen deterrence.

### C. Allied Defense Tech Market Access

- **Proposal**: Create pathways for allied defense technology companies (e.g., Israel's Rafael, South Korea's Hanwha, UK's BAE Systems) to compete directly for U.S. defense contracts on equal footing with domestic firms, when they offer superior technology. This would increase competition and reduce the U.S. primes' domestic monopoly.
- **Precedent**: The U.S. already procures some foreign-developed systems (e.g., the Italian-designed Beretta M9 pistol, the British-designed Harrier jet). Expanding this practice to emerging technology areas would increase competitive pressure.

## 8. Ethical Frameworks and International Norms

### A. Responsible AI Deployment

- **Proposal**: Fully implement the DoD's Responsible AI Strategy (2022) and RAI Toolkit. Require that all AI systems deployed in lethal decision-support roles undergo independent ethical review, rigorous testing for bias and reliability, and be designed with meaningful human oversight mechanisms.
- **Framework**: DoD Directive 3000.09 (Autonomy in Weapon Systems, updated 2023) requires senior-level review of autonomous weapons and mandates that "autonomous and semi-autonomous weapon systems shall be designed to allow commanders and operators to exercise appropriate levels of human judgment over the use of force."

### B. Shape International Norms

- **Proposal**: Rather than pursuing binding treaties that adversaries will ignore, the U.S. should lead the development of voluntary norms, confidence-building measures, and bilateral agreements on AI in warfare. The U.S.-China military AI dialogue (if it can be resumed) should focus on preventing accidental escalation through autonomous systems.
- **Model**: The 2023 Political Declaration on Responsible Military Use of AI and Autonomy, endorsed by 50+ nations, provides a foundation for norm-setting without the enforcement challenges of a treaty.

### C. Testing and Evaluation for AI Systems

- **Proposal**: Invest heavily in AI Test and Evaluation, Verification and Validation (TEVV) infrastructure. Create a dedicated AI testing organization within DOT&E (Director of Operational Test and Evaluation) with the technical expertise and authority to independently assess AI systems before deployment.
- **Mechanism**: Require that all AI systems with lethal applications undergo adversarial red-teaming, bias testing, and operational testing in realistic contested environments before approval for operational use.
- **Impact**: Builds public and military confidence in AI systems; creates a feedback loop between testing results and system improvement; provides a credible answer to critics who argue that AI systems are too unreliable for military use.
