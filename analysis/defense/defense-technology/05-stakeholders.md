# Defense Technology: Stakeholders

## Overview

The defense innovation ecosystem involves a clash between entrenched incumbents and disruptive newcomers, mediated by a massive government bureaucracy, influenced by congressional politics, and shaped by a growing venture capital community that sees defense technology as both a patriotic mission and a lucrative market. Understanding the motivations, leverage, and conflicts among these stakeholders is essential to diagnosing why reform is so difficult and identifying where coalitions for change might form.

## Government Stakeholders

### Executive Branch Leadership

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Under Secretary of Defense for Research and Engineering (OUSD(R&E))** | Advancing technology; maintaining technical superiority over adversaries. | Chief Technology Officer of the DoD; oversees DARPA, DIU, service labs, and the technology roadmap. Current office held significant influence under Heidi Shyu (2021-2025), who championed rapid prototyping and critical technology areas. |
| **Under Secretary of Defense for Acquisition and Sustainment (OUSD(A&S))** | Buying and maintaining systems efficiently; industrial base health. | The primary buyer; manages major acquisition programs, the acquisition workforce, and contracting policy. Tension with R&E is structural: R&E pushes new technology, A&S must make it producible and sustainable. |
| **Deputy Secretary of Defense** | Overall management; driving reform priorities. | Has historically been the champion of innovation initiatives (Bob Work for Third Offset, Kathleen Hicks for Replicator). The DepSecDef's engagement is often the difference between an innovation initiative succeeding or being absorbed by bureaucratic inertia. |

### Innovation Organizations

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Defense Innovation Unit (DIU)** | Accelerating commercial tech adoption. | The bridge between Silicon Valley and the Pentagon. Uses Commercial Solutions Openings (CSOs) and Other Transaction Authorities (OTAs) to contract rapidly. Led successively by Raj Shah, Mike Brown, and Doug Beck. DIU's elevation to report directly to the Secretary of Defense (2023) signaled growing institutional importance. |
| **DARPA** | High-risk, high-reward breakthrough research. | Funds "moonshot" projects that create new paradigms (the Internet, stealth, mRNA vaccines). DARPA's ~250-person staff and rotating program manager model (3-5 year terms) is widely considered the gold standard for government R&D management. Budget ~$4 billion/year. |
| **Chief Digital and AI Office (CDAO)** | Scaling AI and data analytics across the DoD. | Central hub for AI strategy, data infrastructure, and JADC2 data architecture. Established 2022, absorbing the Joint AI Center. Led by Dr. Craig Martell (2022-2024) and successors. Responsible for implementing the DoD's AI adoption strategy and the Responsible AI framework. |
| **Strategic Capabilities Office (SCO)** | Creative adaptation of existing systems. | Founded by Will Roper (2012), SCO repurposes proven technologies for new missions -- e.g., modifying Standard Missile-6 for anti-ship use, or adapting commercial swarming algorithms for military drones. Budget ~$1.5 billion. |
| **Service Research Labs** (AFRL, NRL, ARL) | Service-specific research and development. | Conduct basic and applied research tailored to Army, Navy, and Air Force needs. AFRL (Wright-Patterson AFB) focuses on aerospace systems; NRL (Washington, D.C.) on naval science; ARL (Adelphi, MD) on soldier systems. Collectively employ thousands of scientists and engineers. |

### Congress

| Actor | Primary Interest | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Armed Services Committees** (SASC, HASC) | Oversight, authorization, strategic direction. | Authorize defense programs through the annual NDAA. Control whether innovation initiatives receive legislative backing or are constrained by congressional adds for legacy programs. Key members: Chairs and ranking members of SASC and HASC. |
| **Appropriations Committees** (SAC-D, HAC-D) | Funding levels, spending priorities. | Control the actual funding. Can zero out innovation programs or add unrequested funds for legacy systems. The "power of the purse" is the ultimate determinant of whether reform rhetoric translates into real resources. |
| **Individual members with defense industry in their districts** | Jobs, economic stability, re-election. | Members whose districts host F-35 production (Fort Worth, TX), shipyards (Newport News, VA; Bath, ME), or other defense facilities have intense incentives to protect those programs regardless of strategic merit. |

## Industry Stakeholders

### Traditional Defense Industry (The Primes)

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Lockheed Martin** | F-35, missile defense (THAAD, Aegis), hypersonics, space. Revenue ~$71 billion. | The world's largest defense contractor. The F-35 program alone generates approximately $15 billion annually and has subcontractors in 45+ states, giving it unmatched political insulation. |
| **RTX (Raytheon Technologies)** | Missiles (Patriot, Stinger, Javelin), radar, Pratt & Whitney engines. Revenue ~$74 billion. | Dominant in missile defense and precision munitions. The Raytheon-United Technologies merger (2020) combined missiles with jet engines, creating a vertically integrated defense-aerospace conglomerate. |
| **Northrop Grumman** | B-21 Raider bomber, Sentinel ICBM, space systems, autonomous systems. Revenue ~$40 billion. | Sole source for both the next-generation strategic bomber and the replacement ICBM -- two of the most strategically important programs in the DoD. |
| **Boeing Defense** | KC-46 tanker, F/A-18, Apache, MQ-25 Stingray, SLS. Revenue ~$25 billion. | Has struggled with defense program execution (KC-46 deficiencies, Starliner delays, T-7A Red Hawk) and faces competition from newer entrants. |
| **General Dynamics** | Columbia-class submarines, Abrams tanks, IT systems (GDIT). Revenue ~$42 billion. | Controls the most critical undersea platform program (Columbia-class SSBN) and one of the largest defense IT businesses. |

**Collective interests**: Stable, long-term contracts; shareholder value; maintaining sole-source positions; shaping requirements to favor their capabilities; preserving legacy programs. The Big Five collectively spend approximately $60 million annually on lobbying and employ over 300 former government officials as lobbyists or advisors.

### Non-Traditional Defense Companies

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Key Product |
|---|---|---|
| **Anduril Industries** | Disruption of traditional defense acquisition; autonomous systems. | Lattice AI platform, Ghost drone, Altius drone, WISP tower sensors. Founded by Palmer Luckey (Oculus VR co-founder) in 2017, valued at $14+ billion by 2024. Won CCA Increment 1 contract. |
| **Palantir Technologies** | Data analytics and AI for defense and intelligence. | Gotham (intelligence), Foundry (defense logistics), AIP (AI platform). Publicly traded (market cap ~$60+ billion in 2024). Has aggressively challenged traditional IT contractors and the Army's acquisition bureaucracy, including filing (and winning) a protest against the DCGS-A program. |
| **Shield AI** | Autonomous aircraft. | V-BAT drone, Hivemind autonomy stack. Developing AI pilot technology for manned and unmanned aircraft. Raised $500+ million in venture funding. |
| **Kratos Defense** | Low-cost attritable drones, target drones. | XQ-58A Valkyrie, various target drones. One of the few non-traditional firms to have reached production scale in autonomous systems. |
| **SpaceX** | Launch services, satellite internet. | Falcon 9/Heavy, Starship, Starlink. Has fundamentally disrupted the launch market and demonstrated commercial space capabilities in Ukraine. |
| **Rebellion Defense, Epirus, Hermeus, Hadrian** | Various defense tech niches. | Software, directed energy, hypersonic aircraft, advanced manufacturing. Representative of the growing "defense tech" startup ecosystem. |

**Collective interests**: Winning programs of record (not just pilot contracts); reforming acquisition to favor speed and software; accessing defense budgets without being forced to partner with primes as subcontractors; proving that non-traditional firms can deliver at scale.

### Commercial Tech Giants

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Microsoft** | Cloud (Azure Government), enterprise software, IVAS/HoloLens. | Won the $10 billion JEDI cloud contract (later restructured as JWCC); provides Azure Government Cloud and the IVAS augmented reality headset for the Army. Willing to engage with defense despite internal employee pushback. |
| **Amazon Web Services (AWS)** | Cloud (AWS GovCloud), logistics AI. | Major JWCC awardee; provides classified cloud infrastructure. Jeff Bezos has publicly supported defense engagement. |
| **Google** | Cloud (Google Cloud for Government), AI. | Initially withdrew from Project Maven after employee protests (2018). Has since re-engaged with defense through Google Cloud's public sector division, though with stated ethical guardrails. JWCC awardee. |
| **Oracle** | Cloud, database, enterprise software. | JWCC awardee; long history of government IT contracting. |

### Venture Capitalists and Private Equity

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Andreessen Horowitz (a16z)** | Defense tech investment returns. | "American Dynamism" practice explicitly invests in defense and national security startups. Partners include Katherine Boyle, a prominent defense tech advocate. |
| **Founders Fund** | Disruption of legacy defense. | Peter Thiel's firm, early Palantir and Anduril investor. Thiel has been vocal about the need to disrupt the military-industrial complex. |
| **Lux Capital, Shield Capital, Razor's Edge Ventures** | Defense-specific VC. | Specialized firms that understand the defense market's unique timelines and customer dynamics. |

**Venture capital in defense tech**: Total VC investment in defense technology startups exceeded $30 billion cumulatively by 2024, up from near zero a decade earlier. However, the VC model (expecting returns within 5-7 years) remains in tension with defense acquisition timelines (7-10+ years to program of record).

### Think Tanks and Research Organizations

Think tanks play a significant role in shaping defense technology policy through research, convening, and the revolving door of personnel between think tanks and government.

| Organization | Funding Sources | Primary Influence |
|---|---|---|
| **Center for a New American Security (CNAS)** | Foundations, government grants, defense industry | AI policy, defense strategy, autonomous weapons norms |
| **Center for Security and Emerging Technology (CSET)** | Open Philanthropy, foundations | AI workforce, semiconductor policy, data-driven analysis |
| **RAND Corporation** | DoD contracts (FFRDC), foundations | Force structure, acquisition reform, operational analysis |
| **Hudson Institute** | Foundations, defense industry | Force design, defense concepts, naval strategy |
| **Special Competitive Studies Project (SCSP)** | Private philanthropy (Eric Schmidt) | Long-term AI and tech competitiveness policy |
| **Project on Government Oversight (POGO)** | Foundations, individual donors | Waste and fraud oversight, revolving door, accountability |
| **Stimson Center** | Foundations, government grants | Arms control, autonomous weapons governance |

Think tanks that receive defense industry funding (CSIS, Atlantic Council, CNAS) face structural conflicts of interest when assessing the programs of their funders, though most maintain editorial independence. Organizations funded primarily by foundations (CSET, POGO, Stimson) face fewer such conflicts but may have less access to industry data and expertise.

### The Acquisition Workforce

The 187,000-person DoD acquisition workforce is a stakeholder in its own right, distinct from the political leadership that sets policy.

- **Primary interest**: Professional advancement, job security, and process compliance. The acquisition workforce is trained on FAR/DFARS regulations and evaluates its own competence through mastery of those regulations.
- **Leverage**: Day-to-day control of contracting decisions, source selection boards, and requirements documents. While political leaders set broad direction, the acquisition workforce executes -- and can accelerate or slow-roll reforms through implementation choices.
- **Key tension**: Reforms that reduce the centrality of the FAR (OTAs, commercial buying, outcome-based contracting) are perceived by many acquisition professionals as threats to their relevance and expertise. Converting this workforce from potential opposition to reform champions requires retraining, new incentive structures, and respect for their institutional knowledge.

## Stakeholder Conflicts

### Primes vs. Non-Traditionals

- **Conflict**: Primes want to maintain their position as the primary systems integrators for major programs, relegating non-traditional firms to subcontractor roles. Non-traditionals want to sell directly to the government and compete for prime contracts, displacing legacy systems with software-defined alternatives.
- **Dynamics**: Primes lobby for requirements that favor their capabilities (e.g., requiring a track record of managing billion-dollar programs, which no startup can claim). Primes also attempt to acquire successful startups to neutralize competitive threats -- a strategy that some VCs encourage (as it provides their exit) but that reformers oppose (as it reduces competition).
- **Example**: When Anduril won the CCA Increment 1 contract in 2024 alongside General Atomics, it marked a rare victory for a non-traditional firm on a major weapons program, breaking the traditional primes' near-monopoly on combat aircraft.

### R&E vs. A&S (Innovators vs. Buyers)

- **Conflict**: OUSD(R&E) wants to push the envelope with new technology. OUSD(A&S) wants stable, producible, maintainable systems that can be delivered on time and on budget.
- **Dynamic**: This creates the institutional dimension of the "Valley of Death." R&E funds the prototype through DARPA or DIU, but A&S (and the Services) must budget for production, which they are often slow to do because it means reducing funding for existing programs.

### Congress vs. DoD Reformers

- **Conflict**: DoD leaders may want to "divest to invest" -- retiring old aircraft to fund new drones, decommissioning cruisers to fund unmanned surface vessels. Congress often blocks these retirements to protect jobs at factories or bases in their districts.
- **Example**: In FY2024, the Air Force requested to retire 310 older aircraft (including F-15C/Ds, F-16C/Ds, A-10s, and KC-135s) to free up funds for CCA and NGAD. Congress rejected the majority of these divestments, forcing the Air Force to simultaneously maintain aging fleets and invest in next-generation systems with insufficient funding for either.

### Tech Workers vs. Tech Leadership

- **Conflict**: Employees at major tech firms have protested defense contracts on ethical grounds. Leadership wants the lucrative government revenue and views national security work as a civic responsibility.
- **Evolution**: The initial intensity of the 2018 Google walkout has moderated. By 2024, most major tech firms had established government divisions with dedicated staff who self-selected for defense work. The "tech lash" against defense work has become more nuanced, with many employees accepting defensive and humanitarian applications while remaining skeptical of lethal autonomous weapons.

### Allies vs. ITAR

- **Conflict**: Close allies (UK, Australia, Japan, South Korea) want access to advanced U.S. defense technology for interoperability and shared deterrence. ITAR export control regulations impose lengthy review processes (often 12-18 months for a license) and restrictions that allies view as paternalistic and counterproductive.
- **AUKUS tension**: Implementing AUKUS Pillar II (technology sharing) requires ITAR exemptions for Australia and the UK. Legislation to create these exemptions has been introduced but faces resistance from export control advocates who fear precedent-setting erosion of the regime.

## International Stakeholders

### Allied Governments

| Ally | Primary Interest | Key Defense Tech Capability |
|---|---|---|
| **United Kingdom** | AUKUS Pillar II implementation; access to U.S. technology; maintaining interoperability. | AI (Dstl, Alan Turing Institute), autonomous systems (Team Tempest/GCAP), submarine technology. |
| **Australia** | AUKUS nuclear submarines; building domestic defense tech industry; Pacific security. | Autonomous undersea vehicles, space situational awareness, quantum technology (Q-CTRL). |
| **Japan** | Counter-China deterrence; long-range strike; emerging technology access. | Robotics, hypersonics (joint development with U.S.), semiconductor equipment (Tokyo Electron). |
| **South Korea** | Autonomous systems, AI; defense industry exports. | Hanwha (defense robotics, munitions), Korean defense tech startups, semiconductor manufacturing (Samsung). |
| **Israel** | Combat-proven autonomous systems; defense tech innovation. | Iron Dome, Harop loitering munition, Rafael's AI-enabled systems, MAFAT (DDR&D) rapid acquisition model. |

Allied governments are important stakeholders because technology sharing (or its restriction) directly affects coalition warfighting capability. ITAR restrictions have been a persistent source of friction, with allies arguing that U.S. export controls impede interoperability and push them toward non-U.S. suppliers.

### Adversary Stakeholders

- **China's Central Military Commission (CMC) Science and Technology Commission**: Oversees the integration of advanced technology into PLA modernization. China views technological competition as a "whole of nation" endeavor and does not face the commercial-military divide that hampers U.S. innovation.
- **Russia's Foundation for Advanced Research Projects (FPI)**: Russia's equivalent of DARPA, though with far fewer resources. Focused on hypersonics, electronic warfare, and AI for autonomous systems.

### The Warfighter

Often invoked but rarely heard in acquisition debates, the warfighter -- the soldier, sailor, airman, or Marine who will use these technologies in combat -- has a distinct set of interests:

- **Primary interest**: Technology that works reliably, is easy to maintain, and provides a decisive advantage in combat. Warfighters generally care about capability, not about which company built it or which acquisition pathway was used.
- **Leverage**: Operational test reports, combatant commander priority lists, and direct congressional testimony. Warfighter voices are powerful when they speak publicly, but are often filtered through service headquarters staff before reaching policymakers.
- **Frustration**: Warfighters consistently report that the technology they receive is inferior to what is commercially available and arrives years after it was needed. The phrase "I can buy better technology at Best Buy than what the Army issues me" is a recurring refrain.

The fundamental imbalance is that the actors who benefit from the status quo (prime contractors, congressional members with defense industry in their districts) are highly organized and well-resourced, while those who would benefit from reform (startups, warfighters who need better technology, taxpayers) are diffuse and less politically powerful. The growing venture capital community and non-traditional defense firms represent a potentially disruptive force, but they have not yet accumulated the political weight to overcome incumbent resistance at scale.
