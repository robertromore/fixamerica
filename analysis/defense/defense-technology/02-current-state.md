# Technology and Innovation: Current State

## Research and Development Funding

The Department of Defense's budget for Research, Development, Test, and Evaluation (RDT&E) is at a historic high, reflecting the strategic urgency of technological competition with China and Russia. The FY2025 RDT&E request of $143.2 billion exceeds the entire defense budget of any nation other than the United States and China. However, the allocation of these funds reveals persistent tensions between sustaining legacy systems and investing in transformative capabilities.

### DoD RDT&E Budget by Category (FY2025 Request)

| Category | Amount | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Basic Research (6.1) | $3.0 billion | University-led fundamental scientific study. |
| Applied Research (6.2) | $7.8 billion | Translating basic science into useful applications. |
| Advanced Technology Development (6.3) | $17.5 billion | Developing and demonstrating new technologies (DARPA's primary budget area). |
| Advanced Component Development and Prototypes (6.4) | $38.9 billion | Building and testing prototypes (e.g., B-21, NGAD, CCA). |
| System Development and Demonstration (6.5) | $22.8 billion | Engineering and manufacturing development (EMD) phase. |
| Operational System Development (6.7) | $53.2 billion | Upgrading and improving existing fielded systems. |
| **Total RDT&E** | **$143.2 billion** | |

*Source: DoD Comptroller, R-1 Budget Exhibits, FY2025.*

A critical observation: the single largest category -- Operational System Development (6.7) at $53.2 billion -- funds upgrades to *existing* systems, not new ones. This reflects the reality that the DoD spends more on maintaining and incrementally improving legacy platforms than on developing the next generation of capabilities. The truly forward-looking categories (6.1 through 6.3, which fund basic research through advanced technology demonstration) total only $28.3 billion, or roughly 20% of the RDT&E budget.

## Key Technology Areas

### Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Autonomy

- **Spending**: The DoD requested approximately **$3.5 billion** for AI-specific projects in its unclassified FY2025 budget. The true figure is likely significantly higher when including classified programs and AI embedded within larger systems.
- **Key Initiative -- Replicator**: Announced by Deputy Secretary of Defense Kathleen Hicks in August 2023, this initiative aims to field thousands of "small, smart, cheap" autonomous systems across multiple domains within 18-24 months to counter China's mass advantage. Replicator I focused on fielding systems by August 2025; Replicator II expanded the concept to counter-UAS and other mission areas. The initiative tests whether the DoD can bypass its traditional acquisition timeline.
- **Key Organization -- Chief Digital and AI Office (CDAO)**: Established in June 2022 under the direction of Dr. Craig Martell (former head of machine learning at Lyft) to synchronize and accelerate the adoption of data, analytics, and AI across the department. The CDAO absorbed the former Joint AI Center (JAIC) and oversees the Joint All-Domain Command and Control (JADC2) data architecture.
- **Key Programs**:
    - **Project Maven**: Launched in 2017 to apply AI to processing intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance (ISR) data from drones and satellites. Initially controversial when Google employees protested the company's involvement; Google withdrew, and the contract moved to other vendors including Palantir.
    - **Collaborative Combat Aircraft (CCA)**: Autonomous drone wingmen designed to accompany manned fighter jets (F-35, NGAD). Increment 1 contracts awarded to Anduril and General Atomics in 2024, with initial fielding targeted for the late 2020s. Each CCA is intended to cost roughly one-third of a manned fighter.
    - **Autonomous Surface and Undersea Vessels**: The Navy is developing Large Unmanned Surface Vessels (LUSVs) and Extra-Large Unmanned Undersea Vehicles (XLUUVs, including the Orca program built by Boeing). These are intended to distribute naval combat power across more platforms at lower cost per hull.
    - **Task Force Lima**: Established in 2023 to assess and integrate generative AI tools (large language models) for administrative, analytical, and operational uses across the DoD.

### Cyber Warfare

- **Budget**: The DoD requested **$14.5 billion** for cyberspace activities in FY2025, making cyber one of the fastest-growing investment areas.
- **Force Structure -- Cyber Mission Force (CMF)**: Comprised of 133 teams and approximately 6,200 personnel, organized into Combat Mission Teams (offensive), Cyber Protection Teams (defensive), and National Mission Teams (defending critical infrastructure). The CMF is slated to grow to 147 teams.
- **Strategic Concept -- Defend Forward**: U.S. Cyber Command (USCYBERCOM) aims to counter adversary cyber operations at their source, before they reach U.S. networks. This doctrine was operationalized in actions against Russian interference in the 2018 midterm elections and against ISIS cyber infrastructure.
- **Challenge**: A persistent shortage of trained cyber personnel, with high competition from the private sector. A mid-level cyber operator can earn $150,000-$300,000 in the private sector compared to $80,000-$120,000 in government service, creating a chronic retention problem.

### Hypersonics

- **Definition**: Weapons that travel at speeds greater than Mach 5 (approximately 3,800 mph at sea level) and are highly maneuverable, making them difficult to track and intercept with current missile defense systems.
- **Types**: Two primary categories -- hypersonic glide vehicles (HGVs) that are launched by a rocket booster and glide to target, and hypersonic cruise missiles (HCMs) powered by scramjet engines for sustained hypersonic flight.
- **U.S. Programs**:
    - **Army**: Long-Range Hypersonic Weapon (LRHW) "Dark Eagle" -- a ground-launched boost-glide system with a range exceeding 1,725 miles. Battery fielded to 5th Battalion, 3rd Field Artillery Regiment in 2023, but full operational capability has been delayed.
    - **Navy**: Conventional Prompt Strike (CPS) -- a submarine-launched variant using the same Common Hypersonic Glide Body (C-HGB) as the Army system. Integration into Virginia-class submarines is underway.
    - **Air Force**: Hypersonic Attack Cruise Missile (HACM) -- a scramjet-powered air-launched cruise missile under development by Raytheon and Northrop Grumman. The Air Force's earlier AGM-183A ARRW boost-glide program was canceled in 2023 after multiple test failures.
- **Status**: The U.S. has faced multiple test failures and significant delays. The AGM-183A ARRW failed three consecutive flight tests before cancellation. The U.S. is widely assessed as having fallen behind both China (which has deployed the DF-17 HGV and DF-27) and Russia (which has used the Kinzhal air-launched ballistic missile in Ukraine, though its true hypersonic maneuverability is debated).

### Directed Energy

- **High-Energy Lasers (HEL)**: The DoD is investing in laser weapons for short-range air defense (C-UAS, counter-rocket/mortar), with systems ranging from 50 kW to 300+ kW. Key systems include the Army's DE-SHORAD (Directed Energy Short-Range Air Defense) and the Navy's HELIOS system installed on USS Preble.
- **High-Power Microwave (HPM)**: Systems like the Air Force's THOR (Tactical High-power Operational Responder) can disable swarms of drones by frying their electronics, offering a potential counter to mass drone attacks.
- **Advantages**: Near-zero marginal cost per shot (electricity vs. expensive interceptor missiles), deep magazines, and speed-of-light engagement. A laser shot costs approximately $1-$10, compared to $50,000-$3 million for a conventional interceptor missile.
- **Limitations**: Atmospheric absorption and scattering reduce effectiveness in rain, fog, dust, and smoke. Current systems lack the power for long-range engagement or heavily armored targets. Thermal management (cooling the laser) remains an engineering challenge on mobile platforms.

### Space

- **Organization**: The U.S. Space Force, established December 20, 2019, is responsible for organizing, training, and equipping forces for the space domain. U.S. Space Command (USSPACECOM) is the operational combatant command.
- **Key Challenge**: The increasing vulnerability of U.S. satellite constellations (GPS, communications, ISR, missile warning) to adversary anti-satellite (ASAT) weapons. China tested a direct-ascent ASAT weapon in 2007, destroying a defunct weather satellite and creating thousands of debris objects. Both China and Russia have demonstrated co-orbital ASAT capabilities, ground-based laser dazzling, and GPS jamming.
- **U.S. Response**: Shifting from a small number of large, exquisite satellites to a more resilient architecture of smaller, more numerous satellites in proliferated low-Earth orbit (pLEO). The Space Development Agency (SDA) is building the Proliferated Warfighter Space Architecture (PWSA), a mesh network of hundreds of satellites for missile tracking and data transport.
- **Commercial Integration**: SpaceX's Starlink constellation has demonstrated the military utility of commercial LEO broadband -- Ukraine's military has relied heavily on Starlink for communications since 2022. The DoD is exploring how to leverage commercial space capabilities through programs like the Commercial Augmentation Space Reserve.

### Quantum Science

- **Quantum Computing**: DARPA's Underexplored Systems for Utility-Scale Quantum Computing (US2QC) program is assessing whether quantum computers can reach utility scale within the next decade. Potential defense applications include breaking current encryption (threatening RSA/ECC), optimizing logistics and supply chains, and accelerating materials science.
- **Quantum Sensing**: Potentially nearer-term applications include quantum clocks for GPS-denied navigation, quantum magnetometers for submarine detection, and quantum radar for stealth aircraft detection. The Army Research Laboratory is investing heavily in quantum inertial navigation.
- **Quantum Communications**: Quantum key distribution (QKD) offers theoretically unbreakable encryption. China has demonstrated satellite-based QKD via its Micius satellite (2017) and has built a 2,000-km quantum communication backbone between Beijing and Shanghai.
- **U.S. Position**: The U.S. leads in quantum computing hardware (IBM, Google, IonQ, Quantinuum) but trails China in quantum communications deployment and some quantum sensing applications.

## The Defense Innovation Ecosystem

The DoD relies on a network of organizations to bridge the gap between the military and the technology sector. The effectiveness of these organizations varies widely.

| Organization | Mission | Budget (FY2025 Req.) | Key Characteristic |
|---|---|---|---|
| **DARPA** | Prevent and create strategic surprise. Focus on high-risk, high-reward "breakthrough" technologies. | ~$4.0 billion | Long-term (3-5 year programs), revolutionary research. Has produced the Internet, stealth, GPS, mRNA vaccines. |
| **Defense Innovation Unit (DIU)** | Accelerate the adoption of commercial technology into the military. | ~$900 million (including transition funds) | Uses Other Transaction Authorities (OTAs) and Commercial Solutions Openings (CSOs) to contract in 60-90 days vs. 18+ months. |
| **Strategic Capabilities Office (SCO)** | Re-imagine existing systems for new, unexpected missions. | ~$1.5 billion | Focuses on creative and rapid integration of proven technologies in surprising ways. |
| **Service Research Labs** (AFRL, NRL, ARL) | Conduct service-specific scientific research and advanced development. | Varies by service | Deep technical expertise aligned with Army, Navy, and Air Force needs. |
| **In-Q-Tel** | CIA-affiliated venture capital firm that invests in startups developing technologies relevant to intelligence. | Not publicly disclosed | Has made 500+ investments since 1999, including early investments in Palantir, Keyhole (Google Earth). |

### The SBIR/STTR Pipeline

The Small Business Innovation Research (SBIR) and Small Business Technology Transfer (STTR) programs are the primary federal mechanism for funding early-stage defense technology from small businesses.

- **Annual funding**: Over $2 billion across all federal agencies, with the DoD as the largest single funder (~$1.5 billion).
- **Structure**: Phase I (feasibility, ~$150K); Phase II (prototype, ~$1M); Phase III (transition to production, no set funding limit but no set-aside either).
- **Problem**: The "SBIR mill" phenomenon -- some firms specialize in winning Phase I and II awards but never transition technology to production. GAO has found that fewer than 25% of SBIR Phase II projects transition to Phase III or a program of record.
- **Reform efforts**: The SBIR/STTR Reauthorization Act of 2022 increased the set-aside percentage and expanded "Direct to Phase II" authorities to accelerate timelines.

## Adversary Capabilities

The strategic pivot toward emerging technology is driven by rapid advances by competitors, particularly China.

### China

- **Strategy**: A state-directed "Military-Civil Fusion" (MCF) strategy, formalized in 2015 and elevated to national-level priority under Xi Jinping, that erases barriers between commercial and defense sectors to harness all national resources for technological competition. Unlike the U.S. system, where the government must persuade commercial firms to work with the military, China can compel it.
- **Key Strengths**:
    - **AI**: Massive government investment (the 2017 New Generation AI Development Plan set a goal of becoming the world leader in AI by 2030), vast data availability from a surveillance state with limited privacy protections, and leading commercial AI companies (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent, SenseTime, iFlytek).
    - **Hypersonics**: Has fielded operational systems (DF-17 HGV, reportedly DF-27) and tested a fractional orbital bombardment system with a hypersonic glide vehicle in July 2021, which the chairman of the Joint Chiefs called "very close to a Sputnik moment."
    - **Quantum**: The world's first quantum satellite (Micius, 2016), a 2,000-km quantum communication network, and heavy state investment in quantum computing through the National Laboratory for Quantum Information Sciences in Hefei.
    - **Shipbuilding and Drones**: The world's largest shipbuilder (producing more naval tonnage annually than the U.S.) and the world's largest producer of commercial drones (DJI holds ~70% global market share). This industrial base provides a massive foundation for military scale production.
    - **Microelectronics**: While trailing the U.S. and Taiwan in cutting-edge chip fabrication (sub-7nm), China is investing heavily in mature-node production and has made unexpected progress (Huawei's Kirin 9000s chip, fabricated by SMIC, surprised analysts in 2023).

### Russia

- **Key Strengths**:
    - **Hypersonics**: Has used the Kinzhal air-launched ballistic missile in combat in Ukraine and claims to have deployed the Avangard HGV on ICBMs and the Tsirkon (Zircon) sea-launched hypersonic cruise missile.
    - **Cyber**: Sophisticated and aggressive cyber warfare capabilities demonstrated in attacks on Estonia (2007), Georgia (2008), Ukraine (2015-2016 power grid attacks, NotPetya 2017), and ongoing operations against NATO nations.
    - **Electronic Warfare (EW)**: Advanced capabilities to jam GPS, communications, and drone control signals. Russian EW systems (Krasukha-4, Zhitel, Borisoglebsk-2) have proved effective in Ukraine, degrading Ukrainian drone and precision munition performance in some sectors.
- **Weakness**: Russia's conventional technology and industrial base have been significantly degraded by the war in Ukraine. Sanctions have cut access to Western microelectronics; Russia has reportedly scavenged chips from dishwashers and refrigerators for military equipment. Russia's long-term technological trajectory is declining without access to Western technology inputs.

### Lessons from Ukraine

The Russia-Ukraine war (2022-present) has provided the most significant real-world laboratory for defense technology since the 1991 Gulf War:

- **Commercial tech in combat**: Ukraine's military relies heavily on Starlink satellite internet, commercial DJI and locally-produced FPV drones, open-source intelligence (OSINT), and Western-provided precision munitions. The war demonstrated that commercial technology, rapidly adapted, can be decisive.
- **Drone warfare**: Both sides are consuming thousands of small drones per month for reconnaissance, strike, and electronic warfare. First-person-view (FPV) drones costing $500-$2,000 are destroying armored vehicles worth millions, fundamentally altering the cost calculus of ground warfare.
- **Electronic warfare dominance**: Russia's extensive EW capabilities have degraded GPS-guided munitions (JDAM-ER, Excalibur) and forced Ukraine to adapt with fiber-optic guided drones and terrain-following navigation.
- **Attritable mass matters**: Both sides are burning through equipment and munitions at rates that far exceed peacetime production capacity, vindicating arguments for cheaper, more numerous, and more rapidly producible systems over small numbers of exquisite platforms.

## Microelectronics and Supply Chain Vulnerabilities

The most critical single-point-of-failure in the defense technology ecosystem is the global semiconductor supply chain.

- **Taiwan dependency**: Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC) fabricates approximately 90% of the world's most advanced chips (sub-7nm). A Chinese blockade or invasion of Taiwan would immediately cut off the supply of advanced processors for U.S. weapons systems, communications equipment, and the broader economy.
- **CHIPS and Science Act (2022)**: Authorized $52.7 billion in subsidies for domestic semiconductor manufacturing. TSMC, Samsung, and Intel are building new fabrication facilities in Arizona, Texas, and Ohio, but these plants will not reach full production until 2027-2028 and will initially focus on commercial chips.
- **Trusted Foundry Program**: The DoD maintains a "trusted foundry" program to ensure that chips for classified and sensitive defense applications are fabricated in secure, U.S.-based facilities. However, the trusted foundry ecosystem operates at technology nodes that are 2-3 generations behind the commercial state of the art.
- **Rare earth minerals**: China controls approximately 60% of rare earth mining and 90% of rare earth processing. Rare earths are essential for precision-guided munitions, jet engines, satellite systems, and electronic warfare equipment. The DoD is investing in alternative supply chains through the Defense Production Act Title III program.

## Directed Energy: Current Programs and Status

The DoD is investing in directed energy weapons across multiple services, driven by the need for cost-effective defense against drone swarms and precision munitions.

### Active Programs

| Program | Service | Power Class | Target Set | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| DE-SHORAD (Directed Energy Short-Range Air Defense) | Army | 50 kW | Drones, rockets, mortars | Prototyping; initial fielding targeted for 2025-2026. |
| HELIOS (High Energy Laser with Integrated Optical-dazzler and Surveillance) | Navy | 60+ kW | Small boats, UAVs, ISR sensors | Installed on USS Preble (DDG-88) for at-sea testing, 2024. |
| LANCE (Layered Laser Defense) | MDA | 300+ kW (target) | Cruise missiles, UAVs | Demonstrator; intended for boost-phase missile defense. |
| THOR (Tactical High-power Operational Responder) | Air Force | HPM | Drone swarms | Deployed for testing at undisclosed overseas location, 2023. |
| ODIN (Optical Dazzling Interdictor, Navy) | Navy | Low power | ISR sensors, electro-optical systems | Fielded on multiple destroyers for sensor dazzling. |

### Cost-Per-Shot Advantage

The economic argument for directed energy is compelling:

- Laser shot: ~$1-$10 (cost of electricity)
- Stinger missile: ~$38,000
- Patriot interceptor (PAC-3): ~$4 million
- SM-6 interceptor: ~$4.3 million

Using a $4 million missile to shoot down a $2,000 drone is economically unsustainable. Directed energy weapons offer a path to favorable cost exchange ratios.

## Other Transaction Authorities (OTAs) and Rapid Contracting

Other Transaction Authorities have become the primary tool for engaging non-traditional defense companies.

### OTA Usage Growth

| Fiscal Year | DoD OTA Obligations | Number of OTA Awards | % of Total DoD Contracting |
|---|---|---|---|
| FY2016 | ~$3.7 billion | ~1,200 | <1% |
| FY2019 | ~$7.8 billion | ~3,500 | ~2% |
| FY2022 | ~$18.5 billion | ~6,000+ | ~4% |
| FY2024 (est.) | ~$25+ billion | ~8,000+ | ~5% |

*Sources: GAO; FPDS; Congressional Research Service.*

### OTA Advantages and Criticisms

- **Advantages**: Faster award timelines (60-90 days vs. 12-18 months for FAR-based contracts); ability to attract non-traditional firms that refuse to accept FAR compliance requirements; prototype-to-production authority under 10 U.S.C. 4022.
- **Criticisms**: GAO and some congressional members have raised concerns about reduced competition transparency, limited audit trails, and potential for abuse. A 2023 GAO report found that some OTA awards were made to companies that could not accurately be described as "non-traditional."
- **Reform opportunity**: Codify best practices for OTA oversight without reverting to full FAR compliance, which would destroy the speed advantage.

## Export Controls and Technology Transfer

- **ITAR (International Traffic in Arms Regulations)**: Controls the export of defense articles and services. Critics argue that ITAR is overly broad, preventing the U.S. from sharing technology with close allies (UK, Australia, Japan) while failing to prevent adversary acquisition through cyber theft, academic exchange, and third-country procurement.
- **AUKUS Pillar II**: The AUKUS agreement (2021) between the U.S., UK, and Australia includes a second pillar focused on sharing advanced technologies (AI, quantum, hypersonics, electronic warfare) among the three nations. Implementing Pillar II requires carving out exceptions to ITAR, which has proved politically and bureaucratically difficult.
- **Entity List and Chip Controls**: The Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) has imposed increasingly stringent export controls on advanced semiconductor equipment and chips to China, including the October 2022 controls that restricted sales of chips above certain performance thresholds and of the equipment used to manufacture them.
