# Defense Technology: What You Can Do

## Overview

Engaging with defense technology policy is not just for generals and CEOs. The technologies being developed and deployed -- artificial intelligence, autonomous weapons, cyber capabilities, hypersonic missiles -- will shape the security environment, the economy, and the ethical boundaries of warfare for decades to come. Citizens, students, tech workers, investors, and policymakers all play vital roles in determining whether the United States can sustain its technological edge while deploying these capabilities responsibly.

## For Tech Workers and Engineers

### Serve in Government

- **Defense Digital Service (DDS)**: Apply for a "tour of duty" bringing your technical skills to the Pentagon for 1-2 years. DDS teams have worked on problems ranging from GPS security to military healthcare IT. Positions are typically at the GS-13 to GS-15 level with competitive compensation. Website: [dds.mil](https://dds.mil)
- **Presidential Innovation Fellows (PIF)**: A 12-month fellowship placing mid-to-senior-career technologists in federal agencies, including the DoD, to lead high-impact projects. Run by the General Services Administration. Website: [presidentialinnovationfellows.gov](https://presidentialinnovationfellows.gov)
- **U.S. Digital Corps**: A two-year fellowship for early-career technologists (software engineers, data scientists, designers, cybersecurity specialists) to work on government digital services. Website: [digitalcorps.gsa.gov](https://digitalcorps.gsa.gov)
- **Civilian acquisition workforce**: The DoD employs over 180,000 acquisition professionals and is actively recruiting software engineers, data scientists, AI specialists, and systems engineers. Positions are posted on USAJobs.gov. Pay reform initiatives (Special Rate Tables) are making government tech salaries more competitive, though still below private sector peaks.

### Engage with the Defense Innovation Ecosystem

- **Work at a defense tech startup**: Companies like Anduril, Shield AI, Palantir, Kratos, Epirus, Rebellion Defense, and Hadrian are actively recruiting engineers who want to work on national security problems with startup speed and culture. These firms offer private-sector compensation while working on defense missions.
- **Explore opportunities through DIU**: If you work at a commercial technology company, explore whether your company's products have defense applications through the Defense Innovation Unit's Commercial Solutions Opening (CSO) portal. DIU is actively seeking commercial AI, autonomy, space, cyber, and energy solutions. Website: [diu.mil](https://www.diu.mil)
- **SBIR/STTR programs**: If you are at a small business with a technology that could have defense applications, explore the Small Business Innovation Research and Small Business Technology Transfer programs. DoD SBIR topics are published at [dodsbirsttr.mil](https://www.dodsbirsttr.mil). Phase I awards (~$150K) provide seed funding to assess feasibility.
- **National Security Innovation Network (NSIN)**: NSIN connects entrepreneurs, innovators, and technologists with DoD problem sets. They run "Hacking for Defense" courses at universities and "X-Force" fellowships placing PhDs in defense organizations. Website: [nsin.mil](https://www.nsin.mil)

### Engage Ethically

- **Do not just boycott -- participate**: Rather than categorically refusing to work on defense technology, engage in the conversation about *how* these technologies should be developed and deployed. The ethical guardrails for military AI will be shaped by the engineers who build these systems. If the most ethically conscious technologists opt out, the systems will still be built -- just without their input.
- **Advocate for responsible AI**: Push for rigorous testing, bias detection, and human oversight mechanisms in any AI system with life-or-death consequences. Support your company's adoption of responsible AI frameworks aligned with the DoD's RAI principles.
- **Understand the distinction**: There is a meaningful ethical difference between building AI for nuclear command and control, AI for logistics optimization, and AI for medical triage. Engage with the specifics rather than treating all defense work as equivalent.

## For Students and Researchers

### Build the Talent Pipeline

- **STEM education**: Pursue degrees in artificial intelligence, robotics, quantum science, cybersecurity, electrical engineering, and systems engineering. The nation's ability to sustain technological advantage depends on a deep talent pool in these fields. The NSCAI estimated that the U.S. needs to produce 2x more STEM graduates annually to remain competitive with China.
- **SMART Scholarship**: The DoD's Science, Mathematics, and Research for Transformation (SMART) Scholarship provides full tuition, a stipend, and summer internships in exchange for a commitment to civilian service at a DoD laboratory or facility after graduation. Open to undergraduate and graduate students in STEM fields. Website: [smartscholarship.org](https://smartscholarship.org)
- **National Science Foundation Graduate Research Fellowship**: While not defense-specific, NSF-funded research in AI, materials science, quantum, and cybersecurity has direct defense relevance. The government's investment in basic research is the foundation of long-term technological advantage.
- **Reserve Officers' Training Corps (ROTC)**: For students who want to serve in uniform, ROTC scholarships cover tuition and provide a path to commissioning as an officer. Cyber, space, and intelligence branches increasingly seek STEM graduates.

### Engage in Defense-Relevant Research

- **Hacking for Defense (H4D)**: Take the H4D course (offered at 50+ universities), which pairs student teams with real DoD problem sponsors to develop technology solutions over a semester. The course has produced viable startups and has introduced thousands of students to national security challenges. Website: [h4d.stanford.edu](https://www.h4d.stanford.edu)
- **University Affiliated Research Centers (UARCs)**: Participate in research at UARCs such as the Applied Physics Laboratory (Johns Hopkins), the Applied Research Laboratory (Penn State), or the Software Engineering Institute (Carnegie Mellon). These centers work on cutting-edge defense problems and offer positions for graduate students and post-docs.
- **DARPA programs**: Graduate researchers can participate in DARPA-funded programs through their universities. DARPA's Information Innovation Office (I2O) funds research in AI, human-machine interaction, and cyber. The Young Faculty Award (YFA) provides funding to junior faculty working on defense-relevant research.
- **National labs**: Department of Energy national labs (Sandia, Los Alamos, Lawrence Livermore, Oak Ridge) conduct defense-relevant research in nuclear weapons science, high-performance computing, materials science, and cybersecurity. Internship and fellowship programs are available for students at all levels.

## For Citizens and Voters

### Demand Accountability and Reform

- **Ask the right questions**: Contact your representatives and ask why the Pentagon takes 7-10 years to field technology that commercial companies deploy in months. Ask whether they support PPBE reform, the Technology Transition Fund, and competitive acquisition practices. Specifically ask whether they have blocked the retirement of legacy weapons systems to protect jobs in their district at the expense of military modernization.
- **Support acquisition reform legislation**: Track and support NDAA provisions that advance acquisition reform, software pathway expansion, DIU empowerment, and non-traditional vendor access. The annual NDAA is the primary vehicle for defense innovation policy.
- **Scrutinize congressional adds**: When Congress adds billions of dollars for weapons the Pentagon did not request, ask whether those additions reflect genuine military need or district-level politics. Organizations like the Project on Government Oversight (POGO) and Taxpayers for Common Sense track and analyze congressional adds.

### Support Supply Chain Resilience

- **Advocate for reshoring**: Support policies that bring critical supply chains -- semiconductors, rare earth minerals, pharmaceutical precursors, energetics -- back to the U.S. or to allied nations. The CHIPS Act is a start, but broader industrial policy is needed. This is not just a defense issue; it is an economic security issue.
- **Understand the China competition**: Educate yourself on the technological competition with China. It is not just a military issue; it affects economic competitiveness, privacy (TikTok, Huawei), and the norms governing AI globally. Read the NSCAI Final Report, Chris Miller's *Chip War*, or the SCSP's recommendations for accessible overviews.

### Engage on Ethics

- **Participate in the autonomous weapons debate**: The question of whether machines should make lethal decisions is not merely a technical issue -- it is a democratic one. Attend town halls, write to representatives, and engage with organizations on all sides of the issue (Campaign to Stop Killer Robots, Center for a New American Security, Stimson Center) to develop an informed position.
- **Support transparency**: Push for greater transparency in how the DoD develops and deploys AI. The DoD's Responsible AI framework is a start, but independent oversight and public reporting on autonomous systems deployments, testing outcomes, and civilian harm incidents are needed.

## For Policymakers and Their Staff

### Prioritize Innovation in Oversight

- **Visit DIU, DARPA, and startups**: Do not limit engagement to the Big Five primes. Visit the Defense Innovation Unit in Mountain View, DARPA-funded labs, and defense tech startups to understand the pace of commercial innovation and the barriers that prevent it from reaching the warfighter.
- **Hold hearings on the Valley of Death**: Use Armed Services Committee hearings to spotlight specific cases where promising technologies died between prototype and production. Name the programs, the vendors, and the budget decisions that killed them. Public scrutiny is the most powerful tool for driving reform.
- **Track non-traditional metrics**: Request that the DoD report annually on the percentage of contract dollars going to non-traditional vendors, average time from prototype to production, and the number of startups that entered and exited the defense market. What gets measured gets managed.

### Protect R&D and Modernization Budgets

- **Resist the temptation to cut "science projects"**: In every budget cycle, there is pressure to cut forward-looking R&D (basic and applied research, DARPA programs) to pay for near-term readiness or legacy platform sustainment. This is strategically self-destructive. The technologies that will define the 2030s and 2040s battlefield are being funded (or not funded) in today's R&D budget.
- **Champion the Technology Transition Fund**: Appropriate dedicated bridge funding to prevent startups from dying in the Valley of Death. A $1-2 billion annual appropriation would cost less than a single destroyer but could sustain dozens of innovative companies.

### Fix the Rules

- **Pass PPBE reform legislation**: The PPBE Reform Commission produced actionable recommendations. Implement them through NDAA language: portfolio budgeting, delegated reprogramming authority, software appropriation reform, and outcome-based metrics.
- **Reform the "color of money"**: Champion legislation that creates a unified software appropriation category (BA-8) and allows flexible movement of funds between development, procurement, and sustainment for software-intensive and AI programs.
- **Streamline ITAR for allies**: Pass legislation creating blanket ITAR exemptions for AUKUS partners (Australia, UK) and explore expanded exemptions for Japan, South Korea, and other close allies. Current export controls impede technology sharing with partners more than they prevent adversary acquisition.
- **Expand lateral entry authorities**: Authorize all military services to commission experienced technical professionals at mid-career ranks and establish competitive pay scales for critical technical specialties in the acquisition workforce.

## For Investors and Business Leaders

### Invest in Defense Technology

- **Defense tech venture capital**: Consider allocating a portion of investment portfolios to defense technology funds and startups. The defense tech sector has grown from near-zero VC investment a decade ago to over $30 billion cumulative by 2024. Firms like Andreessen Horowitz (American Dynamism practice), Founders Fund, Lux Capital, Shield Capital, and Razor's Edge Ventures specialize in defense-oriented investments.
- **Dual-use companies**: Invest in companies developing technologies with both commercial and defense applications -- AI, autonomous systems, space, cybersecurity, advanced manufacturing. The dual-use model reduces risk by diversifying revenue streams beyond government contracts.
- **Defense tech IPOs**: Track defense tech companies that have gone public (Palantir, Joby Aviation, Rocket Lab) or are approaching IPO stage. Public market access provides the patient capital that defense startups need to survive the Valley of Death.

### Support the Ecosystem

- **Mentor defense tech founders**: If you have experience in government contracting, acquisition, or military operations, mentor founders who are navigating the defense market for the first time. Organizations like NSIN and TechStars Allied Space Accelerator provide platforms for this engagement.
- **Advocate for procurement reform**: Business leaders have credibility when they testify before Congress or engage with Pentagon officials on acquisition reform. Use that credibility to push for faster contracting, open architectures, and fair IP treatment.

## For Military Personnel (Active Duty and Reserve)

### Champion Innovation from Within

- **Write for professional journals**: Publish articles in *Proceedings*, *Joint Force Quarterly*, *War on the Rocks*, or *The Strategy Bridge* advocating for technology adoption and acquisition reform. Military voices carry unique credibility in the defense debate.
- **Participate in innovation programs**: Volunteer for Hacking for Defense problem sponsorship, innovation challenges (AFWERX, NavalX, Army Futures Command), or Kessel Run-style software factories.
- **Mentor non-traditional companies**: Help commercial firms understand military requirements, operational environments, and acquisition processes. The cultural gap between the military and the tech sector is a two-way problem.
- **Pursue technical education**: Take advantage of fully funded graduate education programs (AFIT, NPS, service-sponsored civilian institution attendance) to develop expertise in AI, cyber, quantum, or systems engineering.

## For Journalists and Media

### Cover Defense Technology Effectively

- **Go beyond the press release**: Defense technology coverage too often consists of repackaging Pentagon press releases or contractor marketing materials. Dig into GAO reports, FPDS contract data, and program manager testimony to assess whether programs are actually delivering capability.
- **Follow the money**: Track RDT&E budget line items, OTA awards, and SBIR transitions to understand where innovation investment is actually going versus where it is claimed to be going.
- **Cover the Valley of Death**: The most important defense technology story is not the latest prototype demo -- it is whether that prototype will ever reach the warfighter. Track the transition success rate and report on the startups that died waiting for production contracts.
- **International comparison**: Compare U.S. acquisition timelines with Israeli, Chinese, and allied approaches. Contextualize DoD programs against what competitors are achieving.

## Why This Matters

The technological competition between the United States and its adversaries is not an abstract strategic concept. It will determine:

- **Whether deterrence holds**: If the U.S. cannot credibly defend Taiwan and its allies in the Western Pacific, the risk of Chinese military action increases.
- **The character of future war**: Whether future conflicts are fought with precision, discrimination, and human oversight -- or with uncontrolled autonomous systems deployed by nations without ethical guardrails.
- **Economic competitiveness**: Defense technology investment drives innovation in AI, quantum, advanced manufacturing, and cybersecurity that has broad economic benefits. The Internet, GPS, and the microchip all originated from defense R&D.
- **Democratic values**: The nation that leads in AI and autonomous systems will have outsized influence in shaping the global norms governing these technologies. If the U.S. cedes this leadership, authoritarian states will fill the vacuum.

Individual action -- whether serving in government, building defense technology, investing in startups, voting for reform-minded representatives, or simply staying informed -- collectively determines whether the United States can sustain the technological advantage that has underpinned its security and prosperity for eight decades.
